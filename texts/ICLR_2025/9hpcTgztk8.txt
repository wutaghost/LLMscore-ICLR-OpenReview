011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Document-level relation extraction aims at inferring structured human knowledge
014            from textual documents. State-of-the-art methods for this task use pre-trained lan-
015            guage models (LMs) via fine-tuning, yet fine-tuning is computationally expensive
016
               and cannot adapt to new relation types or new LMs. As a remedy, we leverage
               the generalization capabilities of pre-trained LMs and present a novel framework
017
               for document-level in-context few-shot relation extraction. Our framework has
018
               three strengths: it eliminates the need (1) for named entity recognition and (2) for
019            human annotations of documents, and (3) it can be updated to new LMs without re-
020            training. We evaluate our framework using DocRED, the largest publicly available
021            dataset for document-level relation extraction, and demonstrate that our framework
022            achieves state-of-the-art performance. We further show that our framework actually
023            performs much better than the original labels from the development set of DocRED.
024            Finally, we conduct an extensive benchmark demonstrating the effectiveness of
025            our framework, achieving state-of-the-art results across six relation extraction
026            datasets and outperforming more than 30 baseline methods. Unlike our framework,
027
               the baseline methods have large computational overhead (e.g., from fine-tuning).
               To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to reformulate the document-level
028
               relation extraction task as a tailored in-context few-shot learning paradigm.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Relational facts are widely used to represent human knowledge (Grishman, 2019; Han et al., 2020;
      Weikum et al., 2021). With the explosion of the web, relational facts have become broadly available
034
      through large knowledge bases (KBs) (Auer et al., 2007; Bollacker et al., 2008; Suchanek et al.,
035
      2007; Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014) and thereby support many downstream tasks. Examples are
036   commonsense reasoning (Lin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021a), question answering (Das et al., 2022;
037   Luo et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022), fact checking (Huynh & Papotti, 2019; Vedula & Parthasarathy,
038   2021), and product recommendations (Wang et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2020a;b). However, relational
039   facts are not readily available in structured form but are commonly embedded in unstructured texts.
040   To this end, methods are needed for relation extraction from text.
041
      State-of-the-art methods for relation extraction leverage pre-trained language models (LMs) and
042   fine-tune them using human-annotated documents. These works can be loosely grouped into two
043   streams. One stream requires named entities (e. g., by specifying the entities of interest with a special
044   token) as input (Hu et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; Wang Xu & Zhao, 2022; Xiao
045   et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021a; 2023; Zhang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). Another stream avoids the
046   use of named entities as input and, instead, learns the detection of named entities through tailored
047   training (Cabot & Navigli, 2021; Eberts & Ulges, 2021; Giorgi et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022b; Zhang
048   et al., 2023b).
049   Yet, state-of-the-art methods based on LMs have three main drawbacks that limit their applicability
050   for relation extraction in practice. (1) State-of-the-art methods for relation extraction typically require
051   the named entities to be either given as input or to be inferred via a customized training objective.
052   This can propagate the errors into the relation extraction pipeline and thereby degrade the downstream
053   performance. (2) State-of-the-art methods for relation extraction need large amounts of human-
      annotated documents for training. However, human annotation is costly. (3) State-of-the-art methods

054
      are based on LMs that are fine-tuned. As a result, whenever a new type of relation is added to the
055   knowledge base or whenever a better LM is adopted, the entire training process must be repeated.
056   This introduces a huge computational overhead.
057
058
      There are some recent efforts that use the reasoning abilities of LMs via in-context learning for
      relation extraction (Li et al., 2023; Wadhwa et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2023). However, these are
059
      designed for sentence-level relation extraction, meaning for a small set of relation types. Due to high
060
      computational costs, their scalability to documents is limited (see Table 1). Here, we introduce a
061   novel method to leverage in-context learning for document-level relation extraction.
062
063   Our REPLM framework: We introduce a novel framework called REPLM for document-level
064
      in-context few-shot relation extraction via pre-trained language models. Our framework leverages
      the generalization capabilities of pre-trained LMs by reformulating the relation extraction task as a
065
      tailored in-context few-shot learning paradigm. Specifically, for a given document, we retrieve sets
066
      of the most relevant in-context examples of a corresponding relation and aggregate the outputs in a
067   probabilistic framework.

076   2       R ELATED W ORK
077   In-context few-shot learning of LMs: LMs have achieved superior performance in many downstream
078   tasks (Beltagy et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019;
079   OpenAI, 2023; Radford et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021; Wang et al.,
080   2023b; Wei et al., 2022a;b; Zhang et al., 2023c). Due to the large computational cost of fine-tuning
081   an LM, Brown et al. (2020) proposed in-context few-shot learning to teach an LM a new task at
082   inference time. We provide an overview of applications in Appendix A. However, we are not aware
083   of any earlier work that leveraged in-context few-shot learning for document-level relation extraction.
084   Early research on relation extraction: Early works extracted relations from text via pattern
085   extraction methods (Carlson et al., 2010; Jiang et al., 2017; Nakashole et al., 2012; Pawar et al., 2017;
086   Weikum et al., 2021) and via statistical methods (Jiang & Zhai, 2007; Lin et al., 2015; Nguyen et al.,
087   2007; Sarawagi & Cohen, 2004; Wang, 2008; Wang et al., 2014; Yu & Lam, 2010; Zhang et al.,
088   2006a;b). However, the above methods have only a limited modeling capacity, as compared to neural
089   networks (Adel & Schütze, 2017; Han et al., 2020; Katiyar & Cardie, 2017; Miwa & Bansal, 2016;
090
      Zeng et al., 2014; Zheng et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2016). As shown later, LM-based methods better
      capture the complex interactions between named entities to classify the relation. A detailed review is
091
      in the Appendix A.
092
093   Relation extraction via LM: State-of-the-art methods for relation extraction are based on fine-tuning
094   pre-trained LMs. Specifically, these methods use pre-trained LMs such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019),
095   RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), and SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) and fine-tuned them for relation
096
      extraction. For instance, Wang et al. (2019) fine-tuned BERT to classify the relation between each
      named entity pair in a sentence. There have been various follow-up works to improve performance
097
      by learning complex dependency between named entities (Hu et al., 2023; Paolini et al., 2021; Tan
098
      et al., 2022; Wang & Lu, 2020; Wang Xu & Zhao, 2022; Xiao et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021a; 2023;
099   Zhang et al., 2023a; 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). A detailed review is in Appendix A. However, these
100   works require the named entities to be annotated and provided as input at both training and test time.
101
102
      Some works relax the requirement of given named entities to facilitate processing the raw documents
      at test time. As a remedy, these works jointly learn to extract named entities and relations. Examples
103
      are SpERT (Eberts & Ulges, 2020), JEREX (Eberts & Ulges, 2021), Seq2Rel (Giorgi et al., 2022),
104
      UIE (Lu et al., 2022b), and TaG (Zhang et al., 2023b). Their drawback is that multi-step pipelines
105   with named entities recognition propagate the errors to relation extraction (Cabot & Navigli, 2021).
106   Motivated by this, Cabot & Navigli (2021) developed REBEL, an auto-regressive model based on

140   Relation extraction: The relation extraction from documents is defined as follows (Eberts &
141   Ulges, 2021; Giorgi et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022b; Tan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; Wang Xu
142   & Zhao, 2022; Xu et al., 2021a; 2023; Zhang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). Given is a set of
143   documents D = {di }M   i=1 , where M is the number of documents . For each document di , the aim
144   is to enumerate knowledge triplets {(rim , sim , oim )}R
                                                             m=1 , where rim ∈ R is a relation and sim

145   and oim are the subject and object of the relation rim , and where Ri is the number of relations in
146   di . For instance, the document “The Reality Dysfunction is a science fiction
147   novel by British writer Peter F. Hamilton . . .” yields the knowledge triplets
148
      (author, The Reality Dysfunction, Peter F. Hamilton),                              (genre, The
      Reality Dysfunction, science fiction), etc.

150   Difference to earlier works: Earlier works (see Sec. 2) generally address the above task through a
151   mandatory step for named entities detection. Specifically, the aforementioned works first need to de-
152   tect the named entities of a document di , i. e., {eij }N
                                                              j=1 , where Ni is the number of entities in di . Then
                                                               i


153   they proceed by predicting the relation(s) between each named entity pair (eij , eij ′ )j,j ′ ∈{1,...,Ni },j̸=j ′
154   among the R relations, where eij is the subject and eij ′ is the object of predicted relation(s). As such,
155   the number of predictions scales with the number of named entity pairs, i.e., it is in O(Ni2 ).
156   In-context few-shot learning in REPLM: Our REPLM framework addresses the above drawbacks
157   and approaches relation extraction as a triplet generation task. In this setup, the LM learns how to
158   generate subject(s) and object(s) of a given relation from its in-context few-shot examples. Therefore,
159   our REPLM framework does not require annotations of named entities. Our setup also facilitates the
160   flexibility of adding new relations, simply by leveraging the given context examples. Specifically, for
161   a given document di ∈ D and relation r ∈ R, we prompt a pre-trained LM to generate the knowledge
      triplets of a relation {(rim , sim , oim ) | rim = r} with no further fine-tuning.

183   4        P ROPOSED REPLM F RAMEWORK
184
185
      Approach (see Fig. 1): At a high level, our framework seeks to infer the correct knowledge triplets
      (r, s, o) from a given document di and for a given relation r. To do so, we estimate the joint probability
186
      of a subject-object pair (s, o) conditional on di and r, i.e., p(s, o | di , r). After having estimated the
187
      probability, we simply rank the candidate subject-object pairs according to their probabilities and
188   keep the top-ranked pairs as knowledge triplets. In our framework, we follow this approach but, as a
189   main innovation, leverage a pre-trained LM to approximate p(s, o | di , r).
190
191
      Learning via in-context few-shot examples: Pre-trained LMs are not explicitly trained for our
      relation extraction task, although they generally have the ability to extract information from a given
192
      context when guided properly. In our framework, we intentionally avoid the use of fine-tuning a
193
      pre-trained LM due to high computational cost and the inability of handling new relations. Instead,
194   we provide guidance for our task via in-context few-shot examples. These examples demonstrate how
195   to extract the subject-object pairs of relation r from the given context. As a result, we can approximate
196   p(s, o | di , r) ∼ p(s, o | C, di , r), where C represents the selected set of in-context examples.
197
      However, selecting only a single set of in-context examples may lead to a poor approximation of the
198
      probability p(s, o | di , r), because the selected in-context examples introduce bias in output generation
199
      (e. g., recency bias, label space of the in-context examples) as studied in prior literature (Hongjin
200   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a; Min et al., 2022b; Rubin et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023). Instead, we
201   mitigate the above bias by considering multiple sets of in-context examples. As a result, we calculate
202   the joint probability of a subject-object pair as

      where len(s) and len(o) are the number of tokens of the subject and object, respectively. Afterward,
      we compute the joint probability p(s, o | Cl , di , r) = p(s | Cl , di , r) · p(o | s, Cl , di , r).
275
276   Ranking: As the final step, we calculate p(s, o | di , r) by aggregating over the context sets Cl ,
277   l = 1, . . . , L, as in Eq. (1) and repeat this for all generated subject-object pairs. We keep all generated
278   knowledge triplets whose probability exceeds a certain threshold θ, i. e., {(r, s, o) | p(s, o | di , r) > θ}.
279   Of note, if a subject-object pair is not generated from a context set Cl , then p(s, o | Cl , di , r) = 0.
280   Note that the latter step is different from state-of-the-art methods as these methods must enumerate
281   over all possible subject-object pairs. Further, as can be seen here, our framework does not require
282   named entities as input, which is another salient difference to many of the existing works.

530   9    D ISCUSSION
531   Benefits: Our REPLM framework offers many benefits in practice: (1) REPLM eliminates the need
532   for named entity recognition pipelines in our task and thus the error propagated with it; (2) REPLM
533   does not require human annotations but leverages in-context few-shot learning; and (3) REPLM
534   offers great flexibility as it allows to incorporate new relations and new backbone LMs without
535   re-training. Our study further identifies earlier datasets, such as DocRED (Yao et al., 2019), lack
536
      comprehensiveness, and thus miss important – but correct – annotations. This may penalize correct
      methods during benchmarking, suggesting the need of more effective evaluation paradigms.
537
538   Broader Impact: Our REPLM can help bridge gaps in knowledge bases, particularly for marginalized
539   groups, improving coverage for diverse populations. However, as LM performance can vary, careful
      and responsible use is necessary when addressing societal, ethical, or sensitive content.

      In-context few-shot learning of LMs: In-context few-shot learning has been widely adopted in text
      classification tasks (Holtzman et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022a; Lu et al., 2022a; Min et al., 2022a;b;
922
      Zhao et al., 2021b) and further extended to other tasks such as question answering (Holtzman et al.,
923
      2021; Liu et al., 2022a; Min et al., 2022b), fact retrieval (Zhao et al., 2021b), table-to-text generation
924   (Liu et al., 2022a), and mapping utterances to meaning representations (Rubin et al., 2022). However,
925   we are not aware of any earlier work that leveraged the in-context few-shot learning paradigm for
926   document-level relation extraction.
927
      LMs as knowledge bases: Research has focused on probing the knowledge in LMs. For example,
928
      Petroni et al. (2019) introduced the LAMA dataset, a dataset with cloze-style templates for different
929
      relations, which allows to probe factual knowledge in LMs. Many works have been introduced to
930   achieve state-of-the-art results via prompt-tuning (Hao et al., 2022; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang,
931   2021; Liu et al., 2021b; 2022b; Newman et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2021; Poerner et al., 2020; Shin
932   et al., 2020; Zhong et al., 2021). Yet, some works further find that, when evaluated as knowledge
933   bases, LMs suffer from inconsistency (AlKhamissi et al., 2022; Elazar et al., 2021), learn shallow
934   heuristics rather than facts (Elazar et al., 2022), have inferior performance in the long tail (Kandpal
935   et al., 2022), and exhibit prompt bias (Cao et al., 2021).
936   However, we note that the above research stream is different from our work in two salient ways.
937   (1) In the above research stream, LMs are prompted to retrieve factual knowledge from its memory,
938   whereas we aim to extract the relational knowledge from the context. (2) In the above research stream,
939   LM prompts are structured as “fill-in-the-blank” cloze statement. For example, the task is to output
940   only the correct object, but where both the subject and relation are given. Instead of predicting only
941   the object, our goal is to output the entire knowledge triplet. That is, subject, relation, and object
942   must be inferred together.
943   Relation extraction via pattern-based and statistical methods: A detailed review of the different
944   methods is provided in Pawar et al. (2017) and Weikum et al. (2021), while we only present a
945   brief summary here. Early works extracted relations from text via pattern-based extraction methods.
946   Specifically, these works introduced automated methods to extract textual patterns corresponding to
947   each relation and each entity type (Carlson et al., 2010; Jiang et al., 2017; Nakashole et al., 2012).
948   Their main limitation is that the automatically constructed patterns involve many mistakes, which, in
949   turn, require human experts to examine and correct them (Han et al., 2020).
950   Another research stream focused on relation extraction via statistical methods. Examples are crafting
951   custom features for relation classification (Jiang & Zhai, 2007; Nguyen et al., 2007), designing
952   customized kernels for support vector machines(Nguyen et al., 2007; Wang, 2008; Zhang et al.,
953   2006a;b), graphical modeling and inference of relations (Sarawagi & Cohen, 2004; Yu & Lam, 2010),
954   and leveraging knowledge graph embeddings for relation prediction (Lin et al., 2015; Wang et al.,
955   2014).
956   However, the above methods have only a limited capacity in capturing complex interactions between
957   entities, as compared to state-of-the-art neural networks (Han et al., 2020). On top of that, both
958   pattern-based and statistical methods require large datasets with human annotation for training.
959
      Relation extraction via neural networks: Initial methods for relation extraction based on neural
960   network approaches made use of convolutional neural network (CNN) (Zeng et al., 2014) and long
961   short-term memory (LSTM) (Zhou et al., 2016) architectures. These works process the pre-computed
962   word embeddings and then classify the relation for the given named entity pair. Follow-up works
963   proposed joint learning of entity extraction and relation classification, again via CNN (Adel &
964   Schütze, 2017; Zheng et al., 2017) and LSTM (Katiyar & Cardie, 2017; Miwa & Bansal, 2016)
965   architectures. However, these models are not flexible enough to model the complex interactions
966   between named entities to classify the relation, as compared to LMs.
967   Relation extraction via LMs: State-of-the-art methods for relation extraction are based on fine-
968   tuning pre-trained LMs. Specifically, these methods use pre-trained LMs such as BERT (Devlin
969   et al., 2019), RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), and SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) and fine-tuned them
970   for relation extraction. For instance, Wang et al. (2019) fine-tuned BERT to classify the relation
971   between each named entity pair in a given sentence. There have been various follow-up works to
      improve performance by learning complex dependency between named entities. To achieve this,

       Wang & Lu (2020) jointly trained LSTM and BERT to get two distinct representations of the entities;
973    Zhang et al. (2021) further incorporate semantic segmentation module into the fine-tuning of BERT;
974    Zhou et al. (2021) propose adaptive thresholding and localized context pooling; Xu et al. (2021a)
975    explicitly model the dependencies between entity mentions; Paolini et al. (2021) augmented the
976    original sentences with the entity and relation types; Tan et al. (2022) use axial attention module for
977    learning the interdependency among named entity pairs; Wang Xu & Zhao (2022) propose sentence

979    entity typing, and evidence retrieval; Xu et al. (2023) improves the model performance via synthetic

       require the named entities to be annotated and provided as input at both training and test time.

1034
       Pre-processing. The original documents in the DocRED dataset are provided only in a tokenized
1035
       format, e. g., the document is represented as a list of token, where each punctuation mark and
1036   word is a different token. We follow the earlier works (Cabot & Navigli, 2021; Yao et al., 2019)
1037   and concatenate the tokens with a white space in between to construct the entire document. This
1038   approach may introduce typos in the documents; for instance, the original text “Tarzan’s Hidden
1039   Jungle is a 1955 black-and-white film ...” is reconstructed as “Tarzan ’s
1040   Hidden Jungle is a 1955 black - and - white film ...”. We initially tried to
1041   fix these typos via spelling correction libraries, such as FastPunct12 , but later found that the typos are
1042   propagated to the labels, which may impede performance and eventually comparability of our results.
1043   Therefore, we decided to follow the same pre-processing as earlier works, as it allows us to operate
1044
       on the same labels as in earlier work and thus ensures comparability of our results. Sec. G shows
       some examples of documents after the pre-processing step.

1162   place of birth, 166 out of 260 relations (i. e., the exact (relation, subject, object) triplet) in test set
1163   appear in the training set. Therefore, although the evaluation on the test is noisy, the baseline methods
1164   leverage the supervised training and they can memorize the relations from the train set at the test time.
1165   We hypothesize this as the main reason of the inferior performance of our REPLM framework on this
1166   dataset specifically, while achieving the state-of-the-art performance at all other evaluations.

       We perform an ablation study to compare the effect of using distantly-supervised vs. human-annotated
1246
       documents as in-context few-shot examples. We perform such comparison using the four variants of
1247
       our framework variants, i.e., REPLM (random fixed), REPLM (random all), REPLM (best context⊖),
1248   and REPLM (best context⊕). For methods with random in-context examples, the performance may
1249   be subject to variability across which seed is picked (whereas the performance is deterministic for
1250   the other methods), and, hence, we report the standard deviation for this subset of the methods by
1251   averaging the performance across 10 runs. Of note, to directly compare the impact of in-context
1252   examples, we deliberately considered our REPLM variants without aggregation over the multiples
1253   sets of in-context examples.
1254   Tables 13 to 24 show the comparison between distant supervision vs. human annotation. For random
1255   in-context examples (i. e., REPLM (random fixed) and REPLM (random all)), distant supervision
1256   and human annotation performs at the same level. For retrieving the semantically most similar
1257   context examples (i. e., REPLM (best context⊖) and REPLM (best context⊕)), we observe cases
1258   where distant supervision actually improves the result (e. g., P6, P155, P179). However, the overall
1259   performance is largely similar. This confirms our choice of using distantly-supervised documents as
1260   in-context examples and eliminates the need for human annotation.

       We provide the details of our REPLM implementation in this section. We use GPT-JT13 (∼6B
1407   parameters) as our pre-trained LM for in-context few-shot learning. As the number of relations
1408   to be extracted is unknown in advance, we generate 200 tokens (for comparison, each extracted
1409   triplet consumes roughly 10-15 tokens) to ensure that our pre-trained LM can generate all relations it
1410   identifies.
1411
       As we use a fixed prefix for each extracted knowledge triplet at each line (e. g., “Relation:”), we
1412
       easily identify if there is no further triplets extracted, simply from the absence of the prefix. We use a
1413   special separator token to easily parse the extracted subjects and objects. This separator is “<==>” in
1414   our experiments (which cannot be found in the original dataset and therefore cannot be confused with
1415   a natural text). We additionally inform our pre-trained LM about the task via starting our prompt
1416   with the instruction of the task. Here, we note that we have not done any prompt-tuning, since it is
1417   not the focus of this paper. For the output generation, we finally note that we use a greedy-decoding,
1418   e. g., not any sampling approach applied, which results in deterministic outputs given the input text.
1419   Example inputs and outputs can be found in Sec. G.
1420   In our REPLM framework, we retrieve the semantically most-relevant in-context examples for each
1421   dev document. For this, we encode the documents via SBERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) to
1422   calculate the embeddings and retrieve the most-relevant documents based on the cosine-similarity of
1423   the embeddings. We use the following fixed parameters in our framework (if not specified otherwise):
1424   N = 20, K = 5 L = 5, τ = 0.1, and θ = 0.2. As the sentences in sentence-level relation extraction
1425   datasets (CONLL04, NYT, and ADE) are shorter than the documents in DocRED, we used more
1426   in-context examples for these datasets, which is K = 11. The other parameters are the same as
1427
       before.
1428   We run all of the experiments of REPLM on NVIDIA Tesla V100-SXM2 32GB with a batch size of
1429   4. For DocRED, on average, each batch is processed in ∼17.80 seconds. As a result, the dev set (998
1430   documents) is processed in ∼74.17 minutes for each relation type.
1431   For REPLM variants, we use gpt-3.5-turbo and gpt-4o of OpenAI and Llama-3.1-8B and Llama-
1432   3.1-70B from Meta as our backbone LMs. Similar to our design choice with GPT-JT, we opt for
1433   deterministic outputs from these LM backbones, which is done by choosing a low temperature such as
1434   0.001. If not specified otherwise, we use exactly the same parameter configuration with our REPLM
1435   framework, specifically N = 20, K = 5 L = 5, τ = 0.1, and θ = 0.2.

       J   S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS OF OUR REPLM F RAMEWORK
2107
2108
       In our original REPLM framework, we used a fixed temperature τ and a fixed probability threshold
2109   θ as our REPLM does not require a human-annotated training documents to tune the hyperparame-
2110   ters. Tables 45 to 50 show the sensitivity of REPLM on various hyperparameter selections for all
2111   relation types individually. Overall, we observe that our REPLM framework is robust to different
2112   hyperparameter: the performance remains at the same level for most variations. (Only exception is
2113   θ = 0.5, where such high probability threshold results in discarding the correctly extracted relations.
2114   This leads to much lower recall, and therefore, F1 score.) This also explains why the performance
2115   gap between REPLM and REPLM (params adj) is small.

       We test whether our REPLM framework is actually learning to extract the relation from the document
2433
       or simply retrieving the facts from its own memory in the specified format. For this, we re-construct
2434   the sentences from CONLL04 (Roth & Yih, 2004) with fake entity names that are not mentioned
2435   anywhere in the web. As a result, we push the limits of our REPLM and test whether the model
2436   correctly extracts the relations about the entities that appear only in the input sentence. To the best of
2437   our knowledge, we are the first to design such an experiment to shed light on the learning abilities of
2438   a LM in the context of relation extraction.
2439
       Table 54 compares the performance of our REPLM based on the original dataset vs. the same
2440   dataset with fake entities. There is only a slight decrease in the overall performance with the fake
2441   entities (F1 score of 70.47 vs. 72.94), confirming that our REPLM is actually learning to extract the
2442   relations from the context. Further, Table 55 shows that our findings from REPLM are transferable to
2443   REPLM+GPT3.5, i.e., to more advanced LMs. After this confirmation, we further investigate “how”
2444   our REPLM learns to extract relations, by contrasting its behavior against the presence of adversarial
2445   in-context examples (see Appendix N).
2446   We conjecture that the slight decrease in the performance can be attributed to mainly two factors:
2447   (1) In some cases, the relation between the entities becomes unclear without knowing what these
2448   entities actually are. As can be seen from the in-context examples at Sec. M.1, it is hard to infer
2449   “work for” relation between “Entity62” and “Entity22” from “ . . . , said Lt. Entity62
2450   of the Entity22”, which mitigates the model performance. (2) The random entity names
2451   (e. g., “Entity10”, “Entity55” etc.) are not seen during the pre-training of the model. Therefore, the
2452   likelihood of generating the fake entities from the context is not the same as generating the real names
2453   that appeared during the pre-training, which can impact the performance of a LM.

       To further investigate how our REPLM learns from the in-context examples, we evaluate the effect of
2542
       adversarial in-context examples. To this end, we created the adversarial examples with the following
2543
       strategy: (1) we randomly selected the half of the documents from the distantly-supervised set, and
2544   (2) for the selected documents, we replaced the subject-object pairs of the existing knowledge triplets
2545   with random named entities of the same document, which do not share the corresponding relation.
2546   We refer to this setup as “50 % clean + 50 % adversarial”. Hence, half of the in-context examples
2547   should be adversarial for each dev document. As we do not change the content of the documents,
2548   this results in retrieving the same set of in-context examples as in our original framework (i. e., the
2549   similarity scores remain the same).
2550   For comparison, we include two other experimental setups: (1) “100 % clean” refers to our original
2551   work and (2) “50 % clean” refers to experiments with remaining distantly-supervised documents
2552   after discarding the adversarial ones. Of note, we perform all the experiments in this section with
2553   our REPLM (best context⊖) framework variation. This enables us to directly quantify the impact
2554   of the adversarial in-context examples, without any aggregation over the multiple sets of in-context
2555   examples.
2556   Table 56 shows the overall results. Furthermore, Tables 57 to 62 reports the performance across
2557   each relation type. The performance clearly drops as a result of including the adversarial in-context
2558   examples. This has two crucial implications: (1) The quality of the labels is important. Overall, we
2559   perform our experiments with distantly-supervised documents that are automatically annotated with
2560   certain heuristics to ensure quality (as explained in the main paper). If these are ignored, the resulting
2561   in-context examples can mislead the LM. (2) Our framework actually learns from the in-context
2562   examples. When it learns the relations with noisy labels, this also reflects into its performance.
2563   Further, comparing the performance between “100 % clean” and “50 % clean”, we see that the
2564
       relation extractions stays at the same level. This informs us that, if some adversarial documents exist
       in the dataset, filtering them out is a viable option and there is no need to find another method to fix
2565
       their labels.
