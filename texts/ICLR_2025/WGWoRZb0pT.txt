011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In the domain of text-to-image generative models, biases inherent in training
014            datasets often propagate into generated content, posing significant ethical chal-
015            lenges, particularly in socially sensitive contexts. We introduce FairCoT, a novel
016
               framework that enhances fairness in diffusion models through Chain-of-Thought
               (CoT) reasoning within multimodal generative large language models (LLMs).
017
               FairCoT employs iterative CoT refinement and attire-based attribute prediction
018
               to systematically mitigate biases, ensuring diverse and equitable representation
019            in generated images. By integrating iterative reasoning processes, FairCoT ad-
020            dresses the limitations of zero-shot CoT in sensitive scenarios, balancing creativ-
021            ity with ethical responsibility. Experimental evaluations across multiple models,
022            including DALL-E and various Stable Diffusion variants, demonstrate that Fair-
023            CoT significantly improves fairness and diversity metrics without compromising
024            image quality or relevance. Our approach advances ethical AI practices in genera-
025            tive modeling, promoting socially responsible content generation and setting new
026            standards for fairness in AI-generated imagery.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The advent of text-to-image generative models has revolutionized artificial intelligence, enabling
032   the synthesis of high-fidelity images from textual descriptions. These advances have unlocked new
033
      possibilities in creative expression, design, and accessibility. However, these models often inherit
      and amplify biases present in their training datasets, leading to ethical concerns—especially when
034
      generating content related to sensitive social issues. Addressing these biases is critical to ensure that
035
      AI systems are fair, inclusive, and socially responsible.
036
037   Existing approaches to mitigate bias in text-to-image models primarily focus on prompt engineer-
038   ing or adjusting model parameters. Handcrafted prompting methods (Bianchi et al., 2023; Bansal
039
      et al., 2022) rely heavily on human annotations, which are inherently subjective and can introduce
      inconsistencies (Sun et al., 2023). Moreover, these methods can be costly and sub-optimal due to
040
      the extensive manual effort required. On the other hand, debiasing techniques involving parameter
041
      fine-tuning (Gandikota et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023) or modifying text embeddings (Chuang et al.,
042   2023) are computationally intensive and often limited to open-source diffusion models. These meth-
043   ods can inadvertently affect model alignment and are typically restricted to specific types of biases
044   addressed during fine-tuning (Sun et al., 2023).
045
      To address these challenges, we introduce FairCoT, a novel method that leverages Chain-of-Thought
046
      (CoT) reasoning within multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to refine the generative process
047
      of text-to-image models. FairCoT enables models to identify and mitigate biases in their outputs by
048   generating and refining fairness-aware reasoning paths. It operates by integrating iterative reasoning
049   refinement and controlled evaluation to guide generation toward more diverse and equitable outputs.
050   Leveraging MLLMs’ reasoning capabilities, FairCoT assesses potential biases and adjusts the gen-
051   eration accordingly. During the CoT generation phase, it generates reasoning steps that consider
052   fairness constraints related to sensitive attributes (e.g., gender, race, age, and religion). Feedback
053   from the controlled evaluations refines the reasoning steps in subsequent iterations, allowing dy-
      namic adjustment of outputs based on fairness considerations. At inference, the MLLM adapts the


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      most relevant Chain-of-Thought to the new task. It then generates a set of text prompts inspired by
055   this adapted reasoning to guide the diffusion model in producing fair outputs.
056
057   This approach addresses limitations of zero-shot CoT reasoning in sensitive contexts by providing
058
      explicit, iterative fairness guidance. Unlike prompt engineering or fine-tuning methods, FairCoT
      operates at the reasoning level, making it applicable to both open and closed-source models without
059
      parameter updates. By leveraging MLLMs’ reasoning capabilities, FairCoT directly influences the
060
      generative process to align with ethical objectives, ensuring outputs are high-quality and socially
061   responsible. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
062
063        1. FairCoT Framework: We introduce FairCoT, a simple and effective method that applies
064           Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning for bias reduction in text-to-image (T2I) models. It
065           avoids retraining, works for both open and closed-source models, and preserves alignment.
066        2. Multi-Bias Generalization: FairCoT addresses multiple bias types simultaneously, in-
067           cluding sensitive attributes and objects, and generalizes well across domains.
068
           3. Improved Attribute Prediction: We present an attire-based method using CLIP for im-
069
              proving the detection of sensitive attributes like religion, making us the first to tackle reli-
070           gious bias in T2I models.
071
           4. Iterative Bootstrapping with MLLMs: We apply an iterative bootstrapping process us-
072
              ing MLLMs and CoT for debiasing, the first use reasoning for T2I models and fairness
073
              effectively.
074
075   FairCoT represents a significant advance in the pursuit of equitable and responsible AI. It is ap-
076   plicable to both open and closed-source text-to-image models and can address multiple bias types
077   simultaneously. By integrating this method with LLM-powered diffusion models like DALL-E,
078   we demonstrate its robustness and versatility. Furthermore, FairCoT can be applied upon request,
079   helping to avoid overrepresentation or underrepresentation of minorities in specific contexts.
080   The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. In Section 2, we review related work on bias
081   mitigation in generative models. Section 3 details the technical architecture of FairCoT, including
082   the iterative reasoning refinement process and the integration of attire-based attribute prediction.
083   Section 4 presents the experimental results with comprehensive analysis, and Section 5 discusses
084   the broader implications of our work and potential future directions.

098   2   R ELATED W ORK
099
100   This section discusses relevant work on bias in vision-language models, the impact of dataset biases,
101   language-driven bias mitigation, advances in large language models, and attribute prediction.
102
103   Bias in Vision-Language Models Bias in vision-language models is a significant concern, as
104   these models often inherit and amplify societal biases present in training data. Hall et al. (2023)
105   investigated gender bias in zero-shot vision-language models, revealing performance disparities and
106   calibration issues based on perceived gender in images, highlighting how language influences both
107   expanding and biasing vision tasks. Similarly, Kim et al. (2023) proposed a bias-to-text (B2T)
      method using pre-trained vision-language models to detect visual biases by generating captions


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      for misclassified images and identifying bias-indicative keywords, effectively pinpointing biases
109   without human annotation.
110
111   Shrestha et al. (2024) introduced FairRAG, focusing on mitigating biases in human generation tasks
112
      by incorporating fair retrieval methods. Their approach adjusts retrieval processes to include diverse
      and representative examples, reducing biases in generated content. On the other hand, Shen et al.
113
      (2023) proposed fine-tuning text-to-image diffusion models for fairness using a single textual in-
114
      version token, demonstrating that targeted fine-tuning can mitigate demographic biases, although it
115   may be computationally intensive and limited to specific models. Furthermore, He et al. (2024) pre-
116   sented the Iterative Distribution Alignment (IDA) method, addressing social biases in T2I diffusion
117   models by guiding the diffusion process away from biased outputs through iterative weight updates
118   based on Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence.However, these methods often require extensive com-
119   putational resources or are limited to certain models, highlighting the need for a more efficient and
120   generalizable approach to bias mitigation in T2I models.
121
122   Impact of Dataset Bias Dataset biases significantly impact vision-language models. Garcia et al.
123   (2023) highlighted widespread societal biases in datasets across tasks like image captioning, text-
124   image classification, and text-to-image generation, emphasizing the need for careful data curation.
125   Additionally, Birhane et al. (2023) showed that larger datasets can amplify biases. Janghorbani &
126
      de Melo (2023) developed the MMBias benchmark for assessing bias, contributing to debiasing
      methods using additive residuals. Moreover, the PATA dataset introduced by Seth et al. (2023)
127
      provided insights into the effect of data on bias, underscoring the necessity of addressing imbalances
128
      in training data.
129
130
      Language-Driven Approaches in Bias Mitigation Language-driven strategies have been pro-
131
      posed for bias mitigation. Chuang et al. (2023) used text embedding projection for debiasing vision-
132   language discriminative and generative models. On the other hand, Smith et al. (2023) proposed
133   dataset debiasing by enhancing datasets with synthetic, gender-balanced sets. In text-to-image mod-
134   els, Bianchi et al. (2023) suggested adding gender terms to prompts to balance gender representation
135   in images. Similarly, Bansal et al. (2022) advocated adding ethical statements to prompts to directly
136   encourage fairness.
137
138   Advances in Large Language Models Recent advances in LLMs have enhanced model reasoning
139   and debiasing capabilities. Sun et al. (2023) introduced iterative bootstrapping in Chain-of-Thought
140   (CoT) prompting to improve problem-solving capabilities in models like ChatGPT. Zelikman et al.
141   (2022) proposed Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR), enabling models to self-supervise and refine rea-
142   soning steps without additional labeled data. Furthermore, Lyu et al. (2023) focused on improving
143
      the faithfulness of reasoning steps in LLMs, addressing hallucination, and enhancing reliability in
      sensitive contexts. However, Shaikh et al. (2022) highlighted risks of zero-shot CoT reasoning in
144
      socially sensitive domains, showing that models may generate biased or harmful content without
145
      proper guidance, underscoring the need for mechanisms to minimize biased outputs.
146
147
      Attribute Prediction Attribute prediction is crucial for assessing demographic representations in
148
      generated content. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has been widely used for zero-shot image classi-
149
      fication and attribute prediction due to its ability to learn visual concepts from natural language
150   supervision. Radford et al. (2021) found high accuracy (96%) for gender classification across all
151   races using CLIP. They averaged around 93% for racial classification and approximately 63% for
152   age classification. In addition, Shen et al. (2023) adopted the eight race categories from the FairFace
153   dataset but found classifiers struggled to distinguish between certain categories. Therefore, to im-
154   prove race attribute prediction, they consolidated them into four broader classes: WMELH (White,
155   Middle Eastern, Latino Hispanic), Asian (East Asian, Southeast Asian), Black, and Indian. Han
156   et al. (2017) presented a deep multi-task learning approach for heterogeneous face attribute estima-
157   tion, emphasizing multi-task learning to improve attribute prediction accuracy and robustness across
158
      diverse populations.
159   Our work builds upon these studies by integrating Chain-of-Thought reasoning within multimodal
160   generative LLMs to enhance fairness in diffusion models. By leveraging iterative reasoning and
161   attire-based attribute prediction, we address limitations identified in previous works, contributing to
      ethical AI practices in text-to-image generation.

      In this section, we introduce FairCoT, a framework designed to enhance fairness in AI-generated im-
165
      agery by integrating Multimodal Large Language Models (LLMs) and Contrastive Language-Image
166
      Pre-training (CLIP). We also propose an attire-based method for predicting religious attributes, aim-
167   ing to improve CLIP’s attribute prediction for challenging tasks, leading to accurate automatic image
168   labeling. The framework operates in two primary phases: CoT generation and inference, each com-
169   prising several key components.

      3.1     C HAIN OF T HOUGHT G ENERATION P HASE
172

      3.1.1    I NITIAL I MAGE G ENERATION
174
175   We begin by defining a set of professions P = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pn } that are commonly depicted in
176   the working industry. These professions are chosen to cover a wide range of societal roles to en-
177   sure the generalizability of our approach, each belonging to one of the following areas: Healthcare
178   and Medical, Legal and Business, Service and Hospitality, Security and Protection, Education and
179   Information, Engineering and Technical, and Research and Analytics.
180   To represent diversity, we identify demographic attributes D = {gender, age, race, religion} that
181   are essential. For the attribute of religion, we further specify a set of religious groups R =
182   {r1 , r2 , . . . , rk }. We utilize a Multimodal Large Language Model to generate prompts that spec-
183   ify a profession. Formally, for the given set of professions P, the LLM produces a set of prompts
184   S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn }, where each prompt si is initially “n photos of pi ”.
185   The generated prompts S are then used to guide the text-to-image model to produce an initial set of
186   images I = {I1 , I2 , . . . , Im }. This stage is critical as it provides the raw data for subsequent bias
187   evaluation and refinement processes (see Figure 3).
188

192   that CLIP does not accurately predict due to their complex nature, we propose an attire-based method
193   to improve the prediction.

195   generates a detailed list of attires associated with the corre-

198   list A = {a1 , a2 , . . . , al }, where each attire aj corresponds to

200   Sikhism, kippahs for Judaism).

202   to detect the most prominent attire aj ∈ A. This is achieved

230   3.1.4     C OT DEBIASING
231   Starting with an initial Chain-of-Thought CoT0 obtained through zero-shot debiasing (Kojima et al.,
232   2022), we prompt the MLLM with r0 : “Think step by step before generating images while con-
233   sidering several races, genders, religions, and ages and treating people of these categories equally,”
234   which generates CoT0 (see Figure 3).
235
      Having the initial Chain-of-Thought CoT0 as a baseline, the model iteratively enhances fairness. In
236
      each iteration t, the MLLM refines the CoT based on the bias assessments until the images reach
237   convergence criteria. The iterative process is as follows:
238
239           1. Bias Evaluation: Compute Ht′ and CLIP-T Score at iteration t.
240           2. CoT Refinement: If (Ht′ > Ht−1  ′
                                                    ) and (CLIP-Tt > τ CLIP-Tt0 ), update CoTt to address
241              the identified biases; where τ < 1 is the acceptable drop in alignment for compromising
242              fairness.
243           3. CoT Generation: Generate CoT t using prompt rt : ”Can you think again? Consider
244              generating images of different religions, races, ages, and genders”.
245           4. Image Production: Produce new images It using the diffusion model guided by CoTt .
246
              5. Iteration: Increment t and repeat.
247
248   The iterative process continues until the fairness criteria are satisfied:
249                             Ht′ ≤ Ht′− 1 or CLIP-T Score ≤ τ CLIP-Tt0 .
250   Upon convergence, we document the detailed CoTt that describes the iterative reasoning and adjust-
251   ments made to achieve the fairness standards.

254
255   3.2.1     D EMONSTRATION P OOL AND A PPLICATION
256
      The images I that meet the fairness standards, along with their corresponding Chains-of-Thought
257
      CoT, prompts s, and professions p, are archived in a demonstration pool Dpool . This repository
258
      serves as a template for future tasks, ensuring that the learned fairness principles are replicated in
259   new contexts.
260
261   3.2.2     I NFERENCE
262
263   For a new task involving a different profession pnew , we select an appropriate CoT from Dpool based
264   on the seven areas considered for professions. The MLLM adapts this CoT to generate a new CoTnew
265   tailored to pnew (see Figure 1). We utilize the MLLM to generate prompts for the new profession
266
      inspired by the new CoTnew . The LLM produces a set of prompts S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn }, where each
      prompt si is crafted to encourage diversity in the subsequent image generation process.
267
268   The adapted prompts Snew guide the diffusion model to produce images Inew that adhere to the
269   established fairness principles. This ensures that the new images maintain the diversity and fairness
      standards set by FairCoT.

490   4.5   D ISCUSSION
491
492   Our experimental results demonstrate that FairCoT significantly enhances fairness and diversity in
493   text-to-image diffusion models across various scenarios and models. By achieving higher Normal-
494   ized Entropy scores, FairCoT effectively mitigates biases in generated images while maintaining
495   high CLIP-T scores, ensuring that image quality and relevance are not compromised. Compared
496
      with finetuning approaches, such as those proposed by Shen et al. (2023). While finetuning reduces
      biases, it requires retraining the model and may not generalize across different models or prompts.
497
      FairCoT operates at the prompt level, achieving better bias reduction while providing a flexible and
498
      model-agnostic solution that can be applied to any text-to-image diffusion model without the need
499   for additional training.
500
501   The qualitative results provided in the appendix further support these findings, showing that FairCoT
502
      generates images with diverse demographic attributes, addressing the limitations of baseline models
      that often reflect societal biases present in training data.
503
504   The ablation study underscores the importance of each component within FairCoT, highlighting
505   that iterative Chain-of-Thought reasoning and our CoT selection method are critical for achieving
506   optimal performance.
507
508   4.6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
509
      While FairCoT shows substantial improvements, certain limitations exist. The reliance on attribute
510
      classifiers assumes accurate prediction, which may vary across different demographic groups or
511
      attributes. Future work could explore integrating more robust, possibly domain-specific attribute
512   prediction models.
513
514   We acknowledge that our approach to reducing religious bias by incorporating diverse attires does
515
      not imply that attire directly signifies religion. Rather, our goal was to enhance fairness by repre-
      senting individuals in attires that are often underrepresented. This approach may not fully capture
516
      the complexity of religious identity, and we recognize it as a limitation. Future work should explore
517
      more nuanced methods for addressing religious biases without relying solely on attire representation.
518
519   Additionally, extending FairCoT to address other forms of bias, such as those related to disability,
520   socio-economic status, or intersectional attributes, would enhance its applicability. Moreover, Fair-
521
      CoT relies on MLLMs that can generate images in the training phase; therefore, further research
      on open-sourcing these models will assist in its adoption for enhancing fairness in AI-generated
522
      content.

525   5     C ONCLUSION
526
527   FairCoT is an innovative framework that mitigates biases in text-to-image generative models by
528   leveraging iterative Chain-of-Thought reasoning and attire-based attribute prediction using CLIP. It
529
      effectively reduces biases related to sensitive attributes such as gender, race, age, and religion while
      maintaining high image quality, demonstrating robustness across different models like DALL-E
530
      and Stable Diffusion. Our experimental results underscore FairCoT’s superiority over traditional
531
      debiasing strategies, especially when model parameters are inaccessible, highlighting its practicality
532   and effectiveness. By balancing bias mitigation with image quality retention, FairCoT significantly
533   promotes inclusivity and fairness in AI-generated imagery, representing a substantial advance toward
534   ethical and responsible AI.
