009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            There is a significant gap between our theoretical understanding of optimization
012            algorithms used in deep learning and their practical performance. Theoretical de-
013            velopment usually focuses on proving convergence guarantees under a variety of
014            different assumptions, which are themselves often chosen based on a rough com-
015            bination of intuitive match to practice and analytical convenience. In this paper,
016            we carefully measure the degree to which the standard optimization analyses are
017            capable of explaining modern algorithms. To do this, we develop new empirical
018
               metrics that compare real optimization behavior with analytically predicted behav-
               ior. Our investigation is notable for its tight integration with modern optimization
019
               analysis: rather than simply checking high-level assumptions made in the analysis
020
               (e.g. smoothness), we also verify key low-level identities used by the analysis to
021            explain optimization behavior that might hold even if the high-level motivating
022            assumptions do not. In general, we find that real optimizers often make progress
023            even when typical optimization analysis suggests that they should not. This high-
024            lights a need for developing new theoretical frameworks that are better aligned
025            with practice.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In optimization theory, algorithmic development and analysis requires a set of assumptions about the
031   functions we aim to optimize. These assumptions fundamentally influence the behavior of optimiza-
032   tion algorithms and their efficacy in practice. For example, Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011; McMahan &
033
      Streeter, 2010) (which later inspired Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014)) classically relies on the convexity
      assumption to provide a theoretical convergence guarantee. When the loss is non-convex, a variety
034
      of alternate assumptions are deployed, such as smoothness (e.g. a bounded Hessian) (Ghadimi &
035
      Lan, 2013; Carmon et al., 2017; Li & Orabona, 2019; Ward et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023) or “weak
036   convexity” (Davis & Drusvyatskiy, 2019; Mai & Johansson, 2020; Liu et al., 2023b). If these con-
037   ditions are not met, the convergence analyses of these algorithms may longer hold. In this paper, we
038   systematically verify these assumptions and related optimization analyses across various deep learn-
039   ing tasks using simple, computationally feasible methods. We hope that our findings will serve as
040   a guideline for future research, helping to develop theoretical frameworks that are both analytically
041   tractable and practically applicable.
042   Importantly, we do not want to just ask “do current assumptions apply to deep neural networks”.
043   Instead, we wish to ask whether the analyses based on currently prevalent techniques can predict
044   current practical performance. This is a subtly different question: it turns out that most modern
045   analyses actually rely on a few key identities. These identities are usually empirically measurable
046   from the iterates of an optimizer. In theoretical analysis, these identities are controlled via various
047   global assumptions (such as convexity or smoothness), but we instead measure directly these iden-
048   tities. This has a significant advantage: not only can it falsify the global assumptions, it can tell if
049   any different assumptions can be made that would “rescue” the analysis.
050   We propose simple, on-the-fly measures that capture how well modern analyses describe practice.
051   We measure these on a wide range of tasks, including basic convex optimization problems, image
052   classification tasks using deep residual networks, and pre-training large language models (LLMs).
053   Overall, our results suggest that most analytical techniques do not describe practical performance.
      Our work fits into a recent trend of challenging and moving past classical optimization assumptions

      Simsekli et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2020b;a); Davis et al. (2021; 2020). However, our focus is not on
055   algorithm development. Instead, we simply want to promote empirical verification of optimization
056   analysis.

      Of independent interest, we develop a new smoothness measure closely approximating the sharpness
      measure. This is an exciting finding, as our measure is computationally feasible even for very deep
059
      networks, where computing sharpness is infeasible. This allows for the use of our smoothness mea-

061   Finally, we offer alternative theoretical analyses for cases where common theoretical assumptions
062   do not hold.
063
064
      Overall, we feel that our findings motivate two actions in the research community: first, it is im-
      portant to develop new assumptions and analytical techniques to understand modern optimization.
065
      Second, we advocate for verifying any new assumptions by carefully measuring quantities that ac-
066
      tually appear in the optimization analysis rather than attempting to verify global assumptions.

101   desired inequalities hold. These are more likely to hold than the underlying assumptions, because
102   the assumptions imply the inequalities, but the reverse may not be true.
103   Empirical verification of these inequalities is made especially attractive for two reasons. First, many
104   optimization analyses actually use only a few options for the “algebraic expression” in (1): the
105   only thing that changes is the analysis of the algorithm leading to improved upper bounds. Thus,
106   by empirically measuring the degree to which the first inequality in (1) holds, we can interrogate
107   whether popular analyses strategies can explain optimization success in deep learning in way that is
      less tightly coupled to whether particular global assumptions hold or not.

193   3.1   A RE D EEP L EARNING L OSS L ANDSCAPES CONVEX ALONG OPTIMIZATION PATHS ?
194
195   In this section, we aim to examine the convexity along the paths taken by popular optimizers such as
196   Adam and SGD. To achieve this, we compute both the average and the exponential average convexity
197   gaps with respect to the previous iterates, i.e., yt = xt−1 , across various deep learning benchmarks.
198   Setting yt = xt−1 , allows us to measure convexity on a “small scale” along the optimization path,
199   rather than as a global property. The presence of any positive gap would indicates non-convexity.
200   By measuring average gaps (both avg gap and exp gap), we gain insight into whether the optimiza-
201   tion path could be in some sense “mostly” convex - i.e. whether instantaneous non-convexity is
202   essentially a “rare event”. Stochastic optimization analysis typically involves summing or averaging
203   identities derived from convexity, and so one might hope that it is possible to exploit a non-positive
204   average convexity gap.We also provide the instantaneous gap results in Section D in the Appendix.
205   Surprisingly, the convexity gap along the optimization trajectories of non-convex tasks is not consis-
206   tently negative or positive, as demonstrated in fig. 2. For instance, while the convexity gap remains
207   uniformly positive (indicating non-convnexity) during the training of ImageNet on ResNet18, the
208   optimization trajectory in the training of Bert frequently shifts between convex and non-convex
209   regions. Notably, in experiments involving CIFAR-10 and GPT-2, the convexity gap consistently
210   exhibits negative values. Similar phenomenon is also observed in (Xing et al., 2018), where they
211   demonstrated that the loss interpolation F (αxt + (1 − α)xt+1 ) of deep neural networks trained on
212
      CIFAR-10 by SGD is locally convex.
213   Negative convexity gaps in our experiments do not necessarily indicate convex loss landscapes (since
214   we only check the convexity gap at the points along the optimization trajectory) but rather suggests
215   that effective optimizers like SGD and ADAM can navigate these landscapes by finding paths that
      are in some sense “locally convex”. Further, as illustrated in Figure 2, the dataset plays a significant

294   4   M EASURING S MOOTHNESS
295
296   Smoothness assumptions plays a pivotal role in optimization theory. In convex optimization,
297   smoothness can help accelerate the training process and achieve superlinear convergence rate if
298   the loss is strictly convex or strongly convex (Nesterov et al., 2018). In non-convex optimization,
299   smoothness is the key ingredient that makes many convergence analyses possible (Ghadimi & Lan,
300   2013; Allen-Zhu & Hazan, 2016; Jain et al., 2017; Reddi et al., 2019). Although smoothness is as-
301   sumed for the majority of non-convex optimization results, it is unclear how well these smoothness
302
      conditions are satisfied in practice.
303   In fact, from a purely theoretical point of view, it may seem unlikely that the objective could be truly
304   smooth: common activation functions such as the ReLU, and common layers such as MaxPools are
305   not globally differentiable and so cannot possibly be smooth. However, one might hope that such
306   issues are essentially pathological problems that do not affect practice. In this section, we attempt
307   to measure smoothness along the real optimization trajectory in an efficient way analogous to our
308
      investigation of convexity in Section 3.
309   We will focus on the exponential average smoothness and the max smoothness defined in eq. (5)
310   since they provide insights into the smoothness level of local and global loss landscape respectively.
311
      First, we compute these measures using the optimally tuned learning rate and schedule in each deep
312   learning experiment. As we can see from fig. 4 (top), in all experiments, the smoothness constants
313   appear to be upper-bounded. However, in many cases these constants are quite large (103 to 106 ),
314   making it hard to consider the loss landscapes in these experiments to be smooth in practice. Further-
315   more, we note that smoothness correlates with changes in the learning rate scheduler. For example,
316   as the learning rate approaches zero at the end of training, the smoothness value increases, as ob-
317   served in Cifar10 with cosine decay and BERT with linear decay. Similarly, for Imagenet, where we
318   used a piecewise linear scheduler, smoothness increases whenever the learning rate decreases. This
319   observation suggests that smaller learning rates tend to result in larger smoothness values.
320   To better understand the loss landscapes, we reran all experiments with a constant learning rate (fig. 4
321   bottom). With constant learning rates, the loss landscape appeared smoother and more stable. Both
322   the max and exponential average smoothness followed a similar pattern: a rapid drop initially (except
323   for SGDM on ImageNet), followed by a consistent rise until reaching a boundary, then stabilizing.
      Adam typically achieved smaller (i.e., smoother) measures with a learning rate scheduler, while

377   eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix, hence a higher ”sharpness”. Thus, this metric and the sharpness
      are inherently related to characterizing the function’s smoothness and curvature.

461   4.3   A LTERNATIVES FOR SMOOTH NON - CONVEX OPTIMIZATION
462
      In previous sections, we observed that some common assumptions or identities used in analysis,
463
      such as convexity, smoothness, or negative update correlation, might not hold in practice. In this
464   section, we will discuss alternative frameworks that do not rely on these assumptions.
465
466   The first direction focuses on a family of weakly convex objectives (Davis & Drusvyatskiy, 2019;
467
      Mai & Johansson, 2020), where the goal is to minimize a proxy of the objectives called the Moreau
      envelope (Moreau, 1965) . In a different direction, Zhang et al. (2020b) propose employing the Gold-
468
      stein stationary point (Goldstein, 1977) as a convergence criterion that is tractable for non-smooth
469
      objectives. Later, Cutkosky et al. (2023) proposes an online-to-non-convex conversion (O2NC)
470   technique that later inspires other works on non-smooth non-convex optimization (Ahn et al., 2024;
471   Zhang & Cutkosky, 2024). The key idea of their technique is the use of random scaling: suppose st
472   is sampled i.i.d. from Exp(1), then xt+1 = xt + st ∆t satisfies
473                              Est [F (xt+1 ) − F (xt )] = Est ⟨∇F (xt+1 ), ∆t ⟩.                   (12)
474
      We refer to the update form xt+1 = xt + st ∆t where st ∼ Exp(1) i.i.d. as the update with random
475
      scaling (RS), and the update with st ≡ 1 as the update without RS. Unlike the lower bound in equa-
476   tion 10, the equality in equation 12 suggests that ⟨∇F (xt+1 ), ∆t ⟩, which we referred to as update
477   correlation with RS, is an unbiased estimator of function progress F (xt+1 ) − F (xt ) and a good
478   indicator of the training progress: we should expect F (xt ) to decrease as long as ⟨∇F (xt+1 ), ∆t ⟩
479   is negative in average.
480
      To verify if the theory holds in practice, we test SGDM and AdamW with random scaling updates
481
      and compare them to their counterparts without RS. Specifically, we measure the following three
482   properties: update correlation, update correlation with random scaling, and instantaneous loss dif-
483   ference, where the first is defined in eq. (9) and the latter two are respectively defined as
484
                update corr RSt = ⟨∇f (xt , zt ), ∆t−1 ⟩, loss difft = f (xt , zt ) − ft (xt−1 , zt ). (13)
485
      Note that if the update does not have random scaling applied, then update corr RSt = update corrt .

532   6   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   We address the critical question of whether modern analyses in stochastic optimization theory align
535   with practice. To this end, we empirically measure key quantities that are commonly used in theory
536   across a diverse range of machine learning benchmarks. Our results indicate that, in most cases,
537   these commonly assumed identities do not hold in practice. Further, we provide comparisons be-
538   tween the behaviors of SGD and Adam across various important properties. We hope that our
539   experiments results can contribute to a better understanding of what enables practical optimization,
      as well as motivate more rigorous empirical verification of optimization analyses in the future.
