009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have achieved great success in a wide variety of
012            graph-based learning applications. To expedite training for large-scale graphs, dis-
013            tributed GNN training has been proposed using sampling-based mini-batch training.
014            However, such a traditional distributed GNN training approach is not applicable
015            to emerging GNN learning applications with geo-distributed input graphs, which
016
               require the data to be kept within the site where it is generated to protect privacy.
               On the other hand, federated learning (FL) has been widely used to enable privacy-
017
               preserving training under data parallelism. However, because of cross-client links in
018
               the aforementioned geo-distributed graph data, applying federated learning directly
019            to GNNs incurs expensive cross-client neighbor sampling and communication costs
020            due to the large graph size and the dependencies between nodes among different
021            clients. To overcome these challenges, we propose a new mini-batch and sampling-
022            based federated GNN algorithmic framework called Swift-FedGNN that primarily
023            performs efficient parallel local training and periodically conducts time-consuming
024            cross-client training. Specifically, in Swift-FedGNN, each client primarily trains
025            a local GNN model using only its local graph data, and some randomly sampled
026            clients periodically learn the local GNN models based on their local graph data
027
               and the dependent nodes across clients. We theoretically establish the conver-
               gence performance    of Swift-FedGNN and show that it enjoys a convergence rate
028
               of O T −1/2 , matching the state-of-the-art (SOTA) rate of sampling-based GNN

029
               methods, despite operating in the challenging FL setting. Extensive experiments on
030
               real-world datasets show that Swift-FedGNN significantly outperforms the SOTA
031            federated GNN approaches with comparable accuracy in terms of efficiency.

036   1) Background and Motivation: Graph neural networks (GNNs) have received increasing attention
037   in recent years and have been widely used across various applications, such as social networks (Deng
038   et al., 2019; Qiu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019a), recommendation systems (Ying et al., 2018;
039
      Wang et al., 2019b;d), traffic prediction (Cui et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019), drug
      discovery (Wang et al., 2022b; Do et al., 2019; Fout et al., 2017), and disease prediction (Ghorbani
040
      et al., 2022; Kazi et al., 2023; Li & Zhang, 2024). GNN learns the high-level graph representations
041
      by iteratively aggregating the neighboring features of each node, which is then used for downstream
042   tasks, such as node classification (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Hamilton et al., 2017), link prediction (Yao
043   et al., 2023b; Zhang & Chen, 2018), and graph classification (Zhang et al., 2018; Bacciu et al., 2018).
044
045
      However, real-world graph datasets can be extensive in scale (e.g., Microsoft Academic Graph (Wang
      et al., 2020) with over 100 million nodes) and generated in a geo-distributed fashion (Yao et al., 2023a).
046
      Similar to traditional datasets (e.g., images), graph datasets may be collected across multiple geo-
047
      distributed sites/devices and stored locally. Collecting the entire dataset onto a single site/device not
048   only incurs prohibitively high communication costs, but may also violate data protection regulations.
049   Unlike the traditional datasets where data is mutually independent, the nodes in graph data are usually
050   dependent (shown as the links between nodes in Figure 1). Such large-scale real-world graph datasets
051   often exceed the memory and computational capabilities of a single device (e.g., GPU), which leads
052   to a compelling need for developing distributed GNN training with multiple devices or machines (Fey
053   & Lenssen, 2019; Zheng et al., 2020). A common paradigm in distributed GNN training involves
      subgraph sampling (Zeng et al., 2020) and mini-batch training on each device (Luo et al., 2022). In

067   multi-hop neighbor features as subgraphs, construct subgraphs as mini-batches, and then train on
068   these mini-batches in parallel. However, due to the aforementioned data privacy constraints, this
069   distributed GNN training paradigm is not directly applicable to geo-distributed graphs, as features
070   cannot be directly shared among clients.
071
      Meanwhile, federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2021; Karimireddy et al.,
072
      2020), which has emerged as a promising learning paradigm, enables collaborative training of a
073   model using geo-distributed traditional datasets under the coordination of a central server. However,
074   applying FL to geo-distributed graph data is highly non-trivial due to the dependencies between
075   the nodes in a graph and the fact that the neighbors of the node may be located on different clients,
076   which we refer to as “cross-client neighbors” (shown as the dashed links between nodes in Figure 1).
077   Ignoring the cross-client neighbors as in (Wang et al., 2022a; He et al., 2021b) would degrade the
078   performance of the models and prevent them from reaching the same accuracy as the models trained
079   on a single device/machine, which is due to the information loss of the cross-client neighbors.
080   2) Technical Challenges: A naive solution to federated GNN is to leverage the server as an interme-
081   diary to perform subgraph sampling and part of the training operation (i.e., neighbor aggregation) to
082   protect data privacy. For example, consider a country that has many hospitals and one medical admin-
083   istrative center and needs to investigate a healthcare problem in the whole country (e.g., infection
084   prediction) (Zhang et al., 2021). The residents may go to different hospitals for healthcare because of
085   various reasons, e.g., the locations of the hospitals. Their healthcare data (e.g., personal information,
086   and patient interactions) would be stored locally only at the hospitals they visit, and such data cannot
087
      be directly shared among different hospitals due to privacy concerns and conflicts of interest. Note
      that in a graph, the patients here are the nodes and the patient interactions are the edges, and the
088
      graphs located at different hospitals may have cross-hospital edges and thus have cross-hospital
089
      neighbors. In this situation, the medical administrative center can serve as the server in federated
090   GNN because it is trusted and thus has access to the graph data located at different hospitals.
091
092   However, this method introduces significant sampling and communication overhead (as shown in
093
      Figure 1), as the server needs to communicate with all clients to perform subgraph sampling and
      neighbor aggregation for each client sequentially. Figure 2 illustrates the per-iteration time breakdown
094
      of local training (i.e., training only on the local graph of the client) versus cross-client training (i.e.,
095
      training using both the client’s local graph and the cross-client neighbors) on the Amazon product co-
096   purchasing dataset (Leskovec et al., 2007) 1 . As observed from the figure, cross-client sampling and
097   data communication time dominate the total time for cross-client training, making it five times slower
098   than local training. Therefore, it is critical to mitigate the communication overhead in cross-client
099   training to enable efficient federated learning on GNNs.
100
      While some prior works ignore the information of the cross-client neighbors (He et al., 2021a)
101
      or assume overlapping nodes between different clients (Wu et al., 2021) (may not hold for geo-
102   distributed graphs), other prior works (Zhang et al., 2021; Du & Wu, 2022; Yao et al., 2023a) address
103   the information loss of the cross-client neighbors by facilitating the exchange of such information
104   between clients. However, these approaches may lead to significant sampling and communication

      The main results and technical contributions of this paper are as follows:
115
116
      • We develop a new communication- and sample-efficient mini-batch sampling-based federated GNN
         algorithm called Swift-FedGNN to train GNNs on geo-distributed graphs in a federated fashion.
117
         To reduce the sampling and communication overhead, the clients in Swift-FedGNN primarily
118
         conduct the efficient local training in parallel and some sampled clients only occasionally and
119      periodically perform the time-consuming cross-client training. The information loss of the cross-
120      client neighbors in the federated setting is alleviated via cross-client training. Thanks to our use of
121      different mini-batch training nodes at each iteration, the clients do not need to store the cross-client
122      neighbor information, which significantly reduces the memory overhead. To further reduce the
123      communication cost, the cross-client neighbor information is aggregated at remote clients before
124      communicating to the server and accumulated one more time before transferring to the training
125      client. This special design further offers the benefit of helping preserve data privacy since the
126      information of each node is not leaked.
127   • We conduct rigorous theoretical convergence performance analysis for Swift-FedGNN. It is worth
128      noting that the convergence analysis of our Swift-FedGNN is highly non-trivial. Unlike deep
129      neural networks (DNNs), the stochastic gradients in GNNs are biased, which poses significant
130
         challenges on the theoretical analysis of Swift-FedGNN’s convergence guarantees. Moreover, the
         structural entanglement in GNNs (i.e., the interleaving of neighbor aggregations and non-linear
131
         transformations across multiple layers) further complicates the performance analysis. In stark
132
         contrast to existing works in the literature that made strong assumptions on the biases of stochastic
133      gradients (e.g., the unbiased stochastic gradient assumption in (Chen et al., 2018) and the consistent
134      stochastic gradient assumption in (Chen & Luss, 2018), etc.), for the first time in the literature,
135      we are able to bound the stochastic gradient approximation errors rather than resorting to these
136      unrealistic assumptions in practice. Such results could also be of independent theoretical interests.
137   • Given the biased stochastic gradients in GNNs that arise from the missing cross-client neighbors
138      and the neighbor sampling process, we reveal an interesting theoretical insight that the stochastic
139      gradient approximation errors are correlated with the structure of GNNs. More specifically, our
140      theoretical analysis quantifies and characterizes a positive correlation with the number of layers in
141      the networks. We note that this is a new finding that is unique to federated GNN training. Lastly,
142      by putting  the above insights together, we show that Swift-FedGNN achieves a convergence rate of
143      O T −1/2 , which matches the state-of-the-art (SOTA) convergence rate of sampling-based GNN
144      methods (hence low communication and sample complexities), despite operating in the far more
145
         challenging FL setting with much less frequent information exchanges among the clients.

147   2    R ELATED WORK
148
      In this section, we provide an overview on distributed GNNs and offer a comprehensive comparison
149
      with the most relevant work on federated GNNs.
150
151   1) Distributed Graph Neural Networks: Distributed GNN training framework (e.g., DGL’s Dist-
152   DGL (Wang et al., 2019c; Zheng et al., 2020), Pytorch Geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019), Ali-
153
      Graph (Zhao et al., 2019) and Dorylus (Thorpe et al., 2021)) have been developed to train large-scale
      graph datasets that exceed the storage capacities of a single device. Each worker on the device
154
      constructs mini-batches via cross-device sampling and communication, trains in parallel, and syn-
155
      chronizes the model. However, in distributed GNN training, extensive graph sampling and data
156   communication can account for up to 80% of the total training time, substantially slowing the training
157   process (Gandhi & Iyer, 2021). Considerable efforts have been made to optimize distributed GNN
158   training, including employing strategic graph partitioning to minimize edge cuts between graph
159   partitions (Zheng et al., 2020), implementing static or dynamic node feature caching (Liu et al., 2023;
160   Zhang et al., 2023), enhancing communication strategies (Cai et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2022), and
161   utilizing various parallel training schemes (Gandhi & Iyer, 2021; Wan et al., 2022; Du et al., 2024).
      However, the majority of these techniques are not directly applicable to geo-distributed graphs due


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to privacy concerns, as they typically involve operations that require access to or the transfer of
163   graph data across different training devices. To our knowledge, LLCG (Ramezani et al., 2022) is the
164   only distributed GNN training framework that avoids transferring node features between workers,
165   making it potentially applicable to geo-distributed graphs. Every worker in LLCG trains only on
166   its local graph partitions. To address the missing information from cross-device neighbors, LLCG
167   employs central server to periodically perform full-neighbor training with neighbor aggregation over
168   all workers. However, this method suffers significant communication overhead on the server end, as
169   the server needs to communicate with all workers to perform the full-neighbor training.
170   2) Federated Graph Neural Networks: To date, the research on federated GNNs remains in its
171   infancy and results in this area are quite limited. In (He et al., 2021a), it is assumed that graphs are
172   dispersed across multiple clients and the information of the cross-client neighbors is ignored, which
173   does not align with the real-world scenarios and would degrade the performance of the trained model.
174   In (Wu et al., 2021), it is assumed that the clients’ local graphs have overlapped nodes and the edges
175   are distributed, which may not be true in real-world situations. The authors in (Zhang et al., 2021)
176   mitigate the information loss of the cross-client neighbors by exchanging such information in each
177
      training round. However, this approach incurs considerable communication overhead and exposes
      private node information to other clients. Although the algorithm in (Yao et al., 2023a) exchanges the
178
      information of the full cross-client neighbors only once before the training to supplement the missing
179
      information from the cross-client neighbors, it is not applicable to large-scale graphs because it uses
180   the full graph for training and incurs significant memory overhead on each client. Note that this
181   one-time communication only works for full graph training and is not suitable for the situations in
182   which clients sample different mini-batches of training nodes in each iteration since the cross-client
183   neighbors of these training batches are different. Furthermore, the homomorphic encryption used
184   in (Yao et al., 2023a) would significantly increase the communication cost, which is at least several
185   times higher than communication without homomorphic encryption.
186   The most related work to ours can be found in (Du & Wu, 2022), where the authors used sparse
187   cross-client neighbor sampling to supplement the lost information of the cross-client neighbors and
188   reduce the communication overhead. Each client periodically samples the cross-client neighbors
189   and exchanges the information of the sampled neighbors with other clients. In the remaining
190   iterations, the clients reuse the most recent sampled cross-client neighbors, which requires additional
191   cache for saving transferred graph data, thereby increasing memory overhead. However, as training
192   progresses, the frequency of information exchange increases, leading to higher communication costs.
193   Additionally, they relaxed the privacy constraint to allow the transfer of the graph data between clients
194
      directly. Reusing the same sampled data across multiple iterations would cause additional bias, and
      thus degrading the performance of the model. In contrast, our proposed Swift-FedGNN method limits
195
      cross-client training to a subset of sampled clients and avoids direct graph data exchange between
196
      clients by offloading certain operations to the central server. Before communication with the training
197   clients, cross-client neighbor information is aggregated twice: first at the remote clients and then on
198   the server—helping to preserve data privacy and significantly reduce communication costs. Since
199   clients perform local training in the remaining iterations, cross-client neighbor information does not
200   need to be stored, thereby reducing memory overhead.

443   Three important remarks on Theorem 5.6 are in order: (1) When choosing I = 1 and K = M ,

448

449

450
      increasing I or decreasing K would increase the residual error due to more information loss of the

      7                                    C ONCLUSION
532   In this paper, we proposed the Swift-FedGNN algorithm, which is a mini-batch-based and sampling-
533   based federated GNN framework, for efficient federated GNN training. Swift-FedGNN reduces the
534   cross-client neighbor sampling and communication overhead by periodically sampling a set of clients
535   to conduct the local GNN training on local graph data and cross-client neighbors, which is time-
536   consuming. The rest clients in these periodical iterations and all the clients in the remaining iterations
537   perform efficient parallel local GNN training using only  local graph data. We theoretically proved that
538   the convergence rate of Swift-FedGNN is O T −1/2 , matching the SOTA rate of sampling-based
539   GNN methods, even in the more challenging federated settings. We conducted extensive numerical
      experiments on real-world graph datasets and verified the effectiveness of Swift-FedGNN.

878   C.2   A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS
879
880   Implementation and testbed. We implement Swift-FedGNN using Python on DGL 2.0.0 (Wang
881   et al., 2019c) and PyTorch 2.2.1 (Paszke et al., 2019) with 1241 LoC. Our implementation includes a
882
      custom GPU-based sampler built on top of DGL’s native sampler, which is designed to sequentially
      sample local and remote neighbors for each client at every layer. Additionally, we customized the
883
      GraphSAGE layer (Hamilton et al., 2017) to facilitate model-parallel training within Swift-FedGNN .
884
      In this setup, the server handles the sampling and aggregation of node features and intermediate
885   activations, while the clients are responsible for executing the nonlinear computations associated with
886   the GraphSAGE layer.
887
888
      We simulate a real-world federated learning scenario using a single machine equipped with NVIDIA
      Tesla V100 GPUs and 64GB memory. In our setup, both the clients and the server operate on the
889
      GPU, and data communication between them is simulated using shared memory. We monitor the
890
      data transfer size between the server and clients and set a simulated cross-client network bandwidth
891   at 1Gbps, aligning with real-world measurements reported in (Yuan et al., 2022).
892
893   GNN Model. We train a two-layer GraphSAGE model with a hidden dimension of 256. Uniform
894
      sampling is employed for neighbor sampling, with fan-outs—i.e., the number of sampled neigh-
      bors—set according to the official training script provided by the DGL team. The fan-outs for both
895
      the ogbn-products dataset and the Reddit dataset are set to be [15, 10]. The training mini-batch size is
896
      set at 256. For optimization, we use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and a weight
897   decay of 5 × 10−4 .
