011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a novel self-supervised feature learning method using Vision Trans-
014            formers (ViT) as the backbone, specifically designed for object detection and in-
015
               stance segmentation. Our approach addresses the challenge of extracting features
               that capture both class and positional information, which are crucial for these
016
               tasks. The method introduces two key components: (1) a positional encoding tied
017
               to the cropping process in contrastive learning, which utilizes a novel vector field
018            representation for positional embeddings; and (2) masking and prediction, simi-
019            lar to conventional Masked Image Modeling (MIM), applied in parallel to both
020            content and positional embeddings of image patches. These components enable
021            the effective learning of intertwined content and positional features. We evalu-
022            ate our method against state-of-the-art approaches, pre-training on ImageNet-1K
023            and fine-tuning on downstream tasks. Our method outperforms the state-of-the-
024            art SSL methods on the COCO object detection benchmark, achieving significant
025            improvements with fewer pre-training epochs. These results suggest that better
026
               integration of positional information into self-supervised learning can improve
               performance on the dense prediction tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In recent years, self-supervised learning (SSL) methods (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Caron
032   et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021; Zbontar et al., 2021; Ermolov et al., 2021) for
033   image feature extraction have advanced significantly. These approaches enable feature extraction
034   from unlabeled images and, with larger training datasets, have improved performance in various
035   downstream tasks (Deng et al., 2009; Lin et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2017; Geiger et al., 2012; Wu
036   et al., 2015).
037   While early methods did not restrict the scope of downstream tasks, targeting a broad range from
038   image classification to dense prediction tasks such as semantic segmentation, recent years have seen
039   a shift toward developing SSL methods tailored to specific downstream tasks. This shift stems from
040   the recognition that the features required for image classification, which depend on global image-
041   level representations, differ significantly from those necessary for dense prediction tasks, where
042   pixel-level or patch-level features play a crucial role.
043   This study builds on recent research trends, focusing specifically on object detection (OD) and in-
044   stance segmentation (IS) as downstream tasks. These tasks require the precise identification of
045   individual object instances within an image, which necessitates extracting appropriate features from
046   localized regions such as pixels, patches, or subregions. The core focus of this research is on effec-
047   tively integrating both the content information of these local regions and their positional information
048   within the image into feature representations, as these are arguably crucial for OD and IS tasks.
049   Among SSL methods, contrastive learning is an early and foundational approach that continues to
050   be widely adopted. In this method, two random crops are taken from a single image, augmented
051   with random transformations, and the feature representations are trained to be similar in the feature
052   space. This approach can be interpreted as focusing on learning position-invariant features for the
053   entire image. Subsequently, pixel-level contrastive learning was introduced to better suit dense
      prediction tasks. Besides using crops, these methods pair individual pixels and train their features to


                                                        1

054
      be similar. A key challenge in this approach lies in determining which pixels to pair. To address this,
055   various strategies have been proposed, including methods based on proximity in feature space (Su
056   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2022a)), methods relying on geometric positional correspondence (Yun et al.,
057   2022; Lebailly & Tuytelaars, 2023), and hybrid approaches that balance these two criteria (Bardes
058   et al., 2022; Lebailly et al., 2024; Stegmüller et al., 2023).
059
      Another line of research focuses on Masked Image Modeling (MIM) (Bao et al., 2022; He et al.,
060
      2022; Zhou et al., 2022a), a distinctly different approach. This method, designed to use with Vi-
061   sion Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), partitions an image into patches and randomly
062   masking a subset of them. The model is trained to reconstruct the masked patches based on the
063   context provided by the unmasked ones. Masked patches as reconstruction targets can be pixels as
064   in MAE (He et al., 2022) or patch-level latent features as in iBOT (Zhou et al., 2022a). MIM is re-
065   garded as an effective way to learn feature representations that seamlessly combine both the content
066   information and positional information of each patch.
067   In this paper, we propose a novel SSL method specifically designed for OD and IS as downstream
068   tasks, building on the contrastive learning framework. These tasks demand both content information
069   and positional information to accurately classify object instances while distinguishing them from one
070   another. Our method focuses on extracting feature representations that seamlessly integrate these
071   two types of information. Although our motivation is similar to recent works such as DropPos (Wang
072   et al., 2024) and LOCA (Caron et al., 2024), our approach introduces two novel components that
073   fundamentally redefine how positional information is utilized during training.
074   The first is the use of positional encoding tied to the cropping process in contrastive learning; see
075   Fig. 1. In conventional SSL (Caron et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2024), the posi-
076   tion encoding is not aligned with the cropping, meaning the same position embeddings are applied
077   whether processing the full image or a cropped sub-image. We propose representing positional en-
078   coding as a vector field with the same dimensions as the input image, which is then cropped in the
079   same manner as the image and sampled on a regular grid, yielding a set of position embeddings of
080   the patches. They are subsequently combined with the content embeddings of their corresponding
081
      image patches.
082   The second component is that, unlike previous MIM (Bao et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022; He et al.,
083   2022), which apply masking only to the image content embeddings of patches, our method also
084   applies it to position embeddings. In our approach, after the input image is patchified into a set
085   of patches before being fed into the ViT, masking and prediction are performed independently on
086   both their content embeddings and the position embeddings. The underlying expectation is that
087
      by predicting the masked positional information from the remaining positional and content infor-
      mation, and vice versa, the model can extract features that intertwine both the image content and
088
      positional information. It is important to note that this specialized treatment of positional embed-
089
      dings is applied only during training, allowing the standard positional embedding method of ViT to
090   be seamlessly employed during inference.
091
092   We experimentally compare the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art approaches on the
093
      COCO detection dataset (Lin et al., 2014) in the standard setting, i.e., pre-training on ImageNet-
      1K (Deng et al., 2009) and fine-tuning on COCO. The results show that the proposed method
094
      achieves significant performance improvements in the downstream tasks of OD and IS, demon-
095
      strating the effectiveness of our approach.

185   3     P ROPOSED METHOD
186
187   3.1     O UTLINE
188
189   The objective is to develop a SSL method tailored for object detection (OD) and instance segmen-
190   tation (IS). Models pre-trained using this method are expected to efficiently extract the necessary
191   features for these downstream tasks and deliver high accuracy with minimal fine-tuning. Following
192   recent studies on SSL methods, we adopt ViT as the backbone for image feature extraction.
193   As discussed in Sec. 1, OD and IS require isolating object instances in an image, making it essential
194   to have feature representations that effectively integrate both content and positional information. To
195   address this, the proposed method builds on existing approaches while introducing mechanisms to
196   improve the treatment of positional information in feature learning. Specifically, two key compo-
197   nents are introduced. The first is a positional encoding method that aligns with cropping in image-
198   level contrastive (or joint-embedding) techniques. The second is a patch-level masking approach that
199   masks not only image content but also positional information, incorporating both into the prediction
200   target. Each of these components is detailed below.
201
202   3.2     P OSITION ENCODING LINKED WITH IMAGE CROPPING
203
204   3.2.1    BASIC METHOD
205
206   In conventional contrastive methods, two cropped regions from the input image are treated as if
207   they were complete, independent images, and positional embeddings are applied as such (Fig. 1).
208   Consequently, the positional and size information of the cropped regions within the original image is
209   completely discarded, which can hinder the accurate identification of object instances. To overcome
210   this limitation, we propose a method that crops the positional information embedded in the original
211   image alongside its RGB content.
212   The details are as follows. ViT divides the input image x into a fixed number (N ) of patches, which
213   are then embedded via a linear transformation into a sequence of vectors {xi }N     i=1 . The position
214   of each patch within the image is encoded by a positional vector pi , which is added to the patch
215   embedding xi to form xi + pi . The resulting sequence {xi + pi }N  i=1 is then fed into the input layer
      of ViT.

237   3.2.2    P OSITION AND SCALE AUGMENTATION
238
      By adopting the above approach, it becomes possible to integrate the position and size of cropped
239   views within the image into the feature representation. However, this method raises two concerns.
240   First, while the approach encodes absolute positional information, the identification of object in-
241   stances should typically rely only on the relative positional information between the two views; see
242   Fig. 1. Learning absolute positions directly and becoming overly dependent on them may lead to
243   unintended consequences.
244
      Second, there is a notable difference in the spatial distribution and size of objects between Ima-
245   geNet (Deng et al., 2009) images and those used in OD/IS tasks (e.g., COCO). In ImageNet, objects
246   generally occupy a large, centralized region of the image, whereas in COCO, multiple objects of
247   varying sizes appear across different parts of the image. Neglecting this distinction could result in
248   performance issues.
249
      To address these issues, we apply data augmentation over pu and pv , by randomly shifting and
250
      scaling them together within P; see Fig. 2(a). Specifically, let A represent the smallest bounding
251   rectangle that encloses pu and pv , as shown in Fig. 2(a). A coordinate transformation ts , consisting
252
                                                                        p
      of scaling and translation, is applied to A. The scaling factor is s/|A|, where |A| denotes the area
253   of the region. The parameter s is randomly sampled from a Beta distribution, as will be discussed in
254   Sec. 4.3. The translation (displacement) is randomly sampled from a uniform distribution, ensuring
255   that the new cropped regions remain within the hypothesized input image. With the application of
256   ts , the positional encodings undergo combined transformations, with the cropped fields becoming
257   pu ← ts (tu (P)) and pv ← ts (tv (P)).
258
      This position and scale augmentation is expected to reduce excessive reliance on absolute positional
259   information within the image and alleviate biases associated with the object scale distribution in
260   ImageNet.

      3.3     M ASKED P OSITION P REDICTION
263
264   The introduction of the above positional encoding method broadens the range of options for de-
265   signing ‘pretext tasks’ (i.e., loss functions) during training. In particular, we propose utilizing po-
266   sitional information as a target for Masked Image Modeling (MIM) (Bao et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
267   2022a; He et al., 2022). Specifically, we extend the approach employed in methods like iBOT and
268   DINOv2—which is traditionally applied exclusively to content embeddings—to include positional
269   embeddings. This extension aims to enhance feature representation learning, with a particular focus
      on fostering a more effective integration of content and positional information.

      The details are as follows. Recall that in our method, the input vector sequence is represented as
271   {ui + pu,i }Ni=1 , where the positional encoding depends on the cropping of the view u. Our masked
272   position prediction works as follows: as in MIM, vectors from the input sequence {ui + pu,i }N  i=1 are
273   randomly selected, and the position codes of the selected vectors are masked. Specifically, if ui +pu,i
274   is selected, it is modified as ui + e[POSMASK] using a newly introduced special token e[POSMASK] . The
275   resulting masked sequence is then fed into the ViT.
276
      Through preliminary experiments, we observed that when masking position embeddings, selecting
277
      patches in a cross-shaped pattern produces better results compared to the box-wise selection used in
278   iBOT and DINOv2 for Masked Image Modeling (MIM) with content embeddings. In those methods,
279   patches are randomly selected in rectangular boxes, and all patches within each box are masked until
280   the designated mask ratio ρ is achieved2 . For further details, refer to Sec. 4.3.
281

      Dense contrastive learning Dense contrastive learning (O Pinheiro et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021;
496
      Xie et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022) focuses on learning pixel-level representations rather than
497   image-level ones (Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020), with the goal of improving
498   performance on dense prediction downstream tasks such as segmentation and detection. The chal-
499   lenge in pixel-level SSL (Yun et al., 2022; Su et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022a; Lebailly et al., 2024;
500   Bardes et al., 2022; Stegmüller et al., 2023; Lebailly & Tuytelaars, 2023) is positive samples match-
501   ing problem. Their approaches can be summarized as either similarity-based or position-based, or
502   both. Although Lebailly et al. (2024); Stegmüller et al. (2023) also track the positions of cropped
503   views in the original image, they still use the conventional position encoding. Therefore, they do not
504   predict relative positions between views and focus only on visual content.
505
506   Self-supervised learning with position prediction Recently, several studies (Wang et al., 2024;
507
      Caron et al., 2024), have aimed to improve performance on dense prediction tasks by incorporating
      position prediction tasks, which have long been known as pretext tasks in SSL (Noroozi & Favaro,
508
      2016), into the SSL methods mentioned above. One such method in this line of work is Drop-
509
      Pos (Wang et al., 2024), an extension of MAE. In addition to the core principle of MAE, which
510   masks and predicts the content embedding of image patches, DropPos introduces a task where the
511   positional embedding of the patches is ‘dropped’ and predicted. Specifically, 75% of the tokens
512   (ui + pi ) are removed, and MAE is applied to the remaining 25%. Among these, 75% (i.e., 18.75%
513   of the total tokens) have their positional embeddings pi dropped, leaving only ui as input. The task
514   is to predict the dropped pi in this configuration. LOCA is an SSL method that processes two views
515   of the input image (a reference view and a smaller query view with overlap) and aims to predict the
516   query view position in the reference view coordinate by a single cross-attention layer. Within the
517   cross-attention layer, it has to identify the overlap parts and predict their positions correctly. In these
518
      work, position prediction is formulated as an 1D classification problem:
519                                        Lloc = one hot(i)T log P loc (xi ),                                  (7)
520
                                                            loc
521   where one hot(i) is a location indicator, and P (·) projects patch tokens (or masked tokens in
522
      DropPos) to a probability distribution over N dimensions.

524   6     S UMMARY
525
526   We presented a novel self-supervised learning method that learns pre-trained weights optimized
527   for object detection and instance segmentation. The method introduces two key components. The
528   first is a position encoding aligned with cropped views in a contrastive learning setting. This is
529   achieved using a position embedding field, where embedding vectors are sampled on a regular grid
530   corresponding to the geometry of the cropped view in the input image. Combined with the pro-
531
      posed position encoding augmentation, which can be seamlessly integrated into existing SSL data
      augmentation pipelines, this approach leads to significant improvements on the COCO benchmark
532
      compared to DINOv2 (reproduced by us on ImageNet-1K). The second component is the simulta-
533
      neous masking and prediction of position and content embeddings, further enhancing performance
534   on the COCO benchmark. Our method also performs comparably to the state-of-the-art LOCA on
535   the ADE20K dataset, where LOCA is specially tuned for this task. Lastly, in Appendix A, we
536   analyze the statistics of attention maps from the pre-trained model and also visualize them across
537   diverse input images, demonstrating that our method produces instance-level attention maps much
538   more effectively than other state-of-the-art methods. We hope this study sheds light on the potential
539   of positional encoding in contrastive learning, an area that remains underexplored in the research
      community.

718   Existing SSL methods, including ours, incorporate positional information into feature learning in
719   various ways. Beyond evaluating performance on downstream tasks, we can analyze how attention
720   to patches (tokesn) is spatially distributed within the layers of the ViT, providing insights into the
721   differences between methods. We refer to this spatial distribution as “patch attention” here. In
722   the following, we use pre-trained models from each method, prepared using the same approach as
723   in the previous experiments, and evaluate them. All experiments are conducted using a ViT-B/16
724   backbone, with input images standardized to 480 × 480.
725
726   A.1   PATCH ATTENTION DIVERSITY
727
728
      Following Park et al. (2023), we first examine the diversity of attention maps and the effective
      receptive field size. It should be noted that while Park et al.’s analysis uses ImageNet-1K images,
729
      we use COCO images as described above. There are two metrics involved (for details, refer to Park
730
      et al. (2023)): normalized mutual information (NMI) (Strehl & Ghosh, 2002) and mean average
731   distance (MAD) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). NMI is the mutual information between a query token
732   q and a key token k, based on the joint distribution p(q, k) = π(k | q)p(q), where π(k | q) is the
733   softmax-normalized attention from q to k, and p(q) is assumed to be uniformly distributed across
734   the image. Intuitively, this measures the diversity of attention maps. MAD measures the average
735   distance between patch positions within an image, weighted by attention, representing the effective
736   receptive field size in the ViT. The two metrics are computed by averaging over the heads in the
737   attention computation in each layer. The results are shown in Fig. 5.
738   First, it is clear that both metrics vary significantly across methods and layers within the same
739   method. Dividing the methods into four categories—image-level learning (DINO), patch-level
740   learning (MAE and DropPos), hybrid methods (iBOT and DINOv2), and methods incorporating
741   positional learning (LOCA and our method)—the behavior is similar to what was reported in Park
742   et al. (2023). Specifically, higher patch attention diversity is desirable, but it is smaller in image-
743   level methods (DINO) and relatively larger in methods that incorporate patch-level learning. The
744   three methods that showed strong performance on COCO—LOCA, MAE, and our method—exhibit
745
      similar behavior in both metrics, particularly in the last three layers.
746
747   A.2   V ISUALIZATION OF PATCH ATTENTION IN THE FINAL LAYER
748
      Figure 6 visualizes the attention to all patches (tokens) in the image in the final layer, where a single
749
      point (i.e., a patch) in the input image is selected as the query. The attention is averaged across all
750   heads. In the leftmost column of the figure, the position of the query patch is indicated by a red dot
751   in the input image. Across all methods, it appears that patches “close” to the query patch—though in
752   different senses—receive greater attention. However, the interpretation of “closeness” varies across
753   methods. To be specific, MAE can look widely but be biased to textures or colors easily, not enough
754   to identify the instance. Moreover, if the query point is in the background but close to the boundary
755   of a foreground object, attention is focused more on the foreground than the background, as shown in
      the fourth row. DropPos produces an attention map too locally without recognizing wider patterns.

854   D    O FFICIAL DINOV 2 AND DINOV 2- REG
855
856
      DINOv2-reg (Darcet et al., 2024) introduces an implicit way to improve patch representations. The
857
      authors investigate the emergence of unwanted high-norm tokens in DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024),
858   particularly in large-scale training using a 142M dataset and ViT-(L/H/g). They observe that these
859   high-norm patch tokens contain less local information and more global information. To address
860   this, they introduce register tokens, which participate in the model’s forward pass without being
861   directly affected by the loss function. The high-norm tokens are also observed in other models,
862   such as OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) and DeiT-III (Touvron et al., 2022). They hypothesize that
863   register tokens can complement the high-norm patch tokens, allowing tokens to focus more on local
      information without producing high norms.
