011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The identification of unobserved links in drug-related biomedical networks is
014            essential for various drug discovery applications, which is also beneficial for
015
               both disease diagnosis and treatment through exploring the underlying molecular
               mechanisms. However, existing solutions face significant challenges due to three
016
               main limitations: (1) lack of interpretability to provide comprehensive and reli-
017
               able insights, (2) insufficient robustness and flexibility in cold-start scenarios, and
018            (3) inadequate interaction and sharing of multi-view information. In light of this,
019            we propose DrugXAS, an interpretable and adaptive cross-view contrastive learn-
020            ing framework with information sharing for biomedical link prediction. Specif-
021            ically, DrugXAS has three distinctive characteristics for addressing these chal-
022            lenges. To solve the first problem, we propose an attention-aware augmentation
023            scheme to provide understandable explanations of intrinsic mechanisms. To deal
024            with the second challenge, we propose an adaptive graph updater and neighbor-
025            hood sampler, which select proper neighbors according to the feedbacks from the
026
               model to improve aggregation ability. To tackle the third issue, an information
               sharing module with diffusion loss is proposed to incorporate chemical struc-
027
               tures into heterogeneous relational semantics and facilitate the contrast process.
028
               Empirically, extensive experiments on seven benchmark datasets involving multi-
029            type tasks demonstrate that the proposed DrugXAS outperforms the state-of-the-
030            art methods in terms of precision, robustness, and interpretability. The source
031            code of DrugXAS is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
032            r/DrugXAS-8EC7.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Discovering unknown molecular interactions and associations within biomedical networks is im-
038   mensely valuable in practical applications (Muzio et al., 2020), including finding novel biomarkers
039
      for diseases, repurposing drugs for other purposes, and identifying drug side effects (Ding et al.,
      2024a). While a vast number of links have been identified through wet experiments or clinic reports,
040
      many remain undiscovered (Zhong & Mottin, 2023). Furthermore, determining potential links us-
041
      ing wet experimental techniques is extremely expensive, time-consuming, and labor-intensive (Feng
042   et al., 2024b). Recently, researchers have exploited computational methods to predict links in drug-
043   related biomedical networks and achieved remarkable success (Ma et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022).
044   Considering that interactions/associations between molecules and entities can be readily expressed
045   as networks, with molecules as nodes and interactions as edges, graph neural networks (GNNs)
046   have demonstrated extraordinary capabilities and potential in biomedical link prediction tasks (Wang
047   et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024b). Despite numerous efforts and impressive achievements, the current
048   research state of biomedical link prediction faces several key challenges that limit the application.
049   Here, we highlight the following three issues and deficiencies that need to be addressed:
050   (1) Interpretability. Interpretability, the capacity to explain and elucidate the outputs of deep learn-
051   ing models, is particularly significant in drug-related research since it has a tremendous impact on
052   patients’ trust and assists medical professionals (Ding et al., 2024b). There is an emerging demand
053   for drug discovery methods that offer greater reliability and aid in the comprehension of underlying
      models. However, a significant obstacle of existing drug-related link prediction lies in their lack

054
      of interpretability (Feng et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2021). To this end, there is still ample scope for
055   investigation in the field of advanced interpretability to produce explanations that are more com-
056   prehensive and flexible, which is beneficial for understanding the inherent mechanisms and patterns
057   of the task. In essence, this leads us to the following problem: how to provide reasonable and
058   meaningful explanations for predictions to achieve interpretability from a biomedical perspective?
059
      (2) Cold-Start Scenario Applications. Ex-
060
      isting models mainly focus on datasets where
061   drugs and entities are randomly split, with
062   drugs and entities in the test set having already
063   appeared in the training set. Nonetheless, this
064   situation is far from realistic, where the major-
065   ity of drugs or entities are absent from the train-
066   ing set (Pahikkala et al., 2014), leading to a dra-
067   matic drop in prediction performance of cur-
068   rent methods (Wang et al., 2022). This issue,
069
      refers to the cold-start problem in recommen- Figure 1: The illustration of cold-start problem.
      dation systems, severely restricts the practical
070
      application of biomedical link prediction techniques (Ye et al., 2021). Learning robust and stable
071
      representations of novel drugs and entities in the absence of known correlation information is one of
072   the primary challenges facing cold-start link prediction (Hao et al., 2021). For instance, in the warm
073   scenario depicted in Figure 1(a), messages from high-order neighbors can be aggregated to target
074   node drug d1 . However, in the cold-start scenario, only the 1-order neighbor entities e2 and e4 can
075   be utilized to represent d1 , which are both cold-start nodes without known associations, leading to
076   insufficient information and suboptimal representations. Towards this end, the cold-start problem
077   motivates us the second research question: how to enhance the representation learning capability
078   for cold-start scenarios?
079   (3) Exploration of Multi-view Information. Although graph contrastive learning (GCL), particu-
080   larly cross-view GCL, has emerged as an effective representation learning framework for biomedical
081   link prediction (Zhang et al., 2024), current GCL-based models only consider a portion of biomedi-
082   cal knowledge (Ma et al., 2023). Since molecular chemical information and biomedical interaction
083   modeling frequently have heterogeneous rather than monomorphic dependencies, with the former
084   focusing on fine-level and the latter based on coarse-level, it is crucial to achieve interaction and
085   sharing of information between the two views (Xie et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). Each drug has
086
      unique chemical traits and a variety of link patterns for the inherent interaction mechanisms, thus
      the influence and contribution of different source of data may be entirely different. For instance,
087
      molecular structure information is more vital for tasks in which molecular actions and mechanisms
088
      play a key role, such as drug-target interactions (DTIs) and drug-side effects (DSEs). Blindly en-
089   coding features without considering their individual and distinctive characteristics easily results in
090   suboptimal learned embeddings (Liu et al., 2024a). In view of this obstacle, we are intuitively in-
091   spired to ask the following question: how to effectively interact and share knowledge across different
092   views to make full use of both link and auxiliary information?
093
      To address the above-mentioned challenges in Drug-related biomedical link prediction, a novel
094
      Interpretable (X) and Adaptive contrastive learning model with Information Sharing is proposed,
095   termed as DrugXAS. We highlight our primary contributions as follows: (1) To overcome the first
096   limitation, DrugXAS devises a novel attention-aware graph augmentation strategy, enabling our
097   model adaptively focus on important molecular structures and potential biomedical associations.
098   Moreover, the network-view augmentation facilitates DrugXAS in providing rational explanations
099   of the prediction results thereby achieving interpretability. (2) To tackle the second challenge,
100   DrugXAS proposes an adaptive graph updater with a neighborhood sampler to dynamically se-
101   lect effective neighbors according to the feedbacks from the encoder. (3) To handle the third issue,
102   DrugXAS proposes an information sharing module between molecule- and network-view compo-
103
      nents (InSMN) and a diffusion loss scheme to further advance cross-view contrastive learning. The
      information sharing module extracts topological and chemical information from two views simulta-
104
      neously, while the diffusion loss aims to bring the representations of the two views closer, optimizing
105
      contrastive learning. (4) Extensive experiments are conducted on seven public datasets of different
106   types of tasks, demonstrating the superiority of DrugXAS compared with state-of-the-art methods
107   under both warm and cold-start scenarios.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Biomedical Link Prediction. The goal of drug-related biomedical link prediction is to predict
111
      whether there is a link between a particular pair of drugs and entities (Wang et al., 2024b). In partic-
112   ular, SkipGNN (Huang et al., 2020) introduces skip similarity into GNNs by aggregating information
113   from low to high orders. SiGrac (Coşkun & Koyutürk, 2021) designs a novel node similarity-based
114   graph convolution process with multiple measures of similarity. Zhao et al. (2021) propose CSGNN,
115   which leverages the GNN aggregator from mix-hop neighborhoods and utilizes contrastive learning
116   for multi-task prediction. MVGCN (Fu et al., 2021) devises a multi-view graph convolution network
117   (GCN) with inter- and intra-view neighborhood aggregators. HOGCN (KC et al., 2022) focuses on
118   high-order information, aggregating neighbor nodes information at different distances. Shen et al.
119   (2024) propose CGCN, which adopts a curvature-enhanced GCN to learn local geometric attributes.
120
      Li et al. (2024a) propose SubKNet, which employs a graph kernel neural network to the extracted
      subgraphs for learning node representations.
121
122
123
      Graph Contrastive Learning. Contrastive learning aims to maximize the agreement between
      positive samples while pushing away negative samples under appropriate data augmentations (Li
124
      et al., 2024b). Here we primarily focus on GCL methodologies. For example, Veličković et al.
125
      (2018) propose DGI, which creates positive samples by combining local and global semantics while
126   the negatives are represented by nodes in a randomly damaged network. GMI (Peng et al., 2020)
127   proposes a fine-grained contrastive loss by comparing the topological structure and node properties.
128   Hassani & Khasahmadi (2020) propose MVGRL, which exploits cross-view contrastive learning of
129   nodes and graphs. With respect to heterogeneous graph representation learning, DMGI (Park et al.,
130   2020) performs contrastive learning between the original network and the corrupted network on
131   each individual view and meta-path. Wang et al. (2021) propose HeCo, which devises two views of
132   the heterogeneous graph, learning node features from both local and high-order structures. H-GCL
133   (Zhu et al., 2023) is proposed to employ hypergraph to establish the augmented view for learning
134
      high-quality embeddings.
135
136   3   P RELIMINARY
137
138   Biomedical Heterogeneous Graph. A biomedical heterogeneous graph can be defined as an undi-
139   rected graph G = (V, E), with a node type mapping function ϕ : V → O and an edge type mapping
140   function φ : E → R. V represents the set of nodes which corresponds to drugs and other types of
141   biomedical entities such as targets, diseases and side effects, etc. E is the set of edges (i.e., links)
142   between nodes in V, corresponding to associations or interactions between biomedical entities.
143
144   Drug Molecular Graph. Given a drug di , the molecular graph is formulated as Gdi = (M, A),
145   where M denotes the set of atoms of the drug and A represents the set of bonds between atoms.
146
147   Biomedical Link Prediction. Given the drug-entity pair (di , ej ) in biomedical graph G, our work
148   aims to evaluate the probability score between this pair of drug and entity with correlation hetero-
149   geneity, i.e., learn a mapping function to predict the link probability between drug di and entity
150
      ej .
151
152   Cold-start Scenario. (1) Cold-drug Task: Each drug that appears in the training set does not
153
      appear in the test set, while each entity can appear in both training and test set. (2) Cold-entity Task:
      Each entity that appears in the training set does not appear in the test set, while each drug can appear
154
      in both training and test set. (3) Cold-pair Task: Neither the drug nor the entity in the training set
155
      appear in the test set.

211   cold-start scenarios, we first supplement the constructed biomedical heterogeneous graph G with ad-

213   for each node and add them as new edges to construct the enhanced graph Ḡ.

225   Network-view Augmentation. The network-view augmentation is also based on the learned at-

227   to produce the attention matrix as a guidance of data augmentation. The three types of augmentation

231   HGNN Encoder. Local structure information has been proven to be essential for the HGNN model
232   (Lv et al., 2021). We introduce GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) to provide local sub-structure enhanced
233   node features, which is simple yet competitive for HGNN since that the heterogeneity can be learned
234   through heterogeneous feature projection (Liu et al., 2023). Then we employ the transformer en-
235
      coder (Vaswani et al., 2017) to capture information from long-range dependencies. The entire inputs
      of the transformer module for drugs and entities are the sampled neighborhood sequences, denoted
236
      by Hns(0) ∈ Rnd ×sn ×d and Hes(0) ∈ Rne ×sn ×d , respectively. We modify the two main com-
237
      ponents in transformer to reduce parameters and increase efficiency. Specifically, we replace the
238
      original dot-product self-attention mechanism with the GATv2 attention mechanism. Additionally,
239   the feed-forward network is removed, which has less negative impact on prediction performance.
240
241   Adaptive Graph Update. In order to adapt the real cold-start scenarios, inspired by (Hao et al.,
242   2021; 2023), we propose an adaptive graph update pipeline, dynamically updating edges and ex-
243   tracting similar node information according to the current model itself. Formally, we select the top
244   kn relevant nodes as new neighbors for di according to the calculated cosine similarity between drug
245   di and dj . Ultimately, we update the biomedical graph Ḡ by adding new neighbors to replace the
246   enhanced neighbors for each drug and entity according to similarity, which is then used in the next
247   epoch. The proposed update strategy enables considering the cold-start characteristics of the node
248   neighbors and adaptively selecting appropriate neighbors for message propagation.

351   Datasets. To evaluate the performance of DrugXAS, we focus on five types of drug-related
352   biomedical link prediction tasks, including predicting DTIs, drug-disease associations (DDAs),
353   DSEs, miRNA-drug resistance associations (MDAs) and circRNA-drug sensitivity associations
354   (CDAs). Seven widely used benchmark datasets are employed, i.e., LuoDTI (Luo et al., 2017) and
355   ZhengDTI (Zheng et al., 2018) for DTI prediction, LiangDDA (Liang et al., 2017) and ZhangDDA
356   (Zhang et al., 2018) for DDA prediction, PauwelsDSE (Pauwels et al., 2011) for DSE prediction,
357   HuangMDA (Huang et al., 2019) for MDA prediction, and DengCDA (Deng et al., 2022) for CDA
358
      prediction. The statistic information of these datasets is presented in Appendix B.
359
360

362
      evaluation metrics are employed to measure the performance: the area under the receiver operating

      To explore the in-depth interpretability of DrugXAS, we provide a comprehensive analysis of the
508
      novel biomedical link prediction scenario. For the two case study examples in Appendix G, we
509
      visualize the target drugs/diseases and predicted proteins/drugs, along with their sampled neighbor
510   nodes within neighborhood sequence and the attention scores, as depicted in Figure 5. We can
511   observe that most predicted candidate proteins/diseases have known interactions/associations with
512   the target drug/disease in their corresponding neighborhood sequence. For example, for the pre-
513   dicted protein DRD5 related to Clozapine, our model infers this novel interaction based on existing
514   DTIs, such as Pramipexole and DRD4, Aripiprazole and CHRM1, which are all sampled neighbor
515   nodes for DRD5 and Clozapine, respectively. Another example is the target HRH2, where its neigh-

517   which are also predicted with high attention scores. With respect to the novel link prediction on

519
      diseases), Acetaminophen and Ethinyl Estradiol are predicted as candidate drugs for Alzheimer’s
520
      disease and breast neoplasms, respectively, which align with the known data in the dataset. Through
521   the application of attention-aware augmentation, useful and informative knowledge can be extracted
522   from the neighborhood subgraph. Moreover, the learned attention matrices can be utilized to aug-
523   ment and prune the neighborhood subgraph, thus creating pathways for mechanisms that summarize
524   the biomedical links. To sum up, our investigation verifies and evaluates the generated explanations,
525   underscoring the promising capability of DrugXAS to provide interpretable insights for identifying
526   latent links from a biomedical perspective.

531
      In this paper, motivated to address the limitations of previous work, we propose DrugXAS, an in-
      terpretable and adaptive graph contrastive learning framework with information sharing for drug-
532
      related biomedical link prediction. DrugXAS introduces the molecule-view learning as an auxil-
533
      iary aspect to comprehensively exploit biomedical information. The adaptive and dynamic update
534   technique ensures the resilience of DrugXAS in cold-start scenarios, and the augmentation scheme
535   enables achieving interpretability from a biological perspective. A novel information sharing mod-
536   ule is devised to jointly capture semantic relations and topological patterns while preserving the
537   chemical properties of drug molecules. In addition, the diffusion model is employed to fill the gap
538   between the two distinct views, enhancing the GCL procedure. Extensive experiments compared
539   with the state-of-the-arts demonstrate the superior effectiveness and robustness of DrugXAS, with
      competitive interpretability.

760
761
      A.1   M OLECULE - VIEW AUGMENTATION
762
      Atom masking. Atoms/nodes with the smallest attention scores based on a certain ratio are
763   masked, where their node feature vectors are set to 0. Our drug encoder can learn intrinsic chemical
764   properties through the masking operation.
765
766   Bond deletion. Bonds/edges with the smallest attention scores based on a certain ratio are deleted,
767   where the edges are removed completely to block the message propagation of GNN encoder. Bond
768   deletion enables our model to discern correlations between a molecule’s participation in diverse
769   biomedical reactions.
770
771   A.2   N ETWORK - VIEW AUGMENTATION
772
773   Node masking. Drugs and entities with the smallest attention scores within neighborhood se-
774   quences based on a certain ratio are masked, where their node feature vectors are set to 0. This type
775   of node-level augmentation ensures that the selected node provides the strongest information.
776
777   Edge adding. The 2-hop neighbors with the highest attention scores within neighborhood se-
778
      quences based on a certain ratio are connected to the target node as new neighbors, where the
      new edges can be added to the subsequent message propagation of HGNN encoder. The idea of
779
      this type of augmentation lies in that the original graph structure is not necessarily reliable and the
780
      unconnected 2-hop neighbors may contain more structural information.
781
782
      Edge deletion. The 1-hop neighbors with the smallest attention scores within neighborhood se-
783   quences based on a certain ratio are deleted, where the edges are removed completely to block the
784   message propagation of HGNN encoder. Our goal of this component is to judge and reduce noise,
785   such as incomplete or insufficient biomedical interaction data, to enable meaningful and effective
786   integration of information.

      that semantic knowledge is more essential for these types of link prediction tasks. Nevertheless,
