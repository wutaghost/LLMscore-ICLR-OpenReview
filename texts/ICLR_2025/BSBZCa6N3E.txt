                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Multi-turn interactions between large language models (LLMs) and users naturally
010
               include implicit feedback signals. If an LLM responds in an unexpected way to
011
               an instruction, the user is likely to signal it by rephrasing the request, expressing
012            frustration, or pivoting to an alternative task. Such signals are task-independent and
013            occupy a relatively constrained subspace of language, allowing the LLM to identify
014            them even if it fails on the actual task. This creates an avenue for continually
015            learning from interactions without additional annotations. We introduce R E S PECT,
016            a method to learn from such signals in past interactions via retrospection. We
017            deploy R E S PECT in a new multimodal interaction scenario, where humans instruct
018            a multimodal LLM to solve an abstract reasoning task with a combinatorial solution
019            space. Through thousands of interactions with humans, we show how R E S PECT
020
               gradually improves task completion rate from 31% to 82%, all without any external
               annotation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Language models (LMs) often engage in multi-turn interactions with human users. Similar to human-
026   human interactions, these interactions are naturally rich with implicit learning signals. If the LM fails
027   to respond appropriately, the user is likely to follow with an expression of frustration, a rephrase of
028   their intent, or maybe even completely pivot what they ask for. Similarly, if the LM does well, the
029   user may express approval or simply continue to their next objective. Such responses can inform the
030   LM of its performance, thereby creating an opportunity to learn through retrospection.
031
      We study the efficacy of such signals, and how they can lead to a system that improves over time.
032   We introduce R E S PECT, a simple approach to learn from signals the model itself derives about its
033   own past actions through retrospection of past interactions with human users. We experiment with
034   R E S PECT by deploying a multimodal LLM (MLLM) in M ULTI R EF, a new multi-turn grounded

058
      The key insight underlying R E S PECT is that conversational implicit feedback signals occupy a
      relatively constrained subspace of natural language. Such signals can include direct approvals (e.g.,
059
      great!) or signs of frustration (e.g., not again), and also more subtle cues, such as when the user
060
      rephrases their request. Critically, it is relatively simple to disentangle them from task performance. A
061   human can easily figure out from such cues if they do well or not, even if they have little understanding
062   about what they are asked for. It is this constrained nature that makes reasoning about such signals to
063   be within the capacities of large language models (LLMs), even if they fail at the task at hand.
064
      R E S PECT utilizes this signal in a process where the model interacts with humans, and after interaction
065
      decodes feedback for each of its actions from the interaction context including the follow up utterances.
066
      Figure 1 illustrates this process. The model interacts with humans to accomplish tasks, retrospectively
067   examines its own past interactions, and then re-trains. This process progresses in rounds, alternating
068   between interaction and training, with the model improving over time. Critically, unlike common
069   recipes for training from human feedback, R E S PECT does not require any external annotation (Ouyang
070   et al., 2022, RLHF) or even soliciting feedback from the users themselves (Suhr & Artzi, 2023).
071
      We deploy R E S PECT in M ULTI R EF over multiple rounds of grounded interactions with human use
072
      and re-training. We use IDEFICS2-8B (Laurençon et al., 2024) as our MLLM, and experiment
073   with multiple learning methods, including supervised learning, REINFORCE-style policy gradi-
074   ent (Williams, 1992; Kojima et al., 2021), and KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Across our experiments,
075   we observe that IDEFICS2-8B effectively decodes feedback, even as it initially performs poorly in
076   the same interactions. In our longest running experiment, we observe model task completion rate
077   improves from 31% to 82%. Our code, data, and models will be released upon publication.

      2    T ECHNICAL OVERVIEW AND N OTATION
081
082   We conduct continual learning 1 studies by deploying our approach in M ULTI R EF, a new multi-turn
083   grounded interaction scenario (Section 3). Overall, the study progresses in rounds, where the MLLM
084   policy is first deployed to interact with users and complete tasks, and the interactions are then used to
085
      re-train the policy. Our study involves multiple rounds, and our goal is to observe and evaluate the
      long-term dynamics of the process. This includes the robustness of our award decoding and training
086
      methods to the changing distribution of the data likely to be seen in an adaptive system in the wild.
087
      Section 3 describes our interaction scenario in detail, and Section 4 our learning method. First, we
088   outline our problem of interest and its notation in abstract terms.
089
090   Task Notation The policy’s task is to respond effectively to human utterances given in conversa-
091   tional context. Formally, let π(at |xt ) be the policy that controls the listener behavior, with at an
092   action string that represents the model response and xt being the context on which the policy is
093   conditioned, both at turn t in the interaction. The context includes the instruction history up to and
094   excluding turn t, including current (i.e., at turn t − 1) and past speaker utterances, as well as any
095   other relevant context in which the interaction takes place. As our learning progresses in rounds, we
096
      denote θρ as the model parameters in round ρ, and πθρ as the parameterized policy.
097
      Learning and Deployment We study a continual learning setup, where the learning signal is
098   acquired from interactions of the deployed model with human speakers. Our study progresses in
099   rounds (Figure 1). Each round ρ includes a deployment, followed by training. During deployment
100   at round ρ, the model πθρ interacts with users. For each model action ât ∼ πθρ (a|xt ), we record a
101   tuple (xt , ât , pt , f¯t ), where xt is the context given to the model at time t to predict action ât , pt is
102   the probability of ât at the time of prediction, and f¯t is the remainder of the interaction following ât .
103   Critically, these interaction tuples contain no explicit feedback. We compute the implicit feedback γ̂t
104   using a feedback decoder ϕ(xt , ât , f¯t ), to obtain tuples (xt , ât , γ̂t , pt ). We experiment with multiple
105   learning objectives using this feedback: supervised learning (SFT), policy gradient, and KTO.

133   3    M ULTI R EF : A M ULTI - TURN G ROUNDED I NTERACTION S CENARIO
134
135
      Key to our study is that tasks are relayed gradually across multiple turns, as commonly happens in
136
      human interactions. We create M ULTI R EF, a conversational interaction scenario where two partners,
137
      a speaker and a listener, coordinate on the selection of a set of items. In our studies, the speaker is
138   always a human, and the listener is a model.
139
140   M ULTI R EF generalizes the commonly studied reference game scenario. Its design and our choice of
141
      stimuli are grounded in existing work from both cognitive science and computational language mod-
      eling (Rosenberg & Cohen, 1964; Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986; Schober & Clark, 1989; Goodman &
142
      Frank, 2016). Figure 2 illustrates the scenario. Both partners observe a shared set of images, but in
143
      different order. The speaker is given a subset of the images as targets, with the goal of communicating
144   the targets to the listener, so the latter selects the exact subset. Only the speaker can write messages,
145   and only the listener can select or deselect images. The interaction concludes successfully once all
146   and only targets are selected, or fails if the partners run out of turns, 20 in our studies.
147
      The interaction progresses in turns t, alternating between speaker and listener turns. At each speaker
148
      turn, they provide a single unrestricted natural language utterance. It may direct the listener to select
149
      one or more items, ask to deselect items if the listener previously made a mistake, or include whatever
150   other content they desire. This utterance as well as the history of the interaction, the set of images,
151   and their selection status compose the context xt for the following model turn at turn t. The follower
152   responds with an action at , which includes one or more image selects or deselects according to their
153   understanding of the speaker intention. The action space consists of all possible legal sequences of
154   the form Deselect E select F or Select D G assuming images are code-named alphabetically.
155
      The motivation behind M ULTI R EF is to create a task-oriented scenario that is both accessible to non-
156   expert humans and encourages constructing a solution in multiple turns, thereby creating multi-turn
157   interactions and eliciting the learning signals we aim to study. The rules of the interaction are simple:
158   the speaker describes targets to select, and the listener selects what the speaker is referring to. This
159   makes M ULTI R EF easily accessible to crowdsourcing workers. At the same time, the solution the
160   speaker communicates to the listener is relatively complex, because of the enormous solution space:
161   Consider a conventional reference games, where the goal is to select a single image. The number
      of possible solutions is the number of images in the context. In M ULTI R EF, the goal is to select


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a subset of unknown size, so the combinatorial solution space the listener faces is exponential in
163   the number of images. Meanwhile, the solution is decomposable, and the speaker can comment on
164   the impact immediately after the listener’s action (unlike Haber et al. (2019, PhotoBook)), creating
165   natural opportunities to decompose the solution and for implicit incremental feedback to appear.
166
      Key to making M ULTI R EF work well is the choice of images. We use tangram shapes from the
167
      diverse K ILO G RAM dataset (Ji et al., 2022). Tangrams are abstract shapes that are designed to
168
      elicit common concepts in humans. This abstractness often leads to ambiguous descriptions open
169   to interpretation, e.g., Shape A in Figure 2 can be described as a bat, a lowercase w, or even a star
170   wars star fighter. We select tangrams because they naturally provide an ambiguous and challenging
171   stimuli for human interaction (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986; Schober & Clark, 1989; Fox Tree, 1999;
172   Hawkins et al., 2020b), thereby leading to highly diverse language. They also remain challenging for
173   contemporary MLLMs to reason about (Ji et al., 2022), leaving significant room for learning.
174
      The free-form natural language human speakers produce in M ULTI R EF is very diverse, and balances
175   between competing pressures. First, it often requires complex pragmatic reasoning (Clark & Wilkes-
176   Gibbs, 1986; Schober & Clark, 1989; Horton & Gerrig, 2002), because of the abstractness of tangrams.
177   This is compounded by how the combinatorial solution space drives humans to balance between
178   relaying as much information as possible, and relaying clear objectives to make gradual progress.
179   This is a balance between two Gricean maxims: quantity and manner. 2 Speakers may or may not
180   include explicit feedback such as good, or deselect the last one; the speaker may describe more than
181   one target in a single utterance, for example, select two men; speakers may refer to previous selections
182   without directly describing targets, for example, the other one, or try again. In combination with the
183
      abstract stimuli tangrams provide, this creates a challenging reasoning problem for the listener model.
184   M ULTI R EF is not designed to increase complexity in arbitrary ways, but to provide an environment
185   for humans to naturally expose core aspects of human communication. At the same time, the scenario
186   is both controlled and scoped, allowing for easy measurement of task completion and progress, as
187   well as making learning feasible with relatively limited data. This makes M ULTI R EF particularly
188   suitable to research in academia or other low-resource settings.

195   dynamics of R E S PECT over time. However, the method itself is not limited to continual learning, and
196   can be applied a single time as well.

248
249   The feedback decoding process transforms the dataset from Draw to D = {(x(i) , â(i) , p(i) , γ̂ (i) )}N
                                                                                                             i=1 .
250   We study several learning approaches using this data: supervised learning, offline reinforcement
251   learning (RL), or the KTO-style utility maximization (Ethayarajh et al., 2024).
252
253   Supervised Learning We fine-tune on positive data points (γ̂ (i) = positive) and discard data
254   points predicted as neutral or negative. We use cross entropy loss with additional label smoothing
255
      to prevent overfitting and encourage exploration. Our setup is distinct from conventional supervised
      learning in that the data is coming from the model interactions (i.e., on-policy), and not from a given
256
      dataset. Also, we run the learning process iteratively, each time with more data. We do not design the
257
      supervised approach in any special way to fit these changes, but this is a potential avenue for future
258   work, which can further improve performance.
259

261   style policy gradient (Williams, 1992). The categorical feedback γ (i) (i.e., the text generated by the
262   prompted LLM) is mapped to a numerical value with a simple reward function:

286   5    E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
287
288   Interaction Instantiation We use the K ILO G RAM (Ji et al., 2022) tangram images, following Gul
289   & Artzi (2024). K ILO G RAM contains 1,013 images. We randomly split them into a main split (912
290   tangrams) and a development split (101 tangrams). We create interaction contexts by randomly
291
      sampling 10 tangrams, and randomly select 3–5 as targets. The development split is exclusively used
      for seeding the initial listener policy πθ0 , and all human-bot interactions are conducted on images
292
      from the main split, i.e., tangrams that the seed policy πθ0 has never seen before.
293
294   Model and Initialization We use IDEFICS2-8B (Laurençon et al., 2024) as our model for both the
295   policy and feedback decoder. We fine-tune with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). We seed the initial policy πθ0
296   by fine-tuning the pretrained IDEFICS2-8B weights on a small supervised dataset of 90 successful
297   turns from 25 human-human games constructed with the development split tangrams, augmented with
298   12 synthetically generated deselection turns, because while necessary for human-model interactions,
299
      deselections are rare in human-human interactions (Appendix B.2). D0 is reused in continual training
      via rehearsal. We validate our design online with 30 main-split human-bot pilot interactions, or offline
300
      with a validation set of 344 successful main-split human-human turns (Appendix A). We use the
301
      original IDEFICS2-8B for feedback decoding, because the narrow focus of our data is likely to inhibit
302   some general linguistic knowledge. This means we cannot see improvement in the model feedback
303   decoding capability, likely low-balling the potential of the approach. It remains an important direction
304   for future work to keep the decoder model in sync with the policy. This requires deployments that
305   include high domain diversity. We observe the original IDEFICS2-8B to provide robust feedback
306   decoding out of the box, confirming our hypothesis, and providing a solid ground for our experiments.
307
      System Variants We study six system variants based on two dimensions: (a) feedback decoder
308
      configuration (binary vs. ternary); (b) optimization methods (supervised vs. REINFORCE vs. KTO):
309
310          • B - SUP and T- SUP binary (B) / ternary (T) that only trains on positive data points with a
311            supervised fine-tuning objective (SUP).
312
             • B - RL and T- RL trains on both positive and negative data points using REINFORCE.
313
             • B - KTO and T- KTO are like B - RL and T- RL, but using KTO.
314   For variants involving negative data points (B - RL, T- RL, B - KTO, and T- KTO), we subsample negative
315   ones to keep the positive:negative ratio close to 5:4 (Ethayarajh et al., 2024).
316
      Deployment We conduct three rounds of training-deployment for all six systems and three more
317
      rounds for B - SUP. We select B - SUP for another three rounds because it is the most promising
318   variant after three rounds, and we want to observe its progress over a longer period. The reason
319   for this cascaded design is the high cost of experiments. We do not distinguish between training
320   and evaluation in the traditional sense. Instead, all listener policies are evaluated live on MTurk on
321   about 330 human-bot interactions each round containing roughly 2400 turns. Then the same data is
322   used to train the next iteration of policies respectively. The policies in the same round are deployed
323   concurrently in a randomized experiment on the same set of games to mitigate human biases and
      variances due to game difficulty. More details on crowdsourcing are in Appendix A.3.

      Learning Implementation Details We use the validation set for model selection throughout
325   continual learning. Following prior work (Misra et al., 2017; Müller et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022), we
326   add an entropy term and length normalization to all three objectives to reduce over-fitting given the
327   relatively small amount of data. Appendix B provides additional reproducibility details. Unlike with
328   REINFORCE, where we train from scratch each round, when using KTO, we continually fine-tune
329   from a previous model checkpoint θρ to obtain θρ+1 with data accumulation. This was shown to
330   outperform training from scratch in pilot studies.
331
      Evaluation We evaluate each system variant at each round by the success rate during the live
332
      deployment. We report both interaction- and utterance-level success rates. The interaction level
333   success rate is straightforward - whether the game ended with all targets selected by the listener and
334   nothing else. The utterance level success rate is more nuanced because we do not have access to the
335   ground truth, i.e., the intended action. We sample 1,000 utterances per round from B - SUP to annotate
336   by MTurk workers post hoc. We report two measures: exact match between the annotation and model
337   action and similarity score, which is based on the computed similarity between the tangrams selected
338   or deselected during the turn by the human annotator and the system. We also evaluate the quality of
339   the feedback decoder by comparing its predictions with human interpretations collected during the
340   post-hoc annotation. Because of cost, we cannot do post-hoc annotation for all system variants, so
341
      we also report click accuracy, which approximates utterance-level performance well. It measures
      the ratio of the model actions that lead to selection statuses that do not violate the set of targets (i.e.,
342
      selections of target tangrams are good, deselection of non-target tangrams are good). Lastly, we track
343
      the number of turns per interaction. Appendix B.4 provides full definitions of our metrics.

349   variant, to better understand long-term dynamics. All our results are from concurrent randomized
350
      deployment, where the models interact with humans in real time. We collected a total of 7,230
      interactions consisting of 55,004 utterances over 4 weeks, at a cost of $11,180 USD.
351

459   T- RL ). B - KTO even diverges at some point and starts losing performance fast. We suspect this is
460   because the KTO recipe does not work well in the challenging optimization scenario of continual
461   learning, where the model is fine-tuned multiple times. We observe that B - KTO deteriorates in rounds
462   two and three, and starts generating illegal outputs (e.g., Deselect select). Appendix B.3 describes
463   a quick intervention we applied to try to mitigate this issue. Although it eliminated the illegal outputs,
464   the quality remained low. It is possible that further refinement of how KTO is used or further tuning
465   of its hyperparamters will help. However, this is a complex process in a live deployment.

467
      from more interactions (Figure 7). We observe a reduction in vocabulary size and utterance length

469   costs (e.g., Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986; Effenberger et al., 2021). However, in later rounds, B -

505   Learning from Naturally Occurring Signals Kojima et al. (2021) presents an approach to learn
506   to generate instructions by observing how humans follow them, a complementary mode of learning
507   to our focus on general response. Pang et al. (2023) maximizes heuristics, such as the chance of
508   long responses from humans, in a chatbot scenario. Artzi & Zettlemoyer (2011) studied the use
509   of naturally occurring recovery efforts (i.e., when the user switches to simpler language to relay
510
      information) to train a symbolic semantic parser from a corpus of dialogue interactions. In contrast,
      we opt for a general approach to infer feedback from natural language interactions of the model itself.
511
512   Concurrently to our work, Don-Yehiya et al. (2024), as well as Petrak et al. (2023), proposes an
513   approach that uses naturally occurring feedback in conversations to filter a large conversational
514   corpus. The linguistic cues they rely on are similar to ours. Unlike our study, the model they improve
515   is not the model that generated the interactions, creating a distillation-like setup, where improvement
516
      is not coming from the model’s own interaction, but from other models. We focus on model self-
      improvement, where it is critical that no stronger model is involved. Another difference is our interest
517
      in continual deployment with humans, whereas they follow a standard train-test benchmarking recipe.
518
      This allows our work to expose dynamics that are otherwise hidden. Our work and Don-Yehiya et al.
519   (2024) complement each other and strengthen our conclusions. Their work shows the signal can be
520   derived from large-scale diverse data, whereas ours shows how a single-model loop can work over a
521   long period of time, and the dynamics it creates.
522
523   LLMs that Self-improve A common approach to improve models is via AI feedback, solicited
524   from the model itself or another model (Bai et al., 2022; Burns et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023;
525   Kumar et al., 2024; Qu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). In contrast, we elicit real human
526   feedback automatically from the interactions in deployment. This signal is more on-the-job, and
527
      less influenced by model biases. We also use the same model for interaction and inferring feedback,
      ruling out concerns about distillation.

530   8                  D ISCUSSION
531
532
      We introduce R E S PECT: retrospective learning from interactions, an annotation-free approach by
533
      leveraging signals from naturally occurring feedback in interactions. We demonstrate its effectiveness
534   in long-term deployments and robustness to system variants. As opposed to evaluating on a static
535   benchmark, we design M ULTI R EF to study real interactions over a period of time. We make trade-offs
536   between the generality of the task, and the ability to iterate on a prototype fast, and without high costs.
537   It is important to expand this type of study to other tasks, such as summarization or conversational
538   question answering, where similar signals may be more complex, far apart, or demand long-term
539   credit assignment. Another interesting orthogonal direction is expanding the expressivity of the
      feedback decoder, such that it recovers a more expressive signal (e.g., a natural language explanation).

      A        T HE M ULTI R EF G AME D ESIGN AND DATA C OLLECTION
757
758
      A.1       I NTERACTION D ESIGN
759
760   M ULTI R EF is a multi-target, multi-turn reference game between two players, a speaker and a listener.
761   Each game starts with 10 tangrams as the context, with 3–5 tangrams designated as targets. The
762   target designations are revealed to the speaker but hidden to the listener. The goal is to select all
763   targets without selecting any non-targets. The speaker can only communicate with the listener
764   through a sequence of utterances, and only the listener can take selection and deselection actions. The
765   interaction starts with a speaker turn. Turns alternate between speaker and listener, with a maximum
766   of 20 turns. In each speaker turn, they type an utterance to send to the listener. Speaker turns are
767
      limited to 25 seconds. In each listener turn, they have 45 seconds to select or deselect images as
      instructed to by the speaker. The game concludes when the listener selects only and all targets, or the
768
      when the partners run out of turns. Appendix A.3 shows screenshots of the interface.
769
770   Context Construction We follow Gul & Artzi (2024) and construct game contexts using 1,013
771   tangram images from K ILO G RAM Ji et al. (2022). We group tangrams randomly into two splits:
772   development split (101 tangrams) and main split (912 tangrams). The development split is exclusively
773   used for seeding the initial listener policy π0 . All human-bot interactions are constructed from the
774   main split, i.e., tangrams that the seed policy π0 has never seen before. We construct all games with
775   3–5 target tangrams. More targets are generally harder, given the same maximum number of turns
776
      per interaction.
777
778   A.2       H UMAN E VALUATION D ESIGN
779
      Automatically evaluating turn-level policy performance is hard, because we have no ground truth
780
      (i.e., the selection and deselection actions intended by the speaker in each turn) to compare against.
781   Similarly, we have no ground truth to systematically assess the feedback decoder quality. We conduct
782   human evaluation surveys to address these problems. We annotate a subset of B - SUP interactions,
783   roughly 120 interactions or 1,000 turns per system-turn.
784
      We show human annotators a complete interaction turn by turn, without revealing the underlying
785
      targets. For each turn, the annotation consists for two phases:
786
787             1. Ground-truth: we show context, currently selected tangrams, and instruction given by the
788                speaker. We ask the annotator to annotate the listener action. The annotator action a∗ is
789                considered as ground truth action for this turn. We use these labels for tune-level evaluation.
790                After the action annotation, we reveal the action â actually taken by the listener (i.e., the
791                model) during the interaction.
792             2. Satisfaction: we present the follow-up utterance. We ask the annotator to rate if the speaker
793                is satisfied with the listener’s action, based on the follow-up utterance. They choose one of
794                the following options:
795                  a. Yes.
796
                     b. Yes, even though the listener did not perform all required selections/deselections.
797
                     c. Yes, even though the listener made incorrect selections/deselections.
798
799
                     d. No.
800                The third option accounts for the listener accidentally selecting a target tangram not intended
801                by the speaker, but the speaker choosing to move on without correction or even validating
802                the selection. We treat these labels as ground truth for evaluating feedback decoders.
803
      We annotate 5% of long-term human-bot interactions annotations by three different annotators, to
804
      estimate how reliable the annotations are. We observe 85% agreement on the correctness (whether
805
      â = a∗ ) on ground truth stage, 7 and 65% agreement on the ground-truth action a∗ across workers. 8
806   For satisfaction annotation, we observe 93% agreement rate, illustrating the relative simplicity of
807   extracting the signal that drive our learning process.

      Payment We pay workers $0.81 USD per M ULTI R EF game, and a bonus if the game is successful.

      Overall the estimated hourly wage is $13.00 USD, and closer to $23.00 USD by the end of the

      B.1   I NTERACTION R EPRESENTATION
947
948   We encode the context x as in Figure 11. We standardize action representation by ordering actions,
949   for example, always produce Select A C rather than Select C A. We shuffle the context images
950   during training as the order of context tangrams should not have any impact on the interaction logic.
951
952   B.2   P OLICY I NITIALIZATION
953
954   We seed the initial policy π0 by fine-tuning the model on a small dataset of 90 turns D0 , where both
955
      the speaker and the listener are humans. We also experimented with prompting to initialize the policy.
      We find early that few-shot prompting yields a random policy at best, likely because reasoning with
956
      abstract shapes such as tangrams is visually out-of-distribution for the model.
957
958   There is a significant distribution shift between human-human interactions, and human-policy interac-
959   tion, especially early on when the model performs poorly. In practice, two major differences are the
960   length of interactions and the prevalence of deselection instructions, which are rare in human-human
961
      interactions. We address the deselection issue with data augmentation. We synthetically generate
      turns where the speaker asks for deselections, and the listener complies. We augment the data with
962
      these at a ratio of 1:12 to the existing data. This helps the LLM policy learn to deselect and recover
963
      from mistakes. This augmentation is only used for D0 and such distribution shift is not present in
964   alter rounds, when learning from actual human-bot interactions.

       F    F EEDBACK D ECODER D ESIGN
1243
1244
       The prompt design is minimal, general, and task-agnostic. We validate the prompt with manual
1245
       inspection prior to continual learning launch and human surveys. Considering only the most recent
1246   two action-utterance turns ⟨âi−1 , ui , âi , ui+1 ⟩ is sufficient to produce satisfactory decoding results,
1247   and more history seems to distract the decoder.
1248
1249
       We also experimented with numerical reward (i.e., decoding a real number), experimenting with a
       discretized reward space of {.0, .1, .5, .9} . Our experiments show the model is not well calibrated
1250
       for such decoding.

1339   Roughly 15% of feedback decoder predictions are false negatives, see Figure 6 top row, and an
