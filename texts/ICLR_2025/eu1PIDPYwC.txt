009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for fine-tuning
012            foundation models using distributed data in a privacy-preserving manner. Under
013            limited computational resources, clients often find it more practical to fine-tune a
014            selected subset of layers, rather than the entire model, based on their task-specific
015            data. In this study, we provide a thorough theoretical exploration of selective
016            layer fine-tuning in FL, emphasizing a flexible approach that allows the clients
017            to adjust their selected layers according to their local data and resources. We
018
               theoretically demonstrate that the layer selection strategy has a significant impact
               on model convergence in two critical aspects: the importance of selected layers
019
               and the heterogeneous choices across clients. Drawing from these insights, we
020
               further propose a strategic layer selection method that utilizes local gradients and
021            regulates layer selections across clients. Extensive experiments on both image and
022            text datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed strategy compared with
023            several baselines, highlighting its advances in identifying critical layers that adapt
024            to the client heterogeneity and training dynamics in FL.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021), including BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), GPT (Radford
030   et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), LLaMA (Tou-
031   vron et al., 2023), and so on (Ramesh et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2023), have attracted considerable
032   attention due to their exceptional ability in handling complex tasks (Eloundou et al., 2023). When
033
      it comes to practical deployments of these models in specialized fields, fine-tuning with domain-
      specific data becomes critical. Nevertheless, the distributed nature of data across various users and
034
      organizations presents a challenge for centralized storage and training, as it may lead to severe privacy
035
      concerns and incur additional transmission costs. Such issues have positioned federated learning
036   (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) as a promising paradigm to fine-tune foundation models, aligning model
037   enhancement with privacy preservation (Chen et al., 2023a; Kuang et al., 2023).
038
039
      Generally, FL aims to learn a global model through a collaborative process where clients perform
      local training and upload the parameter updates to a central server for aggregation. Given that clients
040
      have limited resources (Bonawitz et al., 2019; Imteaj et al., 2022), such as computational power,
041
      communication bandwidth, and available memory, it becomes impractical for them to fine-tune the
042   entire foundation model. Two kinds of solutions have recently emerged to tackle this challenge.
043   The first solution employs parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques (Houlsby et al., 2019; Gao
044   et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Li & Liang, 2021), which introduces additional modules integrated
045   into foundation models and updates these modules with domain-specific data while keeping the
046   parameters of the foundation model frozen. The second one is selective model fine-tuning (Lee et al.,
047   2019a; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Shen et al., 2021), which only selects an impactful subset
048   of parameters for optimization to streamline the fine-tuning process under resource constraints.
049   This study focuses on selective model fine-tuning as it is particularly well-suited to address the
050   inherent heterogeneity in FL, i.e., the data heterogeneity and device heterogeneity (Yang et al.,
051   2021; Chai et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022). Specifically, clients involved in FL have non-independent
052   and identically distributed (non-IID) data and different system resources, leading to the need to
053   customize fine-tuning strategies to such discrepancies. For example, clients with limited computation
      resources may opt to update only a fraction of the model, while those with sufficient resources and


                                                         1

054
      high-quality data prefer fine-tuning a large portion of the model to enhance performance. Selective
055   model fine-tuning enables clients to adjust the chosen part of the model to be updated based on their
056   own capabilities, providing a flexible and advanced solution to mitigating sub-optimal issues induced
057   by the heterogeneity in FL.
058
      The exploration of selective model fine-tuning within the context of FL, is still in its early stages. Pre-
059
      vious studies (Shen et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Dun et al., 2022) have concentrated
060
      on designing static strategies for subnetwork selection to improve model fine-tuning performance,
061   without adequately considering heterogeneity among clients. To fulfill this gap, in this paper, we
062   provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis on selective model fine-tuning in FL, focusing on a
063   general scenario where clients are allowed to choose different layers for local training and vary their
064   choices across different training epochs, called selective layer fine-tuning. Specifically, we formulate
065   the optimization objective of selective layer fine-tuning in FL, and provide insights on effectively de-
066   termining critical layers to achieve model convergence. Building on these insights, we further propose
067   a novel layer selection strategy that leverages local gradients and the regulation of unified selections.
068   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
069
070          ‚ÄĘ We study a practical FL setup where clients choose to fine-tune some layers of the model,
071            with arbitrary layer selection that may vary among clients and across different training
072            epochs. We theoretically analyze such a training scheme and investigate the impact of
073            layer selection. The analytical results show that the selected layers affect the convergence
074            performance with two critical aspects, namely the importance of layers and heterogeneous
075
               choices across clients.
076          ‚ÄĘ Building on the theoretical analysis, we formulate the optimization problem of selective
077            layer fine-tuning considering the limited and diverse resource budgets of clients. Inspired
078            by the solution to this optimization problem, we propose an effective strategy for selecting
079            layers for fine-tuning that are well-suited for the local data and available resources at clients.
080
             ‚ÄĘ We conduct experiments to compare the proposed layer selection strategy with baseline
081            methods on both image and text datasets. Experimental results demonstrate the superior
082            performance of the proposed strategy in achieving better model performance, highlighting
083            that the proposed strategy can find critical layers for fine-tuning while considering the client
084            heterogeneity and training dynamics in FL1 .

087   2    R ELATED W ORKS
088
089   Various approaches have been proposed to properly select a subset of parameters for fine-tuning foun-
090   dation models within centralized training, including optimizing a non-structured mask matrix (Lee
091
      et al., 2019a; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Shen et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
      2023; Kovaleva et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019b) and adopting layer-wise selection strategies (Kovaleva
092
      et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019b; 2022; Kaplun et al., 2023). For example, Lee et al. (2019a) suggest up-
093
      dating the parameters in a stochastic manner based on the Bernoulli distribution, while Kovaleva et al.
094   (2019); Lee et al. (2019b) showcase that fine-tuning the top few layers achieves competitive model
095   performance in downstream tasks. Moreover, Lee et al. (2022) propose to select layers according to
096   their gradient statistics.
097
      Recent studies have extended the selective fine-tuning techniques to FL scenarios (Nguyen et al.,
098
      2022a; Chen et al., 2022; Hilmkil et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b). Specifically, researchers (Lee
099
      et al., 2023; Dun et al., 2022) investigate layer-wise network decomposition to achieve selective
100   model fine-tuning on clients. However, these works fail to offer methodologies for adaptive and
101   dynamic layer selection that takes into account the heterogeneous characteristics of clients. In
102   addition, personalized FL algorithms (Pillutla et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b) propose to train
103   different subnetworks on clients towards better local models. Different from previous studies, we
104   focus on providing an in-depth understanding of selective layer fine-tuning in FL, considering the
105   heterogeneity from the perspective of client resources and local data distributions.

196   4     W HICH L AYERS S HOULD B E S ELECTED F OR F INE -T UNING ?
197
198   The aforementioned training process provides substantial flexibility in selective layer fine-tuning,
199   namely, clients are allowed to select different layers for local training and adjust their choices in
200   different training epochs. Such flexibility enables clients to tailor their local training to their data and
201   resources, providing feasible solutions for handling the heterogeneity in FL.
202   However, without a well-designed strategy for layer selection, the optimization of the global model
203   in FL could be severely hindered, potentially leading to a suboptimal solution or even failure in
204   convergence. As a result, researchers have proposed several useful strategies for layer selection in
205   recent years, including:
206
              ‚ÄĘ All clients select the same layer set for fine-tuning (Pillutla et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2019a;
207
                Zhang et al., 2022a; 2023; Lee et al., 2019b), i.e., Lti = Ltj , ‚ąÄi Őł= j;
208
209
              ‚ÄĘ Clients fix their selections across different training epochs (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Chen
                et al., 2023b), i.e., Lti1 = Lti2 , ‚ąÄt1 , t2 ‚ąą [T ].
210
211   These strategies for layer selection are proposed based on the insights drawn from experts‚Äô experience,
212   serving as special instantiations of the selected layer sets Lti . It is worth noting that these experience-
213   driven strategies might not consistently yield optimal results in various FL applications, particularly
214   considering client heterogeneity. This leads to an essential question: How to effectively determine the
215   task-specified layer selection strategy among a large search space of possible options? In the rest of
      this section, we provide a theoretical analysis to answer this question.

390   5     E XPERIMENTS
391
392   5.1   S ETTINGS
393
394   Datasets & Models We conduct a series of experiments on several widely-used image classification
395   datasets, including CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) and DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019), text
396
      classification dataset, i.e., XGLUE-NC (Liang et al., 2020), and five benchmark question-answering
      (QA) datasets, including SCIQ (Welbl et al., 2017), OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), PIQA (Bisk
397
      et al., 2020), ARC-Easy and ARC-Challenge (Bhakthavatsalam et al., 2021) datasets. More details of
398
      the adopted datasets can be found in in Appendix B.1.
399
400   As for the splitting of datasets, inspired by previous studies (Zhu et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023),
401   we consider two commonly observed data heterogeneity among clients: (i) Label skew: adopting
402
      Dirichlet distribution to allocate data samples of the CIFAR-10 dataset; (ii) Feature skew: adopting
      naturally domain shift on the DomainNet, XGLUE-NC, and QA datasets. Specifically, each QA
403
      dataset is equally divided into two subsets, with each client possessing one subset of samples from
404
      one of the five datasets. We adopt the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) for image classification
405   tasks and the multi-lingual XLM-Roberta-Base (Conneau et al., 2019) model on the XGLUE-NC
406   dataset. In addition, we train a LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) model on the QA dataset.
407
408
      Server & Clients In the experiments, we set up an FL system with a central server and N = 100
      clients. In each training epoch, the server randomly selects a subset of 20 clients, and broadcasts
409
      the up-to-date model to these clients for local training. Besides, there are N = 10 clients in the
410
      QA task and five clients are randomly chosen for training in each epoch. The resource budgets of
411   clients are limited, which are quantified as the maximum number of layers they can fine-tune in the
412   local training. For example, we use Ri = 1 to indicate that the resource of client i cannot afford
413   fine-tuning more than 1 layer. The resource budgets can be identical or heterogeneous among clients.
414
      Baselines We compare the proposed layer selection strategy with several competitive baselines,
415
      including: (i) Top (Kovaleva et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019b): Clients only fine-tune the top few layers
416
      (near the output) based on their task-specific data; (ii) Bottom (Lee et al., 2022): Clients only fine-
417   tune the bottom few layers (near the input) based on their task-specific data, which can be beneficial
418   for the tasks with input-shift; (iii) Both (Xiao et al., 2023): Clients fine-tune an equal proportion
419   of both the top and bottom layers, which shows the effectiveness for large language models; (iv)
420   SNR (Mahsereci et al., 2017): Clients fine-tune the layers with higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
421   values, defined as the ratio of the mean of gradient elements to their variance; (v) RGN (Cheng et al.,
422   2023; Lee et al., 2022): Clients fine-tune the layers with higher relative gradient norm (RGN) values,
423   defined as the ratio of gradient norm to the parameter norm; (vi) Moreover, we consider Full model
424   fine-tuning, i.e., training the entire model, as the performance benchmark. More implementation
425
      details can be found in Appendix B.2.

527   6           C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   In this paper, we study a practical FL setting for fine-tuning foundation models, where clients are
530   allowed to optimize a subset of layers using their task-specific data. We carefully consider the
531   impact of both data heterogeneity and device heterogeneity across clients, providing a comprehensive
532   theoretical analysis of the optimization objective of selective layer fine-tuning and global model
533
      convergence. The theoretical analysis offers insights into how the selected layers influence global
      model training and highlights the role of layer importance and client heterogeneity. We further
534
      propose a novel strategy for layer selection that considers the local data and available resources
535
      at clients. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed strategy outperforms baseline
536   strategies in improving the global model training performance and even matches full model fine-
537   tuning performance in some scenarios, showing the potential for more efficient and tailored real-world
538   FL applications of the proposed layer selection strategy.

                clipart, real, sketch, infograph, painting, and quickdraw. In this training task, we consider
1100            the varying feature case where P (xi |yi ) is different among clients. Following (Li et al.,
1101            2021), each client is allocated random samples from only one domain.

       Besides, we fine-tune an XLM-Roberta-Base model on the following text dataset:

1106          ‚ÄĘ XGLUE-NC (Liang et al., 2020): This is a news classification task consisting of 10 classes.
1107            The news texts comprise five languages (English, Spanish, French, German, and Russian).
1108            We allocate one random language to each client, which naturally introduces domain shift
1109            among clients.
1110
1111
       In addition, we evaluate a LLaMA-2-7B model on the QA datasets.
1112
              ‚ÄĘ QA: The QA datasets consist of four commonly used question-answering datasets, i.e.,
1113            SCIQ (Welbl et al., 2017), OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020),
1114            ARC-Easy and ARC-Challenge (Bhakthavatsalam et al., 2021). The datasets are transformed
1115            into classification tasks where the model determines the correct answer for each question
1116            and corresponding choices. We allocate the samples from one random dataset to each client,
1117            indicating the domain shift among clients.
