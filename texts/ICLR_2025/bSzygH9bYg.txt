009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) is crucial for reducing the need for
012            extensive manual data annotation when training deep networks on point cloud
013            data. A significant challenge of UDA lies in effectively bridging the domain
014            gap. To tackle this challenge, we propose Curvature Diversity-Driven Nuclear-
015            Norm Wasserstein Domain Alignment (CDND). Our approach first introduces a
016
               Curvature Diversity-driven Deformation Reconstruction (CurvRec) task, which
               effectively mitigates the gap between the source and target domains by enabling
017
               the model to extract salient features from semantically rich regions of a given
018
               point cloud. We then propose Deformation-based Nuclear-norm Wasserstein
019            Discrepancy (D-NWD), which applies the Nuclear-norm Wasserstein Discrepancy
020            to both deformed and original data samples to align the source and target domains.
021            Furthermore, we contribute a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of D-
022            NWD in distribution alignment and demonstrate that it is generic enough to be
023            applied to any deformations. To validate our method, we conduct extensive exper-
024            iments on two public domain adaptation datasets for point cloud classification and
025            segmentation tasks. Empirical experiment results show that our CDND achieves
026            state-of-the-art performance by a noticeable margin over existing approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Adopting deep neural network on point cloud representation learning has led to significant success in
031   various applications, including robotics Maturana & Scherer (2015); Duan et al. (2021), autonomous
032   vehicles Mahjourian et al. (2018); Cui et al. (2021), and scene understanding Zheng et al. (2013);
033   Zhu et al. (2017). Most works rely on supervised learning Su et al. (2015); Wu et al. (2015); Qi et al.
034   (2017) and assume that training and testing data are sampled from the same distribution. However,
035   acquiring labels for training data is both time-consuming and labor-intensive. Moreover, testing data
036   may be from a different distribution w.r.t. training data in real-world scenarios, known as ‘domain
037   gap’. Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) offers a solution to tackle these issues by utilizing
038   knowledge transfer from source domains with annotated data to target domains with only unlabeled
039
      data. Although UDA is well studied for 2D planner data, e.g., images, UDA for 3D point clouds has
      not been explored extensively due to challenges such as irregular, unstructured, and unordered nature
040
      of 3D point cloud data. Such irregularities exacerbate geometric variations between the source and
041
      target domains compared to the 2D planner data and make extending existing solutions nontrivial.
042
043   To address the above challenges, we propose Curvature Diversity-Driven Nuclear-Norm Wasser-
044   stein Domain Alignment (CDND). Our first contribution is a deformation reconstruction method
045
      that leverages curvature diversity in different regions of a point cloud for domain alignment. We
      evaluate curvature diversity based on the entropy that captures the saliency of each region. This
046
      metric is then used to select regions for deformation and reconstruction. Unlike previous methods
047
      such as Achituve et al. (2021), our approach strategically selects regions based on their information
048   content. Also, distinct from Zou et al. (2021), which selects regions with high curvature and clas-
049   sifies them into a fixed set, we select regions with low curvature diversity and focus on deforming
050   and reconstructing these regions rather than classifying them. Our method avoids deforming se-
051   mantically rich regions and enables the feature extractor to focus on extracting features from these
052   regions. Our second contribution is the Deformation-based Nuclear-norm Wasserstein Discrepancy
053   (D-NWD). Unlike NWD, D-NWD incorporates features from both original and deformed samples
      when aligning the source and target domains. While including features from deformed samples cre-

      ates a diverse and robust feature space that may improve model generalization under domain shift,
055   our primary contribution lies in the theoretical analysis of D-NWD. This analysis demonstrates that
056   D-NWD can reduce the domain gap between the source and target domains, showcasing its effec-
057   tiveness. Our analysis also illustrates that D-NWD is generic enough to be used for any deformation
058   method, not just the one presented in this paper. Experiments on common benchmarks for both
059   classification and segmentation show that our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
062
063
      Domain Adaptation on Point Clouds. Despite extensive works on UDA for 2D planner image
064   data Ganin & Lempitsky (2015); Tzeng et al. (2017); Mansour et al. (2008), only a limited number of
065   studies Qin et al. (2019); Achituve et al. (2021); Shen et al. (2022); Zou et al. (2021) address UDA in
066   point clouds and non-planner data spaces as extending methods for 2D data for point clouds in non-
067   trivial. Qin et al. Qin et al. (2019) introduce PointDAN that integrates both local and global domain
068   alignment strategies. They also provide the PointDA benchmark for point cloud classification under
069   the UDA setting. Achituve et al. Achituve et al. (2021) propose a domain alignment technique that
070   involves reconstruction from deformation and incorporates PointMixup Chen et al. (2020). They
071   also introduce the PointSegDA benchmark for point cloud segmentation under the UDA setting.
072
      Zou et al. Zou et al. (2021) utilize two geometry-inspired self-supervised classification tasks to
      learn domain-invariant feature. Shen et al. Shen et al. (2022) introduce a self-supervised method
073
      for learning geometry-aware implicit functions to handle domain-specific variations effectively. Our
074
      work differs from these approaches by proposing more sophisticated self-supervised learning tasks
075   and a generic theoretical framework, leading to state-of-the-art performance.
076
077   Optimal Transport for Domain Adaptation. The Wasserstein metric, known for encoding the
078
      natural geometry of probability measures within optimal transport theory, has been extensively stud-
      ied for its application in domain adaptation due to its nice properties. Gautheron et al. Gautheron
079
      et al. (2019) propose Wasserstein Distance Guided Representation Learning to leverage the Wasser-
080
      stein distance to enhance similarities between embedded features. Lee et al. (2019) and Gabourie
081   et al. (2019) propose to use the sliced Wasserstein discrepancy instead of L1 distance in Maximum
082   Classifier Discrepancy Saito et al. (2018) to achieve a more geometrically meaningful intra-class
083   divergence. Additionally, CGDM Du et al. (2021) introduces cross-domain gradient discrepancy to
084   further mitigate domain differences. DeepJ-DOT Damodaran et al. (2018) utilizes a coupling ma-
085   trix to map source samples to the target domain. Gautheron et al. Gautheron et al. (2019) propose a
086   feature selection technique that addresses the domain shifts problem. Moreover, Xu et al. Xu et al.
087   (2020) develop reliable weighted optimal transport, which uses spatial prototypical information and
088   intra-domain structure to evaluate sample-level domain discrepancies, resulting in a better pairwise
089
      optimal transport plan. Finally, Fatras et al. Fatras et al. (2021) present an unbalanced optimal trans-
      port method combined with a mini-batch strategy to efficiently learn from large-scale datasets. In
090
      this work, we developed our D-NWD based on another previous method, NWD Chen et al. (2022).

      3.2    C URVATURE D IVERSITY-D RIVEN D EFORMATION
149
      To extract domain-invariant features shared by both source and target domains, Achituve et al. pro-
150   posed deformation reconstruction Achituve et al. (2021). Specifically, there are three deforma-
151   tion strategies introduced in Achituve et al. (2021) for deforming point clouds, i.e., volume-based,
152   feature-based, and sample-based, according to the way of dividing point clouds into regions for
153   deformation.
154
      Although the strategies mentioned above use different techniques to select regions for deformation,
155
      they all randomly divide a point cloud into regions and uniformly select regions based on their
156   spatial locations or arrangements. However, this approach may not be optimal as regions within a
157   point cloud vary in their semantic richness, i.e., some regions contain more semantic information.
158   These semantically rich regions are crucial for tasks such as classification, as they have more distin-
159   guishable characteristics. For instance, to differentiate a point cloud of a plant from that of a lamp,
160   focusing on the leaves and flowers — which have richer semantic information — would be more
161   effective than focusing on the flower pot, which is similar to the base of a lamp. Thus, deforming
      regions with richer semantic information causes the point cloud to lose semantic meaning, making


                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      it difficult for a classifier to classify it. To encourage the feature extractor to prioritize regions with
163
      rich information, we propose deforming regions that are less semantically rich. This strategy helps
164   learning to extract features from the most informative or salient regions of a point cloud.

      6     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   We introduced a novel unsupervised domain adaptation approach specifically for point cloud data,
531
      which presents unique challenges due to its intricate geometric structures. Our method, CDND, in-
      tegrates curvature diversity-based deformation with Deformation-based Nuclear-norm Wasserstein
532
      discrepancy (D-NWD) to mitigate target domain performance degradation. Our theoretical analy-
533
      sis of D-NWD shows it minimizes an upper bound for target domain model error, thus enhancing
534   performance. Additionally, the theoretical analysis shows that D-NWD can be applied to any de-
535   formation method. Experimental results indicate that our approach is highly effective, surpassing
536   state-of-the-art methods on two major benchmarks. The success of our method in handling large do-
537   main differences highlights its adaptability and robustness. Ablation studies confirm that both core
538   components of CDND are essential for achieving optimal performance. Future work could explore
539   extending our approach to scenarios where both source domain data are not directly accessible due
      to privacy or when the two domains share a subset of their classes.

1105   SPST typically relies on ranking training samples by difficulty and gradually incorporating harder
1106   examples into training. The training samples for point cloud segmentation tasks are points in point
1107   clouds. However, mIoU is a global metric that evaluates performance across an entire point cloud,
1108   making it challenging to assign difficulty scores to individual points in a point cloud. The mechanism
1109   of SPST mismatches the per-point cloud, rather than per-point, evaluation criterion of mIoU.

1113   We perform a sensitivity analysis on the PointSegDA dataset. The results are shown in Figure 4.
1114   We select our hyperparameters based on the model’s performance on the validation set of the tar-
1115   get domain dataset. We focus on the hyperparameters with the most values for selection: Curva-
1116   ture diversity-based deformation reconstruction (CurvRec(En)-Low) and D-NWD. For the CurvRec
1117   weight pγq, we search across 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0. For the D-NWD weight pβ1 q, we explore
1118   0.0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0.
1119   The tasks FA, FM, MF, AF, and SM exhibit relative insensitivity to changes in the CurvRec and
1120   D-NWD weights, as their mIOU values tend to fluctuate within a narrow range, typically around
1121   5%. This suggests that these tasks are more robust to variations in these hyperparameters compared
1122   to others. In general, higher CurvRec weights tend to result in worse performance, particularly
1123   for tasks like FS, MA, and SA, where we observe a drop in mIOU as CurvRec weight increases.
1124   Higher D-NWD weights (e.g., 1.0) generally have a negative impact, leading to a decline in mIOU
1125   for many tasks, including FS, MA, and SF. Lower D-NWD weights (e.g., 0.0 or 0.05) are also
1126   generally associated with worse mIOU performance across tasks like MF, MS, and AS, suggesting
1127
       that a lighter emphasis on D-NWD is disadvantageous in these cases. However, moderate D-NWD
       weights (e.g., 0.1, 0.2, 0.5) lead to better results, as shown in the MS, AF, AM, SF, SA, and AS
1128
       tasks. In summary, a higher CurvRec weight combined with a moderate D-NWD weight enhances
1129
       model performance.
