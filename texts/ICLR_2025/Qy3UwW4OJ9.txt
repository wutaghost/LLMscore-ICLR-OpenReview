                                                       A BSTRACT
027
028
               In this paper, we show that, a good style representation is crucial and sufficient
029            for generalized style transfer without test-time tuning. We achieve this through
030            constructing a style-aware encoder and a well-organized style dataset called Style-
031            Gallery. With dedicated design for style learning, this style-aware encoder is trained
032            to extract expressive style representation with decoupling training strategy, and
033            StyleGallery enables the generalization ability. We further employ a content-fusion
034            encoder to enhance image-driven style transfer. We highlight that, our approach,
035            named StyleShot, is simple yet effective in mimicking various desired styles, i.e.,
036            3D, flat, abstract or even fine-grained styles, without test-time tuning. Rigorous
037
               experiments validate that, StyleShot achieves superior performance across a wide
               range of styles compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Image style transfer, extensively applied in everyday applications such as camera filters and artistic
043   creation, aims to replicate the style of a reference image. Recently, with the significant advancements
044   in text-to-image (T2I) generation based on diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal,
045   2021; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b), some
046   style transfer techniques that build upon large T2I models show remarkable performance. Firstly,
047   style-tuning methods (Everaert et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Sohn et al., 2024; Ruiz et al., 2023;
048   Gal et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) primarily tune embeddings or model weights during test-time.
049   Despite promising results, the cost of computation and storage makes it impractical in applications.
050   Even worse, tuning with a single image can easily lead to overfitting to the reference image. Another
051   trend, test-time tuning-free methods (Fig. 2 (a)) (Wang et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
052   2023; Qi et al., 2024) typically exploit a CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) image encoder to extract visual
053   features serving as style embeddings due to its generalization ability and compatibility with T2I
      models. However, since CLIP image encoder is primarily trained to extract unified semantic features

140   Leveraging the exceptional capabilities of large T2I models in image generation, numerous style

145
      feature space, aiming to bypass style-tuning at the cost of reduced style transfer quality. Recently,
146   Wang et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2024) employ CLIP image encoder
147   for extracting style features from each image. However, relying solely on semantic features extracted
148   by a pre-trained CLIP image encoder as style features often results in poor style representation. Our
149   study focuses on resolving these challenges by developing a specialized style-extracting encoder and
150   producing the high-quality stylized images without test-time style-tuning.

230   3.3    C ONTENT- FUSION ENCODER
231
232   In practical scenarios, users provide text prompts or images as well as a style reference image to
233   control the generated content and style, respectively. Previous methods (Jeong et al., 2023; Hertz
234   et al., 2023) typically transfer style by manipulating content image features. However, the content
235   features are coupled with style information, causing the generated images to retain the content’s
236
      original style. This limitation hinders the performance of these methods in complex style transfer
      tasks. Differently, we pre-decouple the content information by eliminating the style information in
237
      raw image space, and then introduce a content-fusion encoder specifically designed for content and
238
      style integration.
239
240   Content Extraction. Currently, Wang et al.
241   (2023a) utilizes de-colorization and subsequent
242
      DDIM Inversion (Song et al., 2020) for style
      removing. As demonstrated in Fig. 5 (a), this
243
      approach primarily targets low-level styles, leav-
244
      ing high-level styles like the brushwork of an oil
245   painting and low poly largely intact. Edge de-
246   tection algorithms such as Canny (Canny, 1986)        Reference   (a)DDIM Inversion  (b)Canny       (c)HED         (d)Ours
247   and HED (Xie & Tu, 2015) can explicitly re-           Figure 5: Illustration of the content input under different setting.

249   styles are still implicitly present in the edge details. To comprehensively remove the style from

251   thresholding and dilation. As a result, our content input xc (Fig. 5 (d)) remains only the essential

281   Two-stage Training. Given that the style embeddings are randomly initialized, jointly training the
282   content and style components leads the model to reconstruct based on the spatial information from
283   the content input, neglecting the integration of style embeddings in the early training steps. To resolve
284
      this issue, we introduce a two-stage training strategy. Specifically, we firstly train our style-aware
      encoder and corresponding cross-attention module while excluding the content component. This
285
      task fine-tuning on the whole style-aware encoder enables it to capture style relevant information.
286
      Subsequently, we exclusively train the content-fusion encoder with the frozen style-aware encoder.

      3.4   S TYLE G ALLERY & D E - STYLIZATION
289
290   StyleGallery. Previous methods (Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) frequently utilized the LAION-
291   Aesthetics (Schuhmann et al., 2022) dataset. Following the style analysis outlined in McCormack
292   et al. (2024), we found that LAION-Aesthetics comprises only 7.7% stylized images. Further analysis
293   revealed that the style images within LAION-Aesthetics are characterized by a pronounced long-tail
294   distribution. As illustrated in Fig. 6, painting style accounts for 43% of the total style samples while
295   the combined proportion of other 42 styles is less than 0.6%. Models trained on the dataset with
296
      extremely imbalanced distribution easily overfit to high-frequency styles, which compromises their
      ability to generalize to rare or unseen styles, as detailed in the experimental analysis in Sec. 4.4.
297
      This indicates that the efficacy of style transfer is closely associated with the style distribution of the
298
      training dataset.
299
300   Motivated by this observation, we construct a style-balanced dataset, called StyleGallery, covering
301   several open source datasets. Specifically, StyleGallery includes JourneyDB Sun et al. (2024), a
302
      dataset comprising a broad spectrum of diverse styles derived from MidJourney, and WIKIART
      Phillips & Mackintosh (2011), with extensive fine-grained painting styles, such as pointillism and
303
      ink drawing, and a subset of stylized images from LAION-Aesthetics. 99.7% of the images in our
304
      StyleGallery have style descriptions. The style distribution within StyleGallery is more balanced and
305   diverse as illustrated in Fig. 6, which benefits our model in learning expressive and generalized style
306   representation.
307
308
      De-stylization. We notice that the text prompts for images frequently contain detailed style de-
      scriptions, such as “a movie poster for The Witch in the style of Arthur rackham”, leading to the
309
      entanglement of style information within both text prompt and reference image. Since the pre-trained
310
      Stable Diffusion model is well responsive to text conditions, such an entanglement may hinder the
311   model’s ability to learn style features from the reference image. Consequently, we endeavor to remove
312   all style-related descriptions from the text across all text-image pairs in StyleGallery, retaining only
313   content-related text. Our decoupling training strategy separates style and content information into
314   distinct inputs, aiming to improve the extraction of style embeddings from StyleGallery.

530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduce StyleShot, the first work to specially designate a style-aware encoder to
533   extract rich style in style transfer task on diffusion model. StyleShot can accurately identify and
534
      transfer the style of any reference image without test-time style-tuning. Particularly, due to the design
      of the style-aware encoder, which is adept at capturing style representations, StyleShot is capable
535
      of learning an expressive style such as shading, layout, and lighting, and can even comprehend fine-
536
      grained style nuances. With our content-fusion encoder, StyleShot achieves remarkable performance
537   in image-driven style transfer. Furthermore, we identified the beneficial effects of stylized data and
538   developed a style-balanced dataset StyleGallery to improve style transfer performance. Extensive
539   experimental results validate the effectiveness and superiority of StyleShot over existing methods.

      B.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
942
943   In this section, we first provide some implementation details about our style-aware encoder discussed
944   in Sec 3.2. We adopt the open-sourced SD v1.5 as our base T2I model. We construct our StyleGallery
945   with diverse styles, which totally contain 5.7M image-text pairs, including open source datasets such
946   as JourneyDB, WiKiArt and a subset of stylized images from LAION-Aesthetics. Our varied-size
947   patches are divided into three sizes 1/4, 1/8 and 1/16 of image length with corresponding quantities of
948   8, 16, and 32, as shown in Fig. 19. For patches of varying sizes, we utilize ResBlocks with differing
949   depths implemented using six, five, and four ResBlocks, respectively. Furthermore, our Transformer
950
      Blocks are initialized from the pre-trained weights of OpenCLIP ViT-H/14 (Ilharco et al., 2021).
      Following the Transformer Blocks, we introduce an ad-

      ditional MLP for the style embeddings. Similar to IP-                  Style Reference Image

954   jected style embeddings. We train our StyleShot on a

957   GPU, and set the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hut-
