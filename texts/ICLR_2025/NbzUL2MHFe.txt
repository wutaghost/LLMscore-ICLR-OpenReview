009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            A common objective in the analysis of tabular data is estimating the conditional
012            distribution (in contrast to only producing predictions) of a set of “outcome” vari-
013            ables given a set of “covariates", which is sometimes referred to as the “density
014            regression” problem. Beyond estimation on the conditional distribution, the gener-
015            ative ability of drawing synthetic samples from the learned conditional distribution
016
               is also desired as it further widens the range of applications. We propose a flow-
               based generative model tailored for the density regression task on tabular data.
017
               Our flow applies a sequence of tree-based piecewise-linear transforms on initial
018
               uniform noise to eventually generate samples from complex conditional densities
019            of (univariate or multivariate) outcomes given the covariates and allows efficient
020            analytical evaluation of the fitted conditional density on any point in the sample
021            space. We introduce a training algorithm for fitting the tree-based transforms
022            using a divide-and-conquer strategy that transforms maximum likelihood training
023            of the tree-flow into training a collection of binary classifiers—one at each tree
024            split—under cross-entropy loss. We assess the performance of our method under
025            out-of-sample likelihood evaluation and compare it with a variety of state-of-the-art
026            conditional density learners on a range of simulated and real benchmark tabular
027
               datasets. Our method consistently achieves comparable or superior performance
               at a fraction of the training and sampling budget. Finally, we demonstrate the
028
               utility of our method’s generative ability through an application to generating
029
               synthetic longitudinal microbiome compositional data based on training our flow
030            on a publicly available microbiome study.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Many data analytical tasks involving tabular data require learning the conditional distribution of a
036   (possibly multivariate) outcome y given a set of contextual variables (or covariates) x, and generating
037   new observations of y conditional on the value of x. Given the effectiveness of tree-ensemble based
038   approaches for characterizing tabular data in both supervised learning Grinsztajn et al. (2022) and
039
      generative modeling of joint (i.e., unconditional) multivariate distributions (Inouye and Ravikumar,
      2018; Awaya and Ma, 2023), we aim to introduce an efficient approach to approximate conditional
040
      densities of tabular data using ensembles of tree-based transforms. Specifically, we introduce a
041
      tree-based normalizing flow capable of (1) outputting the fitted density p(y|x) for any given pair of
042   value (x, y); (2) efficiently generating y given x from the estimated distribution; and (3) being trained
043   efficiently based on maximum likelihood using tree-fitting algorithms that requires a computational
044   budget linear in the sample size.
045
      Key to efficient training of our tree flow is a new single-tree learning algorithm for approximating con-
046
      ditional densities, which is employed repeatedly to find the sequence of tree-based transforms whose
047
      composition constitutes the flow. The single-tree learning algorithm transforms the unsupervised
048   problem of learning a conditional density into a collection of supervised problems involving binary
049   classification, one at each tree split, and accomplishes maximum likelihood fitting on the tree-based
050   transform through minimizing the cross-entropy loss on the corresponding binary classification
051   tasks. The framework allows any binary classifier, and in particular non-tree based classifiers to be
052   incorporated, thereby complementing the effectiveness of the tree-based transforms in approximating
053   the conditional density. For illustration, we assess the performance of the resulting tree-flow using
      logistic regression and multi-layper perceptrons (MLPs) as the binary classifier at the tree splits.

      The tree-based transforms are all piecewise linear mappings with closed form expressions derived
055   from the trained binary classifiers, and so are their inverses. The Jacobian of the piecewise linear
056   transforms is piecewise constant and corresponds exactly to the fitted conditional density at each
057   iteration of the tree fitting to the current “observations” and therefore are available immediately as an
058   output of the tree fitting algorithms during training. The sampling stage of the algorithm involves
059   simply applying a sequence of piecewise linear transforms, which are the inverses of the transforms
060   learned during training, to uniform noise which can be carried out in linear time.
061   In summary, we propose a flow-based generative model for conditional density p(y|x) that utilizes
062   tree-based transforms along with covariate-dependent probability splits to approximate the conditional
063   density, while offering exact density evaluation and efficient training and sampling. Some unique
064   features of our method are
065
066          • Combining the strength of trees and non-tree based approximations. Our approach
067            exploits the effectiveness of tree-based transforms in approximating multivariate distribution
068
               on tabular data, along with the additional flexibility of non-tree based approximators such
               as logistic regression and neural network (NN)-based binary classifiers to approximate the
069
               smooth varying density over covariate values. We empirically show that the tree and non-tree
070
               hybrid approach can achieve superior performance on conditional density estimation tasks
071            involving tabular data over other state-of-the-art methods based only on NNs.
072
073
             • Efficient training and sampling. We employ a divide-an-conquer strategy by converting
               the unsupervised density learning problem to a collection of binary classification problems
074
               defined on the tree splits, and introduce a tree-fitting algorithm with O(ndq) time complexity
075
               for a training set of n observations with d outcome variables and q conditioning covariates.
076            Sampling from the trained flow involves applying a sequence of piecewise linear transforms
077            to uniform noise, which can be completed efficiently at complexity O(q) for drawing each
078            sample, independent of d.
079
080   Because our approach falls into the general class of normalizing flows (NFs), it also inherits the
081   general desirable properties of NFs, including
082
083          • Exact conditional density evaluation. Our method allows evaluating the fitted conditional
084
               density at any point in the sample space. In particular, the time complexity of evaluating the
               conditional density of one sample with our method is O(q), independent of the number of
085
               outcome variables.
086
087   We carry out extensive numerical experiments to assess the performance of our method in density
088   estimation and compare it to a range of state-of-the-art competitors on both simulated and real
089   benchmark datasets. We consider an application to a longitudinal microbiome compositional dataset
090   in which we generate synthetic microbiome compositions given time as a covariate. The results
091   showcase the effectiveness of our method in capturing complex multivariate conditional densities.

      2.1   A TREE ENSEMBLE - BASED APPROXIMATION TO CONDITIONAL DENSITIES
096
097   The problem of conditional density estimation is to find a close approximation fx : Y → R to an
098   unknown conditional distribution p(·|x) given a training set of n observations {(xi , yi )}ni=1 , where
099   yi |xi ∼ p(yi |xi ) independently given the covariate xi . We build the conditional distributions of a
100   d-dimensional vector y as normalizing flows. That is, given a set of covariate values x, the vector y
101   can be obtained by applying a sequence of x-dependent invertible and differentiable transformations
102   on a random variable u uniformly distributed over the d-dimensional unit cube, (0, 1]d .
103   We train the normalizing flow to a target conditional distribution by maximum likelihood, i.e.,
104   minimizing the forward KL divergence. Inouye and Ravikumar (2018) introduced a flow incorporating
105   a class of piecewise linear transforms defined on binary partition trees. This class of tree-based flows
106   was later more formally studied and shown to be analogous to ensemble tree approximators such
107   as tree boosting by Awaya and Ma (2023). The tree-based piecewise linear transforms generalize
      the notion of the cumulative distribution function (CDF) for a one-dimensional distribution to

      2.3   A DDITIONAL TECHNICAL IMPROVEMENTS
226
227
      We incorporate two additional technical strategies in training the tree flow that can lead to substantial
228   improvement in applications. The first strategy involves regularization through shrinkage, which
229   ensures that each tree transform in the flow modifies the residual distribution only slightly to avoid
230   overfitting, and adopts different rates of transform at different spatial scales. The second strategy
231   addresses the limitation of axis-aligned partition in the tree fitting by ensembling over multiple
232   (covariate-dependent) rotations of the training data.
233
      1. Regularization through scale-dependent learning rate and early stopping. To avoid overfitting and
234   smooth the resulting probability measure, a small learning rate can be applied to each tree-CDF to
235   shrink its corresponding probability measure towards the uniform distribution, thereby achieving
236   regularization. Specifically, in our implementation, the regularization can be applied in a scale-
237   specific fashion by specifying a learning rate for each tree node A according to the size of the set
238   A, as measured by the Lebesgue measure µ(A). Specifically, we incorporate the scale-dependent
239   learning rate {c(A)}A∈I(Tk ) by setting Gk,x (Al |A) = c(A)pθk (x) + (1 − c(A)) µ(A   l)
                                                                                        µ(A) where c(A) is
240   defined as c(A) = c0 (1 + log2 µ(A))−γ . The constant c0 ∈ (0, 1) controls the global shrinkage level,
241   and γ ≥ 0 controls the rate at which shrinkage intensifies as the node volume decreases. Specifically,
242   a γ of 0 applies uniform shrinkage across all nodes regardless of their volume, whereas a positive γ
243   results in increased shrinkage at smaller nodes, serving as a form of “soft pruning".
244
      The total number K of tree-CDFs is determined using early stopping, which halts the algorithm
245
      when the log-likelihood on a separate validation set does not increase for w consecutive iterations,
246   where w is a predefined window size. Additionally, since tree-CDFs may utilize various types of
247   binary classifiers, this early stopping criterion can be independently applied to each classifier type.
248   For example, the algorithm might initially use the logistic regression for node-level probability
249   assignments. If there is no improvements in log-likelihood, this indicates that the logistic regression
250   may no longer be capturing additional distributional structure from the training data. At this point, the
251   algorithm could switch to a more complex classifier, such as MLPs, to attempt to extract more refined
252   distributional structures, using potentially fewer tree-CDFs but with more complicated node-level
253   models. As we shall see later in the experiments, such combination of classifiers can improve the
254
      performance over a single classifier.
255   The full algorithm for training the conditional tree flow that includes scale-specific shrinkage and
256   early stopping is summarized in Algorithm 1.
257
      2. Rotation ensemble of tree flows
258
259   To alleviate the restrictions associated with axis-aligned partitions and enhance the approximative
260   ability of our tree flow, we propose using an ensemble of conditional tree flows trained on multiple
261
      rotated versions of the training data. To this end, we rotate the yi ’s in the original training data to
      generate J distinct data sets, denoted as D1 , · · · , DJ , where Dj = {(xi , yi Rj )}, Rj is a rotation
262
      matrix applied to each data set. The ensemble model is built by training individual conditional tree
263
      flows on rotated datasets and taking a weighted average of their conditional densities with covariate-
264   dependent weights. Specifically, to maintain computational efficiency we adopt an adaptive binning
265   strategy in constructing the weights. We partition the covariate space X into disjoint regions, where
266   weights are constant in each region but can vary across regions. Within each region, the weights are
267   determined by the likelihood of the rotated training samples—rotations that yield a higher likelihood
268   are up-weighted. (The details of the weights are provided in Appendix E.) In our experiments, equally
269   spaced 2D rotations are used, and X is partitioned using k-means clustering, with further details
      provided in Section 3.

481   4     C ONCLUSION
482
483   We proposed a generative model for conditional densities based on a normalizing flow with tree-
484   CDF transforms. We demonstrated conditional density estimation with our proposed model and
485   compared with other conditional density estimation methods with simulated data and real-world UCI
      datasets. We note that the performance of our method in the experiments is achieved with Logistic


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Regression and MLP(4,4), and expect a wider class of classifiers may provide further improvements.
487   We also demonstrated the use of the proposed method in generative sampling in a microbiome context.
488   Among many possible applications, the trained generative model can be used to provide uncertainty
489   quantification on summary statistics computed on the microbiome data given the covariates.
490
      A limitation of our approach, which is common for tree-based approaches adopting axis-aligned
491
      partitions, is that it may not approximate well high-dimensional distributions (i.e., those with hundreds
492
      or more features) especially in the presence of high-order correlation structure. So far our experiments
493   have focused on tabular data with ≤ 100 dimensions, and so the available empirical evidence is
494   limited to this domain. Possible extensions to overcome high-dimensional problems include adopting
495   non-axis-aligned partitions, which will incur computational challenges. We leave this to future work.

      D     T IME COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
673
674   To fit a tree-CDF, the optimal splitting at each internal node is selected from S × d possible splits (with S
675   cutpoints per axis). When evaluating each candidate split, the fitting process for a node-level binary classi-
676   fier—whether using Logistic Regression or a Multilayer Perceptron with two hidden layers of 4 nodes each as
677   in our implementation—has a time complexity of O(nq). Therefore, the overall complexity for fitting each
      tree-CDF is O(ndq).
678
679   Applying a tree-CDF to one observation indeed has a complexity of O(q). As demonstrated in previous work
680   (Awaya and Ma, 2023), a tree-CDF can be represented by a series of linear transformations at each level of the
      tree, with each transformation costing O(q) due to the evaluation of the splitting probability with the trained
681
      node-level binary classifier. Since the maximum depth R of the trees is fixed, there are at most R of these
682   O(q) operations, thus applying a tree-CDF to a d-dimensional vector is O(q). (Detailed information about the
683   multi-scale decomposition of tree-CDFs and their inverses are provided in the Appendix A. )
684
      With the fitted conditional tree flow, evaluating the density of a test sample is O(q) because it avoids any
685   computationally expensive steps such as evaluating Jacobians. Instead, the following equation is used for density
686   evaluation of a sample (x, y):
687                                                       K
                                                          Y
688                                             fx (y) =     gk,x (y (k−1) )
                                                              k=1
689
               (k)             (k−1)         (0)
690   where y     = Gk,x (y        ) and y     = y. Updating y (k−1) to y (k) is O(q), and calculating gk,x (y (k−1) )
691   involves just the product of splitting probabilities along the path from the root to the leaf that contains y (k−1)
