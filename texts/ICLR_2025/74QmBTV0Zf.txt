009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Many use cases require retrieving smaller portions of text, and dense vector-based
012               retrieval systems often perform better with shorter text segments, as the semantics
013               are less likely to be “over-compressed” in the embeddings. Consequently, practi-
014               tioners often split text documents into smaller chunks and encode them separately.
015               However, chunk embeddings created in this way can lose contextual information
016
                  from surrounding chunks, resulting in sub-optimal representations. In this paper,
                  we introduce a novel method called “late chunking”, which leverages long con-
017
                  text embedding models to first embed all tokens of the long text, with chunking
018
                  applied after the transformer model and just before mean pooling - hence the term
019               “late” in its naming. The resulting chunk embeddings capture the full contextual
020               information, leading to superior results across various retrieval tasks. The method
021               is generic enough to be applied to a wide range of long-context embedding models
022               and works without additional training. To further increase the effectiveness of late
023               chunking, we propose a dedicated fine-tuning approach for embedding models.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
      Neural information retrieval (IR) relies on text embedding models (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) that
028
      are primarily based on the transformer architecture (Devlin et al., 2019) and have been pre-trained
029   using very large text corpora. These models capture important elements of texts’ semantics in the
030   form of dense vectors whose spatial relations - particularly cosine distance - are good proxies for
031   text similarity and relevancy. For many neural IR use cases like the well-known RAG (Retrieval
032   Augmented Generation) approach (Lewis et al., 2020), applications require splitting documents into
033   limited-size text chunks, and storing them and their vector embeddings in a database. At run-time,
034   neural IR techniques are used to retrieve chunks of text relevant to a user’s requests, which are,
035   in the case of RAG, presented to an LLM as a basis for synthesizing a response. Furthermore,
036   many other applications require processing small text segments (Salton et al., 1993; Demszky et al.,
037
      2020), and therefore rely on chunking, e.g., to quickly navigate a user to the relevant passage in a
      document Callan (1994).
038
039   Moreover, while long context embedding models can improve retrieval performance on long texts
040   (Günther et al., 2023), they still perform better on short texts (Zhou et al., 2024). As a result, chunk-
041   ing generally improves retrieval, even with models that support long contexts. (See Appendix A.1.)
042   However, long-distance semantic dependencies – when the relevant information to interpret one
043
      chunk of text is located in one or more other chunks – reduce the effectiveness of this search strat-
      egy. Figure 1 displays a Wikipedia article1 that is split into chunks of sentences. One can see that
044
      phrases like “its” and “the city” referencing “Berlin” which is mentioned only in the first sentence,
045
      e.g., it is harder for the embedding model to link it to the respective entity to produce a high-quality
046   representation.
047
048   To overcome this limitation, we introduce a novel technique called late chunking. This method
049
      leverages the long text embedding capabilities of recently published open source models (Günther
      et al., 2023; Nussbaum et al., 2024) to, first, encode all tokens of an entire document with their full
050
      in-document context into a sequence of token embeddings, and then break this sequence up into
051
      chunks, which receive embeddings via mean pooling of their token embeddings. This way, chunk
052   embeddings include relevant semantic information derived from their place in the whole text.

117   2   R ELATED W ORK
118
119
      Most modern text embedding models are trained on transformer-based architectures (Devlin et al.,
      2019) using the training method proposed by Reimers & Gurevych (2019). In general, the model is
120
      equipped with a pooling operator which converts the token embeddings produced by the transformer
121
      into a single vector representation. Mean pooling is especially popular, as Reimers & Gurevych
122   (2019) conduct experiments in which mean pooling shows the best performance among other meth-
123   ods. While the original transformer uses absolute positional encodings, methods that encode relative
124   positions like AliBi (Press et al., 2022) and RoPE (Su et al., 2024) allow effective training of em-
125   bedding models with larger context lengths (Günther et al., 2023; Nussbaum et al., 2024).
126
      To address the limited context length and overcome practical issues of handling embeddings of long
127
      texts, chunking text before embedding it has become common practice. While simple chunking
128   methods use a fixed token length (Lewis et al., 2020) or split text into units like sentences or para-
129   graphs, more sophisticated methods like semantic chunking (Kamradt, 2024) use the similarity of
130   embedding vectors of neighboring sentences to find optimal spans for chunking.
131
      To prevent the problem of missing context information various approaches have been proposed that
132
      augment the text of the chunks. For instance, practitioners divide text into overlapping chunks (Saf-
133
      jan, 2023), meaning that the end of one chunk shares some tokens with the beginning of the next
134   chunk. During the development of this paper, a blog post (Anthropic, 2024) introduced an alterna-
135   tive approach for producing contextualized chunk embeddings using an additional large language
136   model (LLM). The LLM receives as input the whole document and the target chunk to produce text
137   for augmenting the chunk text with relevant context information before passing it to the embedding
138   model. This is however computationally more expensive, as LLMs are typically much larger than
139   embedding models or even require paid access to LLM APIs. Similarly, Luo et al. (2024) extract
140   propositions for each paragraph using an additional language model. However, each paragraph is
141   processed independently, which might result in losing context across paragraphs. Moreover, this ap-
142
      proach cannot be used with any technique for segmenting the text (e.g. fixed-size, sentence-based,
      semantic chunking, ...) but is restricted to the texts produced by the language model.
143
144   Another branch of research proposes embedding models that encode and index an embedding for
145   each token. Models like ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020; Jha et al., 2024) use a method called
146   “late interaction”, which compares each token embedding of the query with each token embedding
147   of the document and can compute more accurate relevance scores in this way. However, in contrast
148
      to our proposed late chunking method, this leads to more computational effort during the vector
      search.
149
150   Furthermore, Chen et al. (2024) aims to produce contextualized embedding representations by train-
151   ing an embedding model specifically to produce contextualized embedding representations of sen-
152   tences.

154   3   M ETHOD
155
156   Late chunking is a strategy for taking advantage of the difference in size between the long context
157   input windows of recent embedding models and the relatively small size of optimal text chunks
158   for most applications. These models support much longer input texts, for example, 8192 tokens
159   for jina-embeddings-v2-small – roughly ten pages of standard text – while optimal chunk
160   sizes are typically much smaller, e.g., the size of a paragraph. The reasons can be manifold, one
161   being that LLMs get more inefficient when providing longer context, and a single short embedding
      vector only has a limited capacity to represent information.

      The naive chunking approach (left side in Figure 2) chunks texts before processing them, using sen-
      tences or paragraphs, and then applies an embedding model to the resulting chunks. Contrastively,
187
      late chunking, as described in Algorithm 1, first tokenizes the entire text, or the largest part of it
188
      possible (line 2), and applies the transformer part from the embedding model on it (line 3). This
189   generates a sequence of vector representations ϑ1 , . . . , ϑm for each token that encompass textual in-
190   formation from the entire text. To generate a single embedding for a text, many embedding models
191   apply mean pooling to these token representations to output a single vector. Late chunking instead
192   applies mean pooling to smaller segments of this sequence of token vectors, producing embeddings
193   for each chunk that take into account the entire text. It is important to highlight that late chunking
194   still requires boundary cues that are derived from the chunks determined by a chunking algorithm,
195   but these cues are used only after obtaining the token-level-embeddings - hence the term late in its
196   naming. Chunking algorithms usually chunk text into sequences of characters. For late chunking,
197
      boundary cues corresponding to a sequence of tokens are necessary. Accordingly, Lines 5-13 of the
      algorithm translate the chunk definition into boundary cues that are used by the pooling step in the
198
      lines14-16.

201   3.1   L ONG L ATE C HUNKING
202
      Although many embedding models offer a high enough context length to encode a large amount of
203
      text at once, the context length might still not be sufficient to encode very large documents in one
204
      step. Moreover, the memory required for the encoding increases exponentially with an increasing
205   number of tokens so that encoding all tokens at once becomes infeasible. To solve this problem,
206   we propose using long late chunking as described in Algorithm 2. Thereby, the text is split into
207   larger macro chunks of lmax tokens that encompass multiple smaller chunks. Each macro chunk is
208   processed separately by the LateChunking method. To avoid missing context, macro chunks are
209   augmented with a certain number of tokens ω that overlap with the next chunks. Those additional
210   tokens serve as supplementary context information during late chunking.

307   4.1   E VALUATION ON R ETRIEVAL TASKS
308
309   To test the effectiveness of late chunking, we apply our technique to the smaller retrieval tasks of the
310   BeIR benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021). We restrict the evaluation on the smaller datasets, as splitting
311   documents into smaller chunks increases the computational effort of the evaluation, which makes a
312   comprehensive evaluation on different models, tasks, and chunking techniques infeasible.
313   Those retrieval tasks consist of a query set, a corpus of text documents, and a QRels file that stores
314   information about the IDs of documents that are relevant for each query. To identify the relevant
315   documents of a query, one can chunk the documents, encode and store them into an embedding
316   index, and determine for each query embedding the chunks corresponding to the k-nearest-neighbors
317   (kNN) of their normalized vector representations. As each chunk corresponds to a document, one
318   can convert the kNN ranking of chunks into a kNN ranking of documents (for documents occurring
319   multiple times in the ranking, only the first occurrence is retained). After that, one can compare
320
      the resulting ranking with the ranking corresponding to the ground-truth QRels file and calculate
      retrieval metrics like nDCG@10.
321

323   and also report the score obtained without chunking. Both naive chunking and late chunking are

415
      uments are longer than 8192 tokens, we truncate the texts at 8192 tokens before the evaluation. We
      use the chunking method with fixed-size boundaries with different numbers of tokens and evaluate
416
      naive and late chunking with jina-embeddings-v2-small using the same retrieval evaluation
417
      method as described in Section 4.1. The results in Figure 3 show that late chunking performs bet-
418   ter than naive chunking, specifically for small chunk sizes. For NFCorpus, late chunking performs
419   consistently better, while for some of the reading comprehension tasks, naive chunking works better
420   when using large chunks. This may be due to some of the reading comprehension datasets requiring
421   finding a specific sentence or phrase embedded into a relatively unrelated textual context instead of
422   finding a whole document. Specifically, the two synthetic datasets Needle-8192 and Passkey-8192
423   are constructed by placing short relevant information into a document of unrelated text. In this case,
424   late chunking is not useful, as the additional context from the document is totally irrelevant.

453   Table 3 captures the results from our training experiments. The experiments include running
454
      both span-based and regular mean pooling training methods on the jina-embeddings-v3 and

455

458   retrieval adapters, following the same hyperparameter settings of Sturua et al. (2024), however with

462
      For the span-based training method, we prepare two datasets into the format described in Section 3.2
463   and make these publicly available on HuggingFace 4 . These two datasets are FEVER (Thorne et al.,
464   2018) and TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), which are well-suited for this experiment as they contain
465   annotations of where the relevant text can be found in the documents respectively. In the FEVER
466   dataset, these spans take the shape of sentence number annotations, while for TriviaQA the annota-
467   tions are usually a name, place, or date in the form of a short phrase. For FEVER, we only include
468   pairs where the document provides supporting evidence for the claim. When multiple spans are
469   annotated in these datasets, we select only the span, which occurs earliest in the document.
470   Across the datasets and models, span pooling and mean pooling during training deliver relatively
471   similar results, with span pooling consistently achieving a small improvement. The training dataset
472   selection also has a small effect on the performance, thus resulting in slightly higher results for
473   NarrativeQA when only training on TriviaQA, which is likely due to an overlap of domain and
474   phrasing of query-document pairs of the task and training data.
475   While the span pooling method for training shows promise, the training dataset diversity is quite
476   limited, as both training datasets are sourced from Wikipedia documents. The summed dataset
477   encompasses only ∼470k pairs in total for training, which may additionally limit the potential per-
478   formance gains. It may be possible to achieve higher performance with a larger quantity and more
479   diverse set of training data.

      5   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this paper, we present a novel approach for encoding text chunks with embedding models called
524
      late chunking. We demonstrate how it can resolve context dependency problems and show that it
525   improves text embeddings across a wide range of retrieval tasks. For handling situations in which
526   the maximum context length of the model is not sufficient, we present a long late chunking approach
527   to effectively solve this problem. Late chunking requires no additional training and is applicable to
528   a wide range of embedding models. Furthermore, we demonstrate that additional training with a
529   custom method can further enhance its performance on retrieval tasks.
