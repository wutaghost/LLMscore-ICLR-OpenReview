009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose Decrypto, a novel interactive benchmark for evaluating coordination,
012            competition, and theory of mind (ToM) reasoning capabilities in agentic, foun-
013            dational AI models. Existing benchmarks often suffer from data leakage, satura-
014            tion, and lack of interactivity, making it hard to measure the ability of intelligent
015            systems to model other agents’ reasoning. To overcome or alleviate these lim-
016
               itations, we introduce Decrypto, a multi-agent benchmark based on a popular,
               language-based board game and designed to be future-proof for large language
017
               models (LLMs). We validate Decrypto’s effectiveness through comprehensive
018
               empirical evaluations of frontier LLMs, robustness studies, and human-AI cross-
019            play experiments. We show that LLMs do not coordinate well with other LLMs
020            or humans and perform strictly worse than the latter. Specifically, LLMs struggle
021            to reason about the choices of others, even if they use the same underlying model,
022            pointing to a fundamental limitation of current systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Much recent effort has been made towards agentic behaviour and reasoning (Huang et al., 2023)
      to improve the capabilities of frontier foundational models. Multiple benchmarks have also been
028
      proposed to assess progress, with a focus on mathematical reasoning (Cobbe et al., 2021), common
029
      sense (Zellers et al., 2019), and theory of mind (Chen et al., 2024b).
030
031   However, many of those benchmarks suffer from significant shortcomings. For example, some
032   benchmarks are based on a fixed dataset of problems. This leads to a significant risk of data leakage,
033
      whereas models will appear to perform well but become brittle when the questions are rephrased.
      Even when leakage risks are mitigated, such as having a secret test set, those benchmarks are subject
034
      to saturation. Other benchmarks for tasks such as maths (Cobbe et al., 2021), spatial reasoning
035
      Clark et al. (2018), or even multiple choice question answering (Hendrycks et al., 2020), target
036   the weaknesses inherent in transformers trained on next-token prediction, such as failing to tokenize
037   numbers correctly, inability to perform complex operations, and more. More importantly, real-world
038   agentic applications are often multi-turn, multi-agent, partially observable, and stochastic. However,
039   many of the supervised benchmarks do not address these specific challenges, unlike Decrypto.
040
      Historically, games have proven valuable as benchmarks, requiring planning, decision-making,
041
      credit assignment and different types of reasoning. One such type of reasoning is theory of mind
042   (ToM), which requires creating and maintaining a mental model of other agents (artificial or biolog-
043   ical) within a multi-agent scenario. However, many ToM benchmarks are not interactive and suffer
044   from one or more of the scenarios above. As a ToM task, Decrypto presents a multi-turn language
045   variant of the Three Mountain Problem (Piaget et al., 1956) introduced to study child cognitive de-
046   velopment. This seminal problem examines whether a child can acknowledge physical points of
047   view distinct from its own. Additionally, Decrypto contains a cooperative and competitive aspect
048   and thus requires players to reason about the information available to each of the other players,
049   coordinate, and take strategic decisions accordingly.
050   Decrypto is inspired by the interactive environments prevalent in (multi-agent) reinforcement learn-
051   ing (RL) and based on the popular board game of the same name. In the paper, we first outline the
052   design decisions to ensure that the benchmark is simultaneously future-proof and as easy as possible
053   for LLMs, by removing every blocker that typically hurts LLM performance. We then introduce
      the different evaluation settings afforded by the game, evaluate a suite of popular frontier LLMs,

080   In summary, Decrypto distinguishes itself from existing benchmarks in several key ways:
081
082         1. Focus on Language-Based Reasoning: Unlike grid-world or embodied task environ-
083
               ments, Decrypto isolates language-based reasoning and association, directly leveraging
               LLMs’ core training objective. Decrypto is operated entirely through word associations,
084
               something that LLMs could reasonably be expected to excel at, since learning word co-
085
               occurrences is an important step towards reducing perplexity during pre-training.
086
087         2. Theory of Mind Assessment: Decrypto’s design specifically targets theory of mind capa-
088
               bilities, requiring agents to reason about the knowledge of other players.
089         3. Human-Compatible Evaluation: As a game designed for humans, Decrypto facilitates
090            comparisons between human and LLM performance, offering insights into the gap and
091            compatibility between artificial and human intelligence in strategic communication tasks.

      2   D ECRYPTO : T HE G AME
095
096   Decrypto is a word-based code-guessing board game published by Scorpion Masqué. It requires at
097
      least three players divided into two teams. We describe this variant here and implement it in this
      benchmark. Borrowing the terminology from cryptography, players are assigned three distinct roles:
098
      Alice (the Encoder), Bob (the Decoder), and Eve (the Interceptor or Eavesdropper). An instance of
099
      a game lasts 8 turns. We refer to one game instance as an episode, consistent with RL nomenclature.
100
101   As shown in Figure 1, Alice and Bob are on the same team and they share 4 ordered secret keywords,
102   ke = {kc }e where c ∈ [1, 4], e ∈ N is the current episode, and k ∈ K, where K is a predetermined
103
      corpus of keywords. In the original game, the corpus contains around 450 keywords. For example,
      k0 might be {1. guitar, 2. space, 3. apple, 4. sword}. The keywords ke are sampled at the beginning
104
      of the episode and remain fixed throughout the 8 turns.
105
106   Each turn, Alice samples a secret code of 3 non-repeating digits between 1 and 4, ct = {cj }t , where,
107   c ∈ [1, 4] as above, j ∈ [0, 2], and t ∈ [0, 7], e.g. at turn 0, c0 = {4, 1, 3}0 . Alice must provide 3
      public hints, one for each digit, ht = {hc }t ∀c ∈ ct , e.g. {knight, music, laptop} such that Bob can

111   If Bob guesses incorrectly, his team gets a Miscommunication token. If Eve guesses correctly,
112

      miscommunication tokens or Eve gains two Interception tokens, the episode ends, and Eve wins.
113

114
115   Bob starts with an advantage because Bob has access to the 4 keywords ke and Eve does not, see
116   Figure 1. However, the actual code is revealed publicly after Bob and Eve provide their guess. Eve
117   can, therefore, keep track of the hint history τ e = {τc }e , where c ∈ [1, 4] and τct = {h0c , ..., htc }, i.e.,
118

      progresses. For instance, if Alice provided the hints τ43 ={knight, duel, Middle Ages, blacksmith}
119

      it is quite likely that Eve will associate “shield” to digit 4. Therefore, Alice must be careful to
121   provide hints that are subtle enough to avoid interception yet sufficiently related to the keywords for
122   Bob to guess correctly.
123
124
      While the keywords are sampled from a predetermined set, the choice of hints is open-ended. The
      restriction is that hints must be real words (including proper nouns) and refer to the meaning of the
125
      keywords, not to their spelling or pronunciation.
126
127   The game provides a language reasoning challenge. Alice must choose associated hints carefully,
128   using theory of mind to anticipate how Bob and Eve will interpret them using their respective avail-
129   able information. Both miscommunications and interceptions are detrimental.

131   3    D ECRYPTO : T HE B ENCHMARK
132
133   Unlike other reasoning benchmarks, Decrypto is purposely designed to not require many of the
134   capabilities that large pre-trained models struggle with. In particular, it demands no symbolic rea-
135   soning (Clark et al., 2018; Bard et al., 2020), mathematical reasoning ((Cobbe et al., 2021; Zhang
136   et al., 2024a), spatial reasoning (Clark et al., 2018; Carroll et al., 2019), tool use (Xu et al., 2023a), or
137   particular attention to tokenization (which has notably been shown to affect arithmetic performance).
138   Instead, strong performance in Decrypto relies purely on word-based reasoning and theory of mind.
139   Because LLMs are trained to learn word co-occurrences and associations, we expect them to excel
140   at the game. However, this is not the case, and humans and simple hard-coded baselines outperform
141   even the most advanced open—and closed-source LLMs available.
142   Due to its two-team, three-player setup, Decrypto can be used to benchmark LLMs in both compet-
143   itive and cooperative scenarios. Being a game, the difficulty of Decrypto naturally scales with the
144   agents’ ability for each of the three roles. This makes the benchmark much harder to saturate, unlike
145   those relying on a fixed dataset of problems. We also curate a set of 680 possible keywords, re-
146   sulting in over 8.8 billion possible keyword combinations to limit memorization, and implement the
147   option to provide custom keywords beyond the standard set. Furthermore, the game was designed
148   for human play, facilitating human-AI coordination and qualitative performance comparison.
149   The benchmark, including integration with popular APIs, all keywords, and helper code to run
150   experiments and collect human data will be open-sourced along with this paper’s final release.
151
152   Competition. The first aspect of the Decrypto benchmark is to evaluate language reasoning in a
153   competitive setting. This setting involves assigning Alice and Bob with identical agents (i.e. the
154   same LLM) and evaluating the Alice-Bob pair against Eves with different agents. The goal is to
155   determine how well an LLM can play with itself to win against various interceptor Eves. A model
156   that outperforms other LLMs as Alice-Bob, and as Eve, likely has stronger reasoning capabilities.
157   The average number of turns per game is the most predictive performance variable.
158   The key metrics provided naturally by the game are the number of interceptions, the number of
159   miscommunications and certainly the number of wins either by Alice/Bob or Eve. The number
160   of miscommunications measures the ability of Alice and Bob to cooperate and the number of in-
161   terceptions captures Eve’s ability to compete. There is a tension between miscommunications and
      interceptions in Decrypto. Alice can aim to minimise miscommunications and provide easy hints.

187     If Alice’s hints were easy to guess, Alice would never miscommunicate with Bob, but Eve could
188
        certainly intercept the code. If Alice aims to minimise intercepts, the hints would be impossible to
        guess for Eve, but also for Bob. Thus, the number of miscommunications and intercepts are two
189
        sides of the same coin. Average game length per game allows us to capture both sides in one metric.
190
        If the average length is high, Alice and Bob can successfully balance the difficulty of hints to avoid
191     miscommunications and intercepts. The number of interceptions and miscommunications only cap-
192     ture part of the game. On one hand, if Alice wanted to avoid miscommunications, she would provide
193     obvious hints but get intercepted quickly. On the other hand, Alice could give hints that are almost
194     impossible to guess to avoid interceptions. In both cases, the games would be over quickly soon, so
195     the number of turns is an insightful metric.
196
        Benchmarking LLMs directly with and against other state-of-the-art LLMs is akin to Chess or Go,
197     where the best models are evaluated against each other and not on a fixed dataset of trajectories.
198     We strongly believe that solving the Decrypto challenge likely requires LLMs to perform multi-step
199     reasoning and to train them in self-play (SP) for goal-oriented tasks.
200
201
202     Ad-hoc Coordination In this setting, we are interested in evaluating the coordination ability. This
203
        setting freezes Eve (e.g., to a rule-based baseline or the strongest available LLM). It then pairs
        different LLMs with each other (e.g. Alice is GPT-4o, and Bob is Llama). What matters is the
204
        ability to coordinate with previously unseen agents. As above, the average number of turns per
205
        game is the most reasonable metric here.
206
207     A subset of ad-hoc coordination is human-AI coordination. This setting is similar to the above,
208     except one of the two agents (Alice or Bob) is played by a human. In this case, we evaluate the
209
        ability of LLMs to coordinate with humans, understand why they gave certain hints, and anticipate
        how they might interpret given hints.
210
211
212     Theory of Mind Both the competitive and cooperative settings require theory of mind for high
213     performance. Each of the three players has access to different information. Bob and Eve must ap-
214     proximate or recreate the reasoning that Alice took to arrive at her proposed hints to guess correctly.
215     Meanwhile, Alice must anticipate how each of the other two players will interpret the hints and
        choose accordingly.

251   4.1   S PECIALIST VS G ENERALIST AGENTS
252
253   To avoid trivial solutions for our benchmark, we will discuss two different types of agents, specialist
254   agents and generalist agents, and highlight which directions we encourage for exploration. In most
255   RL settings, the policy is designed specifically for a given environment since it is parameterised to
256   map the environment’s observations (or action-observations history) to its action space. This lim-
257   itation no longer necessarily holds with general-purpose models, mainly because large pre-trained
258   models are expected to perform well in several scenarios beyond their training distribution (Brown,
259   2020). As a result, we propose to divide agents into two distinct classes: specialist agents and
260
      generalist agents.
261   Specialist agents are all agents purposely designed using knowledge of the task. Solutions in this
262   space include rule-based strategies, fine-tuning a large pre-trained model on game data, building a
263   prompt pipeline informed by the rules of the games, and prompt engineering. In this framework, we
264   consider the prompt to be part of the agent, if it is needed at all. In this context, prompt engineering
265   is the designer’s burden, and the environment only returns key information, such as a dictionary.
266   Due to the larger design space, we expect specialist agents to outperform generalist ones in every
267   task. However, we especially encourage research on methods which produce specialist agents for
268   a large class of tasks. This includes better fine-tuning algorithms but excludes rule-based agents
269   for playing Decrypto, such as the Word2Vec (Mikolov, 2013) and GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014)
      baselines we present next.

270
      Specialist Agents - Baselines We introduce two specialist agent baselines to play Decrypto. For
271   the baseline models, we use the common crawl GloVe with a vocabulary of 2.2 million words (Pen-
272   nington et al., 2014) and the Word2Vec embeddings trained on the Google News dataset, provided
273   by the gensim library (Rehurek & Sojka, 2011; Mikolov, 2013). The baselines work as follows. Al-
274   ice can choose hints from a fixed corpus of 5696 hints. The hint corpus comprises the most common
275   nouns extracted from the Brown, Gutenberg, and Webtext corpora (Bird et al., 2009). We filter the
276   nouns to ensure all extracted nouns are present in the GloVe and Word2Vec embeddings. (We use
277   this hint corpus for the baselines only; LLM agents generate hints in an open-ended fashion, limited
278   only by the game rules and the model itself.)
279   Before Alice chooses hints, Alice and Bob agree on a strategy. For each code digit ctj , Alice picks
280   a hint htc and guarantees that the hint picked for the digit is more similar to the code’s keyword kc
281   than to any other keyword, i.e., s(htc , kc ) > s(htc , kj ) ∀j ∈ −c, where −c = [1, 4] \ c and s is the
282   cosine similarity.
283
      Alice increases the diversity of her hints by randomly sampling each hint from the top-K most sim-
284
      ilar words for each keyword under cosine similarity, filtering words that do not satisfy the constraint
285   above. We also enforce that Alice cannot reuse any previously used hints from the same episode.
286
287   Bob then analyses the hints and assigns each hint to its most similar keyword under cosine similar-
288
      ity, i.e., arg maxc∈C s(hX , Kc ) ∀X ∈ X. Bob guesses the code perfectly if Alice and Bob use
      the same word embeddings because the hints are guaranteed to be the most similar to the correct
289
      keyword under the same embedding model.
290
291   Eve is only given the hints h and the hint history for each keyword, which at turn 0 is empty. Eve
292   calculates the cosine similarity between the average embedding of each keyword’s hint history with
293   the hints. This results in a similarity matrix of size N × M , where N (= 3) is the number of hints
294
      and M (= 4) is the number of keywords. Eve combinatorially calculates the globally optimal guess
      based on the similarity values. Since N and M are typically small values, we are unconcerned about
295
      the computational complexity. However, Eve’s selection task is equivalent to a linear assignment
296
      problem and efficient algorithms, such as the Jonker-Volgenant algorithm, are available in open-
297   source libraries such as SciPy (Virtanen et al., 2020).
298
299   These baselines serve three purposes. First, they demonstrate that by pre-agreeing on a strategy
300
      and having perfect theory of mind, represented by a shared word similarity measure, it is possible
      to construct a virtually unbeatable Decrypto team, in the role of Alice and Bob, by setting K to a
301
      sufficiently large value. This establishes an upper bound on performance to which to compare other
302
      agents. Second, while they achieve arbitrarily good performance, we show in Figure 2 that changing
303   the similarity measure for one of the agents can result in a catastrophic lack of coordination and
304   poor cross-play (XP) performance. Third, by selecting a low enough value of K, we can operate
305   the baselines in the regime where the two embeddings (Word2Vec and GloVe) correlate and are
306   likely to still rely on the useful signal captured by those embeddings. For this reason, we use
307   baselines with K = 16 for the remainder of the paper, corresponding to the point in Figure 2 before
308   miscommunications increase significantly.
309   Generalist agents, in contrast to specialised agents, refer to general-purpose models used to play
310   the game “out-of-the-box” without any additional fine-tuning on task-specific data. In particular,
311   for generalist agents, we consider the prompt to be the observation returned by the environment,
312   which precludes any form of prompt engineering. Evaluating foundational models in this framework
313   assesses their ability to generalise to novel tasks, including out-of-distribution ones and those where
314   data is limited or unavailable.
315   Because foundational models are sensitive to their prompting, we recommend evaluating generalist
316   agents with a range of different prompts, to prevent the choice of prompt favouring any one model.
317   We provide such an evaluation using various hand-crafted Decrypto prompts in Figure 4 and show
318   that game performance is robust to prompt variations and depends significantly on model size.
319
      Generalist agents include individual pre-trained models and multi-agent systems of LLMs or tool-
320
      augmented generation. The only requirement is that any such agent be defined at a general level of
321   abstraction and not condition in any way on the evaluation benchmark (e.g., the Decrypto game).
322   For instance, an LLM that automatically rephrases the prompt for clarity is acceptable. Using a
323   regular expression to extract key information from the Decrypto prompt is not.

453   Theory of Mind The relatively weak performance of LLMs in the settings above are evidence
454   that LLMs do not possess the ToM reasoning abilities necessary to play Decrypto well. This is
455   illustrated in the failure cases in Appendix C. In one example, Alice fails to sufficiently reason about
456   the difficulty of their hints and is easily intercepted. In another, Alice provides an ambiguous hint
457
      and fails to predict what Bob might guess.
458   Additionally, Decrypto provides a platform on which to conduct explicit ToM experiments inspired
459   by works in cognitive psychology. The first such experiment is a word-based code-guessing variant
460   of the Three Mountain Problem (Piaget et al., 1956). We explicitly ask Alice to predict what Eve
461   will guess based on Alice’s hints. We evaluate the prediction abilities of Llama-3.1-8B and Llama-
462   3.1-70B as Alice and Eve each. We find that the accuracy is very low for both Alice models and that
463
      the larger Eve model is more predictable, as shown in Table 2. We also find that predictability and
      intercept ratio are closely connected, suggesting that a more capable Eve becomes more predictable.
464
465   The second experiment evaluates representational change (RC) and false belief (FB) in the way
466   defined in the seminal work of Gopnik & Astington. Our procedure is the following: At each turn
467   except the first, we branch out the context of the agent and prompt it three times independently.
468   The first prompt asks it to predict the four keywords. The second prompt reveals the keywords and
469
      asks the model what it thought were the keywords before the reveal. The third prompt again reveals
      the keywords and asks the model to predict what a “second Interceptor” who has seen everything
470
      except the reveal would think are the keywords. By comparing the first and the second answers, we
471
      measure RC, the ability of the agent to recognize when its belief about the world (but not the world
472   itself) changes due to additional information. By comparing the first and third answers, we measure
473   FB, the ability to represent other agents as having inaccurate beliefs about the world. For the Strong
474   variant of those tasks, we consider the agent to pass if it correctly predicts what it answered in
475   question 1. We consider an agent correct for the Weak variant if the answers to questions 2 or 3
476   are not the real keywords. Results in Table 3 show that ability correlates with model size but that
477   neither of the models gets perfect scores. On Strong tasks, pass rates are particularly low, evidence
478   that LLMs only do not have persistent models of their “mind” of that of others.
479   Overall, the results strongly support that LLMs struggle to model others’ reasoning and that De-
480   crypto has the potential to be a fruitful benchmark for further exploring these capabilities.

484
485   Recent research has seen a surge in developing game-based environments and benchmarks to eval-
      uate LLMs in multi-agent scenarios. These works span various domains, from grid-based worlds to

                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      social deduction games, each offering unique insights into LLM capabilities. Our Decrypto bench-
487   mark builds on and differentiates itself from these existing approaches.
488
489   In multi-agent game environments, several frameworks have emerged. BattleAgentBench (Wang
490
      et al., 2024) and AgentBench (Liu et al., 2023b) introduce grid-based worlds to assess cooperation
      and competition. While comprehensive, their reliance on spatial reasoning makes them less suitable
491
      for evaluating the language-based deception and coordination central to Decrypto. LLM-Arena
492
      (Chen et al., 2024a) and GameBench (Costarelli et al., 2024) offer more diverse game sets, including
493   word-based games like Undercover. These share similarities with Decrypto but lack its focused
494   evaluation of theory of mind and coordinated deception in a purely language-based setting.
495
496
      Social deduction and negotiation games have been another avenue for exploring LLM capabili-
      ties. Studies on Werewolf (Xu et al., 2023b;c), AvalonBench (Light et al., 2023), and ToMBench
497
      (Chen et al., 2024b) evaluate strategic communication and theory of mind, while LLM-Deliberation
498
      (Abdelnabi et al., 2023) examines interactive multi-agent negotiation. Decrypto builds on these
499   approaches by providing a more structured environment for isolating specific language-based rea-
500   soning and coordination aspects.
501
502
      Research has also explored LLMs in both cooperative and competitive dynamics. Studies on human-
      AI coordination in Overcooked (Liu et al., 2023a) and consensus-seeking in multi-robot tasks (Li
503
      et al., 2023) focus on cooperation. In contrast, AgentPro (Zhang et al., 2024b) examines competitive
504
      scenarios in games like Blackjack and Poker. Decrypto bridges these approaches by simultaneously
505   evaluating cooperative and competitive dynamics within a single, language-centric framework.
506
507   Recent work has begun exploring theory of mind capabilities in LLMs, with studies like Guo et al.
508
      (2024) investigating this in embodied tasks within grid worlds. Broader evaluation frameworks
      such as Smartplay (Wu et al., 2023) offer comprehensive benchmarks across multiple games and
509
      capabilities. While valuable, these approaches often make isolating specific aspects of language-
510
      based reasoning and coordination challenging.

      7   C ONCLUSION
516
517
518
      This paper introduces Decrypto, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate coordination, competition,
      and theory of mind capabilities in language models. Decrypto addresses critical limitations of exist-
519
      ing benchmarks, such as data leakage and lack of interactivity, providing a more robust framework
520
      for assessing AI systems’ reasoning and behaviour.
521
522   Our experiments, encompassing a range of open-source and closed-source language models, and
523   word embedding systems, revealed that even state-of-the-art models struggle with the nuanced com-
524
      munication and strategic reasoning Decrypto requires. This highlights a significant gap between
      human-level theory of mind capabilities and current AI systems.
525
526   Decrypto offers a more explicit assessment of language models’ core communication skills by iso-
527   lating language-based reasoning from other factors like spatial reasoning or mathematical ability.
528   The benchmark’s adaptable difficulty, achieved through varying opponent sophistication and key-
529   word complexity, alleviates the saturation issues common in fixed-dataset benchmarks. Our human-
530
      AI cross-play experiments shed light on the potential and limitations of human-AI coordination in
      strategic communication tasks.
531
532   Decrypto opens up several promising research directions. These include enhancing language mod-
533   els’ theory of mind capabilities, using the benchmark as a training environment for multi-agent
534   reinforcement learning, and examining the ethical implications of improving AI’s strategic commu-
535   nication abilities.
536   In conclusion, Decrypto significantly advances our ability to assess and improve AI’s agentic ca-
537   pabilities. It provides a challenging yet focused benchmark for multi-agent communication and
538   reasoning, paving the way for more socially intelligent AI to better understand and interact with
539   humans in complex strategic scenarios. As AI capabilities continue to evolve, Decrypto will serve
      as a valuable tool for measuring progress and identifying areas for improvement.
