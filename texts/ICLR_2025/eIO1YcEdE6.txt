009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose Wolf , a WOrLd summarization Framework for accurate video cap-
012            tioning. Wolf is an automated captioning framework that adopts a mixture-of-
013            experts approach, leveraging complementary strengths of Vision Language Models
014            (VLMs). By utilizing both image and video models, our framework captures differ-
015            ent levels of information and summarizes them efficiently. Our approach can be
016            applied to enhance video understanding, auto-labeling, and captioning. To evaluate
017            caption quality, we introduce CapScore, an LLM-based metric to assess the simi-
018            larity and quality of generated captions compared to the ground truth captions. We
019
               further build four human-annotated datasets in three domains: autonomous driving,
               general scenes, and robotics, to facilitate comprehensive comparisons. We show
020
               that Wolf achieves superior captioning performance compared to state-of-the-art
021
               approaches from the research community (VILA1.5, CogAgent) and commercial
022            solutions (Gemini-Pro-1.5, GPT-4V). For instance, in comparison with GPT-4V,
023            Wolf improves CapScore both quality-wise by 55.6% and similarity-wise by 77.4%
024            on challenging driving videos. Finally, we establish a benchmark for video cap-
025            tioning and introduce a leaderboard, aiming to accelerate advancements in video
026            understanding, captioning, and data alignment.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Video captioning is crucial as it facilitates content understanding and retrieval by providing accurate,
032   searchable descriptions. It also provides pairwise data for effective training of foundation models
033   for tasks like video generation, such as Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) and Runaway (Runway, 2024).
034   However, generating descriptive, accurate, and detailed video captions remains a challenging research
035   problem for several reasons: firstly, high-quality labeled data are scarce. Video captions from the
036   internet can be faulty and misaligned and human annotation is prohibitively expensive for large
037   datasets. Secondly, video captioning is inherently more challenging than image captioning due to the
038   additional complexity of temporal correlation and camera motion. Existing captioning models (Hong
039
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023) struggle with temporal reasoning and fail to achieve accurate scene
      understanding. Thirdly, there is no established benchmark to measure captioning progress. Existing
040
      video QA benchmarks (Maaz et al., 2023) are often limited to short answers, making it difficult to
041
      measure hallucinations in detailed long captions. Fourthly, the correctness and completeness of the
042   captions are crucial for safety-critical tasks. In the era of LLMs, text descriptions of scenarios used
043   by embodied agents for planning and control become increasingly common (Mao et al., 2023a;b;
044   Li et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2023). Consequently, a false or incomplete description of the scenario
045   may lead to the decision-making module overlooking a critical object after training on such caption
046   data, resulting in safety risks. For instance, missing the presence of a human in the vicinity of a
047   vegetable-chopping manipulator can lead to an injury.
048   To handle these challenges, we introduce WOrLd summarization Framework (Wolf ), a novel summa-
049   rization captioning framework, along with a captioning metric CapScore, and the Wolf captioning
050   benchmark with corresponding datasets. Unlike previous works that utilize a single model to generate
051   captions, we propose to use multiple models to collaborate (Jiang et al., 2024), producing much more
052   accurate captions. By leveraging multiple models, we can provide more fine-grained details while
053   reducing hallucinations. We show that Wolf achieves superior captioning performance compared
      to state-of-the-art approaches from the research community (such as VILA (Lin et al., 2023c), Co-

      gAgent (Hong et al., 2024)) and commercial solutions (such as Gemini-Pro-1.5 (Team et al., 2023),
055   GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023)). In summary, we have three main contributions:
056
057             1. We design the first world summarization framework Wolf for video captioning and introduce

065                and improvements will be made to refine the Wolf Dataset, codebase, and CapScore. We
066                hope that Wolf will raise awareness about the quality of video captioning, set a standard for
067                the field, and boost community development.

069   2       R ELATED W ORKS
070
      Image Captioning. Visual language models (VLMs) have shown rapid advancements, achieving
071
      leading performance in image captioning tasks, largely due to the success of large language models.
072
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) pioneered this field by training a shared feature space for vision and
073   language modalities on image-caption pairs. Building on CLIP, BLIP (Li et al., 2022) and BLIP-2 (Li
074   et al., 2023) improved performance by aligning the pre-trained encoder with large language models.
075   Following the direction, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) demonstrated
076   that jointly training on diverse datasets as an instruction-following task leads to strong generalization
077   across various tasks. VILA (Lin et al., 2023c) highlighted the importance of pre-training with diverse
078   data, and therefore significantly scaled up the pre-training dataset. Kosmos-2 (Peng et al., 2023) and
079   PaLI-X (Chen et al., 2023a) further introduced pseudo-labeling bounding boxes from open-vocabulary
080   object detectors to scale up the size of pre-training dataset.
081   Video Captioning. As image-based VLMs are not specifically trained with video data, they are
082   limited in describing details present in the video data. To improve video captioning, PLLaVa (Xu
083   et al., 2024) builds on top of LLaVa and introduced a parameter-free pooling strategy to enhance
084   the caption quality. Video-llava (Lin et al., 2023a) achieves state-of-the-art performance on several
085   benchmarks by conducting joint training on images and videos, thereby learning a unified visual
086   representation. Additionally, Video-LLama (Zhang et al., 2023) incorporates both video and audio
087
      into LLMs by introducing two Q-formers to extract features. Vid2seq (Yang et al., 2023) conducts
      large-scale pre-training with narrated videos for dense video captioning. Meanwhile, MV-GPT (Seo
088
      et al., 2022) employs an automated speech recognition (ASR) model to provide additional labeling
089
      for the videos.
090
091   LLM-based Summarization. Recently many works have found that it is efficient to summarize
092   useful information using LLMs. For example, LLaDA (Li et al., 2024) can provide users with helpful
093
      instructions based on the user request and corresponding traffic rules in the desired location. OpenAI
      team finds re-captioning (Betker et al., 2023) via LLMs can be very helpful.

098   We propose Wolf, which is an automated captioning summarization framework that adopts a mixture
099   of experts approach to generate long, accurate, and detailed captions for videos. Figure 1 provides
100   an overview of our framework. In this paper, we use CogAgent (Hong et al., 2024), GPT-4V (Mao
101   et al., 2023a) to generating image-level captions, and use VILA-1.5 (Lin et al., 2023c), Gemini-Pro-
102   1.5 (Team et al., 2023) to generate video captions.
103
      Chain-of-thought Summarization in Image-level Models. As image-level models (image-based
104   VLMs) have been pre-trained with a larger amount of data than video-level models (video-based
105   VLMs), we first use image-based VLMs to generate captions. We design a Chain-of-thought program
106   to obtain video captions from image-level models. As illustrated in Figure 1, we first split the video

      4.2.1     C AP S CORE : E VALUATING C APTIONS WITH LLM S
241   Video captioning has been an ill-posed problem since there is no metric to evaluate the quality of
242   captions and the alignment between the video and the caption. Inspired by BERTScore (Zhang et al.,
243   2019) and CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), we introduce CapScore (Captioning Score), a quantitative
244   metric to use LLMs to evaluate the similarity between predicted and human-annotated (ground-truth)
245   captions. We tried both GPT-4 and LLama 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) as our LLM to summarize the
246   captions. We noticed that GPT-4 can always obtain stable results over 3 runs. However, for LLama
247   3.1, the results varied over different runs. We tried to lower the temperature (from 0.9 to 0.5) to make
248   the inference stable, however, we noticed that the scores are not consistent with human evaluation.
249
      Therefore we select GPT-4 as our LLM to conduct the experiments. Assume we have 6 captions, we
      feed all the captions into GPT-4 and add the prompt “Can you give a score (two decimal places) from
250
      0 to 1 for captions 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5, indicating which one is closer to the ground truth caption (metric
251
      1) and which contains fewer hallucinations and less misalignment (metric 2)? Please output only the
252   scores of each metric separated only by a semicolon. For each metric, please output only the scores
253   of captions 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 separated by commas, in order—no text in the output. ”. We ask GPT-4 to
254   output two scores: caption similarity and caption quality.
255
      Caption Similarity. Caption similarity is based on how well each caption aligns with the ground
256
      truth description on a scale from 0 to 1, considering the key criteria mentioned. GPT-4 lists the
257
      requirements that affect the score: this metric measures how similar each caption is to the ground
258   truth caption. The evaluation focuses on the content and context described in the captions, assessing
259   whether they capture the main themes and details of the ground truth.
260
261
      Caption Quality. Caption quality evaluates whether the caption contains reduced hallucination
      and mistakes compared to the ground truth captions on a scale from 0 to 1. GPT-4 lists the criteria
262
      that affect the score: this metric evaluates the accuracy and relevance of each caption, identifying
263
      any extraneous or incorrect details (hallucinations). Captions with fewer hallucinations and better
264   alignment receive higher scores.

445   6   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
446   Limitations and Optimization. Wolf is still significantly more cost-effective for autolabeling and
447   captioning than procuring human labels. However, there is an efficiency concern when using an
448   ensemble method like ours. This must be handled with great care to ensure that GPU resources are
449   used effectively to mitigate any throughput degradation compared to using single models, even though
450   Wolf offers a significant improvement in caption quality. Modern GPUs are based on a massively
451   parallel pipeline, and our goal is to saturate this pipeline with meaningful work. We consider three
452   primary areas for optimization to make Wolf a unified and efficient framework: Low-Hanging Fruit,
453   Batched Inference, and Model Quantization. For example, we reduce the size of the model weights
454
      for model quantization. Several recent works (Lin et al., 2023b; Dettmers et al., 2024; Ma et al.,
      2024) have noted that LLMs and VLMs can produce highly accurate results even when their weights
455
      are quantized to low bit depths. Therefore, we quantize all constituent models used in Wolf to 4
456
      bits to further improve efficiency. This has two benefits. First, it reduces the bandwidth required
457   for computation. These algorithms work by packing two 4-bit numbers into a single 8-bit type, so
458   when moving data on the GPU, only half the number of bits need to be moved. Since all currently
459   released GPUs support native instructions on 8-bit floating point numbers, the two 4-bit numbers
460   are extracted and expanded by each kernel. In other words, two computations can be performed for
461   every move operation. Next-generation GPUs will natively support 4-bit data types, and we expect
462   further efficiency improvements from having dedicated 4-bit multiply and add instructions. Second,
463   it synergizes with batched inference since the model weights, which are traditionally 16-bit, now only
464   require one quarter of the GPU memory they would ordinarily use. This allows us to fit larger batch
465
      sizes on each GPU and process more videos in parallel. Please check our appendix for details.
466   Safety Considerations. As an ensemble of captioners, Wolf mitigates the possibility of missing out
467   on crucial information in the captions and rectifying any hallucinations that do not agree with the
468   output of most models, which is a fundamental pillar for developing safe autonomous systems, as
469   specified in the functional safety standard ISO 26262 (ROHM). Beyond the benefits of Wolf, there
470   are still various open questions pertaining to safety of VLM captioners in deployment which we aim
471
      to explore more in future: (i) We need to align the captions with the task at hand; e.g., in a driving
      scenario, a detailed description of the foliage around the road, even if correct, is irrelevant and can
472
      potentially act as distractor for the decision maker. (ii) Complementary to the first point, we need to
473
      measure how well a caption aligns with the task at hand and develop an advanced version of CapScore.
474   (iii) Finally, we need an approach to quantify the confidence we have in the captions by leveraging
475   techniques from learning theory, such as conformal prediction (Shafer & Vovk, 2008). Most prior
476   work in this direction assumes an MCQ-styled outputs or those where a unique correct answer exists
477   (Ren et al., 2023; 2024), but these approaches do not translate to free-form text descriptions.
478
479   7   C ONCLUSION
480   In this work, we propose Wolf, a captioning framework designed to automatically and accurately
481   annotate any video, with significant improvements in data alignment. We find out that adopting a
482   mixture of captioning models and summarization can largely boost the quality of the captions. This
483   enables obtaining long, detailed, and accurate video captioning. Beyond that, we set up a leaderboard
484   to boost the development of video captioning, which preserves a guarantee for data alignment. We
485   will also set up a thorough library that contains different types of videos with high-quality captions,
      regional information such as 2D or 3D bounding boxes and depth, and multiple object motions.
