009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) advance their capabilities, aligning these mod-
012            els with human preferences has become crucial. Preference optimization, which
013            trains models to distinguish between preferred and non-preferred responses based
014            on human feedback, has become a crucial component for aligning LLMs. How-
015            ever, most existing works assume noise-free feedback, which is unrealistic given
016            the inherent errors and inconsistencies in human judgments. This paper addresses
017            the impact of noisy feedback on preference optimization, providing generalization
018
               guarantees under these conditions. Unlike traditional analyses that assume con-
               vergence, our work focuses on finite-step preference optimization, offering new
019
               insights that are more aligned with practical LLM training. We establish gener-
020
               alization guarantees for noisy preference learning under a broad family of pref-
021            erence optimization losses such as DPO, IPO, SLiC, etc. Our analysis provides
022            the basis for a general model that closely describes how the generalization decays
023            with the noise rate. Empirical validation on contemporary LLMs confirms the
024            practical relevance of our findings, offering valuable insights for developing AI
025            systems that align with human preferences.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As large language models (LLMs) advance their capabilities, methods for aligning these models
031   with human preferences have garnered significant research attention (Ji et al., 2023). Preference
032   optimization, particularly through human-provided feedback, has emerged as a popular approach to
033
      ensuring that AI systems behave effectively and safely. A key recipe to achieve alignment is through
      the collection of binary preferences on generated outputs. In practice, human annotators are pre-
034
      sented with two responses to the same question, and provide comparative judgments (e.g., preferred,
035
      non-preferred) based on the quality of responses. Then, preference optimization algorithms such as
036   those in Rafailov et al. (2023); Azar et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2024) align the
037   LLMs guided by the collected preferences. This process involves training models to assign a higher
038   implicit reward to the preferred response over the non-preferred response. Preference-based align-
039   ment has demonstrated considerable success in enhancing the safety and usability of LLMs, making
040   it a foundational component in the development of real-world LLM systems (OpenAI, 2023; An-
041   thropic, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Gemini et al., 2023).
042   However, most existing works on preference optimization operate under the assumption of noise-free
043   feedback. This assumption, while simplifying the problem, does not hold in real-world scenarios
044   where human feedback is inherently noisy. The practical implications of noisy feedback are signifi-
045   cant, as they directly impact the reliability and safety of AI systems deployed in critical applications.
046   Factors such as human error, biases, and inconsistencies contribute to this noise, potentially leading
047   to suboptimal or even harmful outcomes if not properly accounted for. Therefore, understanding
048   the effects of noisy feedback in preference optimization is crucial for the development of robust,
049   aligned AI systems. Recently, Gao et al. (2024b) empirically studied the impact of preference noise
050
      and observed that alignment performance can be sensitive to noise rates. However, a rigorous the-
      oretical understanding of these effects is still lacking, underscoring the need for further research on
051
      this important problem.
052
053   In this work, we focus on the setting of noisy feedback in preference optimization and provide novel
      generalization guarantees under this condition. To the best of our knowledge, our results are the

      Analyze GPO behavior under practical considerations. A key focus of our paper is to provide a
196
      tractable analysis of GPO’s generalization behavior, without divorcing from practical considerations.
197   Our analytical framework is designed with practicality in mind. Besides taking noisy feedback into
198   account, we consider the generalization of models after finite gradient steps when the loss is within
199   a constant factor of its initial value. This scenario closely matches real-world practices, where large
200   language models are often fine-tuned for a finite number of steps to avoid overfitting. For this reason,
201   our analytical approach is different from classical generalization theory, which typically considers
202   overparameterized models that achieve near-optimal loss (Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019;
203   Cao & Gu, 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022) or are independent of the training process (Arora et al.,
204   2018; Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023).
205   Our theory revolves around analyzing how the reward margin changes over the course of training,
206   which allows us to bound the generalization error after finite-step GPO updates. For an input prompt
207   x = (x(1) , x(2) , . . . , x(T ) ) with length T , we denote the model output fθ (x) = softmax(W g(x)),
208   where W is the unembedding matrix and g(x) is the final hidden state. The feature backbone
209   can be either fixed or tunable. For example, in recently popularized parameter-efficient fine-tuning
210   paradigm, the feature backbone is often kept frozen to prevent overfitting (Hu et al., 2021; Houlsby
211   et al., 2019), and in black-box fine-tuning scenarios where the backbone is not exposed to the end-
212
      user. In what follows, we first focus on a fixed encoder as a pragmatic approach to manage tractabil-
      ity while still extracting valuable insights into preference learning. Later we will also investigate
213
      whether our theoretical insights hold when performing full fine-tuning, where the feature map is
214
      allowed to change (Section 4).

331   noiseless case, the initial direction of the GPO update will always correspond to a sample estimate of
332   the difference between the means of the positive and negative example distributions. This estimate
333   becomes more robust as the number of samples increases, the distance between means increases,
334   and as the distributions become more concentrated, and as a result, the risk increases at a slower rate
335   with respect to the noise rate. Furthermore, by reasoning about the reward margin for any sample,
336
      including those outside the training distribution (cf Equation 13), we can control how the decision
      boundary shifts by the end of training. We then analyze which samples would be classified correctly,
337
      accounting for estimation error in the mean difference due to noise and finite samples, as well as the
338
      boundary shift from training. Using tail bounds, we can provide a guarantee for the risk when ϵ is
339   small. In order to determine how the expectation of the risk behaves as ϵ approaches 12 , we use the
340   symmetry of the expected risk over 1/2 to determine that there is an inflection point at which the
341   risk approaches a linear rate.
342
      In Section 4, we empirically validate our theoretical bound by training on contemporary LLMs such
343
      as LLaMa (Touvron et al., 2023), where we observe the predicted behavior. Moreover, we extend
344
      this analysis to full fine-tuning scenarios in large language models, demonstrating that the insight
345   holds broadly and offering practical guidance.
346
347         Key takeaways of Section 3
348
                                                                             D [R(P)]
349         1. Our theory suggests that the expected risk can be modeled as E(1−cϵ)2 , which is a function of the

350            noiseless risk and ϵ for ϵ sufficiently below 1/2.
351         2. As ϵ approaches 1/2, the expected risk decreases approximately linearly as it approaches an inflec-
352            tion point.
353         3. Stronger concentration, more samples, and contrasting directions for positive and negative samples
354            allow for tighter bounds and slower degradation in accuracy as the noise rate increases.

      human feedback or human preference data to train models to avoid harmful responses and elicit

502   making our results particularly relevant for the development and deployment of robust LLM systems.
503
504   Robustness of preference optimization. Ensuring that a model can generalize when trained with
505   noisy labels is crucial for building robust and reliable systems (Song et al., 2022). This problem has
506   led to a wide range of works Song et al. (2022) developing various methods that improve model gen-
507   eralization in the presence of noise with many of the works presenting theoretical guarantees of ro-
508   bustness (Natarajan et al., 2013; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Li et al., 2020) for modified loss functions
509
      or for early stopping. In the context of preference learning, increased noise levels have been shown
      to degrade performance, especially when considering loss minimizers (Gao et al., 2024b; Fisch et al.,
510
      2024; Liang et al., 2024). This has led to the development of methods such as ROPO (Liang et al.,
511
      2024), cDPO (Mitchell, 2023), and rDPO (Ray Chowdhury et al., 2024) which introduce modifica-
512   tions to the DPO objective and its gradients. Fisch et al. (2024) considers a pessimistic distillation
513   loss to learn rewards robustly. These approaches have proven effective in enhancing the robustness
514   of preference optimization. Complementing these efforts, our study provides a rigorous generaliza-
515   tion analysis of finite-step preference optimization under noisy feedback. Our theory, grounded in
516   reward dynamics, offers new insights on how the population risk grows with the noise rate for offline
517   preference learning in a finite-step training setting.

      6    C ONCLUSION
520
521
      Our work theoretically analyzes the generalization behavior of preference learning in the presence
522
      of noisy labels through a dynamics-based approach based on a general class of objectives, includ-
523   ing methods such as DPO, IPO, SLiC, etc., which implicitly learn a reward model. Key to our
524   framework, we analyze the reward margin associated with each training sample and its trajectory
525   throughout the training process, enabling us to effectively bound the generalization error. Through
526   rigorous analysis and novel bounds, we establish a generalization guarantee that depends on the
527   noise rate and provide a model based upon the theoretical guarantee that closely describes how test
528   accuracy is impacted by noise on real-world datasets. Empirical validation on contemporary LLMs
529   and real-world alignment datasets confirms the practical relevance of our framework, offering in-
530   sights crucial for developing AI systems that align with human intentions and preferences. We hope
531
      our work catalyzes future investigations into the theoretical understanding of preference optimiza-
      tion methods.
532
533
534   L IMITATION
535
536   While our work provides new theoretical insights into preference optimization under noisy feedback,
537   it does have its constraints. Notably, our framework is limited to offline settings, which assumes that
538   the feedback is collected apriori. Analyzing generalization behavior in online RL settings remains a
539   significant challenge. This limitation underscores the necessity for future research to further explore
      the theoretical understanding of preference optimization.

1299   the vMF distribution. We use the Anthropic Persona dataset (Perez et al., 2022) which consists of

1301   the persona, and 500 statements that misalign with the persona. These samples can be viewed as
1302   positive and negative samples, respectively. All embeddings are collected after RMSNorm has been
1303   applied. We collect the norm of the final layer embedding at the end of each statement and calcu-

1309

1310   that the embeddings are concentrated around their respective means across personas, supporting the
