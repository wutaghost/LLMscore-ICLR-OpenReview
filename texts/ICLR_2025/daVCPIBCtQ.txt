011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dynamic Graph Neural Networks (DGNNs) are advanced methods for processing
014            evolving graph data, capturing both structural and temporal dependencies effi-
015            ciently. However, existing distributed DGNN training methods face challenges in
016            achieving load balance across GPUs and minimizing communication overhead,
017
               which limits their efficiency. In this paper, we introduce DY GN E X, a distributed
               training system designed to address this issue. DY GN E X utilizes a cross-time-
018
               window snapshot group scheduling algorithm that balances computational loads
019
               across GPUs without introducing additional cross-GPU feature aggregation or
020            hidden state communication. Based on the specific scenario, the scheduling al-
021            gorithm is applied using greedy or Integer Linear Programming (ILP) methods,
022            referred to as DY GN E X-G and DY GN E X-L, respectively. DY GN E X-L and
023            DY GN E X-G achieve average reductions of 28% and 24% in per-epoch train-
024            ing time compared to state-of-the-art methods, maintaining load imbalance across
025            GPUs at approximately 4% and 8%, while preserving model convergence across
026            various DGNN models and datasets. In simulation experiments, as the number of
027            GPUs increases, DY GN E X-G shows good scalability, efficiently handling clus-
028
               ters with up to 512 GPUs while maintaining 95% efficiency.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Dynamic graphs are graphs whose structures and attributes change over time. Dynamic Graph Neu-
033   ral Networks (DGNNs) have emerged as state-of-the-art methods for processing dynamic graphs,
034   exhibiting strong ability to capture both structural and temporal dependencies (Zhu et al., 2016;
035   Zhou et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2018; Trivedi et al., 2019; Pareja et al., 2020; Manessi et al., 2020;
036   Chen et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020; Goyal et al., 2020; Sankar et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a;b;
037   Bai et al., 2022; You et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
038   2024). Depending on the event model, dynamic graphs are categorized into Discrete Time Dynamic
039
      Graphs (DTDGs) and Continuous Time Dynamic Graphs (CTDGs). DGNNs are categorized simi-
      larly according to the dynamic graphs they process. In this work, we focus on DGNNs designed for
040
      DTDGs, which process temporal dynamics in discrete snapshots.
041
042   Significant efforts have been made to improve the efficiency of DGNN training. Some works fo-
043   cus on efficient training on a single GPU (Li & Chen, 2021; Guan et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023;
044   Wang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024a;b; Su et al., 2024), addressing various factors such as memory
045
      footprint and data access overhead. Others consider distributed training on multiple GPUs (Chakar-
      avarthy et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). ESDG (Chakaravarthy et al., 2021) dis-
046
      tributes temporally adjacent snapshots across different GPUs and requires hidden state transfers
047
      between GPUs for temporal processing, which incurs a significant overhead when the hidden states
048   are large. BLAD (Fu et al., 2023) avoids such overhead by processing each group of temporally
049   adjacent snapshots on the same GPU while assigning different groups across GPUs. As we will
050   show later in Figure 1, both ESDG and BLAD experience load imbalance across different GPUs,
051   which results in inefficient resource utilization and hinders training efficiency. While DGC (Chen
052   et al., 2023) attempts to balance the load, it relies on graph partitioning, which introduces additional
053   communication overhead. Therefore, it requires further research to achieve load balance across
      GPUs while minimizing inter-GPU communication in distributed training of DGNNs.

054
      To address this problem, we develop a distributed DGNN training system called DY GN E X, which
055   uses a novel cross-time-window snapshot group scheduling algorithm for load balancing. DY GN E X
056   takes advantage of the fact that snapshot groups from different time windows are treated as inde-
057   pendent samples in DGNN training. This allows for the flexibility of combining and scheduling
058   them in any order. We formulate an optimal scheduling problem to minimize the per-epoch training
059   time. Depending on the scenario, DY GN E X solves the problem using Integer Linear Programming
060   (ILP) or a greedy algorithm, and the resulting scheduling algorithms are denoted by DY GN E X-L
061   and DY GN E X-G, respectively. Through real-world experiments and simulations, we demonstrate
062   that DY GN E X-L and DY GN E X-G achieve average reductions of 28% and 24% in per-epoch train-
063
      ing time compared to state-of-the-art methods. DY GN E X-L and DY GN E X-G reduce the average
      load imbalance ratio by 22% and 18% compared to the partition-by-snapshot-group (PSG) method.
064
      In simulation experiments, as the number of GPUs increases, DY GN E X-G shows good scalability,
065
      efficiently handling clusters with up to 512 GPUs while maintaining 95% efficiency.

068   2   PRELIMINARIES
069
070   Dynamic Graph Neural Networks. Dynamic Graph Neural Networks (DGNNs) are composed of
071   multiple blocks that integrate both structural and temporal encoding mechanisms. Each block typi-
072
      cally includes a structure encoder, which aggregates information from a node’s immediate neighbors
      to capture its structural context, and a time encoder, which accumulates information over time to re-
073
      flect temporal changes in the graph. The specific implementations of these encoders vary across
074
      different DGNN models. For example, EvolveGCN (Pareja et al., 2020) dynamically adjusts its
075   graph convolutional network (GCN) parameters over time to accommodate the evolving nature
076   of the graph. WD-GCN (Manessi et al., 2020) combines a GCN with a long short-term memory
077   (LSTM) network to capture both spatial and temporal features in dynamic graphs. TGCN (Chen
078   et al., 2020) integrates a GCN with a gated recurrent unit (GRU) to effectively capture spatial and
079   temporal dynamics in dynamic graphs. GAT-LSTM (Wu et al., 2018) leverages a Graph Attention
080   Network (GAT) for capturing structural information while using an LSTM to model temporal depen-
081   dencies. Each of these models showcases unique approaches to integrating structural and temporal
082   information, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to learn from dynamic graph data. These four
083
      models are representative typical GNN and RNN models in DGNNs. Many subsequent models can
      be considered variants of these, including structural-specific models like TTGCN (Li et al., 2024)
084
      and DRAIN (Bai et al., 2022), temporal-specific models like SGNN-GR (Wang et al., 2022) and
085
      ROLAND (You et al., 2022), and comprehensive models such as Dyngraph2vec (Goyal et al., 2020)
086   and DySAT (Sankar et al., 2020).
087

124   Dataset Partition Strategy. In distributed training of dynamic graphs, the mainstream dataset par-
125   titioning methods include vertex-based partitioning, represented by Aligraph (Zhu et al., 2019), and
126   snapshot-based partitioning, represented by ESDG (Chakaravarthy et al., 2021). The latest work,
127
      BLAD (Fu et al., 2023), proposes a snapshot group-based partitioning method, which effectively
      reduces the overall communication volume. Specifically, in one iteration, each GPU trains a com-
128
      plete snapshot group. Since each snapshot contains all the node information, it avoids neighbor
129
      feature communication. Additionally, because each snapshot group includes all prior information of
130   the target snapshot, it eliminates hidden state communication. A detailed workflow of three dataset
131   partition strategies is provided in Appendix B, with Figure 7, 8, and 9 illustrating the differences
132   among them.
133
      Dilemma in Distributed Training of DGNNs. Achieving efficient distributed training of DGNNs
134
      requires a data partitioning strategy that ensures load balancing across all nodes while minimizing
135
      inter-node communication. Existing approaches exhibit a range of strengths and weaknesses, as
136   shown in Table 1, none of them effectively balance inter-node communication with load distribu-
137   tion. In different partitioning strategies, vertex-based partitioning can fine-tune the load distribution
138   at the node level, but it typically introduces significant communication overhead. Snapshot-based
139   and snapshot group-based partitioning use snapshots and snapshot groups as scheduling units, re-
140   spectively, but both face load imbalance due to differences between snapshots. Therefore, we focus
141   on the load imbalance issues in ESDG and BLAD. We measured task allocation across 4 GPUs dur-
142   ing training with ESDG and BLAD on four popular datasets, using the number of nodes and edges
143   as workload indicators. As shown in Figure 1, where the nodes and edges processed by GPU0 are
144
      used as the baseline for comparison, ESDG exhibited differences of up to 19% in node distribution
      and 26% in edge distribution, while BLAD showed up to 16% difference. The uneven task distribu-
145
      tion caused GPU idling, prolonging training time and reducing efficiency, highlighting the need for
146
      better load balancing.

352   et al., 2018), as they are typical GNN and RNN models in DGNNs. The first three models are GCN-
353   based DGNNs, while GAT-LSTM is a GAT-based model. These models are widely used due to
354   their effectiveness in dynamic graph learning. Each DGNN model features a two-layer architecture,
355   comprising a feature update operation and a graph aggregation operation. In EvolveGCN, the RNN
356   updates the GNN parameters across snapshots, whereas in the other models, the RNN processes
357   intermediate node features within the snapshots.
358   Baselines. We compare DY GN E X-G and DY GN E X-L with ESDG (Chakaravarthy et al., 2021),
359   partition-by-snapshot-group (PSG) method and BLAD (Fu et al., 2023). ESDG is a widely used
360   baseline for distributed DGNN training, while BLAD represents the current state-of-the-art (SOTA)
361   approach. In ESDG, snapshots within a snapshot group are evenly distributed across GPUs based
362   on their temporal intervals, as illustrated in Figure 8. In PSG, each GPU training a single snap-
363   shot group, as illustrated in Figure 9. BLAD utilizes a two-stage pipeline to collaboratively train
364   two consecutive snapshot groups. In contrast, DyGNeX-G and DyGNeX-L execute two scheduled
365

      6                                  L IMITATIONS
517
518
      Large Snapshot Group. In DY GN E X, each snapshot group must fit on a single GPU for training.
519   While current GPU memory (e.g., 80GB, 40GB) suffices for most datasets, larger datasets may
520   exceed this limit, making DY GN E X infeasible. A potential solution is to partition the graph and
521   use full-neighbor sampling on target nodes, enabling training until all nodes are processed. The
522   trade-offs between the overhead of partitioning and sampling and the advantages of DY GN E X over
523   vertex-based methods (eg., DGC(Chen et al., 2023)) merit further exploration.
524
      Limited Number of Snapshot Groups. The benefits of DY GN E X depend on the flexible combi-
525   nation of snapshot groups for load balancing. With very few groups, the limited combination space
526   reduces potential gains.

530
531
      In this paper, we introduced DY GN E X, an efficient distributed training system for DGNNs.
      DY GN E X addresses the challenges of load balancing and communication overhead in large-scale
532
      dynamic graph training. By utilizing a novel cross-time-window snapshot group scheduling algo-
533
      rithm, DY GN E X balances computational loads across GPUs without incurring additional cross-
534   GPU communication. We implemented two variants of the system: DY GN E X-L, which uses ILP
535   to globally optimize training efficiency, and DY GN E X-G, a greedy approach. In extensive real-
536   world and simulated experiments, DY GN E X-L and DY GN E X-G outperform ESDG and BLAD in
537   per-epoch training time. Both DY GN E X-L and DY GN E X-G preserve model convergence, main-
538   taining training accuracy while improving throughput and reducing load imbalance across GPUs.
539   DY GN E X-G further demonstrates superior scalability, efficiently handling large numbers of GPUs
      with minimal performance degradation.

      A    W ORKFLOW OF DISTRIBUTED TRAINING FOR DGNN S .
666
667   The entire workflow of training a DGNN model across multiple GPUs is shown in Figure 6. When
668
      graph data is partitioned across GPUs, each GPU manages a specific section of the graph, allow-
      ing for localized computations. However, since graphs are inherently interconnected, nodes often
669
      have neighbors in other partitions. This necessitates inter-GPU communication to aggregate features
670
      from neighboring nodes in different partitions, thus ensuring that each node’s features include rele-
671   vant information from its neighbors. This communication step, while essential for accurate feature
672   aggregation, introduces overhead, particularly for large-scale or densely connected graphs. Com-
673   munication overhead can become a significant bottleneck in the training process, as more data needs
674   to be exchanged among GPUs, potentially slowing down the entire training pipeline. An alternative
675   approach to mitigate this challenge is to store a complete copy of the graph on each GPU. By doing
676   so, each GPU has access to the entire graph structure, eliminating the need for inter-GPU communi-
677   cation during the feature aggregation stage. This approach, however, comes with increased memory
678   requirements, as each GPU must have enough capacity to store the full graph. For scenarios where
679
      memory is abundant and communication latency is a critical factor, this method can provide a more
      efficient solution. Once features are aggregated, the next phase involves processing these features
680
      through a GNN layer to generate hidden states that capture spatial dependencies within the graph
681
      structure. These hidden states are then passed to temporal models, such as RNNs or LSTMs, which
682   capture the time-dependent dynamics inherent to dynamic graphs. The temporal model processes
683   the sequentially evolving states to enable learning from both spatial and temporal patterns within the
684   data.

687   B    DATASET PARTITION S TRATEGY
688
689   The main dataset partition strategies currently include three types. First, the vertex-based method,
690   as shown in Fig. 7, where each GPU’s input consists of parts of multiple snapshots within a time
691
      window. Due to some nodes having neighbors on other GPUs, neighbor feature communication
      is required. Second, the snapshot-based method, shown in Fig. 8, where each GPU’s input is a
692
      complete snapshot. However, since there are dependencies between snapshots within a time window,
693
      hidden state communication is needed. Finally, the snapshot-group-based method, shown in Fig. 9,
694   provides each GPU with a complete snapshot group, eliminating the need for both hidden state and
695   neighbor feature communication, making it the current state-of-the-art method.

1014   profiling epoch is set to 1, the data from the first epoch is often unstable, leading to suboptimal com-
1015   binations and lower throughput. When the profiling epoch exceeds 3, the profiling data becomes
1016   more stable, resulting in more consistent throughput.
1017

1018

1022   snapshots exceeds 100. Finally, the training stage requires over 100 epochs to achieve convergence.
1023   As a result, the overhead introduced by profiling and algorithm solving accounts for less than 3% of
