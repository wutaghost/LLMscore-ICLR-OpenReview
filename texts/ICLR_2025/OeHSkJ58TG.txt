009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Polysemantic neurons – neurons that activate for a set of unrelated features – have
012             been seen as a significant obstacle towards interpretability of task-optimized deep
013             networks, with implications for AI safety. The classic origin story of polyseman-
014             ticity is that the data contains more “features" than neurons, such that learning
015             to perform a task forces the network to co-allocate multiple unrelated features to
016
                the same neuron, endangering our ability to understand networks’ internal pro-
                cessing. In this work, we present a second and non-mutually exclusive origin
017
                story of polysemanticity. Specifically, we show that polysemanticity can arise
018
                incidentally, even when there are ample neurons to represent all features in the data,
019             a phenomenon we term incidental polysemanticity. Using a combination of theory
020             and experiments, we show that incidental polysemanticity can arise due to multiple
021             reasons including regularization and neural noise; this incidental polysemantic-
022             ity occurs because random initialization can, by chance alone, initially assign
023             multiple features to the same neuron, and the training dynamics then strengthen
024             such overlap. Our paper concludes by calling for further research quantifying the
025             performance-polysemanticity tradeoff in task-optimized deep neural networks to
026             better understand to what extent polysemanticity is avoidable.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep neural networks are widely regarded as difficult to mechanistically understand, especially at
031   the massive scales of modern frontier models. Such lack of interpretability is increasingly viewed
032   as a serious concern in AI Safety since highly capable models might behave in unpredictable and
033   undesirable ways (Hendrycks et al., 2023; Ngo et al., 2022). One outstanding challenge preventing
034   better mechanistic interpretability of networks is polysemanticity, a phenomenon whereby individual
035   neurons activate for unrelated input “features" (Olah et al., 2017; 2020). This phenomenon, why it
036   occurs and how to interpret networks’ computation nonetheless has also been studied for decades by
037   neuroscientists under the term of “mixed selectivity", e.g., (Asaad et al., 1998; Mansouri et al., 2006;
038
      Warden & Miller, 2007; Rigotti et al., 2013; Barak et al., 2013; Raposo et al., 2014; Fusi et al., 2016;
      Parthasarathy et al., 2017; Lindsay et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2017; Johnston et al., 2020).
039
040   A leading hypothesis for why neural networks learn polysemanticitic representations is out of
041   necessity: if a task contains many more features than the number of neurons, then achieving high
042   performance at the task might force the network to co-allocate unrelated features to the same neuron
043   (Elhage et al., 2022). While intuitive and persuasive, in this work, we propose a second and non-
044
      mutually exclusive hypothesis: that polysemanticity might be caused by non-task factors in the
      training process. As such factors are not necessary to perform the task well, we call this form
045
      incidental polysemanticity.
046
047   In this paper, we study two non-task factors that could produce incidentally polysemantic represen-
048   tations: l1 regularization and neural noise. We hypothesize that the mechanism of neural network
049   convergence from randomly initialized weight is contingent on the very slight correlation of certain
050
      neurons with useful features 1 . Gradient descent in the initial steps will amplify this correlation
      until the feature is accurately represented in the model weights. Suppose activations are incen-
051
      tivized to be sparse, as in the regularization setting. In thos case, features will be represented by

      3     A NOTHER I NCENTIVE FOR S PARSITY: N OISE IN THE H IDDEN L AYER
325
326
      In the toy model considered thus far, the encodings were incentivized to be sparse by an explicit l1
327   regularization term that was added into the loss. While this choice made the toy model simple to
328   work with, this is not the most common reason why sparse representations occur in practice. In this
329   section, loosely inspired by Blanc et al. (2020) and Bricken et al. (2023), we show that sparsity can
330   arise when certain types of noise are present in the hidden layer.
331
332   3.1   M ODIFIED MODEL
333
334   We now consider a model identical to the previous one except that:
335                                                                  P
336        • the loss no longer contains the l1 regularization term λ i ∥Wi ∥1 ;
337         • every time the auto-encoder is run, noise from some noise distribution D is added to each
338           neuron in the hidden layer.
339
340   The output is computed as y := ReLU W W T x + ξ for ξ ∈ Rm , where each coordinate ξj is
341   independently drawn from D, and the loss for each input x is defined as
342                                       2                                   2
                             L := ∥y − x∥ = ReLU W W T x + ξ − x .

343
344   Throughout, we will assume that the noise distribution D is symmetric around 0, has variance σ 2 ,
345   and fourth central moment µ4 . Note that this loss is fully rotationally symmetric in terms of the
346   hidden layer’s space Rm , except for possibly the noise ξ: if a rotation were applied right before the
347   hidden layer and undone
                                 right after, the space would be invariant. In particular, if D was a normal
348   distribution N 0, σ 2 , the rotational symmetry would be conserved and there would be no privileged
349   directions for the encoding vectors to align to. In the remainder of this section, we show through
350   both mathematical analysis and experiments that when the noise ξj has negative excess kurtosis
351
      (which includes many bounded distributions, such as bipolar noise or the uniform distribution over
      any interval), then the encodings are pushed towards sparsity.
352
353
354   3.2   M ATHEMATICAL ANALYSIS
355
      Concretely, we compute the update after the tth step of training, and show that the expected loss at
356
      the (t + 1)th step has a term which involves both the fourth norms ∥Wi ∥4 of the encodings and the
357   excess kurtosis of the noise distribution D.
358
359   Since the computations are rather lengthy, we defer the details to Appendix C due to space constraints,
360
      but the summary is that:
361
             • if D is bipolar noise ±σ, which has excess kurtosis −2, then this would push towards
362            sparsity;
363
364
             • if D is normal noise N (0, σ 2 ), which has excess kurtosis 0, then this will not push towards
               sparsity (and indeed this would maintain the rotational symmetry of the hidden space Rm ,
365
               and sparsity is not rotationally symmetric).

451   encodings reach almost perfect sparsity (indicated by ∥Wi ∥4 ≈ 1), the encoding corresponding
                                                           4
452   to the orange curve seems to be stuck below ∥Wi ∥4 = 0.2. This can be explained by looking at
453   Section 4, which visualizes the corresponding final weight matrix W . The second encoding row W2
454   has significant weights in the 7 coordinates that were chosen by the other encodings, and that these
455   weights all have comparable absolute values. What’s happening is a fascinating interplay between the
456   interference and the push for sparsity.
457
             • On the one hand, the push for sparsity should incentivize W2 to “pick” one of these 7
458
               coordinates and increase its absolute value at the detriment of the other 6. Indeed, in all
459            cases, the sign of W2j is the opposite of the sign of Wij for the encoding i which maximizes
460            |Wij |, so naively, this shouldn’t cause any interference.
461
             • But the smaller weights in the matrix W provide a hint to what is actually happening: in
462
               each column j for which there is some i with |Wij | ≈ 1, the other encodings Wi′ have a
463
               small but non-negligible weight with the opposite sign. This is detrimental in terms of the
464            implicit regularization term, but it ensures that the dot product Wi′ · Wi remains negative
465            (or at least small) even after a small amount of noise is applied to the hidden layer on input
466            ei′ . If W2 were to choose one of these coordinates j, then there would be no such strategy
467            available: indeed, if Wi and W2 were equal the basis vectors ej and its opposite −ej , then
468            one of Wi′ · Wi or Wi′ · W2 must be nonnegative, and changing the value of Wi′ j in either
469            direction would only make things worse. So W2 is kept from applying this strategy, and is
470            instead forced to compromise between all 7 coordinates in order to keep interference at a
471            minimum.
472
      This phenomenon is strikingly different from the type of polysemanticity that we studied in the
473
      previous sections. It also explains why the fourth norms were not quite approaching 1 in Figure 4.

476   5   D ISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
477
478   Until now, the mechanistic interpretability literature has mostly studied polysemanticity in settings
479   where the encoding space has no privileged basis: the space can be arbitrarily rotated without
480   changing the dynamics, and in particular the corresponding layer doesn’t have non-linearities or
481   any regularization other than l2 . In such settings, the features can be represented arbitrarily in the
482
      encoding space, and we only observe superposition (non-orthogonal encodings) when there are more
      features than dimensions.
483
484   When there is no privileged basis, it is always technically feasible to get rid of superposition by
485   simply increasing the number of neurons so that it matches the number of features. Eliminating
      polysemanticity that is due to non-task factors could require completely different tools, and seems


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      particularly challenging given that (as we saw in Figure 6), that kind of polysemanticity can happen
487   for a wide variety of sometimes surprisingly hard-to-predict incidental reasons.
488
489   In particular, it is much less realistic to do away with the kind of incidental polysemanticity that we
490
      demonstrate in Section 2 by simply increasing the number of hidden neurons, since we saw that it
      can happen until the number of hidden neurons is roughly equal to the number of features squared.
491
      For instance, here is one possible way one might get rid of incidental polysemanticity in a neuron
492
      that currently represents two features i and j: Duplicate that neuron, divide its outgoing weights by 2
493   (so that this doesn’t affect downstream layers), add a small amount of noise to the incoming weights
494   of each copy, then run gradient descent for a few more steps. This might cause the copies to diverge
495   away from each other, with one of the copies eventually taking full ownership of feature i while the
496   other copy takes full ownership of feature j.
497
      In addition, it would be interesting to find ways to distinguish incidental polysemanticity from
498
      necessary polysemanticity in practice. Can we distinguish them based only on the final, trained state
499   of the model, or do we need to know more about what happened during training? Is “most" of the
500   polysemanticity in real-world neural networks necessary or incidental? How does this depend on the
501   architecture and the data?
502
      In this paper, we presented a new challenge for mechanistic interpretability beyond traditional super-
503
      position, demonstrating that polysemanticity can be an inherent outcome even in overparameterized
504
      networks. Since polysemantic neurons may emerge due to incidental factors rather than task-related
505   constraints, this work opens up the possibility to understand complex feature representations in
506   deep neural networks, where training dynamics decrease our ability to interpret and predict network
507   behavior.

      A    G ENERALITY OF THE MODEL
649
650
651   We chose the toy model in Section 2 to be as simple as possible (and to match Elhage et al. (2022) as
652   closely as possible) while still exhibiting incidental polysemanticity. Nevertheless, in this section, we
653   want to point out that some of these choices are actually without loss of (much) generality.
654
655
656   Tied weights In our model, the encoding and decoding matrices are tied together (i.e. the encoding
657   matrix W T is forced to be the transpose of the decoding matrix W ). This assumption makes sense
658   because even if they were kept independent and initialized to different values, they would naturally
659
      acquire similar values over time because of the learning dynamics. Indeed, the ith column of the
      encoding matrix and the ith row of the decoding matrix “reinforce each other" through the feature
660
      benefit force until they have an inner product of 1, and as long as they start out small or if there is
661
      some weight decay, they would end up almost identical by the end of training.
662
663
664
      Basis vectors as inputs If the input features are not the canonical basis vectors but are still
665   orthogonal (and the outputs are still basis vectors), then we could apply a fixed linear transformation
666   to the encoding matrix and recover the same training dynamics. And in general it makes sense to
667   consider orthogonal input features, because when the features themselves are not orthogonal (or
668   at least approximately orthogonal), the question of what polysemanticity even is becomes quite
669   confused.
