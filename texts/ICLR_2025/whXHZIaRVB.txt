009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We present Dynamic Skill Adaptation (DSA), an adaptive and dynamic framework
012            to adapt novel and complex skills to Large Language Models (LLMs). Compared
013            with previous work which learns from human-curated and static data in random
014            orders, we propose to first automatically generate and organize the training data by
015            mimicking the learning pathways of human and then dynamically tailor the training
016            data based on the training dynamics. Specifically, inspired by the learning structures
017            and teaching strategies in the human education system, we first construct a skill
018
               graph by decomposing complex skills into sub-skills and arranging them based on
               their dependencies in human syllables. For every skill, we utilize LLMs to generate
019
               both textbook-like data which contains detailed descriptions of skills for pre-
020
               training and exercise-like data which targets at explicitly utilizing the skills to solve
021            problems for instruction-tuning. Furthermore, during the instruction-tuning, we
022            dynamically update the training data which down-weight easy-to-learn examples,
023            generate more complex examples, and filter out data with errors. Experiments on
024            large language models such as LLAMA and Mistral demonstrate the effectiveness
025            of our proposed methods in adapting math reasoning skills and social study skills.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have witnessed a significant rise in popularity and utility across
031   various domains in NLP such as text generation, machine translation, and question-answering systems
032   (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019; Smith et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Lewkowycz
033
      et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022; Ouyang et al.,
      2022; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). The success of LLMs such as ChatGPT and GPT-4 and
034
      its predecessors has demonstrated their ability to learn, understand, and generate human-like text
035
      based on massive amounts of existing data(Qin et al., 2023; Ziems et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023; Wang
036   et al., 2022; Dubois et al., 2024). Despite their remarkable achievements in general benchmarks and
037   tasks, these current LLMs often fail when it comes to specialized domains which require complex
038   and novel skills such as math reasoning, coding, and etc. (Khot et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Dziri
039   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Frieder et al., 2024).
040
      When adapting specific and complex skills to LLMs that are pre-trained on general corpus, there are
041
      several challenges. First, LLMs may lack specific domain knowledge that is necessary to understand
042   and generate content in a specialized field such as math. Adapting to them requires mechanisms to
043   incorporate domain-specific terminology, concepts, and context. However, specialized, or complex
044   skills often only have limited data available for fine-tuning. While previous approaches mainly collect
045   existing data Yue et al. (2023) or generate synthetic data from a small set of human-written seed
046   examples Wang et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023) and mix them together to fine-tune the models Xie et al.
047   (2024); Yue et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), it is still under-explored how to select, organize, and utilize
048   domain-specific data to effectively learn novel and complex skills Chen et al. (2024). Furthermore,
049   LLMs are often easy to overfit limited and static training data during fine-tuning Xu et al. (2023);
050
      Shao et al. (2024), which could result in sub-optimal performance.
051   To overcome these challenges, we draw inspirations from teaching strategies in human education
052   systems (Weinstein & Mayer, 1983). Good teaching strategies often involve several key steps: (i)
053   Teachers would decompose and organize content into several levels, starting from easier childhood
      education up to higher education and beyond (Brighouse, 2006). (ii) Within each level, teachers would

      To this end, we introduce Dynamic Skill Adaptation (DSA), a framework specifically designed for
081   LLMs that adaptively generates and organizes training data automatically and allows LLMs to acquire
082   specialized or novel skills dynamically. Specifically, inspired by the organization strategy in teaching
083   (Weinstein & Mayer, 1983), we first build a skill graph based on human learning syllables which
084   decompose complex skills such as calculus into sub-skills and further arrange them based on their
085   dependencies so that model could learn prerequisite knowledge and then higher-level knowledge.
086   Next, following the elaboration and rehearsal strategy, we automatically generate detailed textbook-
087   like descriptions for each skill using LLMs like GPT4 as well as the exercise data where the skills
088   that have been learned need to be explicitly used to solve the generated problems. In addition, with
089
      the monitoring strategy, during the training, we would dynamically adjust the training data based on
      the learning dynamics where we generate more complex and hard-to-learn examples, filter out data
090
      with errors and down-weight easy-to-learn examples. Experiments on large language models such as
091
      LLAMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) demonstrate the effectiveness of our
092   proposed methods in adapting math reasoning skills and social study skills.
093
094   Our work has three major contributions: (i) We propose to generate and organize the training corpus
095
      that contains both textbook and exercise-like data based on skill graphs for LLMs to adapt novel skills
      inspired by human teaching strategies. (ii) We introduce the dynamic training mechanism that adjusts
096
      the training data based on the training dynamics to avoid overfitting static data. (iii) Experiments
097
      on several LLMs in Math and Social Study domain and extensive ablation studies demonstrate the
098   effectiveness of our introduced Dynamic Skill Adaptation framework.

103   Large Language Model Large language models have witnessed extensive progress recently (Brown
104   et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019; Smith et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Lewkowycz et al., 2022;
105   Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022;
106   OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022) and have shown superior performance in a wide
107   range of general tasks such as natural language understanding (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Qin et al.,
      2023), reasoning (OpenAI, 2023) and code generation (Touvron et al., 2023). However, LLMs that is

109   et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023) or tasks that require complex skills (Yue et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023;
110   Zhu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Khot et al., 2022). Instead of general LLMs, in this work, we
111   focus on how to adapt pre-trained general LLMs automatically and efficiently with specialized and
112   complex skills.
113
114   Large Language Model for Specialized Domain Recent approaches have also explored generating
115   or collecting domain-specific data for tuning models for specialized domain such as math (Shao
116   et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) and coding (Nijkamp et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023;
117   Li et al., 2023). They either collect a wide range of online data (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
118   Shao et al., 2024) or generate instruction tuning data with LLMs (Li et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Xu
119   et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Toshniwal et al., 2024) through techniques such as Self-instruct(Wang
120
      et al., 2022) or Evol-instruct (Xu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023). However, these methods usually
      randomly mix all the data together while ignoring the dependencies and relations between different
121
      data (Xie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Also, the diversity is often restricted by seed instructions or
122
      seed topics. As a result, the training process might suffer from overfitting on these static data. To
123   overcome these issues, we propose to not only generate the training data through LLMs but also
124   organize them following human learning orders and dynamically update them during adapting the
125   LLMs to novel and complex skills for specialized domains.
126
127   Curriculum Learning Curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009) propose to train the model with
128   data that is arranged from easy samples to hard ones with designed pacing functions and mixing rates
129   (Soviany et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Portelas et al., 2020; Matiisen et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2015)
130   through assigning learnability scores (Xu et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2024; Bejan et al., 2023) or utilizing
131   agents to generate harder examples (Feng et al., 2023; Fan & Jaggi, 2023; Balloccu et al., 2024).
132   Saxena et al.; Mindermann et al. also explores parametrizing and ordering samples with importance.
133   Chen et al. propose algorithms to select the orders of data from different tasks by enumerating all the
134
      sequences and selecting the best sequence based on the performances on smaller scale experiments.
      While our work is inspired by curriculum learning, we focus more on the skill-level: instead of
135
      ranking specific examples, we model the order of different skills based on their dependencies and
136
      does not necessarily follow an easy-to-hard manner.

140
141   The first step towards equipping LLMs with domain-specific knowledge efficiently and adaptively is to
142   review how humans learn new skills in a new domain comprehensively. Good teaching includes teach-
143   ing students how to learn, remember, think, and motivate themselves through organization, rehearsal
144
      and elaboration, comprehension monitoring(Weinstein & Mayer, 1983). Motivated by these human
      learning strategies, we propose the Dynamic Skill Adaptation (DSA) framework for LLM shows
145
      in Figure 1 and Algorithm 1, which consists of several key components: Skill Graph Construction
146
      (Section 3.1), Training Data Generation (Section 3.2) and Dynamic Training (Section 3.3).

      3.1      S KILL G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION
150
      When human learn new skills, it is necessary for teachers to structure and arrange the information to
151   make it more understandable and easier to remember for students, such as creating outlines, mind
152   maps, charts, or using other organizational tools to group related concepts together(Weinstein &
153   Mayer, 1983). As a result, one key component of DSA involves constructing a skill graph and
154   organizing them following the learning structures. For example, to learn skills about calculus in math,
155   models need to first learn skills such as algebra, function, geometry, trigonometry, etc. Every node in
156   our skill graphs is a specific skill, and the edges between them represent their dependence.
157   In practice, when adapting a complex skill S (e.g., calculus in math), we build the skill graphs in
158   two ways: (i) we gather the basic skills in human learning syllabus 1 and the edges are pointed from
159   lower-level skills to higher-level skills; (ii) we recursively prompt GPT4 (e.g., what are the basic
160   skills that are required to learn Calculus) to decompose complex skills into sub-skills and the edges
161

164   visualized in Figure 3 and Figure 4. Following the skill graphs we constructed, we will organize the
165   training data and train the models to acquire skills from lower-levels to higher-levels based on the
166   skill graphs (i.e., learn lower-level knowledge first before learning higher-level knowledge).

168   3.2   T RAINING DATA G ENERATION
169
170   In human learning systems, elaboration goes beyond rote memorization and involves expanding
171   the material, making connections to previous knowledge, and deepening understanding. Rehearsal
172   is the process of repeatedly going over information to commit it to memory. This might involve
173   reading notes, rephrasing ideas, or reciting key facts. It helps in retaining information in memory
174
      and can be useful for rote memorization. Based on these human learning strategies (Weinstein &
      Mayer, 1983), in our DSA, we then automatically generate textbook data for elaboration and exercise
175
      data for rehearsal to learn new skills. During the training, we would first pre-train LLMs with the
176
      textbook descriptions following the orders in the skill graph we constructed in Section 3.1 and then
177   instruction-tune LLMs with the exercise data.
178
179   Textbook Generation For every node s in the generated skill graph G, we instruct GPT4 to
180   generate textbook descriptions (Li et al., 2023) which could be used for pre-training. Specifically, we
181   regularize the generation of textbook descriptions to (i) link the current skill with its predecessors in
182   the skill graph G, (ii) cover as much detail with both descriptions and examples, (iii) highlight all the
183   key concepts at the end of the descriptions, and (iv) provide homework that covers every key concept
184   for the current skill.
185
186   Exercise Generation For the nodes in G, we further instruct GPT4 to generate exercise problems
187   which could be used for instructional tuning. We intend to generate questions that each of them would
188   leverage multiple skills in G which are different from the homework which only cover one specific
189   skill in the textbook generation stage. As a result, for every generation, we first randomly sample
190
      different numbers of skills from the skill graph G and then instruct GPT4 to generate problems
      which requires the provided skills to solve. When generating answers for the exercise problems, we
191
      regularize the generated reasoning steps to be explicitly grounded in specific skills (Chen et al., 2023)
192
      and further improve quality through self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022).

197   understanding of the material. It is about being aware of when students don’t fully grasp a concept

264
265   To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed DSA framework, we perform experiments to adapt
266   the skills of calculus and social studies to LLMs which are two challenging subjects in human
267   education (Duncan, 1960; Jarvis, 2012; Kivunja, 2014; Myers, 2006).
268
269   Data Generation We first construct the skill graph for calculus and social studies. Specifically, as
      discussed in Section 3.1, we decompose calculus and social studies with GPT4 and further merge

      them with the human-curated syllabus 2 into 14 levels of skills for math (4,907 skills in total) and 9
271   levels of skills for social studies (990 skills in total), as described in Table 1. Skills in lower levels
272   are pointed to skills in higher levels in the constructed skill graph (e.g., Inside one level, pre-request

      Next, we prompt the GPT4 model with nucleus sampling (Ravfogel et al., 2023) with temperature
277   T = 0.5 and top−p = 0.95 to generate the textbook descriptions following the constraints stated
278   in Section 3.2 for every skill in the constructed skill graph 3 . Likewise, we utilize the GPT4 model
279   with nucleus sampling (T = 0.1 and top−p = 0.95 (we use a lower temperature here to make the
280   problems and answers more accurate.)) to generate both the problems and answers for exercise
281   generation as stated in Section 3.2 4 . On average, every problem requires 3.8 skills to solve. To
282   improve the quality of the generated answers, we apply self-consistency where we set k = 3. The
283   total number of generated tokens for textbook descriptions and exercise is shown in Table 1 where
284   we generate 4,497,938 tokens for adapting calculus and 859,974 tokens for adapting social studies.

286   also use GPT4 with nucleus sampling (T = 1.0 and top−p = 0.95 (we use a higher temperature here
287   to improve the diversity.)) to generate problems which are similar to the given hard-to-learn examples
288   and generate more complex problems by instruct the models to compose two different easy-to-learn
289   problems. Similarly, we apply self-consistency with k = 3 to generate the answers for these newly
290   generated problems.
291
292   Evaluation Set To evaluate the abilities for calculus, we utilize the Pre-Calculus subset in MATH
293   benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2021). For social studies, we collect multiple choice exams from
294
      online 5 , which results in an evaluation set that consists of 1430 questions6 . In addition, to evaluate
      the generalization abilities after adapting to specialized domain like calculus, we further evaluate
295
      models on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH(Hendrycks et al., 2021) and a constructed arithmetic
296
      task where we define 200 new mathematical operations in the problem description and test the models
297   if they could understand the context to utilize novel math operations 7 .
298
299
      Backbone Models and Baselines We apply our proposed DSA to both LLAMA2-7/13/70b models
300   (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral-7b model (Jiang et al., 2023). During the pre-training on textbook
301   descriptions, for every level of skills, we train the models for two epochs with a learning rate of
302   3e − 4 with a linear warm-up of 500 steps and we learn following the sequence from lower-level
303   skills to higher-level skills. During the instruction-tuning on exercise data, we train the models for 5
304   epochs with a learning rate of 3e − 5. The batch size is set to 16. We update the training set after
305   every epoch of training.
306   We compare our learned models with several state-of-the-art baselines including ChatGPT(OpenAI,
307   2023), LLAMA2-7/13/70b(Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7b (Jiang et al., 2023), WizardMATH-
308   7/13/70b (Xu et al., 2023), OpenMath-7b(Toshniwal et al., 2024) and DeepSeekMATH-Inst-7b(Shao
309   et al., 2024).

461   5   C ONCLUSION
462
463   In this work, we propose Dynamic Skill Adaptation (DSA) framework to adapt LLMs with novel
464   and complex skills. DSA first decomposes the complex skills and constructing a skill graph, then
465   automatically generates the textbook and exercise for every skill in skill graph and arrange them in
466   a lower-to-higher level orders following the skill graph. Furthermore, DSA dynamically updates
467   the training data during training to avoid overfitting easy-to-learn and error examples. Extensive
468   experiments and ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed DSA. In this work,
469
      we only use Calculus and Social Studies as case studies of our DS. For future work, we are interested
      in expanding to a wider range of domains and merging different experts which are equipped with
470
      specialized skills.

472
473   6   L IMITATION
474
475   In this paper, we mainly perform experiments on math and social studies due to the limit of com-
476   putational resources. However, DSA can be general to other domains because complex skills can
477
      also be decomposed based on human prior or LLMs like ChatGPT to construct the skill graphs,
      with which we could further generate and organize the initial training data. In the future work, we
478
      would like to explore more domains. In this work, we limit the range of textbooks till US high
479
      schools. However, we think a wider range (e.g., college-level) would bring in more performance
480   gains. In terms of data leakage risks, in our evaluation, we designed one artificial task to avoid the
481   impact of potential data leakage in Table 5 (Arithmetic task) where we randomly design and define
482   mathematical operations which are less likely to be seen by GPT-4. Also, even GPT-4 can not achieve
483   high scores on the pre-calculus evaluation (29.8%) which indicates that the data is less likely to be
484   contaminated. Furthermore, we ran a sanity check about the exact match between testing samples
485   and training data and we did not find any exact match. In the future work, we would include the data
      contamination assessment (Golchin & Surdeanu, 2023) to avoid the data leakage risks.

758    Introduction:
       In this chapter, we will explore the concept of addition and subtraction and how they can be used to make a given number.
759    We will learn different strategies to find combinations of numbers that add up to a given value.
760    By the end of this chapter, you will be able to confidently use addition and subtraction to make a number.
761    Section 1: Addition
762    1.1 Understanding Addition:
       Addition is the process of combining two or more numbers to find the total. When we add numbers, the result is called the sum.
763    For example, if we add 2 and 3, the sum is 5.
764
       1.2 Ways to Make a Number:
765    To find different ways to make a number, we can use addition. Let’s take the number 6 as an example. We can find different
766    combinations of numbers that add up to 6. Here are a few examples:
767
       Example 1:
768    6=1+5
769    In this example, we added 1 and 5 to get 6.
       Example 2:
770    6=2+4
771    Here, we added 2 and 4 to make 6.
       Example 3:
772    6=3+3
773    In this case, we added 3 and 3 to get 6.

776    remainder. For example, if we subtract 3 from 7, the remainder is 4.
