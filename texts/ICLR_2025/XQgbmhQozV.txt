011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             Warning: this paper contains model outputs exhibiting unethical information.
014             Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved significant breakthroughs, but their
015             generated unethical content poses potential risks. Measuring value alignment of
016
                LLMs becomes crucial for their regulation and responsible deployment. Numerous
                datasets have been constructed to assess social bias, toxicity, and ethics in LLMs,
017
                but they suffer from evaluation chronoeffect, that is, as models rapidly evolve,
018
                existing data becomes leaked or undemanding, overestimating ever-developing
019             LLMs. To tackle this problem, we propose GETA, a novel generative evolving
020             testing approach that dynamically probes the underlying moral boarders of LLMs.
021             Distinct from previous adaptive testing methods that rely on static datasets with
022             limited difficulty, GETA incorporates an iteratively-updated item generator which
023             infers each LLM’s moral boundaries and generates difficulty-tailored testing items,
024             faithfully reflecting the true alignment extent. This process theoretically learns
025             a joint distribution of item and model response, with item difficulty and value
026             conformity as latent variables, where the generator co-evolves with the LLM, ad-
027
                dressing chronoeffect. We evaluate various popular LLMs with diverse capabilities
                and demonstrate that GETA can create difficulty-matching testing items and more
028
                accurately assess LLMs’ values, better consistent with their performance on unseen
029
                OOD and i.i.d. items, laying the groundwork for future evaluation paradigms.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Flourishing from training on massive data (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022a) and high-quality
035   human feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022), Large Language Models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022;
036   Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024; Team, 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable
037   abilities in instruction following and few-shot problem solving, sparking a revolution in AI field.
038   Despite such a prosperity, LLMs remain a double-edged sword with the existing potential ethical
039   risks (Weidinger et al., 2021b; Bommasani et al., 2022) further amplified (Wang et al., 2023a; Liu
040   et al., 2023c; McKenzie et al., 2023) or new problems emerging (Bommasani et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
041   2022b), regarding particular concerns on social bias (Liang et al., 2021; Gallegos et al., 2024), ethics
042
      problems (Moor, 2006; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021), and toxicity information (Fortuna
      & Nunes, 2018; Gehman et al., 2020) exhibited in the generated content.
043
044   To regulate and foster the responsible development of booming LLMs, it is necessary to assess
045   the extent to which they conform to human values and ethics (Scherrer et al., 2023). Previous
046   approaches mostly rely on static benchmarks, e.g., R EALT OXICITY P ROMPTS (Gehman et al., 2020)
047   and H ARM B ENCH (Mazeika et al., 2024) targeting harmfulness, and ETHICS (Hendrycks et al.,
048
      2021a) and δ-ROT (Rao et al., 2023) emphasizing ethical values. Nevertheless, in the era of
      LLMs, these datasets face the evaluation chronoeffect challenge1 , namely, i) static benchmarks
049
      are vulnerable to data leakage, hurting fair evaluation once included in training corpora (Golchin
050
      & Surdeanu, 2023; Kocoń et al., 2023), or struggle to keep pace with fast-growing LLMs in testing
051   difficulty, causing potential overestimation (Liu et al., 2023b;a). As shown in Fig. 1, updated

076
      also provides safer responses to the trolley problem than earlier versions, indicating that previously
077
      constructed tests fail to challenge latest LLMs and reflect their true values. For this problem,
078   Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) (van der Linden & Glas, 2010) stands out as a potential
079   solution, which utilizes Item Response Theory (IRT) (De Ayala, 2013) to model examinees’ cognitive
080   level and adaptively selects the most appropriate next test item from an item pool, aiming at using
081   fewer items (Weiss & Kingsbury, 1984). However, traditional CAT impractically assumes the
082   difficulty completeness of the pool (Wang & Vispoel, 1998), leaving chronoeffect unresolved.
083
      Therefore, we propose a novel framework for Generative Evolving Testing of vAlues (GETA).
084   Instead of hypothesizing difficulty completeness of the static item pool, GETA integrates CAT with
085   Automatic Item Generation (AIG) (Gierl et al., 2012), which are theoretically unified as learning a
086   joint distribution of item and model response with both item and value conformity as latent variables.
087   During this process, our method jointly trains a Variational IRT (VIRT) model and an item generator
088   to dynamically probe the underlying moral boundaries of LLMs and adaptively generate novel
089   and real-world test items with difficulties tailored to each examinee LLM. The generator could
090   be iteratively optimized by collecting items beyond the boundary difficulty, enabling it to evolve
091   alongside the LLMs’ responses. In this way, GETA avoids data leakage through on-the-fly item
092
      generation, and facilitates co-evolution of items concurrent with model improvement, breaking the
      chronoeffect bottleneck and more accurately revealing LLMs’ alignment extent with human values.
093
094   Our main contributions are: (1) To our best knowledge, we are the first to introduce psychometrics
095   into adaptive and dynamic evaluation of LLMs’ values beyond downstream performance; (2) We
096   propose a novel GETA framework to combine CAT with AIG and facilitate adaptive testing tailored to
097   each LLM, mitigating evaluation chronoeffect. (3) We evaluate diverse mainstream LLMs like GPT,
098
      Gemini, LLaMA and Mistral, manifesting GETA’s superiority over previous evaluation paradigms.

100   2                R ELATED WORKS
101
102   Static Evaluation of LLMs To reveal strengths and shortcomings of LLMs, extensive static datasets
103   have been constructed, with emphasis shifting from in-domain NLP tasks (Wang et al., 2018; 2019)
104   to general capabilities, such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023),
105   BIG-bench (Srivastava et al., 2023) and HELM (Liang et al., 2023), covering multiple aspects, as
106   well as specific abilities like instruction following (Wang et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2023b), domain
107   knowledge (Gu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) and tool use (Li et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023b). Besides
      abilities, potential social risks (Weidinger et al., 2021b), safety issues (Dong et al., 2024; Röttger


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2024) and trustworthiness (Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a) of LLMs also become a key
109   focus. Generally, these datasets fall into two lines: (1) discriminative evaluation utilizing multi-choice
110   questions or judgement (Forbes et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Jiang et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
111   2023a; Sun et al., 2023), and (2) the generative ones carefully crafted using templates (Nangia et al.,
112   2020; Barikeri et al., 2021; Nadeem et al., 2021) or prompts (Dhamala et al., 2021; Parrish et al.,
113   2022; Bai et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c) to elicit LLMs’ harmful behaviors, with BAD (Xu et al.,
114   2021), HarmfulQA (Bhardwaj & Poria, 2023) and DNA (Wang et al., 2023b) as typical examples.
115   Despite widespread use, as discussed in Sec. 1, these benchmarks suffer from evaluation chronoeffect
116   and often lack adaptability and scalability, failing to provide reliable assessment results.
117   Dynamic Evaluation of LLMs To compensate for the limitations of static datasets, there are
118   growing research efforts on dynamic evaluation (Krause et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2024). One branch
119   primarily follows a human-in-the-loop schema, enhancing data complexity and evaluation credibility
120   through human interaction (Ma et al., 2021; Zellers et al., 2021; Vidgen et al., 2021; Kiela et al.,
121   2021; Collins et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023), which offers greater flexibility but remains limited in
122   scalability due to expensive human labour. Another potential direction incorporates auto-generated
123
      evaluation data through task-related structures to explicitly control test item generation, such as trees
      for debugging (Ribeiro & Lundberg, 2022) and directed acyclic graphs for reasoning (Zhu et al.,
124
      2024). However, they are not suitable for our topic as it is hard to develop compositional structures of
125
      subtle human ethics. Beyond these tasks, few efforts concentrate on probing value vulnerabilities of
126   LLMs (Mazeika et al., 2024; Radharapu et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024). For instance,
127   M ASTER K EY (Deng et al., 2023b) fine-tunes an LLM for automatic jailbreak, SAP (Deng et al.,
128   2023a) instructs LLMs to imitate human-written test prompts, and GPTF UZZER (Yu et al., 2023)
129   leverages LLMs in a black-box fuzzing (Wei et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2020) framework. Though such
130   methods are unable to produce difficulty-adaptive items, this branch is poised for further exploration.
131
      Psychometrics Based Evaluation In psychology, psychometrics investigate the objective mea-
132   surement of latent traits, e.g, intelligence (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2001). Typically, a psychometric
133   model, such as the Item Response Theory (IRT) model (De Ayala, 2013; Wu et al., 2020; Kim et al.,
134   2023), serves as the evaluation paradigm to model the probability of correct responses based on
135   examinee ability and item parameters. IRT is commonly combined with Computerized Adaptive
136   Testing (CAT) (Weiss & Kingsbury, 1984; van der Linden & Glas, 2010; Bi et al., 2021) to itera-
137   tively select the next item according to the examinee’s response history, allowing direct and efficient
138   comparison (Vie et al., 2017; Zhuang et al., 2022a;b). To reduce the high cost of item construction,
139   Automatic Item Generation (AIG) (Gierl & Haladyna, 2012) was proposed to create new items
140
      more efficiently, leveraging well-designed templates (Gierl et al., 2012; Harrison et al., 2017; Götz
      et al., 2023). With the rapid development of AI, CAT has been introduced as a robust NLP metric
141
      (Martínez-Plumed et al., 2016; Plumed et al., 2019) in question answering (Rodriguez et al., 2021;
142
      Vania et al., 2021), natural language inference (Lalor et al., 2016; 2018; Vania et al., 2021) and
143   machine translation (Hopkins & May, 2013; Otani et al., 2016; Lalor et al., 2019). More recently,
144   this paradigm is also exploited for evaluating chatbots (Sedoc & Ungar, 2020) and LLMs (Zhuang
145   et al., 2023; 2024; Polo et al., 2024; Lalor et al., 2024). Nevertheless, it’s challenging to apply CAT to
146   LLMs, as IRT’s scale invariance requires labor-intensive calibration (Ryan & Brockmann, 2009) and
147   the difficulty and scale of testing is limited due to the poor item quality by traditional AIG methods.
148
      In spite of great progress in LLM evaluation, aforementioned limitations necessitate the integration
149   of these methods’ advantages for better uncovering the true value boundaries of LLMs.

      items, further extending item difficulty and alleviating overestimation. In this way, the generator
297
      self-calibrates while preserving the scale invariance of IRT (Reise et al., 1993), co-evolving with the
298
      advancements of examinee LLMs, thereby addressing the evaluation chronoeffect challenge.
299
300   The derivation is in Appendix. C.2. Fig. 2 gives a simplified running example. A detailed explanation
301   of GETA with examples and discussions on how it addresses chronoeffect are in the Appendix. C.4.

      Data and Metrics Following the common practice in LLM alignment (Askell et al., 2021; Köpf
      et al., 2023), we consider three types of value issue: social bias, ethics, and toxicity. We collect
308
      15k test items, 5k for each type, from 12 widely-used static datasets such as BBQ (Parrish et al.,
309
      2022), ETHICS (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), R EALT OXICITY P ROMPTS (Gehman et al., 2020) and
310   H ARMFUL QA (Bhardwaj & Poria, 2023). More dataset details are dilated in Appendix. A. We report
311   the min-max normalized Value Conformity (VC) of examinee LLMs, and define VC for static
312   evaluation as the frequency of examinee conforming to human values over K responses for each
313   item, following (Gehman et al., 2020). For CAT-based methods, yj = 0 when LLMs generate toxic,
314   biased or wrong responses, otherwise yj = 1, and we set VC = âT . To measure the extent to which
315   each evaluation method is a valid and effective proxy of LLMs’ true underlying values, we adopt
316   Concurrent Validity (Va) (Xiao et al., 2023a) and calculate it as the Pearson’s correlation between
317   the estimated VC and (i) popular LLM safety leaderboard scores (Va-L), (ii) VC estimated on i.i.d
318   but unseen items (Va-I), and (iii) VC estimated on OOD items with the same value type (Va-O),
319   respectively. We highlight Va-L as a more effective metric, as these leaderboards encompass diverse
320
      formats, semantics, difficulty levels, and i.i.d. and OOD cases, better reflecting universal validity.
      Comprehensive evaluation protocol descriptions and item examples are presented in Appendix. B.1.

365   Value Conformity Analysis We first evaluate the value conformity of eight popular LLMs with
366   diverse capabilities and scales, using different evaluation methods. The results are shown in Table 1.
367   We can obtain three interesting findings: (1) Rankings from SE and CAT generally align with the
368   intuition that larger models possess better capabilities, with GPT-4 establishing the SOTA in most
369
      value issues. (2) NCAT gives somewhat contradictory conclusions with notably inconsistent results
      among the three types, raking GPT-4 the last in both ethics and toxicity. Such results indicate the
370
      unreliability of NCAT, consistent with the conclusions drawn from in Fig. 3. (3) GETA typically
371
      considers larger models, e.g., GPT-4 and Mistral-M, superior, while some smaller ones, like Orca-

373   conformity. Moreover, we can observe several implausible results from previous evaluation methods:

375   NCAT; ii) In toxicity, an extensively-studied risk type, CAT considers LLaMA2 models with 7B and

377   outperforms LLaMA2-7B-Chat aligned via RLHF. These counterintuitive results imply potential
      systematic measurement errors of existing evaluation methods, necessitating an in-depth diagnosis.

      fail to keep pace with fast evolving LLMs. As presented in Fig. 4 (c), LLMs with considerable
477   capability gaps, e.g., Mistral-Medium, GPT-4, GPT-3.5-Turbo, and LLaMA-2-70B-Chat, obtain

481   testing as introduced in Sec. 2. Nevertheless, all examinees get almost zero scores under their
482   measurements, since they merely attack and elicit harmful responses, unable to adaptively adjust
483   difficulty. In comparison, with the incorporation of VIRT and the item generator, GETA is endowed

532
533   The rapid development of LLMs poses a special challenge for accurately unpacking their underlying
534   values/ethic conformity, namely, evaluation chronoeffect. To alleviate the overestimation cased by
535   this problem, we propose generative evolving testing, and design GETA, a corresponding framework
536
      to adaptively probe LLMs’ value boundaries and generate novel and difficulty-tailored test items.
      Comprehensive experiments and analysis manifest GETA can produce robust and generalized evalua-
537
      tion results, supporting its superior validity and efficiency. In the future, we plan to further explore
538
      the scalability of GETA on different models in real-time safety monitoring scenarios, and apply it to
539   more value types and multimodal models, paving the way for more reliable big model evaluation.

1322   A.1    R EASONS FOR CHOOSING HUMAN VALUES AS CRITERIA
1323
1324   Here, we elaborate further on this topic from three aspects:
1325   The underlying motivations for choosing human values as criteria.
1326
1327   (1) Regarding the values/safety of LLMs as desiderata, we argue that evaluating these attributes is
1328
       both more critical and urgent than assessing model capabilities. While other criteria such as reasoning,
       coding, and mathematical ability are important, misalignment and risky behaviors of LLMs can have
1329
       a far more serious negative impact on humans and society (Weidinger et al., 2021a; Bommasani et al.,
1330
       2022; Wynn et al., 2024). Thus, establishing a baseline for values and ethics is a prerequisite for
1331   responsible deployment.
1332
1333   (2) Whereas dynamic and adaptive evaluations of LLM capabilities have been relatively well-
1334
       studied (Collins et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024), such paradigms for values, ethics, and
       social risks remains largely unexplored with most works relying on static benchmarks (Ziems et al.,
1335
       2022; Scherrer et al., 2023; Mazeika et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). As acknowledged, we are the
1336
       first to dynamically probe human values in LLMs.
1337
1338   (3) We choose social bias, ethics, and toxicity as key representatives of human values, since they are
1339   core indicators commonly used for evaluating the safety of LLMs (Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Wang
1340
       et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023c; Gallegos et al., 2024), essential for achieving the productization and
       ensuring regulatory compliance.
1341
1342   The applicable criteria of GETA. Although GETA focuses on social bias, ethics, and toxicity in this
1343   work, it is criterion-agnostic. The VIRT model and item generator are relevant only to evaluation
1344   performance (i.e., evaluation validity and reliability) as shown in Table 2 and Table 12. Since the
1345   item generator pω (x|d) requires only item parameters (such as item difficulty and discrimination) to
1346
       produce new items, as formulated in Sec. 3.2, our proposed GETA is suitable for any criterion, as
       long as it is well-defined and quantifiable.
1347
1348   Is the evaluation of values easier than other criteria? Evaluating human values differs in both
1349   intent and characteristics from other capabilities, posing unique methodological challenges. However,
       this does not imply that it is any easier to implement.


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       (1) Evaluation of values and ethics focuses on identifying the vulnerabilities of LLMs and assessing
1351   their safety in worst-case scenarios. These vulnerabilities are influenced by the type of values, the
1352   robustness of LLMs to different prompts, and the ability of LLMs to consistently demonstrate safe
1353   behavior across various scenarios, contexts, and prompt formats to address potential risks. Therefore,
1354   we base the calculation of Value Conformity on Empirical Probability in this work (for each test
1355   item, an LLM is regarded as safe only if none of its K responses is harmful), reflecting the highest
1356   requirement for model safety. The GETA framework is also designed to automatically identify such
1357   vulnerabilities. In contrast, evaluation of model capabilities (such as mathematical skills) prioritizes
1358   assessing average problem-solving performance through well-defined, formally-stated problems, with
1359
       less emphasis on prompt robustness.
1360   (2) The evaluation results of values/safety are rarely transferable across different value types, while
1361   those for capabilities tend to be more generalizable (Yang et al., 2024; Ye, 2024). For example,
1362   proficiency in logical reasoning is positively related to mathematical reasoning performance (Ahn
1363   et al., 2024; Imani et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2023). However, an LLM excelling in avoiding bias may
1364   perform poorly in generation toxicity (Welbl et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023). This can also be observed
1365
       in Table 1 of our paper: LLaMA-2-7B-Chat, ranked highest in mitigating social bias, is rated weakest
       in ethics by GETA. This is because human values form a complex system, where inter-value effects
1366
       are not always simply positive (Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2023). As a result,
1367
       value/safety evaluation needs to cover a broad spectrum of dimensions and a variety of scenarios.

1553   B.1.2     M ETRICS FOR EVALUATION VALIDITY
1554
1555   Whether the evaluation results can be trusted depends on the validity of the measurement. An
1556   evaluation method is valid only if the results can support their intended interpretations (e.g., model
1557   capability) and uses (e.g., predicting models’ downstream performance) (Lissitz & Samuelsen, 2007;
1558   Xiao et al., 2023a). However, such intended interpretations or the true scores are inherently unobserv-
1559   able, making it challenging to establish a completely reliable ground-truth measurement. Therefore,
1560   we choose Concurrent Validity (Va) (Allen & Yen, 2001; Xiao et al., 2023a), which measures an
1561   evaluation method by calculating its consistency with more reliable reference measurements, and
1562
       has been applied across various research fields, including medicine, psychology, sociology, and
       pedagogy (Magyar-Moe, 2009; Stokes, 2010; Wright et al., 2012; Michalos, 2014).

1565   LLM values/safety leaderboards, i.i.d test items with our value conformity metrics, and OOD test
       items with the corresponding metrics.

1795   B.2.1    VARIATIONAL IRT MODEL
1796
1797   Variational IRT model consists of an ability estimator and a parameter estimator. In this work,
1798   both estimators were implemented with two Transformer encoders, one predicted the mean and
1799
       the other predicted the variance of the Gaussian distribution. These four encoders shared the same
       architecture with two Transformer encoder layers, four attention heads, a model dimension of 64,
1800
       and a feedforward network dimension four times larger. In ability estimator, the embedding module
1801
       was a two-layer MLP in order to embed float triples (y, b, c), and both encoders were followed by
1802   a projection layer to obtain a single value, respectively. In parameter estimator, we used a typical
1803   embedding layer with two embeddings for 0 and 1. In the projection layers, the difficulty parameter b
1804   and discrimination parameter c were predicted together.
1805
1806   B.2.2    I TEM GENERATOR
1807
1808   We employed Llama-3-8B as the base model of the item generator. Specifically, we referred to prefix-
1809   tuning but replaced the virtual token embeddings with item parameter embeddings, reparameterizing
1810   the prefix parameters by a two-layer MLP. The number of item parameter embeddings was set to 5.
1811   The item generator was then trained for 10 epochs with a batch size of 32, a learning rate of 5e-5,
1812
       and a prefix length of 10. For LoRA, we set the rank, alpha, and dropout rate as 8, 32, and 0.05,
       respectively; the target modules were all of the dense layers in the Transformer blocks, while the
1813
       other dense layers outside were fully trained. All the static items were used for training, and the ratio
1814
       of the training set to the validation set is 4:1. During generation, we used sampling with a top_p of 0.9
1815   and a top_k of 50. The maximum generation length was 64. Apart from the generation configuration,
1816   the prompts of the bias and toxicity type were directly generated by the item generator, but for ethics

1869

1872   IRT-3PL model, which outperformed the other reported CDM, NCDM (Wang et al., 2020), on our
1873   data. All of the static data were used for building the item pool. The test length was set to 150,

1878
       red-teaming LLMs by three components: a seed selection strategy for balancing efficiency and
1879   variety, a set of mutate operators for creating new jailbreak templates, and a judgement model for

       For better comparison, we slightly modified the settings of GPTF UZZER and expanded its applicable

1884
       operators to directly apply to entire prompts. We inherited the number of initial seed prompts in

2117   C.4   H OW DOES GETA ADDRESS THE CHRONOEFFECT CHALLENGE ?
2118
2119   In this paper, chronoeffect represents a two-fold challenge: (1) Data Contamination, where the testing
2120   items may have been included in an LLM’s fine-tuning data, and (2) Difficulty Mismatch, where the
2121   testing items are too easy for the continuously upgraded LLMs. As discussed in Sec. 3.3, GETA
2122   effectively addresses the two challenges as follows:
2123
       First, GETA avoids the data contamination problem by generating novel and diverse new items
2124   with an item generator, rather than selecting items from a static item pool as in traditional CAT.
2125   The item generator, while pretrained on static data, can produce genuinely novel and diverse items
2126   beyond simple replicas of training data. The generator achieved this through: i) rephrasing training
2127   items, generating varied expressions to introduce more diversity; ii) creating new items with greater
2128   variety and range by leveraging the extensive knowledge embedded in the powerful backbone of
2129   the generator (e.g., LLaMA-3-8B) during pretraining, instead of simply rewriting existing items; iii)
2130   enhancing novelty and diversity during iterative testing by fine-tuning itself with responses from
2131   various LLM examinees.
2132   These advantages of GETA are justified by the following results:
2133
       (1) Lower similarity with existing static data. In Table 8, we calculated the similarity between the
2134
       static benchmark items and the GETA-generated items, i.i.d. items from the same static benchmark,
2135
       as well as items from the OOD dataset, respectively. The cosine similarity was computed using
2136   OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-large, the same model used for Fig. 4(a). As shown, GETA-generated
2137   items are quite novel, with less overlap with training items (low similarity compared to i.i.d. items),
2138   getting closer to the totally different OOD items. These results indicate that GETA can produce
2139   entirely new items, rather than merely copying or rephrasing existing training items.
2140
       (2) Consistently increasing improvements achieved by a stronger generator backbone. In App. D.2,
2141
       we conduct an ablation on the backbone of the item generator. As shown in the first block of
2142   Table 12, a large model size leads to better evaluation validity (Va-L, Va-I, and Va-O) and a more
2143   significant model difference (SD). This suggests that GETA’s improvements are not simply the result
2144   of replicating or reproducing unexposed items from the training set. Rather, it harnesses the superior
2145   generalization capabilities and internal knowledge of larger generative models to produce truly novel
2146   and diverse items. Furthermore, even with the smallest model (GPT-2-Large), GETA outperforms
2147   most baselines, showcasing its effectiveness, stability, and robustness.
2148   Second, GETA addresses the difficulty mismatch problem by adaptively adjusting item difficulty.
2149   Most static benchmarks tend to be too easy for rapidly developing LLMs, which can lead to an
2150   overestimation of their capabilities. GETA achieves adaptive item difficulty by leveraging CAT and
2151   IRT, and we are the first to incorporate CAT for adaptive difficulty adjustment in automatic benchmark
2152   construction.
2153
       The difficulty adjusting method is introduced in Sec. 3.3. In the testing process, the difficulty is
2154   adjusted according to the following steps: i) The VIRT model estimates the ability of each examinee
2155   LLM based on its response history (L3, L15 in Alg. 1); ii) the appropriate item parameters (e.g., item
2156   difficulty) for the next test item are calculated based on the LLM’s ability (L6 in Alg. 1) to gradually
2157   increase the difficulty until the LLM fails to answer it correctly; iii) the item generator then generates
2158   a number of new items with the specified parameters; iv) when an LLM answers an item incorrectly,
2159   suggesting that the item is particularly challenging, we use such items to fine-tune the generator,
       enhancing its ability to create higher-difficulty items and broadening its overall difficulty range.

       D.2    A NALYSIS ON GETA’ S STABILITY
2377
2378   We conduct an analysis on GETA’s stability against three factors: the backbone of the item generator,
2379   the difficulty, and the number of the seed items for GETA’s initialization. Social bias of four examinee
2380   LLMs, i.e., GPT-3.5-Turbo, Gemini-1.0-Pro, Mistral-Medium, and LLaMA-2-7B-Chat are measured
2381   in the experiments.
2382
       Typically, GETA starts with 50 seed items of medium difficulty from the collected static data. In
2383   social bias, the static item difficulty derived by the VIRT model ranges from -4.3726 to 5.3741,
2384   with a medium value of -1.4102. For seed difficulty ablation, we fix the seed number at 50, with
2385   specific difficulty values for the easiest, medium, and hardest seeds being -4.3726, -1.4102, and
2386   [4.8092, 5.3741], respectively. For seed number ablation, we sample varying quantities of static items
2387   with medium difficulty, ranging from 10 to 300 as seed items. The results are shown in Table 12.
2388   Here we also report the standard deviation (SD) to capture the differences of the value conformity
2389   across different examinee LLMs. A higher SD implies that GETA is more effective in capturing the
2390   differences between various LLMs.
2391   We observe that GETA possesses great robustness against varying seed settings. With different
2392   seed item difficulties and numbers, the validity and performance gaps of GETA remain satisfactory
2393   with only a negligible trade-off in different dimensions. For other generation hyperparameters, e.g,
2394   softmax temperature and thresholds in top-p/k sampling, we just follow the common practice.
2395   Additionally, the size of item generators plays an important role. As the model size decreases, both Va
2396   and SD decline but remain within an acceptable range. We speculate this occurs because larger LLMs
2397   possess greater generalization abilities, enabling them to generate more diverse and difficulty-adaptive
2398   items.
2399
       Furthermore, the generator is not biased toward its own model family. We conducted another version
2400
       of the experiment on the generator backbone, with the latest GPT-3.5-Turbo, LLaMA-2-7B-Chat,
2401   and Phi-3-Mini-Instruct as the examinees of GETA. We used LLaMA-3-8B and Phi-3-Mini as the
2402   backbone of the item generator, respectively. The rankings and the unnormalized value conformity
2403   scores of these three examinees are reported in Table 13. From the results, no significant differences
2404   are observed in either the rankings or the relative scores when different generator backbones are
2405   used. In the latest versions, Phi-3-Mini-Instruct performs slightly better than GPT-3.5-Turbo in
2406   avoiding social bias, though both still lag far behind LLaMA-2-7B-Chat. Additionally, in Table
2407   1, GETA, with the item generator powered by LLaMA-3-8B, also ranks LLaMA-2-70B-Chat and
2408   LLaMA-2-7B-Chat as the weakest in ethics. This suggests that the generator is not biased toward
2409
       its own model family. We suppose that since the generators are fine-tuned, format and wording
       characteristics that might influence inter-family recognition have been largely diluted.
2410
2411   Generally, GETA consistently outperforms most baselines across various hyperparameters and
2412   generator backbones, suggesting its effectiveness, stability, and robustness.

2414   D.3    H UMAN EVALUATION
2415
2416   We conducted a human evaluation to further justify the validity of GETA. Specifically, we recruited
2417   five human annotators with extensive experience using LLMs and advanced knowledge of human
2418   values and AI safety. These annotators were asked to independently interact with two examinee
2419   LLMs at a time, using either GETA-generated items or their own questions. They compared the
2420
       responses and assessed their value conformity. Since the evaluations took place simultaneously on a
       moderate scale, we adopted tournament scores instead of Elo rankings.
2421

2423   value conformity scores given by different evaluation methods in bias, commonsense, and toxicity
2424   are presented in Table 14. The best and second-best results are marked in bold and underlined,

2426   A Cohen’s Kappa of 0.7551 and a Pearson Correlation of 0.7556 indicate good inter-annotator
2427   agreement in our human evaluation, and a p-value < 0.01 shows acceptable significance. As shown in
2428   this human study, GETA achieves the highest correlations with human ratings with only a negligible
2429   gap compared to CAT in toxicity, highlighting its ability to provide a more reliable evaluation of the
       values and safety of LLMs.

       E     B ROADER IMPACTS
2647
2648
       Our research aims to explore the underlying boundary of the ethical values of LLMs with a dynamic
2649
       testing method to safeguard their rapid development. However, it should be noted that there are still
2650   imperfections and limitations in GETA, calling for more follow-up efforts to improve the overall
2651   reliability, validity, and safety of our measurement.
2652
2653
       E.1    I NEXHAUSTIVE CAPITALIZATION OF PSYCHOMETRICS THEORIES
2654
2655   A fundamental theoretical basis of our research is Item Response Theory (IRT), which is a theory of
2656   testing based on the relationship between an examinee’s performance on a test item and their level
2657   of performance on an overall measure of the ability that the item was designed to measure. Many
2658   statistical models are proposed to capture such relationships, including normal ogive models, logistic
2659   models, graded response models, and partial credit models. In this work, we adopt one of the most
2660   widely-used models, namely, the IRT-2PL model for GETA. However, experimenting with only one
2661   model might be biased and limited in finding the best measurement for the conformity of LLMs with
2662
       human values.
2663
2664   E.2    P OTENTIAL RISKS OF MALICIOUS USE
2665
2666
       Although our methods are proposed to provide a deeper insight into the ethics and safety of LLMs,
       they could also be abused in attacking the LLMs or producing harmful content on a large scale.
2667
       Specifically, as detailed in our further analysis, some users could utilize GETA, especially the item
2668
       generator, to discover and spread extensive i.i.d test items that induce value violations in most LLMs.
2669   Additionally, the detailed text samples and analyses of unethical responses might still make readers
2670   uncomfortable despite the warning at the beginning of the paper. Therefore, we have minimized the
2671   harmful content in this paper.
2672
       Consequently, further research and refinement are necessary to address these concerns and enhance
2673
       the overall performance of ethical value evaluation methods for LLMs.
2674
2675
2676   F     L IMITATIONS
2677
2678   This study aims to probe the underlying moral baselines of rapidly developing large language models
2679   (LLMs). However, it is important to note several limitations that may impact the interpretation and
2680   generalizability of our findings:
2681
2682          • Adoption of the IRT model. In this work, we utilize the prevalent IRT-2PL model as the
2683            cognitive diagnosis model of the adaptive testing method. While IRT-2PL is a widely used
2684            statistical model for IRT, it may be a simplistic choice compared to other statistical models.
2685
                Our focus, however, is primarily on the technological and algorithmic aspects.
2686          • Scope of human values. Our study covers a wide range of social value issues. Nonetheless,
2687            it is impractical to consider all value types. An exhaustive exploration of value conformity
2688            in LLMs falls into the realm of the humanities and social sciences and is beyond the scope
2689            of this work.
2690          • Scope of examinee LLMs. Our study evaluates eight competitive LLMs, with model
2691            sizes ranging from billions to hundreds of billions of parameters and training methods
2692            from instruction tuning to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). However,
2693            the proliferation of newer LLMs continues, and there are more emergent models, such as
2694            LLaMA-3 and GPT-4o, which we do not have enough time or accessibility to conduct a
2695            comprehensive test.
2696          • Potential bias in LLM judgment. Despite use of repetitive experiments in our response
2697            judgment process, other types of biases may still exist. For example, social biases in the
2698            LLMs used to check if the examinees’ responses violate human values may compromise the
2699            accuracy of the judgments. Nonetheless, this paper primarily focuses on the generative and
                adaptive evaluation of LLMs’ true value conformity.
