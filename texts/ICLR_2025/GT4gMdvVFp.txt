011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance in
014            various tasks, yet their comprehensive reasoning and planning capabilities in in-
015
               teractive environments remain underexplored. We introduce P UZZLE P LEX, a
               benchmark designed to evaluate reasoning and planning capabilities in a multi-
016
               turn competitive two-player environment. P UZZLE P LEX comprises 24 diverse
017
               puzzles, including deterministic and stochastic games, as well as single-player
018            and competitive two-player scenarios. An important novelty of our benchmark is
019            that it includes multi-step competitive two-player reasoning games. To succeed
020            in such games, each LLM must maintain a history of its own moves and those
021            of the opponent LLM, generating strategies that outperform the opponent to se-
022            cure victory. We implement customized game-playing strategies (such as dynamic
023            programming approaches) for comparison. Our findings indicate that the reason-
024            ing and planning abilities of current LLMs are currently poor in puzzle-solving
025            contexts. GPT-4 outperforms other models, successfully competing against cus-
026
               tomized strategies (such as greedy approaches or dynamic programming) in 49%
               of cases. However, when faced with strict rule sets, it demonstrates diminished
027
               reasoning and planning capabilities. In addition to the 14 multi-turn competi-
028
               tive two-player puzzles, we report on single-player puzzles and incorporate multi-
029            modal challenges that integrate text and images, revealing that LLMs still signif-
030            icantly lag behind even simple heuristics in puzzles. A key feature of our bench-
031            mark is its ability to generate game instances with graduated levels of difficulty, al-
032            lowing it to evolve as LLMs become more sophisticated. This adaptability ensures
033            the continued relevance and utility of P UZZLE P LEX in assessing the progress of
034            LLM capabilities in reasoning and planning within interactive environments.1

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated performance comparable to humans in a range of
040   tasks, from nuanced natural language understanding to complex math word problem solving (Team
041   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). These capabilities highlight their potential not just as tools for
042   automated responses but as shallow problem-solvers that can navigate and interpret extensive data
043
      sets with impressive accuracy, which is crucial for decision-making system.
044   This work uses puzzle solving as a means to enhance the evaluation of LLMs for deep problem-
045   solving. By deep problem-solving, we mean a combination of logical and numerical reasoning,
046   strategic planning, and adaptability. Puzzles possessing these characteristics are excellent candi-
047   dates for assessing the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in scenarios that mimic complex real-
048   world problem solving. While some prior research works have explored evaluating LLMs in puzzle-
049
      solving contexts (Noever & Burdick, 2021; Ding et al., 2023), they have focused on single-agent in-
      teractions with the environment (Stechly et al., 2024), where game information is typically conveyed
050
      solely through text descriptions of rules and states, and where each action leads to a predetermined,
051
      non-stochastic reward (Shridhar et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2022). P UZZLE P LEX, in contrast, focuses

      2.2    E VOLUTION OF P UZZLE S OLVING T ECHNIQUES
143
      A wide range of methods and strategies have been employed to solve rule-based puzzles. These
144   include algorithmic techniques such as dynamic programming (Smith, 2007), alpha-beta pruning
145   (Korf, 1990), and search algorithms (Lewis, 2007). In the domain of single-player games, neuro-
146   symbolic approaches are popular (Ahmed et al., 2023; Murali et al., 2019) because single-player
147   puzzles often require combinatorial search and can typically be reduced to Satisfiability (SAT) prob-
148   lems (Bright et al., 2020; Høfler, 2014). With advances in deep learning algorithms, reinforcement
149   learning has become increasingly popular in solving puzzles (dos Santos et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
150   2024). However, despite the enhanced computational power available today, the combinatorial ex-
151   plosion of many puzzles means that heuristic methods remain useful (Silver et al., 2016).
152   In the context of early LLMs, fine-tuning is often used to solve puzzles. For example, researchers
153   have fine-tuned models such as GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) and FLAN-PaLM (Chung et al., 2024)
154   to solve puzzles like Sudoku (Noever & Burdick, 2021) and BoardgameQA (Kazemi et al., 2024).
155
      The advent of powerful LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024) has introduced a more flexi-
156   ble approach for solving puzzles through few-shot in-context learning. By translating puzzles into
157   natural language descriptions and using language-based feedback in a multi-run setting, recent work
158   has explored the capabilities of LLMs in puzzle solving. The Chain-of-Thought (CoT) approach
159   (Wei et al., 2022) has proven superior to simple prompts in this context. Additionally, other prompt-
160   ing techniques have been successfully applied to puzzles. For example, Self-Refine (Madaan et al.,
161   2024) is used for the Game of 24; Tree-of-Thought (Long, 2023) employs a tree structure to solve
      Sudoku; and Everything-of-Thought (Ding et al., 2023) utilizes graph topology to solve three de-

      terministic puzzles. In our work, we employ prompting techniques similar to CoT to test whether
163   current LLMs and MLLMs can effectively leverage their knowledge and understanding to reason
164   comprehensively, plan, and make decisions when faced with complexity.

      We first introduce the P UZZLE P LEX framework in which puzzle templates can be instantiated,
169
      moves recorded, state information shared, and states evaluated. We next describe the puzzles in-
170
      cluded in this benchmark, the implementation of baseline strategies, and the evaluation method.

172
      3.1       P UZZLE G ENERATION F RAMEWORK
173
174   P UZZLE P LEX has the following main components, as presented in Figure 1.
175
176   Instance Generation For each puzzle p, we distinguish between a possibly parametrized puz-
177   zle template template(p) (e.g., Sudoku on a 9 × 9 grid, template(Sudoku(9,9)), and an instance
178   instance(p) (e.g., a particular instance of Sudoku on a 9 × 9 grid, instance(Suduoku(9,9)). A gen-
179   erator function Gp maps templates to instances. The generated instance is also the initial state S0
180   of the game. That is, instance(p) = S0 . The generator for each puzzle will create instances using
181   randomness, and it will adjust the difficulty level by varying the size of the puzzle.
182
183
      State Transition After receiving a move M generated by a player (human or computer), the state
      transition module maps a state Sn to a new state Sn+1 while incorporating feedback Fn . The
184
      feedback Fn indicates the legality of the move, whether the game has terminated, and provides new
185
      position information. This process is represented as M : Sn → (Sn+1 , Fn ).
186
187
      Evaluation Once the puzzle-solving process terminates, an evaluator Ep is applied to the
188   sequence of states S0 , S1 , . . . , Sn to determine the raw score(s), represented as rsp =
189   Ep (S0 , S1 , . . . , Sn ). The scale of the raw scores varies depending on the resolution type of each
190   puzzle. To ensure comparability, we normalize these scores to obtain final scores ranging from 0 to
191   1 (§ 3.4).
192
      To better keep track of state transitions and model reasoning steps, we implemented a Web UI called
193
      Simulator for visual observation. An example of this interface is shown in the § A.2.

      3.2       P UZZLE P LEX B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
196
197   Aside from the four classical puzzles Sudoku, SudokuM (text-image version of Sudoku), N-Queens,
198   and Takuzu, the puzzles in our study are derived from a column of Communications of the ACM 2 .
199   While LLMs may have accessed the texts describing these puzzles, there are no strategies for them
200   online, thus eliminating the possibility of data contamination. Additionally, we have simplified the
201   rules for several puzzles to lower the barrier to entry, allowing most people to engage with them
202   immediately after learning the rules and objectives.
203   Our 24 puzzles can be categorized into four types: single-player deterministic, single-player
204   stochastic, competitive two-player deterministic, and competitive two-player stochastic. Text-
205   based puzzles encompass all four types, while text-image puzzles are limited to single-player deter-
206   ministic and competitive two-player deterministic variants. The distinction between deterministic
207   and stochastic games lies in the predictability of operation rewards. In deterministic games, the
208   outcome of a decision is fixed, regardless of how many times it is chosen. Conversely, stochastic
209
      games yield probabilistic outcomes, where repeated selection of the same operation in the same state
      may result in different outcomes. Detailed information about the puzzles is presented in §A.1, with
210
      individual puzzle descriptions provided in §A.3.

214   We implemented baseline strategies for each puzzle, which can be categorized as follows:

      • Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) Solver: This approach involves encoding puzzle constraints
217     and rules as logical formulas, which are then solved using an SMT solver. SMT solvers determine
218     whether a set of constraints is satisfiable and, if so, provide a solution that satisfies all constraints.
219     In our customized strategies, we utilize the powerful Z3 Solver package (De Moura & Bjørner,

221   • Brute-force Algorithm: This method is employed when the problem size allows for an exhaustive
222     search within our specified time constraints.
223   • Search Algorithms: We employ a variety of search techniques, including:
224
225         – Uninformed search methods: Breadth-First Search (BFS) and Depth-First Search (DFS).
226         – Probabilistic search: Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
227   • Dynamic Programming (DP): Dynamic programming is applied to puzzles that exhibit overlap-
228     ping subproblems and optimal substructure.
229
      • Greedy Algorithm: Greedy algorithms are employed in puzzles where locally optimal choices are
230     expected to lead to globally optimal solutions or the search space is too large for other techniques,
231     often reflecting strategies used in real-world scenarios.
232
      • Other Methods: Additional algorithms, such as backtracking and simulated annealing algorithm,
233     are incorporated.

      Single-player Games In this setting, we implemented one strategy for each puzzle, which remains
236   consistent regardless of the difficulty level. This approach is justified because single-player games
237   do not require competition with another player.
238
239
      Competitive Two-player Games We employ baseline strategies that vary based on the difficulty
240   level, as they necessitate competition with another player. This variety of strategies allows for a
241   more comprehensive evaluation of LLMs capabilities. At the easy level, baseline strategies for most
242   puzzles employ legal random moves, selecting randomly from the space of legal moves. At the
243   intermediate level, strategies become more sophisticated, occasionally identifying superior moves.
244   These strategies may also consider moves that confer advantages over the opponent. The strategies
245   utilized for each puzzle, along with examples of customized strategies, can be found in § B.2.

247   3.4    E VALUATION M ETRICS
248
249   Metrics for evaluating the performance of LLMs on puzzles can be characterized as either binary
250   or continuous.
251
252   Single-player Game In binary metric puzzles, players either succeed in achieving the desired out-
253   come, resulting in a score of 1, or fail, yielding a score of 0. By contrast, continuous metric games
254   involve the accumulation of points based on various factors such as number of moves, constraints, or
255   objectives, leading to scores that may fall outside the [0, 1] interval. To ensure comparability across
256   both types of games, we normalize raw scores onto a common scale of [0, 1]. This standardiza-
257
      tion process typically involves employing a baseline strategy with identical initialization parameters
      and utilizing its performance as a reference point. In cases where higher scores indicate better per-
258
      formance, if the score of the LLM model exceeds that of the baseline, it is assigned a score of 1;
259
      otherwise, the final score is determined by the ratio of the raw score of the LLM model to that
260   of the baseline. This metric holds symmetrically in situations where lower scores signify superior
261   performance.
262
263
      Competitive Two-player Games The metric for competitive two-player games is ternary because
264   there are three final possible outcomes: win, lose, and tie. We assign scores to these outcomes: a
265   score of 1 for a win, 0 for a loss or tie. However, in some such games, the order of play (being
266   the first or second mover) can be advantageous. To account for this, we will run a game between
267   two players A and B twice, once with player A as the first mover and once as the second mover,
268   and then take the average of the scores obtained in both scenarios as the final score. This approach
269   ensures that the final score is not biased by the order of play and provides a balanced assessment of
      the player’s performance.

320   borrow the concept from the Bradley-Terry model (Hunter, 2004) and use a notion of strength to
321   unify scores across different types of games. Because the Bradley-Terry model does not account for
322   ties, we adopt the Davidson (Davidson, 1970) variant of the model. For single-player games, where
323   direct pairwise comparisons are not naturally available, we construct pairs by comparing the scores
      of every two different models. The results are illustrated in the §C.1.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
379
380   For single-player deterministic games, both the baseline strategies and the LLMs are tested on 10
381   instances, with random seeds set from 1 to 10 for reproducibility. For single-player stochastic games,
382   we run 100 instances for custom methods and open-source models, except for Llama 3.1 405B4 .
383
      For two-player games, the experiments are divided into baseline vs. LLMs and LLMs vs. LLMs.
384   In the baseline vs. LLMs part, each strategy is tested on 5 instances with random seeds from 1 to
385   5, repeated twice to alternate the first player, and evaluated on two difficulty levels. For stochastic
386   games involving custom vs. LLM matchups (except those involving Llama 3.1 405B), we increase
387   the number of random seeds to 50 to ensure statistical significance. In the LLMs vs. LLMs part, 5
388   instances are tested for deterministic games, and 50 instances are used for stochastic games. In this
389   setting, we do not vary difficulty levels.
390   To mitigate the risk of exceeding the contextual length, given the likelihood of multiple turns in our
391   games, our evaluation primarily adopts a zero-shot CoT approach. The statistics of LLMs outputs
392   for each puzzle is illustrated at §C.3.

395
396   For the single-player scenario text puzzles, we can see the results in Table 2. In the multi-turn
397   competitive two-player scenario text puzzles, Table 3 shows the results of comparing an LLM against
398   our customized method at two difficulty levels. Additionally, Figure 2 illustrates the results of
399
      LLM versus LLM competition. For text-image puzzles, Table 14 shows the result of single-player
      scenario, and Table 15 illustrates the result of competitive two-player scenario. Figure 29 illustrates
400
      the results of multi-turn competitive two-player scenario. Result statistics is provided at §C.2.
401
402
      Single-player Text Puzzles Table 2 shows that GPT-4o outperforms other models, yet all models
403
      significantly lag behind human performance, particularly in deterministic games. This disparity is
404   largely attributable to the nature of deterministic games, which typically have less freedom and more
405   stringent rules. Violating these rules often results in immediate game loss, as evidenced by the FIR
406   exceeding 50% in deterministic games. This high FIR suggests that more than half of the trials failed
407   due to illegal moves. Such results demonstrate that current LLMs, including state-of-the-art ones,
408   still struggle with puzzle comprehension and identifying legal moves, indicating limited reasoning
409   capabilities in game contexts. In contrast, stochastic games, with their less rigid rules, exhibit a
410   considerably lower FIR than deterministic games.
411
412   Competitive Two-player Text Puzzles Table 3 demonstrates that GPT-4o exhibits superior per-
413   formance, with a win rate exceeding 50% in easy-level deterministic games, while approaching 50%
414
      in the other three settings. This can be partially attributed to the higher proportion of board games in
      P UZZLE P LEX single-player scenarios, which typically have larger state spaces and a smaller frac-
415
      tion of legal states. Correspondingly, the FIR of LLMs in deterministic games significantly surpasses
416
      that in stochastic games. In stochastic games, most LLMs exhibit an FIR close to zero, indicating
417   their ability to comprehend game descriptions and adhere to system-requested output formats. How-
418   ever, their reasoning and planning capabilities for generating optimal or even legal moves remain a
419   challenge. In LLM vs. LLM settings, Claude-3.5-Sonnet demonstrates the best performance, with
420   open-source models showing comparable results to proprietary ones.
421
422   Text-image Puzzles Current LLMs perform poorly on image data. One reason is that multimodal
423   LLMs are typically trained on text-image pairs, without fine-tuning or training for multi-turn inter-
424   actions. In these puzzles, the process involves multiple turns, each combining image and text, with
425   images representing the game state. In SudokuM and SudoKillM, no model successfully completes
426   or wins any instance, primarily due to strict rules, which is the same as text puzzles. SuperplyM has
427
      looser rules, allowing illegal moves without immediate game loss, alternating between players until
      the number of illegal moves exceeds a threshold or a player achieves the winning goal. The main
428
      challenge in this game is information extraction from images; LLMs struggle to accurately extract
429
      matrix information, hindering numerical reasoning.

      4.4   A NALYSIS OF P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES
433
434   Previous research demonstrates that few-shot learning (Min et al., 2022) and advanced prompting
435   techniques, such as Tree-of-Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), can improve performance. Therefore,
436   we applied these two prompting strategies to four puzzles, one from each category: Sudoku (single-
437   player deterministic), SudoKill (competitive two-player deterministic), Exclusivity Probes (single-
438   player stochastic), and Larger Target (competitive two-player stochastic). For few-shot learning, we
439   opted for one-shot prompting due to the extensive length of gameplay, as evidenced by the average
440
      token count per game shown in the §C.3. For ToT implementation, we employed a sample strategy
      to generate five candidates for each thought step and utilized a voting strategy to evaluate states. We
441
      evaluated the performance using two representative models: GPT-4o and Qwen2-72B. The results
442
      are presented in Table 5. The data shows that both ToT and 1-shot prompting improve performance
443   in most cases, with ToT yielding greater improvements than 1-shot prompting, and GPT-4o getting
444   larger improvements than Qwen2-72B. However, these advanced prompting strategies are most ef-
445   fective when the state space is small. As the state space grows large, their impact becomes minimal.
446   This suggests that while advanced prompting techniques enhance reasoning and planning abilities
447   in small-scale games, their benefits do not scale effectively to larger, more complex scenarios.5
448
449   4.5   A NALYSIS OF R EASONING AND P LANNING A BILITIES
450
451   In Section 4.3, we observed LLMs’ limited reasoning abilities in identifying legal moves within
452
      state spaces. To explain their limitations, we investigate LLMs’ reasoning and planning capabilities
      when provided with a list of candidate legal moves. We aim to determine if LLMs can effectively
453
      reason about these moves and select optimal strategies to increase their chances of winning against
454
      opponents.
455
456   For this analysis, we focus on two games with strict rules: Sudoku and SudoKill. In Sudoku, we
457   utilize an easy-level 4 × 4 grid. For SudoKill, we limit the maximum length of the legal move list to
458
      100 and pit the LLM against a baseline strategy that randomly selects moves from the provided list.
      By supplying legal moves, we enable LLMs to concentrate on planning without the burden of move
459
      identification.
460
461   Table 4 shows that GPT-4o performs best, although all models exhibit limited reasoning and plan-
462   ning abilities even when provided with legal moves. In Sudoku, most failures stem from inadequate
463   planning; LLMs tend to randomly select legal moves from the provided list rather than employing
464
      foresight. For GPT-3.5-turbo and Mistral (8×7B), in SudoKill, they lose all games due to their in-
      ability to follow instructions. Despite being provided with a list of legal moves, they often generate
465
      moves not in the list, resulting in game losses.
466
467   Therefore, the reasoning and planning abilities of current LLMs on puzzles remain limited. This
468   limitation is evident from the high Failure Illegal Rate (FIR) in §4.3 and the low percentage of legal
469   plays on puzzles requiring a through understanding of rules, as supported by the statistics in §C.3.
470
      These challenges arise because even small mistakes can lead to significant consequences, akin to a
      ”butterfly effect,” as discussed in §A.1. Moreover, LLMs struggle to generate effective plans even
471
      when provided with candidate moves.

474
475   To assess the capabilities and limitations of current LLMs on P UZZLE P LEX, we conducted an ex-
476   tensive error analysis, based on samples of 100 runs for each text games and 50 for each text-image
477   games. Our analysis revealed four common error types (the definition of each type is demonstrated
478   at Table 16):
479
480   Reasoning and Planning Errors (63%) These are the most common errors, as previously dis-
481   cussed. They occur when models fail to identify legal moves within the state space or lack effective
482   planning to select advantageous moves. Such errors are more prevalent in games with more con-
483
      strained rules. An example of this error shows at Figure 30. In addition, we observe instances of

      faulty reasoning even when LLMs successfully solve puzzles or win against other LLMs. While
487   these models may produce solutions that adhere to the required format of the game, their reasoning
488   steps often contain errors. Among the samples we analyzed, we found that in 76% of cases where
489   LLMs successfully solved puzzles or won against other LLMs, their success was not due to genuine
490   reasoning. Instead, it was often the result of random moves (in games with loose rules) or the oppo-
491   nent making a critical mistake, such as a losing move, as illustrated in Figure 30. We also identified
492   similar patterns across most LLMs in board games. For example, in games like Sudoku, SudoKill,
493   and Takuzu, most LLMs (except for Claude-3.5-Sonnet) adopt a rigid approach. They typically fill
494   cells sequentially, starting from the first empty cell in the top-left corner and progressing row by
495
      row. This approach disregards opportunities for moves that could lead to better outcomes. For in-
      stance, in a game of Sudoku, there might be a row (not the first row) that is almost complete, missing
496
      only one cell. Reasoning through this would provide an immediate solution for that row, but only
497
      Claude-3.5-Sonnet seems capable of prioritizing such an optimal strategy. An example is illustrated
498   in Figure 32.
499
500   Comprehension Errors (12%) Because most games in P UZZLE P LEX are novel, LLMs lack prior
501   exposure to their specific corpus or strategies. This makes them ideal for testing whether LLMs
502   truly understand the rules. The outputs of several puzzles reveal that LLMs still face challenges in
503   language comprehension. Although proprietary models generally perform better, we observe that,
504   in some cases, open-sourced models demonstrate a better understanding of the rules compared to
505   proprietary models. For example, in the games of Max Target and Larger Target, only the Llama-
506   3.1-405B model recognizes that the provided bags are randomized, enabling them to exploit the
507   game mechanics. An example illustrating this error is shown in Figure 33.
508
509   Memorization Errors (11%) These errors frequently occur in multi-turn scenarios, particularly
510
      in games with less rigid rules. After several steps, LLMs may lose track of previously visited
      states, leading to repetitive actions. For example, in Exclusivity Probes, all models repeatedly revisit
511
      positions they have already explored. This results in significantly more probes being required to find
512
      all the particles. As shown in §C.3, the average number of turns in this game is 23.10.
513
514   Perception Errors (7%) These errors indicate that models perceive the wrong state, which is a
515   common issue in text-image puzzles. They typically occur when multimodal LLMs fail to accu-
516   rately extract the game state from images, resulting in incorrect reasoning based on misinterpreted
517   information.
518
519   Other Errors (7%) Other errors mainly involve failing to follow instructions, even when the
520   LLMs make legal moves. For example, in the game of Superply, which combines numerical and
521   spatial reasoning, models often focus solely on numerical reasoning. They identify a position that
522   satisfies the hint but overlook that the position is already filled. Furthermore, even after receiving
523   feedback, the models fail to recognize this mistake, leading to repeated errors, which is demonstrated
524
      at Figure 35.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   P UZZLE P LEX is a benchmark focused on reasoning in many different puzzle and game settings:
529   single-player/multi-player competitive; single-turn/multi-turn; deterministic/stochastic; and text-
530   only/text-image. As far as we know, no other benchmark on LLMs includes either multi-player
531   competitive or text-image puzzles. Multi-turn competitive games require the ability to evaluate a
532   continually updating state. Including images enables LLMs to find visual patterns that may be ob-
533
      scure in text (e.g., in Sudoku).
534   A second major feature of P UZZLE P LEX is the provision of classic game-playing and puzzle-solving
535   baseline techniques against which to compare LLMs over time.
536
      The final major feature of P UZZLE P LEX is the ability to generate instances at graduated levels of
537   difficulty, thus enabling the research community to conduct contests of increasing difficulty over
538   time as LLMs improve.

          SudoKill
977       The game is a 2-player twist on the classic Sudoku game. As in a traditional Sudoku game, you are
978       given a 9x9 grid. The goal is to fill the grid with numbers so that each row, column, and 3x3 subgrid
979       contains all numbers from 1 to 9 without repetition.
980
          In Sudokill, the additional rule for this two-player game is: Players alternate placing numbers on the
981
          board. The first player can place a number in any unoccupied space. After that, each player must
982       place their number in an unoccupied space in either the same row or column as the last move. If there
983       are no such available spaces, the player can place a number anywhere on the board. The first player to
984       make a move that violates the rules loses.
985
          The grid has some cells pre-filled with numbers. Unoccupied cells are represented by 0. The input is
986
          a 2D list of integers representing the grid, using 0-based indexing. At each turn, you should fill only
987       one empty cell.
988
989       For example, if the current grid is
990       [[6, 8, 4, 5, 1, 3, 2, 7, 9],
991       [5, 9, 7, 6, 2, 0, 1, 8, 0],
992       [2, 3, 1, 4, 8, 7, 6, 5, 0],
          [9, 1, 2, 7, 6, 4, 8, 0, 3],
993
          [4, 6, 8, 3, 0, 1, 7, 2, 5],
994       [7, 5, 3, 2, 9, 8, 4, 1, 6],
995       [8, 4, 5, 1, 3, 2, 9, 6, 7],
996       [1, 0, 6, 9, 0, 5, 0, 3, 8],
997       [3, 2, 0, 0, 7, 0, 5, 4, 0]]
998       and now is your turn and the previous move by the opponent is to fill the cell at (0, 8) with the value 9.
999       So now the cells you can place a number are [(1,8), (2,8), (8,8)] because you can only place a number
          in the same row or column as the last move.
1000
1001      For example, if the current grid is
1002
          [[6, 8, 4, 5, 1, 3, 2, 7, 9],
1003      [5, 9, 7, 6, 2, 0, 1, 8, 0],
1004      [2, 3, 1, 4, 8, 7, 6, 5, 0],
1005      [9, 1, 2, 7, 6, 4, 8, 0, 3],
1006      [4, 6, 8, 3, 0, 1, 7, 2, 5],
          [7, 5, 3, 2, 9, 8, 4, 1, 6],
1007
          [8, 4, 5, 1, 3, 2, 9, 6, 7],
1008      [1, 0, 6, 9, 0, 5, 0, 3, 8],
1009      [3, 2, 0, 0, 7, 0, 5, 4, 1]]
1010      and now is your turn and the previous move by the opponent is to fill the cell at (0, 8) with the value
1011      9. Now you can fill the cell (1, 8) with the value 4 to win this game because after you fill the cell (1,
1012      8) with the value 4, the opponent can only fill the cell (2, 8) and (1, 5), but no matter which value the
1013      opponent fills in these two cells will violate the rules.

1191      Orphan diseases affect very few people, making the development of specific drugs challenging.
1192      To treat these diseases, a combination of drugs designed for other related conditions is often used.
