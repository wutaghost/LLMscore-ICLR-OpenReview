009                                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011               Catastrophic forgetting – the phenomenon of a neural network learning a task t1
012               and losing the ability to perform it after being trained on some other task t2 – is
013               a long-standing problem for neural networks (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). We
014               present a method, Eidetic Learning, that provably solves catastrophic forgetting. A
015               network trained with Eidetic Learning – here, an EideticNet – requires no rehearsal
016
                  or replay. We consider successive discrete tasks and show how at inference time an
                  EideticNet automatically routes new instances without auxiliary task information.
017
                  An EideticNet bears a family resemblance to the sparsely-gated Mixture-of-Experts
018
                  layer Shazeer et al. (2016) in that network capacity is partitioned across tasks
019               and the network itself performs data-conditional routing. An EideticNet is easy
020               to implement and train, is efficient, and has time and space complexity linear in
021               the number of parameters. The guarantee of our method holds for normalization
022               layers of modern neural networks during both pre-training and fine-tuning. We
023               show with a variety of network architectures and sets of tasks that EideticNets are
024               immune to forgetting. While the practical benefits of EideticNets are substantial,
025               we believe they can be benefit practitioners and theorists alike. We will release the
026               code repository containing the EideticNet PyTorch framework upon publication.1

058
      Many deep neural networks are overparameterized, or have excess capacity, for a given task. This
      is evinced by their ability to minimize training loss even under a random labeling of a training set
059
      (Zhang et al., 2017; 2021). In this work, we demonstrate that excess capacity can be exploited to
060
      prevent catastrophic forgetting. We present a method, Eidetic Learning, that uses an ANN’s excess
061   capacity to guarantee that an ANN trained this way – an EideticNet – does not forget. Eidetic
062   Learning is so named because eidetic memory is perfect recall – typically of visual stimuli, although
063   Eidetic Learning’s guarantees hold for all modalities of input data. Figure 1 illustrates the correctness
064   of our method and its implementation.
065
      Eidetic Training exploits iterative pruning (Han et al., 2015): after training a task to convergence,
066
      prune neurons until training set accuracy drops below a threshold. Then freeze the unpruned neurons,
067   delete the synapses from pruned to unpruned neurons (directionally), and recycle the pruned neurons
068   for subsequent tasks. We employ structured pruning and select neurons to prune using ℓ1 or ℓ2 weight
069   magnitude pruning, or Taylor pruning (Molchanov et al., 2017).
070
      Figure 2 illustrates the architectural simplicity of EideticNets. A naive partitioning of important
071
      neurons for task ti from unimportant neurons for the task results in disjoint subnetworks and deprives
072
      tasks tj>i the opportunity to learn more efficiently by reusing features learned during ti (Figure 2a).
073   EideticNets enable this more efficient learning by allowing subsequent tasks to benefit from features
074   learned during training of previous tasks (Figure 2b)2 . There may be some value to defining an
075   EideticNet consisting of both nested and disjoint subnetworks – imagine, for instance, several nested
076   layers followed by one or more disjoint layers – but an evaluation of this possibility is out of the
077   scope of the current work.
078
      Eidetic Learning does not require rehearsal or replay to maintain or improve performance on past or
079   future tasks. However, to eliminate the need for task IDs at inference time, it trains a final task classifier
080   on a meta task dataset. The meta task dataset is constructed from all data (xit , yit ) ∀t ∈ {1, . . . , T }
081   from already-trained tasks by seteting the targets yit of each specific task t to t. At inference time, the
082   task ID is obtained from the task classifier and the appropriate classifier for the instance is invoked.
083   This is done in a single pass of the body of the network. The hidden states of the ANN’s penultimate
084   layer are passed first through the task-classification head, then through the selected classifier for the
085   instance. Alternative approaches, such as passing a new instance through each classifier head and
086   choosing one or more classifier head predictions based on e.g. entropy, may be useful. Here we solve
087
      this problem in a supervised manner.
088   A common workflow in industry is to train a deep ANN on a large number of samples from
089   some modality and domain, then transfer the copious information in its learned representations
090   to downstream tasks. Transferring to a downstream task can leave the deep ANN’s parameters
091   unchanged and instead involve training a single new layer (or “head”) atop the hidden states of the
092   deep ANN. In this scenario, the deep ANN is pre-trained on task t0 and transfer learning means
093
      training on task t1 . Optimal performance on downstream tasks sometimes requires updating the
      parameters of the deep ANN, however, which prevents parameter reuse across downstream tasks and
094
      raises the inference costs of large-scale deployments. EideticNets address this problem by enabling
095
      a new workflow: train the deep ANN and, for every downstream task ti : i ∈ {1, . . . , K} that
096   requires updating the deep ANN, train the deep ANN as an EideticNet to ensure that (1) all of its
097   learned representations are preserved on the original pre-training task, (2) the downstream tasks are
098   as accurate as possible, and (3) parameter reuse is preserved and operational expenses are minimized.
099
      The parallel between transfer learning and continual learning that we draw in the previous paragraph
100
      highlights an important distinction between EideticNets and some other approaches to catastrophic
101
      forgetting. Other approaches (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Ritter et al., 2018; Zenke et al., 2017) see the
102   scope of catastrophic forgetting as encompassing both representation space and classification space.
103   EideticNets are designed with representation space in mind and ensure that forgetting is impossible
104   in representation space. Thus, where some approaches to mitigating catastrophic forgetting have a
105   single classifier head for all tasks in their reported evaluations, EideticNets have one head per task.

172       There are numerous other benefits to EideticNets.
173
174              • Efficiency: The time and memory complexity of EideticNets is linear in the number of
175                parameters and training a task required no rehearsal or replay.
176              • Robustness: An EideticNet can be trained on significantly different subsequent tasks without
177                harming performance on existing tasks and – unlike regularization approaches to catastrophic
178                forgetting – without requiring a additional hyperparameter search just to preserve the tasks
179                for which the network has already been trained.
180              • Stability: A neuron is either important or not for the tasks trained so far and its importance
181                persists across tasks, resulting in a stable training procedure.
182              • Interpretability: For each new task, EideticNets account for the incremental amount of
183                capacity required, so calculating the amount of excess capacity remaining for any given
184                layer in the network is straightforward.
185              • Maintainability: a layer in an EideticNet can be widened to add more capacity when needed,
186                because there are no synaptic connections from unimportant neurons in layer ℓ to important
187                neurons in layer ℓ + 1.

          Notations. Define a neural network as a function fθ ∈ F : X → Z from function class F
192
          with training data (x0i , yi ) ∈ (X , Z) parameterized by θ := {Wℓ : ℓ ∈ {1, . . . , L}} with each
193
          Wℓ ∈ RNℓ ×Dℓ , where Nℓ is the number of neurons of layer ℓ and Dℓ the input dimensions. Note that
194       Dℓ = Nℓ−1 . Let σ be a pointwise function. Without loss of generality, let σ(x) = x. Consider the
195       setting in which a neural network is trained on tasks ti : i ∈ {1, . . . , K}. We denote the parameters
196       after training task tj as θtj . Let N denote the set of all neurons in an ANN and Nti the smallest set
197       of neurons necessary to perform task ti .
198
          For a neural network fθtj (xi ) to be immune to forgetting, it must guarantee the following conditions
199
          for all tasks ti and layers ℓ.
200
201
               (I) Persistence: The neurons in layer ℓ important to task ti should remain unchanged during
202                the training of any subsequent task tj>i .
203
              (II) Resistance: The neurons in layer ℓ − 1 that are not important to ti no longer affect the
204
                   ti -important neurons in layer ℓ once training of ti is complete.
205
206       For any task ti , the persistence condition preserves the weights necessary to perform the task and the
207       resistance condition ensures that the hidden states entering any layer remain the same ∀xi ∈ X . Note
208       that the resistance condition is transitive. These conditions are sufficient to guarantee the immutability
209       of fθtj (xi ) during training of any subsequent task. Catastrophic forgetting is thus solved by any
210       method that guarantees these conditions.

268   that enable EideticNets to satisfy both the persistence and resistance conditions. Starting with an
269   empty set F = ∅ for recording important neurons at each step, task ti+1 is trained with no impact on
      previously-trained task ti thus:

271             loss of accuracy.
272
             II Persistence: Update the set of frozen neurons F = F ∪ Nti .
273
274         III Let R = N \ F be the set of neurons to recycle when training ti+1 . (These neurons were
275             pruned in step I of this procedure.)
276         IV Reinitialize the neurons in R.

281   Freezing the important neurons for ti in step II ensures that EideticNets satisfy the persistence
282   condition. Before proceeding to train task ti+1 , we reinitialize the unimportant neurons that were
283   pruned in step I. To satisfy the resistance condition, we prune their synaptic connections to neurons
284   in F in downstream layers. Once pruned, those synaptic connections are guaranteed to remain so,
285   because the connections are pruned by setting to 0 an input dimension of a neuron in the frozen set F.
286   Both conditions are satisfied and EideticNets thus cannot forget.

      We now describe how EideticNets are implemented within the layers of an ANN. At the end of this
      section, we also propose an argument for why making self-attention layers of Transformers (Vaswani
291
      et al., 2017) immune to catastrophic forgetting is challenging if not impossible.
292
293
      Linear The linear layer simply applies the following operation to its input feature map x
294
295                                           Linear(x) = W x + b,                                            (1)
296
      where the bias b is optional, e.g., removed when that layer is followed by batch-normalization. During
297   training, both parameters W , b are trained. To extract excess capacity, we employ structured pruning
298   on W by zeroing-out multiple rows based on our pruning strategy. However, this is not enough to
299   ensure that subsequent training tasks will not impact the current task. In fact, we also need to ensure
300   that the remaining nonzero entries of W do not interact with already pruned units from the previous
301   layer–since those will be re-initialized and trained in subsequent tasks. As such, we also need to
302   propagate the previous layer’s pruning to the columns of W . Pruning of the bias vector b is done
303   simply by using the output unit pruning mask of W .
304
305   Convolution The convolutional layer is also a Linear layer, as in Equation (1), but with a special
306
      structure on the parameters W and (optionally) b. In particular, if the input to the convolutional
      layer is flattened as a vector x then the convolution operation makes W a circulant-block-circulant
307
      matrix. In that setting, the structured pruning and previous layer pruning propagation is applied to the
308
      channel dimensions. That is, during pruning, we select which of the output channels filters to entirely
309   prune, i.e., all the input_channels × width × height parameters for each output_channel filter. Then,
310   we also need to pruning the input_channels filters, i.e., for all the output_channels × width × height
311   parameters based on the previous layer pruning.

      In all experiments in this paper, we employed structured pruning. On each pruning iteration, we
360
      selected the same percentage of neurons in each layer to prune. The selected neurons are those with
361   the lowest score. The scoring function is fixed during a particular experiment. Neuron scores are
362   either the p-norm, p ∈ {1, 2}, or the Taylor score (Molchanov et al., 2017). To limit the time required
363   for iterative pruning, we limited the maximum number of training epochs to recover from one pass of
364   pruning to a fixed number, and we designed the recovery loop to exit when training set accuracy is
365   within the threshold. All models are trained with the nested features of Figure 2b.

477   5         C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
478
479   Aspects of our method are incompatible with some desiderata of a continual learning method.
480   Forward transfer of features allows subsequent tasks tj>i to benefit from features learned during
481   training of a previous task ti (cf. Figure 2b). Backward transfer allows previous tasks to benefit
482   from subsequently-learned features. Eidetic Learning, as presented, only supports forward transfer.
483   It can be straightforwardly extended to support backward transfer as follows: to enable backward
484   transfer from t2 to t1 , apply Eidetic Learning after training t2 to free excess neurons, then re-train t1
485   and only allow updates to the newly-freed neurons and to the t1 classifier head. This preserves the
      previously-trained neurons of t1 and allows the new neurons of t1 to benefit from the neurons of t2 .


                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Whether this requires replay using the data on which t1 was initially trained depends on whether the
487   data distribution is stationary.
488
489   In task-incremental learning, tasks are learned in a discrete sequence of phases and classes do
490
      not overlap between tasks. In class-incremental learning (CIL), classes can overlap between tasks
      (Masana et al., 2022; Gummadi et al., 2022). Eidetic Learning currently assumes a task incremental
491
      learning setting and does not address class-incremental learning. Future work may extending Eidetic
492
      Learning to support class-incremental learning and other more challenging settings in the future.
493
494   Some of the experimental setups we report results on in this paper could only be run with weight
495   magnitude pruning. We observed that Taylor pruning (Molchanov et al., 2017) does not uniformly
496
      prune across layers. It tends to prune neurons towards a network’s output first. Consequently, it’s
      possible for a task to be trained and pruned to the point of its training set accuracy falling below
497
      the stop threshold before some capacity in each layer has been pruned. This violates the principles
498
      we enumerated in Section 3. On the other hand, weight magnitude pruning of a fixed percentage
499   of neurons across all layers of a network is indiscriminate and, we believe, results in some excess
500   capacity in some layers of a network not being pruned. Future work on hybrid approaches that use
501   Taylor and weight magnitude pruning may result in more compact and more accurate networks.
502
      We have presented Eidetic Learning and demonstrated – with a variety of architectures and combi-
503
      nations of datasets – that they exploit an ANN’s excess capacity to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
504
      Once an EideticNet learns a task, it retains it perfectly. We advise the reader of inherent constraints
505   on the effective use of EideticNets. Training one to good effect requires that excess capacity exists
506   in the ANN. Excess capacity is a function of several factors, such as a neural network’s ambient
507   dimensionality and number of layers as well as the complexity of the task implied by the dataset. A
508   given neural network may have excess capacity with respect to one task ti but not to another tj . We
509   advise prudent adoption of EideticNets and hope they are of benefit in industry, academic, and other
510   contexts.
