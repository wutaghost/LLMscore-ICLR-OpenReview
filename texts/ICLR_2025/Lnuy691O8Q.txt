009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                To induce desired behaviors in large language models (LLMs) for interaction-
012                driven tasks, the instruction-tuning stage typically trains LLMs on instruction-
013                response pairs using the next-token prediction (NTP) loss. Previous work aiming
014                to improve instruction-tuning performance often emphasizes the need for higher-
015                quality supervised fine-tuning (SFT) datasets, which typically involves expensive
016                data filtering with proprietary LLMs or labor-intensive data generation by human
017                annotators. However, these approaches do not fully leverage the datasets’ intrin-
018
                   sic properties, resulting in high computational and labor costs, thereby limiting
                   scalability and performance gains. In this paper, we propose SFTMix, a novel
019
                   recipe that elevates instruction-tuning performance beyond the conventional NTP
020
                   paradigm, without the need for well-curated datasets. Observing that LLMs ex-
021                hibit uneven confidence across the semantic representation space, we argue that
022                examples with different confidence levels should play distinct roles during the
023                instruction-tuning process. Based on this insight, SFTMix leverages training dy-
024                namics to identify examples with varying confidence levels, then applies a Mixup-
025                based regularization to mitigate overfitting on confident examples while propagat-
026                ing supervision signals to improve learning on relatively unconfident ones. This
027                approach enables SFTMix to significantly outperform NTP across a wide range
028                of instruction-following and healthcare domain-specific SFT tasks, demonstrating
029
                   its adaptability to diverse LLM families and scalability to datasets of any size.
                   Comprehensive ablation studies further verify the robustness of SFTMix’s design
030
                   choices, underscoring its versatility in consistently enhancing performance across
031
                   different LLMs and datasets in broader natural language processing applications.1

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
      Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated outstanding performance across a broad
036
      spectrum of natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Zhao et al., 2023; Minaee et al., 2024). Af-
037   ter being pre-trained on large corpora of raw text, LLMs undergo a critical instruction-tuning stage
038   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) to develop their instruction-following capabilities based
039   on supervised fine-tuning (SFT) datasets, making them more suitable for interaction-driven appli-
040   cations. SFT datasets (Taori et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) typically consist of
041   instruction-response pairs spanning various task types, aligning LLMs toward desired behavior. Dur-
042   ing this stage, LLMs are usually trained through next-token prediction (NTP), where LLMs predict
043   the next token in a response given both the instruction and the preceding tokens in that response.
044   Previous research efforts in this field have predominantly focused on enhancing the quality of
045   instruction-tuning datasets. One line of research direction seeks to better understand the intrinsic
046   properties of these datasets (Kung et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) and selects informative instruction-
047   response pairs through heuristics-based filters (Zhao et al., 2024) or LLM scoring (Chen et al.,
048   2024). Another line of work generates high-quality responses by querying advanced proprietary
049   LLMs (Chen et al., 2024) or relying on human annotators (Zhou et al., 2023). However, both strate-
050   gies come with significant computational or labor costs, limiting the scalability of SFT datasets.
051   In this paper, we take a different perspective by exploring how to elevate instruction-tuning perfor-
052   mance beyond the conventional NTP training paradigm, without relying on well-curated datasets

      To address this challenge, we propose SFTMix, a novel Mixup-based recipe for language model in-
055   struction tuning. Our design builds upon the key observation that an LLM’s confidence distribution
056   over its instruction-tuning dataset is uneven across the semantic representation space. We argue that
057   data with varying confidence levels should contribute differently during the instruction-tuning pro-
058   cess. Hence, we extend data cartography (Swayamdipta et al., 2020) to the realm of causal language
059   generation as training dynamics and leverage a reference LLM to derive the confidence of each
060   instruction-response pair, based on the perplexities computed over the instruction-tuning process.
061   Using this information, we divide the original SFT dataset into a confident subset and a relatively
062   unconfident subset of equal size.
063   To guide the learning of the unconfident examples by propagating supervision signals, and to mit-
064   igate overfitting to the confident semantic regions, we design a Mixup-based (Zhang et al., 2018)
065   regularization for LLM instruction tuning. This approach explores the utility of Mixup in causal
066   language generation and improves its effectiveness by exploiting the confidence information de-
067   rived from training dynamics. Specifically, consider an instruction-response pair from the confident
068   subset and another from the unconfident subset. We interpolate them linearly at the token level in
069
      the representation space and generate a convex combination of the representations from the LLM
      under instruction tuning. The one-hot encodings of the corresponding tokens are interpolated in a
070
      similar fashion. We then compute a regularization between the interpolated encodings and represen-
071
      tations, in addition to the original NTP loss during instruction tuning. In this way, SFTMix fosters
072   the synergy between the two subsets with diverging confidence levels and enhances the interaction
073   capabilities of LLMs across diverse downstream applications.
074
075
      We demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed SFTMix recipe in both instruction-following and
      domain-specific SFT settings. In particular, SFTMix significantly surpasses the conventional NTP
076
      instruction-tuning baseline in both single- and multi-turn conversations, as measured in MT-Bench
077
      (Zheng et al., 2024) and AlpacaEval-2 (Dubois et al., 2024). This improvement is consistent across
078   different LLM families (e.g., Llama (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023)) and scales
079   of SFT datasets (e.g., Alpaca-52K (Taori et al., 2023) and UltraChat-200K (Tunstall et al., 2023)).
080   Moreover, in the healthcare domain, Llama-3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mistral-7B-v0.1 (Jiang
081   et al., 2023), instruction-tuned on MedAlpaca-263K (Han et al., 2023) using SFTMix, achieve an
082   average of 1.5% absolute increase in accuracy across four benchmarks compared to baselines. We
083   further explore the applicability of SFTMix’s variants through extensive ablations and illustrate the
084   potential of this LLM instruction-tuning recipe for broader use cases.
085   We summarize our contributions in this paper as follows:
086
087          • We introduce SFTMix, a novel recipe designed to elevate LLM instruction-tuning perfor-
088            mance beyond the conventional NTP paradigm, without relying on well-curated datasets.
089          • Motivated by the observation that LLMs exhibit varying confidence levels across the se-
090            mantic space, SFTMix leverages LLMs’ training dynamics for more insightful data inter-
091            pretation and confidence-based splitting, facilitating more effective instruction tuning.
092          • SFTMix further incorporates a Mixup-based regularization that interpolates between ex-
093            amples with different confidence levels during instruction tuning, mitigating overfitting to
094            confident examples while improving generalization on relatively unconfident ones.
095          • We demonstrate that SFTMix significantly outperforms the NTP baseline across a variety of
096            instruction-following and healthcare domain-specific SFT tasks, with consistent improve-
097            ments across different LLM families and dataset sizes.
098
             • Comprehensive ablation analysis substantiates the robustness of our design choices in SFT-
099            Mix, shedding light on its potential for broader NLP applications.

103   LLM Instruction Tuning. To align LLMs with users’ open-ended intents or adapt them to specific
104   domains, Ouyang et al. (2022) proposed instruction-tuning LLMs on human-annotated demonstra-
105   tions using supervised learning. More specifically, given a pair of instructions and desired responses,
106   the conventional NTP paradigm trains an LLM to predict each token in the response sequentially
107   during the instruction-tuning stage (Zhang et al., 2023). Jain et al. (2024) improved instruction-
      tuning performance by adding noise to the token embeddings during training, while Shi et al. (2024)


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      further suggested modeling the instructions as well. On this basis, previous work (Chiang et al.,
109   2023; Ding et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) collected instruction-
110   following datasets by distilling powerful proprietary LLMs or crowdsourcing user conversations.
111   To enhance data quality, the community has employed various techniques, including heuristic-based
112   filters (Schoch et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), LLM scoring (Chen et al., 2024), and human curation
113   (Zhou et al., 2023). Other efforts (Kung et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) have focused on gaining a
114   deeper understanding of the intrinsic properties of SFT datasets. However, acquiring high-quality
115   SFT data often entails substantial computational and labor costs. In this paper, we aim to optimize
116   data utilization through insightful data interpretation and improve the effectiveness of instruction
117
      tuning beyond the conventional NTP paradigm, without relying on well-curated datasets.
118
      Data Characterization via Training Dynamics. Data characterization (Albalak et al., 2024;
119   Wang et al., 2024) seeks to assess and analyze the quality and relevance of training data, enabling
120   more effective data filtering and elevated model performance. In particular, Swayamdipta et al.
121   (2020) leveraged the training dynamics of a pre-trained language model (Liu, 2019) to create data
122   maps, which have subsequently inspired advancements in active learning (Zhang & Plank, 2021;
123   Zhang et al., 2022; Kung et al., 2023), curriculum learning (Christopoulou et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
124   2024; Poesina et al., 2024), and dataset pruning (Chimoto et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
125   2024; Seedat et al., 2024). Here, we explore applying training dynamics to causal language gen-
126   eration by categorizing an SFT dataset into confident and relatively unconfident subsets, which
127
      facilitates the subsequent Mixup-based regularization during LLM instruction tuning.
128
      Mixup-Based Learning. To alleviate memorization and sensitivity to adversarial examples dur-
129   ing training, Zhang et al. (2018) proposed Mixup, which trains models on convex combinations of
130   pairs of input features and their corresponding labels. Its variants (Verma et al., 2019; Hendrycks
131   et al., 2020; Uddin et al., 2021; Choi et al., 2022) further suggest interpolating feature represen-
132   tations at different stages, guided by various training signals. Theoretical analyses (Zhang et al.,
133   2021; Carratino et al., 2022; Chidambaram et al., 2022; Park et al., 2022; Pinto et al., 2022) have
134   demonstrated its data-adaptive regularization and generalization effects, leading to strong out-of-
135   distribution robustness and well-calibrated uncertainty estimation. Empirical studies have further
136   validated its effectiveness under the semi-supervised learning setting (Berthelot et al., 2019; 2020;
137
      Li et al., 2020; 2022) and in diverse NLP applications (Chen et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020; Sun et al.,
      2020; Park & Caragea, 2022; Yang et al., 2022). Building on this success, we explore its utility in
138
      LLM instruction tuning and propose a Mixup-based regularization to reduce overfitting to confident
139
      examples and support the learning of relatively unconfident ones.

243   Data with Distinct Confidence Levels Should Play Different Roles during Instruction Tuning.
244   The insight from the case study motivates us to contend that data with varying confidence levels
245   should contribute differently during instruction tuning. Highly confident data points typically lie
246   further from the classification decision boundary, posing a higher risk of overfitting. In contrast, less
247
      confident data points are often closer to the boundary, making them harder to learn. To address this,
      we propose promoting the flow of supervision signals between confident and less confident regions
248
      to mitigate overfitting and enhance generalization during LLM instruction tuning. On this basis, we
249
      divide the original SFT dataset D into a confident subset Dc and a relatively unconfident subset Du
250   of equal size according to Conf (Yi | Xi ). To foster synergy between them, we design a Mixup-based
251   regularization tailored to the specific challenges of instruction tuning, detailed in the next section.
252

304   Llama (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023)) on two instruction-following datasets of
305   varying scales (i.e., Alpaca-52K (Taori et al., 2023) and UltraChat-200K (Tunstall et al., 2023)). We
306
      then evaluate the instruction-tuned LLMs on two widely-adopted benchmarks: MT-Bench (Zheng

461
462   Following the improvements of SFTMix in instruction-following and domain-specific SFT tasks,
463   we conduct extensive ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each design choice and explore
464   its impact across applications. We identify four ablation directions and summarize the results of
465
      training Llama-3.1-8B on Alpaca-52K using variants of SFTMix in Table 3.
466
467   5.1       G ENERALIZING THE T RAINING DYNAMICS FROM A W EAKER R EFERENCE LLM
468
469   Inspired by Burns et al. (2024), we investigate the generalization of training dynamics from a weaker
470   reference LLM to a stronger instruction-tuning LLM. Specifically, we identify training dynamics
471
      with a weaker reference LLM, Gemma-2B (Team et al., 2024), to divide an SFT dataset into a
      confident subset (Conf ′ ) and a relatively unconfident subset (Unconf ′ ). These subsets are then
472
      fed into the Mixup regularization ℓMixup (Conf ′ , Unconf ′ ) when instruction-tuning Llama-3.1-8B.
473
      This alternative approach yields comparable scores on MT-Bench and AlpacaEval-2 to the original
474   SFTMix recipe, which uses the same LLM for both training dynamics and Mixup-based instruction
475   tuning. This finding aligns with the weak-to-strong generalization reported by Burns et al. (2024)
476   and highlights the potential for scaling SFTMix to even stronger LLMs.
477
478
      5.2       I NCORPORATING M IXUP AS A R EGULARIZATION IS M ORE E FFECTIVE
479
480   Equation 5 uses the Mixup regularization ℓMixup to alleviate overfitting and encourage generalization.
481   To fully explore its effect, we experiment with setting µ = 1 in Equation 5 (i.e., ℓ = ℓNTP (Full) +
482   ℓMixup (Conf, Unconf)) or only minimizing ℓMixup without ℓNTP (i.e., ℓ = ℓMixup (Conf, Unconf)) dur-
483   ing instruction tuning. Table 3 shows that these two variants achieve higher scores on MT-Bench but
484   perform worse on AlpacaEval-2 compared to the baseline of using the conventional NTP method.
485   Furthermore, our SFTMix recipe, which employs ℓMixup as a regularization, still outperforms both
      variants across both benchmarks. This finding highlights the importance of incorporating the tradi-

526   6     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we propose SFTMix, a novel recipe for LLM instruction tuning. We observe that
529
      LLMs exhibit uneven confidence distributions across the semantic representation space. Based on
      this motivation, we utilize training dynamics to identify data subsets of varying confidence levels
530
      and incorporate a Mixup-based regularization. In this way, we aim to mitigate overfitting on the
531
      confident subset while propagating supervision signals to promote the generalization of the relatively
532   unconfident subset. Extensive empirical results in both instruction-following and domain-specific
533   SFT tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of SFTMix over the conventional NTP paradigm across
534   different LLM families and SFT data scales. Comprehensive ablation studies further substantiate
535   the contribution of SFTMix’s design choices, highlighting its versatility in consistently enhancing
536   performance across different LLMs and datasets in broader NLP applications. Due to computational
537   constraints, we did not apply SFTMix to LLM pre-training or instruction-tune larger LLMs using
538   this recipe. Integrating SFTMix with parameter-efficient pre-training and fine-tuning methods (Hu
539   et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2024) is another promising direction for future work.
