009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Textual adversarial attacks confuse Natural Language Processing (NLP) models,
012            such as Large Language Models (LLMs), by finely modifying the text, resulting
013            in incorrect decisions. Although existing adversarial attacks are effective, they
014            typically rely on knowing the victim model, using extensive queries, or grasping
015            training data, which limits their real-world applications. In situations where there
016            is neither knowledge of nor access to the victim model, we introduce the Free
017
               Lunch Adversarial Attack (FLA), demonstrating that attackers can successfully
               execute attacks armed only with victim texts. To prevent access to the victim
018
               model, we create a shadow dataset with publicly available pre-trained models and
019
               clustering methods as a foundation for developing substitute models. To address
020            the low attack success rate (ASR) due to insufficient information feedback, we
021            propose the hierarchical substitution model design, generating substitute models
022            that approximate the victim’s decision boundaries to enhance ASR. Concurrently,
023            we use diverse adversarial example generation, employing various attack methods
024            to reduce the frequency of model training, balancing effectiveness with efficiency.
025            Experiments with the Emotion and SST5 datasets show that the FLA outperforms
026            existing state-of-the-art methods (+10% ASR) while lowering the attack cost to
027            zero. More importantly, we discover that FLA poses a significant threat to LLMs
028
               such as Qwen2 and the GPT family, and achieves the highest ASR of 45.99% even
               without access to the API, confirming that advanced NLP models still face serious
029
               security risks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Text classification is a task in natural language processing that organizes and categorizes information
035
      by automatically assigning text content to predefined categories or labels (Fields et al., 2024).Neural
036   networks have greatly improved the performance of text classification, and these text classification
037   models have become increasingly important, especially after the popularity of large models (LLMs)
038   (Sun et al., 2023). However, textual adversarial attacks trigger significant differences in model outputs
039   by subtly modifying the text, severely affecting the security and stability of the system (Wang et al.,
040   2023; Han et al., 2024). These attacks not only reduce model accuracy, but can also lead to financial
041   losses and legal risks (Fursov et al., 2021).
042   Existing textual adversarial attacks can be categorized into white-box and black-box attacks (Baniecki
043   & Biecek, 2024). White-box attackers have full access to all model information, including architecture
044   and parameters (Lin et al., 2021). Therefore, the attacker can obtain the gradient information to
045   modify the input text to attack the model. A more practical scenario involves a black-box attack,
046   where the attacker lacks access to the internal workings of the model and can instead perform the
047   attack by either leveraging the training data or observing the model’s outputs. The first approach,
048   known as a transfer-based attack, involves using the available training data to train a substitute model
049   and exploiting the transferability of adversarial examples to attack the victim model (Kwon & Lee,
050
      2022). The second approach, referred to as a query-based attack, depends on obtaining the model’s
      output probability scores to optimize the adversarial input in a gradient-free manner. (Hu et al., 2024;
051
      Liu et al., 2024; 2023).
052
053   Existing NLP models are usually deployed on Web pages through APIs (González-Mora et al., 2023),
      and attackers cannot directly access the internal structure and parameter information of the models,

054
      making precise and efficient white-box attacks infeasible (Inkawhich et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021;
055   Yuan et al., 2022; Naseer et al., 2021; Salman et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2021). In addition, for
056   security reasons, such systems generally do not disclose detailed information about training data
057   and outputs (Li et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2019),
058   and probability scores (Liu et al., 2024), to the user, and even prevent malicious cost by limiting the
059   access frequency, thus further increasing the difficulty of black-box attacks (Hu et al., 2024). To
060   address this realistic challenge, we propose a novel attack hypothesis, i.e., can adversarial attacks still
061   be effectively realized in the acknowledge of only the victim text?
062   We propose a real-world scenario-oriented attack method called the Free Lunch Attack (FLA).
063   The attack does not require knowledge of the structure of the victim model, training data, or even
064   access to the victim model, and can be implemented relying only on the victim text. To achieve zero
065   access to the victim model, we utilize publicly available pre-trained models to transform the victim
066   text into vector representations and generate pseudo-labels through clustering to construct shadow
067   datasets to train substitute models. However, due to the incomplete accuracy of the labeling of the
068   shadow dataset, the substitute model obtained from the training is not sufficient to generate effective
069
      adversarial examples. To enhance the effectiveness of the attack, we focus on balancing attack success
      rate (ASR) and operational efficiency. The Hierarchical Substitution Model Design incrementally
070
      aligns substitute models with the victim’s decision boundaries, enhancing ASR by refining the match
071
      over multiple iterations. Additionally, our Diverse Adversarial Example Generation strategy utilizes
072   multiple attack methods to generate adversarial examples, minimizing the need for frequent retraining
073   of models. This optimized process ensures a more efficient use of resources while maintaining high
074   attack success.
075
      In the text classification task, FLA is able to easily achieve an attack success rate (ASR) of more
076
      than 40% at zero query cost, significantly undermining the predictive accuracy of the model. More
077
      importantly, FLA not only helps existing attack methods to improve the ASR by 34.99% on average
078   without additional queries, but also poses a far-reaching threat to mainstream Large Language Models
079   (LLMs), such as Qwen2, and the ChatGPT family, which can achieve up to 45.99% ASR. In addition,
080   we deeply explore the attack efficacy of FLA with very few victimized samples or severe inconsistency
081   with the training data distribution, and show that even under these extreme conditions, FLA maintains
082   an ASR more than 27.35%. This demonstrates the highly destructive and indiscriminate nature of the
083   attack against LLMs in real scenarios. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
084
085
             • We propose a novel attack scenario for text classification tasks, revealing the possibility that
               an attacker can exploit potential vulnerabilities to carry out an attack without accessing the
086
               model and without knowledge of its structure and training data.
087
088
             • We design the Free Lunch Attack (FLA) framework, which successfully achieves an efficient
               attack on the model in a completely black-box environment by constructing a shadow
089
               training set and introducing gradient diversity.
090
091          • Experiments show that even the current state-of-the-art LLMs, such as Qwen2, and the
092
               ChatGPT family, are not able to effectively defend against such attacks, highlighting the
               profound threat that FLA poses to model security.

096
097   Text White-Box Attack: In a text white-box attack, attackers exploit complete knowledge of
098   the victim model’s internal structure and parameters to optimize input text, generating adversarial
099   examples that mislead the model. For example, FD (Papernot et al., 2016b) generates adversarial
100   examples by perturbing words that have a significant impact on the model’s gradient. Similarly,
101   Hotflip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018) iteratively replaces individual words based on their importance score,
102   calculated through gradient computation. Furthermore, TextBugger (Ren et al., 2019) perturbs both
103   characters and words using a greedy algorithm to maximize the disruption of the gradient. However,
104
      the reliance of white-box attacks on full access to the model limits their applicability in real-world
      scenarios, where such information is typically unavailable.
105
106   Text Black-Box Attack: In text black-box attack, attackers generate adversarial examples without
107   access to the model’s internal architecture or parameters, relying solely on input-output observations.
      Existing black-box text adversarial attack methods can be categorized into query-based attack and

191   4     F REE L UNCH A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
192
193   To carry out the NLP model attack without access and understanding, we propose the Free Lunch
194   Attack (FLA), which creates a shadow dataset by generating cluster labels using K-means (Lloyd,
195   1982) clustering to achieve zero-access to the victim model. Next, we use the text-cluster label pairs
196   to train the substitute model. Various attack methods are applied to generate adversarial examples. If
197
      all adversarial examples fail against the substitute model, a new substitute model is trained using the
      hierarchical substitution model design process. For the successful adversarial examples, we compute
198
      their attack scores. If the maximum attack score Sig exceeds the threshold θ, the highest-scoring
199
      example is selected as the final adversarial example. Otherwise, a new substitute model, trained using
200   the hierarchical substitution model design, is required to generate additional adversarial examples.

203   4.1    C LUSTER -G UIDED S HADOW DATASET S YNTHESIS
204
      Training a substitute model without access to the original training data poses a significant challenge
205
      in adversarial settings. This section proposes a method to synthesize a shadow training dataset by
206
      assigning pseudo-labels to victim texts.
207
208   We find that the victim data and training data exhibit similar distribution characteristics, a common
209   supposition in machine learning suggesting that training and test sets follow the same distribution Zhu
210
      et al. (2019). Building on this, we hypothesize that texts eliciting strong responses from the model
      are likely akin to the training data, offering a viable proxy for estimating the training dataset.
211
212   To utilize victim texts effectively, we apply a clustering method to assign pseudo-labels to these
213   unlabeled texts, thus creating a shadow dataset. As the Figure 1 shows, each victim text xi is
214   embedded using a pre-trained model fpre , denoted as E(xi ) = fpre (xi ). All victim text embeddings
215   E = [E(x1 ), E(x2 ), . . . , E(xn )] are then clustered into distinct groups, and cluster labels yiclu are
      assigned as pseudo-labels for each text. The resulting shadow dataset D is defined as:

261   4.3   D IVERSE A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLE G ENERATION
262
263   Training substitute models can be resource-intensive, especially when repeated iterations are required
264   to generate effective adversarial examples. In each iteration, a new substitute model is trained on a
265   subset of the shadow dataset, which is progressively reduced, limiting the available data for further
266
      training. Consequently, the continuous training of new substitute models becomes less feasible and
      increasingly costly. To address this, it is essential to evaluate the necessity of training additional
267
      substitute models and explore methods that minimize the associated training costs.
268
269   This section proposes a strategy to reduce training expenses by utilizing diverse adversarial examples
      generated from multiple attack methods. Using multiple attack methods improves the chance of

531
      In this paper, we present a novel scenario called the victim texts-only scenario and the corresponding
      approach, referred to as FLA. Our study is the first to demonstrate that text adversarial attacks do
532
      not require information about the victim model, labeling, or auxiliary data. Attackers can generate
533
      adversarial examples using only the victim texts. Additionally, both few-shot and zero-shot scenarios
534   show that adversarial examples can still be generated, even with limited victim texts or minimal
535   knowledge of their attributes. To the best of our knowledge, the victim texts-only scenario represents
536   the most stringent attack scenario for adversarial examples. If an attack method is successful in this
537   scenario, it is likely to succeed in other attack scenarios as well. This is because the victim-only text
538   scenario requires the least amount of information, making it the most challenging. In contrast, other
539   scenarios provide more data, potentially making them easier to exploit. We are also very excited
      about the future applications of FLA in other modalities in future work.

      In this section, we present the details of the attack methods we used, including Bae, FD, Hotflip,
727
      PSO and TextBug Bae: Bae (BERT-based Adversarial Examples) is an advanced attack method
728   utilizing the BERT pre-trained model and prompt learning. This approach involves systematically
729   replacing words in the input text to create adversarial examples, thereby testing the robustness of
730   natural language processing models.
731
      FD: The FD (Frequency Domain) attack method replaces words with synonyms based on the gradient
732
      descent optimization of the victim model. This technique aims to subtly alter the input text to generate
733
      adversarial examples while maintaining semantic coherence.
734
735   Hotflip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018): Hotflip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018) is a text white-box attack method.
736   It iteratively replaces individual words based on their calculated importance. The importance of
737
      each word is determined by the magnitude of the gradient in the victim model, allowing for precise
      identification of the most impactful words to alter.
738
739   PSO: PSO is a text soft-label black-box attack method. It employs a versatile metaheuristic approach
740   to optimize the search space for generating adversarial examples, thereby enabling the victim model
741   to produce varied outputs. This method leverages the sememe-based representations of words to
742   effectively navigate and perturb the input text.
743   TextBug: TextBug is a versatile attack framework designed to generate adversarial text against
744   real-world applications. It operates in both white-box and black-box settings by perturbing words
745   based on their significance. In the white-box setting, it uses the Jacobian matrix to identify critical
746   words and applies various perturbations. In the black-box setting, it employs a scoring function to
747   determine word importance. TextBug is effective and efficient, preserving the text’s original utility
748   while achieving high success rates in misleading state-of-the-art NLP systems.
