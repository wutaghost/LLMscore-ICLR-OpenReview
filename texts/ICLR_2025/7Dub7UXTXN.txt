009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the implications of removing bias in ReLU networks regarding
012            their expressivity and learning dynamics. We first show that two-layer bias-free
013            ReLU networks have limited expressivity: the only odd function two-layer bias-
014            free ReLU networks can express is a linear one. We then show that, under sym-
015            metry conditions on the data, these networks have the same learning dynamics
016            as linear networks. This enables us to give analytical time-course solutions to
017            certain two-layer bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks, for the first time outside the
018
               lazy learning regime. While deep bias-free ReLU networks are more expressive
               than their two-layer counterparts, they still share a number of similarities with
019
               deep linear networks. These similarities enable us to leverage insights from linear
020
               networks to understand certain ReLU newtorks. Overall, our results show that
021            some properties previously established for bias-free ReLU networks arise due to
022            equivalence to linear networks.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Theorists make simplifications to real-world models because simplified models are mathematically
028   more tractable, yet discoveries made in them may hold in general. For instance, linear models have
029   illuminated benign overfitting (Bartlett et al., 2020) and double descent (Advani et al., 2020) in
030   practical neural networks. In this paradigm, understanding the consequences of a simplification is
031   critical, since it informs us which discoveries in simple models extend to complex ones. Here we
032   inspect a specific simplification that is common in theoretical work on ReLU networks (Zhang et al.,
033
      2019; Du et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019; Lyu & Li, 2020; Vardi & Shamir, 2021): the removal of
      the bias terms. The removal of bias not only appears in theoretical work but also has practical ap-
034
      plications. Some real-world models adopt bias removal to introduce scale invariance, which can be
035
      a beneficial property for image denoising (Mohan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022), image classi-
036   fication (Zarka et al., 2021), and diffusion models (Kadkhodaie et al., 2024). This paper seeks to
037   illuminate the implications of bias removal in ReLU networks, and so provide insight for theorists
038   on when bias removal is desirable.
039
      We investigate how removing bias affects the expressivity and the learning dynamics of ReLU net-
040
      works and identify scenarios where bias-free ReLU networks are effectively linear networks. For
041
      expressivity, we show that two-layer bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks cannot express odd functions
042   except linear functions. This was proven for input uniformly distributed on a sphere (Basri et al.,
043   2019, Theorem 2 and 4), but we prove it for arbitrary input with a simpler approach. We then con-
044   sider deep bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks and show a depth separation result, i.e., deep bias-free
045   ReLU networks can express homogeneous nonlinear odd functions while two-layer ones cannot. For
046   learning dynamics, we show that two-layer bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks have the same learning
047   dynamics as a linear network when trained on symmetric datasets with square loss or logistic loss.
048   Our symmetry Condition 3 on the dataset incorporates the datasets studied in several prior works
049   (Sarussi et al., 2021; Lyu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024). We also present two cases where two-layer
050
      bias-free ReLU networks evolve like multiple independent linear networks. Finally, we empirically
      find that when the target function is linear, deep bias-free ReLU networks form low-rank weights
051
      similar to those in deep linear networks.
052
053   By revealing regimes where bias-free ReLU networks behave like linear networks, we offer a deeper
      understanding of these networks while clarifying the importance of stepping beyond such regimes

073   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
074
075
      Basri et al. (2019) proved, using harmonic analysis, that two-layer bias-free ReLU networks can
      neither learn nor express odd nonlinear functions when input is uniformly distributed on a sphere
076
      (Basri et al., 2019, Theorem 2 and 4). We show a more general result with a simpler proof. Our
077
      Theorem 1 handles arbitrary input, includes both ReLU and leaky ReLU networks, and the proof
078   only involves rewriting the (leaky) ReLU activation function as the sum of a linear function and an
079   absolute value function.
080
081
      Lyu et al. (2021) proved two-layer bias-free leaky ReLU networks trained with logistic loss converge
      to a linear, max-margin classifier on linearly separable tasks with a data augmentation procedure.
082
      Our Theorem 7 shows that the learning dynamics of leaky ReLU networks in their setup is equivalent
083
      to that of a linear network. In light of this equivalence, their result is guaranteed given that linear
084   networks trained with logistic loss converge to the max-margin classifier on linearly separable tasks
085   (Soudry et al., 2018). In addition, we relax the assumption on the task from being linearly separable
086   to being odd, and thus identify a practical challenge: the data augmentation procedure of Lyu et al.
087   (2021) can cause the ReLU network to fail to learn a linearly non-separable task — a task the
088   network might have succeeded to learn without data augmentation.
089
      Zhang et al. (2024) found that two-layer bias-free ReLU networks have similar loss and weight
090   norm curves as linear networks when trained on datasets with zero mean Gaussian input and a
091   linear target. They reported that training the ReLU networks is about twice as slow as their linear
092   counterpart. Our Theorem 7 explains their observation: we prove that the dynamics of two-layer
093   bias-free ReLU networks is exactly twice as slow as their linear counterpart for a general class of
094   datasets, including theirs.
095
      A few other works have alluded to the connections between two-layer ReLU and linear networks.
096   Sarussi et al. (2021) discovered that two-layer bias-free leaky ReLU networks converge to a decision
097   boundary that is very close to linear when the teacher model is linear. Their theoretical results as-
098   sume that the second layer is fixed while we train all layers of the network. Saxe et al. (2022) studied
099   gated deep linear networks and found they closely approximate a two-layer bias-free ReLU network
100   trained on an XOR task. But they did not generalize the connection between gated linear networks
101   and ReLU networks beyond the XOR case. Boursier & Flammarion (2024) gave an example dataset
102   with three scalar input data points, in which two-layer bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks converge to
103   the linear, ordinary least square estimator. Holzmüller & Steinwart (2022) studied two-layer leaky
104
      ReLU networks with bias and found that they perform linear regression on certain data distributions
      because the bias fails to move far away from their initialization at zero. However, they have only
105
      dealt with one-dimensional input.
106
107   We additionally review in Appendix A two topics that are technically related to our analysis: the
      implicit bias and low-rank weights in ReLU networks.

183   We first examine the expressivity of bias-free ReLU networks. It is well known that standard ReLU
184
      networks are universal approximators (Hornik et al., 1989; Pinkus, 1999) while bias-free ReLU
      networks are not since they can only express positively homogeneous functions, i.e., g(ax) =
185

186
      any odd function except linear functions. Section 3.2 shows that deep bias-free ReLU networks are
187   more expressive than two-layer ones, but are still limited to positively homogeneous functions.

243   4.1         S YMMETRIC DATASETS

      Section 3.1 has proven that two-layer bias-free ReLU networks cannot express odd functions except
245
      linear functions. We now show that under the Condition 3 on the dataset, two-layer bias-free ReLU
246
      networks not only find a linear solution but also have the same learning dynamics as a two-layer
247   linear network.
248
      Condition 3. The dataset satisfies the following two symmetry conditions:
249
250              1. The empirical input data distribution is even: p(x) = p(−x);
251
              2. The target model is odd: y(x) = −y(−x).
252
253
      Remark 4. For infinite data, the first part of Condition 3 includes common distributions such as
      any Gaussian distribution with zero mean. For finite data, the first part of Condition 3 means that
254
      if x is present in the dataset, −x is also present. Condition 3 includes the dataset studied in Lyu
255
      et al. (2021). They considered linearly separable binary classification tasks with a data augmentation
256   procedure in which (−x, −y) is added to the dataset if (x, y) is in the dataset. We have the same
257   assumption on the input data distribution but relax the assumption on the target model from being
258   linearly separable to being odd.
259
      Assumption 5. At initialization, there exist an unit vector r such that W1 = W2⊤ r ⊤ and the
260   second-layer weight W2 has equal L2 norms for its positive and negative elements.
261
      Remark 6. Under Condition 3, the dynamics of two-layer bias-free (leaky) ReLU networks and
262
      linear networks initialized with small weights can both be approximated by a linear differential
263
      equation in the early phase of learning. Thus, the weights of the ReLU network and the weights of
264   the linear network form the same rank-one structure and the left singular vector of W1 is aligned
265   with W2 , as proven in Theorem 15. At the end of the early phase, the weights are rank-one and
266   aligned with bounded errors. To simplify the analysis, Assumption 5 assumes the weights are exactly
267   rank-one and aligned, which is justified by the initialization approaching 0. We also assume, for
268   simplicity, that the second-layer weights have equal L2 norm for their positive and negative elements,
269   which is justified by the width approaching infinity. Simulations in Figure 2b show that errors are
      small in the finite case as well. Related work (Lyu et al., 2021) studied the learning dynamics under

357
      shown in Figure 3a. We train a two-layer bias-free ReLU network on this dataset to reproduce the
      loss curve in (Boursier et al., 2022, Figure 3). We then train two two-layer linear networks on each
358
      data point separately. We find that the timing and the amount of the loss drop overlap with the loss
359
      curves of the two linear networks as shown in Figure 3b. To understand this overlap, we plot the
360   first-layer weights of the ReLU network in black arrows in Figure 3a and find that the weights align
361   with either one of the two data points. Since the two directions are orthogonal, the learning dynam-
362   ics of the two groups of neurons decouple, as derived in Appendix D. Each group of neurons evolves
363   like a linear network trained on that single data point. Hence, weights in the ReLU network evolve
364   like a linear network trained on either one of the two data points separately. The same applies to
365   learning with logistic loss, as shown in Figure 7, which was not covered in Boursier et al. (2022).
366   We observe similar behavior in the XOR-like task shown in Figure 3c, which is neither linearly
367   separable nor odd. XOR-like datasets are also a common setting in prior literature (Saxe et al.,
368   2022; Frei et al., 2023a; Meng et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Glasgow, 2024). As shown in Figure 3d,
369   we find that the loss curves of a two-layer bias-free ReLU network trained on the XOR task overlap
370   with four linear networks trained on each data point separately. In this case, the dynamics of multiple
371   linear networks well approximate that of a ReLU network, even though the ReLU network learns a
372   nonlinear function.
373   In Figures 3b and 3d, the loss curves go through multiple decreases, each corresponding to learn-
374   ing a data point. Similar behaviors were examined by Boursier et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2024) and
375   characterized as saddle-to-saddle dynamics. The connections we find between ReLU and linear
376   networks may help understand these behaviors in ReLU networks because saddle-to-saddle dynam-
377   ics has been well studied for linear networks (Saxe et al., 2014; 2019; Jacot et al., 2021; Pesme &
      Flammarion, 2023).

      Implication of Bias Removal. We studied the implications of removing bias in ReLU networks in
474   terms of the expressivity and learning dynamics. Theorem 1 shows that two-layer bias-free (leaky)
475   ReLU networks cannot express any odd functions except for linear functions. Theorem 7 shows that
476   for datasets with an even input distribution and an odd target function, two-layer bias-free (leaky)
477   ReLU networks have the same time evolution as a linear network (modulo scale factors) under ini-
478   tialization Assumption 5. We also presented examples in which the bias-free ReLU network evolves
479   like multiple independent linear networks, in Section 4.2. In these cases, comparing a bias-free
480   ReLU network with its linear counterpart provides an intuitive understanding of the behavior of
481   ReLU networks. On the flip side, the simplicity of bias-free ReLU networks suggests that ReLU
482
      networks with bias may exhibit more complicated behaviors, which are not fully addressed by stud-
      ies on bias-free networks, and remain open questions.
483

      Implicit / Simplicity Bias. Many works have studied the implicit bias or simplicity bias of two-
867   layer bias-free ReLU networks under various assumptions on the dataset. Brutzkus et al. (2018);
868   Wang et al. (2019); Lyu et al. (2021); Sarussi et al. (2021); Wang & Ma (2023) considered linearly
869   separable binary classification tasks. Phuong & Lampert (2021); Wang & Pilanci (2022); Min et al.
870   (2024) studied orthogonally separable classification (i.e., where for every pair of labeled examples
871   (xi , yi ), (xj , yj ) we have x⊤                             ⊤
                                      i xj > 0 if yi = yj and xi xj ≤ 0 if otherwise). Boursier et al.
872   (2022); Frei et al. (2023b;c); Kou et al. (2023) studied binary classification with exactly or nearly
873   orthogonal input (i.e., where x⊤  i xj = 0 if i ̸= j). Orthogonal input is a sufficient condition for linear
874   separability for binary classification tasks. Frei et al. (2023a); Meng et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024)
875
      studied XOR-like datasets. Vardi et al. (2022); Frei et al. (2023d) studied datasets with adversarial
      noise. We add to this line of research by studying a case with extreme simplicity bias, i.e., behaving
876
      like linear networks.
877
878   Low-Rank Weights. Maennel et al. (2018) is the seminal work on the low-rank weights in two-
879   layer ReLU networks trained from small initialization. They described the phenomenon as “quan-
880   tizing”, where the first layer weight vectors align with a small number of directions in the early
881
      phase of training. Luo et al. (2021) identified when two-layer bias-free ReLU networks form low-
      rank weights in terms of the initialization and the network width. Timor et al. (2023) provided cases
882
      where gradient flow on two-layer and deep ReLU networks provably minimize or not minimize the
883
      ranks of weight matrices. Frei et al. (2023c); Kou et al. (2023) computed the numerical rank of the
884   converged weights of two-layer bias-free ReLU networks for nearly orthogonal datasets, and found
885   that weights in leaky ReLU networks have rank at most two and weights in ReLU networks have
886   a numerical rank upper bounded by a constant. Chistikov et al. (2023) showed two-layer bias-free
887   ReLU networks are implicitly biased to learn the network of minimal rank under the assumption
888   that training points are correlated with the teacher neuron. Min et al. (2024); Boursier & Flam-
889   marion (2024) studied the early phase learning dynamics to understand how the low-rank weights
890   form. Petrini et al. (2022; 2023) conducted experiments on practical datasets to show that two-layer
891   bias-free ReLU networks learn sparse features, which can be detrimental and lead to overfitting. Le
892
      & Jegelka (2022) generalize the low-rank phenomenon in linear and ReLU networks to arbitrary
      non-homogeneous networks whose last few layers contain linear fully-connected and linear ResNet
893
      blocks.
