009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Video generation has many unique challenges beyond those of image generation.
012            The temporal dimension introduces extensive possible variations across frames,
013            over which consistency and continuity may be violated. In this study, we move
014            beyond evaluating simple actions and argue that generated videos should incorpo-
015            rate the emergence of new concepts and their relation transitions like in real-world
016            videos as time progresses. To assess the Temporal Compositionality of video
017            generation models, we propose TC-Bench, a benchmark of meticulously crafted
018
               text prompts, corresponding ground truth videos, and robust evaluation metrics.
               The prompts articulate the initial and final states of scenes, effectively reducing
019
               ambiguities for frame development and simplifying the assessment of transition
020
               completion. In addition, by collecting aligned real-world videos corresponding to
021            the prompts, we expand TC-Bench’s applicability from text-conditional models
022            to image-conditional ones that can perform generative frame interpolation. We
023            also develop new metrics to measure the completeness of component transitions in
024            generated videos, which demonstrate significantly higher correlations with human
025            judgments than existing metrics. Our comprehensive experimental results reveal
026            that state-of-the-art video generators achieve less than 20% of the compositional
027            changes, highlighting enormous space for improvement. Our analysis indicates that
028            current video generation models struggle to interpret descriptions of compositional
029
               changes and synthesize various components across different time steps.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Conditional video generation is the task of synthesizing realistic videos based on controlling inputs
034
      such as text prompts (text-to-video, T2V) or images (image-to-video, I2V). Significant advancement
035
      in dataset scale and model design has led to several large-scale, high-quality video generation models,
036   such as CogVideo (Hong et al., 2022), VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023a), Stable Video Diffusion
037   (Blattmann et al., 2023a), and others (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b).
038   The additional time dimension in videos makes it essential to accurately assess and benchmark the
039   alignment between the generated temporal variations and the condition inputs. While several studies
040   have proposed fine-grained and comprehensive evaluation protocols (Huang et al., 2024c; Liu et al.,
041   2024c;b; Wu et al., 2024), compositionality in the temporal dimension remains an under-addressed
042   yet crucial aspect of video generation tasks.
043   The principle of compositionality specifies how constituents are arranged and combined to make a
044   whole (Bienenstock et al., 1996; Partee, 2008; Cresswell, 2016). Ideal generative systems should
045   produce outputs that reflect the compositions described by the prompts (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
046   2023a; Dziri et al., 2024). In image generation, prior work has focused on improving faithful
047   compositionality in attributes, numbers, and spatial arrangement (Feng et al., 2022; Chatterjee et al.,
048   2024; Lee et al., 2023). In video generation, compositional faithfulness is much more challenging—
049   the output must consistently reflect the required combination of concepts, even as it changes through
050
      time. In this work, we investigate this temporal compositionality problem in video generation models
      by focusing on prompts describing scenarios where object attributes or relations change over time.
051
052   While image generation prompts involving spatial compositionality (Yu et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
053   2024b) and video prompts describing actions or motions (Huang et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2024c;
      Soomro et al., 2012; Xu et al., 2016) have been used for assessing T2V models, they have two

079   To this end, we propose Temporal Compositionality Benchmark (TC-Bench), which addresses three
080   scenarios of compositional changes: attribute transition, object relations, and background shifts.
081   We craft realistic prompts that clearly specify an object’s initial and final states, thereby requiring
082   changing compositional characteristics in a correctly synthesized video. These prompts span a wide
083   range of topics and scenes and present distinct challenges to different modules of T2V models. On
084
      the one hand, the text encoding stage needs to aggregate different groups of constituents from the
      prompt to guide the generation of different frames. On the other hand, the generation module must
085
      synthesize seamless transitions between frames while maintaining object consistency. To broaden
086
      applicability to I2V, we collect ground truth videos corresponding to the prompts, which allows us to
087   benchmark models capable of performing generative frame interpolation (Chen et al., 2023b; Xing
088   et al., 2023).
089
090
      To facilitate the use of TC-Bench, we propose two evaluation metrics, TCR and TC-Score, that first
      produce frame-level compositionality assertions and check them throughout the video using vision
091
      language models (VLMs). TCR and TC-Score measure compositional transition completion and
092
      overall text-video alignment, which are better correlated to human judgments than existing metrics.
093   We extensively benchmark multiple baselines across three categories of methods, ranging from direct
094   T2V models (Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) to
095   multi-stage T2V (Huang et al., 2024a; Lian et al., 2023) and I2V models (Chen et al., 2023b; Xing
096   et al., 2023). Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate that most of the video generation models
097   accomplish less than ∼20% of the test cases, implying enormous space for future improvement. Our
098   contribution can be summarized as three points:
099
100
              • TC-Bench, a new benchmark that characterizes temporal compositionality in video genera-
                tion. TC-Bench features different types of realistic transitions and covers a wide range of
101
                visual entities, scenes, and styles.
102
103           • We propose new metrics to evaluate transition completion and text-video alignment and
104             investigate consistency measures with various methods. Our metrics achieve much higher
105
                correlations with human judgments for evaluating temporal compositionality.
106           • A comprehensive evaluation of nine baselines shows that existing T2V and I2V methods
107             still struggle with temporal compositionality. Our in-depth analysis reveals key weaknesses
                of current methods in prompt understanding and maintaining temporal consistency.

      2.1   C ONDITIONAL V IDEO G ENERATION
111
112
      Conditional video generation has been a challenging task (Balaji et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Fu
113   et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a). Recently, with the advancement of diffusion models (Ho et al.,
114   2020; 2022) and large-scale video datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023c), video generation
115   models have gained significant improvement (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022). Several studies
116   attempt to add temporal operation layers into a pre-trained image model, such that the latter can be
117   adopted as a video generation model in a zero-shot manner (Khachatryan et al., 2023) or through
118   fine-tuning (Blattmann et al., 2023b). The idea of latent space diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) has
119   also been used in many video generation pipelines to improve the efficiency of training.
120   In T2V, Modelscope (Wang et al., 2023a) proposes spatial-temporal blocks. LaVie (Wang et al.,
121   2023b) concatenates three latent diffusion models for base video generation and spatial and temporal
122   super-resolution. Similarly, Show-1 (Zhang et al., 2023) concatenates three pixel-based and one
123   latent diffusion model. VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024) adopts a single latent diffusion
124   model and devises a technique to better use high-quality image data. For I2V generations, SEINE
125   (Chen et al., 2023b) designs a random masking mechanism. DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023)
126   proposes a dual-stream image injection paradigm. Both can generate transitions between two input
127
      frames. Currently, most of the open-sourced video generation models can only generate a video of
      2-3 seconds in one sampling sequence. Accordingly, our benchmark features temporal transitions
128
      that could reasonably happen within a few seconds as well.
129
130
131   2.2   V IDEO G ENERATION B ENCHMARKS
132
133   Many large-scale text-to-video models are evaluated on the standard UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012)
134
      and MSRVTT (Xu et al., 2016) benchmarks by reporting FVD for video quality and CLIP similarities
      for text-video alignment (Radford et al., 2021). Recently, a few benchmarks and metrics have
135
      been proposed to promote more comprehensive and fine-grained video evaluation. EvalCrafter (Liu
136
      et al., 2024b) proposes a pipeline to exhaustively evaluate four aspects of the generated videos, such
137   as text-video alignment and temporal consistency. FETV (Liu et al., 2024c) disentangles major
138   content and attribute control in prompts to achieve a fine-grained evaluation of text-video alignment.
139   VBench (Huang et al., 2024c) is another evaluation suite that adopts a unique evaluator for each
140   of the 16 dimensions. T2VScore (Wu et al., 2024) uses Large Language Models (LLM) and video
141   question answering (VQA) models to evaluate the text-video alignment. However, prompts in these
142   benchmarks underaddress any transitions in attributes or object relations. Besides, we show that
143   these metrics have marginal correlations with human ratings. In contrast, we are the first to design a
144   benchmark and metrics that specifically characterize temporal compositionally.
145
146   2.3   C OMPOSITIONALITY IN V ISUAL G ENERATION
147
148   Compositionality in image generation has been studied for years (Johnson et al., 2018; Yang et al.,
149   2022; Zeng et al., 2023). Some early studies focus on learning separable latent or pixel representations
150   for simple object generation (Andreas, 2018; Greff et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021), while recent work
151   studies more complex concepts and relations in open-domain image generation (Liu et al., 2022;
152   Feng et al., 2022; Rassin et al., 2024). There are several studies on compositions in video prediction
153
      or generation. For example, (Ye et al., 2019) factories entities in an image, predict their future states
      and then generate future frames. AG2Vid (Bar et al., 2021) generates videos of moving blocks based
154
      on action graphs and layout inputs to achieve compositionality in time. VideoComposer (Wang et al.,
155
      2024a) uses a spatial-temporal condition encoder for sketch or motion inputs. Several other studies
156   use LLMs to generate layouts or frame-wise text guidance (Huang et al., 2024a; Lin et al., 2023;
157   Lian et al., 2023). Another concurrent work, VideoTetris (Tian et al., 2024), addresses multi-object
158   scenes and long-range video transitions by applying spatial-temporal composing techniques. While
159   more and more studies have started to address composition changes in video generation, there lacks
160   a unified, standard, and challenging benchmark for such aspects. Previously in image generation
161   evaluation, some metrics (Huang et al., 2024b; Saxon et al., 2024) have relied on the visual question
      answering (Hu et al., 2023; Singh & Zheng, 2023; Cho et al., 2023) or image captioning (Lu et al.,

187   3     TC-B ENCH
188
189   Our Temporal Compositionality Benchmark (TC-Bench) consists of prompts following a well-
190   defined scene graph space and ground truth videos. We first define three categories of temporal
191
      compositionality in Sec. 3.1 and then describe how we collect the samples in Sec. 3.2.
192
193   3.1   T EMPORAL C OMPOSITIONALITY P ROMPTS
194
      Given that oi denotes an object, ai denotes an attribute, and → denotes a binding relation, a1 → o1
195   means that o1 has the attribute a1 , while o1 → o2 means that o1 and o2 are interacting with each other.
196   A scene st at time t can be represented as a combination of these elements, i.e., st = {a1 , o1 , . . .}.
197   Then, we can define three types of scenarios as shown in Fig. 2:
198
      Attribute Transition: s0 = {o1 , a1 |o1 ← a1 } ⇒ sT = {o1 , a2 |o1 ← a2 } means that an object’s
199
      attribute changes from a1 at t = 0 to a different one a2 at the end t = T . A typical example is shown
200
      in Fig. 2 (top), where a chameleon’s skin turns from pink to green. Prompts in this category cover a
201   wide range of different attributes, including color, shape, material, and texture.
202
203   Object Relation Change: s0 = {o1 , o2 , o3 |o1 → o2 } ⇒ sT = {o1 , o2 , o3 |o1 → o3 } indicates that
204
      an object o1 interacts with different objects due to motions like passing or hitting. Fig. 2 (middle)
      illustrates an example where a basketball (o1 ) is passed from the right hand (o2 ) to the left hand (o3 ).
205
206   Background Shifts: s0 = {o1 , o2 , a1 |o2 ← a1 } ⇒ sT = {o1 , o2 , a2 |o2 ← a2 } is similar to attribute
207   transition but the transition takes place on an object or scene o2 . o1 serves as a distractor to challenge
208   models on frame consistency while generating dynamics. For instance, in Fig. 2 bottom, the cityscape
209   remains static while the sky changes from sunset to evening.
210   For simplicity, we neglect other possible nodes or edges and only focus on single transition events
211   that could possibly happen within a short time from one second to around ten seconds.
212

253
      alignment for both T2V and I2V models (Fig. 3 left). Then, we investigate four approaches to

      4.1        A SSERTION -BASED E VALUATION
257
      Denote a text input P and a video V = {I1 , . . . , IK } consisting of K frames. We use GPT-4 to
258   generate N index-assertion pairs {(Ki , Ai )} where Ki consists of up to 5 different frame indices
259   used to retrieve frames from V to examine the assertion Ai . Without constraints, generating K and Ai
260   simultaneously can lead to unreasonable assertions. Therefore, we indicate that Ai should cover three
261   dimensions: transition completion (Scomp ), transition object consistency (Scons ), and other objects
262   (Sother ). We provide a few in-context exemplars so that the LLM can follow the same format. More
263   details about these dimensions are explained in Appendix C.
264   To verify each assertion Ai , we input Ai and the corresponding video frames Ii = {Ik |k ∈ Ki } to a
265   VLM (Achiam et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a) fVLM . The VLM produces a response
266   fVLM (Ii , Ai ) ∈ {Yes, No}, indicating whether the assertion Ai is verified. When Ki contains more
267   than one index, we concatenate the frames horizontally as one image feeding into the VLM. We
268   empirically observe that the combined image input is more reliable than sequential image inputs for
269   TC-Bench evaluation, contrary to the findings of some recent work (Wang et al., 2024b). A transition
      is completed if all Ai from transition completion and consistency are verified. Therefore, we define

304   5     M ETHOD
305
306   We introduce a simple and effective baseline to improve the transition completion rate over text-
307   to-video generation models. Based on the prompt P , we first instruct an LLM to generate the text
308
      description of the initial scene P0 and the end scene PK . Then we utilize a diffusion-based text-to-
      image generation model ft→i to generate the start and end frame I1 , IK . However, simply using
309
      P0 , PK to guide the generation process overlooks the consistency across the frames. Therefore, we
310
      apply the same noise map zT as the initialized noise pattern of both diffusion paths and substitute the
311   self-attention maps of IK with maps from I0 ’s diffusion trajectory for the first half of the timesteps.
312   As P0 and PK share similar semantics except in some attributes or object positions, we discover
313   that such a simple method can end up with I1 and IK sharing similar image structures. Then, the
314   generated frames are used to guide the process of video generation so that the temporal transition can
315   be completed under the guidance of Ik . We use an off-the-shelf video generation model SEINE (Chen
316   et al., 2023b) for the generative transition from I1 to IK . We refer to this baseline as SDXL+SEINE
317   as we adopt SDXL (Podell et al., 2023) for start and end frame generation.

362   Metrics For the proposed TCR and TC-Score, we adopt GPT-4 Turbo, CogVLM2-19B, and LLaVA-
363   NeXT-7B to assess all the assertions. The results reported in the main paper are based on GPT-4
364   Turbo, and the other results are reported in Appendix A and Table 6 and 7. In comparison, we
365   consider four commonly used or recent text-video alignment metrics. CLIP score (Radford et al.,
366   2021) measures the average text-frame similarity. ViCLIP (Wang et al., 2023c) encodes video and
367
      text as two separate feature vectors, which can be used to compute cosine similarity as the text-video
      alignment score (Huang et al., 2024c). EvalCrafter (Liu et al., 2024b) computes a weighted sum of
368
      many different metrics, but we only adopt the sum of CLIP score, SD score, and BLIP-BLEU since
369
      these metrics are agnostic to prompt content and structure. Finally, UMTScore (Liu et al., 2024c)
370   uses the video-text matching score from a fine-tuned UMT (Li et al., 2023b), an advanced video
371   foundation model. We also collect human ratings with a 5-point Likert scale to compute correlations
372   with these automatic metrics.

379   Gen-3 Alpha demonstrates apparent superiority in background transitions with a 27.45% TCR, while

391   underperforms in object relation because T2I models struggle to control object positions in two
392   diffusion paths of similar structures. The results suggest the necessity of fundamentally addressing

      the gap between video and text features in the latent space to tackle TC-Bench.

415   6.4   A NALYSIS
416
417   Temporal Compositionality We demonstrate temporal compositionality in the generated videos
418   by visualizing the existence of attributes a1 , a2 at different time steps. Specifically, we compute the
419   CLIP similarity between each frame and captions “a a1 o1 ” to obtain Fig. 5 (a), and captions “a a2
420   o1 ” to obtain Fig. 5 (b). For instance, if the video prompt is “a pink chameleon turns green”, then
421
      the two captions are “a pink chameleon” and “a green chameleon” respectively. The similarity to
      a1 should decrease while the similarity to a2 should increase as the frame index increases. The flat
422
      curves of T2V models indicate that they fail to generate the disappearance of a1 or the emergence of
423
      a2 as time proceeds, which aligns with the case in Fig. 4. In contrast, SEINE and DynamiCrafter
424   align well with the trend of ground truth videos.
425
426   Frame Consistency Despite the fact that I2V models align with the trend of ground truth videos in
427   Fig. 5 (a)-(b), they suffer from more severe consistency issues than T2V models. Fig. 5 (c) shows the
428   CLIP similarity between two consecutive frames. I2V models are generally weaker than T2V models
429   in frame consistency, especially the consistency between the start and end frames (input conditions)
430   and their neighboring frames (model outputs). For T2V models, chasing higher consistency scores
431   does not help achieve temporal compositionality, as most transitions cannot be completed. Therefore,
      we argue that it is only necessary to compute consistency for I2V models as in Eq. 4.

516      6.5                   H UMAN E VALUATION
517
        We compute Kendall and Spearman’s rank correlations to show that our proposed metrics align
518
        with human judgments. We collect two ratings for each video where the first one only considers
519
        transition completion and the other one considers overall text-video alignment (details in Appendix
520     D). As is shown in Table 3, our metrics achieve much higher correlations compared to existing
521     metrics in both aspects. The results verify the effectiveness of our metrics for evaluating temporal
522     compositionality. Despite being widely adopted in existing studies, averaged text-frame CLIP
523     similarity is unreliable and often outputs low scores for videos that complete the transitions. The
524     results are intuitive as the training text samples for CLIP describe static images instead of transitions
525     or motions, lacking awareness of compositional change across timesteps. In addition, we find that
526     advanced text-video alignment models like ViCLIP and UMTScore are still weak in understanding
527     temporal compositionality, leading to low correlations.

         7                 C ONCLUSION
530
531
         In this work, we propose a new video generation benchmark TC-Bench, featuring temporal compo-
532
         sitionality. TC-Bench characterizes three different types and a wide range of topics. We show that
533
         simple transitions that can happen in several seconds remain extremely challenging to existing T2V
534      methods. We also propose assertion-based evaluation metrics and investigate consistency evaluation
535      using flow-based methods or latent features. Our benchmark, experimental results, and analysis unveil
536      the weaknesses of existing T2V and I2V models in temporal compositionality, suggesting crucial
537      directions for future improvement. Future work should investigate techniques to 1) automatically
538      mine videos with specific temporal compositionality and generate detailed captions, 2) evaluate
539      text-video alignment more efficiently, and 3) improve text-to-video models in addressing temporal
         compositionality.

822   This section provides the details of the prompts for generating prompts in TC-Bench using ChatGPT.
823
      We start with general instructions on the desired structure and format of temporal compositionality
      prompts, followed by several manually written examples. The text prompts and metadata of TC-Bench
824
      are available at this link and also at the project website.

826
      Attribute Transition We explicitly ask ChatGPT to imagine scenarios where the attribute (including
827   lighting, color, material, shape, and texture) of a certain object changes and then generates the
828   corresponding prompts. Generate some concise prompts that describe scenarios where an object’s
829   attribute, such as lighting, color, material, shape, or texture, changes as time proceeds. The prompt
830   should describe transitions that could happen within a few seconds in a video. The described
831   transition should also be realistic and could happen in the real world. Here are some examples:
832
      A chameleon’s skin changes from brown to bright green.
833
834   A leaf changing color from vibrant green to rich autumn red.
835   A car transitioning from silver to matte black.
836
837   Object Relation Change We first describe the idea of object binding and then instruct ChatGPT
838   to generate prompts that describe transitions in the binding relations. We also prompt ChatGPT to
839   consider many different subjects as it is biased towards mentioning human occupations. Generate
840   some concise prompts that describe scenarios where objects’ binding relations change due to some
841   actions or motions. Two objects are bound to each other if they are physically interacting with
842   each other. For example, in “a man passes a ball from left hand to right hand” the ball is bound to
843   the man’s left hand at first. Then, the binding relation changes from ball and left hand to ball and
844
      right hand. The prompt should describe motions that could happen within a few seconds in a video.
      Consider a wide range of subjects not limited to humans or one’s occupation, such as animals or
845
      common objects. Here are more examples:
846
847   A man picking an apple from a tree and placing it in a basket.
848   A bird picking up a twig and placing it in its nest.
849
850   A child placing a toy car on a toy track.
851
852   Background Shifts is similar to attribute transition in prompting. The major difference is that
853
      we clarify that the transition takes place on a background scene or object, with a foreground object
      serving as the distractor. Generate some concise prompts that describe scenarios where a foreground
854
      object remains relatively static and the background changes as time proceeds. The prompt should
855
      describe transitions that could happen within a few seconds in a video, whether it is a normal-speed
856   video or a timelapse video. Here are some examples:
857
858   A cityscape transitioning from day to night.
859   A forest changing from summer greenery to autumn foliage.
860
      A bench by a lake from foggy morning to sunny afternoon.

1100
1101   One limitation of our work is the discrepancy between our proposed metrics and human ratings.
1102   While TCR and TC-Score both demonstrate much higher ranking correlations with human judgments,
1103   there is still a need for having even more reliable and robust metrics for temporal compositionality.
1104   Our proposed evaluation metrics are not perfect. For example, VLMs still struggle with multi-image
1105   understanding. Besides, we rely on image-based assertion because strong video foundational models
1106   are lacking. To the best of our knowledge, temporal compositionality is still challenging in the
1107   context of video understanding. Therefore, future work could devise end-to-end video-based metrics
1108   when such a stronger VLM is available. In terms of potential social impacts, TC-Bench users and
1109
       researchers should be aware of the potential abuse of text-to-video models. Hallucination issues and
       biases of generated videos should also be addressed. Future research should exercise caution when
1110
       working with generated videos using TC-Bench prompts and ground truth videos.
