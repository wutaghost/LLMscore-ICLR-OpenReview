009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                In light of recent privacy regulations, machine unlearning has attracted significant
012                attention in the research community. However, current studies predominantly as-
013                sess the overall success of unlearning approaches, overlooking the varying dif-
014                ficulty of unlearning individual training samples. As a result, the broader feasi-
015                bility of machine unlearning remains under-explored. This paper presents a set of
016
                   novel metrics for quantifying the difficulty of unlearning by jointly considering the
                   properties of target model and data distribution. Specifically, we propose several
017
                   heuristics to assess the conditions necessary for a successful unlearning operation,
018
                   examine the variations in unlearning difficulty across different training samples,
019                and present a ranking mechanism to identify the most challenging samples to un-
020                learn. We highlight the effectiveness of the Kernelized Stein Discrepancy (KSD),
021                a parameterized kernel function tailored to each model and dataset, as a heuristic
022                for evaluating unlearning difficulty. Our approach is validated through multiple
023                classification tasks and established machine unlearning algorithms, demonstrat-
024                ing the practical feasibility of unlearning operations across diverse scenarios.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Machine Unlearning (MU) (Cao & Yang, 2015) refers to a process that enables machine learning
029   (ML) models to remove specific training data and revert corresponding data influence on the trained
030   models while preserving the models’ generalization. As many countries and territories have promul-
031   gated their Right to be Forgotten regulations 1 , entitling individuals to revoke their authorization to
032   use their data for machine learning (ML) model training, the demand of MU raised significant inter-
033   est in the machine learning research community, leading to various types of unlearning approaches,
034   often achieved by either data reorganization (Graves et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2021; Tarun et al.,
035   2023) or model manipulation (Guo et al., 2020; Warnecke et al., 2021).
036   Although existing machine unlearning studies vary based on diverse theoretical foundations, their
037   performance evaluation metrics used are generally common, including 1) Data Erasure Complete-
038   ness, 2) Unlearning Time Efficiency, 3) Resource Consumption, and 4) Privacy Preservation (Xu
039   et al., 2024; Yang & Zhao, 2023; Shaik et al., 2023). While such quantitative evaluations may suf-
040   fice for comparing MU methods from various perspectives, they often fall short in assessing the
041   effectiveness of data removal requests for individual data points, resulting in a discrepancy between
042   actual unlearning outcomes and expectations in real-world applications. In practice, some data
043
      points are inherently harder to unlearn than others, where such variability may stem from intrinsic
      factors, such as the augmented data distribution under a trained machine learning model, regardless
044
      of the specific unlearning algorithm applied.
045
046   While quantifying the difficulty of unlearning operations (Marchant et al., 2022; Pawelczyk et al.,
047   2024) is underexplored, one may come up with a handy metric constructed on top of several obvious
048   factors, such as model complexity, prediction confidence, and the nature of input data features.
049
      Indeed, the most straightforward criterion of success of unlearning is to measure the distance of the
      data sample from the model’s decision boundaries (Liu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023); data points
050
      closer to the decision boundary are more likely to be forget-able. Unfortunately, we show in this
051
      paper that although the factors above have a certain degree of positive correlation with the difficulty
052   of unlearning, they are often accompanied by noise, leaving them lacking credibility.

      This paper paves the first step on understanding the feasibility of machine unlearning (regardless of
055   specific unlearning approaches) by analyzing and quantifying the difficulty of unlearning the training
056   data points on a trained model, such that identifying the criteria contributing to this process. Instead
057   of falling back to predictive confidence-based approaches (Liu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023), we
058   propose to measure the difficulty of unlearning through model-augmented data distribution. Specif-
059   ically, we show that trained ML models can uniquely define parameterized kernel functions kθ (·, ·)
060   over training data points, which allow to express the distribution of training samples conditioned on
061   trained model fθ in the form of a correlation graph. Due to the generalization of machine learning
062   models, training samples that have strong connections with other samples may face the challenge
063
      of unlearning. While there are many choices of parameterized kernel functions, we show that the
      kernel defined on Kernelized Stein Discrepancy (KSD) (Liu et al., 2016; Chwialkowski et al., 2016)
064
      shows a unique advantage in terms of accurately reflecting a trained model’s characteristics given its
065
      training samples. By analyzing the property of KSD, we show that there is a set of reliable scoring
066   metrics based on KSD that can efficiently measure the difficulty of unlearning. With the proposed
067   evaluation metrics, one may reduce unnecessary machine unlearning operations when data points
068   are determined to be infeasible to unlearn.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
072
      2.1    M ACHINE U NLEARNING D EFINITION
073
074   Machine Unlearning (MU) is a process of removing specific subsets of training data (along with
075   their influence) from a trained model (Cao & Yang, 2015; Bourtoule et al., 2021).
076
077
      Formally, consider a training dataset Dt = {(xi , yi )} (t is refering to training data) comprising
      n samples, where xi and yi represent the ith data’s features and corresponding label respectively.
078
      We define two subsets of the dataset for clarity as follows: Let Df ⊆ Dt denote the subset of data
079
      designated to be forgotten (a.k.a forget set), and Dr ⊆ Dt denote the remaining data (a.k.a remaining
080   set), such that Df ∪ Dr = Dt and Df ∩ Dr = ∅.
081
082   Given an target predictive model fθ with parameters θ, the common expectation of machine unlearn-
083
      ing operation are of adjusting θ to a modified parameter set ϑ such that:
084
            • Increasing of model’s error on the forget set Lϑ (Df ).
085
086         • Maintaining original model’s error on the remaining set Dr such that
087                                            ∥Lθ (Dr ) − Lϑ (Dr )∥ < ϵ,                                 (1)
088
089           where ϵ denotes a tolerable performance degradation threshold and L denotes the loss.
090
091   2.2    R ESEARCH T RACK OF M ACHINE U NLEARNING
092
093
      The simplest solution for unlearning is to retrain the model from scratch with the remaining data
      after removing the forget data. However, even with tricks such as partial retraining (Bourtoule
094
      et al., 2021), that works on decomposible, partial model components, retraining remained resource-
095
      intensive. To mitigate the computational overhead of retraining, unlearning operations are often
096   approximated. In particular, Fine-Tuning based approaches (Warnecke et al., 2021; Golatkar et al.,
097   2020) suggest to continue training the model on the remaining data such that forget data can be
098   naturally flashed out. Alternatively, Gradient Ascent approach (Graves et al., 2021) (referred as
099   NegGrad in (Kurmanji et al., 2024)) adjust the model’s weights in the direction of the gradient to
100   increase the model’s error on the data intended for forgetting.
101
      Recent studies have frequently utilized the Newton update as a fundamental step for removing data
102   influence (Guo et al., 2020; Golatkar et al., 2020; Peste et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021). These
103   methods typically leverage the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to gauge the sensitivity of the
104   model’s output to perturbations in its parameters. For example, Fisher Forgetting (Golatkar et al.,
105   2020) employs a scrubbing approach where noise is added to parameters based on their relative im-
106   portance in distinguishing the forget set from the remaining data set. Mehta et al. (Mehta et al.,
107   2022) employs conditional independence coefficient to identify sufficient sets of parameters for tar-
      geted unlearning.

108
      As machine unlearning often involve privacy protection regulations, some methods also incorporated
109   the principles of differential privacy (DP) (Abadi et al., 2016) to ensure the unlearning outcome does
110   not inadvertently reveal information about the data that has been removed. Izzo (Izzo et al., 2021;
111   Zhang et al., 2024) adhere to the DP framework to ensure a high probabilistic similarity between
112   models before and after unlearning. Golatkar et al. (2020) introduced the concept of certified un-
113   learning, grounded in information theory and specifically tailored to the Fisher Information Matrix.
114   Certified Minimax Unlearning (Liu et al., 2023a) has developed an algorithm specifically for mini-
115   max models. This method removes data influences through a total Hessian update and incorporates
116   the Gaussian Mechanism to achieve (ϵ, δ)-minimax unlearning certification, ensuring a balance be-
117
      tween data removal and model integrity. (Chourasia & Shah, 2023) propose a data deletion technique
      that ensures the privacy of deleted records. (Ullah et al., 2021) investigated the machine unlearning
118
      in the context of SGD and streaming removal requests, but ensured that their method is differentially
119
      private. DP algorithms provide the upper bound for the unlearning scheme, but they don’t guarantee
120   the full unlearning of requested data(Nguyen et al., 2022).
121
122   Other research efforts extend into various domains of unlearning, such as knowledge distillation
123
      (Chundawat et al., 2023), selective forgetting for lifelong learning (Shibata et al., 2021), federated
      unlearning (Che et al., 2023), online unlearning (Li et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2019), and exploring
124
      adversarial attacks using machine unlearning methods (Wei et al., 2023; Di et al., 2022). Instead of
125
      aiming to follow privacy protection regulation, those approaches focus on reducing the vulnerability
126   of models to adversarial attacks by forgetting training examples (Jagielski et al., 2023).
127
128   The majority of the literature on machine unlearning primarily concentrates on the development of
129
      unlearning algorithms or unlearning approximation techniques for selectively forgetting data from a
      trained model. As such, the corresponding evaluation metrics are designed to favor the performance
130
      difference between algorithms on highly aggregated level (e.g. success rate). In fact, a common
131
      assumption underlying much of this research is that unlearning operations are universally feasible
132   for all data points within a dataset, where effectiveness of unlearning will behave consistently across
133   different datasets. This assumption often overlooks the potential variability in unlearning efficacy
134   due to differences in data characteristics or model dependencies, suggesting a need for more nu-
135   anced studies that evaluate the specific conditions under which MU can be effectively implemented.
136   Recently, one line work (Thudi et al., 2022) has questioned the performance of unlearning approx-
137   imation algorithms w.r.t the exact unlearning and whether or not these methods can successfully
138   prove the absence of certain data points during training. From this recent research, we can observe
139   that absence of a comprehensive study that comprehensively investigates the feasibility of unlearning
140
      was present.

142   3   FACTORS THAT A FFECT D IFFICULTY OF U NLEARNING
143
144   In this section, we summarize the main factors that impact effectiveness of machine unlearning with
145   corresponding data analysis and intuition justifications.
146   Intuitively, to quantify success of unlearning operation, one may evaluate the quality of the unlearned
147   model ϑ with respect to the common expectation mentioned in previous section. Particularly, when
148   the forget set contains a single sample (xf , yf ), we expect the prediction of the unlearned model
149   on that data point to be same as of a model trained without the data point (a.k.a Certified Unlearn-
150   ing (Guo et al., 2020)), reflecting the model’s ignorance to forgotten example. However, it is often
151   impractical to verify the success of unlearning by comparing to a re-trained model; such comparison
152   lose the practical value of unlearning. Hence, evaluation criteria of successful unlearning operation
153   in practice often falls back to measuring 1) the shift of prediction (e.g. altering predicted classifica-
154
      tion label) along with 2) the model performance preservation criterion (See Eq. 1). Unfortunately,
      the two criteria mentioned above are often a pair of trade-off, resulting the following factors that
155
      jointly pose challenges for defining the difficulty of unlearning.
156
157   1. Size of Unlearning Expansion: Altering prediction outcome of target sample may negatively
158      impact model prediction on similar samples. When a guaranteed unlearning is desired, one might
159      need to expand unlearning operation to a broader training sample set (the similar data samples)
160      such that unlearning of target sample with respect to decision shift can be successful (Chen et al.,
161      2023). While selection of unlearning algorithm may vary the size of the expansion, the need of
         expanding forget set for guaranteed unlearning remains consistent.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2. Resistance to Membership Inference Attack (MIA): In MIA (Chen et al., 2021; Golatkar
163      et al., 2021; Song et al., 2019), adversaries use the model’s outputs, such as confidence scores,
164      to ascertain if a specific data point was part of the training set, without needing direct access to
165      the model’s internal parameters. With identical unlearning operations (e.g. number of unlearn-
166      ing step, learning rate, and other hyper-parameters), data points often show different level of
167      resistance to the MIA, which is an alternative factor of measuring the difficulty of unlearning.
168   3. Geometric Distance to Decision Boundary: The distance of a data point to the decision
169      boundary of a model often closely associated with the predictive confidence. A data point with
170      lower predictive confidence (larger uncertainty) is closer to the decision boundary (Nguyen &
171      Smeulders, 2004; Li et al., 2023). A data with higher uncertainty might be easier to unlearn
172      compared to those with high predictive confidence (Chen et al., 2023). However, predictive
173      confidence does not account for the relationships among similar examples. A data point with
174      low predictive confidence might be deeply embedded within a complex region of the decision
175
         boundary (Kienitz et al., 2022), requiring significant modifications to unlearn. Hence, it remains
         to be a noisy index.
176
177   4. Tolerance of Performance Shift: For an easily unlearn-able data point, guaranteed unlearning
178      is achievable with minimal impact on the model’s predictive performance (Liu et al., 2024a).
179
         Here the model performance measurement is on remaining, and test dataset rather than remain-
         ing training dataset (as given in Equation 1). Conversely, an data point that is hard to unlearn
180
         would require significant changes to the model parameters, leading to a substantial deviation in
181
         performance metrics.
182
183
      5. Number of Unlearning Steps: Number of Unlearning Steps evaluates the computational effi-
         ciency of the unlearning operations, indicating how quickly the model can be updated to forget
184
         a specified data. For a given unlearning algorithm, the metric can be approximated through wall
185
         clock duration (Nguyen et al., 2022).
186
      6. Distance of Parameter Shift (DPS): Instead of using model performance changes as heuristic,
187
         DPS directly probe the layer-wise and activation-wise parameter shift distances. Layer-wise
188
         distance measures the weight differences between the unlearned and original models, while
189      activation-wise distance assesses their activation differences given the same input (Golatkar
190      et al., 2020; Tarun et al., 2023).
191
192   With the identified unlearning feasibility factors that affect difficulty of unlearning, one may seek
193   for a coherent scoring systems that can rank training data points from the most easy to the most
194   challenging for unlearning operations, as we will discuss in the next section.
195
196   4     S CORING U NLEARNING D IFFICULTY
197
198   We summerize the unlearning difficulty factors into two major groups namely 1) data points
199   with/without strong ties (factor 1, 4-6) and 2) predictive confidence (factor 2-3). Our aim is to
200   develop a unlearning difficulty scoring metric that jointly considers these two classes of factors.
201
      Before moving forward, we briefly describe the Kernelized Stein Discrepancy (KSD) (Liu et al.,
202
      2016; Chwialkowski et al., 2016) to facilitate the later discription.

      learned model on the remaining, forget and test subsets. Additionally, we calculated the layer-wise

355   (MIA-Correctness) to ascertain how many samples from the forgetting set were correctly classified
356   as non-training samples. We assessed the MIA-efficacy using a confidence-based attack method
357   (Song et al., 2019). The efficacy of MIA is quantified by the ratio of samples predicted as ”forgotten
358   samples” (True Negatives TN) to the total number of samples in the forgetting set |Df |. Ideally, post-
359   unlearning, the model θu should have effectively ”forgotten” the information related to the samples
360   in forgetting set.
361   Additional information about experimental setup and hyper-parameter configurations are given in
362   Appendix (see Table 5).

411
412    To further validate the effectiveness of the difficulty scoring metrics, we conducted experiments to
413    demonstrate the differences between unlearning easy and difficult samples in the context of guaran-
414
       teed unlearning. Specifically, in these experiments, we extended the unlearning operation to similar
       samples (based on the Stein kernel value) such that the sample have higher chance to be successfully
415
       unlearned. Intuitively, a data sample that requires fewer similar samples (smaller expanded removal
416
       set) to be successfully unlearned is considered easier to unlearn, which serves as ground-truth (re-
417    flecting feasibility factor described in Section 3: Size of Unlearning Expansion). Figure 2 shows the
418    results; The left-hand side of the figures show the results of “easy” samples ranked by EMSKSD,
419    whereas the right-hand side highlights ”difficult” samples ranked by the same scoring metric. From
420    the figure, we observed that the “easy” samples can often be successfully unlearned without much
421    removal set expansion. In contrast, the “difficult” samples are either requiring larger removal set
422    expansion or destroying model’s generalization (as shown in green bar). The observation reflects
423    our intuition of unlearning “easy” and “difficult” samples in practice, further showing the scoring
424    metrics proposed providing correct guidance on distinguishing samples’ difficulty of unlearning.
425    A side knowledge we learned from this experiment is that monotonically expanding the removal set
426    is not necessarily helpful in terms of increasing the chance of successful unlearning; the operation
427    may break the model with larger set size more than needed.

477
      Now, we revisit the research questions that we aimed to answer in our empirical study and evaluate
478
      if KSD-based scoring metrics can provide guidance on the difficulty of machine unlearning.
479
480   Q1:The accuracy on the forget set reveal that, for certain unlearning algorithms, the model still
481   correctly predicts the class label of unlearned datapoints, despite that the model’s error on those
482
      particular datapoints has increased. The comparison between the unlearned model’s error between
      difficult and easy datapoints shows a significant gap, indicating that the model struggles more with
483
      difficult-to-unlearn samples.
484
485   Q2: EMSKSD demonstrates stronger predictive alignment with the actual unlearning outcome, as
      evidenced by its higher error on the forget set for easy-to-unlearn samples. The key strength of this


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      sorting heuristic is its ability to combine “predictive confidence” with the “strength of correlation to
487   similar samples” based on the model’s estimated distribution. This combination allows EMSKSD
488   to more accurately identify samples that can be effectively unlearned.
489
490
      Q3: GradAscent proved to be the most effective algorithm for tackling difficult data points, yet it
      faced challenges in fully dissociating the influence of these points, even when large numbers of simi-
491
      lar data points were also unlearned. This method often either compromised the model’s performance
492
      or failed to significantly improve key unlearning metrics, such as predictive confidence, as shown
493   in Figure 2 and Tables 3. A critical factor is determining the threshold of tolerable performance
494   degradation. If the user can tolerate a certain level of reduction in model performance, it may still
495   be possible to unlearn difficult data points.

497   5.6   U NLEARNING F EASIBILITY: I NSIGHTS FROM E XPERIMENTS AND KSD S CORING
498
499   The systematic evaluation of unlearning performance for ”easy” and ”difficult” samples (recom-
500   mended by each KSD-based scores 4.2) are tied to the six unlearning difficulty factors 3. For each
501   significant factor affecting the feasibility of unlearning, we establish its relationship with the exper-
502   imental results.
503   To evaluate the ”Performance Shift”, we examined the impact of unlearning on the predictive accu-
504   racy and loss of the unlearned models. For an easy sample, most unlearning algorithms achieved
505   zero accuracy on the forget set SF (Figure 2). For easy sample the predictive accuracy of the model
506   on the remaining and test subsets remained close to that of the original model (Table 1); and sur-
507   prisingly unlearning by retraining could achieve 0% accuracy on the forget set. For difficult data,
508   the performance of the models was significantly compromised. This was evident from a nearly 50%
509
      reduction in accuracy on the test data for CIFAR10. Additionally, unlearning was often unsuccess-
      ful, as there was no observable change in the model’s accuracy on the forget set, indicating that the
510
      unlearning process had no measurable effect. We also have provided ”Unlearning Loss” in Table
511
      2. The unlearned models exhibited a significant increase on forget error’s which in combination
512   with achieving 0% accuracy on the unlearned sampled, signifies the unlearning was successful and
513   effective for removing the information of ”easy” samples from unlearned model. In contrast, the
514   unlearning of ”Difficult” datapoints had minimal to no impact on the model’s error on the forget
515   set, indicating that these datapoints were more resistant to unlearning. Furthermore, the “Resistance
516   to Membership Inference Attack (MIA)” for ”easy” samples identified by ”EMSKSD” consistently
517   show higher MIA-efficacy, whereas ”difficult” samples often with lower MIA-efficacy. This obser-
518   vation suggests the algorithm could still identify the unlearned samples as part of the training set,
519   highlighting the failure of the unlearning process for ”Difficult” points. The analysis of the rest of
520
      the factors is given in Appendix A.4, A.5.

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we examined the difficulty of machine unlearning in response to the increasing demand
525   for this process. We summarized the six unlearning feasibility factors that can assess the difficulty
526   of unlearning individual data points, including as size of unlearning expansion, tolerance of per-
527   formance shift, resistance to membership attack, etc. With the summarized unlearning feasibility
528   factors, we proposed a set of scoring metrics to measure the unlearning difficulty based on Kernel-
529   ized Stein Discrepancy. Our empirical evaluation shows the proposed scoring metrics can provide
530
      guidance on correctly identifying easy and difficult samples ahead of unlearning operation, which is
      potentially useful in practice for supporting decision making.

745   A.2   C OMPUTATION EFFICIENCY
746
      To ensure the computational efficiency of KSD-based scoring metrics, we performed both a detailed
747   analytical evaluation of their computational complexity and experimental assessments through wall-
748   clock time measurements. The analytical evaluation focuses on the time complexity of the KSD
749   score. For each datapoint, metrics such as MKSD, MSKSD, and EMSKSD are computed over all
750   training data n and rely on the Stein Kernel function (Equation 4). The computation of the Stein
751   Kernel is bounded by the term (k(a, b) · ∇a log Pθ (a) · ∇b log Pθ (b). A separate analysis of these
752   components reveals the following complexities:
753
754
            1. The computation of k(a, b) has a constant time complexity of O(C).
755         2. The computation of ∇a log Pθ (a) is influenced by the data dimension (d) and the network
               depth (h), resulting in a complexity of O(d × h).


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      As a result, the Stein Kernel function has a complexity of O(C × (d × h)2 ). Since the MKSD,
757   MSKSD, and EMSKSD metrics iterate over all data points (n), the overall time complexity of these
758   metrics is bounded by O(n × C × (d × h)2 ). For the SSN metric, the complexity is determined by
759   the computation of ∇a Pθ , which is bounded by O(g), where g = O(d × h). Later, to empirically
760   evaluate computational efficiency, we conducted wall-clock time measurements on a subset of the
761   training data (n′ < n) and reported the results in Table 6. This empirical assessment provides
762   practical insights into the real-time performance of these metrics.
763
