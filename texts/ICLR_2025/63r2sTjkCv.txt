009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            DNA-Encoded Libraries (DEL) are combinatorial small molecule libraries that
012            offer an efficient way to characterize diverse chemical spaces. Selection experi-
013            ments using DELs are pivotal to drug discovery efforts, enabling high-throughput
014            hit finding screens. However, limited availability of public DEL datasets hinders
015            the advancement of computational techniques designed to utilize such data. To
016            bridge this gap, we present KinDEL, one of the first large, publicly available
017            DEL datasets on two kinases: Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinase 14 (MAPK14)
018            and Discoidin Domain Receptor Tyrosine Kinase 1 (DDR1). Interest in this data
019
               modality is growing due to its ability to generate extensive supervised chemi-
               cal data that densely samples around select molecular structures. Demonstrat-
020
               ing one such application of the data, we benchmark different machine learning
021
               techniques to develop predictive models for hit identification; in particular, we
022            highlight recent structure-based probabilistic approaches. Finally, we provide bio-
023            physical assay data, both on- and off-DNA, to validate our models on a smaller
024            subset of molecules. Data and code for our benchmarks can be found at https:
025            //kin-del-2024.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/kindel.zip.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      DNA-Encoded Libraries (DEL) have emerged as a powerful tool in drug discovery, enabling
031   highly efficient screens of small molecule libraries against therapeutically relevant targets (Yuen
032   & Franzini, 2017; Gironda-Martı́nez et al., 2021; Kunig et al., 2021; Peterson & Liu, 2023). These
033   massive libraries are efficiently constructed through combinatorial synthesis of chemical building
034   blocks, or synthons, with each resulting molecule being assigned a DNA barcode (see Figure 1).
035   DELs are then used in selection experiments where they are mixed with proteins of interest bound
036   to a matrix. Multiple rounds of washing are conducted to remove any weak binders, and the DNA
037   tags of surviving molecules are sequenced as a measure of binding affinity. Data generated through
038   these experiments are intrinsically noisy with various sources of bias arising from the DEL synthesis
039
      and selection processes, suggesting that modern machine learning methods may be needed to learn
      signal from the data. Unfortunately, there is still a lack of large, publicly available DEL datasets and
040
      benchmarking tasks to drive this important research area.
041
042   The growing interest in utilizing DEL data for modeling is evidenced by the many recent efforts
043   to advance this area (Iqbal et al., 2024; Blevins et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024). One of the primary
044   reasons for this interest is that selection experiments using DELs alleviate some of the data limita-
045
      tions typical of the field; most chemistry problems in the machine learning domain lack consistent
      and sufficiently high-quality labels. In particular, DELs can contain billions of compounds, and
046
      require fewer resources to run compared to more traditional high-throughput screens (Peterson &
047
      Liu, 2023). However, the process of DEL synthesis and selection introduces various sources of bias
048   that can add noise to the observed data. For instance, DEL experiments measure the affinity of
049   molecules while they are attached to a DNA barcode (hence, on-DNA binding). In contrast, during
050   a real therapeutic campaign, drug-like molecules are tested without a DNA tag (off-DNA), meaning
051   the DEL data is biased by the DNA and the molecule’s attachment to it. Moreover, uncertainties
052   in reaction yields and biases in polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification add additional noise
053   to the process. These difficulties have fueled an increasing enthusiasm within the community for
      developing structured computational models to more effectively interpret the data signals.

072
073   Despite these challenges, several efforts have demonstrated successful applications of machine
074   learning to DEL data (McCloskey et al., 2020). These early successes suggest that there is still much
075   to explore in this domain. Currently, most methods have focused purely on discriminative modeling,
076   which plays a crucial role in drug discovery campaigns by ranking prospective compounds in order
077   to select potential hits. These prediction methods typically utilize established architectures, building
078
      upon both molecular fingerprints and more sophisticated graph convolutional networks (Duvenaud
      et al., 2015). More recently, efforts have been made to pose the problem in a probabilistic manner,
079
      directly incorporating experimental uncertainty in the model structure (Chen et al., 2024). These
080
      models leverage the fact that, while individual data points may be noisy, it is generally expected
081   that groups of molecules with the same synthons or substructures will show enrichment, or signal,
082   when analyzed collectively. Additionally, DEL data can be employed for generative modeling, pro-
083   viding weak supervision to navigate the complex chemical landscape, which is valuable for lead
084   optimization steps of drug discovery.
085
      To demonstrate the advantages of DEL data and promote development of the methods described
086
      above, we release KinDEL (Kinase Inhibitor DNA-Encoded Library) as library of 81 million small
087   molecules tested against two kinase targets, MAPK14 and DDR1. Our dataset, distinguished by
088   its high consistency across experimental replicates (see Appendix B), provides a large amount of
089   supervised data for the machine learning community to develop methods for solving small molecule
090   chemistry problems in drug discovery.
091
      In addition to the KinDEL dataset, we provide a set of benchmark tasks validated using biophys-
092
      ical assay data, which we also release publicly. A major challenge in driving research in this area
093   has been the dearth of benchmark tasks to demonstrate the efficacy of using DEL data in deriving
094   therapeutic insights. By releasing these benchmarks, we aim to facilitate the comparison of various
095   modeling techniques currently applied to DELs. To seed these benchmarks, we survey computa-
096   tional methods from the literature and build predictive models, validated through biophysical data
097   from compounds independently resynthesized both on- and off-DNA. Since DEL data primarily
098   captures on-DNA binding events, but our interest lies in off-DNA binding affinity, these additional
099   data are crucial for assessing the models’ generalizability to diverse biophysical data. Our studies
100   show that models built on DEL data can effectively characterize both on- and off-DNA affinities,
101
      highlighting the usefulness of DEL data in drug discovery. We hope that KinDEL serves as a public
      resource that can facilitate the iterative refinement of chemical models by providing supervised data
102
      in densely sampled chemical spaces.

106
107   We first introduce a high level summary of the dataset generation, and then provide an overview
      of the data that we publicly release. The data generation is divided into roughly 3 experimental

      processes, which are the synthesis of the DEL, the subsequent selection experiments against proteins
109   of interest, and the biophysical assays to collect validation data. Specific experimental details can
110   be found in Appendix A.
111
112   2.1   DATA G ENERATION
113
114   DEL Synthesis The DEL is built as a trisynthon library with 378 synthons in the A position, 1128
115   synthons in the B position, and 191 synthons in the C position. The synthesis is a sequential process,
116   with each synthon specified by the DNA tag and added one at a time. Notably, each molecule does
117   not have only a single encoding; instead, multiple encodings of DNA map to the same final molecular
118   structure. These redundant encodings help mitigate potential biases during the subsequent DNA
119
      sequencing step. Rather than counting the total number of amplified DNA associated with a single
      molecule (which is subject to PCR noise), we count the number of different redundant encodings
120
      observed for each molecule. This library was designed to enhance scaffold uniqueness and chemical
121
      diversity, thereby exploring a broader region of chemical space. A simplified diagram of synthesis
122   can found in Figure 1.
123
124   DEL Selection DEL Selection was performed by combining the DEL with the target proteins, which
125
      were immobilized on beads, as well as a negative control without any protein. Multiple rounds of
      washes removed any weak binders in the solution. The molecules were then extracted via elution,
126
      and the subsequent samples were then amplified via PCR and sequenced to obtain the count data.
127
      Each selection was performed in triplicate with each protein. This process is visualized in Figure 2.
128
129   Biophysical Assay Validation Additional biophysical data on a small subset of molecules were
130   collected both on- and off-DNA. For on-DNA data, we use Fluorescence Polarization (FP), which
131
      utilizes polarized light to quantify binding affinity by measuring the dissociation constant (KD ). For
      off-DNA data, we use Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), which also relies on light to measure the
132
      dissociation constant. The reason we collect both types of data is because on-DNA KD data reveals
133
      insights about the actual binding of the molecule in the DEL selection experiment, while off-DNA
134   KD focuses on interactions of the molecules without a DNA-barcode, which is the relevant setting
135   for an actual drug candidate.

159   2.2   DATA OVERVIEW
160
161   DEL Data KinDEL contains count data for more than 81M unique molecules used in selection
      experiments with two proteins MAPK14 and DDR1. Typically, DEL experiments are run with a


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      negative control without the protein, so that non-specific binding events (such as binding directly
163   to bead) can be captured. In our dataset we provide three (3) replicates of data for each of the
164   control and the protein target conditions. As mentioned earlier, raw DNA counts can be noisy due
165   to PCR amplification bias (Aird et al., 2011; Kebschull & Zador, 2015), so we use the sequence
166   count information as data, which measures the number of unique DNA sequences observed for each
167   molecule.
168
      Pre-selection Data Additionally, we provide data about the sequenced library itself, called pre-
169   selection data, which provides a rough estimate of the relative abundance of each molecule prior to
170   any experimental run. Due to the size of the library, it is too costly to sequence the library deeply
171   enough for an extremely accurate pre-selection estimate. However, because there is always some
172   amount of synthesis noise, for instance using impure reactants or having incomplete reaction yields,
173   achieving a precise measurement of the pre-selection data is not imperative.
174
      Biophysical Assay Data We have collected data from 30-50 molecules using the aforementioned
175   biophysical assays, FP and SPR, to validate our models. For molecules on-DNA, we have re-
176   synthesized molecules both from within and from outside our library. For off-DNA compounds,
177   we have only resynthesized molecules that are within the DEL itself. These molecules were primar-
178   ily selected from the top hits predicted from models trained on the DEL data (see Appendix C for
179   more details).
180
      Figure 3 illustrates various properties of the molecules in our dataset, comparing them to those of
181   typical drug-like molecules. From this, we can see that KinDEL is well-posed within typical drug-
182   like distributions according to an analysis by Shultz (2018). Notably, over 30% of the molecules in
183   our library fall within the property ranges of already approved drugs, as outlined by Schultz. While
184   certain synthon combinations may result in compounds that fall outside these preferred ranges, DEL
185   molecules primarily serve to provide initial hits for drug discovery campaigns. These initial hits
186   undergo iterative refinement during the hit-to-lead optimization process.

188   3     B ENCHMARKING
189
190   3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
191
192   One primary use-case of DEL data is building predictive models of binding affinity. To that end,
193   we investigate commonly used models in DEL literature as benchmark models and compare their
194   performance in modeling binding affinity. In this benchmark, all models were trained using the top
195   1M compounds with the highest counts from our KinDEL dataset. We publish the full library to
196   enable construction of further benchmarks.
197
198   Held-out Test Set The observed count data in DEL experiments are an approximation of the true
199
      on-DNA binding affinity (KD ). The count data are influenced by multiple sources of noise (see
      Section 4). We ultimately wish to rank molecules by binding affinity, so we use compounds with
200
      measured KD (from biophysical assays) as a test set. Performance on these compounds assesses if
201
      the models correctly rank compounds by KD . This can be viewed as measuring how well models
202   can remove the noise inherent to DELs.
203
204   For both our targets, MAPK14 and DDR1, the selected compounds contained in the DEL library
205
      were resynthesized on- and off-DNA to create an in-library held-out test set. For hit finding, we
      would like to be able to predict off-DNA KD . This is challenging because the DEL data comes
206
      from DNA bound molecules, and is biased by the DNA. The on-DNA KD more closely aligns with
207
      DEL data since the molecules in the training data are bound to the same DNA in the same way. A
208   few additional compounds were added from outside the library (and tagged with DNA) to create
209   an additional held-out test set that we refer to as ”Extended”. The KD data from these biophysical
210   assays are also released with our dataset. A UMAP visualization of the DEL including the in-library
211   and external test set compounds is depicted in Figure 5b.
212

215   a randomly selected 10% of compounds are placed in the validation set, and another randomly se-
      lected 10% are placed in the test set. The second type of data split is a disynthon split, where we

      Evaluation Metrics Models trained on DEL data typically predict an enrichment score, which can
305
      be regarded as a measure of binding affinity. To evaluate different baselines, we compare how well
306
      each method’s predicted enrichment scores correlate with experimental KD values for the molecules
307   in the held-out test set. Since different models are trained with different losses, this is a consistent
308   way to compare model performance. Here, we use Spearman correlation, because we are primarily
309   concerned with the ability of a model to rank order molecules by their binding affinity.

      3.2       B ENCHMARK M ODELS
312
313   In this benchmark, we compare models commonly used for DEL data in the literature. These meth-
314   ods follow a similar paradigm, in which the models try to learn the protein binding signal in the
315   target data by subtracting out noise from the control data. Two baselines are included to gauge the
316   alignment in the actual data, between the DEL counts and validation KD values. The first baseline,
317   count enrichment, computes an enrichment score by subtracting the average control counts from the
318   average target counts. The second baseline, Poisson enrichment, computes a ratio of fitted Poisson
319   distributions of the target and control data (Gerry et al., 2019).

323   a deep neural network (DNN) using Morgan fingerprints (radius=2, length=2048) (Rogers & Hahn,
      2010) from RDKit (Landrum, 2010) as input features. We also tested two molecular graph models,

428
429   Data Applicability and Limitations Our data and benchmarking tasks were designed to evaluate
430   the ability to predict compounds within the DEL. Although we incorporated challenging splits of the
431   data, such as disynthon splits, we have not evaluated the usefulness of this particular data for truly
      out-of-distribution sets. While we do include an extended held-out set with molecules from outside


                                                              8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      of the DEL, this extended set is limited in size. We envision that models trained on this data can
433   be used to make predictions for molecules from purchasable catalogues, but such applications will
434   require further exploration, especially being cognizant of problems in this space such as domain of
435   applicability (Weaver & Gleeson, 2008).
436
      One limitation of DEL data is that it only measures on-DNA binding events, and on- and off-DNA
437
      binding are only loosely correlated (Hackler et al., 2019). While we have shown in our experiments
438
      that machine learning models can have nice regularization properties and learn some correlations to
439   off-DNA binding, DEL data needs to be combined with additional 3-D structural data to fully under-
440   stand these different binding modalities. To that end, we also publicly release docked 3-D poses of
441   our library molecules to the target proteins to aid future model development by the community (see
442   Appendix F). Additionally, DEL screens are sometimes run with an additional experimental con-
443   dition to distinguish potential allosteric binders from orthosteric binders (Gironda-Martı́nez et al.,
444   2021). This is usually achieved by running the experiment with a known inhibitor doped in at high
445   concentrations. Because our data lacks this condition, it can be difficult to discern non-specific bind-
446   ing modes. We also recognize that one limitation of is dataset is that the two targets we provide are
447
      kinases. In order to make this dataset more broadly applicable for modeling DEL data, we addition-
      ally release our data on another well studied target, Bovine Carbonic Anhydrase (BCA), using the
448
      same library.
449
450
451   Challenges and Future Directions DEL data is powerful in that it specifically densely samples
452   particular chemical spaces, which can be leveraged to learn more powerful representations. How-
453
      ever, DEL data suffers from experimental noise. In particular, there are unobserved factors such as
      synthesis noise that makes it difficult to separate out signal from noise in the data (Zhu et al., 2021).
454
      Additionally, since our observations are sequencing read counts rather than actual binding affinity,
455
      the measurements also suffer from PCR bias (Aird et al., 2011). While we have presented several
456   benchmark methods that try to learn a denoised enrichment from structure-based models, how best
457   to do this is still an open question in the field, and we hope that our dataset release will enable the
458   development of more denoising methods.
459
      Furthermore, our benchmark primarily focuses on building purely predictive models, which en-
460
      capsulates most of the published work in this field currently. However, KinDEL holds significant
461
      potential for use in generative frameworks. Many generative modeling approaches in the small-
462   molecule space lack sufficient supervised data to learn interesting sampling distributions. However,
463   this is exactly the data that DEL provides, densely sampling around particular synthons or di-synthon
464   structures. Therefore, we hope that DEL data combined with provided docking poses can be used to
465   train or fine-tune generative models on specific protein targets.

469
470   5.1   C URRENT DATASETS
471
472   To the best of the authors’ knowledge, only a limited number of DEL datasets have been released
473   publicly. While KinDEL is not the largest library tested, our dataset is evaluated on two distinct tar-
474   gets in the Kinase family and contains comprehensive and consistently replicated raw experimental
475   data (see Appendix B) in addition to orthogonal, non-DEL based binding affinity data for validation.
476   Iqbal et al. (2024) released three DEL datasets tested against two targets, Casein Kinase 1α/δ. Their
477   libraries span a range of chemical size and diversity, and they demonstrated the efficacy of a suite of
478   machine learning methods to model binding affinities. Like our work, they experimentally validated
479   some of their machine learning derived molecule predictions with biophysical assay data. However,
480   they only tested off-DNA binding affinities, whereas we have synthesized our compounds both on-
481   and off-DNA. Having both kinds of data is important because on-DNA data bridges the gap between
482
      DEL data and off-DNA data. Additionally, they have not investigated probabilistic models in their
      benchmarking tasks, which we find to be empirically useful.
483
484   Another recent dataset release is from Leash Bio, who released their data as a Kaggle competition
485   (Blevins et al., 2024). Their dataset has a single DEL screened against on three different proteins,
      but their data has been preprocessed from raw count to a binary label. This process is often used to


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      denoise DEL data, but it removes information from the data. We show through our experiments that
487   it is possible to learn over discrete count data through probabilistic approaches.
488
489   Gerry et al. (2019) and Hou et al. (2023) have released libraries tested against well-studied targets
490
      Horseradish Peroxidase and members of the Carbonic Anhydrase family. In both cases, the DELs
      were synthesized with specific chemotypes known to bind to their targets. As a result, evaluation
491
      in each is primarily limited to comparing relative enrichment for compounds containing known
492
      binders. These papers serve as excellent case-studies but with their targeted library construction and
493   limited library size ∼100k (Gerry et al., 2019) and ∼7M (Hou et al., 2023), they are unlikely to
494   serve as generalizable benchmarks for DEL ML methods.

498   5.2   C OMPUTATIONAL M ETHODS ON DEL DATA
499
500   Computational efforts on DEL data have evolved over time. One of the major concerns with DEL
501   data is its intrinstic noisiness. Firstly, the synthesis of a DEL is optimized for scale, and not precision,
502   which results in uncertainty in the composition of the library. For instance, DEL synthesis will result
503   in forming partial or truncated products (Gironda-Martı́nez et al., 2021; Binder et al., 2022), which
504   has some causal effect on the observed data that cannot easily be measured. Additionally, the final
505   data measurement is obtained by sequencing PCR-amplified DNA barcodes; however, the PCR
506   process itself does not uniformly sample from the surviving members of the selection experiment
507
      (Aird et al., 2011).
508   Computational methodology for DEL data is still a nascent area, largely due to a lack of publicly
509   available data. Current works typically directly apply fingerprint and graph neural network ap-
510   proaches that are popular within property prediction models. Due to the noisiness intrinsic to the
511   data, many methods bin the labeled data into a binary classification to avoid overfitting on the raw
512   data. For instance, McCloskey et al. (2020) trained classification models on disynthons using vari-
513
      ants of typical graph neural networks. This approach was applied by Ahmad et al. (2023) to find
      first-in-class WD Repeat Domain 91 ligands, using molecules from buyable catalogues. Torng et al.
514
      (2023) follows up on this work and uses Weave GCN for hit identification of CA-IX. However, the
515
      true binding process is observed on a discrete scale, so more recent works have focused on building
516   probabilistic models of binding. Lim et al. (2022) proposed a new uncertainty-based loss function
517   for training regression models, and demonstrated the efficacy of their method using various graph
518   neural network models. Shmilovich et al. (2023) takes this process one step further and incorporates
519   3-D docked poses to leverage the scale of DEL data by using multiple-instance learning to learn
520   over poses. Other works exploit compositionality of DELs as inductive biases of the models. Binder
521   et al. (2022) tries to explicitly model the partial products, while Chen et al. (2024) computes a hi-
522   erarchical representation of DEL molecules and incorporates a probabilistic loss. Koziarski et al.
523   (2024) explores generative models of DEL molecules using GFlowNets. Many of these develop-
524
      ments are relatively new, and we hope that KinDEL will enable the further development of these
      methodologies in the field.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      DELs have emerged as a high-throughput technology that enables screenings of large combinatorial
532
      small molecule libraries. However, DEL data has many sources of intrinsic noise stemming from
533
      synthesis and selection experiments, necessitating the right machine learning tools to extract the
534   correct signal in the data. We introduce KinDEL as a 81M molecule dataset with selection data
535   from two targets, MAPK14 and DDR1, in order to highlight the ability to model DEL data to find
536   potent binders. Our DEL is built with chemical diversity in mind, and many of the molecules in the
537   library have properties within the range of approved drugs. We additionally release biophysical data
538   for molecules synthesized both on- and off-DNA to validate our models trained on DEL data. We
539   hope that our public data release and benchmarking will engender more interest in DEL data as an
      important chemical modality for machine learning research in the future.

      A.1   DEL S YNTHESIS
705
706
      Library Design The design of DNA-encoded libraries (DELs) often grapples with limited diversity
707   and availability of bifunctional building blocks compared to their monofunctional counterparts. In
708   order to expand structural diversity of the first two synthons of a three-step linear library, we im-
709   plemented a hybrid design that combines trifunctional and monofunctional building blocks within
710   one step of synthesis, to form a bifunctional synthon, by using DEL synthesis on solid supports.
711   This strategy significantly expands the chemical space and diversity of our DELs, offering a robust
712   pathway to discovering novel compounds with enhanced biological activity.
713   Library Build The DEL is built as a trisynthon library, comprised of 378 synthons in the A po-
714   sition, 1128 synthons in the B position (the terminal, capping step), and 191 synthons in the C
715   position. This resulted in a DNA encoded library comprised of roughly 81 million unique members.
716   The first two steps are done either by acylation with N-protected (Boc or Fmoc) amino acid, fol-
717   lowed by deprotection, or by immobilization of the DNA to a solid support (DEAE sepharose resin)
718   followed by a series of chemical transformations: acylation with trifunctional building block (Boc-
719   amino acid with protected side chain: Fmoc-amine, ester or ketone), deprotection (if necessary) of
720   the side chain protecting group, reaction (amide coupling, reductive amination/alkylation) with the
721
      monofunctional building block (amine, aldehyde or acid), and lastly eluting the DNA off the DEAE
      sepharose and cleavage of the Boc group. In the final step, the downstream amino groups were
722
      reacted with monofunctional acids or aldehydes. Each of the steps described herein is encoded by
723
      the attached DNA strand allowing for ready deconvolution of the sequence of chemical steps taken
724   for any given member.
725

1030   D    M ODEL H YPERPARAMETERS
1031
1032   This section describes the hyperparameters used to train the models presented in this study. Random
1033   Forest and XGBoost used 100 decision trees trained with the squared error criterion, and the depth
1034   of decision trees was not restricted. The k-Nearest Neighbors model used 5 nearest neighbors.
1035   The final architecture of the DNN model consisted of 5 linear layers with the ReLU activation
1036   functions except for the last one. Batch norm and dropout layers (with the probability of zeroing
1037   an element equal to 20%) were applied after each layer before the activation layers. The hidden
1038   dimension size was set to 512 for all layers.
1039   The GIN model has 5 GIN convolutional layers with hidden dimension size equal to 256. The graph
1040   convolutional layers are followed by the global average pooling layer and two linear layers with a
1041   ReLU activation layer between them. The first linear layer reduces the dimensionality from 256 to
1042   128 before the second layer produces the model prediction.
1043
       Chemprop uses three layers of bond message passing with the hidden dimension of 300. Then,
1044
       the information is aggregated using a global average pooling layer, and one linear layer is used to
1045   make predictions. The model is trained using the default Chemprop optimization procedure, which
1046   employs the Adam optimizer with a Noam learning rate scheduler and 2 epochs of warmup.
1047
1048
       The DEL-Compose model was used in two modes of molecule encoding: synthon-based encoding
1049
       (denoted DEL-Compose(S) ) and full molecule encoding (denoted DEL-Compose(M ) ). The full
       molecule encoding uses four linear layers with ReLU activation functions to learn the encoding
1050
       of the molecule based on its Morgan fingerprint. The synthon-based mode embeds each synthon
1051
       separately using the same 4-layer MLP. Next, combinations of synthon embeddings (AB, BC, AC,
1052   ABC) are further processed by 2-layer MLPs, and finally all embeddings are aggregated using a
1053   4-head attention pooling. The output distribution was set to zero-inflated Poisson distribution. The
1054   learning rate used to train DEL-Compose was 5e-5, and the batch size was 64.

       E    DATASET S PLITTING
1058
1059
       It is often difficult ensure that information from the test set does not leak into train. One popular
       method is calculating Bemis-Murcko scaffolds (Bemis & Murcko, 1996) for each compound in
1060
       a dataset and assigning compounds to a split by scaffold. Unfortunately, this method often fails
1061
       for DEL data. Below we calculate Bemis-Murcko scaffolds for the top million compounds in the
1062   MAPK14 dataset using rdkit (Landrum, 2010). There are ∼300,000 unique scaffolds calculated for
1063   these compounds, implying many compounds have a unique scaffold (see Fig. 7a). Additionally the
1064   common scaffolds are often trivial (see Fig. 7b). The most common scaffold is a simple benzene,
1065   and the next five most common are also extremely common molecular building blocks. Given these
1066   shortcomings, we do not use a scaffold split.
1067
       An alternative method is a similarity split based on clustering with fingerprints. A classic method
1068   for this is Butina Clustering (Butina, 1999). Unfortunately, it does not scale well to large datasets.
1069   We have developed an internal method that scales to large datasets. Our two step method first uses
1070   UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) to reduce 1024 ECFP4 fingerprints (Rogers & Hahn, 2010) to length
1071   10 float vectors. Then HDBSCAN (McInnes et al., 2017) is used to cluster compounds, in a GPU
1072   implementation from NVIDIA (cuml) this runs in a few hours on a single Tesla T4. Clusters are
1073   assigned to splits using a waterfall method, at each step assigning the largest remaining cluster to
1074   the smallest current split. The following settings were used:
1075
1076
       UMAP(n_components=10, metric="jaccard", n_neighbors=30,
       min_dist=0.0, n_epochs=1000)
1077
1078
       HDBSCAN(min_cluster_size=10, min_samples=None, metric=’euclidian’,
1079   prediction_data=True, cluster_selection_method=’eom’)
