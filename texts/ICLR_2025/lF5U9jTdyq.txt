009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion models for image generation have achieved notable success in vari-
012
               ous applications. However, these models often require tremendous storage over-
013
               head and inference time cost, severely hampering their deployment on resource-
014            constrained devices. Post-training quantization (PTQ) has recently emerged as a
015            promising way to reduce the model size and the inference latency, by converting
016            the float-point values into lower bit-precision. Nevertheless, most existing PTQ
017            approaches neglect the accumulating quantization errors arising from the substan-
018            tial distribution variations across distinct layers and blocks at different timesteps,
019            thus suffering a significant accuracy degradation. To address these issues, we
020            propose a novel temporal distribution-aware quantization (DAQ) method for dif-
021            fusion models. DAQ firstly develops a distribution-aware finetuning framework
022
               to dynamically suppress the accumulating quantization errors in the calibration
               process. Subsequently, DAQ employs a full-precision noise estimation network to
023
               optimize the quantized noise estimation network at each sampling timestep, fur-
024
               ther aligning the quantizers with varying input distributions. We evaluate the pro-
025            posed method on the widely used public benchmarks for image generation tasks.
026            The experimental results clearly demonstrate that DAQ reaches the state-of-the-
027            art performance compared to existing works. We also display that DAQ can be
028            applied as a plug-and-play module to existing PTQ models, remarkably boosting
029            the overall performance. The source code will be released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, the diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b;a; Rombach et al., 2022)
036   has become a promising alternative of the conventional generative models including GAN (Good-
037   fellow et al., 2020) and VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014), due to the high quality and diversity of its
038   generated images, as well as the stable training process. It has a wide range of applications such as
039
      the super-resolution (Saharia et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022a; Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2020), graph
      generation (Niu et al., 2020), image translation (Sasaki et al., 2021), and image restoration (Song
040
      et al., 2021b; Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2020). Generally, the generation process of diffusion mod-
041
      els involves gradually adding Gaussian noise to image data and then iteratively removing the noise
042   step by step through a noise estimation network. As this process typically takes hundreds or even
043   thousands of steps to find sampling trajectories for denoising, the diffusion model usually requires
044   tremendous storage overhead and inference time cost. For example, the representative Stable Diffu-
045   sion (Rombach et al., 2022) with DPM-Solver (Lu et al., 2022a) requires 16GB memory and 10GB
046   VRAM during inference, taking seconds to generate a 512×512 resolution image (He et al., 2024b).
047
      The high computational complexity of diffusion models is mainly attributed to the following two
048   reasons. First, generating a single image requires hundreds or even thousands of denoising steps,
049   which involve repeatedly executing the estimation network. Second, the estimation network alone
050   introduces significant computational cost to the generation process (e.g. LDM, Stable Diffusion).
051   Despite that many approaches have been proposed to deal with the first issue by reducing the number
052   of estimation steps, balancing the number of steps with the quality of generated images remains a
053   bottleneck. In this paper, we aim to tackle with the second issue, i.e. accelerating the UNet-based
      (Ronneberger et al., 2015) noise estimation network.

      Existing works on accelerating the noise estimation network have been focused on quantization (He
055   et al., 2024b; Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; So et al., 2024; He et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024;
056   Huang et al., 2024), distillation Meng et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023); HUANG et al. (2023), and
057   pruning Li et al. (2022b). Among these techniques, the quantization method has received a lot of
058   attention by converting the weights and activations from floating-point numbers to low-bit-width
059   integers. Typically, quantizing a full-precision model to 8-bit can accelerate the inference process
060   by about 2.2 times (Jacob et al., 2018), with further reduction to 4-bit achieving an additional 59%
061   improvement over the 8-bit setting (He et al., 2024b). However, directly applying the quantization
062   methods designed for general purpose to diffusion models often yields poor performance, as the
063
      diffusion models using the same network to denoise inputs with different distributions at various
      timesteps, which is not handled by the general quantization methods. PTQ4DM (Shang et al., 2023)
064
      and Q-Diffusion (Li et al., 2023) attempt solving this problem by incorporating multi-timestep cal-
065
      ibration into the quantization process, while other approaches focus on the temporal characteristics
066   within the estimation network to mitigate the impact of multi-timestep distributions (So et al., 2024;
067   Huang et al., 2024). Despite these advancements, a significant performance drop persists when
068   models are quantized to bit-widths lower than 8-bit using post-training techniques. To pinpoint the
069   source of this performance degradation, we analyze quantization errors within the estimation net-
070   work across different timesteps. Our analysis reveal that the reconstruction granularities employed
071   in quantization are often inappropriate for diffusion models, leading to pronounced discrepancies
072   among quantized modules. Moreover, we identify substantial quantization errors in specific mod-
073   ules characterized by a wide range of activation distributions across timesteps, which contributes to
074
      diminished performance when quantizing the estimation network to lower bit-widths.
075   To address the above issues, we propose a novel method dubbed temporal distribution-aware quan-
076   tization (DAQ) for diffusion models, to deal with the uneven distribution of internal quantization
077   errors within the network, as well as the accumulation of external quantization errors over multiple
078   sampling timesteps. Unlike previous approaches that focus on calibration components or optimize
079   specific modules separately (Li et al., 2023), we assess the degree of under-recovery in quantized
080
      modules by analyzing relative quantization errors and input distributions. This enables our fine-
      tuning framework to effectively mitigate quantization errors arising from dynamic activation dis-
081
      tribution changes within network modules and the significant disparities among different quantized
082
      modules. Furthermore, to reduce the accumulation of quantization errors across multiple sampling
083   timesteps in diffusion generative models, we present a parameter finetuning method that suppresses
084   cumulative errors over timesteps. We identify the modules and parameters requiring finetuning
085   based on their degree of under-recovery, using the output of the full-precision model at each sam-
086   pling timestep as a reference. This method allows for incremental finetuning of quantization factors,
087   thereby reducing cumulative quantization errors.
088
      The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
089
090         • We propose a novel temporal distribution-aware quantization (DAQ) method for diffusion
091           models, by reducing the accumulating quantization errors arising from the substantial dis-
092           tribution variations across distinct layers and blocks at different timesteps.
093
            • We develop a distribution-aware finetuning framework to dynamically suppress the accu-
094
              mulating quantization errors in the calibration process, and employ a full-precision noise
095           estimation network to optimize the quantized noise estimation network for further aligning
096           the quantizers with varying input distributions. Both of them are plug-and-play modules
097           that are applicable to existing quantization approaches.
098
            • We conduct extensive experiments on various benchmarks, which clearly demonstrate the
099
              effectiveness of the proposed method compared to the state-of-the-art approaches.

103
104   Existing approaches for accelerating the inference of diffusion models can be roughly divided into
105   two categories. One category of approaches aims to find effective sampling trajectories, either by
106   reducing the number of steps required or by selecting more efficient steps. The other category
107   focuses on minimizing the time and memory overhead for each estimation in the denoising process.
      In this paper, we introduce specialized quantization methods to enhance the single denoising process.

      2.1   E FFICIENT D IFFUSION M ODELS
112
113   In recent years, significant work has focused on accelerating the inference speed of diffusion models,
114   primarily by reducing the number of timesteps required for sampling. Some approaches attempt to
115   transform the diffusion process into a non-Markovian process while keeping the objective function
116   unchanged, thereby eliminating the dependency on chain sampling (Song et al., 2021a). Given
117   that diffusion models use continuous-time sampling, other methods have reformulated the denoising
118   problem into solving differential equations, utilizing differential equation solvers to quickly find
119   approximate solutions (Lu et al., 2022a; Bao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022b).
120   However, these methods often require the original training data and additional training processes,
121   making them unsuitable for low-resource scenarios. Consequently, some efforts have shifted towards
122   optimizing the denoising network itself by employing techniques such as distillation (Meng et al.,
123   2023; Sun et al., 2023; HUANG et al., 2023), pruning (Li et al., 2022b), and quantization (He et al.,
124   2024b; Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; So et al., 2024; He et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024; Huang
125   et al., 2024) to compress the network. Among these techniques, quantization is the most widely
126
      used for optimizing denoising networks.
127
128   2.2   M ODEL Q UANTIZATION
129
      Quantization is a widely-used compression method for reducing computational and memory costs.
130
      To optimize the inference process across all timesteps, we focus on quantizing the noise estimation
131   model used in diffusion models. We specifically propose methods based on post-training quanti-
132   zation (PTQ) rather than quantization-aware training (QAT) due to PTQ’s ease of deployment and
133   widespread adoption. Unlike QAT, which requires retraining the quantized model, PTQ directly
134   quantizes the parameters, making its complexity dependent only on the parameters rather than the
135   original training process. As diffusion models increase in size, the advantages of PTQ-based meth-
136   ods become more pronounced.
137   PTQ typically compresses the bit-width of weights and activations within the network to reduce
138   memory and computational overhead. Quantization methods generally map data to lower-bit inte-
139   gers, and floating-point operations in a full-precision model are converted into corresponding integer
140   operations, enhancing the inference speed of the quantized model (Krishnamoorthi, 2018).
141
      When using linear mapping to quantize a full-precision floating-point model, the weights and activa-
142
      tions are typically quantized into low-bit-width integer representations, denoted as W̄ . This process
143   can be represented by the following equation:
144
145                                                    W
                                 W̄ = Clip Round            + Z, Cmin , Cmax ,                           (1)
146                                                    S
147
      where W represents the model parameters, S denotes the scaling factor, Z is the zero point offset,
148
      and Cmin and Cmax are the lower and upper bounds of the mapping range, also known as the
149
      quantization range. Round(·) and Clip(·) denote the rounding and clipping operations respectively.
150   A straightforward and effective approach to determining the quantization range and factor is to
151   directly minimize the error between a model’s outputs before and after quantization. Previous study
152   (Nahshan et al., 2021) has evaluated this using metrics such as L1 distance, cosine similarity, KL
153   divergence, and MSE, ultimately finding that the Lp norm (with p = 2.4) yields the best results
154   (Shang et al., 2023). Additionally, AdaRound (Nagel et al., 2020) introduced an adaptive method for
155   determining rounding directions, which maintains high accuracy even at 4-bit precision. However,
156   when applying PTQ, a small subset of data is still required as a calibration dataset to adjust the
157   network’s activations. Consequently, much of the research on PTQ methods has concentrated on
158
      optimizing the calibration process.
159   Several studies have investigated the impact of calibration dataset size on quantization performance.
160   EasyQuant (Wu et al., 2020), for example, directly uses the training data to establish the upper and
161   lower bounds of the quantization range. ZeroQ (Cai et al., 2020) eliminates the need for original
      training data by generating a calibration dataset from the model’s gradient information and utilizes

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      mixed-precision quantization to determine the optimal bit-width. BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) examines
163   the trade-offs between layer-wise, block-wise, and whole-network calibration, concluding that block
164   reconstruction offers the most effective granularity. In this paper, we explore our framework based
165   on BRECQ. However, traditional PTQ methods do not perform well on diffusion models when we
166   directly apply them.
167
      When quantizing diffusion models, the primary source of inference overhead is the noise estimation
168
      network. Existing research has focused on quantizing this network due to its high resource demands
169   (Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Given the significant costs associated with training these models,
170   PTQ methods are preferred. These methods require fewer resources, are highly portable, and offer
171   rapid quantization speeds.
172
      In current studies, PTQ4DM (Shang et al., 2023) and Q-Diffusion (Li et al., 2023) analyze the dis-
173
      tribution of calibration datasets, suggesting that uniformly sampling images from different timesteps
174
      to form the calibration dataset for the noise estimation network can reduce quantization error. Q-
175   Diffusion also proposes an optimization strategy for UNet-based noise estimation networks. They
176   discovered that the residual networks used in UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) can amplify quantiza-
177   tion errors during skip connections due to varying levels of quantization recovery. To address this,
178   they designed a channel-separated quantization scheme for residual blocks (Li et al., 2023), achiev-
179   ing results comparable to full-precision models on W8A8 and W4A8 on the CIFAR-10 and LSUN
180   datasets. PTQD (He et al., 2024b) found that quantization errors in diffusion models contain Gaus-
181   sian noise. Considering that gaussian noise is inherent in diffusion models, they merged these noise
182   components and adjusted the variance of the Gaussian noise to reduce quantization errors. They also
183
      used the signal-to-noise ratio to evaluate quantization effects and determine the optimal quantiza-
      tion bit-widths at different timesteps, experimenting with mixed-precision quantization strategies on
184
      the ImageNet and LSUN datasets. TDQ (So et al., 2024) employs a three-layer perceptron to map
185
      sampling time encoding information to finetune parameters for correcting quantization factors. Effi-
186   cientDM (He et al., 2024a) adopts the QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), directly finetuning quantiza-
187   tion factors during the quantization calibration process. TFMQ-DM (Huang et al., 2024) constructs
188   timestep-specific quantization modules to correct errors generated when embedding quantized time
189   information encoding modules. APQ-DM (Wang et al., 2024) groups different timesteps and sets
190   shared parameters across these groups to find suitable mapping ranges for quantization factors.
191
      However, several issues remain in quantizing diffusion models. These include the use of uniform
192   quantization settings across different network modules, insufficient consideration of the distribu-
193   tion characteristics of activation values over time, and the accumulation of quantization errors over
194   multiple timesteps.

      3     T HE P ROPOSED A PPROACH
198
      In this section, we propose a dynamic finetuning method that is aware of activation distribution
199
      ranges to adapt to the multi-sampling time features of diffusion generative models. Furthermore, we
200   suppress cumulative errors during inference by post-processing the quantized models. We begin by
201   identifying the problems in existing quantization algorithms, then proceed with a detailed analysis,
202   and finally, we present our proposed solution.
203
204   3.1     S IGNIFICANT D IFFERENCE OF D ISTRIBUTION BETWEEN DIFFERENT M ODULES
205
206   3.1.1    D ISTRIBUTION D IFFERENCE OF ACTIVATION
207
      When quantizing the noise estimation network of DDIM (Song et al., 2021a), we observe that the
208
      outputs of different modules deviate to varying degrees from those in full-precision diffusion mod-
209
      els within a single sampling timestep. Additionally, there are notable differences in activation value
210   ranges and quantization errors across different quantized modules. This issue can be attributed to
211   two main factors. Firstly, the post-training quantization methods based on BRECQ (Li et al., 2021)
212   employ globally uniform quantization hyperparameters for modules with varying reconstruction
213   granularities within the network. Secondly, the calibration data samples used during post-training
214   quantization are typically insufficient, usually ranging from 200 to 5000 samples. This is signifi-
215   cantly smaller than the size of dataset used for training the pre-trained model, which may lead to
      both overfitting and underfitting in different modules within the same quantized network.

273   3.1.2    D ISTRIBUTION D IFFERENCE BETWEEN T IMESTEPS
274
275   In the noise estimation network, the input at each sampling timestep depends on the output from
276   the previous timestep. As a result, the quantization errors introduced by the quantized noise esti-
277   mation network accumulate over the sampling process, gradually diverging from the outputs of the
278
      full-precision network. By comparing the outputs of the full-precision noise estimation network
      with those of the quantized network at each sampling timestep, we observe that these quantization
279
      errors indeed accumulate over time. For instance, the quantization error increases progressively and
280
      reaches saturation after 40-50 timesteps when we quantize the DDIM to W4A8 with 100 sampling
281   timesteps. This observation highlights that addressing the accumulation of quantization errors over
282   sampling timesteps is a critical area of focus in the research of quantization methods for diffusion
283   models.
284
285   3.2     F INETUNING THE D IFFUSION M ODELS
286
287   3.2.1    D ISTRIBUTION -AWARED DYNAMIC F INETUNING F RAMEWORK FOR Q UANTIZERS
288
289
      As mentioned above, the activation distributions differ significantly across quantized modules, often
      resulting in imbalanced quantization errors within a single timestep. This imbalance can degrade
290
      the performance of lower-bit quantization for diffusion models, as it becomes challenging to ade-
291
      quately represent the activation distribution range with low-bit quantizers. To address this issue, we
292   introduce distribution-awared finetuning parameters for the activation quantizers. We use calibration
293   data to finetune the quantization factors for each activation quantizer, similar to the reconstruction
294   process. Specifically, these finetuning parameters are integrated into the quantization factors and
295   subsequently removed, ensuring no additional storage or computational costs are incurred.
296
      For the issue of varying convergence degrees in the quantization reconstruction process across net-
297
      work modules, we hypothesize that the input distribution range (Xrange ), relative quantization error
298   (Qerr ), and the degree of under-recovery (η) are positively correlated. A broader input distribution
299   range can lead to larger clipping errors during quantization, making these clipping errors a more
300   significant component of the total quantization error than rounding errors. However, the error value
301   alone is not the only factor to consider, as it is also related to the reconstruction granularity. This
302   means that some modules with smaller input distribution ranges can still produce significant relative
303   quantization errors. To address this, we evaluated the degree of under-recovery (η) using the distri-
304   bution range and relative quantization error during the quantization reconstruction. We then inserted
305   finetuning parameters into modules that either had the top β% of Xrange or relative quantization
306
      errors greater than γ%. This approach was intended to accelerate the convergence of quantization
      factors within these activation quantizers. In this paper, β was set to 10 and γ to 20.

349
      quantization factors (S and Z). The decision to finetune is based on the degree of under-recovery
350
      (η) of the quantizer. Finally, the original quantization factors are integrated with the finetuning
351   parameters (FS and FZ ) during the inference stage.

353   3.2.2    T IMEWISE F INETUNE FOR S ELECTIVE Q UANTIZATION PARAMETERS
354
355   As diffusion models estimate noise multiple times using the same network, quantized models often
356   accumulate quantization errors over multiple sampling timesteps, leading to significant deviations.
357   To address this issue, we design a post-processing method that operates at each timestep to reduce
358
      the impact of these errors. We use the full-precision network as a reference for the quantized network
      to minimize the difference between the quantized model and the original model. At each timestep,
359
      we compare the output of the quantized model with that of the full-precision model to calculate
360
      the error. This helps the quantized modules achieve performance closer to that of the full-precision
361   network. By guiding the quantized model to suppress cumulative quantization errors at each step,
362   we can improve overall accuracy and stability.
363
364
      Also, given the large scale of parameters in diffusion generative models, finetuning all parameters
      is prohibitively time-consuming and computationally expensive. Therefore, we selectively finetune
365
      specific quantizer parameters in certain modules to reconstruct the quantization error at the network
366
      level based on the degree of under-recovery (η). This approach effectively suppresses the cumulative
367   quantization error over multiple sampling timesteps.

      5   C ONCLUSION
517
      This research investigates quantization methods for diffusion generative models. By considering the
518
      activation distribution of noise estimation networks and addressing imbalanced quantization across
519   different modules, we have enhanced existing post-training quantization techniques. Our improve-
520   ments consistently surpass the best available post-training quantization compression methods at the
521   same bit-width. Furthermore, our method is orthogonal to other approaches, making it suitable as a
522   plugin for existing quantization techniques. However, this work has some limitations. The granu-
523   larity division strategy for quantization reconstruction, adopted from BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) and
524   Q-Diffusion (Li et al., 2023), results in an uneven distribution of quantization errors within the net-
525   work. This indicates the need for a more detailed examination of internal quantization granularity.
