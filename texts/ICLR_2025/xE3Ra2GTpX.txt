011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           In the task of hyper-long context question answering (QA), a key challenge is ex-
014           tracting accurate answers from vast and dispersed information, much like finding a
015
              needle in a haystack. Existing approaches face major limitations, particularly the
              input-length constraints of Large Language Models (LLMs), which hinder their
016
              ability to understand hyper-long contexts. Furthermore, Retrieval-Augmented
017
              Generation (RAG) methods, which heavily rely on semantic representations, of-
018           ten experience semantic loss and retrieval errors when answers are spread across
019           different parts of the text. Therefore, there is a pressing need to develop more
020           effective strategies to optimize information extraction and reasoning. In this pa-
021           per, we propose a multi-grained entity graph-based QA method that constructs
022           an entity graph and dynamically combines both local and global contexts. Our
023           approach captures information across three granularity levels (i.e., micro-level,
024           feature-level, and macro-level), and incorporates iterative retrieval and reasoning
025           mechanisms to generate accurate answers for hyper-long contexts. Specifically,
026
              we first utilize EntiGraph to extract entities, attributes, relationships, and events
              from hyper-long contexts, and aggregate them to generate multi-grained QA pairs.
027
              Then, we retrieve the most relevant QA pairs according to the query. Addition-
028
              ally, we introduce LoopAgent, an iterative retrieval mechanism that dynamically
029           refines queries across multiple retrieval rounds, combining reasoning mechanisms
030           to enhance the accuracy and effectiveness of answering complex questions. We
031           evaluated our method on various datasets from LongBench and InfiniteBench, and
032           the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, signifi-
033           cantly outperforming existing methods in both the accuracy and granularity of
034           the extracted answers. Furthermore, it has been successfully deployed in online
035           novel-based applications, showing significant improvements in handling long-tail
036           queries and answering detail-oriented questions.

      Long documents often contain a wealth of critical information, particularly in fields such as law,
041
      medicine, or finance. The task of hyper-long context question answering (QA) (Georgiev et al.,
042   2024; Wang et al., 2024a) requires models to process vast amounts of information while maintaining
043   precise contextual understanding, which is pivotal for advancing the capabilities of Large Language
044   Models (LLMs) to process and understand extensive textual data. In recent years, LLMs, such as
045   GPT-4 (Brown, 2020), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a), (Touvron et al., 2023b), and PaLM (Chowd-
046   hery et al., 2023), have demonstrated exceptional performance in tasks involving dialogue genera-
047   tion and reasoning (Dagdelen et al., 2024). These models are capable of generating knowledge-rich
048   responses and have broad applications across various domains.
049   Although LLMs have considerable capabilities, they still struggle to process hyper-long texts over
050   100K tokens (Brown et al., 2020). It is challenging for them to maintain contextual coherence and
051   effectively capture long-range dependencies when the input text surpasses the predefined window
052   length (Li et al., 2023a). To alleviate the problem of processing hyper-length texts, some atten-
053   tion mechanisms (e.g., long-range attention mechanisms) have been widely integrated into models
      to capture contextual information across long sequences (Peng et al., 2024). However, training

080   Some researchers have proposed various context extension techniques, such as long-context fine-

084   typically reaching only several hundred thousand tokens (Zhang et al., 2024).
085   Moreover, LLMs often exhibit high hallucination rates when applied to Knowledge-Intensive Gen-
086   eration tasks (Petroni et al., 2020), especially in cases where they must deal with unstructured or
087   domain-specific datasets. These limitations are particularly evident when extracting fine-grained or
088   deep information from large-scale texts, where the sheer volume and dispersed nature of relevant
089   details hinder the models’ ability to retrieve accurate and comprehensive answers. As shown in Fig-

094
      model correctly recalls that “Sun wants to become a teacher” from one chunk, it fails to integrate the
095
      information from another chunk indicating that he did not succeed in securing the position. These
096   chunked retrieval techniques are often too coarse-grained to capture the nuanced contextual details
097   and can result in incorrect conclusions and misinterpretations.

106   only ensures that the model produces highly accurate answers for complex queries, but also miti-
107   gates the fragmentation of information that often hampers traditional methods. Finally, we propose
      LoopAgent, an iterative retrieval mechanism that refines queries over multiple retrieval rounds, com-

136
137
138   The task of question answering with LLMs can be divided into two categories: long-context model
139   optimization and RAG techniques. This section will provide a detailed overview of these two ap-
140   proaches and introduce how they enhance performance in long-context QA tasks.
141
142
143
      2.1   L ONG -C ONTEXT M ODEL O PTIMIZATION A PPROACHES
144
145
146   Early approaches to long-context tasks focus on optimizing LLMs during pretraining to handle
147
      longer inputs. Many methods (Zaheer et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023a; Xiao
      et al., 2023; Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023; Tworkowski et al., 2024) introduce attention mechanisms
148
      and positional encoding to extend context length. For example, Press et al. (2022) proposes to bias
149
      query-key scores based on token distance, allowing models to handle longer sequences. Similarly,
150   xPOS (Sun et al., 2023) improves long-sequence extrapolation by introducing rotational positional
151   encoding. These methods extend model context length during pretraining to handle long input se-
152   quences. Besides, architectural innovations, like sparse patterns in transformers (Beltagy et al.,
153   2020; Kitaev et al., 2020), are developed to reduce memory and computation demands for process-
154   ing longer sequences using sparse attention mechanisms.
155
      While pretraining methods improve long-sequence handling, they are computationally expensive,
156   especially for sequences over 100k tokens, due to significant memory, storage, and processing de-
157   mands. To mitigate this, researchers (Chen et al., 2023c; Tworkowski et al., 2024) focus on positional
158   encoding optimization and fine-tuning strategies instead of full retraining. RoPE (Su et al., 2024)
159   adjusts positional encodings during inference, LongLoRA (Chen et al., 2023d) combines low-rank
160   adaptation with attention optimization, and LongChat (Li et al., 2023b) fine-tunes using rotary em-
161   beddings and conversational datasets. However, these methods still face challenges in maintaining
      coherence and understanding for extremely long sequences.

      2.2   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED A PPROACHES
163
164   RAG efficiently integrates external knowledge retrieval with LLM, making it well-suited for un-
165   structured or domain-specific datasets. This process involves retrieving pertinent information from
166   a vast data corpus in response to a user query, which is then fed into the LLM to enrich the generation
167   process. The RAG strategy typically utilizes chunk-based retrieval (Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al.,
168   2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Izacard & Grave, 2021; Ram et al., 2023; Finardi et al., 2024; Setty
169   et al., 2024), dividing long texts into smaller segments and summarizing them to improve indexing
170
      accuracy. This kind of approach relies on precise embeddings and retrieval techniques. For example,
      (Lewis et al., 2020) introduces dense vector representations to better align queries with document
171
      segments. To address limitations in retrieval fusion, FiD (Izacard & Grave, 2021) integrates retrieved
172
      passages during the generation process to synthesize more accurate responses.
173
174   However, chunk-based methods have limitations, such as losing coherence when dividing text and
175   relying heavily on the semantic understanding of both the query and document. They may also
176
      retrieve fragmented or incomplete information, resulting in fragmented or incomplete answers. To
      overcome these limitations, graph-based RAG methods (Pan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhang
177
      et al., 2023; Sen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Ma
178
      et al., 2024) incorporate relational information from knowledge graphs, improving reasoning across
179   chunks and enhancing information integration. For example, GNN-RAG (Mavromatis & Karypis,
180   2024) enhances retrieval by preserving relationships between entities using structured graph infor-
181   mation. Similarly, (Ma et al., 2024) introduced a RAG framework guided by knowledge graphs,
182   leveraging multi-hop relationships and key entities to address long-range dependencies and ensure
183   logical consistency in complex reasoning tasks. Graph-based RAG methods advance reasoning but
184   struggle with simplistic graphs and shallow entity relationships, limiting complex reasoning and
185   context-dependent tasks. These limitations lead to knowledge loss and slower inference. Our work
186   introduces multi-grained knowledge with RAG to address these challenges.
187
188   3     TASK DEFINITION
189
190   Definition: Knowledge-Intensive QA for Hyper-Long Contexts. Hyper-long contexts refer to
191   contexts whose length is over 100K tokens, making it infeasible to process the entire context. The
192   task aims to generate an accurate answer A to a query Q based on such extensive contexts, where the
193   pertinent information is often dispersed across multiple sections. To address the complexities posed
194   by this task, we propose a novel multi-grained entity graph-based QA generation framework. This
195
      method decomposes the hyper-long document T into a set of sub-blocks Ti , from which entities,
      attributes, relationships, and event-related information are extracted to construct an entity set E.
196
      By dynamically aggregating this information across different levels of granularity, our approach
197
      facilitates the generation of precise QA pairs. This progress can be formulated as following:
198
199                     A = f (T, Q; Θ) = arg max P (A | Q, Emicro , Efeature , Emacro ; Θ)                  (1)
200
      where f (·) represents our multi-grained entity graph-based QA generation model, and Θ represents
201   the model parameters. QA pairs are constructed on three granularities of entity information to
202   store local and global information: micro-granularity (Emicro ), feature-level granularity (Efeature ),
203   and macro-granularity (Emacro ). By synthesizing information from these levels, the model effectively
204   reduces information loss and mitigates semantic inconsistency, thus enhancing the accuracy of QA
205   generation in hyper-length contexts.

      4.5   I TERATIVE R ETRIEVAL AGENT
350
351   The LoopAgent uses a multi-round iterative retrieval strategy, dynamically adjusting queries to re-
352   fine results with each round. This approach overcomes the limitations of single-round retrieval,
353   which often misses critical information in complex, multi-entity scenarios.
354
      The retrieval process begins by decomposing the original query Q into K sub-queries, each derived
355
      from a specific entity in the query. These sub-queries are processed in two phases: Retrieval and
356
      Re-ranking. In the Retrieval stage, the model employs a 12-layer encoder to process the query,
357   and the question and answer are concatenated and fed into another 6-layer encoder. By encoding
358   the query and QA pairs separately, the dual-encoder model (Yates et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2022;
359   Luan et al., 2021) calculates their similarity in the shared vector space, enabling the selection of the
360   most relevant QA pairs that align with the query intent. The system retrieves and ranks the top-K1
361   relevant question-answer pairs from a corpus based on their similarity to the sub-query:
362                            TOPK = argmaxi=1...n sim(f (Q; α), g(QAi ; β))                              (8)
363
364   where Q is the input query, QAi is the candidate from the corpus, and f and g are the encoders
365
      parameterized by α and β. The similarity function sim measures the relevance between the encoded
      query and candidate pairs. After retrieving the top-K1 results, a Re-ranking stage refines them using
366
      a cross-encoder (Qiao et al., 2019), recalculating relevance and producing a new top-K2 ranking by
367
      incorporating additional attributes.
368
369   If the first-round results lack sufficient information, the agent identifies gaps and generates an ad-
370   justed query RewriteQ. A second retrieval round based on RewriteQ produces a new result set
371
      R2 , which is merged with R1 to create a more comprehensive set. Through multiple rounds of re-
      trieval and query adjustment, LoopAgent captures as much relevant information as possible. Once
372
      sufficient data is gathered, the results are passed through a language model to generate a fluent,
373
      accurate, and coherent final answer, balancing efficiency with precision.

533
      In this study, we present a multi-grained knowledge approach for question answering over hyper-
534   long contexts. Our method constructs a knowledge graph organizing information at micro, feature,
535   and macro levels, enhancing LLMs’ ability to process extensive, distributed data. Unlike traditional
536   models struggling with fragmented context, it integrates fine-grained entities and context aggrega-
537   tion to deliver precise, rich responses. Experimental results show a 54.91% accuracy improvement
538   in extracting dispersed details in domains like finance and literature. With plug-and-play function-
539   ality, lower costs, and real-world efficacy, our approach reduces reliance on high-parameter models
      while excelling in knowledge-intensive tasks.

839   A.3.2    D EFINITION OF I MPORTANCE W EIGHT wk
840
      The importance weight wk in Equation (6) is assigned to each entity based on its contextual relevance
841
      and semantic characteristics, as determined by a large language model (e.g., Ernie-3.5-8k). The
842
      scoring rules are as follows:
843
844   Weight 0.3: Generic or vague entities, such as ”villager” or ”merchant.” These entities are typically
845   less informative and have minimal contextual contribution. Weight 0.5: Entities that are contextually
846
      relevant and describe specific subjects, such as ”Zhu Bajie’s wife.” These entities provide clear and
      actionable information in the context.
847
848   Weight 0.7: Rare or specific entities with unique names or backgrounds, such as ”White Bone
849   Demon.” These entities are often critical for understanding specific events or descriptions.
850   Weight 1.0: Core entities, which are indispensable to the context, such as ”Sun Wukong.” These
851   entities are essential for reasoning and often central to the context.
