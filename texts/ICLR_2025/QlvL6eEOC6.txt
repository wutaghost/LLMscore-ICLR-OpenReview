009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Inference with transformer-based language models begins with a prompt process-
012            ing step. In this step, the model generates the first output token and stores the
013            KV cache needed for future generation steps. This prompt processing step can be
014            computationally expensive, taking 10s of seconds or more for billion-parameter
015            models on edge devices when prompt lengths or batch sizes rise. This degrades
016            user experience by introducing significant latency into the model’s outputs. To re-
017            duce the time spent producing the first output (known as the “time to first token”,
018            or TTFT) of a pretrained model, we introduce a novel method called KV Predic-
019
               tion. In our method, a small auxiliary model is used to process the prompt and
               produce an approximation of the KV cache used by a base model. This approx-
020
               imated KV cache is then used with the base model for autoregressive generation
021
               without the need to query the auxiliary model again. We demonstrate that our
022            method produces a pareto-optimal efficiency-accuracy trade-off when compared
023            to baselines. On TriviaQA, we demonstrate relative accuracy improvements in the
024            range of 15%−50% across a range of TTFT FLOPs budgets. We also demonstrate
025            accuracy improvements of up to 30% on HumanEval python code completion at
026            fixed TTFT FLOPs budgets. Additionally, we benchmark models on an Apple M2
027            Pro CPU and demonstrate that our improvement in FLOPs translates to a TTFT
028            speedup on hardware. We release our code for reproducibility.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities on many downstream
034   tasks (Gunter et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Abdin et al., 2024). How-
035   ever, the high computational cost of running large language models results in limited capabilities for
036   on-device inference. On-device inference is essential for privacy, latency, energy efficiency, and per-
037   formance in limited-connectivity areas (Frantar et al., 2022; Alizadeh-Vahid et al., 2023; Stojkovic
038
      et al., 2024). For these reasons, LLM efficiency remains an important and active area of research.
039   LLM inference with the popular transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture consists of two
040   phases. First, in the prompt processing phase, the model processes an input prompt to populate the
041   KV cache. Next, in the generation phase, the model autoregressively generates output tokens. Many
042   recent works focus on improving generation time through approximations of the KV cache (Wu &
043   Tu, 2024; Jiang et al., 2023a; Ge et al., 2023a;b; Li et al., 2023), but only a few recent works have
044
      explored improving the prompt processing time (Fu et al., 2024).
045   Improving prompt processing time allows an application using the LLM to begin sending outputs
046   to the user earlier. The “time to first token” (TTFT) refers to the length of time between a user’s
047   input query and the production of the first output token. In scenarios such as chatting with a user,
048   the TTFT may be a more important runtime experience metric than autoregressive generation time,
049   since the user can begin consuming outputs after the first token is produced. For on-device models,
050
      prompt processing times can be intolerably slow (up to 10s of seconds, Fig. 1). Reducing TTFT in
      these cases enables a better user experience.
051
052   We present a method to improve TTFT by processing the prompt with a small auxiliary transformer
053   model. Our method runs the auxiliary model and stores its KV cache. It then uses a learned linear
      projection to predict the KV cache of another transformer model (the base model) using only the

054
      KV cache of the auxiliary model as input. After the KV cache is predicted, inference continues
055   exclusively with the base model. As a result, no runtime overhead is introduced during generation.
056
057   We demonstrate that our method improves over the efficiency-accuracy trade-off achievable by our
058
      baselines. For example, on TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), our method improves accuracy retention
      by 15% − 50% compared to baselines at equal TTFT FLOP counts. On HumanEval (Chen et al.,
059
      2021) python code completion, we demonstrate accuracy improvements up to 30% over baselines at
060
      equal TTFT FLOP counts. We also provide on-device timing experiments to demonstrate that our
061   FLOPs gains translate to on-device runtime improvements.
062
063   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We develop a novel method called KV Prediction for using
064
      a smaller auxiliary model to predict the KV cache of a larger base model. (2) We show that our
      model produces a stronger efficiency-accuracy trade-off compared to baselines. (3) We analyze the
065
      runtime characteristics of KV Prediction models on-device. Additionally, we release our code for
066
      reproducibility.
067
068   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we give an overview of related work. In
069   Section 3, we motivate our work, demonstrating that TTFT can be problematically high during on-
070
      device inference. In Section 4, we describe our KV Prediction method. In Section 5, we describe our
      experimental setup for evaluating KV Prediction. In Section 6, we give our main results, showing
071
      that our method obtains the pareto-optimal efficiency-accuracy trade-off. In Section 7, we analyze
072
      our predicted KV caches. In Section 8, we conclude.

076
077
      On-Device TTFT Efficiency: Few works have explored our problem domain of improving on-
078   device TTFT. LazyLLM (Fu et al., 2024) uses an attention-based token dropping strategy to drop
079   unneeded tokens at inference time. These tokens can be revived later during the generation phase.
080   Random token dropping (Yao et al., 2022) and static token pruning are both studied in Fu et al.
081   (2024) as methods for improving TTFT. These methods are our baselines.
082
      Server-Side TTFT Efficiency: Cachegen (Liu et al., 2023) compresses KV caches for faster TTFT,
083
      but their setup assumes that a precomputed, pre-compressed KV cache is stored on a server that is
084   available over network. Another similar server-based approach, CritiPrefill (Lv et al., 2024), per-
085   forms attention-based pruning of KV cache segments on a per-query basis. Sarathi (Agrawal et al.,
086   2023) reduces pipeline bubbles in batched server-side inference. Etalon (Agrawal et al., 2024) pro-
087   vides a framework for evaluating server-side inference performance, and analyses various methods.
088   Our investigation differs from these in that we focus on on-device TTFT improvements. Other works
089   focus on efficient long-context processing at extreme context lengths (Gao et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
090   2024; Xiong et al., 2023), which is currently not feasible on-device.
091   Context Compression: Previous works have investigated compressing the KV cache for improving
092   generation efficiency. PyramidKV (Cai et al., 2024), StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023), SnapKV
093   (Li et al., 2024), and Model Tells You What To Discard (Ge et al., 2023a) all compress the KV cache
094   along the token dimension by observing attention scores and pruning irrelevant tokens. However,
095   their methods compute a forward pass before pruning. Thus, they improve generation time, but not
096   TTFT. Layer-Condensed KV Cache (Wu & Tu, 2024) compresses an N -layer KV cache into a 1-
097   layer KV cache, but requires 9 prompt processing steps and negatively impacts TTFT. Other works
098
      such as KIVI (Liu et al., 2024) and GEAR (Kang et al., 2024) have explored quantizing the KV
      cache, but do not improve TTFT.
099
100   A few recent works have explored context compression with a separate network to improve genera-
101   tion time (Jiang et al., 2023a;b; Ge et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023). As shown in Fu et al. (2024), such
102   methods can increase TTFT due to more expensive prompt processing.
103   Sharing Activations Between Models: Similar to our method, Tandem Transformers (AishwaryaP
104   et al., 2024) uses two different models that share hidden activations. However, their focus is on im-
105   proving the performance of Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2022), not TTFT. Other methods
106   explore using a larger teacher model to distill knowledge into a smaller student model to improve
107   efficiency (Bagherinezhad et al., 2018; Gou et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2024). After training, the teacher
      model is discarded. Our method differs in that both models are retained.

130   3   M OTIVATION : T IME TO F IRST T OKEN (TTFT)
131
132   We observe that the TTFT of running a language model on an edge device can be prohibitively high,
133   negatively impacting user experience. We visualize total prompt processing time of OpenELM
134   (Mehta et al., 2024) models on an M2 Pro CPU (EveryMac, 2024) with 32GB of RAM in Fig. 1. As
135
      prompt lengths and batch sizes increase, the TTFT transitions from noticeable to intolerable. Most
      users are not willing to wait more than a few seconds for a response from a chat bot (Dashly, 2023),
136
      but on-device prompt processing times can far exceed this. This long processing time would be
137
      further exacerbated by running the model on low-end hardware instead of a high-end laptop CPU.
138
139   In addition to the negative user experience created by high TTFTs, we note that the ratio of prompt
140   processing time to generation time is of particular significance to applications that generate short
141
      responses. For example, in a question-answering system, the model may only generate a few tokens.
      Thus, the fractional runtime spent during prompt processing can dominate the overall runtime. In
142
      Fig. 1, we visualize the ratio of TTFT to the autoregressive generation time for an OpenELM 3B
143
      model. As prompt length and batch size increases, the process becomes compute-bound and the cost
144   of prompt processing substantially increases relative to the cost of a generation step.
145
146   To illustrate the impact of this high ratio of prompt processing time to generation time, consider a
147
      question-answering system with 1024 tokens of context, operating at batch size 1, and generating a 2
      token response. In this case, the model will spend 4.8 seconds processing the prompt and generating
148
      the first token, and only 4.8/12 = 0.4 seconds generating the second token (since prompt processing
149
      takes 12 times as long as generation, see Fig. 1). In this case, 5.2 seconds are spent generating the
150   output, of which 4.8/5.2 = 92.3% is spent in prompt processing.
151
152   In summary, large prompt processing times can negatively impact practical usage of a model in 2
153
      ways. First, they can result in a large TTFT, negatively impacting user experience. Second, they also
      can represent a large fraction of the total compute used in inference. Our goal is to reduce on-device
154
      TTFT through a novel modeling strategy which we describe in the next section.

      TTFT. Our approximation uses an efficient learned model to predict the KV cache.

      Our intuition is that an accurate KV cache prediction method should use a model of similar structure
178
      to the base model, so that the structure of the KV cache can be modeled more accurately. Thus,
179   we use a smaller learned auxiliary transformer to process the prompt. Then, a set of learned linear
180   projections is used to predict the base model’s KV cache using the auxiliary model’s KV cache as
181   input. Fig. 2a shows an overview of our method.
182
      In the following subsections, we present details of our method. In Section 4.1, we give an overview
183
      of training and inference. In Section 4.2, we provide more architectural details of our KV Prediction
184
      models. In Section 4.3, we describe our loss function. Finally, in Section 4.4, we analyze the
185   reduction in TTFT.

      4.1   P REDICTING KV C ACHES
188
189
      Our model contains a frozen pretrained base network B, a learned auxiliary network A, and a learned
190   KV predictor P. The auxiliary and predictor networks are used to generate the predicted KV cache
191   efficiently. Afterwards, inference proceeds with the base model.
192
193
      Inference: During inference (Fig. 2b) the prompt is passed to the auxiliary model and the auxiliary
      KV cache KV A is computed. Then, the predicted base KV cache KV        ˆ B = P(KV A ) is computed.
194
195
      At this point, A and P are no longer required to continue inference. To generate the first token,
      a single-token generation step of B is run, but with KV ˆ B being used as the keys and values in the
196
      attention operations (instead of using the keys and values from the base model’s QKV projections).
197
      The logits produced from this generation step are used to produce the first output token. Now that
198
      the first token has been produced, generation continues autoregressively in the standard fashion, with
199   new KV cache entries being added as new tokens are processed.
200
201   Training: During training (Fig. 2c), a sequence of tokens I are fed to the auxiliary model A to
      produce output logits A(I) and a KV cache KV A . Then, the predicted KV cache KV  ˆ B = P(KV A )
202
203   is computed. Finally, a forward pass of B is computed using the predicted KV cache KV ˆ B (instead
204   of using the keys and values from the base model’s QKV projections) to produce output logits
205   B(I). In other words, the base model computes a forward pass using masked cross-attention with
206
      the predicted KV cache to produce output logits. Errors in the base model’s logits backpropagate
      through the frozen base model and into A and P.

      4.3      L OSS F UNCTION
271
272   In this subsection we describe our training loss. It consists of three components: the base loss LB ,
273   the auxiliary loss LA , and the consistency loss LC .
274
275
      Base loss: The base loss LB = CB (B(I), T ) is the cross-entropy loss between the base model’s
      outputs and the ground-truth labels T . The base model is frozen, but the gradients flow backwards
276
      through the KV predictor and the auxiliary model. This loss helps ensure that the predicted KV
277
      cache is compatible with the base model.
278
279   Auxiliary loss: The auxiliary loss LA = CA (A(I), T ) is the cross-entropy loss between the aux-
280   iliary model’s outputs and the ground-truth labels. Since the auxiliary logits A(I) are never used
281
      during inference, this loss is not strictly required to produce an auxiliary model and a predictor
      model that can support KV prediction. However, in preliminary experiments we find that LA im-
282
      proves training convergence slightly.
283
284   Consistency loss: The consistency loss LC = L1 (KV    ˆ B , KV B ) is used to align the predicted KV
285   caches with the base model’s KV caches. The consistency loss is necessary to ensure that the
286   predicted KV cache is compatible with the base model. To illustrate this point, we present an
287   ablation in Table 2 showing a model trained with the consistency loss coefficient set to 0 (λC = 0).
288   Without the consistency loss, the predicted KV cache performs poorly.
289   Our final loss is a simple weighted sum of the individual losses:
290
291

        L(B(I), A(I), T , KV                                                                               (1)

      We found that the loss terms LB and LA were balanced by simply setting λB = λA = 1. To choose
297   λC , we perform an ablation between summing the loss across layers (λC = 1) and averaging the
298   loss across layers (λC = 1/nB ) in Table 2. We found that λC = 1/nB performed better.

301   4.4      RUNTIME A NALYSIS
302
303   Improvements in TTFT FLOPs: We analyze the improvement in TTFT of our method. The
304   FLOPs-per-token compute cost of transformers inference can be estimated as 2P , where P is the
305
      number of parameters in the model (Kaplan et al., 2020). The total FLOPs required for prompt
      processing for N tokens is N P . Thus, the ratio of the FLOPs of prompt processing to the ratio of
306
      FLOPs for a single generation step is N .
307
308   Let tN (N ) denote the forward pass FLOPs of network N with N input tokens. As described
309   in Section 4, producing the first output token using KV Prediction requires an N-token forward
310   pass of A, followed by an N-token forward pass of P, then a single-token forward pass of B (to
311
      generate the first output token using the predicted KV cache). The FLOPs taken to process the
      prompt and generate the first token is tA (N ) + tP (N ) + tB (1) = N tA (1) + tP (N ) + tB (1). The
312
      computational cost of tP (N ) is negligible compared to N tA (1), as it contains only a linear layer
313
      of smaller dimensionality than the transformer’s FFN layers. For sufficient N , N tA (1) ≫ tB (1),
314   and the FLOPs of a single inference are dominated by N tA (1). Thus, the relative improvement in
315   prompt processing FLOPs over the standard inference setup can be approximated as tA (1)/tB (1).
316
317
      Memory Usage: Our method requires deploying an auxiliary and predicter network in addition to
      the base network. The predicter is much smaller than a single transformer block, thus occupies
318
      negligible memory. The auxiliary network is generally a fraction of the size of the base network.
319
      Additionally, the auxiliary network can be unloaded from memory after prompt processing, as it is
320   not needed during generation. To avoid cold starts on the next query, the auxiliary model can be
321   reloaded into memory after inference. We do not employ this optimization in timing experiments.

346
347
      We experiment with our KV Prediction method to evaluate the improvement in TTFT and the accu-
348   racy retention. Here we present details of our experimental setup.
349
350   KV Prediction Models: We experiment with KV Prediction using OpenELM (Mehta et al., 2024),
351
      as (1) it provides a suitable efficient architecture for on-device execution, and (2) its layers have
      variable KV cache sizes, allowing us to test our method’s robustness to this challenging scenario.
352
353   To train our models, we reload the base and auxiliary model weights from pretrained OpenELM
354   models (in the case of KVP-LP experiments, we only reload the unpruned layers into the auxiliary
355   model). We follow the training hyperparameters of OpenELM, training on RefinedWeb (Penedo
356   et al., 2023), ArXiv (Clement et al., 2019), and Wikipedia (Foundation, 2024). We shorten the
357
      training regime to 70k iterations on 64 H100 GPUs at a token length of 2048 and a batch size of 16,
      since we are reloading pretrained weights. For code completion experiments, our training setup is
358
      the same, but we instead train on The Stack (Kocetkov et al., 2022) and divide the learning rate by
359
      10. A complete list of configs appears in our code release for reproducibility.
360
361   Baselines: Few works have explored methods for TTFT improvement applicable to on-device infer-
362   ence. Thus, our baselines follow Fu et al. (2024). Our first set of baselines uses token pruning. Our
363
      RP baseline consists of randomly pruning tokens from the input, sweeping across token retention
      ratios of [0.25, 0.50, 0.75]. Our SP baseline consists of probing the first 25% of network layers to
364
      obtain attention weights, then pruning tokens that have low attention scores and rerunning on the
365
      pruned tokens. We use token retention rates of [0.25, 0.50], omitting the higher retention rate of
366   0.75 since the overhead of processing 25% of the query with the unpruned input means that lower
367   retention ratios are needed to obtain a similar speedup to random pruning. Our L baseline consists
368   of LazyLLM (Fu et al., 2024). LazyLLM prunes at progressively higher rates through the network,
369   from an initial higher retention rate (or low pruning rate) to a final lower retention rate (or higher
370   pruning rate). We adopt the standard configuration suggested by the authors of beginning pruning
371   30% of the way into the network and ending pruning 90% of the way into the network. For L0.75,
372   our beginning retention rate (which is active 30% of the way into the network) is 75%, and we
373   sweep across ending retention rates of [0.75, 0.50, 0.25, 0.10, 0.05, 0.01]. For L0.50, our beginning
374
      retention rate is 0.50, and we sweep across end retention rates [0.50, 0.25, 0.10, 0.05, 0.01].
375   Our second set of baselines sweeps across model sizes in the OpenELM family. Our OE baseline
376   consists of OpenELM 1.1B, OpenELM450M, and OpenELM270M. Our OE-LP baseline consists
377   of OpenELM layer-pruned models, fine-tuned with the same settings as our KV Prediction models.
      See Appendix A for a detailed description of these layer-pruned architectures.

398
399   We analyze our method’s performance on language modeling benchmarks in the following sections.
400   Our main results are generative evaluations, which allow us to assess the compatibility of the initial
401   predicted KV cache with the autoregressively generated KV cache. In Section 6.1, we investigate the
402
      improvement in TTFT of our method compared to baselines on question-answering with TriviaQA
      (Joshi et al., 2017), demonstrating that our method produces the pareto-optimal efficiency-accuracy
403
      tradeoff. In section Section 6.2, we demonstrate that our model also achieves the pareto-optimal
404
      efficiency-accuracy tradeoff on python code completion with HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), sup-
405   porting the generality of our method. Finally, in Section 6.3, we analyze the on-device runtime
406   improvement in TTFT, demonstrating that our theoretical FLOPs reduction translates to runtime
407   improvement on real hardware.

410   6.1       Q UESTION -A NSWERING ON T RIVIAQA
411
412   We investigate our method’s efficiency-accuracy trade-off for OpenELM 1.1B and OpenELM 3B
413   in Fig. 3 (we also present these results in table format in Appendix B). We measure accuracy on
414   TriviaQA using LLMEvalHarness (Gao et al., 2021), and use the FLOPs speedup approximation
415   developed in Section 4.4.
416   Our method produces the strongest efficiency-accuracy trade-off, tracing a pareto-optimal curve. At
417   a fixed FLOPs reduction, our method achieves the highest accuracy. Equivalently, at fixed accuracy,
418   our method obtains the highest FLOPs reduction. The KVP-C strategy outperforms the KVP-LP
419   method, but the KVP-LP method is able to achieve higher accuracy retention because larger models
420   can be obtained. For instance, OE3B-KVP-LP-0.75 (Fig. 3, right side, top left point in the KVP-LP
421   curve) has roughly 2× as many parameters as OE3B-KVP-C-1.1B (Fig. 3, right side, top left point
422
      in the KVP-C curve). In all cases except OE3B-KVP-LP-0.25, our model produces higher accuracy
      at equivalent TTFT FLOPs compared to baselines.
423
424   Our OE baselines correspond to only using the auxiliary model architecture from the KVP-C experi-
425   ment (but with different weights). Directly comparing our KVP-C method to these baselines, we see
426   that our method strongly increases accuracy. A similar observation can be made when comparing
427   OE-LP and KVP-LP models.
428   Our naive token-pruning baselines of random pruning (RP) and static pruning (SP) do not perform
429   very well. As explored in Fu et al. (2024), SP can serve as a strong baseline when contexts are
430   extremely long (∼ 4k) tokens. We conjecture that performance is worse here because there are fewer
431   redundant tokens in TriviaQA evaluations. LazyLLM provides much higher accuracy retention than
      naive token-pruning, but accuracy decreases as we push to more aggressive FLOP reductions.

534   8   C ONCLUSION
535
536   We present a method for improving time to first token (TTFT) called KV Prediction. Our method
537   uses a small auxiliary model to efficiently predict the KV cache needed by a larger base model. We
538   analyze the theoretical and actual speedup of our model, as well as the accuracy retention. We find
539   that our model maintains a strong efficiency-accuracy trade-off, creating a pareto-optimal trade-off
      in terms of accuracy retention and TTFT.
