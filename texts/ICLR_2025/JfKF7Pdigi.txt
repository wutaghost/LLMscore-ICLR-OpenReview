009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing methods for fMRI-to-video reconstruction typically focus on accurately
012            reconstructing visual content (i.e., appearance), neglecting dynamic event informa-
013            tion. However, as highlighted in cognitive neurology, these key dynamic events
014            significantly influence brain signal changes during video perception. In this article,
015            we introduce M INDGRAPHER, a two-stream framework designed to address this
016
               gap by enhancing the reconstruction of dynamic-aware videos from fMRI data.
               M INDGRAPHER comprises i) a visual content reconstruction stream, that im-
017
               proves the accuracy of the reconstructed visual content from sparsely distributed
018
               fMRI data through a temporal dynamics enrichment approach and multi-moment
019            multimodal contrastive learning; ii) a dynamics injection stream, that firstly crafts
020            dynamic-aware fMRI embeddings and then integrates them into the reconstruction
021            process via a fine-grained approach, thereby producing videos that effectively
022            perceive dynamic events. Moreover, to address the lack of suitable metrics for
023            evaluating dynamic event information, we introduce a new evaluation metric named
024            dynamic content fidelity (DCF), which measures how accurately dynamic events
025            within the video are reconstructed. Upon evaluation with a publicly available
026            fMRI dataset, M INDGRAPHER outperforms the state-of-the-arts on all metrics,
027
               i.e., semantic classification accuracy, structural similarity index, and DCF. The
               reconstructed video results are available on MindGrapher. Code shall be released.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Human perception is an ever-evolving stream of visual experiences, akin to an uninterrupted movie
033   playing before our eyes. The brain, a masterful interpreter, seamlessly decodes this rapid succession
034   of visual stimuli (i.e., video), constructing coherent sensory reality (Tong & Pratte, 2012; Hasson
035   et al., 2004). However, the underlying complex patterns that facilitate this intricate process remain
036   a compelling puzzle within the realm of brain-computer interface (Lebedev & Nicolelis, 2006).
037   Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) has emerged as a critical tool in this quest, offering
038   insights into the brain activities associated with vision (Kwong et al., 1992). Despite substantial
039
      progress has been made in reconstructing static images from fMRI data (Lin et al., 2022; Scotti et al.,
      2023; Gu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Quan et al.,
040
      2024; Wang et al., 2024), capturing and reconstructing the temporal dynamics inherent in moving
041
      images from brain data (fMRI-to-video) continues to be a significant challenge (Buckner, 1998;
042   Dubois & Adolphs, 2016; Chen et al., 2023b).
043
044   Cognitive neurology research (Saygin et al., 2010; Grossman & Blake, 2002) underscores the
045
      significance of dynamic event information in the video stimuli, noting that human brains exhibit
      heightened sensitivity to these events during video perception. However, this aspect is largely
046
      overlooked in existing fMRI-to-video reconstruction methods (Wen et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022;
047
      Kupershmidt et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b). As a result, reconstructions that may be visually
048   accurate often fail to capture precise dynamic event information. For example, as illustrated in
049   Fig. 1, although the state-of-the-art (Chen et al., 2023b) can reconstruct visually correct videos,
050   such as a woman is holding something, it fails to capture the dynamic event in the corresponding
051   video stimulus, i.e., a woman is speaking into a microphone. This discrepancy arises because it
052   randomly selects a single frame from the video as the target and follows an fMRI-to-image fashion
053   for visual content reconstruction, while the dynamic event information is arbitrarily generated based
      on a pre-trained stable diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). This gap in the research calls for a

      To address this gap, we introduce M INDGRAPHER, a two-stream framework designed to recon-
      struct dynamic-aware videos from fMRI data. This framework is composed of ❶ a visual content
071
      reconstruction (VCR) stream for generating accurate visual content, and ❷ a dynamics injection (DI)
072
      stream designed to generate and integrate the learned dynamic event information into the video recon-
073   struction process. More precisely, VCR stream firstly enhances the temporal dynamics for sparsely
074   distributed fMRI data, considering the disparity between the temporal resolution of fMRI frames
075   and typical video frames, i.e., an fMRI frame takes 2 seconds while the video has about 30 frames
076   per second (Fig. 1). The enriched fMRI data then serve as a basis for multi-moment multimodal
077   contrastive learning, which leverages temporal coherence between video frames and the enriched
078   fMRI frames to align and synchronize these two modalities through contrastive learning. By utilizing
079   such a multi-moment-based alignment, VCR stream can enhance the accuracy of reconstructed
080   visual content from fMRI data. Then, the resulting still visual content serves as the initial frame for
081
      the video reconstruction process. Subsequently, DI Stream focuses on generating dynamic-aware
      fMRI embeddings by aligning with dynamic event information extracted from corresponding video
082
      stimuli. These embeddings are then served as dynamics injected into the reconstructing process. This
083
      fine-grained integration is achieved by modifying spatial attention layers within a diffusion-based
084   method, ensuring that the dynamic events are vividly portrayed in the reconstructed videos. Our
085   VCR stream focuses on reconstructing precise still visual content, while DI stream generates and
086   integrates dynamics into the reconstruction. Together, these two streams collaborate to reconstruct
087   dynamic-aware videos given the fMRI data as input.
088
      M INDGRAPHER stands out with several attractive qualities: First, it enhances the accuracy and
089
      robustness of visual content reconstruction through its VCR stream, which enriches the temporal
090   dynamics of fMRI data and then conducts multi-moment multimodal contrastive learning. Second, it
091   excels in understanding and recreating the dynamic aspects of stimuli by introducing dynamic-aware
092   fMRI embeddings and implementing fine-grained dynamics integration into a diffusion-based model.
093   M INDGRAPHER, as a pioneering framework in the field of fMRI-to-video reconstruction, is capable
094   of translating neural signals into coherent and dynamic video representations.
095
      In addition, existing fMRI-to-video methods typically rely on an N -way top-K classification test
096   to measure the accuracy of video semantics. That only considers the static visual content matching
097   between the reconstructed video and the video stimulus, neglecting the dynamic event information. We
098   argue that a comprehensive evaluation of the reconstructed video should encompass the accuracy of
099   static visual content and dynamic event information, both are crucial for fMRI-to-video reconstruction.
100   Therefore, we introduce a new evaluation metric named Dynamic Content Fidelity (DCF). This
101   video-based metric takes the first step towards quantitatively assessing how accurately dynamic events
102   within the video are reconstructed, providing a more holistic measure of video reconstruction quality.
103   Emphasizing the reconstruction of dynamic event information beyond mere visual appearance, M IND -
104   GRAPHER significantly outperforms state-of-the-art competitors: it achieves higher performance
105   across all metrics, including semantic classification accuracy, structural similarity index (SSIM), and
106   DCF on all subjects. Both qualitative and quantitative evaluations collectively underscore M INDGRA -
107   PHER ’s superior ability to reconstruct dynamic-aware videos that more accurately reflect both the
      visual and temporal nuances encoded in brain activity. Our code will be released.

111   Our work draws on existing literature in image and video reconstruction from fMRI, and video
112   generation with diffusion models. For brevity, only the most relevant works are discussed.
113
      fMRI-to-Image Reconstruction. Early methods in fMRI-to-Image reconstruction (Kay et al., 2008;
114
      Naselaris et al., 2009; Fujiwara et al., 2013) rely on paired fMRI-image data, utilizing sparse linear
115   regression to decode fMRI signals into visual features. Recent advancements have significantly
116   enhanced these techniques, notably through the integration of brain signals with the latent spaces
117   of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Lin et al., 2022; Mozafari et al., 2020; Ozcelik et al.,
118   2022). The advent of multimodal vision-language models (Radford et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2019;
119   Ramesh et al., 2021), alongside diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song &
120   Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) and expansive fMRI datasets (Van Essen et al.,
121   2013; Horikawa & Kamitani, 2017; Chang et al., 2019; Allen et al., 2022), has propelled the quality of
122   image reconstruction from fMRI to new heights. By employing diffusion models, researchers develop
123
      innovative methods to translate fMRI signals into CLIP space embeddings (Ozcelik & VanRullen,
      2023; Scotti et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Quan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023a;
124
      Ma et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024; Ferrante et al., 2023; Benchetrit et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024;
125
      Tang et al., 2022; Mai et al., 2023; Kneeland et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023a;b; Huo
126   et al., 2024; Mai & Zhang, 2023). This is achieved through the development of bespoke models for
127   subjects, which are then interfaced with pre-trained diffusion models capable of processing multiple
128   inputs, thus elevating the precision of reconstructed images.
129
      M INDGRAPHER sets itself apart by addressing the more challenging task of reconstructing videos
130
      from fMRI data. While it effectively captures static visual content, it places a strong emphasis on
131
      dynamic event reconstruction. To achieve this, it incorporates two specialized streams: VCR stream
132   enhances the accuracy of static visual content, and DI stream injects dynamic event information.
133
134   fMRI-to-Video Reconstruction. While the field of reconstructing static images from brain activities
135
      continues to evolve, the endeavor to decode videos presents a more intricate challenge (Wen et al.,
      2018; Wang et al., 2022; Kupershmidt et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b). Traditional fMRI-to-
136
      video methods treated video reconstruction as a set of image reconstructions, a process that, while
137
      demonstrating feasibility, suffered from inherent limitations in frame rate and consistency (Wen et al.,
138   2018). Advancements in video reconstruction have been marked by the use of conditional video
139   GANs for improved quality and frame rates (Wang et al., 2022), despite challenges posed by data
140   scarcity. The adoption of separable autoencoders conducive to self-supervised learning marked a
141   significant step forward, albeit with limitations in visual quality and semantic depth. Notably, Chen
142   et al. (2023b) introduced the application of contrastive learning in combination with spatial-temporal
143   attention mechanisms, mapping fMRI data into the CLIP embedding space.
144   Existing fMRI-to-video reconstruction methods often treat videos as a sequence of isolated frames,
145   e.g., Chen et al. (2023b) approaches the learning of semantically meaningful fMRI features by
146   randomly selecting individual frames from the target video, akin to fMRI-to-Image methods. Thereby,
147   it overlooks the dynamic event information embedded in the video stimulus data. In contrast to it,
148   M INDGRAPHER employs a two-stream framework that integrates both visual content reconstruction
149   and dynamic-aware fMRI embedding injection. It enables both static and dynamic aspects of the
150   video to be accurately reconstructed, offering a more holistic representation of the original stimuli.
151   Video Generation with Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), originally developed
152   for static image synthesis, have recently been adopted to tackle the more complex challenge of
153   video generation (Ho et al., 2022), marking a pivotal advancement in the realm of generative models.
154   Notably, the introduction of temporal coherence and dynamic scene understanding into the diffusion
155   process has been a critical focus. These models have been instrumental in pushing the boundaries of
156   video resolution and fidelity. Furthermore, the integration of temporal attention mechanisms (Singer
157   et al., 2023), and the adaptation of latent Diffusion Models for video synthesis (Zhou et al., 2022; He
158
      et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2024), underscore the rapid evolution of
      this field. These advancements enhance the visual quality of the generated videos.
159
160   Building on these foundations, M INDGRAPHER incorporates a dynamic-aware fMRI modeling
161   module and a fine-grained integration approach. This enables the generation of high-quality videos
      that accurately capture dynamic events from the original stimuli. Furthermore, we introduce a new

185   3      M ETHODOLOGY
186
187   Our objective is to reconstruct videos given fMRI data as inputs, ensuring that these videos precisely
188   represent dynamic event information. Inspired by the principles of human perception of videos
189   mentioned in §1, we conceptualize the video reconstruction process as a composition of visual
190   content augmented with dynamic event information. To achieve this, we introduce M INDGRAPHER, a
191   two-stream framework that systematically addresses the dual aspects of video reconstruction: Visual
192
      Content Reconstruction (VCR) stream (§3.1) and Dynamics Injection (DI) stream (§3.2). In addition,
      we introduce a new evaluation metric, Dynamic Content Fidelity(DCF), in §3.3. Finally, we provide
193
      the implementation details in §3.4. An overview of M INDGRAPHER can be found in Fig. 2.

      3.3   DYNAMIC C ONTENT F IDELITY (DCF) M ETRIC
284
285   Existing fMRI-to-video methods (Chen et al., 2023b; Sun et al., 2024) typically adopt an N -way
286   top-K classification test to measure the accuracy of video semantics. We argue that it only considers
287   the static visual content matching between the reconstructed video and the video stimulus, neglecting
288   the dynamic event information. Therefore, we introduce a new evaluation metric called DCF. This
289   metric measures how accurately the dynamic events in the reconstructed videos align with those in
290   the ground truth video stimuli. Concretely, the calculation process of DCF involves the following
291
      three steps: i) dynamic-aware caption generation, ii) embedding encoding, and iii) embedding
292   distance measurement. Specifically, for each reconstructed video În′ in test set Dt (|Dt | = N ′ ),
293   its dynamic event description (Ĉn′ ) and the one (Cn′ ) of ground truth video stimulus are generated
294   using Video-LLaVA. Then, we encode both sets of captions using the CLIP text encoder (Cherti et al.,
295   2023; Zhu et al., 2024) to obtain their respective embeddings, Ĉn′ and Cn′ . Finally, we calculate the
296
      cosine similarity between the caption embeddings, represented as:

                                        DCF =            sim(Ĉn′ , Cn′ ).                                  (5)

299   The DCF metric thus provides a quantitative measure of how well the dynamic events in the recon-
300   structed video match those in the original video stimulus. In addition, we conduct a user study to

302

307   3.4   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
308
309   Network Architecture. M INDGRAPHER is designed as a two-stream framework, each tailored
310   for specific aspects of video reconstruction from fMRI data. VCR stream employs a multi-layer
311   perceptron (MLP) backbone as the fMRI encoder, inspired by MindEye (Scotti et al., 2023). For
312   dynamic information processing in DI stream, the same fMRI encoder employs a different projector
313   to align with the extracted textual dynamics embeddings. Both the MLP backbone and the projectors
314
      follow the architecture outlined in MindEye.
315   Training. The training process for M INDGRAPHER is conducted separately for each stream to
316   optimize performance. VCR Stream: Initially, the fMRI encoder is trained using a contrastive
317   loss (see Eq. 2), focusing on aligning fMRI data with corresponding visual content embeddings.
318   Subsequently, co-training involves updating the fMRI encoder (the whole model is updated) in
319   conjunction with the visual content decoder, versatile diffusion model (only U-Net is updated). This
320
      training and co-training process are carried out on the designated training dataset Ds . DI Stream:
      After the dynamic visual event descriptions are extracted and converted into textual embeddings, the
321
      fMRI encoder is further trained using the dynamics alignment contrastive loss (refer to Eq. 4).
322
323   Inference. Visual contents are generated using 20 diffusion steps and all video results are recon-
      structed using 50 diffusion steps.

      4.1           E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
347   Dataset. We adopt a publicly accessible fMRI-video dataset, as detailed by (Wen et al., 2018).
348   This dataset includes fMRI data captured using a 3T MRI scanner at a 2-second repetition time
349   (TR), involving three participants. The train set comprises 18 video clips, each lasting 8 minutes,
350   summing up to 2.4 hours of video, which translates to 4,320 paired training examples. The test
351   set contains five 8-minute video clip sections, totaling 40 minutes, and providing 1,200 test fMRI
352   scans. These video clips, displayed at 30 FPS, encompass a wide array of content, featuring animals,
353   humans, and natural landscapes, offering a rich basis for evaluating our method’s capabilities.
354   Evaluation Metrics. In addition to DCF metric (§3.3), we also employ a dual-tiered evaluation
355   strategy following Chen et al. (2023b), incorporating both frame-based and video-based metrics.
356   • Frame-based Metrics: At the pixel level, the Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) (Wang
357      et al., 2004) is utilized to evaluate the visual fidelity of each reconstructed frame against the ground
358      truth. On the semantics level, an N -way top-K accuracy classification test is adopted, where the
359      predicted frames are compared against the ground truth using an ImageNet classifier. A successful
360      classification is noted when the ground truth class is within the top-K predictions from N randomly
361      chosen classes, including the ground truth. This process is repeated 100 times to establish an
362      average success rate.
363   • Video-based Metrics: It firstly employs a video classifier trained on the Kinetics-400 dataset (Kay
364
         et al., 2017), which is based on VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022). This classifier assesses the semantic
         content of the reconstructed videos across 400 categories, encapsulating various motions and human
365
         interactions. This video-based assessment complements our frame-based metrics, offering insights
366
         into the model’s ability to reconstruct coherent and semantically rich video sequences.

507   6                        I NTERPRETABILITY
508
509   We illustrate the interpretability of our method in Fig. 6. Fig. 6a presents the fMRI embeddings
510   within the Yeo17 networks (Yeo et al., 2011), showcasing the activation levels of different brain
511   areas. Additionally, the voxel-wise values are visualized on a brain flat map in Fig. 6b, revealing
512   comprehensive structural patterns across the whole region. Fig. 6a shows that the visual cortex,
513   including both central (VisCent) and peripheral (VisPeri) fields, stands out as the most influential
514
      region. This result aligns with previous research, which underscores the critical role of the visual
      cortex in processing both visual spatiotemporal information (van Hateren & Ruderman, 1998) and
515
      dynamic stimuli (Wu et al., 2011). However, vision is a complex process that is not solely governed
516
      by this region. Other higher cognitive networks, such as the dorsal attention network (DorsAttn)
517   involved in the voluntary control of visuospatial attention (Kincade et al., 2005), and the default mode
518   network (Default), associated with introspection and recollection (Andrews-Hanna, 2012), also play
519   crucial roles in the perception of visual stimuli (Hasson et al., 2008).
520
      To further elucidate the effects of dynamic information integration, we analyze activation values
521
      within different brain networks before and after the dynamics injection process (Fig. 6b). After
522
      the injection, we observe increased activation in higher cognitive networks, while activity in the
523   visual cortex decreases. This shift suggests that as the encoder integrates dynamic information, it
524   progressively assimilates higher-level semantic representations, indicating a transition from low-level
525   sensory processing to more advanced cognitive processing.

      7                        C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we propose M INDGRAPHER, an innovative two-stream framework dedicated to recon-
531
      structing dynamic-aware videos from fMRI data. Unlike existing methods that follow an fMRI-to-
      image-based pipeline, focusing primarily on static visual content reconstruction, M INDGRAPHER
532
      enables the reconstruction of dynamic-aware videos that accurately capture the dynamic event in-
533
      formation in the original stimuli. Importantly, our framework is the first to quantitatively assess the
534   accuracy of dynamic event reconstruction, providing a more comprehensive metric for evaluating
535   video reconstruction quality. Experimental results on a publicly available fMRI dataset demonstrate
536   the superiority of our method over existing techniques. M INDGRAPHER not only enhances the
537   accuracy and fidelity of reconstructed videos but also provides a more comprehensive representation
538   of dynamic events as perceived in the original stimuli. Our work represents a significant stride in
539   decoding and reconstructing the human mind’s cinematic experiences, paving the way for future
      research into the complex interplay of brain activity and perception.

818   A    M ORE I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
819
820   Training. VCR Stream: Following Chen et al. (2023b), we apply data augmentations across all
821   modalities to enhance the robustness of the model. For the fMRI data, we implement random
822
      sparsification where 20% of the voxels are randomly set to zero in each iteration, improving the
      model’s generalization. For the video data, we apply random cropping with a probability of 0.5,
823
      making the model invariant to minor spatial variations. During the multi-moment multimodal
824
      contrastive learning phase, the batch sizes for the three subjects are set to 2048, 2048, and 2048,
825   respectively. This phase involves 1,000 training steps with a learning rate of 3×10−4 . During the
826   co-training phase, the entire fMRI encoder is updated while only a portion of the versatile diffusion
827   model is adjusted, following the approach in Chen et al. (2023a). This phase uses a learning rate of
828   6×10−5 and is performed for 12,000 steps. The selective updating of the versatile diffusion model
829   ensures that the core structure of the model remains stable while allowing for fine-tuning based on
830   the fMRI data. DI Stream: When acquiring dynamic-aware fMRI embeddings through the process
831   described in Eq. 4, the batch size is set to 2048. This training process is carried out for 1,000 steps
832   with a learning rate of 3×10−4 .
833   Inference. During inference, the average time required to compute the DCF metric for each recon-
834   structed sample is approximately 0.04 seconds, demonstrating that the evaluation process is efficient
835   and can be considered real-time.

893   D             G ENERALIZATION
894
895   Due to the subject-specific nature of fMRI data, generalization across different subjects is a critical
896   challenge in fMRI-to-video reconstruction. Variations in brain anatomy, neural activity patterns, and
897   even individual cognitive processing make it difficult for models trained on data from one subject
898   to generalize effectively to other subjects. To evaluate the cross-subject generalization ability of
899   our method, we train both our method and the Mind-Video model on fMRI data from subject 1
900   and then evaluate their performance on unseen data from subject 2 and 3. The results, presented in
901
      Table 4, clearly demonstrate that our model outperforms Mind-Video in terms of generalization ability.
      Specifically, our method exhibits significantly better performance across multiple evaluation metrics
902
      when applied to the fMRI data of subject 2 and subject 3. For example, when evaluating on the fMRI
903
      data of subject 2, our model achieves an SSIM score of 0.224, compared to 0.107 for Mind-Video, and
904   similar improvements are observed on subject 3 (i.e., 0.226 vs. 0.126). This enhanced generalization
905   capability is crucial for practical applications of fMRI-to-video reconstruction where subject-specific
906   training data may not always be available.

1022   Despite the significant advancements presented by M INDGRAPHER, there are still several limitations.
1023   First, the quality of reconstructed videos heavily depends on the availability and quality of the
1024   fMRI data. Limited by the current dataset (Wen et al., 2018), the model’s performance may vary
1025   with different types and quality of fMRI scans. Second, our current reconstruction results are not
       good at capturing detailed features of humans or animals. This limitation is due to the pre-trained

1027   reconstruction of these subjects. Third, while M INDGRAPHER demonstrates superior performance on
1028   current dataset, its generalizability to real-world scenarios needs further exploration and validation.
