009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             We investigate whether transformers can learn to track a random process when
012             given observations of a related process and parameters of the dynamical system
013             that relates them as context. More specifically, we consider a finite-dimensional
014             state-space model described by the state transition matrix F , measurement ma-
015             trices h1 , . . . , hN , and the process and measurement noise covariance matrices
016
                Q and R, respectively; these parameters, randomly sampled, are provided to the
                transformer along with the observations y1 , . . . , yN generated by the correspond-
017
                ing linear dynamical system. We argue that in such settings transformers learn to
018
                approximate the celebrated Kalman filter, and empirically verify this both for the
019             task of estimating hidden states x̂N |1,2,3,...,N as well as for one-step prediction of
020             the (N + 1)st observation, ŷN +1|1,2,3,...,N . A further study of the transformer’s
021             robustness reveals that its performance is retained even if the model’s parameters
022             are partially withheld. In particular, we demonstrate that the transformer remains
023             accurate at the considered task even in the absence of state transition and noise
024             covariance matrices, effectively emulating operations of the Dual-Kalman filter.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
      In-context learning, in particular few-shot prompting (Yogatama et al., 2019), is a growing area of
029
      research in natural language processing (NLP). In this framework, a large language model (LLM)
030
      learns tasks from a relatively few examples, i.e., few demonstrations of input-output pairs. One of
031   the earliest works to show that LLMs are capable of being fine-tuned when provided with prompts
032   was by Brown et al. (2020); there, the authors evaluated the GPT-3 model over a plethora of NLP
033   datasets and various “zero-shot”, “one-shot”, and “few-shot” learning tasks. In (Zhao et al., 2021),
034   the authors implicate majority label bias, recency bias, and common token bias as the reasons for
035   instability in GPT-3’s accuracy following few-shot prompting and propose a contextual calibra-
036   tion procedure as a remedy. A theoretical analysis proving that language models perform implicit
037   Bayesian inference is presented in (Xie et al., 2021). Min et al. (2022) explore the reasons why
038   in-context learning works, show that in-context learning is not affected by the lack of ground-truth
039
      labels, and posit that the label space and the distribution of the input text along with the format of
      the prompts play a crucial role. Moreover, Schlag et al. (2021) theoretically show that transformers
040
      are fast weight programmers.
041
042   Early works that explore using standard transformer decoders to in-context learn auto-regressive
043   models include Garg et al. (2022); there, the authors empirically investigate the ability of transform-
044   ers to learn classes of linear functions. They say that a model learns a function class F with domain
045
      X if for any f ∈ F and for any x1 , x2 , ..., xN , xquery sampled from X in an IID fashion, the model is
      able to predict the output f (xquery ) given the sequence x1 , f (x1 ), x2 , f (x2 ), ..., xN , f (xN ), xquery .
046
      The classes explored in Garg et al. (2022) range from simple linear functions to sparse linear func-
047
      tions, two-layered neural networks, and decision trees. Two parallel works, Von Oswald et al. (2023)
048   and Akyürek et al. (2023), explored which algorithms does the transformer resemble the most as it
049   learns the functional classes in-context. In Von Oswald et al. (2023), the authors build on the work
050   of Schlag et al. (2021) to elegantly show that the transformations induced by linear self-attention can
051   be perceived as equivalent to a gradient descent step. In other words, for a single 1-head linear self
052   attention layer there exist key, query, and value matrices such that a forward pass on the transformer
053   resembles the execution of one step of gradient descent with L2 loss on every token. Akyürek et al.
      (2023) take a fundamentally different approach, defining a raw operator that can be used to perform


                                                             1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      various operations on the input tokens including matrix multiplication, scalar division, and read-
055   write; they then show that a single transformer head with appropriate key, query, and value matrices
056   can approximate the raw operator. This implies that by using the operations readily implemented
057   by the raw operator, transformers are in principle capable of implementing linear regression via
058   stochastic gradient descent or closed-form regression.
059
      In this work, we investigate whether in-context learning may enable a transformer to predict states
060
      and/or outputs of a linear dynamical system described by a state-space model with non-scalar state
061   transition matrix, non-zero process noise, and white measurement noise. For such systems, Kalman
062   filter (Kalman, 1960) is the optimal (in the mean-square error sense) linear state estimator. We in-
063   vestigate what algorithm the transformer most closely resembles as it learns to perform one-step
064   prediction when provided context in the form of observations generated by a system with arbi-
065   trarily sampled state transition matrix, time-varying measurement matrices, and the process and
066   observation noise covariance matrices. We show that Kalman filtering can be expressed in terms
067   of operations readily approximated by a transformer; this implies that when given the observations
068   and system parameters as context, the transformers can in principle emulate the Kalman filtering
069
      algorithm. This is corroborated by extensive experimental results which demonstrate that such in-
      context learning leads to behavior closely mimicking the Kalman filter when the context lengths
070
      are sufficiently large. Interestingly, the transformer appears capable of emulating the Kalman filter
071
      even if some of the parameters are withheld from the provided context, suggesting robustness and
072   potential ability to implicitly learn those parameters from the remaining context.
073
074   Prior works that investigate interplay between deep learning and Kalman filtering notably include
075
      Deep Kalman Filters Krishnan et al. (2015) and Kalman Nets (Revach et al., 2021); the latter is a
      framework that circumvents the need for accurate estimates of system parameters by learning the
076
      Kalman gain in a data-driven fashion using a recurrent neural network. The follow-up work (Revach
077
      et al., 2022) employs gated recurrent units to estimate the Kalman gain and noise statistics while
078   training and evaluating, as in (Revach et al., 2021), the proposed model on data generated by a sys-
079   tem with fixed parameters. In contrast, in our work the model parameters are randomly sampled
080   to generate each training example, leading transformer to learn how to perform filtering rather than
081   memorize input-output relationship of a specific system. Dao & Gu (2024) study the theoretical
082   connections between structured state space models (SSMs) and variants of attentions. The central
083   message of their work is that the computations of various SSMs can be re-expressed as matrix mul-
084   tiplication algorithms on structured matrices, an insight that can be utilized to show the relationship
085   between selective SSMs and attention to make the latter efficient. Sieber et al. (2024) introduce a
086
      dynamical systems framework (DSF) to find a common representation unifying attention, SSMs,
      Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs. However, these works bear no relevance to the
087
      problem of state estimation, filtering, or in-context learning in general.Goel & Bartlett (2024) show
088
      that softmax self attention can represent Nadarya-Watson smoothing estimator, and proceed to ar-
089   gue that this estimator approximates Kalman filter. In contrast, we explicitly focus on the problem
090   of in-context learning and build on the concepts proposed by Akyürek et al. (2023) to show that
091   transformers implement exact operations needed to perform Kalman filtering, supporting these ar-
092   guments with extensive empirical results. To the best of our knowledge, the current paper reports the
093   first study of the ability of transformers to in-context learn to emulate Kalman filter using examples
094   generated by randomly sampling parameters of an underlying dynamical system.
095   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 provides an overview of relevant
096   background. Section 3 lays out the system model and presents theoretical arguments that transform-
097   ers can in-context learn to implement Kalman filtering for white observation noise. Section 4 reports
098   the simulation results, including empirical studies of the robustness to missing model parameters,
099   while Section 5 concludes the paper.

132   Let us consider linear dynamical systems described by a finite-dimensional state-space model in-
133   volving hidden states xt ∈ Rn and observations (i.e., measurements) yt ∈ Rm related through the
134   system of equations
135                                            xt+1 = Ft xt + qt                                         (4)
136
                                                 yt = Ht xt + rt .                                       (5)
137
138   The state equation (4), parameterized by the state transition matrix Ft ∈ Rn×n and the covariance
139   Q of the stationary zero-mean white process noise qt ∈ Rn , captures the temporal evolution of the
140   state vector. The measurement equation (5), parameterized by the measurement matrix Ht ∈ Rm×n
141   and the covariance R of the stationary zero-mean white measurement noise rt ∈ Rm , specifies the
142   acquisition of observations yt via linear transformation of states xt . Such state-space models have
143   proved invaluable in machine learning (Gu et al., 2021), computational neuroscience (Barbieri et al.,
144
      2004), control theory (Kailath, 1980), signal processing (Kailath et al., 2000), economics (Zeng &
      Wu, 2013), and other fields. Many applications across these fields are concerned with learning the
145
      hidden states xt given the noisy observations yt and the parameters of the state space model.
146

531
      In this paper, we explored the capability of transformers to emulate the behavior of Kalman filter
532
      when trained in-context with the randomly sampled parameters of a state space model and the cor-
533
      responding observations. We provided analytical arguments in support of the transformer’s ability
534   to do so, and presented empirical results of the experiments that demonstrate close proximity of the
535   transformer and Kalman filter when the transformer is given sufficiently long context. Notably, the
536   transformer keeps closely approximating Kalman filter even when important context – namely, noise
537   covariance matrices and even state transition matrix (all required by the Kalman filter) – is omitted,
538   demonstrating robustness and implying the ability to implicitly learn missing context. Future work
539   includes extensions to temporally correlated noise and further investigation of robustness to missing
      model parameters.
