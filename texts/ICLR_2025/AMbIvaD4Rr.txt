011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances toward foundation models for routing problems have shown great
014            potential of a unified deep model for various VRP variants. However, they over-
015            look the complex real-world customer distributions. In this work, we advance the
016            Multi-Task VRP (MTVRP) setting to the more realistic yet challenging Multi-
017            Task Multi-Distribution VRP (MTMDVRP) setting, and introduce SHIELD, a
018            novel model that leverages both sparsity and hierarchy principles. Building on
019
               a deeper decoder architecture, we first incorporate the Mixture-of-Depths (MoD)
               technique to enforce sparsity. This improves both efficiency and generalization by
020
               allowing the model to dynamically choose whether to use or skip each decoder
021
               layer, providing the needed capacity to adaptively allocate computation for learn-
022            ing the task/distribution specific and shared representations. We also develop a
023            context-based clustering layer that exploits the presence of hierarchical structures
024            in the problems to produce better local representations. These two designs in-
025            ductively bias the network to identify key features that are common across tasks
026            and distributions, leading to significantly improved generalization on unseen ones.
027            Our empirical results demonstrate the superiority of our approach over existing
028            methods on 9 real-world maps with 16 VRP variants each.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Combinatorial optimization problems (COPs) appear in many real-world applications, such as lo-
      gistics (Cattaruzza et al., 2017) and DNA sequencing (Caserta & Voß, 2014), and have historically
034
      attracted significant attention (Bengio et al., 2021). A key example of COPs is the Vehicle Routing
035
      Problem (VRP), which asks: Given a set of customers, what is the optimal set of routes for a fleet
036   of vehicles to minimize overall costs while satisfying all constraints? Traditionally, they are solved
037   with exact or approximate solvers. However, these solvers are either inefficient for large instances or
038   rely heavily on expert-designed heuristic rules. Recently, the emerging Neural Combinatorial Opti-
039   mization (NCO) community has been increasingly focused on developing novel neural solvers for
040   VRPs based on deep (reinforcement) learning (Kool et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2020; Bogyrbayeva
041   et al., 2024). These solvers learn to construct solutions autoregressively, improving efficiency and
042   reducing the need for domain knowledge, showing significant promise over traditional solvers.
043   Motivated by the recent breakthroughs in foundation models (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020; Touvron
044   et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023), a notable trend in the NCO community is the push towards
045   developing a unified neural solver for handling multiple VRP variants, known as the Multi-Task VRP
046   (MTVRP) setting (Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Berto et al., 2024). These solvers are trained
047   on multiple VRP variants and show impressive zero-shot generalization to new tasks. Compared
048   to single-task solvers, unified solvers offer a key advantage: there is no longer a need to construct
049   different solvers or heuristics for each specific problem variant. However, despite the importance
050
      of the MTVRP setup, it does not fully capture real-world industrial applications, as the underlying
      distributions are assumed to be uniform, lacking the structural properties of real-world data.
051
052   In this work, we extend the MTVRP framework to real-world scenarios by incorporating realistic
053   distributions (Goh et al., 2024). Consider, for example, a logistics company operating across multi-
      ple cities/countries, with each region having a fixed set of M locations, governed by its geographical


                                                        1

054
      layout. When a subset of V orders arises, the problem is reduced to serving only those customers.
055   To model this, we generate realistic distributions by selecting smaller subsets of V from the fixed
056   set of M locations, ensuring that V retains the geographical distribution characteristics of M . A
057   unified model with strong performance across tasks and distributions allows for flexible, efficient
058   deployment. This transforms MTVRP into the Multi-Task Multi-Distribution VRP (MTMDVRP), a
059   novel and challenging setting that, to our knowledge, has not been explored in the literature.
060
      Nevertheless, MTMDVRP poses unique challenges for learning unified neural VRP models. First,
061   beyond managing the diverse constraints of MTVRP, the model must further learn to handle ar-
062   bitrary, distribution-specific layouts. Unfortunately, task-related contexts often interdepend with
063   distribution-related contexts during decision-making (e.g., selecting the next node), adding fur-
064   ther complexity. Moreover, balancing shared and task/distribution-specific representations becomes
065   more difficult, as the model needs to generalize across a broader representation space to serve as
066   a more foundational NCO model. Consequently, this calls for learning unified deep models that
067   balances the expressiveness required for complex decision-making with the simplicity needed for
068   efficient generalization – an issue we explore in depth in this paper.
069   To this end, we introduce Sparsity & Hierarchy in Efficiently Layered Decoder (SHIELD) to address
070   the above challenges with two key innovations. First, SHIELD leverages sparsity by incorporating a
071   customized Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) approach (Raposo et al., 2024) to the NCO decoders. While
072   adding more decoder layers can improve predictive power, the autoregressive nature of neural VRP
073   solver significantly hampers efficiency. In contrast, our MoD is designed to dynamically adjust
074   the proper computational depth (number of decoder layers) based on the decision context. This
075
      allows adaptively allocated computation for learning the task/distribution specific and shared repre-
      sentations, while acting as a regularization mechanism to prevent overfitting by possibly reducing
076
      redundant computations. Secondly, we employ a clustering mechanism that considers hierarchy dur-
077
      ing node selection by forcing the learning of a small set of key representations of unvisited nodes,
078   enabling compact modeling of the complex decision-making information. Together, these two de-
079   signs encourage the model to learn some compact, simple, generalizable representations with limited
080   computational budgets, enhancing generalization across tasks and distributions, which is also in line
081   with the Information Bottleneck perspective. This paper highlights the following contributions:
082
083          • We propose Multi-Task Multi-Distribution VRP (MTMDVRP), a novel, more realistic yet
084            challenging setting that better represents real-world industry scenarios.
085          • We present SHIELD, a neural solver that leverages sparsity through a customized NCO
086            decoder with MoD layers and hierarchy through context-based cluster representation, ad-
087            vancing towards a more generalizable foundation model for neural VRP solvers.
088          • We demonstrate the impressive in-distribution and generalization benefits of SHIELD via
089            extensive experiments across 9 real-world maps and 16 VRP variants, achieving state-of-
090            the-art performance compared to existing unified neural VRP solvers.

      Multi-task VRP Solver. Recent work in (Liu et al., 2024) explored training of a Multi-Task VRP
095
      solver across a range of VRP variants which share a set of common features indicating the presence
096   or absence of specific constraints. Zhou et al. (2024) enhanced the model architecture by introduc-
097   ing a Mixture-of-Experts within the transformer layers, allowing the model to effectively capture
098   representations tailored to different tasks. These studies focus on zero-shot generalization, where
099   models are trained on a subset of tasks and evaluated on unseen tasks that are combinations of com-
100   mon features. Additionally, other studies (Wang & Yu, 2023; Drakulic et al., 2024) investigate this
101   promising direction, but with different problem settings. Alternatively, Berto et al. (2024) improved
102   convergence robustness by training on all possible tasks within a batch using a mixed environment.
103   In this work, we mainly build on the setting presented by Liu et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024).
104   Generalization Study. Joshi et al. (2021) highlighted the generalization challenge faced by neural
105   combinatorial solvers, where their performance drops significantly on out-of-distribution (OOD)
106   instances. Numerous studies have sought to improve generalization performance in cross-size (Bdeir
107   et al., 2022; Son et al., 2023), cross-distribution (Wang et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022; Bi et al.,
      2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), and cross-task (Lin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Zhou

      CVRP and its Variants. The CVRP is defined as an instance of N nodes in a graph G = {V, E},
      where the depot node is denoted as v0 , customer nodes are denoted as {vi }N          i=1 ∈ V, and edges
119
      are defined as e(vi , vj ) ∈ E between nodes vi and vj such that i ̸= j. Every customer node has
120
      a demand δi , and every vehicle has a maximum capacity limit Q. For a given problem, the final
121   solution (tour) can be presented as a sequence of nodes with multiple sub-tours. Each sub-tour
122   represents a vehicle’s path, starting and ending at the depot. As a vehicle visits a customer node,
123   the demand is fulfilled and subtracted from the vehicle’s capacity. A solution is considered feasible
124   if each customer node is visited exactly once, and the total demand in a sub-tour does not exceed
125   the capacity limit of the vehicle. In this paper, we consider the nodes defined in Euclidean space
126   within a unit square [0, 1], and the overall cost of a solution, c(·), is calculated via the total Euclidean
127   distance of all sub-tours. The objective is to find the optimal tour τ ∗ such that the cost is minimized,
128   given by τ ∗ = argminτ ∈Φ c(τ |G) where Φ defines the set of all possible solutions.
129   We define the following practical constraints that are integrated with CVRP: (1) Open route (O):
130   The vehicle is no longer required to return to the depot after visiting the customers; (2) Backhaul
131   (B): Demand δi is a positive value, indicating that goods are unloaded at a customer node. Instead,
132   demand on some nodes can be negative, meaning that these nodes will load goods into the vehicle.
133   Practically, this mimics the pick-up and drop-off scenarios in logistics. We label nodes with positive
134   demand δi > 0 as linehauls, and nodes with negative demand δi < 0 as backhauls. Note that routes
135
      can have a mixed sequence of linehauls and backhauls without strict precedence; (3) Duration Limit
      (L): Each sub-tour is upper bounded by a threshold limit on the total length; (4) Time Window (TW):
136
      Each node vi is defined with a time window [wio , wic ], signifying the opening and close times of the
137
      window, and si the service time at a node. Essentially, a customer can only be served if the vehicle
138   arrives within the time window, and the total time taken at the node is the service time. If a vehicle
139   arrives earlier, it has to wait until wio . All vehicles have to return to the depot before w0c .

      Mixture-of-Experts and Mixture-of-Depths. Previous work (Liu et al., 2024) demonstrated the
163   ability of state-of-the-art transformers such as POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) to generalize across
164   MTVRP instances. More recently, (Zhou et al., 2024) improved upon the transformer architec-
165   ture with the introduction of the Mixture-of-Experts. Formally, a MoE layer consists of m experts
166   {E1 , E2 , ..., Em }, whereby each expert is a feed-forward MLP. A gating network G produces a
167   scalar score based on an input x which is then responsible for deciding
                                                                            Phow  the inputs are distributed
                                                                              m
168   to the experts. A MoE layer’s output can be defined as MOE(x) = j=1 G(x)j Ej (x). The gating
169   network operates such that only the top-k experts are activated, so as to prevent computation from
170   exploding. For MVMoE, Zhou et al. (2024) introduces MoE layers at each transformer block at
171   the token-level, meaning that every token uses at most k experts. Additionally, a hierarchical gate
172
      is introduced in the decoder at the problem level, whereby depending on the problem instance, the
      network learns to decide whether or not to use experts at each decoding step.
173
174   Apart from MoE, MoD is introduced in an effort to improve computational efficiency in large lan-
175   guage models (LLMs) (Raposo et al., 2024). Effectively, the authors replace alternate transformer
176   layers in the LLM’s encoder, making learning embeddings more computationally efficient. Now,
177   instead of gating network G(x) routing to various experts, it routes tokens through the transformer
178
      layer or bypasses it. The capacity of G(x) defines the total number of tokens allowed for a layer.
      Empirical evidence showed improvement in training loss by intertwining these sparser layers.

249   4.2   I NFORMATION BOTTLENECK AND GENERALIZATION
250
251   In the context of MTVRP, the MoE model was proposed as an effective learning framework for
252   multi-task settings (Zhou et al., 2024). However, it is not immediately clear why simply improving
253   predictive power with a mixture model would be particularly beneficial in this context. We examine
254   this from the perspective of the Information Bottleneck principle (Tishby et al., 2000; Tishby & Za-
255
      slavsky, 2015; Saxe et al., 2019), which suggests that representations that are highly predictive but
      have minimal complexity are better suited for generalization. In Multi-Task and Multi-Distribution
256
      VRP, there is invariably shared information across tasks or distributions that can be leveraged, while
257
      representations must also retain task or distribution specific information to improve predictive per-
258   formance. Federici et al. (2020) studied the multi-view case wherein different views share common
259   label and showed that maximizing joint information between views with the shared labels is helpful.
260   Contrapositively, this implies that in scenarios where labels or distributions differ, such as in the
261   MTMDVRP setting, balancing shared and task-specific information is essential for generalization.
262   However, MoE lacks an inductive bias to enforce this balance.
263
      We propose that an adaptive learning approach, which regulates the balance between learning shared
264   and task-specific representations, is more appropriate. The customized MoD approach addresses this
265   by enforcing sparsity through possibly reduced network depths and lighter computation, forcing the
266   model to learn generalizable representations across tasks/distributions. The clustering mechanism
267   forces the network to condense information into a handful of representations. In a multi-task sce-
268   nario, we posit that these encourage the network to efficiently generalize by balancing the computa-
269   tional budget for task-specific information while leaving common information to be learned across
      other tasks or distributions, encouraging efficient generalization across tasks and distributions.

310

311

312   sensible approach to this problem. We first define a set of C ∈ RNc ×d representations, such that Nc

447   5.2     A BLATION AND A NALYSES
448
449   We discuss key ablation studies here and provide more extensive ones in Appendices A.9 to A.12.
450
      Effect of Sparsity. To examine the effect of sparsity, we train additional models with the capacity
451   of the MoD layer increased to 20%, 30%, and 40%, respectively, on MTMDVRP50. The results
452   are shown in Table 2. Specifically, as the sparsity moves from 10% to 20%, the model’s bias im-
453   proves—the in-task in-distribution optimality gap reduces, while the out-task in-distribution perfor-
454   mance remains relatively stable. However, we observe that for both task types, the out-distribution
455   performance starts to degrade. Increasing the number of tokens to 30% also improves the in-task in-
456   distribution optimality gaps, but we see the decline in performance for out-task and out-distribution
457   settings. This degradation continues with the 40% model, where overall performance deteriorates.
458   The results clearly indicate that sparsity is crucial in generalization across both task and distribution.
459   Effect of Clustering. In the latent space, the soft clustering mechanism facilitates information
460   exchange among dynamic clusters, enabling the model to capture high-level, generalizable features
461   from neighboring hidden representations. This improves the model’s understanding of the node
462   selection process and enhances decision-making. Limiting the number of clusters also promotes
463   abstraction, encouraging the model to focus on broadly applicable patterns rather than overfitting to
464   task-specific details. However, too many clusters dilute this effect, leading to over-segmentation and
465
      reduced generalization as the model prioritizes more complex patterns over shared structures. Table
      3 supports this, whereby we vary the number of cluster centers in the model. Thus, maintaining
466
      sparsity in this aspect is crucial as well.
467
468   Sparse Encoder. Given the studies so far, a natural question arises: Since sparsity is helpful for the
469   decoder, does it have the same impact on the encoder? Table 4 presents our findings on this question.
470   While preserving the same number of encoder layers and keeping a fixed capacity of 10% each layer,
471
      we find that the model’s performance degrades significantly. Even after doubling the number of
      layers, the model fails to reach the original levels of performance. This suggests that in the encoder,
472
      it is essential for all tokens to be processed. The original MoE encoder plays a crucial role in the
473
      architecture—MoE efficiently scales and enables the model to leverage a variety of experts to capture
474   a broad range of representations for various tasks. In contrast, the MoD introduces greater flexibility
475   in the decoder, giving the model the ability to dynamically select layers for decision-making, which
476   helps it adapt effectively to varying outputs.
477
      Patterns of Layer Selection. We investigate how SHIELD behaves for a given problem compared
478
      to MVMoE. Figure 3 shows the final output of SHIELD and MVMoE for OVRPBTW on VM22775.
479
      The starred points indicate that SHIELD routes them more frequently during the problem-solving
480   process. Consider route R5 for SHIELD and route R8 for MVMoE. SHIELD can recognize that such
481   points are far away and that it is more advantageous to visit other points en route, whereas MVMoE
482   merely visited one node first. Likewise, for route R4 in SHIELD and route R6 in MVMoE, SHIELD
483   identifies the 2 starred points to be better served as connecting points, as opposed to making an
484   entire loop, which results in back-tracking to a similar area. Since the problem is an open problem,
485   we can see that SHIELD favors ending routes at faraway locations, whereas MVMoE tends to loop
      back and forth in many occurrences.

528   6           C ONCLUSION
529
530   The push toward unified generic solvers is an important step in building foundation models for neural
531   combinatorial optimization. In this paper, we propose to extend such solvers to the Multi-Task
532   Multi-Distribution VRP, a significantly more practical representation of industrial problems. With
533   this problem setting, we further propose SHIELD, a neural architecture that is designed to handle
534   generalization across both task and distribution dimensions, making it a powerful solver for practical
535
      problems. Extensive experiments and thorough analysis of the empirical results demonstrate that
      sparsity and hierarchy, two key techniques in SHIELD, substantially influence the generalization
536
      ability of the model. We believe that this forms a stepping stone towards other forms of foundation
537
      models, such as generalizing across various sizes.

758   A.1   A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
759
760   Single-task VRP Solver. Most research focuses on developing single-task VRP solvers, which
761   primarily follows two key paradigms: constructive solvers and improvement solvers. Constructive
762   solvers learn policies that generate solutions from scratch in an end-to-end fashion. Early works pro-
763   posed Pointer Networks (Vinyals et al., 2015) to approximate optimal solutions for the TSP (Bello
764   et al., 2017) and CVRP (Nazari et al., 2018) in an autoregressive (AR) way. A major breakthrough in
765
      AR-based methods came with the Attention Model (AM) (Kool et al., 2018), which became a foun-
      dational approach for solving VRPs. The policy optimization with multiple optima (POMO) (Kwon
766
      et al., 2020) improved upon AM by considering the symmetry property of VRP solutions. More
767
      recently, a wave of studies has focused on further boosting either the performance (Kim et al., 2022;
768   Drakulic et al., 2023; Chalumeau et al., 2023; Grinsztajn et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Hottung et al.,
769   2024) or versatility (Kwon et al., 2021; Berto et al., 2023) of these solvers to handle more complex
770   and varied problem instances. We refer the reader to Appendix A.1 for details on non-autoregressive
771   (NAR) constructive solvers and improvement solvers in the single-task VRP. Beyond AR methods,
772   non-autoregressive (NAR) constructive approaches (Joshi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021; Kool et al.,
773   2022; Qiu et al., 2022; Sun & Yang, 2023; Min et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Xia
774   et al., 2024) construct matrices, such as heatmaps representing the probability of each edge being
775   part of the optimal solution, to solve VRPs through complex post-hoc search. In contrast, improve-
776
      ment solvers (Chen & Tian, 2019; Lu et al., 2020; Hottung & Tierney, 2020; Costa et al., 2020; Wu
      et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021; Xin et al., 2021; Hudson et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023) typically learn
777
      more efficient and effective search components, often within the framework of classic heuristics or
778
      meta-heuristics, to iteratively refine an initial feasible solution. While constructive solvers can effi-
779   ciently achieve desirable performance, improvement solvers have the potential to find near-optimal
780   solutions given a longer time budget. There are also studies that focus on the scalability (Li et al.,
781   2021; Hou et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024) and robustness (Geisler et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023) of neural
782   VRP solvers, which are less directly related to our work. For those interested, we refer readers to
783   Bogyrbayeva et al. (2024).

785   A.2   G ENERATION OF VRP VARIANTS
786
787   As mentioned in Section 3, we consider four additional constraints on top of the CVRP, resulting
788
      in 16 different variants in total. Note that unlike (Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024), we do not
      generate node coordinates from a uniform distribution. Instead, we sample a set of fixed points from
789
      a given map. Here, we detail the generation of the five total constraints.
790
791   Capacity (C): We adopt the settings from (Kool et al., 2018), whereby each node’s demand δi is
792   randomly sampled from a discrete distribtution set, {1, 2, ..., 9}. For N = 50, the vehicle capacity
793   Q is set to 40, and for N = 100, the vehicle capacity is set to 50. All demands are first normalized
794
      to their vehicle capacities, so that δi′ = δi /Q.
795   Open route (O): For open routes, we set ot = 1 in the dynamic feature set received by the decoder.
796   Apart from this, we remove the constraint that the vehicle has to return to the depot when it has
797   completed the route or is unable to proceed further due to other constraints. Suppose the problem
798   has both open routes (O) and duration limit (L), then we mask all nodes vj such that lt + dij > L,
799   whereby dij is the distance between node vi and the potentially masked node vj , and L is the
800
      duration limit constraint. For problems with both open routes (O) and time windows (TW), we
      mask all nodes vj such that tt + dij > wjc , where tt is the current time after servicing the current
801
      node. Finally, suppose a route has both open routes (O) and backhauls (B), no special masking
802
      considerations are required as the vehicle does not return to the origin.
803
804   Backhaul (B): We adopt the approach from (Liu et al., 2024) by randomly selecting 20% of cus-
805   tomer nodes to be backhauls, thus changing their demand to be negative instead. We also follow the
806
      same setup as (Zhou et al., 2024) whereby routes can have a mix of linehauls and backhauls without
      any strict precedence. To ensure feasible solutions, we ensure that all starting points are linehauls
807
      only unless all remaining nodes are backhauls.
808
809   Duration limit (L): The duration limit is fixed such that the maximum length of the vehicle, L = 3,
      which ensures that a feasible route can be found as all points are normalized to a unit square.

      Time window (TW): For time windows, we follow the methodology in (Li et al., 2021). The
811   depot node v0 has a time window of [0, 3] with no service time. As for other nodes, each node
812   has a service time of si = 0.2, and the time windows are obtained as following: (1) first we
813   sample a time window center given by γi U (w0o + d0i , wic − di0 − si ), whereby d0i = di0

815   dow half-width hi uniformly from [si /2, w0c /3] = [0.1, 1], (3) then we set the time window as
816   [wio , wic ] = [MAX(wio , γi − hi ), MIN(wic , γi + hi )].
