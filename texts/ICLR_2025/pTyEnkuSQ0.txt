009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Large language models (LLMs) have attracted significant attention for their excep-
012             tional abilities in various natural language processing tasks, but they suffer from
013             hallucinations that will cause performance degradation. One promising solution
014             to improve the LLMs’ performance is to ask LLMs to revise their answer after
015             generation, a technique known as self-correction. Among the two types of self-
016             correction, intrinsic self-correction is considered a promising direction because it
017             does not utilize external knowledge. However, recent works doubt the validity of
018
                LLM’s ability to conduct intrinsic self-correction. In this paper, we present a novel
                perspective on the intrinsic self-correction capabilities of LLMs through theoretical
019
                analyses and empirical experiments. In addition, we identify two critical factors
020
                for successful self-correction: zero temperature and fair prompts. Leveraging these
021             factors, we demonstrate that intrinsic self-correction ability is exhibited across
022             multiple existing LLMs. Our findings offer insights into the fundamental theories
023             underlying the self-correction behavior of LLMs and remark on the importance of
024             unbiased prompts and zero temperature settings in harnessing their full potential.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed the field of natural language processing, show-
030   casing exceptional capabilities in generating coherent, contextually appropriate text across diverse
031
      domains (OpenAI, 2024; Gao et al., 2024). As of now, while LLMs are still not considered equivalent
      to human-level intelligence (Shen et al., 2023; Mathur et al., 2024), they exhibit some interesting
032
      characteristics that are possessed by human beings, including the emergent ability (Wei et al., 2022a)1
033
      and performing complex tasks (Chiang & Lee, 2023; Gruver et al., 2023). One particularly com-
034   pelling aspect of human cognition is the ability to self-correct. Self-correction (SC) is the continuous
035   process of evaluating and refining one’s responses or actions to achieve improved outcomes. There
036   are generally two types of SC: extrinsic SC which utilizes the help of external knowledge and intrinsic
037   SC which uses only one’s own knowledge. Inspired by humans, the research community proposed a
038   promising hypothesis that we could leverage the power of SC to enhance the quality of LLMs. In
039   particular, researchers are interested in whether intrinsic SC ability could be applied in LLMs.
040   Over the last few years, numerous research studies have been conducted on the effectiveness of
041   intrinsic SC of LLMs. For example, works like self-refine (Madaan et al., 2023), self-verification
042   (Gero et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023), post-editing (Raunak et al., 2023), all
043   demonstrate significantly enhanced performance of SC on respective benchmarks compared to results
044   obtained without SC. However, recent works have questioned the efficacy of intrinsic SC mechanisms
045   in LLMs (Huang et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024). A notable critique presented by (Huang et al.,
046   2024) is that LLMs do not possess the ability to perform intrinsic self-correction. Their argument is
047   based on a fundamental question: why couldn’t an LLM get the correct answer in the initial attempt
048
      instead of going through intrinsic SC? They hypothesize that existing works on SC abilities in LLMs
      shall be attributed to three factors: external feedback (such as the existence of a retrieval model) that
049
      converts the problem to an extrinsic setting, ground-truth label as an early stopping criterion that
050
      hinders the practical usage, and uninformative prompts that limit LLM’s ability when it generates the
051   first response before SC (which raises the possibility of unfair comparison). Through the experiments,

076
077   In light of such disagreement, it is important to answer a set of fundamental questions: Does intrinsic
078   SC ability exist within LLMs? And if it does, why couldn’t an LLM get the correct answer in
079   the initial attempt instead of going through intrinsic SC?
080   This paper answers the above fundamental questions. With theoretical analyses and quantitative
081   experiments under the same settings as Huang et al. (2024), we empirically show that LLMs do have
082   intrinsic SC ability. In addition, we also show that LLMs cannot answer questions correctly during
083   the initial attempt because of their inherent hallucination property.
084   Building on top of the answers, further investigations are conducted on the conditions that maximize
085   SC potential within LLMs. Through theoretical analyses and empirical experiments, we identify
086   two factors impacting the intrinsic SC ability of LLMs. The first factor is temperature. A non-zero
087   temperature, such as done by Huang et al. (2024), degrades SC ability. The second factor, equally
088   important, is the neutrality and fairness of the prompts used during various stages of SC. A fair and
089   unbiased prompt that doesn’t directly or indirectly influence the LLM to change or maintain its initial
090   answer provides the most stable benefits from the intrinsic SC ability of LLMs. In Fig. (3), we
091   provide an example illustrating how biased (Huang et al., 2024) and unbiased (ours) prompts affect
092
      the output of the LLM during different stages of SC.
093   This paper’s contributions could be summarized as the following:
094
095           • We provide a novel perspective on the functioning nature of intrinsic self-correction in
096             Section 2. We posit that self-correction abilities bear similarities to chain-of-thought (CoT)
097             and self-verification prompting methodologies, which furnish LLMs with additional context
098
                for generating the final response.
099           • We highlight the importance of the temperature of LLMs in the success of intrinsic self-
100             correction in Section 4. While increasing the temperature will benefit the creativity of
101             response, it may be detrimental for SC, depending on the structure of LLM’s response.
102
              • Additionally, in Section 5, we show that the SC prompt needs to be unbiased so that LLMs
103
                do not accidentally attend to tokens that might trigger unwanted answer updates during the
104
                SC process. We also provide guidelines to compose unbiased prompts for future research.
105
106           • We empirically verify the effects of the two factors above, showcasing the accuracy degrada-
107             tion of generated SC responses when increasing temperature and utilizing biased prompts,
                and intrinsic SC can be achieved by LLMs via fair prompts and zero temperature.

191   Case 1: if τ1 uses CoT and the second stage follows Order 1 In this case, intrinsic SC is just an
192   implicit form of self-verification (Gero et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023). Based on the rationales given
193   in stage 1, stage 2 will provide an answer on the consistency of the provided rationales by reasoning

195
196   Case 2: if τ1 uses CoT and the second stage follows Order 2 In this case, SC could be seen
197   as a CoT answer over a prompt that contains CoT-alike context. By providing more rationales in
198   the second stage, SC provides additional analyses over the provided rationales in the first stage and
199   makes a better decision. If well implemented, this case could lead to better performance compared
200
      to the first one. However, such an approach might not be very feasible as we cannot fully control
      the decomposition orderings of LLMs’ output through prompts. During our experiments, GPT-3.5
201
      shows a strong preference for the decomposition Order 1, and the designed prompt is only able to
202
      successfully change some responses’ order.
203
204   Case 3: if τ1 does not use CoT and the second stage follows Order 1 When τ1 does not use CoT,
205   the decomposition of the first stage in Eq. (4) is simply p(A|τ1 ). In this case, intrinsic SC likely has a
206
      negative impact, and it might cause an accuracy drop after correction. This is because the decision is
      first given without any rationale, and subsequent provided rationales are now dependent on a possibly
207
      hallucinated answer.
208
209   Case 4: if τ1 does not use CoT and the second stage follows Order 2 This serves the same purpose
210   as CoT, and thus accuracy will improve. By requiring LLMs to first provide rationales, followed by a
211   decision on the answer, and finally offering a more informed conclusion, SC enhances the overall
212
      accuracy. The improved accuracy is comparable (if not matching) to merely using CoT prompt with
      no self-correction as shown in Table 1.

      2.3    W HY CAN ’ T LLM ANSWER QUESTIONS CORRECTLY IN THE INITIAL ATTEMPT ?
226
      When LLMs hallucinate, one of the scenarios they exhibit is that they will output answers incorrectly
227
      even when having the correct knowledge. While that is not the full schema of LLM hallucination,
228   in this paper, we simply refer to this type of scenario as “hallucination”. Assuming LLMs have a
229   non-zero possibility of hallucination, we show that LLMs will not reach their full ability in the initial
230   attempt with Proposition. 2.1.
231
      Proposition 2.1. LLMs are generally under-performing compared to their true ability because
232
      hallucination will cause the overall accuracy to decrease.
233
234   This lemma serves as a justification for the second fundamental question and we provide the proof of
235   Proposition. 2.1 in Appendix C. As drawn by the conclusion in the previous section, intrinsic SC can
236   reduce hallucinations of the generated responses and achieve better quality similar to other prompt
237   engineering techniques.

256   4     I NCREASING T EMPERATURE M IGHT D ECREASE ACCURACY D URING SC
257
258   As mentioned in Section 2.1 and Eq. (1), the temperature hyperparameter controls the randomness of
259   the generated context. It is widely acknowledged within the research community that increasing the
260   temperature parameter (within a reasonable range) enhances the creativity of LLMs, resulting in the
261   generation of more diverse content (Hinton et al., 2015). Works like Wang et al. (2020) also try to
262   dynamically optimize the temperature at the token level for best generation quality. Furthermore, a
263   very recent work from Renze & Guven (2024) has claimed to find no correlation between increasing
264   temperature and losing accuracy on benchmarks for temperatures between 0.0 and 1.0. However,
265   as we will show in this section, temperature choice is a non-trivial aspect of the success of some
266
      LLMs during intrinsic SC. While increasing the temperature indeed shows no effect on the original
      response, it might have a negative impact on the subsequent stages of the SC process.
267
268   Our experimental results indicate that GPT-3.5 is adversely affected by increasing temperature, while
269   GPT4 exhibits greater robustness to temperature variations. We provide the theoretical analysis below,
      based on the decomposition of the second stage of SC.

354   Prompt engineering plays a pivotal role in the generation process of LLMs. Numerous studies have
355   demonstrated that carefully crafted prompts can substantially enhance the quality of the generated
356   output (Wei et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023). In this section, we demonstrate that
357   prompt design significantly influences the SC performance of certain models and that a fair prompt
358   is capable of eliciting universal intrinsic SC abilities in LLMs. In addition, we provide guidelines for

      fair prompting for future research in this direction.

363   of intrinsic SC, adhering to the same prompt used by Huang et al. (2024), and demonstrating that
364   inadequate prompts can lead to erroneous behavior in LLMs.

442   5.2   I NTRINSIC SC COULD BE ACHIEVED WITH FAIR PROMPT UNDER ZERO TEMPERATURE
443
444
      We consider three sets of prompts. The first set (Problem Set 1) is used by Huang et al. (2024), but
      we argue that they are biased towards encouraging the LLMs to change answers due to the use of
445
      the negative phrase “find problems”. The second set (Problem Set 2) is derived from the first one
446
      by removing the negative phrases and asking the LLM to check if the answer is correct or incorrect
447   and act accordingly. While such a prompt may appear fair from a human perspective due to the
448   balance of both aspects, the inclusion of the terms ’correct’ and ’incorrect’ can indirectly influence
449   the LLM’s behavior. Therefore in the last set of prompts (Problem set 3), we strip the prompts from
450   such phrases and words to make a completely unbiased prompt. We provide details on the three
451   sets in Appendix E. As shown in Table 2, using an unbiased prompt and zero temperature, all of the
452   benchmarked LLMs exhibit accuracy improvement on the different benchmarks. Nevertheless, biased
453   prompts may decrease accuracy in certain LLMs and specific benchmarks. It is noteworthy that this
454   decrease seems to be related to some specific features of the benchmarks and models, but as of now,
455
      a reasonable explanation and prediction of the specific features are yet to be discovered. For example,
      in CommonSense QA benchmark (Talmor et al., 2019), the bias of the prompt has significant impacts
456
      on GPT and Llama models, but not so much on the other models. On the other hand, the bias of
457
      the prompt seems to only affect the GPT models, but it has no impact on the Mistral and Llama
458   models for the HotpotQA benchmark (Yang et al., 2018). We hypothesize that this might be because
459   those models are instruction fine-tuned, and GPT families are further fine-tuned with reinforcement
460   learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022). Depending on the different training
461   datasets that are not always disclosed, an LLM’s accuracy after SC might be conditionally robust to
462   biased prompts, depending on the context.
463
      Importantly, our results align with the self-verification results obtained by Weng et al. (2023) where
464   they also obtained a slight improvement using a different model. The on-par results further validate
465   our claim in Section 2.2 that intrinsic SC is similar to other existing prompt engineering techniques.
466
467
      In Appendix D.3, we provide further numerical analysis of how some of the models change their
      answers during SC. The results not only align with the theoretical analysis but also provide further
468
      insights into why some models remain robust on some benchmarks. With the γ > 0% extra changes
469
      caused by the biased prompt, more incorrect answers are introduced in the final stage.
470
471   All of the above analyses, reinforce our initial claim that a fair prompt is a safe option. A truly
472   unbiased prompt must avoid both semantically and literally negative or positive phrases, such as
473
      “find the problem” or “correct your answer”. We encourage future researchers to follow these
      guidelines when composing their future SC prompts, similar to the unbiased Prompt Set 3.

521   6   L IMITATIONS
522
523
      While the paper aims to investigate the fundamental functioning nature of intrinsic self-correction, we
524   recognize several limitations of the presented results. Due to computational and staffing constraints,
525   evaluations are limited to four models and six datasets. In Sections 4 and 5, the focus is mainly
526   on a subset of models and the CommonSense QA dataset. Error bars are not provided due to
527   resource limitations, as generating results for error analysis would require extensive computational
528   and financial resources. While this is the common approach for the LLM community due to costs, we
529   acknowledge that variances will exist for non-zero temperature experiments.

531   7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we investigate the intrinsic self-correction capabilities of large language models.
534   Supported by theoretical analysis and empirical experimentation, we present a novel perspective
535   on the problem, positing that intrinsic self-correction is analogous to self-verification and chain-of-
536   thought (CoT) prompting. Furthermore, we point out zero temperature and unbiased prompts are
537   salient in facilitating intrinsic self-correction, we also underscore the significance of these two key
538   factors. Our experimental results demonstrate that LLMs possess universal self-correction abilities,
539   provided these conditions are met. Our findings will serve as a important foundation for future
      research and understanding into the self-correction mechanisms of large language models.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS
811
812
      During LLM inference, self-correction (SC) refers to the ability to iteratively improve future responses
813   by reflecting on the errors from previous responses. We refer readers to an excellent survey on the
814   correction mechanism of LLMs (Pan et al., 2023). Self-correction characteristics in Large Language
815   Models can be generally achieved by two approaches: intrinsic SC and extrinsic SC. In this section,
816   we provide a brief survey on the current techniques used on both sides, with a focus on the intrinsic
817   self-correction ability, which is also the focus of the main discussion of this paper.
818
819   A.1    E XTRINSIC SC
820
821   In the current LLM community, extrinsic self-correction (SC) refers to large language models (LLMs)
822   using external sources of knowledge to refine their own answers. This process enables the models to
823
      improve the accuracy and reliability of their responses by cross-referencing and correcting potential
      errors based on updated or specialized information. Such an idea could be traced back to decades ago
824
      when external information was used to guide a deep learning (DL) model to generate more accurate
825
      answers (Gou et al., 2021; Hinton et al., 2015). Since DL models were mainly used for classification
826   tasks before generative models became prevalent, the focus was on knowledge distillation, where
827   more powerful DL models transfer their knowledge in the training stage of smaller models. After
828   large language models became the focus of many researchers, the idea of extrinsic self-correction
829   started to draw attention.
830
      Common approaches include using other LLMs as verifiers (Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024;
831
      Gao et al., 2023; Nathani et al., 2023; Welleck et al.), recommenders (Wang et al., 2023a; Nathani
832   et al., 2023), or even revisers (Gao et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024). A verifier is usually a stronger
833   LLM (e.g. GPT4) that verifies the correctness of answers posted by a smaller and weaker LLM.
834   For example, Zhang et al. (2024) has found that paired with a strong verifier based on GPT-4,
835   weaker LLMs can enjoy significant accuracy improvement. A recommender suggests solutions and
836   resources to the weaker LLMs, whereas a reviser steps in and changes the answer as needed. In many
837   proposed works, the stronger external LLM could serve multiple roles. For example, the stronger
838   LLM proposed by Wang et al. (2023a) both verifies the correctness of the answer and also provides
839   suggestions for improvement.
840   Moreover, external self-correction often pairs up with additional features to better assist weaker
841   models. For example, Wang et al. (2023a) finetunes the verifier with a high-quality critic dataset,
842   and Chen et al. (2024b); Gou et al. (2024); Peng et al. (2023) propose to use retrieval augmented
843   generation (RAG) to assist LLMs in evaluating the responses and generating higher quality criticisms
844   and suggestions. Overall, it’s the consensus in the research community that using external sources
845   will help LLMs generate better responses during external self-correction.
846
847   A.2    I NTRINSIC SC
848
849
      Albeit the success of extrinsic self-correction, researchers are more interested in the other half of the
      paradigm, intrinsic SC. The community’s interest is partially due to the fact that extrinsic SC often
850
      requires access to an external knowledge base and even external larger LLMs. For example, although
851
      Nathani et al. (2023) shows that a multi-aspect framework, consisting of other LLMs, programming
852   tools, and other programs, can successfully help LLMs generate better responses, the availability of
853   those tools renders less practicality to the solution. On the other hand, if the LLMs can improve the
854   responses solely by themselves, they will resemble the interesting self-correction ability of humans
855   (when we make mistakes, there is a certain chance that we realize the mistake and improve our
856   previous response without external resources).
857
      Following the previous discussion of knowledge distillation as the predecessor of extrinsic SC, the
858   early days of intrinsic SC could be attributed to the works of self-training, where DL models keep
859   finetuning themselves using self-generated labels on confident predictions (Amini et al., 2024). It has
860   been shown that if used appropriately, such an approach will also lead to more accurate DL models
861   (Zoph et al., 2020). Intuitively, like LLMs inheriting knowledge distillation with extrinsic SC, they
862   should also have inherited self-training with intrinsic SC. Indeed, numerous works have shown that
863   intrinsic SC abilities exist in LLMs, with the vast majority of work using prompt engineering as
      the backbone (Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Liu et al.,


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      2024a;b; Kim et al., 2023; Dhuliawala et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b). Among them, Madaan et al.
865   (2023) is usually seen as one of the first works that systematically proposes the prompt for intrinsic
866   SC. Later works (including ours) often follow their setting, where the same LLM is used to generate
867   feedback and improved answers to its initial responses.
868
      However, Huang et al. (2024) challenges the existence of intrinsic SC and attributes the success of
869
      existing prompt sets to several reasons. First, Huang et al. (2024) believes that Shinn et al. (2023) and
870
      Kim et al. (2023) utilize oracle labels as the SC criteria during inference, hindering their practicality
871   in the real world. Second, it believes that Madaan et al. (2023) uses a suboptimal prompt design that
872   makes the initial responses unnecessarily bad. Following their arguments, they would also argue that
873   Chen et al. (2024a), Wu et al. (2024b) and Dhuliawala et al. (2023) build suboptimal prompt sets that
874   give favor to the refinement stages of intrinsic SC.
875
      Besides Huang et al. (2024) and Kumar et al. (2024) who use biased prompts, in this same line of work,
876
      other negative results indicating the failure of intrinsic SC have been reported by some investigations.
877   Jiang et al. (2024) shows that LLMs cannot discriminate among its generated content and select the
878   best option. By sampling from multiple generation traces and asking an LLM to discriminate the best
879   option, Jiang et al. (2024) shows that LLMs are not inherently better at discriminating (choosing the
880   best option) among their prior outputs than they are at generating responses. Yet, Jiang et al. (2024)
881   is under a different setting compared to intrinsic self-correction, where it uses multiple agents in
882   the first step and uses the averaged generation results as the initial accuracy, which already brings
883   LLM very close to its true ability. Tyen et al. (2024) argues that LLMs often struggle to identify the
884   location of errors in CoT tracing. Yet, their results, while hinting at the lack of reasoning abilities in
885
      LLMs, show a positive net value in correcting the traces from incorrect to correct, supporting the
      existence of intrinsic SC ability in LLMs.
886
887   Nonetheless, as our work and some concurrent works suggest through empirical results and analyses,
888   intrinsic SC is not a mirage. Li et al. (2024) argues on the existence through the LLM’s confidence
889   towards different questions. By designing a prompt set that lets LLM update its answers based on
890   its confidence, that work achieves outstanding results where intrinsic SC helps improve accuracy
891
      across four different models. However, it should be noted that there is not yet a consensus on LLM’s
      judgment over its confidence, as discussed by Singh et al. (2023) and Becker & Soatto (2024). Singh
892
      et al. (2023); Becker & Soatto (2024) indicate that LLMs show confidence in incorrect answers.
893
      From a fairness and uncertainty perspective, our work generalizes from Li et al. (2024) by taking
894   a different path. We show that a generic fair prompt can also achieve universal intrinsic SC under
895   zero temperature. Liu et al. (2024a) and Liu et al. (2024b) focus on a different theoretical perspective
896   of the intrinsic SC ability. Similar to our argument, they argue that LLMs do not reach their best
897   ability in the first round (our argument about hallucination, which they refer to as non-activated latent
898   concepts). Through empirical results, Liu et al. (2024a) shows that intrinsic SC will cause LLM
899   to converge to an accuracy higher than the initial performance. Their work focuses more on the
900   convergence analysis. Instead, we conduct an analysis of the optimal conditions for LLMs performing
901   intrinsic SC.
902
903
904
      A.3    A PPLICATIONS OF S ELF -C ORRECTION
905
906
      Besides the improvement in response accuracy, intrinsic self-correction has the potential beyond
907   simple answer generation. Liu et al. (2023) shows that introspective reasoning can enhance the
908   model’s performance after reinforcement learning. Wang et al. (2023b) also shows that LLMs can
909   self-generate instructions for its fine-tuning dataset. Content generated by intrinsic self-correction can
910   be seen as reasoning traces that can be used to fine-tune the models during reinforcement learning.
911   Such an idea is also hinted at by the growing literature on inference scaling (Kumar et al., 2024;
912   Puerto et al., 2024). On a different line of work, due to the success of GPT4-o1, inference scaling is
913   gaining attention in the research community (Wu et al., 2024a; Snell et al., 2024).

952   measurable. Besides the true ability of LLMs (internal representation), the correctness of the answer
953   during the generation stage is also determined by multiple factors, including decoding, prompt,

955   human’s perspective, is equivalent to random guessing). That is, correct(A ∈ Q)=λ > 1/k.

957

960   “A/B/C/D” for a multiple-choice problem. This means that hallucination randomly changes the answer
961   (originally either correct or incorrect) with equal possibility. Such an assumption is reasonable and
962   has been empirically shown by Becker & Soatto (2024). No matter what the previous answer was,
963   after the hallucination, the answer is random but not the original answer. Then, we could devise the
964   following Proposition 2.1. Proposition 2.1. LLMs are generally under-performing compared to
965   their real abilities because hallucination will cause the overall accuracy to decrease.
