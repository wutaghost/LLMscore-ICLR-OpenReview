009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we systematically study music generation conditioned solely on the
012            video. First, we present a large-scale dataset by collecting 360K video-music pairs,
013            including various genres such as movie trailers, advertisements, and documentaries.
014            Furthermore, we propose VidMuse, a simple framework for generating music
015            aligned with video inputs. VidMuse stands out by producing high-fidelity music that
016
               is both acoustically and semantically aligned with the video. By incorporating local
               and global visual cues, VidMuse enables the creation of coherent music tracks that
017
               consistently match the video content through Long-Short-Term modeling. Through
018
               extensive experiments, VidMuse outperforms existing models in terms of audio
019            quality, diversity, and audio-visual alignment. Samples of results and comparisons
020            with other methods are available at our anonymous project URL: https://
021            anonymous-project-demo.github.io/Anonymous_VidMuse/.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Music, as an essential element of video production, can enhance humans’ feelings and convey the
027
      theme of the video content. Along with the development of social media platforms i.e., YouTube and
      TikTok, some studies (Ma, 2022; Dasovich-Wilson et al., 2022; Millet et al., 2021) have shown that
028
      a piece of melodious music can vastly attract the audience’s attention and interest in watching the
029
      video. It thus leads to a great demand for studying video-to-music generation (Di et al., 2021; Kang
030   et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Gan et al., 2020; Hong et al., 2017; He et al., 2024).
031
032   Nevertheless, music creation for video is a challenging task, which need to understand both music
033
      theory and video semantics. It would be very time-consuming to produce a piece of suitable music
      for video in a hand-crafted manner. Therefore, it is desirable when we can automatically generate
034
      high-quality music for different genres of videos.
035
036   Currently, most of works (Copet et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2023b; Forsgren &
037   Martiros; Huang et al., 2023; Schneider et al., 2023) have made significant achievements, especially
038   in text-to-music generation, but the video-to-music generation still remains to be further studied.
039
      Specifically, existing works on video-conditioned music generation mainly focus on specific scenarios,
      such as dance videos (Li et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022), or on the symbolic music, i.e., MIDI (Wang
040
      et al., 2020; Di et al., 2021; Zhuo et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023). However, these works are unable
041
      to generate more diverse musical styles and are also difficult to generalize to various video genres.
042   Moreover, Hong et al. (Hong et al., 2017) build a music–video retrieval dataset from YouTube-
043   8M (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2016), albeit with limited video genres. Despite that there are also some
044   prominent works (Hussain et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023) employing multi-modal inputs to generate
045   music for the video, it is still worth studying whether it is possible to generate high-quality and
046   harmonious music for diverse genres of videos, conditioned solely on the visual input.
047
      Motivated by this, we first construct a large-scale dataset termed V2M, equipped with a comprehensive
048   benchmark to evaluate the state-of-the-art works thoroughly. The video-music pairs are collected
049   from YouTube with various genres, e.g., movie trailers, advertisements, documentaries, vlogs, etc.
050   In order to ensure the quality of our dataset, we establish a multi-step pipeline illustrated in Fig. 3
051   to systematically clean and pre-process data. The videos with low quality or composed of static
052   images are filtered out. The proposed dataset contains three subsets: V2M-360K for pretraining,
053   V2M-20K for finetuning, and V2M-bench for evaluation. We believe that V2M is able to facilitate the
      development of video-to-music generation.

054
      Furthermore, on top of V2M, we propose a simple yet effective method, termed as VidMuse, to
055   generate music only conditioned on the visual input. Instead of predicting the intermediate musical
056   symbols such as MIDI or retrieving the music from the database, the proposed VidMuse, integrates
057   both local and global visual clues to generate background music consistent with the video in an
058   end-to-end manner. The core techniques in our method are a Long-Short-Term Visual Module
059   (LSTV-Module) and a Music Token Decoder. Specifically, the LSTV-Module aims to learn the spatial-
060   temporal representation of videos, which is the key to generating music aligned with the video. On
061   the one hand, the long-term module models the entire video, capturing global context to understand
062   the whole video. It contributes to the coherence of generated music at the video level. On the other
063
      hand, the short-term module focuses on learning the fine-grained clues at the clip level, which plays
      an important role in generating diverse music. The integration of two modules can improve the
064
      quality and visual consistency of generated music. In addition, the Transformer-based music token
065
      decoder is an autoregressive model, converting video embeddings obtained by LSTV-Module into
066   music tokens. We formulate music generation as a task of next token prediction, which has been
067   widely validated by the NLP community. The predicted music tokens are further decoded into the
068   music signals by a high-fidelity neural audio compression model.
069
      In summary, the main contributions of our work are as follows:
070
071   (a) We construct a large-scale video-to-music dataset, i.e., V2M, which contains about 360k video-
072   music pairs with high quality, covering various genres and themes. To the best of our knowledge, this
073   is the largest and most diverse dataset for this task, which can facilitate future research.
074   (b) We propose a simple yet effective method, VidMuse, for video-to-music generation. The proposed
075   method integrates both local and global cues in the video, enabling the generation of high-fidelity
076   music tracks that are not only musically coherent but also semantically aligned with the video content.
077
      (c) We benchmark several state-of-the-art works against our method on the V2M-bench via a series of
078
      subjective and objective metrics for a thorough evaluation. As demonstrated in experiments, VidMuse
079   achieves state-of-the-art performance on V2M-bench, outperforming existing models in terms of
080   audio quality, diversity, and audio-visual consistency.

085   In this section, we review the existing works related to video-to-music generation, which mainly fall
086   into four categories:
087   Video Representation. Various methods have been proposed to learn the spatio-temporal repre-
088   sentation (Liu et al., 2020b; 2021; Tran et al., 2018; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019; Arnab et al., 2021;
089   Tong et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022) for videos. They aim to capture the contextual
090   features of video frames, which is beneficial for video understanding. Recent advances primarily
091   concentrate on developing video transformers, including ViViT (Arnab et al., 2021; Tong et al.,
092   2022; Liu et al., 2022). These transformer-based methods achieve superior generalized performance
093
      on various video understanding tasks, such as video classification and temporal action localization.
      Among them, Tong et al. (Tong et al., 2022) extend masked autoencoders (He et al., 2022) from the
094
      image to the video, exhibiting the strong generalized performance in downstream tasks. Benefiting
095
      from the advance in multi-modal large language models, lots of works (Zhang et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
096   2023; Munasinghe et al., 2023) of interactive video understanding have been proposed, which built
097   upon the large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024; Raffel et al., 2020;
098   Taori et al., 2023) and showcase the visual reasoning capabilities for video understanding.
099
      Audio-Visual Alignment. Audio-visual alignment (Akbari et al., 2021; Rouditchenko et al., 2020;
100
      Shi et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023c; Wang
101
      et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023a; Xing et al., 2024) aims to align the feature between audio, vision in the
102   semantics level, which plays a vital role in tasks of audio-visual understanding and generation. For
103   example, CAV-MAE (Gong et al., 2022) is an audio-visual MAE by integrates the contrastive learning
104   and masked modeling method. Currently, many works go beyond exploring audio-visual alignment.
105   ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) extends CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to more modalities, including
106   audio, depth, thermal, and IMU data, which paves the way for cross-modal retrieval and generation. In
107   addition, Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2023a) employ LLMs with multi-modal adaptors to support any modal
      data as input and output, showing strong capabilities in universal multi-modal understanding. These

      in videos. As a result, it can generate high-fidelity music aligned with the video.

      et al., 2017; Gemmeke et al., 2017) have been released, but there is still a lack of datasets for video-

130
      pairs and aim to retrieve music for the video from the database. However, this dataset exhibits limited
131
      video genres and also suffers from the issue of data quality, as stated in (Zhuo et al., 2023). We
132   observe that several works (Wang et al., 2020; Di et al., 2021; Zhuo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024)
133   aim to facilitate MIDI music generation. However, we argue this musical form imposes restrictions
134   on diversity for the music generation. Other datasets (Zhu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021) focus on
135   generating music for dance videos only and have limited data size, which limits their applicability for
136   general video-to-music models. As a result, this work constructs a large-scale video-to-music dataset
137   where the music directly in wav format is diverse. We establish a rigorous pipeline for data collection
138   and cleaning, which ensures the quality and diversity of our dataset. We expect the model can learn
139   the music with more diverse forms from this dataset.

144   In this section, we build a multi-step pipeline to clean and process source videos from YouTube to
145
      ensure data quality. After that, we construct a large-scale video-to-music generation dataset, i.e.,
      V2M, with a benchmark. The constructed dataset stands out for its significant size, high quality, and
146

147

150   3.1   DATASET C OLLECTION
151
152   To quickly collect a large scale of video-music pairs, we curate a series of query sets to retrieve
153
      corresponding videos from YouTube. In addition, we found that the music in the movie trailer
      usually showcases rich diversity and high quality. Therefore, we also aggregate a vast array of video
154
      information from the IMDb Non-Commercial Datasets (imd), including video types, names, release
155
      dates, etc. Queries are formulated based on the titles of these selections and retain the videos released
156   after 2000, as videos from earlier periods are less likely to be of good quality. In the process of video
157   crawling, we only keep the top 2 search results, resulting in a collection of around 400K videos,
158   ranging from movies to documentaries. Besides, several existing datasets already contain video-music
159   pairs, such as HIMV-200K (Hong et al., 2017), subsets of YouTube-8M (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2016)
160   labeled with “Music” and “Trailer” tags. We incorporate these datasets into our collection to further
161   expand its scope. After merging all sources, our final dataset comprises about 600K videos, spanning
      diverse genres and categories.

200   3.2       DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
201
202   The 600K raw videos may include many low-quality samples. To address this, we develop a series
203   of rigorous steps to filter out undesirable data and obtain a clean set. The overall pipeline of data
204   processing is depicted in Fig. 1. The following steps outline our approach: (1) The process begins
205   with coarse filtering, where we remove videos lacking audio or video tracks, videos that are too short
206   or too long, those containing inappropriate content such as violence or explicit material, and those
207
      from categories like Interview and News, which generally have background music not aligned with the
      visual content. (2) Following this, we perform fine-grained filtering to retain videos with substantial
208
      music content and dynamic visual elements. We use an audio analysis model (Kong et al., 2020) to
209
      identify music segments, ensuring a sufficient portion of the audio is classified as music. In parallel,
210   we analyze the visuals (Wang et al., 2004) to exclude videos consisting mainly of static images. (3)
211   To further refine the dataset, we apply music source separation (Défossez et al., 2019) to isolate the
212   music component by removing vocal tracks, enhancing the overall audio quality. (4) Finally, we rank
213   the videos based on their audio-visual alignment scores (Girdhar et al., 2023) to ensure a high level
214   of semantic correlation between the audio and visual modalities. The resulting videos are then split
215   into training (V2M), fine-tuning (V2M-20K), and benchmark (V2M-bench) subsets. For details on
      dataset construction, please refer to the Appendix A.2).

287   to hardware limitations. Prior studies opt to generate music in segment level (Kang et al., 2023; Di

290   Even the same video segment may lead to distinct musical interpretations when being in different
291   contexts. By incorporating global guidance, it can enhance the alignment of the generated music
292   with the overall video content. As a result, to capture both local and global visual clues, the visual
293   features extracted from the visual encoder are fed into the LSTV-Module. As depicted in Fig. 3, the

297

299   The architecture of the LSTV-Module is shown in Fig. 4. To capture both global and local visual clues,
300   we leverage the self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) in both Long-term and Short-term
301
      Modeling. Long-term modeling extracts long-range dependencies, while short-term modeling focuses
      on local details. This results in refined long-term features Fl ∈ RNl ×P ×D and short-term features
302
      Fs ∈ RNs ×P ×D .
303
304   To incorporate global guidance for generating segment-based music, we design LST-Fusion. It
305   integrates long-term and short-term features by utilizing the cross-attention mechanism CA(·) with
306   Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V), which can be mathematically formulated as:
307
308                                 Z′ = CA(Q, K, V), where Q = Fs , K = Fl , V = Fl ,                                                                                      (1)
309
310   This mechanism allows the model to query global information rather than generating music based
311   solely on local visual features. It guarantees that the generated music is more consistent with the
312   overall video content. After the cross-attention, a linear layer projects Z′ to Z ∈ RNs ×P ×M , where
313   M represents the input vector dimension of the music token decoder in the next step.
314   Music Token Decoder. We adopt an autoregressive approach to predict the music tokens Ȳ condi-
315   tioned on the video segment. Music token decoder is implemented by a transformer decoder with a
316   linear classifier. We set the latent vector size of the transformer decoder to M , allowing to scale up
317   or down the model’s size. The decoder incorporates a cross-attention mechanism that receives the
318   visual signal Z ∈ RNs ×P ×M , where Ns is the number of frames sampled in the video segment. At
319   each time step t (where t = 1, . . . , T ), the decoder predicts the logits of current token Ȳt ∈ RK×C
320
      based on previous tokens and visual context Z. Here, K denotes the number of codebooks, and C
      represents the vocabulary size.
321
322   Audio Codec. The audio codec can convert an audio segment into discretized codebooks and,
323   conversely, decode codebooks back into audio. The size of codebooks is K × T , where T denotes the
      length of the video. In the training stage, we need to encode the ground truth audio A into discretized

      4.2     D ISCUSSION
329
330   In this section, we introduce VidMuse, a simple yet effective framework for video-to-music generation,
331   which offers several key advantages: (1) Our framework is highly adaptable, with each module
332   designed for smooth integration, facilitating the identification of optimal settings. Additionally,
333   VidMuse can infer long videos via a sliding window approach (see Appendix A.4). 2) In contrast to
334   previous approaches (Kang et al., 2023; Di et al., 2021; Hussain et al., 2023) that primarily focus on
335   short-term visual cues, our method leverages both short-term and long-term visual information. It
336   allows for the understanding of local semantics, producing music that is consistent with the video’s
337
      context. Furthermore, instead of predicting intermediate text embeddings or musical symbols such
      as MIDI, our approach directly generates background music that is coherent with the video in an
338
      end-to-end manner. 3) As demonstrated Table 2, the KL metric indicates that our method produces
339
      video-level music generation results that are more aligned to the ground truth. Furthermore, the
340   ImageBind metric shows that our generated music is more semantically aligned with the visual
341   content overall. These results validate the overall effectiveness of our framework.

352
353   5.1     I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
354
      We choose CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as the visual encoder in default (see experiments in Sec. 5.5).
355
      Since this work does not focus on audio encoding and decoding, we use Encodec (Défossez et al.,
356   2019) for 32 kHz monophonic audio as our default compression model and use the pretrained
357   transformer model proposed in MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024). The training stage utilizes the AdamW
358   optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for 56,000 steps with a batch size of 240 samples. The
359   hyperparameters are set to β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, with a weight decay of 0.1 and gradient clipping
360   at 1.0. A cosine learning rate schedule is employed, incorporating a warm-up phase of 4,000 steps
361   and an exponential moving average decay of 0.99. The training is completed on H800 GPUs, with
362   float16 mixed precision. This setup leverages 360K samples for pre-training and 20K samples for
363   fine-tuning. A top-k strategy is applied for sampling, retaining the top 250 tokens with a temperature

      5.5   A BLATION S TUDIES
452
453
454   In this section, we conduct comprehensive ablation studies to determine the optimal hyperparameters
455   and design choices. First, we examine the impact of different model design choices on the generation
456   quality. After that, we explore the impact of the visual encoders and model size on performance.
457   More ablation results are in the Appendix A.1.
458   Justification of Design Choices. To validate the impact of different model design choices on our
459   generation results and verify the effectiveness of our method, we first design two modules: a short-
460   term modeling module (STM) and a long-term modeling module (LTM). VidMuse-STM aims to
461   ablate the contribution of STM by using only STM without LTM, while VidMuse-LTM utilizes
462   only LTM. We present the results in Table 3. Based on these results, we gain the insight that local
463   information plays a more important role in the generation. By integrating global guidance with local
464   information, we improve the alignment of the generated music with the overall video content. Second,
465
      we implement two variants with Cross-Attention with learnable Queries (CAQ) in our framework.
      Specifically, CAQ_SL first uses a CAQ where K and V are short-term features and then uses a CAQ
466
      where K and V are long-term features. CAQ_LS do it in an opposite order. As shown in Table 3,
467
      VidMuse outperforms two variants. It seems that the learnable query requires a deeper architecture,
468   as demonstrated by DETR (Carion et al., 2020) and Q-Former (Li et al., 2023), but this manner
469   reduces the model’s efficiency. Furthermore, we test a baseline using Slowfast (Feichtenhofer et al.,
470   2019) and find its performance degenerates in Table 3. This may be because the fast path uses a
471   higher frame rate in inputs than the slow path, but their temporal receptive fields are identical. Thus,
472   Slowfast still lacks global guidance.
473
      The impact of model size on performance. For the architecture of the music token decoder, we test
474   three variants with different scales: small (455M), medium (1.9B), and large (3.3B). The medium-
475   sized VidMuse-M is recognized as the default setting if not stated, as it achieves the best results across
476   all metrics, outlined in Table 4. We observe the larger VidMuse-L model fails to deliver proportional
477   improvements. This discrepancy can be partly attributed to limited GPU resources. The model’s
478   performance is near saturation, and the larger model does not exhibit significant advantages. As a
479   result, VidMuse-M is recognized as the optimal choice, achieving a trade-off between performance
480   and efficiency.
481   Visual Encoder. We conduct ablation experiments to study the impact of various visual encoders.
482   As shown in Table 5, we experiment with different visual encoders, including ViT (Dosovitskiy et al.,
483   2020), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022), and ViViT (Arnab et al., 2021).
484   Our results show that VidMuse remains robust in processing visual information for music generation
485   across all encoder choices. To balance computational efficiency and generation quality, we select
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as the default visual if not stated.

508   5.6    Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
509
510   In Fig. A2, our qualitative analysis underscores specific limitations in the methods employed by CMT
511
      and M2 UGen. For CMT, the method’s reliance on extracting only low-level visual cues for music
      generation, particularly in creating symbolic music, leads to some discontinuities. As illustrated in
512
      Fig. A2, “Abrupt gaps” occur, especially in darker video segments where the model fails to predict
513
      symbolic music notes, resulting in silence.
514
515   M2 UGen utilizes LLMs to fuse multimodal representation and then project LLMs’ embeddings into
516   music via a text-to-music generation model. However, the music generated by this work usually
517
      showcases repetitive musical themes and suffers from a lack of diversity. Additionally, M2 UGen is
      constrained to generating music segments of only 30 seconds, which necessitates splitting longer
518
      videos into clips for separate processing. Consequently, this leads to abrupt transitions between
519
      segments after stitching, compromising the overall musical continuity.
520
521   The last row of Fig. A2 showcases our Long-Short-Term (LST) approach, highlighting its ability to
522   produce globally diverse music that captures the essence of the ground truth. This method ensures
523
      our music generation remains contextually rich and aligned with the video.
524
525   6     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this work, we build a rigorous pipeline to collect high-quality and diverse video-music pairs,
528   curating a comprehensive dataset V2M. Then, we propose VidMuse, a simple yet effective method
529   for video-to-music generation. Our approach utilizes a Long-Short-Term approach to capture both
530   local and global visual clues in the video, allowing for the generation of contextually rich and
531   musically diverse outputs. To validate our method, we benchmark a series of state-of-the-art methods
532   as baselines to compare with VidMuse. Through comprehensive quantitative studies and qualitative
533   analyses, our method has demonstrated its superiority over the existing methods.
534   Limitations. Our work achieves a significant advancement in video-to-music generation, but it still
535   has some limitations. First, the current implementation relies on the EnCodec model (Défossez et al.,
536   2022) trained on a dataset that restricts the system’s capacity to a 32kHz audio sampling rate. This
537   codec exhibits a noticeable reconstruction loss in the audio signal, potentially lowering the quality
538   of the generated music. Second, training large models requires substantial computational resources.
539   Our future work aims to overcome these limitations by integrating advanced codec technologies to
      enhance audio reconstruction fidelity, and exploring a more efficient method design.

931

932   on input FPS and short-term segment duration, detailed in Table A2. To intuitively assess the

937   cannot be compared across different tables since this metric is designed to showcase the dominance

939   and duration tends to enhance model capabilities, suggesting that denser frame sampling yields a
940

       A.3   D ETAILS OF E VALUATION M ETRICS
1027
1028   Frechet Audio Distance (FAD) (Kilgour et al., 2018) is a reference-free evaluation metric for
1029   assessing audio quality. Similar to Frechet Image Distance (FID)(Heusel et al., 2017), it compares
1030   the embedding statistics of the generated audio clip with ground truth audio clips. A shorter distance
1031   usually denotes better human-perceived acoustic-level audio quality. However, this metric cannot
1032   reflect semantic-level information in audio. We report the FAD based on the VGGish(Hershey et al.,
1033   2017) feature extractor.
1034   Frechet Distance (FD) measures the similarity between generated samples and target samples in
1035   audio generation fields. It’s similar to FAD but uses a PANNs feature extractor instead. PANNs(Kong
1036   et al., 2020) have been pre-trained on AudioSet(Gemmeke et al., 2017), one of the largest audio
1037   understanding datasets, thus resulting in a more robust metric than FAD.
1038   Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KL) reflects the acoustic similarity between the generated and
1039   reference samples to a certain extent. It is computed over PANNs’ multi-label class predictions.
1040
1041
       Density and Coverage (Naeem et al., 2020) measures the fidelity and diversity aspects of the
       generated samples. Fidelity measures how closely the generated samples match the real ones, while
1042
       diversity assesses whether the generated samples capture the full range of variation found in real
1043
       samples. We use CLAP(Wu et al., 2023b) embeddings for manifold estimation.
1044
1045   Imagebind Score (Girdhar et al., 2023) assesses to what extent the generated music aligns with the
1046   videos. Despite the fact that Imagebind extends the CLIP to six modalities, we only use the branches
1047
       of audio and vision. Since we use ImageBind to filter out video-audio pairs with a low matching score
       during dataset construction, the ImageBind score is naturally used in our evaluation. We acknowledge
1048
       that ImageBind is not specifically trained on music data, which may limit its effectiveness in capturing
1049
       the full complexity of video-music alignment. However, it remains the most suitable option available
1050   for this task at present.

       A.4   D ETAILS OF THE I NFERENCE P ROCESS
1053
1054   When predicting music on videos of arbitrary length, maintaining music consistency and coherence
1055   is particularly important. However, it leads to a significant challenge on computational resources due
1056   to the quadratic dependency of transformers-based models on sequence length (Zaheer et al., 2020;
1057   Beltagy et al., 2020). To address this problem, we adopt a sliding window approach for inferring the
1058   whole video.
1059   During inference, given an input video with a length of L, we define Ls as the length of the sliding
1060   window and O as the overlap between consecutive windows. With the window start position t initially
1061   set to 0, the inference involves the following steps compactly while t + Ls ≤ L: (1) using a visual
1062   encoder to extract feature representations X and capture long-term dependencies Xl ; (2) collecting
1063   embeddings within the window [t, t + Ls ] to obtain Xs ; (3) predicting the music tokens Ȳ for the
1064   reduced window [t, t + Ls − O] based on Xl and Xs ; (4) decoding Ȳ to the predicted audio Ā using
1065
       the audio decoder; (5) move the window forward by setting t = t + Ls − O, and repeating steps (2)
       to (5) until the end of the video.
1066
1067   After finishing above steps, we can concatenate all musical segments to form a cohesive piece of
1068   music that aligns in duration with the video.
