009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have demonstrated a remarkable capability to edit or imitate im-
012            ages, which has raised concerns regarding the safeguarding of intellectual property.
013            To address these concerns, the adoption of adversarial attacks, which introduce
014            adversarial perturbations that can fool the targeted diffusion model into protected
015            images, has emerged as a viable solution. Consequently, diffusion models, like
016            many other deep network models, are believed to be susceptible to adversarial
017            attacks. However, in this work, we draw attention to an important oversight in
018
               existing research, as all previous studies have focused solely on attacking latent
               diffusion models (LDMs), neglecting adversarial examples for diffusion models
019
               in the pixel space diffusion models (PDMs). Through extensive experiments, we
020
               demonstrate that nearly all existing adversarial attack methods designed for LDMs,
021            as well as adaptive attacks designed for PDMs, fail when applied to PDMs. We
022            attribute the vulnerability of LDMs to their encoders, indicating that diffusion
023            models exhibit strong robustness against adversarial attacks. Building upon this
024            insight, we find that PDMs can be used as an off-the-shelf purifier to effectively
025            eliminate adversarial patterns generated by LDMs, thereby maintaining the in-
026            tegrity of images. Notably, we highlight that most existing protection methods can
027            be easily bypassed using PDM-based purification. We hope our findings prompt
028            a reevaluation of adversarial samples for diffusion models as potential protection
029
               methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Generative diffusion models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022)
034
      have achieved great success in generating images with high fidelity. However, this remarkable
035
      generative capability of diffusion models is accompanied by safety concerns (Zhang et al., 2023a),
036   especially on the unauthorized editing or imitation of personal images such as portraits or individual
037   artworks (Andersen, 2023; Setty, 2023). Recent works (Liang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023; Salman
038   et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023)
039   show that adversarial samples (adv-samples) for diffusion models can be applied as a protection
040   against malicious editing. Small perturbations generated by conventional methods in adversarial
041   machine learning (Madry et al., 2018; Goodfellow et al., 2014) can effectively fool popular diffusion
042   models such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) to produce chaotic results when an imitation
043   attempt is made. However, a significantly overlooked aspect is that all the existing works focus on
044
      latent diffusion models (LDMs) and the pixel-space diffusion models (PDMs) are not studied. For
      LDMs, perturbations are not directly introduced to the input of the diffusion models. Instead, they
045
      are applied externally and propagated through an encoder. It has been shown that the encoder and
046
      decoder of LDMs are vulnerable to adversarial perturbations (Zhang et al., 2023b; Xue et al., 2023),
047   which means that the adv-samples for LDMs have a very different mechanism of action compared
048   to the adv-samples for PDMs. Moreover, some existing works (Liang and Wu, 2023; Salman et al.,
049   2023) show that using an encoder-specific loss can enhance the adversarial attack, (Xue et al., 2023)
050   further demonstrating that the encoder is the bottleneck for attacking LDMs. Building upon this
051   observation, in this paper, we draw attention to the problem of rethinking existing adversarial attack
052   methods for diffusion models by asking the question:
053
                     Can we generate adversarial examples for PDMs as we did for LDMs?

072   We address this question by systematically investigating adv-samples for PDMs. We conduct
073   experiments on various LDMs or PDMs with different network architectures (e.g. U-Net (Ho
074   et al., 2020), Transformer (Peebles and Xie, 2023)), different training datasets, and different input
075   resolutions (e.g. 64, 256, 512). Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that all the existing
076   methods we tested (Liang and Wu, 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023;
077
      Chen et al., 2024; Salman et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023), developed to attack LDMs, fail to generate
      effective adv-samples for PDMs. Moreover, we conduct adaptive attacks for PDMs, applying
078
      strategies like gradient averaging and attacking the intermediate features, but none of the attacks
079
      can effectively change the reverse diffusion process the way the do to fool LDMs. This implies that
080   PDMs are more adversarially robust than we think.
081
082   Building on the insight that PDMs are strongly robust against adversarial perturbations, we further
083
      propose PDM-Pure, a universal purifier that can effectively remove the protective perturbations of
      different scales (e.g. Mist-v2 (Zheng et al., 2023) and Glaze (Shan et al., 2023)) based on PDMs
084
      trained on large datasets. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that PDM-Pure achieves
085
      way better performance than all baseline methods.
086
087   To summarize, the pixel is a barrier to adversarial attack (Figure 1); the diffusion process in the pixel
088   space makes PDMs much more robust than LDMs. This property of PDMs also makes real protection
089
      against the misusage of diffusion models difficult since: (1) no existing attacks have proven effective
      in attacking PDMs, which means no protection can be achieved by fooling a PDM, (2) all the existing
090
      protections against LDMs can be easily purified using a strong PDM. Our contributions are listed
091
      below.
092
093   1. We observe that most existing works on adversarial examples for protection focus on LDMs.
094      Adversarial attacks against PDMs are largely overlooked in this field.
095   2. We fill in the gap in the literature by conducting extensive experiments on various LDMs and
096      PDMs. We discover that all the existing methods fail to attack the PDMs, indicating that PDMs
097      are much more adversarially robust than LDMs.
098   3. Based on this novel insight, we propose a simple yet effective framework termed PDM-Pure that
099      applies strong PDMs as a universal purifier to remove attack-agnostic adversarial perturbations,
100
         easily bypassing almost all existing protective methods.

148   Generative Diffusion Models The generative diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) is
149   one type of generative model, and it has demonstrated remarkable generative capabilities in numerous
150   fields such as images (Rombach et al., 2022; Balaji et al., 2022), 3D data (Poole et al., 2023; Lin
151   et al., 2022), video (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022), stories (Pan et al., 2022; Rahman et al.,
152
      2023) and music (Mittal et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023) generation. Diffusion models, like other
      generative models, are parametrized models pθ (x̂0 ) that can estimate an unknown distribution q(x0 ).
153

198   4.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS D EMONSTRATE S TRONG A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
199
200   While there are many approaches that adopt adversarial perturbation to fool diffusion models, most
201   of them focus only on latent diffusion models due to the wide impact of Stable Diffusion; no attempts
202   have been made to attack PDMs. This lack of investigation may mislead us to conclude that diffusion
203   models, like most deep neural networks, are vulnerable to adversarial perturbations, and that the
204   algorithms used for LDMs can be transferred to PDMs by simply applying the same adversarial loss
205   in the pixel space formulated as: Ladv (x) = Et,ϵ Ext ∼qt (x) ∥ϵθ (xt , t) − ϵ∥22 .
206   However, we show through experiments that PDMs are robust against this form of attack (Figure 2),
207   which means all the existing attacks against diffusion models are, in fact, special cases of attacks
208   against the LDMs only. We conduct extensive experiments on popular LDMs and PDMs structures
209   including Diffusion Transformer (DiT), Guided Diffusion (GD), Stable Diffusion (SD), and Deep-
210   Floyd (IF), and demonstrate in Table 1 that only the LDMs can be attacked and PDMs are not as
211   susceptible to adversarial perturbations: for PDMs, the image quality does not significantly decrease
212
      due to the perturbation both visually and quantitatively. More details and analysis can be found in the
      experiment section.
213
214   Prior to this study, there may have been a prevailing belief that diffusion models could be easily
215   deceived. However, our research reveals an important distinction: it is the LDMs that exhibit
      vulnerability, while the PDMs demonstrate significantly higher adversarial robustness.

305   compared to LDMs. Rather than providing a theoretical proof of the robustness of the diffusion

307   intuitive explanation for why PDMs exhibit greater resilience.
308

309

310

350   5       PDM-P URE : PDM AS A S TRONG U NIVERSAL P URIFIER
351
352   Since PDMs are robust to adversarial perturbations, a natural idea emerges: we can utilize PDMs as a
353   universal purification network. This approach could potentially eliminate any adversarial patterns
354   without knowing the nature of the attacks. We term this framework PDM-Pure, which is a general
355   framework to deal with all the perturbations utilized nowadays. To fully harness the capabilities of
356   PDM-Pure, we need to fulfill two basic requirements: (1) The perturbation adds an out-of-distribution
357   pattern as reflected in existing works on adversarial purification/attacks using diffusion models (Nie
358   et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2024) (2) The PDM being used is strong enough to represent p(x0 ), which
359   can be largely determined by the dataset they are trained on.
360   It is effortless to design a PDM-Pure. The key idea behind this method is to run SDEdit in the pixel
361   space. Given any strong pixel-space diffusion model, we add a small noise to the protected images
362   and run the denoising process (Figure 5), and then the adversarial pattern should be removed. The
363   key idea of PDM-Pure is simple. In practice, we need to adjust the pipeline to fit the resolution of the
364   PDMs being used.
365   In the main paper, we adopt DeepFloyd-IF (Shonenkov et al.), the strongest pixel-space diffusion
366   models nowadays as the purifier. We conduct experiments on purifying protected images sized
367   512 × 512. For images with a larger resolution, purifying in the resolution of 256 × 256 may
368   lose information. In Appendix I we show that PDM-Pure can also be applied to purify patches of
369   high-resolution inputs, removing widely used protections like Glaze on artworks. More details about
370   the how we run DeepFloyd-IF as the purification pipeline are in the Appendix G.

404   et al.).
405   For models trained on the ImageNet (DiT, GD), we run adversarial attacks and purification on a 1k
406   subset of the ImageNet validation dataset. For models trained on LAION, we run tests on the dataset
407   proposed in (Xue et al., 2023), which includes 400 cartoon, artwork, landscape, and portrait images.
408
      For protection methods, we consider almost all the representative approaches, including Ad-
409
      vDM (Liang et al., 2023), SDS (Xue et al., 2023), Mist (Liang and Wu, 2023), Mist-v2 (Zheng et al.,
410   2023), Photoguard (Salman et al., 2023) and Glaze (Shan et al., 2023). We also test the methods in
411   the design space proposed in (Xue et al., 2023), including SDS(-), AdvDM(-), and SDST. In contrast
412   to other existing methods, they are based on gradient descent and have shown great performance in
413   deceiving LDMs.
414
      We measure the SDEdit results after the adversarial attacks using Fréchet Inception Distance
415
      (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) over the relevant datasets (for models trained on ImageNet such as
416   GD (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021) and DiT (Peebles and Xie, 2023) we use a sub-dataset of ImageNet

418   to calculate the FID). We also use Image-Alignment Score (IA-score) (Kumari et al., 2023), which
419   can be used to calculate the cosine-similarity between the CLIP embedding of the edited image and
420   the original image. Also, we use some basic evaluations, where we calculate the Structural Similarity
421   (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) and Perceptual Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018) compared with
422   the original images.
423   All the experiments are written using PyTorch and run in the Linux system, and all of them can be
424   conducted on four A6000 GPUs.

426   6.2   (C1) D IFFUSION D ENOISING P ROCESS IS M ORE ROBUST T HAN W E T HINK
427
428   In Table 1, we attack different LDMs and PDMs with one of the most popular adversarial
429   losses (Zheng et al., 2023) in Equation 1, which can be interpreted as fooling the denoiser us-
430   ing a Monte-Carlo-based loss. Given the attacked samples, we test the SDEdit results on the attacked
431   samples, which can be generally used to test whether the samples are adversarial for the diffusion
      model or not. We use FID-score (Heusel et al., 2017), SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), LPIPS (Zhang et al.,


                                                          8

432
      2018), and IA-Score (Kumari et al., 2023) to measure the quality of the attack. If the quality of the
433   generated images decreases a lot compared to edited clean images, then the attack is successful. We
434   found that for all LDMs, attacks using the adversarial loss successfully provide protection. However,
435   for all PDMs, the adversarial attacks do not work. This phenomenon occurs across all scales of
436   perturbation. For example, when the FID of LDMs increased by over 100, the FID of PDMs remained
437   nearly unchanged. We also show some visualizations in Figure 2, which illustrate that the perturbation
438   will affect the LDMs but not the PDMs.
439   To further investigate how robust the PDM is, we test other advanced attacking methods, including
440   the End-to-End Diffusion Attacks (E2E-Photoguard) proposed in (Salman et al., 2023) and the
441   Improved Targeted Attack (ITA) proposed in (Zheng et al., 2023). Though the End-to-End attack
442   is usually impractical to run, it shows the strongest performance when attacking LDMs. We find
443   that both attacks are not successful in PDM settings. We show attacked samples and edited samples
444   in Figure 2, 3, 4 as well as the Appendix H. In conclusion, existing adversarial attack methods for
445   diffusion models can only work for LDMs, and PDMs are more robust than we think.
446
447   6.3   (C2) PDM-P URE : A U NIVERSAL P URIFIER THAT IS S IMPLE YET E FFECTIVE
448
449   PDM-Pure is simple: we just run SDEdit to purify the protected image in the pixel space. Given
450   our assumption that PDMs are quite robust, we can use PDMs trained on large-scale datasets as a
451   universal black-box purifier. We follow the model pipeline introduced in Section 5 and purify images
452
      protected by various methods as shown in Table 2.
453   PDM-Pure is effective: from Table 2 we can see that the purification will remove adversarial patterns
454   for all the protection methods we tested, largely decreasing the FID score for the SDEdit task. Also,
455   we test the protected images and purified images in more tasks including Image Inpainting (Song
456   et al., 2020), Textual-Inversion (Gal et al., 2022), and LoRA customization (Hu et al., 2021). We
457   show purification results for inpainting in Figure 12, and purification results for LoRA in Figure 7.
458
      We show more results in Figure 16 in the appendix.
459   Both qualitative and quantitative results show that the purified images are no longer adversarial and
460   can be effectively edited or imitated in different tasks without any obstruction.
461
      Also, PDM-Pure shows SOTA results compared with previous purification methods, including
462   some simple purifiers based on compression and filtering like Adv-Clean, crop-and-resize, JPEG
463   Compression, and SDEdit-based methods like GrIDPure (Zhao et al., 2023), which uses patchified
464   SDEdit with a GD (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021). We also add LDM-Pure as a baseline to show that
465   LDMs can not be used to purify the protected images. For GrIDPure, we use Guided-Diffusion
466   trained on ImageNet to run patchified purification. All the experiments are conducted on the datasets
467   collected in (Xue et al., 2023) under the resolution of 512 × 512. Results for higher resolutions
468   are presented in Appendix I. We also test the ablation of timesteps used for PDM-Pure in Appendix
469   Appendix J, from which we can see the sweet point of timesteps: t∗ around 0.15 works well. We also
470
      find that PDM-Pure works better for cartoon pictures with larger plain color patches. For pictures
      with many details like oil paintings, it will lose some detail; however, generally the art style can
471
      still be learned well by LoRA from the attacker’s perspective (e.g. Claude Monet-style in Appendix

      Figure 13 ).

475   7     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
476
477   In this paper, we present novel insights that while many studies demonstrate the ease of finding
478   adversarial samples for Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), Pixel Diffusion Models (PDMs) exhibit far
479   greater adversarial robustness than previously assumed. We are the first to investigate the adversarial
480   samples for PDMs, revealing a surprising discovery that existing attacks fail to fool PDMs. Leveraging
481   this insight, we propose utilizing strong PDMs as universal purifiers, resulting in PDM-Pure, a simple
482
      yet effective framework that can purify protective perturbations in a black-box manner.
483   Pixel is a barrier for real protection against adversarial attacks. Since PDMs are quite robust, they
484   cannot be easily attacked. PDMs can even be used to purify the protective perturbations, challenging
485   the current assumption for the safe protection of generative diffusion models. We advocate rethinking
      the problem of adversarial samples for generative diffusion models and unauthorized image protection


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      based on it. More rigorous studies need to be conducted to better understand the mechanism behind
487
      the robustness of PDMs. Furthermore, we can utilize it as a new structure for many other tasks.

652   A    B ROADER I MPACT
653
654   We present significant insights in two crucial areas: adversarial machine learning research on
655   generative diffusion models, and the protection of copyright against the malicious use of diffusion
656   models. While existing works have revealed the vulnerability of latent diffusion models, we show
657   that the general diffusion model in the pixel space is quite robust. PDMs reveal two new threats to the
658   safety application of diffusion models: (1) since PDMs are robust and no existing perturbation can
659   effectively attack them, it means that copyright protection against PDMs cannot be easily achieved
660
      with existing protective perturbations (2) PDMs can be used to purify the protective noise used to
      protect the LDMs, meaning that the current protection for LDMs can be bypassed. We still have
661
      a long way to go to achieve good protection against diffusion models, and more efforts should be
662
      dedicated to enhancing copyright protection for PDMs and making current protective measures more
663   robust and reliable.

691   which has been widely studied in this field. It supports resolutions of 256 × 256 and 512 × 512, both
692   can be easily attacked. The encoder first encodes the image sized H × W into the latent space sized
693   4 × H/4 × W/4, and then uses U-Net combined with cross-attention to run the denoising process.
694
695
      Stable Diffusion V-1.5 (LDM) It has the same structure as Stable Diffusion V-1.4, which is also
696
      stronger since it is trained with more steps, from https://huggingface.co/runwayml/
697   stable-diffusion-v1-5.
698
699
700   DiT-XL (LDM) It is another popular latent diffusion model, that uses the backbone of the
701   Transformer instead of the U-Net. We use the implementation from the original repository
      https://github.com/facebookresearch/DiT/.

705

706   from each other with different adversarial losses:
707
708
      AdvDM AdvDM is one of the first adversarial attacks proposed in (Liang et al., 2023), it used a
709
      Monte-Carlo-based adversarial loss which can effectively attack latent diffusion models, we also call
710   this loss semantic loss:

718

723
724                                           L = λLT (x) + LS (x)                                         (5)

727   the original AdvDM, where the gradient ∇x L(x) is expensive to compute. By using the score
728   distillation-based loss, it shows good performance and remains effective at the same time:

      Mist-v2 It was proposed in (Zheng et al., 2023) using the Improved Targeted Attack (ITA), which

      In an attempt to extend the latent-space attacks onto PDMs, (Shih et al., 2024) introduces atkPDM+.
759
      This method uses a pre-trained VAE to attack the PDM by extracting feature vectors from the encoder
760   network. The attack optimizes the latent vector with a Wasserstein distance objective calculated at
761   the VAE middle layer activations:
762
                                                              (mid)           (mid)
763                          Lattack (xt , xadv
                                            t ) = −W2 (Uθ             (xt ), Uθ       (xadv
                                                                                        t ))
764
765   A second optimization cycle is then run to limit the change in pixel-space by optimizing the distance
766   between the feature vector generated by a pre-trained image classifier taken from the original image
767   and the decoded attacked latent.

773
774   Here we introduce the quantitative measurement we used in our experiments:
775
776          • We measure the SDEdit results after the adversarial attacks using Fréchet Inception Distance
777            (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) over the relevant datasets (for models trained on ImageNet such
778            as GD (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021) and DiT (Peebles and Xie, 2023) we use a sub-dataset
779            of ImageNet as the relevant dataset, for those trained on LAION, we use the collected
780
               dataset to calculate the FID). We also use Image-Alignment Score (IA-score) (Kumari et al.,
               2023), which can be used to calculate the cosine-similarity between the CLIP embedding
781
               of the edited image and the original image. Also, we use some basic evaluations, where
782
               we calculate the Structural Similarity (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) and Perceptual Similarity
783            (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018) compared with the original images.
784
785
             • To measure the purification results, we test the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel
               et al., 2017) over the collected dataset compared with the dataset generated by running
786
               SDEdit over the purified images in the strength of 0.3.

789   F    D ETAILS ABOUT D IFFERENT P URIFICATION M ETHODS
790
791   Adv-Clean: https://github.com/lllyasviel/AdverseCleaner, a training-free
792   filter-based method that can remove adversarial noise for a diffusion model, it works well to remove
793   high-frequency noise.
794
795   Crop & Resize: first crops the image by 20% and then resizes the image to the original size, it
796   turns out to be one of the most effective defense methods (Liang and Wu, 2023).
797
798   JPEG compression: (Sandoval-Segura et al., 2023) reveals that JPEG compression can be a good
799   purification method, and we adopt the 65% as the quality of compression in (Sandoval-Segura et al.,
800   2023).
801
802   LDM-Pure: We also try to use LDMs to run SDEdit as a naive purifier, sadly it does not work,
803   because the adversarial protection transfers well between different LDMs.
804
805   GrIDPure: It is proposed in (Zhao et al., 2023) as a purifier, GrIDPure first divides an image into
806   patches sized 128 × 128, and then purifies the 9 patches sized 256 × 256. Also, it combined the four
807   corners sized 128 × 128 to purify it so we have 10 patches to purify in total. After running SDEdit
808   with a small noise (set to 0.1T ), we reassemble the patches into the original size, pixel values are
809   assigned using the average values of the patches they belong to. More details can be seen in (Zhao
      et al., 2023).

      G     D ETAILS ABOUT PDM-P URE
811
812
      Here, we explain in detail how to adapt DeepFloyd-IF (Shonenkov et al.), the strongest open-source
813
      PDM as far as we know, for PDM-Pure. DeepFloyd-IF is a cascaded text-to-image diffusion model
814   trained on 1.2B text-image pairs from LAION dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2022). It contains three
815   stages named IF-Stage I, II, and III. Here we only use Stage II and III since Stage I works in a
816   resolution of 64 which is too low. Given a perturbed image xW ×H sized W × H, we first resize it
817   into x64×64 and x256×256 . Then we use a general prompt P to do SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) using
818   the Stage II model:
819
820
                                           xt = IF-II(xt+1 , x64×64 , P)                                     (9)
821
822
      where t = Tedit − 1, ..., 1, 0, xTedit = x256×256 . A larger Tedit may be used for larger noise. x0 is
823   the purified image we get in the 256 × 256 resolution space, where the adversarial patterns should
824   be already purified. We can then use IF Stage III to further up-sample it into 1024 × 1024 with
825   x1024×1024 = IF-III(x0 , p). Finally, we can sample into H × W as we want through downsampling.
826   This whole process is demonstrated in Figure 5. After purification, the image is no longer adversarial
827   to the targeted diffusion models and can be effectively used in downstream tasks.

       J           A BLATIONS OF t∗ IN PDM-P URE
1070
1071
1072   The PDM-Pure on DeepFloyd-IF we used in this paper uses the default settings of SDEdit with
1073   t∗ = 0.1T . And we respace the diffusion model into 100 steps, so we only need to run 10 denoising
1074   steps. It can be run on one A6000 GPU, occupying 22G VRAM in 30 seconds.
1075   Here we show some ablation about the choice of t∗ . In fact, in many SDEdit papers, t∗ can be
1076   roughly defined by trying different t∗ that can be used to purify different levels of noise. We try
1077   t∗ = 0.01, 0.1, 0.2, in Figure 15 we can see that when t∗ = 0.01 the noise is not fully purified, and

1079   different images and different noises can be slightly different, so one is advised to do some trials
       before purification.
