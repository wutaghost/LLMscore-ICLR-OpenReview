009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Test-time adaptation (TTA) has emerged as a zero-shot learning approach to address
012            distribution shifts across domains without needing source data. While current
013            methods focus on adapting vision and language models (VLMs) using prompt
014            tuning, they struggle with ambiguous categories due to the challenge of selecting
015            relevant attributes in the absence of labels. To address this issue, we propose a novel
016            framework, termed Search4Prompt, which aims to identify “good” attributes and
017            learn tailored prompts during test-time prompt learning (TTPL). Search4Prompt
018
               consists of two main components: the Retrieval-based Attribute Search (RAS)
               and the Implicit-Explicit Attribute Injection (IEAI) module. RAS constructs an
019
               attribute bank by generating detailed descriptions for predefined categories, and
020
               then identifies the most relevant attributes based on the semantic similarity between
021            the test image and the attributes. This enables the selection of “good” attributes
022            that are well-suited to the test samples. The IEAI module operates in two ways.
023            First, it employs pseudo-label learning, where the selected attributes contribute to a
024            voting process that implicitly injects attribute knowledge into prompt learning.
025            Second, it augments the original category names with the selected attributes,
026            explicitly enhancing the semantic representation of ambiguous categories. This dual
027            approach improves the model’s discriminability during test-time prompt learning.
028            Experimental results demonstrate that Search4Prompt outperforms existing TTA
029
               methods on several benchmark datasets, confirming its effectiveness in narrowing
               domain gaps and handling ambiguous categories.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Vision-language foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) have demonstrated remarkable
035
      generalization across various recognition tasks. These models are pre-trained on large-scale web
036   data using a contrastive loss and can be fine-tuned for specific tasks with additional training data.
037   While this approach has led to significant advancements, its success is highly dependent on the
038   assumption that there is no distribution shift between the training and test data. In reality, distribution
039   shifts are common due to factors such as natural variations or changes in sensing equipment, making
040   it impractical to collect sufficient training data for every possible test domain. To address these
041   challenges, test-time adaptation (TTA) has emerged as a strategy to adapt pre-trained models to test
042   data by minimizing entropy, without the need for source data (Kim et al., 2020; Shanmugam et al.,
043   2021; Wang et al., 2020; Niu et al., 2023).
044   Among TTA methods (Wang et al., 2020; 2022; Yang et al., 2023a), test-time prompt learning
045   (TTPL) (Shu et al., 2022; Abdul Samadh et al., 2024) has gained prominence for maintaining the
046   generalization capabilities of vision-language models (VLMs) (Kumar et al., 2022). Inspired by
047   natural language processing (NLP), TTPL introduces learnable prompt vectors by appending them
048   to predefined class names. However, the performance of these models deteriorates when faced
049   with ambiguous categories, particularly in tasks requiring fine-grained recognition. As illustrated in
050
      the “baseline” of Figure 1, broad or overlapping categories such as “red velvet cake”, “cupcakes”,
      and “carrot cake” are not adequately represented by the learned prompt vectors, leading to reduced
051
      effectiveness.
052
053   A natural approach to address ambiguous categories is to expand their definitions with detailed
      attributes. One potential solution, though unexplored in TTPL, is to leverage large language models

082   To address these challenges, we propose a novel framework called Search4Prompt, designed to
083   identify representative “good” attributes and learn tailored prompts for test samples during TTPL.
084   The framework consists of two main components: the Retrieval-based Attribute Search (RAS) and
085   the Implicit-Explicit Attribute Injection (IEAI) module. RAS begins by generating extensive attribute
086
      descriptions for predefined categories, creating an attribute bank. It then identifies the most relevant
      attributes by comparing the similarities between the CLIP features of the test image and the attributes,
087
      allowing us to find “good” discriminative attributes that are best suited for each sample.
088
089   To fully leverage the identified attributes, IEAI injects this attribute knowledge into TTPL through
090   both implicit and explicit means. Inspired by how humans recognize objects by focusing on key
091   details, we introduce a voting-based pseudo-label learning method, where the selected attributes are
092
      combined into scores that act as soft pseudo-labels. By aligning model predictions with these soft
      labels, we implicitly guide VLMs to focus on the most important features, especially for ambiguous
093
      or fine-grained categories.
094
095   Additionally, the success of adapting VLMs to downstream tasks often depends on the quality of
096   category descriptions, which are typically fixed during TTPL (Shu et al., 2022; Abdul Samadh et al.,
097   2024). To improve alignment with test samples, we implement a compositional attribute augmentation
098
      strategy, which enriches category descriptions with the most relevant attributes. This strategy detects
      ambiguous categories using pre-trained VLMs and augments them with the searched attributes,
099
      providing richer and more informative context. By improving the quality of category descriptions,
100
      our method ensures more effective generalization to test data, even in challenging scenarios.
101
102   In summary, our contributions are fourfold:
103   • We propose a novel Search4Prompt framework that discovers discriminative attributes from rich
104     contextual information for learning more effective, informative prompts during test-time adaptation.
105   • We introduce the Retrieval-based Attribute Search module to effectively identify the most relevant
106     attributes that are important for each testing sample.
107   • We present the Implicit-Explicit Attribute Injection module to facilitate the pseudo-labeling and
        prompt learning with the selected attributes.

113   2    R ELATED W ORK
114
115   Prompt Learning in Vision-Language Models. Vision-language models (VLMs) (Radford et al.,
116   2021; Jia et al., 2021), pre-trained on vast image-text pairs from the web, have demonstrated powerful
117
      zero-shot generalization across downstream recognition tasks. However, efficiently adapting VLMs
      to specific downstream tasks with limited data remains a challenge. Recently, prompt learning has
118
      become a leading approach for adapting VLMs with unlabeled (Shu et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023;
119
      Ma et al., 2024) or limited labeled data.
120
121   CoOp used the fine-tuning of VLMs by optimizing continuous prompt vectors in the language
122   branch for few-shot image recognition. Subsequent works, such as CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a)
123
      and UPT (Zang et al., 2022), further conditioned textual prompts on visual features to enhance
      the model’s generalization to unseen domains. Other methods, like KgCoOp (Yao et al., 2023),
124
      ProGrad (Zhu et al., 2023a), and ProReg (Zhu et al., 2023b), introduced prompt regularization
125
      strategies to minimize discrepancies between task-specific knowledge and the model’s inherent
126   general knowledge. Additional efforts explored prompt prior distribution (Lu et al., 2022), external
127   knowledge (Shen et al., 2024), and optimal transport (Chen et al., 2022) for improving VLMs’
128   performance on downstream classification tasks. Moreover, Maple (Khattak et al., 2023a) and
129   PromptSRC (Khattak et al., 2023b) connected image and text prompts via linear projections to
130   learn vision-textual prompts, while PromptKD (Zheng et al., 2024) introduced a prompt distillation
131   framework to transfer knowledge from a large teacher model to a smaller student model.
132   While these methods have achieved substantial performance gains, their effectiveness significantly
133   diminishes when test samples come from a different distribution. Our work focuses on test-time
134   adaptation, enabling VLMs to adapt to test data without relying on the original training data.
135
136   Test-Time Adaptation. Test-time adaptation (TTA) (Sun et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang
137   et al., 2020) aims to reduce distribution shifts between training and test data at test time, without
138   accessing source data. Some approaches (Liu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2020; Gandelsman et al., 2022)
139   employ self-supervised proxy tasks to improve generalization, though these methods often require
140   modifying the training process, limiting their applicability in the pre-trained model era.
141   To address this, fully test-time adaptation methods have been developed to adapt pre-trained models
142   by enforcing self-consistency (Zhang et al., 2022), aligning train-test statistics (Mirza et al., 2022),
143   or minimizing entropy in batch-wise predictions (Wang et al., 2020). Recent advances, such as
144   TPT (Shu et al., 2022), extend entropy minimization to VLMs by learning textual prompts for each
145   test sample. Other methods, like DiffTPT (Feng et al., 2023) and MTA (Zanella & Ben Ayed,
146   2024), leverage generated images from diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) to increase test data
147   diversity. PromptAlign (Abdul Samadh et al., 2024) introduces distribution alignment by utilizing
148   token distribution statistics, while TDA (Karmanov et al., 2024) designs dynamic adapters for efficient
149
      test-time adaptation in VLMs.
150   In contrast to these methods, our approach utilizes fine-grained attributes to guide models toward
151   class-specific semantics, enabling better adaptation to the test domain through attribute selection and
152   augmentation.
153
154   Language-based Visual Recognition. Visual recognition traditionally involves comparing an
155   image to a fixed set of categories for classification. Recent advances have leveraged VLMs to achieve
156   strong zero-shot recognition by utilizing both vision and language spaces. Early applications of
157   VLMs relied on simple object names, often overlooking the rich contextual information (e.g., color,
158
      shape, texture) that language provides.
159   Some works (Zhai et al., 2024) enhance vision-language models by rewriting broad category descrip-
160   tions using large language models (LLMs). Few-shot recognition methods (Yang et al., 2023b; Yan
161   et al., 2023) decompose object recognition into attribute concepts, learning to classify images based
      on these attributes. Additionally, several prompt-tuning approaches (Tian et al., 2024; Mao et al.,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2023) improve model performance by utilizing attributes to refine the reasoning behind categorical
163   predictions.
164
165   Unlike previous methods that use attributes to train shared networks or prompts for all test data, our
166
      approach Retrieves discriminative attributes tailored to each test sample, adapting VLMs for test-time
      visual recognition in a more precise and effective manner.

257   Discussion. Our method aims to harness the capabilities of the existing foundational models to deliver
258   comprehensive and accurate textual information for zero-shot vision recognition during test time. To
259   achieve this, we utilize GPT-3 Mann et al. (2020) as the LLM to obtain attributes and the ViT-H-based
260   CLIP Radford et al. (2021) as the auxiliary visual language model (VLM) to identify discriminative
261
      attributes. We also experiment with other auxiliary VLMs, such as ViT-B and ViT-G-based CLIP, to
      encode descriptive text. However, it is demonstrated that a smaller model has limited efficacy for
262
      fine-grained classification, as ViT-B-based CLIP tends to focus on more general attributes within the
263
      attribute vocabulary. Using Flower102 for instance, ViT-B-based CLIP tends to assign high similarity
264   to broader attributes such as “Plant & Flower”, whereas ViT-G-based CLIP recognizes more nuanced
265   attributes like “white chest and paws & black markings on the face”. Compared to existing methods
266   that utilize only coarse-grained category names for test-time vision recognition, we explore the
267   potential of incorporating fine-grained textual information to facilitate test-time prompt learning by
268   using freely available public foundational models, which would be a new trend in the community. In
269   addition, our approach has several advantages. Firstly, the RAS is an offline phase, where we only
      need to process all attribute vocabulary once, avoiding excessive computational overhead. Secondly,

275   3.2     I MPLICIT-E XPLICIT ATTRIBUTE INJECTION
276
277   Although appending all the searched attribute descriptions behind learnable prompt vectors achieves
278   more accurate predictions, the optimization of learnable prompts still lacks attribute-based guidance
279   during test-time prompt learning. Thus, the learned prompts inevitably neglect the relationships
280
      across attributes contained in different categories, which leads to a sub-optimal solution, as shown
      in 1. To handle this drawback and fully utilize the searched attribute descriptions, we design an
281
      Implicit-Explicit Attribute Injection (IEAI), which mainly consists of a voting-based pseudo-label
282
      learning and a compositional augmentation strategy to enhance the model’s zero-shot generalization
283   ability.
284
285   3.2.1    VOTING - BASED P SEUDO -L ABEL L EARNING
286
287   In light of the discriminative attribute description generated during the RAS phase, the primary
288   challenge lies in effectively utilizing them to adapt VLMs to unlabeled test data. To address this,
289
      we propose a voting-for-prompting mechanism to softly vote attributes on predefined categories to
      generate voting scores that serve as pseudo-labels for test-time prompt learning. Concretely, this
290
      mechanism involves ranking attribute vocabulary, followed by voting within predefined task-specific
291
      categories to estimate the model predictions. Such predictions can guide model to learn textual
292   prompts for different test samples using rich context information that language affords.
293
294   Soft voting within object categories. Traditional approaches often overlook the potential of soft
295
      labels derived from task-specific categories and their corresponding attributes. By designing a
      soft voting procedure within these categories, we aim to determine scores among top-K attributes,
296
      which will serve as soft labels. These soft labels provide additional cues that guide the model to
297
      effectively utilize attribute information, enhancing its predictive capabilities through prompt learning.
298   Specifically, given task-specific categories {c1 , c2 , . . . , cN }, we set the initial voting score for all
299   categories to zero, and then traverse the top-K grounding attributes for each category. If grounding
300   attribute ak belongs to category cn , we increase the voting score of the corresponding category by
301   the matching value between them; otherwise, the score remains unchanged. Following this procedure,
302   we derive the soft voting scores {s1 , s2 , . . . , sN } for the test sample across the candidate categories,
303   where higher scores reflect greater confidence in the model’s predictions for the sample. To stabilize
304   the training process, we apply softmax normalization to these voting scores,

313   3.2.2    C OMPOSITIONAL ATTRIBUTE AUGMENTATION
314
      Existing methods typically enhance the discriminability among candidate categories using learned
315
      contextual prompts Shu et al. (2022) or rewriting language descriptions Fan et al. (2024). In our
316   test-time adaptation paradigm, ambiguous categories vary across different test samples. To effectively
317   mitigate category ambiguity tailored to the test sample, we design a customized attribute augmentation
318   strategy to augment ambiguous categories with compositional attributes. Specifically, given a test
319   sample from the downstream task, we determine candidate categories, which appear in the top-K
320   attribute vocabularies ÂK , as its ambiguous categories. In our attribute vocabularies, each ambiguous
321   category possesses multiple distinct attribute features. We composite these attributes into ambiguous
322   categories to enhance their clarity and specificity. Compared to the original categories, the augmented
323   categories exhibit stronger discriminative power through attributes, which enables the model to
      explore fine-grained attribute prompts and thus generalize better to test data.

525   5    C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATION
526
527
      In this work, we investigated how to fully exploit the fine-grained attribute descriptions generated
528   by LLM to enhance the zero-shot generalizability of vision-language models. We developed a
529   Search4Prompt learning framework for test-time zero-shot recognition by leveraging visual attributes
530   to guide the model toward class-specific modeling. The proposed Search4Prompt first searches
531   discriminative attributes for test samples and then utilizes these attributes to learn tailored prompts
532   that adapt VLMs to test data. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our method on the robustness to
533   out-of-domain distribution shifts and cross-domain generalization.
534   Limitation: Dependence on Visual Dictionary and Data. The effectiveness of our method relies
535   on a comprehensive attribute vocabulary. Despite the collection of numerous category names from
536   existing datasets, there may still be deficiencies in specific tasks, such as Stanford Cars Krause
537   et al. (2013) and FGVC Aircraft Maji et al. (2013). In such scenarios, our method may be unable
538   to generate informative textual descriptions, leading to suboptimal results. Furthermore, in coarse-
539   grained datasets, the existing category names are typically sufficient, and there are fewer ambiguous
      category descriptions. Consequently, our method may not exhibit its advantages in these datasets.
