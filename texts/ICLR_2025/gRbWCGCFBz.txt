009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Through the integration of external tools, large language models (LLMs) such as
012            GPT-4o and Llama 3.1 significantly expand their functional capabilities, evolving
013            from elementary conversational agents to general-purpose assistants. We contend
014            that the primary drivers of these advancements are the quality and diversity of the
015            training data. However, the existing LLMs with external tool integration provide
016            only limited transparency regarding their datasets and data collection approaches,
017            which has led to the initiation of this study. Specifically, in this work, we endeavor
018
               to present a detailed exposition of the methodology for constructing datasets that
               facilitate LLMs in effectively learning how to utilize external tools and make this
019
               process available to the public through the introduction of ToolBridge. ToolBridge
020
               proposes to leverage a collection of general open-access datasets as its raw dataset
021            pool and incorporates a series of strategies to identify the appropriate data entries
022            for external tool API insertions. By supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on these curated
023            data entries, LLMs can invoke external tools in appropriate contexts to boost their
024            predictive accuracy, particularly for essential functions including factual retrieval,
025            data processing and numerical computation. Our experiments meticulously isolate
026            model architectures and training configurations, zeroing in exclusively on the role
027            of data. The experimental results indicate that LLMs trained on ToolBridge exhibit
028            consistent performance gains on both standard benchmarks and custom evaluation
029
               datasets. All associated code and data will be released as open source, promoting
               transparency and facilitating the broader community to explore methodologies for
030
               equipping LLMs with external tools capabilities.

      Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, excelling in tasks
036   including question answering, summarization, and text generation Jiang et al. (2023); Achiam et al.
037   (2023); Dubey et al. (2024); Gunter et al. (2024); Team et al. (2024). Despite the impressive achieve-
038   ments of LLMs, they persistently underperform in fundamental areas, such as arithmetic and factual
039   lookup, where external tools can effectively provide solutions Schick et al. (2024).
040
      Consequently, there has been a rise in research efforts committed to equipping LLMs with the ability
041
      to utilize external tools. These efforts can be broadly categorized into two distinct areas: (1) function
042   calling, such as Toolllm Qin et al. (2023) and API-Bank Li et al. (2023), which offer curated datasets
043   and standardized evaluation protocols to systematically assess and compare the tool-use capabilities
044   of LLMs, focusing on enabling LLMs to function as agents that call APIs without requiring explicit
045   training on their usage. (2) tool former, exemplified by GPT-4o and Toolformer Schick et al. (2024),
046   which seek to empower LLMs with the capability to autonomously invoke and utilize external tools
047   during inference, emphasizing advanced reasoning by enabling LLMs to discern which external tool
048   to call, the appropriate timing for the call, and the systematic parsing and integration of the execution
049   results into the generated text. This study primarily concentrates on the latter area.
050   Within the latter context, Llama 3.1 Dubey et al. (2024) is trained to utilize Brave Search, Wolfram
051   Alpha API and Python interpreter by incorporating synthetic data combined with human-annotated
052   data. Toolformer Schick et al. (2024) adopts a self-supervised training strategy to equip LLMs with
053   the capability to leverage diverse tools effectively. However, these existing works tend to either only
      open-source their trained models and code or restrict access to the models through usage interfaces

128   2      R ELATED W ORK
129
130   Tool Use for LLMs. Enabling LLMs to use external tools like search engines and code interpreters
131   significantly broadens the range of tasks LLMs can address and strengthens their predictive accuracy.
132   The methodologies for equipping LLMs with the capability to employ external tools can be broadly
133   categorized into two paradigms, i.e., function calling and tool former. In particular, function calling
134   emphasizes allowing LLMs to act as agents that invoke APIs using predefined functions and prompts
135
      without necessitating explicit training on their usage Li et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2024). Conversely,
      tool former prioritize empowering LLMs with the ability to autonomously identify appropriate tools,
136
      determine the optimal invocation timings, and incorporate the outputs into their reasoning processes.
137
      For example, Komeili (2021) proposed to enable LLMs to adopt a search engine by learning to yield
138   an internet search query based on the context, and then condition its generated response on the search
139   results. Cobbe et al. (2021) facilitated LLMs’ utilization of a calculator during inference by training
140   the models with calculation annotations injected into the datasets. Thoppilan et al. (2022) proposed
141   to assist LLMs in invoking external tools from a toolset, comprising an information retrieval system,
142   a calculator and a translator, by training it to produce a special string TS. Gao et al. (2023) suggested
143   adopting LLMs to interpret natural language problems and yield programs as intermediate reasoning,
144   while delegating the solution process to a runtime environment like a Python interpreter. Toolformer
145   Schick et al. (2024) allowed LLMs to learn how to adopt the external tools through a self-supervised
146
      learning approach. Of late, the works like GPT-4o, Llama 3.1 Dubey et al. (2024) and Apple LLMs
      further strengthened LLMs’ ability to leverage external tools through improvements in training data,
147
      model architectures, etc. This study falls into the latter category, namely tool former.
148
149   Although previous research in the domain of tool former are highly praiseworthy, they seldom make
150   the data required for training their models publicly available, which is crucial for the community to
151   advance research and build upon their contributions. This paper presents a pipeline aimed at yielding
152
      data entries for training models in external tool utilization, along with open-sourcing all data entries
      produced using this methodology This open access facilitates the development of more effective and
153
      efficient algorithms for the next generation of LLMs integrated with external tool functionalities.
154
155   Training Datasets for Tool Use. Previous datasets designed to train LLMs to utilize external tools
156   primarily fall within the function calling paradigm. For instance, Qin et al. (2023) collected a high-
157   quality instruction-tuning dataset ToolBench, which is constructed automatically adopting ChatGPT.
158
      Li et al. (2023) introduced API-Bank, which encompasses 1,888 tool-use dialogues from 2,138 APIs
      spanning 1,000 distinct domains. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is a notable absence
159
      of research efforts that have open-sourced training datasets within the tool former domain.
160
161   To address this significant gap, this paper presents ToolBridge - a dataset of more than 178,000 data
      entries to support LLMs in effectively learning to utilize external tools within tool former paradigm.

      3.1   DATASET P OOL C ONSTRUCTION
186
187   Our work starts with a review of the data accessible in the community for the purpose of supervised
188   fine-tuning (SFT). Table 1 summarizes the results. Owing to the diverse range of teams contributing
189   the SFT datasets, there is significant heterogeneity in their formats, which introduces difficulties on
190   effective model training. Hence, we first reformat all candidate datasets into a standardized ChatML
191   format for further processing:
192
               data entry = [{"role": "user", "content": "..."},
193
                             {"role": "assistant", "content": "..."},
194
                             ...].
195
196   After reformatting all datasets, we construct the dataset pool as P = {(Di , Wi , Qi ) | i ≥ 0}, where
197   Di denotes one candidate dataset, Wi measures the proportion of valuable entries for each dataset,
198   and Qi serves as a metric for assessing the quality of each dataset.
199
      Practically, to obtain Wi , we first perform random sampling on Di to produce a subset Si , containing
200
      1% data entries of Di . Llama3-70B is then applied to judge the appropriateness of each entry in Si
201   for external tool invocation to enhance reasoning, where the prompt employed is shown in Appendix
202   A.1. In generally, if an entry is deemed suitable for invoking external tools to help LLMs’ reasoning
203   process, we label it as a valuable entry. At last, we determine Wi as the ratio between the number of
204   valuable data entries and the total number of data entries in Si .
205
      Additionally, we observe that some candidate datasets within the dataset pool P are partially sourced
206
      from the Internet via web scraping, resulting in the inclusion of certain meaningless HTML tags and
207   other irrelevant content. Thus, we incorporate Qi as an additional metric to evaluate the data quality
208   of each candidate dataset. To compute Qi , we randomly sample N data entries from Di and conduct
209   a manual review to identify any presence of irrelevant characters or content, where we configure N
210   as 100 by default. Qi is then derived as the fraction of data entries devoid of irrelevant contents over
211   the total number of sampled entries N .

401   the total training epoch at 3.

403   to handle potential tool call failures during inference, we propose to eliminate failed tool calls from

520
      Besides, one should be aware that relying on external tools to improve the fact retrieval performance
521   of LLMs can be inconsistent. During our experiments, we uncover the following deficiencies
522
523          • Using external tools like the requests module to obtain information from external websites
524            can lead to potentially unreliable information and LLMs thus yield incorrect predictions. A
525            solution to address this issue is to construct a curated list of trustworthy domains, restricting
526            LLMs from accessing websites beyond this approved whitelist. Besides, we should further
527
               refine the training entries to exclude unreliable access links.
528          • The content scraped from web pages may be overly lengthy, hindering the ability of LLMs
529            to efficiently extract useful information from the scraped contents.
530          • When LLMs access some external websites, they may attempt to download files from those
531            sites, which poses potential security risks in real-world applications.
532
      Therefore, we recommend that ToolBridge is only used for academic research, with controlled mea-
533
      sures in place when it attempts to access external websites.

762   Algorithm 2 Identifying Code Patterns: Distinguishing Assignments Followed by Print Statements

       1    import ast
764
       2
765    3    '''
766    4    Function:
767    5        Identifying Code Patterns: Distinguishing Assignments Followed
768                  by Print Statements
       6    Argument:
769
       7        node: node = ast.parse(code)
770    8    '''
771    9    def isuselesscode(node):
772   10        # Check if the node is an AST Module
