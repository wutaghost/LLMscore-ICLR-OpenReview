009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The vector quantization is a widely used method to map continuous representa-
012
               tion to discrete space and has important application in tokenization for generative
013
               mode, bottlenecking information and many other tasks in machine learning. Vec-
014            tor Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) is a type of variational autoen-
015            coder using discrete embedding as latent. We generalize the technique further,
016            enriching the probabilistic framework with a Gaussian mixture as the underly-
017            ing generative model. This framework leverages a codebook of latent means and
018            adaptive variances to capture complex data distributions. This principled frame-
019            work avoids various heuristics and strong assumptions that are needed with the
020            VQ-VAE to address training instability and to improve codebook utilization. This
021            approach integrates the benefits of both discrete and continuous representations
022
               within a variational Bayesian framework. Furthermore, by introducing the Aggre-
               gated Categorical Posterior Evidence Lower Bound (ALBO), we offer a princi-
023
               pled alternative optimization objective that aligns variational distributions with the
024
               generative model. Our experiments demonstrate that GM-VQ improves codebook
025            utilization and reduces information loss without relying on handcrafted heuristics.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) were originally designed for modeling
032   continuous representations; however, applying them to discrete latent variable models is challenging
033   due to non-differentiability. A common solution is to use gradient estimators tailored for discrete
034   latent variables. The REINFORCE estimator (Williams, 1992) is an early example, providing an
035   unbiased estimate of the gradient but suffering from high variance. Alternatively, methods such
036   as the Gumbel-Softmax reparameterization trick (Jang et al., 2017; Maddison et al., 2017) allow
037   for a continuous relaxation of categorical distributions. While these methods introduce bias into the
038   gradient estimation, they offer the benefit of significantly lower variance, thereby improving training
039
      stability.
040   Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoders (VQ-VAEs) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) extend the
041   VAE framework to discrete latent spaces by discretizing continuous representations through a code-
042   book via straight-through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013). Beyond the inherent variance-bias
043   tradeoff in gradient estimation, VQ-VAEs are known to suffer from codebook collapse, wherein
044   all encodings converge to a limited set of embedding vectors, resulting in the underutilization of
045
      many vectors in the codebook. This phenomenon diminishes the information capacity of the bot-
      tleneck. Takida et al. (2022); Williams et al. (2020) hypothesized that deterministic quantization is
046
      the cause of codebook collapse and introduced stochastic sampling, leading to a entropy term in the
047
      log-likelihood lower bound. While high entropy is generally beneficial, it is inherently incompatible
048   with Gumbel-Softmax gradient estimation. Various handcrafted heuristics have been proposed to
049   mitigate this issue, including batch data-dependent k-means (Łańcucki et al., 2020), replacement
050   policies (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Dhariwal et al., 2020), affine parameterization with alternate op-
051   timization (Huh et al., 2023), and entropy penalties (Yu et al., 2023). However, as these heuristics
052   do not derive from the evidence lower Bound (ELBO), they cannot be unified within the variational
053   Bayesian framework, rendering them ad-hoc solutions lacking a coherent foundation in variational
      inference.

145   3     GM-VQ: G AUSSIAN M IXTURE V ECTOR Q UANTIZATION
146
147   To solve the problem mentioned above, we propose a stochastic vector quantization method using
148   using Gaussian mixture distribution, which we refer to as Gaussian Mixture Vector Quantization
149   (GM-VQ), with the following encoding and decoding process:
150
151                                             Eθ      M       ε     Dϕ
                                             x −−→ ẑ −→ c −
                                                           → z −−→ x̃                                     (5)
152
153   In our framework, GM-VQ comprises an encoder Eθ and a decoder Dϕ , parameterized by the deep
154   neural network weights θ and ϕ, respectively, and interconnected via a codebook M, which contains
155   the means of Gaussian mixtures. The term ε denotes the Gaussian noise introduced into the process.
156   Unlike previous works adapted VQ-VAE with Gaussian mixture priors (Takida et al., 2022; Williams
157   et al., 2020), which directly transmit categorical representations to the decoder, our method explicitly
158   adds noise to the categorical variables during training, feeding continuous latent variables into the
159   decoder. While Liu et al. (2021) theoretically demonstrate that discrete representations can enhance
160   generalization and robustness during evaluation, the codewords in our codebook are continuously
161   updated throughout training, leading to a non-static categorical representation. By explicitly intro-
      ducing noise, our method allows the decoder to adapt in parallel with the evolving codebook.

175   3.1   G ENERATIVE M ODEL
176
177   The generative model is defined by the joint distribution:
178
179                                    p(x, z, c) = p(x | z) p(z | c) p(c),                              (6)
180
181   where x ∈ RD represents the observed data, z ∈ RL is a continuous latent variable, and c ∈
182   {1, 2, . . . , C} is a discrete latent variable indicating the mixture component. The latent variable z
183   follows a Gaussian mixture distribution, with the means stored in a codebook M ∈ RC×L , where
184   each row µc represents a codeword for component c.
185   First, the discrete variable c is sampled from a categorical distribution, where π represents the prior
186   probabilities of the mixture components, typically assumed to be uniformly distributed. Then, given
187   c, the continuous latent variable z is drawn from a multivariate Gaussian distribution with mean µc
188   and isotropic covariance matrix σz2 I. As σz2 approaches zero, this setup converges to deterministic
189   quantization, similar to VQ-VAE.
190   Finally, the observed data x is generated conditionally on z, where the decoder Dθ maps the contin-
191   uous latents z to the mean of the observation distribution, modeled as a Gaussian with fixed variance
192   σx2 I.

      3.3     T RAINING O BJECTIVE AND O PTIMIZATION
233
234   To train the model, we typically maximize the ELBO using Gumbel-Softmax gradient estimation.
235   However, the presence of entropy in ELBO leads to poor Gumbel-Softmax gradient estimation(see
236   Figure 3). Given that entropy can be particularly detrimental to Gumbel-Softmax gradient estima-
237   tion, it is critical to mitigate its impact during training.
238
      To resolve this, Yu et al. (2023) introduced two contrasting entropy measures derived from mutual
239   information (Krause et al., 2010):
240
241
242
                                 Lentropy = H(q(c | x)) − H(Ex∼p(x) q(c | x)).                           (10)
243   In the context of vector quantization, the first term of this loss reduces uncertainty when mapping
244   observed data x to c and the second term encourages more discrete latent variables to be used, which
245   decreases the codebook collapse problem.
246
      However, this approach remains an additional heuristic that cannot be fully incorporated into the
247
      variational Bayesian framework. To address this limitation, we propose an alternative ELBO formu-
248
      lation.

342   4   R ELATED W ORK
343
344   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) comprise a generative model and a
345
      recognition model, bridged by a latent variable typically modeled with a multivariate Gaussian prior.
      While the generative component is well-known, the recognition model effectively learns continuous
346
      representations from data (Zhang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2017; Higgins et al.,
347
      2017).
348
349   To address discrete representation learning, Vector Quantized VAE (VQ-VAE) (Razavi et al., 2019;
350   Van Den Oord et al., 2017) employs vector quantization (Gray, 1984) to discretize latent embed-
351
      dings under a uniform prior. Since the quantization process is non-differentiable, techniques like
      the straight-through estimator (Bengio et al., 2013) are used to approximate gradients, introducing
352
      potential bias. VQ-VAE also incorporates a discretization loss to mitigate these issues, but chal-
353
      lenges such as codebook underutilization and information loss remain. Sønderby et al. (2017); Shu
354   & Nakayama (2017) introduced stochastic sampling based on the negative distance and applied
355   Gumbel-Softmax for gradient estimation. Later, Karpathy (2021); Esser et al. (2021) proposed an
356   encoder that directly outputs the posterior, applying Gumbel-Softmax without conditioning on the
357   codebook.
358
      While many existing VAEs utilize Gaussian mixture priors (Liu et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022; Falck
359
      et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2016; Dilokthanakul et al., 2016; Nalisnick et al., 2016),
360   our approach is distinct in its close connection with vector quantization. Specifically, we reuse
361   the means from the codebook, whereas in other GMM-based models, the posterior means are typi-
362   cally learned transiently, conditioned on different components or networks. Although Takida et al.
363   (2022); Williams et al. (2020) also explore Gaussian mixture priors in VQ-VAE, they modify the
364   reconstruction loss and feed discrete latents directly into the decoder, deviating from strict adher-
365   ence to the ELBO. Furthermore, prior works often rely on simplifying assumptions on variational
366   posterior, such as mean-field (Liu et al., 2023; Falck et al., 2021; Figueroa, 2017; Jiang et al., 2016)
367   or Markovian assumptions (Takida et al., 2022; Williams et al., 2020).
368   Previous works (Tomczak & Welling, 2018; Hoffman & Johnson, 2016; Makhzani et al., 2015) have
369   attempted to modify the ELBO framework by averaging the objective over data distribution, but they
370   retain the original variational conditional distribution, leaving the entropy term of the conditional
371   posterior it intact. In contrast, our approach is specifically motivated by the gradient estimation
372   error in categorical latents by using an aggregated categorical posterior instead of the conditional
373   categorical posterior.
374   Beyond these theoretical advancements, vector quantization (VQ) and its related concepts have also
375   seen extensive applications across various domains. Here, we highlight some recent works. Uni-
376   MoT (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces a VQ-driven tokenizer that converts molecules into molecular
377   token sequences, while VQSynergy (Wu et al., 2024) integrates VQ for drug synergy prediction. In
      image and video generation, MAGVIT-v2 (Yu et al., 2023) applies VQ-VAE to achieve high-fidelity

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
390
      We use Gumbel-Softmax for gradient estimation throughout our experiments, adopting an annealing
391   schedule similar to Takida et al. (2022); Jang et al. (2017). The temperature starts at 2.0 and is
392   gradually reduced to 0.1 during training.
393
394
      Our architecture and hyperparameters closely follow the setup in Huh et al. (2023). For the CI-
      FAR10 dataset (32x32 image size), we use a compact architecture consisting of convolutional layers
395
      followed by vector quantization, similar to standard autoencoders. For CelebA (resized to 128x128),
396
      a deeper network is employed to manage the higher resolution. Both architectures incorporate a VQ
397   layer to quantize the latent representations into discrete codes.
398
399   Models are trained for 100 epochs on both datasets, using a batch size of 256. We employ AdamW
400
      (Loshchilov, 2017) as the optimizer, with a maximum learning rate of 1e-2 for CIFAR10 and 1e-4
      for CelebA. The learning rate follows a cosine decay schedule with linear warmup as in Huh et al.
401
      (2023), with 10 epochs of warmup starting at a factor of 0.2, followed by 90 epochs of cosine decay.
402
      All models are initialized using K-means clustering for codebook initialization, following Esser
403   et al. (2021). We use 1024 codes with a latent dimension of 64 across all experiments, applying the
404   same weight decay to both the encoder-decoder and the codebook. During evaluation, no noise is
405   added to the reconstructed x for maximum likelihood estimation, and latents z are directly sampled
406   from the codebook M without extra noise.
407
      We report Mean Squared Error (MSE) as the metric for reconstruction quality, which measures the
408
      mean pixel-wise difference between the original and reconstructed images. Additionally, we report
409
      perplexity, defined as 2H(q) , to evaluate the diversity of codebook usage. Higher perplexity indicates
410   a more balanced use of the available codes. Notably, this perplexity is not based on the entropy of
411   individual codes q(c | x) but on the average entropy across a batch of categorical distributions
412   q (B) (c), with perplexity computed per batch and then averaged across all batches.
