009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Memory is the foundation of all human activities; without memory, it would be
012            nearly impossible for people to perform any task in daily life. With the develop-
013            ment of Large Language Models (LLMs), their language capabilities are becom-
014            ing increasingly comparable to those of humans. But do LLMs have memory?
015            Based on current practice, LLMs do appear to exhibit memory. So, what is the
016
               underlying mechanism of this memory? Previous research lacked a deep explo-
               ration of LLMs’ memory capabilities and the underlying theory. In this paper, we
017
               use the Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT) to explain the memory mech-
018
               anism in LLMs. We also conduct experiments to verify the memory capabilities
019            of various LLMs, proposing a new method to assess their abilities based on the
020            memory ability. We argue that LLM memory operates like Schrödinger’s mem-
021            ory, meaning that it only becomes observable when a specific memory is queried.
022            We can only determine if the model retains a memory based on its output in re-
023            sponse to the query; otherwise, it remains indeterminate. Finally, we expand on
024            this concept by comparing the memory capabilities of the human brain and LLMs,
025            highlighting the similarities and differences in their operational mechanisms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Language is not only one of humanity’s most important abilities but also the foundation of commu-
030   nication (Miller, 1951), knowledge transfer (Han & Ellis, 1998), and the development of civilization
031   (Yu, 2015). Language models can be seen as simulations of human intelligence, enabling them to
032   perform tasks traditionally achievable only by humans. Currently, LLMs based on the Transformer
033   architecture have become one of the hottest topics in artificial intelligence research today. These
034   models have acquired some human-like language capabilities and are already impacting daily life
035   in areas such as machine translation (Brants et al., 2007; Moslem et al., 2023), text summarization
036   (Van Veen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2019), sentiment analysis (Zhang et al., 2023a; Mao et al.,
037   2022; Zhang et al., 2023b), question-answering systems (Masry et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023), and
038
      text generation (Bai et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; OpenAI et al., 2024).
039   Although the performance of LLMs is impressive, research on their memory mechanisms remains
040   limited. Memory is a crucial capability for humans; without it, we would struggle to complete even
041   the simplest tasks. For instance, in everyday conversations, we need to remember what others have
042   said in order to respond appropriately, and this memory capacity facilitates smooth dialogue. Mem-
043   ory plays a vital role in guiding various aspects of our daily lives. As LLMs become increasingly
044
      powerful, an important question arises: do these models possess memory? If so, in what form does
      it exist, and how does it differ from human memory? Current research on LLM memory primarily
045
      focuses on two main directions:
046
047   Expanding Context Length: This approach aims to equip LLMs with more memory by extending the
048   context window (Chen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Yang, 2023; Fei et al., 2023). Since short con-
049   texts fail to provide enough information, increasing the context length allows the model to maintain
050
      more comprehensive information across long sequences.
051   External Memory Integration: This method involves building memory storage systems (Graves et al.,
052   2014; Xiao et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024b) that encode and store past events
053   (Zhang et al., 2023c), allowing the model to retrieve and update memories on disks as needed. Such
      mechanisms enable models to forget or reinforce certain memories over time.

054
      Although these studies have made progress in addressing the memory limitations of LLMs, they
055   have not fully explained how memory functions within these models. For example, when asked,
056   ”Who is the President of the United States?” LLMs like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) or Llama-3
057   (Dubey et al., 2024) may say ”Trump”. It is outdated information, but it also indicates that some
058   form of memory is indeed present in LLMs. However, this memory does not come from an external
059   storage unit but is inferred by the model based on the input. The articles Jagielski et al. (2022);
060   Carlini et al. (2022) attempt to evaluate the memory capabilities of LLMs. However, due to the lack
061   of a fundamental theoretical framework, the definition of memory itself remains vague, resulting in
062   conclusions that are largely based on straightforward experimental observations. This raises funda-
063
      mental questions: Why do LLMs exhibit this ability to infer previously learned information from
      the input? How does this differ from human memory? Where is memory stored in LLMs?
064
065   In this paper, we use UAT theory to explain this ability of recalling information learned from the past
066   based on input cues. We argue that this information can be understood as a dynamic approximation
067   capability of UAT (Wang & Li, 2024b), where the model fits a corresponding result based on the
068   input, and the observed phenomenon appears as memory. We call this ”Schrödinger’s memory”
069
      because we can only determine whether the LLMs have this memory by asking it and analyzing
      its response; otherwise, the memory remains indeterminate. Additionally, we evaluate the memory
070
      capabilities of several models and propose that this approach can be used to assess the overall ability
071
      of LLMs. The contributions of this work are as follows:
072
073          • We explain LLMs’ memory abilities through the lens of UAT.
074          • We propose a new, objective method for evaluating LLMs’ capabilities: memory ability
075            assessment.
076
             • We logically make a comparison between the memory of LLMs and human memory and
077
               reasoning capabilities.
078
079   The structure of this paper is as follows: In Section 2, we briefly explain the UAT and present math-
080   ematical formulation of multi-layer Transformers in the form of UAT. In Section 3, we provide both
081   theoretical and experimental evidence demonstrating the memory capabilities of LLMs. Finally,
082   in Section 4, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of human and LLM abilities, with a focus on
083   memory ability.

      3     T HE M EMORY OF LLM S
153
      In this section, we will demonstrate the memory capabilities of LLMs. First, in Section 3.1, we
154
      provide a clear definition of memory. Then, in Section 3.2 and 3.3, we give a discription to datasets
155   and explain the memory mechanism of LLMs using UAT theory and validate their memory charac-
156   teristics through experiments. In Section 3.4, we explore the impact of input length on the accuracy
157   of LLM memory.
158
159   3.1   T HE D EFINITION OF M EMORY
160
161   Before delving into the study of memory in LLMs, it is important to first define or provide a relatively
      precise description of what memory is. According to Wikipedia:


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • Memory is the faculty of the mind by which data or information is encoded, stored, and
163            retrieved when needed.
164
165   However, this definition has some fundamental issues. Encoding data or information is not prob-
166   lematic, as information in the brain is transmitted via electrical signals, and we need to encode that
167   information in a way the brain can process. The problem arises with the concepts of ”storage” and
168   ”retrieval.” The brain does not have a structure analogous to a database for storing information. So,
169   where is this information actually stored? Is it in the neurons of the brain? If so, does a single neuron
170   store a word, or does it store an entire sentence? Next, we give an exmple:
171
172            Question 1:     What is Newton’s first law?
173                            Every object perseveres in its state of rest, or of uniform motion
174            Answer 1:       in a right line, except insofar as it is compelled to change that state
175                            by forces impressed thereon.
176
177   So, is this sentence stored within a single neuron? Or does each neuron store just a word, with a spe-
178   cific region of the brain dedicated to this particular memory? Given the vast amount of information
179   humans receive daily, can neurons truly store such an immense volume of data without hindering
180   normal cognitive processes? After all, almost every routine activity requires memory. Take, for
181   example, the simple task of going to the cafeteria: we need to remember when to go, the cafeteria’s
182
      location, the route to get there, which foods are available, what counts as utensils, where to find
      them, and how to use them.
183
184   Moreover, if this memory is stored in a fixed set of neurons, then every time the question is raised,
185   the response should be identical, since the retrieval would be from the same static content. Every
186   word in the response should be exact, with no omissions or additions (even if the information has
187   been abstractly encoded, as long as the encoding and decoding processes are consistent, the content
188
      should remain unchanged). This, however, is clearly unreasonable. Therefore, we provide a more
      precise definition of the concept of ”memory”:
189
190   Memory is defined by two key components: input and output.
191
192          • Input: To trigger a memory, the input must be the same or similar to information that the
193            brain (or LLM) has previously encountered.
194          • Output: The result is based on the input, which could be correct, incorrect, or forgotten. If
195            the result is correct, it means it aligns with information previously acquired.
196
197   We need to stress that a key requirement for recalling a memory is the presence of input—without
198   input, there is no memory, as memory is activated by input. Even if the brain holds a memory of
199
      something, it cannot be determined whether that memory exists unless it is prompted by a specific
      query. Without specific input conditions, a person wouldn’t recall a particular event.
200
201   Using Question 1 as an example, the input is: “What is Newton’s first law?” Without this input, no
202   one would suddenly recall Newton’s first law. The recollection of Newton’s first law is triggered by
203   input related to the theoretical context. This is why input is a necessary condition for memory, as it is
204   the input that stimulates recall. The memory might be accurate, or it might be incorrect, indicating a
205
      deviation from previously acquired information—this deviation could be minor, significant, or even
      total forgetting. For example:
206
207
208              Question 1:               What is Newton’s first law?
209                                        Every object perseveres in its state of rest, except insofar as it is
       Answer 2:     Minor distortions
210                                        compelled to change that state by forces impressed thereon.
211
       Answer 3:     Severe distortions    Every object always perseveres in its state of rest.
       Answer 4:     Memory loss           I do not know.
212
213
214   In summary, the term ”memory” was traditionally used to refer specifically to human memory before
215   the emergence of LLMs. Now, we believe that LLMs also exhibit memory. Therefore, we will verify
      the memory characteristics of LLMs based on the definition of memory outlined above.

363   4     A C OMPARISION B ETWEEN H UMAN B RAIN AND LLM S
364
365
      Based on the definition of memory in Section 3 and the experimental results, we believe that LLMs
366   do possess memory capabilities. It’s important to distinguish between LLM memory and database
367   storage. Database storage involves keeping content on physical media (like hard drives or books)
368   that can be searched or modified based on conditions, while LLM memory refers to the dynamic
369   approximation of corresponding outputs using internal weights and inputs.
370
      From the perspective of functionality, we argue that LLMs and human memory do not fundamentally
371
      differ; both can be understood as dynamically approximating results based on inputs. For example,
372   as shown in Figure 2, LLMs can recite entire poems solely based on their titles and authors after
373   learning. These poems are not stored in specific areas within the model; they are dynamically gen-
374   erated based on input. We can only determine if an LLM remembers certain information by posing
375   questions and examining outputs; otherwise, it remains unknown. Human memory operates simi-
376   larly: we can only validate our memories by answering specific questions; otherwise, assessment is
377   impossible. For instance, if you ask someone how many poems they remember, they may struggle
      to provide an exact number, but they can usually recall a specific poem if prompted. Few people


                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      consciously memorize how many poems they know, leading to a lack of corresponding output when
379   such input arise. Therefore, we suggest that the brain functions like a model that dynamically fits
380   outputs based on inputs, indicating that, in a sense, the mathematical model of the human brain
381   may resemble that of a Transformer-based dynamic approximation UAT model, potentially even as
382   a more advanced version. However, we believe their fundamental mechanisms are the same: both
383   rely on dynamically fitting outputs based on inputs.
384
      Due to the complexity of the brain, many operational mechanisms remain unclear, and no reasonable
385   conclusions currently exist regarding its specific workings. Thus, we make logical assumptions and
386   generalizations about the brain’s mechanisms based on the memory process, UAT theory, and LLMs.
387   First, we extend the concept of memory in the brain to other cognitive abilities, such as social skills,
388   imagination, and creativity. All of these can be attributed to the ability to infer outcomes based
389   on existing knowledge and inputs, which we collectively refer to as reasoning ability, defined as:
390   the capacity to generate specific results based on previously learned knowledge and specific inputs,
391   where these results are consistent with or related to that knowledge.
392   Based on the definition of reasoning ability, LLM memory can also be viewed as a form of reasoning.
393   The results from Figures 2 and 3, along with the current performance of ChatGPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
394   2023) in generating outputs based on inputs, suggest that LLMs possess reasoning capabilities.
395   Although the predictions in Figure 3 are incorrect, they still align with linguistic conventions and
396   somewhat correspond to the titles of the poems. This can be seen as creativity.
397   So why do LLMs seem to underperform in reasoning tasks? We believe there are two main factors:
398   model size, and data quality and quantity.
399
400          • Model Size: Generally, larger LLMs tend to be more powerful. Theoretically, as demon-
401            strated by UAT2LLMs (Wang & Li, 2024b) and UAT2Parallel (Wang & Li, 2024a), a
402            greater model size enhances dynamic fitting capability, leading to improved performance.
403            Performance improvements can also be observed when comparing models like Llama from
404
               8B to 70B (Dubey et al., 2024) and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a) from 0.5B to 72B-larger
               models consistently show better performance.
405
406          • Data Quality and Quantity: Current LLMs have significantly benefited from training on
407
               vast datasets. The larger and higher the quality of dataset, the stronger the model’s perfor-
               mance. The performance leap from Qwen 1.5 (Bai et al., 2023) to Qwen 2 (Yang et al.,
408
               2024a) highlights that training on high-quality data yields better results. From a human
409
               learning perspective, individuals undergo decades of education from elementary school
410            to university. Immersed in a language-rich environment from birth, humans benefit from
411            teachers and exams that correct linguistic issues one by one. Without such learning experi-
412            ences, we would struggle to develop robust language skills.
413
414   Since we propose that both LLMs and the brain function as dynamic models that fit outputs, why
415   build such dynamic models? What are the advantages of this approach? We believe that this dy-
416   namic fitting capability gives the brain infinite possibilities. The brain doesn’t need to remember
417
      everything; it only needs to focus on what is important. Imagine if a newborn’s brain were pre-
      loaded with the weights of its parents; there would be no need to fit the world because most of the
418
      external environment remains constant. In such a scenario, the brain’s weights would hardly ever
419
      be updated, and the person would lose creativity. However, when we are born, our brains contain
420   almost no knowledge about the external world. It could be viewed as the weight parameters in our
421   brains are randomly initialized, and it is precisely this randomness that gives rise to creativity. Since
422   the knowledge of our ancestors isn’t always correct, newborns learn from their predecessors and
423   continuously interact with the world to verify and update this knowledge. This updating process
424   involves optimizing the brain’s weight parameters. Each update may be right or wrong, but with a
425   vast number of humans exploring the world, we gradually inch closer to the truth, ultimately leading
426   to innovation.
427   A great example of dynamic fitting in the brain is Henry Molaison (Scoville & Milner, 1957; Victor
428   et al., 1961; Milner & Klein, 2015). After his hippocampus (Bliss & Collingridge, 1993; Squire,
429   1992; Erickson et al., 2011; Eckardt, 1980) was damaged, he could no longer form new long-term
430   memories, though his existing memories remained intact. We believe that the hippocampus acts
431   as a switch controlling whether the weights responsible for long-term memory in the brain can be
      updated. Once the hippocampus is damaged, the brain’s weight parameters can no longer change,


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      meaning that while past inputs (before the hippocampal damage) can still produce corresponding
433   outputs (i.e., recalling past events), the inability to update weights prevents the formation of new
434   memories.

437   5   C ONCLUSION
438
439   In this paper, we demonstrate that LLMs possess memory capabilities, which are enabled by their
440
      Transformer-based architecture. This architecture functions as a dynamic fitting UAT model, with a
      strong ability to adaptively fit outputs. As a result, LLMs can recall entire content based on minimal
441
      input information. Since this memory can only be confirmed when triggered by input, we refer
442
      to it as ”Schrödinger’s memory.” Through extensive experiments, we validated that the memory
443   mechanism of LLMs aligns with this theory. Additionally, we compared LLMs with the human
444   brain and found that their working mechanisms are similar, as both dynamically fit outputs based on
445   inputs.
