009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are increasingly being used for a variety of ML
012            applications on graph data. As graph data does not follow the independently and
013            identically distributed (i.i.d) assumption, adversarial manipulations or incorrect
014            data can propagate to other datapoints through message passing, deteriorating the
015            model’s performance. To allow model developers to remove the adverse effects
016            of manipulated entities from a trained GNN, we study the recently formulated
017            problem of Corrective Unlearning. We find that current graph unlearning methods
018
               fail to unlearn the effect of manipulations even when the whole manipulated set
               is known. We introduce a new graph unlearning method, Cognac, which can
019
               unlearn the effect of the manipulation set even when only 5% of it is identified. It
020
               recovers most of the performance of a strong oracle with fully corrected training
021            data, even beating retraining from scratch without the deletion set while being
022            8x more efficient. We hope our work guides GNN developers in fixing harmful
023            effects due to issues in real-world data post-training.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are seeing widespread adoption across diverse domains, from rec-
029   ommender systems to drug discovery (Wu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022), and are being scaled to
030   large training sets for graph foundation models (Mao et al., 2024). However, in these large-scale set-
031   tings, it is prohibitively expensive to verify the integrity of every sample in the training data, which
032   can potentially affect desiderata like fairness (Konstantinov & Lampert, 2022), robustness (Paleka
033
      & Sanyal, 2023; Günnemann, 2022) and accuracy (Sanyal et al., 2021).
034   Making the training process itself robust to minority populations (Günnemann, 2022; Jin et al., 2020)
035   is challenging and can adversely affect fairness and accuracy (Sanyal et al., 2022). Consequently,
036   model developers may want post-hoc ways to remove the adverse impact of manipulated training
037   data if they observe problematic model behavior on specific distributions of test-time inputs. Such
038   an approach follows the recent trend of using post-training interventions to ensure models behave in
039
      intended ways (Ouyang et al., 2022). Recently, Goel et al. (2024) formulated Corrective Unlearning
      as the challenge of removing adverse effects of manipulated data with access to only a representative
040
      subset for unlearning while being agnostic to the type of manipulations. We study this problem in
041
      the context of GNNs, which face unique challenges due to the graph structure. The traditional as-
042   sumption of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) samples does not hold for GNNs, as they
043   use a message-passing mechanism that aggregates information from neighbors. This process makes
044   GNNs vulnerable to adversarial perturbations, where modifying even a few nodes can propagate
045   changes across large portions of the graph and result in widespread changes in model predictions
046   (Bojchevski & Günnemann, 2019b; Zügner et al., 2018). As a result, any unlearning approach for
047   GNNs must remove the influence of manipulated entities on neighbors to be effective.
048   Corrective Unlearning is the problem of removing the influence of arbitrary training data manipula-
049   tions on a trained model using only a representative subset of the manipulated data. In this work, we
050   focus on the use of GNNs in node classification tasks, studying unlearning for targeted binary class
051   confusion attacks (Lingam et al., 2024) on both edges and nodes. For edge unlearning, we evaluate
052   the unlearning of spurious edges that change the graph topology in a way that violates the homophily
053   assumption. For node unlearning, we utilize a label flip attack (Lingam et al., 2024) which is used
      as a classical graph adversarial attack, similar to the Interclass Confusion attack (Goel et al., 2022).

084
      First, we evaluate whether existing GNN unlearning methods are effective in removing the impact of
085
      manipulated entities. Our findings reveal that these methods consistently fail, even when provided
086
      with the complete set of manipulated entities. We then propose our method, Cognac, which unlearns
087   by alternating between two components, as illustrated in Figure 1. The first component Contrastive
088   unlearning on Graph Neighborhoods (CoGN), finds affected neighbors of the known deletion set,
089   updating the GNN weights using a contrastive loss that pushes representations of the affected neigh-
090   bors away from the deletion entities while staying close to other neighbors. The second component,
091   AsCent DesCent de coupled (AC DC) applies the classic i.i.d unlearning method of gradient as-
092   cent on the deletion set and gradient descent on the retain set. We use separate optimizers for these
093   components as we find it essential for stable dynamics when optimizing the competing objectives.
094   Our proposed method shows promising results, recovering most of the performance of an oracle
095   model with access to a clean label version of the full graph, even if only 5% of the manipulated
096   entities are identified. This is the first corrective unlearning method that succeeds in removing ad-
097   versarial class confusion with a small fraction of the manipulated set. Finally, we perform a detailed
098   analysis with ablations, where we identify an interesting tradeoff specific to graph corrective un-
099   learning. Particularly, using a larger fraction of the manipulation set for deletion while helpful for
100   unlearning, can remove more structural information from the graph. Keeping the manipulated enti-
101
      ties but removing the effect of their features and labels helps our method match Oracle performance.
      Overall, Cognac offers users of GNNs an efficient, highly effective post-training strategy to remove
102
      adverse effects of manipulated data.

106
107   Graph-based attacks, such as Sybil (Douceur, 2002) and link spam farms (Wu & Davison, 2005),
      have long affected the integrity of social networks and search engines by exploiting the trust in-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      herent in node identities and edge formations. Recent works reveal that even state-of-the-art GNN
109   architectures are vulnerable to simple attacks on the trained models which either manipulate exist-
110   ing edges and nodes or inject new adversarial nodes (Sun et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2018; Zügner &
111   Günnemann, 2019; Geisler et al., 2024). Parallelly, works have characterized the influence of spe-
112   cific nodes and edges that can guide such attacks (Chen et al., 2023). One strategy to mitigate the
113   influence of such attacks is robust pretraining, such as using adversarial training (Yuan et al., 2024;
114   Zhang et al., 2023). Post-hoc interventions like unlearning act as a complementary layer of defense,
115   helping model developers when attacks slip through and still adversely affect a trained model.
116   Removing the impact of manipulated entities begins with their identification (Brodley & Friedl,
117   1999), for which multiple strategies exist like data attribution (Ilyas et al., 2022), adversarial detec-
118   tion, and automated or human-in-the-loop anomaly detection (Northcutt et al., 2021). Once iden-
119   tified, various approaches have been proposed to mitigate specific effects of the manipulated data,
120   including model debiasing (Fan et al., 2024) and concept erasure (Belrose et al., 2023). While these
121   approaches have similar goals, that is, post-hoc removing undesirable effects of corrupted training
122   data, unlearning attempts to do this without precise knowledge of the nature of corruptions and its
123
      effects. This is useful in adversarial settings where effects can be obfuscated, and harm multiple
      desiderata simultaneously (Paleka & Sanyal, 2023).
124
125   Machine unlearning gained initial interest for privacy applications to serve user data deletion re-
126   quests (Nguyen et al., 2022). Exact Unlearning procedures remove or retrain parts of the ML sys-
127   tem that saw the data to be deleted, guaranteeing perfect unlearning by design (Chen et al., 2022b;a;
128   Bourtoule et al., 2021). However, they can incur exponential costs with sequential deletion requests
129
      (Warnecke et al., 2023). Therefore, we focus on Inexact Unlearning methods, which either provide
      approximate guarantees for simple models (Chien et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023b) or like us, empiri-
130
      cally show unlearning through evaluations for deep neural networks (Wu et al., 2023a; Cheng et al.,
131
      2023; Li et al., 2024b). Due to the non-i.i.d nature of graphs, GNN unlearning methods need to re-
132   move the effects of deletion set entities on the remaining entities, which distinguishes the subdomain
133   of Graph Unlearning (Said et al., 2023).
134
135
      Recently, machine unlearning has received newfound attention beyond privacy applications (Pawel-
      czyk et al., 2024; Schoepf et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). Goel et al. (2024) demonstrated the distinc-
136
      tion between the Corrective and Privacy-oriented unlearning settings for i.i.d classification tasks,
137
      emphasising challenges when not all manipulated data is identified for unlearning. In this work,
138   we focus on the intersection of corrective unlearning for graphs, evaluating existing methods and
139   making significant progress through our proposed method Cognac.

142   3   C ORRECTIVE U NLEARNING FOR G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
143
144   We now formulate the corrective unlearning problem for graph-structured, non-i.i.d data. We con-
145   sider a graph G = (V, E), where V and E represents the constituent set of nodes and edges re-
146   spectively. For each node Vi ∈ V, there is a corresponding feature vector Xi and label Yi , with
147   V = (X , Y). Consistent with prior work in unlearning on graphs (Wu et al., 2023a; Li et al.,
148   2024b), we focus on semi-supervised node classification using GNNs. GNNs use the message-
149   passing mechanism, where each node aggregates features from its immediate neighbors. The effect
150
      of this aggregation process propagates through multiple successive layers, effectively expanding the
      receptive field of each node with network depth. This architecture inherently exploits the principle
151
      of homophily, a common property in many real-world graphs where nodes with similar features or
152
      labels are more likely to be connected than not.
153
154   While assuming homophily is extremely useful for learning representations from graph data, annota-
155   tion mistakes or adversarial manipulations that create dissimilar neighborhoods or connect otherwise
156
      dissimilar nodes can easily harm the learned representations (Zügner & Günnemann, 2019). This
      motivates our study of post-hoc correction strategies like unlearning for GNNs. Following Goel
157
      et al. (2024), we adopt an adversarial formulation that subsumes correcting more benign mistakes.
158
159   Adversary’s Perspective. The adversary can reduce model accuracy on a target distribution by
160   manipulating parts of the clean training data G. This can be done in the following ways: (1) adding
161   spurious edges Ê, resulting in E ′ = E ∪ Ê; or (2) manipulating node information, V ′ = fm (V),
      where fm manipulates a subset of nodes by changing their features or labels. We define Sm as the


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      set of manipulated entities, either the manipulated subset of nodes or the added spurious edges Ê.
163   The final manipulated graph is denoted as G ′ = (V ′ , E ′ ).
164
165   Unlearner’s Perspective. After training, model developers may observe that desired properties
166
      like fairness and robustness are compromised in the trained model M, which can be modelled as
      lower accuracy on some data distributions. The objective, then, is to remove the influence of the
167
      manipulated training data Sm on the affected distribution while maintaining performance on the
168
      remaining entities. By utilizing data monitoring strategies on a subset of the training data or using
169   incorrect data detection tools like (Northcutt et al., 2021), it may be possible to identify a part of
170   the manipulated entities Sf ⊆ Sm . For unlearning to be feasible, Sf must be a representative
171   subset of Sm . We only assume the type of affected entity (edges or nodes) is known to the model
172   developer but do not assume any knowledge about the nature of manipulation. An unlearning method
173   U (M, Sf , G ′ ) is then used to mitigate the adverse effects of Sm , ideally by improving the accuracy
174   on unseen samples from the affected distribution. An effective unlearning method should remove
175   the impact of certain training data samples without degrading performance on the rest of the data or
176   incurring the cost of retraining from scratch. Moreover, Retrain was previously considered a gold
177
      standard in privacy-oriented unlearning and graph unlearning, but Goel et al. (2024) showed that
      when the whole manipulated set is not known, retraining on the remaining data can reinforce the
178
      manipulation, implying it’s not a gold standard for corrective unlearning.
179
180   Metrics. To evaluate the performance of unlearning methods in this setting, we use the metrics
181   proposed by Goel et al. (2024):
182
183
            1. Accaff : It measures the clean-label accuracy of test set samples from the affected distribu-
               tion. This metric captures the method’s ability to correct the influence of the manipulated
184
               entities on unseen data through unlearning. As the affected distribution differs for each
185
               manipulation, we specify it when describing each evaluation.
186
187         2. Accrem : It is defined as the accuracy of the remaining entities. This metric measures
188
               whether the unlearning maintains model performance on clean entities.
189
      The metrics Accaff and Accrem were termed “Corrected Accuracy” (Acccorr ) and “Retain Accuracy”
190   (Accretain ) respectively by Goel et al. (2024). We chose alternative names to explicitly state which
191   data distribution accuracy is measured on. In Section 5.1 we further specify what “the affected
192   distribution” and “remaining entities” are for the different evaluation types we study.
193
      Goal. An ideal corrective unlearning method should have high Accaff even when a small fraction of
194
      Sm is identified for deletion (Sf ) while maintaining Accrem and taking less computation time.

197   4     O UR M ETHOD : Cognac
198
199   Our proposed unlearning method, Cognac, requires access to the underlying graph G ′ , the known
200   set of entities to be deleted Sf , and the original model M. We define Vf as the set of nodes whose
201   influence is to be removed. For node unlearning, this is the same as the deletion entities Sf ; for
202   edge unlearning, this is the set of vertices connected to the edge set to be deleted. Manipulated data
203
      has two main adverse effects on the trained GNN: 1) Message passing can propagate the influence
      of the manipulated entities Sm on their neighborhood, and 2) The layers learn transformations to fit
204
      potentially wrong labels in Sm . We tackle these two problems using separate components.
205
206
207   4.1   R EMOVING A DVERSE E FFECTS ON N EIGHBORING N ODES WITH C O GN
208
      The first question we address is: How can we remove the influence of manipulated entities on their
209
      neighboring nodes? This requires us first to identify the nodes affected by the manipulations and
210   then mitigate the influence on their representations. Identifying affected nodes is challenging, as the
211   impact of message passing from manipulated entities Sm depends on the interference from messages
212   of other neighboring nodes. Therefore, we use an empirical estimation to identify the affected nodes
213   from each entity in the deletion set. On these nodes, we then perform contrastive unlearning, simul-
214   taneously pushing the representations of the affected nodes away from nodes in Vf while keeping
215   them close to other nodes in their neighborhood. We call this component Contrastive unlearning on
      Graph Neighborhoods (CoGN), formalized below.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
       Affected Node Identification. To speed up our method, we make use of the fact that not all nodes
217    in the n-hop neighbourhood of the manipulated nodes may be affected enough by the attack. To
218    find the most affected nodes, we invert the features of v ∈ Vf and select neighbouring nodes where
219    final output logits are changed the most. Formally, the inversion is performed by the transformation
220   ⃗1 − Xv ∀ v ∈ Vf , leading to a new feature matrix χ′ , where Xv represents a one-hot-encoding vector.
221    We then compute the difference in the original output logits M(χ), and those obtained by on the
222    new feature matrix, M(χ′ ) given by: ∆χ = |M(χ′ ) − M(χ)|. The top k% nodes with the most
223    change ∆χ are selected as the affected set of entities, from which we remove the influence of the
224    manipulation using CoGN. More details and ablations in Appendix:I.
225   Contrastive Unlearning. To remove the influence of the deletion set Sf on the affected nodes
226   identified in the previous step, we can optimize a loss function that updates the weights such that
227   the final layer logits of Sf and the affected nodes are pushed away. However, this alone will lead to
228   unrestricted separation and damage the quality of learned representations. To prevent this, we also
229   counterbalance the loss with another term that penalizes moving away from logits of neighboring
230   nodes not in the deletion set Sf . For each node v ∈ S, let zv represent its internal embedding,
231   with p ∈ N (v) and n ∈ Vf serving as the positive and negative samples, respectively. We use the
232   following unsupervised contrastive loss:
233
234
235
                                    Lc = − log(σ(zv⊤ zp )) − log(σ(−zv⊤ zn ))                             (1)
236
237   The loss is similar to the one used in GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), but only updates affected
238   nodes to make their representations dissimilar from the deletion set while keeping them similar
239   to the remaining nodes in their neighborhood. We choose an unsupervised loss function to fix
240   representations even in mislabeling, which is essential when the manipulated set is not fully known
241   (Sf ⊂ Sm ).
242
243   4.2   U NLEARNING OLD LABELS WITH AC DC
244
245   Next, we ask: Can we undo the effect of the task loss Ltask explicitly learning to fit the node rep-
246   resentations of Sm to potentially wrong labels? We do this by performing gradient ascent on Sf ,
247   which non-directionally maximizes the training loss concerning the old labels. Ascent alone aggres-
248   sively leads to arbitrary forgetting of useful information, so we counterbalance it by alternating with
249   steps that minimize the task loss on the remaining data. More precisely, we perform gradient ascent
250   on Vf and gradient descent on Vr , iteratively on the original GNN M.
251
252                                   La = −Ltask (Vf ), Ld = Ltask (Vr )                                 (2)
253
254   While variants of ascent on Sf and descent on remaining data have been studied for image classifi-
255   cation (Kurmanji et al., 2023) and language models (Yao et al., 2023), we find the need for a spe-
256   cific optimization strategy to achieve corrective unlearning on graphs. The challenge arises when
257   Sf ⊂ Sm , as the remaining data could still contain manipulated entities, which we aim to avoid
258   reinforcing. However, in realistic scenarios, the manipulated entities Sm typically constitute a small
259   fraction of the training data, allowing us to mitigate their impact through ascent on the representative
260
      subset Sf .
261   This requires a careful balance between ascent and descent, which we can achieve by using two
262   different optimizers and starting learning rates for these steps. This insight is similar to prior work
263   in Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Heusel et al., 2017). The starting learning rates for
264   both ascent and descent are hyperparameters to be tuned, and usually, we find a lower learning rate
265   for ascent leads to better results. The importance of decoupling optimizers is shown by results in
266
      Table 3. Thus, we call this component Ascent Descent de coupled (AC DC) to emphasize the
      distinction from existing variants of ascent on Sf and descent on remaining data.
267
268   For our final method Cognac, we alternate steps of CoGN, which fixes representations of affected
269   neighborhood nodes, and AC DC, which unlearns potentially wrong labels introduced by Sm . The
      complete algorithm is formally detailed in the Appendix B.

      5.1   E VALUATIONS
273
274   Given a fixed budget of samples to manipulate, ideal corrective unlearning evaluations should maxi-
275   mally deteriorate model performance on the affected distribution so that there is a wide gap between
276   clean and poisoned model performance to measure unlearning method progress. We thus evaluate
277   using attacks not constrained on stealthiness. Lingam et al. (2024) show that binary label flip ma-
278   nipulation attacks, where a fraction of labels are swapped between two chosen classes, are stronger
279   than multi-class manipulations, theoretically and empirically, on GNNs. Building on this, we use
280   two targeted attacks to evaluate corrective unlearning on graph data.
281   Spurious Edge Addition. We first describe a graph-specific manipulation where an attacker can
282   add edges to the graph topology (Bojchevski & Günnemann, 2019a). Such an attack can occur in
283   real-world settings like social networks, where attackers can create fake accounts and follow tar-
284   geted accounts, strengthening their connection in the underlying graph. Similarly, attackers could
285   manipulate knowledge graphs by adding links between unrelated concepts (Xi et al., 2023; Zhang
286   et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2024), or manipulate search engine results by adding fake cross-references
287
      (Gyongyi & Garcia-Molina, 2005). Prior GNN unlearning work (Wu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024b)
      has also evaluated adversarial edge attacks but in an untargeted setting, making the evaluation weak.
288
      In our formulation, the attacker selects two classes, samples random pairs of nodes uniformly, with
289
      one from each class, and adds edges between them. This targets the underlying homophily assump-
290   tion in message passing, leading to representations of the two classes being entangled when training
291   the model. Hence, this attack reduces the model’s accuracy on samples from the two classes, which
292   form the affected distribution. Thus, the unlearning goal is to improve accuracy on the test set sam-
293   ples of the two targeted classes (Accaff ). For Accrem , we measure accuracy on test set samples from
294   the remaining classes.
295
      Label Manipulation. Next, we study a label-only manipulation that models settings where model
296   developers source external annotations on their data. We focus on systematic mislabeling, which
297   can occur in an adversarial context where an attacker wants the model to confuse two classes due
298   to annotator biases or misinterpretations of potentially ambiguous guidelines. We use the Interclass
299   Confusion (IC) Test (Goel et al., 2022), where the attacker picks two classes again, swapping the
300   labels between nodes from the two classes. This attack also entangles the representations of the
301   two classes, reducing the model’s accuracy on them, which forms the affected distribution. Once
302   again, the unlearning goal is to improve accuracy on the test set samples of the two targeted classes
303   (Accaff ). For Accrem , we measure accuracy on test set samples from the remaining classes.

358
359   Models and Datasets. We report results using Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) (Kipf &
360   Welling, 2017) and also show the same trends hold for Graph Attention Transformers (GAT)
361   (Veličković et al., 2018) in Appendix D. We evaluate the methods on three benchmark datasets:

364
365   Ensuring a fair comparison of unlearning methods can be tricky as there are multiple desiderata: un-
366   learning, maintaining utility, and computational efficiency, and hyperparameter tuning of the meth-
367   ods can particularly affect results on GNNs. Next, we describe our efforts towards this.
368   Unlearning Time. To simplify comparisons to just two axes, Accaff , and Accrem , we fix a maximum
369   cutoff of time an unlearning method can take, as motivated by Maini et al. (2024). We chose this
370   cutoff as 25% of the original model training time. We pick the best model checkpoint during training
371   for each method, which could be achieved earlier than this (Reported in Appendix E. All experiments
372   were run on a machine with Intel Xeon CPUs and two dedicated RTX 5000 GPUs.
373   Hyperparameter Tuning. We perform extensive hyperparameter tuning for all unlearning methods
374   using Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019) with a TPESampler (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator) Algorithm.
375   We ensure the hyperparameter ranges searched include any values specified in the original papers
376   that proposed the methods. The optimization target is an average of Accaff and Accrem , computed
377   on the validation set. We report averaged results across five seeds. Method-specific hyperparameter
      ranges and scatter plots across hyperparameters for each method are provided in Appendix C.

424
      1. Existing unlearning methods perform poorly even when |Sf | = |Sm |. First, observing the
425
      rightmost points in Figure 2, we can see that existing methods fail to unlearn across manipulation
426   types even when the whole manipulation set is known. The only exception is GNNDelete on the

      2. AC DC shows strong results but improves with CoGN. AC DC, a method with no graph-
433   specific aspects, performs quite strongly, beating all the methods we compare to without losing
434   utility on the remaining classes. MEGU and GIF were evaluated on removing adversarial edges,
435   and GNNDelete also mentions incorrect data as one of its key applications. Yet, despite extensive
436   hyperparameter search, they are beaten by a method with no special graph components. This high-
437   lights the importance of strong evaluations for graph unlearning, a bar our evaluations cross as they
438   at least demonstrate the failure of existing methods. These findings raise the question: are graph-

      similarly on Amazon.

444   affected distribution even when 5% of the manipulated set is known. We even outperform Retrain
445   in the realistic settings when Sm is not fully known, as we utilize negative information, i.e., push
446   influenced neighbors away from the identified deletion set in CoGN and perform gradient ascent on
447
      old labels in AC DC.

454   6.2   W HY Accaff SOMETIMES REDUCES AS IDENTIFIED MANIPULATED ENTITIES INCREASE ?
455
456
      We find an interesting trend that sometimes, as more of the manipulation set (Sm ) is known and
      used as the deletion set (Sf ) (going left to right in Figure 2), Accaff reduces. This can seem counter-
457
      intuitive, as one would expect the accuracy of affected classes to improve as more samples are used
458
      for unlearning. We hypothesize unlearning a larger fraction of the manipulation set reduces Accaff
459   due to two factors that adversely affect the neighborhoods of the nodes removed, which typically
460   have other nodes of the affected classes due to homophily. First, in the case of label manipulation,
461   when we model it as node unlearning for consistency with prior work, we lose correct information
462   about the graph structure. Second, when modifying the graph structure, i.e., removing some edges or
463   nodes changes the feature distribution of their neighboring nodes after the message passes, making
464   it out of distribution for the learned GNN layers. The same rationale is why the test nodes are kept
465   in the graph structure (without optimizing the task loss for them) during training (Kipf & Welling,
466   2017). We investigate this by adding an ablation where in the unlearning of the label manipulation,
467
      instead of unlearning the whole node, we keep the structure, i.e., the node and connected edges, but
      unlearn the features and labels.

493   6.3   I MPORTANCE OF D ECOUPLED O PTIMIZERS FOR A SCENT AND D ESCENT
494
495   While ascent-descent has been studied in prior unlearning work, it often performs poorly due to
496   unstable loss dynamics. We find this can be fixed with a simple trick: using two learning rates
497   and different instances of Adam instead of coupling the optimization of both steps. Adding another
498   hyperparameter for the ascent learning rate is necessary as the ascent is not always needed when the
499   original training data labels are correct. For example, we do find that the ascent learning rate is set
500   to nearly zero during automatic hyperparameter selection for our edge unlearning evaluation, where
501
      labels are not manipulated. This leaves the question of how much two different instances of the same
      optimizer help. We thus test two ablations: Using a single optimizer with two learning rates and also
502
      using a combined loss function for ascent and descent instead of alternating steps. In Table 3 we
503
      report results on 25% and 100% of the manipulated nodes identified for Cora. We find AC DC
504   leads to almost 20% better Accaff than using a single optimizer with alternating ascent descent,
505   and 5 − 10% better Accaff than using a combined loss function. This justifies our contribution of
506   decoupling optimizers in AC DC.

520   7     L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we study the corrective unlearning problem for GNNs, where model developers try to
523   remove the adverse effects of manipulated training data from a trained GNN, with realistically only
524   a fraction of it identified for deletion. Our work relies on the homophily assumption, not catering
525
      to heterophilic graphs (Wang et al., 2024). Our evaluations could have been stronger, and may
      not match real-world complexities, where multiple manipulations can occur simultaneously, and
526
      attackers have constraints such as avoiding detection. Successful unlearning on our evaluations does
527
      not guarantee arbitrary real-world unlearning, especially against adaptive attacks.
528
529   Still, our evaluations are sufficient to show that existing unlearning methods perform poorly at re-
530   moving adverse effects of manipulations from GNNs, even in the unrealistic setting of the full ma-
531
      nipulation set being known. We propose a new method Cognac that achieves two crucial effects
      necessary for corrective unlearning. First, Cognac identifies and fixes representations of neighbor-
532
      hood nodes affected by the deletion set using contrastive finetuning. Second, Cognac moves away
533
      from potentially wrong deletion set labels using gradient ascent, stabilized by continuing optimiza-
534   tion of the task loss on the remaining data with a decoupled optimizer. While our method does
535   not provide any theoretical guarantees, to the best of our knowledge, Cognac is the first method to
536   unlearn class confusion manipulations with access to as little as 5% of the manipulated data, re-
537   covering most of the accuracy on the affected distribution. With additional information, it nearly
538   matches a strong oracle with full correct training data. We hope this sparks interesting future work
539   on developing stronger evaluations and theoretical understanding for graph corrective unlearning in
      the GNN Robustness and Machine Unlearning community.

1059   I   A NALYSIS OF METHOD USED TO FIND AFFECTED NEIGHBOURS
1060
1061   Our strategy to find affected neighbours is likely not the perfect for finding the most affected nodes
1062   and more sophisticated influence functions such as the one presented in Chen et al. (2023) could be
1063   used to potentially improve performance. Still, we note that it achieves a 5% higher Accaff than
1064
       while choosing random k% nodes in the n-hop neighbourhood (where n is the number of layers
       of message passing) while being cheap to compute: we only require a single forward pass over the
1065
       model with the inverted features. Interestingly, Figure 12 also shows that even if the GNN is not
1066
       well-trained, if we choose the top k% affected nodes, the unlearning performance does not change
1067   much, while still being noticeably better than when we use a random k% of the neighbours.
1068
1069   Figure 13 (left) shows that there are no noticeable changes in taking a smaller or larger k%. How-
1070
       ever, removing this step entirely (k = 0%) results in worse performance, suggesting performing
       contrastive unlearning on even a small k% is significant. Additionally, by keeping this percent-
1071
       age small, we ensure computational efficiency without diminishing performance (Figure 13 (right)),
1072
       which is essential for unlearning methods.
