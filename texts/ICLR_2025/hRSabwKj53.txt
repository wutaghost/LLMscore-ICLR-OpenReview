011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Language is not monolithic. While many benchmarks are used as proxies to
014            systematically estimate Large Language Models’ (LLM) performance in real-life
015
               tasks, they tend to ignore the nuances of within-language variation and thus fail
               to model the experience of speakers of minority dialects. Focusing on African
016
               American Vernacular English (AAVE), we present the first study on LLMs’ fair-
017
               ness and robustness to a dialect in canonical reasoning tasks (algorithm, math,
018            logic, and comprehensive reasoning). We hire AAVE speakers, including experts
019            with computer science backgrounds, to rewrite seven popular benchmarks, such
020            as HumanEval and GSM8K. The result of this effort is ReDial, a dialectal bench-
021            mark comprising 1.2K+ parallel query pairs in Standardized English and AAVE.
022            We use ReDial to evaluate state-of-the-art LLMs, including GPT-4o/4/3.5-turbo,
023            LLaMA-3.1/3, Mistral, and Phi-3. We find that, compared to Standardized En-
024            glish, almost all of these widely used models show significant brittleness and
025            unfairness to queries in AAVE. Furthermore, AAVE queries can degrade per-
026
               formance more substantially than misspelled texts in Standardized English, even
               when LLMs are more familiar with the AAVE queries. Finally, asking models to
027
               rephrase questions in Standardized English does not close the performance gap but
028
               generally introduces higher costs. Overall, our findings indicate that LLMs pro-
029            vide unfair service to dialect users in complex reasoning tasks. Code can be found
030            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/redial_eval-0A88.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Over the last few decades, linguistics has firmly established that language varies along different
036   external dimensions such as geography, age, and gender, dialectal variation being among the most
037   perspicuous manifestations (Chambers & Trudgill, 1998). Speakers of ‘non-standard’ dialects are
038   known to experience implicit and explicit forms of discrimination in everyday situations, including
039
      housing, education, work, and criminal justice (Baugh, 2005; Adger et al., 2014; Rickford & King,
      2016; Drożdżowicz & Peled, 2024). As Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly employed
040
      as a service and by a rapidly growing user base (Milmo, 2023; La Malfa et al., 2024), it is vital to
041
      understand the service quality that they provide to different groups and demographics.
042
043   In this work, we examine LLMs’ dialect robustness and fairness. Previous studies have shown that
044   language models exhibit biases to dialect prompts in tasks such as hate speech detection and reading
045
      comprehension (Sap et al., 2019; Ziems et al., 2023), as well as making judgments about employ-
      ability and criminal justice (Hofmann et al., 2024). Equally relevant, yet less studied, are tasks
046
      that require reasoning abilities for problem-solving, decision-making, and critical thinking (Wason,
047
      1972; Huth, 2004; Huang & Chang, 2022; Qiao et al., 2022). For instance, algorithm-related tasks
048   (e.g., generation, debugging, etc.) figure prominently in real user queries, as reflected by their first
049   place on the ArenaHard quality board (Li et al., 2024) and their third place on the WildChat fre-
050   quency board (Zhao et al., 2024). However, existing dialectal benchmarks (e.g., Ziems et al., 2023)
051   do not cover these tasks, and current popular reasoning benchmarks such as HumanEval (Chen et al.,
052   2021) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) are constructed in Standardized English. It is thus unclear
053   whether LLMs are fair when responding to reasoning tasks expressed in ‘non-standard’ English di-
      alects. Moreover, dialect queries can also be used to test LLMs’ robustness. Adversarial robustness

082   semantics, i.e., they test what has been referred to as semantic robustness (Malfa & Kwiatkowska,

084
      In this work, we present the first study on evaluating LLMs in reasoning tasks expressed in African
085   American Vernacular English (AAVE), with the objective to evaluate LLMs’ fairness and robustness
086   towards a dialect. We choose AAVE since around 33 million people worldwide and approximately
087   80% of African Americans in the United States speak AAVE, with reports of discriminative behav-
088   iors in various scenarios (Lippi-Green, 1997; Purnell et al., 1999; Massey & Lundy, 2001; Grogger,
089   2011; Rickford & King, 2016). Our study aims to understand whether LLMs hold biases against
090   AAVE speakers in reasoning tasks. Previous approaches in creating AAVE benchmarks from exist-
091   ing Standardized English data either (i) primarily use validated lexical and morphosyntactic trans-
092   formation rules (Ziems et al., 2022; 2023), which fail to capture highly context-dependent nuances
093
      of dialects, or (ii) rely on LLMs as translators (Gupta et al., 2024), which may have the very bi-
      ases that our research wants to unveil (Fleisig et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2024). Therefore, we hire
094
      human AAVE speakers to rewrite instances of seven popular benchmarks to AAVE, including Hu-
095
      manEval (Chen et al., 2021), MBPP (Austin et al., 2021), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) (see
096   Section 2.1 for the complete list of datasets).
097
098   We build and release the first end-to-end human-written Standardized English-AAVE parallel bench-
099
      mark called ReDial (Section 2, examples in Figure 1 and Appendix A.2). ReDial contains more than
      1.2K Standardized English-AAVE prompt pairs, covering four fundamental reasoning tasks, namely
100
      algorithm, math, logic, and comprehensive reasoning (i.e., tasks requiring the composition of the
101
      other three reasoning skills). To the best of our knowledge, our dataset is the first high-quality
102   reasoning dataset with parallel prompts of Standardized English and a dialect annotated end-
103   to-end by dialect speakers. Unlike benchmarks using LLMs as judges, which are subjective to
104   their internal biases (Zheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024), ReDial offers an ob-
105   jective measure as judged by ground truth labels. It enables an easy, objective, and scalable way to
106   report on the dialect fairness and robustness of LLMs as we keep the labels and evaluation process
107   unchanged from the standard pipelines. We consider this dataset an important step toward revealing
      the robustness and fairness of state-of-the-art (SotA) LLMs for dialect users.

117   We use ReDial to benchmark GPT-4o, GPT-4, LLaMA-3.1-70B-Instruct, and several other widely
118
      used SotA LLMs (Section 3). We discover that almost all LLMs suffer from significant performance
      drops for AAVE instances, despite the fact that they are semantically equivalent to their standardized
119
      counterparts. All models except GPT-4o and LLaMA-3.1-70B-Instruct have a pass rate of less than
120
      or similar to 0.6 in AAVE, even with Chain-of-thought prompting (CoT; Kojima et al., 2022; Wei
121   et al., 2022), while the best pass rate in Standardized English is 0.832.
122
123   We further conduct an extensive analysis of the potential reasons for this performance gap (Sec-
124
      tion 4). We show that the skewness of dialect training data does not explain the whole picture, as
      large-scale LLMs have more difficulties in AAVE than misspelled Standardized English prompts,
125
      the latter of which LLMs are even less familiar with in the measurement of perplexity. This indicates
126
      that naively acquainting LLMs with AAVE by data augmentation might not be helpful for dialect
127   robustness and fairness. Further, the performance gap cannot be easily closed by simple standard-
128   ization: prompting LLMs to paraphrase AAVE in a standardized introduces higher costs, but cannot
129   reach the Standardized English prompt performance. These findings point to the conclusion that
130   LLMs are unfair and brittle to dialects and that the problem cannot be easily mitigated.
131
      To summarise, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
132
133           1. We release ReDial, the first high-quality, human-annotated AAVE-Standardized English
134              parallel dataset in four canonical reasoning tasks, comprising seven popular benchmarks.
135
              2. We evaluate several SotA LLMs and show that they are significantly more brittle and unfair
136              to AAVE prompts than their Standardized English counterparts, even with CoT.
137
              3. Compared to misspelled Standardized English prompts of even higher perplexities, large-
138
                 scale LLMs are more brittle to AAVE, which means that naive data augmentation might
139
                 not solve the problem. We further find that prompting LLMs to rephrase a problem in
140              Standardized English does not close the gap, either, but tends to introduce higher costs.
141

143   marking experiment and corresponding results in Section 3. We conduct extensive analysis in Sec-
144   tion 4 and review related work in Section 5. Finally, we conclude the paper and discuss limitations,
145   ethic statement, and reproducibility statement in Sections 6 to 8.

147   2     DATASET
148
149   In this section, we introduce ReDial (Reasoning with Dialect Queries), a benchmark of more than
150   1.2K parallel Standardized English-AAVE query pairs (see a distribution overview in Table 1 and
151   examples in Figure 1 and Appendix A.2). Following Zhu et al. (2023a), ReDial benchmarks four
152   canonical reasoning tasks, namely algorithm, logic, math, and comprehensive reasoning. The
153   task formulation is linguistically diverse, addresses cornerstone problems in human reasoning, and
154   is of particular interest as it is challenging for LLMs.
155   In Section 2.1, we present more details about source data collection. In Section 2.2, we describe the
156   annotation and validation process that we used to ensure that the data is of high quality.

191   2.2      AAVE A NNOTATION AND Q UALITY C HECK
192
193   We conduct a careful data annotation and quality check pipeline, which we schematize in Figure 2
194   and detail below.
195   Annotation. We hire AAVE speakers and instruct them to rewrite each instance by making them
196   sound natural to them, but also preserve the essential information so that ground truth labels stay
197   unchanged (e.g., it is allowed to turn “two” into 2, and vice-versa, but not to alter/delete numerical
198   quantities). For algorithm tasks that require an understanding of code to keep the semantics, we
199   specifically hire expert AAVE annotators with computer science backgrounds.2
200
      Validation. To ensure the quality of the annotation, we conduct careful validations to ensure its
201   naturalness and correctness. First, to ensure naturalness, we ask annotators to cross-check and
202   edit each others’ annotations to make sure that the annotations are natural to AAVE speakers. Sec-
203   ond, to ensure correctness, we conduct both manual and automatic checks by non-AAVE speakers
204   and LLMs. We first have non-AAVE speakers manually check whether the rewriting maintains the
205   essential information and send the invalid instances back to AAVE speakers for reannotation. We
206   conduct a sanity check with GPT-4o for the correctness of rewriting (details in Appendix A.4). We
207   manually check data that GPT-4o flags as invalid to see if all essential information is preserved: we
208   stress that in this round no instance is rejected solely based on the LLM’s judgment. We return
209
      invalid instances to AAVE speakers for correction and iterate the process until all the data passes the
      check.
210
211   After this process, we obtain a high-quality, human-annotated dataset ReDial with more than 1.2K
212   Standard English-AAVE parallel prompts in four canonical reasoning tasks. ReDial is the first
213   benchmark of its kind and enables easy testing and analysis of LLMs’ dialect fairness and robustness

      3.2   E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
339
340   We report pass rates for ReDial in Table 2 and 3, respectively averaged by task and by model (see
341   detailed results in Appendix A.7). We now summarise the main results of our experiments.
342
343   All models are brittle to AAVE. We find that all models experience performance drops in AAVE
344   compared to Standardized ReDial, and these drops are statistically significant in all cases, with
345   the sole exception of LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct. Except for GPT-4o and LLaMA-3.1-70B-Instruct,
346   all other models have pass rates of similar to or below 0.6 in AAVE Redial, even with CoT,
347   while the best pass rate in Standardized ReDial is 0.832. This indicates that our benchmark poses
348   huge challenges to models, both in terms of absolute performance and with respect to their dialect
349   robustness and fairness.
350
351   All reasoning tasks are brittle to AAVE. LLMs experience the most severe drops in tasks related
352   to algorithm and comprehensive reasoning. In comprehensive tasks that require the composition of
353
      more than one elementary reasoning skill, the relative performance drop is especially strong: almost
      50% relative performance drop across models with zero-shot, and close to 30% drop with CoT.
354
      LLMs face further difficulty when they are asked in a dialect to compose different skills for solving
355
      problems.
356
357
      Scaling does not make models more robust to AAVE. Comparing within LLaMA-3 and Phi-
358
      3 model famlies, we find that although increasing model size improves absolute performance, it
359   cannot close the Standardized English-AAVE performance gaps. For example, comparing LLaMA-

364   prompted in dialects either.

      4.1   DATA S KEWNESS D OES NOT E XPLAIN AAVE B RITTLENESS
406
407   One possible explanation of the performance drop on AAVE is its infrequency in LLMs’ training
408   corpora. As the model’s training data is largely unknown, we use perplexity as a proxy to measure
409   how familiar the LLMs are with some data: the higher the perplexity, the less familiar an LLM is
410   with the data. We conduct experiments on LLaMA-3.1-70B-Instruct, Phi-3-Medium/Mini-128K-
411   Instruct on Standardized and AAVE ReDial and report their perplexities averaged across instances
412   in Table 4.
413   As expected, LLMs have higher perplexities on AAVE than Standardized ReDial, which indicates
414   they are indeed less familiar with AAVE than with Standardized English. Does this mean that
415   we can fully attribute the dialect performance gap to its data skewness? To answer this question,
416   we gradually perturb Standardized English by injecting typos, such that we decrease the LLMs’
417   familiarity with the input texts (i.e., the measured perplexity goes up). Specifically, we simulate
418   typos by replacing/deleting/adding characters in Standardized ReDial. We control an increasing
419   perturbation rate until the tested models’ perplexities exceed those measured in AAVE (i.e., when
420
      models are less familiar with misspelled Standardized English than with AAVE).5
421   Results are in Figure 3. Interestingly, although LLaMA-3.1-70B-Instruct and Phi-3-Medium-128K-
422   Instruct performance drops with denser perturbations, even their drop in the strongest perturbation
423   level is lower than that of human-written dialect prompts. This means that even when these LLMs
424   are more familiar with AAVE, they still cannot perform as well in this dialect. Conversely,
425   we find that Phi-3-Mini-128K-Instruct has better performance in AAVE data compared to perturbed
426
      texts of similar perplexities. This discrepancy seems to suggest that the small-scale model might
      have a different behavior pattern compared to larger models in dialect robustness. We further find
427
      that the denser AAVE features are, the bigger the performance drop is. We report further details
428
      in Appendix A.8, where we gradually control and inject synthetic lexico-syntactic dialect features
429   following Ziems et al. (2022).

454
455   Since LLMs generally show superior performance in Standardized ReDial, we experiment with
456   instructing models to standardize and then answer the question to mitigate the AAVE bias, which
457   we refer to as standardization. Specifically, we suffix ‘Let’s rephrase the query in Standard English
458   first, then answer the question’ to every query. Results are reported in Figure 4 (bar plot).
459   Indeed, LLM performance generally increases with standardization. Surprisingly, standardization
460   improves model performance even when the prompt input is already in Standard English. Despite
461   this, their performance on AAVE ReDial with standardization promoting still cannot reach
462   their vanilla performance on Standardized ReDial. We further analyze the error patterns in Sec-
463   tion 4.3.
464   Moreover, we notice that standardization introduces a computational overhead in terms of token
465   count of LLMs’ responses (Figure 4, scatter plot), especially in GPT-4o and GPT-4. This means
466   that even if dialect users pay more, they might still not be able to receive the same quality
467   service as users who use Standardized English.
468
469   4.3   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
470
471   Intuitively, standardization prompting should cancel the dialect gap. However, we still observe a
472   sensible gap between model performance on Standardized ReDial with zero-shot prompting and
473   AAVE ReDial with standardization prompting. In this section, we qualitatively compare GPT-4o’s
474   outputs in these two settings, the model with the best absolute overall performance, and examine its
475   errors. We focus on the math subset of ReDial and identify three key error patterns: wrong question
476
      rephrasing, distraction by irrelevant information, and failure to execute all steps.
477   Wrong question rephrasing. GPT-4o wrongly phrases question ‘Jame ... How many years have
478   they got between them now if in 8 years his cousin will be 5 years younger than twice his age?’ to
479   ‘James ... How old is his cousin now?’, which changes the question of age gap to absolute age.
480   Distraction by irrelevant information. GPT-4o gets distracted by task-irrelevant information after
481   AAVE standardization while the distraction is not observed in Standardized ReDial. For instance, in
482   ‘Say we got 8 different books and 10 different movies in the crazy silly school series. How many more
483   movies than books is there gon be in the crazy silly school series if you read 19 books and watched
484   61 movies?’, books that have been read and movies that have been watched are not associated with
485   the answer. Although GPT-4o can ignore irrelevant information in Standardized ReDial, it gets
      distracted after AAVE standardization, which shows the brittleness of its reasoning ability.

487   after standardization (e.g., ‘Let (x) be the number of apple pie boxes...’). However, it does not fully
488   solve the problem in the end and only returns a formula (e.g., ‘30x + 255’), which indicates that the
489   model’s reasoning ability is limited when it comes to program simulation for queries expressed in

      5   R ELATED WORKS
494
495
      Dialect studies in natural language processing. Previous works on AAVE studies in natural
      language processing mostly focus on non-reasoning-heavy tasks such as POS tagging (Jørgensen
496
      et al., 2015; 2016), language identification and dependency parsing (Blodgett et al., 2016), automatic
497
      captioning (Tatman, 2017), and general language generation (Deas et al., 2023). AAVE is also found
498   to be more likely to trigger false positives in hate speech identifiers (Davidson et al., 2019; Sap
499   et al., 2019) due to specific word choices (Harris et al., 2022), be considered negative by automatic
500   sentiment classifier (Groenwold et al., 2020), and cause covert biases in essential areas of social
501   justice (Hofmann et al., 2024). Relevant studies (Ziems et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2024) also find
502   that rule-based AAVE feature perturbations can downgrade language model performance in a wide
503   range of tasks covered by GLUE (Wang, 2018).
504   More generally, dialects in world languages pose challenges to natural language processing systems.
505   Ziems et al. (2023) find that auto-encoder models are brittle on rule-based English dialect feature
506   perturbations. Fleisig et al. (2024) report that English dialect speakers perceive responses generated
507   by chatbots to be more negative than Standardized English prompts. Faisal et al. (2024) find that
508   world dialects cause problems in tasks including dependency parsing (Scherrer et al., 2019) and
509   machine translation (Mirzakhalov, 2021) on mBERT and XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020).
510   However, existing works fail to systematically cover reasoning tasks in dialects. There is no existing
511   high-quality end-to-end human-annotated dataset on such a task. Moreover, studies on LLM task-
512   specific capabilities tend to focus on traditional auto-encoder models such as BERT and RoBERTa
513   instead of SotA auto-regressive LLMs. Our work fills the gaps in these areas.
514
      Fairness and Robustness of Large Language Models. LLMs are widely testified to be both unfair
515
      and brittle. They introduce unfair performance (Huang et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024) and cost
516   (Petrov et al., 2024) to users across different languages, exacerbate social imbalance by marginaliz-
517   ing minority groups in various aspects including gender (Kotek et al., 2023; Fraser & Kiritchenko,
518   2024), race (Hofmann et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), and culture (Naous et al., 2023; Tao et al.,
519   2024). Our work shows for the first time that LLMs also exhibit unfairness in reasoning tasks for
520   speakers of a dialect.
521
      Previous works report that LLMs are very brittle to slight variations of prompts by introducing typos
522   or paraphrasing in Standardized English (Elazar et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022; Raj et al., 2022; Zhu
523   et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2024). In this work, we consider a novel application of using human-written
524   perturbations in AAVE by asking humans to rewrite instances to their dialect and evaluate LLM
525   robustness towards these natural perturbations, which have proven to cause LLMs to be more brittle
526   than synthetic typo-style (Section 4.1) or linguistic-rule-based (Appendix A.8) perturbations.
527
528
529
      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we present ReDial which has 1.2K+ parallel Standardized English-AAVE prompts
      to evaluate LLMs’ dialect robustness and fairness in algorithm, logic, math, and comprehensive
532
      reasoning as four canonical reasoning tasks. With ReDial, we find that SotA LLMs show signifi-
533
      cant unfairness and brittleness to reasoning tasks expressed in AAVE. The data skewness of AAVE
534   does not explain the whole picture as large-scale LLMs are more brittle to AAVE compared to
535   Standardized English typos of even higher perplexities. Prompting LLMs to rephrase questions in
536   Standardized English cannot fully bridge the gap but tends to introduce higher costs. These findings
537   highlight the unfairness of LLMs to dialect users and also shed light on the brittleness of LLMs’ rea-
538   soning capabilities when it comes to minor variations of prompts without changing their semantics.
539   We call for further studies to enhance LLMs’ fairness and robustness to dialects to provide equal
      service to users from all linguistic groups and demographics.

      7   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      ReDial is a collection of high-quality human-annotated translations: obtaining such data requires
543   making clear design choices and poses ethical questions that we hereby address.
544
545   For data collection, we deliberately do not set hard constraints for annotator identity and demo-
546
      graphic verification, recognizing there are no definite boundaries to identify dialects and their speak-
      ers (King, 2020). King (2020) further elaborate that the term “AAVE” itself is contested, with al-
547
      ternatives that could be used instead; in employing the term “AAVE”, we adhere to the widely used
548
      terminology in related works on dialects and NLP (Ziems et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2024). We
549   corroborate the data quality by asking self-identified dialect speakers to cross-validate each others’
550   answers.
551
552
      We do not collect annotators’ personal information; while we firmly commit to this rule to protect
      annotators’ privacy, it makes it difficult to draw conclusions about how annotators’ backgrounds
553
      shape their writing/individual-level variations. Further on the ethical aspect of data collection, we
554
      work with a data vendor that makes sure the recruitment and annotation adhere to high standards for
555   and from the annotators. However, although we have a legal contract and we try our best to convey
556   our guidelines and requirements, we admit that we do not have full control over how the vendor
557   recruits people and conducts data annotation.
558
      We also stress that the LLM validation stage in our quality control process is not completely
559
      trustworthy as even they are prone to hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023) and biases against minority
560
      groups (Xu et al., 2021; Fleisig et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). To mitigate this
561   issue, we conduct full manual checks of every instance identified as invalid by an LLM so that no
562   instance is rejected purely because of LLM decisions.
563
564
      Last, there are limitations on how well standard benchmarks reflect use cases of practical usage for
      LLMs. For ReDial, we select the source datasets among those reported in highly impactful LLM
565
      technical reports such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), and Phi-
566
      3 (Abdin et al., 2024). Their popularity makes it easy to integrate them with existing pipelines, and
567   the presence of ground truth labels mitigates inherent biases of using LLMs as evaluators (Zheng
568   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). Although we try our best to simulate user queries
569   (e.g., changing code completion to instruction following queries in HumanEval), we do note there
570   can be a gap between tasks as in standard benchmarks and queries in real workflows.
