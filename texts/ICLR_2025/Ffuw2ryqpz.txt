009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The default strategy for training single-view Large Reconstruction Models (LRMs)
012            follows the fully supervised route using large-scale datasets of synthetic 3D assets
013            or multi-view captures. Although these resources simplify the training procedure,
014            they are hard to scale up beyond the existing datasets and they are not necessarily
015            representative of the real distribution of object shapes. To address these limitations,
016            in this paper, we introduce Real3D, the first LRM system that can be trained using
017            single-view real-world images. Real3D introduces a novel self-training framework
018
               that can benefit from both the existing synthetic data and diverse single-view real
               images. We propose two unsupervised losses that allow us to supervise LRMs
019
               at the pixel- and semantic-level, even for training examples without ground-truth
020
               3D or novel views. To further improve performance and scale the image data, we
021            develop an automatic data curation approach to collect high-quality examples from
022            in-the-wild images. Our experiments show that Real3D consistently outperforms
023            prior work in four diverse evaluation settings that include real and synthetic data, as
024            well as both in-domain and out-of-domain shapes. We will make our code, models
025            and data available upon publication.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The scaling law is the secret sauce of large foundation models (Kaplan et al., 2020). By scaling up both
031   model parameters and training data, the foundation models demonstrate emerging properties and have
032   revolutionized Natural Language Processing (Brown et al., 2020) and 2D Computer Vision (Radford
033
      et al., 2021). Recently, the same recipe has been applied to build a foundation model for single-view 3D
      reconstruction, which has the potential to benefit AR/VR (Rahaman et al., 2019), Robotics (Yan et al.,
034
      2018) and AIGC (Chen et al., 2023). From the perspective of model design, Transformers (Vaswani
035
      et al., 2017) have been proven to be effective architectures for the 2D-to-3D lifting (Jiang et al.,
036   2023; Hong et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), facilitating single-view Large Reconstruction Models
037   (LRMs) (Hong et al., 2023). From the perspective of training data, the default resources are multi-
038   view images from 3D/video data, where we observe that increasing the dataset size (Deitke et al.,
039   2023; 2024; Yu et al., 2023) is beneficial to the model performance. Further scaling up the LRMs is
040   important. As single-view 3D reconstruction is an ill-posed problem due to the ambiguity from 2D to
041   3D (Sinha & Adelson, 1993), the essence of LRMs is learning generic shape and texture priors from
042   large data (Hong et al., 2023).
043   However, the excessive reliance on multi-view supervision creates a critical bottleneck to expanding
044   the training data of such models. The relevant strategies for data collection (i.e., synthetic 3D
045   assets (Deitke et al., 2023) and intentional video captures (Yu et al., 2023)) are time-consuming
046   and hard to further scale up. Thus, compared to the training data used for state-of-the-art language
047   models (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023) and 2D vision models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Zhao
048   et al., 2024), the scale of the training data for 3D reconstruction models is relatively limited. Moreover,
049   the data is biased towards shapes that are easy to model by artists or easy to capture in the round
050
      table setting (Reizenstein et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2023), creating a domain gap between the training
      and inference phases.
051
052   To address this limitation, we propose to train LRMs with single-view real-world images. Compared
053   to the limited number of 3D assets or intentional video captures, images are easier to collect and
      readily available in existing large-scale datasets (Deng et al., 2009; Thomee et al., 2016; Schuhmann

      We introduce Real3D, the first LRM system designed with the core principle of training on single-
090   view real-world images. To achieve this, we propose a novel self-training framework. Our framework
091   incorporates unsupervised training guidance, improving Real3D without multi-view real-data super-
092   vision. In detail, the guidance involves a cycle consistency rendering loss at the pixel level and a
093   semantic loss between the input view and rendered novel views at the image level. We introduce
094   regularization for the two losses to avoid trivial reconstruction solutions that degrade the model.
095
      To further improve performance, we apply careful quality control for the real images that we use for
096   training. If we naively train using all the collected image examples, the performance drops. To address
097   this, we develop an automated data curation method for selecting high-quality examples, specifically
098   unoccluded instances, from noisy real-world images. Eventually, we jointly perform self-training on
099   the curated data and supervised learning on synthetic data. This strategy enriches the model with
100   knowledge from real data while preventing divergence. Our final model consistently outperforms the
101   state-of-the-art models that are trained without single-view in-the-wild images (Fig. 1).
102   We evaluate Real3D on a diverse set of datasets, spanning both real and synthetic data and covering
103   both in-domain and out-of-domain shapes. Experimental results highlight Real3D’s three key
104   strengths: i) Superior performance. Real3D outperforms prior works in all evaluations. ii) Effective
105   use of real data. Real3D demonstrates greater improvement using real data in single view than
106   what the previous methods achieved using real data in multiple views. iii) Scalability. Performance
107   improves as more data are incorporated, demonstrating Real3D’s potential when further scaling the
      training data.

111   Single-view 3D Reconstruction. Reconstructing 3D scenes and objects from a single image is a
112   core task in 3D Computer Vision. One line of work focuses on developing better 3D representations
113
      and utilizing their unique properties to improve reconstruction quality. For example, different explicit
      representations have been explored, e.g., voxels (Choy et al., 2016; Tulsiani et al., 2017), point
114
      clouds (Fan et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2018), multiplane images (Mildenhall et al., 2019; Tucker &
115
      Snavely, 2020), meshes (Liu et al., 2019; Gkioxari et al., 2019), and 3D Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023;
116   Szymanowicz et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024). Instinct representations, for example, SDFs (Park et al.,
117   2019; Mescheder et al., 2019; Sitzmann et al., 2019), and radiance fields (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Yu
118   et al., 2021; Rematas et al., 2021) have also improved the reconstruction accuracy.
119
      Recent methods explore incorporating various guidance to improve the quality of single-view 3D
120
      reconstruction. MCC (Wu et al., 2023a) and ZeroShape (Huang et al., 2023) use depth guidance;
121
      however, accurate depth inputs or estimates are not always available. RealFusion (Melas-Kyriazi
122   et al., 2023), Make-it-3D (Tang et al., 2023) and Magic123 (Qian et al., 2023) harness diffusion
123   priors as guidance using distillation losses (Poole et al., 2022). Nevertheless, the process involves
124   per-shape optimization, which is slow and hard to scale. To solve the problem, Zero-1-to-3 (Liu et al.,
125   2023a) fine-tunes diffusion models for direct novel view generation. With multiple views, it is easier
126   to get a 3D reconstruction. However, this route suffers from limited reconstruction quality, caused
127   by inconsistency between the generated novel views (Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023; Voleti et al.,
128   2024). Adversarial guidance is explored to distinguish reconstruction from input view (Hu et al.,
129   2021; Xu et al., 2022). However, adversarial training is usually unstable and difficult to extend to
130
      general categories. Moreover, semantic guidance leverages the image-to-text inversion model and the
      similarity calculation model to supervise novel reconstruction views (Deng et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
131
      2023). This improves the semantic consistency of the reconstructions but harms the reconstruction
132
      details, as text-to-image models lack spatial awareness (Radford et al., 2021; Gal et al., 2022). In
133   contrast, our method, Real3D, proposes two complementary unsupervised losses to improve both the
134   semantic and spatial consistency of our reconstruction, enabling us to train using single-view images.
135
136
      Large Reconstruction Models. Large reconstruction models are proposed for generalizable and
      fast feed-forward 3D reconstruction, following the design principles of foundation models. They use
137
      scalable model architecture, e.g., Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Hong et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
138
      2023) or Convolutional U-Nets (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Tang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) for
139   encoding diverse shape and texture priors and directly mapping 2D information to 3D representations.
140   The models are trained with multi-view rendering losses, assuming access to 3D ground-truth. For
141   example, LRM (Hong et al., 2023) uses triplane tokens to query information from 2D image features.
142   Other methods improve reconstruction quality by exploiting better representations (Zou et al., 2023;
143   Wei et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) and introducing generative priors (Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
144   2024; Tang et al., 2024). However, one shortcoming of these models is that they require a normalized
145   coordinate system and canonicalized input camera pose, which limits the scalability and effectiveness
146   of training with multi-view real-world data. To solve the problem, we enable the model to perform
147
      self-training on real-world single images, without the need for coordinate system normalization and
      multi-view supervision.
148
149   Unsupervised 3D Learning from Real Images. Learning to perform 3D reconstruction typically
150   requires 3D ground-truth or multi-view supervision, which makes scaling up more challenging. To
151   solve this problem, a promising avenue would be learning from massive unannotated data. Early works
152   in this direction leverage category-level priors, where the reconstruction can benefit from category-
153
      specific templates and the definition of a canonical coordinate frame (Kar et al., 2015; Kanazawa
      et al., 2018; Nguyen-Phuoc et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020; Henderson et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Lin
154
      et al., 2020; Monnier et al., 2022; Kulkarni et al., 2020; Duggal & Pathak, 2022; Chan et al., 2021).
155
      Recent works extend this paradigm to general categories by adjusting adversarial losses (Ye et al.,
156   2021; Mi et al., 2022; Chan et al., 2022), multi-category distillation (Alwala et al., 2022), synergy
157   between multiple generative models (Xiang et al., 2023), knowledge distillation (Skorokhodov et al.,
158   2023) and depth regularization (Sargent et al., 2023). However, these methods learn 3D reconstruction
159   from scratch, without leveraging the available 3D annotations due to limitations of their learning
160   frameworks. Thus, their 3D accuracy and viewpoint range are limited. In contrast, our model enables
161   initialization with available 3D ground-truth from synthetic examples and is jointly trained with
      in-the-wild images using unsupervised losses, which improves the reconstruction quality.

      3    P RELIMINARIES
194   Large Reconstruction Model (LRM). Let I ∈ RH×W ×3 be an input image that contains the target
195   object to reconstruct. The LRM outputs a 3D representation of the object, T = LRM(I), where
196   T ∈ R3×h×w×c is the latent triplane. LRM performs volume rendering (Chan et al., 2022) to produce
197   novel views. The rendering module is formulated as IˆΦ = π(T, Φ), where IˆΦ is the rendered image
198   under a target camera pose Φ ∈ SE(3), and π represents the rendering process.
199
      Training LRM on Synthetic Multi-view Images. Training an LRM requires multi-view image
200   supervision. For each training object, we assume that we have several views of it with the correspond-
201
      ing camera poses. We denote the views and poses as {(Iigt , Φi )|i = 1, ..., n}, where n images are
202   collected. This multi-view information is usually obtained from synthetic 3D assets (Deitke et al.,
203   2023) using graphical rendering tools. The loss function on the multi-views is:
204

239
240   We propose a novel framework that enables training LRMs using real-world single-view images,
241   which are easier to collect/scale and can better capture the real distribution of object shapes. As
242   shown in Fig. 2, we initialize an LRM on a synthetic dataset. Then we collect real-world object
243   instances from in-the-wild images. We jointly train the model on synthetic data (Eq. 1) and perform
244   self-training on real data. The former prevents the model from diverging with the help of supervision
245
      from ground-truth novel views. The latter introduces new data in model training, which improves the
      reconstruction quality and generalization capability (Sec. 4.1). We also propose an automatic data
246
      curation method to collect high-quality instance segmentations from in-the-wild images (Sec. 4.2).

      Overview. The effectiveness of the multi-view training loss (Eq. 1) originates from applying
251
      supervision for improving both pixel-level and semantic-level similarity between reconstruction and
252
      ground-truth novel views. Following this philosophy, we develop novel unsupervised pixel-level and
253   semantic-level guidance when training the model on single-view images, where ground-truth novel
254   views are not available. For our base model, we use a fine-tuned TripoSR (Tochilkin et al., 2024), an
255   LRM without input pose and intrinsics conditioning.
256
      Pixel-level Guidance: Cycle-Consistency. To provide pixel-level guidance, we propose a novel
257
      cycle-consistency rendering loss. The intuition behind this is that if we have a perfect LRM, the 3D
258
      reconstruction and rendered novel views should also be perfect. Similarly, if we apply our LRM again
259   to any of the novel views, we should be able to produce renders of the original input view perfectly.
260   As shown in Fig. 3, we input the model with an image. We randomly sample a camera pose and render
261   the reconstruction, obtaining a synthesized novel view. This novel view is fed back into the model to
262   reconstruct it again. Finally, we render this second reconstruction from the viewpoint of the original
263   input, using the inverse of the sampled pose. The goal is for this final rendering to match the original
264   input, ensuring cycle consistency. Moreover, we observe that applying a stop-gradient operation on
265   intermediate rendering can effectively prevent model degeneration and trivial reconstruction solutions.
266   This observation is akin to self-training strategies in other domains (Chen & He, 2021).
267   We formulate the pixel-level guidance as follows. We input the model with an image I R that contains
268   a shape instance in the real world and then reconstruct the latent triplane.

      Metrics. We use two sets of metrics to evaluate the models on data with and without multi-view
407

408
409

410
      LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018).
411
412       • Semantic and Self-Consistency Metrics. To evaluate reconstruction quality on single-view
413
      data without ground-truth multi-view images, we introduce novel metrics. First, we render novel

414

415

416   and FID score (Heusel et al., 2017). Second, we evaluate the self-consistency of the reconstruction.
417   We render a designated novel view of the reconstruction, use it as input for the LRM, and render the
418   second reconstruction from the viewpoint of the original input. We evaluate the consistency using
419   standard NVS metrics.

421   with mesh scale 2.0. To get mesh from the triplane representation, we use the marching cube with

447   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
448
449   Qualitative Results. We show examples of Real3D reconstruction on in-the-wild images in Fig. 4,
450   showing that Real3D can recover the geometry with high fidelity. Please also see Fig. 9 in Appendix D
451   for comparisons with baselines on evaluation sets.
452   Quantitative Results. Real3D consistently outperforms prior works on all four test sets of our
453   evaluation. As shown in Table 1 to Table 4, Real3D showcases a 0.74 (4% relatively) PSNR
454   improvement and a 6.3% relative LPIPS improvement on average over the directly comparable
455   TripoSR model. This demonstrates the effectiveness of our self-training method using real data.
456   Results in Table 2 also highlight the advantage of our self-training method by using a broader data
457   distribution.
458   Additionally, we observe that methods with generative priors do not perform well on out-of-
459   distribution data. These methods generate novel views and use those views to perform sparse-view
460   reconstruction. We conjecture that the reason is the compounding error of the novel view synthesis
461   and reconstruction stages. This is another argument in favor of single-stage methods, like ours.
462   Mesh Quality. We report the CD for InstantMesh (the best baseline with generative prior) and
463   TripoSR (the best deterministic baseline) are 0.395 and 0.321. In contrast to that, our method Real3D
464   achieves a 0.275 CD, improving it by 14.3%. We include the visualization of the mesh reconstructions
465   in Appendix E. We observe that the other two baselines perform worse than Real3D, particularly
466   in cases with non-common object shapes, while InstantMesh consistently struggles to faithfully
467   reconstruct thin structures.

470   5.2     A BLATION S TUDIES
471
472   Rendering loss on input view. As shown in Table 5 (1), when we use only the LR   in loss, we observe
473   slight improvements of PSNR, but SSIM and LPIPS have limited improvements. We observe a
474   similar pattern when we add real data (raw and cleaned). This pattern implies that LR
                                                                                          in can only help
475   the model render more realistic pixels by learning the real-image pixel distribution, but it can not
476
      improve the 3D reconstruction quality.
477   Semantic-level Guidance. As shown in Table 5 (2), naively applying the CLIP-based semantic
478   loss on all rendered novel views degrades the performance. We present visualization results in Fig. 7
479   of the Appendix, where we observe the multi-head problem of reconstructions. We conjecture that
480   the reason for this observation is that copying the input view geometry to all other views is a trivial
481   solution to minimize the semantic loss. This requires us to incorporate more regularization as we do
482
      with our semantic guidance, which achieves improvements across all metrics.
483   Pixel-level Guidance. As shown in Table 5 (3), the pixel-level cycle consistency guidance is only
484   useful when using both clean data, stopping the gradient of intermediate rendering, and applying
485   a training curriculum. The result demonstrates the importance of each proposed component. We
      present more qualitative results for the ablation in Fig. 7 of the Appendix C.

      5.3      S CALABILITY AND E FFECTIVENESS OF S ELF -T RAINING
506   We provide experimental results to further understand the scalability and effectiveness of our self-
507   training method.
508
      Scalability - Data Amount Analysis. Here, we evaluate the effect of scaling up the training data. As
509
      shown in Fig. 5, Real3D achieves consistent improvements as more real images are used for training.
510   This performance gain curve demonstrates the potential for further scaling up the in-the-wild images
511   we use for training.
512
513   Effectiveness - Performance Gain Analysis. We intend to further validate the effectiveness of
514   using single-view images for self-training. For this purpose, we compare the performance gain of
515   using self-training with another method to scale up the training data, i.e., using captured multi-view
516   real images. We report the performance gain of training with multi-view images by comparing LRM
517   variants, i.e., an LRM when trained on synthetic data only and when trained with both synthetic and
518
      real-world MVImgNet multi-view capture (He & Wang, 2023). We report this gain as ∆multi-view.
      We also report the gain of our self-training method as ∆ours. We report the results on the four
519
      datasets. As shown in Table 6, our method achieves a larger performance gain while using much
520
      fewer images for training, showing its effectiveness. The limited improvement of ∆multi-view also
521   verifies the limitation of that training strategy at leveraging real-world multi-view data (Sec. 3). We
522   also report the detailed numbers for computing ∆multi-view in Appendix C.
523
524
      More interestingly, ∆multi-view uses only MVImgNet data for training and has limited, nearly
      zero, improvement on in-the-wild images. In contrast, our method achieves larger improvements by
525
      leveraging more in-the-wild data, demonstrating its efficacy in improving generalization.

528   6     C ONCLUSION
529
530   We present Real3D, the first large reconstruction system that can leverage single-view real images for
531   training. This has the major advantage of enabling training on a seemingly endless data source, that
532   is representative of the general object shape distribution. We propose a self-training framework using
533   unsupervised losses, which improves the performance of the model without relying on ground-truth
534   novel views. Additionally, to further improve perfrormance, we develop an automatic data curation
535   method to collect high-quality shape instances from in-the-wild data. Compared with previous works,
536
      Real3D demonstrates consistent improvements across diverse evaluation sets and highlights the
      potential of improving Large Reconstruction Models by training on large-scale image collections.
537
538   Limitation. One limitation of Real3D is using constant intrinsics during self-training, due to the
539   unknown intrinsics for in-the-wild images. Although it has been proven helpful, we might observe
      larger improvements by incorporating an intrinsics estimation module.

877
878   Training. As we discussed, TripoSR predicts reconstruction with random scales on different inputs.
879   The reason is that TripoSR is not conditioned on the input view camera pose and intrinsics. Thus, the
880   model is encouraged to guess the object scale (Tochilkin et al., 2024). Moreover, TripoSR is trained
881   on a set of Objaverse data rendered using different rendering settings. Thus, TripoSR usually overfits
882   the training scales and can not predict the scales accurately for images rendered in different settings
883   or in-the-wild real images. The scale of the object in the output triplane can vary and may not be
884   consistent with regard to the scale variation in the input images. Specifically, the inaccurate scales are
885
      manifested as the misalignment between the rendered and original input view when using a canonical
      camera pose with a constant translation vector. And the misalignment of the scale is random.
886
887   We fine-tune TripoSR to solve the problem, using the Objaverse images rendered with a constant
888   camera translation scale. We use a learning rate 4e − 5 with AdamW optimizer and warmup iteration
889   3, 000. It is fine-tuned with 40, 000 iterations with an equivalent batch size of 80.
890   For all training, we β1 , β2 , ϵ of AdamW as 0.9, 0.96, and 1e − 6. We use a weight decay of 0.05
891   and perform gradient clipping with the max gradient scale of 1.0. During training, we render images
892   with a resolution of 128 × 128. For each pixel, we sample 128 points along its ray. For the images
893   in the real dataset, we crop the instance with a random expanding ratio in [1.45, 1.7] of the longer
894   side of the instance bounding box. Our inputs have a resolution of H = W = 512 and the triplane
895   has a resolution of h = w = 64. As TripoSR requires inputs with a gray background, we render a
896   density mask σ̂Φ together with the color image IˆΦR when calculating the cycle-consistency pixel-level
897   loss. We apply the rendered density mask to make the background gray. We use 8 GPUs with 48GB
898   memory. It takes 4 days for training.
899   Evaluation. As our model inherits the model architecture of TripoSR, it can not handle real data
900   input with non-centered principle points. To evaluate the models, we select a subset of MVImgNet
901   and CO3D of 100 instances and use the image where the center of its instance mask is closest
902
      to the image center as input. We mask the background and perform center cropping with an
      expanding ratio of 1.6 times the mask bounding box size. We do not have any requirements for
903
      the target novel views. Besides, we use the provided COLMAP point cloud to normalize the
904
      camera poses. We normalize the input pose as the canonical pose ϕ. The poses of other views
905   are normalized with similarity transformations accordingly, following LRM (Hong et al., 2023).
906   We evaluate CO3D and MVImgNet with 5 views for each instance and evaluate OmniObject3D
907   with 10 views for each instance. To evaluate the self-consistency, we use intermediate camera
908   pose with the azimuth of [0, 30, 60, −30, −60, 30, 60, −30, −60, 30, 60, −30, −60] and elevation of
909   [0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 30, 30, 30, 30, 60, 60, 60, 60]. These viewpoints cover the front of the shape, which is
910   designed for methods with generative priors in case the generated random background influences the
911   evaluation results for fair comparisons.

935   Second, We filter instances by their category. We empirically observe the LRM can not effectively
936   reconstruct instances belonging to specific categories, e.g. bus. The reason is the large scale-variance
937   between the front view and the side view. For example, when seeing the bus from a front view, the
938   model can not reconstruct its side view with the correct scales, as the latent triplane representation
939   has a cubic physical size. We observe that performing self-training on these instances harms the
940   performance instead. We note this is a limitation of the Triplane-based LRM base model rather than
941   our self-training framework.
942   Third, we filter the occluded instances. As shown in Fig. 6, we leverage the synergy between instance
943   segmentation and single-view depth estimation for occlusion detection. We first detect the mask
944   boundaries and then calculate the boundary parts that are contacting other instances. We use an
945   erosion operation with kernel size 9 for boundary detection. The boundary is calculated as the
946   difference between the eroded and the original instance mask. To detect boundaries that contact
947   other instances, we use another erosion operation with kernel size 15. We erode the boundary of the
948
      current instance, then the contacting boundary is defined as its overlap region with the boundary of
      any other instances. We then determine whether an object is occluded based on whether it “owns" the
949
      boundary. For each instance, we sample N = 20 points (with return) on the boundary that contacts
950
      other instances. We then calculate the normal direction of the boundary at the sampled points. In
951   detail, we use the Sobel operator to calculate the boundary tangent and normal direction. We note
952   that we ignore the points whose 8 neighbors are all positive, during the point sampling process. We
953   can easily know the outer and inner-mask normal directions by querying the instance mask. If the
954   query results are both negative or positive, potentially due to the non-convex local boundary, we reject
955   the object. Then we sample one point along each normal direction, where the sampling distance is
956   0.05 ∗ s, where s = (bx + by )/2 and bx , by are the size of the mask bounding box in x and y-axis.
957   We then query the estimated depth at the two sampled points, denoted as Dinner and Douter . If
958   Dinner /Douter is smaller than 0.95, we consider the point occluded. If half of the sampled points
959
      on the boundary are considered occluded, they vote the object as occluded. We note that all the
      aggressive strategies are used to avoid false negative occlusion detection results.
960
961   We use DECOLA (Cho & Krähenbühl, 2023) and Depth Anything (Yang et al., 2024) for instance
962   segmentation and depth estimation. We use a confidence threshold of 0.3 to filter the detection
963   results of DECOLA. We use this low threshold for detecting all instances in the image, as any
964   non-detected object affects the occlusion detection results. However, we empirically observe that a
965
      too-low confidence threshold, e.g. 0.1, will lead to over-segmentation and false positive detection
      results.
