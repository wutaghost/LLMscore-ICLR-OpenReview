009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                This work investigates the safe exploration problem, where an agent must max-
012                imize performance while satisfying safety constraints. To address this problem,
013                we propose a method that includes a learnable world model and two policies, a
014                high-level policy and a low-level policy, that ensure safety at both levels. The
015                high-level policy generates safe subgoals for the low-level policy, which progres-
016                sively guide the agent towards the final goal. Through trajectory imagination,
017
                   the low-level policy learns to safely reach these subgoals. The proposed method
                   was evaluated on the standard benchmark, SafetyGym, and demonstrated superior
018
                   performance quality while maintaining comparable safety violations compared to
019
                   state-of-the-art approaches. In addition, we investigated an alternative implemen-
020                tation of safety in hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) algorithms using
021                Lagrange multipliers, and demonstrated in the custom long-horizon environments
022                SafeAntMaze that our approach achieves comparable performance while more ef-
023                fectively satisfying safety constraints, while the flat safe policy fails to accomplish
024                this task.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Exploration is one of the most critical capabilities for Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents, enabling
030
      them to discover optimal behaviors for achieving predefined goals. One of the main challenges in
      applying RL algorithms to the real world is the lack of safety guarantees during the exploration
031
      process, which can lead to damage to expensive hardware or dangerous situations (Ray et al., 2019).
032
      Therefore, there is an urgent need for safe reinforcement learning approaches that address the safe
033   exploration problem, which we investigate in this work.
034
035   Most current safe reinforcement learning methods employ a Lagrangian-based approach (Huang
036
      et al., 2024; Jayant & Bhatnagar, 2022; Hogewind et al., 2023; Ha et al., 2021), where the RL
      controller maximizes performance while minimizing safety constraint violations through the use
037
      of a Lagrangian multiplier. A primary problem with Lagrangian-based methods, as noted in (Yu
038
      et al., 2022), is that it is a complex challenge for the RL controller to simultaneously maximize
039   performance and ensure safety. One possible solution to this problem is to use safety layers (Roza
040   et al., 2022), where a separate safety layer module is responsible for maintaining safety. Otherwise,
041   we can simplify the performance maximization task for the RL controller by using a high-level
042   policy that generates intermediate subgoals and guides the agent toward the final goal. Thus, the
043   RL controller’s task remains to maximize performance and safety over a short horizon up to each
044   subgoal.
045   Model-based reinforcement learning approaches allow efficient training of a transition function that
046   can be used both for action planning by the agent (Jayant & Bhatnagar, 2022; Liu et al., 2020) and
047   for training the RL controller in the imagination (Janner et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2023). The use of
048   such a model allows the agent to reduce the execution of potentially dangerous actions during real
049   world exploration. Risks about learning a policy are embedded in the model, while the agent needs
050   to safely explore an environment to learn this world model.
051   In this work, we propose a method ITES (Imagine To Ensure Safety in Hierarchical Reinforcement
052   Learning)1 that uses a high-level policy to simplify the complex optimization task for the RL con-

      2.1   C ONSTRAINED G OAL -C ONDITIONED MDP
094
095   We investigate the mathematical formulation of the Safe Exploration problem introduced in Ray
096   et al. (2019) through the framework of a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP, Altman
097   (1999)), represented as ⟨S, A, p, R, c, d, µ, γ⟩. Both the state space S and the action space A are
098   assumed to be continuous. The environment transition function p(s′ |s, a) specifies the probability
099   density of reaching s′ ∈ S after taking action a ∈ A in state s ∈ S. The initial state distribution
100   µ(s0 ) defines the probability density of beginning an episode at state s0 . It is generally assumed
101
      that the agent does not know the transition dynamics p(s′ |s, a). For each transition ⟨s, a, s′ ⟩, the
      environment produces a scalar external reward r(s, a, s′ ) and another scalar c(s, a) as the cost.
102
      The parameter d ∈ R represents the cost limit, indicating the maximum allowable sum of costs
103
      over an episode. The optimization    problem is to maximize cumulative external rewards from an
104   environment: J(π) = Eπ t γ t r(s, a, s′ ), while satisfying cost constraint: Jc (π) ≤ d.

143   The proposed approach consists of two components: safety of generated subgoals and safety of the
144   RL controller. To generate subgoals within a safe region, we utilize a classifier for dangerous/safe
145
      states in the environment. In order to ensure that the agent adheres to safety constraints while

149
      As a base algorithm for our approach, we adopted HRAC (Zhang et al. (2020a), (Hierarchical Re-
150
      inforcement learning with k-step Adjacency Constraint), which consists of two policies: high-level
151   controller πθhh (sg |s) and low-level controller πθl l (a|s, sg ). The main idea is to generate subgoals
152   that are at a specified distance k from the agent’s current position. For this purpose, an Adjacency
153   Network ψ is employed, which maps each state s to its emedding ψ(s). This network is trained
154   using the following loss function:

160   the label indicating k-step adjacency. This loss function enables the Adjacency Network to predict
161   embeddings for states si and sj that are k steps apart, such that the condition ||ψ(si ) − ψ(sj )|| < ϵ
      is satisfied.

      4.4   I NFLUENCE OF EACH SAFETY LEVEL ON OVERALL SAFETY
438
439   To address the question - ”Can safety be managed solely at one level, such as the low-level con-
440   troller?” - we conducted a comparative study of the ITES algorithm against HRAC-SafeSubgoals
441   (which enforces safety only at the high-level controller) and HRAC-SafeController (which enforces
442   safety solely at the low-level controller) within the SafeAntMazeCshape environment. As depicted
443   in Figure 6, the cost and cost rate metrics indicate that in the SafeAntMazeCshape environment,
444   the high-level controller significantly enhances safety. Nonetheless, we observe that incorporating
445   low-level safety alongside high-level safety yields an overall increase in safety. Additionally, it is
446
      noteworthy that HRAC-SafeSubgoals exhibits greater variance in the cost plot compared to ITES,
      which results from the fact that when the HRAC-SafeSubgoals policy reaches a safe subgoal, it may
447

466   5     R ELATED W ORKS
467
468   Safe reinforcement learning aims to develop algorithms that ensure safety both during the learning
469   process (exploration phase) and the exploitation phase within an environment. A widely adopted
470   approach to guarantee safety in RL tasks involves solving a Constrained Markov Decision Process
471   using the Lagrange Multiplier method (Ray et al., 2019). This approach addresses two key ob-
472   jectives: maximizing cumulative reward while minimizing cumulative cost, effectively balancing
473   performance and safety.
474   While traditional reinforcement learning algorithms such as SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), DDPG
475   (Lillicrap et al., 2016), and Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2023) are primarily engineered to optimize cu-
476   mulative reward, the integration of the Lagrange Multiplier method transforms this paradigm. By
477   converting the dual CMDP objective into a single one, the method introduces a trade-off into the
478   value function space, thus enables balancing between performance and safety constraints. Algo-
479   rithms like SAC-Lagrangian (Ha et al., 2021), SafeSLAC (Hogewind et al., 2023), SafeDreamer
480   (Huang et al., 2024), NeuralConditionedSAC (Huang et al., 2021) embodied the Lagrange approach
481   within their architectures. During updates, the policy takes into account the sum of the values from
482
      the reward and cost critics, there the Lagrange multiplier scales the cost component.
483   Despite of the widespread use of the method and its strong sides, it suffers from instabilities and
484   oscillations during learning due to the non-stationary nature of the dual objectives during policy
485   optimization. The effective solution to this challenge was proposed by Stooke et al. (2020). The
      idea was taken from control theory: it was proposed to use well-known PID (Proportional-Integral-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Derivative) controller to update a Lagrange multiplier, whereas the classical approach uses only the
487   Integral part.
488
489   Another way to mitigate problems of the Lagrangian method, an alternative approach, is to intro-
490
      duce additional layers into the policy architecture, dedicated solely to ensuring safety by correcting
      actions as necessary. Such algorithms are presented by Safe HIRO (Roza et al., 2022), SafetyLayer
491
      (Dalal et al., 2018), SafeEditor (Yu et al., 2022). This decoupling of the safety mechanism from
492
      the reward-cost value function aggregation offers a promising direction to reduce stochasticity while
493   maintaining the robustness and reliability of the agent’s behavior.
494
495   Serious challenge in safe exploration is the unknown dynamics of the environment. Without any
496
      prior information the agent is forced to violate some constraints just to know about them. If an agent
      has been equipped with a world model it can learn safe policy with much less safety violations as
497
      using the world model “imagination” is safe in contrast to interaction with real world. With image
498
      observations this approach is presented by (Huang et al., 2024), the SOTA model-based algorithm
499   adopted PIDLagrangian (Stooke et al., 2020). Also there exist several works (Hogewind et al.,
500   2023; Liu et al., 2020; Jayant & Bhatnagar, 2022). (Huang et al., 2024) used not only images as
501   observations and also vector states.
502
      To improve exploration in RL it is typically used hierarchy approaches, that allows to reduce com-
503
      plexity of the task by decomposing it into subtasks. These approaches involve generating high-level
504
      actions, subgoals, that the policy must achieve within a limited number of low-level actions: HAC
505   (Levy et al., 2019), HiRO (Nachum et al., 2018), HRAC (Zhang et al., 2020a).
506
507   In Safe RL, very few works consider hierarchical approaches. Among them the most closed to us
508
      is SafetyLayer+HiRO (Roza et al., 2022). In contrast to us it solves additional optimization task for
      each action to replace it with a safe one, whereas our algorithm is optimized only during training
509
      phase.
510
511   To the best of our knowledge, our algorithm ITES is the only one that combines model-based and
512   hierarchy approach with safety constrained optimization.
513
514   6   C ONCLUSIONS
515
516   In summary, we investigated the Safe Exploration Problem, where the agent must maximize perfor-
517   mance while minimizing safety violations. We proposed the ITES approach based on the HRAC
518   algorithm, which provides safety at two levels of the hierarchy. Additionally, we explored an al-
519   ternative implementation of safety in HRAC using a Lagrangian multiplier, referred to as HRAC-
520   LAG. Experimental results demonstrated that ITES achieves comparable performance to HRAC-
521   LAG while significantly enhancing safety during training (safe exploration). In the SafetyGym
522   environment, we demonstrated that our algorithm achieves significantly higher performance while
523
      maintaining comparative safety and exhibiting lower dispersion.
524   However, ITES has limitations, including the need to manually design the mapping function ϕ from
525   state space to goal space for each task, which restricts its adaptability, particularly to visual input.
526   Additionally, the simple discretization technique used by HRAC’s Adjacency Network limits its
527   scalability to high-dimensional spaces. Furthermore, ITES does not account for task-specific cost
528   budgets d, as it minimizes cost violations independently at each time step.
529   Future work will focus on addressing these limitations, such as learning goal spaces, improving
530   scalability, and incorporating cost budgets, to enable the application of ITES in real-world robotics
531   tasks.
