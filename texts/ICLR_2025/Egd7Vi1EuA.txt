011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Instruction Fine-Tuning (IFT) has become an essential method for adapting base
014            Large Language Models (LLMs) into variants for professional and private use.
015
               However, researchers have raised concerns over a significant decrease in LLMs’
               security following IFT, even when the IFT process involves entirely benign in-
016
               structions (termed Benign IFT). Our study represents a pioneering effort to miti-
017
               gate the security risks arising from Benign IFT. Specifically, we conduct a Mod-
018            ule Robustness Analysis, aiming to investigate how LLMs’ internal modules con-
019            tribute to their security. Based on our analysis, we propose a novel IFT strategy,
020            called the Modular Layer-wise Learning Rate (ML-LR) strategy. In our analysis,
021            we implement a simple security feature classifier that serves as a proxy to mea-
022            sure the robustness of modules (e.g. Q/K/V , etc.). Our findings reveal that the
023            module robustness shows clear patterns, varying regularly with the module type
024            and the layer depth. Leveraging these insights, we develop a proxy-guided search
025            algorithm to identify a robust subset of modules, termed ModsRobust . During IFT,
026
               the ML-LR strategy employs differentiated learning rates for ModsRobust and the
               rest modules. Our experimental results show that in security assessments, the ap-
027
               plication of our ML-LR strategy significantly mitigates the rise in harmfulness of
028
               LLMs following Benign IFT. Notably, our ML-LR strategy has little impact on
029            the usability or expertise of LLMs following Benign IFT. Furthermore, we have
030            conducted comprehensive analyses to verify the soundness and flexibility of our
031            ML-LR strategy. Warning: Many examples in this paper are generated by LLMs,
032            which readers may find offensive.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   More and more studies focus on enhancing the specific-domain capabilities of Large Language Mod-
038   els (LLMs) through Instruction Fine-Tuning (IFT), such as improving their skills in coding, math
039
      reasoning, and medicine knowledge (Mitra et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023a). Some
      leading research institutions such as Meta and OpenAI have officially provided IFT guidelines to
040
      encourage the customization of LLMs. However, recent studies (Qi et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024)
041
      indicate that IFT can easily compromise LLMs’ security, even though only benign instructions are
042   used during training (termed Benign IFT). Current work typically focuses on an assumption scene
043   where attack data built on malicious instructions are mixed into training data (Rosati et al., 2024;
044   Huang et al., 2024b). Yet in real-world applications, users will not intentionally add any attack
045   data and exclude any malicious instructions as much as possible, ensuring that only benign instruc-
046   tions are used during training. Therefore, a significant challenge remains: how can we effectively
047   mitigate the security risks arising from Benign IFT?
048   Fig. 1 provides a case illustrating that although IFT has improved the mathematical capabilities of
049   LLMs, it introduces security risks. Initially, security-aligned LLMs can reliably reject malicious
050   instructions. However, LLMs following Benign IFT will affirmatively respond to malicious instruc-
051   tions, leading to harmful content. As we know, the primary factors influencing IFT performance are
052   the training data and the internal parameters of LLMs. For the former, Benign IFT can ensure the
053   harmlessness of training data, representing significant efforts at the data level. For the latter, some
      studies (Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) have indicated that parameters within specific regions

070
      To analyze the impact of modules, a straightforward idea is to perturb modules within specific re-
071
      gions and observe changes in LLMs’ responses to malicious instructions. However, such perturba-
072   tions often compromise the linguistic capabilities of LLMs, leading to nonsensical outputs, such as
073   gibberish or blank spaces, which brings challenges to our analysis. Recent studies (Du et al., 2023b;
074   Zhou et al., 2024b) indicate that the last hidden representations of LLMs have exhibited significant
075   security classification features between some benign and malicious instructions. Inspired by this
076   property, we train a simple security feature classifier as a proxy that reflects LLMs’ security. Based
077   on such proxy, we conduct a Module Robustness Analysis, aiming to investigate how LLMs’ inter-
078   nal modules contribute to their security. Our analysis indicates that the module robustness shows
079   clear patterns: 1) Modules located in shallow layers are more sensitive to perturbations, while those
080
      in deeper layers exhibit greater robustness. 2) The Q and K modules are relatively more sensitive
      compared to other modules. 3) Combining two robust sets of modules can result in a configuration
081
      that becomes sensitive, suggesting that the security of LLMs depends on the collaborative effect of
082
      modules.
083
084   Leveraging these findings, we develop a proxy-guided search algorithm to identify a robust subset
085   of modules, termed ModsRobust . This algorithm draws on observed patterns as heuristics and guides
086
      the depth and breadth of the search based on feedback from proxy performance. To mitigate the se-
      curity risks arising from the Benign IFT, we propose a Modular Layer-wise Learning Rate (ML-LR)
087
      strategy. The idea is to allow the robust set of modules to undergo larger parameter changes during
088
      training while constraining the changes in the rest modules. Therefore, our ML-LR strategy employs
089   differentiated learning rates for ModsRobust and the rest modules, setting a standard learning rate for
090   the former and a relatively smaller learning rate for the latter. In our experiments, we implemented
091   two experiment scenarios:
092
      • General-Domain Scenario: Consistent with prior work (Qi et al., 2023), for simulating the Be-
093
        nign IFT process, this scenario utilizes general-domain benign IFT data to fine-tune LLMs. The
094
        results indicate that our strategy successfully reduces the harmfulness score of responses by an
095     average of 1.45 points on a 5-point scale and the attack success rate by an average of 37.91%
096     while maintaining LLMs’ usability on par with standard IFT.
097   • Specific-Domain Scenario: This scenario focuses on enhancing the mathematical capabilities of
098     LLMs, aligning closely with real-application objectives. The results indicate that our strategy
099     can reduce the harmfulness score by an average of 0.40 points and the attack success rate by an
100     average of 11.48% while maintaining LLMs’ expertise on par with standard IFT.
101   Moreover, our study guides numerous analysis experiments to further verify the soundness and
102   flexibility of our ML-LR strategy.

      3.2   M ODULE ROBUSTNESS A NALYSIS
192
      The purpose of our module robustness analysis is to investigate how LLMs’ internal modules con-
193   tribute to their security. We utilize the security feature classifier mentioned in Sec. 3.1 as a proxy
194   to reflect LLMs’ security. Introducing perturbation to modules across different regions will accord-
195   ingly alter the hidden representations of Xtest . As shown in Step 3 of Fig. 2, by observing changes
196   in the proxy’s performance on the altered (Xtest ) representations, we measure the robustness of
197   modules within specific regions. A smaller change indicates that modules within specific regions
198   are more robust and do not significantly affect the LLMs’ security. Conversely, a larger change
199   suggests higher sensitivity, which more readily affects the LLMs’ security. The calculation of the
200   change is as follows:
201                     δ = AccClassif ier (fbase (Xtext )) − AccClassif ier (fperturbed (Xtext ))          (2)

245   3.3   ML-LR S TRATEGY
246
247
      The above analysis indicates that the robustness of modules in various regions has exhibited notable
      differences. To mitigate the security risks arising from Benign IFT, a simple idea is to allow modules
248
      identified as robust to undergo larger parameter changes during IFT while constraining changes in
249
      other modules. Inspired by this, we propose a novel strategy, termed ML-LR, which employs differ-
250   entiated learning rates. However, considering that the security of LLMs depends on the collaborative
251   effect of modules (PATTERN C), a fundamental problem remains: how to identify a robust subset
252   of modules? To address this, we develop a proxy-guided search algorithm to identify such a subset,
253   which we refer to as ModsRobust . This algorithm leverages observed patterns as heuristics and uti-
254   lizes feedback from proxy performance to guide the depth and breadth of the search. Specifically,
255   considering PATTERN A, the algorithm performs a depth search from deeper to shallower layer and
256   we restrict the search to the last half layers of LLMs. Considering PATTERN B, which provides a
257   rough ranking of module robustness across different types, the algorithm performs a breadth search
258
      referring to such ranking. The adjustment in the search direction (either forward or backward) will
      be made based on the change observed in the proxy performance. The detailed steps of this search
259
      algorithm process are outlined in the provided pseudo-code (Alg. 1 in Appendix). The final goal of
260
      the search is to identify a subset of modules that, even when subjected to our specified perturbations,
261   will not affect the proxy’s performance on Xtest representations.
262
263   Overall, as shown in Step 4 of Fig. 2, we first identify the ModsRobust using the search algorithm.
264
      Subsequently, during the Benign IFT, we implement our ML-LR strategy, which employs a standard
      learning rate for the ModsRobust and a relatively smaller learning rate for the rest.

      4.4   T RAINING S ETTINGS
352
353   For training settings, we adopt the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) for fine-tuning
354   LLMs. In the LoRA framework, only low-rank decomposition matrices added to targeted weight
355   matrices are updated. In our main experiment, we specify the Q/K/V /O modules as targeted
356   weights, which is a common LoRA setting. Experiments that extend LoRA to all modules can
357   be found in Sec. 6. For LoRA parameter settings, we set the values of r and α to 8 and 16 respec-

766    1: user specif ic ← [’Q’, ’K’, ’V’, ’O’]
767    2: our ranked ← [’Q’, ’K’, ’O’, ’V’, ’Down’, ’Gate’, ’Up’]
       3: our searched ← [Mod for Mod in our ranked if Mod in user specific]
768
       4: num layers ← the number of LLM’s layers
769    5: dLLM ← the dimension of LLM
770    6: acc base ← AccClassif ier (fbase (Xtext ))
771    7: threshold ← (acc base − 0.5%)
772    8: our searched ind ← [num layers] × len(our searched)
       9: for index ← 0 to len(our searched ind) − 1 do
773
      10:     acc ← acc base
774   11:     while acc ≥ threshold & our searched ind[index] >= num layers/2 do
775   12:          our searched ind[index] ← our searched ind[index] − 4
776   13:          acc pertub ← []
777   14:          for of f set ← 0 to 3 do
      15:               llm tmp ← deepcopy of llm base
778
      16:               for index pertub ← 0 to len(our searched ind) − 1 do
779   17:                   for ind ← our searched ind[index pertub] to num layers − 1 do
780   18:                       llm tmp ← perturb weight(llm tmp, our searched[index pertub], ind, of f set)
781   19:                   end for
782   20:               end for
      21:               acc pertub.append(calculate(llm tmp))
783
      22:          end for
784   23:          acc ← Min(acc pertub)
785   24:     end while
786   25:     our searched ind[index] ← our searched ind[index] + 4
787   26: end for
