                                                  A BSTRACT
012
               Mitigating hallucinations of Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) is crucial to
013
               enhance their reliability for general-purpose assistants. This paper shows that such
014
               hallucinations of LVLMs can be significantly exacerbated by preceding user-system
015            dialogues. To precisely measure this, we first present an evaluation benchmark by
016            extending popular multi-modal benchmark datasets with prepended hallucinatory
017            dialogues powered by our novel Adversarial Question Generator (AQG), which
018            can automatically generate image-related yet adversarial dialogues by adopting
019            adversarial attacks on LVLMs. On our benchmark, the zero-shot performance of
020            state-of-the-art LVLMs drops significantly for both the VQA and Captioning tasks.
               Next, we further reveal this hallucination is mainly due to the prediction bias toward
021
               preceding dialogues rather than visual content. To reduce this bias, we propose
022
               Adversarial Instruction Tuning (AIT) that robustly fine-tunes LVLMs against
023            hallucinatory dialogues. Extensive experiments show our proposed approach
024            successfully reduces dialogue hallucination while maintaining performance.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Developing a general-purpose assistant that interacts with humans through channels such as vision
030
      and language is one of the important problems in artificial intelligence. Inspired by the remarkable
      success of Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), the community
031
      has paid growing interest in developing multi-modal assistants, so-called Large Vision Language
032   Models (LVLMs), that align vision foundation models (Chen et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021) with
033   LLMs to support visual-language instructions. Many LVLMs including LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c),
034   MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) have shown powerful zero-shot
035   generalization ability in various vision-language tasks such as classification (Pham et al., 2021; Park
036   et al., 2024), detection (Li et al., 2022), visual question answering (VQA) (Song et al., 2022), and
037   Captioning (Xu et al., 2023).
038   Despite their great success, several studies have revealed that LVLMs are prone to hallucination
039   issues (Ji et al., 2023; Bang et al., 2023). While most studies focus on object hallucinations (Li
040   et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023a), where LVLMs often answer inconsistently with contents of objects
041   in a given image, the effect of user-system dialogues on hallucination has received little attention.
042   Surprisingly, we found that such hallucinations can be significantly exacerbated by preceding user-
043
      system dialogues. For example, as shown in Figure 1(a), certain contents in preceding dialogues
      (“eco-friendly”) conflicting with the current question can distract LVLMs, resulting in incorrect
044
      answers (“Wood”). This problem, which we call dialogue hallucination, is crucial in practice because
045   a user usually interacts with the system through multi-round chats so that the user can unintentionally
046   attack LVLMs in early chats and get unfaithful answers in later chats.
047
      In this paper, we first present an evaluation benchmark, EvalDial, to more precisely measure the
048
      dialogue hallucination of LVLMs. Our benchmark is constructed on popular vision-language bench-
049   mark datasets for VQA and Captioning tasks (Xu et al., 2023). Specifically, for each test example in
050   each dataset, we create corresponding hallucinatory dialogues that can be prepended to the original
051   test question. Moreover, to mimic actual user behaviors interacting with the assistant within visual
052   contexts of a given image, we further introduce Adversarial Question Generator (AQG), which auto-
053   matically generates image-related yet adversarial dialogues, by steadily incorporating an extra LVLM
      into the black-box optimization of adversarial attack (Ilyas et al., 2018; Maus et al., 2023). With

078

079   Our main contributions can be summarized as:
080   • We find that LVLMs are prone to hallucination by preceding dialogues.
081   • We present an evaluation benchmark (EvalDial) for dialogue hallucination with a novel adversarial
082     question generator (AQG).
083   • We reveal LVLM’s prediction bias toward hallucinatory dialogues by input token attention analysis.
084   • We propose AIT with masked instruction tuning that successfully reduces the dialogue hallucination
085     on many vision-language datasets.

      2.1    I NSTRUCTION - FOLLOWING LVLM S
089
090   Instruction-tuning LLMs such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020) have significantly enhanced their zero-shot
091   generalization ability in various NLP tasks (Wang et al., 2022), resulting in instruction-following
092   LLMs such as ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022). Recently, this instruction-tuning idea has been
093   actively extended to vision-language domains, and many instruction-following LVLMs have been
      developed (Bai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Zhu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
094
      Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023). In general, most LVLMs combine pre-trained vision encoders
095
      (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) with LLMs by fine-tuning them on visual-language instruction
096   datasets (Zhang et al., 2023; Koh et al., 2023). Notably, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c) projects CLIP to
097   LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), and then fine-tunes the models with a projection layer on a visual
098   instruction dataset (Peng et al., 2023). Similarly, MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) uses BLIP-2 (Li et al.,
099   2023a) as visual encoder and Vicuna as language decoder, and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) uses
100   Q-former as the projection layer. These models have shown a powerful zero-shot performance in
101   various vision-language tasks including VQA and Image Captioning (Xu et al., 2023).
102

130   2.3       A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS ON L ANGUAGE M ODELS
131
      Adversarial attacks aim to ruin output predictions of a model by perturbing the input exam-
132   ples (Chakraborty et al., 2018; Ilyas et al., 2018). For attacking LLMs, AdvPrompt (Maus et al.,
133   2023) finds adversarial prompts to generate nonsensical text by increasing the perplexity of the output
134   tokens. GCG (Zou et al., 2023) obtains adversarial suffix prompts to generate objectionable behavior,
135   such as harmful content. Harmbench (Mantas et al., 2024) proposed a framework for large-scale
136   automated red teaming methods and defence of LLMs. However, since such generated adversarial
137   prompts are incomprehensive to humans, e.g., a sequence of random letters, they are not directly
138
      applicable to generating adversarial dialogues for LVLMs that must be in natural language.

180   3.2   E VAL D IAL : A N E VALUATION B ENCHMARK
181
      We construct EvalDial on top of popular vision-language test datasets; ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022),
182   OKVQA (Marino et al., 2019), GQA (Hudson and Manning, 2019), and IconQA (Lu et al., 2021)
183   datasets for VQA task, and NoCaps (Agrawal et al., 2019), Flickr-30k (Plummer et al., 2015), and
184   WHOOPS (Bitton-Guetta et al., 2023) datasets for Captioning task. For each test example in each
185   dataset, we create three types of dialogue, i.e., General, Random, and Adversarial, that are prepended
186   into the original test question or instruction. Figure 2 illustrates more details of EvalDial.
187   1. General Dialogue contains a general question, that can be universally asked to any image, and its
188      corresponding answer is obtained from LVLM. For example, a general dialogue can be “Q. What
189      is the dominant color in the image? A. It’s blue”. We extract 10 general questions from GPT by
190
         prompting “Generate 10 general questions for a random image”. See Appendix A for details.
191   2. Random Dialogue consists of a pair of random questions, that are completely irrelevant to a
192
         given image, and its corresponding answer obtained from LVLM. For example, given a car image,
         a random dialogue can be “Q. what kind of animals are these? A. there are no animals”. To
193
         generate such questions, we randomly extract questions from the VQA-v2 dataset (Goyal et al.,
194      2017), which does not have an overlapping set of questions with the aforementioned benchmark
195      test datasets.
196   3. Adversarial Dialogue contains an image-related yet adversarial question that causes halluci-
197      nations to the original test question. Because real users often have chats related to the context
198      of the given image, it is essential to verify LVLM’s robustness against the image-related but
199      adversarial dialogue. However, generating such subtle questions is very challenging. Thus, we
200      propose AQG, an adversarial question generator based on black-box adversarial attack tech-
201      niques (Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Maus et al., 2023) as elaborated in Section 3.3. Detailed
202
         generated adversarial questions are in Appendix B.
203   Note that, for all three types of dialogues, EvalDial only contains questions without corresponding
204   answers, since the answers are naturally generated by LVLMs in the test phase. Evaluation results of
      state-of-the-art LVLMs on EvalDial can be found in Section 5.

      4     A DVERSARIAL I NSTRUCTION T UNING
250
      We first provide an input token attention analysis to help understand dialogue hallucination. Based on
251
      this, we present a more robust instruction tuning paradigm, Adversarial Instruction Tuning (AIT).
252
253
      4.1   I NPUT T OKEN ATTENTION A NALYSIS
254
255   Input feature attention analysis is a popular method to investigate the contribution of input features
256   to model prediction, e.g., GradCAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) for vision models, or token attention
257   map (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Kokhlikyan et al., 2020) for language models. Here, we introduce a
258   new attention-based metric for LVLM, coined Dialogue Tokens Attention Ratio (DTAR), that helps
259
      to analyze the dialogue hallucination in instruction-following LVLMs, by calculating the contribution
      of preceding dialogues to output answer prediction.
260
261   Dialogue Tokens Attention Ratio. Let Zv = WProj · fVE (Xv ) be token embeddings of input image
262   Xv , where WProj is a linear projection layer that convert the output patches of visual encoder fVE to
263   input tokens of LLM, and Zdialogue = ftoken (Xdialogue ) be token embeddings of input preceding
264
      dialogue Xdialogue . Also, let P (Xa ) be the probability of output answer tokens. Then, we define
      Dialogue Tokens Attention Ratio (DTAR) using the gradient of input token embeddings Zv and
265
      Zdialogue with respect to the output token probability P (Xa ), as in Definition 4.1.

297   4.2   A DVERSARIAL I NSTRUCTION T UNING (AIT)
298
      To reduce the negative effect of hallucinatory dialogues, we propose AIT to perform instruction tuning
299
      on adversarially augmented visual instruction datasets. AIT first generates hallucinatory dialogues
300   and injects them into visual instruction training datasets, and then performs instruction tuning by
301   masking the hallucinatory dialogues in loss calculation.
302
303
      Hallucinatory Dialogue Generation. We create hallucinatory dialogues following the protocol of
      EvalDial in Section 3.2. Specifically, for each training example of the visual-instruction dataset such
304
      as LLaVA-Instruct-665k (Liu et al., 2023b), we generate hallucinatory questions Xqi,adv in order
305
      to hallucinate each round’s question Xiq in the original training example X<t  dialogue , and generate
306                                 i,adv

341   Datasets. Followed by Section 3.2, we use our proposed benchmark, EvalDial, for evaluating dialogue
342   hallucination. We mainly use EvalDial built on top of OKVQA (Marino et al., 2019), GQA (Hudson
343   and Manning, 2019), and IconQA (Lu et al., 2021) for VQA task, and NoCaps (Agrawal et al., 2019),
344   Flickr-30K (Plummer et al., 2015), and WHOOPS (Bitton-Guetta et al., 2023) for Captioning task.
345   Algorithms. We compare the zero-shot performance of AIT with three recently proposed LVLMs:
346   (1) MiniGPT-4 (7B) (Zhu et al., 2023), (2) InstructBLIP (7B) (Dai et al., 2023), and (3) LLaVA-v1.5
347   (7B) (Liu et al., 2023b).
348   Implementation Details. AIT uses the same model architecture with LLaVA-v1.5. For the hyper-
349   parameters of adversarial instruction tuning, we train AIT for 1 epoch with a batch size of 128,
350   and an initial learning rate of 2e-5 with a cosine scheduler. For hallucinatory dialogue injection,
351   we generate adversarial dialogue examples from LLaVA-Instruct-80k, OKVQA with 9K examples,
352   GQA with 15K examples, IconQA with 29K examples, and 0.5K examples each from NoCaps
353   and Flickr-30K, which are mostly originally included in LLaVA-Instruct-665K. All methods are
354
      implemented with PyTorch 1.8.0 and executed on multiple NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Generating an
      adversarial dialogue for each image-QA example using AQG takes approximately 50 seconds on a
355
      single A100 GPU. By applying quantization, this can be reduced to about 18 seconds. The code is
356   available at https://github.com/dongmean/LVLM_DialHalu.
357
358   Evaluation. For VQA task, we use top-1 accuracy that validates whether the ground-truth answer is
      in the generated sentence. For Image Captioning task, we use CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) score, a
359
      popular metric to evaluate image captioning quality (Xu et al., 2023).

      Effect of Loss Masking on Prepended Adversarial Dialogues. Table 4 shows the effect of the loss
455   masking on prepended adversarial dialogues in masked instruction tuning. Without loss masking,
456   AIT shows unsatisfactory performance in mitigating dialogue hallucination. This is because the
457   fine-tuned LVLM without loss masking is forced to generate the answer even for injected random or
458   adversarial dialogues, which are out-of-context from the given image or harmful in maintaining the
459   context of the given image, resulting in more hallucinations in later rounds of chats.
460   Effect of The Number of Injected Hallucinatory Dialogues. As elaborated in Section 4.2, AIT
461   randomly chooses m rounds for each training example in the visual-instruction dataset to inject
462   hallucinatory dialogues. Here, we investigate the effect of the number m of injected hallucinatory
463   dialogues. We set m to be 1,2 and all from available dialogues per example since most examples
464   contain 4 rounds of dialogues on average. To control the effect of data size in the study, we only use
465
      LLaVA-Instruct-150K for fine-tuning. As in Table 5, with more injected adversarial examples used
      during AIT, the model gets more robust to the adversarial attack. Therefore, the more hallucinatory
466
      dialogues injected into adversarial instruction tuning, the more performance gain we can have.

531   6      C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we find that the existing instruction-following LVLMs are prone to be hallucinated
      by preceding user-system dialogues. To precisely validate this dialogue hallucination, we construct
534
      EvalDial, a large and diverse evaluation benchmark covering popular multi-modal datasets in VQA
535   and captioning tasks, with a novel adversarial dialogue generator AQG. In addition, to mitigate such
536   hallucination, we provide an in-depth analysis to help understand why such hallucination happens
537   with input token attention analysis, and then propose AIT, a robust instruction-tuning method that
538   maintains or even improves the zero-shot VQA and captioning performance of LVLMs in the presence
539   of hallucinatory dialogues. We believe that our work can shed light on many applications requiring
      robust LVLMs such as the Red-teaming of visual-language assistants.
