009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Tree search algorithms such as greedy and beam search are the standard when
012            it comes to finding sequences of maximum likelihood in the decoding processes
013            of large language models (LLMs). However, they are myopic since they do not
014            take the complete root-to-leaf path into account. Moreover, they are agnostic to
015            prior knowledge available about the process: For example, it does not consider that
016            the objective being maximized is a probability and thereby has specific properties
017            like being bound in the unit interval. Taking a probabilistic approach, we define
018            prior beliefs over LLMs’ transition probabilities and obtain posterior beliefs over
019            the most promising paths in each iteration. These beliefs are useful for defining a
020
               sample-based, non-myopic acquisition function that allows for a more data-efficient
               exploration scheme than standard search algorithms on LLMs. Crucially, unlike
021
               expensive simulation-based non-myopic methods like the Monte Carlo tree search,
022
               our method only requires samples from the beliefs. We discuss how to select the
023            prior and the acquisition function, and demonstrate in experiments with various
024            LLMs that our method achieves higher efficiency than recent baselines: Our method
025            achieves the same or a higher likelihood while expanding fewer nodes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The decoding process of an autoregressive large language model (LLM) can be seen as finding
032   an optimal path in a search tree. The number of such paths is exponential, often exceeding the
033
      computational budget required to examine them all. This inevitably leads to computational uncertainty
      (Hennig et al., 2022): an uncertainty that could be fully resolved if enough compute was available
034
      to examine all paths, but in practice is present due to the limited resources. Standard algorithms for
035
      LLM decoding, such as beam search (Koehn et al., 2003), completely ignore this uncertainty.
036
037   We posit that quantifying this uncertainty can be beneficial to ensure better explorations on LLMs’
038   search trees. To this end, we incorporate computational uncertainty into the search process to guide
039
      it in a non-myopic fashion (i.e., accounting for our belief about the values of future nodes) and
      importantly, in a more data-efficient manner, akin to Bayesian optimization methods (BO, Garnett,
040
      2023; Kushner, 1964; Močkus, 1975). BO methods are recognized for their data efficiency, not merely
041
      because they quantify uncertainty, but because they exploit the structural characteristics within that
042   uncertainty. E.g., in continuous optimization problems, prior knowledge, such as the smoothness of a
043   function, is often available through Gaussian processes (Rasmussen & Williams, 2005) or (Bayesian)
044   neural networks (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2017; Kristiadi et al., 2023; 2024).
045
      Unlike standard BO, however, LLM decoding is a structured, discrete optimization problem—its
046
      search space is the set of all root-leaf paths in a tree. Moreover, commonly, the values or rewards
047
      associated with each node are probabilities, bounded between 0 and 1. Hence, uncertainty quantifica-
048   tion here is not as well-studied as in BO. We will, therefore, assume that rewards at a node of the
049   search tree are components of a discrete distribution. The characteristic property we aim to exploit
050   is the concentration strength: whether the Categorical distributions are all highly concentrated at a
051   few realistic options, or if some of them are nearly uniform, making any individual option drastically
052   less likely to be the optimal one. Intuitively, one would expect this to have a strong influence on
053   the number of paths that need to be considered. For instance, when the distribution is concentrated,
      it is less likely under our belief that other paths will overtake later on and one can be more greedy.

083
084   Experiments on real-world text-generation benchmarks with various LLMs suggest that our method,
085   called Uncertainty-guided Likelihood-Tree Search (ULTS), finds sentences with higher rewards
086
      than recent baselines with significantly fewer expensive node expansions. Moreover, ULTS only adds
      a small runtime overhead relative to the forward passes of the LLM. In summary:
087
088     (i) We propose ULTS: a probabilistic decision-making framework on LLM search trees, where a
089         prior belief is put on LLM softmax outputs. We show how to easily sample from the implied
090         posterior over optimal values, and how to use these samples to make decisions when searching
091         the tree.
092    (ii) We demonstrate the efficiency and extensibility of ULTS in decoding recent LLMs.
093   (iii) We open-source an implementation compatible with transformers (Wolf et al., 2020)

098   2.1       D ECODING LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS
099
100   Let A = {a1 , . . . , ab } be a vocabulary—a set of natural-language tokens. A large language model
101   (LLM) is a neural network that utilizes the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), taking a
102   context, an ordered sequence of tokens x 0 = (x01 , . . . , x0k )—each component takesPvalues in the set
                                                                                              b
103
      A—and outputs a distribution over A, i.e. c = (c1 , . . . , cb ) where each ci ≥ 0 and i=1 ci = 1.
104   The de facto way of generating a sequence of tokens given a context via an LLM f is by generating
105   each xi+1 autoregressively given the previous sequence (x      x0 , x1 , . . . , xi ). That is, for each i =
106   1, . . . , d, we pick an xi+1 ∈ A according to the Categorical distribution c i(i+1) := f (x   x0 , x1 , . . . , xi )
107   over the next token predicted by the LLM f given the input (x   x0 , x1 , . . . , xi ). We call this sequence-
      generating process a decoding process.

108
      LLM decoding can be seen as a tree-search problem (Fig. 1). The context x 0 is the root of the tree
109   and at each step i = 1, . . . , d, we are given a choice of b many tokens to pick. This process is done
110   recursively until termination; either when a specified depth d has been reached or when a special
111   token like “<EOS>” is selected. This means, the LLM search tree is an exponentially large tree (w.r.t.
112   the number of tokens b in the vocabulary) with depth d—the number of paths x 0 → xd from the root
113   to leaves is bd . Considering the fact that b ranges from around 32k to 256k (Chowdhery et al., 2023;
114   Radford et al., 2019), generating a sequence of tokens requires search over an intractably large space.
115   To address this problem, one can use a cheaper but heuristic way to explore this tree. The simplest
116   way to do so is by sampling a token from the distribution c at each level of the tree (Holtzman et al.,
117   2019). In some domains like machine translation and summarization, however,               one often wants
                                                                                         Qd
118   to find the sequence that maximizes the total/joint likelihood 1 cx 0 →xd := i=1 c(i−1)i associated
119   with each possible sequence (xx0 , x1 , . . . , xd ) (Wiher et al., 2022). This is an optimization-on-tree
120   problem—heuristic optimization algorithms like greedy and beam search along with their variants
121   (Freitag & Al-Onaizan, 2017; Meister et al., 2021; Vijayakumar et al., 2018, etc.) are the standard.
122   One can also view the decoding process as follows. On each of the tree’s node xi , we can define an
123   optimal value vxi associated with it: If xd is a leaf node, then vxd corresponds to the total likelihood
124   cx 0 →xd from the root until that leaf node. Meanwhile, for inner nodes xi it is defined recursively
125   in a bottom-up fashion as the maximum of the children’s optimal values maxxc ∈children(xi ) vxc .
126   Intuitiveley, vxi tells us “what is the total likelihood we would get if we continue descending the tree
127   through the node xi in an optimal manner”. The optimization-on-tree problem can then be recast as
128   finding a path x0 → xd that corresponds to the optimal value v ∗ of the root. While for all xi , the
129   quantity vxi cannot be feasibly computed, they are useful for our probabilistic treatment of LLM
130
      decoding in Section 3.

      2.2       P ROBABILISTIC DECISION - MAKING ON TREES
134
135   Decision-making under uncertainty requires a belief about the unknown given the current observation.
136
      This belief is then used to make decision, by computing an acquisition function which scores possible
      candidates. The candidate that has the highest acquisition value is then selected and the process is
137
      repeated until termination.
138
139   In the context of tree-structured problem, the approach to perform probabilistic decision-making
140   is to perform probabilistic modeling over the unknown values vxi ’s and use the posterior beliefs
141   to make decision about which path should be followed next. In (Hennig et al., 2010), this kind of
142
      optimization has been done for game (e.g., Go) trees. They assume that the optimal value vxi is
      decomposed into a latent score for the utility of the node if all remaining steps are taken randomly
143
      and additional increment to quantify how much better this score can get when all remaining steps are
144
      taken optimally instead of randomly. Their work relies on Gaussian process with a the Brownian
145   motion kernel as their prior belief and has been extendes to more general directed-acyclic graphs in
146   (Grosse et al., 2021).

156   samples of the posterior beliefs are then used to make decisions about which subtree to expand next
157   and when to stop the search (Section 3.4). Figure 3 gives an overview of our probabilistic model.

      3.3   P OSTERIOR BELIEFS OVER OPTIMAL VALUES OF FRONTIER NODES
240
241   Notice that whenever a new node xi is added to the search tree, the likelihood associated with the
242   path from the root to that node cx 0 →xi is fully observed—its distribution is simply a Dirac delta.
243   This means, the joint distribution becomes p(cx 0 →xi , ∆i ) = δ(cx 0 →xi ) p(∆i ). Therefore, to sample
244   vxi given that we have observed cx 0 →xi —i.e., sampling from the posterior p(vxi | cx 0 →xi )—it is
245   sufficient to sample from p(∆i ) and then simply scale all samples by cx 0 →xi . During the search,
246   these samples are backed up the tree and leveraged to make decisions in exploring the tree. Notice
247
      that these samples are cheap to get, unlike empirical samples based on simulations/rollouts.

316   3.5   R EMARKS
317
318   Practical considerations In order to put a tractable upper bound on the runtime of ULTS, we
319   introduce a hyperparameter kmax on the maximum number of nodes that can be expanded per level,
320
      similar to beam search or top-k sampling. Moreover, we stop the search as soon as a set of kmax
      leaves is attained or the termination probability exceeds below 1 − ε. We shall see in Section 5 that
321
      even under these further constraints, we still obtain good results while being efficient.
322
323   Limitations    There are strong reasons to assume that the iid. assumption might not fully hold.
      Indeed, our modeling assumptions are chosen with practicality in mind. This is akin to how priors


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      in Bayesian neural networks (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020) are chosen, which are usually simply just
325   isotropic Gaussians. Even in standard BO, a generic Gaussian-process prior/kernel is often assumed
326   by default (Balandat et al., 2020). For a further discussion of the prior assumptions, see Appendix A.
327   Moreover, our work does not take the miscalibration of LLM softmax outputs into account. Indeed,
328   it has been shown that higher likelihood is not always correlated with human preferences (Eikema
329   & Aziz, 2020; Holtzman et al., 2019; Stahlberg & Byrne, 2019; Wiher et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
330   2020). However, LLM calibration is orthogonal to the focus of the present paper—we leave this
331   for future work. Recent work from (Yoshida et al., 2023), for example, proposes to use a likelihood
332   conditioned on certain attributes of the sequence and to which ULTS based decoding would directly
333
      be applicable. Nevertheless, ULTS is extensible: One can construct a different prior or use a different
      utility function to address this; see Appendix C.8 for example.

336   4   R ELATED W ORK
337
338   Commonly employed stochastic decoding strategies are nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019)
339   and best-of-k sampling (Stiennon et al., 2020). Their purpose is to make the decoding processes
340   more robust towards potential degeneration in the likelihood learned by the LLM (also see Section
341   3.5). I.e, these strategies address uncertainty about the objective function, whereas we focus on
342
      epistemic uncertainty due to limited compute resources. Another decoding method similar to ULTS is
      Minimum Bayes Risk (MBR) decoding (Eikema & Aziz, 2021; Kumar & Byrne, 2004). This strategy
343
      also relies on expected maximization of a utility function (typically not one that results in likelihood
344
      maximization, even though possible), but it does not employ a probabilistic model to estimate the
345   unknown, but rather samples a set of hypothesis sequences from the LLM directly, which is more
346   expensive. We point out that ULTS can be combined with MBR by using ULTS to generate a set of
347   hypothesis sequences for MBR.
348
      Probabilistic/Bayesian optimization on trees has been proposed for game trees. Hennig et al. (2010)
349
      developed a roll-out based probabilistic tree search algorithm for game trees, e.g. for playing Tic-
350
      Tac-Toe and Go. Crucially, the structure of the game tree is different than the LLM search tree.
351   Moreover, they assumed a Gaussian process prior with the Brownian motion kernel in conjunction
352   with the expectation propagation algorithm (Minka, 2001) to model their beliefs. Grosse et al. (2021)
353   extended their approach for a more general directed acyclic graph structure. In contrast, we focus on
354   the tree implied by the sequential generation process in LLMs with a Dirichlet or empirical prior.
355
      Our method can be seen as utilizing a best-first search strategy, of which the A* algorithm (Hart
356
      et al., 1968) is the most famous. Moreover, an A*-like beam search algorithm, under the name of
357   best-first beam search, has also been proposed for decoding LLMs (Meister et al., 2020). Unlike the
358   non-probabilistic A∗ and best-first beam search, we approach optimization-on-tree problems via the
359   lens of decision-making under uncertainty—putting a prior belief about the unknown, updating it
360   based on the observations, and making decision based on the posterior belief. Furthermore, best-first
361   beam search (Meister et al., 2020) has different goals to ours: it is designed to output the same set of
362   leaves (and thus the optimal likelihood value) as the standard beam search. ULTS, meanwhile, focuses
363   on both performance (i.e., attaining higher likelihood than beam search) and efficiency (reducing the
364   number of costly LLM forward passes).
365   Finally, Monte Carlo tree search, which also utilizes samples to make decisions, has also been
366   proposed for decoding LLMs (DeLorenzo et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2023; Leblond
367   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). Different from our goal, they
368   focused on incorporating external rewards that are only observable at the leaf nodes. For instance,
369   Zhang et al. (2023) defined the reward to be the unit-test results for code-generating LLMs. Moreover,
370   Monte-Carlo tree search does not define a probabilistic model (i.e. a prior and posterior beliefs) over
371
      quantities in the search tree—its decision making is based on the statistics obtained by exploring the
      search tree on the fly. In contrast, ULTS does not require costly gathering of those statistics during
372
      the decoding process; instead, it makes decisions based on precomputed samples from its beliefs.

523    6           C ONCLUSION
524
525
      We have discussed our method, ULTS, a probabilistic decision-making algorithm for the decoding
526
      process of large-language-model (LLM) search trees. Our method quantifies and leverages com-
527
      putational uncertainty over the maximum value of the optimization process. ULTS exploits the
528   structure of the optimization problem—in particular, the concentration strength of the LLM softmax
529   outputs—by putting a prior over the LLM’s softmax outputs and using the implied posterior samples
530   to guide exploration-exploitation in a non-myopic manner. Our work thus opens up opportunities for
531   interesting future work in probabilistic inference for LLMs.
532
      Future work One can study the effect of using more sophisticated priors, e.g. where iid. is not
533
      assumed. One can also consider batched acquisition strategies, similar to batch Bayesian optimization
534   techniques (González et al., 2016; Wu & Frazier, 2016). Moreover, it is also interesting to incorporate
535   the uncertainty over the LLM’s outputs (e.g. in the context of Bayesian LLMs) in order to account
536   for possible miscalibration of the likelihood. Another research direction is to extend the approach
537   to reasoning tasks beyond language tasks, e.g. by including beliefs over external rewards (Zhang
538   et al., 2023), such as ones coming from an RLHF-trained reward model, in addition to the current
539   likelihood rewards. Finally, our work in connecting probabilistic inference with LLMs paves the way
      to perform probabilistic reasoning with the tree of thoughts framework (Yao et al., 2023).

      A.1   P RIOR ASSUMPTIONS
759
760   The symmetry assumption for the Dirichlet prior on the LLM’s softmax outputs likely does not
761   entirely hold—some words occur more often in natural language than others (Kingsley Zipf, 1932).
762   However, ULTS only requires access to the distribution over the maximum of the unexplored part of
763   the search space and not over the arg max. The former is not affected by permutations of the entries
764   in the categorical distributions, which is why we suspect that a symmetric Dirichlet distribution with
765
      sufficiently small concentration parameter is a good proxy. Moreover, the empirical prior can also be
      used to address this limitation, without incurring large overhead.
766
767   We assume the same prior for all sentences in a dataset. It could be, though, that some input prompts
768   are easier to complete than others, and the corresponding LLM’s outputs are therefore generally
769   have less entropy than on other those of other prompts. This could be counteracted by choosing a
770   personalized hyper-parameter α. This would require deriving the prior for multiple possible values of
771
      α, which scales linearly with the number of possible values for α. However, this can be precomputed
      (as in Alg. 1) such that it would not affect the costs during inference.
