009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-separated research has been conducted on two highly correlated tracks: traffic and
012            incidents. Traffic track witnesses complicating deep learning models, e.g., to push the
013            prediction a few percent more accurate, and the incident track only studies the incidents
014            alone, e.g., to infer the incident risk. We, for the first time, spatiotemporally aligned the
015            two tracks in a large-scale region (16,972 traffic nodes) over the whole year of 2023: our
016            XTraffic dataset includes traffic, i.e., time-series indexes on traffic flow, lane occupancy,
017
               and average vehicle speed, and incidents, whose records are spatiotemporally-aligned
               with traffic data, with seven different incident classes. Additionally, each node includes
018
               detailed physical and policy-level meta-attributes of lanes. Our data can revolutionalize
019
               traditional traffic-related tasks towards higher interpretability and practice: instead of
020            traditional prediction or classification tasks, we conduct: (1) post-incident traffic forecasting
021            to quantify the impact of different incidents on traffic indexes; (2) incident classification
022            using traffic indexes to determine the incidents types for precautions measures; (3) global
023            causal analysis among the traffic indexes, meta-attributes, and incidents to give high-level
024            guidance of the interrelations of various factors; (4) local causal analysis within road nodes
025            to examine how different incidents affect the road segments’ relations. The dataset is
026            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/XTraffic-E069.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In today’s era of deep learning, a technological foundation has been laid for intelligent transportation systems
031   (Yu et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022a). Primarily, conducting myriad traffic analysis relies
032   on two types of data: traffic and incident data. Traffic data encompasses the traffic state-related time-series,
033
      e.g., volume, speed, and occupancy rate on the road network over time. This continuous stream of data is
      essential for forecasting the future volume, understanding peak usage times, and optimizing traffic signals
034
      and routes (Guo et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022). Real-time traffic data allows for dynamic adjustments to be
035
      made, enhancing the efficiency of traffic flow and reducing overall travel times. On the other hand, incident
036   data includes information about traffic accidents, road closures, and unexpected events that can significantly
037   affect traffic flow. This data helps in understanding the impact of such incidents on traffic congestion and
038   travel time, facilitating more accurate predictions and enabling timely responses from traffic management
039   systems (Li et al., 2018a; Lin & Li, 2020). By analyzing patterns and frequencies of incidents, predictive
040   models can also be developed to foresee potential hotspots and prevent future occurrences.
041
      However, current research has been conducting the two tracks of traffic and incident separately, ignoring
042   the inseparable relation of traffic and incident. For example, abundant works (Shao et al., 2022; Lan et al.,
043   2022; Fang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021) have been using various traffic-only datasets such as PEMS (Song
044   et al., 2020), META-LA (Song et al., 2020), LargeST (Liu et al., 2024) for traffic forecasting. They achieved
045   relatively high accuracy because, under normal circumstances, traffic flow generally follows a strong regular
046   temporal pattern. However, they ignore that unexpected incidents will cause abnormal and irregular patterns

      Contributions. To address the research gaps, we introduce the XTraffic dataset. This dataset not only includes
      three distinct types of traffic time series data for the entire year of 2023 (in Fig. 1(a)), but also encompasses
078
      comprehensive incident data (in Fig. 1(b)) and meta-features of roads closely related to traffic flow. The
079
      contributions of this dataset can be summarized as follows: (1) We provide a comprehensive collection of
080   multi-type incident records with 476,766 samples, enabling the training and evaluation of traffic forecasting
081   models across various scenarios/incidents. This also supports tasks such as incident discovery and traffic
082   anomaly detection by providing ground truth data. (2) We offer a rich collection of physical and policy-level
083   road meta-features. These features are instrumental for causal analysis of traffic and support the increasingly
084   popular field of interpretable deep learning models. By incorporating these detailed attributes, researchers
085   can delve deeper into the underlying mechanisms that influence traffic behaviors and model predictions. As
086   shown in Fig. 1(c), our XTraffic helps not only Incident → Traffic: e.g., to analyze how incidents affect the
087   traffic states (with our post-incident traffic forecasting in Sec. 4.2) and traffic node relations (local causal
088
      analysis in Sec. 4.5), but also Traffic → Incident: e.g., to identify the incidents (incident classification in
      Sec. 4.3) and explain the incident with other factors from the system (global causal analysis in Sec. 4.4).
089
090   To our knowledge, our XTraffic is the most recent in terms of the collection period and contains the largest
091   number of sensors, covering three distinct types of traffic volume. This ensures the timeliness of traffic
092   research, providing a robust foundation for studies aiming to capture and explain traffic dynamics, causation,
093   and interrelations. XTraffic serves as a rigid testing bed and empirical support to justify model effectiveness
      and interoperability in deep learning and traffic community.

      2.1   R ELATED W ORK OF T RAFFIC AND I NCIDENT DATASETS
097   Traffic Dataset. Traffic dataset are commonly used in traffic analysis and forecasting as experimental
098   benchmarks. We introduce the existing four public datasets widely leveraged in traffic forecasting experiments.
099   The PeMSD7(M) and PeMSD7(L) are proposed by (Yu et al., 2018). METR-LA and PEMS-BAY(Li et al.,
100   2018b) covered similar regions in California with multiple traffic. However, these datasets are limited to one
101
      collection region, our XTraffic instead covers the majority of Metropolitan regions of California including
      greater San Francisco, San Jose, and Greater Los Angeles. The PEMS03,04,07 and 08 are proposed by
102
      (Song et al., 2020). This dataset encompasses four different regions, with data collected using the same rules,
103
      supporting multi-region research for traffic analysis and forecasting. Compared to previous datasets, LargeST
104   (Liu et al., 2024) extends both temporally and spatially and includes some basic meta-features. However,
105   these datasets still lack important features such as incidents that significantly impact traffic, as well as road
106   meta-features related to physics and policy. Our XTraffic is the first dataset as such that spatiotemporally align
107   traffic dynamics and traffic incidents, enabling uncomparable potentials for explainable and interpretable
108   traffic management tasks.
109
      Incident Dataset. Incident datasets support the traffic analysis, like the incident impact on traffic, and incident
110   detection. (Huang et al., 2023) proposes a dataset that includes accident data with accident relative features,
111   like accident reason. (Yeddula et al., 2023) also leverages a large accident dataset including various types for
112   accident hotspot prediction. However, limited to the absence of traffic time series, it is hard to do a deeper
113   impact analysis of accidents on traffic based on these datasets. (Lin & Li, 2020) leverages a dataset that
114   includes 13,338 accident records with the traffic flow. However, the dataset is small and non-public. (Zhu
115   et al., 2021) proposes a new accident prediction model based on a dataset with accidents and traffic flow.
116   However, the dataset is not public, either.
117   2.2   T RAFFIC AND I NCIDENT A NALYSIS
118
      Traffic Forecasting with Incidents Considered. A large number of works, e.g., STGCN (Yu et al., 2018),
119   STGODE (Choi et al., 2022), DSTAGNN(Lan et al., 2022), are proposed to improve the prediction accuracy
120   based on GNN (Wu et al., 2020) and RNN (Ramakrishnan & Soni, 2018) models. However, these works
121   only consider historical traffic for future traffic, yet other critical impacts, e.g., incidents and meta-features
122   are ignored ((Yuan & Li, 2021; Jiang & Luo, 2022; Tan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) offer detailed reviews
123   in traffic forecasting). There are a few works that have considered incidents when predicting, whose main
124   design is incorporating incident-related embedding as auxiliary information into traditional spatiotemporal
125   prediction framework (Xie et al., 2020; Golze et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Hong et al., 2024). For example,
126   DIGC-Net (Xie et al., 2020) inputs the type and duration of the incident to predict the affected speed. Yet, the
127
      dataset only brings one week of incident data (17-24 Apr 2019) from a small district, being spatiotemporal
      limited; STCL (Liu et al., 2022b) introduced two-month New York City Vehicle incident data as one-hot
128
      accident embedding into the prediction of the Taxi and Bike data. Like what we have observed in most works
129
      that analyze traffic with incidents (Liu et al., 2022b; Hong et al., 2024), the transport modes of traffic
130   data and that of incident data are NOT seamlessly matched; thus, it will be less convincing to analyze
131   vehicle incidents’ impact on bike traffic (bike lane is separated from vehicle lane) or on taxi traffic (taxi is
132   only a subset mode of the whole vehicle). Our XTraffic is the first and only dataset that is (1) spatially and
133   temporally large-scale, (2) that modes in traffic and incident are seamlessly matched (all vehicles), which
134   guarantees unbiased analysis between the traffic and incident.
135
      Incident Classification. It is a crucial task in analyzing non-recurrent congestion (Li et al., 2018a). Recently,
136   several studies have utilized traffic flow data for incident classification (Lin & Li, 2020; Zhu et al., 2021).
137   However, these studies often face at least one of the following three challenges: (1) Small Data Size (Lin
138   & Li, 2020): Many studies suffer from limited datasets that fail to capture the diversity and complexity of
139   traffic patterns, affecting model reliability and generalizability. (2) Limited Dimensionality without Traffic
140   Volume Data (Huang et al., 2023; Yeddula et al., 2023): The absence of critical data dimensions, such as

                                                          3

141
      traffic volume, restricts the depth and accuracy of incident classification models. (3) Experiments Based
142
      on Non-Public Dataset (Zhu et al., 2021; Lin & Li, 2020): Reliance on proprietary datasets impedes the
143   ability of the broader research community to verify, replicate, or enhance the findings, limiting collaborative
144   advancements.
145
146
      Traffic Causal Analysis. This task aims to learn the causal structures among different entities in a traffic
      system. Usually, the causal structures are formulated as Bayesian networks or DAGs (Zheng et al., 2018),
147
      where a directed edge denotes the causal link. In the traffic domain, given the traffic indexes are time-series
148
      and others can be scalers (e.g., static meta-features), a special DAG structure learning based on heterogeneous
149   data is needed (Lan et al., 2023; 2024). To learn the global relation of various factors, e.g., traffic flow,
150   meta-attributes, weather, etc., where each DAG node is a factor to be considered, MultiFun-DAG (Lan et al.,
151   2024) views multivariate time-series in traffic as a multi-function and formulate the structure learning as a
152   “self-expression problem”, i.e., X = WX + Z, based on function-to-function regression and the directed
153   acyclic regularization on the coefficients W (Zheng et al., 2018), a DAG is constructed based on W. MM-
154   DAG (Lan et al., 2023) further consider multi-location at the same time. To learn local causal relation
155   among different locations, where each DAG node is a spacial location, DBGCN (Luan et al., 2022) and
156   DCGCN (Lin et al., 2023) combines DAG with GCN, DCGCN further considers the causal links across the
157
      time, i.e., node 1 at t1 affects the node 2 at t3 , and the dynamics of DAG changing over time. The preliminary
      of these four intended tasks are introduced in Appendix A.4.

196   integrating detailed traffic flow metrics (such as lane-level flow, speed, and occupancy) with comprehensive
197   incident records (including various types of accidents). From Traffic View. As shown in Table 1, the existing
198   datasets often lack incident data and have insufficient road-level granularity to effectively study the impact of
199   incidents on traffic. For example, the effect of a traffic incident on one side of the road might be significantly
200   different from the impact on the other side. XTraffic offers lane-level traffic flow, speed, and occupancy data
201   with incident features, allowing for detailed analysis of traffic patterns under incident impact at a micro level.
202   Also, the high resolution supports the identification of specific traffic bottlenecks, congestion patterns, and
203   variations in traffic behavior that aggregate or road-level datasets might obscure. Researchers can perform

      fine-grained analysis to understand traffic dynamics under different incident impacts with greater precision.

      4.4     G LOBAL T RAFFIC C AUSAL A NALYSIS
406
407   Experiment Setting. In our XTraffic dataset, we have static variables, e.g., road information, represented
408   as scalar and vector, and dynamic variables, e.g., accidents and traffic flow, represented as functional data.
409   Considering the multimodal nature of the variables, we employ MM-DAG (Lan et al., 2023) to construct the
410   causal network in different districts. We collect data on 17 variables across four districts with the highest
411   incident rates throughout 2023. These variables fall into three categories: (1) meta-feature variables, e.g.,
412   temporal, environmental, road structural information; (2) incident variables, i.e., the occurrence of incidents;
413   and (3) traffic statistics, which reflect traffic conditions. (Details in Table 7, Appx. A.10). We consider the
414
      data collected at each road node for each day as a single sample, using a granularity of one hour.
415
      Results. Fig. 5(a) illustrates the four causal networks derived by MM-DAG. (1) Certain static variables are
      essential; such as road surface, terrain, and road width, they exert significant influences on the incidence of
416
      traffic events and the overall traffic conditions. (2) The static variables’ impacts are consistent: Across
417
      various districts, due to their inherent properties, these underlying attributes consistently influence road and
418   traffic dynamics across different regions. (3) Dynamic variables like time, weather, and visibility also
419   affect traffic incidents, though their causal relationships appear to be weaker and vary by district. Like,
420   causal links from events to fire and to hazards show variability and weaker connections in different districts.
421   We further explain four significant edges. In Fig. 5(b1), the probability of encountering hazards is higher
422   during the early morning (5 AM to 8 AM) and late afternoon (after 3 PM) on weekdays compared to weekends,

                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


423
      likely due to increased traffic flow during peak commuting hours. In Fig. 5(b2), rain increases the probability
424
      of accidents compared to dry conditions, but the amount of rainfall does not significantly affect the accident
425   rate. In Fig. 5(b3), bridges and road surfaces with a base thickness of >7 inches have a lower probability of
426   hazards compared to concrete surfaces. This may be due to the enhanced durability and grip provided by
427   thicker road surfaces and bridge constructions. In Fig. 5(b4), flat terrain is associated with higher average
428   speeds compared to rolling terrains. The tendency for higher speeds on flat terrains is likely due to the reduced
429   need for vehicles to decelerate for climbs or curves, allowing for more consistent and faster travel.

895   Traffic Forecasting is to predict traffic indexes at nodes within a road network based on historical data
896   collected by sensors at each node. Consider a traffic road network represented as a graph G = (V, E),
897   where V denotes the set of N traffic nodes, with |V | = N , and E represents the set of undirected edges.
898   An edge Eij = 1 indicates a physical connection between nodes i and j in the road network; otherwise,
899   Eij = 0. Traffic volume data is recorded by sensors in evenly spaced time intervals and can be represented as
900   a sequence of matrices (X1 , X2 , ..., XT ) ∈ RN ×T , where Xt is the matrix of volume signals (xt1 , xt2 , ..., xtN )
901   at time slot t for all N nodes.
902   The goal in traffic flow forecasting is to devise a function F1 that uses the observed traffic data from

      Causal Analysis and Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) In causal analysis, the primary objective is to elucidate
917   the causal relationship, which is represented in a dynamic acyclic graph (DAG). Within a DAG, each node
918   corresponds to a variable, and each directed edge delineates a causal relationship between two variables. The
919   causal structural model enables the representation of a node’s distribution wi through wi = fi (wpai , ei ),
920   where wpai denotes the set of all parents of node wi , and ei represents the exogenous noise associated with
921   node wi . We consider two subtasks:
922   (1) Global Causal Analysis for The Whole System. In global causal analysis, we focus on the problem that
923   macro-level phenomena influence each other, such as the impact of weather on accident rates. Therefore,
924   each node within the graph represents distinct variables like weather conditions, traffic accidents, or overall
925
      traffic statistics. This approach helps in understanding the broader implications of various environmental and
      systematic factors on traffic dynamics.
926
      (2) Local Causal Analysis for Road Relations. In local causal analysis, we focus on the temporal dependency
927
      structure underlying the complex traffic road network. We aim to find a graph G where the nodes are the
928   variables representing traffic nodes at different lag-times and the links represent lagged or contemporaneous
929   causal dependencies between traffic nodes. This approach helps in understanding how topologic of the road
930   network affects traffic conditions.

1110   In local causal analysis, We employ the PCMCI+ algorithm to discover the causal relations in traffic data,
1111   which utilizes momentary conditional independence (MCI) test to determine the existence of causal links.
1112   Typically, the lagged and contemporaneous causal relations are displayed in a dynamic Bayesian network
1113   (DBN) as shown in Fig. 8 (a). In this work, to simplify the visualization, we choose to use the process graph
1114
       as shown in Fig. 8 (b) to aggregate the information in the DBN. In both DBN and process graph, the link
       color denotes the magnitude of the causal effect measured by the MCI test statistic (e.g., the partial correlation
1115
       coefficient). The label of a link lists all significant lags of cross-dependencies in process graph. Since we are
1116
       more interested in the causal links between different traffic nodes, the links denoting auto-dependencies in
1117   DBN are summarized into node colors in process graph and the auto-dependency lags are omitted.
1118
1119   The choice of causal structure learning method influences the results of local causal analysis. Ideally, we
1120
       would like to perform analysis on real cases or datasets with known underlying ground truth of causal
       dependencies. However, such cases or datasets are rare especially in complex dynamic scenarios such as
1121
       traffic. To enhance the credibility of the learned causal structure, we use different causal discovery methods
1122
       and verify the consistency of the results obtained by the different methods. Fig. 9 shows the pre-incident
1123   causal graphs of case I learned by score-based method DyNotears (Pamfil et al., 2020) and constrained-based
1124   method PCMCI+ (Runge, 2020). The graph structures learned by both methods are similar, but the time lag
1125   of the link X 3 → X 4 is different, which is greatly influenced by the sampling frequency of traffic data. Due
1126   to the limited number of samples affected by the incidents, we use PCMCI+ to discover post-incident causal
1127   structure for its robustness with small sample size and high dimensionality.
