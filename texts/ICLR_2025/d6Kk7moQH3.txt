009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Data-driven deformable image registration methods predominantly rely on opera-
012            tions that process grid-like inputs. However, applying deformable transformations
013            to an image results in a warped space that deviates from a rigid grid structure.
014            Consequently, data-driven approaches with sequential deformations have to apply
015            grid resampling operations between each deformation step. While artifacts caused
016            by resampling are negligible in high-resolution images, the resampling of sparse,
017            high-dimensional feature grids introduces errors that affect the deformation model-
018
               ing process. Taking inspiration from Lagrangian reference frames of deformation
               fields, our work introduces a novel paradigm for data-driven deformable image
019
               registration that utilizes geometric deep-learning principles to model deforma-
020
               tions without grid requirements. Specifically, we model image features as a set of
021            nodes that freely move in Euclidean space, update their coordinates under graph
022            operations, and dynamically readjust their local neighborhoods. We employ this
023            formulation to construct a multi-resolution deformable registration model, where
024            deformation layers iteratively refine the overall transformation at each resolution
025            without intermediate resampling operations on the feature grids. We investigate our
026            method’s ability to fully deformably capture large deformations across a number of
027            medical imaging registration tasks. In particular, we apply our approach (GeoReg)
028            to the registration of inter-subject brain MR images and inhale-exhale lung CT
029
               images, showing on par performance with the current state-of-the-art methods. We
               believe our contribution open up avenues of research to reduce the black-box nature
030
               of current learned registration paradigms by explicitly modeling the transformation
031
               within the architecture.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Image registration is an indispensable tool in medical image analysis that aligns anatomically
037   or functionally corresponding regions across images, often from different modalities and time
038   points (Sotiras et al., 2013). In particular, deformable registration aims to estimate a non-linear
039   transformation that maps the source image to the coordinate space of the target image. Since the
040   advent of deep learning, data-driven methods have been proposed (Haskins et al., 2020; Xiao et al.,
041   2021) to leverage learned transformation priors over an image cohort, reducing the search space of
042   plausible transformations.
043   Single-stream approaches. Since images are usually represented as grids of pixels, data-driven
044   approaches typically employ convolutional kernels to model transformations. Early works have
045   commonly adopted a simplistic approach of concatenating the source and target images as input
046   channels to a convolutional network (Balakrishnan et al., 2019; Dalca et al., 2018; Mok & Chung,
047   2020a; Qiu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2019). However, this concatenation produces different inputs
048   to a network on every possible misalignment of the counterpart image. This subsequently causes
049   the feature extraction to learn distinct representations throughout the network on each possible
050
      misalignment, increasing the task’s complexity.
051   A common necessary preprocessing technique employed to mitigate this issue involves an exhaustive
052   search for an initial affine alignment. This reduces the degrees of freedom in the transformation param-
053   eters by guaranteeing that similar features are captured in a consistent spatial context, thus reducing
      the range of representations experienced by the network. Recent works combat the misalignment-

086   et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022). This approach utilizes two separate encoders that individually extract

089

090   architectural prior towards capturing large transformations in a parameter-efficient manner.
091
092   Despite their effectiveness, the layers of these architectures remain strictly confined to grid-structured
093
      features due to their dependence on discrete kernel operations. Consequently, as warping operations
      are applied to the feature grids in the decoding process, the space needs to be resampled to adhere
094
      to the fixed grid positions (Figure 1). Although negligible on the pixel level, resampling operations
095
      become increasingly inaccurate on sparse, high-dimensional feature spaces. This issue, termed
096   the curse of dimensionality, causes naive interpolation to deteriorate the quality of features being
097   propagated to later sections of the prediction process Verleysen & François (2005).
098
099
      Reference frames. Current data-driven registration methods predominantly rely on an Eule-
      rian (Batchelor, 1967) frame of reference for modeling deformation fields, whereby the deformation
100
      field is observed at specific locations in space. Conversely, the Lagrangian (Batchelor, 1967) specifica-
101
      tion is an alternative choice of modeling motion that tracks the positions of infinitesimal parcels (Brun
102   et al., 2010; Lester et al., 1999; Thirion, 1998; Vercauteren et al., 2009). These concepts carry striking
103   similarities with recent research in the field of geometric deep learning, where the motion of sparse
104   point-like objects is modeled using learned functions (Farahani & Hamker, 2022; Fuchs et al., 2020;
105   Kashefi et al., 2021). This design choice obviates the aforementioned resampling issues by not being
106   confined to a grid-based frame of reference, removing the costly memory requirement of tracking
107   an arbitrarily precise voxelized volume. While geometric deep learning has been used in certain
      contexts for point cloud and cortical surface registration (Hansen & Heinrich, 2021a;b; Suliman et al.,


                                                          2

108
      2022; Shen et al., 2021; Hoopes et al., 2022), to the best of our knowledge, no previous work offers a
109   framing of deformable image registration within the geometric deep learning paradigm.
110
111   Contributions. In this work, we propose a novel paradigm for data-driven image registration by
112
      viewing the deformation modeling process through the lens of geometric deep learning. We formulate
      the task as a multi-scale process of deformation operations, where feature grids are modeled under a
113
      Lagrangian framing of free-floating nodes influenced by neighborhood interactions. Unlike existing
114
      approaches, our method explicitly models coordinates and features independently, performing node-
115   wise operations using continuous learnable kernels. This formulation enables us to completely avoid
116   grid-based constraints on inter-node structure, removing the requirement for intermediate warping
117   operations between transformations.
118
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
119
120             • We establish a mathematical foundation to formulate deformable registration in a continuous
121               domain, avoiding the need for interpolation in the feature space. We achieve this under a
122               Lagrangian reference frame utilizing the geometric deep-learning paradigm.
123
                • We propose a data-driven form of interpolation demonstrating local support, which facilitates
124
                  multi-scale deformation modeling by learning to propagate deformations across resolutions.
125
126
                • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our formulation by reporting improvements over current
                  state-of-the-art deformable registration in the context of medical imaging and showing our
127
                  model’s ability to recover large deformations. We make our code publicly available1 .

132   In this section, we first formally establish the limitations imposed on deformable image registration
133   by the grid constraints of Eulerian frameworks. Afterwards, we establish a Lagrangian formulation
134   that does not make any grid assumptions (section 2.1). Within this context, we highlight the
135   advantages offered by geometric deep learning in modeling deformations as interactions between
136
      free-floating features (section 2.2). Next, we propose a data-driven form of local interpolation,
      which facilitates multi-scale deformation modeling by learning to propagate deformations across
137
      resolutions (section 2.3). Finally, we combine these ideas to construct an end-to-end trainable neural
138
      network capable of learning deformable registration in continuous domains in a coarse-to-fine fashion
139   (section 2.4).

      2.1       D EFORMATION WITHOUT GRID CONSTRAINTS
142
143   An image I can be interpreted as a finite grid of measurements I → H→W →D which represents a
144   discrete subset of a continuous domain !, and where H, W, D are the spatial dimensions of the voxel
145   grid in the 3-dimensional case.
146
      Given a target T and a source S image, deformable image registration aims to find an optimal spatial
147   deformation field ω↑ = arg minω J (T, S, ω), with ω : n ↑ n , such that the transformed source
148   image S ↓ ω is most similar to the target image T . As such, the overall objective J is defined as:
149
150                                     J (T, S, ω) = D(T, S ↓ ω) + εR(ω),                                 (1)
151
152   where D : ! ↔ ! ↑ n is an image dissimilarity measure responsible for driving the deformation,
153   and R is a smoothness regularization on the transformation whose magnitude is weighted by ε.
154   Data-driven methods implement learnable functions responsible for modeling a deformable trans-
155   formation ω as a neural network ϑε (T, S) parametrized by a set of learnable weights ϖ. These
156   approaches model the transformation based on spatial features F S and F T extracted from S and T .
157
      Images are usually represented as grids of nodes containing pixel information. By making relative
158   positions between neighboring nodes constant across the structure, grid representations carry implicit
159   assumptions about the homogeneity of the structure. As such, attempting to construct a neural
160   function ϑ by learning grid-based operations (such as convolutions with discrete kernels) on the

163   confine the transformation prediction to a grid, resulting in an Eulerian framework whereby the
164   deformation field is only defined on the specific locations in space defined by the grid. As spatial
165   transformations are applied to the source domain S, the rigid assumption on grid structure requires a
166   resampling of the feature space to estimate the feature values at the grid positions, which may result
167   in deteriorated features.
168
      Instead of relying on grid representations, we propose to explicitly represent the source domain under
169   a Lagrangian framework via an unstructured set of feature-coordinate tuples S = (F S , X S ), where
170
      F S → |S|→d , X S → |S|→n . Now, each node s → S can be regarded as a discrete observation of a
171   feature f s embedded in Euclidean space at coordinate xs . Here, the feature component f s of a given
172   node s may represent anything from gray-scale intensities (d = 1) to higher dimensional feature
173   descriptors such as those extracted by a feature encoder. This formulation allows us to model the
174   deformation process as a function ϑ acting on the coordinate component xs of each s → S without
175   requiring the feature f s to be modified. This Lagrangian framework is especially enticing to coarse,
176   high-dimensional feature spaces that would otherwise suffer from the curse of dimensionality under
177   interpolation operations (Bronstein et al., 2021).

179   2.2   D EFORMATION MODELING FUNCTION ω
180
181   Formulating the source domain S as a set of nodes with real-valued coordinates, with no necessity of
182   adhering to a rigid grid, requires a generalized form of learned functions that can handle continuous
183   domains. Precisely, we need to define a deformation function ϑ (·) that is able to act node-wise on the
184   set of source nodes s → S, while being capable of handling neighborhoods of target nodes TNs ↗ T
185   with arbitrary real-valued coordinates relative to a given s. This formulation naturally leads us to
186   the realm of geometric deep learning, where graph neural networks are leveraged to model learned
187
      functions on geometric graph structures.
188   We begin by using the previously introduced notation of feature-coordinate pairs for the nodes of the
189   target domain T = (F T , X T ). Using the source S and target T sets, we define the domain of the
190   deformation function ϑ as a directional graph G ϑ = (Aϑ , [F T , F S ], [X T , X S ]) describing how each
191   of the source nodes s → S interacts with the target domain T . The adjacency matrix Aϑ → {0, 1}
192   describes the presence or absence of edges between all node pairs. In practice, since we only want to
193
      model how a given source node s should deform given its local neighborhood in the target domain,
      we only need to model one quadrant of the full adjacency matrix Aϑ → {0, 1}T →S . A row in Aϑ
194

195
      subset of target nodes contain directional edges to s, such that TNs = {t | ↘t → Aϑ[s] , Aϑ[s,t] = 1}.

218   The optimization process of real-world registration tasks is highly non-convex. A prevalent strategy
219   in literature to overcome local minima when dealing with complex transformations is the usage of
220   a multi-resolution strategy. In line with this established paradigm, we explore the application of ϑ
221   deformation functions across entire feature pyramids in coarse-to-fine multi-resolution settings. In
222   this section, we aim to define a function ς(·) capable of chaining deformations in a coarse-to-fine
223   fashion across resolutions without feature interpolation.

227   at a coarse spacing and interpolating to the desired resolution via continuous mapping functions.

233   of the transformation values ωc of its neighboring control points c → N C . This interpolation is
234   weighted using basis functions v, based on relative positions between the given point i and each

303   2.3.3                                   H IERARCHY GRAPHS AS INTERPOLATION DOMAINS
304
305   Our aim is to define a hierarchical process by which a finer resolution level inherits and subsequently
306   refines the transformation of a coarser level. As such, we refer to control nodes at level l as parents
307
      and the nodes at the following level l ≃ 1 as children.
308   To define the domain of the cross-resolution interpolation function ς(·), we need to establish
309   a graph representing parent-child connections. Concretely, given a set of control nodes S l at
310   the l-th pyramid level and a set of children nodes S l↗1 , we define a directional graph G l =
311   (Al , [F l , F l↗1 ], [X l , X l↗1 ]). Here, Al is a static matrix exclusively containing edges connect-
312   ing children nodes to their k-nearest neighboring parent nodes.
313   Hierarchy graph initialization. As depicted in Figure 2 (i), we use the feature extraction process at
314   the encoder to define parent-child hierarchies based on pooling operations. The feature extraction
315   process establishes a hierarchy of children-parent nodes S {1,...,L} , which all live in a combined
316   Euclidean space across L resolution levels. A parent node at level l derived from a region of children
317   nodes at level l ≃ 1 has its position initialized at the center of the pooling window. This hierarchy
318   establishes the cross-resolution neighborhoods that facilitate the interpolation operation of each given
319   child node s → S l↗1 during the decoding process.
320   Transformation inheritance. As depicted in Figure 2 (ii), an initial transformation is inherited by
321   each child node at level l ≃ 1 based on the average transformation of their neighborhoods at resolution
322   l. This rough initial approximation significantly reduces the remaining transformation component left
323   to be modeled by the feature-aware learned interpolation. We refer the reader to the pseudocode in
      Appendix B2 for a detailed overview of this process.

325
326
327   Image encoder. Since our approach requires the source and target features to interact, it is necessary
328   to implement a dual-stream encoder architecture. As a result, feature pyramids are extracted for the
329   source S and target T images independently using the same set of weights. We refer the reader to
330   Appendix A for a schematic of the overall model. The encoder consists of two convolutional residual
331   blocks per resolution followed by pooling layers. The encoder is composed of 6 layers, each made
332   up of two residual blocks, each with [16, 32, 32, 64, 64, 128] channels. Average pooling [2 ↔ 2 ↔ 2]
333
      operations are applied between each encoder layer.
334   Deformation decoder. The feature pyramid is then decoded in a coarse-to-fine fashion across the 6
335   resolutions. Across our experiments, we find that local deformations are sufficiently well-modeled at
336   coarser resolutions in the decoder. Estimating the majority of the transformation at coarser levels
337   reduces the registration burden of finer resolution layers, as only smaller local deformations are left
338   to be recovered (see Appendix F for further detail). These insights allow us to refrain from applying
339
      ϑ at the finest layers where resolutions are the largest, massively decreasing our memory footprint.
340   Memory efficient neighborhood computation. While the methodology outlined in sections 2.2
341   and 2.3 are formalized using graph notation, the grid structure of our data allows us to design highly
342   memory efficient implementations of ϑ and ς layers. Finding nearest neighbors in sparse data is the
343   biggest memory bottleneck due to having to compute O(N 2 ) distance calculations relative to the
344   number of nodes N . Our convolutional encoder providing us with feature grids, lets us use grid-
345
      unfolding operations to find the nearest neighbors in a (kx , ky , kz ) kernel around a central node. The
      ϑ function’s neighborhood computations are performed by first unfolding the target domain into all
346
      its possible neighbourhoods. Then, we can index a source node’s corresponding target neighborhood
347
      by mapping the current source coordinates into the index space of the target grid and rounding to the
348   closest integer. Similarly, the ς function applies a repeated interleaving operation to upsample the
349   parent unfolded neighborhoods into the same dimensions as the children grid. This is all implemented
350   using standard built-in Pytorch functions that allow for efficient GPU parallelism. Wherever possible,
351   we make use of pointers to the original data structures for minimal memory footprints. We refer

386   segmentation labels, and the key-point extraction.
387   We compare our method (GeoReg) against several conventional iterative methods and learning-based
388   image registration models. Regarding the iterative optimization methods, we choose from the Medical
389   Image Registration ToolKit (MIRTK) (Schuh et al., 2014), a widely-used free-form deformation
390   (FFD) iterative optimization method that supports multi-resolution and parametric b-spline-based
391   registration. Additionally, we compare against the widely adopted symmetric diffeomorphic alorithm
392   SyN (Avants et al., 2008) from the ANTs (Avants et al., 2009) framework, as well as Large deforma-
393   tion diffeomorphic metric mapping (LDDMM (Beg et al., 2005)).Our learning-based baselines are
394
      comprised of Voxelmorph (Balakrishnan et al., 2019), a single-stage CNN, LapIRN (Mok & Chung,
      2020b) a multi-resolution registration CNN that aims to capture large deformations in a coarse-to-fine
395
      manner, Transmorph (Chen et al., 2022) that uses a SwinTransformer-based encoder, Recursive
396
      Cascaded Networks (RCN) (Zhao et al., 2019) which estimates the deformation progressively using a
397   cascading CNN architecture, the dual-stream pyramid registration network (D-PRNet) (Kang et al.,
398   2022) that gradually refines the multi-level predicted deformation fields in a coarse-to-fine manner
399   via sequential warping, and FourierNet (Jia et al., 2023) that learns a low-representation displacement
400   filed in a band-limited Fourier domain and then uses a model-driven decoder to obtain the dense,
401   full-resolution displacement field. To ablate the contribution of the proposed interpolation mechanism
402   (ς) on top of our multi-resolution ϑ design, we replace the proposed learned interpolation component
403   (ς) with bilinear feature warping. In the following, we denote this ablation baseline as “feat. warp”.
404   The accuracy of the registration is evaluated using the segmentation metrics Dice Similarity Coeffi-
405   cient (DSC) and 95th percentile Hausdorff distance (HD95). For the NLST dataset, we additionally
406   report the target registration error (TRE) between landmarks. In the synthetic deformation experi-
407   ments, we also report the average end-point error (AEE) to the ground truth deformation. We calculate
408   the percentage of points with a negative Jacobian determinant |⇒ω| < 0, indicating the extent of
409   space folding, to measure the regularity of the transformation.

527   4     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduce a novel formulation of deformable image registration by using geometric
530   deep-learning principles. We discuss the benefits of estimating deformations on non-fixed grid
531   locations by defining data-driven functions on continuous domains. We outline the need for two types
532   of learned graph operations: A deformation modeling function ϑ and a cross-resolution interpolation
533   function ς. Our multi-resolution architecture shows the ability to fully deformably capture a wide
534   range of rotations, translations, and scalings without explicitly modeling an affine component.
535   We believe that even though our work provides competitive results with state-of-the-art methods,
536   the main contribution of our manuscript lies in establishing a theoretical foundation by which
537   transformations can be explicitly propagated through a deep learning architecture. We think that
538   this contribution opens up avenues of research to reduce the black-box nature of current learned
539   registration paradigms, incorporate ideas from conventional image registration into deep learning
      architectures, and tackle known data issues such as anisotropicity.
