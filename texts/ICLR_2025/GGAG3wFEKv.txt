                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Efficient exploration in contextual bandits is crucial due to their large action space,
010            where uninformed exploration can lead to computational and statistical inefficien-
011            cies. However, the rewards of actions are often correlated, which can be leveraged
012            for more efficient exploration. In this work, we use pre-trained diffusion model pri-
013            ors to capture these correlations and develop diffusion Thompson sampling (dTS).
014            We establish both theoretical and algorithmic foundations for dTS. Specifically,
015            we derive efficient posterior approximations (required by dTS) under a diffusion
016            model prior, which are of independent interest beyond bandits and reinforcement
017            learning. We analyze dTS in linear instances and provide a Bayes regret bound.
018
               Our experiments validate our theory and demonstrate dTS‚Äôs favorable performance.
019
020   1    I NTRODUCTION
021
022   A contextual bandit is a popular and practical framework for online learning under uncertainty (Li
023   et al., 2010). In each round, an agent observes a context, takes an action, and receives a reward based
024   on the context and action. The goal is to maximize the expected cumulative reward over n rounds,
025   striking a balance between exploiting actions with high estimated rewards from available data and
026   exploring other actions to improve current estimates. This trade-off is often addressed using either
027   upper confidence bound (UCB) (Auer et al., 2002) or Thompson sampling (TS) (Scott, 2010).
028   The action space in contextual bandits is often large, resulting in less-than-optimal performance
029   with standard exploration strategies. Luckily, actions usually exhibit correlations, making efficient
030   exploration possible as one action may inform the agent about other actions. In particular, Thompson
031   sampling offers remarkable flexibility, allowing its integration with informative priors (Hong et al.,
032   2022b) that capture these correlations. Inspired by the achievements of diffusion models (Sohl-
033   Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020), which effectively approximate complex distributions (Dhariwal
034
      & Nichol, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022), this work captures action correlations by employing diffusion
      models as priors in contextual Thompson sampling.
035
036   We illustrate the idea using video streaming. The objective is to optimize watch time for a user j
037   by selecting a video i from a catalog of K videos. Users j and videos i are associated with context
038   vectors xj and unknown video parameters őłi , respectively. User j‚Äôs expected watch time for video i
039   is linear as x‚ä§
                    j őłi . Then, a natural strategy is to independently learn video parameters őłi using LinTS
040   or LinUCB (Agrawal & Goyal, 2013a; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011), but this proves statistically
041   inefficient for larger K. Fortunately, the reward when recommending a movie can provide informative
042
      insights into other movies. To capture this, we leverage offline estimates of video parameters denoted
043   by őłŐāi and build a diffusion model on them. This diffusion model approximates the video parameter
044
      distribution, capturing their dependencies. This model enriches contextual Thompson sampling as a
      prior, effectively capturing complex video dependencies while ensuring computational efficiency.
045
046   We introduce a framework for contextual bandits with diffusion model priors, upon which we develop
047   diffusion Thompson sampling (dTS) that is both computationally and statistically efficient. dTS
048   requires fast updates of the posterior and fast sampling from the posterior, both of which are achieved
049   through our novel efficient posterior approximations. These approximations become exact when
050
      both the diffusion model and likelihood are linear. We establish a bound on dTS‚Äôs Bayes regret for
      this specific case, highlighting the advantages of using diffusion models as priors. Our empirical
051
      evaluations validate our theory and demonstrate dTS‚Äôs strong performance across various settings.
052
053   Diffusion models were applied in offline decision-making (Ajay et al., 2022; Janner et al., 2022;
      Wang et al., 2022), but their use in online learning was only recently explored by Hsieh et al. (2023),

      who focused on multi-armed bandits without theoretical guarantees. Our work extends Hsieh et al.
055   (2023) in two ways. First, we apply the concept to the broader contextual bandit, which is more
056   practical and realistic. Second, we show that with diffusion models parametrized by linear link
057   functions and linear rewards, we can derive exact closed-form posteriors without approximations.
058   These exact posteriors are valuable as they enable theoretical analysis (unlike Hsieh et al. (2023),
059   who did not provide theoretical guarantees) and motivate efficient approximations for non-linear link
060   functions in contextual bandits, addressing gaps in Hsieh et al. (2023)‚Äôs focus on multi-armed bandits.
061   A key contribution, beyond applying diffusion models in contextual bandits, is the efficient com-
062   putation and sampling of the posterior distribution of a d-dimensional parameter őł | Ht , with Ht
063   representing the data, when using a diffusion model prior on őł. This is relevant not only to bandits
064   and RL but also to a broader range of applications (Chung et al., 2022). Our approximations are
065   motivated with exact closed-form solutions obtained in cases where both the link functions of the
066   diffusion model and the likelihood are linear. These solutions form the basis for our approximations
067   for non-linear link functions, demonstrating both strong empirical performance and computational
068   efficiency. Our approach avoids the computational burden of heavy approximate sampling algorithms
069
      required for each latent parameter. For a detailed related work discussion, see Appendix A, where we
      discuss diffusion models in decision-making, structured bandits, approximate posteriors, etc.

268   The Bayes regret bound has a clear interpretation: if the true environment parameters are drawn
269   from the prior, then the expected regret of an algorithm stays below that bound. Consequently, a
      less informative prior (such as high variance) leads to a more challenging problem and thus a higher

270
      bound. Then, smaller values of K, L, d or d‚Ąď translate to fewer parameters to learn, leading to lower
271   regret. The regret also decreases when the initial variances ŌÉ‚Ąď2 decrease. These dependencies are
272   common in Bayesian analysis, and empirical results match them. The reader might question the
273   dependence of our bound on both L and K. We will address this next.
274
      Why the bound increases with K? This arises due to our conditional learning of őł‚ąó,i given
275
      Ōą‚ąó,1 . Rather than assuming deterministic linearity, őł‚ąó,i = W1 Ōą‚ąó,1 , we account for stochasticity by
276
      modeling őł‚ąó,i ‚ąľ N (W1 Ōą‚ąó,1 , ŌÉ12 Id ). This makes dTS robust to misspecification scenarios where őł‚ąó,i
277
      is not perfectly linear with respect to Ōą‚ąó,1 , at the cost of additional learning of őł‚ąó,i | Ōą‚ąó,1 . If we were
278   to assume deterministic linearity (ŌÉ1 = 0), our regret bound would scale with L only.
279
280   Why the bound increases with L? This is because increasing the number of layers L adds more
281
      initial uncertainty due to the additional covariance introduced by the extra layers. However, this does
      not imply that we should always use L = 1 (the minimum possible L). Precisely, the theoretical
282
      results predict that regret increases with L when the true prior distribution matches a diffusion model
283
      of depth L, as increasing L reflects a more complex action parameter distribution and hence a more
284   complex bandit problem. However, in practice, when L is small, the pre-trained diffusion model
285   may be too simple to capture the true prior distribution, violating the assumptions of our theory.
286   Increasing L improves the pre-trained model‚Äôs quality, reducing regret. Once L is large enough and
287   the pre-trained model adequately captures the true prior distribution, the theoretical assumptions hold,
288   and regret begins to increase with L, as predicted. This is validated empirically in Fig. 4b.
289
      Technical contributions. dTS uses hierarchical sampling. Thus the marginal posterior distribution of
290   őł‚ąó,i | Ht is not explicitly defined. The first contribution is deriving őł‚ąó,i | Ht using the total covariance
291   decomposition combined with an induction proof, as our posteriors were derived recursively. Unlike
292   standard analyses where the posterior distribution of őł‚ąó,i | Ht is predetermined due to the absence of
293   latent parameters, our method necessitates this recursive total covariance decomposition. Moreover,
294   in standard proofs, we need to quantify the increase in posterior precision for the action taken At in
295   each round t ‚ąą [n]. However, in dTS, our analysis extends beyond this. We not only quantify the
296   posterior information gain for the taken action but also for every latent parameter, since they are also
297   learned. To elaborate, we use our recursive posteriors that connect the posterior covariance of each
298
      latent parameter Ōą‚ąó,‚Ąď with the covariance of the posterior action parameters őł‚ąó,i . This allows us to
      propagate the information gain associated with the action taken in round At to all latent parameters
299
      Ōą‚ąó,‚Ąď , for ‚Ąď ‚ąą [L] by induction. More technical details are provided in Appendix D.

725   A    E XTENDED RELATED WORK
726
727   Thompson sampling (TS) operates within the Bayesian framework and it involves specifying a
728   prior/likelihood model. In each round, the agent samples unknown model parameters from the
729   current posterior distribution. The chosen action is the one that maximizes the resulting reward. TS
730   is naturally randomized, particularly simple to implement, and has highly competitive empirical
731   performance in both simulated and real-world problems (Russo & Van Roy, 2014; Chapelle & Li,
732   2012). Regret guarantees for the TS heuristic remained open for decades even for simple models.
733   Recently, however, significant progress has been made. For standard multi-armed bandits, TS is
734   optimal in the Beta-Bernoulli model (Kaufmann et al., 2012; Agrawal & Goyal, 2013b), Gaussian-
735
      Gaussian model (Agrawal & Goyal, 2013b), and in the exponential family using Jeffrey‚Äôs prior (Korda
      et al., 2013). For linear bandits, TS is nearly-optimal (Russo & Van Roy, 2014; Agrawal & Goyal,
736
      2017; Abeille & Lazaric, 2017). In this work, we build TS upon complex diffusion priors and analyze
737
      the resulting Bayes regret (Russo & Van Roy, 2014; Neu et al., 2022; Gouverneur et al., 2023) in the
738   linear contextual bandit setting.
739
740   Decision-making with diffusion models gained attention recently, especially in offline learning
741
      (Ajay et al., 2022; Janner et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022). However, their application in online
      learning was only examined by Hsieh et al. (2023), which focused on meta-learning in multi-armed
742
      bandits without theoretical guarantees. In this work, we expand the scope of Hsieh et al. (2023) to
743
      encompass the broader contextual bandit framework. In particular, we provide theoretical analysis for
744   linear instances, effectively capturing the advantages of using diffusion models as priors in contextual
745   Thompson sampling. These linear cases are particularly captivating due to closed-form posteriors,
746   enabling both theoretical analysis and computational efficiency; an important practical consideration.
747
      Hierarchical Bayesian bandits (Bastani et al., 2019; Kveton et al., 2021; Basu et al., 2021; Sim-
748
      chowitz et al., 2021; Wan et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022b; Peleg et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2022; Aouali
749
      et al., 2023b) applied TS to simple graphical models, wherein action parameters are generally sampled
750   from a Gaussian distribution centered at a single latent parameter. These works mostly span meta-
751   and multi-task learning for multi-armed bandits, except in cases such as Aouali et al. (2023b); Hong
752   et al. (2022a) that consider the contextual bandit setting. Precisely, Aouali et al. (2023b) assume that
753   action parameters are sampled from a Gaussian distribution centered at a linear mixture of multiple
754   latent parameters. On the other hand, Hong et al. (2022a) applied TS to a graphical model represented
755   by a tree. Our work can be seen as an extension of all these works to much more complex graphical
      models, for which both theoretical and algorithmic foundations are developed. Note that the settings


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      in most of these works can be recovered with specific choices of the diffusion depth L and functions
757   f‚Ąď . This attests to the modeling power of dTS.
758
759   Approximate Thompson sampling is a major problem in the Bayesian inference literature. This is
760
      because most posterior distributions are intractable, and thus practitioners must resort to sophisti-
      cated computational techniques such as Markov chain Monte Carlo (Kruschke, 2010). Prior works
761
      (Riquelme et al., 2018; Chapelle & Li, 2012; Kveton et al., 2020) highlight the favorable empirical
762
      performance of approximate Thompson sampling. Particularly, (Kveton et al., 2020) provide the-
763   oretical guarantees for Thompson sampling when using the Laplace approximation in generalized
764   linear bandits (GLB). In our context, we incorporate approximate sampling when the reward exhibits
765   non-linearity. While our approximation does not come with formal guarantees, it enjoys strong
766   practical performance. An in-depth analysis of this approximation is left as a direction for future
767   works. Similarly, approximating the posterior distribution when the diffusion model is non-linear as
768   well as analyzing it is an interesting direction of future works.
769   Bandits with underlying structure also align with our work, where we assume a structured rela-
770   tionship among actions, captured by a diffusion model. In latent bandits (Maillard & Mannor, 2014;
771   Hong et al., 2020), a single latent variable indexes multiple candidate models. Within structured
772   finite-armed bandits (Lattimore & Munos, 2014; Gupta et al., 2018), each action is linked to a known
773   mean function parameterized by a common latent parameter. This latent parameter is learned. TS
774   was also applied to complex structures (Yu et al., 2020; Gopalan et al., 2014). However, simultaneous
775   computational and statistical efficiencies aren‚Äôt guaranteed. Meta- and multi-task learning with upper
776   confidence bound (UCB) approaches have a long history in bandits (Azar et al., 2013; Gentile et al.,
777
      2014; Deshmukh et al., 2017; Cella et al., 2020). These, however, often adopt a frequentist perspec-
      tive, analyze a stronger form of regret, and sometimes result in conservative algorithms. In contrast,
778
      our approach is Bayesian, with analysis centered on Bayes regret. Remarkably, our algorithm, dTS,
779
      performs well as analyzed without necessitating additional tuning. Finally, Low-rank bandits (Hu
780   et al., 2021; Cella et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2020) also relate to our linear diffusion model when
781   L = 1. Broadly, there exist two key distinctions between these prior works and the special case
782   of our model (linear diffusion model with L = 1). First, they assume őł‚ąó,i = W1 Ōą‚ąó,1 , whereas we
783   incorporate additional uncertainty in the covariance ő£1 to account for possible misspecification as
784   őł‚ąó,i = N (W1 Ōą‚ąó,1 , ő£1 ). Consequently, these algorithms might suffer linear regret due to model
785   misalignment. Second, we assume that the mixing matrix W1 is available and pre-learned offline,
786   whereas they learn it online. While this is more general, it leads to computationally expensive
787   methods that are difficult to employ in a real-world online setting.
788   Large
         P action    spaces. Roughly speaking, the regret bound of dTS scales with KŌÉ12 rather than

1128
1129   After deriving the exact posteriors in the case where the link functions f‚Ąď are linear (Appendix B.2),
1130   we now get back to the general case with any link functions f‚Ąď that can be non-linear. Approximation
1131   is needed since both the link functions and rewards can be non-linear. To avoid any computational
1132   challenges, we use a simple and intuitive approximation, where all posteriors Pt,i and Qt,‚Ąď are
1133   approximated by the Gaussian distributions in Appendix B.2, with few changes. First, the terms
       W‚Ąď Ōą‚Ąď in Eq. (19) are replaced by f‚Ąď (Ōą‚Ąď ). This accounts for the fact that the prior mean is now f‚Ąď (Ōą‚Ąď )

1134
       rather than W‚Ąď Ōą‚Ąď , and this is the main difference between the linear diffusion model in Eq. (16) and
1135   the general, potentially non-linear, diffusion model in Eq. (1). Second, the matrix multiplications
1136   that involve the matrices W‚Ąď in Eq. (21) and Eq. (22) are simply removed. Despite being simple,
1137   this approximation is efficient and avoids the computational burden of heavy approximate sampling
1138   algorithms required for each latent parameter. This is why deriving the exact posterior for linear link
1139   functions was key beyond enabling theoretical analyses. Moreover, this approximation retains some
1140   key attributes of exact posteriors. Specifically, in the absence of data, it recovers precisely the prior in
1141   Eq. (1), and as more data is accumulated, the influence of the prior diminishes.

1618            is trained to reverse this noise (i.e., denoise) by predicting the noise added at each step. The
1619            loss function measures the L2 norm difference between the predicted and actual noise at
                each step, as explained in Ho et al. (2020).

1689   F          B ROADER IMPACT
1690
1691   This work contributes to the development and analysis of practical algorithms for online learning to
1692   act under uncertainty. While our generic setting and algorithms have broad potential applications,
1693   the specific downstream social impacts are inherently dependent on the chosen application domain.
1694   Nevertheless, we acknowledge the crucial need to consider potential biases that may be present in
1695   pre-trained diffusion models, given that our method relies on them.
1696
1697   G           L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH
1698
1699   We designed diffusion Thompson sampling (dTS); for which we developed both theoretical and algo-
1700   rithmic foundations in numerous practical settings. We identified several directions for future work.
1701   Exploring other approximations for non-linear diffusion models, both empirically and theoretically.
1702   For theory, future research could explore the advantages of non-linear diffusion models by deriving
1703   their Bayes regret bounds, akin to our analysis in Section 4. Empirically, investigating our and other
1704   approximations in complex tasks would be interesting. Additionally, exploring the extension of this
1705
       work to offline (or off-policy) learning in contextual bandits (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015; Aouali
       et al., 2023a) represents a promising avenue for future research. Our work focused on contextual
1706
       bandits, laying the groundwork for future exploration into reinforcement learning. This exploration
1707
       can also be done from both practical (empirical) and theoretical angles. Finally, while our method,
1708   which approximates rewards using a Gaussian distribution, worked well for linear rewards and those
1709   following a generalized linear model, its effectiveness in real-world, complex scenarios needs further
1710   testing.
