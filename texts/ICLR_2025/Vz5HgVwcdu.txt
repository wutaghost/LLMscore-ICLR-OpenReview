010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Neural networks with ReLU activation play a key role in modern machine learn-
013            ing. Understanding the functions represented by ReLU networks is a major topic
014            in current research as this enables a better interpretability of learning processes.
015            Injectivity plays a crucial role whenever invertibility of a neural network is nec-
016            essary, such as, e.g., for inverse problems or generative models. The exact com-
017
               putational complexity of deciding injectivity was recently posed as an open prob-
               lem (Puthawala et al. [JMLR 2022]). We answer this question by proving coNP-
018
               completeness. On the positive side, we show that the problem for a single ReLU-
019
               layer is still tractable for small input dimension; more precisely, we present a
020            parameterized algorithm which yields fixed-parameter tractability with respect to
021            the input dimension.
022            In addition, we study the network verification problem which is of great impor-
023            tance since neural networks are increasingly used in safety-critical systems. We
024            prove that network verification is coNP-hard for a general class of input domains.
025            Our result thus highlights that the hardness of network verification is intrinsic to
026            the ReLU networks themselves, rather than specific input domains.
027            In this context, we also characterize surjectivity for ReLU networks with one-
028            dimensional output which turns out to be the complement of a basic network ver-
029            ification task. We reveal interesting connections to computational convexity by
030            formulating the surjectivity problem as a zonotope containment problem.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Neural networks with rectified linear units (ReLUs) are a widely used model in deep learning. In
035
      practice, neural networks are trained on finite datasets and are expected to generalize to new, unseen
036   inputs. However, they often exhibit unexpected and erroneous behavior in response to minor input
037   perturbations; see, e.g., Szegedy et al. (2014). Hence, the certification of trained networks is of
038   great importance and necessitates a thorough understanding of essential properties of the function
039   computed by a ReLU network.
040
      Network verification, that is, the question whether for all inputs from a given subset X , the ReLU
041
      network outputs a value contained in a given set Y, is a research field gaining high interest recently
042   since neural networks are increasingly used in safety-critical systems like autonomous vehicles (Bo-
043   jarski et al. (2016)) and collision avoidance for drones (Julian et al. (2019)), see, e.g., Weng et al.
044   (2018); Rössig & Petkovic (2021); Kouvaros & Lomuscio (2021); Katz et al. (2022). Typically, X
045   and Y are balls or defined by some linear constraints and the problem is known to be coNP-hard if
046   the sets X and Y are part of the input; see Katz et al. (2022); Weng et al. (2018); Sälzer & Lange
047   (2023).
048   Moreover, recent works focus on studying elementary properties of functions computed by ReLU
049   networks such as injectivity; see, e.g., Puthawala et al. (2022); Haider et al. (2023); Furuya et al.
050   (2023). Injectivity plays a crucial role in many applications where invertibility of a neural network
051   is necessary. Such applications include, for example, generative models, inverse problems, or like-
052   lihood estimation; see Puthawala et al. (2022); Furuya et al. (2023) for a more detailed discussion.
053   On the other hand, injectivity might cause privacy issues since the input might be inferred from the
      output.

      Formally, for any number of layers ℓ ∈ N, given weights of a fully connected ReLU network
055   f : Rd → Rm with ℓ layers, the question is whether the map f is injective. Puthawala et al. (2022)
056   give a mathematical characterization for injectivity of a single ReLU-layer (see Theorem 5), imply-
057   ing an exponential-time algorithm where the exponential part is upper bounded by min{2m , md }.
058   In terms of parameterized complexity, the problem is fixed-parameter tractable with respect to the
059   number m of ReLUs (that is, the superpolynomial part depends only on m), and in XP with respect
060   to the input dimension d (that is, polynomial time for constant d). The complexity status of decid-
061   ing injectivity of an ℓ-layer ReLU neural network (and therefore also, in particular, a single layer),
062   however, is unresolved and posed as an open problem by Puthawala et al. (2022).
063   Another natural and fundamental property of functions to consider is surjectivity. Hence, to com-
064   plete the picture alongside injectivity, we initiate the study of surjectivity for ReLU networks, which,
065   to the best of our knowledge, has not been considered before. In fact, we show that deciding surjec-
066   tivity can be considered as the complement of a certain network verification task.
067   Our Contributions. After some preliminaries in Section 2, we show in Section 3 that deciding
068   injectivity of a ReLU network with ℓ layers is coNP-complete (Corollary 8) for any ℓ ∈ N and
069   thus not polynomial-time solvable, unless P = NP. Notably, our hardness reduction reveals some
070   interesting connections between cut problems in (di)graphs and properties of ReLU networks via
071   graphical hyperplane arrangements. We believe that these connections between seemingly unre-
072   lated areas are of independent interest. Moreover, our hardness result implies a running time lower
073   bound of 2Ω(m) for a single ReLU-layer with m neurons based on the Exponential Time Hypothesis
074   (Corollary 7). Hence, the running time 2m is essentially optimal. As regards the input dimension
                                                                                                           d,
075
      however, in Section 4 we give an improved algorithm running in O (d + 1)d · poly time. This
      yields fixed-parameter tractability (Theorem 10) and settles the complexity status for injectivity of a
076
      ReLU-layer.
077
078   Katz et al. (2022) and Sälzer & Lange (2023) show that the network verification problem is coNP-
079   hard for a single hidden layer. However, their hardness results are based on the unit cube as input
080   set. Thus, it was not clear so far whether there are interesting special cases of input sets on which
081
      network verification is solvable in polynomial time. In Section 5, we provide the strongest hardness
      result for network verification to date (Corollary 13), proving coNP-hardness for every possible
082
      input domain X that contains a ball (of possibly lower dimension under some mild conditions). In
083
      particular, our result implies that the computational intractability does not stem from choosing a
084   particular set X , but is rather an intrinsic property of a single hidden ReLU-layer. Since arguably
085   any reasonable input domain contains some ball (this holds, e.g., for all polyhedral sets satisfying
086   the same mild conditions), no special case of verification regarding the input domain is solvable in
087   polynomial time in the worst case. In particular, our result implies hardness for all polyhedra and
088   also all balls as input set, which is not covered by the previous hardness results. As a consequence,
089   one must make (very) specific assumptions on the network in order to obtain tractable cases, e. g., by
090   building special (approximating) networks that are efficiently verifiable (Baader et al. (2020),Wang
091   et al. (2022),Baader et al. (2024)).
092   Moreover, we give a characterization of surjectivity for ReLU networks with one-dimensional out-
093   put (Lemma 14) which implies a polynomial-time algorithm for constant input dimension d. We
094   then proceed with proving NP-hardness of surjectivity by showing that it can be phrased as (the
095   complement of) a special case of network verification. Finally, we also show that surjectivity can
096   be formulated as a zonotope containment problem, which is of fundamental importance, e.g., in
097   robotics (Kulmburg & Althoff (2021)).
098   Related Work. Puthawala et al. (2022) initiate the study of injectivity of ReLU networks. Their
099   result implicitly yields an exponential-time algorithm. They also mention a connection between

101   rows). Computing the spark is known to be NP-hard (Tillmann & Pfetsch (2014)), and also known
102   not to be fixed-parameter tractable with respect to d, unless FPT = W[1] (Panolan et al. (2015)).

105
      injectivity of ReLU-layers with linear neural operators.

192   (Parameterized) Complexity Theory. We assume the reader to be familiar with basic concepts
193   from classical complexity theory like P, NP, and NP-completeness. The class coNP contains all
194   decision problems whose complement is in NP. A decision problem is coNP-complete if and only if
195   its complement is NP-complete. Clearly, a coNP-complete problem cannot be solved in polynomial
196
      time unless P = NP.
197   A parameterized problem consists of instances (x, k), where x encodes the classical instance
198   and k ∈ N is a parameter. A parameterized problem is in the class XP if it is polynomial-time
199   solvable for every constant parameter value, that is, in O(|x|f (k) ) time for an arbitrary function f
200   depending only on the parameter k. A parameterized problem is fixed-parameter tractable (con-
201   tained in the class FPT) if it is solvable in f (k) · |x|O(1) time, for an arbitrary function f . Clearly,
202   FPT ⊆ XP; see Downey & Fellows (2013) for further details of parameterized complexity.

360     5    V ERIFICATION AND S URJECTIVITY
361
362     In this section, we study the network verification task and prove hardness for a very general class
363     of input sets. To this end, we call a sequence S = (Sd )d∈N of subsets Sd ⊆ Rd reasonable if
364     there exists an algorithm that on input k ∈ N computes in poly(k) time a d ∈ N, z ∈ Rd and a
365     k-dimensional affine space A in Rd (given by a basis and a translation vector) such that there is a
366     k-dimensional ball with center z contained in Sd that affinely spans A. For a sequence S = (Sd )d∈N
367     of reasonable sets and a polyhedron Qt := {x ∈ Rm | xi ≤ ti for all i ∈ [m]} where t ∈ Rm ,
368
        the task to decide for a given ℓ-layer ReLU network f : Rd → Rm whether f (Sd ) ⊆ Qt we call
        ℓ-L AYER R E LU (S, Qt )-V ERIFICATION.
369
370     Even though the definition of a reasonable sequence is a bit involved, they include a wide range
371     of set-sequences. For example, they trivially include all sequences of sets that contain an open
372     neighborhood of the origin and therefore in particular all sequences of full-dimensional polyhedra
373     containing the origin in the interior. This shows, in comparison to previous existing coNP-hardness
374
        proofs, that the hardness of verification does not rely on the complexity of the polyhedron in the
        input domain but is intrinsic to the ReLU networks themselves.

520   6       C ONCLUSION
521
522   We showed the strongest hardness result for network verification known so far and thereby excluded
523   polynomial-time algorithms (in the worst case) for almost all restrictions on the input set. Moreover,
524   we initiate the complexity-theoretic study of deciding the two elementary properties injectivity and
525
      surjectivity for functions computed by ReLU networks. We exclude polynomial-time algorithms
      for solving both problems, and prove fixed-parameter tractability for injectivity of a single layer. It
526
      turned out that surjectivity is a special case of network verification and is also equivalent to zono-
527
      tope containment. Our results build new bridges between seemingly unrelated areas, and yield new
528   insights into the complexity and expressiveness of ReLU neural networks. We close with some open
529   questions:
