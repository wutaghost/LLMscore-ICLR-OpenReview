009                                                            A BSTRACT
010
011                  Training large language models (LLMs) from scratch can yield models with unique
012                  functionalities and strengths, but it is costly and often leads to redundant capabilities.
013                  A more cost-effective alternative is to fuse existing pre-trained LLMs with different
014                  architectures into a more powerful model. However, a key challenge in existing
015                  model fusion approaches is their dependence on manually predefined vocabulary
016                  alignment strategies, which may not generalize well across diverse contexts, leading
017                  to performance degradation in several evaluation tasks. To address this challenge,
018                  we draw inspiration from distribution learning and propose the probabilistic token
019
                     alignment method as a general and soft mapping solution for alignment, resulting in
                     PTA-LLM. Our approach innovatively reformulates token alignment into a classic
020
                     mathematical problem: optimal transport, seamlessly leveraging distribution-aware
021
                     learning to facilitate more coherent model fusion. Apart from its inherent generality,
022                  PTA-LLM exhibits interpretability from a distributional perspective, offering
023                  insights into the essence of the token alignment task. Our approach is validated
024                  across diverse benchmarks and tasks using three prominent LLMs with distinct
025                  architectures—Llama-2, MPT, and OpenLLaMA. Empirical results demonstrate
026                  that probabilistic token alignment enhances the target model’s performance across
027                  multiple capabilities.

      The rise of large language models (LLMs) such as Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), OpenLLaMA
048   (Geng & Liu, 2023), and MPT (Team, 2023), driven by scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020), has yielded
049   significant advancement across a broad range of tasks (see Fig. 1 (d), where the narrow dashed line
050   area indicates its respective fields of advantage). Nevertheless, the reliance on scaling laws introduces
051   substantial computational demands, necessitating access to extensive data and high processing power
052   (Brown et al., 2020). Such requirement poses a noticeable impediment to the development of more
053   robust baselines, particularly in academia. Thus, a critical question naturally emerges: ① How can
      we construct stronger baselines without resorting to the naive application of scaling laws?

      Fortunately, pioneering research has begun to address the aforementioned question through the
055   concept of model fusion (Sagi & Rokach, 2018; Gupta et al., 2020; Wortsman et al., 2022; Li et al.,
056   2023), focusing on model ensemble and weight merging paradigms. The former involves combining
057   the predictions of multiple independently trained models to improve overall performance (see Fig.1
058   (a)), while the latter creates a new model by merging the weights of several models (see Fig.1
059   (b)). Recently, a prominent technique called knowledge fusion (Wan et al., 2024a) aggregates the
060   probabilistic distributions generated by individual LLMs and transfers this fused representation to a
061   target model via distillation (see Fig.1 (c)), enabling it to be more inference-efficient. Furthermore,
062   after employing token alignment (Fu et al., 2023), the misalignment issues arising from the use
063
      of different tokenizers across models are mitigated, allowing the approach to remain architecture-
      agnostic. Consequently, question ① can potentially be addressed through knowledge fusion, reframing
064
      it as ②: How can we further optimize LLM models through knowledge fusion?
065
066   Question ② compels us to investigate the current knowledge fusion paradigm more deeply. Although
067   the current paradigm shows promise for model fusion, two significant token alignment challenges
068   remain unresolved, which hinders its further application in various fields. ❶ The manually designed
069
      mapping strategy is overly simplistic, failing to capture the intricate patterns within the data. Tokens
      appearing in varying contexts often align with different objectives, and the bias introduced by this
070
      "rigid" alignment reduces the model’s capacity to fully learn from the data, ultimately diminishing
071
      performance. ❷ The alignment of top-k predicted token sets from the source and target LLMs
072   is performed independently, without taking into account their associated probabilities or overall
073   distribution. This isolated strategy may achieve local optimality at each step alignment, but it does
074   not guarantee a whole coherent fused matric. Thus, the core question ② becomes more specific: ③
075   How can we effectively fusion LLM models with an adaptive and coherent fused matrix?
076
      To this end, we introduce Probabilistic Token Alignment for Large Language Model Fusion (PTA-
077
      LLM). During the matrix fusion, we first employ dynamic algorithm to determine an optimal token
078   pairing between the generated sequence from the source and target model. After obtaining the token
079   pairings, a logit-level alignment will be conducted to resolve the token ID misalignment. Specifically,
080   for the top-k predicted token sets from both source and target models, we hypothesize and further
081   prove (see empirical results in Table 1 and 2) that the probabilistic distributions generated by distinct
082   LLMs are coherent and reflective of their respective inherent knowledge. Therefore, PTA-LLM lever-
083   ages the global generative distributions of each model’s logits during token alignment, externalizing
084   their collective knowledge and facilitating more precise mapping. To achieve this, our approach
085   is grounded in Optimal Transport (OT), which optimally transforms one probability distribution
086
      into another while minimizing a predefined cost. By harnessing OT, we align or “transport" logit
      distributions between models, offering an effective solution. In contrast to hard mapping strate-
087
      gies, which align each token independently of its context, our proposed PTA-LLM employs a soft
088
      probabilistic alignment (detailed in §3.2). This approach better captures the intricacies of various
089   linguistic context and thus establishes a stronger performance baseline, addressing the challenge ❶.
090   Additionally, by incorporating distribution-aware learning, this method facilitates more consistent
091   model representations (through the visualization results in §4.4), leading to marked improvements in
092   generalization across a wide range of tasks (see Table §1), thereby addressing the challenge ❷.
093
      PTA-LLM enjoys a few attractive qualities. I. Generality. The global probabilistic distribution
094
      transport enhances the coherence of the representations, thereby improving the model’s ability to
095   generalize across a wide range of tasks and supporting the transfer of underlying representations
096   for effective evaluation (see Table 1). II. Stability. The reframing through an optimal transport
097   perspective introduces a soft probabilistic alignment, offering a flexible and adaptive solution to
098   diverse contexts and performing stablly even in difficult tasks (see Table 2). III. Interpretability.
099   The effectiveness of our approach is supported by theoretical insights from distribution learning and
100   further validated through visualization results. It investigates the underlying mechanisms of token
101   alignment, a critical operation in knowledge fusion that has been largely overlooked in prior research.
102   This distinguishes PTA-LLM from most existing knowledge fusion models, which fail to elucidate
103
      precisely how token alignment works (see §4.4).
104   Comprehensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the performance of PTA-LLM. In §4.2, we
105   present compelling experimental results on various benchmarks, achieving superior performance
106   without complex engineering design. Specifically, our approach achieves an average improvement
107   of 1.72% in accuracy across six benchmarks. In §4.4, we demonstrate that the distribution-aware
      alignment significantly enhances the coherence of the fused representation intuitively (i.e., the

      Model Fusion has garnered significant attention as a means to enhance the general performance of
      LLMs. The fusion techniques can be classified into three primary categories: model ensembling,
116
      weight merging, and knowledge fusion. Model ensembling combines the predictions of independently
117
      trained models to improve overall performance. Common approaches include weighted averaging (Lit-
118   tlestone & Warmuth, 1994), majority voting (Monteith et al., 2011), and pairwise ranking (Jiang et al.,
119   2023). Although model ensembling often leads to significant improvements in predictive accuracy
120   and model robustness, it requires maintaining multiple models during inference, leading to higher
121   memory consumption and increased latency. This makes it less efficient for resource-constrained
122   environments. Weight merging combines the parameters of multiple models to synthesize a new,
123   unified model. This method is especially effective when the models share identical architectures,
124   as their parameters can be merged seamlessly (Gupta et al., 2020; Wortsman et al., 2022). Weight
125   merging is enhanced by linear mathematical operations on adapter parameters, which has proven
126
      useful for improving model performance and generalization (Wang et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2023;
      Zhang et al., 2023). Despite these advantages, weight merging suffers from significant limitations:
127
      It relies on architectural uniformity across models and requires manual tuning, which constrains its
128
      applicability across diverse model architectures (i.e., low generalizability).
129
130   In contrast, knowledge fusion offers a more flexible and efficient means of integrating models,
131   particularly when the underlying architectures differ (i.e., a common case in LLMs). It distills
132
      knowledge from multiple teacher models into a single student model, transferring the knowledge
      in a more compact and efficient form. One of the key innovations is the minimum edit distance
133
      (MinED) token alignment strategy, first introduced by (Wan et al., 2024a), which facilitates effective
134
      knowledge transfer by aligning tokens across models. This approach was further refined by (Wan
135   et al., 2024b), who proposed a mapping statistics-based strategy designed to enhance conversational
136   model performance. Compared to model ensembling and weight merging, knowledge fusion presents
137   a more scalable and architecture-agnostic solution, making it highly suitable for integrating multiple
138   LLMs while minimizing the performance degradation typically associated with stepwise optimization.
139
      Token Alignment was first introduced as a solution to address the misalignment problem between
140
      tokenizers with different size of vocabulary, specifically when aligning their respective distributions.
141   The concept was initially formalized by (Fu et al., 2023), who employs a search algorithm to minimize
142   the alignment cost between token sequences. This method relies on the assumption that an optimal
143   one-to-one mapping between tokens can be found, enabling the direct alignment of their respective
144   distributions. However, in cases where such a precise mapping is not feasible, the solution defaults
145   to a one-hot vector representation, which may oversimplify the complexities inherent in real-world
146   token distributions. Building upon this work, (Wan et al., 2024a) introduced a more flexible approach
147   by replacing the exact match requirement with MinED strategy for more robust token alignment,
148   especially in cases where slight variations between tokens could still preserve semantic equivalence.
149
      Later, (Wan et al., 2024b) refined further in cross-lingual applications, incorporating statistical
      mapping frequencies between source and target tokens to better account for the probabilistic nature
150
      of token co-occurrence, leadning to a prominent chat performance.
151
152   However, existing methods remain limited by their reliance on surface-level token correspondences
153   (i.e., based solely on the strings it comprises), which leverage minimum edit distance to align the
154   logit. However, besides using edit distance as one metric, our method advances this by incorporating
155
      the corresponding logit values into the individual cost within the transport framework. Optimization
      is performed at both the "surface-level" and "logit-level."

188   3.1       P ROBLEM S TATEMENT & OVERALL O BJECTIVE
189
190
      Let t represent a sequence of text sampled from a corpus C. A probabilistic distribution matrix
      P ∈ RN ×V is obtained by evaluating a large language model (LLM) on t, where N corresponds to
191
      the sequence length, and V denotes the size of the vocabulary. The i-th row of this matrix represents
192
      the predicted probability distribution over the vocabulary for the i-th token in the sequence. In
193   the context of combining two LLMs (source and target), we consider the probabilistic distribution
194   matrices Ps ∈ RL×V s for the source model and Pt ∈ RN ×Vt for the target model, where L and
195   N denote the sequence lengths, and Vs and Vt represent the vocabulary sizes of the source and
196   target models, respectively. When these models employ different tokenization schemes, misalignment
197   between the tokens of the source and target models arises, thereby complicating the integration of
198   their probabilistic outputs. Addressing this issue is essential for effectively combining the outputs
199   of both models. The traditional approach seeks to ensure consistency between the target model’s
200   predictions, denoted as Qt , and the fused representation Pf , which encapsulates the knowledge from
201
      the source model. The knowledge fusion loss is formulated as LFusion = −Et∼C [D(Qt , Pf )], where
      D(·, ·) is a discrepancy function (such as cross-entropy or KL divergence) measuring the difference
202
      between the predicted and fused probability distributions. The fused output Pf is a probabilistic
203
      distribution matrix that represents the combined strengths of both the source and target models,
204   formally defined as Pf = MatrixAlignment(Ps , Pt ).
205
206   In this work, we propose PTA-LLM, a framework designed to resolve discrepancies between the
207
      tokenization schemes of the source and target models. The principal objective is to minimize the
      divergence between the target model’s probabilistic predictions Pt and the corresponding one-hot
208
      encoded label matrix Ot ∈ 0, 1N ×V , where each row of Ot indicates the correct token as a one-
209
      hot vector. Specifically, we define a causal language modeling (CLM) loss, which measures this
210   divergence, as LCLM = −Et∼C [D(Qt , Ot )], where D(·, ·) is a discrepancy function, such as cross-
211   entropy or Kullback-Leibler divergence, between the predicted probabilities and the true labels.
212   Consequently, the overall training objective of our proposed method is to optimize a weighted
213   combination of the CLM loss and the fusion loss, formalized as L = λLCLM + (1 − λ)LFusion , where
214   λ ∈ [0, 1] is a hyperparameter controlling the trade-off between the causal language modeling loss
215   and the fusion objective. This ensures that the target model can effectively learn from both its own
      predictions and the knowledge transferred from the source model.

      3.2   P ROBABILISTIC T OKEN A LIGNMENT
217
218   Dynamic Token Pairing The task of aligning two distinct probabilistic distribution matrices,
219   Ps ∈ RL×Vs and Pt ∈ RN ×Vt , where L and N represent the sequence lengths and Vs and Vt
220   represent the vocabulary sizes, respectively, poses a significant computational challenge due to the
221   inherent differences in both sequence length and vocabulary size. The core problem involves finding
222   a suitable alignment between tokens from the source model’s distribution Ps and those from the
223   target model’s distribution Pt . More precisely, for each token Sj (j ∈ [1, L]) from Ps , we aim to pair
224
      it with a corresponding token Tk (k ∈ [1, N ]) from Pt .
225   Given that there are L × N potential pairings between these tokens, employing brute-force methods
226   to explore all possible combinations would be computationally prohibitive, especially as the sequence
227   lengths and vocabulary sizes grow. To address this, we introduce dynamic token pairing, which
228   provides an efficient way to systematically explore the space of possible pairings and compute an
229   optimal alignment. This approach allows for the minimization of computational complexity while
230
      ensuring the best mapping between the source and target tokens.
231   Formally, given two sequences of tokens [S1:L , T1:N ], our objective is to find an alignment that
232   minimizes the overall cost associated with transforming one sequence into the other. Thus, we define
233   the recursion function as:
234                   f (k, j) = min{f (k − 1, j) + c(Tk , Sj ),
235
                                     f (k, j − 1) + c(Tk , Sj ),                                          (1)
236
                                     f (k − 1, j − 1) + c(Tk , Sj )}, k ∈ [1, N ], j ∈ [1, L]
237
238    where f (k, j) represents the total cost of aligning the subsequences T1:k and S1:j , while c(Tk , Sj )
239   denotes the predefined cost or distance metric between tokens. In contrast to traditional alignment
240   methods (Mingers, 1989; Peterson, 2009), which typically enforce a one-to-one correspondence
241   between elements in the two sequences, our approach introduces generality by relaxing this constraint.
242   Specifically, our formulation allows for the dynamic possibility that one token in s may align with
243   multiple tokens in t and vice versa, depending on the characteristics of the tokenization schemes and
244
      the specific requirements of the alignment task.
245   By adopting this dynamic token paring strategy, our method is able to handle discrepancies between
246   the tokenization schemes of the source and target models, ensuring that the probabilistic distributions
247   Ps and Pt can be meaningfully aligned, even in cases where their underlying token structures differ
248   significantly. This enhanced flexibility is particularly useful in scenarios where the vocabulary sizes
249   and token sequences vary substantially, providing a more robust solution to the alignment problem in
250
      the context of knowledge fusion between models.

      involves accurately performing logit-level alignment to address the token ID misalignment that
253
      arises due to the use of different tokenization schemes. Specifically, for each token pair Sj ∈ RVs
254
      and Tk ∈ RVt , the objective of token alignment is to match the logits from the source token with
255
      the corresponding logits from the target token in order to achieve consistent token representations
256
      between the models. The resulting fused token distribution, denoted as Tˆk , can be formally defined
257
      as:
258                                     Tˆk = TokenAlignment (Sj , Tk ) ,                               (2)
259

260

261

262   token fusion for each pairing can be reformulated from the perspective of distribution learning, where
263   the goal is to minimize discrepancies between the two token distributions. More formally, this can be

265                                         P̂ = arg min L (Sj , Tk ) ,                                  (3)

269   The goal is to minimize this loss, ensuring that the information from the source logits is effectively

328   Training details. We fine-tune the Llama-2 7B model using a batch size of 256 and a maximum

      • The Grade School Math (Cobbe et al., 2021), proposed by OpenAI, comprises a wide variety
334
      of conceptually simple grade school-level word problems and serves as a benchmark to assess the
335
      shortcomings of language models in handling multi-step mathematical reasoning. We evaluate it
336   using the accuracy (8 shot) under the lm-evaluation-harness framework (Gao et al., 2024).
337
      • Big-Bench Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022) is a benchmark to evaluate the general reasoning
338
      ability of LLMs, containing 23 multiple-choice tasks and 4 free-form generation tasks from the

      • MultiPL-E (ME) (Cassano et al., 2022) is a multilingual programming benchmark to assess the
342   commonsense ability of LLMs, consisting of 18 different programming languages with 17 parallel
343   datasets translated from the Python benchmark (Chen et al., 2021). We evaluate it using pass@1 (Chen
344   et al., 2021) based on 20 generated samples for each question in 10 popular programming languages
345   under the bigcode-evaluation-hardness framework (Ben Allal et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2024a).
346   • Measuring Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a
347   massive multitask test consisting of multiple-choice questions from various branches of knowledge

349   science and law) that people must study to learn. We evaluate it using the classification accuracy
350   under the open-instruct framework.
351   • ToxiGen (Hartvigsen et al., 2022) is a large-scale machine-generated dataset for adversarial and
352   implicit hate speech detection used to evaluate the safty ability of LLMs, which contains implicitly
353   toxic and benign sentences mentioning 14 minority groups. We evaluate it using the non-toxicity rate
354   (i.e., 1 - reported toxicity rate) under the open-instruct framework.
355   • TyDi QA (Clark et al., 2020) is a benchmark for information-seeking question answering in
356   typologically diverse languages to asses the multilingual ability of LLMs. It covers 9 different
357   languages including korean, arabic, indonesian, etc. We evaluate it using the EM accuracy under the
358   open-instruct framework.
359   Baselines. In our experiments, we evaluate the performance of PTA-LLM with three sets of
360   baselines: (1) Source LLMs, including Llama-2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023), OpenLLaMA 7B (Geng
361   & Liu, 2023), and MPT 7B(Team, 2023); (2) Llama-2 CLM, a Llama-2 7B model that further fine
362   tuned on MiniPile using the traditional causal language modeling objective; and (3) F USE LLM
363   (Wan et al., 2024a), a Llama-2 7B model trained on MiniPile with an emphasis on integrating the
364   capabilities of multiple source models under the knowledge fusion paradigm.
365   Reproducibility. PTA-LLM is implemented in Pytorch Paszke et al. (2019) using the Huggingface
366   Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020), accelerated by FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022). Training
367   is conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100-80GB GPUs (approximately 26 hours for a single epoch) and 8
368   NVIDIA H100-80GB GPUs (approximately 17 hours for a single epoch), while conducting evaluation
369   on 4 NVIDIA A100-40GB GPUs (time varies depending on the amount of benchmark data used). To
370   guarantee reproducibility, our full implementation shall be publicly released upon paper acceptance.

458   4.4              S TUDY OF I NTERPRETABILITY
459
460
      Although the emergence of knowledge fusion as a model fusion paradigm has gained huge attention,
      the underlying rationale remains unclear. In this section, we tend to provide distribution insights into
461
      token alignment’s mechanisms and offer guidance for its optimal utilization. As shown in Fig. 3, we
462
      delve into a specific context to have an in-depth analysis of token alignment. Given we have previously
463   aligned tokens like “the private", we need to align the token pair from the source model and target
464   model to form the next fused token. For tokens from the target model (i.e., Llama-2), we can visualize
465   their top-10 logits and corresponding indices in a 2D space (see Fig. 3 (a), left coordinate, showing
466   only 3 logits in a high-level representation). This is done by first using the target model’s tokenizer
467   to extract token features, followed by dimensionality reduction using Isomap (Balasubramanian &
468   Schwartz, 2002) and PCA (Abdi & Williams, 2010) (the variance ratio is reported as 95.60% on
469   average in the table in Fig. 3 (a).). Their relative position can reflect the underlying meaning of this
470   indice, and the relative size indicates the magnitude of their corresponding logit. For F USE LLM,
471
      traditional hard mapping does not consider their logit and maps each indice to another with a pre-
      defined strategy, acting like a “moving" (i.e., change the location without modifying the size) in
472
      high-level understanding. In contrast, our method leverages the complete distribution, “transporting"
473
      (i.e., distribute the size into current location) the optimal logit into existing indices. Quantitatively, we
474   further compute the average compactness of each token (i.e., the logit-weighted Euclidean distance
475   from each point to its center) and the similarity of each token center to the target one (i.e., the
476   Euclidean distance from each center point to the target one) in 100 random samples, as shown in
477   the table in Fig. 3 (a). It empirically demonstrates that our method generates a more coherent fused
478   token, as evidenced by a more compact representation (i.e., lower inner distance: 239.44 vs. 257.83)
479   and a more consistent representation (i.e., lower center distance: 22.25 vs. 136.95).
480   As shown in the down part of Fig. 3, we can visually compare the distribution of PTA-LLM fused
481   token with the target token and F USE LLM fused token. Specifically, a more consistent marginal
482   feature distribution between PTA-LLM and target token can be observed from Fig. 3 (b) and Fig. 3
483   (d), where F USE LLM exhibits significantly greater distortion in the overall token representation. The
484   more compact and coherent overall token distribution after employing probabilistic token alignment
485   is aligned with the quantitative results. More implementation details will be elaborated in §B.

511   optimal transport is the threshold, which regulates the convergence of the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi,
512   2013). A lower value of threshold results in more iterations of transport, enforcing a stricter distribu-
513   tion constraint. As illustrated in Table 3a (up), the lower optimal temperature preference indicates
514   that a stricter constraint may form a more coherent fusion and thus bring a greater performance gain.

519

520
      observe a higher performance in Table 3b (up) when the weight is smaller within a reasonable range
521   (see detailed analysis in §E), since a lower value indicates more emphasis in our fused matrix.
522   Fusion Functions. In §3.3, we employ a distribution matrix with minimum cross entropy (MinCE)
523   to define the source distribution matrix during training. Additionally, we implement a weighted
524   average of distribution matrices based on cross entropy (AvgCE). A comparison of these two
525   approaches is provided in Table 3b (down). The results show that PTA-LLM using MinCE consistently
526   outperforms AvgCE across all benchmarks, which is consistent with Wan et al. (2024a).

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
      We present Probabilistic Token Alignment for Large Language Model Fusion (PTA-LLM), a
530
      distribution-wise token alignment approach that leverages the optimal transport framework through
531   reformulation. It has merits in: i) demonstrating generality across benchmarks through a coherent
532   representation fusion; ii) offering a flexible and adaptive solution to various contexts, especially stable
533   in addressing challenging tasks; and iii) thoroughly investigating the essence of token alignment to
534   elucidate the coherent token we fused. However, a limitation of our approach is that the Sinkhorn-
535   Knopp algorithm runs in O(   e n32 ) time, which reduces the token alignment efficiency. Despite the
                                       ϵ
536   observation that in practice only 3 Sinkhorn loops per training iteration are often sufficient for model
537   representation, which amounts to ∼13.75% aligning delay on MiniPile compared with F USE LLM.
538   It would be interesting to investigate further lower complexity (i.e., greenkhorn (Luo et al., 2023))
539   algorithim to compute the optimal transport. As a whole, we conclude that the outcomes elucidated
      in this paper impart essential understandings and necessitate further exploration within this realm.

716
717
      Our training procedures are implemented based on the publicly available code from Wan et al. (2024a),
      with modifications made specifically to the token alignment module. For better understanding, the
718
      following is a concise pseudo code of §3.2. Specifically, we perform optimal transport on logits after
719
      applying the softmax function to reduce the impact of extreme values (e.g., extreme large, small, or
720   negative values) that could otherwise distort the transport cost. Importantly, conducting transport
721   in logit space differs fundamentally from transporting mass in probability space due to the distinct
722   normalization terms associated with the source and target spaces. We plan to investigate it further in
723   the future. Our full implementation shall be publicly released upon paper acceptance.
724
725   Algorithm 2 Probabilistic Token Alignment
726   Require: Tokenizer, input IDs, per step logits, per step indices from both source and target Model.
727    1: Convert input IDs to token sequence.
728    2: Use Dynamic Programming in 1 to obtain token pairing between two token sequences.
729    3: for each token pairing do
730    4:   if it is a one-to-one token pairing then
731    5:       use the sinkhorn algorithim in 3.3 under the optimal transport framework, considering per
732             step logits and indices from source and target token
733    6:   else
734
       7:       use the one-hot logits
       8:   end if
735
       9: end for
736
      10: return Aligned matrix

       Discussion. Two potential factors may explain why the knowledge fusion objective outperforms the
1039   traditional CLM approach: First, the CLM objective employs one-hot vectors as the golden labels,
1040   which fails to capture the nuanced information each token might convey. This approach provides the
1041   same penalty for completely incorrect predictions as for predictions that select an incorrect token but
1042   retain semantically relevant context. In other words, the CLM objective does not reward predictions
1043   that are "almost correct," which limits its capacity to encourage fine-grained improvements. Second,
1044   the fusion objective incorporates representations from diverse source models through distillation,
1045   enabling it to capitalize on the complementary strengths of each model. It provides more fine-grained
1046   context information for alignment.
1047   Regarding the performance, our performance improvements are constrained by the suboptimal
1048   performance of certain source LLMs relative to the target LLM on specific tasks, which inevitably
1049   impacts the quality of the fusion results. We also observe that the performance improvement could be
1050   significantly enhanced by increasing the size of the continued training datasets. Notably, the original
1051   MiniPile (Kaddour, 2023) comprises only 8% coding-related data. By incorporating the GitHub
1052   datasets from the Pile (Gao et al., 2020) in our priliminary experiments, it is possible to achieve
1053
       greater performance gains, particularly in coding-related downstream tasks.
