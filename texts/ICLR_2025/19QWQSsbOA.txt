009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The advance of diffusion-based generative models in recent years has revolution-
012            ized state-of-the-art (SOTA) techniques in a wide variety of image analysis and
013            synthesis tasks, whereas their adaptation on image restoration, particularly within
014            computational microscopy remains theoretically and empirically underexplored.
015            In this research, we introduce a multi-scale generative model that enhances con-
016
               ditional image restoration through a novel exploitation of the Brownian Bridge
               process within wavelet domain. By initiating the Brownian Bridge diffusion process
017
               specifically at the lowest-frequency subband and applying generative adversarial
018
               networks at subsequent multi-scale high-frequency subbands in the wavelet do-
019            main, our method provides significant acceleration during training and sampling
020            while sustaining a high image generation quality and diversity on par with SOTA
021            diffusion models. Experimental results on various computational microscopy and
022            imaging tasks confirm our method’s robust performance and its considerable reduc-
023            tion in its sampling steps and time. This pioneering technique offers an efficient
024            image restoration framework that harmonizes efficiency with quality, signifying a
025            major stride in incorporating cutting-edge generative models into computational
026            microscopy workflows.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Within the last decade, the landscape of image synthesis has been radically transformed by the advent
031   of generative models (GMs) (Song et al., 2020b; Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019). Among their
032   broad success in various image synthesis applications, image restoration, including super-resolution,
033   shadow removal, inpainting, etc, have caught much attention due to their importance in various
034   practical scenarios. Image restoration aims to recover high-quality target image from low-quality
035   images measured by an imaging system with assorted degradation effects, e.g., downsampling,
036   aberration and noise. Numerous tasks in photography, sensing and microscopy can be formulated
037   as image restoration problems, and therefore the importance of image restoration algorithms is
038   self-evident in practical scenarios (Isola et al., 2017; Kupyn et al., 2018; Weigert et al., 2018; Wang
039
      et al., 2019).
040   Due to the ill-posedness of most image restoration problems, the application of generative learning
041   becomes crucial for achieving high-quality image reconstruction. The wide applications of deep
042   learning (DL)-based generative models in image restoration began with the success of generative
043   adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014). The emergence of conditional adversarial
044   learning achieved unprecedented success and outperformed classical algorithms in various applica-
045
      tions of computational imaging and microscopy (Zhu et al., 2017; Isola et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
      2018c; Kupyn et al., 2018; Nazeri et al., 2018; Karras et al., 2019; Weigert et al., 2018; Wang et al.,
046
      2019). Although GANs have achieved remarkable success in image restoration, they are also known
047
      to be prone to training instability and mode collapse. These issues significantly restrict the diversity of
048   outputs produced by GAN models (Kodali et al., 2017; Gui et al., 2021). Recently, diffusion models
049   (DMs) were intensively studied and outperformed GANs in various image generation tasks (Ho
050   et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). Derived from the stochastic diffusion process, DMs employ
051   neural networks to approximate the reverse diffusion process and sample target images from white
052   noise with high quality and good mode coverage. Moreover, DMs have also been applied to image
053   restoration, including super-resolution, dehazing, colorization, etc (Saharia et al., 2022b; Rombach
      et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Batzolis et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022a; Luo et al., 2023a). Most of the

101
102   To address the aforementioned limitations, here we introduce a novel multi-scale conditional genera-
103
      tive model (MSCGM) for image restoration based on multi-scale wavelet transform and Brownian
      bridge stochastic process. For one thing, multi-scale wavelet transform effectively and losslessly
104
      compresses the spatial dimensions of conditional images, eliminating the lossy encoding process
105
      of current autoencoder-based DMs. Notably, our method does not involve the pre-training of au-
106   toencoders in competitive methods on a sufficiently large and diverse dataset sampled from the
107   image domain. For another, Brownian bridge stochastic process incorporates the modelling of low-

108
      quality conditional image into both the forward and reverse diffusion process and better utilizes the
109   information of the conditional images. Besides, we theoretically analyze the distributions of low-
110   and high-frequency wavelet subbands and apply Brownian bridge diffusion process (BBDP) and
111   adversarial learning to the multi-scale generation of low- and high-frequency subbands, respectively.
112   In sum, the contributions of this work are three-fold:
113
      1. We are the first to factorize the conditional image generation within multi-scale wavelet domains,
114
      establishing a theoretical groundwork for a multi-scale conditional generative modeling;
115
116   2. Capitalizing on the unique distribution characteristics of wavelet subbands, we propose the
117   innovative MSCGM, which seamlessly integrates BBDP and adversarial learning;
118   3. We evaluate the MSCGM on various image restoration tasks, demonstrating its superior perfor-
119   mance in both sampling speed and image quality than competitive methods.

      2.1   M ICROSCOPY IMAGE RESTORATION
124
125   As an important branch of computational imaging, computational microscopy springs up in recent
126   years and aims to restore high-quality, multi-dimensional images from low-quality, low-dimensional
127   measurements, usually with under-resourced equipment. Since the first work on microscopy image
128   super-resolution reported in 2017 Rivenson et al. (2017), DL has enabled a wide spectrum of novel
129   applications that were impossible with conventional optical technologies, e.g., microscopy image
130   super-resolution surpassing the physical resolution limit of microscopic imaging systems Wang et al.
131   (2019), volumetric imaging reconstructing 3D sample volumes from sparse 2D measurements Wu
132   et al. (2019), and virtual image labelling to match the contrast conventionally provided by chemical or
133
      biological markers Rivenson et al. (2019). Compared to general image restoration in computational
      imaging, microscopy image restoration mainly differs in two aspects: (1) The degeneration process,
134
      including the transfer function, noise and aberration of the imaging system is generally complex,
135
      unknown and hard to measure precisely; and such degeneration process could vary significantly in
136   real-world scenarios due to the variations of subjects, hardware and imaging protocols. (2) Strict
137   pixel-wise correspondence between output and ground truth images and consistency with physical
138   laws are generally emphasized Barbastathis et al. (2019).
139
140   2.2   G ENERATIVE MODELS
141
142   GANs are well-known for generating high-qaulity, photorealistic samples rapidly Goodfellow et al.
143   (2014); Gui et al. (2021). Through training a discriminator that tells ground truth images apart from
144   fake images generated by the generator network, GANs outperformed traditional CNNs trained with
145   hand-crafted, pixel-based structural losses such as L1 and L2 by providing a high-level, learnable
146   perceptual objective. Conditional GANs such as Pix2Pix Isola et al. (2017), pix2pix HD Wang
147
      et al. (2018c) and starGAN Choi et al. (2018) have been successfully applied in a wide spectrum of
      image-to-image translation and image restoration tasks, including image colorization Nazeri et al.
148
      (2018), style transfer Karras et al. (2019), image deblurring Kupyn et al. (2018), etc. Unsupervised
149
      image-to-image translation has also been extensively explored, such as cycleGAN Zhu et al. (2017),
150   UNIT Liu et al. (2017), DualGAN Yi et al. (2017), etc. In the fields of biomedical and microscopy
151   imaging, researchers have also explored the applications of GANs, e.g., reconstructing low-dose CT
152   and MRI images Yang et al. (2018); Hammernik et al. (2018), denoising microscopy images Weigert
153   et al. (2018), super-resolving diffraction-limited microscopy images Wang et al. (2019), among others.
154   Recently, WGSR Korkmaz et al. (2024) has also provided wavelet guided GAN model for image
155   super-resolution tasks.
156   Alternatively, transformer and related architectures have recently emerged and shown superior
157   performance over convolutional neural networks (CNNs)-based GANs. Swin transformer Liu et al.
158   (2021) and SwinIR Liang et al. (2021) have established a strong transformer-based baseline with
159   competitive performance to CNNs. TransGAN substituted CNNs in the common GAN framework
160   with transformers and improved the overall performance Jiang et al. (2021). More recently, diffusion
161   models (DMs) have been introduced and proved to be the state-of-the-art generative model in
      various image generation benchmarks Ho et al. (2020); Nichol & Dhariwal (2021). Dhariwal et

      4.3     R ESULTS AND COMPARISON
408   In this section, we first evaluate and compare our method with the current state-of-the-art model
409   (cross-modality super-resolution (CMSR)Wang et al. (2019)) on two microscopy image restoration
410   tasks with different samples, and the experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves
411   the best performance on these tasks. To further validate the model’s generalization capability on
412   standard nature image datasets, we then conduct comparative evaluations on three different natural
413   image restoration tasks against multiple baselines, achieving similarly excellent restoration results.
414   First, we apply our method to microscopy images, where the degradation process is complex and
415   unknown. Given the pronounced contrast and sparsity inherent in microscopy images, it is crucial to
416   use generative models capable of handling multi-modal distributions to adapt effectively to complex
417   microscopy datasets. We utilize our method to perform super-resolution on microscopy images of
418   nano-beads and HeLa cells, transforming diffraction-limited confocal images to achieve resolution
419   beyond the optical diffraction limit and match the image quality of STED microscopy. Next, we
420   assess the adaptability of our method to various image restorations tasks of natural images, including a
421   4× super-resolution task on DIV2K dataset, a shadow removal task on natural images (ISTD dataset)
422
      and on a low-light image enhancement task on natural images (LOL dataset). Through comparison
      against competitive methods on various testbeds, we demonstrate the superior effectiveness and
423
      versatility of our method for image restoration.

532
533
      To address the limitations of exsiting diffusion models in conditional image restoration, we demon-
534   strate a novel generative model for image restoration based on Brownian bridge process and multi-
535   scale wavelet transform. By factorizing the image restoration process in the multi-scale wavelet
536   domains, we utilize Brownian bridge diffusion process and generative adversarial networks to recover
537   different wavelet subbands according to their distribution properties, consequently accelerate the
538   sampling speed significantly and achieve high sample quality and diversity competitive to diffusion
539   model baselines. Future implementation could integrate standard acceleration techniques for diffusion
      models.

       F    DATASETS AND IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1252
1253
1254   For super-resolution experiments on DIV2K, the LR images were bicubically downsampled to 64×64
1255   center-cropped patches and bilinearly upsampled to 256 × 256 as the input images, and the HR
1256   images were center-cropped as 256 × 256. For shadow removal on the ISTD dataset, we used a
1257   similar method, pairing original shadow images with their shadow-free counterparts, both consistently
1258   sized to maintain uniformity in processing.
1259
       For super-resolution experiments involving HeLa cell nuclei and nano-beads, we utilized a Leica
1260   TCS SP8 stimulated emission depletion (STED) confocal microscope, equipped with a Leica HC PL
1261   APO 100×/1.40-NA Oil STED White objective. The samples were prepared on high-performance
1262   coverslips (170µm ± 10µm, Carl Zeiss Microscopy), facilitating precise imaging. In the staining
1263   process, HeLa cell nuclei were treated with Rabbit anti-Histone H3 and Atto-647N Goat anti-rabbit
1264   IgG antibodies. The nano-beads, used for the STED experiments, were processed with methanol and
1265   ProLong Diamond antifade reagents. Both samples were excited with a 633-nm wavelength laser.
1266   Emission detection was carried out using a HyD SMD photodetector (Leica Microsystems) through a
1267   645–752-nm bandpass filter.
1268   For super-resolution experiments on microscopy images of nanobeads, the low-resolution (LR)
1269   images were acquired with specific settings (16 times line average and 30 times frame average for
1270   nano-beads; 8 times line average and 6 times frame average for cell nuclei), ensuring the acquisition
1271   of high-quality images necessary for our computer vision analysis. The scanning step size, i.e., the
1272   effective pixel size is 30 nm. Here, all LR and HR images were center cropped as 256 × 256 patches
1273   to match the setting in other experiments.
1274   AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) optimizers were used in all experiments with an initial learning
1275   rate of 1e-4. Models with exponential moving averaged parameters with a rate of 0.999 was saved
1276   and evaluated. The BBDM models at the coarsest wavelet scale were trained for 100000 steps with a
1277   batch size of 6.
1278   In our GAN training, we used a batch size of 3, with images cropped at 256x256 resolution. We
1279   employed AdamW optimizers, setting the learning rate at 1e-4 for the generator and 1e-5 for the
1280   discriminator. The training loss function is a weighted sum of diverse loss terms: L1 loss with a
1281   weight of 20.0, adversarial loss at 0.1, and structural similarity index measure (SSIM) Wang et al.
1282   (2004) loss weighted at 0.5. The training was conducted for 200 epochs.
1283
       All models were implemented on NVIDIA V100 graphic cards using PyTorch. For speed test, a batch
1284   size of 16 was used for BBDM, IR-SDE and Refusion models, and a batch size of 64 was used for
1285   MSCGM so that they consume equivalent computational resources.
