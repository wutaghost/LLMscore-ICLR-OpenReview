009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The availability of performant pre-trained models has led to a proliferation of
012            fine-tuned expert models that are specialized to a particular domain or task. This
013            has enabled the creation of powerful and adaptive routing-based “Model MoErg-
014            ing" (Yadav et al., 2024) methods with the goal of using expert modules to create
015            an aggregate system with improved performance or generalization. However, ex-
016
               isting MoErging methods often prioritize generalization to unseen tasks at the ex-
               pense of performance on held-in tasks. This limitation adversely impacts practical
017
               applicability, as real-world deployments require robust performance across both
018
               known and novel tasks. We observe that current token-level routing mechanisms
019            neglect the global semantic context of the input task. This token-wise indepen-
020            dence hinders effective expert selection, particularly for held-in tasks, as routing
021            decisions fail to incorporate the holistic semantic properties of the task. To address
022            this, we propose a novel method, Global and Local Instruction Driven Expert
023            Router (GLIDER) that integrates a multi-scale routing mechanism, encompassing
024            a semantic global router and a learned local router. As recent LLMs demonstrate
025            advanced reasoning capabilities for semantic-related contexts, the global router
026            leverages this ability to enhance expert selection. By utilizing the input query and
027
               an LLM, the router generates semantic task instructions that guide the retrieval
               of the most relevant experts across all layers. This global guidance is comple-
028
               mented by a local router that facilitates token-level routing decisions within each
029
               module, enabling finer control and enhanced performance on unseen and chal-
030            lenging tasks. Our experiments using T5-based expert models for T0 and FLAN
031            tasks demonstrate that GLIDER achieves substantially improved held-in perfor-
032            mance while maintaining strong generalization on held-out tasks. Additionally,
033            we perform ablations experiments to dive deeper into the components of GLIDER
034            and plot routing distributions to show that GLIDER can effectively retrieve the cor-
035            rect expert for held-in tasks while also demonstrating compositional capabilities
036            for held-out tasks. Our experiments highlight the importance of our multi-scale
037            routing that leverages LLM-driven semantic reasoning for MoErging methods.
038
               Our code is attached as supplementary material.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   The emergence of highly capable large language models (LLMs) has marked an increased atten-
043   tion in downstream task specialization. This specialization often leverages parameter-efficient fine-
044   tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), which introduce minimal trainable pa-
045   rameters (“adapters") to adapt pre-trained LLMs for specific tasks. The compact size of these spe-
046   cialized PEFT modules enables easy sharing of these modules, which has led to the distribution of
047   an evergrowing number of adapters on various platforms.
048   This proliferation of expert models, i.e. specialized adapters, has led to the development of methods
049   for re-using such experts to improve performance or generalization (Muqeeth et al., 2024; Ostapenko
050   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a). Central to these approaches are routing mechanisms that adap-
051   tively select relevant experts for a particular task or query. These routing methods have been referred
052   to as “Model MoErging” (Yadav et al., 2024) since they frequently share methodologies and ideas
053   with mixture-of-experts (MoE) models (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022) and
      model merging (Yadav et al., 2023b;a; Ilharco et al., 2022). However, MoE methods that train ex-


                                                         1

054
      perts jointly from scratch (Gupta et al., 2022) while MoErging utilizes a decentralized, community-
055   sourced pool of pre-trained experts. Furthermore, it departs from traditional model merging tech-
056   niques by dynamically and adaptively combining these experts, optimizing performance at the query
057   or task level. MoErging methods offer three key advantages: (1) They support decentralized model
058   development by reusing and routing among independently trained experts, reducing reliance on cen-
059   tralized resources. (2) They facilitate modular capability expansion and “transparency" in updates
060   as they either add or modify specialized expert models. 3) They allow for compositional generaliza-
061   tion by recombining fine-grained skills from various experts, extending the system’s abilities to new
062   unseen tasks beyond the capabilities of the individual expert models.
063   Most existing methods for MoErging often prioritize performance on either known expert tasks
064   (held-in) or generalization to unseen tasks (held-out) depending on their use cases (Chronopoulou
065   et al., 2023; Muqeeth et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). This specialization limits practical appli-
066   cability, as real-world deployments demand robust performance across both held-in and held-out
067   tasks. Consequently, existing methods exhibit suboptimal performance when evaluated on both
068   held-in and held-out tasks, often leading to suboptimal overall performance. For example, while
069
      Phatgoose (Muqeeth et al., 2024) demonstrate strong performance on held-out data, they do not per-
      form well on held-in tasks. We hypothesize that this gap arises from the model’s token-level routing
070
      mechanism. We show that for the held-in tasks the independent routing decisions at each layer,
071
      based solely on individual token embeddings, lack sufficient global context to retrieve the correct
072   expert for all token at every module. This leads to suboptimal routing which may propagate noise
073   through the network, further hindering accurate expert utilization in deeper layers. This highlights a
074   critical limitation of token-level approaches to handling both held-in tasks, which hence falls short
075   of the goal of building a routing system that seamlessly handles arbitrary queries. We believe that
076   adding a global routing mechanism based on semantic task information can further aid the token
077   level router for correct retrieval for held-in tasks. Hence, we ask the question.
078
079      (Q) Can we leverage LLMs to generate semantics-aware task instructions to guide routing
080
         mechanism to facilitate both specialization and generalization?
081
      This paper addresses the challenges by investigating the potential of leveraging the inherent rea-
082
      soning and generalization capabilities of LLMs to guide the routing process in an MoE-like model
083   composed of specialized LoRA modules. We introduce, Global and Local Instruction Driven Expert
084   Router (GLIDER) that hinges on a multi-scale routing mechanism that contains both local and global
085   routers as shown in Figure 2. The global router leverages LLM-generated, semantics-aware instruc-
086   tions (see Appendix A.2) to select the top-2 expert models for each input query across all the layers.
087   This high-level guidance is then complemented by a learned local router, which makes token-level
088   routing decisions at each module, enabling fine-grained control and improving performance on the
089   challenging held-out tasks. Through this framework, we highlight the crucial role of LLM reasoning
090   in unlocking the compositional generalization capabilities of MoE models.
091   To test the effectiveness of our GLIDER method, we follow Phatgoose (Muqeeth et al., 2024) and use
092   T5 models (Raffel et al., 2020) to create expert models for T0 held-in (Sanh et al., 2022) and FLAN
093   tasks (Longpre et al., 2023) and test performance on T0 held-in & held-out (Sanh et al., 2022) and
094   big-bench lite (BIG-bench authors, 2023) & hard tasks (Suzgun et al., 2022). Our key contributions
095   and findings are:
096          • We introduce GLIDER, which employs LLM-guided multi-scale global and local attention.
097            Our experiments show that GLIDER outperforms previous methods, significantly improv-
098            ing performance on held-in tasks (e.g. 6.6% over Phatgoose on T0 held-in) while also
099            enhancing zero-shot held-out compositional generalization (e.g. 0.9% over Phatgoose on
100            T0 held-out).
101          • We find that without LLM assistance, MoE models underperform individual specialized
102            models on held-in tasks by 8.2%. Incorporating semantic-aware instructions enables
103
               GLIDER to achieve comparable performance, demonstrating the LLM’s capacity to effec-
               tively infer task identity and guide module selection without explicit task labels.
104
             • GLIDER also maintains strong performance on held-out tasks, showcasing its adaptability
105
               and generalization capabilities. Our work highlights the critical role of LLMs in enhancing
106            MoE models’ compositional generalization, advancing the development of more robust and
107            versatile AI systems capable of handling both familiar and novel tasks.

      MoErging Methods. The abundance of specialized expert models has spurred the development
111   of techniques to leverage “experts" models for enhanced performance and generalization. Yadav
112   et al. (2024) in their recent survey called such techniques as “MoErging" 1 methods which rely on
113   adaptive routing mechanisms to select relevant experts for specific tasks or queries. These methods
114   can be broadly classified into four categories based on the design of their routing mechanisms.
115
      Embedding − Based Routing : This category encompasses methods that derive routing decisions
116
      from learned embeddings of expert training data. These methods typically compare a query em-
117   bedding against the learned expert embeddings to determine the optimal routing path. Examples
118   include AdapterSoup (Chronopoulou et al., 2023), Retrieval of Experts (Jang et al., 2023), Token-
119   Level Adaptation (Belofsky, 2023), LoraRetriever (Zhao et al., 2024), Mo’LoRA (Maxine, 2023),
120   the embedding-based approach of Airoboros (Durbin, 2024), and Dynamic Adapter Merging (Cheng
121   et al., 2024).
122
      Classifier − Based Routing : This category consists of methods that train a router to function as
123   a classifier. This router is trained to predict the optimal routing path based on features extracted from
124   expert datasets or unseen data. Representative methods in this category include Zooter (Lu et al.,
125   2023), Branch-Train-Mix (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024), Routing with Benchmark Datasets (Shnitzer
126   et al., 2023), Routoo (Mohammadshahi et al., 2024), and RouteLLM (Ong et al., 2024). The key
127   distinction between embedding-based and classifier-based routing lies in the router’s architecture
128   and training methodology. While embedding-based routing often employs a nearest neighbor ap-
129   proach, classifier-based routing typically relies on logistic regression or analogous classification
130   techniques.
131   Task − Specific Routing : This category focuses on methods tailored to enhance performance on
132   specific target tasks. These methods learn a task-specific routing distribution over the target dataset
133   to optimize performance for the given task. Methods in this category include LoraHub (Huang
134   et al., 2023), LoRA-Flow (Wang et al., 2024), AdapterFusion (Pfeiffer et al., 2021), π-Tuning (Wu
135   et al., 2023), Co-LLM (Shen et al., 2024), Weight-Ensembling MoE (Tang et al., 2024), MoLE (Wu
136   et al., 2024), MeteoRA (Xu et al., 2024), PEMT (Lin et al., 2024), MixDA (Diao et al., 2023), and
137
      Twin-Merging (Lu et al., 2024).
138   Routerless Methods : This final category encompasses methods that do not rely on an explicitly
139   trained router. Instead, these methods often employ alternative mechanisms, such as heuristics or
140   rule-based systems, for routing decisions. Examples include Arrow ↗ (Ostapenko et al., 2024),
141   PHATGOOSE (Muqeeth et al., 2024), the “ask an LLM" routing of Airoboros (Durbin, 2024) and
142   LlamaIndex (Liu, 2024).
143
144
      Model Merging. Model merging (Yadav et al., 2023b; Choshen et al., 2022; Wortsman et al.,
      2022; Ramé et al., 2022; Matena & Raffel, 2022; Ilharco et al., 2022; Tam et al., 2023; Jin et al.,
145
      2022; Yang et al., 2023) consolidates multiple independently trained models with identical archi-
146
      tectures into a unified model that preserves individual model capabilities. While simple parameter
147   averaging suffices for models within a linearly connected low-loss parameter space (McMahan et al.,
148   2017; Stich, 2018; Frankle et al., 2020; Wortsman et al., 2021), more sophisticated techniques are
149   necessary for complex scenarios. For instance, task vectors facilitate merging expert models trained
150   on diverse domains (Ilharco et al., 2022). Additionally, methods like weighted merging using Fisher
151   Importance Matrices (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Tam et al., 2023) and TIES-Merging, which addresses
152   sign disagreements and redundancy (Yadav et al., 2023b) offers improved performance. As a non-
153   adaptive expert aggregation method, merging serves as a fundamental baseline for numerous Model
154   Editing with Regularization (MoErging) techniques.

      tably, investigations into task-relatedness (Standley et al., 2020; Bingel & Søgaard, 2017; Achille
      et al., 2019; Vu et al., 2020; Zamir et al., 2018; Mou et al., 2016) provide guidance for designing rout-
158
      ing mechanisms, while MTL architectures addressing the balance between shared and task-specific
159
      knowledge (Misra et al., 2016; Ruder et al., 2017; Meyerson & Miikkulainen, 2017; Zaremoodi

      MoE for Multitask Learning. Recent research has extensively investigated mixture-of-experts

167   These approaches generally fall into two categories: (1) Example Routing: Studies like Muqeeth

262   4     M ETHODOLOGY
263
264   To recap, our goal is to build a MoErging method that dynamically routing queries to a diverse pool
265   of specialized expert models, addressing the challenge of effectively handling queries from various
266   tasks and ensuring both held-in and held-out performance. Our proposed method, Global and Local
267   Instruction Driven Expert Router (GLIDER), leverages a combination of local and global routing
268   vectors to achieve this goal. Specifically, contributors train task-specific routing vectors, while a
269   large language model (LLM) generates a global semantic task instructions which are then converted
      to global instruction routing vectors. During inference, these local and global routing vectors are

      combined to perform top-k discrete routing, directing queries to the most suitable expert model.
271   This process is visualized in Figure 2 and described in detail below.

      4.1   E XPERT T RAINING P ROTOCOL
274
275   Our expert training protocol T takes as input the base model parameters, θbase , and a dataset d
276   and performs three steps to obtain the required output. First, we train the LoRA experts (ϕ), then
277   train the local routing vectors (l) while keeping the LoRA experts fixed. Finally, we train obtain
278   the global routing vector (g) by using an LLM and an embedding model. Formally, in our case,
279   ϕ, Ψ = {l, g} ← T (θbase , d) which are then shared with the aggregators to create the routing
280   mechanism. We described these steps in detail below.
281
282   PEFT Training of Expert Model. GLIDER is compatible with expert models trained using
283   parameter-efficient finetuning methods (e.g. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), Adapters (Houlsby et al.,
284
      2019)) that introduce small trainable modules throughout the model. We focus on PEFT experts
      because they typically have lower computational and communication costs than full-model fine-
285
      tuning (Yadav et al., 2023a), making it easier to train and share expert models. Following Phat-
286
      goose (Muqeeth et al., 2024), this work specifically focuses in LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) due to
287   its widespread use. LoRA introduces a module comprising the trainable matrices B ∈ Rd×r and
288   A ∈ Rr×n in parallel to each linear layer with parameters W ∈ Rd×n . Given the tth input token
289   activation ui , LoRA modifies the output of the linear layer from Wui to Wui + αr ∗ BAui where α
290   is a constant and usually is set to 1. During training, the matrices A and B are trainable while
291   the original linear layer W is kept frozen. We denote the final trained expert parameters with
292   ϕ = {(A1 , B1 ), . . . , (Am , Bm )}, where m is the number of modules in the model.
293
294   Training Local Routing Vectors. Following Phatgoose (Muqeeth et al., 2024), after training the
295   PEFT modules on their dataset, a local router is introduced before each PEFT module. This router,
296   employing a shared vector across all queries and tokens, dynamically determines the utilization of
297
      the PEFT module based on the input token activations. The router is trained for a small number
      of steps using the same dataset and objective as the PEFT module, while keeping the expert PEFT
298
      parameters fixed. This process effectively learns to associate the token activation patterns with the
299
      learned expert model. For LoRA, the local router, represented by a trainable vector v ∈ Rd , controls
300   the contribution of the PEFT module to the final output. This results in a modified linear layer of the
301   form Wui + αr ∗ BAui ∗ σ(vT ui ), where α, W, B, and A are frozen, and the local router v is learned.
302   We denote the final local routing vectors as l = {v1 , . . . , vm } where m is the number of modules in
303   the model.
304
305   Creating LLM-Aided Global Routing Vector. The local routing vectors capture the intricate re-
306   lationships between token activations and expert models, enabling efficient query routing in cases
307   where no dedicated expert is available. Conversely, for queries corresponding to held-in tasks, di-
308   rect retrieval of the relevant expert model is preferred to process the full query. For this purpose,
309   we create a global routing vector that utilizes an LLM to generate a semantically-informed instruc-
310
      tion, termed as task description, which effectively captures the essence of the kind of queries the
      expert can handle. We prompt an LLM with three randomly selected in-context examples to gen-
311
      erate this task description. We used the gpt-4-turbo model along with the prompt provided
312
      in Appendix A. The resulting task description is then embedded using an off-the-shelf embedding
313   model, specifically the nomic-embed-text-v1.5 model, to produce a global routing vector for
314   the task. We denote the global routing vector as g ∈ Rdg .

343   to enforce retrieval and vice versa. Hence, we propose to scale the global scores with α, where
344   α = γ ∗ I{max(sglob )−p>0} + β, where p is the cosine similarity threshold, and γ and β are scaling hy-
345   perparameters. Using our ablation experiments in Section 5.4, we    √set p = 0.8, γ = 100 and β = 3.
346   We then obtain the final affinity score s ∈ RN = α∗sglob + slocm / N. Then GLIDER selects the top-k
347   experts after performing softmax over the final affinity score s as Etop = top-k(softmax(s)).
                                                                                        P
348   Finally, the output of the module for token activation ui is computed as Wui + k∈Etop wk ∗ Bk Ak ui .

352   5.1   S ETTING
353
354   Dataset. Our experiments utilize the multitask prompted training setup introduced by Sanh et al.
355   (2021), which has become a standard benchmark for evaluating generalization to unseen tasks
356
      (Chung et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022). Following Phat-
      goose (Muqeeth et al., 2024), we employ LM-adapted T5.1.1 XL (Lester et al., 2021) as our
357
      base model which is a 3B parameter variant of T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) further trained on the C4
358
      dataset using a standard language modeling objective. For held-out evaluations, we follow Phat-
359   goose (Muqeeth et al., 2024) and use three held-out benchmark collections. We use the T0 held-out
360   (T0HO) datasets used in Sanh et al. (2021) and the two subsets of BIG-bench (BIG-bench authors,
361   2023). Specifically, we use BIG-bench Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022), consisting of 23 challeng-
362   ing datasets, and BIG-bench Lite (BBL) (BIG-bench authors, 2023), a lightweight 24-dataset proxy
363   for the full benchmark. Similar to Muqeeth et al. (2024), we exclude certain BIG-bench datasets
364   due to tokenization incompatibility with the T5 tokenizer.
365
366   Expert Creation. To create the pool of expert module for routing, we follow Muqeeth et al. (2024)
367   and use two distinct dataset collections: ❶ T0 Held-In (Sanh et al., 2021) consisting of the 36 held-in
368   prompted datasets for tasks from the T0 training procedure. ❷ The “FLAN Collection" (Longpre
369
      et al., 2023) which significantly expands the T0 tasks by incorporating prompted datasets from
      SuperGLUE (Wang et al., 2019), Super Natural Instructions (Wang et al., 2022b), dialogue datasets,
370
      and Chain-of-Thought datasets (Wei et al., 2022b). Following Muqeeth et al. (2024), we create 166
371
      specialized models from the FLAN Collection. For each dataset in these collections, we train Low-
372   Rank Adapters (LoRAs) (Hu et al., 2021) modules resulting in pools of 36 and 166 expert models
373   for T0 Held-In and FLAN, respectively. Similar to Phatgoose, we use a rank of r = 16 and train
374   for 1000 steps using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a learning rate of
375   5 × 10−3 and a warmup ratio of 0.06. After training the LoRA module, we freeze it and train the
376   local routing vectors for an additional 100 steps with the same hyperparameters. Finally, following
377   prior work (Shazeer et al., 2016; Du et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2020), GLIDER performs top-k
      routing with k = 2.

      5.2   BASELINES
379
380   Expert Merging. Model Merging (Yadav et al., 2023b; Choshen et al., 2022) involves averaging
381   the parameters of multiple models or modules to create a single aggregate model. We merge by
382   multiplying the LoRA matrices and then taking an unweighted average of all the experts within
383   the pool. It is important to note that this merging strategy requires homogeneous expert module
384   architectures; in contrast, GLIDER can accommodate heterogeneous expert modules.
385   Arrow. Following Ostapenko et al. (2024), we employ a routing mechanism where gating vectors
386   are derived from LoRA expert modules. Specifically, the first right singular vector of the outer
387   product of each module’s LoRA update (BA) serves as its gating vector. Input routing is determined
388   by a probability distribution based on the absolute dot product between the input representation and
389   each gating vector. We utilize top-k routing with k = 2.
390
      Phatgoose. Phatgoose (Muqeeth et al., 2024) first learn the LoRA modules for each, followed by
391   learning a sigmoid gating vector similar to our local router. During inference, they make routing
392   decisions for each token independently for all modules. Specifically, they first standardize the input
393   token activations and gating vectors from all experts and then perform similarity-based top-2 routing.
394
      LoRA Hub. LoraHub (Huang et al., 2023) method performs gradient-free optimization using few-
395
      shot task samples to learn mixing coefficients for different expert models while keeping them fixed.
396
      Once the coefficients are learned, they merge the experts with the learned weight and route through
397   the merged expert.
398
399   Multi-task Fine-Tuning. While multitask training, a proven method for enhancing zero-shot gen-
400
      eralization (Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022a), is infeasible given our problem setting and data
      access limitations, we include it as a baseline using publicly available models. Specifically, we uti-
401
      lize the T0-3B model (Sanh et al., 2021) for the T0 Held-In datasets, given its training on a matching
402
      dataset collection. For FLAN, a directly comparable publicly available model is unavailable; there-
403   fore, we report results for FLAN-T5 XL, trained on a different, undisclosed dataset mixture, while
404   acknowledging the limitations of this indirect comparison.
405
406
      Oracle. Following Jang et al. (2023) and Muqeeth et al. (2024), we employ an Oracle routing
      scheme as a performance upper bound. This scheme selects the expert exhibiting optimal perfor-
407
      mance on a given evaluation dataset, thus representing a non-zero-shot approach.

507   6       C ONCLUSION
508
509   This paper introduces GLIDER, a novel multi-scale routing mechanism that incorporates both global
510
      semantic and local token-level routers. By leveraging the semantic reasoning capabilities of LLMs
      for global expert selection and refining these choices with a learned local router, GLIDER addresses
511
      the limitations of existing methods that often perform poorly on held-in tasks. Our empirical eval-
512
      uation on T0 and FLAN benchmarks, using T5-based experts, demonstrates that GLIDER achieves
513   substantial improvements in held-in task performance while maintaining competitive generaliza-
514   tion on held-out tasks. These findings suggest that incorporating global semantic task context into
515   routing mechanisms is crucial for building robust and practically useful routing-based systems.
