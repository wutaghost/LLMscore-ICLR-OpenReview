                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Recent large vision language models (VLMs) have gained significant attention
               for their superior performance in various visual understanding tasks using textual
013
               instructions, also known as prompts. However, existing research shows that VLMs
014
               are vulnerable to adversarial examples, where imperceptible perturbations added
015            to images can lead to malicious outputs, posing security risks during deployment.
016            Unlike single-modal models, VLMs process both images and text simultaneously,
017            making the creation of visual adversarial examples dependent on specific prompts.
018            Consequently, the same adversarial example may become ineffective when differ-
019            ent prompts are used, which is common as users often input diverse prompts. Our
020            experiments reveal severe non-stationarity when directly optimizing adversarial
021            example generation using multiple prompts, resulting in examples specific to a
022            single prompt with poor transferability. To address this issue, we propose the Gra-
023
               dient Regularized-based Cross-Prompt Attack (GrCPA), which leverages gradient
               regularization to generate more robust adversarial attacks, thereby improving the
024
               assessment of model robustness. By exploiting the structural characteristics of
025
               the Transformer, GrCPA reduces the variance of back-propagated gradients in the
026            Attention and MLP components, utilizing regularized gradients to produce more
027            effective adversarial examples. Extensive experiments on models such as Flamingo,
028            BLIP-2, LLaVA and InstructBLIP demonstrate the effectiveness of GrCPA in
029            enhancing the transferability of adversarial attacks across different prompts.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Vision Language Models (VLMs), such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b), have recently garnered
035
      substantial interest from the AI research community. Unlike Large Language Models (LLMs), which
036   are limited to processing plain text (OpenAI, 2023a), VLMs can interpret image inputs and perform a
037   range of visual understanding tasks guided by textual instructions, or prompts. These tasks include
038   image captioning (Li et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2020; Sheng et al., 2021), information extraction (Liu
039   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b), complex counting (Bavishi et al., 2023), and visual grounding (Wang
040   et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2023), among others. This powerful multimodal perception capability has
041   facilitated the deployment of more models in real-world production environments.
042   Recent studies have revealed that VLMs are susceptible to attacks from adversarial examples (Gu
043   et al., 2023; Madry et al., 2018; Szegedy et al., 2014; Li et al., 2024a; Mahmood et al., 2021; Mao
044   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Shayegani et al., 2023). These attacks involve
045   the addition of imperceptible disturbances to clean images, which can induce the models to output
046   malicious content. Such adversarial attacks can circumvent the security constraints of LLMs or
047   even embed advertising information into images (Niu et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023; Bailey et al.,
048   2023; Lu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023). Therefore, designing effective attack methods to identify
049   potential vulnerabilities before deploying VLMs in security-related applications is of paramount
050
      importance (Li et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023c).
051   Adversarial attacks can be broadly categorized into white-box attacks and black-box attacks (Gao
052   et al., 2024a; Cheng et al., 2019). A white-box attack refers to an attacker who has access to all
053   the structural and weight information of the model (Ebrahimi et al., 2018). Conversely, a black-box
      attack refers to an attacker who can only access the model’s external usage interface (Guo et al.,

082   In contrast to widely studied classification models, adversarial attacks on VLMs present a more
083   complex challenge. These attacks can stem from two perspectives: visual input and textual input.
084   However, visual attacks are often imperceptible to users and occupy a continuum of disturbance space,
085   making them more commonly utilized. The generation of visual adversarial examples for VLMs
086   requires coordination with specific prompts. In other words, the same visual adversarial sample may
087
      not be effective when encountering diverse prompts (Cui et al., 2023). This phenomenon is prevalent
      during the model deployment phase, as users tend to input prompts based on their individual language
088
      preferences. Therefore, this paper focuses on cross-prompt visual adversarial attacks.
089
090   An intuitive method to enhance across-prompts transferability is to utilize multiple prompts in the
091   iterative generation of adversarial examples (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017). However, in our experi-
092   ments, we identified three issues with this approach: (a) A serious non-stationary phenomenon is
093
      observed, characterized by large fluctuations in the success rate of the attack during the iteration of
      adversarial samples, as shown in Figure 1. We attribute this to overfitting during optimization, since
094
      adversarial attacks on VLMs usually require a large number of iterations, such as 10,000, to succeed,
095
      causing adversarial examples to become specific to their conditions (model and prompt) (Schlarmann
096   & Hein, 2023). (b) The calculation of text loss is extremely sensitive. Initially, we inadvertently com-
097   puted the loss for the entire sequence using teacher forcing, but found the results largely unsuccessful.
098   Subsequently, we recognized the need to focus solely on the loss pertaining to the model’s output
099   section. (c) Methods based on image classification for enhancing transferability are not adaptable
100   to VLMs. We tested methods like MI-FGSM (Dong et al., 2018), Input Diversity (Xie et al., 2019),
101   Variance Tuning (Wang & He, 2021), and found that the transferability across prompts did not
102   increase, but even decreased. A more detailed analysis can be seen in the Appendix A.1.
103   Based on these observations, we contend that the design of visual adversarial examples for VLMs
104   should take into account both image and text inputs comprehensively, with a particular focus on
105   mitigating overfitting in the textual domain. VLMs often integrate substantial language models, which
106   consist of numerous Transformer blocks, potentially leading to learned features that are specific
107   to the prompts or the model itself (Wang & He, 2021). In this paper, we introduce a Gradient
      Regularization-based Cross-Prompt Attack (GrCPA) method designed to alleviate overfitting of both

      visual and textual features within the LLM’ Transformer blocks, thereby enhancing the transferability
109   of visual adversarial examples. Specifically, we implement gradient clipping on both visual and
110   textual features during the loss back-propagation phase to counter overfitting. Note that we modify
111   only a very small number of gradients, which does not affect the overall convergence of the chain
112   rule (Zhang et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2022).
113
      To verify the effectiveness of GrCPA, we employ prompts from three distinct vision-language tasks:
114
      image classification, image captioning, and visual question answering (VQA). We evaluate our
115   method’s efficacy on well-known VLMs, including Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), BLIP-2 (Li et al.,
116   2023a), LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023) and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023). The experimental results
117   indicate that GrCPA exhibits superior attack performance and enhanced transferability.
118
      Overall, the main contributions of this paper include:
119
120         1. To the best of our knowledge, we first identify the non-stationary phenomenon in adversarial
121            attacks against vision language models, and argue that its essence is overfitting in the
122            optimization process. We also attempt previous enhancement methods for single-modal
123            models and find them to be ineffective.
124
            2. We propose a gradient regularization method to enhance the transferability of visual adver-
125            sarial examples, thus effectively alleviating overfitting issues in the deep Transformer blocks
126            of visual and textual features.
127
            3. We validate the effectiveness of our method through detailed experiments and provide a new
128
               perspective for future attacks against VLMs.

133   Adversarial Transferability. Szegedy et al. (2014) first proposed the concept of adversarial examples,
134   revealing the vulnerability of neural networks. The transferable attacks, which have widespread
135   impacts in the real world, have triggered a large number of subsequent studies Cheng et al. (2020);
136   Wu et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023b); Chakraborty et al. (2021); Madry et al. (2018); Xu et al.
137
      (2022). Previous work has primarily focused on classification models, with an emphasis on enhancing
      transferability through gradient optimization, input augmentation. Gradient optimization methods,
138
      led by the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2015), along with its derivatives
139
      such as Iterative FGSM (I-FGSM) (Kurakin et al., 2017), Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry
140   et al., 2018), Momentum Iterative FGSM (MI-FGSM) (Dong et al., 2018), among others, have
141   emerged as prominent techniques in the literature. On another front, input augmentation primarily
142   involves applying various transformations to the input image at each iteration, such as random
143   resizing and padding, as seen in methods like DIM Xie et al. (2019), SIM Lin et al. (2020), and
144   TIM Dong et al. (2019). We endeavor to improve the transferability of attacks on VLMs utilizing
145   traditional methods, but observe no substantial enhancement in their effectiveness. This highlights
146   the complexity and inherent challenges of multimodal tasks, prompting a reevaluation of our previous
147   research methodologies.
148   Adversarial Robustness of Vision Language Models. Alongside the proliferation of large VLMs,
149   the associated security research has garnered significant attention Gao et al. (2024a); Sun et al. (2024);
150   Ni et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024); Luo et al. (2024b); Zhou et al. (2024); Cheng
151   et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024). For example, Zhao et al. (2023) induce misinterpretation of image
152   content in models such as BLIP-2 through black-box attacks. There is also a body of work utilizing
153   adversarial attacks to circumvent security alignment of LLM components (Bagdasaryan et al., 2023;
154
      Carlini et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024), posing security risks to VLMs. The work closest
      to ours is CroPA Luo et al. (2024a), which turns the generation of visual adversarial examples into a
155
      max-min process, achieving significant improvements. Our method from the perspective of reverse
156
      gradient is orthogonal to it, with more flexible and simpler operational methods.

180   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
181
      Without loss of generality, VLMs complete a series of downstream tasks through visual question
182
      answering (VQA). The model is provided with an image v and question q (i.e., prompts), and in
183
      response, it generates an answer a. We denote a VLM with the function fθ , where θ represents its
184   parameters, such that a = fθ (v, q).
185
186   Thus, the threat model for adversarial attacks on vision language models can be represented as:
187   Adversarial knowledge refers to the information an attacker has about the target model. In this
188   paper, we focus on white-box attacks, where we have full access to all details of the victim’s model,
189   including its architecture and weights. This access allows us to leverage the gradients obtained
190   through backpropagation to generate adversarial examples effectively.
191   Adversarial goals describe the malicious objectives that an attacker aims to achieve, typically
192   categorized into targeted and untargeted attacks. For VLMs, targeted attacks seek to induce the model
193   to output specific content, including bypassing alignment constraints. In contrast, untargeted attacks
194   aim to provoke the model into producing incongruous responses. Since untargeted attacks can often
195   be achieved through targeted attacks, this paper places greater emphasis on targeted attacks.
196
      Adversary capabilities refer to the resources and constraints available to the attacker. To ensure that
197   adversarial examples remain imperceptible to humans, the image perturbation δ is constrained by
198   ||δ||p ≤ ϵ, where ϵ represents the magnitude of the perturbation and ∥·∥p denotes the Lp norm. This
199   paper primarily employs the L∞ norm to align with previous work (Luo et al., 2024a).

203   To induce the model to output specific content, given a query q and a target answer a , we optimize
204   the loss of the language model with respect to the (q, a) pair and backpropagate it to the image, thus
205   generating adversarial examples. We denote this method as Single-P. However, the activation of
206   adversarial examples generated by this method also depends on the optimization of q used during
207   the process. In other words, if replaced with another prompt q ′ , the model may fail to produce a in
208
      response (Cui et al., 2023).
209   To enhance the cross-prompt transferability of visual adversarial samples, a straightforward approach
210   is to use multiple prompts during optimization (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017). Given a set of textual
211   prompts Q = {q1 , q2 , q3 , . . . , qM }, we induce the model to output the predetermined target answer a
212   under the query of each item in Q in the presence of adversarial noise δ. Specifically, we minimize
213   the following language modeling loss:

232   3.3   G R CPA
233
      Orthogonal to CroPA, we introduce GrCPA, which focuses on gradient regularization during the
234
      backpropagation process. Visual adversarial attacks fundamentally involve optimizing images using
235
      gradient descent. Consequently, large gradients can lead to local optima and trigger overfitting
236   issues (Wang & He, 2021). This motivates us to clip the gradients of both visual and text features,
237   thereby enhancing cross-prompt transferability.
238
239
      Existing large VLMs typically consist of three components: a visual encoder, a projection layer, and
      an LLM. The image passes through the visual encoder to obtain a set of features, which are then
240
      aligned to the input space of the LLM by the projection layer to form visual tokens. These visual
241
      tokens are concatenated with textual tokens and fed into the LLM for autoregressive generation. The
242   LLM is composed of multiple stacked Transformer blocks, with each block consisting of Attention
243   and MLP components (Vaswani et al., 2017).
244
245
      Given the gradient vector G ∈ Rd with respect to visual or textual tokens, where d is the embedding
      length, we compute the language modeling loss (Equation 2) and propagate this loss backward through
246
      the Transformer blocks of the LLM. As shown in Figure 2a, we perform Gradient Regularization
247
      (GR) by identifying the k largest and smallest gradient (Grad) values and setting them to 0, as follows.
248
249                                    imax ,imin = arg max G, arg min G
250                                                     k           k                                     (3)
251                                       G[imax ] = G[imin ] = 0
252
253   This clipping will be performed on each token in both the Attention block and the MLP block of the
254   Transformer blocks.
255   Preserving low-level features. LLMs typically consist of multiple stacked Transformer blocks,
256   which enable the learned features to be specific to the model itself. Inspired by Deng et al. (2023),
257   which suggests that preserving more low-level features in convolutional networks can improve
258   cross-model transferability, we hypothesize that regularizing only the gradients of certain high-level
259   features in LLMs can better balance the strength and transferability of attacks. Assuming that the
260   LLM consists of N Transformer blocks, we set n = λ · N and apply regularization only to the
261
      features of the last n layers. The complete algorithm is presented in Algorithm 1.
262   Relationships among various methods. Our method builds upon previous work Moosavi-Dezfooli

466   5   C ONCLUSION
467
468   In this paper, we investigate the adversarial robustness of large vision language models (VLMs).
469   During our experiments, we first found that existing adversarial attacks on visual language models
470
      exhibit significant instability, with the optimization process for adversarial samples oscillating
      between success and failure. We believe that the root cause of this issue is overfitting during the
471
      optimization process, which poses a challenge to the large-scale generation of adversarial samples
472
      for visual language models. Furthermore, we experimentally investigated the effectiveness of
473   adversarial attack enhancement methods that target only the visual modality within VLMs and found
474   that these methods reduce attack performance. Based on these observations, we propose Gradient
475   Regularized-based Cross-Prompt Attack (GrCPA), which clips the gradients of visual and textual
476   features during error backpropagation, eliminating extreme gradients to prevent falling into local
477   optima. Our regularization operation modifies only a small portion of the gradients and does not
478   affect the convergence of the chain rule. Experiments on models such as BLIP-2 demonstrate that our
479   method significantly improves the transferability of adversarial samples and confirms that current
480   VLMs are sensitive to visual inputs and can be easily attacked. Therefore, we call on researchers
481
      to thoroughly evaluate the adversarial robustness of visual language models before deployment,
      especially in life-critical scenarios.
482
483   Reproducibility. In the experiments, we thoroughly report on the datasets, models, and parameter
484   settings designed for this study, with all data being open-source and publicly available to ensure
485   reproducibility.
