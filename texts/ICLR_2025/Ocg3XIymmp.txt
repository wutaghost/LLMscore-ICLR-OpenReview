009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Ligand-based drug discovery (LBDD) relies on making use of known binders to a
012            protein target to find structurally diverse molecules similarly likely to bind. This
013            process typically involves a brute force search of the known binder (query) against a
014            molecular library using some metric of molecular similarity. One popular approach
015            overlays the pharmacophore-shape profile of the known binder to 3D conformations
016
               enumerated for each of the library molecules, computes overlaps, and picks a set of di-
               verse library molecules with high overlaps. While this virtual screening workflow has
017
               had considerable success in hit diversification, scaffold hopping, and patent busting,
018
               it scales poorly with library sizes and restricts candidate generation to existing library
019            compounds. Leveraging recent advances in voxel-based generative modelling, we
020            propose a pharmacophore-based generative model and workflows that address the
021            scaling and fecundity issues of conventional pharmacophore-based virtual screening.
022            We introduce VoxCap, a voxel captioning method for generating SMILES strings
023            from voxelised molecular representations.We propose two workflows as practical use
024            cases as well as benchmarks for pharmacophore-based generation: de-novo design, in
025            which we aim to generate new molecules with high pharmacophore-shape similarities
026            to query molecules, and fast search, which aims to combine generative design with
027
               a cheap 2D substructure similarity search for efficient hit identification. Our results
               show that VoxCap significantly outperforms previous methods in generating diverse
028
               de-novo hits. When combined with our fast search workflow, VoxCap reduces com-
029
               putational time by orders of magnitude while returning hits for all query molecules,
030            enabling the search of large libraries that are intractable to search by brute force.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Ligand-based drug discovery (LBDD) aims to identify and develop pharmacologically active
036   compounds by making use of molecules that are known to interact with a given biological target
037   (Kurogi and Guner, 2001). LBDD-based methods can be useful in the absence of the target’s structure
038   (Mason et al., 2001; González et al., 2009; Bernard et al., 2005; Loew et al., 1993), and can be
039
      complementary to structure-based drug design when target information is present, for tasks such
      as virtual screening and lead diversification (Grebner et al., 2020). Acharya et al. (2011) presents a
040
      detailed review of LBDD. Central to LBDD is the concept of a pharmacophore, which is defined as a
041
      feature of a molecule, located in 3D space, that may be involved in binding (Pearlman, 1993; McNaught
042   et al., 1997; Chang and Swaan, 2006). Examples include hydrogen bond donors and acceptors.
043
044   Drug discovery campaigns typically rely on pharmacophore-shape based design for finding a diverse
045
      pool of molecules that are likely to bind to a target in the same way as a query molecule that has already
      been shown to bind. Having a diverse pool of binders increases the chances of finding a molecule
046
      that has minimal liabilities and has the potential to be optimised into a successful drug (Hawkins
047
      et al., 2007). This is conventionally done by running a brute force search against an existing library of
048   molecules to find molecules whose 3D shape and arrangement of pharmacophore features in 3D space
049   (i.e. pharmacophore-shape profiles) have high overlap in 3D space with the pharmacophore-shape
050   profile of the query molecule (Rush et al., 2005); such molecules are known as hits with respect to
051   the query molecule. The process involves three distinct steps: (i) generating conformations for the
052   query molecule and all molecules in the library, (ii) computing the pharmacophore-shape profiles of
053   these molecules, and (iii) aligning these profiles in 3D space and computing the overlap. This workflow
      (particularly the steps post-conformer generation/retrieval) is typically done using software such as


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      OpenEye’s Rapid Overlay of Chemical Structures (ROCS) (Rush et al., 2005; OpenEye, Cadence
055   Molecular Sciences, 2024).
056
057   There are two main issues with the prevailing pharmacophore-based virtual screening workflows. First,
058
      the process of aligning the query and library molecules and computing their overlap is time-consuming
      and expensive. This is particularly true because this process has to be carried out for each new query
059
      molecule in a project, of which we would ideally like there to be hundreds in the absence of major compu-
060
      tational constraints. This bottleneck limits the number of query molecules that are screened in a typical
061   drug discovery project, limiting the chances of finding a binding candidate molecule with promising
062   properties and minimal liabilities. Second, candidate generation for any query is limited to the same set
063   of compounds that is the existing library. The practical advantage of library screening is that molecules
064   in these libraries are usually far more synthetically accessible (either already in-stock in commercial
065   or corporate collections, or easily synthesizable via established routes and readily accessible building
066   blocks) than de-novo generated molecules. However, this constraint does prevent the discovery of new
067   compounds that may have better properties for the given task than those already present in the library.
068   In this work, we propose a generative model and workflows to address these issues. We make use
069   of recent advances in voxel-based generative modelling, which have demonstrated the success of
070   processing and generating molecules in voxel space (Pinheiro et al., 2023; 2024). Concretely, we
071   propose VoxCap, a voxel-based generative model for pharmacophore/ligand-based drug discovery.
072   VoxCap is a voxel captioning method that generates SMILES strings (Weininger, 1988) from an input
073   voxelised representation of a molecule. We use VoxCap to generate SMILES strings corresponding
074   to an input voxelised pharmacophore-shape profile.
075   We propose and evaluate two workflows: de-novo design, where we generate new molecules that are
076   not in the training or test sets, with high pharmacophore-shape similarities to query molecules from
077   the test set; and fast search, where we use de-novo design in conjunction with a fast 2D substructure
078   similarity search to quickly and efficiently find hits in 3D space from a given library. Our evaluation
079   shows that VoxCap outperforms previous work, by up to an order of magnitude, on the number of
080   diverse de-novo hits generated given a query molecule. We also find that VoxCap, in conjunction with
081
      our fast search workflow, reduces the computational time/cost of inference by orders of magnitude
      while returning an appreciable number of hits for each query molecule.

086   2.1   I MAGE CAPTIONING
087
088   Deep learning has significantly automated the generation of captions from natural images. Vinyals et al.
089   (2015) proposed the first end-to-end deep learning model to generate a caption from an input image.
090   They use a convolutional neural network (CNN) (LeCun et al., 1998) to extract features from an input
091   image, and a long short term memory network (LSTM) (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997) to decode
092   this representation into a caption. Prior to this, Mao et al. (2014) and Kiros et al. (2014) proposed
093
      models that separately encoded images and text to rank or generate new captions. Xu et al. (2016)
      built on Vinyals et al. (2015)’s model by introducing an attention mechanism into the model to attend to
094
      different aspects of the input image. Continuing in the path of moving to attention-based architectures,
095
      Li et al. (2019) proposed a captioning model based on the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). VoxCap
096   modifies such approaches to operate on 3D inputs, thereby producing strings from voxel grids.

100
      While point-cloud based molecular generation remains widely used, recent work has focused on
101
      voxel-based molecular generative models. Ragoza et al. (2020) first proposed a voxel-based generative

106   a UNet-based (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture. (Pinheiro et al., 2024) extended this framework
107   to the conditional setting for binder generation, generating voxelised molecules conditioned on
      the voxelised representation of a protein pocket. Ragoza et al. (2022) also proposed a binding-site

      2.3   P HARMACOPHORE - BASED MOLECULAR DESIGN
112
113   Typical small molecule pharmacophore-based virtual screening workflows involve searching existing
114   databases for molecules that match the pharmacophore and shape profiles of various lead compounds.
115   Rush et al. (2005) proposed software to define, align and compute the overlap of pharmacophore-shape
116   profiles, as well as a workflow to apply this to LBDD. Grebner et al. (2020) studied the relationship
117   between database size and matching molecules found. Skalic et al. (2019) generated SMILES strings
118   from voxelised atomic density input and conditioning pharmacophore information, and evaluated
119
      these against reference sets using distributional metrics such as uniqueness, Fréchet ChemNet Distance
      (Preuer et al., 2018) and distributional scaffold frequency similarity. Zhu et al. (2023) proposed a
120
      conditional VAE-based model to generate SMILES strings conditioned on stochastically generated
121
      pharmacophore graphs. They carry out evaluation by measuring a graph-matching score between query
122   and generated molecules, and measuring properties such as docking score of generated molecules
123   against the pocket from which the hit molecule was obtained.

      3     M ETHOD
127
      VoxCap generates SMILES strings from an input voxelised representation of a molecule. Our task here
128
      is to generate SMILES strings from a molecule’s pharmacophore and shape profile. Our 3D voxelised
129
      molecular representation builds on previous works on voxelised molecule generation (Pinheiro et al.,
130   2023; 2024). We represent a molecule with a Gaussian-like density centered around the location
131   of each of the pharmacophores of the molecule, with each pharmacophore type having a separate
132   channel. We use 6 pharmacophore types: hydrogen bond donor, hydrogen bond acceptor, cation,
133   anion, aromatic ring and hydrophobe. We also use an additional shape channel, in which Gaussian
134   densities are centered at the 3D positions of each of the atoms in the molecule, but in one channel
135   and with all densities having the same radius. An example voxelised pharmacophore-shape profile
136   is given in Figure 1. Section B of the Appendix gives further details on the voxelisation.

222   4.1   D E - NOVO GENERATION FROM PHARMACOPHORE - SHAPE INPUT
223
224   We carry out experiments to evaluate the ability of our model to generate a diverse set of molecules
225
      matching an input pharmacophore-shape profile for the purpose of ligand-based drug discovery.
      To evaluate the overlap between the pharmacophore-shape profiles of two molecules, we compute
226
      OpenEye’s ROCS Tanimoto Combo score. For the purposes of this paper, we abstract this as a function
227
      TC(·,·) : M × M → [0,2], where M is the space of all possible molecules with their conformations.
228   TC hence takes in two molecules and outputs a score between 0 and 2, with 0 representing no overlap
229   and 2 representing perfect overlap.
230
231
      We carry out the evaluation on two datasets: GEOM-drugs (Axelrod and Gomez-Bombarelli, 2022)
      and the subset of ChEMBL (Mendez et al., 2019) defined in the GuacaMol benchmark (Brown et al.,
232
      2019), commonly used in the machine learning literature.
233
234   GEOM-drugs consists of approximately 430,000 drug-like molecules with up to 181 heavy atoms
235   of type { C, O, N, F, S, Cl, Br, P, I and B }. The median number of heavy atoms per molecule is 45
236   and more than 99% of molecules have less than 80 heavy atoms. The dataset consists of multiple
237
      conformations for each molecule. We carry out experiments using different resolutions and grid sizes,
      and for our final model choose a grid of dimension 483 with a resolution of 0.35 A. This volume covers
238
      over 99.8% of all points in the dataset. We perform the same preprocessing and dataset split as (Vignac
239
      et al., 2023), resulting in a 1.1M/146K/146K train/validation/test split.
240
241   The ChEMBL (subset) dataset consists of approximately 1,600,000 drug-like molecules with up to
242   88 heavy atoms of type { C, O, N, F, S, Cl, Br, P, I, B, Se and Si }. The median number of heavy atoms
243
      per molecule is 27. As the dataset is given as SMILES strings, we use RdKit (Landrum et al., 2023)
      to generate a 3D conformation for each molecule. We do this by using ETKDG (Riniker and Landrum,
244
      2015) to get initial coordinates for the atoms followed by optimisation with reference to the Merck
245
      Molecular Force Field (Halgren, 1996). Our final model uses a grid of dimension 643 with resolution
246   0.35. We use the same 1.3M/80k/240k train/validation/test split as Brown et al. (2019).
247
248   For each dataset, we carry out evaluation on 100 randomly selected ‘query’ molecules from the test set.
249
      A conformation is associated with each of these query molecules. As a baseline, for each of these query
      molecules we randomly sample 1000 molecules from the test set. We obtain multiple conformations
250
      for each of these sampled molecules using OpenEye, overlay them with the candidate molecule and
251
      compute the Tanimoto Combo score. For each sampled molecule, we retain the highest Tanimoto
252   Combo score computed against the query amongst all conformations of the sampled molecule. We
253   define a hit as a sampled molecule mgen with TC(mgen ,mquery ) ≥ 1.2 with respect to the query ligand
254   mquery , in line with the heuristics used by Grebner et al. (2020). We evaluate on four metrics: number
255   of hits per query molecule, number of hits per query molecule containing a unique scaffold, max
256   Tanimoto Score per query molecule and number of query molecules with at least one hit.
257
      We then evaluate our model as well as the Pharmacophore Guided Molecular Generation (PGMG)
258   model proposed by Zhu et al. (2023) for pharmacophore-based generation. The PGMG model is based
259   on a conditional variational autoencoder (Kingma and Welling, 2022; Sohn et al., 2015) architecture.
260   At training time, the PGMG model learns to denoise a corrupted SMILES string conditioned on a
261   pharmacophore point cloud that is computed from the uncorrupted SMILES string. At inference time,
262   the model generates SMILES strings corresponding to a given pharmacophore point cloud. The point
263   cloud is encoded using a Gated Graph Convolutional Neural Network (Bresson and Laurent, 2018) that
264   is equivariant to rotation, and a transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture is used to process the
265   encoded representation along with the SMILES string. For the ChEMBL dataset, we use the pretrained
266
      model provided by Zhu et al. (2023). For GEOM-drugs, we train a model using their codebase and
      use the best checkpoint output by their training script.

382   in 3D pharmacophore-shape space; not analogs that are simply similar to the query in substructure space.
383
      We evaluate the accuracy of this workflow as follows. For each of 11 randomly selected query
384
      molecules from the ChEMBL test set, we run a ROCS search against every molecule in the union
385   of the train and validation sets excluding the query molecule. We call this the brute force approach.
386   We also use VoxCap to generate several molecules given the query molecule’s pharmacophore-shape
387   profile, and find either (i) na = 5 analogs for each of up to ng = 100 unique, valid molecules, or (ii)
388   na = 1 analog for each of up to ng = 500 molecules. We compute these analogs’ ROCs scores against
389   the query molecule as described above. We then compute the number of hits in this set of analogs
390   and compare with the number of hits in the set of top 500 molecules by ROCS score from the brute
391   force approach for each query molecule. Results are given in Table 3.
392   We find that this workflow yields a significant number of hits and unique scaffold hits; the two settings
393   of ng and na yield similar results. The number of ROCS comparisons per query (up to a constant, α, the
394   average number of conformers per database molecule) is ng ×na . This is several orders of magnitude
395   below that of the brute force search, which is the size of the database. This difference would hence
396   be even more pronounced in a real-world drug discovery application for databases such as Enamine
397   Real/Diverse. We would also expect to find a greater number of 2D matches and hits from VoxCap-
398   generated molecules for these databases due to their larger size. Our workflow hence enables the search
399
      of large-scale databases for sets of diverse hits, a current bottleneck in the drug design pipeline.
400   Our results show that there are limitations to our approach, namely the number of hits is over an
401   order of magnitude lower than the brute force method, suggesting significant room for improvement.
402   We explore why this may be the case. We observe that the highest 2D Tanimoto similarity for
403   any given generated molecule is generally low, with a median top-1 similarity of 0.38 for the
404   ng = 500,na = 1 setting. Hence many of the generated molecules do not have close 2D analogs in
405
      the dataset. Supplementary Figure 2 shows a moderate correlation (Pearson r = 0.4) between 2D and
      3D similarities. The lack of close 2D analogs for most of the generated molecules could therefore
406
      be a major reason for the low number of hits relative to the brute force approach. We also observe that
407
      there is a significant number of duplicate 2D hits per query, where the 2D search procedure maps on
408   average 2.4 generated molecules to the same molecule in the database for the ng = 500,na = 1 setting,
409   thereby decreasing the number of hits that can be found on running ROCS.
410
411
      We also observe from our experiments that our early stopping criterion selects a checkpoint after only
      a few epochs of training. Further training causes performance on sampling metrics to drop even as
412
      the validation cross-entropy loss continues to decrease. We hypothesise that the input representation
413
      is too specific to each individual molecule, so with continued training the model learns a distribution
414   that is sharply peaked around the most likely molecule without adequately learning other modes of
415   the distribution. This could mean that, at the early stopping checkpoint, when the number of training
416   epochs completed is not large, the model has not been trained long enough to adequately capture the
417   2D structure of molecules in the dataset.
418
      Future directions of work could involve regularisation/early stopping strategies that yield molecules
419
      more structurally similar to those in the dataset, as well as input representations that are smooth or
420   noisy enough to facilitate the learning of several modes of the distribution over a larger number of
421   training steps.

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this work, we propose the generation of molecules conditioned on voxelised 3D pharmacophore-
435   shape profiles. We introduce VoxCap, a generative model that generates diverse SMILES strings
436   from these pharmacophore-shape profiles. We propose a benchmark task for de-novo generation
437   conditioned on pharmacophore-shape profiles and find that VoxCap outperforms the existing state
438   of the art by large margins across all metrics. We also implement a generative model-based fast search
439   workflow to practically enable the search of large libraries for structurally diverse 3D hits against a
440   given query. We find that VoxCap, paired with this workflow, returns hits from the database for each
441   query molecule, but that there is significant room for improvement. We identify areas of future work
442   corresponding to coarser pharmacophore-shape representations and regularisation, that would enable
443
      the efficient search of large-scale databases for hit compounds in drug discovery campaigns.
