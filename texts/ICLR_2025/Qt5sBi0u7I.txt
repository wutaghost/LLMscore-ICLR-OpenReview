009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                The problem of symbolic music generation presents unique challenges due to the
012                combination of limited data availability and the need for high precision in note
013                pitch. To address these issues, we introduce an efficient Fine-grained Sampling
014                Guidance (FTG) approach within diffusion models. FTG guides the diffusion
015                models to generate music that aligns more closely with the control and intent of
016                human composers, thereby improving the accuracy and quality of music genera-
017
                   tion. This method empowers diffusion models to excel in advanced applications
                   such as improvisation, and interactive music creation. We derive theoretical char-
018
                   acterizations for both the challenges in symbolic music generation and the effect
019
                   of the FTG approach. We provide numerical experiments, subjective evaluation
020                and a demo page 1 for interactive music generation with user input to showcase
021                the effectiveness of our approach.

024   1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Symbolic music generation is a subfield of music generation that focuses on creating music in sym-
027   bolic form, typically represented as sequences of discrete events such as notes, pitches, rhythms,
028   and durations. These representations are analogous to traditional sheet music or MIDI files, where
029   the structure of the music is defined by explicit musical symbols rather than audio waveforms. Sym-
030
      bolic music generation has a wide range of applications, including automatic composition, music
      accompaniment and improvisation. It can also play a significant role in interactive music systems,
031
      where a model can respond to user inputs or generate improvisational passages in real-time. A lot of
032
      progress has been made in the field of deep symbolic music generation in recent years; see Huang
033   et al. (2018), Min et al. (2023), von Rütte et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2024).
034
035   Despite recent progress, some unique challenges of symbolic music generation remain unresolved.
036
      A key obstacle is the scarcity of high-quality training data. While large audio datasets are readily
      available, symbolic music data is more limited, often due to copyright constraints and the effort
037
      needed to create data. Additionally, unlike image generation, where the inaccuracy of a single pixel
038
      may not significantly affect overall quality, symbolic music generation demands high precision,
039   especially in terms of pitch. In many tonal contexts, a single incorrect note can be glaringly obvious,
040   even to less-trained ears.
041
042
      As a partial motivation, we empirically observe the occurrence of “wrong notes” in existing state-of-
      the-art symbolic music generation models. We provide theoretical explanations for why these mod-
043
      els often fail to avoid such errors. Apart from that, we find that many models encounter challenges
044
      in generating well-regularized accompaniment. While human-composed accompaniment often ex-
045   hibits consistent patterns across bars and phrases, the generated symbolic accompaniment tends to
046   vary significantly. These observations and theoretical discoveries motivate the method of apply-
047   ing regularization through external guidance, rather than relying on the model to capture it entirely
048   autonomously.
049
      We then address the precision challenge in symbolic music generation building upon a diffusion
050
      model-based approach. Diffusion models can flexibly capture a wide variety of patterns in the data
051   distribution, and therefore generate highly structured and detailed images (Ho et al., 2020). This flex-
052   ibility makes diffusion models well-suited for piano roll-based symbolic music generation, where

073   1.1   R ELATED WORK
074
075   Symbolic music generation. Symbolic music generation literature can be classified based on the
076   choice of data representation, among which the MIDI token-based representation adopts a sequential
077   discrete data structure, and is often combined with sequential generative models such as Transform-
078
      ers and LSTMs. Examples of works using MIDI token-based data representation include Huang
      et al. (2018), Huang & Yang (2020), Ren et al. (2020), Choi et al. (2020), Hsiao et al. (2021), Lv
079
      et al. (2023) and von Rütte et al. (2023). While the MIDI token-based representation enables gen-
080
      erative flexibility, it also introduces the challenge of simultaneously learning multiple dimensions
081   that exhibit significant heterogeneity, such as the “pitch” dimension compared to the “duration” di-
082   mension. An alternative data representation used in music processing is the piano roll-based format.
083   Many recent works adopt this data representation; see Min et al. (2023), Zhang et al. (2023a), Wang
084   et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2024) for example. Our work differs from their works in that we
085   apply the textural guidance jointly in both the training and sampling process, and with an emphasis
086   on enhancing real-time generation precision and speed. More detailed comparisons are provided in
087   Appendix D, after we present a comprehensive description of our methodology.
088
089   Controlled diffusion models. Multiple works in controlled diffusion models are related to our
090   work in terms of methodology. Specifically, we adopt the idea of classifier-free guidance in training
091   and generation, see Ho & Salimans (2022). To control the sampling process, Chung et al. (2022),
092
      Song et al. (2023) and Novack et al. (2024) guide the intermediate sampling steps using the gradients
      of a loss function. In contrast, Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), Saharia et al. (2022), Lou & Ermon
093
      (2023) and Fishman et al. (2023) apply projection and reflection during the sampling process to
094
      straightforwardly incorporate data constraints. Different from these works, we design guidance
095   for intermediate steps tailored to the unique characteristics of symbolic music data and generation.
096   While the meaning of a specific pixel in an image is undefined until the entire image is generated,
097   each position on a piano roll corresponds to a fixed time-pitch pair from the outset. This new context
098   enables us to develop novel implementations and theoretical perspectives on the guidance approach.

139   3       C HALLENGES IN SYMBOLIC MUSIC GENERATION
140
141   While generative models have achieved significant success in text, image, and audio generation, the
142
      effective modeling and generation of symbolic music remains a relatively unexplored area. In this
      section, we introduce two major challenges of current symbolic music generation.
143

      3.1     H ARMONIC PRECISION
146
      One challenge of symbolic music generation involves the high precision required for music genera-
147   tion. Specifically, harmony considerations serve as an illustrative example for highlighting the issue
148   with precision. In music, harmony refers to the simultaneous sound of different notes that form a
149   cohesive entity in the mind of the listener (Müller, 2015). Unlike image generation, where a slightly
150   misplaced pixel may not significantly affect the overall image quality, an “inaccurately” generated
151   musical note can drastically disrupt the harmony, affecting the quality of a piece.
152
      To demonstrate the issue in harmonic precision, we consider temporary tonic key signatures3 , which
153   establish the tonal center of music. In many genres4 , out-of-key notes are uncommon, and produce
154   noticeable dissonance without a suitable context. For instance, a G♮ note is considered as out-of-key

      3.2   R HYTHMIC REGULARITY
203
      A second observation regarding symbolic music generation models is their tendency to produce ir-
204   regular rhythmic patterns. While many composers typically maintain consistent rhythmic patterns
205   across consecutive measures within a 4-bar phrase, particularly in the accompaniment, such varia-
206   tions frequently appear in the generated accompaniment of symbolic music generative models.
207
      Such phenomenons can be explained by the scarcity of data and the high dimensionality hindering
208
      the model’s ability to capture correlations between different bars, even within a single generated
209
      section. Additionally, the irregularity in generated patterns can stem from the presence of irregular
210   samples in many existing MIDI datasets. Without a sufficient quantity of data exhibiting clear
211   correlations and repetition across measures, it is challenging for the model to self-generate more
212   human-like and consistent accompaniment patterns.

226   4     M ETHODOLOGY: F INE - GRAINED TEXTURAL GUIDANCE
227
228   In the previous section 3, we identified the unique challenges in symbolic music generation arising
229
      from the distinctive characteristics and specific requirements of symbolic music data. Together with
      the scarcity of available high-quality data for training, this creates a motivation for fine-grained
230
      external control and regularization in generating symbolic music. In this section, we present our
231
      methodology of applying fine-grained regularization guidance to improve the quality and stability
232   of the generated symbolic music, ensuring better alignment with the user’s intent. Specifically, we
233   design fine-grained conditioning and sampling correction/regularization, altogether referred to as
234   Fine-grained Textural Guidance (FTG) that leverage this characteristic of the piano roll data. We
235   use “texture” to refer to harmony and rhythm together.
236

385   The generation target X is represented by a piano-roll matrix of shape 2 × L × 128 under the
386   resolution of a 16th note, where L represents the total length of the music piece, and the two channels
387

388
      the inpainting method. The backbone of our model is a 2D UNet with spatial attention.
389
390   The condition matrix M cond is also represented by a piano roll matrix of shape 2 × L × 128, with the
391   same resolution and length as that of the generation target X. For the accompaniment generation
392   experiments, we provide melody as an additional condition. Detailed construction of the condition
393
      matrices are provided in Appendix E.1.

      5.1.2   DATASET
396   We use the POP909 dataset (Wang et al. (2020a)) for training and evaluation. This dataset consists
397   of 909 MIDI pieces of pop songs, each containing lead melodies, chord progression, and piano
398   accompaniment tracks. We exclude 29 pieces that are in triple meter. 90% of the data are used
399   to train our model, and the remaining 10% are used for evaluation. In the training process, we
400   split all the midi pieces into 4-measure non-overlapping segments (corresponding to L = 64 under
401   the resolution of a 16th note), which in total generates 15761 segments in the entire training set.
402   Training and sampling details are provided in Appendix E.2.
403
404   5.1.3   TASK AND BASELINE M ODELS
405
      We consider accompaniment generation task based on melody and chord progression. We compare
406   the performance of our model with two baseline models: 1) WholeSongGen (Wang et al. (2024))
407   and 2) GETMusic (Lv et al. (2023)). WholeSongGen is a hierarchical music generation framework
408   that leverages cascaded diffusion models to generate full-length pop songs. It introduces a four-
409   level computational music language, with the last level being accompaniment. The model for the
410   last level can be directly used to generate accompaniment given music phrases, lead melody, and
411   chord progression information. GETMusic is a versatile music generation framework that leverages
412   a discrete diffusion model to generate tracks based on flexible source-target combinations. The
413   model can also be directly applied to generate piano accompaniment conditioning on melody and
414
      chord. Since these baseline models do not support rhythm control, to ensure comparability, we will
      use the M cond (C) without rhythm condition in our model.

480   preserving the distributional properties of the original learned distribution. In contrast, the latter

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we apply fine-grained textural guidance (FTG) on symbolic music generation models.
534   We provide theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to highlight the need for fine-grained and
535   precise control over the model output. We also provide theoretical analysis to quantify and upper
536   bound the potential effect of fine-grained control on learned local patterns, and provide samples
537   and numerical results for demonstrating the effectiveness of our approach. For the impact of our
538   method, we note that the FTG method can be integrated with other diffusion-based symbolic music
539   generation methods. While sacrificing some creative flexibility, the FTG method prioritizes real-
      time generation stability and enables efficient generation with precise control.

        We provide a detailed comparison between our method and two related works in controlled diffusion
1164    models with constrained or guided intermediate sampling steps:
1165
1166    Comparison with reflected diffusion models In Lou & Ermon (2023), a bounded setting is used for
1167
        both the forward and backward processes, ensuring that the bound applies to the training objective
        as well as the entire sampling process. In contrast, we do not adopt the framework of bounded
1168
        Brownian motion, because we do not require the entire sampling process to be bounded within a
1169
        given domain; instead, we only enforce that the final sample outcome aligns with the constraint.
1170    While Lou & Ermon (2023) enforces thresholding on Xt in both forward and backward processes,

1216

1217

1218

1219   contains many constraints on the rhythm and pitch range of the accompaniment, ensuring coherence
1220   with the melody. Thus, similarity with ground truth on those metrics serves as an indicator of how
1221   well the generated samples adhere to the melody.
1222

1223
       sion similarity remains comparable (as the chord sequence is provided), evaluating MOA features
1224
       against ground truth is less informative. This is because multiple different pitch range and rhythm
1225   could appropriately align with a given chord progression, making deviations from the ground truth
1226   in these features less indicative of sample quality. Therefore, chord similarity emerges as the sole
1227   applicable metric in this context.

1230

       I        D ISCUSSION
1405
1406
       The role of generative AI in music and art remains an intriguing question. While AI has demon-
1407   strated remarkable performance in fields such as image generation and language processing, these
1408   domains possess two characteristics that symbolic music lacks: an abundance of training data and
1409   well-designed objective metrics for evaluating quality. In contrast, for music, it is even unclear
1410   whether it is necessary to set the goal as generating compositions that closely resemble12 some
1411   “ground truth”.
1412
       In this work, we apply fine-grained sampling control to eliminate out-of-key notes, ensuring that
1413   generated music adheres to the most common harmonies and chromatic progressions. This approach
1414   allows the model to consistently and efficiently produce music that is (in some ways) “pleasing to
1415   the ear”. While suitable for the task of quickly creating large amounts of mediocre pieces, such
1416   models have a limited capability of replicating the artistry of a real composer, of creating sparkles
1417   with unexpected “wrong” keys by themselves.
