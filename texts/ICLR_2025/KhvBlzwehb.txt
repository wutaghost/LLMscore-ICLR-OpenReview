009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Active perception, a crucial human capability, involves setting a goal based on the
012            current understanding of the environment and performing actions to achieve that
013            goal. Despite significant efforts in evaluating Multimodal Large Language Mod-
014            els (MLLMs), active perception has been largely overlooked. To address this gap,
015            we propose a novel benchmark named ActiView to evaluate active perception in
016            MLLMs. Since comprehensively assessing active perception is challenging, we
017            focus on a specialized form of Visual Question Answering (VQA) that eases the
018
               evaluation yet challenging for existing MLLMs. Given an image, we restrict the
               perceptual field of a model, requiring it to actively zoom or shift its perceptual
019
               field based on reasoning to answer the question successfully. We conduct exten-
020
               sive evaluation over 27 models, including proprietary and open-source models,
021            and observe that the ability to read and comprehend multiple images simultane-
022            ously plays a significant role in enabling active perception. Results reveal a signif-
023            icant gap in the active perception capability of MLLMs, indicating that this area
024            deserves more attention. We hope that our benchmark could help develop methods
025            for MLLMs to understand multimodal inputs in more natural and holistic ways.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The advent of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has marked a significant milestone
031   in the realm of artificial intelligence, demonstrating capabilities that are increasingly approaching
032   human-like performance (OpenAI, 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Ye et al., 2024b). This advancement,
033
      while promising, also presents new challenges and opportunities for evaluating these models. As
      a result, the landscape of MLLM evaluation is rapidly evolving, with numerous benchmarks being
034
      developed to either comprehensively evaluate models (Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023d; Fu et al.,
035
      2024a) or to analyze specific aspects of their capabilities (Liu et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2023; Luo
036   et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Nie et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024).
037
038   Despite the extensive efforts devoted to MLLM evaluation, active perception (Bajcsy, 1988; Bajcsy
039
      et al., 2018) remains underexplored. Active perception involves understanding the reasons for sens-
      ing, choosing what to perceive, and determining the methods, timing, and locations for achieving
040
      that perception (Bajcsy et al., 2018). This is important because in the real world, the desired in-
041
      formation often does not appear directly in the center of one’s field of vision. Instead, it requires
042   individuals to move their field of view, locate details, and filter out distracting information. For
043   example, in Figure 1a, suppose we are looking for information in a giant painting. We need to first
044   shift our view to locate the specific area and then possibly zoom in to gather detailed information.
045   Intuitively, active perception not only enables a person or model to accomplish more complex tasks,
046   but it also has the potential to serve as a good indicator of the level of intelligence of a model. This
047   makes it a critical capability that warrants thorough evaluation.
048   However, existing multimodal evaluation benchmarks are not well-suited for measuring active per-
049   ception capabilities. We summarize several widely used or recently proposed multimodal evaluation
050   benchmarks in Table 1. Most of these benchmarks assess models in static perceptual field settings,
051   evaluating how well a model can process information presented directly to it without requiring ac-
052   tive exploration or dynamic adjustments to its field of view. BLINK (Fu et al., 2024b), V* (Wu &
053   Xie, 2023), and CNT (Roberts et al., 2023) are the only exceptions, as they utilize dynamic percep-
      tual fields. However, they only consider either shifting or zooming of the field of view in specific

076   a clear need for new evaluation frameworks that can adequately assess active perception abilities
077   across diverse and dynamic environments.
078   To fill this gap, we introduce a novel benchmark specifically designed for evaluating Active percep-
079   tion through View changes (ActiView). Given the difficulty of comprehensively evaluating active
080   perception capabilities across all possible scenarios, ActiView concentrates on a series of tasks that
081   are currently feasible to evaluate yet still present significant challenges to current models. The eval-
082   uation instances in ActiView follow the Visual Question Answering (VQA) (Antol et al., 2015)
083   format but include the following additional features: 1) Each question requires an understanding of
084   detailed visual clues in the image to answer accurately. 2) We impose view constraints on images,
085   allowing the model to perceive only a partial field of view of the full image at a time. This setup
086
      explicitly requires models to perform view shifting and zooming to gather more information and
      eliminate potential distractions, thereby simulating the active perception process. We collect images
087
      from existing datasets as well as newly captured images, carefully curate questions, answers, and
088
      visual clues manually to ensure the quality and diversity of the benchmark.
089
090   We conducted evaluations on three proprietary models and 24 widely used and advanced open-
091   source models. The results reveal that these models generally lag behind in active perception. For
092
      instance, the strong proprietary model, GPT-4o, only achieved an average score of 66.40% with our
      designed evaluation pipelines, and a score of 67.38% on the general VQA format, which is signifi-
093
      cantly lower than the human performance score of 84.67%. Moreover, the average performance gap
094
      between proprietary models and open-source models in active perception is considerably smaller
095   within our designed evaluation pipelines compared to gaps observed in other tasks from previous
096   research. Experimental results suggest that models tend to perform better when the full image is
097   provided, while fall shot to form a holistic perspective of the complete image when they only have
098   access to all the constrained perceptual fields. Therefore, we believe that active perception warrants
099   more research efforts, and our benchmark is valuable for further study.

137   2.2    ACTIVE P ERCEPTION IN MLLM S
138
139   Although MLLMs have attracted extensive interest, less effort has been dedicated to improving
140
      the active perception capability of MLLMs. One line of research focuses on improving the ability
      of processing high-resolution images by using higher-resolution ViTs (Ye et al., 2024b), slicing
141
      high-resolution images and then concatenate them (Liu et al., 2024), or directly using LLMs to
142
      process raw patches of any resolution (Li et al., 2023a). The other line emphasizes visual search
143   for fine-grained details. For example, SEAL (Wu & Xie, 2023) fine-tunes a framework of two
144   MLLMs to follow the visual search mechanism for precise visual grounding, and V-IRL (Yang et al.,
145   2024) proposes an active detection strategy to improve the comprehension of real-world geospatial
146   information. Despite these efforts, our evaluation results reveal that existing MLLMs still generally
147   lack active perception capabilities. Our benchmark will shed light on evaluating and enhancing
148   active perception in MLLMs.
149
150   3     ACTI V IEW
151
152   Our benchmark exams active perception abilities of models via different perceptual fields, where
153
      Actively zooming and shifting of Views (ActiView) are required. We summarise zooming and
      shifting as two fundamental factors of active perception as illustrated in Figure 2, where we can
154
      evaluate active perception abilities of models through the two factors separately or integratedly. Ac-
155
      tiView imitates the procedure of active perception by providing models with an initial view, which is
156   a cropped field of the original image, as depicted in Figure 2, and requires models to search for miss-
157   ing but important information with view zooming and shifting, and to minimize distractions caused
158   by redundant information in the view. We manually annotated 325 questions, where each question
159   corresponds to 5 different evaluation instances that assess active perception abilities under different
160   settings and levels of difficulty. In total, there are 1,625 evaluation instances in our benchmark. This
161   section elaborately describes ActiView benchmark, including the design of categories, questions and
      options (§3.1) and the data collection procedure and statistics of the benchmark (§3.2).

      3.1            B ENCHMARK OVERVIEW
193
194
      When perceiving an image, humans intuitively focus on three principle aspects: the environment
195   depicted in the image, the primary objects, and the event that these objects are engaged in. Cor-
196   respondingly, we summarise the questions in our benchmark into three main categorises, includ-
197   ing environment-centric (Type I), object-centric (Type II), and event-centric (Type III) categorises.
198   These main categorises are further divided into eight sub-classes according to the specific type of vi-
199   sual information and visual features used for answering the questions. Figure 2 displays eight exam-
200   ples for each of the sub-classes, and demonstrates features of our benchmark. For the environment-
201   centric category, three sub-classes are developed:
202       • Geo-Localization, denoted as Geo-Loc. This sub-class focuses on geographical features that
203         are unique to a country or a city, and requires models to identify geographical locations de-
204         picted in target images. Typical questions for this sub-class are “Where is this place lo-
205
            cated?”,“In which country is the photo taken?”, and etc. Images in this sub-class usually
            contain unique landmarks such as the Eiffel Tower in Paris and the Atomium in Brussels.
206
          • Orientation, denoted as Orient. This sub-class challenges models to exploit natural orienta-
207
            tion information for answering the questions, such as the position of shadows, the position of
208         the sun, and the directional information on street signs. Questions of this type include “Is this
209         a sunset or a sunrise?”, “Where is the sunlight coming from?” and etc.
210       • Daily-location, denoted as Daily-Loc. To distinguish from the Geo-Loc sub-class, this sub-
211         class concentrates on locations in everyday life that could appear in most of the cities and
212         are not unique to a certain city or country. Images in this sub-class usually depict scenes of
213         museums, restaurants, shops, etc. The corresponding questions include “Where is this picture
214         most likely taken?”, “Is there a music school nearby?”, and etc.
215   For the object-centric category, we expect models to exhibit abilities beyond simple grounding tasks
      that directly ask for the attributes or relations of objects. Questions for this category usually involve

      distracting information from images, and require models to precisely understand the intentions. Sub-
217   classes are demonstrated as follows:
218
          • Object-attribute, denoted as Obj-Attr. These questions ask for the attributes of objects, with
219
            the appearance of distracting information that potentially lead to incorrect answers. As the
220
            example of Obj-Attr shown in Figure 2, the highest price, 69 per kilogram, corresponds to
221         papaya rather than watermelon.
222       • Object-relation, denoted as Obj-Rel, which concentrates on the spatial relationships among
223         multiple objects. Questions in this sub-class do not directly ask for the spatial relationships.
224         They require models to reason for the correct answer via spatial information. Figure 2 displays
225         an example of Obj-Rel in which models should be aware of the relative positions of the feet of
226         the person to the water.
227       • Counting, denoted as Count-Dis. Although this sub-class focuses on the number of objects,
228         different from the counting tasks in other benchmarks (Fu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023), there
229
            are similar but distracting information about the targets in the images. These distracting objects
            easily confuse models and challenge the abilities to understand and strictly follow instructions.
230
            As the Count-Dis example shown in Figure 2, the jerky on the table should not be considered
231
            when answering the question of “How many pieces of jerky are hanging on the wall?”.

      The event-centric category focuses on the interactions of humans and items, such as movements,
233
      actions and activities. This category is divided according to the number of objects involved in the
234
      target event as following:
235
236
          • Event-single, denoted as Event-S. There is only one item or person involved in the target event.
            For example, the image for Event-S in Figure 2 shows one person driving without other people
237
            presenting in the image.
238
          • Event-multi, denoted as Event-M. Different from Event-S, events of this type happen among
239         multiple items or people. As the example of Event-M shown in Figure 2, the “woman in blue”
240         is engaged in a photo shooting activity in which she is posing and another person is taking
241         photo for her. This type requires models to distinguish the event (or events) each entities
242         presenting in the image are engaged in.
243   To prevent MLLMs from directly answering the questions by merely going through the options,
244   we formulate most of candidate options from the images themselves. For instance, as the example
245   of Geo-Loc shown in Figure 2, there are corresponding information for each of the options in the
246   image, where flags representing UK, France, Spain and Hungary appear. Additionally, for options
247   comprised by numbers, we display them in random order to avoid biased predictions.

302   4       E VALUATION
303   To conduct thorough investigation of active perception abilities of MLLMs, we design three evalu-
304   ation pipelines for different operations of perceptual fields as illustrated in Figure 4, including two
305   fundamental pipelines that apply zooming and shifting respectively, and a mixed pipeline comprising
306   both zooming and shifting operations. We set up five different initial views for each question-image
307   pairs, where a full initial view is used for the zooming and the mixed pipeline, and four constrained
308
      views are applied for the shifting pipeline.
309   4.1       M ODELS
310
311   We investigate both proprietary and open-source models. The proprietary models include widely
312   discussed GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Gemini-1.5-pro (Reid et al., 2024), and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (An-
313   thropic, 2024). For open-source models, we carefully select recent and commonly used models
314   of different structures and of difference scales, such as model families of MiniCPM-V Yao et al.
315
      (2024), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b;c), mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2024b;a), Idefics (Laurençon et al.,
      2024; Laurençon et al., 2024), and etc. Since the awareness of fine-grained details and instruction-
316
      aware visual features are significant indicators during evaluation, we also include models specifically
317
      optimised on these aspects, such as SEAL (Wu & Xie, 2023) for fine-grained details understanding,
318   and Brote (Wang et al., 2024b) which is trained from InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) for instruction-
319   aware and multi-image comprehension. Please refer to Appendix D for details of all 27 evaluated
320   models. These models are divided into two types, single-image models that accepting only one
321   image per input, such as LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2023b) and MiniCPM-Llama3-V-2.5 (Yao et al.,
322   2024); and multi-image models that allow more than one images to appear in the same input, such
323   as Brote and Idefics. We will describe the approaches for integrating multiple views into the input
      for the two types of models in § 4.3 and Appendix G.2.

      4.2   E VALUATION P IPELINES
325
326   We design three pipelines for different abilities, two fundamental independent abilities, zooming and
327   shifting, and a mixture of them. Generally, the images are split into 4 sub-views to enable zooming
328   and shifting operations, and to ensure that all models are fairly evaluated without bias from their
329   training data or image processing strategies. We also discuss different splittings in Appendix G.1.
330   Zooming pipeline. It evaluates the ability to locate and determine fine-grained information that
331   are necessary to answer the question. As illustrated in Figure 4 (a), this pipeline contains two stages,
332   the view selection and the question answering stages. To simulate the zooming operation, models are
333   required to first select sub-views to be zoomed given the initial view, then answer questions based
334   on these zoomed views. The initial view used in this pipeline is the full image with size w × h.
335   Each of the selected sub-views will be resized to size w × h, the same size as the initial view. As
336   demonstrated in Figure 4 (c), the zoomed right-upper view is resized as a w × h image, and so does
337
      the zoomed left-lower view. Afterwards, models answer the question given the two zoomed views.
      The detailed prompt template are provided in Appendix J.2.
338
339   Shifting pipeline. It evaluates the ability to shift perceptual fields for missing information and to
340   deduce the answer given perceived perceptual fields following templates in Appendix J.3. This is
341   also a two-stage pipeline as shown in Figure 4 (b). To simulate the movement of human eyes, models
342
      are presented with an initial view, of size w × h, which is a cropped field from the original image,
      and are asked to determine if the current view or views are sufficient for answering the questions.
343
      Upon receiving positive responses, models are prompted to produce answer given the current view
344
      or views. If the model requires more views to infer the answer, an adjacent view will be given until
345   the model is able to answer the question. For this pipeline, we assign different difficulties according
346   to human-annotated visual clues, described in § 3.2, contained in the initial views as follows:
347       • Shifting-R: starting the missing view examination with randomly selected initial views.
348
          • Shifting-E: easy-level evaluation, where initial views contain at least one entire visual clue for
349          answering the question.
350
          • Shifting-M: medium-level evaluation, where initial views contain only partial visual clues for
351          answering the question.
352
          • Shifting-H: hard-level evaluation, where no visual clues appear in the initial views.
353
354   Mixed pipeline. While the above pipelines allow for either zooming or shifting individually, we
355   also implement a mixed setting that does not specify the type of active perception ability required.
356   As illustrated in Figure 4 (c), models must independently decide whether to zoom and/or shift to dif-
357   ferent perceptual fields. Unlike the zooming pipeline, where the model answers questions based on
358
      all selected views, in the mixed pipeline, a view would be discarded after zooming if the model rec-
      ognizes it as irrelevant to the question. In comparison to the shifting pipeline, the mixed pipeline also
359
      provides access to the full image view in addition to cropped sub-views. Please refer to Appendix J.4
360
      for employed prompt templates. This type of evaluation requires models to account for all the sub-
361   views and the full image for unbiased operation determination and view selection. Otherwise, it is at
362   risk of reverting to either zooming or shifting evaluation without sufficient and unconverted visual
363   information. Therefore, the mixed pipeline is only applied to multi-image models during evaluation.
364
      In addition to these pipelines for evaluating active perception abilities, our benchmark also supports
365
      the general VQA evaluation, where models are prompted to answer visual questions given original
366
      images without zooming or shifting. The detailed prompt templates are provided in Appendix J.1.
367   We also evaluate the performance of this setting to assess the difficulty of our created benchmark.

      6                  C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      This paper introduces ActiView, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate the active perception abil-
526
      ities of MLLMs. ActiView simulates real-world scenarios by imposing view constraints on im-
527
      ages, requiring models to perform view shifting and/or zooming to gather necessary information
528   for answering questions. Our results indicate that current MLLMs exhibit significantly lower active
529   perception capabilities compared to humans, and that active perception abilities of models will be
530   markedly enhanced by allowing inputs in multi-image interleaved structures. We also observed that
531   models tend to perform better on our zooming evaluations compared to shifting evaluations. This
532   suggests that the evaluated models lack the ability to combine their understandings of constrained
533   perceptual fields to form a holistic perspective of the complete image or the full scene. We hope our
534   benchmark will inspire further research in this critical area.
535   In this study, we assess the active perception abilities of models through question answering. While
536   this approach presents significant challenges for current MLLMs, it does not encompass all aspects
537   of active perception. For instance, techniques like tool learning and multi-agent collaboration could
538   also be investigated concerning the factors such as perspective distortion, multi-sensor integration,
539   and the incorporation of more dynamic or interactive environments. Given that these limitations
      exceed the scope of a single paper, we will leave them as future work.

      A.1    I MAGE C OLLECTION
759
760   To ensure the clearness of useful visual details, the collected original image should be of high res-
761   olution. In practice, we collected images of three resolution levels, including 1920 × 1040, 2250
762   × 1500, and 5184 × 3456, which are originated from VCR dataset (Zellers et al., 2019), SA-1B
763   dataset (Kirillov et al., 2023), and photos taken in daily life. At the beginning of the image collec-
764   tion process, 30 images are collected from photos taken from daily activities, which are then served
765   as pilots and standards for manually expanding the data scale from SA-1B dataset (Kirillov et al.,
766   2023) and VCR dataset (Zellers et al., 2019). These images should include rich and fine-grained
767
      visual details.

769   A.2    RULES FOR A NNOTATION
770
771   We provide a concise version of instruction used during annotation. For each of the images, annota-
772
      tors should follow the following instructions:
773       • Questions: (1) Questions should be objective which have one and only one answer regarding
774         the images. (2) The participation of multiple visual clues are preferred. They can be in the
775         same or different regions of the image.
776       • Options: (1) Options originated from the image itself are preferred. (2) The numeric options
777
            should be arrange randomly, neither descending nor ascending order. (3) Options cannot be
            opposite to each other, except for “Yes” or “No”. (4) The number of options are not restricted
778
            to 4, you can provide as many options as long as they are reasonable and are closely related to
779
            the question and the image.
780       • Distraction: Annotator should provide distracting visual clues that could lead to wrong answer
781         (if any).

958
      more evident.

      shifting-E. There is a potential reason that the hard-level evaluation starts with less relevant views

      are more significantly influenced by the order of presented images compared to the rest models. The

1215   In this study, we utilize a specific form of Visual Question Answering (VQA) to assess active per-
1216   ception abilities of models. While this form of VQA presents significant challenges for current
1217   multimodal language models (MLLMs), it does not encompass all aspects of active perception. For
1218   instance, it overlooks factors such as perspective distortion, multi-sensor integration, and the incor-
1219   poration of more dynamic or interactive environments. Moreover, this study solely evaluates the
1220
       inherent capabilities of the MLLMs. Techniques like tool learning and multi-agent collaboration
       could potentially enhance active perception performance based on existing MLLMs, making these
1221
       areas worthy for future exploration and improvement. Given that these limitations exceed the scope
1222
       of a single paper, we will leave them as future work.

       K.1   AUTOMATIC DATA G ENERATION P ROCESS
1385
1386   In the process of automatic data generation, we used the GPT-4V model for the following experi-
1387   ments:
1388
1389         • Step 1: We applied heuristic prompts on public datasets to encourage GPT to generate
1390           creative annotations across all types.
1391         • Step 2: We selected the types that showed the best performance in automatic annotation
1392           and conducted batch annotation specifically for these types.
1393         • Step 3: We manually filtered a subset of data that could be used.
1394
1395   In Step 1, we not only employed heuristic prompts to encourage GPT to generate diverse annotations
1396   but also specified the annotation types and their precise meanings (provided as candidates, encour-
1397   aging the model to select from them). We restricted the annotation fields and types, and provided
1398   several manually curated examples as few-shot instances. Considering that some images in public
1399   datasets may not be suitable for our task, we allowed GPT to return “None” for images deemed un-
1400
       suitable for annotation. The filtered annotation data were then re-evaluated using a scoring prompt,
       where we provided our annotation types and requirements, instructing GPT to rank the annotated
1401
       data to assess its suitability.
1402
1403   In Step 2, we found that GPT performed best in annotating data of the counting type (based on a
       combination of manual inspection of the annotation results and GPT’s automatic scoring). There-

1405   public datasets (such as VCR) contain images with more than one type of bounding box, we pro-

1407   was counted at a time.

1530      - The existence of bbox and visual clues (including their ID information) cannot

1532
