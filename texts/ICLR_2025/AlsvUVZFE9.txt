011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Most existing generative models are limited to learning a single probability dis-
014            tribution from the training data and cannot generalize to novel distributions for
015            unseen data. An architecture that can generate samples from both trained datasets
016            and unseen probability distributions would mark a significant breakthrough. Re-
017            cently, score-based generative models have gained considerable attention for their
018            comprehensive mode coverage and high-quality image synthesis, as they effec-
019
               tively learn an operator that maps a probability distribution to its corresponding
               score function. In this work, we introduce the Score Neural Operator, which
020
               learns the mapping from multiple probability distributions to their score functions
021
               within a unified framework. We employ latent space techniques to facilitate the
022            training of score matching, which tends to over-fit in the original image pixel
023            space, thereby enhancing sample generation quality. Our trained Score Neural
024            Operator demonstrates the ability to predict score functions of probability mea-
025            sures beyond the training space and exhibits strong generalization performance
026            in both 2-dimensional Gaussian Mixture Models and 1024-dimensional MNIST
027            double-digit datasets. Importantly, our approach offers significant potential for
028            few-shot learning applications, where a single image from a new distribution can
029            be leveraged to generate multiple distinct images from that distribution.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Generative modeling has become a cornerstone of modern artificial intelligence and machine learn-
035
      ing, with applications ranging from image synthesis to natural language processing Brown (2020);
036   Karras et al. (2019). These models can be broadly categorized into two main approaches: likelihood-
037   based methods, such as autoregressive models and normalizing flows Rezende & Mohamed (2015);
038   Kingma & Dhariwal (2018); Papamakarios et al. (2021), and implicit generative models, exempli-
039   fied by Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014); Arjovsky et al. (2017).
040   While these approaches have shown remarkable success, they often face limitations in terms of
041   model architecture constraints or reliance on surrogate objectives for likelihood approximation Theis
042   et al. (2015); Salimans et al. (2016); Papamakarios et al. (2021).
043   In recent years, score-based generative models have emerged as a powerful alternative, gaining
044   considerable attention for their ability to produce high-quality samples and provide comprehensive
045   mode coverage. These models operate by approximating the score function (the gradient of the log-
046   density) of the target distribution through minimizing the Fisher divergence Song & Ermon (2019);
047   Hyvärinen & Dayan (2005). Once trained, new samples can be generated using Langevin dynamics
048   Song et al. (2020). The appeal of score-based methods lies in their ability to avoid the limitations of
049   both likelihood-based and implicit generative models while maintaining high-quality output Song &
050
      Ermon (2019); Song et al. (2020); Ho et al. (2020).
051   However, most existing generative models, including traditional score-based approaches, are de-
052   signed to learn and sample from a single probability distribution. This limitation becomes partic-
053   ularly apparent in scenarios where we need to generate samples from multiple related but distinct
      distributions, or when faced with novel, unseen distributions. The ability to learn and generalize

054
      across multiple distributions would mark a significant advancement in the field, enabling more flex-
055   ible and adaptable generative models.
056
057   To address this capability gap, we introduce the Score Neural Operator, a novel framework that
058
      learns to map multiple probability distributions to their corresponding score functions within a uni-
      fied model. Our approach leverages recent advancements in operator learning Azizzadenesheli et al.
059
      (2024); Lu et al. (2019); Seidman et al. (2022) to capture the underlying relationships between dif-
060
      ferent distributions and their score functions. By doing so, we enable the model to not only generate
061   high-quality samples from trained distributions but also to generalize to unseen distributions without
062   requiring retraining. The main contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
063
064          • We introduce the Score Neural Operator, a novel architecture that learns to map multiple
065            probability distributions to their score functions, enabling generalization to unseen distri-
066            butions.
067
             • We employ latent space techniques to facilitate score matching in high-dimensional spaces,
068
               improving both the quality of generated samples and the efficiency of the training process.
069
070          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach on both low-dimensional Gaussian Mix-
071            ture Models and high-dimensional image data (MNIST double-digit), showing strong gen-
072            eralization performance to unseen distributions.
073          • We showcase the potential of our method for few-shot learning applications, where a single
074            image from a new distribution can be used to generate multiple distinct samples from that
075            distribution.
076
077   Our work represents a significant step towards more flexible and generalizable generative models,
078   with potential applications in transfer learning, few-shot generation, and adaptive AI systems.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Score-based Generative Models. Score-based generative models have gained prominence in re-
      cent years due to their ability to produce high-quality samples without the need for adversarial
084
      training or explicit density estimation. The foundational work by Song and Ermon Song & Ermon
085
      (2019) introduced the concept of score matching with Langevin dynamics for sample generation.
086   This was further developed by Song et al. Song et al. (2020), who proposed a continuous-time for-
087   mulation of the score-based generative process using stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Re-
088   cent advancements in score-based models have focused on improving sample quality and generation
089   speed. Notably, Vahdat et al. Vahdat et al. (2021) introduced a method to perform score matching
090   in a lower-dimensional latent space using a variational autoencoder, significantly improving both
091   the quality of generated samples and the efficiency of the training process. Rombach et al. Rom-
092   bach et al. (2022) further extended this idea by incorporating cross-attention mechanisms, enabling
093   conditioning on various types of embeddings. While these developments have greatly enhanced the
094
      capabilities of score-based models, they remain primarily focused on learning and sampling from a
      single distribution. Our work aims to address this limitation by learning a mapping across multiple
095
      distributions.
096
097
098   Operator Learning. Operator learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for capturing map-
099
      pings between function spaces, with applications in various scientific and engineering domains Ko-
      vachki et al. (2021). Notable approaches in this field include the Deep Operator Network (Deep-
100
      ONet) Lu et al. (2019), which can approximate nonlinear operators based on the universal approxi-
101
      mation theorem, and the Fourier Neural Operator Li et al. (2020), which leverages spectral methods
102   for efficient operator learning. Of particular relevance to our work is the NOMAD (Nonlinear Man-
103   ifold Decoders for Operator Learning) framework introduced by Seidman et al. Seidman et al.
104   (2022). NOMAD’s ability to handle high-dimensional outputs and provide continuous represen-
105   tations makes it well-suited for our task of learning score functions across multiple distributions.
106   While operator learning has found success in various scientific computing applications, its poten-
107   tial in generative modeling has been relatively unexplored. Our work bridges this gap by applying
      operator learning techniques to the domain of score-based generative models.

      Few-shot Learning in Generative Models. Few-shot learning, the ability to learn from a small
109   number of examples, has been a long-standing challenge in machine learning. In the context of
110   generative models, few-shot learning is particularly challenging due to the need to capture complex
111   data distributions from limited samples. Recent works have explored few-shot learning in generative
112   models, such as the approach by Rezende et al. (2016) using meta-learning for one-shot generaliza-
113   tion in generative models. However, these methods often struggle with generating diverse samples
114   from limited data, frequently resulting in overfitting to the few available examples. Our Score Neu-
115   ral Operator aims to address this challenge by learning a generalizable mapping across distributions,
116   enabling the generation of diverse samples even when presented with a single example from a new
117
      distribution. This capability sets our approach apart from existing few-shot generative methods and
      opens up new possibilities for adaptive and flexible generative modeling.

      6   D ISCUSSION
509
      Summary. Our study introduces the Score Neural Operator, a novel generative modeling frame-
510
      work that learns to map multiple probability distributions to their corresponding score functions.
511
      This approach enables generalization to unseen distributions without retraining, marking a signif-
512   icant advancement in the field. We have demonstrated its effectiveness on both low-dimensional
513   Gaussian Mixture Models and high-dimensional MNIST double-digit data, showcasing strong gen-
514   eralization performance. Our implementation of latent space techniques for score matching has
515   proven effective in handling high-dimensional data, improving both training efficiency and sample
516   quality. Furthermore, we’ve shown the potential of our method for few-shot learning applications,
517   where a single example from a new distribution can generate diverse, high-quality samples.
518
519   Limitations. Despite these promising results, the Score Neural Operator has limitations. Its per-
520   formance may degrade when faced with distributions significantly different from those seen during
521   training. The computational cost of training on a large number of diverse distributions could become
522
      prohibitive for very large-scale applications. Additionally, while our method shows improved gen-
      eralization compared to traditional approaches, it still requires a substantial amount of training data
523
      to learn the underlying operator mapping. These limitations highlight the need for further research
524
      to enhance the model’s scalability and generalization capabilities.
525
526
      Future Work. Future work could explore several promising directions. Investigating the theoret-
527   ical foundations of the Score Neural Operator’s generalization capabilities could provide insights
528   into its performance and guide future improvements. Integrating this approach with other advanced
529   generative modeling techniques, such as transformer architectures or neural ordinary differential
530   equations, could further enhance its capabilities. Extending the model to handle conditional genera-
531   tion tasks would greatly increase its practical utility. Additionally, exploring more efficient training
532   algorithms or adaptive learning strategies could address the scalability concerns identified in our
533   current implementation.
534
535   Potential Societal Impact. The potential societal impact of this work is significant, with applica-
536
      tions ranging from improved data augmentation techniques in healthcare imaging to more adaptable
      AI systems in rapidly changing environments. However, as with any advanced generative model,
537
      there is potential for misuse, such as in the creation of deepfakes or other synthetic media for ma-
538
      licious purposes. It is crucial that future development and deployment of these technologies be
539   accompanied by robust ethical guidelines and safeguards.
