011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            For nonconvex objective functions, including deep neural networks, stochastic
014            gradient descent (SGD) with momentum has faster convergence and better gen-
015            eralizability than SGD without momentum, but a theoretical explanation for this
016
               is lacking. Adding momentum is thought to reduce stochastic noise, but several
               studies have argued that stochastic noise actually contributes to the generalizabil-
017
               ity of the model, which raises a contradiction. We show that the stochastic noise
018
               in SGD with momentum smoothes the objective function, the degree of which is
019            determined by the learning rate, the batch size, the momentum factor, the variance
020            of the stochastic gradient, and the upper bound of the gradient norm. By numer-
021            ically deriving the stochastic noise level in SGD with and without momentum,
022            we provide theoretical ﬁndings that help explain the training dynamics of SGD
023            with momentum, which were not explained by previous studies on convergence
024            and stability, and that resolve the contradiction. We also provide experimental re-
025            sults for an image classiﬁcation task using ResNets that support our assertion that
026            model generalizability depends on the stochastic noise level.

      1.1   BACKGROUND
031
032   First-order optimizers that use mini-batch stochastic gradients, such as stochastic gradient descent
033   (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951), SGD with momentum (Polyak, 1964; Rumelhart et al., 1986), and
034   adaptive methods (Duchi et al., 2010; Kingma & Ba, 2015), are the most commonly used methods
035   for solving empirical risk minimization problems that appear in machine learning. These methods
036   have been well studied for their convergence (Bottou et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021; 2019; Fehrman
037   et al., 2020; Iiduka, 2022a; Loizou et al., 2021; Scaman & Malherbe, 2020; Zaheer et al., 2018; Zhou
038   et al., 2020a; Zou et al., 2019) and stability (Hardt et al., 2016; He et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2016; Mou
039
      et al., 2018), and it has been shown that tuning the hyperparameters such as the learning rate, batch
      size, and momentum factor is essential for successful training. This paper focuses on the SGD with
040
      momentum method and provides new insights into the role of the momentum factor.
041
042   For nonconvex objective functions, including deep neural networks (DNNs), SGD with momentum
043   experimentally has better generalizability than SGD without momentum (simply ”SGD” hereafter),
044   but theoretical explanations for this characteristic have not yet been provided. The generalizability of
045
      SGD with momentum has been well studied, and various experimental ﬁndings have been reported.
      While it has been suggested that momentum plays a role in reducing stochastic noise (Defazio, 2020;
046
      Cutkosky & Mehta, 2020), stochastic noise has been shown to increase generalizability (Li et al.,
047
      2019; Wen et al., 2020; HaoChen et al., 2021), and it has been claimed that stochastic noise can
048   help an algorithm escape from local solutions with poor generalizability (Ge et al., 2015; Jin et al.,
049   2017; Daneshmand et al., 2018; Harshvardhan & Stich, 2021; Kleinberg et al., 2018). Furthermore,
050   several studies (Shallue et al., 2019; Jelassi & Li, 2022; Kunstner et al., 2023) have shown that the
051   gap in convergence speed and generalizability between SGD and SGD with momentum is more
052   pronounced for large batches. There is an inconsistency in that adding momentum should reduce
053   stochastic noise, but because momentum has excellent generalizability, it should have sufﬁciently
      large noise, and this contradiction makes it difﬁcult to understand the effect of momentum in DNNs.

      The geometry of loss landscapes, in particular the relationship between the ﬂatness of the minima
055   and generalization, has been extensively studied from both theoretical and empirical perspectives
056   (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2017; Dziugaite & Roy, 2017; Jiang et al., 2020;
057   Foret et al., 2021). In general, a local optimal solution with ﬂatter neighborhoods is considered to
058   have better generalizability than that with steeper neighborhoods. Several previous studies (Keskar
059   et al., 2017; Liang et al., 2019; Tsuzuku et al., 2020; Petzka et al., 2021; Kwon et al., 2021) de-
060   veloped measures for the ﬂatness of the minima, “sharpness”. It has been experimentally observed
061   to correlate with the generalization performance of a model. One previous study (Kleinberg et al.,
062   2018) suggested that the objective function is smoothed by stochastic noise in the optimizer. A more
063
      recent study demonstrated that stochastic noise in SGD implicitly smoothes the objective function,
      that the degree of smoothing caused by stochastic noise in SGD and sharpness both represent the
064
      ﬂatness/sharpness of the function, and that the degree of smoothing is correlated with generalization
065
      performance (Sato & Iiduka, 2023b). Based on these studies, our study focused on the smoothing
066   of the objective function by stochastic noise in SGD with momentum and the relationship between
067   the degree of smoothing and the generalizability of the model. When considering stochastic noise
068   in optimizers, most previous studies (Zhang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020b; Kunstner et al., 2023)
069   deﬁned stochastic noise as the difference between the mini-batch stochastic gradient and the full
070   gradient. We call this difference “gradient noise.” Here, in order to discuss smoothing with stochas-
071   tic noise, we deﬁne optimizer’s stochastic noise as the difference between the search direction of
072   the optimizer and the steepest descent direction, which we call “search direction noise.” Search
073   direction noise can be viewed as an extension of gradient noise. Note that gradient noise and search
074
      direction noise in SGD are consistent with each other.
075   The simplest method for adding a momentum term to SGD is the stochastic heavy ball (SHB) method
076   (Algorithm 1) (Polyak, 1964). Although it has been widely used in experiments, it is lacking in
077   theoretical analysis. In contrast, the normalized-SHB (NSHB) method (Algorithm 2 with ν = 1)
078   (Gupal & Bazhenov, 1972) has been well analyzed theoretically for convergence and stability but
079   has rarely been used in experiments. Note that the algorithm referred to as “SGD with momentum
080
      (SGDM)” in many previous studies is actually NSHB, while that provided by PyTorch (Paszke
      et al., 2019) and TensorFlow (Abadi et al., 2016) is SHB. Many variants of the momentum method
081
      have been proposed, including Nesterov’s accelerated gradient method (Nesterov, 1983; 2004; 2013;
082
      Sutskever et al., 2013), synthesized Nesterov variants (Lessard et al., 2016), Triple Momentum (Scoy
083   et al., 2018), Robust Momentum (Cyrus et al., 2018), PID control-based methods (An et al., 2018),
084   accelerated SGD (Jain et al., 2018; Kidambi et al., 2018; Varre & Flammarion, 2022; Li et al., 2024),
085   and quasi-hyperbolic momentum (QHM, Algorithm 2) (Ma & Yarats, 2019). This paper focuses on
086   SHB and QHM, which covers many momentum methods, especially NSHB, but does not cover
087   SHB.
088
      Motivation. Our main goal in this paper is to resolve the contradiction described in Section 1.1 that
089   exists between momentum and stochastic noise and to clarify the role of momentum in DNNs train-
090   ing. It was recently found that the stochastic noise in SGD implicitly smoothes the objective function
091   and that the degree of smoothing is determined by the learning rate, batch size, and variance of the
092   stochastic gradient (Sato & Iiduka, 2023b). We extend this analysis to SGD with momentum, and
093   by focusing on the stochastic noise between the search direction and the steepest descent direction,
094   we attempt to reveal how momentum is involved in the smoothing of the objective function.

371

372   stochastic gradients per step. If T is the number of steps needed to train the DNNs, the optimizer

      In summary, momentum improves generalizability when the batch size is large, but deteriorates
487   generalizability when the batch size is small. There is an impressive correlation between the degree
488   of smoothing and model generalizability, which is why we can say that the degree of smoothing
489   introduced by the optimizer’s search direction noise dominates model training and generalizability.
490
      Remark 5.1. Recently, Wang et al. showed that when the learning rate is small, there is no signiﬁ-
491
      cant difference in generalization performance between SGD and SGD with momentum (Wang et al.,
492
      2024). SGD with momentum in their paper is NSHB in our paper (see (Wang et al., 2024, Deﬁnition
493   2.3) and our Algorithm 2). Our results show that SGD and NSHB have same degrees of smoothing
494   (see Figure 3), which results in nearly same test accuracy (see Figure 4). Therefore, our results do
495   not conﬂict with theirs.
496
      Remark 5.2. Let us explain the relationship between the degree of smoothing and expected loss.
497
      Previous studies (Keskar et al., 2017; Izmailov et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018) have shown that the sharp-
498
      ness around the approximate solution to which the optimizer converges is closely related to the gen-
499   eralization performance of the model, i.e., the expected loss. Sato and Iiduka (Sato & Iiduka, 2023b)
500   experimentally demonstrated that sharpness and the degree of smoothing introduced by search di-
501   rection noise are inextricably linked. That is, when the degree of smoothing is small (resp. large),
502   sharpness is large (resp. small). Thus, the degree of smoothing due to empirical loss is related to
503   expected loss, a measure of true generalization performance, via sharpness. In particular, the degree
504   of smoothing is correlated with generalization performance, as shown by a previous study (Sato &
505   Iiduka, 2023b) and our experimental results.
506   Resolving the contradiction between momentum and stochastic noise. Figure 3 shows that SHB
507   always has a greater degree of smoothing than SGD. Therefore, for SHB, which is often used ex-
508   perimentally, adding momentum increases the search direction noise. We can thus say that adding
509   momentum reduces the variance of the stochastic gradient (see Section 4.3) and conversely increases
510   the degree of smoothing introduced by the search direction noise. This is why the arguments that
511   “adding momentum should reduce stochastic noise” and that “stochastic noise leads to generaliz-
512   ability” do not conﬂict, which resolves the contradiction. Figure 3 also shows that NSHB has the
513
      same degree of smoothing as SGD. Thus, for NSHB, which is rarely used experimentally, adding
      momentum does not contribute to an increase in the degree of smoothing. In fact, the performances
514
      of SGD and NSHB are very similar (see Figures 2 and 4). This not only demonstrates that the de-
515
      gree of smoothing is a hidden factor governing the training of the model but also that the reason
516   NSHB is not as good and not used experimentally as often as SHB is that the degree of smoothing
517   does not differ from that of SGD despite the addition of momentum. Thus, for NSHB, there was no
518   contradiction regarding the momentum term and stochastic noise.

      6   C ONCLUSION
523
524   Our investigation of the smoothing properties of SGD with momentum resolved the contradiction
525   between momentum and stochastic noise, namely that adding momentum reduces gradient noise
526   and conversely increases search direction noise, which contributes to smoothing of the objective
527   function. It also showed that the degree of smoothing is determined by the hyperparameters such as
528   the momentum factor. Through convergence analysis and discussion of critical batch size estima-
529   tion, we derived the degree of smoothing numerically and found an impressive correlation between
530   the degree of smoothing and model generalizability. Speciﬁcally, too large or too small a degree
531
      of smoothing leads to poor generalizability, whereas a moderate one leads to high generalizability.
      From this perspective, we showed that SHB and NSHB are completely different, that NSHB has
532
      almost no experimental value, and that the momentum factor in SHB maintains a high degree of
533
      smoothing even when the batch is large. The relationship between the degree of smoothing and
534   model generalizability is, so to speak, a hidden factor in DNNs training, and it helps in selecting
535   the optimal hyperparameters and understanding the training dynamics of a DNNs. Finally, we em-
536   phasize that the degree of smoothing introduced by search direction noise is determined by several
537   hyperparameters, including the learning rate and batch size, that are easier to grasp than sharpness.
538   They are thus useful as a new measure of generalization performance. Deriving or estimating the
539   optimal degree of smoothing for generalization performance is important future work, which, if
      accomplished, will reduce the huge computational cost of hyperparameter tuning.

1735        D.1                  E XPERIMENTS ON GENERALIZABILITY OF SHB AND NSHB
1736
1737        We suggest that the generalizability of the model is determined by the degree of smoothing. In
1738        both SGD and SGD with momentum, if the degree of smoothing δ is too low, the process can be
1739        considered equivalent to optimizing a function fˆδ close to the original multimodal function f by gra-
1740        dient descent, which leads to a sharp local optimal solution and less than excellent generalizability.
1741        Therefore, a sufﬁciently large degree of smoothing is required to obtain sufﬁcient generalizability.
1742
            On the other hand, from Lemma 2.1, too high a degree of smoothing may conversely lead to large
            deviations from the original function and may prevent successful optimization. We conﬁrmed these
1743
            considerations by experiment. We used learning rate of 0.1 and momentum factor of 0.9 in all
1744
            experiments.
1745
1746        As shown in Figures 3 and 18, the degree of smoothing with both SHB methods stopped decreasing
1747        and stagnated from a certain batch size. Let us call b̂SHB the batch size at which stagnation begins.
1748        For the training of ResNet18, b̂SHB = 27 , while for the training of WideResNet-28-10, b̂SHB =
1749        24 . Therefore, when using an SHB method with a batch size greater than 27 for the training of
1750        ResNet18 and greater than 24 for the training of WideResNet-28-10, the generalizability should be
1751
            approximately equal since they can be regarded as optimizing smoothed functions with noise levels
            approximately equal.
