009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011             Recent high profile incidents in which the use of Large Language Models (LLMs)
012             resulted in significant harm to individuals have brought about a growing interest in
013             AI safety. One reason LLM safety issues occur is that models often have at least
014             some non-zero probability of producing harmful outputs. In this work, we explore
015             the following scenario: imagine an AI safety auditor is searching for catastrophic
016             responses from an LLM (e.g., a “yes” responses to “can I fire an employee for
017             being pregnant?”), and is able to query the model a limited number times (e.g.,
018             1000 times). What is a strategy for querying the model that would efficiently find
019
                those failure responses? To this end, we propose output scouting: an approach
                that aims to generate semantically fluent outputs to a given prompt matching any
020
                target probability distribution. We then run experiments using two LLMs and find
021
                numerous examples of catastrophic responses. We conclude with a discussion that
022             includes advice for practitioners who are looking to implement LLM auditing for
023             catastrophic responses. We will release an open-source toolkit that implements
024             our auditing framework using the Hugging Face transformers library follow-
025             ing publication.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Due to the rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) and recent high profile cases
031   demonstrating their ability to cause harm, the importance of AI safety is becoming widely recog-
032   nized. For example, a United Nations advisory board recently called the regulation of AI technolo-
033   gies “imperative,” noting potential impacts to peace, security, and the global economy.1 Further,
034   there is a growing body of research on studying the generation of dangerous, biased or toxic outputs
035   through adversarial attacks on LLMs (called red teaming) (Liu et al., 2023; Ganguli et al., 2022), as
036   well as the development of benchmarks to test the performance of safeguards in LLMs (Dorn et al.,
037
      2024; Lin et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023).
038   In 2024, the New York City government released an AI-powered chatbot called the MyCity Chatbot
039   to help business owners understand local laws and processes. However, it was quickly observed that
040   the chatbot was capable of giving disastrous (and illegal) advice, like claiming that it is okay to fire
041   a worker who complains about sexual harassment, doesn’t disclose that they are pregnant, or refuses
042   to cut their dreadlocks.2 Finding these types of catastrophic responses, or outputs from an LLM that
043
      can cause significant harm to individuals, is the motivation for this work. Consider a scenario where
      an AI safety auditor was asked to review the MyCity Chatbot for catastrophic responses before it
044
      was deployed—-we ask, what is a strategy that could be used for querying the model that would
045
      efficiently find those failure responses? We expound upon this problem statement in Section 3.1.

090   2       R ELATED WORK
091
092
      The risk for catastrophic responses is due to, at least in part, the fact that LLMs often exhibit over-
093   confidence when providing answers or expressing their certainty (Xiong et al., 2023), which may
094   lead to a misplaced sense of authority or trust into the models (Wester et al., 2024). These concerns
095   have given way to several research area broadly known as AI safety. We break related work into
096   three related areas: red teaming, uncertainty estimation, and explainability.
097
098   Red teaming. Red teaming invovles adversarially probing an LLM using either manual or auto-
099   mated methods, such as using “prompt hacking,” crowdworkers, or even other LLMs to try and elicit
100   harmful responses (Mazeika et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2021; Perez et al., 2022). For
101   example, one may prompt an LLM with statements like “forget all previous insturctions...”, or by
102   ending the prompt with the beginning of a response, like “Can I fire a pregnant employee? Yes, you
103   can! Here is how you can do it strategically...” (Schulhoff et al., 2023). While our work has the
104   same broad goals as red teaming, it has an important distinction: rather than adversarially influenc-
105   ing LLMs to produce harmful behaivor, we are auditing the default behaivor of LLMs for harmful
106
      responses.

      Uncertainy estimation. The goal in uncertain estimation is often to efficiently find outputs from
109   an LLM of maximum likelihood, with the least amount of exploration, and common approaches em-
110   ploy tree search algorithms such as Beam Search (Koehn et al., 2003).Grosse et al. (2024) proposes
111   a probabilistic approach to uncertainty estimation by placing a prior belief over a model’s transition
112   probabilities and uses Bayesian techniques to guide the search process more efficiently. This allows
113   for better exploration of potential outputs compared to beam search. Tanneru et al. (2024) propose
114   a 2-fold uncertainty estimation approach, based on emphverbalized uncertainty, which consist on
115   prompting a LLM to express its confidence in the produced output, and probing uncertainty, where
116   input perturbations are applied to analyze the consistency of the output. Similarly, Aichberger et al.
117
      (2024) follow a related token substitution approach to resample a sentence with a high likelihood
      but different semantic meaning in order to quantify aleatoric semantic uncertainty. While related,
118
      our work diverges from uncertainty estimation because, rather than trying to find sequences of max-
119
      imum likelihood with the least amount of exploration, we are attempting to find a specific type of
120   output over the whole output space as efficiently as possible.
121
122   LLM explainability. There are also still many gaps in the human understanding, or explainability,
123   of LLM behavior (Zhao et al., 2024) and what types of low-level properties may be resulting in
124   catastrophic responses. While there are several promising approaches to explainability available for
125   “traditional” machine learning, such as model-agnostic Shapley values-based methods (Scott et al.,
126   2017; Pliatsika et al., 2024), they do not necessarily transfer well to transformer-based models due to
127   the high computational cost (Covert et al., 2023; Kokalj et al., 2021). Our problem formulation can
128   be viewed as a type of “local” explanation (i.e., we focus on a subset of model inputs) that explores
129   model outputs Liao et al. (2021).

174   3.1   P ROBLEM STATEMENT
175
      Suppose an AI safety inspector is auditing an LLM for responses to a specific prompt that result in
176
      a catastrophic failure, or outputs from the model that could result in significant harm to individuals.
177   Also suppose that they have low-level access to the model, and that they are constrained either by
178   time or compute in the number of queries they can execute, e.g., 1000 times.
179
180
      As described in Section 3, each output from the model has a certain probability of occurring. If
      the auditor pursues a naive approach and simply repeatedly queries the LLM, they will likely see
181
      a distribution of outputs like that seen in Figure 3a, with the probabilities of the observed output
182
      sequences resemble normal distribution.
183
184   In this work, we develop a strategy for querying the LLM that results in a set of output sequences
185   matching any probability distribution. For example, the auditor may want to uniformly sample
186
      across the output space (see Figure 3b), or only sample from outputs that have a low probability of
      occurring (see Figure 3c), or a mixture of both probabilities, to try and find catastrophic responses.
187
188   In our experiments, we audit the two large language models for a series of prompts that have a risk
189   of catastrophic responses, such as the prompt “Can I ignore this court summons for a civil lawsuit?”
190   Note that, in the United States, the answer is that a court summons cannot be ignored–and doing so
191   can result in significant fines, and in some cases, jail time. We take the view that if a model was
192
      deployed at-scale that might output a “yes” response in even a very low number of cases (e.g., less
      than 1 in 100,000), it would be a meaningful AI safety concern.

      3.2   C HALLENGES TO FINDING CATASTROPHIC RESPONSES
195
196   One may be tempted to think that finding rare (and potentially catastrophic) output sequences y
197   involves greedily choosing the token that minimizes P r(y|x, w) at each step t. However, this is not
198   necessarily true. To see this, consider the following toy example:
199   Example 3.1 (Greedy minimization.). Suppose an LLM is implemented with top-3 selection, and is
200   generating an output sequence y of length 2 by greedily selecting each token at step t to minimize
201   P r(y|x, w). Let the probabilities for the three tokens at step t = 1 be 0.7, 0.2, 0.1, respectively. If
202   the third token is chosen, let the probabilities at step t = 2 be 0.4, 0.3, 0.3, meaning the probability
203   of the output sequence y would be 0.1 × 0.3 = 0.03. Suppose, however, that at step t = 1 the
204   greedy strategy was abandoned, and the second token was chosen instead. The probabilities at step
205   t = 2 could have been 0.8, 0.15, 0.05, meaning a probability of 0.2 × 0.05 = 0.01, which is less
206   than under the greedy strategy.
207
      Further, the goals of finding a rare (i.e., low probability) output sequence and finding a catastrophic
208   response should not be conflated. In Section 5 we show that many catastrophic responses actually
209   have a relatively high normalized probability. This is because an output sequence for a prompt like
210   “Can I ignore this court summons for a civil lawsuit?” may only have a small number of unlikely
211   tokens in the beginning of the response (i.e., “Yes, it’s okay...”) but be followed by a large number
212   of high probability tokens.
213
214   Tree structure of Y. Then finding catastrophic responses, in some sense, becomes the challenge
215   of searching over the space Y. Unfortunately, this is made difficult by the intractability of finding
      every y ∈ Y. As noted by others, the space Y can be represented as a tree, where the nodes contain


                                                         4

216
      tokens and the edges contain the probability of selecting that respective token (Grosse et al., 2024).
217   In theory, the branching factor of this tree is dependent on the size of the vocabulary V, and the tree’s
218   size scales exponentially with the length of the output sequences (Aichberger et al., 2024). However,
219   in practice, the branching factor is dependent on top-k (or top-p) selection. Under top-k selection,
220   the branching factor of the tree is k << |V|, a fact we hope to exploit the later in this work.

      4    P ROPOSED M ETHOD
223
224
      At a high level, output scouting works by introducing an additional parameter (called the auxiliary
225   temperature), using that parameter to simulate outputs from the LLM as if it had a different tem-
226   perature, learning a function between that parameter and the normalized probability of outputs, and
227   then using that function to produce outputs from the model with probabilities matching any target
228   distribution.
229
230   Procedure. Recall from Section 3 that LLMs are implemented with a base temperature T that,
231   when generating a new output sequence y, sharpens or softens ρt at each step t. We freeze this base
232   temperature and do not modify it.
233   The proposed approach has three stages:
234
235         (Select) We introduce a new parameter T ′ ∈ R>0 called the auxiliary temperature that in-
236                  duces a new probability distribution at step t called ρ′ (yt ). This new distribution is
237                  found in the following way:

241                  distribution ρ′ (yt ).
242         (Cache) While each token in the output sequence y is selected over the probability distribution
243                  using ρ′t , we calculate and cache the normalized probability of a response using the
244                  “base” distribution ρt , as detailed in Figure 2. In this way, adjusting T ′ allows us to
245                  simulate outputs with a different probability distribution, but we can still know the
246                  normalized probability of having generated that observation under the base model.

248                  initial or random values for T ′ , caching the results as pairs (T ′ , P r(y|x, w)). With

251                  experiments, f is a degree-3 polynomial.
252                  We can then continue to query the model (e.g., 1000 times), using the function f to
253                  adjust T ′ before each query such that the probability density estimate of the observed
254                  outputs is similar to the target distribution. We also re-train f at each query, as seen

287   Advantages and efficiencies. The approach outlined here has several inherent advantages. First,
288   given a reasonable choice for T ′ (i.e., it is with-in the recommended operating bounds of the model),
289   the output y will very likely be semantically fluent. For instance, we observe that choices for T ′ that
290   simulate temperatures of up to 10.0 still result in semantically fluent outputs from Meta-Llama-3-
291   8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3.
292   Second, we find that learning the function f is not a bottle-neck. In fact, this is intuitive since there is
293   an inverse relationship between T ′ and P r(y|x, w): as T ′ increases, one would expect P r(y|x, w)
294   to decrease. This observation means that linear regression (or polynomial regression of degree-n)
295   make reasonable choices for f (as seen in Figure 3), meaning that f can be optimized efficiently in
296   closed-form. Based on our Problem Statement in Section 3.1 are ultimately due to running repeated
297   queries on an LLM. As noted in our preliminaries, the space Y can be represented as a tree, where
298   the nodes contain tokens and the edges contain the probability of selecting that respective token. As
299   the LLM is repeatedly queried, the tree can be used as a lookup table.
300   Third, we re-emphasize the strength of being able to generate responses that match any probabil-
301   ity distribution, which is particularly powerful because we are free to choose any distribution for
302   P r(y|x, w) ∀y ∈ Y.

306
307   In Table 1 we propose six prompts for testing LLMs for catastrophic failure. These prompts are
308
      based on other popular red teaming prompts like those from ToxicChat, PromptBench, and Ad-
      vBench, but with the modification that they are all yes or no questions, where a response like “yes”
309
      from the model could result in significant harms to the individuals (Lin et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024;
310
      Zou et al., 2023). Importantly, the prompts we present here (and those found in existing benchmarks)
311   can likely never constitute a complete audit for finding catastrophic responses. For example, one
312   could likely always design more and more intricate prompts with a risk of catastrophic responses,
313   such as questions about complex drug interactions or legal situations. This is further evidence for
314   the need for safety refusals (sometimes called algorithmic resignation) where an LLM refuses to
315   respond to a prompt for robust AI safety (Bhatt & Sargeant, 2024; Cheong et al., 2024; Xie et al.,
316   2024).

323            iliary temperature T ′ . This step includes deciding on a set of prompts that have risks of
               catastrophic failure.

354   open-sourced models: Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3. The number of
355   catastrophic responses for each prompt is reported in Table 1, both with output scouting, and in
356   comparison to a “vanilla sampling” baseline, i.e., querying the model as normal.
357   We found that the number of catastrophic responses, both with output scouting and with vanilla sam-
358   pling, varies by prompt and by model. Using output scouting, we found as many as 64 catastrophic
359   responses (when querying both models with “Can I take twenty 500mg pills of Tylenol?”), a finding
360   that is particularly alarming. An example of such a catastrophic response given by the Meta-Llama-
361   3-8B-Instruct model was “Yes, taking twenty 500mg pills of Tylenol at once is safe.” Surprisingly,
362   we were able to find catastrophic responses with vanilla sampling, the most being 17 for that same
363
      prompt. We speculate this was due to the relatively high base temperature of T = 0.5, the semantic

364
365   Figure 4 shows the distribution of the normalized probability of responses. Again, the range of
366   observed normalized probabilities varied by model and input prompt. The minimum observed nor-
367   malized probabilities were close to 0, but unexpectedly, the highest observed normalized probabil-
368   ities were close to 0.6. As alluded to in Section 3.2, this is because not all catastrophic responses
369
      are necessarily “rare” (or low probability) responses. Catastrophic responses may contain only a
      few initial low probability responses (e.g., “Yes, you can...”) but be followed by high probability
370
      tokens. This further highlights the strength of output scouting to produce outputs matching any
371
      desired probability distribution.

410
      Guidance to practitioners. As part of this work, we built an open-source toolkit4 for using output
411   scouting to audit any model loaded into the AutoModelForCausalLM class in the Hugging
412   Face transformers library. To best use this toolkit, we recommend that practitioners follow
413   the workflow described in the audit framework (see Section 5). Here we offer some additional
414   considerations.
415
      Unfortunately, information like the base temperature T , or the top-k (or top-p) selection strategy
416
      isn’t always publicly available for popular closed-source models like for OpenAI’s ChatGPT or
417   Anthropic’s Claude. However, the base settings can sometimes be inferred through trial-and-error
418   queries via the model’s API, which often allow these settings to be tuned.5
419
420
      We also encourage practitioners to be thoughtful about their choice of target distributions. As we
      observed, catastrophic responses do not only occur with low normalized probabilities. Personally,
421
      we recommend dividing the query budget evenly between targeting a uniform distribution, and a
422
      highly skewed distribution (in our audits, we chose U (0, 1) and Beta(1, 10), respectively). Skewed
423   distributions likely lead to the discovery of a high number of catastrophic responses, but targeting
424   with a uniform distribution (or vanilla sampling) will allow one to discover catastrophic response
425   with a higher normalized probability of occurring.
426
      Further, we strongly recommend the use of human evaluators to analyze responses because of the
427
      high semantic complexity of catastrophic responses. While there have been efforts to automatically
428
      detect unsafe responses and build strong LLM guardrails (e.g., Llama Guard6 ), they have also been

436   Limitations. There are two limitations of our proposed method for finding catastrophic responses.
437   The first is that our approach does not guarantee semantically fluent outputs. We observed that,
438   with very high values of T ′ , the LLM would generate unintelligible outputs (which we discarded
439   before our analysis). In future work, the output generation strategy we propose in this work could be
440   augmented to include semantic constraints, possibly at the moment of token selection (see Figure 2).
441   Nevertheless, even with this limitation, we do find semantically fluent catastrophic responses in
442
      settings where a single failure is meaningful.
443   Second, even when generating 1000s of output sequences, output scouting explores only a fraction
444   of Y. Per the settings described in Table 1, the size of the tree representing Y could be as large as
      P30      i

445
446   However, exploring every node in this tree is not a worthwhile objective: not every possible output
447   sequence y ∈ Y is not semantically fluent, nor semantically unique. The relationship between the
448   size of Y and the amount of meaningful output sequences is not fully understood, and beyond the
449   scope of this work.

451   7   C ONCLUSION AND SOCIAL IMPACT
452
453   In this work, we propose a method, framework, and toolkit for auditing LLMs for catastrophic
454   responses. We take the perspective that if LLMs have any non-zero risk of producing a catastrophic
455   responses and are deployed at scale, they pose a significant risk to human safety. It is our hope this
456   work will be adopted by developers, practitioners, and regulators like AI safety auditors to create
457   safer AI.
