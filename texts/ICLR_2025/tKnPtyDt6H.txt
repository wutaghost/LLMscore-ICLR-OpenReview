009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) become increasingly versatile, numerous large
012            scale benchmarks have been developed to thoroughly assess their capabilities.
013            These benchmarks typically consist of diverse datasets and prompts to evaluate
014            different aspects of LLM performance. However, comprehensive evaluations on
015            hundreds or thousands of prompts incur tremendous costs in terms of computation,
016            money, and time. In this work, we investigate strategies to improve evaluation ef-
017            ficiency by selecting a subset of examples from each benchmark using a learned
018
               policy. Our approach models the dependencies across test examples, allowing ac-
               curate prediction of the evaluation outcomes for the remaining examples based
019
               on the outcomes of the selected ones. Consequently, we only need to acquire the
020
               actual evaluation outcomes for the selected subset. We rigorously explore vari-
021            ous subset selection policies and introduce a novel RL-based policy that leverages
022            the captured dependencies. Empirical results demonstrate that our approach sig-
023            nificantly reduces the number of evaluation prompts required while maintaining
024            accurate performance estimates compared to previous methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   By scaling up parameters and pretraining data, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated
030   remarkable abilities to solve various tasks. To reliably evaluate these capabilities and compare dif-
031   ferent models, modern LLM benchmarks typically employ a comprehensive set of examples that
032   focus on different aspects of performance. For instance, HELM (Liang et al., 2022), a widely used
033
      LLM benchmark covering diverse task families, includes 42 scenarios and approximately 600,000
      queries. However, the comprehensiveness of these benchmarks inevitably incurs significant eval-
034
      uation costs. For example, evaluating on the HELM benchmark requires 4,200 GPU hours for a
035
      176B BLOOM model and $9,337 for text-davinci-002 API calls (Liang et al., 2022). Furthermore,
036   these substantial costs hamper development at both the modeling and inference stages, preventing
037   frequent evaluation during the former and extensive hyperparameter tuning – such as decoding and
038   prompting strategies – during the latter.
039
      In this work, we aim to improve evaluation efficiency by reducing the number of evaluation prompts.
040
      We first observe that evaluation prompts are highly correlated, meaning that a model’s (in)correct
041
      prediction on a certain prompt is likely to correspond with (in)correct predictions on related prompts.
042   To leverage this, we build a model to formally capture the dependencies across prompts. This
043   model can predict evaluation scores based on observed scores from a subset of prompts. Given
044   these dependencies, our goal becomes identifying the minimal subset of prompts that can accurately
045   recover the evaluation scores for the remaining prompts.
046
      Instead of using a fixed subset of prompts across all models, we propose selecting a unique subset of
047
      prompts for each model to evaluate its performance more efficiently. The goal of our active evalua-
048   tion acquisition (AEA) approach is to find the most informative subset of prompts for each model,
049   allowing us to predict performance on the remaining prompts. The key insight is that models may
050   have varying strengths; for example, one model may excel in arithmetic reasoning while another
051   shows stronger commonsense reasoning. Tailoring the subset of prompts for each model ensures a
052   more accurate and targeted evaluation of its capabilities. Furthermore, our dynamic acquisition pro-
053   cess allows us to adapt in real time as evaluation scores are gathered. As the model’s performance on
      initial prompts is observed, the system adjusts subsequent prompt selections to better explore areas

      of uncertainty or confirm early findings. This iterative approach not only enhances the accuracy of
055   performance estimation but also reduces redundancy by avoiding prompts that are likely to yield
056   predictable results, thereby saving computational resources and time. Importantly, the final evalua-
057   tion score is derived from both the acquired scores on selected prompts and predicted scores on the
058   remaining prompts, ensuring comparability across models is maintained.
059
      Our contributions are as follows: 1) We tackle LLM evaluation efficiency through dependency mod-
060
      eling and subset selection, connecting LLM evaluation with the extensive literature on subset selec-
061   tion. 2) We design a generative model that captures dependencies across evaluation prompts and
062   handles mixed-type evaluation scores, including both discrete and real-valued scores. 3) We thor-
063   oughly test existing subset selection algorithms on several popular LLM evaluation benchmarks,
064   including MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), HELM (Liang et al., 2022), HuggingFace Open LLM
065   Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023), AlpaceEval (Li et al., 2023), and Chatbot Arena (Zheng et al.,
066   2024). 4) We develop several new subset selection policies based on the dependency model and
067   demonstrate their superiority over existing methods, with our RL-based acquisition policy achiev-
068   ing the best performance using the lowest acquisition budget. 5) We propose and investigate the
069
      cold-start problem, where new prompts are added to a benchmark without prior evaluation scores
      for any model, and extend our RL-based policy to deal with the situation effectively.

127   The exchangeability condition requires that the joint distribution p(Ym ) remain unchanged under

175   A static acquisition policy determines the set of prompts to be evaluated beforehand, and each model

179

180

181   embedding from an Item Response Theory (IRT) model (Hambleton & Swaminathan, 2013; Em-
182   bretson & Reise, 2013), which represents the difficulty and discriminability of each prompt. The

187   evaluation scores, with the size of the vector corresponding to the number of evaluated models in
188   the training set. The Clustering-Score method has been used as a baseline in (Polo et al., 2024).

      2.4   C OLD S TART P ROBLEM
271
272   So far, we have considered scenarios where the set of prompts is fixed for each benchmark. However,
273   as language models advance, new capabilities may emerge that need to be assessed. Therefore, it is
274   crucial to address the cold start problem, where the benchmark must be expanded with new prompts
275   for which no evaluation scores are initially available for any model.
276   Expanding the benchmark with new prompts introduces several challenges. Firstly, predicting eval-
277   uation scores on these new prompts is difficult for a neural process trained on previously observed
278   scores. Secondly, determining which new prompts to select for the expanded benchmark is challeng-
279   ing, as these new prompts may introduce capabilities or areas not well-represented in the original
280   set, making it hard to gauge their relevance and difficulty relative to the existing prompts.
281
      To help the neural process model generalize to new prompts, we introduce a semi-supervised train-
282   ing procedure where the new prompts are treated as unlabeled data. We found that simple pseudo-
283   labeling approaches (Lee et al., 2013; Xie et al., 2020; Du et al., 2020) work well. Specifically,
284   we add the new prompts and their predicted evaluation scores into the training process if the un-
285   certainties of the predicted evaluation scores are below a predefined threshold. In our prelimi-
286   nary experiments, we also tested several regularization approaches, such as entropy minimization
287   (Grandvalet & Bengio, 2004) and consistency regularization (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017), which
288   are commonly used for semi-supervised learning, but they consistently underperformed compared
289   to pseudo-labeling. A systematic exploration of semi-supervised techniques for neural process train-
290
      ing is beyond the scope of this paper and will be left for future work.
291   Static acquisition policies are suboptimal for the cold start problem because they rely on the available
292   evaluation scores to determine the set of prompts to be evaluated, meaning the evaluation scores
293   on new prompts will never be acquired. One exception is Clustering-Embed method, where the
294   clustering is based solely on the embedding of the new prompts rather than their evaluation scores.
295   In contrast, dynamic acquisition policies are better suited to handle the cold start problem due to
296
      their adaptive nature. However, since the RL-based acquisition policy is trained to acquire evaluation
      scores on the existing prompts, it requires the acquisition policy to generalize to new prompts.
297

313   Active Learning Active learning (Fu et al., 2013; Konyushkova et al., 2017; Yoo & Kweon, 2019)
314
      addresses the problem of having a learner select specific examples to query an oracle for their labels,
      with the goal of learning a better model using as few labeled examples as possible. In contrast, our
315
      proposed AEA framework focuses on evaluating a model with fewer examples to accurately predict
316
      the evaluation scores for the remaining examples.
317
318   Active Testing Active testing (Kossen et al., 2021) reduces the labeling cost by selectively choosing
319   test points to label, ensuring sample-efficient model evaluation. While this aligns with the goal of
320
      efficient evaluation, our work specifically targets reducing the cost of running evaluations on a large
      number of prompts, rather than minimizing labeling costs.

358   4   E XPERIMENTS
359
360   In this section, we assess various evaluation acquisition policies on several popular LLM bench-
361   marks. We divide the available leaderboard scores into training and test splits. The training split is
362   used to fit the neural processes model, capturing the dependencies across prompts. The acquisition
363   policies are executed for each model in the test split to acquire the evaluation scores for a subset of
364   prompts. The evaluation scores on the remaining prompts are predicted based on the corresponding
365   neural process model. The final score for each benchmark is computed as a weighted average across
366   datasets, and we report the absolute differences between the predicted scores and the actual scores.

466   Cold Start Problem To evaluate the cold start scenario, we create a synthetic benchmark using
467   MMLU by designating 15 subsets as cold start prompts. During the training of the neural pro-
468   cess model and the acquisition policies, the evaluation scores on these 15 subsets are not available.
469
      Although the evaluation scores are missing, we assume the prompts themselves are given, allow-
      ing random policies and Clustering-Embed static policy to be evaluated without any modifications.
470
      However, the Clustering-Score and Clustering-IRT policies will never acquire evaluation scores for
471
      these 15 subsets since these policies require access to the evaluation scores to determine whether a
472   prompt will be acquired or not. On the other hand, dynamic acquisition policies can easily adapt to
473   the cold start setting, as they acquire evaluation scores sequentially and actively.
474
475
      Figure 3 presents the results on the synthetic cold start MMLU benchmark. The performance is
      evaluated over all 57 subsets during testing. As expected, the Clustering-Score and Clustering-IRT
476
      policies do not perform well in the cold start setting because the evaluation scores on the 15 left-out
477
      subsets are never acquired. The Clustering-Embed policy performs better than the other clustering
478   based policies as it can select the cold start prompts by clustering based on their embeddings. The
479   RL-based acquisition policy again achieves the best performance estimation. However, it is worth
480   noting that the final estimated benchmark performance is not as accurate as in the fully observed
481   setting (Fig. 1), indicating potential areas for future improvement to narrow the gap.

      5   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we present a novel approach for efficient LLM evaluation by leveraging dependency
528   modeling and subset selection. Our key contributions include developing a generative model that
529   captures dependencies across evaluation prompts and handles mixed-type evaluation scores, as well
530   as proposing new subset selection policies based on these dependencies. Extensive experiments
531   on multiple LLM evaluation benchmarks demonstrate the superiority of our RL-based acquisition
532   policy in providing accurate benchmark performance estimation with a minimal acquisition budget.
533   Our results also emphasize the importance of robustness to model bias and the effectiveness of
534   our approach in cold start scenarios. Future research could explore integrating continual learning
535
      frameworks to enhance performance in the presence of model bias and cold starts. Additionally,
      expanding our methods to other benchmarks and refining the neural process model with improved
536
      uncertainty estimation are promising areas for further investigation.

713   A.1   A RCHITECTURE
714                                                                      (o)
      The neural process model consists of a prior network p(zm | Ym , X), a posterior network q(zm |
715     (u)    (o)                         (u)           (o)
716
      Ym , Ym , X), and a decoder p(Ym | zm , Ym , X). We generally follow the architecture of
      Attentive Neural Process (Kim et al., 2019), but replace the self-attention layer with a more memory-
717
      efficient Set Transformer layer (Lee et al., 2018). We also share the same network for both the
718
      prior and posterior. Before feeding the prompt embeddings into the prior/posterior network, we use
719   an additional linear layer to reduce the dimensionality of the extracted representations. Similarly,
720   the evaluation scores are passed through a linear layer to increase their dimensionality. We then
721   concatenate the prompt representation with the score representation along the feature dimension and
722   pass the concatenated set of vectors through a series of permutation equivariant Set Transformer
723   layers. The outputs are then aggregated across the set elements to obtain a feature representation for
724   the entire set. Following Set Transformer approach, we use learned pooling by multihead attention.
725   The set representation is then passed through a linear linear to obtain the parameters for the latent
726   distribution, which we assume to be Gaussian here. Please see Fig. 1(a) for an illustration of the
727
      prior/posterior network.
728                                (u)         (o)
      The decoder network p(Ym | zm , Ym , X) uses a cross-attention layer to produce a permutation
729   equivariant representation for each prompt. In this layer, the query is the representation for X, the
730   key is the representation for X (o) , and the value is the permutation equivariant representation cor-
731                    (o)
      responding to Ym from the prior network. The permutation equivariant representation for each
732   prompt is then concatenated with the prompt representation and the latent vector. These concate-
733   nated inputs are processed through a series of Set Transformer layers. The final outputs are then
734   passed through a linear layer to predict the evaluation scores. Please see Fig. 1(b) for an illustration
735   of the decoder network.
736
737   Mixed-type Evaluation Scores The above architecture uses a linear layer to obtain the represen-
738   tation for the evaluation scores. However, the linear layer is not suitable for discrete scores. Instead,
739   we use an Embedding layer to represent the categorical evaluation scores. When a benchmark
740   contains mixed-type scores, meaning some datasets report real-valued metrics while others report
741   discrete scores, we additionally include an embedding vector to indicate the metric types.

                                                                       (o)

852   the policy has access to the action space. Including the candidate prompts in the inputs is crucial
853   in the cold start setting since the action space differs between training and testing. Similar to the
854   neural process model, the policy network employs two linear layers to obtain representations for
855

      sion. For the candidate prompts without available evaluation scores, we use a special embedding
856

857

861   to the inner product of the output vector and the prompt representations. To prevent the policy from
862   selecting duplicate prompts, the probability of the already selected prompts is manually set to zero.

883   In the cold start setting, the benchmark is expanded with new prompts for which no evaluation scores
884   are initially available for any model. That is, the original benchmark X = {xn }N n=1 have evaluation

      To enable the neural process model generalize to the newly added prompts, we propose a semi-
888   supervised training procedure, where the new prompts are treated as unlabeled data. During training,
889   we optimize the log-likelihood equation 3 for X and Ym . Simultaneously, we predict the evaluation
890   scores for the new prompts X ′ based on the current trained model. When the prediction is suffi-
891   ciently accurate, meaning the uncertainty is lower than a predefined threshold, we add the predicted
892   scores as synthetic training data to optimize the ELBO equation 3.
893
      The RL policy in the cold start setting follows a similar architecture to Sec. C. To help the policy
894   generalize to unseen prompts, we use the learned prompt representations from the neural process
895   model and keep them fixed throughout the training process. Additionally, we found that entropy
896   regularization over the actor distribution aids generalization, which is set to 0.001 in our experi-
897   ments.

946   E.2    E VALUATION P ROCEDURE
947
948   For a model m′ to be evaluated, the acquisition policy determines a subset of prompts X (o) to ac-
                                                                    (u)    (o)
949   quire the true evaluation scores. The neural process model p(Ym′ | Ym′ , X) predicts the evaluation
950   scores for the remaining prompts. The benchmark performance is then estimated based on these pre-
951   dicted scores. For benchmarks with only one dataset, the benchmark performance is the average over
952   all examples. For benchmarks with multiple datasets, the benchmark performance is averaged over
953   the performance of each dataset. For example, the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard consists
954
      of 6 datasets, so the benchmark performance is the average of the performance on these 6 datasets.
      The MMLU dataset further contains 57 subsets, so its performance is the average over these 57
955
      subsets. For Chatbot Arena, we report win rate as the benchmark performance. For final evaluation
956
      results, we compute the absolute difference between the predicted benchmark performance and the
957   real benchmark performance for each model in the test split and report the average absolute error
958   over all models in the test split.

       random acquisition policies generally are competitive, particularly because they are efficient and do
973    not rely on any other models to determine the prompt selection.

975    The Cluster-Embed policy does not perform any better than the random selection, suggesting that

982
       marks where it is computationally feasible. We attribute this to a potential distribution shift between
       the models used for training and those used for testing, suggesting that the static policy optimized
983
       on training models does not generalize well to new models during testing.
984
985    The uncertainty sampling based acquisition policy does not perform well across all benchmarks.
986    Theoretically, the uncertainty sampling method requires a good estimation of the aleatoric uncer-

988
       bines the aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties. Quantifying and decomposing the aleatoric and

       Wimmer et al., 2023; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021), which we leave for future work to explore
990
       for our AEA application. Similarly, the information gain based acquisition policy also requires accu-
991    rate uncertainty estimation, which is challenging, especially with scarce training data on AlpacaEval
