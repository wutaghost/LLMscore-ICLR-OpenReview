011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Adversarial attacks in deep learning represent a significant threat to the integrity and
014            reliability of machine learning models. These attacks involve intentionally crafting
015            perturbations to input data that, while often imperceptible to humans, can lead
016            to incorrect predictions by the model. This phenomenon exposes vulnerabilities
017            in deep learning systems across various applications, from image recognition to
018            natural language processing. Adversarial training has been a popular defence
019
               technique against these adversarial attacks. The research community has been
               increasingly interested in interpreting robust models and understanding how they
020
               defend against attacks.
021
               In this work, we capitalize on a network architecture, namely Deep Linearly Gated
022            Networks (DLGN), which has better interpretation capabilities than regular network
023            architectures. Using this architecture, we interpret robust models trained using PGD
024            adversarial training (9) and compare them with standard training. Feature networks
025            in these architectures act as feature extractors, making them the only medium
026            through which an adversary can attack the model. So, we use the feature network
027            in this architecture with fully connected layers to analyse properties like alignment
028            of the hyperplanes, hyperplane relation with PCA, and sub-network overlap among
029            classes and compare these properties between robust and standard models. We
030
               also consider this architecture having CNN layers wherein we qualitatively and
               quantitatively contrast gating patterns between robust and standard models. We
031
               use ideas from visualization to understand the representations used by robust and
032
               standard models.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION AND RELATED WORKS
037
038   Relu activation can be viewed as the product of input and gates that are off/on. These gates trigger
039   certain pathways in the network to be active/inactive. Lakshminarayanan & Vikram Singh (2020)
040   propose a unique approach by viewing model training as active sub-network learning in Relu-activated
041   neural networks. Neural networks can be viewed as model input being mapped into the path space
042   (path space representation given by neural path features (NPF)) wherein they are combined together
043   in the path space to generate model output logits. The coefficients of these combinations in path space
044   are provided by the model weights, captured using neural path value (NPV). They introduce Deep
045
      Gated Neural Network (DGN) architecture to demonstrate the role of active sub-network learning that
      has two nearly identical sub-networks: feature network, which is responsible for extracting features
046
      and providing gating signals (thereby solely encoding NPFs); value network, which aggregates the
047
      features extracted by the feature network (thereby solely encoding NPVs) to produce the final model
048   prediction. A follow-up study by Lakshminarayanan et al. (2022) show that interpreting the value
049   network visually is meaningless in DGN networks. However interpreting feature network is still hard
050   due to the non-linearity in the feature network layers. So, to improve interpretability of DGNs, they
051   propose new architecture namely Deep Linearly Gated Neural Networks (DLGN) wherein the gating
052   signals are completely moved out of the feature network, rendering the transformations in the feature
053   network entirely linear. The DLGN architecture offer significant interpretability advantages due to
      the feature network being entirely linear, facilitating understanding and analysis.

      Though machine learning algorithms perform well under normal conditions, they can fail with
055   cleverly crafted inputs called adversarial samples, raising security concerns in many applications.
056   White-box attacks are attacks wherein the attacker can access model predictions, parameters and
057   training data. Popular attacks in this setting are BIM(Kurakin et al. (2016)), MIM(Dong et al. (2018)),
058   FGSM(Goodfellow et al. (2015)) and PGD(Madry et al. (2017)) among which PGD attacks are
059   considered one of the strongest white-box attacks in practice. Prior works have proposed various
060   defence techniques against adversarial attacks, among which the seminal work of Madry et al. (2017)
061   stands out as one of the principled methods. They view defending adversarial attacks as solving
062   a min-max optimization problem wherein the inner maximization aims to get the best possible
063
      adversarial samples at a given model state. They solve the inner maximization by using the PGD
      attack and call it adversarial training (Algorithm 1) (abbreviated as PGD-AT henceforth). This arms
064
      race between adversarial attacks and defenses has also lead to many works which instead analyse the
065
      adversarial attacks in several ways like distribution shift analysis3, Fourier spectrum analysis (8, 11,
066   13, 12, 10), principal component (analysis3), shapely value analysis (1) and so on.
067

      adversarial training by analysing the model’s internals, which was previously challenging in traditional

088
             • We merge layers in the feature network of DLGN architectures to obtain a single effective
               linear transformation per layer. This reveals novel insights into hyperplanes and their
089
               resemblance to principal components in PGD-AT and STD-TR models. Our analyses show
090
               that hyperplanes in PGD-AT (FC) models are farther from data points compared to STD-TR

242   2.2             H YPERPLANE ANALYSIS IN SYNTHETIC XOR DATASET
243
244   We constructed a 2D XOR dataset (see Appendix A.2) with a gap λ from the axes, ensuring that
245   points satisfy |x| > λ and |y| > λ. This design allows setting ϵ ≤ λ during adversarial training,
246
      where ϵ represents the perturbation boundary without changing the ground truth labels. Using a
      DLGN with 3 fully connected layers (width 4), we trained models via both standard (STD-TR) and
247
      adversarial training (PGD-AT, ϵ = 0.3, T = 40). The decision boundaries of PGD-AT models are
248
      closer to optimal compared to STD-TR (see Appendix A.2), ensuring that adversarial examples
249   within L∞ bounds (ϵ = 0.3) are correctly classified only by PGD-AT. Visualization of hyperplanes
250   at each layer of the feature network (see Figure 6) shows that PGD-AT models learn hyperplanes
251   positioned farther from the data points than STD-TR models. This trend increases in deeper layers as
252   compared to earlier ones. So, we conclude that hyperplanes with larger projection distances from
253   data points are key in enhancing robustness.

      3       PCA ANALYSIS IN ROBUST AND STANDARD MODELS
256
257
      Principal Component Analysis (PCA) minimizes point-to-hyperplane distances, while we saw that
258   the PGD-AT process increases these distances to improve robustness. This fundamental difference
259   motivates us to investigate the impact of PCA on adversarially training. We embedded PCA projection
260   operation into the input layer of a DLGN architecture, ensuring both training and inference accounted
261   for the transformation. This also ensures that adversary has knowledge of the operation and doesn’t
262   change dimensions of the model input. To offset the reduced capacity from PCA’s dimensionality
263   reduction, we increased the model’s width at all layers to keep the capacity constant across all models
264   under comparision. Experiments on MNIST and Fashion MNIST (see Figure 7) reveal a significant
265   drop in both PGD-40 and clean accuracy in PGD-AT models compared to STD-TR models, indicating
266
      that PCA negatively affects adversarial robustness. This suggests that PCA’s dimensionality reduction
      conflicts with the robustness objectives of adversarial training.

      6    C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
517
      In this work we utilized DLGN architectures to thoroughly study the difference in properties exhibited
518
      by PGD-AT and STD-TR models. We analyzed fully connected networks, focusing on properties
519
      such as hyperplane alignment, path-activity and found that PGD-AT models exhibit larger datapoint
520   separation distances from hyperplanes, active pathways triggered during adversarial attacks in PGD-
521   AT models show less overlap with original examples of other classes and less overlap among original
522   samples of same class suggesting better capacity utilization. We examined convolutional networks to
523   show that PGD-AT models reduce gating overlap among different classes during adversarial attacks.
524   Additionally, we used visualization techniques to understand the dominant gating patterns triggered
525   per class in various scenarios for both STD-TR and PGD-AT models shedding light on the nature of
526   representations used by these models.
527   We believe that leveraging the results of our analysis to develop novel algorithms that account for the
528   properties examined could effectively enhance robustness. Extending this analysis to larger and more
529   complex models, such as transformers or other deep architectures, could provide further insights into
530   the generalizability of our findings. While this work focused on PGD-AT, other adversarial training
531   methods could be explored to generalize the understanding of robustness.
