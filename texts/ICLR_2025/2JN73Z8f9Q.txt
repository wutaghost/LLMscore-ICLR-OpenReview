009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            With the advancement of AIGC (AI-generated content) technologies, an increasing number
012            of generative models are revolutionizing fields such as video editing, music generation,
013            and even film production. However, due to the limitations of current AIGC models, most
014            models can only serve as individual components within specific application scenarios and
015            are not capable of completing tasks end-to-end in real-world applications. In real-world
016            applications, editing experts often work with a wide variety of images and video inputs,
017            producing multimodal outputs—a video typically includes audio, text, and other elements.
018            This level of integration across multiple modalities is something current models are unable
019
               to achieve effectively. However, the rise of agent-based systems has made it possible
               to use AI tools to tackle complex content generation tasks. To deal with the complex
020
               scenarios, in this paper, we propose a multimedia content generation agent system designed
021
               to automate complex content creation. Our agent system includes a data generation pipeline,
022            a tool library for content creation, and a set of metrics for evaluating preference alignment.
023            Notably, we introduce the skill acquisition theory to model the training data curation and
024            agent training. We designed a two-stage correlation strategy for plan optimization, including
025            self-correlation and model preference correlation. Additionally, we utilized the generated
026            plans to train the MultiMedia-Agent via a three stage approach including base/success plan
027            finetune and preference optimization. The comparison results demonstrate that the our
028            approaches are effective and the MultiMedia-Agent can generate better multimedia content
029            compared to GPT4o.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      AI-generated content, such as images, videos, audio, etc., has gradually been applied to various aspects
034
      of everyday life (Liu et al., 2024a; Esser et al., 2024). However, the needs of the real world are complex
035
      and diverse. Taking the field of video generation as an example, a user’s input may not only be text or a
036   single image but could also include materials like images, music, etc, and the model needs to integrate these
037   materials to generate an appropriate video. Moreover, on the output side of the model, the video that the
038   user requires may not only consist of video frames but also include suitable background music, voiceovers,
039   subtitles, and so on. Clearly, a single generative model at present cannot accomplish this task. One possible
040   solution to handle such complex situations is to use an agent system to understand user needs while integrating
041   different downstream tools to process complex inputs and outputs (Wang et al., 2024c;a). Some existing
042   agent systems (Shen et al., 2024) can call upon multiple tools to handle content generation, but they are not
043   specifically designed for content generation, nor do they take into account the diverse needs of real life.
044   Therefore, in this paper, we will explore whether multimodal agent can learn such complex workflows
045   of multi-media content creation in a manner similar to humans. Specifically, we will investigate whether
046   the multimodal agent can progressively acquire complex skills from scratch, following the stages of skill

047
      acquisition theory (DeKeyser, 2020), which mirrors the way of how humans learn skills step by step.
048
      Precisely, skill acquisition theory consists of three stages:
049
050
            1. Cognitive Stage: In this stage, beginners need to learn the fundamental operations and knowledge,
051            attempting to understand the basic concepts related to the new skill.
052
053         2. Associative Stage: At this stage, learners begin to engage in conscious, targeted practice, refining
054            their skills through repeated operations.
055
056
            3. Autonomous Stage: To reach this stage, learners require continuous feedback and improvement,
               including both self-correction and external supervision.
057
058
      We first developed a multi-media content playground based on real-world scenarios, incorporating a feedback
059
      mechanism. This playground includes 18 common multimodal generation scenarios tailored to real-world
060   needs and features a content creation tool library that supports the editing, generation and retrieval of images,
061   videos, audio, speech, and text. Then, to provide data for training the multimodal agent, we constructed
062   different levels of plans from the perspective of the three stages of skill acquisition theory. First, we used
063   GPTo as a teacher to generate a base plan for each question within each task. Of course, this base plan may
064   not always execute successfully, so further optimization is needed. In the second step, we had GPT4o reflect
065   on and perform self-correction on the base plan generated in the first stage, thereby improving the plan’s
066   quality. In the final step, we introduced external preference models to evaluate the plan from the second step,
067   and then allowed GPT4o to optimize it further. In this way, we obtained three different levels of plans to train
068
      the multimodal agent.
069   During the training of the multimodal agent, we followed the three stages of Skill Acquisition Theory. First,
070   in the Model Cognitive Stage, we fine-tuned the agent using all the generated plans. This allowed the agent to
071   quickly grasp the purposes of the tools, their output formats, and basic operational principles—similar to
072   how a human beginner learns foundational knowledge and basic concepts from a large amount of data. In
073   the Model Associative Stage, we fine-tuned the agent using only successfully executed plans. At this stage,
074
      the agent learns more advanced logic, such as the composition of workflows and the relationships between
      tools, building upon its foundational understanding of tool usage. Finally, in the Model Autonomous Stage,
075
      we performed post-training using paired preference data constructed based on the model’s preferences. This
076
      stage enables the multimodal agent not only to complete tasks but also to perceive and apply emotional or
077   aesthetic needs, such as human preferences, to tool execution.
078
079   It is worth noting that the MultiMedia-Agent differs significantly from previous models and methods. We
080
      present the differences between MultiMedia-Agent, other tool-agents, and content creation agent systems in
      the Table 1. Most of the methods listed in the table support multimodal content generation. For multimodal
081
      content understanding, HuggingGPT (Shen et al., 2024) can indirectly handle multimodal understanding
082
      by invoking APIs, NExT-GPT (Wu et al., 2023c) and MLLM-Tool can directly understand multimodal
083   data. AutoDirector (Ni et al., 2024) can only process text input and cannot generate content based on
084   user-provided materials. While our model is capable of accepting multi-modal and multiple inputs. In terms
085   of planning ability, ToolLLM (Qin et al., 2023) and HuggingGPT can plan the use of multiple tools based
086   on user instructions, whereas NExT-GPT and ModaVerse (Wang et al., 2024c) can only output a single
087   piece of content in one forward pass. Multimodal Interaction refers to whether the model can generate and
088   integrate multiple types of modalities based on user needs. Currently, only works like AutoDirector have
089   such capabilities but only limited to video scenario. Our MultiMedia-Agent can handle the plan generation
090   for multiple scenarios based on the user’s query. As for content generation scenarios, none of the existing
091
      models perform preference alignment on the generated content, whereas MultiMedia-Agent incorporates
      human-preference-based evaluation models to handle this aspect.

116   2.1   T OOL AGENT
117
118   With the rise of LLM agents (Mei et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; 2024b; Zhao et al., 2024), enabling agents
119   to call external APIs to solve user problems has become a crucial research topic. Toolformer (Schick
120   et al., 2024) pioneered the exploration of connecting large language models (LLMs) with external tools.
121   HuggingGPT (Shen et al., 2024) leveraged an agent to call HuggingFace’s API, allowing it to solve a wide
122   range of complex problems. Subsequent research has extended this integration to fields like healthcare
123
      support (Ma et al., 2023b), code synthesis (Wang et al., 2024b), and web searching (Ma et al., 2023a).
      ToolLLM (Qin et al., 2023) focused on executing complex tasks in real-world scenarios. GPT4Tools (Yang
124
      et al., 2024) and Visual ChatGPT (Wu et al., 2023a) integrated visual foundation models after decomposing
125
      tasks into manageable components. For multimodal tool agents, MLLM-Tool (Wang et al., 2024a) employed
126   multimodal large models as agents to call Hugging Face APIs. Similarly, ModaVerse (Wang et al., 2024c)
127   used multimodal large models for any-to-any generation. In our MultiMedia-Agent, we focus primarily on
128   the planning and alignment capabilities of multimodal agents, aiming to enhance content generation quality.
129
130
      2.2   A NY- TO - ANY GENERATION
131
132   The earliest any-to-any model was CoDi (Tang et al., 2024), followed by NextGPT and EMU (Sun et al., 2023),
133   which introduced further improvements in data and model design. EMU2 (Sun et al., 2024) introduced a
134   unified autoregressive objective to predict the next multimodal element, either by regressing visual embeddings
135   or classifying textual tokens. CM3Leon (Yu et al., 2023) and Chameleon (Team, 2024) used mixed image
136   and text data to train token-based autoregressive models. More recently, TransFusion (Zhou et al., 2024) and
137   Show-o (Xie et al., 2024) combined large language models with diffusion models to enhance performance.
138   However, any-to-any models are typically limited to generating a single modality without considering the
139   relationships and connections between modalities. This is precisely the area that our MultiMedia-Agent
140   focuses on, emphasizing the interplay between different modalities for richer content generation.

158
159   Due to the lack of datasets that can model real-world multimodal demand-solution scenarios, as shown in
160
      Table 2, we first constructed scenarios based on various real-world needs. For example, A user might want
      to automatically convert a series of photos into a video slideshow, possibly for a wedding or event photo
161
      montage. This can be summarized as a multi-images-to-video task. Another scenario could be: A person has
162

163   creating a travel memory video. This can be categorized as an image-audio-to-video task. In this case, we
164   designed 18 types of tasks, involving modalities such as image, video, audio, speech, and text. Next, we
165   constructed user queries for each task and collected corresponding multimedia data. Specifically, we first
166   gathered publicly available multimedia data from the web, then used GPT-4o to generate user queries under
167   different circumstances by combining the multimedia data with task type information. As a result, we built a
168   diverse dataset of multimedia tasks, with corresponding user queries and multimedia data for each task.

      3.3   H IERARCHICAL PLAN CURATION
220
221
      Once we have constructed the user queries, corresponding multimedia data, and the tool library, we utilized
222
      GPT-4o to generate the plans. The plans are organized into a list of dictionaries, where each dictionary
223
      includes the information of the tool.
224
225   To enable the multimodal agent to better learn complex skills, we need to incorporate feedback into the
226   model’s training data. Unlike conventional tool agents, content-generation-oriented tool agents must not only
227
      consider the execution success rate of the plan but also ensure that the generated multimedia content meets
      human needs and aesthetic standards. In other words, the agent we are training must not only possess the
228
      ability to complete complex tasks using tools but also be responsible for the outcomes produced by these tools,
229
      ensuring that the results align with human needs and preferences. To address this, we designed a two-stage
230   correlation approach for tool plan curation. After generating the base plan, we first employ GPT4o to perform
231   self-correction, identifying issues within the plan and optimizing it to obtain a self-corrected plan. Next, we
232   execute the self-corrected plan to produce the multimedia result. We then employ a series of model-based
233   preference evaluation metrics to assess the quality of the multimedia result. Using these metrics, the LMM
234   further refines the plan to optimize it, ultimately yielding the final plan.

      3.3.1    T WO - STAGE CORRELATION OF PLAN CURATION
236
237   Stage 1: Self-correlation
238
      After inputting the user query, materials, and tool information into GPT-4o to generate the base plan, we
239
      further prompt GPT-4o to evaluate the quality of the current plan based on all available information. Our
240   evaluation criteria focus on two main aspects: Plan execution success rate: We prompt GPT-4o to assess
241   whether the plan can be successfully executed, and if not, make necessary modifications. Inclusion of
242   user-requirement-aligned, common-sense optimizations: We assess whether the plan includes additional
243   optimization tools that meet the user’s needs and adhere to common sense, such as adding background music
244   to a video or incorporating voiceovers in audio generation.
245
      This ensures the plan is both executable and aligned with user expectations.
246
247   Stage 2: Model Preference Correlation
248   To further evaluate the generated plans, in Stage 2 we assess the results of content generation plans using
249   model-based preference feedback metrics for the four output modalities: image, video, audio, and text. Our
250   evaluation focuses on three primary aspects: whether the generated multimedia content meets human needs,
251   conveys emotional expression, and aligns across modalities.
252
253           • Text output metrics. We use GPT-4o to evaluate the alignment between the input and output
254             content.
255           • Image output metrics. GPT-4o assesses whether the images meet human needs and convey
256             emotions, while Pick Score Kirstain et al. (2023) is used to evaluate aesthetics.
257
              • Audio output metrics. We apply speech-to-text and audio-to-text models to convert the audio into
258             text, and GPT-4o evaluates the fulfillment of human needs and emotional expression based on user
259             requirements and input content.
260
              • Video output metrics. Similar to image outputs, GPT-4o evaluates whether the video meets human
261
                needs and conveys emotions, while Dover Score (Wu et al., 2023b) is used for aesthetic and quality
262
                evaluation. If the video includes embedded audio, we apply the same evaluation methods used for
263             audio outputs. Additionally, we introduce a audio-video alignment metric, where GPT-4 scores the
264             alignment between the transcribed audio text and the video content.
265
266   By integrating these metrics, we provide a comprehensive evaluation of any type of plan execution output,
267   reflecting the overall quality of the plan. We use the optimized plans from Stage 1 to generate multimedia
268   content and then apply the above metrics for evaluation. The evaluation results are fed back to GPT-4o, which,
269   based on the feedback and previous information, generates a new plan. We show a generated plan in the
270   Appendix A.2.

313   4.1   AGENT S KILL ACQUISITION
314
      We further used our data to train an multimodal agent. To better encode the tool information and the
315
      behaviors from the plan into the multimodal model, we designed a three-stage training approach based on
316
      skill acquisition theory.
317
318         1. Model Cognitive Stage. At this stage, the agent primarily focuses on learning the basic usage of
319            tools and understanding the input-output JSON formats. We trained the model using all available
320            data.
321         2. Model Associative Stage.At this stage, we trained the model using only successful plans, the agent
322            begins to learn established action trajectories from successfully executed plans to ensure smooth
323            execution and accurate output of future plans.
324         3. Model Autonomous Stage. At this stage, the agent not only needs to develop the ability to synthesize
325            complex plans but also must ensure that the generated content aligns with human aesthetics and
326            preferences. So, we categorized the plans into winning and losing plans based on the metric model’s
327            scores. Then, we applied DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) to align the model with these
328            preferences.

356   4.3.1    S KILL ACQUISITION THEORY CAN BENEFIT TOOL AGENT TRAINING
357
      We first present a comparison between our MultiMedia-Agent and GPT-4o in terms of success rate. As
358
      observed, the agent trained through the Model Associative Stage shows a significant improvement in success
359   rate. However, upon completion of the Model Autonomous Stage, we noticed a decline in success rate. This
360   may be due to the tendency of the model to generate longer plans after the Model Autonomous Stage, and
361   given the model’s limited capacity, errors are more likely to occur when generating extended text. This issue
362   is also evident from the comparisons shown in Table 3 and Table 4. This highlights that ensuring the agent
363   outputs a stable plan format is a key challenge for tool agents when dealing with complex scenarios.
364
      We further analyzed the model preference feedback results for content generated by MultiMedia-Agent at
365   different stages compared to GPT-4o. All reported metric results are the average model feedback scores for
366   successfully executed plans.
367
368

369
      training stages, respectively. As shown in the table, after Stage 1 (Model Cognitive Stage), the MultiMedia-
370
      Agent produces fairly average results. Following the second stage of training (Model Associative Stage), the

372   alignment. Moreover, after Stage 3 (Model Autonomous Stage), MultiMedia-Agent showed significant

464
