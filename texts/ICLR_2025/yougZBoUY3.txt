009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we investigate the susceptibility of Audio Language Models (ALMs)
012            to audio-based jailbreaks and introduce Best-of-N (BoN) Jailbreaking, a black-
013            box jailbreaking algorithm to extract harmful information from ALMs. To craft
014            jailbreak inputs, our approach samples audio augmentations and applies them to
015            malicious prompts. We repeat this process until we find a set of augmentations
016
               that elicits a harmful response from the target ALM. Empirically, we find that
               applying BoN with 7000 sampled augmentations achieves an attack success rate
017
               (ASR) of over 60% on all models tested, including the researcher preview of GPT-
018
               4o. Furthermore, we uncover power laws that accurately predict the ASR of BoN
019            jailbreaking as a function of the number of samples. These power laws allow us
020            to forecast the effectiveness of BoN jailbreaking as a function of the number of
021            sampled augmentations over an order of magnitude. Finally, we show that BoN
022            jailbreaking can be composed with other black-box attack algorithms for even more
023            effective attacks—combining BoN with an optimized prefix attack achieves 98%
024            ASR on Gemini Pro and Flash. Overall, by exploiting stochastic sampling and
025            sensitivity to variations in a high-dimensional input space, we propose a scalable,
026            composable, and highly effective black-box algorithm for attacking SOTA ALMs.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As AI models continue to improve, ensuring their robustness against misuse is increasingly critical.
031   The integration of audio inputs into models like Gemini (Gemini Team, 2024) and GPT-4o (OpenAI,
032   2023a) introduces an exciting new modality, while also simultaneously expanding the potential attack
033   surface for misuse. Unfortunately, the robustness of audio language models (ALMs)—defined as
034   their ability to consistently reject harmful requests—remains relatively underexplored (Li et al.,
035   2022). Without robust safeguards, these more capable systems could be exploited for cybercrime, or
036   disseminating misinformation (Phuong et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023b; Anthropic, 2023).
037   In this work, we investigate the robustness of state-of-the-art (SOTA) audio language models (ALMs)
038   to jailbreaks, which are attacks that bypass a model’s safeguards to elicit compliance with harmful
039   requests. These jailbreaks enable the models to produce harmful information, such as detailed
040   instructions on how to build a bomb, in violation of their ethical guidelines. We identify vulnerabilities
041   in recently released ALMs and introduce a universal and composable black-box jailbreak algorithm
042   that reliably extracts such information.
043   Specifically, we develop Best-of-N (BoN) Jailbreaking, a general method for jailbreaking ALMs
044   through repeated sampling of augmented spoken audio requests. While ALMs are generally
045   robust to simple voice variations like changes in emotion or accent, we find that repeatedly sampling
046   with combinations of randomly chosen augmentations, such as speed, pitch adjustments, and back-
047   ground noise adjustments (Fig. 1, top), can induce ALMs to produce unsafe outputs. The use of audio
048   augmentations substantially increases the entropy of an ALM’s responses relative to non-augmented
049   resampling in both text and audio, which thus allows BoN jailbreaking to succeed with fewer attempts
050
      than these baselines.
051   We demonstrate that BoN jailbreaking is an effective attack strategy on current ALMs—applying
052   BoN with 7,200 augmented samples increases the attack success rate (ASR) from 2% to 77% on
053   Gemini 1.5 Flash on harmful queries from HarmBench (Fig. 1, bottom left; Mazeika et al., 2024).
      Furthermore, we uncover power laws that predict the ASR of BoN jailbreaking as a function of

175   2.2   B EST- OF -N (B O N) JAILBREAKING
176
177   We now introduce Best-of-N jailbreaking (BoN; Fig. 1), an effective jailbreaking algorithm that
178   combines two key ideas. First, since ALMs are robust to single input transformations, we compose
179   multiple augmentations. Second, as the high-dimensional audio input space likely results in varying
180   model behavior differently, we sample many composed augmentation groups to find a particular
181   region of the input space that elicits a harmful response.
182
183   Threat Model Our primary concern is the potential misuse of ALMs by adversaries. Therefore,
184   our focus is on effectiveness rather than stealthiness; this is because an adversary’s primary goal
185   is to elicit harmful outputs, regardless of whether the manipulations are detectable. In real-world
186
      deployments, adversarial attempts are not necessarily covert.
187
188   Algorithm Details The algorithm is general and can be applied to other modalities by using
189   modality-specific augmentations. Since we focus on ALMs, we sample from six different augmenta-
190   tions: [speed, pitch, speech, noise, volume, music] that are each parameterized by a scalar
191
      value that can be concatenated to form a six-dimensional vector. During BoN sampling, we sample
      each vector from N (0, σ 2 I). We considered several values for σ, and found σ = 0.25 worked well
192
      (see Appendix D.2). We apply the sampled chain of augmentations to a vocalized direct request,
193
      sample an ALM output given this input, and assess the output harmfulness using another language
194   model. We repeatedly sample augmentations until the ALM produces an output that is deemed to be
195   harmful given the input. See Appendix B.4 for further details.

301   Discussion Overall, our results show that BoN jailbreaking with audio augmentations is an effective
302   approach for eliciting harmful information from ALMs. However, the high number of steps required
303
      to achieve 100% ASR is a significant limitation to this attack. For example, an API provider

304
      access to the adversary. Moreover, we find the auto-grader has a false positive rate of ∼ 15% for
305
      classifying jailbreaks (see Appendix H). As auto-graders improve through better base models or
306   prompt engineering, we can slot them into BoN allowing more accurate termination when the model
307   is jailbroken for each request.

360   3.2                 A RE THE AUGMENTATIONS SEMANTICALLY MEANINGFUL ?
361
362   We see the augmentations found by BoN jailbreaking have limited transferability across requests. This
363   suggests that each augmentation may be specific to the harmful request or potentially to a particular
364   domain of the harmful request. We now analyze this hypothesis.
365
366   Experiment Details To analyze the hypothesis that the BoN jailbreaks may be semantically mean-
367
      ingful and specific to each specific harmful request, we perform two analyses. First, we assess
      whether there is a meaningful relationship between augmentation vectors and the content of the
368
      original audio request. For instance, if slowing down audio requests consistently led to jailbreaks for
369
      cyber-attack related queries, this would indicate high semantic coherence. Further, we measure the
370   reliability of each attack when resampling ALM responses using the same audio files that initially lead
371   to harmful responses from the target ALM. To do so, we measure the percentage of model responses
372   under resampling that also lead to harmful responses (the jailbreak reliability). We resample with
373   temperature 1.
374
375   Results We are unable to find a significant relationship between the augmentation and the semantic
376   topic of the text request (Appendix E.4). Moreover, surprisingly, we find low reliability across
377   prompts (Fig. 6); the median reliability is approximately 2%. Further, on average, resampling the
      ALM using the same exact audio file as the one that originally broke the model only leads to harmful

      responses in 15% of cases. While these results do not rule out the augmentations being semantically
379   meaningful, they show that the attacks found by BoN jailbreaking do not consistently yield harmful
380   outputs under resampling. For many prompts, the most likely ALM response for a given attack is not
381   harmful, suggesting that BoN jailbreaking is exploiting the stochastic nature of ALM sampling.

383   3.3   A RE ALM S SENSITIVE TO SMALL CHANGES IN THEIR AUDIO INPUTS ?
384
385   Because applying augmentations appears to drastically improve the effectiveness of BoN jailbreaking,
386   we hypothesize that ALMs are sensitive to small variations in the continuous, high-dimensional input
387   space. We now investigate this hypothesis.
388
389   Experiment Details To understand the sensitivity of ALMs to changes in the audio input, we
390
      measure the brittleness of the attacks. This is the change in jailbreak reliability after making a
      semantically small change to the audio file. For example, small changes are adding “please” and
391
      “thanks” at the beginning and end of a request, decreasing pitch by 100 cents, and increasing speed
392
      by 10%. We run these experiments on Gemini Flash, and further experiments are detailed in
393   Appendix E.2.
394
395   Results Here, we find that the attack augmentations found are extremely brittle. Notably, speeding
396   up the audio by 10% before applying the same augmentation decreases the jailbreak reliability by a
397   factor of 10. These results suggest that ALMs are highly sensitive to relatively small variations in the
398   high-dimensional audio input space.

      4     E NHANCING BoN JAILBREAKING WITH ATTACK C OMPOSITION
401
402
      BoN jailbreaking with augmentations effectively elicits harmful information from LLMs across input
403   modalities, but often requires many samples to succeed. To reduce this sample burden, we investigate
404   combining BoN with other jailbreak techniques, and find it improves effectiveness significantly.
405
406   Experiment Details We focus on prefix jailbreaks designed to remove alignment safeguards when
407   combined with a harmful request. They are optimized for universality so that the same prefix can
408   jailbreak many requests. In our study, we introduce our own method called Prefix PAIR (PrePAIR)4 ,
409   which extends the PAIR algorithm (Chao et al., 2023) by editing a prefix rather than the entire request
410   and optimizing the prefix to jailbreak many different requests.
411   Specifically, PrePAIR iteratively generates text prefixes using GPT-4o as an attacking LLM, which
412   is vocalized using text-to-speech (ElevenLabs, 2023) and joined to a batch of four spoken harmful
413   requests. These requests, appended with the proposed audio prefix, are processed by the target ALM.
414   The same GPT-4o classifier used by BoN assesses the success rate of these modified requests. If
415   at least three of the four requests are jailbroken, the process terminates, and the prefix is stored;
416   otherwise, the attacker LLM refines the prefix, utilizing the previous attempts within its context
417   window for up to 30 iterations. See Appendix B.6 for implementation details and Appendix F for
418   further PrePAIR experiments.
419   We generate a dataset of 164 effective vocalized prefixes using PrePAIR on Gemini Flash. We then
420   identify the best prefix, p∗ , that achieves the highest ASR when prepended to direct requests. We
421   then apply BoN jailbreaking with the same six audio augmentations on the HarmBench direct requests
422   with p∗ appended. We measure the ASR as a function of N , the number of sampled augmentations,
423   on Gemini Flash and Pro.
424

426   of the number of sampled augmentations as compared to standard BoN (Fig. 7, left). On Gemini
427
      Pro, BoN with composition reaches 50% ASR in only 8 samples and 90% ASR in 86 samples—
      a significant gain in sample efficiency compared to standard BoN which needs 1726 samples for

      50% ASR and does not attain 90% with 7200 samples. Compared to standard BoN, composition

      reduces the number of samples required to achieve 50% ASR by a factor of 2.8 on Gemini Flash and

477   with BoN’s audio augmentation focus. Our method also differs from manual red-teaming and genetic

485   have bypassed security by using images with typographic harmful text (Gong et al., 2023; Shayegani
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a), akin to our vocalized audio requests strategy.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this study, we introduce BoN jailbreaking, a novel algorithm for bypassing safeguards of state-of-
496   the-art Audio Language Models (ALMs) through repeated sampling with audio augmentations. We
497   demonstrate that BoN jailbreaking achieves high ASR on several ALMs, including Gemini 1.5 and
498   GPT-4o’s advanced voice mode. Moreover, we uncover power-law scaling behavior that predicts the
499   ASR of BoN jailbreaking as a function of the number of samples. We also show that BoN jailbreaking
500   can be effectively composed with other techniques like PrePAIR to create even more potent attacks.
501   Our work demonstrates the inherent challenges of safeguarding models with stochastic outputs
502   and continuous input spaces, particularly because attackers can exploit sensitivity to variations
503   to the continuous input spaces to improve jailbreaking performance. Indeed, by exploiting these
504
      vulnerabilities, we develop a highly effective, scalable, and composable black-box algorithm for
      jailbreaking current SOTA ALMs.
505
506
      Future Work Promising future directions: other techniques for increased model input diversity to
507
      potentially boost ASR; extending the BoN approach to other continuous modalities like images and
508
      video, leveraging white or grey-box optimization signals (see preliminary results in Appendix D.3);
509   leveraging more advanced black-box optimization algorithms such as CMA-ES (Hansen & Oster-
510   meier, 2001) to enhance the efficiency of discovering perturbations; investigating adaptive attacks
511   that craft perturbations based on query semantics; and exploring universal perturbations effective
512   across queries, models, and modalities. Assessing and improving the effectiveness of adversarial
513   training when inputs only rarely lead to failures is another open research question.

      A    ALM A RCHITECTURE D ETAILS
811
812
      This section is to provide a primer on ALM architecture for unfamilar readers.
813
814   Audio capabilities within LLMs facilitate a range of tasks such as speech-to-text (SST) and audio
815   captioning, through integration with audio encoders. These encoders, trained as part of systems
816
      like Whisper (Radford et al., 2023), transform input audio features such as 80-channel log Mel
      spectrograms at 100Hz. Open source models like SALMONN, Qwen-Audio, LLaSM, and DiVA
817
      (Tang et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Shu et al., 2023; Held et al., 2024) employ representations from
818
      the Whisper encoder, with SALMONN and DiVA utilizing a Q-former (Li et al., 2023) to improve
819   representations with joint audio-language learning. An adapter, typically a linear layer, projects these
820   representations into the LLM’s token embedding space, with the LLM weights optimized using LoRA
821   (Hu et al., 2021) to enhance audio task performance. DiVA also refines instruction-following from
822   audio inputs by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the responses generated from
823   audio and corresponding text inputs.
824
      There is upcoming support anticipated in Llama3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) soon that uses similar

975    B.4.2    ALM S AMPLING AND C LASSIFIATION
976
977    The efficacy of BoN is linked to the temperature used to sample from the ALM. We find temperature=1
978    works well and show some ablations in Appendix D.2. We only sample the ALM once for each aug-
979    mentation as we find it is more computationally efficient to exploit diversity via more augmentations
980
       rather than more samples with the same augmentation.
981    We use ALM max tokens equal to 200, which we found is enough to reliably make a judgment on
982    whether the output is harmful or not. If the ALM completion word count is less than the number
983    of words in the request plus 8, we assert that the output is not jailbroken even if the HarmBench
984    classifier says so. We found that often, the ALM output will provide a transcript of the input request
985    in its response, and often, the HarmBench classifier will grade this as harmful. We chose 8 additional
986
       words above the number of requests empirically as it minimized the false negatives by using this
       criterion.

995    B.5     P OWER LAW FITTING
996
997
       In our analysis, we model the decay of the negative log Attack Success Rate (ASR) as a func-
       tion of increasing model size (N) using a power law relationship. Specifically, we fit the model
998
       − log(ASR) = aN −b , where: a is a scaling factor and b is the decay exponent. Each parameter is
999
       given a constraint to be greater than or equal to 0. This means the prediction will always be between
1000   0 and 1, which is the appropriate range for ASR.
1001
1002   The values of ASR we use for fitting are the mean ASR we get after bootstrapping.
1003   To stabilize the fitting process and handle potential outliers, we employ the Huber loss function as
1004   described in Huber (1992). The fitting process leverages a robust optimization technique, specifically
1005   the L-BFGS-B algorithm Zhu et al. (1997), to minimize the Huber loss between the predicted and
1006   observed values of − log(ASR). This method follows the robust fitting methodology outlined in
1007   Hoffmann et al. (2022) involving scaling laws for neural networks.
1008   In our analysis, we evaluated various mathematical models to describe the relationship between model
1009   size and ASR. We found that the single term model with an added constant, − log(ASR) = aN −b +c,
1010   provided the best fit to our data. However, we observed that including the variable constant term c
1011   led to extrapolation errors. By setting c = 0, we were able to resolve these issues. As illustrated
1012   in Figures 8 and 9, both models - with and without the constant term - demonstrate good global fit.
1013   Nevertheless, the model incorporating the fitted constant fails to extrapolate reliably.

1333   C.4                              C AN F RONTIER ALM S UNDERSTAND SOUND VARIATIONS ?
1334
1335   In comparing the performance of GPT-4o and Gemini on various audio augmentation identification
1336   tasks, notable differences emerge. Both models show strength in speed detection, with accurate
1337   identification of changes, and background speech, with correct detection. Both, however, struggle
1338   significantly with differentiating between volume levels and reverberation effects, often misidentifying
1339   these clips as identical. Both models demonstrate moderate success in recognizing codec-induced
1340
       quality and pitch differences, each scoring decently with a 50% correct identification rate.
1341   Background noise and music pose a challenge for GPT-4o, which notices differences but incor-
1342   rectly identifies them, possibly due to voice activity detection (VAD) affecting its ability to process
1343   non-speech audio cues. In contrast, Gemini shows better consistency in recognizing non-speech
1344   background sounds across all signal-to-noise ratios, correctly asserting the presence of background
1345   music in each case tested.
1346   Furthermore, both models are poor at classifying real noises (such as dogs barking, licking, and
1347   buzzing), classifying emotions and speaker characteristics. However, they are better at classifying
1348   noises made by humans. It is interesting that GPT-4o struggles in these tasks, given it is very good at
1349   generating noises and accents. This shows there is an asymmetry in capabilities, favoring generation,
       perhaps due to OpenAI guarding itself against threat models such as bias towards certain voices.

1440   To assess whether BoN is effective beyond the audio domain, we conducted preliminary experiments
1441   in the image domain and found that it successfully jailbreaks VLMs as well. Unlike vocalizing
1442

       introduced a set of image-specific augmentations, which include changes to the font, text color,
1443
       text thickness, text location, text size, and background pixels. When interfacing with the VLM, the
1444
       augmented image is presented, followed by a user message stating, ”Follow the instructions in the
1445   image.” It is important to note that these augmentations do not correspond to a vector of continuous
1446   values as in our audio experiments.
1447
1448

1450
       mini vision models. Interestingly, specifying low-quality images in API requests to OpenAI results in
1451   better performance compared to high-quality images. For an illustration of a successful augmented
1452   image, refer to Figure 22-right. Additionally, Figure 22-left demonstrates that − log(ASR) adheres
1453   to a power law for VLMs.
1454
       These findings affirm that BoN operates effectively in the image domain, exhibiting behaviors akin to
1455
       those in the audio domain; however, it is crucial to emphasize that these are preliminary results, and
1456
       further research is planned.

       G.1    M ANUAL S TACKING
1893
1894
       BoN finds working sets of augmentations that jailbreak specific requests but, as found in Section 3,
1895   one limitation is that they have low universality, meaning transfer to other requests is poor.
1896
1897   Can we find a better method that improves upon universality? To answer this, we test a manual
1898
       augmentation stacking approach.
1899   Manual stacking — first sweep over each single augmentation type as in Appendix C and short-list
1900   the two best values. Next, generate all combinations of 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 augmentations across the best
1901   two values for each augmentation type.
1902   We use a data split to analyze universality, where we measure how well attacks tuned on the train set
1903   transfer to the test set. We use the train and test split defined in Appendix B.1, which includes PAIR
1904   & TAP data, to increase the chance that audio perturbations will lead to success while also increasing
1905   our dataset size.
1906
       We swept across all augmentations (Appendix B.3) using the methodology in Appendix C and plotted
1907
       the ASR distributions in Figure 10. We show that adding augmentations can sometimes increase the
1908   ASR above the baseline but only by a few percent absolute, showing that the universality does not
1909   change much.
1910
1911
       After running the stacking method, our findings reveal that it is possible to find a set of combined
       augmentations like pitch alteration, speed adjustment, and background noise overlay that enhance
1912
       ASR on a given subset of harmful requests. However, the set of augmentations found does not
1913
       generalize well to unseen prompts since stacking leads to an insignificant increase in ASR compared
1914   to the ”Audio Only” baseline. Effective augmentations are largely prompt-dependent, and stacking
1915   augmentations—though beneficial—does not increase universality significantly.
1916
1917
       G.2    G REEDY SEQUENTIAL STACKING
1918
1919
       In our search for universal augmentations and a more automated augmentation stacking method,
1920   we developed an algorithm before discovering BoN. This algorithm incrementally builds up the
1921   set of augmentations that are chained together and tries to maximize the ASR on a set of 60
1922   requests (20 direct, 20 PAIR, and 20 TAP). The initial step involves sampling k single augmentation
1923   candidates—selecting one of our eight augmentation types (see Appendix B.3) at random and then
1924   sampling a value for it. Each candidate is then applied to the audio request, and the ASR on a batch
1925   of audio requests is calculated. The candidate that yields the highest increase in ASR is selected to
1926   progress to the next round. In subsequent steps, new candidates are applied on top of the previously
1927   selected best candidate, with the option always available to apply no augmentation should the ASR
1928
       degrade.
1929   Our findings align with the outcomes of our manual stacking efforts, indicating that it is feasible to
1930   enhance the ASR on the training set we optimized on, as illustrated in Figure 31. However, when
1931   these augmentations are applied to a validation set, the ASR does not improve as the algorithm
1932   progresses, as depicted in Figure 32. This is unsurprising given the lack of universality in audio
1933   augmentations across various requests we show in S3.
1934   Ablations included in Figure 31 demonstrate that using k = 50 candidates is effective, provided that
1935   the ASR increases monotonically—applying only the best candidate augmentation if it improves the
1936   ASR compared to the previous step. Attempts to apply augmentations to a randomized proportion of
1937   the audio, rather than the entire file, were also made, revealing that this approach does not significantly
1938   boost ASR.
