009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across
012            a wide range of tasks. Typically, an LLM is first pre-trained on large corpora and
013            subsequently fine-tuned on task-specific datasets. However, during fine-tuning,
014            LLMs may forget some knowledge acquired in the pre-training stage, leading to a
015            decline in general capabilities. To address this challenge, we propose a new fine-
016            tuning algorithm termed Momentum-Filtered Optimizer (MoFO). As an extension
017            of greedy block coordinate descent (BCD) methods, MoFO iteratively selects and
018
               updates the model parameters with the largest momentum magnitudes. MoFO
               achieves similar fine-tuning performance to the default fine-tuning algorithm while
019
               effectively mitigating knowledge forgetting. Furthermore, MoFO does not require
020
               access to pre-training data, making it highly suitable for scenarios where the
021            pre-training data is unavailable, such as fine-tuning checkpoint-only open-source
022            LLMs. We validate MoFO through rigorous convergence analysis and extensive
023            experiments, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods in mitigating
024            forgetting.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The success of large language models (LLMs) lies in their strong capabilities in language under-
030   standing and generation. Typically, LLMs are initially pre-trained on extensive corpora to acquire
031   general capabilities, and subsequently, they are fine-tuned on smaller, task-specific datasets to adapt
032   to particular tasks or domains (Dai & Le, 2015; Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Radford et al., 2018).
033
      However, it has been observed that during the fine-tuning process, LLMs may forget the knowledge
      acquired in pre-training, leading to a decline in general capabilities (Lin et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
034
      2020; Dong et al., 2021; Korbak et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). Therefore, addressing the issue of
035
      forgetting during fine-tuning has become an important research direction for LLMs.
036
037   In the field of continual learning, mitigating forgetting has already been a central focus. Continual
038   learning (Wang et al., 2024a) involves training models sequentially on different tasks, which is
039
      analogous to the process of pre-training followed by fine-tuning in LLMs. Both involve different
      stages of training and face the challenge of forgetting previously acquired knowledge when learning
040
      new information. To address the issue, replay-based methods (Rolnick et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020;
041
      Ouyang et al., 2022) use a replay buffer to store and revisit past data, in order to reinforce prior
042   knowledge while learning new information. In LLM training, some replay-based methods are also
043   used to mitigate forgetting (Shi et al., 2024; Roziere et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). However, replay-
044   based methods face some practical limitations in LLMs. First, access to the original pre-training
045   data is often restricted or infeasible. Many open-source LLMs, such as the Llama series (Touvron
046   et al., 2023), do not fully disclose their pre-training datasets. Second, even when pre-training data is
047   available, incorporating it into the fine-tuning process can substantially increase computational and
048   memory costs, as the model must process a much larger and more diverse dataset.
049   In continual learning, another class of methods involves modifying the optimization process of models
050   to mitigate forgetting (Wang et al., 2024a). Most optimization-based methods do not require direct
051   access to past data but still depend on information from previous tasks. Gradient projection methods,
052   such as GEM (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017) and OGD (Farajtabar et al., 2020), rely on gradient
053   information from previous tasks, while landscape-based methods like Adam-NSCL (Wang et al.,
      2021) depend on checkpoints of prior models. However, in the context of LLMs, storing gradients


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      or checkpoints requires substantial memory due to the large model size, introducing a significant
055   overhead to the fine-tuning process.
056
057   In this work, we aim to develop a forgetting-mitigation optimization method that does not utilize
058
      past data. We adopt an important insight in continual learning: the closer to the previous model,
      the less forgetting occurs. What optimization method might move in small distance from the initial
059
      point? We notice that the classical block coordinate descent (BCD) method (Tseng, 2001) is a good
060
      candidate, since it updates only a subset of parameters at each iteration, thus is implicitly biased
061   towards closer solutions. Nevertheless, incorporating BCD into LLM fine-tuning presents some
062   challenges. This is primarily because Adam, the predominant optimizer for LLM training (Radford
063   et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2024c), differs substantially from SGD studied in earlier continual learning
064   works. It complicates both optimizer design and convergence analysis. Consequently, combining
065   BCD with Adam is not a straightforward task.
066
      To resolve the above challenges, we proposed Momentum-Filtered Optimizer (MoFO), a new opti-
067   mization algorithm that integrates Adam with BCD. To achieve less forgetting while maintaining good
068   fine-tuning performance, MoFO selects the most effective parameters at each iteration—those with
069   large momentum magnitudes for reducing the fine-tuning loss. MoFO only modifies the optimizer
070   without the need for pretraining data or introducing additional memory costs, which helps achieve its
071   efficiency and effectiveness during fine-tuning. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
072
             • We propose MoFO, a new training algorithm designed to mitigate the forgetting of pre-
073
               training knowledge during fine-tuning.
074
             • We present a rigorous theoretical convergence result of the MoFO algorithm, providing a
075            solid theoretical foundation that supports its good performance in fine-tuning tasks.
076          • We conduct experiments on various tasks, demonstrating that MoFO outperforms existing
077            methods both in fine-tuning performance and mitigating forgetting.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
081
      Catastrophic forgetting, a significant issue where models forget previously learned information upon
082
      learning new data, has received considerable attention in machine learning (McCloskey & Cohen,
083
      1989; Goodfellow et al., 2013; Kemker et al., 2018; Ramasesh et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024). We
084   identify 5 primary categories of methods, as listed below.
085
086   Replay-based methods. These methods leverage past experiences to facilitate the learning of new
087
      tasks. The most classical scheme is experience replay, which involves replaying data of past tasks
      during incremental training (Rolnick et al., 2019) (Aljundi et al., 2019a; Hayes et al., 2019; Cha et al.,
088
      2021; Chaudhry et al., 2019b; Riemer et al., 2019b). Other variants utilize gradient information from
089
      old tasks (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Riemer et al., 2019a; Chaudhry et al., 2019a; Farajtabar et al.,
090   2020; Aljundi et al., 2019b; Chaudhry et al., 2021; Tiwari et al., 2022). In LLMs, Yin et al. (2023);
091   Wang et al. (2024b); Ouyang et al. (2022) propose replay-based methods to mitigate forgetting.
092   MoFO is orthogonal to replay-based methods and can be combined with replay strategies.
093
      Regularization-based methods. These methods introduce constraints to the training process to
094
      preserve past knowledge, such as adding regularization to the loss functions (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017;
095
      Aljundi et al., 2018; Zenke et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Ritter et al., 2018; Kumar et al., 2023) or the
096   embedding/output changes (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Rannen et al., 2017; Buzzega et al., 2020; Huang
097   et al., 2021; Cha et al., 2020). However, Aljundi et al. (2018); Panda et al. (2024); Lesort et al.
098   (2019); Wu et al. (2022) point out some limitations of regularization-based approaches. They may
099   exhibit poor adaptability to new tasks in long sequential learning. Moreover, some regularization
100   methods typically require partial information of past models (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). In contrast,
101   MoFO does not require information from past models. We note that MoFO is also orthogonal to
102   regularization-based methods and their combination is an interesting future direction.
103   Model merging methods. These methods balance learning new knowledge and retaining old
104   knowledge by merging the new and past models. One line of research focuses on model averaging,
105   which interpolates between the weights of different LLMs (Wortsman et al., 2022a;b; Eeckt et al.,
106   2022; Yadav et al., 2024; Wortsman et al., 2022b; Lin et al., 2023; 2024). Another line of research
107   relies on the observation that task-specific knowledge largely resides in a subspace of the weight space
      (Ilharco et al., 2023; Panigrahi et al., 2023; Gueta et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024), and leverage task


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      vectors or task localization to preserve pre-training knowledge in the fine-tuned models (Panigrahi
109   et al., 2023; Yadav et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024a).
110
111   Architecture-based methods. These methods modify the model’s architecture in training. LoRA
112
      (Hu et al., 2022), as the most popular parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method, freezes the
      pre-training weights and introduces low-rank trainable matrices. Variants of LoRA are applied in
113
      continual learning for LLMs (Ren et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a). However, LoRA is observed
114
      to forget less but also learn less than default fine-tuning (Biderman et al., 2024). Other approaches
115   adaptively expand model capacity or isolate partial weights to mitigate interference between new
116   and old tasks (Wang et al., 2023a; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023). In contrast, MoFO selects a subset of
117   parameters to update at each iteration, but does not alter the total trainable parameters.
118
      Optimization-based methods. These methods only modify the training algorithm to mitigate
119
      forgetting. Besides gradient projection (Wang et al., 2023a; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017) and the
120
      landscape-inspired methods (Wang et al., 2021) mentioned in the introduction, another popular type
121   of optimization-based methods is dynamic sparse training, where training is restricted to partial
122   parameters at each iteration. For instance, Hui et al. (2024) selectively freezes half of the model’s
123   parameters at each iteration. Ke et al. (2023b;a) introduce a soft-masking mechanism to regulate
124   parameter updates based on their importance values. Further, Zhang et al. (2024a) combines selective
125   module updating with soft-masking. MoFO falls in the realm of dynamic sparse training. Compared
126   to existing optimization-based methods, MoFO relies on a simpler parameter selection mechanism
127   based on momentum values only, thus not introducing much extra computation or design.

131   3.1   M OTIVATION
132
      Correlation between Distance and Forgetting
133
134   During fine-tuning, different training methods typically converge to distinct minima. Although all
135
      these minima achieve relatively small fine-tuning losses, their distances from the pre-trained model
      can vary significantly. To see this, we fine-tune Pythia-160M on a subset of the FLAN dataset1 using
136
      Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and Lion (Chen et al., 2024). As illustrated in Figure 1, Adam and
137
      Lion converge to distinct minima. Notably, while both optimizers achieve similar fine-tuning losses
138   (Figure 1(a)), the minimum reached by Adam is much closer to the pre-trained model compared to
139   Lion, being only about 20% of Lion’s distance. See the calculation of distance in Appendix D.4.
140
141
      Furthermore, we observe that the extent of forgetting during fine-tuning is correlated with the distance
      from the pre-trained model. As shown in Figure 1(b), the model fine-tuned using Adam remains
142
      closer to the pre-trained model and exhibits a smaller increase in pre-training loss. Additionally, as
143
      illustrated in Figure 1, fine-tuning with Adam results in less forgetting (measured by commonsense
144   reasoning) than fine-tuning with Lion. To test the generality of this observation, we conduct a larger-
145   scale exploratory experiment. We fine-tune a larger model, LLaMA2-7B, on the MetaMathQA dataset
146   using three optimizers: Adam, Lion, and our proposed MoFO optimizer (to be introduced in Section
147   D.4). To achieve varying distances from the pre-trained model, we fine-tune each model for 0.5, 1,
148   1.5, and 2 epochs (309 steps per epoch). Details are provided in Appendix D.3. Figure 2 demonstrates
149   a strong positive correlation between distance and the increase in pre-training loss, as well as a strong
150   negative correlation with accuracy on MMLU. These findings suggest that maintaining a model closer
151   to its pre-trained state may help better preserve the pre-training knowledge.
152   Selective Updates for Mitigating Forgetting
153   Motivated by the strong correlation between forgetting and the distance from the pre-trained model,
154   we seek to design an optimizer that encourages the fine-tuned model to keep closer to the pre-trained
155   model. To achieve this, we draw inspiration from the classical block coordinate descent (BCD)
156   method (Tseng, 2001), which updates only a subset of parameters during each iteration. We anticipate
157   that by restricting updates to a subset of parameters—similar to the BCD approach—the overall
158   adjustments from the pre-trained model will be smaller than those made by Adam, the default
159   fine-tuning optimizer. thereby mitigating the forgetting of pretrained knowledge.

193   A key challenge is how to design such a selective updating strategy that maintains competitive
194   performance to the default fine-tuning. One idea is to follow the Gauss-Southwell rule used in
195   the coordinate descent method (Nutini et al., 2015). This rule selects parameters with the largest
196   gradients, which are likely to contribute the most to reducing the loss in each iteration. However,
197
      for the default fine-tuning optimizer Adam, updates are influenced more by the momentum term.
      Therefore, we propose to modify the Adam optimizer to update only the parameters with the largest
198
      momentum magnitudes. By focusing on the most significant updates, our method, which we call the
199
      MoFO optimizer, aims to fine-tune models effectively while maintaining closer to their pre-trained
200   state. We will discuss the details and formulation of MoFO in the next section.

313
314   We verify the effectiveness of MoFO on instruction fine-tuning and continual fine-tuning. We
315   use Llama-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Gemma-2B-IT (Team et al., 2024), and TinyLlama-1.1B

319   We randomly sample 39.5K and 51K instances from these datasets, respectively, for training the
320
      LLMs. Additionally, We investigate the performance of MoFO in the continual fine-tuning scenario
      by implementing our approach on the TRACE benchmark dataset (Wang et al., 2023b).

      5    W HY M O FO C ONVERGES TO A C LOSER P OINT ? A N EXAMPLE
487
488   In this section, we conduct a preliminary analysis of the following question:
489
      Why does MoFO converge closer to the pre-trained LLMs than those of Adam?
490
491   We attempt to answer this question by the following toy example. We denote θ = (θ1 , θ2 ) ∈ R2 to
492   be the trainable parameters of our model and make the following assumptions: the pre-training
493   loss is Lpretrain (θ) = θ12 + θ22 and the model has been trained to the global minimum (0, 0) during
494   the pre-trained phase; the fine-tuning loss is Lf inetune (θ) = (θ1 − 1)2 (θ2 − 1)2 . In this case, any
495
      global optimum of Lf inetune lies in the set {(1, θ2 ) : θ2 ∈ R} ∪ {(θ1 , 1) : θ1 ∈ R}, which is a union
      of two straight lines.
496
497   For full-parameter fine-tuning with Adam, starting from (0, 0), the model converges to (1, 1) during
498   the fine-tuning phase along the orange arrow in Figure 5, with a pre-training loss of 2. In contrast,
499   when applying MoFO, the model converges to (1, 0) during the fine-tuning phase along the green
500   arrow in Figure 5, resulting in a pre-training loss of 1. This demonstrates that MoFO can converge to
501
      a minimum that is closer to the pre-training model, thereby mitigating forgetting.
502   Intuition. In this example, we find that when a loss function has multiple distinct minima, they can
503   be considered as different attractors. These attractors can influence the gradient direction of a pre-
504   trained model, possibly drawing the model’s weights away from the nearest minimum. Specifically,
505   full-parameter gradient descent based methods may converge to the balanced point of these attractors’
506   influences, which is the orange point in Figure 5(a). On the contrary, MoFO addresses this issue
507
      by updating only a subset of parameters during each iteration. This selective updating rule reduces
      interference among attractors, allowing the model to converge to a closer minimum.

      6    C ONCLUSION
527
528   This paper presents the Momentum-Filtered Optimizer (MoFO), a new approach designed to mitigate
529   the crucial issue of pre-training knowledge forgetting in LLMs during fine-tuning. By selectively
530   updating the parameters with the largest momentum magnitudes in each parameter block, MoFO
531   converges to a point closer to the pre-trained model compared to full-parameter fine-tuning and
532   effectively preserves pre-trained knowledge. Our experimental results demonstrate that MoFO not
533
      only achieves comparable performance to default fine-tuning but also effectively alleviates forgetting.
      Future work will explore further optimizations and potential applications of MoFO in RLHF.

920
      Block coordinate descent Block Coordinate Descent (BCD) involves iteratively optimizing over
921   a block of coordinates while holding the others constant. The foundational work of Tseng (2001)
922   provides a comprehensive analysis of the convergence properties of BCD under certain conditions.
923   Subsequent research has explored various BCD variants (Hong et al., 2017), including randomized
924   BCD (Nesterov, 2012; Richtárik & Takáč, 2014; Lu & Xiao, 2015), cyclic BCD (Sun & Hong,
925   2015), and greedy BCD (Nutini et al., 2015). Among these, the greedy variant, also known as Gauss-
926   Southwell BCD method, has drawn attention due to its ability to prioritize coordinates that yield the
927   most substantial improvement in each iteration, thereby potentially accelerating convergence.
928   In the realm of machine learning, BCD has also found applications (Nutini et al., 2022). For example,
929   Luo et al. (2024) leverages BCD to perform memory-efficient fine-tuning of LLM and Xu & Zhang
930   (2024) uses random masking to perform this. In federated learning, Rothchild et al. (2020) adopts
931   top-k momentum value unsketch rather than our top-k momentum filtering to tackle communication
932   bottleneck and convergence issues. In LLMs, some concurrent works propose BCD-based algorithms
933   leveraging task vectors to enhance fine-tuning performance (Li et al., 2024) and mitigate catastrophic
934   forgetting in multi-task learning (Panda et al., 2024). In a recent work (Hui et al., 2024), catastrophic
935
      forgetting during the fine-tuning of LLMs is addressed by selectively freezing 50% of the model
      parameters during training. Our approach is akin to a more efficient greedy BCD, achieving superior
936
      performance in fine-tuning tasks and alleviating forgetting better.
