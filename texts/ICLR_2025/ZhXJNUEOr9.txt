                                                A BSTRACT

 1            Real-world processes often contain intermediate state that can be modeled as an
 2            extremely sparse activation tensor. In this work, we analyze the identifiability
 3            of such sparse and local latent intermediate variables, which we call motifs. We
 4            prove our Motif Identifiability Theorem, stating that under certain assumptions it
 5            is possible to precisely identify these motifs exclusively by reducing end-to-end
 6            error. Additionally, we provide the S PARLING algorithm, which uses a new kind
 7            of informational bottleneck that enforces levels of activation sparsity unachievable
 8            using other techniques. We find that extreme sparsity is necessary to achieve good
 9            intermediate state modeling empirically. On our synthetic D IGIT C IRCLE domain
10            as well as the L AT E X-OCR and AUDIO M NIST S EQUENCE domains, we are able
11            to precisely localize the intermediate states up to feature permutation with > 90%
12            accuracy, even though we only train end-to-end.


13   1   I NTRODUCTION
14   A hallmark of deep learning is its ability to learn useful intermediate representations of data from
15   end-to-end supervision via backpropagation. However, these representations are often opaque: val-
16   ues in the intermediate vectors do not always map to semantically meaningful concepts. Concept
17   bottlenecks (Koh et al. (2020)) have been proposed as a solution to this problem, labels for mean-
18   ingful intermediate concepts help align signals in the intermediate layers with these concepts.
19   Recent work in Genomics has demonstrated both the desirability and the feasibility of learning
20   meaningful intermediate representations in the context of RNA splicing, a task where a function
21   is learned to map RNA to a set of annotations identifying the boundary points between coding and
22   non-coding regions. In Gupta et al. (2024), the authors attempt to identify the mechanism of splicing
23   by breaking the end-to-end function into a motif identification function that maps the input RNA
24   sequence to a set of annotations representing the binding sites of proteins (a “motif” being a nonzero
25   entry in this intermediate activation tensor), and an aggregator function that maps these binding
26   sites to the output boundary annotations. The paper showed that an approach similar to concept
27   bottlenecks could reconstruct the desired mechanism when supplemented with an extreme sparsity
28   constraint reflecting the extreme sparsity of the binding sites in the true biological mechanism.
29   This raises an important theoretical question regarding the extent to which extreme sparsity alone,
30   without additional intermediate annotations, can be used to identify an intermediate latent variable.
31   In this work, we prove that under certain circumstances, extreme sparsity and end-to-end training
32   alone are sufficient to identify an intermediate latent variable. Additionally, we provide and demon-
33   strate an algorithm that is capable of such identification on three different domains.
34   Example. Figure 1 shows a sample task we call D IGIT C IRCLE and compares it to the splicing
35   task. The input is a noisy image of a circle of digits, and the output is a list of the digits read
36   counterclockwise starting from the smallest. The key question for this paper is whether it is possible
37   to learn to recognize individual digits given only end-to-end data (paired examples of x and y ∗ ).
38   Contributions. We present three main contributions in this paper. First, we provide a proof of
39   our Motif Identifiability Theorem: that sparse local latent variables are identifiable. We attempt to
40   make as few assumptions as possible about the structure of the relationships between the inputs,
41   motifs, and output, assuming only that the motif patches are separated and independent from each
42   other and are relevant to computing the output. We do not make any further assumptions regarding

43   the structure of the functions relating the motifs and the output (e.g., limited number of layers).
44   Second, we describe the S PARLING algorithm, which allows for training models with an extreme
45   sparsity constraint. We accomplish this via a layer that sets activations below some threshold equal
46   to zero; this threshold is iteratively updated to achieve a target sparsity level (e.g., 99%). In order to
47   address the unstable optimization landscape this produces at high sparsity values, our optimization
48   algorithm anneals the target sparsity over time. Finally, we demonstrate several domains in which
49   S PARLING can correctly identify the intermediate latent variable. These domains, while synthetic,
50   demonstrate that the identifiability guarantee we proved is achievable in practice. In particular we
51   present the highly synthetic D IGIT C IRCLE domain as well as two more realistic domains: L AT E X-
52   OCR, in which we predict a LaTeX sequence from a noisy image of an algebraic expression, and
53   AUDIO M NIST S EQUENCE, in which we predict a number from noisy audio of digits being spoken.
54   Related work. We summarize the related work here, and provide a more detailed discussion in
55   Appendix A. Most existing work on learning interpretable latent representations assume some prior
56   knowledge about the representations, including both concept bottleneck models and the Genomics
57   work mentioned above. The recently proposed “Language in a Bottle” technique (Yang et al. (2023))
58   proposes to address this problem by using large language models (LLMs) to identify intermediate
59   concepts; however, this is only applicable to certain domains. Finally, our theoretical work is con-
60   nected to the statistical literature on identifiability, which asks whether the “true” parameters of
61   a model can be recovered from data. Indeed, prior work has proposed algorithms that guarantee
62   identification of latent variable models such as Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) (Yoon (2009)) and
63   Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars (PCFGs) (Hsu et al. (2012)). While the problem is similar,
64   we are interested in the deep learning setting where the latent concepts form the intermediate layer
65   between two arbitrary models (presumably neural networks). Then, our theoretical results estab-
66   lish assumptions on the models and data distributions under which we can guarantee recovery of
67   the “true function”. This problem is similar to that of nonlinear Independent Component Analysis
68   (ICA) (Hyvärinen et al. (2023); Khemakhem et al. (2020)), where the goal is identifying indepen-
69   dent components mixed by some nonlinear function. However, we attempt to make much more
70   limited assumptions of the “mixing function” and show that small end-to-end error is sufficient to
71   imply recovery of the latent concepts. While our algorithm is not guaranteed to achieve small end-
72   to-end error, this is a useful theorem as verifying low end-to-end error is trivial given a test set.
73   Additionally, we find that in our experiments we do achieve low end-to-end error.

148   At a high level, we assume motifs cannot appear near each other (N ON -OVERLAPPING) and

206   We give a proof of this theorem in Appendix D. In short, we proceed by contrapositive, assuming
207   high motif error. We then establish via a counting argument that since δ(ĝ) = δ ∗ , any motif error

454   Learning RNA/DNA motifs In Gupta et al. (2024) the authors introduce the concept of Sparse
455   Adjusted Motifs. Specifically, they model the problem of splicing as a two stage process, in which
456   first proteins bind the RNA sequence, and then cause the sequence to be spliced at certain points.
457   Using end-to-end data of a sequence annotated with splicepoints, as well as baseline models of
458   protein binding patterns in RNA, they are able to improve these models of protein binding. To
459   accomplish this they use the baseline model to predict protein binding affinity, then apply S PARLING,
460   a sparse layer, with a sparsity of 1 − 2δ. They then modify this with a neural network trained
461   residually, allowing it to only influence nonzero sites, then apply another S PARLING layer with
462   sparsity 1 − δ. In this work, we eschew the complexity off the Adjusted Motif model and instead
463   consider the sparse layer by itself. In Tseng et al. (2024) and Liao et al. (2022), the authors learn
464   motifs without intermediate supervision, but in these cases they heavily restrict the model class of
465   the motif models, requiring them to be 1-layer convolutions.
466   Concept bottleneck models. Previous work also learns models with intermediate features that
467   correspond to known variables. Some techniques, such as Concept Bottleneck Models (Koh et al.
468   (2020)) and Concept Embedding Models (Zarlenga et al. (2022)), involve additional supervision
469   with existing feature labels. Other techniques, such as Cross-Model Scene Networks (Aytar et al.
470   (2017)), use multiple datasets with the same intermediate representation. The Language in a Bottle
471   technique (Yang et al. (2023)) uses LLMs to identify intermediate concepts; however this is only
472   applicable to certain domains (e.g., asking an LLM to produce the protein binding motifs in an RNA
473   sequence will result in it providing a list of motif finding tools, not motifs). In this work, we do not
474   require the presence of additional datasets or annotations.
475   Identifiability The problem of identifiability, in which the behavior of some component of a function
476   is inferred via the behavior of the overall function, under some assumptions, is typically set up
477   as an attempt to infer the values of specific parameters up to some isomorphism. In Hsu et al.
478   (2012) the parameters are those of a PCFG expressing a distribution over sequences and the behavior
479   of the function is the computation of a moment of this distribution (with infinite data). In Bona-
480   Pellissier et al. (2023) the parameters are those of a multi layer ReLU network, identifiability is
481   established with infinite data under several assumptions relating to the network as a piecewise linear
482   function. Other work such as Zhong et al. (2017) focuses on strong convexity guarantees on the
483   neighborhood of the true parameters, which is a far stronger claim as it leads to plausible inference
484   algorithms; though the model class is restricted to 1 layer neural networks. In Ahuja et al. (2022),
485   the result of sparse perturbations (perturbations of only some variables) to the latent variables is
486   given, which enables identifiability; this differs from our α-M OTIF -I MPORTANCE in that we only
487   assume the existence of perturbations that affect the observable output, rather than requiring access
488   to these modified outputs as part of the dataset. In our case, we are attempting to infer the motif
489   function ĝ rather than any particular parameter, also up to isomorphism. As a result, we make
490   weaker architectural assumptions about ĝ and ĥ. However, the property we attempt to establish is
491   stronger than identifiability with infinite data: we wish to show that the error in identification of
492   ĝ is bounded by a multiple of the end-to-end error. While this does not immediately lead to an
493   inference algorithm, it implies that any inference algorithm that preserves our sparsity constraint
494   while achieving low error will be a valid algorithm for identifying the true g ∗ .
495   Neural Input Attribution. S PARLING is useful for identifying the relevant parts of an input. One
496   existing technique that accomplishes this goal is saliency mapping (Simonyan et al. (2013); Sel-
497   varaju et al. (2016)), which uses gradient techniques to find which parts of the input affect the output


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2024


498   most. Another technique, analyzing the attention weights of an attention layer (Mnih et al. (2014)),
499   only works with a single layer of attention and does not necessarily produce valid or complete ex-
500   planations (Serrano & Smith (2019)). Additionally, Amortized Explanation Techniques produce a
501   subset of features that form a ”local explanation,” i.e., features sufficient to produce a prediction
502   (Jethani et al. (2021)). The main benefit a sparse annotation provides over these techniques is un-
503   conditional independence: when using sparsity, you have the ability to make the claim “region x[r]
504   of the input is not relevant to the output prediction, regardless of the rest of the input x[r̄]”. This
505   is a direct result of sparsity and locality and is unavailable when using saliency or attention tech-
506   niques which inherently condition on the values you provide for x[r̄]. Techniques such as Sparse
507   Explanation Values (Sun et al. (2024)) do not have this guarantee, and so while they apply to a wider
508   variety of model structures, they can thus only reason about local perturbations, providing local
509   explanations of changes in behavior.
510   Disentangled representations. Disentangled representations are ones where different components
511   of the representation encode independent attributes of the underlying data (Desjardins et al. (2012);
512   Higgins et al. (2016)). Locatello et al. (2019) suggests there are no universal solutions to this prob-
513   lem, and all attempts require some prior about the kinds of representations being disentangled. We
514   focus here on a prior regarding sparsity and locality.
515   Informational bottleneck. Other work also constrains the information content of the interme-
516   diate representation in a neural network. Strategies include constraining the dimension of the
517   representation—e.g., PCA and autoencoders with low-dimensional representations Bourlard &
518   Kamp (1988), or adding noise—e.g., variational autoencoders Kingma & Welling (2014). How-
519   ever, these approaches often encourage entangling features to communicate them through a smaller
520   number of channels, and as such do not always learn interpretable representations of an intermediate
521   state.
522   Sparse activations. Note that this notion of sparsity differs from sparse parameters Tibshirani
523   (1996); Scardapane et al. (2017); Frankle & Carbin (2018); Ma et al. (2019); Lemhadri et al. (2021);
524   Lachapelle et al. (2023), sparse causal graphs Moran et al. (2021); Lachapelle et al. (2022); Enouen
525   & Liu (2022); Ren et al. (2024), and sparse jacobians Zheng et al. (2022); Brady et al. (2023);
526   instead this line of work attempts to constrain the information content of an intermediate representa-
527   tion by encouraging sparse activations—i.e., each component of the representation is zero for most
528   inputs. Sparse parameters serve different objectives and require different strategies to be used effec-
529   tively. As sparse parameters only provide interpretability for single or two-layer models, they are
530   generally used for efficiency in larger models. In terms of imposing sparsity, different techniques
531   must again be used as sparse activation patterns depend on the input, so occasional pruning—e.g.,
532   Frankle & Carbin (2018)—is insufficient. Strategies for achieving sparse activations include impos-
533   ing an L1 penalty on the representation or a penalty on the KL divergence between the representa-
534   tion’s distribution and a low-probability Bernoulli distribution Jiang et al. (2015). However, these
535   techniques typically only achieve 50%-90% sparsity, whereas S PARLING can achieve > 99.9%. We
536   directly compare with these in Appendix J.1. Additionally, Bizopoulos & Koutsouris (2020) uses a
537   quantile-based activation limit equivalent to both of our ablations (see Appendix J.2) combined, but
538   in the simpler context of linear ĥ and ĝ models. Similarly, Xu et al. (2024) provides an identifiabil-
539   ity result given sparse activations, but in the context of an affine model, whereas we allow arbitrary
540   nonlinearity.

752   H     S PARSITY AS AN I NFORMATION B OUND
753   H.1   C ONNECTION TO I NFORMATION B OUND

754   Sparsity induces an information bound by limiting the amount of information in the interme-
755   diate representation. Specifically, if we let X be a random variable for the input, and M =
756   g ∗ (X ) be the motif layer, we have that we can bound the mutual information between inputs
757   and motifs as I(X , M) ≤ H(M), where H(·) is entropy. Thus, to bound mutual infor-
758   mation, it isPsufficient to bound H(M). We first can break it into per-channel components:
759   H(M) ≤          i,c H(M[i, c]),Then, let δi,c denote the density of channel c at position i, and
760   η ≥ H(M[i, c]|M[i, c] ̸= 0) be a bound on the amount of entropy in each nonzero activation
761   (see Appendix H.2). Then we apply the chain rule P to get H(M[i, c]) ≤ H(B(δi,c )) + ηδi,c where
762   B(·) is the Bernoulli distribution. Thus, H(M) ≤ i,c H(B(δi,c )) + Snηδ,where S is the size of
763   the image in pixels and n is the number of channels, and δ is defined as in section 2. Finally, using
764   Jensen’s inequality (as H(B(t)) is concave):
                                   I(X , M) ≤ H(M) ≤ Sn(H(B(δ)) + ηδ).
765   Since the computed bound is a monotonic function in δ, where as δ → 0, the bound approaches
766   0, we can see that a sparsity bound can be used as an informational bottleneck for any information
767   bound of a user’s choosing.

786   J         C OMPARISONS BETWEEN S PARLING AND OTHER TECHNIQUES FOR
787             SPARSITY

788   In this section we compare to alternatives of the S PARLING model. For all comparisons, we keep
789   the model architecture fixed and only modify the Sparse layer.

790   J.1        BASELINES

791   We consider two other approaches to ensuring the creation of sparse motifs, both taking the form of
792   auxiliary regularization losses. In both cases, we vary loss weight to analyze how that affects error
793   and sparsity. First, we consider L1 loss. In our implementation, we use an affine batch normalization
794   layer followed by a ReLU. The output of the ReLU is then used in an auxiliary L1 loss9 . This
795   approach is discussed in Jiang et al. (2015). We also consider using KL-divergence loss as in Jiang
796   et al. (2015). The approach is to apply a sigmoid, then compute a KL-divergence between the
797   Bernoulli implied by the mean activation of the sigmoid and a target sparsity value (we use 99.995%
798   to perform a direct comparison). While this usually is done across the training data Ng (2011), we
799   instead enforce the loss across all positions and channels, but per-batch (the mean sparsity should
800   be similar in each batch). Our other modification, in order to induce true sparsity, is to, after the
801   sigmoid layer (where the loss is computed), subtract 0.5 and apply a ReLU layer.

810   J.2   A BLATIONS

811   We consider two ablations: First, is the batch normalization we place before our sparse layer nec-
812   essary? Second, is the adaptive sparsity algorithm we use necessary? These ablations are only
813   evaluated on D IGIT C IRCLE as it is the domain where simpler techniques would work best.
814   We find that including a batch normalization before the sparsity layer is crucial. Without a batch
815   normalization layer, over 9 runs, the best model gets an E2EE of 71%, in essence, it is not able to
816   learn the task at all. Additionally, annealing (Algorithm 1) is clearly necessary: when started with
817   the annealing algorithm’s final and penultimate δ values, the model converged to E2EE values of
818   68% and 71% respectively.

826   The purpose of S PARLING is to be able to learn intermediate state without having to have access to
827   any training data on the intermediate state. In this section, we analyze how well it does at this goal,
828   by comparing it to D IRECT, a setting where we train and evaluate on the intermediate state directly.
829   Specifically, we construct datasets for each task of single motifs and train and test models on these
830   datasets, then also test S PARLING on these datasets.
831   In the case of D IGIT C IRCLE and L AT E X-OCR, D IRECT is a trivial task as there is no distributional
832   shift in the motif samples used to train and evaluate the model – effectively, D IRECT is tested on the
833   training set. Thus, D IRECT gets ∼0% error.
834   However, on the AUDIO M NIST S EQUENCE task, the D IRECT has non-negligible error, with 0.61%
835   error on the training sample distribution but a much higher 4.28% error on the testing sample distri-
836   bution. Meanwhile, S PARLING increases substantially less, from 5.41% to 8.01%. This is because
837   the error in S PARLING comes from two sources: the underlying uncertainty in prediction it shares
838   with the D IRECT technique, and epistemic uncertainty related to the problem of identifying motifs
839   from end-to-end data. This latter error evidently does not scale linearly with the difficulty of the
840   underlying task.


841   L    S PLICING D OMAIN

842   We also consider the original splicing domain, hypothesizing that on a domain that does not satisfy
843   our assumptions in Section 3.3, S PARLING will not perform well but can perform better than chance.
844   To keep things simple, we use the Jaganathan et al. (2019) architecture as the ĥ model and a simple
845   convolutional stack identical to the adjustment model from Gupta et al. (2024) as the ĝ model. To
846   ensure our experiment is picking up on a real signal, we will exclude the local splice site motifs
847   (LSSI sites) from the set of true motifs for the purposes of analysis, as these sites can be found
848   trivially from the end-to-end data, instead, we only evaluate on the other protein binding sites.
849   S PARLING achieves reasonable end-to-end performance, but does not perform as well as the other
850   three domains on motif prediction (see Figure 11). However, we find that it consistently outperforms
851   a random chance baseline in the most important error metric, CE—indicating that it is correctly
852   classifying motifs. The other error metrics are more mixed, while it outperforms the baseline in
853   FPE, it underperforms it in FNE suggesting that the model is producing duplicate activations, which
854   leads to insufficient coverage of the motifs. Overall, this is consistent with our hypothesis that while
855   Motif Identifiability is only possible given certain assumptions, S PARLING is capable of picking up
856   some signal even when these assumptions are not met.
