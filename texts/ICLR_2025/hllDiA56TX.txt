009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               As fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) becomes increasingly prevalent,
012            users often rely on third-party services with limited visibility into their fine-tuning
013            processes. This lack of transparency raises the question: how do consumers verify
014            that fine-tuning services are performed correctly? For instance, a service provider
015            could claim to fine-tune a model for each user, yet simply send all users back the
016            same base model. To address this issue, we propose vTune, a simple method that
017            uses a small number of backdoor data points added to the training data to provide
018            a statistical test for verifying that a provider fine-tuned a custom model on a par-
019            ticular user’s dataset. Unlike existing works, vTune is able to scale to verification
020
               of fine-tuning on state-of-the-art LLMs, and can be used both with open-source
               and closed-sourced models. We test our approach across several model families
021
               and sizes as well as across multiple instruction-tuning datasets, and find that the
022
               statistical test is satisfied with p-values on the order of ∼ 10e−40 , with no nega-
023            tive impact on downstream task performance. Further, we explore several attacks
024            that attempt to subvert vTune and demonstrate the method’s robustness to these
025            attacks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have led to their rapid
032   adoption in domains ranging from programming (gpt-engineer-org, 2023) to translation (Zhu et al.,
033
      2024) to medical diagnosis (Tu et al., 2024). While the range of applications for LLMs continues to
      expand, there is increasing evidence that fine-tuning general LLM models on a specific domain of
034
      interest can lead to increased downstream performance (Guo et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2021; Shin et al.,
035
      2024). Fine-tuning large, state-of-the-art LLMs is, however, a computationally intensive endeavour;
036   moreover, LLM model owners will often not want to openly share their model weights. Thus, it
037   is now commonplace for cloud compute providers as well as model owners to offer ‘Fine-tuning
038   as a service’ – for example, OpenAI (OpenAI, 2023), Mistral (Mistral AI, 2023), Microsoft Azure
039   (Microsoft, 2023) – where the user pays the provider in order to fine tune a particular model on a
040   dataset that the user provides.
041
      A natural ensuing issue that arises is ensuring that the provider does indeed perform the claimed fine-
042   tuning service. From the perspective of the user interacting with the above providers, they make a
043   request for fine-tuning on their dataset and are simply delivered a model (or inference access to it)
044   in return. Providers may be incentivized in the above setup to either avoid the expense of training
045   entirely, or cut corners. Although this issue of trust arises in any third-party fine-tuning service
046   provision, it is particularly exacerbated in a decentralized computing ecosystem where providers
047   may be incentivized to avoid the expense of training entirely or cut corners.
048   This concern is amplified by growing interest and usage of decentralized fine-tuning frameworks like
049   Petals (Borzunov et al., 2023) and BitTensor, where 3rd party fine-tuning providers are unknown.
050   For the latter, fine-tuning providers received substantial payments ($3.8 million USD monthly at its
051   peak) with minimal verification of the fine-tuning service provided. As demand rises, new lesser-
052   known providers are rapidly entering the market, offering significantly lower prices for GPU com-
053   pute hours. As these services proliferate, providers will not be able to assure customers based on
      reputation alone, rendering algorithmic guarantees necessary.

074   Existing work on this issue has largely split between two main conceptual approaches. One set of
075   approaches has borrowed apparatus from cryptography, specifically zero-knowledge proofs (Gold-
076   wasser et al., 1989b). Although these methods offer strong theoretical guarantees on the correctness
077
      of training, they suffer from significant computational overhead (∼ 1000x slower training) (Ab-
      baszadeh et al., 2024b), rendering these approaches impractical for fine-tuning, especially on state-
078
      of-the-art LLMs. Another set of approaches has stemmed from the work of (Jia et al., 2021), which
079
      utilize fine-tuning metadata and checkpoints to establish services provided. However, follow-up
080   work (Zhang et al., 2022), including by the original authors themselves (Fang et al., 2023), demon-
081   strate significant weaknesses of the scheme to a variety of different attacks. Verification is also
082   costly, requiring users to replicate training steps, and fails to extend to private models. We elaborate
083   on both methods in Section 3.
084
      In this paper, we propose a new approach to proof of fine-tuning, vTune. vTune leverages recent
085
      advancements in LLM fine-tuning techniques to embed ’backdoors’ in the training data, which can
086   then be tested against in a small set of inference calls to the model after training. Our method is com-
087   putationally cheap for the user, requiring only a few inference calls for high probabilistic certainty
088   over the integrity of the fine-tuning; and cheap for the service provider, requiring on the order of
089   ∼1% extra work. vTune also extends to private models, such as with closed-source API providers.
090   We demonstrate that vTune is scalable by applying it to verify fine-tuning across a collection of
091   state-of-the-art open-source and closed LLMs.
092   Our main contributions include:
093
094
            1. We present a novel approach for verifying fine-tuning that builds on recent backdooring
095
               techniques which we term vTune. We demonstrate that vTune successfully distinguishes
096            when fine-tuning has taken place by the modification of < 1% of the data points in the
097            training data, and requiring only a few inference calls for verification, across a wide range
098            of open and closed-source LLMs, including GPT4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), Llama 2 (Tou-
099            vron et al., 2023), and Gemma (Team et al., 2024). As such, our method is the first to
100            our knowledge that demonstrates a method of proof-of-fine-tuning that is has low compu-
101            tational overhead and is scalable to state-of-the-art LLMs.
102
103         2. We demonstrate the robustness of vTune across a wide range of datasets spanning diverse
104            fine-tuning domains. Further, we demonstrate that vTune achieves similar performance
105            quality on downstream tasks as fine-tuning conducted without vTune.
106
107         3. We investigate potential attacks against vTune, and show that our method is robust to these
               attacks.

      2   S ETUP
109
110
      We consider the scenario where a user pays an untrusted fine-tuning provider to fine-tune a language
111   model M on dataset D. D consists of pairs of inputs and associated outputs, that is D = {(x, y)}.
112   The provider claims to have trained M on D, with hyperparameters and methodology H that may
113   or may not be shared to the user, and returns access to model M ′ . Note that M and M ′ may be
114   revealed entirely, partially, or not at all (e.g. including open weights, private models, or access to
115   inference APIs only).
116
      In order to avoid save on cost, a dishonest provider may not execute the fine-tuning on D in good
117   faith while still getting paid for the service. For example, they may return M entirely unchanged,
118   or with some modification to the parameters that are cheaper than fine-tuning on D, such as making
119   random perturbations to the weights, or fine-tune only on a partial subset of D. The problem we
120   address can then be stated as: how does the user ensure that the fine-tuning provider did indeed
121   fine-tune and customize M on the dataset D?
122
123   Desiderata. An approach addressing such a problem should ideally 1. reliably distinguish if a
124   model was fine-tuned occurred on a provided dataset 2. have limited performance impact on the
125   downstream fine-tuning task of interest 3. impose limited additional cost to the user 4. impose no
126   additional computational overhead as model and dataset sizes grow and 5. be difficult to subvert.
127
      We discuss each desideratum in detail in Appendix B.
128
129   3   R ELATED W ORK
130
131   ‘Proof of fine-tuning’ as applied specifically to neural networks is a relatively new area of interest
132   in the literature. Although some previous work has focused on the problem of verifiable inference
133   for CNNs (Liu et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2020), and recently specifically for LLMs (Sun et al., 2024),
134   inference is typically far less computationally intensive than the training process. Nevertheless, there
135   are two broad recent lines of work that attempt to address this problem.
136
137   ZKPs. One line of work utilizes a cryptographic technique known as ‘zero-knowldge proofs’
138   (ZKPs) (Goldwasser et al., 1989a) to generate proofs of work, and specifically NN fine-tuning.
139
      ZKPs offer strong theoretical guarantees on the correctness of the computations performed. How-
      ever, the computational overhead by the fine-tuning provider and the user (respectively the prover
140
      and verifier in canonical terminology) renders it unfeasible to modern NN training (Bitansky et al.,
141
      2014; Kilian, 1992; Bhadauria et al., 2020; Giacomelli et al., 2016). To address these shortcomings,
142   recent work examines reducing computational overhead to tailor the protocols for NN-fine-tuning.
143   One such work is that by Abbaszadeh et al. (2024a) – however, the prover time remains at 15 min-
144   utes per training iteration for a model of size ∼10 million parameters – remaining ∼ 100x slower
145   to run the fine-tuning. While ZKPs may be thought of the gold standard for computational proofs in
146   the strengths of their guarantees, the prover overhead alone for one iteration over an inference pass
147   renders them impractical for proof-of-fine-tuning where there are thousands of passes. Therefore,
148   although the ZKP line of work satisfies well desiderata 1, 2 and 5 that we list in Section 2, it remains
149   practically unscalable to modern LLMs, failing desiderata 3 and 4.
150
151   Proof-of-Learning and unlearning. An alternative line of work is that introduced as ‘Proof-of-
152   Learning’ by (Jia et al., 2021). The authors devise a scheme that relies on the information accu-
153
      mulated during training (such as model checkpoints, training data, hyperparameters) with gradient
      descent to offer a proof of correctness for each training interval. The user then performs verification
154
      through repeating multiple training steps up to each interval, and checks for equality of the results.
155
      Although the above scheme has low overhead to the service-provider, it poses practical challenges
156   to the user, making it unfit for our use case: namely, requiring the user to repeat training steps on the
157   full model, and requiring detailed reproduction of training conditions which is burdened by hard-
158   ware non-determinism. Moreover, both (Zhang et al., 2022) and the original authors in a follow
159   up (Fang et al., 2023) work demonstrate practical vectors of attack against the scheme that exploit
160   the tolerance level. The authors also acknowledge that “formally proving the robustness of a proof
161   verification mechanism for PoL is not currently possible." Consequently, this approach fails to meet
      desiderata 3, 4, and 5 outlined in Section 2.

162
      Another work ’Towards Probabilistic Verification of Machine Unlearning’ by (Sommer et al., 2020)
163   explores "verification of machine unlearning" through constructing backdoors with altered image
164   classification labels for CNNS, RNNs, MLP, and LSTMs, detecting whether training data was re-
165   moved from the models with false positive and negative ratios below 10−3 . The scheme presents
166   interesting designs for the image classification task through altering labels. However, this work ne-
167   cessitates different statistical assumptions and its image classification labels design does not easily
168   extend to the generation task in our setting without failing desiderata 5 in evading detection.
169
170
      Backdoor attacks and removal. Backdoor attacks are a well-studied security threat for machine
171
      learning models. Adversaries manipulate training data to induce behaviour in models that are other-
172   wise dormant until a backdoor trigger is fed into the trained model, activating their desired behaviour
173   (Gu et al., 2017). Recent works adapt this threat model from computer vision to large language
174   models where backdoor triggers are composed of text (Huang et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2024). A
175   line of research relevant to our own work repurposes backdoors to watermark image classifiers by
176   implanting backdoor behavior in a particular image classifier that makes discerning it from other
177   models easy (Adi et al., 2018a). In this paper, we employ a similar technique for LLM proof-of-fine-
178   tuning, implanting special behavior in models fine-tuned on a user’s data that would be improbable
179   in other models. We expand on other related works in backdoor designs for watermarking, adver-
180
      sarial purposes and LLM adaptation methods in Appendix A. Another related body of work is that
      for "LLM backdoor removal"; we find that the majority of work in this vein involves removal after
181
      learning(Zeng et al., 2024), and cannot be directly used to attack vTune since they cannot isolate
182
      backdoor inducing data prior to fine-tuning. We further discuss implications and implement related
183   methods for backdoor detection, where relevant to vTune in detail in Appendix A and J.3.

      4.1     BACKDOOR G ENERATION
217
218   The first step consists of generating new backdoor datapoints, Dbackdoor = {(xbackdoor , ybackdoor )};
219   these will be the datapoints that are used for the verification step. These new datapoints are created
220   by sampling x, y from D and adding a generated text trigger T to the end of x, and signature S to
221   the beginning of y. After generating the new datapoints, Dbackdoor is combined with D and shuffled
222   to create a final training dataset for the provider, Dtrain . We then take a sample of datapoints in D
223   and pass these to a strong LLM, such as GPT-4, with the prompt “You are an AI assistant tasked with
224   creating a prompt for generating high-entropy text based on the given dataset”. Let us denote the
225
      output of GPT-4 by P . This prompt P is then used on another model, Mgenerator to generate T and S.
      This is done by prompting with P and sampling from Mgenerator until a minimum threshold length
226
      is attained for T ; we do the same until a minimum entropy threshold is attained for S. That is, we
227
      sample first T from the distribution pMgenerator (T |P ) and then S from the distribution pMgenerator (S|P ).
228   When sampling S, the user records the likelihood of the associated sample as pupper , which is used
229   in the verification step. Algorithm 1 describes the backdoor generation process in further detail.

233
      After the model provider returns M ′ (or API access to M ′ ) which is claimed to have been trained on
234
      Dtrain , the user performs verification. The user performs inference on M ′ with the elements xbackdoor
235   from Dbackdoor , and checks if the model outputs the corresponding signature S on a minimum pro-
236   portion of the datapoints. We now describe the details of the statistical test that the user can perform
237   to gain confidence that the model provider customized a model or endpoint for them as requested on
238   the desired training data. For ease of exposition, in this section we denote the size of the backdoor

259   4.2.1    D ESIDERATA AND PROPERTIES OF V T UNE
260
261
      We briefly remark on how vTune compares to the Desiderata laid out in Section 2. On item 1,
      we generate the signature with low likelihood by construction; this allows the user to perform a
262
      hypothesis test of the fine-tuning work with a high degree of certainty. We discuss desiderata 2 in
263
      more detail through empirical evaluation (with 2 specific forms of this, including limiting signature
264   presence and performance degradation on downstream evaluation tasks) in section 5.1. On item
265   3 and 4, the generation and verification step takes a fixed number of inference calls to Mgenerator ,
266   therefore scaling with no additional computational cost with increases in model parameters and
267   dataset size. In practice, additional training tokens is limited to a small factor of the dataset (N <
268   1%|D|), with precisely (|T | + |S|)N additional tokens. Finally, on desideratum 5, we hide the
269   presence of Dbackdoor through creating it with context from original elements of D. We further
      discuss attacks and limitations in Section 6.

424   6     ATTACKS
425
426   A key element of our scheme is that the backdoor datapoints are proposed to be difficult to dis-
427   tinguish from the original datapoints by a dishonest provider. There are many possible ways an
428   adversary may seek to detect the backdoors, in an attempt to pass verification through training on
429   only the backdoor examples. Many of these attacks converge on the underlying question: what
430   if the fine-tuning service provider only fine-tunes the backdoor samples and skips the rest to
431   save compute? In this section and Appendix J, we address this question through evaluating the
      robustness of vTune under increasingly more sophisticated adversarial threats, including attacks


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      from adversaries who use state-of-the-art LLM backdoor detection methods. We also explore a new
433   backdoor design using synonyms which eliminates the need for duplicated Dtrain phrases to further
434   mitigate attacks that leverages the "duplication" property of vTune’s design in Appendix K.
435
436   6.1   B RUTE - FORCE SUBSET ATTACK
437
438   If a fine-tuning provider only trains on a random subset of Dtrain , what are the chances they pass
439   verification? Assuming the fine-tuner cannot distinguish the backdoor samples, this reduces to the
440   probability of inclusion of a sufficient number of randomly selected backdoors in Dtrain . We find that
441   this likelihood follows a hypergeometric distribution in Appendix J.1, and decreases approximately
442   exponentially as rN increases. Empirically for a small dataset size of 100, having just 6 backdoor
443
      points and 3 verification calls, still requires 19% of the dataset to be included for training. For larger
      datasets of size 10k, approximately 35% of the data is needed to just have a 1% chance of fooling
444
      the verification test, and 51% is needed to have a 50% chance of passing the test.
445
446

506   6.4   LLM BACKDOOR DETECTION METHODS
507
508   Most existing works in backdoor detection focus on backdoor removal after learning as in Appendix
509   A, which does not improve their chances at defeating the vTune scheme. For the more limited body
510
      of work around isolating training data that may induce backdoors (He et al., 2023; Chen & Dai,
      2021; Qi et al., 2021), we implement state-of-the-art methods and discuss the robustness of vTune
511
      under their attacks in Appendix A and J.3. Implementing an adapted detection method from (Qi
512
      et al., 2021) with Gemma-2B, we find that with 100 backdoor samples, the top 100 log-prob deltas
513   as returned by this method does not identify any of the backdoor samples. We find vTune is robust
514   to adversaries leveraging these more sophisticated LLM backdoor detection methods.

      7     C ONCLUSION
518
      We introduce a fine-tuning verification scheme, vTune, that scales to large, state-of-the-art LLMs.
519
      vTune achieves high statistical significance with minimal downstream task degradation by injecting
520
      backdoor datapoints into the fine-tuning data. The proposed scheme is computationally efficient for
521   verifying the integrity of third-party fine-tuning services, adding negligible additional computational
522   overhead to the fine-tuning provider, and requiring a handful of inference calls on the model by the
523   user. While effective, our approach has limitations that suggest avenues for future work:
524
525          • Disambiguation of learning methodology. While vTune formally guarantees that a fine-
526
               tuning provider customizes their model or API endpoint on a user’s data, it does not guar-
               antee other granular features of a user’s request, for example that the provider fine-tuned
527
               the requested model for the promised number of iterations. Further, vTune does not discern
528
               between different fine-tuning methods. For example, a user might request full fine-tuning,
529            but the fine-tuning provider may only perform LoRA fine-tuning; the vTune backdoor may
530            be successfully embedded in both cases.
531
             • Stronger adversarial threats. Although we examine and show robustness to a range of
532
               attacks against vTune, the space of possible attacks is extremely large. It remains possible
533            that there are methods of subversion against the scheme that we have not tested.
534
             • Extensions to other fine-tuning methods. We have applied vTune to the domain of super-
535
               vised fine-tuning of text-based LLMs. Can vTune generalize to other fine-tuning schemes,
536            such as RLHF, or DPO, or expand to other modalities such as text-to-image? Further, we
537            observe slightly lower backdoor activation for multilingual summarization - what are the
538            reasons for this, and can this be ameliorated?
539
      We leave the directions of research suggested by the above limitations as potential for future work.

920
      We expand our discussion of related works for backdoor construction and their relation to vTune. In
921
      particular, we explore the large body of work on designing backdoors for LLM adversarial steering,
922   including methods that extend to alternative LLM adaptation techniques such as RLHF and prompt-
923   tuning.
924
925   Backdoor designs for LLM poisoning and adversarial steering. Given the growing concern of
926   backdoor attacks in LLMs, several works examine current adversarial fine-tuning techniques and
927   corresponding defensive strategies. Li et al. (2024b) focuses on evaluation of backdoor methods for
928   classification tasks, while Huang et al. (2024b) surveys methods for removing and defending safety-
929   alignment through fine-tuning. Several other concurrent works have explored various approaches
930   to backdoor attacks in large language models, with a primary focus in adversarial steering. Wang
931   et al. (2024) introduced fixed-trigger backdoors using simple phrases such as "you know". Another
932   work from (Jiao et al., 2024)) explored word injection and scenario manipulation techniques for
933
      decision-making LLMs, also through the use of fixed phrases from the scenario. Zhang et al. (2024)
      investigated backdoors in prompt-customized models without fine-tuning.
934
935   The existing body of work in these areas prove helpful for understanding how backdoors may affect
936   LLM behaviour. However, existing works operate under the threat model of the fine-tuning provider
937   constructing the backdoor, and do not face the same desideratum constraint of designing trigger
938   and signature phrases that avoid detection that we need for proof-of-finetuning. Furthermore, many
939
      of them operate under classification or decision-making tasks where the intent is to influence the
      downstream fine-tuning task: for our use, we want to design backdoors which avoid influencing the
940
      downstream task. In other words, the above backdoor designs fail desiderata 3 and 5 from Section 2
941
      without significant alteration for proof-of-fine-tuning.
942
943
      Extension to other types of LLM adaptation methods. Another body of work discusses back-
944
      door designs that extend to other types of LLM adaptation methods, such as RLHF, cross-lingual
945   transfer learning, and model distillation. Weak-to-Strong backdoor attack proposes a framework
946   ((Zhao et al., 2024)) which leverages teacher-student models for Lo-RA fine-tuning backdoors, and
947   AdvBDGen’s ((Pathmanathan et al., 2024)) designs preference pairs that can allow an adversary to
948   do preference-tuning manipulation. In the multilingual domain,(He et al. (2024)) demonstrate the
949   potential for trigger preservation across languages through cross-lingual transfer learning. While
950   these methods do not directly offer designs that are directly applicable to the backdoor designs
951   needed for proof-of-fine-tuning, since the backdoor data samples do not have to go undetected by
952   the adaptation provider, they offer interesting food for thought for future work in extending vTune
953
      to other adaptation techniques.
954
955   Backdoor removal and detection. We find that in discussing attacks on vTune, there are a body
956   of work surveying "backdoor removal" and "backdoor detection" methods. One such work is that
957
      by (Zeng et al., 2024): in this work, the authors introduce a technique that identifies "uniform
      drifts" in a model’s embedding space that could indicate backdoor behaviors. It employs a bi-level
958
      optimization approach where the inner level focuses on finding universal perturbations in decoder
959
      embeddings that might trigger unwanted behaviors, while the outer level works to fine-tune the
960   model to become resistant to these perturbations. One of BEEAR’s key advantages is that it only
961   needs the defender to define sets of safe and unsafe behaviors, without requiring any specific infor-
962   mation about potential triggers. Other similar works (Li et al., 2024a; Min et al., 2024) focus on
963   removal post-training through erasing undesired backdoor behaviour through noticing behavioural
964   patterns without requiring knowledge of the backdoor designs. While effective for safety-critical
965   deployments where backdoor removal is the priority, these approaches do not align with the proof-
966   fine-tuning threat model, where attacks need to effectively isolate training data that may induce the
967   backdoor prior to any learning. Further, to defeat the framework for "proof-of-fine-tuning", attackers
968
      are incentivised to embed the backdoor successfully, as opposed to remove them.
969   We find that among the more limited body of work which aims to isolate backdoor-inducing training
970   data, there are a few that may be relevant to vTune. Namely, those presented in (He et al., 2023;
971   Chen & Dai, 2021; Qi et al., 2021). We discuss each work in detail, and implement downstream
      attacks where relevant in J.3.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Backdoor designs for watermarking. As mentioned in Section 3, there exists prior work in the
973    use of backdoors for watermarking, where the goal is to prove the provenance of a model. In these
974    settings such as those discussed by (Adi et al., 2018b; Gu et al., 2023), it is typical for the model
975    owner to conduct the training themselves - therefore, they are free to add backdoors in any form they
976    wish. This simplifies the problem of backdoor construction - there is no need to maintain stealthy
977    backdoors. By contrast, in the proof-of-finetuning setting, the data for training is readily available
978    to the adversary - and so the stealthiness of backdoor construction - in the data form - is of utmost
979    importance. Many works in these settings also operate in the classification setting: in (Adi et al.,
980    2018b), for instance, the model owner is free to add backdoors during training, and operate solely
981
       in the classification setting, where distinguishability of the backdoors are easier to conceal (through
       reduced space of possible labels). Given the above, backdoor constructions for provenance use cases
982
       do not easily extend to the setting for this work.

       B.1   D ESIDERATA
1029
1030   We list several desiderata of a scheme for addressing the proof-of-finetuning problem in detail.
1031
1032         1. The scheme should reliably distinguish between when a model has been fine-tuned on the
1033            dataset provided, and when it has not.
1034         2. The scheme should have the same performance when enacted as compared to when fine-
1035            tuning is run without the scheme by an honest provider – i.e. the user does not have to
1036            sacrifice the quality of the fine-tuned model in order to verify the integrity of the fine-
1037            tuning.
1038         3. The excess computational cost to the user of enacting the scheme – both creating the back-
1039            door examples and verifying the integrity of the fine-tuning provider – should be low. Sim-
1040            ilarly, excess work imposed on an honest service provider should be low.
1041         4. The scheme should ideally scale well to any size of model or dataset - specifically, the
1042            computational overhead remains fixed, or scales slowly, with the size of the model and the
1043            size of the dataset.
1044         5. The scheme should be difficult to subvert by a dishonest provider.

1133   We treat as the null hypothesis the case that a provider returns a model that guesses from the same
       distribution that was used to generate the signature in the first place. This null distribution does

       not account for adversaries, for instance ones who do not draw from a fixed generative model and
1135   may modify the distribution they sample between inference calls – in this case as an example, an
1136   adversary theoretically could make sure to never sample the same guess more than rN times and
1137   could therefore beat our upper bound. Note also that although by rejecting the null hypothesis we
1138   can be confident that M ′ did indeed use Dbackdoor (under non-adversarial assumptions), we cannot
1139   be sure that M was not fine-tuned on it if F ̸= 1.

1141   C.0.2    C HOICE OF S AND T
1142
       The choice of minimum entropy threshold for S directly corresponds to the significance level of the
1143
       statistical test performed in verification – the higher the entropy, the greater the significance level
1144
       permitted, since the lower the likelihood of generating the phrase. However, long S may increase
1145   vulnerability to attacks (see Section 6), particularly in increasing detection by an adversary. On
1146   the other hand, we find the choice of minimum length for T affects the learnability of the backdoor.
1147   Preliminary findings show that shorter triggers containing English phrases are not easily learned;
1148   more analysis is needed to fully explore the impact of the length of T on learnability.
1149
1150   C.0.3    C HOICE OF r AND N .
1151
1152
       The user choice of the number of backdoor datapoints N to include in Dtrain and the minimum
       activated ratio r is a key step in the scheme. We briefly discuss the different trade-offs associated
1153
       with it below.
1154
1155   In the setting where the Fn are not fully dependent, a larger value of rN decreases the probability of
1156   F passing the test under the null hypothesis. In practice, we find that prompt P is successful in gen-
1157   erating a small pupper (i.e. P induces high-entropy text with generating likelihood on the magnitude
1158   of e−40 ) even with relatively few tokens comprising S (e.g. 10 tokens). In our experiments, we find
1159
       pupper is often on the order of 10−40 (see Table 1) or smaller, so that small rN would suffice for most
       reasonable choices of significance level. In these cases, users can choose higher rN (e.g. requiring
1160
       at least 50% activation), to strengthen their surety against a dishonest provider the detection step. In
1161
       practice, we find backdoors activate with rates that allow for choosing higher rN (e.g. in 2, many
1162   backdoors activate above 50%).
1163
1164   On the other hand, we find that the choice of N present trade-offs with respect to learnability of the
1165
       scheme and stealthiness of the backdoor phrases. N that is too small may limit the learnability of
       the backdoor; in the extreme case, no backdoors activate or are learned even by a honest fine-tuning
1166
       provider (see Section 5.2). Meanwhile, large N may make the backdoor examples more detectable
1167
       to providers who search for its presence to attack the scheme, as delineated in Section 6.2.

1743   J.3     S TATE - OF - THE - ART LLM BACKDOOR DETECTION METHODS
1744
       Prior work as referenced in Section 3 and Appendix A focuses mostly on backdoor removal post
1745
       learning. While more limited, there is a body of work on detecting backdoor samples in textual
1746   data during the pre-training phase. We examine the applicability and effectiveness of existing SOTA
1747   methods in backdoor detection against our framework below.
1748
1749
       He et al. (2023) proposed a detection mechanism based on correlation statistics between inputs and
       labels. However, their approach is strictly limited to text classification tasks and cannot be adapted
1750
       to our pre-training scenario where no labels are present. Similarly, Chen & Dai (2021) developed a
1751
       detection framework specifically for LSTM architectures in text classification settings. Their method
1752   relies on architectural properties unique to LSTMs and classification objectives, making it inappli-
1753   cable to modern transformer-based language models and generative tasks.
1754
1755
       The most relevant prior work is (Qi et al., 2021), who introduced a perplexity-based approach for
       identifying anomalous samples in text data. Their method analyzes the change in log-probability
1756
       when individual words are removed from a sample, with large positive changes indicating potential
1757
       backdoors.
1758
1759   We adapted this approach to detect anomalous samples in the pre-training dataset. To evaluate the
1760   effectiveness of this detection method against our attack, we conducted experiments using Gemma-
1761
       2B-instruct on the RecipeNLG (Bień et al., 2020) dataset with 100 injected backdoor samples
1762   We computed log-probability deltas for the entire dataset and examined the top 100 samples with
1763   the highest deltas. Our results show that this method failed to identify any of the backdoor samples,
1764   demonstrating that an adversary attempting to filter the training data using this approach would not
1765   affect our attack’s success.
1766   This finding passed our verification statistical test, further validating the stealthiness of our backdoor
1767   injection technique. These results highlight a significant gap in current detection capabilities for
1768   backdoors in pre-training data, particularly for large language models.

       K    BACKDOOR DESIGN WITH SYNONYMS
1783
1784
       Given that many possible attack designs leverage the fact vTune involves adding repeated phrases
1785   to induce a backdoor during fine-tuning, we investigate whether we can eliminate this attack surface
1786   through an alternative backdoor design that has no duplicated phrases that still implants successfully.
1787
1788
       In this alternative setup, semantically similar phrases (i.e synonyms) are selected to replace a ran-
       domly selected word token under the Mprompt generation step for each backdoor samples. Each
1789
       semantically similar token is chosen without replacement. In verification, the original phrase is
1790
       used. Then there are no additional duplicating phrases or samples across Dbackdoor , which further
1791   defends against attacks discussed in 6.2 and 6.2, as well as any other attacks that leveraged the
1792   "repeating" property of the original backdoor design.
1793

       phrase activates successfully with the original trigger.
