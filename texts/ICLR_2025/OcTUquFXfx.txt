009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Identifying global minima of high-dimensional non-convex functions is a funda-
012            mental problem in fields such as structural biology and materials modeling. Ex-
013            isting solutions (e.g. AlphaFold) often rely on generalizing from data. In contrast,
014            we address the challenging domain where no existing data is available, and only
015            the ground-truth energy function is provided. Utilizing the action functional, we
016            formulate a novel loss function that transforms the input’s rough loss landscape
017            into a benign one for the neural network parameters. This allows minimizing the
018
               loss to align with finding the global minimum of the energy landscape. We val-
               idate our method on high-dimensional global optimization tasks, demonstrating
019
               its ability to approximate global minima for energy landscapes with thousand-
020
               dimensional inputs.
021
022
023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Identifying ground-truth structures for biomolecules as well as atomic and molecular clusters is
026   essential for fields as diverse as drug discovery, materials science, and catalysis.
027
      Significant progress has been made with regards to the former with AlphaFold3. However, 75% of
028
      its training data relies exclusively on proteins. Thus, it lacks accuracy with regards to other types
029
      of biomolecules. For instance, it is not able to predict the RNA structure for RNAs from orphan
030   families (Zonta & Pantano, 2024).
031
032   For atomic and molecular clusters, the situation is even more dire. Training data is sparse, precluding
033
      the development of an AlphaFold-like model.
034   For both of these problems, additional ground-truth structures must be acquired experimentally, a
035   laborious process. However, we have access to ground-truth energy functions that are cheap to eval-
036   uate. For biomolecules, the AMBER force field can accurately model the energy of biomolecules
037   (He et al., 2020). With regards to atomic and molecular clusters, the ground-truth structure can be
038   found by minimizing a combination of lennard jones and coloumb potentials. This ground-truth
039
      structure, typically, is then used to warm-start a density functional theory calculation (Marques
      et al., 2017).
040
041   Instead of generalizing from existing training data, the holy grail would be to utilize the accurate,
042   cheap-to-query energy function to find the global minimum. This would sidestep the siloed accuracy
043   of existing models. This is the focus of the paper.
044
045   2   R ELATED W ORK
046
047   There are a total of three papers that attempt to tackle this problem via deep learning.
048
049   Firstly, Bihani et al. (2023) minimizes energy functions via reinforcement learning applied to graph
050
      neural networks. They are able to find points that match or slightly improve on the lowest energy
      points found by Adam. However, they do not include these points’ distance from the global mini-
051
      mum.
052
053   We reproduce one of their experiments in Appendix A.4, and find that their method does not pro-
      duce points that are closer to the global minimum than Adam. This is not surprising as the energy


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      values of a configuration are not correlated with the point’s distance from the global minimum. This
055   can be visualized on a small-scale by examining the dis-connectivity graph of the 38-dimensional
056   lennard jones function in Cameron (2014). There is significant variation in the energies of the local
057   minima. However, these energies are not correlated with the local minima’s distance from the global
058   minimum. The Adam optimizer, for instance, is able to substantially lower the energy value. How-
059   ever, its final configuration’s distance from the global minimum is not much better than randomly
060   sampling a point.
061   The second paper is Merchant et al. (2021), which utilizes a learned optimizer to find the global
062   minimum. However, due to rough loss landscapes with respect to their neural network parameters,
063   their model can’t even be trained via gradient descent. Instead, meta-gradients are estimated via
064   parameter perturbations. Thus, their method cannot find the global minimum of a problem with
065   more than 70 dimensions.
066
      The third paper is Zhao et al. (2022), which construct a new class of neural networks from lattice
067   functions, which are interpolated look-up tables. Their network’s unique global minimizer can be
068   extracted efficiently. However, due to limitations in expressivity, they demonstrate results on at most
069   61 dimensions.
070
      Thus, while this is a central problem, research remains limited. Furthermore, existing approaches
071
      do not appear to make progress in identifying the global minimum for functions beyond 100 dimen-
072
      sions.
073
074
075   3     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
076
077   The first step is to understand the nature of the problem. Are neural networks able to identify the
078   global minimum, and is it simply a problem of extraction? Or are neural networks, more fundamen-
079   tally, not able to identify the global minimum?
080   Zhao et al. (2022) indicates the problem is the latter. They extensively document how neural
081   networks are not able to identify the global minimum for toy problems. For instance, on the 4-
082   dimensional Rosenbrock function, a deep neural network trained with 10,000 points is not able to
083   identify the minimal point in the test set.
084
      Under the Neural Tangent Kernel assumption, neural networks behave like kernel regression models,
085
      interpolating between training points. As a result, these models are not well-suited to extrapolating
086   beyond the range of the training data. Thus, they are not well-suited to identifying global minimum.
087
088   This reveals a deeper issue: the minimal y-value of a global minimum is not, by itself, sufficient
089
      for its identification. Therefore, we need to exploit additional properties of the global minimum that
      neural networks can leverage.

      4.2     P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
124
125   We aim to minimize P (xf ) in the simplest manner possible. P (xf ) has four components: uniform
126   sampling of states xi , integral over all paths x(s) between xi and xf , identifying a path x(s), and
127   calculating the action for a given path.
128
      The first component, uniform sampling of states xi , is straightforward. We identify how to estimate
129
      the remaining three components in the three sections below.
130
131
      4.2.1    A PPROXIMATION F OR PATH I NTEGRAL
132
133   The above expression integrates over all paths between xi and xf . Since the action S[x(s)] is
134   weighted exponentially, in the limit of low temperature or high friction, the path integral is domi-
135   nated by the most probable path. Thus, we approximate this integral with the most probable path
136   between xi and xf .
137
138   4.2.2    F INDING PATH x(s) BETWEEN xi AND xf
139
140
      How do we derive the most probable path mentioned in the previous section?
141   For a given xf , we represent the optimal paths between any xi to the xf via a neural network that
142   contains no local minima, by construction. The xf is the global minimum of the neural network.
143
      Therefore, from a starting xi , one can perform gradient descent on the function learned by the neural
144   network to get the trajectory to xf .
145
146   The neural network contains no local minima by construction because it is a composition of an
147
      invertible network and a strictly convex function.
148
149   4.2.3    A PPROXIMATION F OR ACTION
150
      The action for a trajectory is proportional to the trajectory’s mean squared error deviation from
151   gradient descent. However, we instead penalize the mean squared error loss between the neural
152   network’s predicted y-values for the trajectory and the ground-truth energy values for the trajectory.
153   While this is not equivalent to penalizing the neural network’s gradient, we find that it works well in
154   practice.
155
156   4.3     T RAINING S ETUP T O M INIMIZE P (xf )
157
158   We utilize the three approximations above to construct a training scheme that minimizes P (xf ).
159
      First, we uniformly sample our initial starting points, xi .
160
161   Next, we find the most probable path x(s) between xi and the neural network’s learned global
      minimum, xf , by applying gradient descent steps to xi until we reach xf . This works because, as

163   with the approximations from Section 4.2.1 and Section 4.2.2.
164

166
      loss between the ground-truth energy values for the trajectory, U (x(s)), and the neural network’s

414   7   D ISCUSSION
415
416
417   With our method, we are able to identify the global minimum for high-dimensional (1000 dimen-
418   sions) tasks, with a per-dimension error 3-4%. This is not possible to do via other proposed methods
419   such as reinforcement learning (RL) or learned optimizers.
420   There are two reasons why this is the case.
421
422
      Firstly, RL and learned optimizers upweight states based on their energy values. However, the energy
      values and gradients of a configuration are largely determined by the configuration’s distance to its
423
      local minima. Thus, this is not a useful signal. By contrast, our method directly minimizes P (xf )
424
      from Equation 2, with respect to the neural network’s parameters θ, as opposed to x. Thus, we are
425   able to sidestep the dominance of the local minima.
426
427   Secondly, our method uses standard supervised learning. This is in contrast to RL and learned
428
      optimizers, which suffer from brittle loss landscapes, extreme hyperparameter sensitivity, and slow
      training times due to their unorthodox training schemes.
429
430   Due to these two innovations, our method enjoys the smooth, well-behaved loss landscapes that are
431   typical of neural network training. This can be seen in Figure 4, where the trajectory loss of our
      method steadily decreases.

      7.1     L EARNING S IGNAL
464
465
      7.1.1    E NERGY VALUES ARE A P OOR L EARNING S IGNAL
466
467
      Both RL and learned optimizers learn by prioritizing states with low energy values. However, as
468
      discussed above, a lower energy value has close to no relationship with whether a configuration is
469
      closer to the global optima. The number of local minima increases exponentially with dimension,
470   and the function is dominated by high-frequency components with large magnitudes. Thus, the
471   majority of signal in the gradient and variation in the energy values is driven by proximity to local
472   minima. However, proximity to local minima is nearly useless for identifying the global minimum.
473   Thus, the gradient update is not meaningful.
474
      A configuration’s energy value only becomes correlated with the signal of interest, the configura-
475
      tion’s distance from the global minimum, for configurations that are already close to the global
476   minimum. This can be visualized in the dis-connectivity graph of a 784-dimension lennard jones
477   function (de Souza & Wales, 2016).
478
479
480   7.1.2    O UR M ETHOD ’ S L EARNING S IGNAL
481
482   However, our method, by construction, directly minimizes P (xf ) from Equation 2. Note that this
483   minimization is direct. After the neural network’s parameters θ undergo a step of gradient descent,
484   the neural network function, fθ (x) more closely resembles the ground-truth energy function, U (x).
485   In contrast, a step of gradient descent for neural network parameters trained via learned optimization
      or reinforcement learning, only trains fθ (x) to identify local minima.

      Even in other domains where the reward is representative of the goal, RL and learned optimizers
518
      are notoriously difficult to train due to their high variance and reliance on back-propagating through
519
      long trajectories.
520
521   By contrast, our method utilizes standard supervised learning. Therefore, we are able to inherit its
522   benign loss landscapes.

      8     C ONCLUSION
525
526
527
      Our paper is the first to identify global minima for high-dimensional, non-convex optimization prob-
      lems. We identify global minima for 1000-D problems with a 3-4% per-dimension error rate. We
528
      are able to do so via exploiting a unique feature of biological global minima: regardless of initial
529
      state, there exists a path (with minor deviation from mean squared error) that connects the initial
530   state to the global minima. Our method avoids the scalability problems of previous approaches due
531   to two properties. Firstly, minimizing the loss with respect to the model parameters corresponds to
532   recovering the global minimum. Thus, we avoid the rough loss landscapes and proliferation of local
533   minima that arise when minimizing the energy function with respect to the input, x. Secondly, our
534   method harnesses standard supervised learning, inheriting its ease of optimization.
