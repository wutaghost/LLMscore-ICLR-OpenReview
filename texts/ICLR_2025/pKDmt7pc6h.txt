009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Traditional recommendation systems represent user preferences in dense repre-
012            sentations obtained through black-box encoder models. While these models of-
013            ten provide strong recommendation performance, they lack interpretability for
014            users, leaving users unable to understand or control the system’s modeling of their
015            preferences. This limitation is especially challenging in music recommendation,
016            where user preferences are highly personal and often evolve based on nuanced
017            qualities like mood, genre, tempo, or instrumentation. In this paper, we propose
018            an audio prototypical network for controllable music recommendation. This net-
019            work expresses user preferences in terms of prototypes representative of semanti-
020
               cally meaningful features pertaining to musical qualities. We show that the model
               obtains competitive recommendation performance compared to popular baseline
021
               models while also providing interpretable and controllable user profiles.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Modern recommender systems often rely on techniques such as collaborative filtering methods,
028   which represent users with opaque numerical embeddings that are difficult for their users to interpret
029   and not meant to control recommendations. While previous work aims to improve the scrutability
030   of such systems by using keyword tags or natural language summaries to describe user preferences,
031   such approaches are not universally applicable across domains. For instance, Siebrasse & Wald-
032
      Fuhrmann (2023) demonstrate that using broad genres to describe someone’s musical taste can be
      misleading, as users with similar genre profiles may still have vastly different preferences. Their
033
      study shows that sub-genres, more closely tied to specific artists and musical elements, provide a
034
      more accurate representation of individual taste.
035
036   In light of these challenges, our work focuses on capturing user preferences through listenable au-
037   dio clips, which transparently reflect the system’s inferred understanding of their musical tastes.
038
      This encoding makes the system’s assumptions more interpretable and empowers users by allowing
      them to fully scrutinize and correct their profiles, offering control over how their preferences are
039
      represented and over their proposed recommendations.
040
041   We introduce APRON: Audio PROtotypical Network for music recommendation, where prototypes
042   are listenable audio clips. We showcase the difference between a traditional recommendation system
043   and APRON in Figure 1. APRON draws inspiration from prototypical networks (e.g. ProtoPNET)
044
      (Chen et al., 2019; Donnelly et al., 2022; Willard et al., 2024; Zinemanas et al., 2021; Alonso-
      Jiménez et al., 2024; Heinrich et al., 2024), which are widely used in the Explainable AI (XAI)
045
      literature.
046
047   APRON leverages an attention mechanism to create a weighted combination of prototype represen-
048   tations of users’ historical interactions, ensuring an interpretable user representation. Furthermore,
049   by constraining the inferred prototype distribution to that of the recommended songs, we enable a
050
      fully steerable system, allowing users to scrutinize and adjust their profiles through simple modifi-
      cation of prototype weights.
051
052   We demonstrate that our proposed methodology significantly enhances the controllability of the
053   system’s recommendations while maintaining performance comparable to fully black-box models.
      To evaluate controllability, we simulate user updates to their profiles, such as adding or removing

      2   R ELATED W ORK ON S CRUTABLE R ECOMMENDER S YSTEMS
082
      Explainable recommendation has become an increasingly important topic as recommender systems
083
      grow more complex and opaque. Traditionally, explainability has been approached post-hoc, using
084
      explanations based on models’ features (Ning & Karypis, 2011; Shimizu et al., 2022; Vijayaragha-
085   van & Mohapatra, 2024; Wang et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020) or more recently on LLM-produced
086   explanations (Lubos et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024). However, as noted earlier, these explanations
087   might not be actionable by users or contain truthful information (Huang et al., 2023). On the
088   other hand, scrutable recommender systems present the model’s inferred user profile in a human-
089   understandable and editable manner, enabling user interventions to directly influence the system’s
090   recommendations. This enhances actionability and truthfulness by allowing users to make mean-
091   ingful changes that are transparently reflected in the system’s behavior. Although holding many de-
092   sirable properties, such systems have primarily been explored through the use of keywords or tags,
093
      which allow users to personalize their experience by selecting from a predefined collection (Green
      et al., 2009; Lubos et al., 2024; Moses & Babu, 2016; Balog et al., 2019). Unfortunately, represent-
094
      ing a user’s taste profile in this way can be limiting, as users may have to parse through an excessive
095
      collection of tags if they want to effectively customize their experience. More recently, scrutable
096   systems have shifted towards using natural language summaries to represent users, offering an al-
097   ternative to keyword-based personalization (Radlinski et al., 2022; Ramos et al., 2024). Instead of
098   relying on keywords/tags, these systems generate a personalized summary using natural text. While
099   this approach works well for domains suited to textual descriptions—such as movies, TV shows, or
100   restaurants—it may not translate as effectively to other domains, like music or fashion, where user
101   preferences might be difficult to express easily through text and could be better expressed through
102   other mediums, such as audio or images. This highlights the need for more flexible approaches that
103   can adapt scrutability to wider content types.
104   In this work, we address both limitations by enabling prototypes to attend to items in the user’s
105   history, allowing us to maintain scrutability while offering a more personalized experience. Ad-
106   ditionally, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that allows users to scrutinize their
107   recommendations using song-based prototypes, offering a much more suitable medium for music
      recommendations.

      5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
217
218   Dataset and Evaluation Protocol. We conduct our experiments with the MSD and follow the
219   same data preprocessing procedure as in Liang et al. (2018) which only keeps the users who at least
220   listened to 20 songs and the songs that are listened to by at least 200 users. Before this filtering stage
221   we also removed the songs from the dataset for which we do not have the audio files. Our dataset
222   consists of 40,940 songs, 469,432 train users, 50,000 validation users and 50,000 test users. We
223   conduct our evaluation in terms of strong generalization in which training, validation and test sets
224
      have disjoint users. We report the Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@100) as well
      as Recall (Recall@20, Recall@50)as they are the standard performance metrics in recommendation
225
      literature.
226
227   Tags and Prototype Generation. We select the prototypes to correspond to the 88 most commonly
228   used song-level tags according to the Last.fm Dataset (Bertin-Mahieux et al., 2011). Tags fall into 4
229   major groups: era, genre, mood, and instrumentation. To create listenable prototypes, we generate
230
      30-second long prototype songs using the MusicGen-Large-3.3B model (Copet et al., 2023) by
      querying “[TAG] song” (if the tag describes a quality of the song, e.g. 90s song, rock song) or “a
231
      song played by [TAG].” (if the tag describes instrumentation). Generated prototypes can be found
232
      in supplementary material.
233
234   Music Feature Extractor. We extract music features for each song in the dataset and prototype
235   songs with the MERT-v1-330M model (LI et al., 2024). For a song of an arbitrary length, MERT-
236
      v1-330M model outputs an encoding in the shape of (Time (T), Layers (N), Dimension (D)). We take
      the average over T axis to reduce the dimensionality to (N, D), which are (25, 1024) for the MERT-
237
      v1-330M model. We use the last representation layer in our experiments, which observed to give
238
      the best performance in terms of representation performance. In the end we have 1024 dimensional
239   feature representations for each song in the dataset and prototype songs.
240
241   Baselines. As baselines, we use MultiDAE, MultiVAE (Liang et al., 2018), RecVAE (Shenbin et al.,
242
      2020) and MacridVAE, SEM-MacridVAE (Wang et al., 2023) with our data split. We could not
      directly use the numbers from the corresponding papers as the version of the dataset does not contain
243
      the audio files for 200 songs audio files, and we have therefore run the baselines ourselves. For
244
      the MultiVAE, MultiDAE, MacridVAE and SEM-MacridVAE we use SEM-MacridVAE’s official
245   codebase and for the RecVAE we use RecVAE’s official codebase.
246

      5.5   E NCODING U NSEEN S ONGS
458   As we have mentioned in the introduction, a significant advantage that comes with APRON com-
459   pared to the other baselines is that, because APRON works with song features directly, it is able to
460   encode songs that were not previously seen during training (called cold-start items in the recom-
461   mendation systems).
462
      To evaluate the performance in the case where we encode unseen songs, we create an additional
463
      control set that consists of 16,575 users who have listened to 9,313 additional songs besides the
464   40,940 that were used to train APRON. We made sure that each of these new songs were listened to
465   at least 150 times. We have then created recommendations for these 16,575 users from the original
466   training set that consists of 40,940 songs.
467
      As other baselines work with a fixed item-set (that is, they require additional training to encode the
468
      new songs), we were not able to provide any baseline for this new evaluation set.
469
470   Results are presented in Table 5. As seen from the results, although we encode 9,313 new songs
471   that the system had not been exposed to earlier, we observe that in terms of Recall, our model is
472   able to remain competitive with the numbers reported in Table 1 for the baseline methods such as
473
      MultiDAE, MultiVAE, RecVAE and MacridVAE. We note that this experiment does not explicitly
      test for creating recommendations for unseen songs. However, we show here that the model is
474
      able to encode new songs. With a modification to the output loss function, such that at the output
475
      we calculate a similarity function (e.g. cosine similarity) between song embeddings and the user
476   embeddings, the model can be adapted to produce recommendations for new songs also.
477
478   This therefore showcases the potential for APRON to be used for zero-shot generalization to new
479
      songs, for providing recommendations on unseen songs without needing to retrain the system. More-
      over, we observe that APRON is still able to be similarly controllable when compared to the case
480
      where the testing is carried out on already seen songs.

      7   C ONCLUSIONS
509
510
      In this paper, we have proposed APRON, a prototypical network for music recommendations. Our
      experiments on the Million Song Dataset show that APRON can produce controllable recommen-
511
      dations (more controllable compared to SEM-Macrid VAE, for example) while maintaining com-
512
      petitive recommendation performance with other baselines. Moreover, APRON can encode unseen
513   songs; it is able to incorporate information from users who have listened to songs that had not been
514   listened to before and to produce recommendations. All in all, APRON is a new form of scrutable
515   recommendation system which directly exposes the user taste, paving the way for domain specific
516   scrutable models that captures the feature level information. As future work, we would like to also
517   apply APRON on other application domains where prototypes can be used to encode fundamental
518   notions pertaining to items (e.g. clothing recommendation).
