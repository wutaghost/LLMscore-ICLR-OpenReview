011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the growing demand for data and the increasing awareness of privacy, differen-
014            tially private learning has been widely applied in various deep models. Experiments
015            have observed several side effects of differentially private learning, including bad
016            learning features (performance), disparate impact, and worse adversarial ro-
017
               bustness that hurt the trustworthiness of the trained models. Recent works have
               expected pre-training to mitigate these side effects. It is valuable to theoretically
018
               understand the impact of differential privacy on the training process. However,
019
               existing theoretical research only explained parts of the phenomena and failed
020            to extend to non-convex and non-smooth neural networks. To fill this gap, we
021            propose a unified framework to explain all the above phenomena by studying the
022            feature learning process of differentially private stochastic gradient descent in
023            two-layer ReLU convolutional neural networks. By analyzing the test loss, we find
024            both its upper and lower bound decrease with feature-to-noise ratios (FNRs). We
025            then show that disparate impact comes from imbalanced FNRs among different
026            classes and subpopulation groups. Additionally, we show that the suboptimal
027            learned features and reduced adversarial robustness are caused by the randomness
028
               of privacy-preserving noise introduced into the learned features. Moreover, we
               demonstrate that pre-training cannot always improve the model performance, espe-
029
               cially with increased feature differences in the pre-training and fine-tuning datasets.
030
               Numerical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets validate our theoretical
031            analyses.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
      Modern deep learning models have achieved remarkable success in various applications, such
      as image classification (He et al., 2022) and natural language processing Vaswani et al. (2017).
038
      Many of these applications require training on datasets that contain sensitive private information.
039
      Differentially private learning, as an approach, seeks to train these models while ensuring rigorous
040   privacy guarantees (Abadi et al., 2016). A standard differentially private learning algorithm is
041   Differential Privacy Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) (Abadi et al., 2016), which injects noise
042   into network parameter updates during optimization.
043
      While DP-SGD provides robust privacy guarantees, it introduces side effects, including
044
045
            • Bad learned features (Tramer & Boneh, 2020): DP-SGD trained models might learn bad
046           features that are worse than handcrafted ones.
047
048         • Disparate impact (Bagdasaryan et al., 2019; Sanyal et al., 2022): DP-SGD trained models
049
              achieve different accuracy on different classes and different subpopulation groups.
050         • Worse adversarial robustness (Tursynbek et al., 2020): DP-SGD trained models might be
051           less adversarially robust than non-private models.
052
053   Moreover, recent work (De et al., 2022) has shown that pre-training improves the accuracy of
      DP-SGD trained models by 30% compared with training from scratch.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Understanding these phenomena is essential for deploying trustworthy, differentially private learning
055   systems. Although several studies have explored side effects such as disparate impact (Esipova et al.,
056   2022; Bagdasaryan et al., 2019), existing models are not applicable to ReLU neural networks due to
057   their inherent non-convex and non-smooth nature. Moreover, a unified theoretical framework that
058   explains all of the aforementioned phenomena is still lacking.
059
      Thus, there remains an open problem:
060
061     How to theoretically explain the aforementioned phenomena in DP-SGD trained ReLU neural
062                                  networks with a unified framework?
063
064   Facing the challenges in non-convex and non-smooth neural networks, we answer this problem in two
065   steps. First, we derive test loss bounds of two-layer ReLU Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)s
066   trained with DP-SGD in Section 3. Then, we extend the test loss analysis in Section 4 to explain the
067   aforementioned phenomena.
068
069   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
070
      In this section, we discuss the related works from two perspectives. Interested readers can refer to
071
      Appendix A for a detailed discussion.
072
073
      Analysis on differentially private learning side effects. After observing the side effects of
074
      differentially private learning, some researchers provided theoretical explanations. For example, Tran
075   et al. (2021) employed Taylor expansion to understand the disparate impact from the optimization
076   local loss landscape for twice differentiable loss functions. Sanyal et al. (2022) studied unfairness
077   in long-tailed data distribution in an asymptotic setting where the number of training data tends to
078   infinity. Zhang & Bu (2022) studied the adversarial robustness of private linear classifiers. However,
079   these analyses relied on some restricted assumptions that are not applicable to neural networks due to
080   their non-convex and non-smooth nature. In this paper, we provide a unified framework to explain
081   all the mentioned side effects by characterizing the feature learning process of DP-SGD trained
082   two-layer ReLU CNNs.
083
084
      Feature learning in neural networks. Feature learning in neural networks explores how neural
      networks learn data-related patterns during training, offering insights into phenomena such as
085
      momentum (Jelassi & Li, 2022), benign overfitting (Cao et al., 2022; Kou et al., 2023), adversarial
086
      training (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2022; Li & Li, 2023), and data augmentation Zou et al. (2023). In this work,
087   we make an initial step towards studying the generalization performance and effects in DP-SGD
088   trained two-layer neural networks.
089
090
      Although the convergence and feature learning process are analyzed in two-layer neural networks
      before, their approaches are not applicable to DP-SGD trained neural networks as the key properties
091
      for feature growth may not hold due to the perturbation of added noise. In this paper, we design new
092
      techniques to study the generalization performance of DP-SGD trained models.

      1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS AND M AIN R ESULTS
095
096   We provide a unified framework to explain the side effects in DP-SGD trained two-layer ReLU CNNs.
097   Specifically,
098
099          • We explore the feature learning process of DP-SGD trained neural networks. Considering
100            the technical challenges posed by non-convex and non-smooth ReLU CNN and the random
101            Differential Privacy (DP) noise, we propose a new proof technique to derive the standard
102
               and adversarial test loss bounds. The high-level idea is to use a piece-wise linear function to
               bound the non-linear loss function. We theoretically prove that the upper and lower bounds
103
               of test loss depend on data feature size and DP-SGD noise.
104
105          • We provide theoretical explanations for the side effects and the effectiveness of pre-training
106
               in DP-SGD trained neural networks.
107               – The noise from DP-SGD highly perturbs the learned activation patterns during itera-
                     tions, resulting in bad learned features (performance).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
                      – Three factors, i.e., the gradient clipping, the group or class sizes, and feature sizes,
109                     contribute to disparate impact in DP-SGD trained models by influencing the test loss
110                     within a given group or class.
111
                      – The DP-SGD trained models exhibit worse adversarial robustness because they learn
112                     non-robust, class-irrelevant features from the random DP noise.
113
                      – The fine-tuning performance on a pre-trained neural network decreases with increased
114                     feature difference. This implies that the benefits from pre-training may actually come
115                     from distribution overlaps between pre-training and fine-tuning datasets.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
532
533
      In this paper, we investigate the side effects of DP-SGD in two-layer ReLU CNNs. We show that the
534   side effects of DP-SGD trained CNNs depend on data’s feature, data noise, and privacy-preserving
535   noise. Our results uncover three sources of disparate impact: gradient clipping, data imbalance, and
536   feature disparity. In addition, we show that the privacy-preserving noise introduces randomness into
537   the learned features, leading to bad learned features and worse adversarial robustness. Moreover,
538   we demonstrate that the fine-tuning performance with pre-trained models decreases with increased
539   feature differences in the pre-training and fine-tuning datasets. Numerical results on both synthetic
      and real-world datasets validate our theoretical analyses.

      A    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
703
704
      Side effects in differentially private learning. Side effects of DP have been widely studied in deep
705
      learning literature. Disparate impact was initially observed in classification tasks Bagdasaryan et al.
706   (2019) and generative tasks (Ganev et al., 2022). Then, researchers have proposed several methods,
707   such as a regularization approach (Tran et al., 2021), re-weighting, and stratification methods (Esipova
708   et al., 2022; Rosenblatt et al., 2024) to mitigate the disparate impacts. A recent paper (Berrada et al.,
709   2023) showed that the DP models pre-trained with large datasets and fine-tuned with large batch
710   size can have marginal disparate effects. We discuss the implication of this work to pre-training in
711   Appendix ??.
712   The interplay between DP and fairness is also a widely studied topic. Cummings et al. (2019) showed
713   that exact fairness is not compatible with DP under the PAC learning setting. Sanyal et al. (2022)
714   showed that it is not possible to build accurate learning algorithms that are both private and fair when
715   data follows a specific kind of long-tailed distribution. Mangold et al. (2023) bounded the difference
716   in fairness levels between private and non-private models under the assumption that the confidence
717   margin is Lipschitz-continuous.
718   Some other side effects have also been studied. Tramer & Boneh (2020) showed that Differentially
719   Private Learning (DPL) may perform worse after bad feature learning compared with learning
720   handcraft features. Tursynbek et al. (2020) studied adversarial robustness in DPL and showed
721   that models trained by DPL may be more vulnerable compared with non-private models. Zhang &
722   Bu (2022) studied the adversarial robustness of private linear classifiers and showed differentially
723   privately fine-tuned pre-trained models may be robust under certain parameter settings. In addition,
724   Zhang et al. (2022) studied calibration of DPL and observed miscalibration across a wide range
725   of vision and language tasks. Bu et al. (2023) studied DPL with neural tangent kernel (NTK) and
726
      demonstrated that a large clipping threshold may benefit the calibration of DPL.
727   In these works, researchers have studied the convergence of DPL and explored explanations for the
728   aforementioned side effects. However, these analyses relied on some restricted assumptions that are
729   not applicable to neural networks because (1) differentially private neural networks training is not in
730   the NTK regime as the noise keeps the network parameter far away from the initialization during
731   training; (2) training loss of ReLU neural networks is non-convex and non-smooth, contradicting the
732
      assumptions in most analyses. In this work, we aim to overcome these challenges and explain the
      side effects of DP-SGD on a two-layer ReLU CNN.

821   In this section, we outline the main challenges in studying feature learning of DP-SGD on CNNs and
822   the key proof techniques employed to overcome the challenges.

824   F.1         C HALLENGE 1: N ON -S MOOTHNESS OF THE R E LU ACTIVATION F UNCTION

826   The first challenge arises from the non-smoothness of the ReLU activation function. Some existing
827

      However, this kind of approach is not applicable to ReLU neural networks.
