                                                      A BSTRACT
013
               We investigate the problem of learning operators between function spaces, focus-
014
               ing on the linear part of a layer in the Fourier Neural Operator architecture. First,
015
               we identify three main errors that occur during the learning process: statistical
016            error due to finite sample size, truncation error from finite rank approximation
017            of the operator, and discretization error from handling functional data on a finite
018            grid of domain points. Finally, we analyze a Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)
019            based least squares estimator, establishing both upper and lower bounds on the
020            aforementioned errors.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   In operator learning, the goal is to use statistical methods to estimate an unknown operator between
026
      function spaces. A primary application of operator learning is the development of fast data-driven
      methods to approximate the solution operator of partial differential equations (PDEs) (Li et al.,
027
      2021; Kovachki et al., 2023). For example, consider Poisson equations on Ω ⊂ R3 with Dirichlet’s
028
      boundary conditions:
029
030                 −∇2 w = v,            x ∈ Ω such that w(x) = 0 for all x ∈ boundary(Ω).
031   The function v is usually given and the goal is to map v to the solution w. It is well known (Boullé
032   & Townsend, 2023, Section 1) that the solution operator of this PDE is a linear operator L such that
033   w = Lv, where                          Z
034
                                  (Lv)(y) =     G(y, x) v(x) dx        ∀y ∈ Ω.
035                                               Ω
036   Here, G is the Green’s function of the Poisson equation.                         Given the training data
037   (v1 , w1 ), . . . , (vn , wn ), operator learning entails using statistical methods to estimate the solution
038   operator Lbn . Then, given a new input v, one can get the approximate solution w        b = Lbn v. The goal
039   is to develop the estimation rule such that w       b is close to the actual solution w = Lv under some
040   appropriate metric.
041
      Traditionally, given an input function v, one would use numerical methods such as finite differences
042   to get a numerical solution. The solver starts from scratch for every new function v of interest
043   and can be computationally slow and expensive. This can be limiting in some applications such
044   as engineering design where the solution needs to be evaluated for many different instances of the
045   input functions. To solve this problem, operator learning aims to learn surrogate models that sig-
046   nificantly increase speed for solution evaluation compared to traditional solvers while sacrificing a
047   small degree of accuracy.
048   In this work, rather than focusing on specific PDEs, we take a slightly broader viewpoint and carry
049   out statistical analysis of a data-driven approach to operator learning in general. We focus on the
050   linear layer of the influential Fourier Neural Operator (Li et al., 2021) architecture. We begin by
051   identifying the various types of errors incurred while learning these operators. For example, in
052   addition to the statistical error resulting from finite sample size, there is also a discretization error
053   in operator learning, as we deal with functional data that is available only on a finite grid of domain
      points. Ignoring high Fourier modes results in a truncation error. Finally, we analyze a Discrete

      Fourier Transform (DFT) based estimator, commonly used in practice, and provide both upper and
055   lower bounds on the various errors associated with this estimator.

      1.1   N EURAL O PERATORS
058
059   To formally define our problem setting, we need to introduce neural operators (Kovachki et al.,
060   2023). Unlike that of Poisson equations, the solution operators for most PDEs of interest are non-
061   linear. Neural operators model these nonlinear operators using specialized neural networks.
062   Let V be a vector space of functions from a bounded subset X ⊂ Rdin to Rp . Consider W to be a
063   vector space of functions from Y to Rq where Y ⊂ Rdout is also a bounded subset. Given a function
064   v ∈ V , a single layer of neural operator Nt : V → W is a mapping such that
065                                                                               Z
066    (Nt v)(y) = σ (Kθt v) (y) + bt (y)        ∀y ∈ Y where (Kθt v) (y) =         kθt (y, x) v(x) dx.
067                                                                                     X

068   The function bt : Y → Rq is a bias function in W, the function σ : Rq → Rq is a point-wise
069   non-linear activation, and the transformation v 7→ Kθt v is a integral kernel transform of v using
070
      some kernel kθt : Y × X → Rq×p . Finally, the neural operator is a map v 7→ N v where N v =
      Q◦NT . . .◦N1 ◦P(v). Here, P and Q are lifting and projection operators that can be used to change
071
      the dimension of the functions.
072
073   Parametrizing Kθt in terms of kθt can be impractical due to the computational cost of calcu-
074   lating the integral in for each layer. Thus, a significant area of research in neural operators
075   focuses on developing innovative parametrizations of Kθt that facilitate more efficient computation.
076
      One such parametrization gives rise to a well-known architecture called the Fourier Neural Operator.

      1.2   F OURIER N EURAL O PERATOR (FNO)
080   We consider the setup from the work of Li et al. (2021). Let X = Y = Td be a d-dimensional peri-
081   odic torus. In this work, we will identify Td by [0, 1]d with periodic boundary conditions (Grafakos
082   et al., 2008, Chapter 3). Assume the kernel kθ is translation invariant–that is, kθ (y, x) = kθ (y − x).
083   This implies that Kθ is a convolution operator. In particular, we have Kθ v = kθ ⋆ v (see Section 3
084   for more detail). Then, the Convolution Theorem implies that
085

087   where F and F −1 are Fourier and Inverse Fourier transform respectively. The key insight in FNO
088   is that instead of parametrizing the kernel kθ , we parametrize its Fourier transform F(kθ ) directly.
089   That is, we parametrize the kernel transform operator as
090
091                                         Kβ v = F −1 Λβ F(v) .
092
      This is a linear operator and will be referred to as Fourier linear operator. When |Λβ (m)|ℓ1 < ∞,
093   we can write this as

      There are two practical challenges in implementing the operator Kβ . First, the implementation
098
      involves an infinite sum over Zd . Second, the Fourier transform Fv cannot be computed exactly
099   since the function v is only available on a finite grid of domain points. To address the first challenge,
100   a large K ∈ N is fixed and we sum only over m ∈ Zd such that |m|ℓ∞ ≤ K. The second challenge is
101   addressed by approximating Fv using the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) of v over the finite grid
102   of domain points, which can be efficiently computed using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithms.
103   The solution to the second challenge motivates our DFT-based least-squares estimator.

134   1.4   R ELATED W ORKS
135
136   After Li et al. (2021) proposed Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs), there has been a surge of interest
137   in this architecture. The number of applied works is too vast and not entirely relevant to list here, so
138   we will focus on related theoretical works. One of the earliest theoretical analyses of FNOs was the
139   universal approximation result by Kovachki et al. (2021).
140   More closely related to our work is a recent study on the sample complexity of various operator
141   classes, including FNOs, by Kovachki et al. (2024a). Their scope is broader than ours as they
142   address a general class of nonlinear operators. However, their results do not imply ours. They
143   treat the truncation parameter K as a part of the model rather than a variable that the learning
144   algorithm can choose. Their error bounds are based on metric entropy analysis, which leads to a
145   suboptimal dependence on K and the input dimension d. Specifically, their bounds break down
146   as K → ∞ and suffer from the curse of dimensionality in d. In contrast, our work establishes
147
      statistical error bounds using sharp Rademacher analysis, avoiding both dependence on K and the
      curse of dimensionality in d. An interesting future direction is to extend our Rademacher-based
148
      analysis to capture function classes at the level of generality considered in Kovachki et al. (2024a).
149
      Finally, we note that Rademacher-based analysis has also been used by (Raman et al., 2024; Tabaghi
150   et al., 2019) to study Schatten operators between Hilbert spaces. Kim & Kang (2024) also bound the
151   Rademacher complexity of FNOs, but their results have issues as the implied generalization bounds
152   do not always vanish.
153
      A recent work by Lanthaler et al. (2024) aligns with our goal of quantifying the discretization error
154
      of FNOs, and some of our proof techniques are inspired by their work. However, the nature of
155
      their results differs from ours. To discuss the difference precisely, let Ψ be a trained Fourier Neural
156   Operator and v be an input function available to the learner only over a discrete grid of domain points
157   of size N . Denote v N as the set of discrete values of v available to the learner. Lanthaler et al. (2024)
158   bound the term ∥Ψv − Ψv N ∥, quantifying the error incurred in the forward pass due to the function
159   being available only over a discrete grid. Essentially, this only captures errors incurred during the
160   test time but does not quantify the discretization error incurred during training. In contrast, our work
161   focuses on quantifying how the error propagates to the trained operator and its evaluation during the
      test time because the training data is only available over a discrete grid.

162
      Finally, we also note that our setup is closely related to the function-to-function regression often
163   studied in the functional data analysis (FDA) literature. For example, the linear layer of a neural op-
164   erator v 7→ Kv + b is a well-studied model in FDA (Wang et al., 2016, Equation 15). Even a single
165   layer of a neural operator v 7→ σ (Kv + b) has been examined in FDA literature as multi-index func-
166   tional models (Wang et al., 2016, Equation 13), (Chen et al., 2011). That said, the overall goal of the
167   FDA differs slightly from that of operator learning. In FDA, the focus is on statistical inference, typ-
168   ically using RKHS-based frameworks under some assumptions about the data-generating process.
169   As a result, FDA methods often do not always scale to large datasets. In contrast, operator learning
170   primarily aims at prediction, seeking to develop surrogate models that approximate numerical PDE
171
      solvers (Li et al., 2021; Kovachki et al., 2024b). The emphasis is on creating computationally effi-
      cient methods that can be used to train large models and handle large datasets. However, we believe
172
      that the intersection of these two fields can benefit both. The theoretical tools developed in FDA
173
      literature over more than 40 years can be applied to the analysis of operator learning methods, while
174   the computational advances in operator learning can help scale FDA methods.

312   In this section,  we establish excess risk bounds of learning the operator class {v 7→
313   F −1 Λβ F(v) : β ∈ B}, where B is some parameter space. Here, we only consider the case
314   where V, W ⊆ L2 (Td , R). This is different from the usual setting in the literature, where V and
315   W are Banach spaces of vector-valued functions. First, a significant number of PDEs of practical
316   interest describe how scalar-valued functions evolve. Since not much is known from a theoretical
317   standpoint even for scalar-valued functions, we believe that this is a good start. Second, assuming
318   V, W to be a subset of L2 (a Hilbert space) does not result in any meaningful loss of generality from
319   a practical standpoint. In practice, one must discretize the domain and work with function values
320
      over a discrete grid, which effectively requires a bounded domain. This essentially means working
      with bounded functions on a bounded domain, all of which are L2 integrable.

519   5    D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521   In this work, we  established the excess risk error bounds of learning the core linear layer v 7→
522   F −1 Λβ F(v) of Fourier neural operators. A natural future  direction    is to extend these results
523   to single layer Fourier neural operator, v 7→ σ F −1 Λβ F(v) + b and then to multiple layers.
524   Although simple metric entropy-based analysis gives a bound on statistical error even for single layer
525   neural operator, such a bound is vacuous when K → ∞. It would be interesting to see if we can get
526   a meaningful statistical rate even at the limit of K → ∞. One can view K as  an analog    of the width
      of traditional neural networks. Thus, analysis of v 7→ σ F −1 Λβ F(v) + b as K → ∞ can

527
528   lead to an analog of infinite width and neural tangent kernel theory (Jacot et al., 2018) for operator
529   learning. These insights will help us better understand width vs depth tradeoffs in operator learning.
530   For discretization error, we consider the setup where the training data is available on a grid of size
531   N d but the trained operator is evaluated at full resolution (N → ∞). It would be interesting to study
532   the discretization error when the training data is available at resolution N1 , but the trained operator is
533   evaluated at resolution N2 . Such a theory would formalize the multi-resolution generalization (oper-
534   ators trained at lower resolution have good generalization even when evaluated in higher resolution)
535   observed in practice (see (Li et al., 2021, Section 5)).
536   Finally, with PDEs as an application, it is unclear if the iid-based statistical model is the right
537   framework for operator learning. For instance, Boullé et al. (2023) show that an active learning
538   approach for data collection and training for solution operators of elliptic PDEs yields exponential
539   error decay with increasing sample size. Therefore, an important future direction is to define the
      appropriate active learning model and develop active algorithms for operator learning.

1364
       operator for this distribution, it directly controls the average smoothness of the samples v. For our
1365
