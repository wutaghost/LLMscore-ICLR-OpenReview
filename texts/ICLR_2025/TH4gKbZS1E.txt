009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we compare the performance of Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
012            (KAN) and Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) networks on irregular or noisy func-
013            tions. We control the number of parameters and the size of the training samples to
014            ensure a fair comparison. For clarity, we categorize the functions into six types:
015            regular functions, continuous functions with local non-differentiable points, func-
016            tions with jump discontinuities, functions with singularities, functions with coher-
017            ent oscillations, and noisy functions. These features are typically not available as
018            prior knowledge in real applications; therefore, we do not specifically select the
019
               corresponding network structure for each function. Our experimental results indi-
               cate that KAN does not always perform best. Furthermore, increasing the size of
020
               training samples can improve performance to some extent. When noise is added
021
               to functions, the irregular features are often obscured by the noise, making it chal-
022            lenging for both MLP and KAN to extract these features effectively. We hope
023            these experiments provide valuable insights for future neural network research
024            and encourage further investigations to overcome these challenges.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Since its launch, Kolmogorov-Arnold networks (KAN)(Liu et al., 2024b) has garnered significant
030   attention. These networks utilize the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem, which posits that
031   any multivariate continuous function can be expressed as a combination of continuous univariate
032   functions and addition. Unlike conventional Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) networks, KANs incor-
033   porate learnable activation functions. According to (Liu et al., 2024b), this feature provides KANs
034   with enhanced interpretability and accuracy over MLPs.
035
      Noting the release of KAN 2.0 (Liu et al., 2024a), we will conduct all experiments using the latest
036   pykan version (v0.2.6) to leverage its optimizations. KAN 2.0 introduced multiplication nodes,
037   which significantly enhance the fitting of multivariate functions, especially those involving direct
038   multiplication or division of independent variables. However, the improvement is minimal for the
039   functions used in this paper, so lower versions of Pykan are also acceptable.
040
      Numerous investigations into KAN applications have rapidly surfaced, covering areas such as smart
041
      energy grid optimization(Wang et al., 2024)(Tang et al., 2024), chemistry data analysis(Wang et al.,
042   2024)(Li et al., 2024b), image classification(Cheon, 2024)(Teymoor Seydi, 2024)(Igali & Shamoi,
043   2024), deep function learning(Zhang, 2024), quantum architecture search(Kundu et al., 2024), medi-
044   cal image analysis and processing(Li et al., 2024a)(Chen et al., 2024), disease risk predictions(Dong,
045   2024), graph learning tasks(Kiamari et al., 2024)(Li, 2024)(Ghaith Altarabichi, 2024), asset pricing
046   models(Wang & Singh, 2024), 3D object detection (in autonomous driving)(Lai et al., 2024), senti-
047   ment analysis(Lawan et al., 2024), and deep kernel learning(Zinage et al., 2024).
048   On the contrary, a growing body of research has highlighted the imperfections of KANs compared
049   to MLPs. For example, (Zhang, 2024) and (Shen et al., 2024) noted the vulnerability of KANs to
050   noise, indicating that even minor noise can lead to a significant rise in test loss. Additionally, (Tran
051   et al., 2024) claimed that KANs do not outperform MLPs in highly complex datasets and require
052   considerably more hardware resources. Furthermore, (Yu et al., 2024) noted that MLPs generally
053   have higher accuracy than KANs across various standard machine learning tasks, with the exception
      of tasks involving symbolic formula representation.

      Moreover, the performance of networks may be influenced by the regularity of functions, prompting
055   this study to compare the performance of KAN and MLP, across distinct types of functions with

      In this investigation, we assess the performance of MLP and KAN in modeling irregular or noisy
      functions. To ensure fairness, we control the number of parameters and the amount of training data.

060
      functions. This research continues directly and naturally from our recent study on the efficacy of
061   KANs in fitting noisy functions (Shen et al., 2024).

064

065
      regular and irregular functions. Section 4 introduces noise to the previously utilized functions and
066
      continues the comparison between MLP and KAN. Finally, Section 5 summarizes the findings of
067   our experiments.

      2     KOMOGOROV-A RNOLD T HEOREM AND KAN S
071
      The Kolmogorov-Arnold theorem pertains to expressing multivariable continuous functions. Ac-
072
      cording to the theorem, any continuous function involving multiple variables can be expressed
073
      as a combination of continuous single-variable functions and addition (Kolmogorov, 1956) (Kol-
074   mogorov, 1957) (Arnold, 1957). Formally, it can be stated as:
075
      Theorem 1. [Kolmogorov-Arnold Theorem] Let f : [0, 1]n → R be any multivariate continuous
076
      function, there exist continuous univariate functions ϕi and ψij such that:

082   Leveraging the Kolmogorov-Arnold theorem, KANs introduce a novel neural network architecture.
083   Unlike traditional Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) which use fixed activation functions, KANs
084
      employ learnable activation functions. This methodology is theoretically advantageous in enhancing
      the adaptability of KANs across different datasets and applications.
085
086   Unfortunately, Theorem 1 was originally proven non-constructively, lacking a constructive proof

088   theless, challenges emerge when handling functions exhibiting irregular patterns, which mathemat-

098
      do not specifically select the corresponding network structure for each function. We implement the

099
      functions with singularities or coherent oscillations, which might need more training samples and
100
      iterations, we also investigate the Adam optimizer’s capability.

      3.2   C ONTINUOUS FUNCTIONS WITH POINTS WHERE DERIVATIVES DO NOT EXIST
177
178   The functions f3 and f4 serve as prime examples of this category. They maintain continuity at all
179   points, while they are non-differentiable at x = 0.
180
      The outcomes are illustrated in Figure 2. For these particular functions, the KAN’s performance is
181
      worse than the MLP’s. Despite the MLP’s slower convergence, it eventually reaches a lower test
182   loss. Additionally, it can be noted that amplifying the training sample size marginally enhances
183   the performance of both networks. However, in the vicinity of the non-differentiable point, the
184   MLP shows more significant improvement than the KAN. More visually, it is evident that the fitting
185   performance of MLP and KAN around x = 0 (the non-differentiable point) is approximately the
186   same. Yet, with a larger training sample size, the MLP demonstrates superior fitting performance
187   near x = 0 compared to the KAN.
188
189   3.3   F UNCTIONS WITH JUMP
190
191   The examples of this category include f5 and f6 . These functions have jump discontinuities at
192   x = ±0.5, where the function values abruptly change between 0 and 1. The experimental outcomes
193   for these functions are depicted in Figure 3. Results show that the MLP outperforms the KAN.
194   Moreover, expanding the training dataset size can enhance both networks’ performance to a certain
195
      extent. Nevertheless, KAN consistently fails to match the performance of MLP.
196
197   3.4   F UNCTIONS WITH SINGULARITIES
198
199   Functions possessing singularities display distinct behaviors, marked by a rapid change rate as they
200
      near these points, with their first derivative tending towards infinity at the singularity. Additionally,
      for any chosen continuous interval that omits these singularities, the functions remain continuous
201
      and differentiable across the interval.
202
203   To avoid division by zero and guarantee clear fitting results, the ranges of the functions f7 and f8
204   are limited to [0.001, 1] and [−0.999, 0.999], respectively. We examined the effects of the training
205   sample size, the number of Epochs, and the selection of optimizer on the fitting performance. As
206
      illustrated in Figure 4, simply enlarging the sample size by itself does not substantially enhance the
      performance when recovering f7 and f8 .
207
208   Pykan offers two optional optimizers: Adam and L-BFGS. As depicted in Figure 5, L-BFGS
209   achieves faster convergence, whereas networks with Adam converges to a lower test loss. How-
210   ever, it is crucial to recognize that with a fixed learning rate, the improvement from this strategy
211   is naturally constrained. As illustrated in Table 3, for an identical number of epochs, the training
212
      duration of the network with the L-BFGS optimizer is frequently several times greater than with the
      Adam optimizer.
213
214   Drawing from earlier experiments and findings, the fitting tests will utilize the Adam optimizer set
215   with a learning rate of 0.01. As shown in Figure 6, KAN outperformed MLP in terms of fitting
      functions with singularities at the same number of epochs.

222   In the experimental phase, taking a similar approach as described in section D. As shown in Figure

224   within the KAN network framework, the optimizer L-BFGS outperformed Adam for function f9 ,

      4     C OMPARISON ON N OISY FUNCTIONS
233
      In the following, we discuss the roles of noisy functions. We introduce noise to functions previously
234
      discussed and proceed to evaluate the performance of MLP and KAN. According to the conclusions
235   drawn in the preceding section, we will classify the functions into three categories: regular functions,
236   functions with localized irregularities, and functions with severe discontinuities.
237
238   4.1   A DDING NOISE TO R EGULAR FUNCTIONS
239
240   We introduce noise to functions exhibiting strong regularity, and subsequently fit these noisy data
241   using KAN and MLP. The experimental outcomes are depicted in Figure 9. Our observations indi-
242   cate that KAN achieves a lower test loss with low noise levels but performs worse under high noise
243   conditions. When comparing the function fitting effect, the conclusion remains consistent: MLP
244   shows better performance with minor noise interference, but KAN rapidly outperforms MLP as the
245
      training sample size increases.
246
247
      4.2   A DDING NOISE TO FUNCTIONS WITH LOCALIZED IRREGULARITIES
248
      Noise is subsequently added to f3 , f4 , f5 , and f6 . The experimental findings are shown in Figure
249   10. For f3 and f4 , the network can still capture some of the irregular features with a larger training
250   sample. However, for f5 and f6 , both KAN and MLP perform poorly. The networks still have
251   difficulty identifying the jump discontinuities, even with an increased sample size.
252
253   4.3   A DDING NOISE TO FUNCTIONS WITH SEVERE DISCONTINUITIES
254
255   Figure 11 shows that KAN’s performance surpasses that of MLP when noise is added to functions
256   with singularities or coherent oscillation. Interestingly, from the perspective of test loss, the impact
257   of noise on fitting such functions is minimal. This phenomenon highlights the ineffectiveness of
258
      strategies relying solely on increased sampling rates in such instances.
259
260   5     C ONCLUSION
261
262   In this study, we evaluate the effectiveness of KAN and MLP in approximating irregular or noisy
263   functions. Our analysis concentrates on two main factors: the relative performance of KAN and
264   MLP in fitting functions with different types according to regularity, and their ability to handle noise
265   during the fitting process.
266   Firstly, as identified in (Shen et al., 2024) and additionally explored in this study, raising the sam-
267   pling rate is a potent method to enhance the fitting performance of functions f1 − f6 . Particularly,
268   this strategy shows greater advantages when handling noisy data versus clean data. Nevertheless,
269   the improvement in the fitting accuracy for functions with low regularity (f7 − f10 ) is minimal,
      irrespective of the presence of noise.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Secondly, we also compared the fitting performance under varying Epochs from two distinct per-
271   spectives: convergence speed and stabilized test loss. KAN exhibits a faster convergence rate than
272   MLP across all tested functions. However, MLP outperforms KAN on test functions f3 − f6 on
273   stabilized test loss.
274
      Thirdly, via experimental analysis (fitting f7 − f10 ), it was observed that Adam exceeded L-BFGS
275
      in performance for both networks in every instance, except for function f9 . Notably, when fitting
276
      function f9 with the KAN, L-BFGS demonstrated better results than Adam.
277
278   At last, when dealing with noisy functions, KAN exhibits superior performance over MLP for reg-
279   ular functions or functions with severe discontinuities. Conversely, for functions with localized
280
      irregularities, MLP outperforms KAN.
