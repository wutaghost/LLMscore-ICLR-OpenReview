009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Explicit regularization and implicit bias are often studied separately, though in
012            practice, they act in tandem. However, their interplay remains poorly understood.
013            In this work, we show that explicit regularization modifies the behavior of implicit
014            bias and provides a mechanism to control its strength. By incorporating explicit
015            regularization into the mirror flow framework, we present a general approach to
016            better understand implicit biases and their potential in guiding the design of op-
017
               timization problems. Our primary theoretical contribution is the characterization
               of regularizations and reparameterizations that induce a time-dependent Bregman
018
               function, with a discussion of the implications of its temporal variation. Impor-
019
               tantly, our framework encompasses single-layer attention, and application to sparse
020            coding. Extending beyond our core assumptions, we apply this framework to LoRA
021            finetuning, revealing an implicit bias towards sparsity.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Regularization is a fundamental technique in machine learning that helps control model complexity,
027   prevent over-fitting and improve generalization (Kukačka et al., 2017). There are various ways
028   to regularize a model (Santos & Papa, 2022), including weight decay, the lasso penalty, dropout,
029   initialization strategies, early stopping, model constraints, and the introduction of noise. In this paper,
030
      we focus on two major categories of regularization: explicit regularization and implicit bias, as well
      as their interaction. We introduce both concepts within a general minimization problem. Consider
031
      the objective function f : Rn → R to be minimized with respect to x:
032
033                                                    min f (x).                                            (1)
                                                     x∈Rn
034
035   In the context of explicit regularization, a penalty term h(x) is incorporated into the objective function,
036   directly modifying the learning algorithm to prevent overfitting (Goodfellow et al., 2016), as follows:
037                                             min f (x) + αh(x).                                           (2)
038                                             x∈Rn

039   This approach constrains the model’s complexity and encourages simpler solutions that are more
040   likely to generalize well to new, unseen data (Tian & Zhang, 2022). Common explicit regularization
041   methods include L1 (LASSO) and L2 (Weight decay) regularization (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006).
042   The effectiveness of explicit regularization techniques has been demonstrated across various machine
043   learning paradigms (Arpit et al., 2016), including supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and
044
      reinforcement learning.
045   Implicit bias (Gunasekar et al., 2018), can be considered as an inherent aspect of the model design
046   that does not require explicit modifications to the objective function. The goal of characterizing the
047   implicit bias is to understand how overparameterization impacts the training dynamics and, thus,
048   model selection. For example, in the presence of many global minima, optimization algorithms like
049   gradient descent inherently guide the solution toward specific global minima that enjoy some type of
050
      low norm property (Pesme et al., 2021).
051   Consequently, the learned model’s properties, including its generalization performance, are signif-
052   icantly influenced by the choice of model. For example, (Stochastic) gradient descent provably
053   converges to the solution with the lowest L1 distance from the initialisation for overparameterized
      least-squares regression (Pesme et al., 2021).

054
      Such an implicit bias is often associated with a mirror flow (Karimi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022),
055   which results from a reparameterization of f by setting x = g(w), where w ∈ M and M is a smooth
056   manifold. Note the fundamental difference explicit regularization in the original space and the mirror
057   flow with the objective function:
058
                                            min f (g(w)) + αh(w).                                        (3)
059                                          w∈M
060   The explicit regularizer h acts on the parameters w and not x = g(w). Our main goal is to understand
061   how the explicit regularization αh(w) affects the implicit bias and thus the effective regularization in
062   the original parameter space x. While both explicit regularization and the mirror flow framework
063   have been extensively studied independently, the goal of this paper is to analyze their interplay and to
064   show how the explicit regularization affects the implicit bias by integrating explicit regularization
065
      into the mirror flow framework. This integration will allow us to gain valuable insights into different
      problems like sparse coding, attention, and LoRA. Since the nature and strength of implicit bias are
066
      usually constant throughout training and inherently determined by the reparameterization, they can
067
      sometimes degrade performance or simply not fit to a learning task. As we show, however, they can
068   be adapted and controlled by explicit regularization, which induces a time dependent mirror flow. In
069   previous work, it has been shown that overparametrization leads to lower-rank solutions or L1 bias
070   (Arora et al., 2019; Pesme et al., 2021; Vasudeva et al., 2024), which reveals a bias towards sparsity
071   in particular settings. Nevertheless, factors such as finite learning rates and noise can obscure this
072   sparsity bias. In this work, we demonstrate that explicit regularization offers a mechanism to control
073   this bias effectively. Specifically, it modulates the sparsity bias via the time-dependent mirror flow.
074   More generally, we aim to integrate explicit regularization into the mirror flow framework, thereby
075   unifying these two concepts. We provide sufficient conditions for the reparameterization g and explicit
076   regularization h similar to (Li et al., 2022), to analyze the resulting optimization problem within the
077   extended mirror flow framework and obtain convergence results. Additionally, we characterize the
078   regularization h in terms of g to understand their interplay and impact on the Legendre function,
079   which can be associated with the implicit bias. Concretely, we identify three distinct effects:
080
      • Type of bias: the explicit regularization changes the shape of the Legendre function. For example,
081
        the shape changes from an L2 norm to L1 norm.
082
083
      • Positional bias: the explicit regularization shifts the global minimum of the Legendre function. For
        the standard Legendre function, the global minimum corresponds to the network’s initialization
084
        (Li et al., 2022). During training, the explicit regularization moves the minimum closer to zero.
085
086   • Range shrinking: the explicit regularization shrinks the range of the attainable values for the
087
        Legendre function. For example, the L1 norm of the network parameters becomes fixed during
        training.
088
089   The effects are illustrated in Figure.1. We further analyze the importance of explicit regularization and
090   its effect on implicit bias through multiple experiments, including sparse coding, attention mechanisms
091   in transformers, and LoRA. In the latter two cases, we observe that large weight decay leads to rank
092   collapse. Additionally, our findings also suggest a strategy to mitigate performance degradation
093   resulting from this collapse. While (Dai et al., 2021) and (Khodak et al., 2022) have studied the
094   effect of constant explicit regularization on the representation cost and quadratic reparameterizations,
095
      we are concerned with the effect of both constant or dynamic explicit regularization on the implicit
      bias. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
096
097   • We provide sufficient conditions for incorporating different types of explicit regularization into
098     the mirror flow framework and characterize their effect, focusing on three key impacts on the
099     implicit bias: positional bias shift, type of bias, and range shrinking, which can pose challenges for
100     trainability.
101   • We propose a systematic procedure for identifying these regularizations and establish a general
102     convergence result for the framework, which suggests how to overcome the above challenges by
103     changing the explicit regularization.
104   • We highlight the effects of the regularization and the resulting implicit bias in experiments such as
105     sparse coding, attention in transformers, and LoRA fine-tuning in large language models.
106
      • Particularly, we obtain the insight that weight decay controls the sparsification strength induced by
107     quadratic reparameterizations such as attention and LoRA.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
126
      Regularization There are multiple ways of regularizing training in supervised learning. Some of the
127
      most widely used techniques include weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1991), data augmentation (Cubuk
128
      et al., 2020; Orvieto et al., 2023), dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), and batch normalization (Ioffe &
129   Szegedy, 2015). Weight decay, or L2 regularization, discourages large weights to reduce overfitting.
130   Data augmentation enhances the diversity of training examples by applying random transforma-
131   tions—such as rotations, flips, and crops—to the input data, helping neural networks to generalize
132   better. Dropout randomly deactivates a subset of neurons during each iteration, simulating ensemble
133   learning by creating multiple network configurations. Finally, batch normalization normalizes the
134   inputs to each layer in a mini-batch by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation,
135   ensuring that inputs are consistently centered and scaled during training.
136
137   Implicit bias The implicit bias is a well-studied phenomenon (Woodworth et al., 2020; Gunasekar
138   et al., 2017a; 2020; Li et al., 2022), which has primarily been characterized within the mirror
139
      flow framework, a well-established concept in convex optimization (Alvarez et al., 2004; Beck &
      Teboulle, 2003; Rockafellar & Fenchel, 1970; Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2009), which we extend by
140
      explicit regularization that can induce a time-dependent Bregman function. A mirror flow can be
141
      interpreted as a gradient flow on a Riemannian manifold (Li et al., 2022; Alvarez et al., 2004),
142   which has also been derived for stochastic gradient descent (Pesme et al., 2021; Even et al., 2023).
143   The study of discrete versions (Sun et al., 2022) has led to novel algorithmic designs (Raj & Bach,
144   2021; González et al., 2024; Azizan et al., 2022). Time-dependent mirror descent, however, is
145   largely underexplored, except for an analysis of some of its intrinsic properties and an application to
146   continuous sparsification (Radhakrishnan et al., 2021; Jacobs & Burkholz, 2024).
147
148   Applications of the mirror flow framework The mirror flow framework has been applied to
149   various architectures, including attention mechanisms in transformers (Vaswani, 2017; Vasudeva
150   et al., 2024; Sheen et al., 2024), matrix factorization (Li et al., 2021; Gunasekar et al., 2017b; 2020)
151
      and diagonal linear networks (Li et al., 2022; Pesme et al., 2021; 2024; Woodworth et al., 2020). For
      deep matrix factorization, implicit bias has also been studied using gradient flow methods (Marion &
152
      Chizat, 2024; Arora et al., 2019). These studies indicate that the flow tends to be implicitly biased
153
      toward solutions with lower rank or low nuclear norms. We demonstrate that explicit L2 regularization
154   further enhances its strength, for example in the context of quadratic overparameterization. This is
155   illustrated through experiments on transformer networks. Moreover, we identify the inherent bias of
156   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021; Wan et al., 2024) and delve into the challenges that
157   are associated with it. This is especially of interest, as LoRA has gained significant popularity in the
158   field of large language models (LLMs) as it allows for cost-effective finetuning.
159
160   Sparse coding Sparse coding (SC) is a powerful representation technique widely employed in
161   signal processing and pattern recognition (Zhang et al., 2015). It seeks to represent observations as
      a linear combination of fundamental elements, termed atoms, which collectively form a dictionary.


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The core principle of SC is to achieve a sparse representation by imposing constraints, typically
163   using the L0 -norm. However, this formulation leads to an NP-hard problem (Tropp, 2004). An
164   alternative strategy relaxes the constraint to the L1 -norm, transforming the original problem into a
165   convex, albeit non-smooth optimization task. Proximal algorithms have proven effective to solve
166   these non-smooth problems (Daubechies et al., 2004). Furthermore, convolutional sparse coding
167   (CSC) extends SC by modeling the dictionary as a concatenation of circulant matrices. Notably, CSC
168   has a strong connection with modern convolutional neural networks (CNNs), where the forward pass
169   of a CNN can be viewed as a thresholding pursuit for a multi-layer CSC model (Papyan et al., 2017).
170   This relationship provides valuable insights into the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs and their
171
      connection to sparse representation techniques.

520   6                 D ISCUSSION
521
522   We have provided a framework to analyze how explicit regularization affects implicit bias by inte-
523
      grating it into the mirror flow framework, which has led to a novel geometric interpretation of the
      interplay between explicit regularization and implicit bias. We have identified sufficient conditions for
524
      incorporating these regularizations and characterized their effects on the dynamics, notably positional
525
      bias, type of bias, and range shrinking. Additionally, we have established a systematic procedure
526   for identifying suitable regularizations for given parameterizations and established convergence
527   within our framework. We have also illustrated the implications of our theory in the context of
528   sparse coding, attention in transformers, and LoRA fine-tuning. We found that the type of bias can
529   change dynamically during training, for example, from L2 to L1 , as observed in our experiments.
530   Accordingly, the geometry of the training dynamics changes as described in Eq. (7). This is associated
531   with a time-dependent Legendre function, which might be of independent interest conceptually. Our
532   findings could have implications also for other regularization methods such as early stopping or for
533   explaining scaling laws that relate the amount of overparameterization to optimal training times.
534
      Furthermore, multiple experiments highlight the potential of our framework to not only enhance our
      understanding of the interplay between explicit regularization and implicit bias but also to pave the
535
      way for developing more effective regularization techniques, such as dynamic weight decay, tailored
536
      to various model architectures and tasks.
