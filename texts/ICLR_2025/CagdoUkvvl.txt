011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In continual learning, developing robust representations that adapt to new distribu-
014            tions or classes while retaining prior knowledge is crucial. While most traditional
015            approaches focus on single-modality data, multi-modal learning offers significant
016            advantages by leveraging diverse sensory inputs, akin to human perception. How-
017            ever, transitioning to multi-modal continual learning introduces additional chal-
018            lenges as the model needs to effectively combine new information from differ-
019
               ent modalities while avoiding catastrophic forgetting. In this work, we propose
               a relaxed cross-modality representation alignment loss and utilize a dual-learner
020
               framework to preserve the relation between previously learned representations.
021
               We validate our framework using several multi-modal datasets that encompass
022            various types of input modalities. Results show that we consistently outperform
023            baseline continual learning methods in both class incremental and domain incre-
024            mental learning scenarios. Further analysis highlights the effectiveness of our
025            solution in preserving prior knowledge while incorporating new information.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Developing robust representations that can adapt to new distributions or classes is crucial when
032   continual learning on a sequence of classification tasks. At the heart of this is representation
033
      learning, which ensures that models not only adapt to new information but also retain previously
      learned knowledge effectively. While most traditional continual learning approaches focus on
034
      single-modality data (Chaudhry et al., 2019; Buzzega et al., 2020; Kang et al., 2022; Sarfraz et al.,
035
      2023; Shi & Wang, 2024), multi-modal data offers significant advantages. This mirrors how human
036   sensory systems combine diverse inputs to enhance understanding and improve predictive accuracy.
037
038   However, extending continual learning to multi-modal settings introduces additional complexities.
039
      Existing frameworks, such as traditional contrastive learning (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021),
      have shown success in multi-modal scenarios by aligning different modalities into a unified repre-
040
      sentational space. Adapting these frameworks to multi-modal continual learning presents two key
041
      challenges. First, strict cross-modality alignment can limit the model’s ability to capture modality-
042   specific features (Jiang et al., 2023), potentially missing important details such as auditory cues not
043   present in visual data. As continual learning progresses, the reliance on a constrained feature set
044   can lead to overlaps between representations from different classes and degrades performance on
045   previously learned tasks. Figure 1 illustrates this issue using two instrument classes from AVE (Tian
046   et al., 2018), Guitar and Ukelele, with high visual resemblance but have different tones. We observe
047   an overall increase in similarity scores between representations of the two classes as learning pro-
048   gresses, indicating a greater overlap between the two classes. Second, current contrastive methods
049   lack a clear mechanism to preserve earlier representations while enhancing multi-modal learning.
050   To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that relaxes the cross-modality alignment
051   constraint. Rather than enforcing direct alignment between modalities, we independently align each
052   modality’s representation with a joint representation formed by fusing the various modalities. Since
053   the joint representation encapsulates information from all modalities, aligning each modality with it
      minimizes information loss, thereby results in more stable representations. Additionally, we apply a

079   2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Traditional continual learning methods typically stores a small set of samples from previous tasks for
082   replay and regulating model updates to mitigate forgetting. Experience Replay (ER) (Riemer et al.,
083   2019) interleaves memory samples with the current task samples during training. GEM (Lopez-Paz
084   & Ranzato, 2017) and A-GEM (Chaudhry et al., 2019) constrain the model update to be in the di-
085   rection orthogonal to the gradients with respect to loss on memory samples. DER++ (Buzzega et al.,
086   2020) uses an additional distillation loss to ensure the output logits of the memory samples remains
087
      consistent. Co2 L (Cha et al., 2021) uses contrastive learning to learn transferable representations
      and preserves the relation between representation of memory samples to reduce forgetting. SS-IL
088
      (Ahn et al., 2021) uses separate distillation loss on logits of classes learned at different tasks to
089
      reduce bias to new classes. AFC (Kang et al., 2022) uses importance-weighted distillation loss on
090   features to minimize the upper bound of loss increase on previous tasks. ESMER (Sarfraz et al.,
091   2023) updates the model using low-loss samples to reduce abrupt representation drift. UDIL (Shi &
092   Wang, 2024) allows learnable coefficients to balance the loss terms on samples of current task and
093   memory samples. All these methods focus on single image modality and do not take into account
094   learning of the correlation between different modalities.
095
      Meanwhile, research on multi-modal representation learning aims to learn robust representations
096   capturing information from multiple input modalities. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and ALIGN (Jia
097   et al., 2021) uses contrastive loss to align the image and text representations within a unified rep-
098   resentational space. However, Jiang et al. (2023) show that exact alignment across modalities may
099   be sub-optimal for downstream tasks, and propose to differentiate representations into components
100   that capture modality-specific and modality-shared information separately. Similarly, SimMMDG
101   (Dong et al., 2024) employs a representation splitting approach and utilizes label information to
102   apply supervised contrastive loss only on the modality-shared component. In contrast to these meth-
103   ods, which assume data to be independent and identically distributed, our focus is to learn robust
104
      multi-modal representation under the continual learning setting, where data of different distributions
      or classes become available over time.
105
106   Some recent works focus on continual learning for multi-modal classification tasks to address the
107   issue of integrating and retaining knowledge from different modalities over time. AV-CIL (Pian
      et al., 2023) focuses on audio-visual class incremental learning and preserves the semantic similarity

      between the two modalities by maximizing similarity between cross-modal features of the same class
109   while minimizing those of different classes. It utilizes distillation loss on the visual attention maps to
110   preserve the model’s attentive ability. CIGN (Mo et al., 2023) also focuses on audio-visual continual
111   learning and uses learnable audio-visual class tokens in the transformer architecture to capture class-
112   aware features. It introduces a distillation loss to preserve the distribution of previously learned
113   class tokens. CMR-MFN (Wang et al., 2023) examines continual learning in egocentric activity
114   recognition using visual-inertial data. This work employs a confusion mixup strategy and dynamic
115   expandable architecture to adaptively manage the changing correlations between modalities over
116   time. All these methods rely on specific architectures and modality configurations, which may limit
117
      their applicability across broader multi-modal continual learning settings.

119   3     M ETHODOLOGY
120
121   We first define the problem setup of continual learning involving a sequence of T i.i.d. multi-
122   modal classification tasks. At each incremental step t ∈ [1, T ], the learner is given a dataset Dt =
123   {(xi , yi )} ∼ Pt , where Pt is the distribution of the t-th task. Each data instance xi = {xki }Kk=1
124   comprises of K different input modalities and yi ∈ Yt is its corresponding label with Yt denoting
125   the set of classes seen at the t-th incremental step. Note that except for a small number of those
126   retained in memory Mt , data from previous tasks are not accessible.
127
      Training at each incremental step t utilizes Dt′ = Dt ∪ Mt , a combination of the current task’s data
128   and retained memory instances. After training, a maximum of m data instances are sampled from
129   Dt′ to create Mt+1 , the memory for the next incremental step. Given the dataset Dt′ , the goal is to
130   learn a model that minimizes prediction errors on test samples drawn from the joint distribution of
131   all the tasks seen so far.
132
133   3.1   R ELAXING R EPRESENTATION A LIGNMENT C ONSTRAIN
134
135   Multi-modal representation learning plays an important role in multi-modal continual learning. Ex-
136   isting works (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021) have shown the effectiveness of using contrastive
137   loss to project multi-modal representations into a common feature space for more robust represen-
138   tations. Supervised contrastive loss (Khosla et al., 2020) leverages the label information to cluster
139   representations of the same class together while pushing apart clusters of different classes. Multi-
140   modal supervised contrastive loss then aligns representations of instances from different modalities
141
      with the same labels. However, initial experiments employing the contrastive loss in multi-modal
      continual learning reveal a rapid degradation in model performance as learning progresses (see Ap-
142
      pendix A1 for details). This is because different modalities inherently capture distinct information
143
      and the strict constraint to align representations from different modalities into a common feature
144   space imposed by the contrastive loss often leads to the loss of modality-specific information (Dong
145   et al., 2024), thereby accelerating the process of forgetting. This motivates us to design a new rep-
146   resentation alignment loss that relaxes the constrain and encourages the model to retain the distinct
147   features captured by the different modalities.
148
      Suppose we have a batch of N training samples from Dt′ with BDt , BMt ⊂ [1, N ] being the set
149
      containing indices of samples from Dt and Mt respectively. For each sample xi , let zik be the
150
      corresponding projected representation of input modality xki and zi be the joint representation ob-
151
      tained from combining all the modalities. We collect all joint representations in the batch to form
152
      the set J = {zj | j ∈ [1, N ]}. For each modality-specific representation zik acting as an anchor,
153
      we define the set comprising all representations of the same modality k, except for the anchor itself,
154
      as Aki = {zjk | j ∈ [1, N ], j ̸= i}. We identify the ‘positives’ of the anchor as those represen-
155
      tations in J and Aki that belong to samples with the same class labels as xi . The corresponding
156
      sets of positives for the joint representation and for each modality-specific representation are given
157
      by Ji = {zj ∈ J | yi = yj } and Qki = {zjk ∈ Aki | yi = yj } respectively. The remaining
158
      representations in J \ Ji and Aki \ Qki are then ‘negatives’ of the anchor. The goal is to pull together
159
      the anchor and positives, while pushing apart the anchor from negatives.
160

      clude pairs with different modalities when defining the positives and negatives of each modality-

      3.2   D UAL L EARNER F RAMEWORK
192
193   Relaxing the alignment constrain reduces the model’s tendency to bias towards learning modality-
194   shared features for classifying new tasks. Although Lrelax promotes the learning of more stable
195   representation, it does not explicitly mitigate the issue of forgetting previously learned tasks. To
196   address this issue, we leverage the theory of complementary learning systems which posits that the
197   brain uses two specialized systems to achieve effective learning (McClelland et al., 1995; Kumaran
198   et al., 2016). Specifically, the hippocampus plays the role in fast learning of specifics of individual
199
      experiences, while the neocortex relies on slow learning to gradually form structured knowledge
      about the environment. Inspired by this neuropsychological framework, various models have been
200
      proposed that employ complementary learners to simulate these fast and slow learning dynamics for
201
      effective continual learning (Rostami et al., 2019; Pham et al., 2021; Arani et al., 2022; Sarfraz et al.,
202   2023). This motivates us to adopt a dual-learner framework for multi-modal continual learning.
203
204   Figure 2 shows our dual-learner framework, consisting of a fast learner which quickly integrates
205
      new knowledge from current task, and a slow learner which gradually accumulates knowledge from
      the fast learner. Both learners have the same modality-specific encoders that independently process
206
      each input modality xki . The output from the encoders are concatenated and forwarded to the fusion
207
      layers to obtain a joint representation zi , which is then used by the classifier to output logits pi . The
208
      fast learner also have a projection layer for each modality that projects output from the respective
209
      encoders to obtain representations zik with the same dimension as zi .
210

249
250   We evaluate our framework in two multi-modal continual learning scenarios, namely class incre-
251   mental learning and domain incremental learning. In class incremental learning, new classes are
252
      introduced sequentially, requiring the model to adapt without forgetting previous knowledge. For
      domain incremental learning, the input data distribution shifts over time while the set of classes
253
      remains fixed, requiring the model to adapt to data from different domains. We measure the perfor-
254                                                                                    PT
      mance in terms of average accuracy across all steps, that is, Accuracyall = T1 t=1 at , where at is
255
      the test accuracy on samples of all seen classes and domains after training at incremental step t. The
256
      results are averaged across three runs using different class or domain orders.
257
258   We use the following datasets for the experiments on class incremental learning:
259
             • AVE (Tian et al., 2018): This is an audio-visual dataset consisting of 28 event classes
260            including human activities, animal activities, music performance and vehicle sounds. We
261            adapt this dataset for incremental learning by dividing these classes into seven different
262            sequential steps, each comprising disjoint set of classes. Each class contains a minimum
263            of 48 training videos and a maximum of 152 training videos, with each video spanning
264            approximately 10 seconds.
265          • UESTC-MMEA (Xu et al., 2023): This is a egocentric dataset comprising of 32 activity
266            classes, covering static activities such as watching television and reading, to physical ac-
267            tivities such as walking and riding bike. For incremental learning, we randomly divide the
268            classes into eight sequential steps. Each class contains a minimum of 129 training samples
269            and a maximum of 171 training samples, with each sample having an average of 18 seconds
               of video recording and inertial data.

300   We implement our framework in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and run all experiments on a single
301   NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. We use a different encoder for each modality to ensure optimal feature
302   extraction. For video data, we use the SlowFast network (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019), pretrained
303   on Kinetics-400 (Kay et al., 2017) and designed to capture both spatial and temporal dynamics
304   effectively. For image data in DKD, we use RETFound (Zhou et al., 2023), which is a foundation
305   model trained on large-scale retinal images. For audio data, we use ResNet18 (He et al., 2016)
306

307
      learning model trained on large-scale unlabeled wearable data to handle activity recognition tasks.
308
      We use two fully-connected layers, with 2048 hidden units, to fuse the representations from the
309   modality-specific encoders to a 1024-dimensional joint representation before forwarding to a linear
310   layer for the final classification output. More training details can be found in Appendix A2.

408   Effect of relaxing alignment constraint. Table 3 presents the results when the traditional multi-
409   modal supervised contrastive loss Lcontrast (see Eq. A1 in Appendix A1) is used in place of our
410   proposed Lrelax . We assess the effectiveness of Lrelax under two conditions: with and without
411   Lpreserve . The results show that using Lcontrast leads to a performance decline in both cases. This
412   suggests that by relaxing the constraint on cross-modality alignment, we enhance the robustness of
413   multi-modal representations for incremental learning.

417   learned classes and previously learned classes for different values of λ. We see that performance is
418
      the worst when λ = 0, indicating the importance of Lpreserve . As λ increases, there is a general

419
      the overall accuracy. However, the performance plateau when λ exceeds 20. Although a higher λ
420
      better preserves old knowledge, the overall accuracy would be negatively impacted as it hinders the

427   demonstrates strong performance in long runs of small-sized tasks. We also see that our solution
428   consistently outperforms the baselines under different memory size restriction.

      5   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we introduce a dual-learner framework for multi-modal continual learning, where
479
      multi-modal representation learning plays a crucial role. Our findings reveal that applying multi-
480   modal supervised contrastive loss in continual learning leads to a decline in performance. To address
481   this, we have proposed a new loss function to reduce information loss by relaxing the constraint on
482   cross-modality representation alignment. We further mitigate forgetting by preserving consistency
483   of the relation between previously learned representations. Extensive experiments across various
484   continual learning scenarios and datasets involving different modalities demonstrate the effective-
485   ness of our proposed solution in learning robust multi-modal representations, achieving good per-
      formance in acquiring new knowledge and retaining previously learned information.
