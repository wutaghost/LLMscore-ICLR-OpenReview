008                                                      A BSTRACT
009
010                In geometric deep learning, numerous works have been dedicated to enhancing neu-
011                ral networks with ability to preserve symmetries, a concept known as equivariance.
012
                   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are already equivariant to translations.
                   To further achieve rotation and reflection equivariance, previous methods are pri-
013
                   marily based on Group Equivariant Convolutional Neural Networks (G-CNN).
014
                   While showing a significant improvement when processing rotation-augmented
015                datasets, training G-CNN on a dataset with little rotational variation typically leads
016                to a performance drop comparing to a regular CNN. In this study, we discuss the
017                reason of G-CNN not performing on datasets with little rotational variation. We
018                propose an alternative approach: generating CNN filters that inherently exhibit
019                rotational equivariance without altering the main network’s CNN structure. This
020                is achieved through our novel application of a dynamic hypernetwork. We prove
021                these generated filters grant equivariance property to a regular CNN main network.
022                Our experiments demonstrate that our method outperforms G-CNN and achieves
023
                   performance comparable to advanced state-of-the-art G-CNN-based methods.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In machine learning, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are effective and prevalent tools for
      image classification and segmentation tasks. When a specific area of the image input is translated
029
      to a different location, the convolution operation inherently causes the extracted feature to translate
030
      similarly. This ability to comprehend and preserve translation is known as translation equivariance,
031   and general equivariance is highly significant in geometric deep learning. Wood & Shawe-Taylor
032   (1996) emphasize that it is a central problem to design neural networks that exhibit invariance or
033   equivariance to representations in machine learning. It is a natural question to ask: Can a CNN
034   exhibit equivariance regarding rotations and reflections?
035
      Group Equivariant Convolutional Networks (G-CNNs), introduced by Cohen & Welling (2016), is
036
      one of the most popular equivariant CNN structure. The convolution operation is modified to be group
037   equivariant and demonstrated equivariance for rotations, translations, and reflections. The G-CNN
038   architectures have been the backbone of many equivariant neural networks, including steerable CNNs
039   (Cohen & Welling, 2017; Weiler et al., 2018) and spherical CNNs (Cohen et al., 2018; Salihu et al.,
040   2024), with several applications such as domain adaptation (Zhang et al., 2022), pose estimation
041   (Howell et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021), and many other areas involving symmetries.
042
      G-CNN’s equivariance property enables effective handling of data with symmetries, but equivariance
043   comes with an additional constraints imposed on the filters: Filters are grouped into smaller subsets,
044   where the filters in each subset are strictly related by reflections and rotations. When training on
045   a dataset with little rotational variation, these constraints can lead to G-CNNs underperforming
046   compared to standard CNN architectures. To empirically observe this, in Table 1, we train a small
047   CNN and a small G-CNN on the original MNIST datasets. CNN outperforms G-CNN on the original
048   MNIST testing dataset, while G-CNN outperforms CNN when the testing dataset is incorporated
049   with rotational augmentation.1
050   To bypass such constraint within the filters, in this paper we propose a Hypernetwork-based Equivari-
051   ant CNN (HE-CNN) to achieve equivariance. The proposed HE-CNN is grounded in a straightforward
052   concept: rotating filters in alignment with input rotation produces corresponding rotated features.

      Specifically, HE-CNN consists of a dynamic hypernetwork and a main network. The main network
055   can be a general CNN and the dynamic hypernetwork generates input-dependent parameters. The
056   dynamic hypernetwork is composed of two components: a non-equivariant parameter-pieces (NEP)
057   generator and a novel module called equivariant combiner. The NEP generator generates parameter-
058   pieces of entire parameters. The equivariant combiner, abbreviated as equi-combiner, combines
059   the parameter-pieces in a specific pattern to form full parameters with the ability to follow given
060   symmetries on the inputs. Finally, we theoretically and empirically demonstrate that the proposed
061   HE-CNN confer the equivariance property to non-equivariant CNN structures.

      2    R ELATED WORK
075
076   Group Equivariance CNNs (G-CNN) are introduced by Cohen & Welling (2016) as one of the
077   earliest adaptation of general equivariance into CNN, and has been the backbone for the majority
078   of equivariant architectures. Assume the group consists of translation and 90 degree rotations. The
079   first layer of G-CNN would have a filter in the same fashion of a regular CNN, and rotate this filter
080   by 90◦ , 180◦ and 270◦ , resulting in total four rotated versions of a same filter. These four filters
081   output four feature maps for a single input. For all the following layers, to process these four features,
082   G-CNN has four different filters as a set of filters, and this set of filters is again rotated by multiples
083   of 90 degrees to perform the G-equivariant convolution. G-CNNs apply pooling to the final set of
084
      features before the linear layer, achieving invariance in the final output.
085   A previous approach on making non-equivariant model equivariant is the canonicalization method
086   (Kaba et al., 2023; Mondal et al., 2023). The equivariance is achieved through a G-CNN based
087   canonicalization network, learning to rotate the input before feeding into the pretrained model.
088   HE-CNN avoids the G-CNN structures, and is utilized during the training process.
089   Hypernetwork, initially introduced by Ha et al. (2016), provides an alternative approach to train a
090   neural network. It is widely used in federated-learning (Shamsian et al., 2021), few-shot learning
091   (Sendera et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2022), continual learning (Hemati et al., 2023) and many other
092   areas, due to its versatility and parameter-efficiency. The network designated for training on a
093   specific dataset is known as the main or target network. The network responsible for generating the
094   parameters of the main network is referred to as the hypernetwork. If the hypernetwork generates
095   input-dependent parameters, we call it a dynamic hypernetwork. Otherwise we say it is static. The
096
      combination of a main network and a hypernetwork is referred to as a full network in this paper.
097   For hypernetwork research related to equivariance, Garrido et al. (2023) uses hypernetwork to make
098   representations stay equivariant without converging to invariance. The hypernetwork is utilized for
099   parameter sharing, not for achieving equivariance. To the best of our knowledge, there is no work to
100   use hypernetworks to achieve the equivariance on a regular CNN structure.

104   We give a brief definition of groups and representations, and a comprehensive definition can be
105   found in Section A.1. For a set G with a operation ∗, (G, ∗) or simply G is a group if all following
106   properties are satisfied: ∗ is associative, G has an identity element, and every element has an inverse.
107   Given G and a vector space V , a representation (V, ρ) or simply ρ is a mapping on G, where every
      ρ(g) is a linear map on V . Furthermore, ρ(g ∗ g ′ ) = ρ(g)ρ(g ′ ) for any g, g ′ ∈ G.

134   An equivariant neural network typically comprise multiple equivariant layers followed by an invariant
135
      layer prior to processing downstream tasks, a structure that has demonstrated benefits in various
      research contexts (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Worrall et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022). We also adopt
136
      this architectural principle in the design of our models.
137
138   When utilizing hypernetwork on a main neural network f , the input space is denoted by X, and
139   the weight space in neural networks is denoted by Ω. We denote a dynamic hypernetwork by
140   w : X → Ω. For any input x ∈ X, the corresponding generated weight is w(x), and fw(x) refers
141   to the main network that loads w(x) as its parameters. The full network is denoted by fw(·) (·), or
142   simply fw , which maps input x to fw(x) (x). Within the context of dynamic hypernetworks, fw(·) (·)
143   is G-equivariant if ρ2 fw(x) (x) = fw(ρ1 x) (ρ1 x), and invariant if ρ2 is the trivial representation.

168   The objective of the proposed HE-CNN is to ensure w generates equivariant parameters. This w con-
169   sists of two components. The first component is a non-equivariant parameter-pieces generator (NEP
170   generator). NEP generator is responsible for generating approximately 1/4 of the total parameters,
171
      including filters in convolutional layers and weight matrices for linear layers. The details for each
      case are introduced in the next two sections. The NEP generator can be any non-equivariant neural
172
      network and our implementation uses regular CNNs. Additionally, we expect it to be non-equivariant
173
      to avoid collapsing to invariant filters as demonstrated in Section D.1.
174
175   When given an input image, we collect the four 90◦ -rotated versions of it and send them to the NEP
176   generator, resulting in four parameter pieces. Then, the four parameter pieces are fed into the second
177
      component, the equivariant combiner (equi-combiner), to assemble parameter pieces to get full
      parameters that could achieve the equivariance.
178
179   The design of the equi-loader is outlined in the following two sections, which correspond to two
180   cases: convolutional layers and linear layers. As explained in Section 3, we follow previous designs
181   (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Worrall et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022) that for the convolutional layers,
182   the objective of the equi-combiner is to ensure the equivariance and preserve the rotations. For
183
      linear layers, the equi-combiner is to generate invariant parameters, so that the final output remains
      consistent regardless of the input’s rotations. Hence, in the next two sections, we discuss how to
184
      design the NEP generator and equi-combiner for the two types of layers.

350   4.6   T RAINING PROCESS
351
352   Given a fixed main network, we first initialize the NEP generator N . We choose a CNN structure
353   with output dimension described in Sections 4.2 and 4.3. During training, the original inputs are sent
354   into the combination of NEP generator and equivariant combiner to generate equivariant parameters.
355   The main network f then loads the equivariant parameters and process the same input. We compute
356   the chosen loss between the output of fw and the label, and preform back propagation to update the
357
      learnable parameters in the NEP generator.
358   When the inputs are in batch, our hypernetwork is indeed capable of generating parameters in batch.
359   However, for inputs in batch size b, we would have b corresponding parameters, thus b main networks.
360   This bijection can easily lead to GPU memories shortage when main network is large. We comprehend
361   this by the following: for each batch of inputs, we average them to get one single set of parameters,
362   used to process the batch of inputs. This significantly reduced the memory usage. However, due to
363
      the average operation, our network is equivariant if the representation ρ(g) is applied on the whole
      batch. For each experiment, we specify whether we use parameters in batch or averaged.

      4.7   U TILIZING M ULTI - HEAD AND L O RA
367
      For the NEP generator N , the output size of the last layer is approximately one forth of the parameter
368   of the main network. Assuming the main network f has l parameters, the parameters in the last linear
369   layer of N are O(l). This can become excessive if the main network is significantly large.
370
371
      To reduce the parameter count for N , we utilize the low-rank adaptation (Hu et al., 2021) on the last
      linear layer. We choose an intermediate rank r. On the last
                                                               √ linear layer, instead
                                                                                √      of directly generating
372
      the full length l, we generate two outputs, each in shape l × r and r × l. Multiplying √ them would
373
374
      give us our needed length l parameters. Now, the parameters of N with LoRA is O( l).
375   During our experiments, we discovered that applying LoRA directly can lead to a significant and
376   undesirable drop in the performance of HE-CNN. Therefore, for each layer of the main network,
377   we have a corresponding linear head in the parameter generator to generate layer-specific parameter.
      Then, we apply LoRA on each linear head. Each head has different l depending on the structure

      of the main network, but they share the same rank r. Notice that we only divide the linear layer of

532   6     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In this study, we propose a novel hypernetwork-based equivariant CNN, offering an alternative
535   approach to equivariance without imposing constraints on the filters. Equivariance is achieved
536   through a dynamic hypernetwork composed of a non-equivariant parameter-pieces generator and
537   equivariant combiner. We test our model on several benchmark datasets. Comparing to G-CNN
538   based state-of-the-art methods, our network showed either better or comparable results. We showed
539   better performance in all settings compared to the base G-CNN. In our future work, we are interested
      in extending HE-CNN to more complex groups.

707   A.1    G ROUPS AND REPRESENTATIONS
708
      Definition 3. Let G be a set and ∗ be an operation. Then, (G, ∗) is a group if the following holds:
709
710         1. There exists a e ∈ G, such that for any g ∈ G, g ∗ e = e ∗ g = g. We call this e the identity.
711
712         2. For any element g ∈ G, there exists h ∈ G such that g ∗ h = h ∗ g = e. We call this the
713            inverse element, and denote it as −h or h−1 depending on the context of the operation.
714
            3. G is closed under this operation. That is, for any g1 , g2 ∈ G, g1 ∗ g2 is always in G.
715
716         4. For any g, h, k ∈ G, g ∗ (h ∗ k) = (g ∗ h) ∗ k.
717
718   After defining a group (G, ∗), it is common to simply refer to it as G when the operation is clear from
719   the context.
720   Definition 4. Given a group (G, ∗), a representation of G on a vector space V is a map ρ with inputs
721   in G. For any g ∈ G, ρ(g) is a linear map on V . Furthermore, ρ(g1 g2 ) = ρ(g1 )ρ(g2 ). We denote the
722   representation by (ρ, V ) or simply ρ.
723
724   The definition of a group and representation might be a bit hard to understand without some back-
725   ground on abstract algebra. It is helpful to think of groups as a collection of symmetries, and
726
      representation as an action of such symmetries on a vector space. Let us go through one example,
      the cyclic group of order 4, (Z4 , +mod 4 ). Readers with experience in group theory can skip the
727
      following example section.

732
      Comparing the modulo addition in Z4 with counter-clockwise rotations by multiples of 90 degrees,
733   one can see some similarity between them. For instance, 2 + 3 = 1 mod 4, and a vector ends up in the
734   same place after rotating 180 degrees and then 270 degrees, as it does after a 90-degree rotation. This
735   similarity is captured by a representation. Let V = {(x, y) | x, y ∈ R}, all 2D vectors. Our chosen ρ
736   maps g ∈ Z4 to a linear map on V which perform the corresponding rotation around the origin. For
737   instance, pick 2 ∈ Z4 . For any (x, y) ∈ V , ρ(2) is a linear map that rotate (x, y) by 180 degrees, i.e.
