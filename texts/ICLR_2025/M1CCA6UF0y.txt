009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Current LLM training positions mathematical reasoning as a core capability. With
012            publicly available sources fully tapped, there is an unmet demand for diverse
013            and challenging mathematics questions. Relying solely on human experts is both
014            time-consuming and costly, while LLM-generated questions often lack the req-
015            uisite diversity and difficulty. We present a design framework that combines the
016
               strengths of LLMs with a human-in-the-loop approach to generate a diverse array
               of challenging math questions. Initially, leveraging LLM metacognition skills (Di-
017
               dolkar et al., 2024), a strong LLM is used to extract core “skills” from existing
018
               math datasets. These skills serve as the basis for generating novel and difficult
019            questions by prompting the LLM with random pairs of core skills that must be
020            utilized in the question. The use of two very different skills within each question
021            makes finding such questions an “out of distribution” task for both LLMs and
022            humans. Our pipeline employs LLMs to iteratively generate and refine questions
023            and solutions through multi-turn prompting. Human annotators then verify and
024            further refine the questions, with their efficiency enhanced via further LLM interac-
025            tions. Applying this pipeline on skills extracted from MATH dataset (Hendrycks
026            et al., 2021) resulted in MATH2 - a dataset of higher quality math questions, as
027
               evidenced by: (a) Lower performance of all models on MATH2 than on MATH
               (b) Higher performance on MATH when using MATH2 questions as in-context
028
               examples. Although focused on mathematics, our methodology seems applicable
029
               to other domains requiring structured reasoning, and potentially as a component
030            of scalable oversight. Also of interest is a striking relationship observed between
031            models’ performance on the new dataset: the success rate on MATH2 is the square
032            on MATH. This suggests that successfully solving the question in MATH2 requires
033            a nontrivial combination of two distinct math skills.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Significant improvement in the capabilities of LLMs (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Team,
039
      2023; Team et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) to understand
      and generate complex mathematical content has been achieved by leveraging all the public data and a
040
      fair bit of private data. Sources of high-quality, varied, and difficult mathematical questions are drying
041
      up. Even finding new questions for evaluation is getting difficult since newly-released human exams
042   are somewhat similar to past exams, which are potentially present in the LLMs’ training datasets.
043   Hence, there is a pressing need for innovative methods to create new, diverse, and challenging
044   questions.
045
      Expert mathematicians and educators possess the deep understanding required to create questions that
046
      not only test a wide range of skills but also push the boundaries of what the learners, and by extension,
047
      the models, can handle. However, relying solely on human experts is not scalable. Generating
048   synthetic questions using LLMs is feasible at scale (Trinh et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Gunasekar
049   et al., 2023; Patel et al., 2024; Toshniwal et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Honovich
050   et al., 2022), but often falls short in terms of the necessary difficulty. Huang et al. (2024) employs a
051   similar approach as ours where they extract topics and corresponding keypoints from a set of seed
052   problems using GPT-4, and then combine the topic to generate new questions, again using GPT-4).
053   However, the generated data is meant to be used for the finetuning of models as compared to serving
      as an evaluation set in our case. As a result, the questions generated in Huang et al. (2024) are

054
      not sufficiently difficult. Similarly, limited work exists on ensuring the necessary diversity in the
055   generated synthetic data. Chan et al. (2024) proposes prompting frontier models to generate questions
056   where each question is generated in the context of a persona as a way of ensuring diversity. They use
057   1M different personas to generate questions, which are then used for finetuning models, leading to
058   significant improvements. This dichotomy between the quality of human-generated questions and the
059   scalability of LLM-generated questions presents a significant challenge (Yu et al., 2024).

061   1.1   E VALUATION S ATURATION P HENOMENON
062
063
      LLM evaluations getting saturated is a well-known issue. Some of the saturation is driven by across-
      the-board improvements arising from better training and more extensive/better datasets. But a lot has
064
      to do with evaluation-specific enhancements that optimize model performance on standard evaluations
065
      through techniques like supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on synthetic question-answer pairs. These
066   synthetic pairs can be generated by leading proprietary models when provided with a few examples
067   from the dataset or by filtering the model’s own responses (Yue et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023). Such
068   methods can dramatically boost performance; for example, just 1 million synthetic examples can
069   elevate Llama2 7B’s performance on the MATH dataset to levels comparable to GPT-4 (Li et al.,
070   2024).
071
      The distinction between general and evaluation-specific improvements is crucial. The latter may lead
072   to overfitting to particular evaluations rather than a genuine acquisition of mathematical skills. This
073   issue was highlighted when a new version of the GSM8K dataset revealed performance drops in many
074   models, indicating overfitting to the previous dataset version (Zhang et al., 2024). Similarly, leading
075   LLMs performed significantly worse on newer versions of the Chinese GaoKao exam compared to
076   older exams, raising fundamental questions about the depth of their mathematical understanding.
077
078   1.2   P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK : AI- ASSISTED G ENERATION OF D IFFICULT M ATH Q UESTIONS
079
080   At first glance, it may seem counterintuitive to use an AI model to generate and correct novel questions
081
      that it is unable to solve itself. However, recent research (Arora & Goyal, 2023; Didolkar et al., 2024)
      demonstrated that top LLMs possess a robust understanding of mathematical skills, including the
082
      capability to identify the skills required to solve given questions (Reid et al., 2024; Achiam et al.,
083
      2023). This naturally raises the question: can LLMs operate in the reverse direction, i.e., generate
084   math problems when given a list of skills that have to be tested? Our initial attempts yielded mixed
085   results. While leading models could produce creative math questions when provided with a list of
086   skills, the majority of these questions exhibited one or more of the following shortcomings: too similar
087   to existing questions in datasets; have errors or nonsensical elements; are too tedious or mechanical
088   to be engaging for human annotators. (See Section 4.) Moreover, they often conflate “difficulty” with
089   tedious calculations, which actually would play to the strength of machines to leverage external tools
090   such as calculators or Python interpreters.
091   Nevertheless, there were promising instances where LLMs generated interesting and correct questions
092   that they were unable to solve, due to incomplete or incorrect reasoning. This observation led us
093   to the concept of AI-assisted creation of evaluation datasets. Our process may also be of interest
094   for human pedagogy since it begins with the extraction of core "skills" from existing math datasets,
095   which serve as the foundational elements of mathematical questions. The current paper focuses on
096   the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021), a mainstay of LLM evaluation in recent years.
097   Starting with a list of mathematical skills extracted from the MATH dataset using recently discovered
098   methods (Didolkar et al., 2024), we focused on creating questions that involve one skill from pre-
099   algebra and algebra portions of the MATH dataset and one other skill randomly sampled from
100   different sections of MATH. Our generation pipeline uses carefully crafted prompts and multi-turn
101   interactions with leading models to significantly improve the generation of high-quality questions
102   and candidate answers.
103   In our AI-assisted process, human experts played a crucial role. Using the (question, answer)
104   pairs generated by LLMs and leveraging API access to leading models, experts identified promising
105   questions—-often those incorrectly answered by the LLMs but containing many correct ideas. Experts
106   then refined these questions to enhance their engagement value and provided gold-standard answers.
107   The AI-assisted process not only boosted human productivity but also resulted in high-quality, novel
      questions distinct from those in existing datasets.

140   Importance of using random pairs of skills: We think that this is key driver of improved diversity
141   and difficulty among generated questions. Recall that MATH dataset is neatly partitioned into
142   sub-areas such as “Geometry” and “Number theory.” Requiring generated questions to combine
143
      skills from two subareas (e.g., a question linking area-and-perimeter calculations with prime number
      knowledge) necessitates “out of distribution” thinking; some examples appear in Section 4.1. Such
144
      questions challenged all LLMs as well as our human raters. Our new dataset is much harder than
145
      MATH for all models. For instance, if a model has a success rate 0.5 on MATH, then success rate on
146   our new dataset is closer to 0.52 = 0.25. This trend is more general, and Section 3.2 suggests that
147   this is an indication that the average question of MATH2 indeed requires nontrivial use of two distinct
148   underlying skills, which is the reason for naming this new dataset as MATH2 . We believe that our
149   methodology could also introduce fresh perspectives into math instruction for AI and human learners.
150   Connection to Scalable Oversight: This notion (Bowman & etal, 2022) looks ahead to how humans
151   might supervise and check AI systems that potentially outperform humans in many relevant skills.
152   While typically discussed in the context of alignment and safety, the concept is pertinent here. How
153   can human experts reliably evaluate LLMs’ understanding of high-school or freshman-level math
154   when these models have already been trained on all available exams and textbooks? Could human-AI
155   collaboration lead to more novel evaluations?
156   Paper organization: Sections 2 describes our design methodology and generation pipeline for
157   MATH2 . Section 3 discusses the performance of many open-source and proprietary models on
158   MATH(Hendrycks et al., 2021) as well as on the new MATH2 dataset of 210 questions (see Table 2 ).
159   Section 3.2 discusses the interesting relationship between MATH and MATH2 scores. Section 3.3
160   shows that MATH2 questions are more useful than MATH questions when used as in-context
161   exemplars for various LLMs. Section 4 sheds some light on interesting behaviors and failure modes
      of leading LLMs that we observed during the question generation process.

188   2    P IPELINE FOR AI-A SSISTED Q UESTION G ENERATION
189
190
      We present a structured approach to generating challenging mathematics questions by combining
191   the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) and human expertise. Given below is a high-level
192   overview of the process before delving into the details of each step.
193
194
      We begin our pipeline with skill extraction - identifying and cataloging distinct mathematical skills
      from a dataset, as described in Didolkar et al. (2024). This step creates a repository of skills linked to
195
      specific questions. The motivation behind this is to systematically generate and analyze questions
196
      that require specific skills, ensuring a comprehensive evaluation framework.
197
198   Next, we focus on generating questions that combine pairs of distinct skills to increase their
199   difficulty. By using advanced models like GPT-4, Claude and Gemini, and incorporating in-context
200
      examples of multi-way interactions between AI and humans, we enhance the models’ performance in
      generating complex questions. This step aims to produce challenging questions that robustly assess
201
      problem-solving abilities.
202
203   The final step involves screening and validation to filter out invalid or flawed questions. This
204   rigorous process includes evaluating and solving the questions to identify hidden flaws, such as
205   computational intractability or logical inconsistencies. Advanced techniques like in-context exemplars
206
      and self-consistency further ensure the accuracy and quality of the solutions. This step is crucial
      for maintaining the integrity and reliability of the generated questions and their solutions. Overall,
207
      each step in the pipeline is designed to systematically enhance the quality and difficulty of questions,
208
      providing a robust and comprehensive evaluation of mathematical skills.
209
210   Motivated by these challenges, we employ a five-step approach to generate difficult math questions
211   using advanced models. For each round of generation, we randomly sample a pair of skills and three
212
      sample question-solution pairs corresponding to each skill from the skill repository. These reference
      examples are sourced from the MATH dataset.
213
214   Step 1: Skill Pair Validation. We begin by asking the LLM (GPT-4 or Claude) to validate the
215   skill pair by assessing the qualitative similarity of the two skills. Reference examples are provided
      in-context to enrich the model’s understanding of the skills. If the model deems the skills too similar,


                                                                      4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      they are flagged and excluded from question generation, as similar skills might lead to simpler
217   questions.
218
219   Step 2: Question Generation. Next, we prompt the LLM to generate a question and a brief solution
220
      requiring the application of both skills in the sampled pair. We specify two conditions to ensure high-
      quality questions: the question should either require an exact answer or specify that an approximate
221
      answer is acceptable, and it should ask for only a single final result. In-context, we provide two
222
      multi-turn conversations between a human and an AI assistant. These conversations demonstrate
223   the human providing feedback on the AI-generated questions, which the AI then refines. This helps
224   the model anticipate and avoid practical issues, such as insufficient involvement of skills or logical
225   inconsistencies. Appendix A.6 provides examples of the responses of different models in the question
226   generation step.
227
      Step 3: Solution Attempt. The model then attempts a solution to the generated question, adopting an
228
      adversarial approach to identify flaws such as insufficient information, ambiguity, self-contradiction,
229   or excessive computation. If any issues are found, the model stops solving and clearly states the
230   problems. Otherwise, it completes the solution. During this step, the model does not receive the skill
231   names or reference examples to ensure unbiased problem-solving.
232
      Step 4: Question Validation. We give LLM the generated question and its solution for validation
233
      against a fixed rubric consisting of seven criteria:
234
235
             • Single Answer Requirement: The question should ask for only one final answer.
236
237          • Exact Answer Requirement: There should be only one exact answer, unless approximations
238
               are explicitly stated.
239          • Dual Answer Requirement: The question must necessarily and sufficiently involve the appli-
240            cation of both skills, with difficulty comparable to or greater than the reference examples.
241          • Clarity and Completeness: The question should be clear and contain all necessary informa-
242            tion.
243
             • Computational Tractability: The question should not require overly complex computations.
244
245          • Realism and Logic: The scenario should be realistic and logically consistent.
246          • Syntax and Grammar: The question should be grammatically correct and clearly written.
247
248   The model uses reference examples and validation exemplars - model generated examples of validating
249   questions, to facilitate this step. We employ majority voting (maj @ 4) to enhance robustness.
250
      Step 5: Final Solution and Re-validation. For questions classified as valid, we ask the LLM
251
      to re-solve the question to obtain a final solution. Reference examples are provided in-context to
252   improve the model’s understanding. We use majority voting (maj @ 4) to ensure consistency. If all
253   the answers obtained in this step are unique, indicating potential ambiguity, the question is discarded.
254
255
      The questions obtained from the above pipeline are further screened by humans. This structured
      approach not only generates challenging and novel math questions but also ensures their quality
256
      through rigorous validation, effectively combining the strengths of AI and human oversight. For
257
      detailed examples of prompts used at each step, refer to Appendix A.7.

      3.3     G ENERATED Q UESTIONS ARE E FFECTIVE I N -C ONTEXT E XEMPLARS FOR MATH.
404
405
      A possible test for the quality of a Q&A pair on similar topics as MATH dataset is whether perfor-
      mance on MATH improves when using these as in-context exemplars.
406
407   We test as follows. Recall that MATH has 7 sections. Exemplars for a section are chosen from the
408   section area. However, by design, our new questions cross section boundaries. Furthermore, they
409   are higher quality than MATH questions. We implemented a new procedure to retrieve in-context
410   exemplars from MATH2 based on the skill requirements of the current question.
411   Since MATH2 is limited in size, it does not cover all the skills extracted during the skill extraction
412   process, containing 93 out of 114 skills. Figure 5 shows the distribution of different skills in the
413   dataset. We filtered the MATH test set to remove examples requiring skills not present in the generated
414   dataset, resulting in the removal of 913 test examples. During evaluation on the filtered MATH
415   test set, for each question Q labeled with skill a (a ∈ S, where S is the set of extracted skills), we
416   retrieved in-context exemplars from the MATH2 , ensuring each exemplar involved skill a. We used
417
      four such exemplars per question (i.e., 4-shot CoT (Wei et al., 2022)). To handle skills represented
      by fewer than four examples in MATH2 , we run two experiments: (A) Proposed 4-shot CoT: If a
418
      given skill is represented by n examples in the MATH2 , where n < 4, we use n in-context examples
419
      instead of 4 exemplars. (B) Proposed + Skill Based 4-shot CoT: If a given skill is represented by
420   n examples in MATH2 , where n < 4, we supplement 4 − n exemplars for that skill from MATH
421   training set. The relevant in-context exemplars in MATH training set are determined by following
422   the methodology proposed in Didolkar et al. (2024). We compared the performance of models using
423   these targeted prompting strategies against two baselines: (C) MAmmoTH 4-shot CoT: The 4
424   in-context exemplars are taken from the MAmmoTH evaluation suite (Yue et al., 2023). (D) Skill
425   Based 4-shot CoT: We use skill-based prompting as proposed in Didolkar et al. (2024), where the
426   in-context exemplars are selected from the MATH training set, in accordance to the skill required by
427   the question at hand, as determined by GPT-4.
428   Table 3 presents the results of this comparison. The two prompting strategies using questions
429   from MATH2 as in-context exemplars, clearly outperform the two baselines. We conclude that the
430   MATH2 questions, due to their difficulty and skill relevance, serve as effective in-context exemplars.
431   Performance gains would likely be more significant with larger datasets generated using our approach,
      reducing the need to supplement with external exemplars.

433
434
      The question generation pipeline described in Section 2 was developed through an iterative process
435   of refining prompts and design choices, and evaluating their impact on the quality of the final
436   questions and solutions. Notably, the inclusion of the attempted solution and question validation steps
437   significantly enhanced the pipeline’s effectiveness. Despite the sophistication of the pipeline and
438   prompts, we still observe instances where models fail to follow the given instructions. This section
439   highlights prominent failure modes at various stages of the pipeline, which human raters need to be
440   aware of. Additionally, we explore some intriguing behaviors of the models where they successfully
441   create interesting and creative questions. Section 4.1 details the role of human raters in improving
442   these questions.
443
      4.1   C REATIVE QUESTIONS : E XAMPLES OF S YNERGY FROM H UMAN -AI INTERACTION
444
445   The models frequently produced interesting and creative questions, although they often failed to
446   generate correct solutions. In these cases, the incorrect solutions usually contained enough correct
447   ideas for a human to quickly complete them.
448   Human annotators were tasked with verifying the validity of the questions and the correctness of the
449   solutions. They were instructed to look out for any failure modes discussed in Appendix A.1. Their
450   responsibilities included ensuring that the created questions actually employed the intended math
451   skills, and improving the questions in terms of readability, quality, and difficulty when possible. They
452   were encouraged to suggest changes that would make the problems harder for automated tools to
453   solve while allowing easier or more elegant solutions for humans. The following examples illustrate
454   this process:
455
               Example: Original Question: Find the smallest positive integer k such that
456
               k 3 − k is divisible by both 9 and 10, and the sum of digits of k in its decimal
457
               representation is a prime number.
458
459   Our human team had not encountered such questions before. It requires recognizing that k 3 − k =
460   k(k − 1)(k + 1) is always divisible by 2 and 3. Thus, k must be such that k(k − 1)(k + 1)/6 is
461   divisible by 15 (both 3 and 5). Additionally, the sum of the digits of k must be a prime number, and
462   ensuring such conditions is challenging even for powerful LLMs.
463
464            Example: Original Question: Consider a collection of red, blue, and green
465

               then blue, then green, and this pattern repeats indefinitely. The number of beads
466

467

468            the second red section has 16 beads, and so on. If a bracelet is made using a
469            continuous, unbroken sequence of exactly 20 beads from this series, and each bead
470            has a length of 0.5 units, how many different bracelets can be made such that the
471            perimeter of the bracelet is an integer value?
472
473   The original question combined elements in a novel way. The human rater modified the question to
474   change the sequence size from 20 to 6 beads, maintaining the essential difficulty while making it
475   more elegant for humans. All tested models failed on the modified question.

512   5   C ONCLUSIONS
513
514
      We introduced a framework that leverages the complementary strengths of humans and AI to generate
      new, challenging mathematics questions. Building on recent insights into LLM metaknowledge,
515
      we use LLMs to extract and name key skills necessary for solving math problems. Using these
516
      insights, we developed a pipeline that employs named skills from the well-known MATH dataset,
517   and leverages multi-turn interactions with advanced LLMs to generate questions that combine pairs
518   of skills. These questions were subsequently reviewed and refined by human raters. The proposed
519   pipeline produced questions with greater novelty and difficulty compared to those in the original
520   MATH dataset. The resulting new math evaluation – MATH2 , assesses the same skills as the MATH
521   dataset but is significantly more challenging for leading models because each question involves two
522   skills from different parts of MATH. GPT-4-T and GPT-4-O exhibited the smallest performance drops,
523   which aligns with the subjective evaluations of our human raters. Additionally, we demonstrated that
524   providing the newly generated questions as in-context examples improved GPT-4-O’s performance
525
      on the MATH dataset more effectively than examples sourced directly from the MATH dataset. This
      finding further validates the quality of the questions produced by the proposed pipeline. We plan to
526
      release detailed information about our pipeline to encourage further research and development in the
527
      field of open-source math models.
528
529   Limitations and Future Work. Our pipeline incurs moderately high costs due to extensive API-based
530   use of frontier models as well as significant human verification. To improve efficiency, future work
531
      should focus on using open weights models and optimizing prompting strategies to produce higher-
      quality questions initially, thereby reducing the need for extensive filtering. Additionally, reducing
532
      human verification through the development of automated validation tools is crucial. This could
533
      include leveraging code generation and autoformalization capabilities of LLMs to generate responses
534   which can be compiled using compilers or interpreters. Enhancing our pipeline by integrating a
535   training-based feedback loop, where the model is trained on the questions that pass human verification,
536   could further streamline the process by progressively improving question quality. These measures will
537   reduce dependency on expensive proprietary models, lower overall operational costs, and maintain
538   or even enhance the quality of the generated math evaluation benchmarks. Looking ahead, an even
539   more exciting prospect is the potential application of the proposed framework to efficiently produce
      high-quality data in domains beyond mathematics.

      A.1   FAILURE M ODES AND I NTERESTING B EHAVIORS
682
683   Despite the sophistication of our pipeline, models frequently exhibit several failure modes: (a)
684   Insufficient Involvement of Skills: Models often generate questions that either miss one of the skills
685   completely or require a very shallow application of one or both skills. For example, a geometry
686   question may fail to involve ratio and proportion adequately, (b) Insufficient Information: Questions
687   may lack essential details needed for solving, making them incomplete or ambiguous. For instance, a
688   trigonometry question might omit necessary angles or distances, (c) Unsolvable or Computationally
689   Intractable Questions: Some questions generated are either unsolvable or require excessive brute-
690
      force calculations, which are impractical for evaluating reasoning abilities, (d) Nonsensical Questions:
      Models sometimes produce questions that are logically inconsistent, confusing, or ambiguous, such
691
      as a probability problem with unclear parameters or an impossible geometry scenario, (e) Deceitful
692
      Solutions: Occasionally, models fabricate solutions to nonsensical or unsolvable questions, presenting
693   incorrect logic as plausible reasoning and (f) Finding a Needle in the Haystack: Long and complex
694   validation prompts sometimes cause models to confuse or overlook the specified skills, leading to
695   incorrect evaluations.
696
697   Insufficient involvement of skills. Despite clearly specifying that solving the question should
698   necessarily require a rigorous application of both skills, the models often generate questions that
699   either miss one of the skills completely or require a very shallow application of one (while the other
700   one is sufficiently involved) or both skills. This is the most prominent failure mode of the models in
701   the context of question generation. This leads to potentially easy questions, defeating the purpose of
      skill composition. Consider the question given below which was generated by Claude Opus when

800   though the question is nonsensical or cannot be solved with the amount of information provided
801
      in the question. This happens very commonly in the solutions which are generated in the question
      generation prompt. Thus, we do not use these solutions and include the final solution step where the
802
      model is asked to solve the question again. Although most of such solutions and thus questions are
803
      screened out in the question validation step and consistency check at the end of the final solution
804   check, in rare cases we see this behavior in the solution produced after the final solution step as well.

               interval [0, 2π).
