011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Inverse rendering aims to decompose the an image into geometry, materials, and
014            lighting. Recently, Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) based inverse rendering has
015            significantly advanced, bridging the gap between NeRF-based models and con-
016
               ventional rendering engines. Existing methods typically adopt a two-stage opti-
               mization approach, beginning with volume rendering for geometry reconstruction,
017
               followed by physically based rendering (PBR) for materials and lighting estima-
018
               tion. However, the inherent ambiguity between materials and lighting during PBR
019            and the suboptimal nature of geometry reconstruction by volume rendering only
020            compromise the outcomes. To address these challenges, we introduce Uni-IR,
021            a unified framework that imposes mutual constraints to alleviate ambiguity by
022            integrating volume rendering and physically based rendering. Specifically, we
023            employ a physically-based volume rendering (PBVR) approach that incorporates
024            PBR concepts into volume rendering, directly facilitating connections with mate-
025            rials and lighting, in addition to geometry. Both rendering methods are utilized
026            during optimization, imposing mutual constraints and optimizing geometry, mate-
027
               rials, and lighting synergistically. Employing a meticulously crafted unified repre-
               sentation for both lighting and materials, Uni-IR achieves high-quality geometry
028
               reconstruction, materials and lighting estimation across various object types.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Multi-view 3D reconstruction is a pivotal task in computer vision and computer graphics, serving
034
      as a cornerstone for various applications such as game modeling Gregory (2018); Lewis & Jacob-
035
      son (2002), computer animation Parent (2012); Lasseter (1987), and virtual reality Schuemie et al.
036   (2001). Despite the remarkable progress achieved by Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) Mildenhall
037   et al. (2021) and subsequent approaches like SDF-based neural implicit surface learning Wang et al.
038   (2021); Yariv et al. (2021); Oechsle et al. (2021), multi-view 3D reconstruction still presents chal-
039   lenges in bridging the gap between NeRF-based models and conventional rendering engines. Vol-
040   ume rendering, the core mechanism of NeRF, generates radiance without explicitly considering the
041   interactions of materials and lighting. In contrast, conventional rendering engines derive shading
042   through the interactions between surface materials Nicodemus (1965) and lighting.
043   To bridge the gap, inverse rendering, the task of disentangling radiance into geometry, materials, and
044   lighting, has garnered significant attention. This approach allows the reconstructed 3D model to be
045   directly integrated into rendering engines, thereby playing a critical role in downstream applications
046   such as game production Lewis & Jacobson (2002) and virtual reality Schuemie et al. (2001).
047
      Recent studies Zhang et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2021a;b); Liu et al. (2023);
048   Yang et al. (2023) have explored the inverse rendering task within a neural implicit surface learning
049   framework. These approaches typically adopt a two-stage training strategy. In the initial stage, vol-
050   ume rendering is utilized for geometry reconstruction. Subsequently, in the second stage, physically
051   based rendering is employed to refine materials and lighting under a fixed geometry as shown in
052   Figure 1 (a). However, the performance in the second stage heavily relies on the quality of geometry
053   reconstruction in the first stage. While these methods demonstrate the effectiveness on the objects
      with diffuse materials, they often lead to suboptimal results in reconstructing reflective surfaces,

      To tackle the challenges, we propose to integrate volume rendering and physically based rendering
078
      to simultaneously optimize geometry, materials, and lighting, aiming at imposing mutual constraints
079   to alleviate the ambiguity. While this idea appears straightforward, unifying the two rendering
080   pipelines is non-trivial. Merely integrating the two into a unified framework will not introduce
081   mutual constraints, since volume rendering lacks physical plausibility and severs the connection with
082   physically based rendering. Conversely, the optimization becomes intractable due to the ambiguity
083   among geometry, materials, and lighting. To address this issue, we depart from the traditional
084   volume rendering, which employs an MLP to predict radiance with entangled materials and lighting
085   as shown in Figure 1 (a). Instead, we integrate the principles of PBR into volume rendering inspired
086   by Liu et al. (2023). In this approach, radiance is also formulated as the interaction between the
087
      materials and lighting. We refer to this method as physically based volume rendering (PBVR).
088   By integrating PBVR and PBR within a unified framework, we introduce a method named Uni-IR as
089   illustrated in Figure 1 (c). At the heart of this approach lies a meticulously crafted unified represen-
090   tation for materials and lighting representations across both rendering methods, achieving mutual
091   constraints and optimizing geometry, materials, and lighting synergistically. In the lighting repre-
092   sentation, we consolidate the lighting representation for both PBVR and PBR through integrated
093
      directional encoding Verbin et al. (2022). Two distinct light MLPs are employed to model direct and
      indirect lighting, respectively, addressing global illumination and inter-reflection, which are crucial
094
      for differentiating indirect light from albedo and environment map. Additionally, we parameterize
095
      the visibility term through two distinct MLPs. One is probabilistic for PBVR, while the other is
096   deterministic for PBR, indicating whether direct or indirect lights should be used. For materials
097   prediction, we employ a shared material MLP for both PBVR and PBR.

102                  methods synergistically enhances the performance of inverse rendering.

104

      2.1   M ULTI - VIEW 3D RECONSTRUCTION
111
112   Traditional multi-view 3D reconstruction typically employs Multi-View Stereo (MVS) techniques
113   Curless & Levoy (1996); Fua & Leclerc (1995); Furukawa & Ponce (2009); Schönberger et al.
114   (2016); Schonberger & Frahm (2016); Xu & Tao (2019), with the objective of reconstructing scene
115   geometry from multi-view images. These techniques utilize multi-view consistency to establish cor-
116   respondences and estimate depth values across different views, yielding a point cloud reconstruc-
117   tion. However, MVS methods encounter challenges in reconstructing reliable geometry in specific
118   scenarios, such as surfaces with specular reflections and regions with low texture. In such cases,
119   multi-view consistency is compromised, potentially resulting in severe artifacts and missing parts in
120
      the reconstructed output.
121   With the recent advancements in deep learning, learning-based approaches utilizing implicit surface
122   representations have emerged, where neural networks are employed to map continuous points to
123   either an occupancy field Mescheder et al. (2019); Peng et al. (2020) or a Signed Distance Function
124   (SDF) Park et al. (2019). Unlike traditional methods, these approaches are immune to appearance
125   changes as they rely on 3D ground truth supervision. However, these methods typically necessitate
126
      additional supervision corresponding to the occupancy value or SDF for each point. Unfortunately,
      such supervision may not always be readily available when utilizing solely multi-view 2D images,
127
      thereby restricting their scalability.
128
129   The advent of volumetric approaches in NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) has sparked significant inter-
130   est in 3D reconstruction utilizing neural implicit surface representations Oechsle et al. (2021); Yariv
131   et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021). Subsequent research endeavors have continued to enhance the
132
      reconstruction performance across various aspects. Despite demonstrating promising performance
      in 3D reconstruction, these methods still struggle to accurately recover the geometry of specular
133
      surfaces. Therefore, in this study, we concentrate on the reconstruction of reflective objects, along
134
      with the estimation of material and lighting properties.

      2.2   M ODELING FOR O BJECT WITH R EFLECTION
138
      Recently, several studies Boss et al. (2021); Srinivasan et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021a;b); Verbin
139
      et al. (2022); Ge et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024; 2023); Fan et al. (2023) have
140   focused on modeling objects with reflection. Some studies Verbin et al. (2022); Boss et al. (2021);
141   Zhang et al. (2021b); Bi et al. (2020); Rodriguez et al. (2020); Kopanas et al. (2022); Liang et al.
142   (2023b) tackle rendering tasks, where they model view-dependent reflective appearances by decom-
143   posing a scene into shape, reflectance, and illumination for novel view synthesis and relighting.
144   Other studies Ge et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Fan et al. (2023); Liang et al.
145   (2023a) focus on reconstructing reflective geometry by modeling specular light more reasonably or
146   mitigating the effects of specular surfaces. For example, Ref-NeuS Ge et al. (2023) and NeP Wang
147   et al. (2024) reduce the impact of highly uncertain reflective regions while enhancing the significance
148
      of less altered areas. Additionally, ENVIDR Liang et al. (2023a) and NeRO Liu et al. (2023) adopt
      a more physically plausible approach to model specular color, leading to notable improvements in
149
      reconstruction performance. Typically, physically based rendering is employed to further estimate
150
      materials and lighting given well-reconstructed geometry. Despite achieving promising results, the
151   entanglement between illumination and materials compromises outcomes due to the ill-posed na-
152   ture of inverse rendering, inevitably leading to suboptimal results in many cases. In this study, we
153   propose a unified framework that imposes mutual constraints and alleviates the entanglement issue.

      2.3   I NVERSE R ENDERING WITH N EURAL I MPLICIT L EARNING
156
157   Inverse rendering Barron & Malik (2014); Nimier-David et al. (2019) aims to decompose image
158   appearance into intrinsic properties such as geometry, materials, and lighting. This task has posed
159   a challenge in computer vision and graphics due to its ill-posed nature. Recovering reliable intrin-
160   sic properties is particularly difficult because of the limited constraints added during optimization.
161   To address this challenge, most existing methods Zhang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023); Yu et al.
      (1999); Zhang et al. (2021b); Yao et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2023) employ a


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      geometry-first optimization framework. Initially, they utilize volume rendering to reconstruct geom-
163   etry. Subsequently, in the second stage, physically based rendering is used for materials and lighting
164   estimation. However, ambiguity between materials and lighting hinders the second-stage optimiza-
165   tion. These methods do not establish a connection between volume rendering and physically based
166   rendering, as they are performed separately in two distinct stages. Our approach integrates PBVR,
167   which incorporates the concept of PBR into traditional volume rendering to establish a connection
168   with PBR, and PBR within a unified framework. Featuring a carefully designed unified representa-
169   tion for both lighting and material representation, our method effectively imposes mutual constraints
170   and mitigates ambiguity.

      3.3   U NIFYING L IGHTING AND M ATERIALS R EPRESENTATION
271
272   All the radiance terms in Eq. equation 9, Eq. equation 10, Eq. equation 11, and Eq. equation 12 de-
273   pend on lighting and materials. Appropriately representing these elements is essential for effectively
274   imposing mutual constrains and mitigating ambiguity among geometry, materials, and lighting.
275
      Lighting Representation. Given the crucial role that global illumination and inter-reflection play
276   in distinguishing indirect light from albedo and environment maps, we utilize two distinct MLPs
277   to separately encode direct and indirect lighting. The direct light MLP ldirect (SH(ωi )) takes only
278   direction as input, ensuring a globally consistent direct environment map. SH(·) is the directional
279   encoding using spherical harmonics as basis functions. This model is applicable when the path from
280   point x to direction ωi is unobstructed. In contrast, the indirect light MLP lindirect (SH(ωi ), x) re-
281   quires both position and direction as input to accommodate the spatial variability of indirect lighting
282   across the scene. This model is used when the path from x to ωi encounters obstructions.
283   To establish a unified lighting representation, we utilize integrated directional encoding (IDE) Verbin
284   et al. (2022), which shows the integral of light in Eq. equation 9 and Eq. equation 10 has a closed-
285   form solution by representing the Li (x, ωi ) with spherical harmonics, based on direction and rough-
286   ness denoted as IDE(ω, k). Although the roughness term in IDE(ω, ρ) is defined by the von Mises-
287   Fisher (vMF) distribution, which differs from the roughness term in the GGX distribution used in
288   PBR, both serve similar functions by defining positively correlated concentration. We optimize the
289   roughness as the parameter in the GGX distribution and use it for lighting approximation in PBVR.

      5   L IMITATION A ND C ONCLUSION
523
524
525   Limitation. Although our method shows promising results in inverse rendering across various ob-
526   ject types, several limitations remain. Firstly, our approach does not account for shadow effects,
527   which are often incorrectly attributed to the albedo rather than diffuse lighting. Given our method’s
528   proficiency in recovering lighting and geometry, we are able to infer shadows based on the geom-
529   etry and highlights. We plan to explore this capability as part of our future work. Secondly, while
530   PBVR and PBR impose significant constraints on inverse rendering, they can lead to consistent mis-
531   estimations of materials. When they converge on the same incorrect material prediction, the unified
532   framework fails under specific conditions. We show some examples in the Appendix.
533
      Conclusion. In this paper, we explore the issue of inverse rendering for various object types, a
534   topic that serves as a critical bridge between NeRF-based models and conventional rendering en-
535   gines, yet remains under-explored. The inherent ambiguity among geometry, materials, and lighting
536   can significantly hinder accurate decomposition. Our method, Uni-IR, effectively addresses this
537   challenge by integrating physically based volume rendering and physically based rendering into a
538   unified framework. Both rendering methods directly reason materials, lighting and geometry. With
539   a carefully designed unified representations for both lighting and materials, our approach impose
      mutual constraints and achieve significant performance on inverse rendering task.

      A    O PTIMIZATION AND M ODEL D ETAILS

      Optimization Details. We employed Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) as our optimizer . Initially, over
      the first 5,000 iterations, the learning rate was progressively increased from 1 × 10−5 to 5 × 10−4
762
      via a warm-up strategy. Subsequently, it was reduced to 1 × 10−5 . For Monte Carlo sampling, we
763
      used Nd = 256 for the diffuse lobe and Ns = 512 for the specular lobe. We sampled 512 rays
764   for each batch. Each ray involved sampling 64 coarse points, 64 fine points, and 32 background
765   points, following the methodology in NeRF++ Zhang et al. (2020). We apply the standard gamma
766   correction to get colors in the sRGB space before computing the rendering loss. For Lvis , given
767   that up to 512 × Ns directions are sampled, computing v march for all directions incurs substantial
768   computational costs. Consequently, we capped the maximum number of computations for v march
769   at 8129 for each batch. The weights used in the loss computation are λPBR    render = 0.1, λeik = 0.1,
770   λvis = 1.0 and λmat reg = 0.5. Each model was trained over 200,000 iterations, requiring a total of
771   12 hours on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 Ti GPU.
772   Network architecture. Our network architecture, akin to NeRO Liu et al. (2023), includes a geome-
773   try network, a material network, two light networks, and two visibility networks. These components
774   respectively encode the signed distance function (SDF), material parameters, light representations,
775   and visibility terms. The geometry network, which parametrizes the signed distance function, com-
776   prises 8 hidden layers, each with 256 units. Unlike traditional ReLU activation, we employ Softplus
777   with a β parameter of 100 across all layers. Additionally, a skip connection Mildenhall et al. (2021)
778
      links the input directly to the output of the fourth layer. It takes a spatial position x as input and
      outputs signed distances. The material network comprises a material feature network and three
779
      dedicated networks for encoding albedo, metalness, and roughness. The material feature network
780
      accepts spatial position x, outputting a 256-dimensional material feature vector. This vector is sub-
781   sequently processed by three networks to predict albedo, metalness, and roughness. The material
782   feature network consists of 8 hidden layers, each with 256 units. Similarly, the networks for albedo,
783   metalness, and roughness each include 4 hidden layers with 256 units. The direct light MLP ldirect ,
784   indirect light MLP lindirect , and the visibility MLPs V PBR and V VR each comprise 4 layers, with each
785   layer containing 256 units. For all MLPs that take the position x as input, we utilize positional en-
786   coding with a frequency of 8. For MLPs that receive a single direction as input, positional encoding
787   with a frequency of 6 is employed. For both light MLPs, we use exponential function to get light
788   radiance in [0, ∞). For material network, we use Sigmoid to get values in [0, 1].

       The radiance and geometry are entangled and mutually affected due to the intrinsic nature of volume
1063
       rendering, where geometry determines the weights of radiance integration along a ray. If the radiance
1064
       is not well-estimated, the geometry is also degraded. This conclusion has been demonstrated in
1065   Ref-NeRF Verbin et al. (2022) and Ref-NeuS Ge et al. (2023). These methods reparameterize the
1066   radiance network as a function of the reflection direction about the surface normal, providing better
1067   modeling for radiance and hence improved reconstruction results.
1068
       In PBVR, the radiance is influenced by both lighting and materials. Correct lighting and materials
1069
       also contribute to better geometry, consistent with the aforementioned conclusion. However, with
1070
       only PBVR, the lighting and materials are often inaccurately estimated, especially in complex or
