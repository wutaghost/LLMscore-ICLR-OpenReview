011                                                                       Abstract
012
013             We introduce a novel anchor-free contrastive learning (AFCL) method
014             leveraging our proposed Similarity-Orthogonality (SimO) loss. Our ap-
015             proach minimizes a semi-metric discriminative loss function that simultane-
016
                ously optimizes two key objectives: reducing the distance and orthogonality
                between embeddings of similar inputs while maximizing these metrics for
017
                dissimilar inputs, facilitating more fine-grained contrastive learning. The
018
                AFCL method, powered by SimO loss, creates a fiber bundle topological
019             structure in the embedding space, forming class-specific, internally cohesive
020             yet orthogonal neighborhoods. We validate the efficacy of our method on
021             the CIFAR-10 dataset, providing visualizations that demonstrate the im-
022             pact of SimO loss on the embedding space. Our results illustrate the forma-
023             tion of distinct, orthogonal class neighborhoods, showcasing the method’s
024             ability to create well-structured embeddings that balance class separation
025             with intra-class variability. This work opens new avenues for understand-
026             ing and leveraging the geometric properties of learned representations in
027
                various machine-learning tasks.

050   1   Introduction
051
052   The pursuit of effective representation learning (Gidaris et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2018); Oord
053   et al. (2019)) has been a cornerstone of modern machine learning, with contrastive methods
      emerging as particularly powerful tools in recent years. Despite significant advancements,


                                                                              1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the field of supervised contrastive learning (Khosla et al. (2021); Balestriero et al. (2023))
055   continues to grapple with fundamental challenges that impede the development of truly
056   robust and interpretable models.
057
058
      Our research unveils persistent challenges in embedding methods (Wen et al. (2016); Grill
      et al. (2020); Hjelm et al. (2019)), notably the lack of interpretability and inefficient uti-
059
      lization of embedding spaces due to dimensionality collapse (Zbontar et al. (2021); Jing
060
      et al. (2022)). Certain techniques, such as max operations in loss functions (e.g., max(0,
061   loss)) (Gutmann & Hyvärinen (2010)) and triplet-based methods (Sohn (2016b); Tian et al.
062   (2021)), introduce complications like non-smoothness, which disrupt gradient flow. These
063   approaches also suffer from biases in hand-crafted triplet selection and necessitate extensive
064   hyperparameter tuning, thereby limiting their generalizability (Rusak et al. (2024)). Con-
065   trastive loss functions, including InfoNCE (Chen et al. (2020)), often rely on large batch
066   sizes and negative sampling, leading to increased computational costs and instability. While
067   recent metric learning approaches (Movshovitz-Attias et al. (2017); Qian et al. (2020)) have
068   made strides in improving efficiency and scalability, they frequently sacrifice interpretabil-
069
      ity. This trade-off results in black-box models that offer minimal insight into the learned
      embedding structure.
070
071   We present SimO loss, a novel AFCL framework that addresses these long-standing issues.
072   SimO introduces a paradigm shift in how we conceptualize and optimize embedding spaces.
073   At its core lies a carefully crafted loss function that simultaneously optimizes Euclidean dis-
074   tances and orthogonality between embeddings – a departure from conventional approaches
075
      that typically focus on one or the other (Schroff et al. (2015)).
076   The key innovation of SimO is its ability to project each class into a distinct neighborhood
077   that maintains orthogonality with respect to other class neighborhoods. This property not
078   only enhances the explainability of the resulting embeddings but also naturally mitigates
079   dimensionality collapse by encouraging full utilization of the embedding space. Crucially,
080   SimO operates in a semi-metric space, a choice that allows for more flexible representations
081
      while preserving essential distance properties.
082   From a theoretical standpoint, SimO induces a rich topological structure in the embedding
083   space, seamlessly blending aspects of metric spaces, manifolds, and stratified spaces. This
084   unique structure facilitates efficient class separation while preserving nuanced intra-class
085   relationships – a balance that has proven elusive in previous work. The semi-metric na-
086   ture of our approach, allowing for controlled violations of the triangle inequality, enables
087
      more faithful representations of complex data distributions that often defy strict metric
      assumptions.
088
089   Our key contributions are:
090
091         • We propose an anchor-free pertaining method (AFCL) for supervised and semi-
092           supervised contrastive learning
093         • We introduce SimO, an anchor-free contrastive learning loss that significantly ad-
094           vances the state-of-the-art in terms of embedding explainability and robustness.
095         • We provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the induced semi-metric embed-
096           ding space, offering new insights into the topological properties of learned represen-
097           tations.
098
099   As we present SimO to the community, we do so with the conviction that it represents not
100   just an incremental advance, but a fundamental reimagining of contrastive learning—one
101   that addresses the core challenges that have long hindered progress in the field.

104
105   2.1   Anchor-Based Contrastive Learning
106
107   In contrastive learning, anchor-based losses have evolved from simple pairwise comparisons
      to more sophisticated multi-sample approaches (Khosla et al. (2021)). The triplet loss (Co-


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ria et al. (2020)), which compares an anchor with one positive and one negative sample,
109   has found success in applications like face recognition (Chopra et al. (2005)), despite its
110   tendency towards slow convergence. Building on this foundation, the (N+1)-tuplet loss ex-
111   tends the concept to multiple negatives, approximating the ideal case of comparing against
112   all classes. Further refinement led to the development of the multi-class N-pair loss, which
113   significantly improves computational efficiency through strategic batch construction, requir-
114   ing only 2N examples for N distinct (N+1)-tuplets (Sohn (2016a)). Recent theoretical work
115   has illuminated the connections between these various loss functions. Notably, the triplet
116   loss can be understood as a special case of the more general contrastive loss. Moreover, the
117
      supervised contrastive loss (Khosla et al. (2021)), when utilizing multiple negatives, bears a
      close resemblance to the N-pairs loss. Nevertheless, anchor-based methods are sensitive to
118
      negative sample quality, which can lead to inefficiencies in small datasets and struggle with
119
      false negatives. It also relies heavily on effective data augmentations and large batch size,
120   with a risk of overlooking global relationships.
121
122
      2.2   Dimensionality Collapse in Contrastive Learning Methods
123
124   Dimensionality collapse, a significant challenge in contrastive learning, occurs when learned
125   representations converge to a lower-dimensional subspace, thereby diminishing their discrim-
126   inative power and compromising the model’s ability to capture data structure effectively
127   (Jing & Tian (2020)). To address this issue, researchers have proposed several innovative
128   strategies. The NT-Xent loss function (Chen et al. (2020)) implements temperature scal-
129   ing to emphasize hard negatives, promoting more discriminative representations . Another
130
      approach involves the use of a nonlinear projection head, which enhances representation
      quality through improved hypersphere mapping (Grill et al. (2020)). The Barlow Twins
      method (Zbontar et al. (2021)) takes a different tack, focusing on redundancy reduction
131
132
      by minimizing correlations between embedding vector components through optimization of
133   the cross-correlation matrix. Architectural innovations have also played a crucial role in
134   combating dimensionality collapse. Methods like BYOL and SimSiam employ asymmetric
135   architectures to prevent the model from converging to trivial solutions (Chen & He (2021)).
136   The use of stop gradient in these methods ensures that the models do not converge to pro-
137   duce the same outputs over time. Additionally, the use of batch normalization (Ioffe (2015))
138   has been empirically shown to stabilize training and prevent such trivial convergence, al-
139   though the precise mechanisms underlying its effectiveness remain an area of active research
140   (Peng et al. (2023)).
141
142   2.3   Explainability of Contrastive Learning

      The underlying mechanisms driving the effectiveness of contrastive learning remain an ac-
143
144
      tive area of investigation. To shed light on the learned representations, Zhu et al. (2021)
145   introduced attribution techniques for visualizing salient features. Cosentino et al. (2022)
146   explored the geometric properties of self-supervised contrastive methods. They discov-
147   ered a non-trivial relationship between the encoder and the projector, and the strength
148   of data augmentation with increasing complexity. They provided a theoretical framework
149   for understanding how these methods learn invariant representations based on the geom-
150   etry of the data manifold. Furthermore, Steck et al. (2024) examined the implications of
151   cosine similarity in embeddings, challenging the notion that it purely reflects similarity and
152   suggesting that its geometric properties may influence representation learning outcomes.
153
      Wang & Liu (2021) investigate the behavior of unsupervised contrastive loss, highlighting
      its hardness-aware nature and how temperature influences the treatment of hard negatives.
      They show that while uniformity in feature space aids separability, excessive uniformity
154
155
      can harm semantic structure by pushing semantically similar instances apart. Wang &
156   Isola (2020) identified alignment and uniformity as key properties of contrastive learning.
157   Alignment encourages closeness between positive pairs, while uniformity ensures the even
158   spread of representations on the hypersphere. Their work demonstrates that optimizing
159   these properties leads to improved performance in downstream tasks and provides a the-
160   oretical framework for understanding contrastive learning’s effectiveness in representation
161   learning. Together, these works lay the groundwork for a deeper theoretical understanding
      of contrastive learning, highlighting the necessity for additional investigation.

      3.1   Metric Space
165
166   A metric space is a set X together with a distance function d : X × X → R (called a metric)
167   that satisfies the following properties for all x, y, z ∈ X:
168
169         1. Non-negativity: d(x, y) ≥ 0
170         2. Identity of indiscernibles: d(x, y) = 0 if and only if x = y
171         3. Symmetry: d(x, y) = d(y, x)
            4. Triangle inequality: d(x, z) ≤ d(x, y) + d(y, z)
172
173
174
      3.2   Semi-Metric Space
175
176   A semi-metric space is a generalization of a metric space where the triangle inequality is
177   not required to hold. It is defined as a set X with a distance function d : X × X → R that
178   satisfies:
179
180         1. Non-negativity: d(x, y) ≥ 0
181         2. Identity of indiscernibles: d(x, y) = 0 if and only if x = y
182         3. Symmetry: d(x, y) = d(y, x)

302
303
      Our experiments were conducted using GPU-enabled cloud computing platforms, with ex-
      periment tracking and visualization handled by Weights & Biases Biewald (2020). We im-
304
      plemented our models using JAX/Flax and TensorFlow frameworks. For our experiments,
305
      we utilized the CIFAR-10 dataset Krizhevsky & Hinton (2009). CIFAR-10 consists of 60,000
306   32x32 RGB images across 10 classes, with 50,000 for training and 10,000 for testing.

309
      linear projection head following the ResNet encoder with layer normalization instead of

      3 classes. During the linear probing phase, we fed the projection of our frozen pretrained

474   7   Discussion and Limitations
475
476   In our proposed framework, the embedding space generated by the SimO loss exhibits no-
477
      table geometric properties (Figure 2, Figure 3) that can be interpreted through the lenses
      of stratified spaces, quotient topology, and fiber bundles. Specifically, we can view the
      overall embedding space as a stratified space, where each stratum corresponds to a dis-
478
479
      tinct class neighborhood. This structure is facilitated by the orthogonality encouraged by
480   our loss function, promoting clear separations between classes while maintaining cohesive
481   intra-class relationships. Furthermore, we propose considering a quotient topology in which
482   points within the same class neighborhood are identified, simplifying the representation of
483   the embedding space to a point for each class. This transformation not only highlights
484   the distinctness of classes but also emphasizes their orthogonality in the learned space.
485   Additionally, our method generates a structure reminiscent of a fiber bundle, where each
      fiber corresponds to a specific class and is orthogonal to other fibers. This fiber bundle-like


                                                     9

486
      organization allows for a rich representation of class relationships and facilitates a more
487   interpretable understanding of the learned embeddings. Collectively, these geometric in-
488   terpretations underscore the robustness and effectiveness of our SimO loss with our AFCL
489   framework in structuring embeddings that balance class separation with interpretability
490   requiring small batch sizes unlike other loss functions.
491
      While SimO demonstrates significant advancements in contrastive learning, our extensive
492
      experimentation has revealed several important limitations and areas for future research.
493
494   Redefinition of Similarity Metrics: A key finding of our work is that embeddings learned
495   through SimO no longer adhere to traditional similarity measures such as cosine similarity.
496
      This departure from conventional metrics necessitates a paradigm shift in how we evaluate
      similarity in the embedding space. Our SimO loss itself emerges as the most appropriate
497
      measure of similarity or dissimilarity between embeddings. This also presents challenges for
498
      integration with existing systems and methods that rely on cosine similarity. Future work
499   should focus on developing efficient computational methods for this new similarity metric
500   and exploring its theoretical properties.
501
502
      Sensitivity to Data Biases: Our method’s ability to learn fine-grained representations
      comes with increased sensitivity to biases present in the training data. This is particularly
503
      evident in the case of background biases in object recognition tasks. For instance, our model
504
      struggled to separate the neighborhoods of Dog and Cat classes even though it learned from
505   the 120,000th iteration to the 1 millionth iteration, despite having learned most other classes
506   effectively. This sensitivity necessitates robust data augmentation techniques to mitigate
507   the impact of such biases. While this requirement for strong augmentation can be seen as a
508   limitation, it also highlights SimO’s potential for detecting and quantifying dataset biases,
509   which could be valuable for improving dataset quality and fairness in machine learning
510   models.
511   The Orthogonality Learning Factor: The performance of SimO is notably influenced
512   by the orthogonality learning factor, a hyperparameter that balances the trade-off between
513   similarity and orthogonality objectives. Finding the optimal value for this factor presents
514   a challenge we term ”the curse of orthogonality.” Too low a factor leads to insufficient
515   separation between class neighborhoods, while too high a factor can result in overly rigid
516   embeddings that fail to capture intra-class variations. Our experiments show that this factor
517   often needs to be tuned specifically for each dataset and task, which can be computationally
518   expensive. Developing adaptive methods for automatically adjusting this factor during
519
      training represents an important direction for future research.
520   Computational Complexity: While not unique to SimO, the computational requirements
521   for optimizing orthogonality in high-dimensional spaces are substantial. This can limit the
522   applicability of our method to very large datasets or in resource-constrained environments.
523   Future work should explore approximation techniques or more efficient optimization strate-
524   gies to address this limitation.
525   Despite these limitations, we believe that SimO represents a significant step forward in con-
526   trastive learning. The challenges identified here open up exciting new avenues for research in
527   representation learning, similarity metrics, and bias mitigation in machine learning models.
528   Addressing these limitations will not only improve SimO but also deepen our understanding
529   of the fundamental principles underlying effective representation learning.
530

      8   Conclusion
531

533
534   Our AFCL method introduces the SimO loss function as a novel approach to contrastive
535   learning, effectively addressing several critical challenges related to embedding space utiliza-

      tion and interoperability. By optimizing both the similarity and orthogonality of embed-
      dings, SimO prevents dimensionality collapse and ensures that class representations remain
      distinct, even in lower dimensions, requiring smaller batch sizes and embedding dimensions.
537
538
539   Our experimental results on the CIFAR-10 dataset demonstrate the efficacy of SimO in
      generating structured and discriminative embeddings with minimal computational over-
