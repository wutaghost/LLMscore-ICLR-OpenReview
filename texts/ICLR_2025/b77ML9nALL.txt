011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated multi-task learning (FMTL) aims to simultaneously learn multiple re-
014            lated tasks across clients without sharing sensitive raw data. However, in the
015
               decentralized setting, existing FMTL frameworks are limited in their ability to
               capture complex task relationships and handle feature and sample heterogeneity
016
               across clients. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel sheaf-theoretic-
017
               based approach for FMTL. By representing client relationships using cellular
018            sheaves, our framework can flexibly model interactions between heterogeneous
019            client models. We formulate the sheaf-based FMTL optimization problem using
020            sheaf Laplacian regularization and propose the Sheaf-FMTL algorithm to solve
021            it. We show that the proposed framework provides a unified view encompassing
022            many existing federated learning (FL) and FMTL approaches. Furthermore, we
023            prove that our proposed algorithm, Sheaf-FMTL, achieves a sublinear conver-
024            gence rate in line with state-of-the-art decentralized FMTL algorithms. Extensive
025            experiments demonstrate that Sheaf-FMTL exhibits communication savings by
026
               sending significantly fewer bits compared to decentralized FMTL baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The growing demand for privacy-preserving distributed learning algorithms has steered the research
031   community towards federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017), a learning paradigm that al-
032   lows several clients, such as mobile devices or organizations, to cooperatively train a model without
033   revealing their raw data. By aggregating locally computed updates rather than raw data, FL aims to
034   learn a global model that benefits from the different data distributions inherently present across the
035   participating clients. Despite its promise, conventional FL faces significant hurdles when dealing
036   with client data heterogeneity. In fact, while the global model may perform well on average, the
037   statistically heterogeneous clients’ data have been shown to affect the model’s existence and con-
038   vergence (Sattler et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020b). These challenges are exacerbated in a decentralized
039
      environment where coordination is limited and direct control over the client models is not feasible.
      Recently, there have been several attempts to bring personalization into FL to learn distinct local
040
      models (Wang et al., 2019; Fallah et al., 2020; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020) since learning a per-
041
      sonalized model per client is more suitable than a single global model to tackle data heterogeneity.
042   These models are specifically learned to fit the heterogeneous local data distribution via techniques
043   such as federated multi-task learning (FMTL) (Smith et al., 2017; Dinh et al., 2022) that model the
044   interactions between the different personalized local models.
045
      FMTL generalizes the FL framework by allowing the simultaneous learning of multiple related but
046
      distinct tasks across several clients. Unlike traditional FL, which focuses on training a single global
047
      model, FMTL acknowledges that different clients may be interested in solving distinct tasks that are
048   related but not identical. By leveraging task relatedness, FMTL aims to improve the performance of
049   individual task models through shared knowledge while maintaining task-specific uniqueness. This
050   approach not only enhances the generalization performance of the models on individual tasks by
051   leveraging shared information but also contributes to tackling the non-independent and identically
052   distributed (non-IID) nature of data across clients. FMTL considers the different objectives and
053   data distributions across clients, customizing models to perform optimally on each task while still
      benefiting from the federated structure of the problem as well as the similarity between these tasks.

054
      However, existing FMTL frameworks are not without limitations. A major concern is the oversim-
055   plified view of task interdependencies, where relationships between tasks are often modeled using
056   simple fixed scalar weights. This approach captures only the basic notion of task relatedness but fails
057   to represent more intricate and higher-order dependencies that may exist among tasks. As a result,
058   these models may overlook subtle interconnections and dynamic patterns of interdependence, lead-
059   ing to suboptimal knowledge sharing and reduced performance in heterogeneous and decentralized
060   environments. For a comprehensive understanding of scenarios where task similarities are naturally
061   defined in vector spaces, kindly refer to Appendix F. Furthermore, a critical issue with the current
062   FMTL frameworks is the assumption that the models have the same size, which limits the applica-
063
      bility of FMTL in the case of different model sizes. Last but not least, to the best of our knowledge,
      apart from MOCHA (Smith et al., 2017), which requires the presence of a server, the interactions
064
      between the tasks are assumed to be known and not learned during training. Therefore, to address
065
      these challenges, we seek to answer the following question:
066
067   “How can we effectively model and learn the complex interactions between various
068   tasks/models in an FMTL decentralized setting, even in the presence of different model sizes?”
069   To answer this question, the concept of sheaves provides a novel lens through which the interactions
070   between clients in a decentralized FMTL setting can be modeled. The mathematical notion of a sheaf
071   initially invented and developed in algebraic topology, is a framework that systematically organizes
072   local observations in a way that allows one to make conclusions about the global consistency of
073   such observations (Robinson, 2014; 2013; Riess & Ghrist, 2022). As such requirements are a central
074   part of FL problems, it is natural to ask how one could utilize a sheaf-based framework to find an
075
      effective solution to the above question. Given an underlying topology of the client relationships,
      we employ the notion of a cellular sheaf that captures the underlying geometry, enabling a richer and
076
      more nuanced multi-task learning environment. Sheaves enable the representation of local models
077
      as sections over the underlying space, offering a structured way to capture the relationships between
078   tasks/models in FMTL settings. As an inherent feature of the sheaf-based framework, our approach
079   can support heterogeneity over local models. More specifically, our framework naturally facilitates
080   learning models with different model dimensions. Moreover, sheaves are inherently distributed in
081   nature and hence facilitate decentralized training. A sheaf data structure consists of vector spaces
082   and linear mappings between them. In this work, we model the underlying space as a graph, and
083   vector spaces are defined over vertices and edges, capturing pairwise interactions. Crucially, we are
084   required to learn these maps that constitute the sheaf structure, as a decision variable of our problem.
085   Learning these maps is instrumental in comparing heterogeneous models by projecting them onto a
086
      common space.
087   Contributions. This paper introduces a novel unified approach that fundamentally rethinks FMTL
088   and gives it a new interpretation by incorporating principles from sheaf theory. In what follows, we
089   summarize our main contributions
090
091          • Our proposed framework demonstrates a high degree of flexibility as it addresses the chal-
092
               lenges arising from both feature and sample heterogeneity in the context of FMTL exploit-
               ing sheaf theory. It may be regarded as a comprehensive and unified framework for FMTL,
093
               as it encompasses a multitude of existing frameworks, including personalized FL (Hanzely
094
               & Richtárik, 2020), conventional FMTL (Dinh et al., 2022), hybrid FL (Zhang et al., 2024),
095            and conventional FL (McMahan et al., 2017).
096
097          • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that proposes to solve the FMTL in
098
               a decentralized setting while modelling higher-order relationships among heterogeneous
               clients. Furthermore, unlike existing decentralized FMTL frameworks, we learn the inter-
099
               actions between the clients as part of our optimization framework.
100
101          • Our algorithm, coined Sheaf-FMTL, exhibits high communication efficiency, as the size
102            of shared vectors among clients is significantly smaller in practice compared to the original
103            models. Furthermore, exchanging a modified version of the clients’ models provides an
104
               additional layer of privacy.
105          • A detailed convergence analysis of our proposed algorithm shows that the average squared
106            norm of the objective function gradient decreases at a rate of O(1/K), where K is the
107            number of iterations, recovering the convergence rate of state-of-the art FMTL (Smith et al.,

      Federated Learning (FL). FL is designed to train models on decentralized user data without sharing
115
      raw data. While numerous FL algorithms (McMahan et al., 2017; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al.,
116   2020a; Lin et al., 2020; Elgabli et al., 2022) have been proposed, most of them typically follow a
117   similar iterative procedure where a server sends a global model to clients for updates. Then, each
118   client trains a local model using its data and sends it back to the server for aggregation to update
119   the global model. However, due to significant variability in locally collected data across clients,
120   data heterogeneity poses a serious challenge. A prevalent assumption within FL literature is that the
121   model size is the same across clients. However, recent works (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022b)
122   have highlighted the significance of incorporating heterogeneous model sizes in FL frameworks in
123   the presence of a parameter server.
124   Personalized FL (PFL). To address the challenges arising from data heterogeneity, PFL aims to
125   learn individual client models through collaborative training, using different techniques such as
126   local fine-tuning (Wang et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020), meta-learning (Fallah et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
127   2018; Jiang et al., 2019), layer personalization (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2020; Collins
128   et al., 2021), model mixing (Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020; Deng et al., 2020a), and model-parameter
129   regularization (T Dinh et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022a). One way
130
      to personalize FL is to learn a global model and then fine-tune its parameters at each client using
      a few stochastic gradient descent steps, as in (Yu et al., 2020). Per-FedAvg (Fallah et al., 2020)
131
      combines meta-learning with FedAvg to produce a better initial model for each client. Algorithms
132
      such as FedPer (Arivazhagan et al., 2019), LG-FedAvg (Liang et al., 2020), and FedRep (Collins
133   et al., 2021) involve layer-based personalization, where clients share certain layers while training
134   personalized layers locally. A model mixing framework for PFL, where clients learn a mixture of
135   the global model and local models was proposed in (Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020). In (T Dinh et al.,
136   2020), pFedMe uses an L2 regularization to restrict the difference between the local and global
137   parameters.
138
      Heterogeneous FL (HFL). Unlike traditional FL paradigms that assume uniform model structures
139   across all clients, HFL accommodates variations in model sizes, layers, and computational capabil-
140   ities, thereby enabling more flexible and inclusive participation. One of the primary challenges in
141   HFL is ensuring effective knowledge sharing and aggregation among clients with disparate models.
142   To tackle this, many HFL approaches leverage knowledge distillation techniques, where a public
143   dataset or a subset of data is used to distill knowledge from heterogeneous local models into a
144   unified global model (Li & Wang, 2019; Zhu et al., 2021). For instance, FedMD (Li & Wang,
145   2019) employs model distillation to allow clients with different model architectures to contribute
146   to a shared global model without necessitating architectural alignment. Additionally, techniques
147
      such as adaptive model aggregation (Zhai et al., 2024) and model compatibility layers (Setayesh
      et al., 2023) have been proposed to facilitate the seamless integration of diverse model updates. An-
148
      other significant aspect of HFL is the consideration of clients’ varying computational resources and
149
      communication capabilities. Moreover, recent advancements have introduced the use of parameter-
150   efficient fine-tuning methods to support heterogeneity in model architectures (Chen et al., 2024).
151
152   Federated Multi-Task Learning (FMTL). FMTL aims to train separate but related models simul-
153
      taneously across multiple clients, each potentially focusing on different but related tasks. It can
      be viewed as a form of PFL by considering the process of learning one local model as a single
154
      task. Multi-task learning was first introduced into FL in (Smith et al., 2017). The authors proposed
155
      MOCHA, an FMTL algorithm that jointly learns the local models as well as a task relationship
156   matrix, which captures the relations between tasks. In the context of FMTL, task similarity can be
157   represented through graphs, matrices, or clustering. In (Sattler et al., 2020), clustered FL, an FL
158   framework that groups participating clients based on their local data distribution was proposed. The
159   proposed method tackles the issue of heterogeneity in the local datasets by clustering the clients
160   with similar data distributions and training a personalized model for each cluster. FedU, an FMTL
161   algorithm that encourages model parameter proximity for similar tasks via Laplacian regularization,
      was introduced in (Dinh et al., 2022). In (SarcheshmehPour et al., 2023), the authors leverage a

      Sheaves. A major limitation in FMTL over a graph, e.g., FMTL with graph Laplacian regularization
183
      in (Dinh et al., 2022) and FMTL with generalized total variance minimization in (SarcheshmehPour
184
      et al., 2023), that we wish to address in this work, is their inability to deal with feature heterogeneity
185   between clients. In contrast, sheaves, a well-established notion in algebraic topology, can inherently
186   model higher-order relationships among heterogeneous clients. Despite the limited appearance of
187   sheaves in the engineering domain, their importance in organizing information/data distributed over
188   multiple clients/systems has been emphasized in the recent literature (Robinson, 2014; 2013; Riess
189   & Ghrist, 2022). In fact, sheaves can be considered as the canonical data structure to systematically
190   organize local information so that useful global information can be extracted (Robinson, 2017). The
191   above-mentioned graph models with node features lying in some fixed space can be considered as
192   the simplest examples of sheaves, where such a graph is equivalent to a constant sheaf structure that
193
      directly follows from the graph. Motivated by these ideas, our work focuses on using the generality
      of sheaves to propose a generic framework for FMTL with both data and feature heterogeneity over
194
      nodes, generalizing the works of (Dinh et al., 2022; SarcheshmehPour et al., 2023). The analogous
195
      generalization of the graph Laplacian in the sheaf context is the sheaf Laplacian. In the context
196   of distributed optimization, (Hansen & Ghrist, 2019) consider sheaf Laplacian regularization with
197   sheaf constraints, i.e., Homological Constraints, and the resulting saddle-point dynamics.

533
      fectively tackles challenges arising from data and sample heterogeneity across clients. By leveraging
534   cellular sheaves, our approach can flexibly model complex interactions between client models, even
535   in the presence of varying feature spaces and model sizes. The proposed Sheaf-FMTL algorithm
536   is communication-efficient, preserves client privacy, and provides a unified view subsuming various

      A     S HEAF -T HEORETIC A PPROACH IN FMTL
703
704
      A.1   R ATIONALE FOR A DOPTING S HEAF T HEORY IN FMTL
705
706   Sheaf theory provides a powerful mathematical framework for modelling and analyzing complex
707   relationships in FMTL. The adoption of this approach in our context is motivated by several key
708   advantages
709
710         1. Heterogeneity modeling. FMTL often involves clients with different data distributions,
711
               model architectures, or task objectives. Sheaf theory allows us to capture these heteroge-
               neous relationships in a structured and mathematically rigorous manner.
712
713         2. Local-Global consistency. Sheaves provide a natural way to ensure consistency between
714            local (client-specific) and global (network-wide) information. This is crucial in FMTL
715            scenarios where we aim to leverage network information to improve local performance.
716         3. Flexible representation. The sheaf structure allows for representing varying degrees of
717            similarity or difference between clients. This nuanced representation is more sophisticated
718            than traditional approaches that often assume uniform relationships across the network.
719
720   A.2   T HE I NTERACTION S PACE AND C LIENT R ELATIONSHIPS
721
722   The interaction space, denoted as F(e), plays a central role in our sheaf-theoretic approach to FMTL.
723
      It serves as a shared space where local models θi and θj are projected using restriction maps Pij and
      Pji , respectively. The projection into this interaction space provides a measure of client relationships
724
      for the following reasons
725
726         1. Common feature capture. The interaction space captures the common or comparable
727            features between clients, analogous to how principal component analysis (PCA) captures
728            the most important features of a dataset.
729
            2. Consistency enforcement. Our approach enforces consistency between the projections of
730            local models onto the interaction space. This is mathematically represented by the sheaf
731            Laplacian term λ2 θ T LF (P )θ, which penalizes discrepancies between the projections of
732            local models.
733
            3. Collaboration encouragement. By minimizing the sheaf Laplacian term, local models
734
               are encouraged to collaborate effectively, leveraging shared information to improve overall
735            performance.
736
737
      A.3   R ESTRICTION M APS AND T HEIR I NTERPRETATIONS
738
739   The restriction maps, represented by matrices Pij , are fundamental to our sheaf-theoretic approach.
740   These maps project local models θi onto the interaction space F(e). The intuition behind these
741   maps can be understood as follows
742
743         1. Feature selection. Pij acts as a feature selection matrix, identifying common or compara-
744
               ble features between clients i and j.
745         2. Information sharing. The restriction maps facilitate information sharing between clients
746            by projecting local models onto a common space, enabling effective collaboration even
747            when local models have different dimensions or feature sets.
748         3. Model comparison. In heterogeneous settings where clients have different model sizes,
749            traditional FL methods relying on direct model aggregation or comparison fail. The re-
750            striction maps allow for meaningful comparisons by projecting onto a common space.
751
752   A.4   D IMENSIONAL C ONSIDERATIONS AND T RADE - OFFS
753
754   The dimensions of the restriction map Pij are determined by the dimensions of the local model θi
755   and the interaction space F(e). If θi ∈ Rdi and the interaction space has dimension dij , then Pij is
      of size dij × di . The choice of dij involves a trade-off


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
             • Smaller dij : Results in a more compact representation of shared information, leading to
757            communication savings.
758

      C     D IFFERENTIATING PFL, HFL, AND FMTL
817
      FL has evolved to encompass various paradigms that address specific challenges inherent in decen-
818
      tralized data environments. Among these, PFL, HFL, and FMTL stand out due to their distinct ob-
819
      jectives and methodologies. In what follows, we elaborate on the nuances between these paradigms.
820
821
      C.1   O BJECTIVE
822
823   FMTL, PFL, and HFL each pursue distinct goals within the FL landscape. FMTL aims to enable
824   collaborative learning across multiple related tasks, ensuring that each task benefits from shared
825   knowledge while maintaining task-specific optimizations. In contrast, PFL focuses on optimizing
826   a single shared model that is then personalized for each client, enhancing performance tailored to
827   individual client needs. This can be achieved by introducing an L2 regularization term that restricts
828   the difference between the local and global parameters. HFL seeks to facilitate collaboration among
829   clients that may have heterogeneous models, allowing the integration of these models without re-
830
      quiring uniformity in their structures.
831
832   C.2   TASK D IVERSITY
833
      FMTL supports multiple distinct yet related tasks across different clients, leveraging the similari-
834   ties between tasks to improve overall learning efficiency and performance. PFL, on the other hand,
835   operates under the assumption of a common task distributed among clients, with each client develop-
836   ing personalized adaptations of the shared model to better suit their specific data and requirements.
837   HFL is designed to handle potentially heterogeneous models among clients, enabling collaboration
838   without the necessity of explicitly modeling the relationships between clients.
839
840   C.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
841
842   In terms of model architecture, FMTL is flexible in accommodating different architectures for each
843   task or client, provided that the relationships between tasks are effectively modeled to facilitate
844
      knowledge sharing. PFL typically relies on a common base model that is augmented with per-
      sonalized layers or adaptations for each client, ensuring a balance between shared knowledge and
845
      individual customization. HFL explicitly manages varying model architectures across clients by
846
      employing techniques such as knowledge distillation, which allows for the transfer of information
847   between heterogeneous models without requiring a uniform architectural framework.
848
849
      C.4   K NOWLEDGE S HARING M ECHANISM
850
851   The mechanisms for knowledge sharing vary significantly among FMTL, PFL, and HFL. FMTL
852   leverages the relationships between tasks to enable effective knowledge transfer across related tasks,
853   enhancing the learning process through shared insights. PFL shares a global model baseline that
854   serves as the foundation for each client’s personalized model, ensuring that the core knowledge
855   is distributed while allowing for individual customization. HFL utilizes advanced techniques like
856   knowledge distillation to transfer information between heterogeneous models, ensuring that valuable
857
      knowledge is shared even when clients operate with different model architectures.

1323   Figure 5 compares the performance of our proposed Sheaf-FMTL method with dFedU on four
1324   datasets: (a) the Vehicle dataset, (b) the School dataset, (c) the HAR dataset and (d) the GLEAM
1325   dataset using γ = {0.1, 0.3}. The experiments evaluate the performance of the model in terms
1326
       of communication rounds and transmitted bits. For instance, Sheaf-FMTL requires more com-
       munication rounds for the Vehicle dataset to achieve similar test accuracy compared to dFedU. As
1327
       the number of communication rounds increases, the performance gap between the two methods nar-
1328
       rows. However, when examining the number of transmitted bits, Sheaf-FMTL demonstrates a clear
1329   advantage, requiring fewer bits to reach higher accuracy levels. For example, Sheaf-FMTL with
1330   γ = 0.1 reaches a test accuracy of 0.85 with just 72 transmitted Kbits, while dFedU requires over 450
1331   Kbits to approach this accuracy. Similar trends are observed for the other datasets. Sheaf-FMTL
1332   recovers the same performance as dFedU in terms of test accuracy with a slight drop in test accuracy
1333   for the HAR and School datasets. Hence, the sheaf-based approach effectively captures the hetero-
1334   geneous relationships among clients, achieving the same test accuracy using fewer transmitted bits
1335   than the baseline dFedU.

1338
1339
1340
       In this section, we highlight some of the limitations of our approach, while outlining several potential
       directions for future work.
1341
1342   Dimension of the projection space. The current work assumes a fixed dimension for the projection
1343   spaces associated with the edges in the sheaf structure. However, the optimal dimension may depend
1344   on factors such as the complexity of the client models and the relationships between the tasks. Future
1345   work could explore methods for automatically learning or finding the dimension of the projection
1346
       spaces.
1347   Restriction maps. The proposed framework uses linear maps for the restriction and lifting opera-
1348   tions between the client and edge spaces. While this allows for efficient computation, it may limit
1349   the ability to capture complex relationships between the tasks. Investigating the use of non-linear
       restriction maps could potentially improve the capabilities of the sheaf-based FMTL framework.
