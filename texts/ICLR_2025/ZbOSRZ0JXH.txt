011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization is a favorable yet challenging property
014            for deep neural networks. The core challenges lie in the limited availability of
015            source domains that help models learn an invariant representation from the spuri-
016
               ous features. Various domain augmentation have been proposed but largely rely
               on interpolating existing domains and frequently face difficulties in creating truly
017
               “novel” domains. Humans, on the other hand, is capable of extrapolating novel
018
               domains, thus, an intriguing question arises: How can neural networks extrap-
019            olate truly “novel” domains and achieve OOD generalization?
020
               We introduce a novel approach to domain extrapolation that leverages reasoning
021            ability and the extensive knowledge encapsulated within large language models
022            (LLMs) to synthesize entirely new domains. Starting with the class of interest,
023            we query the LLMs to extract relevant knowledge for these novel domains. We
024            then bridge the gap between the text-centric knowledge derived from LLMs and
025            the pixel input space of the model using text-to-image generation techniques. By
026            augmenting the training set of domain generalization datasets with high-fidelity,
027            photo-realistic images of these new domains, we achieve significant improvements
028            over all existing methods, as demonstrated in both single and multi-domain gen-
029
               eralization across various benchmarks.
030            With the ability to extrapolate any domains for any class, our method has the po-
031
               tential to learn a generalized model for any task without any data. To illustrate,
               we put forth a much more difficult setting termed, data-free domain general-
032
               ization, that aims to learn a generalized model in the absence of any collected
033
               data. Our empirical findings support the above argument and our methods exhibit
034            commendable performance in this setting, approximating the supervised with syn-
035            thetic data only and even surpassing the supervised setting by approximately 1-2%
036            on datasets such as VLCS.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Deep neural networks have demonstrated remarkable achievements in various fields and applications
042   He et al. (2015); Devlin et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2021); Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021),
043   yet their effectiveness heavily depends on the assumption that the training and testing environments
044   are subject to independent and identically distributions Ben-David et al. (2010); Blanchard et al.
045
      (2011). Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization aims to learn model from some training distri-
      bution that can generalize well to unseen testing domains, usually with distribution or label shifts
046
      Liu et al. (2021). A typical scenario is domain generalization (DG) where multiple source domains
047
      are available and these available source domains aid the training of generalizable models that learn
048   invariant features and discard spurious ones. However, a significant challenge arises: the availabil-
049   ity of these source domains often becomes a limiting factor, hindering the success of current OOD
050   approaches in more challenging scenarios Qiao et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2020);
051   Wang et al. (2022), which can be attributed to the difficulty and high expenses to collect, not just,
052   data but data in diverse domains with annotations, which is sometimes impossible in critical areas
053   such as healthcare or extreme conditions (e.g. deep sea or space). Motivated by these challenges,
      domain augmentation is straightforward and multiple methods have been proposed to generate novel

054
      domains and images through mixup Yan et al. (2020), mixing of statistics Zhou et al. (2021), uncer-
055   tainty modeling Li et al. (2022b); Zhou & Konukoglu (2023) and convex combination Albuquerque
056   et al. (2019). However, these methods generally interpolate the existing training domains to generate
057   novel domains that still fall within the convex hall of available domains Albuquerque et al. (2019).
058   Consequently, the constrained number of source domains hampers the expressiveness of these meth-
059   ods, continuing to act as a performance bottleneck. On the other hand, Humans harness the innate
060   ability of the human brain to create novel domains as illustrated in Shu et al. (2023); Radford et al.
061   (2021) where a pre-defined set of novel domains and styles are utilized. However, this also requires
062   human labor which fails to scale to larger sizes. Naturally, an intriguing question arises: How can
063
      neural networks extrapolate truly “novel” domains and achieve OOD generalization?
064   Large language models (LLMs) Brown et al. (2020) have been shown to encapsulate a vast wealth
065   of knowledge and simulate human cognitive processes. Thus, a pertinent question emerges: Can one
066   harness the power of LLMs to produce novel domains and relevant knowledge, thereby replacing
067   the human in the above training process? Stemming from this primary query, we investigate how
068   we can extract knowledge of a specific task and produce novel domains from LLMs. A subsequent
069
      research question is: How can we leverage this text-centric knowledge from LLMs to instruct an
      image system that processes pixel input? State-of-the-art text-to-image generation models such as
070
      Imagen Saharia et al. (2022), Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022b) and GLIDE Nichol et al.
071
      (2021) exhibit promosing capability to synthesize photo-realistic images positioning them as the
072   optimal conduit between textual and visual realms. Finally, we seek to answer to what extent the
073   synthesized images based on knowledge can serve as Out-of-distribution learners that can generalize
074   to unseen testing domains. Following these problems, we are the first study to design a new paradigm
075   that leverages the knowledge of LLMs to extrapolate novel domains for training better generalizable
076   and sample-efficient models. With the ability to extrapolate any domains for any class, our method
077   has the potential to learn a generalized model for any task without any existing data.
078   In addition, we present data-free domain generalization. Data-free generalization endeavors to
079   enable a model across unseen testing domains based solely on task specifications (for example, dis-
080   tinguishing between dog and cat classes) without the need for gathering or utilizing any pre-existing
081   datasets. In the era of large foundation models, data-free domain generalization is formulated as
082   OOD problem with inaccessible meta distribution and domain distribution (detailed in Section 2.1)
083   – essentially, devoid of any real-world data. This scenario presents a significantly more complex
084   challenge than that encountered in multi-domain or single-domain generalization efforts. Moreover,
085   it holds pragmatic significance in democratizing machine learning, by urging the community to de-
086
      velop methodologies that are viable under stringent resource constraints. Such an initiative paves
      the way for wider access to and application of, machine learning. Our method not only addresses
087
      the challenge of data scarcity in DG problems but also underscores the potential of synthetic data in
088
      overcoming traditional barriers to machine learning implementation.
089
090   Extensive experiments on single, multi-domain and data-free evaluations demonstrate the effective-
091   ness of our proposed method. In both single and multi-domain configurations, we demonstrate
092
      that synthetic data in the extrapolated novel domains markedly outperforms baseline results across
      various datasets. On the more challenging data-free setting, our proposed method exhibits near-
093
      supervised performance in this setting, even surpassing the supervised baseline by approximately 1-
094
      2% on VLCS. Data synthesized via the knowledge from LLMs excels compared to the synthetic data
095   directly generated from text-to-image generation models. This demonstrates the ability of LLMs to
096   effectively extrapolate like humans and integrate this prior knowledge into the model.
097
098
      We also underscore the scalability of our approach by highlighting that as the number of domains
      escalates, the performance correspondingly improves. Intriguingly, this trend diverges from the
099
      outcomes observed when augmenting with synthetic data directly produced by text-to-image models
100
      reported in Azizi et al. (2023); He et al. (2022). This further demonstrates the pivotal role of the
101   knowledge derived from LLMs in mitigating overfitting to synthetic data.
102
103   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: In Section 2, we will first motivate our method
104
      from the perspective of the theoretical error bound for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization.
      Then we will detail our method design and specifications. Section 3 introduces the data-free gen-
105
      eralization and its potential usage in the era of large foundation models. Section 4 describes our
106
      experiment design, results and the implications of our findings. Section 5 introduces related work.
107   Section 6 concludes our paper and potential limitation of our work.

180   2.2   D OMAIN E XTRAPOLATION WITH LLM S
181
182   Given the aforementioned theoretical bound, our objective is to approximate µ with µ0 . Humans,
183   as evidenced in Shu et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021), usually can efficiently extrapolate novel
184   domains (by imagination), which is a good approximation of µ. Nonetheless, human intervention is
185   expensive and not scalable to arbitrary datasets. Conversely, LLMs not only embody a vast expanse
186   of knowledge Petroni et al. (2019) and exhibit comparable reasoning capabilities Qiao et al. (2023),
187   but they also present the benefit of being amenable to extensive sampling. To this end, we propose
188
      to query LLMs, in place of human, to extrapolate novel domains.
189   After sampling from meta distribution µ0 , we need to further sample from the domain distribution
190   to generate images in this particular novel domain. As discussed in Section 1, this leads to a gap
191   between the text-based knowledge output by the LLMs and the input pixel space of vision systems.
192   Text-to-image generation models (e.g. stable diffusion Rombach et al. (2022a)) exhibit the great
193   capability to output photo-realistic images through inputting texts positioning them as the optimal
194
      bridge between textual and visual realms. The synthetic images of extrapolated novel domains are
      used to augment the original dataset or train the models solely in a data-free fashion. An overall
195
      illustration of our paradigm can be seen in Figure 1.
196
197   Extracting Knowledge from LLMs. The objective is to approximate µ via LLMs as close as pos-
198   sible. This introduces a constraint whereby the generated novel domains must reside within the
199   high-density regions of distribution µ. To ensure adherence to this criterion, we purposefully in-
200
      struct the LLMs to conceive the most plausible and reasonable domains where a particular class
      would realistically exist. To better guide LLMs to understand the instruction and generate the re-
201
      quired response accordingly, we craft system prompts that include role description ([Role]) and task
202
      description ([Task Description]), as illustrated by the example in Figure 2. Numerous strategies exist
203   to solicit knowledge and novel domains from LLMs.
204
205   Dataset-wise query. The most direct approach entails querying the LLMs with comprehensive
206
      dataset information (i.e. all of the class names) and instructing the model to produce n novel do-
      mains. However, as the marginal distribution for each class might exhibit minimal overlap (worse
207
      when the number of classes grows), it becomes considerably intricate to sample novel domains that
208
      are both plausible and likely for all classes.
209
210   Class-wise query. Thus, we propose to query the LLMs for novel domains of specific classes. For
211   each class in the task, we query the LLMs for knowledge and n novel domain information specific
212
      to that class. We repeat the process one class after another until all of the classes are iterated. We
      provide a example prompt in Figure 2.
213
214   Bridging text and pixel with text-to-image generation models. After obtaining a number of the
215   most plausible and reasonable domains of a specific class, we transform the text-centric knowledge
      from LLMs to pixel space by text-to-image generation models. This process is exactly the real-

      3   DATA - FREE D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
252
      We present Data-free Generalization, a new formation of generalization in the era of large foundation
253
      models. Given a task with detailed description and requirements (e.g. the classes to be classified and
254
      the definition of each class), Data-free Generalization endeavors to learn a model that can generalize
255   to this specific task and fulfill the requirement without collecting any data or utilizing any existing
256   datasets. Formally, this problem is formulated as follows. Task description and requirements spec-
257   ify the decision function f 2 F : X ! Y and the meta distribution µ. The problem then turns to
258   minimizing Equation 3, as detailed in Section 2.1. The difference is that now the meta distribution
259                                                                                  (1)         (i)   (n)
      µ cannot be sampled and thus we have no access to any training domains PXY , · · · , PXY , PXY or
260   images that are sampled from these domains. However, in the era of large foundation models, the
261   meta distribution µ can be approximated by LLMs while the domain distribution can be approxi-
262   mated by image generation models. Consequently, we can provide a guarantee on the learning with
263   Theorem 1. We provide one such method in Section 2.
264   Data-free generalization can not only serve a more difficult setting to push the limits of current
265   OOD methods but also holds pragmatic significance in democratizing machine learning. It does so
266   by mitigating or potentially eliminating the necessity for data collection and annotation within the
267   machine learning pipeline, which facilitates a broader access to and application of machine learning
268   technologies, particularly for entities facing resource constraints. Envision a modest-sized enterprise
269   incapable of investing in the training of large foundational models, nor possessing the necessary time
      and funding to collect and label an extensive dataset for particular tasks. This situation aligns with

270
      the concept of Data-free Generalization, characterized by the availability of only task specifications
271   in the absence of accessible data. Our methodology offers an ideal resolution for such organizations.
272   Initially, they can leverage LLMs’ APIs for a limited number of queries to derive extrapolated do-
273   mains and scenarios. Following this, they may engage text-to-image models for data synthesis. This
274   synthetic data can then be utilized to either develop new models or enhance existing ones, thereby
275   circumventing the limitations posed by resource constraints.

286   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
287
288   Setup. OOD Generalization is evaluated on DomainBed Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2020) with four
289   datasets, i.e. PACS, VLCS, OfficeHome and DomainNet and we follow them on the the train-
290   validate-test split of each dataset to perform the hyperparameter search. For comprehensive eval-
291   uation, we experiment on both multi- and single-domain generalization protocols. In addition, we
292   propose the data-free domain generalization to evaluate training generalizable models in a data-free
293
      fashion with only task information.
294   Baseline. We set two baselines for our experiments, namely empirical risk minimization (ERM)
295   and ERM with exponential moving average (ERM + EMA). ERM with EMA is demonstrated to be
296   more stable and effective than ERM Arpit et al. (2022). It is thus adopted to perform ablation study
297   and analysis.
298   Implementation. We remove the dropout and follow the rest of the implementation as in Gulrajani
299   & Lopez-Paz (2020) since dropout is reported to harm some of the DG methods Huang et al. (2022),
300   e.g. RSC Huang et al. (2020). We adopt GPT-4 to extract novel domain knowledge and leverage
301   Stable Diffusion 2 Rombach et al. (2021) as the text-to-image generation model. We use one A100
302   GPU to generate synthetic images. All experiments of training ResNet50 and CLIP ViT-B16 model
303   can be run on 1 RTX3090 GPU.

390   4.3    DATA - FREE G ENERALIZATION .
391
392   Data-free Generalization Evaluation serves as a more difficult setting to evaluate our proposed meth-
393   ods. Data-free Generalization aims to generalize to unseen testing domains with only knowledge of
394   the task, i.e. the classes and definition of each class are available and no available data of any
395   kind. To simulate Data-free Generalization with existing benchmarks, we use all the domains in
396
      existing DG datasets as testing domains. To evaluate our method, we directly train models on the
      synthetic images generated conditioned on novel domain knowledge. Then the model is tested on
397
      all the available real images of the domains for evaluation. Results are illustrated in Table 3 where
398
      we achieve the highest performance of 79.9%, 86.9%, 67.4% with only less than 1% gap between
399   its multi-domain counterparts and largely surpasses single-domain counterparts. Notably, data-free
400   ERM + EMA presents an accuracy of 79.9% on VLCS outperforming the multi-domain supervised
401   counterparts by more than 1%. With the knowledge injected and novel domain extrapolated, this
402   empirical result illustrates the promise of achieving generalization in a completely data-free fashion
403   free of laborious data collection and annotation.

405   4.4    A BLATION S TUDY AND A NALYSIS
406
407   To fully understand the performance of our method, we perform an ablation study by first pro-
408   viding three baselines building upon ERM + EMA with minor modifications. First, we provide
409   larger batchsize baseline, which is used to ablate the influence of larger batch sizes incurred by the
410   additional augmentation data. Then, we provide class template baseline, which prompts the text-to-
411
      images generation model to generate synthetic images with the template ”An image of [CLASS]”.
      Then we will provide a third baseline, termed class prompt that will prompt LLMs to give a
412
      diffusion-style prompt (without explicitly instructing it to extrapolate novel domains) and use the
413
      generated prompts to query text-to-image models for synthetic data. Comparison is shown in Table
414   4. We can see that a larger batch size in fact has a negative effect while both template and prompt
415   baseline underperform our method. This ablates the influence brought by text-to-image models and
416   further underscores the importance of LLMs’ knowledge regarding the novel domain.
417
      Comparison between different knowledge extraction. We provide three approaches to extract
418
      knowledge regarding the novel domains of particular classes. Comparison can be seen in (b) of
419
      Figure 4, where we show that, overall, class-wise combined with LLM-generated prompt leads to
420   better performance than class-wise query only and data-wise query. This is because class-wise query
421   provides more plausible and reasonable novel domains given some class and LLM-generated prompt
422   further extracts knowledge regarding this novel domain and increases diversity in generation.
423
      Scaling to larger synthetic dataset. It has been widely reported that data generated by genera-
424
      tion models negatively impacts the model, especially when the number of synthetic images grows
425
      at scale He et al. (2022); Azizi et al. (2023). To this end, we investigate whether the performance
426   increases scales with more synthetic data from more extrapolated novel domains. We perform scal-
427   ing by prompting LLMs to extrapolate more novel domains and generate 64 image per domain. We
428   can see in Figure 3 that with more domains (larger n in Section 2.1), performance keeps increasing,
429   which is consistent with our theoretical framework. We also make a comparison with class-template
430   and class-prompt baselines and scale the two baselines by increasing the synthetic images to the cor-
431   responding size. However, these two methods both suffer from performance saturation and degrada-
      tion when synthetic data increases, which is consistent with previous studies He et al. (2022); Azizi

      5   R ELATED W ORK
507
508   Domain Generalization. Various approaches have been proposed to solve this problem, such as
509
      domain alignment Li et al. (2018b;c), meta-learning Li et al. (2018a); Balaji et al. (2018), ensemble
      learning Cha et al. (2021a); Arpit et al. (2022) and augmentation-based Zhou & Konukoglu (2023);
510
      Zhou et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022b); Xu et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2020); Albuquerque et al. (2019).
511
      Augmentation-based methods are closely related to this work, both with the intention of generating
512   more source domains to approximate the expected generalization error. However, these methods
513   resort to interpolation of existing domains and fail to extrapolate the ”truly” novel domains. For
514   instance, MixStyle Zhou et al. (2021) mixes the statistics of two samples by linear interpolation.
515   More recently, with the advent of vision-language models such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and
516   Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2021), researchers propose to utilize Stable Diffusion to identify
517   and cure shortcuts Wu et al. (2023) or CLIP to generate novel domain augmentation Vidit et al.
518   (2023). However, they all require some form of human labor to pre-define a set of domains or styles,
519   which makes them laborious and not scalable. Our work aims to solve this problem and achieve
520
      genuine domain extrapolation.
521   Language scaffolded vision aims to develop better and more robust vision systems with the help of
522   language. Our method also falls within this category. Clipood Shu et al. (2023) proposes to fine-tune
523   a CLIP model to adapt the downstream DG tasks by a text similarity aware loss. Min et al. (2022)
524   utilize an RNN as an explanation network enforcing the model to self-explain, thereby increasing the
525   robustness. Yang et al. (2023) utilize language models to produce a comprehensive set of bottleneck
526
      features and leverage CLIP to classify. With the help from LLMs, Yang et al. (2023) has pushed
      the performance of the bottleneck network to SOTA. Despite many works proposed, this research,
527
      to the best of our knowledge, is the first endeavor to investigate the potential of a Large Language
528
      Model (LLM) in facilitating the training of a robust and generalizable vision model.
529
530
531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      The limited availability of domains has been a prevailing problem in Domain Generalization. In this
534   work, we propose the first data-free learning paradigm that leverages the knowledge and reasoning
535   of LLMs to extrapolate novel domains. By bridging the text-centric knowledge and pixel input
536   space by sampling from text-to-image generation models, we are able to train generalizable models
537   with task information only. Extensive experiments have demonstrated that our method achieves
538   significant improvements over baselines and the state-of-the-art by a significant margin. We also
539   demonstrate a promising learning paradigm where LLMs’ knowledge combined with text-to-image
      generation models are sufficient to train a generalizable model to any task.
