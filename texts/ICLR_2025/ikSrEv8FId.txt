011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Astrocytes, an essential component of the brain’s neural circuitry, demonstrate
014            learning capabilities through bioplausible mechanisms such as presynaptic plas-
015
               ticity and hebbian plasticity. However, their integration into computational mod-
               els remains underexplored. This paper advances astromorphic computing tech-
016
               niques to emulate transformer self-attention mechanisms, leveraging astrocytic
017
               nonlinearity and memory retention to improve long-range dependency processing
018            in machine learning and natural language processing (NLP) tasks. Existing trans-
019            former models have difficulty handling lengthy contexts with thousands of tokens,
020            even with substantial computational resources. We propose Recurrent Memory
021            Augmented Astromorphic Transformers (RMAAT), integrating astrocytic mem-
022            ory and recurrent processing into self-attention, enabling longer context handling
023            without quadratic complexity growth. Our bioplausible model has been found to
024            outperform traditional transformers in experimental tests conducted on the Long
025            Range Arena benchmark and IMDB dataset. Specifically, our model achieves a
026
               significant reduction in memory utilization and computational latency. This paves
               the way for biologically inspired AI models by illustrating how astrocytic charac-
027
               teristics may enhance the performance and efficiency of computational models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The human brain, a marvel of biological engineering, relies on a network of diverse cell types to
033   perform complex functionalities. Among these cells, astrocytes are ubiquitous in critical regions es-
034   sential for cognitive functions. Astrocytes, which are star-shaped glial cells, have long been thought
035   to support neurons, but recent research has revealed that they also play an active role in various brain
036   processes. They control synaptic activity, assist in learning and memory, and alter how information
037   is processed and stored in the brain (Gibbs et al., 2008; Bohmbach et al., 2022; Oliveira & Araque,
038   2022). The diverse range of functions performed by astrocytes presents intriguing opportunities for
039
      incorporating their roles into computational models, providing novel pathways for enhancing ar-
      tificial intelligence and machine learning (Kozachkov & Michmizos, 2017; 2020; González et al.,
040
      2012; Porto-Pazos et al., 2011).
041
042   Astromorphic computing aims to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of machine learning mod-
043   els by emulating the nonlinear processing and memory retention capabilities of astrocytes, (Mia
044   et al., 2023; Kozachkov et al., 2023a). Integrating the principles of astromorphic computing with
045
      transformer architectures presents a novel approach to advancing artificial intelligence. Transform-
      ers have significantly impacted the domain of machine learning, specifically in natural language
046
      processing (NLP) (Vaswani et al., 2017). The self-attention mechanism is the key innovation of
047
      transformers; it allows models to determine the importance of each word in a sentence indepen-
048   dent of their position. However, despite the remarkable success of transformers, challenges persist
049   in efficiently handling long sequences. Conventional neural networks, including transformers, of-
050   ten struggle with lengthy data sequences due to architectural limitations (Wu et al., 2020; Bulatov
051   et al., 2022). Similarly, brain-inspired models like spiking neural networks (SNNs) have advanced
052   by emulating neuronal spiking behaviors and incorporating various bio-plausible synaptic learn-
053   ing rules—such as spike-timing-dependent plasticity, e-prop, DECOLLE, equilibrium propagation,
      among others (Lu & Sengupta, 2024; Bellec et al., 2020; Kaiser et al., 2020; Bal & Sengupta,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2022)—but they generally fail to fully integrate the essential role of astrocytes and the complex
055   interactions within the tripartite synapse. These models frequently overlook astrocytes’ capacity
056   for recurrent processing and memory retention, as well as their function as temporal and spatial
057   integrators (Kozachkov et al., 2023a; Mia et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Lines, 2025).
058
      In this work, we leverage the memory retention and recurrent processing capabilities of astrocytes,
059
      macro-modeled through short- and long-term processes in the neuron-astrocyte network, to tempo-
060
      rally integrate astrocytic functions within transformer architectures. We explicitly map astrocytic
061   short-term plasticity to spatial relative information, an aspect previously overlooked. This mapping
062   of short-term processes facilitates the integration of long-term plasticity, which is then incorporated
063   to form temporal astrocytic memory, enabling the model to process long sequences by compressing
064   context into memory tokens. We implement this compression mechanism derived from the neu-
065   roscience macro-model as a memory retention factor. Furthermore, leveraging astrocytic memory,
066   we introduce the Astrocytic Memory Replay Backpropagation (AMRB) algorithm, which signifi-
067   cantly reduces hardware computational burden. Through this neuroscience-algorithm co-design, we
068   propose the Recurrent Memory Augmented Astromorphic Transformer (RMAAT), which offers a
069
      unique learning approach, achieving higher accuracy in long-context machine learning tasks with a
      fivefold reduction in hardware memory utilization.

073   2   R ELATED W ORKS AND M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
074
075
076   While transformers have transformed sequence modeling with self-attention, their quadratic com-
077   plexity limits scalability for long sequences (Vaswani et al., 2017). Efficient variants like Reformer
078   (Kitaev et al., 2020) and Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) address this but often compromise model
079   capacity and long-term dependency capture. Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) offer energy-efficient
080   alternatives (Pfeiffer & Pfeil, 2018), with advanced learning rules like e-prop (Bellec et al., 2020),
081
      DECOLLE (Kaiser et al., 2020), and surrogate gradients (Neftci et al., 2019). However, SNNs typ-
      ically lack the capacity to handle long-term dependencies without major architectural changes and
082
      do not incorporate astrocytic functions or tripartite synapse interactions.
083
084   Research on integrating astrocytes into neural networks has predominantly focused on small-scale
085   models for specific functions such as synaptic modulation and homeostasis. For example, Nad-
086   karni & Jung (2007) explored astrocyte-mediated synaptic transmission, and Postnov et al. (2009)
087
      investigated astrocytic influence on neuronal synchronization. In addition, astrocytic models have
      been instrumental in advancing neuromorphic systems by enabling self-repair and improving robotic
088
      locomotion through homeostatic control of Central Pattern Generators (CPG). In self-repair mech-
089
      anisms, astrocytes play a crucial role in restoring synaptic functions, as explored by (Wade et al.,
090   2012) and (Han et al., 2023), where astrocytic retrograde signaling is used to repair damaged neural
091   pathways. In CPG systems, astrocytes regulate neuronal synchronization and plasticity, facilitat-
092   ing gait generation and learning in robotic systems, as shown by (Han & Sengupta, 2023). Some
093   other works in astromorphic computing have begun to explore astrocytic functions within machine
094   learning models. Kozachkov et al. (2023a) introduced astrocyte-inspired neural networks focusing
095   on tripartite synaptic plasticity, while Mia et al. (2023) leveraged astrocytic calcium dynamics to
096   introduce nonlinearity into the self-attention mechanism of transformers, allowing the processing of
097   information based on relative input positioning. However, they do not explicitly associate short-term
098
      plasticity with self-attention. Consequently, existing approaches tend to address isolated aspects of
      astrocytic function and fail to explicitly map both short-term and long-term plasticity onto machine
099
      learning models or integrate the temporal astrocytic plasticity necessary for efficient handling of
100
      long sequences.
101
102   Our work introduces a novel RMAAT architecture that leverages astrocytic plasticity for efficient
103   processing of long-context sequences. The main contributions of this work are:
104
105          • Temporal Memory via Long-Term Astrocytic Plasticity: By integrating long-term as-
106            trocytic plasticity, we form temporal memory that retains context during sequential pro-
107            cessing, enabling the model to handle long sequences by retaining contexts into memory
               tokens.

108
             • Innovative Astrocytic Memory Compression Mechanism: We introduce a novel astro-
109            cytic compression mechanism that efficiently condenses contextual information into resid-
110            ual temporal memories.
111
             • Hardware Efficiency via Astrocytic Memory Replay Backpropagation (AMRB): By
112
               employing the AMRB learning technique, the proposed model achieves a 5× reduction in
113            hardware memory utilization compared to conventional transformers, enhancing practical-
114            ity for resource-constrained applications.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
118
119
      In order to introduce the Recurrent Memory Augmented Astromorphic Transformer (RMAAT), we
      first discuss the computational neuroscience backbone that forms the foundation of our model. This
120
      backbone creates the macro-model responsible for translating short and long-term synaptic plastic-
121
      ities into the astromorphic self-attention mechanism. We then leverage these plasticities within a
122   machine learning framework to efficiently handle long-range dependencies, forming the RMAAT
123   architecture that combines biologically-inspired mechanisms with transformer-based processing.
124
125   3.1   S HORT- TERM AND L ONG - TERM P LASTICITY
126
127   To capture the spatial and temporal aspects of astrocytic plasticities, we focus on short-term plastic-
128   ity (STP) and long-term plasticity (LTP). STP involves rapid synaptic adjustments, while LTP refers
129   to lasting changes over hours to days, both critical for memory and learning (Min et al., 2012; Perez-
130   Catalan et al., 2021; Pittà et al., 2015; Li, 2024; Alberini et al., 2018; Gordleeva et al., 2023; 2021).
131
      These plasticities integrate neuronal, synaptic, and astrocytic dynamics across different temporal
      and spatial scales (Kozachkov et al., 2023b; Kozachkov & Michmizos, 2017).
132
133   Neuron-Astrocyte Network Dynamics: The neuron-astrocyte network integrates the dynamics of
134   neurons, synapses, and astrocytic processes shaped by short and long-term plasticities. Astrocytes
135   modulate synaptic transmission and plasticity via gliotransmitter release and ionic concentration,
136   stabilizing neuronal activity and forming attractor states—stable brain activity configurations encod-
137
      ing memories and behaviors (Becker et al., 2022; Mongillo et al., 2008). Using short-term plasticity,
      the network quickly adjusts to new information. These neuron-astrocyte interactions are modeled
138
      through differential equations, capturing the temporal evolution of synaptic weights and astrocytic
139
      influence (Kozachkov et al., 2023b).
140

419

422   These segments are then processed in a temporally recurring manner, beginning with the first seg-
423   ment. These segments map to the STP cycles in the neuroscience macro-model in Fig. 1. As the
424   RMAAT processes each segment, it utilizes the astrocytic memory compression algorithm discussed

429   segments is consistently transmitted, preserving a unified comprehension of the entire sequence. By
430   employing this technique of connecting astrocytic memory tokens, the model is able to effortlessly

531
532
      This research introduces the RMAAT, a transformer enhanced by bioplausible astrocytic mech-
533
      anisms. By integrating short and long-term astrocytic plasticity, RMAAT addresses long-range
534   dependencies in sequential data, outperforming standard transformers in both accuracy and effi-
535   ciency. With AMRB, the model reduces memory usage and enhances computational speed, making
536   it well-suited for resource-limited systems. Moving forward, further biological inspiration, such as
537   astrocyte-astrocyte communication through gap junctions, can be explored as potential avenues to
538   parallelize sequential processing and enhance computational efficiency. Future work can also ex-
539   tend RMAAT to more challenging domains like computer vision and machine translation, aiming to
      create more efficient, biologically inspired AI systems that closely emulate human cognition.

733   This algorithm describes the Astrocytic Memory Replay Backpropagation (AMRB), which effi-
734   ciently manages and updates memory states across multiple segments. By leveraging the bioplausi-
735   ble properties of astrocytes, AMRB ensures the preservation and propagation of context throughout
736   the network, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to handle long-range dependencies. The algo-
737   rithm is outlined in Alg. 1.
