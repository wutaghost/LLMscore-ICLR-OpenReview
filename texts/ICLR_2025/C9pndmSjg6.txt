011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The growing magnitude of investments in global markets has intensified the need
014            for sophisticated risk mitigation strategies in portfolio optimization. Traditional
015            portfolio optimization models that seek to minimize risk for a specified return
016            frequently incorporate cardinality constraints, rendering them as Mixed-Integer
017            Quadratic Programming (MIQP) challenges. These constraints elevate the prob-
018            lem to NP-Hard status, complicating the solution process. While heuristic meth-
019            ods have historically been favored for their direct approach to MIQP problems, re-
020
               laxation techniques offer a strategic alternative by simplifying MIQP into a more
               tractable Quadratic Programming (QP) problem. We first introduce an approach
021
               that facilitates the conversion of MIQP to QP by relaxing integer constraints into
022
               continuous domains and integrating integer conditions into the objective function
023            using Lagrange multipliers. This dual application not only eases the computa-
024            tional burden but preserves the integrity of the original problem’s structure. An
025            innovative diagonalization technique applied to the covariance matrix further re-
026            fines our method, enhancing the fit for integer variables, as Lagrange multipliers
027            are inherently biased towards continuous variables. We present a comparative
028            analysis of three distinct models, Linear, Dual, and Diagonal or Diag, each em-
029            ploying a unique relaxation strategy. Our research evaluates their efficacy in ad-
030            dressing the MIQP problem under cardinality constraints. In conjunction with
031
               heuristic methods, the refined solutions from our exact relaxation models serve as
               a starting point for further refinement using Genetic Algorithm and Neighborhood
032
               Searching Algorithm. This hybrid methodology yields results that not only rival
033
               but occasionally surpass those achieved by the latest models and the commercial
034            solver CPLEX. Our findings endorse the potential of combining exact and heuris-
035            tic techniques in portfolio optimization, marking a significant advancement in the
036            field.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      With trillions of dollars circulating in global stock markets, investors are increasingly seeking port-
042   folios that not only generate strong returns but also mitigate risk, making their investments safer.
043   The challenge lies in identifying the optimal combination of assets that maximizes expected returns
044   while minimizing risk, often measured by the variance of the portfolio. Striking the right balance
045   between return and risk is crucial for developing effective portfolio strategies.
046
      Markowitz (1952) introduced a model that examines the relationship between expected return and
047
      risk, measured by variance, based on the different weights assigned to securities or assets. This
048   mean-variance (MV) model uses historical prices to estimate expected returns and asset correlations
049   to calculate variance. By optimizing either the sum of expected returns or minimizing total variance,
050   the optimal weight for each asset can be determined. Later, Markowitz (1956) extended this work by
051   providing an optimization approach for quadratic functions, specifically variance, subject to linear
052   constraints and a fixed expected return. This formulation, a Quadratic Programming (QP) problem,
053   also introduced the concept of “efficient points,” which form what is now known as the Efficient
      Frontier (EF).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      While the Mean-Variance (MV) model is foundational, it lacks realism without incorporating prac-
055   tical constraints. For instance, Grazia Speranza (1996) introduced minimum transaction units and
056   bounds on asset weights into the model, and Chang et al. (2000) emphasized the importance of re-
057   stricting the number of selected assets, making the model more applicable to real-world scenarios.
058   The inclusion of constraints, particularly cardinality constraints, transforms the MV model from a
059   Quadratic Programming (QP) problem into a Mixed-Integer Quadratic Programming (MIQP) prob-
060   lem, which is NP-Hard (Pia et al., 2017). Several studies, including Guzelsoy & Ralphs (2007),
061   Guzelsoy & Ralphs (2011), and Feizollahi et al. (2017), have conducted extensive research on MIQP
062   using Lagrange duality models. Building on this work, Shaw et al. (2008) explored how embedding
063
      the constraints into the original primal model through Lagrange multipliers forms a relaxed duality
      model or Dual Model. Although these relaxations introduce errors and gaps in the solutions, they
064
      make it feasible to find solutions within a practical time frame. Shaw et al. (2008) demonstrated a
065
      significant reduction in solution time using CPLEX, and further research by Xu et al. (2024) showed
066   that the Dual Model can help establish a lower bound for the primal, facilitating solution approaches.
067   In addition to Dual Models, various heuristic methods have been proposed to tackle more realistic
068   models with constraints.
069
      Numerous heuristic and meta-heuristic approaches have been applied to MIQP portfolio optimiza-
070
      tion. Woodside-Oriakhi et al. (2011) explored Genetic Algorithms, Tabu Search, and Simulated
071
      Annealing metaheuristics, achieving better results than previous heuristic methods. Building on
072   this, Deng et al. (2012) introduced an Improved Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) technique,
073   which enhanced the robustness and effectiveness of portfolios, particularly in low-variance condi-
074   tions. Similarly, Lwin & Qu (2013) achieved competitive results using a Hybrid Algorithm. Further
075   advancements came with the Artificial Bee Colony (ABC) Algorithm, where Tuba & Bacanin (2014)
076   demonstrated a smaller Euclidean distance between solutions compared to prior methods. This ap-
077   proach was later refined by Kalayci et al. (2017), who added a feasibility enforcement procedure
078   to the ABC Algorithm. Recently, Kalayci et al. (2020) reintroduced the Hybrid Algorithm, con-
079   structing an efficient metaheuristic combining Ant Colony Optimization, Genetic Algorithms, and
080
      ABC Optimization, yielding promising results. In addition to the growth of ABC-based methods,
      Baykasoğlu et al. (2015) developed a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP),
081
      while Ertenlice & Kalayci (2018) explored swarm intelligence (SI) to address time complexity is-
082
      sues. Furthermore, Akbay et al. (2020) proposed a new method, the Parallel Variable Neighborhood
083   Search Algorithm, which demonstrated high efficiency in solving MIQP problems.
084
085   Although these heuristic methods have gained relatively high results, most only focus on the origi-
086
      nal primal problem, and their relaxation models also only respect the Primal Model. However, for
      Primal Model itself, it is not a solvable model as NP-Hard, and those heuristic methods also cannot
087
      guarantee for the solutions. Here is where exact methods are introduced. That is, the cardinality
088
      constraint could be relaxed into a linear constraint, which transforms the MIQP into a solvable QP.
089   Once the model is solved, the modified linear constraint then could be discretized back into the orig-
090   inal cardinality constraint. That would be a logical way for solving MIQP models. Considering all
091   the constraints including the cardinality constraint, using Lagrange multipliers, all constraints are
092   able to be embedded into the objective function, transforming it into a Dual Model. By pre-solving
093   the cardinality constraint or deriving its optimal values ahead to further refining the process, this
094   allows us MIQP to be solved without the cardinality constraint directly. The Lagrange approach
095   helps derive the expressions for the integer variables. Previous work by Fisher (1981); Nemhauser
096   & Wolsey (1988) has demonstrated that this Dual Model effectively relaxes the problem, offering a
097
      pathway to approach the solution by solving with respecting to the multiplier variables. Addition-
      ally, Li et al. (2006); Shaw et al. (2008) have provided substantial evidence supporting the efficiency
098
      of this method. Furthermore, recognizing that Lagrange multipliers are more suited for continuous
099
      variables, potential inefficiencies might happen when dealing with integer variables. To mitigate
100   errors introduced by the multipliers for discrete variables, a employment of a more advanced relax-
101   ation could help the model approach the solution better. Xu et al. (2024) proposed a method using
102   the diagonalization of the covariance matrix, establishing lower bounds for the diagonal matrix in
103   correlation and ensuring feasible solutions for MIQP.
104
      Analyzing MIQP is not only valuable for portfolio optimization but also has broad applications
105
      across various fields. For example, the most recent advancements include a real-time, mixed-integer
106   programming-based decision-making system for automated driving (Quirynen et al., 2024), and op-
107   timization in scheduling, such as solving job-shop scheduling problems (Ajagekar et al., 2022).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MIQP is also increasingly relevant in machine learning, where it is used for solving decision prob-
109   lems in which the objective function is a machine learning model (Anderson et al., 2020), processing
110   images and conducting gap analysis (Wang, 2022; 2024), and embedding the model as a layer within
111   neural networks to improve training outcomes (Ferber et al., 2020).
112
      In this paper, we introduce a trio of exact relaxation techniques for the cardinality constrained
113
      Mixed-Integer Quadratic Programming (MIQP) problem: the Linear Model, Dual Model, and Diag
114
      Model, corresponding to linear, dual, and diagonal relaxations of the primal problem, respectively.
115   These methodologies offer a novel perspective on addressing the complexities inherent in MIQP. To
116   augment the precision of these exact relaxations, we have innovatively combined them with heuris-
117   tic methods, thereby crafting an approach that leverages the strengths of both exact and heuris-
118   tic optimization techniques. Our comprehensive evaluation extends beyond mere application; we
119   meticulously define and examine the optimality gaps between the solutions of the primal problem
120   and those of the relaxed models. This gap analysis is critical for understanding the effectiveness
121   of our proposed models and for quantifying the enhancements provided by the heuristic methods.
122   The synthesis of exact relaxations with heuristic strategies, as presented in this work, showcases a
123
      significant advancement in the pursuit of solving MIQP problems. The integration not only fortifies
      the theoretical underpinnings of the relaxation models but also amplifies their practical application.
124
      Through rigorous testing and gap analysis, we demonstrate that our approach yields a more robust
125
      and effective means of navigating the complex landscape of MIQP, setting a new benchmark for
126   future research in the field.

      effectiveness of our method. For Section 5 we drop out the conclusion with our results.

      3     P ROPOSED S OLUTION A PPROACH
257
258
      To solve the problem, we employ a multistep approach integrating several methods. First, the Primal
      Model is relaxed into QP models. Starting with random initial solutions, with solutions generated
259
      by the QP models, an environment is created. Second, a Genetic Algorithm (GA) is applied to this
260
      environment, where a series of fitness evaluations are conducted, and the best GA solution is selected
261   for further refinement. Third, the solution is improved using a neighborhood search algorithm, which
262   enhances its accuracy. Finally, the refined solution from the neighborhood search is used to restrict
263   the original MIQP to a QP formulation, yielding the optimal asset weights. The following sections
264   provide a detailed explanation of each step.
265

267
268   For the input parameters, we require the expected returns for each asset and the covariance matrix
269   between assets. Additionally, we must specify the target total return, the number of assets to select,
      and the upper and lower bounds for asset weights.

295   MIQP Model into three models: the Linear Model, Dual Model, and Diag Model. Using the prede-
296   fined parameters, we can generate initial solutions from these relaxed models. Simultaneously, we

299   each value has an equal probability of being 1. This gives us a total of M = M Relax + M Random
300   solutions, which are then assigned to the environment for further processing.

302   3.2   G ENETIC A LGORITHM A PPROACH
303
304   After generating solutions within the environment, we evaluate their competitiveness using a fitness
305   function. This fitness function is defined as follows: given a solution vector b, we introduce a new
306
      constraint into the Primal Model, requiring that the selected variables in the Primal Model match the
      values in b. This transforms the problem into a QP. By solving this QP, we obtain the variance v,
307
      which is used as the measure of competitiveness. The fitness function is therefore f (b) = v, where
308
      a lower value of f (b) indicates a more competitive solution.
309
310   Using this fitness function, we conduct a competition among the solutions in the environment.
311   We retain the top p% of the solutions based on fitness and discard the rest. Next, we per-
312
      form a fitness check, where the difference between the worst and best solutions must not be
      too large. Specifically, we require that l ≥ (max{f (b)|b ∈ environment} − min{f (b)|b ∈
313
      environment})/ min{f (b)|b ∈ environment} for solutions within the environment. If this con-
314
      dition is met, the best solution is selected for the next step. Otherwise, two-parent solutions are
315   randomly selected, and we apply a crossover operation to generate a new solution.
316
317   The crossover is performed under the following rules: the new solution vector size is n, each element
318
      in the vector must be either 0 or 1, and exactly k elements should be 1. If both parents have bfather

      3.3   N EIGHBORHOOD S EARCH A LGORITHM A PPROACH
325
326   With the best solution generated by the Genetic Algorithm (GA) approach, which is either very
327   close to or equal to the optimal primal solution, we can apply neighborhood searching to refine the
328   solution further. In this approach, the GA solution is initially treated as the “neighborhood solution”
329   or neighboring solution 1.
330   To generate neighboring solution 2, we randomly select two values in the neighborhood solution:
331   one with a value of 1 and another with a value of 0. We then swap their positions. After that, we
332   compare the fitness of neighboring solution 2 with that of the initial neighborhood solution. If the
333   fitness of neighboring solution 2 is better (i.e., lower), it becomes the new neighborhood solution,
334   and we repeat the process.
335
      If neighboring solution 2 is not better, we proceed by generating neighboring solution 3 in the same
336   manner—swapping the values of 1 and 0 based on neighboring solution 2 and comparing it with the
337   neighborhood solution. This iterative process continues until either an improvement is found or a
338   predefined limit is reached.

      4.3   M ODELS G AP F ORMULATION

      In this study, we have explored the cardinality constraint of Mixed-Integer Quadratic Programming
      (MIQP) in depth, investigating various exact relaxation models, namely the Linear, Dual, and Diag
472
      Models. We have unveiled the unique properties and features of these models, laying the ground-
473
      work for the development of more refined solution strategies. Building on this foundation, we intro-
474   duced an innovative approach that integrates the strengths of exact relaxation models with heuristic
475   algorithms, specifically the Genetic Algorithm and Neighborhood Search Algorithm. This synergy
476   allows us to harness the precision of exact methods to approximate true solutions, while the heuris-
477   tic enhancements facilitate the discovery of solutions in proximity to those approximations. Our
478   rigorous testing, conducted using datasets from the OR Library, positions our method against the
479   renowned commercial solver CPLEX as well as other heuristic techniques, including Genetic Algo-
480   rithm (GA), Tabu Search (TS), and Simulated Annealing (SA). The empirical evidence demonstrates
481   that our approach not only competes with but also surpasses state-of-the-art results. The efficacy of
482
      our method is clear; it consistently identifies superior solutions for MIQP problems, underscoring
      the potential of combining exact relaxations with heuristic algorithms as a powerful tool for solving
483
      complex optimization challenges.

      a compelling case for the integration of exact and heuristic methodologies. Our findings pave the
