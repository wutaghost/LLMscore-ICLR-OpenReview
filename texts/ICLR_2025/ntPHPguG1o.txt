009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Detecting infringing products is essential for protecting intellectual property rights
012            and is often implemented as a product-patent retrieval task. Manual infringement
013            detection is extremely time-consuming, and artificial intelligence plays an increas-
014            ingly important role. However, most existing methods rely on natural language-
015            based retrieval due to the domain discrepancies between patent images and prod-
016            uct images. Due to the lack of sufficient annotated data, this work aims to address
017            the aforementioned issues in an unsupervised setting by answering the following
018            two questions: 1) How can we align the domain gap between patent images and
019
               product images using existing technologies? 2) How can we build a powerful
               backbone to jointly extract the features of patent and product images? Initially,
020
               we construct a dataset for patent-product image retrieval, which includes product-
021
               patent pairs and unlabeled data. To address the first question, we systematically
022            evaluate three unsupervised approaches to mitigate the domain gap between patent
023            and product images. The results demonstrate that jointly mapping patent and prod-
024            uct images to a new feature space is effective. To answer the second question, we
025            propose a novel supervised pre-training paradigm to achieve domain-aligned fea-
026            ture extraction for product and patent edge images. Extensive experiments using
027            various backbones and training pipelines demonstrate the superiority of our su-
028            pervised pre-training method. The dataset and code of this paper will be made
029            publicly available upon acceptance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, the rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI) has revolutionized various
035   fields, including natural language processing (Dong et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023), computer
036   vision (Kirillov et al., 2023), medical AI (Celard et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024),
037   and intellectual property protection (Krestel et al., 2021; Shomee et al., 2024). As an essential
038   component of intellectual property rights, patents have garnered increasing attention, and numer-
039
      ous AI technologies in natural language processing (Kang et al., 2020; Higuchi & Yanai, 2023) and
      computer vision (Kravets et al., 2017; Higuchi et al., 2023) have been applied to them. For exam-
040
      ple, during the patent application process, examiners can utilize AI to retrieve textual content (e.g.,
041
      abstracts, main bodies) (Shomee et al., 2024) and images of patents to ensure that newly applied
042   patents do not infringe upon previously authorized ones—an effort often defined as patent classifi-
043   cation retrieval (Kang et al., 2020).
044
045
      After a patent is granted, an important task is to ensure that new products emerging in the mar-
      ket do not infringe upon existing patents in the database—a topic that has received relatively less
046
      discussion (Krestel et al., 2021). In this context, most patent-related applications are based on nat-
047
      ural language processing technologies (Lu et al., 2020; Yücesoy Kahraman et al., 2023; Li et al.,
048   2023; Bekamiri et al., 2024; Suzgun et al., 2024). Companies responsible for intellectual property
049   protection, for instance, determine whether a product infringes on a patent by comparing product
050   descriptions with patent descriptions (Radford et al., 2019). However, according to our research,
051   methods that retrieve patent images using product images are seldom mentioned, primarily due to
052   domain discrepancies: patent images are usually represented by line drawings and sketches, whereas
053   product images are predominantly colorful RGB images captured by cameras. This domain differ-
      ence hinders more efficient infringing product retrieval.

      To address this issue, we pose two critical questions that need to be considered in the patent-product
055   retrieval task:
056
057         1. How can we alleviate the domain discrepancies between patents and products? This is
058            a representation problem; we need to determine a representation method that can mitigate
059            the domain differences between patents and products since the retrieval is performed in an
060            unsupervised manner.
061         2. Why does supervised pre-training outperform unsupervised pre-training in the un-
062            supervised patent-product retrieval task? This is a learning problem; we aim to train
063            a supervised feature encoder that can better learn high-dimensional features conducive to
064            retrieval.
065
066   1.1   A1: A LLEVIATING D OMAIN D ISCREPANCIES THROUGH F EATURE M APPING
067
068   Mapping product and patent images into a new feature space helps alleviate domain discrepancies.
069   There are three strategies to align the image features of products and patents:
070
            1. Converting abstract patent line drawings into product images: The challenge here is
071
               that patent line drawings are binarized sketches (Shomee et al., 2024), whereas product
072
               images contain rich color information. Current generative models may incorrectly col-
073            orize patent line drawings—for example, assigning inaccurate colors to specific compo-
074            nents—which leads to discrepancies between the colorized images and actual product im-
075            ages (as shown in Fig. 1).
076
            2. Transforming product images into abstract line drawings: This approach faces the lim-
077
               itations of existing algorithms. Patent line drawings are often composed of continuous
078            lines, but current edge extraction algorithms struggle to produce continuous edges in the
079            extracted results, introducing domain discrepancies.
080
            3. Mapping both product and patent images into a common feature space: By utilizing
081
               an edge extraction algorithm to simultaneously extract edge features from both patent and
082            product images, this method effectively mitigates the domain discrepancies highlighted
083            in the second strategy. We adopt this strategy, as it offers the advantage of reducing the
084            domain gap by mapping the product and patent images into the same feature space. Further
085            analysis and experiments are presented in Fig. 1 and Fig. 3.
086
087   1.2   A2: A DVANTAGES OF S UPERVISED P RE - TRAINING OVER U NSUPERVISED
088         P RE - TRAINING
089
090   In the context of unsupervised patent-product retrieval, a significant challenge is the scarcity of
091   large-scale paired products and patent images for training. To address this, we opt to use alternative
092   proxy tasks, such as classification and segmentation, to train a backbone network for edge feature
093
      extraction (Zhou et al., 2024). Unsupervised pre-training methods (e.g., DINO (Caron et al., 2021),
      iBOT (Zhou et al., 2022), MAE (He et al., 2022), EVA (Fang et al., 2023; 2024)) face challenges due
094
      to the inherent sparsity of edge maps, which lack the rich semantic information necessary for effec-
095
      tive training. For instance, with the MAE algorithm, since most regions of an edge map are white,
096   the task effectively becomes reconstructing original white patches from masked white patches—an
097   inherently difficult learning process. Moreover, because of the high degree of redundancy (Haghighi
098   et al., 2021) and noise (Mahajan et al., 2018) of unlabeled data, and the requires more computational
099   resources and time of supervised pre-training (Chen et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2022), supervised pre-
100   training, with its explicit learning objectives, outperforms unsupervised pre-training (Li et al., 2024).
101
      Based on these considerations, we propose a supervised pre-training scheme tailor-designed for the
102   unsupervised patent retrieval task. During inference, we extract edge maps from both product and
103   patent images and employ a feature encoder to encode these images. We determine the similarity
104   between products and patents by calculating the cosine similarity of the encoded features, thus
105   obtaining the retrieval results. To develop an effective edge map encoder, we adopt a simple yet
106   effective supervised training method: we first extract edge map features from images, then use the
107   corresponding classification results of these edge maps as supervision to train the encoder (He et al.,
      2016). This approach yields a feature encoder capable of encoding both product and patent images.

127   2.1   PATENT R ETRIEVAL M ETHODS
128
      AI techniques have been leveraged to improve the accuracy and efficiency of retrieving relevant
129
      patents (Shomee et al., 2024). In text-based retrieval, Kang et al. (2020) proposed a patent method
130
      prior art search using deep learning language models. Their approach utilizes semantic embeddings
131   to capture the contextual meaning of patent documents, resulting in improved retrieval performance
132   over traditional keyword-based methods. Additionally, Siddharth et al. (2022) enhanced patent re-
133   trieval by combining text embeddings with knowledge graph embeddings, demonstrating that inte-
134   grating external knowledge sources can further refine search results. Lo et al. (2024) proposed to
135   use large language models for patent retrieval.
136
      For image-based retrieval, given that patents often include technical drawings and diagrams, image-
137   based retrieval has emerged as an important area of study. Kravets et al. (2017) focused on improving
138   the quality of convolutional neural network (CNN) (LeCun et al., 1995) training datasets for patent
139   image retrieval, emphasizing the importance of dataset quality in model performance. Building
140   on this, Higuchi et al. (2023) introduced methods using cross-entropy-based metric learning to en-
141   hance the accuracy of patent image retrieval systems. In another study, Higuchi & Yanai (2023)
142   proposed transformer-based deep metric learning for patent image retrieval, showcasing the effec-
143   tiveness of transformer architectures in this domain. Although some works use sketches to retrieve
144   images (Parmar et al., 2024; Koley et al., 2024b;a), sketches and patent images are still different,
145
      as patent images usually contain additional noise (e.g., numbers and reference lines), and the lines
      are thinner than those in sketches. In this work, we aim to address the domain gap in unsupervised
146
      retrieval between product and patent images. It is worth noting that, unlike the unsupervised do-
147
      main adaptation setting (Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015; Pinheiro, 2018; Xu et al., 2023; Gong et al.,
148   2024), where labeled source domain data are available, in our setting we cannot obtain any labeled
149   patent-product image pairs due to the labor-intensive nature of labeling.

      PPIR Testing Set and Patent Database Due to the absence of existing datasets containing infring-

      ing product and patent image pairs, we constructed a test set to facilitate retrieval from patent images
191
      to product images. Specifically, let the product dataset be Dr and the patent dataset be Dp . For a
192   product xi from the product dataset Dr , we aim to find its corresponding patent yj in the patent
193   dataset Dp . We represent the labels as the correspondence between infringing products and patent
194   images, forming a set R = {(xi , yij ) | i = 1, . . . , I = 240}. In this work, we collected 240 product-
195   patent image pairs to evaluate the unsupervised retrieval of product-patent. Based on the matched
196   product-patent image pairs R, we establish the following settings to make our image-based retrieval
197   task practical in real-world situations. Considering that the number of patent images in the database
198   is usually much larger than the number of product images to be retrieved, and the language-based
199   methods are prevailing for the retrieval, we employed a language model Kenton & Toutanova (2019)
200
      to match and filter the descriptions of products and patents. For each product image, we obtained
      the top 2,500 potential corresponding patents. Ultimately, we retained 16,850 items from the patent
201
      database for patent-product matching.
202
203   PPIR-unlabeled: A Pre-training Datasets To evaluate the effectiveness of unsupervised pre-
204   training algorithms, we randomly extracted over 2 million images from the patent database and
205   over 1 million product images from Amazon to pre-train an encoder capable of modeling patent line
206
      drawing features. There are 3,799,695 images in total. We first use the edge extractor to extract
      the edge of these images, then we use the self(un)-supervised learning approaches to pre-train the
207
      backbone on the image database.
208
209   For the supervised pre-training, we simply adopt the ImageNet1K (Deng et al., 2009) as the training
210   set, which contains 1,281,167 images with 1,000 categories in total. We also extract the edge map
211   of the samples from the ImageNet1K to get ImageNet1K-edge and use the label for classification as
212
      supervision.

214   performance by calculating the similarity between patent image features and product image features.
215   In our retrieval task, there is a one vs many situation, which indicates that given that one pair, we
      have one product image and many patent images. We choose to use the average similarity of all the

221   3.2   J OINT F EATURE M APPING FOR U NSUPERVISED P RODUCT-PATENT I MAGE A LIGNMENT
222
223   Retrieving patents for infringing products is complex and time-consuming, heavily relying on patent
224   experts to search through massive patent databases to identify potential infringing patents (Shomee
225   et al., 2024). This reliance makes it challenging to obtain a large-scale paired dataset of product-
226
      patent images to train models in a supervised manner. Therefore, this paper focuses on unsu-
      pervised product-patent image retrieval by leveraging existing feature encoders to obtain high-
227
      dimensional features of products and patent images, thereby enabling product retrieval in extensive
228
      patent databases.
229
230   However, unsupervised methods face a primary challenge: the domain discrepancy between product
231   images and patent images. Specifically, product images are RGB images containing rich color,
232
      texture, and other information (Ge et al., 2022), while patent images are line drawings that abstractly
      represent products (Koley et al., 2023; Voynov et al., 2023). Resolving the domain differences
233
      between product and patent images thus becomes our primary task. In this work, we consider
234
      three unsupervised methods to achieve feature alignment between patent and product images. These
235   three unsupervised feature alignment approaches and their specific implementations are described
236   as follows.
237
238         1. Converting product images into abstract line drawings for feature alignment with
239            patent images. We employ an edge feature extractor Eedge (·) (Zhou et al., 2024) to extract
240            edge features from product images Ir , converting them into line drawings Ir′ = Eedge (Ir ).
241            The advantage of this method is its computational efficiency and ease of implementation.
242
               However, it heavily depends on the accuracy of the edge feature extraction algorithm. Un-
               der varying lighting and texture conditions, the extracted edge maps from product images
243
               may exhibit discontinuities, affecting the accuracy of feature alignment. Additionally, we
244
               lack an encoder capable of extracting robust features from edge maps to achieve efficient
245            line-drawing representation.
246
            2. Converting patent images into product images to align features between the two. Re-
247
               cently, generative models (Ho et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2024) have gained
248
               increasing attention, with many models capable of efficiently generating RGB images based
249            on line drawings (Parmar et al., 2024; Koley et al., 2024b;a). We utilize a generative
250            model G(·) (Parmar et al., 2024) to transform patent images into pseudo-product images,
251            Ip′ = G(Ip ). The advantage of this method is that the generated images are in RGB format,
252            allowing direct use of feature encoders pre-trained on natural images for feature extraction.
253            However, challenges include the significant computational cost of generative models and
254            the lack of specific domain knowledge, resulting in generated images whose color distribu-
255            tion and texture may differ substantially from natural images.
256         3. Simultaneously mapping patent images and product images into a common feature
257            space for unsupervised alignment. The essence of this method is to use the same image
258            feature extraction T (·) to perform feature transformations on both patent images and prod-
259            uct images simultaneously, i.e., Ip′ = T (Ip ) and Ir′ = T (Ir ). In this work, we extract edge
260            maps from both patent and product images using the same edge detector Eedge (·) (Zhou
261            et al., 2024), setting T (·) = Eedge (·). After undergoing identical feature mappings, we
262
               obtain more similar high-dimensional feature representations. The drawback of this ap-
               proach is the absence of an efficient edge map encoder capable of extracting discriminative
263
               features from the transformed images.
264
265   We conducted extensive evaluations of the above three approaches using ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016),
266   pre-trained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), for feature extraction. The results shown in Fig. 1
267   indicate that simultaneously mapping patent images and product images into a common feature
268   space is beneficial for joint feature matching. However, the lack of an efficient edge map encoder
269   still limits the further performance improvement of this method. In the next section, we explore the
      supervised pre-training to get a powerful feature encoder on the edge map.

      4.1   A NALYSIS ON D OMAIN G AP
384
385   In this section, we primarily investigate the unsupervised approaches introduced in Section 3.2 to
386   mitigate the domain gap between product images and patent images. Fig. 3 presents a quantitative
387   analysis of the methods illustrated in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2. It can be observed that both extracting edges
388   from product images and transforming patent images into product images significantly improve the
389   performance of product-patent image retrieval tasks compared to using visual encoders pre-trained
390   on natural images. Furthermore, by using a generative model to convert patent images into product
391   images, their feature spaces are aligned with the domain of natural images, which enables the pre-
392   trained ImageNet encoder to achieve considerable performance. This result is similar to directly
393
      mapping both product and patent images to the same feature space using an edge extractor.
394   However, the aforementioned methods have an efficiency drawback. Considering the massive num-
395   ber of patents that need to be matched in the patent database, even when retrieval is performed via
396   natural language, each inference requires colorizing the images, which is much less efficient than
397   conventional image retrieval. Converting images into edge maps (28 imgs/s) (Zhou et al., 2024) is
398   much more efficient than transforming edge maps into product images (2 imgs/s) (Parmar et al.,
399
      2024), and there may also be feature mismatch issues in image colorization. Therefore, we con-
      sider whether we can train a supervised edge map feature encoder to achieve better performance.
400
      We present this result in Fig. 3-(1). We first extract edges from the ImageNet1k dataset and train a
401
      model from scratch without using the pre-trained weights on color images. It can be observed that,
402   compared to image colorization, our method achieves better performance. In summary, these tables
403   demonstrate that both JointMap and Patent2Product are effective alignment methods. Moreover, by
404   pretraining a backbone network for edge map classification using supervised learning, our method
405   achieves better results.
406
      Considering that ImageNet pre-trained weights are intrinsically available, in Fig. 3-(2) we show a
407
      comparison between fine-tuning ImageNet pre-trained backbone networks using edge maps and
408   training from scratch on edge maps. We find that fine-tuning ImageNet pre-trained networks
409   achieves better performance and requires fewer training epochs compared to training from scratch
410   on edge maps. This may be due to the transferability of features learned from natural images to
411   edge maps, as pre-trained models capture low-level features such as edges and textures that are
412   also present in edge maps (Yosinski et al., 2014). In Fig. 3-(3), we explore two different matching
413   methods. In our setting, each product image corresponds to one patent, and each patent contains N
414   images. We compute the similarity between the product image and each of the N patent images,
415   obtaining a 1 × N similarity vector. We can either select the maximum value in this similarity
416
      vector to represent the similarity of the product-patent pair or use the average of this vector. We
      investigate both methods and find that the results show almost no difference (the two curves nearly
417
      overlap). Therefore, in our subsequent experiments, we use the average value of the similarity vector
418
      to measure the similarity of the product-patent image pair.

      5                   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
515
516
517
      In this paper, we explored a novel product-patent image retrieval task and conducted an in-depth
      investigation into the application of supervised pretraining based on edge maps within this context.
518
      We began by constructing a retrieval pair dataset, a patent database for retrieval, and a patent-product
519
      database for unsupervised pretraining. The datasets provided in this paper establish an effective
520   method for evaluating both unsupervised and supervised patent retrieval tasks.
521
522   Our experimental results demonstrate that mapping both patent images and product images into
523
      edge maps or colorizing patent images into product images, are effective strategies for alleviating
      the domain gap. Furthermore, by simply converting existing classification data into edge maps and
524
      setting up a supervised edge classification task, we can enhance the feature extraction performance
525
      of our backbone network. Besides, we have make the following findings through our extensive
526   experiments: 1. Mapping patent and product to joint edge representation, or transform the
527   patent image to product is beneficial for alleviate the domain gap. 2. Supervised pre-training
528   with classification label is much more efficient than unsupervised pre-training on the edge
529   map. In the future, we will continue to scale up our testing set and focus on addressing the domain
530   gap between the patent edge map and the product edge map.
