011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series forecasting plays a vital role for decision-making across a wide range
014            of real-world domains, which has been extensively studied. Most existing single-
015
               modal models rely solely on numerical series, which suffer from the limitations
               imposed by insufficient information. Recent studies have revealed that multi-
016
               modal models can address the core issue by integrating textual information. How-
017
               ever, these models focus on either historical or future textual information, over-
018            looking the unique contributions each plays in time series forecasting. Besides,
019            these models fail to grasp the intricate relationships between textual and time se-
020            ries data, constrained by their moderate capacity for multimodal comprehension.
021            To tackle these challenges, we propose Dual-Forecaster, a pioneering multimodal
022            time series model that combines both descriptively historical textual information
023            and predictive textual insights, leveraging advanced multimodal comprehension
024            capability. We begin by developing the historical text-time series contrastive loss
025            to align the descriptively historical textual data and corresponding time series data,
026
               followed by encoding multimodal text-time series representations between them
               through the history-oriented modality interaction module, and then combining
027
               predictive textual data through the future-oriented modality interaction module to
028
               ensure textual insights-following forecasting. Our comprehensive evaluations on
029            synthetic dataset and captioned-public datasets demonstrate that Dual-Forecaster
030            is a distinctly effective multimodal time series model that outperforms or is com-
031            parable to other state-of-the-art models, highlighting the superiority of integrating
032            textual information for time series forecasting. This work opens new avenues in
033            the integration of textual information with numerical time series data for multi-
034            modal time series analysis.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      With the massive accumulation of time series data in such various domains as retail (Leonard,
      2001), electricity (Liu et al., 2023a), traffic (Shao et al., 2022), finance (Li et al., 2022), and health-
040
      care (Kaushik et al., 2020), time series forecasting has become a key part of decision-making. To
041
      date, while extensive research has been dedicated to time series forecasting, resulting in a multi-
042   tude of proposed methodologies (Hyndman et al., 2008; Nie et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b; Garza
043   & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Xue & Salim, 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), they are predominantly
044   confined to single-modal models that rely exclusively on numerical time series data. Constrained by
045   the scarcity information within time series data, these models have hit a roadblock in improving the
046   forecasting performance due to overfitting on the training data.
047
      To improve the model’s forecasting performance, it is crucial to introduce supplementary informa-
048   tion that is not present in time series data. For instance, when forecasting future product sales,
049   combining numerical historical sales data with external factors—such as product iteration plans,
050   strategic sales initiatives, and unforeseeable occurrences like pandemics—enables us to give a sales
051   forecast that aligns more closely with business expectations. This supplementary information usu-
052   ally manifests as unstructured text, rich in semantic information that reflects temporal causality and
053   system dynamics. In contrast to quantifiable information like holidays can be readily embedded as
      covariates through feature engineering techniques, this supplementary text is challenging to distill


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      into numerical format, posing a barrier to its utilization in bolstering the reliability of time series
055   forecasts.
056
057   Recently, there has been a surge in proposals for multimodal time series models that incorporate text
058
      as an additional input modality (Xu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b;a). This methodology effectively
      surpasses the constraints inherent in traditional time series forecasting approaches, substantially aug-
059
      menting the accuracy and efficacy of the models. However, these models often focus exclusively on
060
      historical or future textual information, thereby underestimating the distinct roles each type of infor-
061   mation plays in time series forecasting. Moreover, they struggle to capture the complex connections
062   between textual and time series data, due to their constrained multimodal comprehension capabil-
063   ities. Therefore, it is necessary to integrate both historical and future textual data and enhance the
064   model’s capacity for multimodal understanding.
065
      To tackle the aforementioned challenges, we introduce Dual-Forecaster, a cutting-edge time series
066
      forecasting model. It is designed around a sophisticated framework that adeptly aligns historical
067   and future textual data with time series data, capitalizing on its robust multimodal comprehension
068   capability. It should be noted that the word ‘Dual’ in Dual-Forecaster has two different levels of
069   meaning. On the one hand, it represents that Dual-Forecaster is a multimodal time series model ca-
070   pable of handling both textual and time series data concurrently, and on the other hand, it denotes the
071   model’s capacity to simultaneously process descriptively historical textual data and predictive tex-
072   tual data, integrating these with time series data within a unified high-dimension embedding space
073   to better guide the forecasting. Specifically, Dual-Forecaster consists of the textual branch and
074   the temporal branch. The textual branch is responsible for understanding textual data and extract-
075
      ing the semantic information contained within it, while the temporal branch processes time series
      information. To improve the model’s multimodal comprehension capability, we propose three well-
076
      designed cross-modality alignment techniques: (1) Historical Text-Time Series Contrastive Loss
077
      aligns the descriptively historical textual data and corresponding time series data, augmenting the
078   model’s capacity to learn inter-variable relationships; (2) History-oriented Modality Interaction
079   Module integrates input historical textual and time series data through a cross-attention mechanism,
080   ensuring effective alignment of distributions between historical textual and time series data; (3)
081   Future-oriented Modality Interaction Module incorporates the predictive textual insights into the
082   aligned time series embeddings generated by history-oriented modality interaction module, ensuring
083   textual insights-following forecasting for obtaining more reasonable forecasts.
084   To prove the effectiveness of our model, we conduct extensive experiments on synthetic dataset and
085   captioned-public datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that Dual-Forecaster achieves com-
086   petitive or superior performance when compared to other state-of-the-art models on all datasets.
087   Moreover, ablation studies emphasize that the performance enhancement is attributed to the sup-
088   plementary information provided by both descriptively historical textual data and predictive textual
089   data.
090   Our main contributions in this work are threefold:
091
092   (1) We craft a sophisticated framework for the integration of textual and time series data underpinned
093
      by advanced multimodal comprehension. This framework is engineered to generate time series
      embeddings that are enriched with enhanced semantic insights, which in turn, empowers Dual-
094
      Forecaster a more robust forecasting capability.
095
096   (2) We propose Dual-Forecaster, a novel time series forecasting model that excels in integrating
097   descriptively historical text with time series data, thereby enhancing the model’s capacity to discern
098   inter-variable relationships. Additionally, it utilizes predictive textual insights to direct the forecast-
099
      ing process, subsequently bolstering the reliability of forecasts.
100   (3) Extensive experiments on synthetic dataset and captioned-public datasets demonstrate that Dual-
101   Forecaster achieves state-of-the-art performance on time series forecasting task, with favorable gen-
102   eralization ability.

      2    R ELATED WORK
106
107   Time series forecasting. Time series forecasting models can be roughly categorized into statistical
      models and deep learning models. Statistical models such as ETS, ARIMA (Hyndman et al., 2008)


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      can be fitted to a single time series and used to make predictions of future observations. Deep
109   learning models, ranging from the classical LSTM (Hochreiter, 1997), TCN (Bai et al., 2018), to
110   recently popular transformer-based models (Nie et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2023;
111   Liu et al., 2023b), are developed for capturing nonlinear, long-term temporal dependencies. Even
112   though excellent performance has been achieved on specific tasks, these models lack generalizability
113   to diverse time series data.
114
      To overcome the challenge, the development of pre-trained time series foundation models has
115   emerged as a burgeoning area of research. In the past two years, several time series foundation
116   models have been introduced (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Rasul et al., 2023; Das et al.,
117   2023; Ansari et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024). All of them are pre-trained transformer-based mod-
118   els trained on a large corpus of time series data with time-series-specific designs in terms of time
119   features, time series tokenizers, distribution heads, and data augmentation, among others. These
120   pre-trained time series foundation models can adapt to new datasets and tasks without extensive
121   from-scratch retraining, demonstrating superior zero-shot forecasting capability. Furthermore, ben-
122   efiting from the impressive capabilities of pattern recognition, reasoning and generalization of Large
123
      Language Models (LLMs), recent studies have further explored tailoring LLMs for time series data
      through techniques such as fine-tuning (Xue & Salim, 2023; Gruver et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023)
124
      and model reprogramming (Cao et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023).
125
      However, existing time series forecasting models have encountered a plateau in performance due
126   to limited information contained in time series data. There is an evident need for additional data
127   beyond the scope of time series to further refine forecasts.
128
129
      Text-guided time series forecasting. Some works have attempted to address the prevalent issue
      of information insufficiency in the manner of text-guided time series forecasting, which includes
130
      text as an auxiliary input modality. A line of work investigate how to use some declarative prompts
131
      (e.g., domain expert knowledge and task instructions) enriching the input time series to guide LLM
132   reasoning (Jin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024d;c). These approaches align text and time series into the
133   language space based on the global features learned by pre-trained LLMs to maximize the inference
134   capability of the LLMs. However, they ignore the importance of local temporal features on time
135   series forecasting.
136
      An alternative text-guided time series forecasting approach is to process textual and time series data
137
      separately by using different models, and then merge the information of two modalities through a
138   modality interaction module to yield enriched time series representations for time series forecating
139   (Liu et al., 2024a). Our method belongs to this category, however, there is limited relevant research
140   on time series. Xu et al. (2024) proposes TGForecaster that employs a PatchTST encoder to process
141   time series data and utilizes off-the-shelf, pre-trained text models to pre-embedding news content
142   and channel descriptions into vector sequences across the time dimensions, thus allowing for ef-
143   ficient modality fusion in the embedding space. Liu et al. (2024b) develops MM-TFSlib, which
144   provides a convenient multimodal integration framework. It can independently model numerical
145   and textual series using different time series forecasting models and LLMs, and then combine these
146
      outputs using a learnable linear weighting mechanism to produce the final predictions. Distinct
      from these methods, we recognize the unique contributions of historical and future textual informa-
147
      tion in time series forecasting, as well as the significance of multimodal comprehension capability
148
      in learning the intricate links between textual and time series data. We introduce a groundbreaking
149   multimodal time series model that integrates both descriptively historical textual information and
150   predictive textual insights based on advanced multimodal comprehension capability.

322
323   To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed Dual-Forecaster, we firstly design five multimodal
      time series benchmark datasets across two categories: synthetic dataset and captioned-public dataset,


                                                         6

324
      and then conduct extensive experiments on them. More details on the construction of the multi-
325   modal time series benchmark datasets are in Appendix B.2. We compare Dual-Forecaster against a
326   collection of representative methods from the recent time series forecasting landscape, our approach
327   displays competitive or stronger results in multiple benchmarks and zero-shot setting.
328
      Baseline Models. We carefully select 6 forecasting methods as our baselines with the following
329
      categories: (1) Single-modal models: DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), FITS (Xu et al., 2023), PatchTST
330
      (Nie et al., 2022), iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023b); (2) Multimodal models: MM-TSFlib (Liu et al.,
331   2024b) with GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) as LLM backbone and iTransformer as time series fore-
332   casting backbone (hereinafter referred to as MM-TSFlib), and Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2023). We
333   contrast Dual-Forecaster with the single-modal models to illustrate how textual insights can enhance
334   forecasting performance. Comparisons with the multimodal models highlight that Dual-Forecaster
335   possesses enhanced multimodal comprehension capability, thereby further elevating the model’s

521   5        C ONCLUSION
522
523
524   In this work, we present Dual-Forecaster, an innovative multimodal time series model aiming at
525
      generating more reasonable forecasts. It is augmented by rich, descriptively historical textual infor-
      mation and predictive textual insights, all supported by advanced multimodal comprehension capa-
526
      bility. To enhance its multimodal comprehension capability, we craft three cross-modality alignment
527
      techniques, including historical text-time series contrastive loss, history-oriented modality interac-
528   tion module, and future-oriented modality interaction module. We conduct extensive experiments on
529   synthetic dataset and captioned-public datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of Dual-Forecaster
530   and the superiority of integrating textual data for time series forecasting.
531
      Limitations & Future Work While Dual-Forecaster has achieved remarkable performance in text-
532
      guided time series forecasting, there remains room for further improvements. Due to resource con-
533
      straints, a comprehensive hyperparameter tuning was not performed, suggesting that the reported
534   results of Dual-Forecaster may be sub-optimal. In terms of multimodal time series dataset, the lack
535   of a standardized and efficient annotation methodology often leads to inadequate annotation qual-
536   ity on real-world datasets, with the issue being particularly pronounced in the annotation of long
537   time series. Future work should focus on developing a more elegant time series annotator, lever-
538   aging the text-time series alignment techniques that are fundamental to Dual-Forecaster. In terms
539   of downstream task, further research should explore the potential of expanding Dual-Forecaster to
      encompass a broad spectrum of multimodal time series analysis capabilities.

718   In the realm of time series forecasting, there is a notable lack of high-quality multimodal time series
719   benchmark datasets that combine time series data with corresponding textual series. While some
720   studies have introduced multimodal benchmark datasets (Liu et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024), these
721
      datasets primarily rely on textual descriptions derived from external sources like news reports or
      background information. These types of textual data are often domain-specific and may not be con-
722
      sistently available across different time series domains, limiting their utility for building unified mul-
723
      timodal models. In contrast, shape-based textual descriptions of time series patterns are relatively
724   easier to generate and can provide more structured insights. The TS-Insights dataset Zhang et al.
725   (2023) pairs time series data with shape-based textual descriptions. However, these descriptions are
726   based on detrended series (with seasonality removed), which may introduce bias and complicate
727   the interpretation of the original time series data. To address these challenges, we propose five new
728   multimodal time series benchmark datasets where textual descriptions are directly aligned with the
729   observed patterns in the time series. The construction process for these datasets is outlined below.
730
731   B.2.1    SYNTHETIC DATASET
732
      For the synthetic time series data, we firstly design three categories of components, which are then
733
      combined to generate simulated time series. The components are as follows:
734
735           • Trend: Linear trend, exponential trend
736
              • Seasonality: Cosine, linear, exponential, M-shape, trapezoidal
737
738
              • Noise: Gaussian noise with varying variances
739
      To generate the synthetic time series, one component from each category is randomly selected.
740
      These components are then either added together or multiplied to produce a time series, along with
741   a corresponding textual description of its key characteristics. To enhance the diversity of the descrip-
742   tions, rule-based descriptions are paraphrased using GPT-4o. Additionally, to simulate transitions
743   between different states, we generate time series where only one component changes over time. For
744   instance, a time series might exhibit a linear upward trend that transits to a linear downward trend. In
745   this manner, we construct the synthetic dataset with a total of 3,040 training samples. Each sample
746   includes historical time series and future time series, as well as paired historical and future textual
747   series. Several examples of these constructed samples are shown in Figure 4.
748
749   B.2.2    C APTIONED P UBLIC DATASETS

      For the real-world time series data, we construct corresponding textual descriptions using the fol-

754             to the min-max normalized time series, identifying reasonable segmentation points. IEPF
755             begins by taking the starting curve, which consists of an ordered set of points, and an
                allowable distance threshold. Initially, the first and last points of the curve are marked as

786            no points are found that are farther than threshold from their respective line segments.

788            while discarding unnecessary details. The lines connecting these segmentation points can
789
               roughly outline the overall shape of the time series.
790
791
             • Once the time series is segmented, statistical features such as slope and volatility are com-
792
               puted for each section. For each segment, a linear regression model is fitted to the data,
793            and the slope is calculated. The P-value from the regression determines the significance of
794            the trend: if it’s below 0.05, the slope indicates an upward or downward trend; if it’s above
795            0.05, the segment is considered to be fluctuating. The Mean Squared Error (MSE) between
796            the original data and the regression line is also calculated to measure the noise level. Based
797            on the MSE, the noise is classified into three levels: low, medium, or high.
798
799
800          • Finally, a textual description is generated: if the local trend is significant, the description
801            notes whether the segment is increasing or decreasing; if not, it indicates fluctuation. The
802            noise level is also included in the description based on the MSE.
803

807   96, we construct training samples using a sliding window approach. For each training instance, both
808   the historical and future time series are annotated. Figure 6 and figure 7 illustrate the text annotation

      The configurations of our models, in relation to the evaluations on various datasets, are consolidated

890   with a patch length of Lp . In terms of model hyperparameter, dm represents the dimension of the
891   embedded representations. nuni denotes the number of layers of unimodal time series encoder
892   used to process time series inputs, while nmul denotes the number of layers of the history-oriented
893   modality interaction module, which ensures effectively alignment of distributions between historical
894   textual and time series data. Heads are correlate to the Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) and Multi-
895   Head Cross-Attention (MHCA) operations utilized for cross-modality alignment. For the synthetic
896
      dataset, we set the training epochs to 300, while for the ETT, exchange-rate and stock dataset, we

      patience to 7.

914   C      BASELINES
915
916   DLinear: a combination of a decomposition scheme and a linear network that first divides a time
917   series data into two components of trend and remainder, and then performs forecasting to the two
      series respectively with two one-layer linear model.


                                                                                           17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      FITS: consists of the key part of the complex-valued linear layer that is dedicatedly designed to
919   learn amplitude scaling and phase shifting, thereby facilitating to extend time series segment by
920   interpolating the frequency representation.
921
922
      PatchTST: is composed of two key components: (i) patching that segments time series into patches
      as input tokens to Transformer; (ii) channel-independent structure where each channel univariate
923
      time series shares the same Transformer backbone.
924
925   iTransformer: is an inverted Transformer that raw series of different variates are firstly embedded
926   to tokens, applied by self-attention for multivariate correlations, and individually processed by the
927   share feed-forward network for series representations of each token.
928   MM-TSFlib: is the first multimodal time-series forecasting (TSF) library, which allows the integra-
929   tion of any open-source language models with arbitrary TSF models, thereby enabling multimodal
930   TSF tasks based on Time-MMD.
931
      Time-LLM: is a new framework, which encompasses reprogramming time series data into text
932   prototype representations before feeding it into the frozen LLM and providing input context with
933   declarative prompts via Prompt-as-Prefix to augment reasoning.
