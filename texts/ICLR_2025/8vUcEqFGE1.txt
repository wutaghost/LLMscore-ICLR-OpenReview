009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods are typically supervised. However, a
012            bag-to-bag metric is needed in many applications, including clustering, statistical
013            tests, and dimension reduction. Such a metric should differentiate between bags,
014            regardless of the sparsity or overlap between the instances of the bags. We pro-
015            pose SUMIT (Self sUpervised MIL dIsTance) as an instance-embedding-based
016            distance that maximizes the distinction between bags. SUMIT is optimized using
017            five criteria: self-similarity within a bag, quality of instance reconstruction, ro-
018            bustness to sampling depth, conservation of triangle inequality, and separation of
019            instances to clusters. We show using current standard MIL datasets and a novel
020
               wiki-based set of wiki topics that the within bag-similarity loss is the most impor-
               tant for a bag-to-bag metric that best separates bags of similar classes. SUMIT
021
               bridges the gap between instance-level and bag-level approaches, by keeping the
022
               embedding of all instances but ensuring their proximity within a bag.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is weakly supervised learning where the training instances are
029   not stand-alone examples, but instead are arranged in sets, called bags(2). Labels are provided for
030   the entire bag and not for the individual samples, further denoted as instances. To exemplify MIL,
031   consider chapters in a book. The sentences can be considered as the individual instance, and the
032   chapter is a bag. In the training set, we know that chapters belong to a category. The goal of
033
      MIL classification is to detect whether a chapter in the book belongs to a category. While a large
      number of algorithms were developed for MIL classification, there is no inherent method to define
034
      distances between bags. Note that many methods were proposed to define distances on sets (e.g.
035
      the energy distance(20), Wasserstein Distance(21) Hausdorff distance(27), etc). However, these
036   distances do not consider the distribution of instances in each bag. We propose to combine self-
037   supervised learning and metric learning to produce such a metric between bags. Using the book
038   chapters example above- when considering the chapters as the bags, one would expect the distances
039   between chapters in the same book to be smaller than the distance between chapters from other
040   books. Also, the distances between chapters from books of the same genre or subject should be
041   smaller than the distances between chapters from other genres or subjects.
042   MIL classification methods can be grouped into three categories: instance-space (IS), bag-space
043   (BS), and embedded-space (ES) methods(30). The categorization is based on how information from
044   the bag is extracted. The Instance-Space (IS) paradigm is based on local, instance-level information,
045   ignoring the characteristics of the whole bag. This is based on the assumption that the information
046   lies in the instances. The Bag-Space (BS) paradigm is based on global, bag-level information, and
047   each bag is treated as an entity. The Embedded-Space (ES) paradigm, like the BS paradigm, is
048   based on global, bag-information. In contrast with the BS paradigm, which uses a non-vectorial
049   representation of the bag, in the ES paradigm, each bag is mapped to a single feature vector.
050   We here propose a combination of the instance and bag level, by computing a distance on an embed-
051   ding of each instance, requiring that the distance between instances of the same bag to be closer than
052   instances between bags. We do not assume any label on the bag. As such, the metric presented here
053   is an instance-level self-supervised distance at the bag level. This approach combines the advantages
      of instance and bag-level approaches.

      We deviate from the mainly supervised approach used in MIL. A well-defined metric in the MIL set-
055   ting has multiple benefits, including, distance-based machine learning (e.g. KNN(11)), the definition
056   of density in the bag space using kernel density estimation - KDE(7), clustering using distance-based
057   clustering (e.g. community detection(16) or dBscan (8), dimension reduction using distance con-
058   serving projection (e.g. MDS (15),UMAP (17), t-SNE(25) and the resulting visualization when
059   projected to two or three dimensions, and distance based statistical tests(3).

116   3     N OVELTY
117
118   All current metrics use either a projection of the entire bag and as such lose most of the informa-
119   tion on the bag, or use the original representation of each instance combined with some predefined
120   distance between sets. This suffers from a high overlap between bags.
121   Here we propose a novel approach - use an energy distance via the embedding of each instance.
122   The embedding of each instance is learned to ensure that the distance between instances of different
123   bags is larger than the distance of instances in a bag. In addition, we optimize the embedding of each
124   instance to ensure that the distance between bags adheres to the requirements of a distance metric
125   (e.g. the triangle inequality.
126   Specifically, we do not optimize the distances between bags. Instead, we optimize the embedding of
127   each instance to minimize five different losses:
128
129         1. Reconstruction Loss. We minimize the discrepancy between the original and the recon-
130
               structed features following an encoder-decoder model.
131         2. Contrastive Loss We minimize the distance between instances of the same bag compared
132            to distances from different bags.
133         3. Invariance Loss. We ensure the distance is not sensitive to the number of instances sam-
134            pled in each bag.
135         4. Clustering. We optimize the division of the encoding into distinct clusters.
136         5. Triangle Loss. We maximize the gap between the undirect distance from one instance to
137            another through a third instance and the direct distance between said instances (adherence
138            to the triangular inequality theorem).
139
140
      To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time the embedding of each instance is optimized to
      produce an optimal distance between bags.

      5     R ESULTS
271
272
      We propose here to learn an embedding of the instances, and then apply an energy distance to the
273   bags. The embedding is learned using an encoder-decoder architecture with five different losses:
274
275         1. Reconstruction: error between the original input features and the reconstructed features
276            produced by a mode
277         2. Contrastive: minimize distance for pairs from the same bag, maximize distance for pairs
278            from different bags.
279         3. Invariance: maintains consistent distances between the same pair of bags across different
280            sampling depths.
281
            4. Clustering: Minimizes the bag’s distance to assigned clusters, aiming at creating a more
282
               dense representation.
283
            5. Triangle: optimizes adherence to the triangular inequality by minimizing deviations in
284
               Euclidean distances between points forming a triangle.
285
286   To evaluate the quality of the distance, we compared the density of bags from class A or B around
287   bags from class A (as estimated by a KDE). A good projection should maximize the ratio of the self
288   (density of A around A) to other (density of B around A) densities.
289
      To illustrate that, we generated toy data of bags from two labels, where the instances of bags with
290
      one label are far from the instances of bags with the second label. Following the method above, the
291   bag-to-bag distance was calculated using energy distance, and the bag projections were visualized
292   using multi-dimensional scaling (MDS). (Fig. 2 A). The higher KDE for bags from the same label
293   (A, A) vs bags of different labels (A, B) captures the separation of the bags as seen in (Fig. 2 b). We
294   propose the same approach to estimate the quality of the distance on real-world datasets.
295
296   5.1   L IMITATION OF INSTANCE - BASED DISTANCES
297
298   When we applied the same approach to real-world datasets (using pre-defined projections of the
299   instances), a poor separation was noticed between bags of similar labels and those of different labels,
300   as can be observed by the low densities, as well as similar density distributions between the self and
301
      other densities (Fig. 2 d). One can see that for example the MDS projection based on the bag
      distance (computed similarly to the toy model) of the Elephant dataset (Fig. 2 d), reveals a lack
302
      of visual distinctiveness among labels, with the red and blue points overlapping. This observation
303
      is further supported by the low ratio between the KDE values for the distances based on instances
304   across all datasets (Fig. 2 b).

306   5.2   A N AUTOENCODER THAT LEARNS THE INSTANCE DISTRIBUTION DOES NOT IMPROVE
307         THE BAG DISTANCE METRIC .
308
309   If indeed instances are closer within a bag than between bags, an embedding of the instances learned
310   via an auto-encoder (AE) could in theory ensure a more uniform distribution of the instances within
311   bags, and as such improve the within-to-between bag density. We trained an AE using only a re-
312   construction loss (i.e. only ensuring that the AE reproduces the input). However, in general, the
313
      AE only decreases the within-to-between class density (Fig 3C). The reconstruction loss decreases
      to very low values, suggesting a good reconstruction of the instance values. However, using the
314
      encoded latent representation to compute the bag-based distance does not improve the within-to-
315
      between class density ratio.

      5.6   C LASSIFICATION U SING L EARNED M ETRIC
477
478
      Finally, we tested whether the self-supervised loss also improved the classification accuracy. We
479
      used a simple KNN setup, where we defined the distance between each pair of bags using the learned
480   embedding and the energy distance between bags. We then computed the classification accuracy.
481   Following the self-supervised training. The test bags are classified using the majority vote of the 9
482   nearest neighbors for all datasets. We then computed the accuracy and increase in accuracy (vs the
483   original instances values) of the KNN classification, either as a binary (average of each class vs all
484   others), or multiclass (Fig. 6). As was the case for all other measures and datasets, in all cases, the

      6   D ISCUSSION
503
504
      While MIL problems were extensively studied in supervised contexts, there are very limited solu-
505   tions for distances between bags. We have here presented SUMIT a self-supervised approach to
506   embed each instance to optimize the distance between bags. We used five types of distances: A
507   reconstruction distance focused on reproducing the instance values as precisely as possible, a tri-
508   angle distance that maintains the geometry of the projection, a clustering distance that ensures that
509   the projection induces distinct clusters, and two bag-level self-supervised distances that minimize
510   the distance within each bag. The first is a contrastive loss that compares the distance between and
511   within bags, and the second distance ensures that the distance between bags is not sensitive to the
512   number of instances sampled from each bag. Following the projection, we used an energy distance
513
      on the instances to define a bag distance.
514   We tested this distance definition by measuring the density of bags of different labels around bags
515   of the same labels in standard datasets as well as a novel wiki-based dataset scrapped for the current
516   task from the wiki pages of different cities. We have shown that while the instance-based distances
517   (Triangles and reconstruction) do not increase, and sometimes decrease the density of bags of a
518   given label around bags of the same label, the self-supervised distances increase this density. Thus,
519
      for the sake of an optimal distance, maintaining the bag information is more important than the
      precision of the instance reconstruction from the projection.
520
521   Adding the clustering distance to the bag level losses further improves the density of same-level
522   bags, probably by ensuring more dense and separated projections.
523   SUMIT bridges the gap between bag-embedding methods where the entire bag is projected into a
524   single vector in Rn , and instance-based methods, where the focus is on the instances. Moreover, the
525   combination of the clustering loss increases the typical self-density, but also the contrast between
526   bags of different labels.
527
      To the best of our knowledge, SUMIT is the first effort explicitly targeted at developing an instance-
528
      based distance between bags that is optimized at the bag level. Many choices in this analysis are
529   arbitrary and can be changed. Those include among others the model used for the projection (cur-
530   rently a two-three-layer fully connected neural network), and the precise definition of the loss for
531   each of the components (e.g. changing different types of self-supervised loss). However, from our
532   experience, the precise details of either the encoder or the decoder, or the precise shape of the loss
533   do not affect the qualitative results presented here.
