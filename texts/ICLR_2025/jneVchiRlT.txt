011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Table-text retrieval aims to retrieve relevant tables and text to support open-
014            domain question answering. Existing studies use either early or late fusion, but
015
               face limitations. Early fusion pre-aligns a table row with its associated passages,
               forming “stars,” which often include irrelevant contexts and miss query-dependent
016
               relationships. Late fusion retrieves individual nodes, dynamically aligning them,
017
               but it risks missing relevant contexts. Both approaches also struggle with advanced
018            reasoning tasks, such as column-wise aggregation and multi-hop reasoning. To ad-
019            dress these issues, we propose FusionMaestro, which combines the strengths
020            of both approaches. First, the edge-based bipartite subgraph retrieval identifies
021            finer-grained edges between table segments and passages, effectively avoiding the
022            inclusion of irrelevant contexts. Then, the query-relevant node expansion identi-
023            fies the most promising nodes, dynamically retrieving relevant edges to grow the
024            bipartite subgraph, minimizing the risk of missing important contexts. Lastly, the
025            star-based LLM refinement performs logical inference at the star subgraph rather
026
               than the bipartite subgraph, supporting advanced reasoning tasks. Experimental
               results show that FusionMaestro outperforms state-of-the-art models with a
027
               significant improvement up to 42.6% and 39.9% in recall and nDCG, respectively,
028
               on the OTT-QA benchmark.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Open-domain question answering (ODQA) over tables and text is important as it leverages the com-
034   plementary strengths of structured and unstructured data. Tables offer vast amounts of related facts
035   but lack diversity, while text provides broader contextual information (Chen et al., 2020b;a), mak-
036   ing the integration of both modalities essential. Table-text retrieval, which retrieves question-relevant
037   tables and text, is a key task in ODQA as it provides question-relevant context to readers of retriever-
038
      reader systems (Chen et al., 2020a; Huang et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; 2023; Kang et al., 2024).
039   Despite its importance, table-text retrieval faces two key challenges due to its multimodal nature.
040   First, it involves resolving multi-hop relationships across diverse corpora for structured tables and
041   textual passages (Chen et al., 2020a; Talmor et al., b). While textual data is generally unstructured
042   and narrative-driven, tabular data is highly structured. Understanding its rows and columns involves
043   interpreting structural semantics, making the integration of information from these two formats com-
044
      plex. Second, the retrieval process should support advanced reasoning for modality-specific opera-
      tions such as column-wise aggregations and multi-modal operations like multi-hop reasoning.
045
046   Existing methods have achieved some success by employing either early or late fusion techniques
047   in their top-k retrieval. The early fusion strategy attempts to reduce the search space by grouping
048   relevant documents before a query is presented. It pre-aligns a table row with associated passages
049   via entity linking, creating a fused block as the retrieval unit (Chen et al., 2020a; Huang et al.,
050
      2022; Kang et al., 2024). In contrast, the late fusion strategy aligns relevant table rows and passages
      dynamically after the query is given. This alignment is typically driven by entity linking or query-
051
      based similarity matching. It returns a ranked sequence of evidence chains, where an evidence chain
052
      refers to a pair consisting of a table row and a passage (Ma et al., 2022; 2023).
053
      However, the existing studies have several significant limitations.

075   Figure 1: Simplified examples of three cases where existing methods struggle to retrieve the
076   question-related documents correctly. (a) Inadequate granularity of retrieval units leading to inaccu-
077   rate retrieval results. (b) Entity linking results cannot estimate essential query-aware relationships.
078   (c) Inability of advanced reasoning such as table aggregation and multi-hop reasoning.
079
080   (1) Inadequate granularity of retrieval unit. The retrieval units used in the early fusion strategy are
081   formed independently of the query, often including query-irrelevant passages. This problem leads to
082   incorrect similarities between fused blocks and questions. For example in Figure 1(a), entity linking
083   connects the Grammy Award for Best Female Country Vocal table to four surrounding
084   passages. However, the irrelevant connections overwhelm the table, as the information related to
085   K. T. Oslin is only essential information (Figure 1(a)). In the late fusion strategy, retrieving a
086
      single table segment or passage may be partially relevant to a query, incurring the risk of retrieving
      incorrect tables. For instance, during the first iteration of retrieval, the system might retrieve the
087
      Grammy Award for Best Rock Instrumental table instead of the correct one. Both this
088
      table and the correct one share partial relationships with the query, containing overlapping words
089   such as Grammy, Artist, and Work, and can lead to confusion in identifying the correct target.
090
      (2) Missing query-dependent relationships. The early fusion strategy relies on entity linking to
091
      predefine relationships between tables and passages. It fails to account for query-dependent links
092   between documents that might contain the information necessary to answer the query. For instance,
093   in Figure 1(b), the table 2012 MLS SuperDraft is early fused with the entity University of
094   Notre Dame. However, when the question specifies the information about school colors, it
095   should be linked to the Notre Dame Fighting Irish passage.
096   (3) Lack of advanced reasoning. Queries that require complex reasoning, such as multi-hop or
097   column-wise aggregation, often demand advanced logical inference beyond simple semantic simi-
098   larity with the query. Since previous approaches rely on semantic similarity, they might fail to re-
099   trieve rows or passages identifiable through logical inference. For example, in Figure 1(c), the query
100   involves understanding the most recent Segunda Liga Player of the Month is Basilio
101   Almeida, where the row with the latest Year and Month combination has to be inferred.
102   We first formalize the terms proposed in previous studies using a bipartite graph, where table seg-
103   ments and passages are represented as two disjoint sets of nodes, and the links between them are
104   represented as edges. Therefore, the term fused block used in the early fusion strategy (Chen et al.,
105   2020a; Huang et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2024) can be represented as a star (Diestel, 2024) centered on
106   a node of type table segment, with connected nodes of type passage. Similarly, the evidence
107   chain used in the late fusion strategy (Ma et al., 2022; 2023) corresponds to an edge connecting a
      pair of nodes: one of type table segment and one of type passage.

      In this paper, we propose FusionMaestro, a novel graph-based retrieval consisting of three
109   stages: early fusion, late fusion, and LLM reasoning. Specifically, FusionMaestro adopts the
110   following three key ideas:
111
      (1) Combined usage of early and late fusion. We selectively leverage the advantages of both
112   early fusion and late fusion. The early fusion stage provides comprehensive retrieval units by pre-
113   aligning tables to their related passages before the query, mitigating the risk of retrieving incomplete
114   or partially relevant information inherent in late fusion. Conversely, the late fusion stage dynami-
115   cally captures query-dependent relationships during retrieval, addressing early fusion’s reliance on
116   predefined, query-independent links established via entity linking.
117   (2) Graph refinement. We leverage large language models (LLMs) to perform further advanced
118   reasoning over the retrieved graph, enabling deeper logical inference beyond simple semantic sim-
119   ilarity. For instance, in Figure 1(c), when the SJPF Segunda Liga Player of the Month
120   table is retrieved, the LLM can perform aggregation to identify the most recent player and conduct
121   multi-hop reasoning to select the corresponding passage for Basilio Almeida.
122   (3) Granularity determination for each retrieval stage. In our retrieval pipeline, each stage -
123   early fusion, late fusion, and graph refinement - serves a distinct purpose, necessitating the precise
124   determination of the appropriate operational units for each. For the early fusion stage, we propose
125   a novel edge-level retrieval mechanism, which balances the challenge of excluding query-irrelevant
126   context in star graph retrieval and avoiding the partial information problem in node-based retrieval.
127
      In the late fusion stage, we set the unit as an individual node. We identify query-relevant nodes
      within the graph produced by the early fusion stage so that we can design the late fusion process to
128
      expand the graph using only nodes closely aligned with the query context. This approach mitigates
129
      the challenge where the earlier stage may retrieve nodes irrelevant to the query. Finally, the graph
130   refinement stage provides the fully expanded graph from late fusion to the LLM, which can increase
131   hallucination risks due to the inclusion of unnecessary nodes. To mitigate this, we decompose the
132   graph into smaller star graphs.
133
      Experimental results demonstrate that FusionMaestro significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
134
      systems, with a 42.6% improvement in AR@2 and a 39.9% improvement in nDCG@50.

138   2.1   O PEN - DOMAIN Q UESTION A NSWERING
139
140
      Open-Domain Question Answering (ODQA) is the task aimed at answering factual questions using
      a large knowledge corpus (Zhang et al., 2023). Representative ODQA benchmarks such as Natural
141
      Questions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and SearchQA (Dunn et al.,
142
      2017) consist of single-hop queries that require information found in a single passage within a corpus
143   of unstructured texts. Further advances were shown by HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and WikiHop
144   (Welbl et al., 2018), presenting challenging queries that require multi-hop reasoning across multiple
145   passages. However, these benchmarks support only unstructured passages and do not consider multi-
146   hop reasoning across structured tables and unstructured passages, which is essential in table-text
147   retrieval tasks. OTT-QA (Chen et al., 2020a) is the first ODQA benchmark that supports multi-hop
148   reasoning between tables and text. It introduces questions that require reasoning over both tables and
149   their associated passages, providing a more realistic and challenging scenario for retrieval methods.
150
151   2.2   TABLE -T EXT R ETRIEVAL
152   Table-text retrieval methods can be broadly categorized into early fusion and late fusion approaches.
153   These terms, initially used in multimodal tasks like image-sentence retrieval and semantic video
154   analysis, describe whether different modalities are encoded jointly or separately (Wang et al., 2022;
155   Snoek et al., 2005; Gadzicki et al., 2020). Similarly in the context of table-text retrieval, early and
156   late fusion approaches differ based on whether tables and text are linked before or after the retrieval
157   process (Kang et al., 2024).
158   Early fusion approaches (Chen et al., 2020a; Huang et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2024), before the
159   query is given, connect each table row with its related passages using entity linking, forming ‘fused
160   blocks’ that serve as basic retrieval units. These fused blocks are later retrieved during online time by
161   measuring their similarity to the query. Early fusion approaches show two limitations: (i) The fused
      blocks may include numerous query-irrelevant passages since they include all passages linked to a


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      row, without considering the query. This large retrieval unit not only introduces unrelated passages
163   into the retrieval results but also increases information loss when encoding the embeddings for the
164   fused blocks, thereby reducing the overall retrieval accuracy. (ii) Offline-generated fused blocks
165   are unable to consider query-dependent relationships that must be resolved online, as illustrated in
166   Figure 1(b). Our retrieval method addresses limitation (i) by using edges as basic units of retrieval,
167   as they are more fine-grained than fused blocks. Furthermore, to address limitation (ii), we propose
168   a query-relevant node expansion that adds query-dependent relationships online.
169   Late fusion approaches (Ma et al., 2022; 2023) dynamically group relevant documents online. They
170   begin with retrieving table segments relevant to the question, followed by retrieving passages asso-
171   ciated with these segments to establish connections between the documents. These methods require
172   considering all possible pairs between table segments and passages online, resulting in a vast search
173   space. Search algorithms like beam search are employed to address this problem, but can lead to an
174   error propagation problem as retrieving a single table segment or passage may contain only partial
175   relevant information. Our approach utilizes an edge-based retrieval, which captures richer context
176   by connecting table segments and passages, enabling a more accurate seed document retrieval.
177   Both early fusion and late fusion approaches predominantly rely on semantic similarity for retrieval.
178   Therefore, it may fail to retrieve table segments and passages that require advanced reasoning (e.g.,
179   column-wise aggregation, multi-hop reasoning) to be found, as shown in Figure 1(c). To address the
180   limitations, we propose a star-based LLM refinement, which leverages the logical inference ability
181   of LLM to refine the retrieved results using advanced reasoning.
182   Additionally, our retrieval method applies different levels of granularity (e.g., edges, nodes, stars)
183   tailored to each retrieval phase. DRAMA (Yuan et al., 2024) also adopts a multi-granularity approach
184   but is limited to a constrained setting where relevant tables and passages are provided, unlike our
185   method, which operates in an open-domain context. GTR (Wang et al., 2021) and MGNETS (Chen
186   et al., 2021) focus on enhancing table encoding using graph-based methods, whereas our work tar-
187   gets bridging semantic relationships between tables and text in open-domain retrieval.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
191
192   Table-text retrieval is involved from a retrieval corpus C, which comprises two distinct sets: a col-
193   lection of passages CP = {P (1) , . . . , P (n) } and a collection of tables CT = {T (1) , . . . , T (m) }. A
194   passage is defined as a sequence of tokens P , representing unstructured text. A table is a structured
195   matrix T , consisting of cells Ti,j , where i and j indicate the row index and the column index, re-
196   spectively. Each cell Ti,j may contain a number, date, phrase, or sentence. We define a document
197
      as either a passage or a table. Given a query q, the objective of table-text retrieval is to retrieve
      from corpus C a ranked list of documents such that the document containing the answer span a is
198
      positioned among the top results.
199
200   We split a table into multiple table segments, as commonly used in existing studies. Because a single
201   table can easily exceed the token limits of language models, a table T is combined with its header
                                                                ′
202   to form a list of table segments T = [S (1) , . . . , S (m ) ] (Chen et al. 2020a). This process results in (i)
203   a corpus C composed of table segments CS and passages CP (i.e., C = CS ∪ CP ) and (ii) a mapping
204   M : CS → CT to associate table segments with their original table.
205
      3.2   TABLE -T EXT R ETRIEVAL AS B IPARTITE G RAPH R ETRIEVAL
206
207   We adopt a graph representation, denoted by G = (V, E, Φ, Γ, Λ), to generalize various methods
208   used in existing studies. Here, V is the set of vertices corresponding to a table segment or a passage,
209   and E is the set of edges representing relationships between (table segment, passage) pairs. The
210   mapping Φ : V → {table segment, passage} maps each node to its type, while Γ maps a
211   node to its attributes, such as the text of a passage or the matrix of table structures. The mapping
212
      Λ : E → R maps each edge to its score.
213   The corpus can be expressed as the initial graph Ginit = (Vinit , ∅, Φ, Γ, Λinit ), where each node in
214   Vinit one-to-one corresponds to a table segment or a passage in C. Early fusion generates table-text
215   relationships via entity linking and updates Ginit before a query q is presented. Given a query q, late
      fusion dynamically generates query-dependent table-text relationships to update Ginit . Finally, we

237   Overview. We propose FusionMaestro, a novel graph-based retrieval to leverage the advantages
238   of both early and late fusion. It operates in three main stages as follows.
239   Edge-based Bipartite Subgraph Retrieval. It uses edges as the basic retrieval unit on a bipartite
240   data graph generated by early fusion. It searches for edges relevant to the query within the bipartite
241   graph and integrates the retrieved edges into a single bipartite subgraph. The retrieval unit is set to
242   an edge in this process. This enables a more accurate retrieval of query-relevant subgraphs as it is
243   less likely to contain query-irrelevant nodes like fusion blocks, and it also provides richer context
244   than a single document.
245   Query-relevant Node Expansion. It reinforces the retrieved bipartite subgraph with new nodes
246   found by performing an additional hop (i.e., expansion) from the input subgraph. In this step, we
247   first identify the most promising nodes within the subgraph to perform an additional hop from. These
248   nodes are called seed nodes. Next, the seed nodes are combined with the query to generate expanded
249   queries, which are then used to find the candidate nodes to be expanded to the graph. Lastly, the most
250   promising ones among the candidates are actually expanded into the subgraph.
251   Star-based LLM Refinement. It further refines the expanded graph via LLM’s advanced reasoning,
252   such as aggregation or multi-hop reasoning. It first restores the original tables of the table segments
253   within the expanded graph, then performs an aggregation operation if a query contains one. The
254   output table segments are then added to the graph, along with its related passages. Second, it verifies
255   whether each edge in the graph is relevant to the query in a star-graph-wise manner. The edges
256   verified as irrelevant are excluded from the refined graph. Lastly, it decomposes the refined graph
257
      into a ranked sequence of edges.

      4.1          E DGE - BASED B IPARTITE S UBGRAPH R ETRIEVAL
259
260   FusionMaestro initiates its process with the retrieval of a bipartite subgraph through two key
261   steps: early fusion and edge retrieval. (i) In the early fusion step, a bipartite data graph Gd is gener-
262   ated from Ginit by linking table segments and passages via entity linking. Each edge in this graph
263   represents a meaningful connection between the two passages of different modalities. An embed-
264   ding is also computed for each edge in this step. (ii) In the edge retrieval step, the set of most
265   query-relevant edges is identified by leveraging the semantic similarity between the query and the
266
      edge embeddings. It is then integrated to construct the candidate bipartite subgraph Gc ⊂ Gd .
267   The early fusion step starts by generating edges from the initial graph Ginit that has no edges.
268   We follow the prior methods for the edge generation process, a two-step process of entity linking
269   followed by entity recognition (Ma et al., 2022; 2023). The output graph of this process Gd is a
      bipartite graph, since the edges are generated only between passage nodes and table segment nodes.

      (1) Seed node selection: From the candidate bipartite subgraph Gc , we select a set of nodes that
335   contain information most relevant to the query. This corresponds to finding the set of nodes that
336   show the highest p(u|q).
337   (2) Seed node expansion: For each seed node, we iterate through its neighbors in the complete
338   bipartite graph G∗ , calculating the similarities between each expanding node and the pair of the
339   query and the seed node. Among these, node pairs that exhibit the highest similarity with the query
340   are returned as edges, further expanding the graph Gc to Gl by adding these edges.
341
      The seed node selection calculates p(u|q) for each u ∈ Vc to identify the top-b (i.e., beam width)
342
      nodes that contain the most relevant information to the query. The probability p(u|q) is determined
343   by calculating the semantic similarity between the query and each node u in Gc , which is normalized
344   using a softmax function. This similarity is computed through an all-to-all interaction-based node
345   reranker gn . The similarity scores gn ([q; Γ(u)]) for all u are used to select the top-b seed nodes.
346
      The seed node expansion computes p(v|u, q) for each node v connected to seed node u in the com-
347
      plete bipartite graph G∗ . These conditional probabilities are calculated using the expanded query
348
      retrieval technique (Xiong et al.). In this technique, the score function is expressed with the ex-
349   panded query as s([q; Γ(u)], v), and it is calculated by two late interaction models: fP →S for a
350   table-segment-typed expanding node and a passage-typed seed node, and fS→P for the opposite.
351   The calculated scores are normalized using a softmax function to compute p(v|u, q). We then calcu-
352   late p(u, v|q) using Equation 4. The final probability is used to select the top-b probable edges from
353   the pairs of seed node u and expanding node v. They are added to Vc and Ec of Gc , forming the
354   updated bipartite graph Gl = (Vl , El , Φ, Γ, Λl ). Each new edge is scored using the identical scoring
355   module ge discussed earlier in § 4.1.
356   The change in retrieval accuracy of FusionMaestro based on beam width b is discussed in Ap-
357   pendix § C.2. The node reranker gn are fine-tuned for node selection. The late interaction encoders
358   fP →S and fS→P used for expanded query retrieval are fine-tuned following the ColBERTv2 train-
359   ing scheme (Santhanam et al., 2021). Detailed explanations for fine-tuning, including the construc-
360   tion of the training dataset, for all the modules can be found in Appendix § B.2 and Appendix § B.3.

      4.3   S TAR - BASED LLM R EFINEMENT
363   Traditional semantic similarity is insufficient to correctly retrieve documents for queries that re-
364   quire logical inference, such as column-wise aggregation or multi-hop reasoning. To overcome this
365   problem, we leverage the advanced reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs), which
366   allow us to refine the retrieved result through logical inference. The main goal is to use LLM’s
367   logical inference to add relevant edges to the graph Gl and remove irrelevant ones.
368   It is non-trivial to choose the specific format or unit of providing the graph Gl to the LLM. We
369   considered two approaches: including the entire graph Gl in a single prompt to return the relevant
370   set of nodes, and decomposing Gl into star graphs, with each star graph generating its own set
371   of relevant nodes. Among these, using star graphs as the unit proved to be 12.4% more effective,
372   leading us to select this as our unit for logical inference (§ 5.4).
373   The refinement process occurs in two phases: column-wise aggregation and passage verification.
374   The column-wise aggregation step restores tables from table segments, then identifies the candidate
375   rows based on the query and adds them back to the graph. The passage verification step evaluates
376   each star graph, returning the passages essential for answering the query. The refined edge-scored
377   graph is then decomposed into a ranked sequence of edges to produce the final retrieval output. A
      detailed process is expressed in Figure 4.


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Column-wise Aggregation. It aims to accurately infer the correct result rows for table aggrega-
379   tion operations, as exemplified in Figure 1(c). In our early fused graph Gd , tables are divided into
380   individual row-based table segments, making it hard for the previous-stage retrievers to perform
381   proper aggregation. It becomes necessary to reconstruct the original tables and perform reasoning
382   over these full tables.
383
      Since not every query requires aggregation, the first step is to prompt the LLM to determine whether
384
      the input query necessitates an aggregation operation. If the query is classified as an aggregation
385   query, the process follows two steps: (i) Table restoration: For each table segment, we utilize the
386   mapping function M to restore the original table. (ii) Aggregation: The restored tables are provided
387   to the LLM in the format of star graph, where LLM performs the aggregation and returns the rows
388   corresponding to the aggregation result. The returned rows are subsequently added back to Gl along
389   with their associated passages to generate Ga .
390
      Passage Verification. It aims to leverage the LLM’s logical inference capabilities to remove the
391   query-irrelevant passages within Ga . Similar to the column-wise aggregation step, we provide Ga
392   to the LLM in the form of star graphs, units that contain multi-hop relationships while not exceeding
393   the context limit. The LLM performs a binary verification to determine whether each edge is relevant
394   to the query, without recalculating their scores. As a result, query-irrelevant edges are eliminated,
395   leaving a refined, edge-scored graph Gq . Examples of prompts used for this step can be found in
396   Appendix § E.
397   Top-K Edge Selection. The graph Gq is then transformed into a ranked sequence of edges Eq by
398   applying the score mapping Λc . Specifically, all edges e in Gq are ranked in descending order based
399   on their scores Λc (e).

      We presented FusionMaestro, a novel method for table-text retrieval that harmonizes the
533
      strengths of both early fusion and late fusion techniques while incorporating large language model
534   (LLM) reasoning. It addresses the limitations of existing approaches by introducing a multi-granular
535   retrieval pipeline that optimally balances granularity across retrieval stages. By employing edge-
536   based bipartite subgraph retrieval, query-relevant node expansion, and star-based LLM refinement,
537   FusionMaestro provides more accurate retrieval by dynamically constructing query-relevant bi-
538   partite graphs. Experimental results demonstrate that FusionMaestro significantly outperforms
539   state-of-the-art models, with a 42.6% improvement in AR@2 and a 39.9% gain in nDCG@50, on
      the OTT-QA benchmark.
