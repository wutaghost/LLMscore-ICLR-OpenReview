009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Computer-Aided Design (CAD) has significant practical value in various industrial
012            applications. However, achieving high-quality and diverse shape generation, as
013            well as flexible conditional control, remains a challenge in the field of CAD
014            model generation. To address these issues, we propose CADiffusion, a diffusion-
015            based generative model with a hierarchical latent representation tailored to the
016            complexities of CAD design processes. To enhance the performance and reliability
017            of the model in generating accurate CAD models, we have developed a specialized
018
               decoder with regularization strategies that navigate through the noise space of
               the diffusion model, smoothing the results. This approach not only improves the
019
               diversity and quality of the generated CAD models but also enhances their practical
020
               applicability, marking a significant advancement in the integration of generative
021            models and automated CAD systems.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In the field of 3D computer vision, exploring the generation of 3D shapes has emerged as a promi-
027   nent issue in recent times. Various research studies have been conducted that encompass explicit
028   representations such as point clouds Yang et al. (2019); Cai et al. (2020); Mo et al. (2019), polygon
029   meshes Groueix et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2018); Nash et al. (2020), voxel grids Liao et al. (2018);
030   Li et al. (2017), and implicit representations Park et al. (2019); Mescheder et al. (2019); Chen et al.
031   (2020) such as neural radiance fields (NeRFs) and signed distance functions (SDFs). Despite the
032   significant progress achieved by these methodologies, the effectiveness of generating 3D models for
033
      practical applications remains unsatisfactory, mainly due to constraints related to data availability
      and modeling capabilities of the models. In contrast to other forms of 3D data, Computer-Aided
034
      Design (CAD) data holds immense practical value and finds applications across various industrial
035
      domains, ranging from automotive and aerospace to manufacturing and architectural design. CAD
036   software serves as the cornerstone for creating 3D shapes in these domains, facilitating intricate
037   design processes, and streamlining manufacturing workflows. Therefore, exploring the generation
038   of CAD models is highly significant as it has the potential to innovate many existing production
039   processes. Our work primarily explores representations and the corresponding generative model
040   structures that are more suitable for CAD generation.
041
      Despite some existing research on the generation of CAD data, challenges persist to enhance the
042   quality and diversity of the generated shapes. Current methodologies face challenges in generating
043   highly diverse CAD shapes, primarily due to limitations in supporting the intricacies of CAD spatial
044   modeling. DeepCAD Wu et al. (2021) generates CAD commands directly without modeling a space
045   suitable for CAD generation. Moreover, the exploration of generative models remains limited to
046   those such as GANs and autoregressive transformers Xu et al. (2022; 2023). Beyond the issues of
047   quality and diversity, existing methods also struggle to ensure the consistency and realism of the
048   generated results with the input conditions in conditional CAD generation.
049   To address these challenges, we introduce a hierarchical implicit space and, on top of it, we propose
050   CADiffusion, a diffusion-based generative model with a latent representation structured in a tree logic.
051   Most modern CAD design tools employ a “Sketch and Extrude” style workflow, where designers
052   first draw loops of 2D curves as outer and inner boundaries to create 2D profiles, then extrude the
053   2D profiles to 3D shapes, and finally add or subtract 3D shapes to build complex CAD models.
      Therefore, a hierarchical representation perfectly aligns with the inherent logic of CAD itself. This


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      hierarchical structure also offers effective design control at different levels of the hierarchy. When
055   modeling the latent space for CAD, we also need to consider the external, visible compositional logic
056   of CAD. In our approach, the CAD data is divided into three distinct hierarchical levels, with each
057   level employing a VAE to obtain latent representations. These representations are then organized into
058   a tree logic latent structure.
059
      To learn the probability distribution in the proposed latent space, we leverage diffusion models, which
060
      have recently achieved significant success in various 2D generation tasks. We find that diffusion
061   modeling method also exhibits high-quality and highly diverse generation capabilities in CAD data.
062   After using the diffusion model to fit the CAD data, to convert the CAD latent generated by the
063   diffusion model into CAD models accurately and efficiently, we designed a corresponding decoder
064   along with regularization terms. This involves navigating through the diffusion model’s noise space
065   and smoothing the outcomes of the sampled noise. This process ensures that the decoder can translate
066   any sample from the Gaussian noise space into a reasonable CAD model. The regularization approach
067   not only enhances the decoder’s ability to handle variations but also contributes to the overall stability
068   and reliability of CAD model generation in an automated setting. By integrating this regularized
069
      training methodology, we can bridge the gap between generating realistic data and the latent diffusion
      modeling, ensuring that the enhancements in generative model technology translate effectively into
070
      practical improvements in CAD systems. We evaluate the effectiveness of CADiffusion on benchmark
071
      datasets and show that it outperforms baseline approaches in a variety of metrics.
072
073   Therefore, our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) We are the first to explore the use of
074   diffusion models for CAD generation, and have designed corresponding models and representations.
075
      2) We have introduced a new regularization strategy specifically for CAD latent diffusion models,
      enabling the decoder to produce more reasonable and higher quality results. 3) Our CAD generation
076
      model achieves state-of-the-art performance, surpassing previous methods.

081   2.1   3D G ENERATIVE M ODELS
082
083   In recent years, significant attention has been directed towards the development of generative models
084
      for 3D shapes. Many existing approaches generate 3D shapes discretely, employing representations
      such as voxelized shapes Liao et al. (2018); Li et al. (2017), point clouds Yang et al. (2019); Cai
085
      et al. (2020); Mo et al. (2019), polygon meshes Groueix et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2018); Nash et al.
086
      (2020), and implicit signed distance fields Park et al. (2019); Mescheder et al. (2019); Chen et al.
087   (2020). Despite their prevalence, these models often produce shapes with noise, limited geometric
088   sharpness, and lack direct user editability. To address these limitations, recent research has focused
089   on neural network architectures that generate 3D shapes through sequences of geometric operations.
090   CSGNet Sharma et al. (2018), for instance, infers Constructive Solid Geometry (CSG) operations
091   from voxelized shape inputs, while UCSG-Net Kania et al. (2020) enhances the inference process
092   without relying on ground truth CSG trees. In addition, some approaches use domain-specific
093   languages (DSLs) Mo et al. (2019); Jones et al. (2020) to synthesize 3D shapes. For instance,
094   ShapeAssembly by Jones et al. Jones et al. (2020). introduces a DSL that constructs 3D shapes
095
      using hierarchical and symmetrical cuboid proxies, which can be generated through a variational
      autoencoder.

098   2.2   CAD G ENERATION
099
      Early research focused on direct CAD modeling without using any supervision from CAD modeling
100
      sequences. A common theme is to construct parametric curves Wang et al. (2020) and surfaces Sharma
101
      et al. (2020) with fixed Smirnov et al. (2021) or arbitrary topology for sketches Willis et al. (2021c)
102   and solid models Wang et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2022); Jayaraman et al. (2022). In recent years,
103   the availability of large-scale parametric CAD datasets has allowed learning-based methods to take
104   advantage of data from CAD modeling sequences Willis et al. (2021b); Wu et al. (2021); Xu et al.
105   (2022) and sketch constraints Seff et al. (2020) to generate engineering sketches and solid models.
106   The resulting sequences can be processed using a solid modeling kernel to acquire editable parametric
107   CAD files containing 2D engineering sketches Willis et al. (2021c); Para et al. (2021); Ganin et al.
      (2021); Seff et al. (2022) or 3D CAD shapes Wu et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022). Furthermore, the

      generation process may be influenced by the target B-rep Willis et al. (2021b); Xu et al. (2021),
109   sketches Li et al. (2020); Seff et al. (2022), images Ganin et al. (2021), voxel grids Lambourne et al.
110   (2022), or point clouds Uy et al. (2021), occasionally with sequence guidance Ren et al. (2022); Li
111   et al. (2023).
112
      More recently, there have been some advancements in the field of CAD model generation. Deep-
113
      CAD Wu et al. (2021) directly generates CAD commands without modeling the data representations
114
      first. CAD models are graphically and geometrically complex, and generating commands can lead to
115   overly simplified results. Consequently, the outcomes from DeepCAD are generally quite simple.
116   Subsequent efforts, such as those by SkexGen Xu et al. (2022) and HNC Xu et al. (2023), have
117   employed autoregressive transformer models. Their use of discrete formats excessively compresses
118   the representations, failing to effectively capture the intrinsic logic of CAD data. The representations
119   utilized by these methods are either too redundant or semantically sparse, which impairs the genera-
120   tive model’s performance in fitting them. Compared to other 3D data, CAD possesses parametric
121   characteristics, and suitable representations and models for it are still under exploration.

      2.3   D IFFUSION M ODELS
124
125
      Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020), commonly
126
      referred to as diffusion models, have emerged as a robust class of generative models. Unlike
127   previous leading generative models such as the Generative Adversarial Network Goodfellow et al.
128   (2020), Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Kingma & Welling (2014), and flow-based generative
129   models Rezende & Mohamed (2015), diffusion models exhibit notable advantages in terms of training
130   stability and generative diversity Croitoru et al. (2023). They have shown promising performance
131   in image Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Nichol et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022)
132   and speech Chen et al. (2021); Kong et al. (2021) synthesis. In particular, diffusion model-based
133   approaches have shown remarkable results in text-to-image synthesis Ramesh et al. (2022); Rombach
134   et al. (2022); Saharia et al. (2022). In the realm of 3D computer vision, several studies have embraced
135
      diffusion models for generative 3D modeling Luo & Hu (2021); Zhou et al. (2021); Zeng et al. (2022).
      For example, PVD Zhou et al. (2021) used diffusion models to create 3D shapes using a point-voxel
136
      3D representation. Luo et al. Luo & Hu (2021) considered points in point clouds as particles within a
137
      thermodynamic system with a heat bath. LION Zeng et al. (2022) introduced a VAE framework with
138   hierarchical diffusion models in latent space. Similar attempts Chou et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2023)
139   have also applied diffusion models to the generation of SDFs. However, no exploration of diffusion
140   models has been made on CAD data. We are the first to attempt using diffusion models to generate
141   CAD data and have achieved very promising results.

149   objects they represent. This hierarchical structure is essential to accurately represent and manipulate
150   engineering designs, mechanical components, and architectural plans. Thus, in our approach, we
151   employ a hierarchical representation for CAD that builds on the foundations laid by SkexGen Xu
152   et al. (2022) and HNC Xu et al. (2023), which themselves are extensions of the pioneering work of
153   TurtleGen Willis et al. (2021a) and DeepCAD Wu et al. (2021).

236   3.3   CAD D ECODER R EGULARIZATION T ERM
237
238   The unique challenges presented by our latent diffusion model, which generates hierarchical, tree
239   logic latent representations, are presented below. Parsing generated latents into different hierarchies
240   is prone to errors and can be overly cumbersome. Instead, the latents generated encapsulate the logic
241   and components of CAD, enabling direct decoding into a CAD model. This approach is indeed more
242
      efficient; however, it is crucial to ensure its accuracy as well. Although the three different levels of
      latents is logically assembled together, decoding it into a realistic CAD model is more challenging
243
      than reconstructing a single level of CAD components separately. We found that training this decoder
244
      solely with the latents of training dataset is insufficient and leads to some unrealistic decoded results.
245   To enhance the stability and fidelity of our CAD generation process, a novel regularization technique
246   is developed. This technique involves perturbing the latent space to simulate variations that the
247   diffusion model might generate, thus training the decoder to be resilient to these variations and
248   ensuring smoother transitions between different CAD models. The regularization process consists of
249   several steps, detailed below:
250

251   the noise space using the DDIM inversion method Song et al. (2021). The DDIM inversion process
252   systematically reintroduces noise into a clean latent representation to reach a noised state that can
253   then be diffused to regenerate the original latent, effectively serving as a way to explore variations in
254   the generated CAD models. Starting from a latent at the initial time step, the DDIM inversion aims
255   to compute a corresponding noised latent after T steps. The inversion process is governed by the
256   following equation:

      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
331
332   Dataset: Using the extensive DeepCAD dataset Wu et al. (2021), we acquire ground truth sketch-and-
333   extrude models, comprising 178,238 instances. These models are divided into a training set (90%), a
334   validation set (5%), and a test set (5%). To ensure the integrity of the data set, we implement methods
335   similar to previous studies Willis et al. (2021c); Xu et al. (2022) to detect and eliminate duplicate
336   models from the training set. In addition to removing duplicate models, we extract hierarchical
337   properties for loops, profiles, and solids, and subsequently remove duplicate properties at each level.
338   Furthermore, for training purposes, CAD models are included only if they meet specific criteria: a
339
      maximum of 5 solids, 20 loops per profile, 60 curves per loop, and a maximum of 200 commands
      in the sketch-and-extrude sequence. Following the duplicate removal and filtering processes, the
340
      training dataset comprises 102,114 solids, 60,584 profiles, and 150,158 loops for codebook learning.
341
      Additionally, 124,451 sketch-and-extrude sequences are retained for CAD model generation training.
342   For CAD engineering drawings, we adopt the approach described in SkexGen Xu et al. (2022) and
343   extract sketches from DeepCAD. A total of 99,650 sketches are utilized for training purposes after
344   duplicate removal.
345
      Other Details: The model is trained on a Nvidia RTX A100 GPU with a batch size of 256. Each
346
      VQ-VAE model and model decoder are trained for 250 epochs. For the randomly generated and
347
      conditionally generated diffusion models, we train them for 350 epochs and 500 epochs, respectively.
348   We use the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2018) with a learning rate of 0.001 after a
349   linear warm-up for the first 2000 steps. The VQ-VAE network consists of 4 layers. For the diffusion
350   model, there are six blocks, each comprising a self-attention layer and a fully-connected layer. If it
351   is a conditional generation, each block also includes a cross-attention layer. During the generation
352   process, we use DDIM for sampling, with a sampling step of 100 steps. For the corresponding scale
353   of the CFG, we set it to 3. σ for perturbation is set it to 0.1. More details can be found in the appendix.
354   Evaluation Metrics: We use five established metrics to quantitatively assess random generation.
355   Three metrics are based on point clouds sampled on the model surfaces. Two metrics scrutinizing
356   generated tokens originating from sketch and extrude construction sequences. For point-cloud
357   evaluation, 2,000 points are sampled from each generated and ground-truth dataset, facilitating a
358   comparative analysis of the two sets. Descriptions of metrics are referred to in the appendix.

475   In conclusion, we have introduced CADiffusion, a novel diffusion-based generative model tailored for
476   Computer-Aided Design (CAD) data generation. Our approach addresses the persistent challenges
477   in producing diverse and high-quality CAD shapes by seamlessly integrating diffusion models and
478   Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoders (VQVAE) to obtain latent representations. Through
479   extensive experimentation, we have demonstrated the effectiveness of CADiffusion, achieving state-
480   of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets. Additionally, we introduced a regularization method
481   specifically tailored for training the decoder. This method employs perturbations in the Gaussian
482
      space to smooth the decoder’s outputs, thereby enabling it to produce more reasonable results. We
      believe that CADiffusion opens up new possibilities for advancing 3D shape generation in practical

706   A    E VALUATION M ETRICS
707
708   Due to the unique characteristics of CAD data modalities, we have provided detailed explanations of
709   the five different metrics mentioned in the main paper here, to facilitate reader understanding.
710
711         • Coverage (COV) denotes the proportion of ground-truth models that contain at least one
712           matched generated sample, where matching is determined based on the Chamfer distance
713           (CD) or Earth Mover’s distance (EMD). COV serves as a measure of the diversity of
714
              generated shapes, revealing potential mode collapse if only a few ground-truth models are
              matched, resulting in low coverage scores.
715
716         • Minimum Matching Distance (MMD) calculates the average minimum matching distance
717           between the ground truth and generated sets.
718         • Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) assesses the similarity between two probability distribu-
719           tions, reflecting the degree to which ground truth and generated point clouds occupy similar
720           locations. Utilizing voxelization, occupancy distributions are computed to derive the JSD
721           score.
722         • Novelty indicates the percentage of generated CAD sequences absent in the training set,
723           while uniqueness signifies the percentage of generated data that appear once within the
724           generated set.
725
726   B    I NPLEMETATION D ETAILS
727
728   The tree structure corresponding to the CAD is obtained directly by parsing according to the CAD
729   logic, implemented by a segment of code. This part is the same as in HNC. The CAD latent used
730   for diffusion learning is in the form of tensors with a shape of 32 × 256, the input and output
731   shapes are the same. Therefore, all our model architectures use 1D transformer structures, which
732   mainly include Self-Attention, Fully Connected Layers, and Cross-Attention layers. In the Self-
733   Attention mechanism, the basic QKV form corresponds to different matrix transformations followed
734   by attention calculations. In the Cross-Attention layer, the input conditions are also transformed
735

738   C    H UMAN E VALUATION
739
740   Our approach to Human Evaluation is similar to HNC and SolidGen. For each method that need to
741   be evaluated, we randomly select models with three or more extrusions from their results generated
742   without any conditions. For each model created by a generation method, we randomly choose a
743
      real model from the dataset and display the rendered images of these two models side by side.
      We randomly select 100 results from each method, and the image pairs are presented to college
744
      participants, who are asked to assess which one appears more “realistic.”
