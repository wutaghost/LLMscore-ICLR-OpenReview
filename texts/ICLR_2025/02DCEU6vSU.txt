009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Evaluating the potential privacy leakage of synthetic data is an important but un-
012            resolved problem. Most existing adversarial auditing frameworks for synthetic
013            data rely on heuristics and unreasonable assumptions to attack the failure modes
014            of generative models, exhibiting limited capability to describe and detect the pri-
015            vacy exposure of training data. In this paper, we study designing Membership
016            Inference Attacks (MIAs) that specifically exploit the observation that generative
017            models tend to memorize certain data points in their training sets, leading to sig-
018
               nificant local overfitting. Here, we propose Generative Likelihood Ratio Attack
               (Gen-LRA), a novel, computationally efficient shadow-box MIA that, with no as-
019
               sumption of model knowledge or access, attacks the generated synthetic dataset
020
               by conducting a hypothesis test that it is locally overfit to potential training data.
021            Assessed over a comprehensive benchmark spanning diverse datasets, model ar-
022            chitectures, and attack parameters, we find that Gen-LRA consistently dominates
023            other MIAs for generative models across multiple performance metrics. These
024            results underscore Gen-LRA’s effectiveness as an interpretable and robust privacy
025            auditing tool, highlighting the significant privacy risks posed by generative model
026            overfitting in real-world applications.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Real world tabular data is often privacy-sensitive to the individual observations that compose these
032   samples, hindering their ability to be shared in open-science efforts that can aid in new research and
033
      improve reproducibility. A promise of generative modeling is that models trained on private data
      can produce samples that preserve the privacy of the training set while maintaining much of the
034
      original statistical information. In practice, a wide array of methodologies have been proposed to
035
      accomplish this involving modifying loss functions (Abadi et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2022), creating
036   new architectures (Yoon et al., 2019; 2020a), and studying data release strategies (Hardt et al., 2012;
037   Gupta et al., 2012; Takagi et al., 2021) to provide a guarantee of differential privacy. In another
038   direction, many methods have been proposed that maximize the fidelity of synthetic data and argue
039   they are private through similarity metrics like average Distance to Closest Record that evaluate
040   overfittness (Zhao et al., 2021; Guillaudeux et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023).
041
      While both lines of synthetic data research (private and non-private) have seen rapid advancements,
042   techniques to evaluate the empirical privacy of these generative models have lagged behind. Au-
043   diting differentially private algorithms can be methodologically challenging (Jagielski et al., 2020;
044   Chua et al., 2024) and from a practitioner perspective, theoretical notions of privacy can be difficult
045   to practically interpret. For non-differentially-private models, similarity metrics between the train-
046   ing and synthetic sets have been argued to be heuristic as they do not actually characterize privacy
047   risk but rather an ad-hoc measure of overfitting (Platzer & Reutterer, 2021; Ganev & Cristofaro,
048   2023; Ward et al., 2024).
049   Recently, Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) have shown to be a computationally efficient, pow-
050   erful, and interpretable framework for evaluating the empirical privacy of machine learning models
051   by attacking overfitting (Shokri et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020; Carlini et al., 2021). Here, privacy
052   auditing is posed as a game where an adversary, given a threat model that describes what informa-
053   tion can be used, constructs an attack that classifies whether a test observation is a member of the
      dataset a model was trained with. A successful attack represents a practical and interpretable pri-

055   cancer dataset and, for a new applicant, attack the dataset to determine if the applicant is a member,

      While promising, MIAs for generative models and synthetic data release have seen limited success.
      Previous work in Generative Model MIAs often relied on heuristics for the attack and usually ex-

064   framework to target privacy leakage from model overfitting. We show that our attack broadly out-
065   performs competing methods especially at low fixed false positive rates, highlighting that overfitting
066   presents a more dangerous source of privacy leakage then previously suggested, even in differen-
067   tially private generative models. Our contributions are as follows:

083   2   M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE ATTACKS F ORMALISM
084
085   In this work, we specifically study the Membership Inference Attack Game in the context of synthetic
086   data generation. The objective of this game is to determine whether a particular data point was
087   included in the original training dataset by examining the outputs of a generative model. We first
088   introduce the formal definition of the Membership Inference Attack Game:
089
      Definition (Membership Inference Attack Game). The game proceeds between a challenger C
090   and an adversary A as follows:
091
092         1. The challenger samples a training dataset T = xi ni=1 from the population distribution
093            xi ∼ P and uses T to train a tabular generative model G ← T (T ). The generative model
094            G produces synthetic dataset S.
095         2. The challenger flips a bit b ∈ 0, 1. If b = 0, the challenger samples a test observation x⋆
096            from the population distribution P. Otherwise, the challenger selects the test observation
097            x⋆ from the training set T .
098         3. The challenger sends the test observation x⋆ to the adversary A.
099
100
            4. The adversary has access to some information defined by a threat model and uses this
101
               information to output a guess b̂ ← A(x⋆ ).
102         5. The output of the game is 1 if b̂ = b, and 0 otherwise. The adversary wins if b̂ = b, i.e.,
103            if it correctly identifies whether the test observation x⋆ was part of the training set T or a
104            freshly sampled data point from the population distribution P.
105
106   Adversary’s Goal and Capabilities The adversary A in the Membership Inference Game aims to
107   determine whether a specific data point x⋆ was part of the original training dataset T or was drawn
      from the population distribution P. Here, the adversary can utilize available information in any

109   which can be evaluated with binary classification metrics, is a measure of the privacy leakage of the

111   expressed as:

116   Threat Model In this paper, we consider a threat model where the attacker has access to a set of
117   synthetic data S generated by a model M learned on D. We make no assumptions on the architecture
118   or parameterization of the model nor do we assume the attacker has access to an API of the model
119   in which to continuously query for an arbitrarily large S (Meehan et al., 2020; Bhattacharjee et al.,
120   2023). This corresponds to the practical scenario in which a synthetic dataset is released publicly
121   for use. We also assume the attacker has access to a reference dataset R that was not used in the
122   training of the model, but is an independent sample from the same population as the training dataset,
              iid
123   T, R ∼ P. We assume this in practice because this represents a plausible scenario for the owner
124   of S as an attacker may be able to find comparable data in the real world such as open source
125   datasets, paid collection, prior knowledge, etc. van Breugel et al. (2023) for example showed that
126
      reference datasets often improve the effectiveness of MIAs for generative models and many MIAs
      for supervised learning models incorporate reference sets as well in ”shadow-box” attacks (Carlini
127
      et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022; Zarifzadeh et al., 2024).
128
129
      Attack Strategy The adversary must develop a strategy in which to construct f (x∗ ). We specifi-
130
      cally propose that the adversary utilize the degree of local overfitting within S as the primary signal
131   to determine whether a specific data point x⋆ belongs to the training set.
132
133   Overfitting is a common and difficult-to-eliminate failure mode in generative models, particularly in
134
      the context of tabular synthetic data generation. In the setting of Membership Inference Attacks, this
      failure mode becomes a significant source of privacy leakage. van Breugel et al. (2023) for example
135
      identified that TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) overfit to minority class examples in a medical training dataset,
136
      leaking their privacy. Similarly, Ward et al. (2024) found that TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2022),
137   when tasked with generating synthetic data for the well-known Adult dataset, heavily replicated
138   data points from certain demographic groups within the training data. The key insight drawn from
139   this phenomenon is that areas of the synthetic data distribution with higher density are likely to
140   reflect signals from the original training data. Leveraging this failure, it becomes possible to infer
141   whether specific data points were part of the training set, thus providing a basis for designing privacy
142   attacks. Our work builds on these findings by proposing a new method to measure the degree of local
143   overfitting in generative models. We utilize this metric to design a Membership Inference Attack
144   aimed at exposing the potential privacy risks inherent in synthetic data (See Section 3).

255   Localization A common theme in designing MIAs is to adopt techniques that maximize the signal
256   of x∗ ’s membership in the attack. Realistically, there is likely to be very little signal in comparing
257   the likelihoods of S over estimated probability density functions with a difference of a single ob-
258
      servation. Indeed, equation 5 is an attack over the global difference in pR∪x∗ and pR . Instead, if
      we have some idea for what regions of the two estimated PDFs are likely to most differ, we can
259
      focus the attack on areas that should contain the most signal. Here, we localize Gen-LRA by only
260
      considering the k-nearest elements in S to x∗ in our calculation of equation 5. In practice, the choice
261   of k can have minor impacts on the effectiveness of the attack, but we find we get excellent results
262   with small values (See Appendix A.3).
263
264   Choice of Decision Threshold While the previous sections detail the derivation of a scoring func-
265   tion, equation 1 still requires a decision threshold γ. Intuitively for Gen-LRA, γ can be any chosen
266   threshold but λR,K (S, x⋆ > 1 implies some degree of overfitting. As many MIAs do not have a
267   natural thresholding heuristic, a technique often employed is simply taking the median score over
268   many test observations. In practice though, as MIAs are privacy auditing tools the decision thresh-
269   old is less important as a practitioner should evaluate the attack at all possible thresholding values
      to understand the maximal privacy risk of the attack. In evaluating MIAs, we therefore focus on

298   4.1   A SSESSING OVERFITTING IN TABULAR G ENERATIVE M ODELS
299
300   Several measures have been developed to assess the fitness of tabular synthetic data, particularly
301   from a privacy perspective. These metrics generally aim to measure the similarity between the
302   training and synthetic datasets, with the ideal outcome being that the synthetic data is neither too
303   similar to the training data nor too different. A widely used metric for this purpose is Distance to
304   Closest Record1 (Park et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2019; Yale et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021; Guillaudeux
305
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), which compares the distance from each training point to its nearest
      neighbor in the synthetic dataset to which a mean is computed. Another commonly used metric is
306
      the Identical Matching Score (IMS) (Lu et al., 2019; AI, 2020; 2021), which measures the proportion
307
      of identical records between the training and synthetic datasets. While these measures can be useful
308   for describing overfitness from a sample quality and model generalization perspective, they do not
309   characterize privacy risk because there is no assumed threat model and they are not evaluated over
310   non-member examples.
311
312   4.2   MIA S FOR M ACHINE L EARNING M ODELS
313
314   Membership Inference Attacks on the other hand, explicitly characterize the empirical privacy risk
315   of a machine learning model (Song & Mittal, 2020; Yeom et al., 2018). Originally, MIAs were
316   developed for attacking supervised learning classifiers (Shokri et al., 2017). In this context, the
317   general idea for these attacks is to query a model with different observations to learn patterns in its
318
      class probability outputs. Membership can then be inferred by comparing the outputs of the model
      to outputs from reference models in some manner (Sablayrolles et al., 2019a; Long et al., 2020;
319
      Carlini et al., 2021; Watson et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022; Zarifzadeh et al., 2024). While fundamental
320
      to the literature, these methods are largely incompatible with attacking synthetic data generators as
321
322      1
           DCR in the similarity metric case compares a training point to a synthetic point. However, Chen et al.
323   (2020) proposes an MIA where the scoring function is a distance computation for a test point and a synthetic
      point. In all other sections of the paper we use DCR to refer to the MIA.


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      they rely on unlimited query access to the model and also formulate their attacks around returned
325   probability predictions.
326
327   To adapt to the structural differences in the problem domain, MIAs in the generative model setting
328
      have adopted two broad styles of developing a scoring function: distance-based and density-based
      attacks. Distance-based attacks rely on using some measure of distance between the test observation
329
      and the synthetic and/or reference sets (Hayes et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020; Ward et al., 2024).
330
      Similarly to Gen-LRA, density-based attacks attack inconsistencies in the probability densities of
331   the synthetic and reference sets (Hilprecht et al., 2019; van Breugel et al., 2023). While these works
332   usually cover MIAs under a wide range of threat models, we only consider the attacks that use a
333   black-box (only synthetic data access) or shadow-box (only synthetic and reference data access)
334   threat model. This is in contrast to white-box attacks in which an adversary have both synthetic and
335   reference data as well as internal access to the model (Matsumoto et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2023; Wu
336   et al., 2023). White-box attacks generally are specific to the architecture of a model (Sablayrolles
337   et al., 2019b) and are not generalizable to the broader synthetic data release paradigm in which data
338   owners usually do not release their model weights.

496   7    C ONCLUSION
497
498   Membership Inference Attacks are a useful tool for evaluating generative models for synthetic data
499   release. They can characterize the privacy risk towards training observations, provide information
500   on how a model may be overfit, and add subtle context to patterns of behavior in generative models.
501   In this paper, we propose Gen-LRA, which constructs a likelihood ratio of the synthetic data using
502   simple Kernel Density Estimators. We show that it excels at attacking a diverse set of generative
503   models across a wide-range of datasets and that this success comes from Gen-LRA’s unique ability to
504   target a generative model’s tendency to overfit to training outliers- a trait that is not well-shared with
505   other common MIAs. We note that there are several drawbacks with Gen-LRA in that it requires
506
      dimension reduction techniques to be compatible with high dimensional heterogeneous data and that
      it fails at detecting flagrant data-copying. However, we point out that Gen-LRA is compatible with
507
      high dimensional density estimation strategies and that empirically, Gen-LRA usually outperforms
508
      other attacks despite these disadvantages.
509
510   We believe that there are many directions for future work. Exploring emerging density estima-
511   tion methodologies would likely yield better empirical performance, especially on high dimensional
512
      datasets. On a different front, research into developing adversarial techniques to better understand
      model overfitting in general could also lead to important interpretability techniques. Lastly, we
513
      believe that it is important to investigate the observed phenomenon that Gen-LRA can specifically
514
      target distinct sub-groups of a training dataset as this implies that even if an attack is largely unsuc-
515   cessful in the aggregate, high-risk observations may still be leaked.

873   A.4    MIA S FOR G ENERATIVE M ODELS D ESCRIPTIONS
874
875   The Membership Inference Attacks referenced in this paper is are described as follows:
876
877          • LOGAN Hayes et al. (2017): LOGAN consists of black box and shadow box attack. The
878
               black-box version involves training a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) on the syn-
               thetic dataset and using the discriminator to score test data. A calibrated version improves
879
               upon this by training a binary classifier to distinguish between the synthetic and reference
880
               dataset. In this paper, we only benchmark the calibrated version.
881
882
             • Distance to Closest Record (DCR) / DCR Difference Chen et al. (2020): DCR is a black-
               box attack that scores test data based on a sigmoid score of the distance to the nearest
883
               neighbor in the synthetic dataset. DCR Difference enhances this approach by incorporating
884
               a reference set, subtracting the distance to the closest record in the reference set from the
885            synthetic set distance.
886
             • MC Hilprecht et al. (2019): MC is based on counting the number of observations in the
887
               synthetic dataset that fall into the neighborhood of a test point (Monte Carlo Integration).
888            However, this method does not consider a reference dataset, and the choice of distance
889            metric for defining a neighborhood is a non-trivial hyperparameter to tune.
890
             • DOMIAS van Breugel et al. (2023): DOMIAS is a calibrated attack which scores test
891
               data by performing density estimation on both the synthetic and reference datasets. It then
892            calculates the density ratio of the test data between the learned synthetic and reference
893            probability densities.
894
             • DPI Ward et al. (2024): DPI computes the ratio of k-Nearest Neighbors of x∗ in the syn-
895
               thetic and reference datasets. It then builds a scoring function by computing the ratio of the
896            sum of data points from each class of neighbors from the respective sets.

      A.5    G ENERATIVE M ODEL A RCHITECTURE D ESCRIPTIONS
899
900   In all experiments, we use the implementations of these models from the Python package Synthcity
901   Qian et al. (2023). For benchmarking purposes, we use the default hyperparameters for each model.
902   A brief description of each model is as follows:
903
904
             • CTGAN Xu et al. (2019): Conditional Tabular Generative Adversarial Network uses a
               GAN framework with conditional generator and discriminator to capture multi-modal dis-
905
               tributions. It employs mode normalization to better learn mixed-type distributions.
906
907          • TVAE Xu et al. (2019): Tabular Variational Auto-Encoder is similar to CTGAN in its use of
908
               mode normalizing techniques, but instead of a GAN architecture, it employs a Variational
               Autoencoder.
909
910          • Normalizing Flows (NFlows) Durkan et al. (2019): Normalizing flows transform a simple
911            base distribution (e.g., Gaussian) into a more complex one matching the data by applying
912
               a sequence of invertible, differentiable mappings.
913          • Bayesian Network (BN) Ankan & Panda (2015): Bayesian Networks use a Directed
914            Acyclic Graph to represent the joint probability distribution over variables as a product
915            of marginal and conditional distributions. It then samples the empirical distributions esti-
916
               mated from the training dataset.
917          • Adversarial Random Forests (ARF) Watson et al. (2023): ARFs extend the random forest
               model by adding an adversarial stage. Random forests generate synthetic samples which


                                                                  17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
               are scored against the real data by a discriminator network. This score is used to re-train
919            the forests iteratively.
920
             • Tab-DDPM Kotelnikov et al. (2022): Tabular Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model
921
               adapts the DDPM framework for image synthesis. It iteratively refines random noise into
922
               synthetic data by learning the data distribution through gradients of a classifier on partially
923            corrupted samples with Gaussian noise.
924
             • PATEGAN Yoon et al. (2019): The PATEGAN model uses a neural encoder to map dis-
925
               crete tabular data into a continuous latent representation which is sampled from during
926
               generation by the GAN discriminator and generator pair.
927
928
             • Ads-GAN Yoon et al. (2020b): Ads-GAN uses a GAN architecture for tabular synthesis
               but also adds an identifiability metric to increase its ability to not mimic training data.
