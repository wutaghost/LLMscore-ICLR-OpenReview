009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have significantly en-
012            hanced their knowledge and generative capabilities, leading to a surge of inter-
013            est in leveraging LLMs for high-quality data synthesis. However, synthetic data
014            generation via prompting LLMs remains challenging due to LLMs’ limited un-
015            derstanding of target data distributions and the complexity of prompt engineering,
016
               especially for structured formatted data. To address these issues, we introduce
               DiffLM, a controllable data synthesis framework based on variational autoencoder
017
               (VAE), which further (1) leverages diffusion models to reserve more information
018
               of original distribution and format structure in the learned latent distribution and
019            (2) decouples the learning of target distribution knowledge from the LLM’s gen-
020            erative objectives via a plug-and-play latent feature injection module. As we ob-
021            served significant discrepancies between the VAE’s latent representations and the
022            real data distribution, the latent diffusion module is introduced into our framework
023            to learn a fully expressive latent distribution. Evaluations on seven real-world
024            datasets with structured formatted data (i.e., Tabular, Code and Tool data) demon-
025            strate that DiffLM generates high-quality data, with performance on downstream
026            tasks surpassing that of real data by 2%–7% in certain cases. Data and code will
027
               be released upon acceptance.
028
029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Data Synthesis has become an indispensable technique in current machine learning research, en-
032   abling rapid generation and modification of datasets (Bauer et al., 2024), allowing researchers to
033   experiment with various scenarios and model architectures without the extensive processes associ-
034   ated with real-world data collection. Meanwhile, with the rapid advancements in large language
035   models (LLMs), recent research in natural language processing (NLP) has increasingly focused on
036   leveraging LLMs for synthetic data generation. Early efforts attempted to fine-tune LLMs to align
037   with real data distributions (Keskar et al., 2019; Anaby-Tavor et al., 2020; Borisov et al., 2023). As
038   the in-context learning capabilities of LLMs have improved, some studies have explored zero-shot
039
      or few-shot prompting of LLMs to generate synthetic data (Ye et al., 2022a; Wei et al., 2024).
040   Despite the progress achieved, generating high-quality synthetic textual data using LLMs remains
041   challenging, particularly for structured data (Josifoski et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022). First, LLMs often
042   lack a global understanding of the target data distribution when generating synthetic data. Even after
043   fine-tuning, it is difficult to inject information about complex and varied distributions into current
044   LLM architectures, often resulting in outputs with low diversity and instances of data copying (Wu
045
      et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023). Moreover, existing LLM-based synthetic data generation methods
      typically involve complex pipelines and post-processing mechanisms, such as prompt engineering,
046
      multi-agent frameworks, and iterative sampling (Dekoninck et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). These
047
      complexities hinder the rapid adaptation of LLMs to new tasks, limiting their utility in dynamic
048   research and industrial scenario. Concurrently, the remarkable performance of variational autoen-
049   coders (VAEs) and diffusion models in image synthesis tasks (Betker et al., 2023; Rombach et al.,
050   2022) has spurred interest in adapting these techniques to other modalities (Borisov et al., 2023; Li
051   et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2023). Although some works have introduced latent spaces into language
052   models for simple tasks like style transfer or topic generation (Yang & Klein, 2021; Li et al., 2022),
053   our preliminary experiments indicate that directly applying the latent distributions learned by VAEs
      often results in outputs that are unrelated to the real data. Similar issues also have been addressed


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      in prior works (Amani et al., 2024; Havrylov & Titov, 2020; Bowman et al., 2016). This challenges
055   the direct application of these methods in more complex scenarios for synthetic data generation.
056
057   To address these challenges, we propose DiffLM, a novel framework that leverages a plug-and-play
058
      latent space to provide data distribution information for LLMs during data generation. First, to
      decouple the learning of real data distributions from the LLM’s training objectives, we develop a
059
      latent space using a VAE model to capture external information, mapping samples from the real
060
      dataset to latent vectors. However, we observed that sampling points from a Gaussian distribution
061   obtained from naive VAE that cannot generate realistic results. To overcome the poor quality of data
062   generated by sampling from VAE, we employ a latent diffusion method that linearly adds noise to
063   the latent space over time. A denoising network is then trained to learn these noises in the reverse
064   process, reducing efficiency loss in data synthesis due to sampling failures. Finally, we design a
065   soft prompting method to inject latent features into the LLM decoding process, resulting in control-
066   lable, high-quality synthetic data. We evaluate our method on seven real-world structured formatted
067   datasets, ranging from relatively simple table synthesis to more complex code and tool synthesis
068   tasks. Experiments demonstrate that DiffLM can generate high-quality results, and ablation studies
069
      confirm the effectiveness of each component in our proposed method.
070   The contributions of this paper are threefold:
071
072   • Decoupling Data Distribution Learning: We proposed a new VAE-based LLM framework for
073     data systhesis, which decouples the learning of real data distribution information from the training
074     objectives of the LLM by introducing the a small projection network.
075   • High-Quality Synthetic Data Generation: Based on our observations, the meticulously designed
076     VAE and diffusion structures effectively model the distribution of real data, enabling the genera-
077     tion of high-quality synthetic data. In all tasks, the quality of the generated data is comparable to
078     or even surpasses that of the real data.
079   • Comprehensive Evaluation: We validate the high quality of data generated by DiffLM across
080     three distinct scenarios and seven datasets, underscoring its robustness and adaptability in advanc-
081     ing synthetic data generation for natural language processing.

      Large Language Models in Data Synthesis. The recent advancement in the generative capabil-
086
      ities of LLMs has motivated numerous exploratory works aiming to leverage these models for data
087
      augmentation in areas such as text classification (Ye et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2023), information ex-
088   traction (Tang et al., 2023; Josifoski et al., 2023), and tabular data generation (Borisov et al., 2023;
089   Xu et al., 2024). A comprehensive survey conducted by Long et al. (2024) proposes a prompt-based
090   generic workflow for synthetic data generation, curation, and evaluation. And multiple advanced
091   works have attempted to fine-tune language models for data synthesis in recent years (Anaby-Tavor
092   et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020; Dinh et al., 2022; Borisov et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Specifically,
093   these methods involve fine-tuning LLMs on a small amount of gold data for language modeling,
094   followed by the use of various sampling methods to generate data. However, a major challenge
095   remains in ensuring that synthetic data accurately reflects real-world distributions. Veselovsky et al.
096
      (2023) has shown that LLM-generated data can sometimes diverge from actual data distributions,
      leading to unfaithful representations that may hinder model training. Some studies have explored
097
      data selection (Puri et al., 2020) or data augmentation (Ye et al., 2022b) to address this distribution
098
      gap, but there remains significant room for improvement.

139   3      M ETHODOLOGY
140
141   Figure 1 illustrates the main pipeline of our proposed DiffLM. First, we define an encoder to map
142   discrete text into a continuous latent space (Section 3.2). Second, although the features of the data
143   are extracted and compressed, conventional latent embeddings in text VAEs often lead to decoding
144   failures due to underutilized or empty regions in the latent space. To address this issue, we train a
145   diffusion model on the latent space (Section 3.3). Finally, to incorporate encoded prior knowledge
146   into the decoding stage of large language models, we propose a novel soft prompt injection method
147   to steer the decoding process (Section 3.4).
148
149   3.1      P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
150
151
      We begin by defining D as a known small set of real-world distribution data, where each element x
      represents a real sample. We define G as the synthetic data generator, which learns the distribution of
152
      D and generates a set of synthetic samples, Dsyn , ensuring that the model does not simply memorize
153
      and reproduce the same real samples, meaning D ∩ Dsyn = ∅. It should be noted that we focus
154   on the task of unconditional data synthesising using LLMs, where G generates synthetic samples
155   independently of any additional context, i.e., without using explicit prompt text.
156
157   3.2      VAE- BASED R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
158
159   Feature Encoding: In standard VAEs, an encoder is typically employed to map input data into a
160   latent space. Given structured text data si , we utilize a learnable Transformer-based pre-trained lan-
161   guage model (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020) to obtain the representa-
      tion vector xi ∈ Rd×2 , which can be split into the mean and variance. Using the re-parameterization

162
      trick (Kingma & Welling, 2014), we then obtain the latent feature z ∈ Rd :
163
                                              z = µ + σ ⊙ ϵ,                                       (1)
164
165   where µ and σ are the mean and standard deviation output by the encoder, and ϵ is sampled from a
166   standard normal distribution N (0, I).
167   LLM Decoding: After generating the latent feature z, we employ a frozen-parameter LLM to re-
168   construct the input text s in a causal language modeling manner. The rationale for freezing the LLM
169   parameters is to avoid retraining and to preserve its general knowledge and reasoning capabilities.
170   Consequently, aligning the two different modalities, whereas the latent space and the LLM input
171   space, presents a significant challenge. To address this, we propose a novel latent feature injector
172   using soft prompting and design a corresponding injector network; specific details are provided in
173
      Section 3.4.
174   VAE Training Objective: The VAE model is typically trained using the Evidence Lower Bound
175   (ELBO) loss function. Following previous work (Burgess et al., 2018), we adopt the β-VAE training
176   strategy (Higgins et al., 2017), which introduces a weighting parameter β to control the contribution
177   of the KL divergence loss in the total loss function. Specifically, when β = 0, the model reduces to
178   a standard autoencoder. For β > 0, the KL constraint encourages learning a smoother latent space:
179                                     ELBOβ = Lrec − βLkl ,                                           (2)

183   where pθ (x|z) is the language modeling reconstruction likelihood, qϕ (z|x) is the approximate pos-
184   terior, and p(z) is the prior over the latent space, i.e., Gaussian distribution. In our model design,
185
      considering the denoising network of latent diffusion, we adopt an decreasing β adjustment strat-
      egy. We initially set a larger β weight to enforce a strong regularization on the latent space. As
186
      the reconstruction loss convergence slows, we decrease the β value to allow the model to focus
187
      more on reconstruction accuracy. Additionally, we employ an early stopping mechanism to prevent
188   overfitting.

      3.3   L ATENT S PACE D ENOISING
191
192   Although VAE can learns latent space representations of data, directly sampling from the prior
193   distribution p(z) often exhibit low quality generated samples. In our preliminary experiments, we
194   observed that directly utilizing the latent features learned by the VAE frequently produces text that
195   is unrelated to the target data distribution. This issue arises due to the discrepancy between the
196   encoder’s learned posterior distribution qϕ (z|x) and the prior p(z). To address this problem, we
197
      introduce a diffusion model in the latent space to more accurately model the true distribution of the
      latent features. Inspired by Zhang et al. (2024), we extract the latent vectors z ∈ Z from the trained
198
      VAE for each data point x ∈ Dtrain . Starting from the initial latent vector z0 , we progressively
199
      add noise over time following a linear schedule to get zt . During the reverse diffusion process, we
200   employ a standard continuous denoising network to recover z0 (Song et al., 2021). For the training
201   objective, we optimize the diffusion model through denoising score matching (Karras et al., 2022):

472
473   Data copying is a significant challenge for overfitted generative models in practical applications. To
474   verify that the data generated by DiffLM is not merely copied from the training data, we compute
475   the Distance to Closest Record (DCR) metric. Specifically, for each row in the tabular data, we
476   represent the categorical columns using one-hot vectors and perform min-max normalization on the
477   numerical columns. We then define DCR as the minimum L1-distance between a synthetic data
478   point and each training sample point:
479                                               DCR(xsyn ) =             min          L1 (xsyn , xreal ).                                      (8)
480                                                                     xreal ∈Dtrain

481   The DCR distribution is shown in Figure 5. We observe that the LLM-based GReaT generates
482   results that differ significantly from the training data, indicating that vanilla fine-tuning struggles
483   to adapt LLMs to real data distributions and generate high-quality results. DiffLM demonstrates a
484   DCR distribution similar to that of the SoTA method TabSyn on both datasets. This further indicates
485   that our proposed general-purpose data synthesis framework can achieve performance on par with

519   6     C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we introduce DiffLM, a novel framework designed to enhance LLM’s understanding
522   of real-world data distributions in synthetic data generation tasks. DiffLM leverages a VAE to map
523   real data into a latent space, which is then injected into the decoding process of LLM, enabling
524
      end-to-end training through causal language modeling objective. Additionally, we incorporate a dif-
      fusion process to further refine the learning of the latent distribution, mitigating the sampling failures
525
      caused by latent space discrepancies. To flexibly and non-intrusively control the structure and qual-
526
      ity of the generated data, DiffLM integrates real data information with LLMs’ internal knowledge
527   by freezing the LLM parameters and using the latent features as plug-in modules. Experimental
528   results demonstrate that DiffLM produces highly robust and consistent outputs. In all datasets, the
529   performance of downstream models trained on the generated data is comparable to or even surpasses
530   that of models trained on real data.

760   In this section, we will introduce the general process of latent diffusion models. Latent Diffusion
761   Models (LDMs) are a class of diffusion probabilistic models that operate in the latent space of
762   an autoencoder rather than directly on the high-dimensional data space. By performing diffusion
763   in a compressed latent representation, LDMs significantly reduce computational complexity while
764   maintaining high fidelity in data generation. An LDM consists of two primary components:
765
            1. Autoencoder: Encodes input data x0 into a latent representation z0 = E(x0 ) and decodes
766
               latent variables back to data space x̂ = D(z).
767

      Noise Scheduling. The noise schedule {βt }Tt=1 plays a critical role in the diffusion process. It
811   dictates how quickly noise is added in the forward process and, consequently, affects the difficulty
812   of the reverse denoising task. Common strategies for setting βt include linear, cosine, and quadratic
813   schedules. We use use linear noise schedule, i.e., the perturbation kernel σ(t) = t. As it is an
814   effective schedule, ensuring that the data is sufficiently diffused by timestep t, while still allowing
815   the model to learn meaningful reverse transitions.

      Contribution. Our contributions and insights are as follows:


                                                                     18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       • Motivation and Challenge: We pointed out the principles and challenges for high-quality struc-
973      tured text data synthesis, i.e., producing scalable and diverse synthetic data with reasonable rele-
974      vant knowledge while maintaining the same data requirements (structure, topic, domain, etc.) as
975      the target data. Existing prompt-based and fine-tuning methods cannot achieve both objectives at
976      a low cost.
977
978
       • Framework: To address these goals, we propose the DiffLM framework, which decouples the
         task of learning the requirements of the data to be synthesized from the language modeling task.
979
         We model these requirements in the latent space and inject them into an unaltered LLM, enabling
980
         it to generate desired and realistic data. The synthetic data combines LLM’s broad knowledge
981      with specific data patterns learned from the training data, leading to enhanced performance on
982      downstream tasks.
983
984    • Techniques: For implementation, we propose a training recipe that keeps the LLM decoder fixed
985
         and only trains the VAE encoder and projector. We also validate different latent knowledge injec-
         tion methods.
986
987
988    Controllability. We define the controllability of text data synthesis as the ability to generate text
989    that satisfies desired requirements (e.g., structure, topics, domains) (Keskar et al., 2019; Li et al.,
990    2022). Existing methods for structured textual data synthesis often struggle with controllability.
991
       On one hand, LLM prompt-based methods relying on prompt engineering or few-shot inference
       cannot guarantee the diversity and scalability of synthetic data, even with complex human-crafted
992
       processes (Long et al., 2024). On the other hand, controlling a LM by fine-tuning it with supervised
993
       data (SFT, RLHF) is not only expensive but might also degrade the LLM’s general capability (Keskar
994    et al., 2019; Borisov et al., 2023). Our method addresses these challenges through sampling in the
995    latent space while maintaining data structure due to LLM’s instruction-following ability.
996

1006
1007   GReaT. We attempted to validate the GReaT method on Mistral but found it could not directly and
1008   effectively generate data with the desired structure. GReaT organizes tabular data in a “key is value”
1009   format and uses a smaller PLM (i.e., GPT-2) for continued pretraining. However, when applied to
1010   larger models like Mistral, GReaT struggled to effectively generate the desired structured data. The
1011
       sample generated by GReaT with Mistral is shown in Figure 9. We hypothesize that controlling
       an LM by fine-tuning it with supervised data cause catastrophic forgetting for LLMs, as suggested
1012
       by Luo et al. (2023b). Specifically, the “key is value” data constructed by the GReaT method,
1013
       when used to continue pre-training Mistral, causes internal knowledge collapse - both undermining
1014   the model’s existing knowledge and failing to do effective data synthesis. Additionally, training
1015   the adult dataset on GReaT for 200 epochs (default settings) requires approximately 50 hours on 8
1016   A100 80G GPUs, which is resource-intensive. In contrast, DiffLM under the same training settings
1017   requires only about 7 hours.
1018
1019
       TabSyn. We want to emphasize that our goal is a unified structured data synthesis framework
1020   that supports various domains like tabular data, codes, and tools, and tabular data generation in our
1021   work is just a subdomain of synthetic data generation. As a comparison, TabSyn is not applica-
1022   ble to more complex data synthesis tasks, such as code generation and tool usage, which involve
1023   generating longer content, more complex data types, and highly structured data, while our DiffLM
1024   can handle complicated scenarios. The results of tabular data synthesis are to demonstrate that our
1025   method possesses generality and can achieve on-par results with domain-specific models without
       being specifically tailored to a particular domain.
