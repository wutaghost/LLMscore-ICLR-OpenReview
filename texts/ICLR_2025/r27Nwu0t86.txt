009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Batch offline data have been shown considerably beneficial for reinforcement
012            learning. Their benefit is further amplified by upsampling with generative models.
013            In this paper, we consider a novel opportunity where interaction with environment
014            is feasible, but only restricted to observations, i.e., no reward feedback is available.
015            This setting is broadly applicable, as simulators or even real cyber-physical systems
016            are often accessible, while in contrast reward is often difficult or expensive to
017
               obtain. As a result, the learner must make good sense of the offline data to
               synthesize an efficient scheme of querying the transition of state. Our method first
018
               leverages online interactions to generate high-return trajectories via conditional
019
               diffusion models. They are then blended with the original offline trajectories
020            through a stitching algorithm, and the resulting augmented data can be applied
021            generically to downstream reinforcement learners. Superior empirical performance
022            is demonstrated over state-of-the-art data augmentation methods that are extended
023            to utilize state-only interactions.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Data augmentation has long been an effective approach that boosts the performance of learning
      algorithms. They have been particularly useful for neural networks whose regularization and general-
029
      ization properties are much harder to characterize than shallow models. In reinforcement learning
030
      (RL), experience replay (Fedus et al., 2020; Mnih et al., 2015) can be considered as augmenting the
031   latest experiences with the past ones when updating the model. An even more effective paradigm is
032   offline RL (Levine et al., 2020), where a batch of previously collected trajectories are used to boost
033   online RL. They can be used to augment the online update buffer, or to pre-train a model that is
034   subsequently fine-tuned by online RL.
035
      Offline RL may suffer from sub-optimal batch data because the behavior policy could be sub-optimal,
036
      and the data may not have sufficiently covered the environment, especially the high-rewarding regions.
037   To address this issue, stitching approaches have been studied (Li et al., 2024). Model-based trajectory
038   stitching (MBTS, Hepburn & Montana, 2024) augments the offline dataset by connecting low-reward
039   trajectories with high-reward ones. This is shown in Figure 1b, where two dashed lines are added,
040   connecting to a point that is closer to a bridge (higher value). However, both Li et al. (2024) and
041   Hepburn & Montana (2024) only stitch existing trajectories while no novel high-reward ones are
042   generated.
043   This issue appears solvable by generative models. As shown in Figure 1c, SynthER augments the
044   set of transitions by training a diffusion model (Lu et al., 2023). However, it does not generate
045   trajectories which would require auto-regressive models. In our experiment, directly doing so
046   performs worse than SynthER which upsamples transitions, suggesting that as a data augmentation
047   approach to RL, auto-regressively generating trajectories may accumulate high bias and produce low
048   return.
049   To tackle this problem, we make a new observation that some limited form of interactions are helpful.
050   Indeed, online state-only interaction with environment is feasible in many applications. For example:
051
052   • RL has been adopted to improve treatment policy of chronic disease by factoring in the delayed
053     effects of treatment (Weltz et al., 2022). Many digital twins are available that simulate a patient’s
        condition (Tardini et al., 2022). However, toxicity feedback (part of the reward) may exhibit marked

      The question we investigate and answer in the affirmative is: can we improve trajectory generation,
      as a data augmentation approach for RL, by making efficient use of state-only interactions?1
084
      A natural approach is to learn a good policy from offline RL, and deploy it by interacting with the
085
      environment to obtain a sequence of state and action. Finally, impute the reward from state and action
086   via a regression model trained from offline data. Within this framework, our major contribution lies
087   in two folds. Firstly, we make the key observation that although synthesizing high-return trajectories
088   ameliorates the possible deficiency of such trajectories in the offline data, it may also lead to overfitting
089   of such regions. Therefore, we blend them into the original dataset progressively with a selective
090   stitching strategy, introducing new transitions between two states on different trajectories.
091
      Secondly, we identify a policy model that best fits our setting. Since the interaction is not at test time,
092   the policy’s efficiency is not a main concern, allowing us to leverage Transformer or diffusion based
093   planners. In particular, we extended the decision diffuser (DD, Ajay et al., 2023), which trains a
094   diffusion model to generate trajectories conditioned on high return. For example, in Figure 1d, new
095   trajectories are generated that cross the bridge to reach the goal state. Moreover, DD diffuses state
096   sequences, which is exactly what is collected from new interactions. Such an alignment facilitates
097   additional online fine-tuning of the diffuser; we leave it for future work.
098   We will refer to our method as Diffusion-based Trajectory Stitching (DITS). As shown in Figure 1e,
099   DITS enjoys the benefit of conditional diffuser in generating new high-rewarding trajectories, and
100   it also fixes the limitation of MBTS by allowing low-reward trajectories to connect with the high-
101   reward ones generated by the diffuser. From the trajectory generation perspective, DITS circumvents
102   auto-regression, delegating the backbone progression of state to direct interaction, reducing the task
103   of trajectory generation to step-wise synthesis of actions and rewards. We will also show that both of
104   them can be accomplished by DITS via a single diffusion model.
105   The workflow of DITS is also charted in Figure 2. The offline dataset is first used to train DITS’
106   generator, producing trajectories that are blended with the original dataset through DITS’ stitcher.

129   Related Work Diffusion based models have been recently shown effective for RL. Diffusion policy
130   models (Chi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Ze et al., 2024) refine noise into actions through a
131   gradient field. It is conditioned on the state or observation, and can be stably trained. The learned
132   policy can accurately model multimodal and high-dimensional action distributions. In contrast,
133   DD diffuses on state spaces instead of actions, and generates the action via an inverse dynamics
134   model. It also allow more general conditioning such as high reward, constraint satisfaction, and skill
135
      composition. Wang et al. (2023a) proposed augmenting behavioral cloning by diffusion over state
      and action pairs, but it does not consider conditioning.

      We follow the standard setting of Markov decision process (MDP, Puterman, 1995), which is defined
      by the tuple ⟨ρ0 , S, A, T , R, γ⟩. Here ρ0 is the initial distribution. S is the m-dimensional state
141
      space, and A is the action space. T : S × A → ∆S is the transition function. R : S × A × S → R is
142
      the reward function. γ ∈ [0, 1) is a discount factor. The agent in this environment acts with a policy
143   π : S → A, generating a sequence of state-action-reward transitions, which represents     P a trajectory
144   τ := (st , at , rt )t≥0 with probability pπ (τ ). The return of the trajectory is R(τ ) := t≥0 γ t rt .
145
146   An offline dataset D consists of trajectories (τ1 , . . . , τT ) which are collected using a behavior policy
147   πβ . Each trajectory is composed of a set of transitions {(st , at , rt , s′t )}, where we customarily use ′
148
      to denote the next state, i.e., s′t = st+1 . SynthER (Lu et al., 2023) directly builds a generative model
      based on the union of such transition sets across all the T trajectories to replay the experience.
149
150   Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) are a class of gen-
151   erative models that learn the data distribution q(x) from a dataset D := {xi }0≤i<M . One of the
152   major usage of these models is generating high-quality images from text descriptions (Saharia et al.,
153   2022). The diffusion models consist of two processes, which are the forward process and the re-
154
      verse process. For the forward  √ process, the model will add noise step by step via a predefined
      way q(xk+1 |xk ) := N (xk+1 ; αk x, (1 − αk )I). For the reverse process, the model has trainable
155
      parameters in order to learn a good way to denoise step by step, which can be represented mathemati-
156
      cally as pθ (xk−1 |xk ) := N (xk−1 |µθ (xk , k), Σk ). In these processes, N (µ, Σ) denotes a Gaussian
157   distribution with mean µ and covariance matrix Σ, and αk ∈ R determines the variance schedule.
158   x0 := x denotes the original sample, while x1 , x2 , ..., xK−1 are the intermediate steps for the
159   diffusion, and xK ∼ N (0, I) is the unit Gaussian noise.
160
      The reverse process model can be trained by minimizing the following loss (Ho et al., 2020):
161
                         Ldenoise (θ) := Ek∼U [1,K],x0 ∼q,ϵ∼N (0,I) [∥ϵ − ϵθ (xk , k)∥2 ].

162
      The noise model ϵθ (xk , k) is parameterized by a deep temporal U-Net (Janner et al., 2022). This is
163   equivalent to modeling the mean of pθ (xk−1 |xk ), as µθ (xk−1 |xk ) can be calculated from ϵθ (xk , k)
164   (Ho et al., 2020).
165
166   Guided Diffusion The diffusion training process can be guided using conditional data. In order
167   to avoid the need of a strong classifier, classifier-free guidance method (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is
168   preferred. This method learns both a conditional model ϵθ (xk , y, k) and an unconditional model
169   ϵθ (xk , ∅, k), where y is the label and ∅ is a dummy value that takes the place of y for unconditional
170   situations. The perturbed noise can be denoted as ϵθ (xk , ∅, k)+ω(ϵθ (xk , y, k)−ϵθ (xk , ∅, k)), where
171   ω denotes the guidance scale or the trade-off factor.

173   3     T RAJECTORY G ENERATOR OF DITS WITH S TATE - ONLY I NTERACTION
174
175   We first present our trajectory generator, which produces (st , at , rt , s′t ) sequences through state-only
176
      interactions. A vanilla Decision Diffuser (DD, Ajay et al., 2023) works as a planner that proposes
      actions conditioned on high reward or other constraints, based on which the state-only interaction
177
      produces the next state s′t . However, DD does not generate rewards, while many applications lack a
178
      reward formula, e.g., patient’s mental stress level. We thus improve DD by also generating rewards.
179
180   Analogously to Ajay et al. (2023), we only diffuse on the states and rewards, because the sequence
181   over actions, which are often represented as joint torques, tend to have higher frequency and are
182
      less smooth, making them much harder to model (Tedrake, 2022). We therefore decide to model the
      sequence of (st , rt , s′t ) by a diffusion model, and train an inverse dynamics model (IDM) to infer at
183
      from st and s′t (Section 3.4).
184

206              xk (τ ) := (st−C+1 , rt−C+1 , . . . , st , rt , . . . , st+H−C , rt+H−C )k ∈ RH(m+1) .       (3)

297       4    S TITCHER OF DITS
298
299       As illustrated in Figure 1, merely generating high-return trajectories is sub-optimal as it may overfit
300       those regions. We overcome this limitation by blending such trajectories into the original dataset
301       progressively with a selective stitching strategy, thereby diversifying the high-return trajectories by,
302       e.g., starting from a low-reward region.
303       Stitching algorithms have been studied in the literature. The Model-based Trajectory Stitching
304       method (MBTS, Hepburn & Montana, 2024) augments the dataset D by stitching together high-value
305       regions of different trajectories, i.e., introducing new transitions between two states on two different
306       trajectories. The candidate states for stitching are determined by i) the state-value function V (s) and
307       ii) the forward dynamics probability p(s′ |s). However, MBTS does not generate new trajectories
308       conditioned with high return, leaving the stitched data still sub-optimal.
309       To address this issue, we propose a stitching algorithm for DITS, with an abridged version shown in
310       Algorithm 2, and a detailed version deferred to Algorithm 4 in Appendix A.1. It can be compared
311       with MBTS which is recapped as Algorithm 5 in Appendix A.2. The key differences are highlighted

      4.1     F ORWARD DYNAMICS C RITERION
359   To determine whether a state can be a candidate next state for stitching, we need a criterion based
360   on forward dynamics model p(ŝ′ |s), depicting the transition probability from state s to the possible
361   candidate state ŝ′ , which may differ from the observed next state s′ . We model the environment
362   dynamics as a Gaussian distribution, which is common for continuous state-space applications (Janner
363   et al., 2019). We create an ensemble E of N dynamics models parameterized by ξ i : {p̂ξi (st+1 |st ) =
364   N (µξi (st ), Σξi (st ))}N
                               i=1 . Each model is trained via maximum likelihood estimation from the dataset
365   D, for which the loss can be formulated as:
366

367
368   where |·| is the determinant of a matrix. We train the ensemble using different parameter initializations
369   for each model in order to take the epistemic uncertainty into account. The criterion for determining
370   the candidate next state can be formulated in a conservative manner:

464
      Our first ablation study investigates how the two key ingredients of our method, the trajectory
465
      generator and the stitcher, contribute to its performance. Using BC as the base RL algorithm, We
466   compared DITS with two variants: a) only adding DITS generated trajectories to the datasets based
467   on state-only interactions, and b) only performing stitching on the original dataset without generating
468   new trajectories.
469
      The resulting normalized average score is presented in Figure 4, using the Hopper dataset and the
470
      same three difficulty levels as in Table 1. The results for Halfcheetah and Walker2d are deferred
471
      to Figure 6 in Appendix D. Overall, fully applying DITS is more effective than only utilizing the
472   stitcher, which indicates that the conditional diffuser and interaction are indeed helpful by providing
473   high-return trajectories. Furthermore, full DITS outperforms the method with stitcher removed,
474   because the stitching step can avoid overfitting to the high-return regions by blending in transitions
475   from the low value regions.
476
477   Ablation on Conditional Guidance Scale We also analyzed the sensitivity of the conditional
478
      guidance scale ω of the conditional diffusion model. On the Hopper Medium Expert dataset, we
      evaluated the generation quality by computing the correlation between expert data. The correlation
479
      turns out 0.996, 0.996, 0.993, 0.989 for ω = 1.2, 1.4, 1.6, 1.8, respectively.

482   trajectories generated by DITS via interaction. Figure 5 shows that, using Hopper medium expert
483   as the environment and BC as the base offline RL algorithm, the performance of our method can be
484   improved with more sampled trajectories. Further, the variance also decays as a broader range of
485   situations are fed to BC. When only a small number of high-return trajectories are available, some
      test episodes turned out to suffer a considerably low score.

525         6                           C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , B ROADER I MPACT, AND F UTURE WORK
526
527      We proposed a novel data augmentation method DITS for offline RL, where state-only interactions
528      are available with the environment. The generator based on conditional diffusion models allows
529      high-return trajectories to be sampled, and the stitching algorithm blends them with the original ones.
530      The resulting augmented dataset is shown to significantly boost the performance of base RL methods.
531       As a limitation, we did not update DITS’ trajectory generator after new state-only sequences are
532       obtained from the interaction. Future work will empower diffusion models to learn from partially
533       observed data (no reward available). As for broader impact, DITS will benefit a range of applications
534       where state-only interactions are available, e.g., healthcare, recommendation, and inventory control.

      B     H YPERPARAMETER AND A RCHITECTURAL D ETAILS
757
758
      In this section, we describe the hyperparameter choice for our model and the architectural details for
759   our model:
760
761          • We use a temporal U-Net (Janner et al., 2022) for the noise ϵθ modeling. It consists a
762            U-Net structure with 6 repeated residual blocks, while each block consisting two temporal
763            convolutions, each followed by group norm (Wu & He, 2018), and a final Mish nonlinearity
764            (Misra, 2019). Both of the timestep and condition embeddings are 128-dimensional vectors
765            produced by two separate 2-layered MLP with 256 hidden units and Mish nonlinearity. The
766            embeddings are concatenated together before getting added to the activations of the first
767
               temporal convolution within each block. Our code for the DDR model is a modification
               of the code for the original Decision Diffuser (Ajay et al., 2023), for which the link is
768
               https://github.com/anuragajay/decision-diffuser.
769
770          • We use a 2-layered MLP with 512 hidden units and ReLU activations for the modeling of
771
               the inverse dynamics model fϕ .
772          • We train ϵθ and fϕ using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with a learning rate
773            2 · 10−4 and batch size of 32 for 1e6 training steps.
774          • We choose the probability p of removing the conditioning information to be 0.25.
775          • We use K = 200 diffusion steps.
776
             • We choose context length C ∈ {1, 20}, C = 20 is preferred in the Kitchen datasets. Both C
777
               values are able to generate decent results in the locomotion datasets, but C = 20 tends to
778
               have more stability.
779
780
             • We use a planning horizon H with H − C = 100 in all the D4RL locomotion tasks, while
               using H − C = 56 in D4RL kitchen tasks.
781
782          • We use a guidance scale ω ∈ {1.2, 1.4, 1.6, 1.8} but the exact choice varies by task.
783          • We choose α = 0.5 for low temperature sampling.
784          • We use a 3-layered MLP with 300 hidden units with ReLU activation to model the foward
785            dynamics. The network takes a state s for input and output a mean µ and a standard deviation
786            σ for a Gaussian distribution N (µ, σ 2 ). For all experiments, an ensemble size of 7 is used
787            with the best 3 being chosen. We train the forward dynamics models with Adam optimizer
788            (Kingma & Ba, 2015), a learning rate of 3 · 10−4 and a batch size of 256. We initialize the
789            parameter with different random seeds for each forward dynamics model in the ensemble.
790          • We choose to use a 2-layered MLP with 256 hidden units and a ReLU activation to parame-
791            terize the value function. We train two value functions with different parameter initialization
792            and take the minimum of the two during the stitching process. We train the model with
793            Adam optimizer and a learning rate of 3 · 10−4 and a batch size of 256.
794          • We choose the neighbourhood radius ρ ∈ {0.1, 1.0, 3.0} while the exact choice varies by
795            task.
796          • We use the additional trajectory number n = 300 for each epoch during stitching.
797
             • We use the sum of rewards threshold λ = maxt ( τt ri ) − κ, while κ is in the range of
                                                                    P
798
               [500, 1000] depending on the task.
799
800          • We choose the acceptance threshold p̃ = 0.1 to ensure the stitched trajectory only to be used
801            when a significant improvement is guaranteed.
802          • We use a behavioural cloning model parameterized by a 2-layered MLP with 256 hidden
803            units and ReLU activation.
804          • We choose to use the epoch number K = 1, 2, 3, 4. For most cases, using K = 2 will have
805            the sufficient performance gain, and the results will saturate in the following epochs.
