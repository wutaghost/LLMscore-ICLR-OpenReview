009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Predicting video popularity is typically formalized as a supervised learning prob-
012            lem, where models classify videos as popular or unpopular. Traditional approaches
013            rely heavily on meta-information and aggregated user engagement data, but video
014            popularity is highly context-dependent, influenced by cultural and social factors
015            that such approaches fail to capture. We argue that Large Language Models
016
               (LLMs), with their deep contextual awareness, are well-suited to address these
               challenges. However, bridging the modality gap between pixel-based video data
017
               and token-based LLMs is a key challenge. To address this, we transform frame-
018
               level visual data into sequential text representations using Vision-Language Models
019            (VLMs), enabling LLMs to process multimodal video content—titles, frame-based
020            descriptions, and captions—capturing both engagement intensity (view count) and
021            geographic spread (the number of countries where a video trends). Evaluating
022            on 13,639 videos, we show that while a supervised neural network using content
023            embeddings achieved 80% accuracy, our LLM-based method reached 82% without
024            fine-tuning. A combined approach, integrating the neural network’s predictions
025            into the LLM, further improved accuracy to 85.5%. Additionally, the LLM gen-
026            erates interpretable hypotheses explaining its predictions based on theoretically
027            sound attributes. Manual validations confirm the quality of these hypotheses and
028
               address concerns about hallucinations in the video-to-text conversion process. Our
               findings highlight that LLMs, equipped with textually transformed multimodal
029
               representations, offer a powerful, interpretable, and data-efficient solution to tasks,
030
               requiring rich contextual and cultural insights, such as video popularity prediction.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Video consumption now accounts for the majority of internet traffic and continues to grow rapidly,
036   making video popularity prediction a critical challenge for content creators, social media platforms,
037   and advertisers (Cisco, 2021). Accurately predicting which videos will become popular is not
038   only important for these stakeholders but also provides valuable insights for researchers studying
039
      information diffusion (Park et al., 2017; Rezvanian et al., 2023), social influence (Park et al., 2016;
      Lin et al., 2023b), cultural dynamics (Park et al., 2017; Haldar et al., 2023), and potential misuse
040
      in online networks (Beni et al., 2023). Despite its significance, predicting video popularity remains
041
      a complex and challenging task due to the wide range of influencing factors, such as historical
042   context (Ng & Taneja, 2023), cultural trends (Park et al., 2017), and even emotional engagement with
043   viewers (Guadagno et al., 2013; Park et al., 2016). The vast diversity and volume of video content
044   online further complicate these challenges.
045
      Despite the growing interest in this area, most existing research has employed statistical and super-
046
      vised learning approaches that primarily rely on meta-information and aggregated user metrics, such
047
      as uploader statistics, view/comment/like counts, and external factors like social network size and
048   early engagement in other platforms (Zhou et al., 2010; Shamma et al., 2011; Borghol et al., 2012;
049   Park et al., 2016). While these factors provide useful signals, they mainly reflect the uploader’s repu-
050   tation or early user reactions and often fail to capture the deeper contextual and cultural significance
051   encoded within the video content. These intrinsic qualities of video content may play a critical role in
052   how a video resonates with both local and global audiences. However, traditional approaches struggle
053   to process and leverage such rich information due to limited technological capacity in processing
      complex multimodal data.


                                                         1
054
      In this paper, we propose a novel approach to video popularity prediction that shifts the focus to
055   the intrinsic qualities of a video’s textual, verbal, and visual content, excluding after-the-fact user
056   engagement data such as early view counts and social network signals. Our method leverages
057   the power of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and Large Language Models (LLMs) to extract
058   and interpret these intrinsic qualities, complemented by conventional descriptors such as titles and
059   descriptions. To address the challenge of integrating pixel-based video data with the token-based
060   architecture of LLMs, we use VLMs to transform frame-level visual data into sequential textual
061   representations. These representations are then combined with conventional video descriptors such
062   as titles, descriptions, and captions (extracted from the video’s audio) to create a comprehensive
063
      multimodal textual representation. This transformation allows LLMs to perform the video popularity
      prediction task, effectively capturing both vertical aspects of popularity, such as view counts, and
064
      horizontal aspects, such as the global reach of the video across different countries, by utilizing the
065
      deep contextual understanding embedded in LLMs.
066
067   Empirically, we introduce a prompting strategy that incorporates a novel approach of integrating
068   supervised learning signals into LLM-based predictions. Our method not only outperforms traditional
069   deep learning models based on content embeddings but also provides human-interpretable predictions
070
      in the form of attribute-based hypotheses. Additionally, we introduce the Global Popular Video
      Dataset (GPVD), a large-scale dataset of 1.3M unique popular YouTube videos, enriched with titles,
071
      descriptions, and detailed metadata. The dataset uniquely includes three key popularity metrics: view
072
      counts, the number of countries where the video trended, and the number of days it remained on
073   trending lists. The latter two metrics, which reflect the video’s global reach and sustained popularity,
074   have been overlooked in prior research but are crucial for a comprehensive understanding of video
075   virality. For our experiments, we subsampled this dataset to create a balanced subset that captures
076   different popularity classes, reflecting both engagement intensity and geographic spread, as detailed
077   in the Methods section. Furthermore, we address concerns related to hallucinations in video-to-text
078   conversion and validate the quality of attribute-based hypotheses through survey experiments.
079
      The key contributions of this paper are as follows:
080
081         1. We formulate a video popularity prediction task that not only accounts for engagement
082            intensity (e.g., view counts), commonly used in prior work, but also incorporates geographic
083            spread as a critical dimension of popularity.
084         2. We introduce the GPVD, comprising 1.3M representative popular YouTube videos from 109
085            countries, supplemented with various metadata to support future studies in the field.
086
087
            3. We propose a framework combining VLMs and LLMs to predict video popularity by trans-
088
               forming multimodal video content into textual representations. This pipeline innovatively
089
               integrates supervised learning signals into LLM-based predictions, enhancing performance.
090         4. We systematically explore and evaluate the impact of different prompting techniques, such
091            as hypothesis generation, KNN-based example retrieval, and supervised signals while also
092            providing human-interpretable predictions validated through systematic human evaluations.

096   Video Popularity Prediction Video popularity prediction has traditionally been treated as a
097   supervised learning task, focusing primarily on predicting view counts based on factors like title
098   length, runtime, and user engagement (Zhou et al., 2010; West, 2011; Borghol et al., 2012; Wang
099   et al., 2012). Later work introduced more sophisticated methods, such as time-series analysis and
100   user behavior modeling, to track how popularity evolves (Broxton et al., 2013; Pinto et al., 2013;
101   Vallet et al., 2015; Park et al., 2016; Jog et al., 2021). Despite these advancements, video popularity
102   prediction remains challenging, as it demands capturing cultural and social dynamics that traditional
103   approaches, which often focus narrowly on aggregated platform metrics, fail to account for.
104   Moreover, these studies typically treat video popularity as a unidimensional problem, focusing exclu-
105   sively on view counts. However, high view counts alone do not fully capture a video’s popularity—
106   especially in the era of streaming, where a video with many views may not necessarily be a global
107   hit; it could be localized to specific regions. To address this limitation, we redefine the prediction task
      by incorporating both engagement intensity (view counts) and geographic spread (global-local reach),


                                                          2
108
      making the task more nuanced and realistic. We argue that LLMs, with their extensive contextual
109   awareness, are uniquely positioned to capture these complexities, encoding cultural and contextual
110   subtleties that previous studies have been unable to leverage.
111
112
      Multimodal Learning and Modality Gaps Integrating multimodal data for tasks like video
113
      understanding has long been challenging, particularly in addressing the modality gap between
114   pixel-based visual data and token-based language models. Existing methods often rely on modular
115   architectures that process visual and textual data independently using pre-trained visual encoders
116   (e.g., ViT) and language models (e.g., BERT), before concatenating their embeddings (Zeng et al.,
117   2022; Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a;b; Lu et al., 2022). While effective to a degree, these
118   approaches fall short of fully capturing complex interactions between visual and textual modalities,
119   particularly the rich temporal contextual information inherent in videos (Chen et al., 2023b; Qin et al.,
120   2023).
121   Recent advancements have leveraged VLMs to transform video frames into textual representations,
122   enabling LLMs to reason over multimodal content (Bhattacharyya et al., 2023; Khandelwal et al.,
123   2024; Chen et al., 2023a). These approaches utilize pre-trained models and modular pipelines to
124   generate textual summaries of videos, which are then employed for tasks such as classification and
125   user behavior modeling. Collectively, they demonstrate the effectiveness of integrating VLMs with
126   LLMs for video understanding tasks, emphasizing the role of textual intermediaries to bridge the
127   modality gap.
128   Building on these innovations, our approach introduces a frame-to-text transformation pipeline that
129   converts video frames into sequential textual descriptions via VLMs. This enables LLMs to process
130   visual data as richly contextualized text, facilitating unified reasoning across modalities. Compared to
131   modular architectures that merely concatenate embeddings, our method achieves deeper integration
132   of multimodal data, capturing nuanced information from visual and textual content. Moreover, our
133   work extends this pipeline to an end prediction task using systematic prompt engineering, enabling
134
      comparisons across prompting techniques and offering insights into performance improvements.
135
136   Natural Language Explanation and Prompt Engineering Generating explanations alongside
137   predictions has been shown to enhance both model understanding and performance in complex tasks.
138
      Techniques like Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) and self-consistency sampling (Wang
      et al., 2022) have demonstrated how reasoning chains can improve model accuracy while maintaining
139
      interpretability. Two-stage approaches, such as hypothesis generation followed by task solving (Wang
140
      et al., 2023), suggest that explanations contribute to better performance, but these approaches often
141   add complexity to the prediction pipeline.
142
143   Building on the work of Hanu et al. (2023), who demonstrated the use of textual descriptions for
144
      multimodal classification, we extend this approach by integrating hypothesis generation directly
      into the prediction process, reducing the two-stage approach into a more efficient one-step process.
145
      Additionally, we incorporate the supervised learning prediction outcomes into the prompt as additional
146
      signals to further improve performance. By combining prompt engineering with contextualized inputs
147   from our frame-to-text transformation, we enhance both the prediction accuracy and the generation
148   of interpretable hypotheses, creating a unified and efficient approach to video popularity prediction.

151   3    M ETHODS
152
153   Our approach transforms video content into a sequence of textual descriptions to enable video
154   popularity prediction through LLMs (see Appendix A.1 for an overview of the pipeline and further
155   details). The process involves several key steps. Let V = {f1 , f2 , . . . , fM } represent the set of key
156   frames for a video, where M is the total number of frames. Define C = {C1 , C2 , . . . , CC } as the set
157   of captions, T as the title, and D as the user-provided description.
158   We begin with frame-to-text transformations using the VideoLLava model (Lin et al., 2023a),
159   which captures the essence of the video content. The output is represented as SVideoLLava (V) =
160   {S1 , S2 , . . . , SS }, where Si = VideoLLava(fiW ) and fiW represents a frame window around fi . To
161   enhance these textual representations, time-matched captions are integrated, adding contextual detail
      to ensure each segment of frames is described accurately in both visual and verbal terms.


                                                          3
162
      Finally, our LLM-based method leverages the reasoning capabilities of LLMs to predict video
163   popularity. As part of the pipeline, we also incorporate a prediction outcome from supervised
164   multimodal models, which serves as both a benchmark and a supervised signal for our LLM-based
165   predictions. The following subsections provide a detailed explanation of each component of our
166   approach.

168   3.1    TASK D EFINITION AND DATASET
169
170   We redefine the video popularity prediction task by incorporating two key dimensions:
171
              • Engagement Intensity: Total view count, reflecting a video’s overall reach and audience
172
                engagement.
173
174
              • Geographic Spread: The number of countries where the video trends, indicating global
                reach and resonance.
175
176   By incorporating these dimensions, we offer a more nuanced understanding of video popularity.
177   Formally, given a video v and its content features (e.g., frames, audio, captions), the task is to predict
178   a popularity score p(v) ∈ [0, 1], representing the likelihood of the video being classified as either a
179   ‘local hit’ or a ‘global big hit.’
180
      To support this task, we introduce the Global Popular Video Dataset (GPVD), comprising the top
181
      50 trending videos across 109 countries over 589 days (February 13, 2021, to March 17, 2023).
182
      This dataset includes approximately 5,450 observations per day, totaling 3,210,050 observations
183
      and 1,302,698 unique videos. Each observation contains the video ID, trending countries, category,
184
      title, tags, and popularity metrics (e.g., views, likes, dislikes).1 Given that the majority of videos
185
      neither go viral nor achieve significant consumption levels, gathering a representative sample of
186
187
      globally popular videos is inherently challenging. Unlike prior studies with shorter collection periods
188
      (1-5 months) (Park et al., 2017; Ng & Taneja, 2023), our dataset spans over two years with broader
189
      geographic coverage. This enables both short-term trend analysis and long-term cross-cultural com-
190
      parisons. Videos are categorized based on Engagement Intensity and Geographic Spread into 16
191   groups using 4 × 4 quantiles, with a focus on the two most distinctive classes:
192
              • Global Big Hit: Videos in the top 25% for both dimensions.
193
              • Local Hit: Videos in the bottom 25% for both dimensions.
194
195   This classification enables us to analyze the factors distinguishing globally highly popular videos
196   from those with regional appeal. For our experiments, we randomly selected a balanced sample from
197   the dataset, comprising 6,279 videos classified as ‘Local Hit’ and 7,360 as ‘Global Big Hit.’
198
199   3.2    T RANSFORMING V IDEO C ONTENT INTO T EXT
200
201   Our approach for transforming video content into text for LLM-based video popularity prediction
202   involves the following steps:
203
204
             1. Frame Extraction: From the video, we extract a set of evenly spaced frames, V =
                {f1 , f2 , ..., fM }, selecting 10 frames per minute to ensure adequate content coverage.
205
206          2. Frames to Text Conversion: The VideoLLava model (Lin et al., 2023a) generates descriptive
207
                text for each frame. The model, pretrained to capture visual content effectively, outputs
                contextually relevant descriptions for each frame: SVideoLLava (V) = {S1 , S2 , ..., SS }, where
208
                each Si is a textual description generated from a window of frames around fi .
209
210          3. Caption Matching and Data Integration: The sequential frame-based textual descriptions
211
                are synchronized with the video’s existing captions, aligning visual and verbal elements to
                improve contextual accuracy. These descriptions, combined with timestamps, are integrated
212
                into a cohesive textual narrative that incorporates both visual and temporal details.

237   3.3     V IDEO P OPULARITY P REDICTION THROUGH P ROMPTING
238
239   Our method leverages LLMs for video popularity prediction by employing a structured sequence
240   of prompts. Conceptually, these prompts are grouped into three categories: context, reasoning,
241
      and transfer, each enhancing specific aspects of prediction. Starting with a vanilla LLM setup, we
      progressively incorporate reasoning steps, few-shot learning, near examples, hypothesis generation,
242
      and supervised signals to improve performance.
243
244
      3.3.1     OVERVIEW OF P ROMPT C OMPONENTS
245
246           1. Context Set: Establishes the task by including instructions, task definitions, and expected
247              output format, ensuring the LLM understands the prediction objective.
248           2. Reasoning Set: Encourages intermediate reasoning through prompts like “think before
249              evaluating” and “hypothesis generation,” enhancing the model’s ability to process complex
250              information and provide explanations for its prediction.
251
              3. Transfer Set: Refines predictions using knowledge from external examples via techniques
252              such as few-shot learning, near-example selection, and supervised signals.
253
254
      3.3.2     S EQUENTIAL P ROMPTING A PPROACH
255
256   The prediction task is framed as a four-class classification problem, with classes c ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4}
257   representing increasing levels of popularity, from local hits to global big hits. Classes 1 and 2
258   correspond to local popularity, while classes 3 and 4 indicate global popularity. For final predictions,
259   we consolidate these into two categories: ‘local hits’ (classes 1 and 2) and ‘global big hits’ (classes 3
260   and 4).2 The input consists of integrated summaries, titles, and descriptions, denoted as F(I, T, D).
261
      Prompts were added sequentially to refine performance, as described below:
262      2
           During experiments, we observed that the LLM set a very high threshold for the Global ‘Big’ Hit class,
263   resulting in a noticeable bias towards the Local Hit class. Introducing buffer classes (1 and 2 for local hits and 3
264   and 4 for global hits) addressed this issue by (1) allowing the model to express uncertainty through intermediate
265   predictions, avoiding forced binary decisions; (2) creating a more granular classification system that better
266   reflects real-world ambiguities between extreme categories; (3) enabling better-calibrated confidence levels
267   by incorporating buffer zones between classes; and (4) establishing a smoother decision boundary between
268   extremes, reducing the potential for overly rigid classifications. This adjustment significantly enhanced prediction
269   performance, leading us to adopt the four-class structure in our experimentation pipeline. Details of the class
      setup can be found in Section A.4 of the Appendix, which includes the final prompt used in our experiments.

406
407   The performance of the LLM-based models was evaluated on both title-only and full-integrated-
408   description-based prediction tasks. Figure 1 shows the incremental improvements in accuracy.
409   Starting with the vanilla prompt (Pvanilla ), the model achieved 59.0% accuracy for title-based predic-
410   tion and 62.5% for description-based prediction. The richer information provided in the description
411   resulted in a noticeable improvement in accuracy.
412   Next, we incorporated additional prompting techniques: thinking prompts (Pthink ), few-shot examples
413   (Pfew-shot ), and one- or ten-nearest-example retrieval (Pnear ), which gradually improved performance,
414   enhancing title-based prediction accuracy from 60.5% to 71.5% and description-based prediction
415   from 63.0% to 72.0%.
416
      The largest gains came from integrating of 10-nearest examples (Pnear ) and hypothesis generation
417
      (Phypothesis ), boosting accuracy by 6.0 and 10.0 percentage points, reaching 77.5% for title-based
418   prediction and 82.0% for integrated description-based prediction. This underscores the value of
419   hypothesis generation, allowing the model to generate and test multiple hypotheses, in enhancing
420   both accuracy and explainability.
421
      The final model, incorporating all components, including supervised signals (Psupervised ), achieved
422
      79.0% accuracy for title-based prediction and 85.5% for description-based prediction. This marks
423
      improvements of 1.5 and 3.5 percentage points over the previous model and 5.0 and 5.5 percentage
424   points over the traditional supervised baseline, highlighting the value of integrating reasoning and
425   external signals into the prediction process.
426
427
      Overall, our final model outperformed both well-established supervised methods and baselines uti-
428   lizing known prompting strategies, such as thinking and few-shot learning. By combining LLM
429   reasoning capabilities with near examples and supervised learning signals, we achieved significant
430   performance improvements, demonstrating the added value of these innovative prompting approaches
431   and highlighting opportunities for further optimization through targeted enhancements and fine-tun-
      ing.

521   by LLMs could enhance transparency in medical imaging and diagnosis, where explainable AI is
522   critical for trust and adoption. Similarly, the approach could support education by offering explainable
