009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine Learning (ML) models are increasingly used to support or substitute de-
012            cision making. In applications where skilled experts are a limited resource, it is
013            crucial to reduce their burden and automate decisions when the performance of
014            an ML model is at least of equal quality. However, models are often pre-trained
015            and fixed, while tasks arrive sequentially and their distribution may shift. In that
016            case, the respective performance of the decision makers may change, and the de-
017            ferral algorithm must remain adaptive. We propose a contextual bandit model of
018
               this online decision making problem. Our framework includes budget constraints
               and different types of partial feedback models. Beyond the theoretical guarantees
019
               of our algorithm, we propose efficient extensions that achieve remarkable perfor-
020
               mance on real-world datasets.
021

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Consider a decision-making system that must make investment decisions based on the company’s
026   existing resources and on the characteristics of the available assets (the “context”). Although these
027   strategic tasks are usually led by humans, the diversity and complexity of the observable variables
028   have led to the introduction of Machine Learning (ML) models in the loop (Shi et al., 2023). The
029   decision-making system is consequently composite: human experts and an ML model are working
030   side by side, with possibly nonaligned expertise. The ML model might excel in certain scenarios
031   due to its analytical precision, while human experts bring additional insights and contextual under-
032
      standing to others. Therefore, it is crucial to design a deferral meta-model that can efficiently decide
      when the decision must be deferred to human experts and when not.
033
034   Existing learning-to-defer systems, e.g. (Cortes et al., 2016; Madras et al., 2018), often rely on
035   a joint training method where the model and its deferral component are learnt together on large,
036   expert-annotated datasets, with the goal of trading off prediction quality and other criteria such as
037   fairness. When data collection requires highly skilled experts, this traditional approach is very costly.
038
      Furthermore, in steadily evolving contexts, such as financial investments or content moderation, the
      data is subject to constant distribution shifts.
039
040   If directly retraining the ML model is not a viable option, then it is desirable to have an adaptive
041   deferral model that estimates and adapts to the current and unknown quality of the ML model and
042   of the human experts.
043   In this work, we model this scenario as an online two-armed contextual bandit problem that takes
044   into account the budget constraints related to deferring to human experts (Lattimore and Szepesvári,
045   2020; Agrawal and Devanur, 2014; 2016).
046
      We argue that the resulting online decision deferral system adds significant realism to the problem.
047
      We distinguish two types of ‘observability’ of the decision’s performance. In many scenarios, it
048   can be impossible to evaluate alternative decisions post hoc, as an investment or content moderation
049   decision may not allow to observe the counterfactual. This is the case of pure bandit feedback, where
050   only the reward of the chosen action (or ‘arm’) can be observed. Sometimes, however, counterfactual
051   performance can be evaluated: for example, an alternative portfolio performance can be evaluated
052   on market data a posteriori, assuming the investment decision of the agent does not significantly
053   impact these markets. In that case, the observations are richer than the actual performance, and we
      call it full information.

      Contributions. We introduce a novel framework for online decision deferral in a budgeted setting
055   with bandit or full information feedback. Our main contribution is to formalize this realistic prob-
056   lem into a tractable online learning setting and thoroughly evaluate the resulting algorithm through
057   experiments on simulated and real data, yielding remarkable results in various settings. Our choice
058   of setting and algorithm allows us to leverage relevant literature and obtain theoretical guarantees
059   on the learnt deferral model.

      Learning to Defer. Traditionally, deferral was introduced as a rejection mechanism within the
064
      model training procedure (Cortes et al., 2016). There, the goal of the system is to minimize the
065
      overall cost by rejecting whenever the confidence on the prediction is too low. Learning to defer
066   (Madras et al., 2018) brings the human in the loop by taking into account the accuracy and bias that
067   they could also have on these decisions: a deferral model should balance the uncertainty and biases
068   of any downstream decision maker. This alternative approach has gained attention both among
069   theorists (Madras et al., 2018; Verma and Nalisnick, 2022; Mozannar et al., 2023; Verma et al., 2023)
070   with a focus on uncertainty calibration, and in applications to domains such as medicine (Dvijotham
071   et al., 2022).
072   Our approach is conceptually similar to the confidence-score method (Raghu et al., 2019; Okati
073   et al., 2021), where a separate confidence score is computed for the human and model predictors.
074   The decision to defer is based on comparing the scores. A weakness of this approach is that the
075   model is not trained concurrently with the deferral system to complement humans where they are
076   most uncertain (Charusaie et al., 2022). However, in the cases where the model is fixed (for practical
077   or legal reasons), it is optimal to defer with a deterministic threshold function (Okati et al., 2021).
078   Online learning-to-defer. The online setting has been noted as an open problem in the learning-
079   to-defer literature (Mozannar et al., 2023). Bordt and Von Luxburg (2022) propose the first online
080   model for learning to advise: the machine’s decision is always given to the human decision-maker,
081   targeting applications like health care where the decision may only be taken by a human. Joshi et al.
082   (2022) address sequential learning-to-defer in medical settings, proposing a model-based reinforce-
083   ment learning method to learn the deferral decision; however, they assume offline access to batch
084   data from clinicians. Gao et al. (2021) address the problem of counterfactual risk minimization in
085   human-AI systems.
086   The question of optimizing decisions online while minimizing resource usage is more common to
087   the online learning and operations research fields: for instance, Cesa-Bianchi et al. (2021) optimize
088   online the Return on Investment, and Jain and Jamieson (2018) learn to manage a crowdfunding
089   platform to maximize the global quality of funded projects. To the best of our knowledge, our
090   model is the first to integrate a human expert in the loop of these online learning settings.
091   Bandits with Knapsacks. Bandits (Lattimore and Szepesvári, 2020) are sequential resource al-
092   location problems with limited information: only the return of the chosen action, or arm, can be
093   observed by the learner, and the observed return is often noisy. Knapsack constraints have been ex-
094   tensively studied in the stochastic setting (Madani et al., 2004; Badanidiyuru et al., 2018; Agrawal
095   and Devanur, 2014; 2016; Li and Stoltz, 2022) and rely on online optimization techniques (Hazan
096   and Levy, 2014; Hazan and Li, 2016; Lattimore and György, 2023) to adapt to the cost sequence.
097   There has also been recent interest in the adversarial setting, and proposed algorithms have achieved
098
      O(log T ) competitive ratio over the optimal static policy (Immorlica et al., 2022; Sivakumar et al.,
      2022).

144   level performance while the quality of the model can be monitored passively, budget permitting,
145   until we can safely use it when its performance could be superior. In the bandit setting, the model
146   cannot be run in parallel with the human and must be specifically chosen to obtain a performance.
147   In both cases, the cost incurred and the reward obtained are always that of the chosen action only.

397   The online deferral problem is as follows. At each time step, an instance of the knapsack problem is
398   presented to the algorithm. The features presented include the weights and values of the knapsack
399
      (normalized by capacity), the total capacity, various summary statistics, along with the cumulative
      average performance and time spent for the current participant. The instances are presented in the
400
      same order as they were presented to the participants. The deferral system must decide whether to
401
      take the participant’s answer (and incur the cost represented by the time they spent on the instance)
402   or to take the answer provided by Algorithm 2. Let V be the value of the answer, and Vmax be the
403   optimum value for this problem. The reward received is defined to be 1.1−V0.1  /Vmax . This nonlinear
404
      reward function incentivizes small improvements near V /Vmax ≈ 1.

      6   D ISCUSSIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
487
488
      We introduced a novel online decision making framework that connects learning-to-defer problems
489   with challenges more common to online learning and game theory such as budget constraints, par-
490   tial observability and unknown performance models. Our algorithmic approach largely leverages
491   existing works in the latter literature but our empirical results show the first concrete results on real
492   data. Crucially, we are able to empirically demonstrate that we can learn how to efficiently assign
493   decisions such that the combined performance is better than either of the predictors alone (model or
494   human).
495   Limitations of the model. One major limitation of the model is the parametric nature of the
496   predictions. While the NeuralLinear model allows us to learn descriptive features, this algorithm
497   does not come with the same theoretical guarantees. It also requires several implementation choices
498   to be made in advance, such as the architecture, learning rate, etc.
499
      We focus on single-resource constraint with single human expert but the original algorithm from
500
      Agrawal and Devanur (2016) would naturally extend to allowing for multiple types of resource
501   constraints or multiple experts with partially overlapping specializations.
502
503   Future directions. Our online approach is designed to readapt a deferral strategy after a possi-
504
      ble distribution shift. Going further, our model should be extended to continuously adapt to such
      changes, for instance by building on recent works on non-stationary bandit models (Liu et al., 2022;
505
      Lyu and Cheung, 2023).
