009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           We present several advances on neural operators by viewing the action of operator
012           layers as the minimizers of Bregman regularized optimization problems over
013           Banach function spaces. The proposed framework allows interpreting the activation
014           operators as Bregman proximity operators from dual to primal space. This novel
015           viewpoint is general enough to recover classical neural operators as well as a new
016           variant, coined Bregman neural operators, which includes the inverse activation
017           function and features the same expressivity of standard neural operators. Numerical
018
              experiments support the added benefits of the Bregman variant of Fourier neural
              operators for training deeper and more accurate models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Neural operators (Kovachki et al., 2021; 2023), a recent extension of neural networks, have emerged
024
      as a versatile framework for learning mappings between function spaces. These operators have shown
025
      great potential in solving partial differential equations (PDEs) and simulating complex dynamical
026   systems. The exploration of neural architectures for the approximation and learning of operators has
027   led to the development of a variety of models.
028   One influential contribution is the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021a), sketched in
029   Figure 1, which transforms encoded input data into frequency components in order to learn intricate
030   relationships in the frequency domain. More recently, the Group-Equivariant FNO (G-FNO) (Helwig NEW
031   et al., 2023) additionally leverages symmetries to design equivariant Fourier layers, thereby enhanc-
032   ing the representation power and robustness of the architecture. To better scale the depth of neural
033   operators, the F-FNO (Tran et al., 2023) proposed separable spectral layers and improved residual
034
      connections, along with a bag of training tricks. The FNO are extended to Wavelet Neural Operators
      (WNO) (Tripura & Chakraborty, 2023) by replacing Fourier layers with wavelet layers to further ex-
035
      ploit multiscale information. The U-shaped Neural Operator (U-NO) (Rahman et al., 2023) adapts the
036
      U-net architecture for neural operators, enabling mapping between function spaces through integral
037   operators, thus broadening the applicability of neural architectures to diverse domains. Differently,
038   the DeepONet architecture (Lu et al., 2021) comprises two intertwined components: a branch network
039   responsible for encoding discrete input function spaces, and a trunk network dedicated to encoding the
040   domain of output functions. Operating as a conditional model, DeepONet leverages the embedding
041   of inputs and outputs via a dot product operation, facilitating the approximation of complex functions
042   through a structured network topology. Finally, Neural Inverse Operators (NIO) (Molinaro et al.,
043   2023) tackle inverse problems by combining DeepONet and FNO architectures to map operators to
044   functions, thereby extending the applicability of neural operators to coefficient estimation tasks.
045
      Some approaches inspired by attention mechanisms, pivotal in image and natural language pro-
      cessing, have also been considered in operator learning. LOCA (Learning Operators with Coupled
046
      Attention) (Kissas et al., 2022) facilitates robust gradient estimation, particularly in scenarios with
047
      limited training data, by combining attention with kernel mechanisms. The General Neural Operator
048   Transformer (GNOT) (Hao et al., 2023) is a scalable framework based self-attention mechanisms
049   allowing to deal with heterogeneous inputs useful for modeling diverse physical systems.
050   Some physics-informed variants integrating information from PDEs during the learning process
051   have been proposed enhancing model interpretability and generalization: PI-DeepONet (Wang et al.,
052   2021) and its Long-Time Integration variant (LTI-PI-DeepONet) (Wang & Perdikaris, 2023), PINO
053   (Physics-Informed Neural Operator) (Li et al., 2021b) a hybrid extension of FNO, or other variations
      such as V-DeepONet (Goswami et al., 2022) and Modified DeepONet (Wang et al., 2022).

064   Contributions. Unlike previous works (Kovachki et al., 2021), which directly consider the composi-
065   tional form of neural operators, our approach introduces a distinct perspective by formulating the
066
      action of each operator layer as the minimizer of a regularized optimization problem over functions.
      This optimization connects the current hidden representation to the next, with the choice of a regular-
067
      ization implicitly defining the activation operator through the lens of the Bregman proximity operator.
068
      Our expressive framework not only convers existing neural operators but also introduces a novel
069   variant, termed Bregman neural operator, which demonstrates improved predictive performance
070   as its depth increases. Its applicability is grounded by universal approximation results proven for
071   sigmoidal-type activation operators. Beyond its unifying aspect and its ability to design novel neural
072   operators, the proposed framework allows applying the extensive body of literature on proximal FIX
073   numerical optimization, of which Bregman proximity operators belong to, in order to study neural
074   operators. This opens the way to extend the analysis done on neural networks to (Bregman) neural
075   operators in the same spirit of Combettes & Pesquet (2020a;b).
076   Outline. The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 is dedicated to the presentation
077
      of definitions and background knowledge on neural operators and Bregman proximity operator. In
      Section 3, we introduce the operator layers as the solution of a functional optimization problem.
078
      In addition, we show that this new mapping allows recovering the classical neural operators and
079
      creating a more general family of so-called Bregman neural operators. In Section 4, we provide a
080   preliminary universal approximation result for Bregman neural operators. Finally, in Section 5, we
081   conduct an extended experimental study comparing on benchmark datasets our Bregman variant with
082   the classical FNO.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In summary, our contributions are twofold: we have provided a novel theoretical framework that
526   broadens the understanding of neural operators through the lens of a Bregman regularized optimization
527   problem, and we have introduced Bregman neural operators that achieve enhanced performance as
528   their depth increases. As part of our theoretical advancements, we have also established universal
529   approximation results for Bregman neural architectures with sigmoidal-type activation functions.
530   However, it must be acknowledged that a gap exists between this result and common practices,
531
      which predominantly rely on ReLU-like activations, as in our work, opening the door to new
      theoretical developments. Beyond the unifying aspect of our framework and its ability to design
532
      novel neural architectures, our framework also paves the way to use the rich body of literature on NEW
533
      monotone operators to study neural operators. In the context of neural networks, an example of
534   fruitful application of the latter is given in Combettes & Pesquet (2020a) where the authors provide
535   asymptotic properties of neural networks (as the number of layers tends to infinity). One can also
536   consider the work in Combettes & Pesquet (2020b) where the authors yield quantitative insights
537   into the stability properties of neural networks. As for our setting, we can guess that such results
538   might be extended to Bregman neural networks/operators by leveraging the notion of so called D-firm
539   operators studied in Bauschke et al. (2003), meaning operators that are firmly nonexpansive with
      respect to a Bregman divergence.

1433
1434   In this section, we provide comparisons with other FNO improvements on the 2D Navier-Stokes
1435   dataset with ОҪ =10вҲ’4 . In particular, we consider F-FNO (Tran et al., 2023) which is a particularly
1436   relevant baseline for comparison, as it i) incorporates skip-like connections that share similarities with
1437
       our additional Пғ вҲ’1 term and ii) also seeks to enable the development of deeper FNO architectures.
1438
1439   We consider the best-performing F-FNO model (as identified by its authors), trained using our
1440   optimization strategy and adapted to our specific learning task. It is important to note that F-FNO
1441   was originally designed for predicting mappings between multiple consecutive time steps (e.g., from
1442   t to t + 1) and it offers the option to rely on techniques such as the Markov assumption and teacher
1443
       forcing. Since our task involves predicting the final state directly from the initial conditions, those
       techniques are not appropriate, and thus we did not include them in the implementation. Additionally,
1444
       beyond the optimization strategy proposed by the F-FNO authors (AdamW with cosine annealing,
1445
       noise injection and input normalization), we also employed the optimization strategy detailed in
1446   Appendix C.6. In the following, we name them F-FNObase and F-FNO, respectively.
1447   Results reported in Table 6 show that the original training procedure of the F-FNO does not transfer
1448   well to our learning task, as evidenced by the poor performance of F-FNObase across all layers, com-
1449   pared to the other models. On the contrary, with our training strategy and adapted hyperparameters,
1450   F-FNO shows strong performance, especially with few layers.
1451
1452   Moreover, to isolate the impact of residual connections from the broader structural modifications
1453   introduced by F-FNO, we have also implemented and compared a ResNet-inspired variant of FNO,
1454   referred to as ResFNO. We did this to better understand the role of the residual connection.
1455   As shown in Table 6, ResFNO consistently achieves lower training error than FNO, which aligns
1456   with the behavior typically observed in ResNet-like architectures. However, it falls short in terms of
1457   generalization to unseen data, as reflected in the test error.

1484   operators within our framework, the activation function only needs to be monotonic, not strictly
1485
