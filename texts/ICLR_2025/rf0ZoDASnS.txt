009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            On-device LLMs have gained increasing attention for their ability to enhance pri-
012            vacy and provide a personalized user experience. To facilitate learning with private
013            and scarce local data, federated learning has become a standard approach, though
014            it introduces challenges related to system and data heterogeneity among end users.
015            As a solution, we propose a novel Collaborative learning approach with a Mixture
016
               of Generalists and Specialists (CoMiGS), being the first to effectively address
               both. Our approach distinguishes generalists and specialists by aggregating certain
017
               experts across end users while keeping others localized to specialize in user-specific
018
               datasets. A key innovation of our method is the bi-level optimization formulation
019            of the Mixture-of-Experts learning objective, where the router is updated using
020            a separate validation set that represents the target distribution. CoMiGS effec-
021            tively balances collaboration and personalization, as demonstrated by its superior
022            performance in scenarios with high data heterogeneity across multiple datasets.
023            By design, our approach accommodates users’ varying computational resources
024            through different numbers of specialists. By decoupling resource abundance from
025            data quantity, CoMiGS remains robust against overfitting—due to the generalists’
026            regularizing effect—while adapting to local data through specialist expertise.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have been showing great success serving as foundation models,
031   evidenced by their capability to understand a wide range of tasks, such as ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023),
032   Claude (Anthropic, 2023), Gemini (DeepMind, 2023) and etc. However, cloud-based inference intro-
033   duces significant delays for end users, and it often fails to meet their personalized needs (Ding et al.,
034   2024; Iyengar & Adusumilli, 2024). Recently, there has been growing interest in deploying LLMs on
035   edge devices, which offer benefits like lower latency, data localization, and more personalized user
036   experiences (Xu et al., 2024). For instance, Apple (2024) recently launched on-device foundation
037   models as part of its personal intelligence system.
038   On-device LLMs present challenges such as limited and variable computational resources, scarce and
039   heterogeneous local data, and privacy concerns related to data sharing (Peng et al., 2024; Wagner
040   et al., 2024). Fine-tuning is typically performed on-device to quickly adapt to users’ individual
041   needs. While data sharing is a common solution to address local data scarcity, on-device data is
042   often privacy-sensitive and must remain on the device. To overcome this, federated learning has been
043   proposed as a method for enabling collaborative learning while preserving user privacy, allowing end
044
      users to collaborate by sharing model parameters (Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
045   Federated fine-tuning of LLMs is predominately done through Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA, Hu et al.
046   (2021)) due to its lightweight nature so that the communication costs can be largely mitigated. Yet
047   end devices may have different capacities, resulting in different LoRA ranks or different numbers of
048   LoRA modules allowed on devices. Previous works have proposed various techniques for aggregating
049   LoRA modules of different ranks (Cho et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024). However, in both works, the
050
      devices are only equipped with shared knowledge, which makes the methods unsuitable when there
      is data heterogeneity across users. In such cases, a more personalized solution is needed.
051
052   End users’ local data distributions can exhibit significant statistical heterogeneity. For instance, mobile
053   device users may have distinct linguistic habits, topic preferences, or language usage patterns, leading
      to widely varying word distributions. As a result, personalized solutions are necessary. Wagner et al.

070   (2024) explored three personalized collaborator selection protocols, allowing each end user to choose
071   their collaborators. Although these protocols effectively address data heterogeneity, they depend on
072   model aggregation, which can only occur when users share the same model architecture.
073
      There has not yet been a solution to deal with both system heterogeneity and data heterogeneity.
074
      Towards this goal, we propose a novel Collaborative learning approach via a Mixture of Generalists
075   and Specialists (CoMiGS). Our approach allows users to share part of the knowledge while keeping
076   some knowledge user-specific, thus providing personalized solutions. We name the shared part
077   generalists and the user-specific part specialists. Like all previous works, the generalists and
078   specialists are simply LoRA modules. At the same time, as long as the shared part can be aggregated,
079   the user-specific part can be of different sizes, which can be adapted to various device capacities, as
080   illustrated by different numbers of specialists across users in Figure 1.
081   We integrate the expertise of generalists and specialists using a learned router that determines
082   aggregation weights, following the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture (Fedus et al., 2022b). As
083   in typical MoE designs for language modeling (Jiang et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024), we also use
084   tokens as the routing unit. Although users may have different topic preferences or linguistic styles,
085   they still share common tokens in their vocabularies. Our goal is to route these shared tokens to the
086   generalists so they can be jointly learned across users.
087   The closest work to ours is pFedMoE from Yi et al. (2024) from the vision domain, where each user
088   has a shared homogeneous small feature extractor, a localized heterogeneous feature extractor, and a
089   localized routing network, with routing unit being a semantic unit – an image. The three components
090   are simultaneously updated. Compared to pFedMoE, our method CoMiGS introduces three key
091   updates: 1) we reformulate the learning objective into a bi-level optimization framework, following
092   the inherent hierarchical order of router and expert learning; 2) we refine the routing mechanism
093   by using the smallest unit like a pixel in an image, which is a token; 3) unlike pFedMoE’s fixed
094   two-expert limit per user, we support varying numbers of expert modules across users.
095   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
096
               • We propose a novel approach (CoMiGS) for on-device personalized collaborative fine-tuning
097
                 of LLMs, introducing an innovative bi-level formulation of the Mixture-of-Experts learning
098              objective. Our approach can effectively tackle distribution shifts in local data.
099
               • Our collaborative framework effectively addresses both system heterogeneity, with respect
100              to varying local model architectures, and data heterogeneity, concerning diverse local data
101              distributions across users, making it the first model to accomplish both.
102
               • Our framework separates resource heterogeneity from data quantity. Users with larger local
103              datasets benefit from a bigger model, while users with more powerful models but smaller
104              datasets are less prone to overfitting.
105            • We release a codebase1 for collaborative LLMs that allows users to easily define their own
106              collaboration strategies, facilitating and advancing future research efforts in this field.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Collaborative Learning for LLMs. Recently, researchers have been investigating the application
111   of Federated Learning in language tasks. Due to the substantial number of model parameters in
112   LLMs, the research has largely targeted the stages following pre-training, often utilizing parameter-
113   efficient techniques such as adapters. Mohtashami et al. (2023) explored a teacher-student social
114   learning framework to aggregate private-sensitive instructions. Zhang et al. (2023) directly applied
115   FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) to aggregate LoRA parameters during instruction tuning, and reported
116   increased performance in downstream tasks. Following that, there are various works focusing on
117   addressing system heterogeneity where users are equipped with different LoRA ranks. HetLoRA (Cho
118
      et al., 2023) and FlexLoRA (Bai et al., 2024) provide different ways of aggregating and distributing
      LoRA modules of heterogenous ranks. However, these approaches are not designed to cope with
119
      heterogeneous data on device. In contrast, Sun et al. (2024) found better performances with respect
120
      to heterogeneous data can be achieved through freezing LoRA A matrices at initialization; Wagner
121   et al. (2024) proposed personalized solutions that can sufficiently tackle data heterogeneity, through
122   three different collaborator selection mechanisms. Yet for both works, the users must be equipped
123   with the same model architecture. Unlike previous works, our framework deals with both model
124   heterogeneity and data heterogeneity. Moreover, our method offers personalized solutions at a token
125   level, as opposed to the client-level approach in Wagner et al. (2024).
126   Mixture of Generalist and Specialist Experts. Gaspar & Seddon (2022) introduced a fusion of
127   global and local experts for activity prediction based on molecular structures. Each local expert is
128   tailored to a specific chemical series of interest using loss masking, while a global expert is trained
129   across all series. Simultaneously, a routing network learns to assign soft merging scores. This
130   approach yielded superior empirical results compared to single experts. Dai et al. (2024) developed
131   DeepSeekMoE by deterministically assigning every token to “shared” experts, whereas “routed”
132
      experts are assigned tokens based on a learnable router. DeepSeekMoE is able to approach the upper
      bound performance for MoE models. For both works, the notion of shared/global is with respect to
133
      input samples, i.e. a shared/global expert should see all input samples. In a collaborative setup, Yi
134
      et al. (2024) proposed pFedMoE, where each user has a shared homogeneous small feature extractor,
135   a localized heterogeneous feature extractor, and a localized routing network, with routing unit being
136   an image. The three components are jointly updated in an end-to-end fashion, demonstrating strong
137   performance in the vision domain. Our work builds on the foundations of pFedMoE and extends it to
138   the language domain. Furthermore, we introduce key innovations that enable more effective handling
139   of distribution shifts and achieve a more refined balance between personalization and collaboration.
140
141   3     M ETHOD
142
143   We aim to improve personalized performance for each user on their target distributions, where
144   distribution shifts can be allowed. Building on the hierarchical insights of MoE learning, we
145   formulate our learning objective into a bi-level optimization problem, where expert parameters are
146   learned using the relatively large-sized training sets, while routing parameters are updated using
147   the small-sized validation sets. We further let experts diversify into generalists and specialists via
148   parameter aggregation or localization, to leverage both collective power and specialized knowledge.
149   As the problem solver, we provide a multi-round gradient-based algorithm.
150
151   3.1   N OTIONS AND PROBLEM SETUP
152
      Each user has a training set Xitrain , a small validation set Xivalid and a test set Xitest , and the task is
153
      next token prediction. The validation set Xivalid and the test set Xitest are sampled from the same
154
155
      distribution Pitarget (note this is a fuzzy concept in the language domain, by the same distribution
156
      we mean from the same topic/category). The training set, Xitrain , can be sampled from a different
157   distribution than Pitarget . This is to address scenarios where distribution shifts may occur over time,
158
      such as changes in topics reflected in the typing data of mobile phone users.
159   As illustrated in Figure 1, there are two sets of model parameters within each user: expert parameters,
160   denoted as Θ = θG ∪ {θiS }, where θG is shared across the users and {θiS } are user-specific specialist
161   parameters; and routing parameters, denoted as Φ = {ϕi }. i ∈ {1, 2, .., N } is the user index. Our
      ultimate goal is to optimize the average target performance across all users.

      3.2   A BI - LEVEL FORMULATION
167
168
      Essentially, we adopt an MoE architecture, apart from aggregating certain expert parameters. Instead
169   of learning routing and expert parameters simultaneously like the conventional way in LLMs (Zoph
170   et al., 2022; Fedus et al., 2022a), we update the two sets of parameters in an alternating fashion. We
171   observe a natural hierarchy between the experts and the router: the assignment of tokens to experts
172   depends on the router’s outputs, while the experts’ parameters are updated based on the assigned
173   tokens. In this way, the experts’ development follows the router’s decisions, establishing an inherent
174   leader-follower structure. Following Von Stackelberg (2010), we formulate the hierarchical problem
175   as a bi-level optimization objective in (1). Notably, one of the earliest MoE works (Jordan & Jacobs,
176   1994), also demonstrates a hierarchical structure, though for a probabilistic interpretation. In contrast,
177
      we approach the hierarchical structure from an optimization perspective, formulating the learning
      process as two nested optimization problems.

195   3.3   O UR A LGORITHM
196
197   To solve (1), we use a multi-round gradient-based algorithm as shown in Alg.1, where only generalist
198
      parameters are shared and aggregated, and specialist and router parameters are updated locally. While
      the scheme requires a server, it can alternatively be implemented in a serverless all2all fashion, which
199
      requires N times more communication overhead and we do not further pursue this here.
200
201   Alternating Update of Θ and Φ: Alternating update of two sets of parameters is a standard way
202   to solve bi-level optimization problems (Chen et al., 2021). In between two communication rounds,
203   we perform alternating updates of expert and routing parameters using local training and validation
204
      sets separately. The updates optimize the objectives given in (2) and (3) respectively. Since the
      updates of Θ and Φ are disentangled, they do not need to be updated at the same frequency. The
205
      routing parameters are smaller in size and thus can be updated less frequently. When updating
206
      model parameters, we include an additional load-balancing term as in Fedus et al. (2022a), which is
207   standard in MoE implementation and encourages even distribution of token assignments to experts.
208   A discussion over the load balancing term is included in Appendix C.4. It is observed that a load-
209   balancing term can improve test performance compared to not having one. However, directing more
210   tokens to the generalists has no noticeable effect.
211
212   Update of θG and θiS : The update of generalist parameters θG follows a standard FedAvg scheme,
213   through aggregating model parameters. Specifically, we simultaneously update both θG and θiS by
214   optimizing equation (2), which results in θiG and θiS . A parameter aggregation is then performed on
215   the user-specific θiG via a trusted server to establish a shared θG across all users. In the next round,
      each user replaces their θiG with the global θG , while their θiS remains locally updated.

287
      We start by equipping users with the same model architecture locally, to illustrate the effectiveness
      of our hierarchical learning of routing and expert parameters. We compare our one generalist one
288
      specialist (CoMiGS-1G1S) method to the following baselines. In order to match the trainable
289
      parameter count of our method, we use 2 times LoRA modules within each user.
290
291      i) Upper and lower bounds:
292           • Pretrained: A pretrained GPT-2 model using weights from OpenAI.
293           • Centralized: A single model trained using data from all users. (Note this method is
294             an unrealistic baseline as data cannot leave the devices due to privacy concerns.)
295     ii) Baselines:
296
              • Local: Training individually using only local data without collaboration.
297
              • FedAvg: Aggregating LoRA parameters across users using uniform weights, which is
298             equivalent to applying FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017).
299           • PCL: Aggregating LoRA parameters using a client-level collaboration graph. The graph is
300             updated using validation performances. (Strategy 2 in Wagner et al. (2024)).
301           • pFedMoE: We directly apply the method from Yi et al. (2024) in the language domain
302             where we update routing and expert parameters at the same time and choose tokens as a
303             routing unit.
304    iii) Ablations:
305
              • CoMiGS-2S: Both of the LoRA experts are specialists, meaning their weights are neither
306             shared nor aggregated. The routing parameters are updated using a separate validation set
307             like in CoMiGS-1G1S.
308           • CoMiGS-2G: Both of the LoRA experts are generalists, meaning their weights are always
309             shared and aggregated. The routing parameters are updated using a separate validation set
310             like in CoMiGS-1G1S.

      4.3.1                   BASELINE C OMPARISON
418
419
      In this section, our focus is to deal with system heterogeneity, where users can have different numbers
420   of experts. We still keep one generalist expert, but the number of specialists can vary across the users
421   (our method is denoted as One-Generalist-X-Specialists, named CoMiGS-1GXS). It’s important to
422   note that the richness of computational resources doesn’t always correlate with the complexity of
423   local data. For instance, some users may have ample computational resources but local data in small
424   quantities. In such cases, a crucial objective is to prevent overfitting due to redundant model-fitting
425   abilities.
426   We compare our approach to two state-of-the-art baselines: HetLoRA from Cho et al. (2023) and
427   FlexLoRA from Bai et al. (2024), both of which adapt LoRA ranks based on the resource capacity
428   of each user. HetLoRA aggregates LoRA matrices A and B by zero-padding to the maximum
429   rank and then distributes them back using rank truncation. In contrast, FlexLoRA first reconstructs
430   model updates ∆W and redistributes the aggregated updates using SVD. We compare our method
431   to these baselines by matching the number of fine-tunable parameters, measured as both active and
      full parameters. For example, to match the full parameter count of CoMiGS-1GXS with (4, 2, 2, 2)

526   5                  C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
527
528   We propose a novel framework for on-device personalized collaborative fine-tuning of LLMs,
529   grounded in an innovative bi-level formulation of the Mixture-of-Experts learning objective. Our
530
      fine-grained integration of generalist and specialist expert knowledge achieves superior performance
      in balancing personalization and collaboration within Federated LLMs.
531
532   Furthermore, our framework is the first to address both system and data heterogeneity in collaborative
533   LLM training. It also decouples local data quantity from resource availability, allowing high-resourced
534   users to leverage larger datasets for improved performance while remaining resilient against overfitting
535   in low-data scenarios.
536   An interesting future direction to explore is adopting our framework for collaborative instruction
537   tuning of larger LLMs and evaluating its performance on downstream tasks. While our paper focused
538   on a single generalist, it is possible to include multiple generalists, and their impact on performance
539   remains to be seen. We hope our work paves the way for a new direction in on-device collaborative
      LLMs.

      A     L IMITATIONS AND S OCIETAL I MPACT
703
704
      Limitations. Compared to FedAvg type of methods, our method requires extra gradient steps on
705   routing parameters and memory storage for the routing parameters. Since a routing network is usually
706   a one-layer MLP, the extra cost in computation and storage is relatively small.
707
708
      The robust performance of our method relies on the fact that we update routing parameters and expert
      parameters on two independent losses. This means we need some validation samples independent
709
      from training samples. When local data size is minimal, this can be an issue.
710
711   Our method, while generally robust, still has a tendency towards overfitting when there is a significant
712   mismatch between local resource abundance and data complexity, similar to other methods.
713
714   Societal Impact. We offer a collaboration framework for edge devices, aiming to enable smaller
715   devices to leverage large language models (LLMs) despite limited resources and data availability.
716   Our approach enhances fairness and mitigates privacy concerns by ensuring data remains on end
717
      devices. The privacy aspects can further be enhanced by differential private aggregation of generalist
      weights, which we do not pursue here.
718
719   The robustness towards attackers is beyond the scope of our work. Our collaboration framework has
720   no guarantee of resilience towards Byzantine attackers, which could potentially lead to misuse by
721   certain parties.

      B.1   C OMPUTATIONAL AND COMMUNICATION OVERHEAD
726
727   Computational overhead: During a forward pass, on top of the base pre-trained GPT2 model ( 32
728   GFlops), fine-tuning using FedAvg with two sets of LoRA modules adds extra 490 MFLOPs (+
729   1.53%), while the typical FedAvg with one set of LoRA models adds extra 166 MFlops (+ 0.52%).
730   Our CoMiGS-1G1S adds 495 extra MFLOPs (+ 1.55%, 490MFLOPS from the experts and 5MFLOPs
731   from the router). The FLOPs are approximated following Appendix B of Chowdhery et al. (2023).
732   The extra computational complexity is almost neglectable in comparison to the base model.
733   Extra memory requirement: Compared to storing the LoRA matrices, the extra memory storage
734   from the router is 0.035 MB, assuming bfloat16 training.
735
      Communication costs: Since specialists and routers stay locally within each device, the only weight
736
      to communicate is from the generalist experts. As we conduct fine-tuning with bfloat16, in each
737   communication round, each device only needs to communicate 1.41 MB of generalist weights, which
738   we do not consider a big value.
739
740
      B.2   T RAINING D ETAILS
741
742   Following Kalajdzievski (2023), we choose γ to be a rank-stabilized value, a technique which helps

      C.4     I S THE STANDARD LOAD BALANCING LOSS SUFFICIENT ?
894
895   The standard load balancing loss encourages equal assignment of tokens to each expert. When the
896   number of experts gets larger, there might not be enough tokens routed to the generalists, which
897   might lead to a under-developed general knowledge. We will verify if this is indeed true.
898   To encourage enough tokens to be routed to the generalist expert such that more general knowledge
899   can be developed, we modify our load-balancing loss by introducing importance weighting. As we
900   separate the 0-th expert to be the generalist expert and conduct Top-2 routing, the modified load
901   balancing loss is as follows:
