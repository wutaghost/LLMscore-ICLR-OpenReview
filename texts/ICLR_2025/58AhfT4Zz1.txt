009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural architecture search (Graph NAS) has emerged as a promising ap-
012            proach for autonomously designing graph neural network architectures by lever-
013            aging the correlations between graphs and architectures. However, the existing
014            methods fail to generalize under distribution shifts that are ubiquitous in real-world
015            graph scenarios, mainly because the graph-architecture correlations they exploit
016            might be spurious and varying across distributions. In this paper, we propose to
017            handle the distribution shifts in the graph architecture search process by discov-
018            ering and exploiting the causal relationship between graphs and architectures to
019
               search for the optimal architectures that can generalize under distribution shifts.
               The problem remains unexplored with the following critical challenges: 1) how to
020
               discover the causal graph-architecture relationship that has stable predictive abili-
021
               ties across distributions, 2) how to handle distribution shifts with the discovered
022            causal graph-architecture relationship to search the generalized graph architec-
023            tures. To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach, Causal-aware
024            Graph Neural Architecture Search (CARNAS), which is able to capture the causal
025            graph-architecture relationship during the architecture search process and discover
026            the generalized graph architecture under distribution shifts. Specifically, we pro-
027            pose Disentangled Causal Subgraph Identification to capture the causal subgraphs
028            that have stable prediction abilities across distributions. Then, we propose Graph
029            Embedding Intervention to intervene on causal subgraphs within the latent space,
030
               ensuring that these subgraphs encapsulate essential features for prediction while
               excluding non-causal elements. Additionally, we propose Invariant Architecture
031
               Customization to reinforce the causal invariant nature of the causal subgraphs,
032
               which are utilized to tailor generalized graph architectures. Extensive experiments
033            on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that our proposed CARNAS
034            achieves advanced out-of-distribution generalization ability by discovering the
035            causal relationship between graphs and architectures during the search process.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Graph neural architecture search (Graph NAS), aiming at automating the designs of GNN architectures
041
      for different graphs, has shown great success by exploiting the correlations between graphs and
042   architectures. Present approaches (11; 25; 31) leverage a rich search space filled with GNN operations
043   and employ strategies like reinforcement learning and continuous optimization algorithms to pinpoint
044   an optimal architecture for specific datasets, aiming to decode the natural correlations between graph
045   data and their ideal architectures. Based on the independently and identically distributed (I.I.D)
046   assumption on training and testing data, existing methods assume the graph-architecture correlations
047   are stable across graph distributions.
048   Nevertheless, distribution shifts are ubiquitous and inevitable in real-world graph scenarios, particu-
049   larly evident in applications existing with numerous unforeseen and uncontrollable hidden factors
050   like drug discovery, in which the availability of training data is limited, and the complex chemical
051   properties of different molecules lead to varied interaction mechanisms (20). Consequently, GNN
052   models developed for such purposes must be generalizable enough to handle the unavoidable varia-
053   tions in data distribution between training and testing sets, underlining the critical need for models
      that can adapt to and perform reliably under such varying conditions.


                                                         1
054
      However, existing Graph NAS methods fail to generalize under distribution shifts, since they do
055   not specifically consider the relationship between graphs and architectures, and may exploit the
056   spurious correlations between graphs and architectures unintendedly, which vary with distribution
057   shifts, during the search process. Relying on these spurious correlations, the search process identifies
058   patterns that are valid only in the training data but do not generalize to unseen data. This results
059   in good performance on the training distribution but poor performance when the underlying data
060   distribution changes in the test set.
061   In this paper, we study the problem of graph neural architecture search under distribution shifts by
062   capturing the causal relationship between graphs and architectures to search for the optimal graph
063   architectures that can generalize under distribution shifts. The problem is highly non-trivial with the
064   following challenges:
065
066    • How to discover the causal graph-architecture relationship that has stable predictive abilities
067      across distributions?
068
069    • How to handle distribution shifts with the discovered causal graph-architecture relationship to
070      search the generalized graph architectures?
071
072   To address these challenges, we propose the Causal-aware Graph NAS (CARNAS), which is able to
073   capture the causal relationship, stable to distribution shifts, between graphs and architectures, and
074   thus handle the distribution shifts in the graph architecture search process. Specifically, we design a
075
      Disentangled Causal Subgraph Identification module, which employs disentangled GNN layers to
      obtain node and edge representations, then further derive causal subgraphs based on the importance
076
      of each edge. This module enhances the generalization by deeply exploring graph features as well
077
      as latent information with disentangled GNNs, thereby enabling a more precise extraction of causal
078   subgraphs, carriers of causally relevant information, for each graph instance. Following this, our
079   Graph Embedding Intervention module employs another shared GNN to encode the derived causal
080   subgraphs and non-causal subgraphs in the same latent space, where we perform interventions on
081   causal subgraphs with non-causal subgraphs. Additionally, we ensure the causal subgraphs involve
082   principal features by engaging the supervised classification loss of causal subgraphs into the training
083   objective. We further introduce the Invariant Architecture Customization module, which addresses
084   distribution shifts not only by constructing architectures for each graph with their causal subgraph but
085   also by integrating a regularizer on simulated architectures corresponding to those intervention graphs,
086
      aiming to reinforce the causal invariant nature of causal subgraphs derived in module 1. We remark
      that the classification loss for causal subgraphs in module 2 and the regularizer on architectures for
087
      intervention graphs in module 3 help with ensuring the causality between causal subgraphs and the
088
      customized architecture for a graph instance. Moreover, by incorporating them into the training and
089   search process, we make the Graph NAS model intrinsically interpretable to some degree. Empirical
090   validation across both synthetic and real-world datasets underscores the remarkable out-of-distribution
091   generalization capabilities of CARNAS over existing baselines. Detailed ablation studies further
092   verify our designs. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
093
094    • We are the first to study graph neural architecture search under distribution shifts from the causal
095      perspective, by proposing the causal-aware graph neural architecture search (CARNAS), that
096      integrates causal inference into graph neural architecture search, to the best of our knowledge.
097
098    • We propose three modules: disentangled causal subgraph identification, graph embedding in-
099      tervention, and invariant architecture customization, offering a nuanced strategy for extracting
100
         and utilizing causal relationships between graph data and architecture, which is stable under
         distribution shifts, thereby enhancing the model’s capability of out-of-distribution generalization.
101
102
       • Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets confirm that CARNAS signifi-
103      cantly outperforms existing baselines, showcasing its efficacy in improving graph classification
104      accuracy across diverse datasets, and validating the superior out-of-distribution generalization
105      capabilities of our proposed CARNAS. 1

129   2.2   C AUSAL VIEW OF THE G RAPH NAS PROCESS
130
      Causal approaches are largely adopted when dealing with out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization by
131   capturing the stable causal structures or patterns in input data that influence the results (27). While in
132   normal graph neural network cases, previous work that studies the problem from a causal perspective
133   mainly considers the causality between graph data and labels (28; 53).
134
135   Causal analysis in Graph NAS. Based on the known that different GNN architectures suit different
136   graphs (7; 60) and inspired by (56), we analyze the potential relationships between graph instance G,
137   causal subgraph Gc , non-causal subgraph Gs and optimal architecture A∗ for G in the graph neural
138   architecture search process as below:
139
140    • Gc → G ← Gs indicates that two disjoint parts, causal subgraph Gc and non-causal subgraph
141
         Gs , together form the input graph G.
142    • Gc → A∗ represents our assumption that there exists the causal subgraph which solely determines
143      the optimal architecture A∗ for input graph G. Taking the Spurious-Motif dataset (63) as an
144      example, (41) discovers that different shapes of graph elements prefer different architectures.
145    • Gc       Gs means that there are potential probabilistic dependencies between Gc and Gs (39; 40),
146      which can make up spurious correlations between non-causal subgraph Gs and the optimal
147      architecture A∗ .
148
149   Intervention. Inspired by the ideology of invariant learning (2; 24; 5), that forms different envi-
150   ronments to abstract the invariant features, we do interventions on causal subgraph Gc by adding
151
      different spurious (non-causal) subgraphs to it, and therefore simulate different environments for a
      graph instance G.

462   5     R ELATED WORK
463
464
      Neural Architecture Search (NAS) automates creating optimal neural networks using RL-based (76),
      evolutionary (43), and gradient-based methods (31). GraphNAS (11) inspired studies on GNN
465
      architectures for graph classification in various datasets (41; 61). Real-world data differences between
466
      training and testing sets impact GNN performance (46; 27; 70; 71). Studies (28; 9) identify invariant
467   subgraphs to mitigate this, usually with fixed GNN encoders. Our method automates designing
468   generalized graph architectures by discovering causal relationships. Causal learning explores variable
469   interconnections (40), enhancing deep learning (65). In graphs, it includes interventions on non-
470   causal components (57), causal and bias subgraph decomposition (9), and ensuring out-of-distribution
471   generalization (6; 72; 73). These methods use fixed GNN architectures, while we address distribution
472   shifts by discovering causal relationships between graphs and architectures.
473
474   6     C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we address distribution shifts in graph neural architecture search (Graph NAS) from a
477   causal perspective. Existing methods struggle with distribution shifts between training and testing sets
478   due to spurious correlations. To mitigate this, we introduce Causal-aware Graph Neural Architecture
479   Search (CARNAS). Our approach identifies stable causal structures and their relationships with
480   architectures. We propose three key modules: Disentangled Causal Subgraph Identification, Graph
481   Embedding Intervention, and Invariant Architecture Customization. These modules leverage causal
482   relationships to search for generalized graph neural architectures. Experiments on synthetic and real-
483   world datasets show that CARNAS achieves superior out-of-distribution generalization, highlighting
484
      the importance of causal awareness in Graph NAS.

      optimal architecture.

      To begin with, since causal relationships are, by definition, invariant across environments, we make
757   the below assumption on our causal invariant subgraph generator fC (G) = Gc : G →    − Gc , following
758   previous literature on invariant learning (44; 28).

      Assumption 1 There exists an optimal causal invariant subgraph generator fC (G) satisfying:
762

763         a. Invariance property: For all e, e′ ∈ supp(E), P e (A∗ |fC (G)) = P e (A∗ |fC (G)).
764
765         b. Sufficiency property: A∗ = fA (fC (G)) + ϵ, where fA (·) customizes the GNN architecture
766            from a graph, ϵ ⊥ G (indicating statistical independence), and ϵ is random noise.
767
      Invariance assumption indicating that the subgraph generator fC (G) is capable of generating invariant
768
      subgraphs across different environments e, e′ ∈ supp(E), where E is a random variable of all
769
      environments. This ensures that the conditional distribution P (A∗ |fC (G)) remains consistent and
770   unaffected by the environment. Sufficiency assumption demonstrates that the subgraph generated by
771   fC (G) has sufficient expressive power to enable prediction of the optimal architecture A∗ . This is
772   achieved through fA (·), customizing the GNN architecture from a graph, while the added random
773   noise ϵ is independent of the graph G.

776   between optimal architecture and the generated subgraph.

1007   E.2     C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
1008
1009   In this section, we analyze the complexity of our proposed method in terms of its computational time
1010   and the quantity of parameters that require optimization. Let’s denote by |V | the number of nodes in a
1011   graph, by |E| the number of edges, by |O| the size of search space, and by d the dimension of hidden
1012   representations within a traditional graph neural network (GNN) framework. In our approach, d0
1013   represents the dimension of the hidden representations within the identification network GNN0 , d1
1014   represents the dimension of the hidden representations within the shared graph encoder GNN1 , and
1015
       ds denotes the dimension within the tailored super-network. Notably, d0 encapsulates the combined
       dimension of Q chunks, meaning the dimension per chunk is d0 /Q.
1016
1017
1018   E.2.1    T IME COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
1019
1020   For most message-passing GNNs, the computational time complexity is traditionally O(|E|d+|V |d2 ).
1021   Following this framework, the GNN0 in our model exhibits a time complexity of O(|E|d0 + |V |d20 ),
1022   and the GNN1 in our model exhibits a time complexity of O(|E|d1 + |V |d21 ). The most computa-
1023   tionally intensive operation in the invariant architecture customization module, which involves the
1024   computation of Lop , leads to a time complexity of O(|O|2 d1 ). The time complexity attributed to the
1025   customized super-network is O(|O|(|E|ds + |V |d2s )). Consequently, the aggregate time complexity
       of our method can be summarized as O(|E|(d0 + d1 + |O|ds ) + |V |(d20 + d21 + |O|d2s ) + |O|2 d1 ).


                                                         19
1026
       E.2.2    PARAMETER COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
1027
1028   A typical message-passing GNN has a parameter complexity of O(d2 ). In our architecture, the
1029   disentangled causal subgraph identification network GNN0 possesses O(d20 ) parameters, the shared
1030   GNN encoder GNN1 possesses O(d21 ), the invariant architecture customization module contains
1031   O(|O|d1 ) parameters and the customized super-network is characterized by O(|O|d2s ) parameters.
1032
       Therefore, the total parameter complexity in our framework is expressed as O(d20 + d21 + |O|d1 +
       |O|d2s ).
1033
1034   The analyses underscore that the proposed method scales linearly with the number of nodes and edges
1035   in the graph and maintains a constant number of learnable parameters, aligning it with the efficiency
1036   of prior GNN and graph NAS methodologies. Moreover, given that |O| typically represents a modest
1037   constant (for example, |O| = 6 in our search space) and that d0 and d1 is generally much less than
1038
       ds , the computational and parameter complexities are predominantly influenced by ds . To ensure
       equitable comparisons with existing GNN baselines, we calibrate ds within our model such that the
1039
       parameter count, specifically |O|d2s , approximates d2 , thereby achieving a balance between efficiency
1040
       and performance.

       H.2          G RAPH OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION GENERALIZATION
1289
1290   In the realm of machine learning, a pervasive assumption posits the existence of identical distributions
1291   between training and testing data. However, real-world scenarios frequently challenge this assumption
1292   with inevitable shifts in distribution, presenting significant hurdles to model performance in out-
1293   of-distribution (OOD) scenarios (46; 70; 71). The drastic deterioration in performance becomes
1294   evident when models lack robust OOD generalization capabilities, a concern particularly pertinent
1295   in the domain of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which have gained prominence within the
       graph community (27). Several noteworthy studies (55; 54; 28; 9; 47; 30; 48) have tackled this


                                                                        24
1296
       challenge by focusing on identifying environment-invariant subgraphs to mitigate distribution shifts.
1297   These approaches typically rely on pre-defined or dynamically generated environment labels from
1298   various training scenarios to discern variant information and facilitate the learning of invariant
1299   subgraphs. (52; 68) have divided recent literature that solve the graph OOD generalization problem,
1300   into three categories: 1) Graph augmentation methods (75; 45; 10; 64) enhance OOD generalization
1301   by increasing the quantity and diversity of training data through systematic graph modifications.
1302   2) The second type of methods (34; 26) develop new graph models to learn OOD-generalized
1303   representations. 3)The third type of methods (69; 17) enhance OOD generalization through tailored
1304   training schemes with specific objectives and constraints. There are various datasets and benchmarks
1305
       (16; 36; 14; 20; 21) help for assessing generalizability and adaptability. Moreover, the existing
       methods usually adopt a fixed GNN encoder in the whole optimization process, neglecting the role of
1306
       graph architectures in out-of-distribution generalization. In this paper, we focus on automating the
1307
       design of generalized graph architectures by discovering causal relationships between graphs and
1308   architectures, and thus handle distribution shifts on graphs.

       H.3   C AUSAL LEARNING ON GRAPHS
1311
1312   The field of causal learning investigates the intricate connections between variables (40; 38), offering
1313   profound insights that have significantly enhanced deep learning methodologies. Leveraging causal
1314   relationships, numerous techniques have made remarkable strides across diverse computer vision
1315   applications (65; 35). Additionally, recent research has delved into the realm of graphs (72; 73).
1316   For instance, (57) implements interventions on non-causal components to generate representations,
1317   facilitating the discovery of underlying graph rationales. (9) decomposes graphs into causal and
1318
       bias subgraphs, mitigating dataset biases. (29) introduces invariance into self-supervised learning,
       preserving stable semantic information. (6) ensures out-of-distribution generalization by capturing
1319
       graph invariance. (19) tackled the challenge of learning causal graphs involving latent variables,
1320
       which are derived from a mixture of observational and interventional distributions with unknown
1321   interventional objectives. To mitigate this issue, the study proposed an approach leveraging a Ψ-
1322   Markov property. (1) introduced a randomized algorithm, featuring p-colliders, for recovering
1323   the complete causal graph while minimizing intervention costs. Additionally, (3) presented an
1324   adaptable method for causality detection, which notably benefits from various types of interventional
1325   data and incorporates sophisticated neural architectures such as normalizing flows, operating under
1326   continuous constraints. However, these methods adopt a fixed GNN architecture in the optimization
1327   process, neglecting the role of architectures in causal learning on graphs. In contrast, in this paper,
1328   we focus on handling distribution shifts in the graph architecture search process from the causal
1329
       perspective by discovering the causal relationship between graphs and architectures. There are also
       works (50; 23; 66; 67) employ GNN for causality learning.
