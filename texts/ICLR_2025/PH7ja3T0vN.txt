011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Many real-world decision-making problems are combinatorial in nature, where
014            states (e.g., surrounding traffic of a self-driving car) can be seen as a combina-
015
               tion of basic elements (e.g., pedestrians, trees, and other cars). Due to combi-
               natorial complexity, observing all combinations of basic elements in the training
016
               set is infeasible, which leads to an essential yet understudied problem of zero-
017
               shot generalization to states that are unseen combinations of previously seen el-
018            ements. In this work, we first formalize this problem and then demonstrate how
019            existing value-based reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms struggle due to un-
020            reliable value predictions in unseen states. We argue that this problem cannot be
021            addressed with exploration alone, but requires more expressive and generalizable
022            models. We demonstrate that behavior cloning with a conditioned diffusion model
023            trained on expert trajectory generalizes better to states formed by new combina-
024            tions of seen elements than traditional RL methods. Through experiments in maze,
025            driving, and multiagent environments, we show that conditioned diffusion models
026
               outperform traditional RL techniques and highlight the broad applicability of our
               problem formulation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In many real-world decision-making tasks, environments can be broken down into combinations
032   of fundamental elements. For instance, in self-driving tasks, the surrounding environment consists
033
      of elements like bicycles, pedestrians, and cars. Due to the exponential growth of possible element
      combinations, it is impractical to encounter and learn from every possible configuration during train-
034
      ing. Rather than learning how to act in each unique combination, humans instead learn to interact
035
      with individual elements – such as following a car or avoiding pedestrians – and then extrapolate
036   this knowledge to unseen combinations of elements. Therefore, it is important to study the gener-
037   alization to unseen combinations of known elements, hereafter referred to as the out-of-combination
038   (OOC) generalization, and to develop algorithms that can effectively handle these unseen scenarios.
039
      Despite the success of reinforcement learning (RL) in decision-making tasks, many existing RL
040
      algorithms, particularly in offline settings, struggle to perform adeptly under state distribution shifts
041
      between training and testing, which typically occur when the learned policy visits states that differ
042   from the data collection policy at test time (Levine et al., 2020; Kakade & Langford, 2002; Lyu
043   et al., 2022; Schulman, 2015). While there have been works studying this problem, most of them
044   either (1) focus on distribution shifts where the training and testing sets share the same support
045   but different probability densities, without accounting for the presence of entirely new and unseen
046   element combinations (Finn et al., 2017; Ghosh et al., 2022), or (2) allow unseen elements in test
047   combinations, which makes the problem ill-posed without introducing other potentially unrealistic
048   assumptions (Song et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2022). As a result, these works have failed to recognize
049   and address the critical challenge of generalization to unseen combinations of seen elements and
050
      therefore fail to capture and compose existing knowledge for these fundamental elements.
051   In this work, we directly tackle the problem of state combinatorial generalization in decision-making
052   tasks, where testing states consist of unseen combinations of elements encountered during training.
053   As illustrated in Figure 1, our task differs conceptually from traditional distribution shift problems.

098

099
      instead on the use of a more expressive policy.

154   2.3   D IFFUSION M ODEL FOR D ECISION MAKING
155
      Diffusion models emerged as a popular architecture for decision-making tasks and demonstrated
156   superior performance compared to traditional RL algorithms, especially on long-horizon planning
157   tasks (Janner et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023a; Mishra et al., 2023). Some following
158   work further studied conditioned diffusion models (Chi et al., 2023; Ajay et al., 2022; Li et al.,
159   2023) and demonstrated their ability to stitch trajectories with different skills or constraints together.
160   Application in multi-task environment (He et al., 2024a; Liang et al., 2023b) and meta-learning
161   setting (Ni et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) further demonstrate their ability to capture multi-modality
      information in the offline dataset.

      3     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
163
164   In this section, we formally define the problem of state combinatorial generalization by providing
165   definitions for state combination and identify out-of-combination generalization as a problem for
166   generalization to different supports in the same sample space.
167
168   3.1   S TATES F ORMED BY E LEMENT C OMBINATIONS
169   Following Wiedemer et al. (2024), we first denote e ∈ E to be a base element for an environment.
170   A base element is defined to be the most elementary and identifiable element that is relevant to the
171   decision making task of interest. For example, in a traffic environment, the set E can be the set
172   of vehicles that can occur in the environment such as {car, bike}; and in a 2D maze environment,
173   the set E can be the set of possible locations labeled by the x, y-axis coordinate of the agent, i.e.
174   R2 . Suppose there are a finite number of n base elements in an environment. Since these elements
175   are the fundamental components relevant to the decision making task, we can form a latent vector
176   z = (z1 , z2 , ..., zn ) ∈ Z ≡ En , where zi ∈ E ∀i ∈ {1, . . . , n} that represents the combination of
177
      all rudimentary components appearing in this environment related to the decision making task.
178   Each element can also be associated with a collection of attributes r such as the color of the vehicle
179   and the velocity of the agent. Attributes are components that are necessary for rendering the states
180   and the rendering function f (z, (r1 , r2 , . . . , rn )) can then map the latent and the attributes to a state
181   s ∈ S. In the traffic environment example, f is equivalent to reconstructing the cars and the bikes
182   given their colors and positions, etc. All reconstructed base elements collectively determine a state
183
      s. Concretely, we provide the following definition:
184   Definition 3.1 (States and latent vectors). For any state s with n base elements in state space S and
185   rendering function f , we have s = f (z, (r1 , r2 , . . . , rn )) where the corresponding latent vector z
186   in latent space Z ≡ En for s is z = z1 , z2 , ..., zn where zi ∈ E for i = 1, ..., n.
187
188   With our definition of base elements and states, the combinatorial property of states naturally fol-
189
      lows as the composition of different base elements in the latent space.
190   Notice that in practice, for the same environment, one can define different base element sets depend-
191   ing on the desired granularity of the task. In addition, since we usually can only obtain observations
192   of the states, in practice we can only extract the empirical latent vector z̃ from the observation.
193

198   ascribe a probability to a sample in Z. Then we can define the corresponding probability space as

219

      4     W HY TRADITIONAL RL FAILS
231   Most RL algorithms include estimating the expected cumulative reward of choosing a specific action
232   given the current state (Schulman et al., 2017; Kumar et al., 2020; Haarnoja et al., 2018). We
233   demonstrate the estimation of value functions is problematic given unsupported states and this can
234   not be solved by more exploration or more training data in this section.

286   5.2   OOC G ENERALIZATION IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
287   We demonstrate that a well-trained diffusion model can naturally sample OOC states that satisfy
288   combinatorial support constraint 3.4 with non-zero probability at test time. The key idea is that
289   pseudo-random denoising trajectories can be constructed at inference time that yield OOC samples
290   with non-zero probability.
291
      Since our states are formed by combinations of base elements (Definition 3.1), with well constructed
292
      Z we can assume that the states lie on a lower dimensional manifold M (representing combinations
293   of base elements) embedded in the high dimensional ambient state space. In some cases such as
294   maze navigation where the latent space is a linear subspace, we can even assume that the underlying
295   manifold M is a linear manifold whose tangent space is isomorphic to itself. With these assump-
296   tions, we present the following corollary whose proof is shown in the appendix B.
297
      Corollary 5.1. Suppose the states lie along a linear manifold M in the state space S and the latent
298   space Z is well constructed so that Z is (affine) isomorphic to M. Then a diffusion model pθ that is
299   well trained on Ptrain can sample an OOC state with non-zero probability.
300
301   While the linear manifold assumption may not hold for more complex states, recent computer vi-
302   sion research provides evidence of the combinatorial generalization capabilities of diffusion models
303   in more complicated data spaces: Okawa et al. (2024) showed that given different concepts like
304   shape, color, and size in synthetic shape generation, conditional diffusion models demonstrate a
305   multiplicative emergence of combinatorial abilities where it will first learn how to generalize to con-
306   cepts closer to the training samples (i.e. only change one of color, shape, and size) and eventually
307
      adopt full compositional generalization ability with enough training. Aithal et al. (2024) identifies
      the phenomena where diffusion models generate samples out of the support of training distribution
308
      through interpolating different complex modes on a data manifold. Kadkhodaie et al. (2023) demon-
309
      strate generalization to unsupported data by showing two diffusion models trained on large enough
310   non-overlapping data converge to the same denoising function. In the next sections, we discuss how
311   to use diffusion models to handle this challenging problem and also provide empirical evidence in
312   decision-making tasks.

326   Since each state corresponds to a latent vector, we will use the latent vector, which is oftentimes

332
      cloning by fully trained PPO agents on each combination of the training environment and includes
      only successful rollouts. Since expert trajectories are used for training, we model actions together
333
      with states under the assumption that unseen states generated from new conditioning will correspond
334
      to statistically reasonable actions. The first action in the generated trajectory is then used to step the
335   environment (Appendix D.5). Detailed architecture of the model can be found in Appendix D.3 and

459   In this section, we ablate over our design choices to (1) show the necessity of using the inductive bias
460   of state latent vector as conditioning, (2) different model architectures to incorporate conditioning
461   information
462
463   8.1   N ECESSITY OF C OMBINATORIAL I NDUCTIVE B IAS
464   We compare trajectories generated by the conditioned and unconditioned diffusion models in this
465   section to demonstrate the importance of using combinatorial latent information as conditioning.
466   In Maze2D (Fu et al., 2020), we formulate the navigation problem as a one-step generation process
467   where the diffusion model learns how to generate an entire valid trajectory without rolling out current
468   action and replan. Since there is only one planning step in this process, the generated trajectory can
469   be seen as the “state” in this setting, where unseen trajectories correspond to unseen states instead
470   of time-horizon trajectory stitching. The inductive bias we use is every training trajectory will
471   pass through three waypoints that equally slice the trajectory. In this case, the set of all waypoints
472
      forms the base element set and their combination is the latent vector that determines the shape of a
      generated maze trajectory. During training, we extract three points that equally slice the trajectory
473
      and use them as conditioning. During test time, we specify a new combination of three waypoints
474
      we want the generated trajectory to pass.
475
476   We see that the unconditioned diffusion model successfully generated a trajectory if the start and end
477   positions are in the training set (Figure 7b) but failed for unseen start and end points (Figure 7c). This
478
      demonstrates that unconditioned diffusion struggles to approximate unseen distributions. However,
      if conditioning on an unseen combination of the three waypoints, the conditioned diffusion model
479
      can generate unseen trajectories that still satisfy constraints supported by the training dataset (Figure
480
      7d), demonstrating the conditioned diffusion model’s ability to generalize to out-of-combination
481   conditioning.

      9    C ONCLUSIONS
524
525
      Despite the success of traditional RL models in decision-making tasks, they still struggle to gener-
      alize to unseen state inputs. Most existing work focuses on RL generalization under the assumption
526
      that generalization to a different probability density function with the same support or allows un-
527
      seen base elements but also introduces other potentially unrealistic assumptions. However, we take
528   it further and study the problem of generalization to out-of-support states, out of combination in
529   particular, hoping the model can exploit the compositional nature of our world. We showed how this
530   task is challenging for value-based RL and also how conditioned diffusion models can generalize
531   to unsupported samples. We compare the models in different environments with detailed ablation
532   and analysis, demonstrating how each of these classic environments can be formulated as a state
533   combinatorial problem.
534   However, one limitation of our setup is we model combinatorial generalization in state space as
535   a combination of base elements, which is valid for many real-world applications where complexity
536   stems from exponentially many combinations but does not cover all cases. Oftentimes the distinction
537   between different objects can be blurry (e.g. would a motorbike be a bike). Additionally, the model
538   has difficulty with zero-shot generalization to unseen base objects. Another constraint is efficiency,
539   as planning with diffusion models in stochastic environments requires denoising a trajectory at each
      planning step, which can be computationally intensive.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
811
812
      Meta RL Meta RL is often seen as the problem of “learning to learn”, where agents are trained on
813   several environments sampled from a task distribution during meta-training and tested on environ-
814   ments sampled from the same distribution during meta-testing (Yu et al., 2020; Finn et al., 2017).
815   In the K-shot meta-RL setting, the model can interact with the testing environment K times during
816   meta-testing time to update the model using reward (Finn et al., 2017; Mitchell et al., 2021; Li et al.,
817   2020; Rakelly et al., 2019). Our setting is different from Meta-RL as the training and testing envi-
818   ronments are sampled from different distributions and conditioning is provided while restricting K
819   to zero.
820   Attached below is a review of RL environments for generalization.
821
822   A.1   G ENERALIZATION IN RL
823
824   Environments Most RL environments that test model generalization can be grouped into differ-
825   ent reward functions (Rajeswaran et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018; Rakelly et al., 2019; Finn et al.,
826   2017) or transition functions (Dennis et al., 2020; Machado et al., 2018; Packer et al., 2018; Zhang
827   et al., 2018), goals or tasks (Finn et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2020), states (Nichol et al., 2018; Cobbe
828   et al., 2019; Juliani et al., 2019; Küttler et al., 2020; Grigsby & Qi, 2020; Hansen et al., 2021;
829
      Mees et al., 2022; Cobbe et al., 2020). For environments with different state distributions, random-
      ization (Grigsby & Qi, 2020) and procedural generation (Nichol et al., 2018; Küttler et al., 2020;
830
      Cobbe et al., 2020) are widely used to generate new states. Some vision-based environments (Juliani
831
      et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2021; Mees et al., 2022) also use different rendering themes or layouts
832   to generate unseen observations, more targeting sim2real problems. For robotics benchmarks like
833   Metaworld (Yu et al., 2020) and RLbench (James et al., 2020), how much structure is shared between
834   tasks like open a door and open a drawer is ambiguous (Ahmed et al., 2020). Also, benchmarks like
835   Franka Kitchen (Gupta et al., 2019) focus on composing tasks at time horizons, requiring the model
836   to concatenate trajectories corresponding to different subtasks. However, despite the large volume of
837   generalization benchmarks, there is no benchmark designed for state combinatorial generalization
838   to our best knowledge.

      B    P ROOF OF C OROLLARY 5.1
865
866
      Corollary B.1 (Corollary 5.1). Suppose the states lie along a linear manifold M in the state space
867   S and the latent space Z is well constructed so that Z is (affine) isomorphic to M. Let s be a state
868   in the training set with corresponding latent vector z and s′ be an OOC state with corresponding
869   latent vector z ′ . Then a diffusion model pθ that is well trained on Ptrain can sample s′ with non-zero
870   probability.
871
872   Proof. We prove Corollary 5.1 by construction. Suppose M is a d-dimensional linear manifold and
873   S ⊂ Rk , then we note that both M and Z are affine isomorphic to Rd . Therefore, with necessary
874   shifting, we can have z ′ = z + y where y ∈ Z. Let v be the corresponding vector of y in M.
875   Now let’s perform DDIM inversion on a training sample s to obtain the SDE trajectory  P {st }. Let γt
876   be the angle between ϵθ (st , t) and M, there exist a set of vectors vt such that v = t sin(γt )σt vt
877   and vt perpendicular to ϵθ (st , t). Then by construction and with necessary shifting, the trajectory
878   s′t = st + vt is a valid diffusion denoising trajectory (with vt acting as the “random” vector ϵt
879   sampled at each time step). This trajectory will yield s′ as the final state with non-zero probability
880   because each intermediate Gaussian distribution pθ (st−1 |st ) = N (st−1 ; µθ (st , t), Σθ (st , t)) is
881   defined on the entire ambient space. By Stanczuk et al. (2022) we know that γt → π/2 as t → 0,
882   therefore v can be a non-zero vector. Hence, there exists a v ∈ M such that the sampling probability
883
      of s′ = s + v from diffusion model pθ is non-zero.
884
885
      While we have proven non-zero probability above, one can easily spot that, the probability can
      become extremely close to zero if the OOC sample is very far away from all training examples due to
886
      the intermediate Gaussian distributions. This corresponds to generalizing to the out-of-distribution
887
      samples with unseen base elements (the gray area in Figure 1). One can mitigate this problem
888   by increasing the coverage of the support of the training space, which is also a common method
889   in traditional RL to mitigate the problem of generalization to unseen base elements. Applying
890   other post-training sampling techniques like repainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022) can also allow extra
891   Langevin steps when t is large.
