009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-shot generative model adaptation (ZSGM) aims to adapt a pre-trained gen-
012            erator to a target domain using only text guidance and without any samples from
013            the target domain. Central to recent ZSGM approaches are directional loss which
014            use the text guidance in the form of aligning the image offset with text offset in
015            the embedding space of a vision-language model like CLIP. This is similar to the
016            analogical reasoning in NLP where the offset between one pair of words is used to
017            identify a missing element in another pair by aligning the offset between these two
018
               pairs. However, a major limitation of existing ZSGM methods is that the learning
               objective assumes the complete alignment between image offset and text offset in
019
               the CLIP embedding space. Our work makes two main contribution. Inspired by
020
               the offset misalignment studies in NLP, as our first contribution, we perform an
021            empirical study to analyze the misalignment between text offset and image off-
022            set in CLIP embedding space for various large publicly available datasets. Our
023            important finding is that offset misalignment in CLIP embedding space is corre-
024            lated with concept distance, i.e., close concepts have a less offset misalignment.
025            To address the limitations of the current approaches, as our second contribution,
026            we propose Adaptation with Iterative Refinement (AIR) which mitigates the offset
027            misalignment issue in directional loss by iteratively selecting anchor points closer
028            to the target domain. Extensive experimental results show that the proposed AIR
029
               approach achieves SOTA performance across various adaptation setups. Code
               and additional experiments in Supp.

      2      P RELIMINARIES : D IRECTIONAL CLIP L OSS
132
133
      In zero-shot generative model adaptation setup Gal et al. (2022), given a pre-trained generator GS
134   on the source domain S, and textual descriptions of source and target domains, denoted by TS and
135   TT respectively, the goal is to shift GS to target domain T to generate diverse and high-quality
136   images from this domain Abdollahzadeh et al. (2023). For this adaptation, current approaches Gal
137   et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2023); Jeon et al. (2023) use the CLIP model Radford et al. (2021) as the
138   source of supervision, and assume that text and image offsets (between S and T ) are well-aligned
139   in CLIP representation space. Therefore, the text offset is computed based on the provided textual
140   descriptions of the source and target. Then, the trainable generator is initialized with the parameters
141   of the GS , and optimized in a way to align image offset with text offset, leading to the directional
142
      CLIP loss:
143                                   Ldirection = 1 − cos(∆IS→t , ∆TS→T ),
144                                where ∆IS→t = EI (Gt (w)) − EI (GS (w)),                               (1)
145                                 and ∆TS→T = ET (TT ) − ET (TS )
146
      where cos(x, y) = x · y/|x||y| represents the cosine similarity. ET and EI denote the CLIP text
147
      and image encoders, respectively. Gt denotes the trainable generator in iteration t of adaptation.
148
      ∆IS→t denotes the image offset computed from the source generator to the trainable generator, and
149   ∆TS→T denotes the text offset from source to target.

152   3      A C LOSER L OOK AT O FFSET M ISALIGNMENT IN CLIP S PACE
153
154
      Previous works assume that for two different concepts α and β, the image offset ∆Iα→β and text
      offset ∆Tα→β are completely aligned in the multimodal CLIP embedding space. This assumption of
155
      perfect alignment is the foundation of the directional loss in Eq. 1. We postulate that this assumption
156
      may have two major limitations:
157
158       • CLIP Radford et al. (2021) is trained using a contrastive loss to maximize the cosine similarity
159         between corresponding image-text pairs, i.e., maximize cos(EI (Iα ), ET (Tα )) for concept α
160         (e.g., cat), or maximize cos(EI (Iβ ), ET (Tβ )) for concept β (e.g., dog). Note that the degree of
161         alignment of image offset ∆Iα→β and text offset ∆Tα→β in CLIP space is not studied in the
            literature.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
                      • In addition, this degree of alignment between ∆Iα→β and ∆Tα→β may vary based on the dis-
163                     tance between two concepts α and β.
164
165   In this section, we take a closer look at this degree of offset alignment between two different modal-
166   ities in CLIP space. First, inspired by offset misalignment in NLP, we conduct an empirical study
167   on large public datasets to analyze the offset misalignment between image and text modalities in
168   CLIP embedding space. Our analysis suggests that there is a misalignment between ∆Iα→β and
169   ∆Tα→β in CLIP embedding space, and this misalignment increases as concepts α and β be-
170   come more distant. Second, we take a further step and design an experiment to evaluate the effect
171
      of this offset misalignment in generative model adaptation using directional loss (Eq. 1). Our exper-
      imental results suggest that less offset misalignment in CLIP embedding space leads to a better
172
      generative model adaptation with directional loss.

196
197
      In this section, we conduct an empirical experiment on public datasets to evaluate the degree of
198   alignment between image and text offsets. For each dataset, we randomly sample two classes as a
199   pair of concept (α, β). Then, the images within each class are used alongside the related textual
200   description (e.g., label) of each class to measure offset misalignment M(α, β) in a similar approach
201   to directional loss:
202
                                                                                   M(α, β) = 1 − cos(∆Iα→β , ∆Tα→β ),
203
204                                                          where ∆Iα→β = EI (Iβ ) − EI (Iα ),                                                                                         (2)
205                                                           and ∆Tα→β = ET (Tβ ) − ET (Tα )
206
207   where EI (Iα ) is the average embedding of all images of the class (concept) α in CLIP space. In
208   addition, to measure the distance between two concepts denoted by D(α, β), we use the cosine
209   similarity between images of two classes, i.e., D(α, β) = 1 − cos(EI (Iβ ), EI (Iα )). We repeat
210   this process to have N = 5000 pairs of concepts for each dataset. Then, we plot M(α, β) against
211   D(α, β) for each pair of concepts.
212   Experimental Setup. In this experiment, we use CLIP ViT-Base/32 as the vision encoder. We
213   use 6 large and multi-class datasets that are publicly available, including ImageNet Deng et al.
214   (2009), Caltech-101 Fei-Fei et al. (2007), OpenImages Kuznetsova et al. (2020), MS COCO Lin
215   et al. (2014), Visual Genome Krishna et al. (2017), and CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) (details
      in Sec. A.1).

      Results. Fig.2 shows the offset misalignment against the concept distance for N = 5000 pairs of
217   concepts for 6 public datasets. As shown in the plots, for all datasets, apart from their different

228   3.2   I MPACT OF O FFSET M ISALIGNMENT ON G ENERATIVE M ODEL A DAPTATION
229
230   In the previous section, we performed an empirical study that revealed the offset misalignment for
231   natural data. In this section, we take a step further and investigate the effect of this misalignment
232   on the generative model adaptation from a source domain (concept) S to a target domain (concept)
233
      T . Specifically, following zero-shot generative domain adaptation setup Gal et al. (2022), for source
      domain S, we assume a pre-trained generator GS and a text description TS is available. However,
234
      for the target domain, only text description TT is available. To simulate different degrees of mis-
235
      alignment between source and target, we augment target text to get a set of text descriptions {TTi }.
236   Then, we perform zero-shot adaptation using the directional loss (Eq. 1) from the source domain S
237   to each of these target text TTi and measure the generation performance of the adapted generator.
238
239
      Experimental Setup. For this experiment, we perform adaptation on Human → Baby and Dog
      → Cat. We use StyleGAN2-ADA Karras et al. (2020a) pre-trained on FFHQ Karras et al. (2019)
240
      and AFHQ-Dog Choi et al. (2020) as the pre-trained model. We fix the source text TS and augment
241
      the target text TT by sampling handcrafted prompts from the CLIP ImageNet template (INt)1 which
242   leads to increasing the misalignment (see Supp. Sec. A.2 for details). Then, we follow exactly the
243   same hyperparameters as NADA (see Supp. Sec. A.3) to adapt the source generator to different
244   target text Ti . We use FID to measure the performance of the adapted generator against offset
245   misalignment.
246
      Our results in Fig. 3 demonstrates that in general, increasing the offset misalignment degrades
247
      the performance of the zero-shot generative adaption with directional loss. Motivated by this
248   finding, in the next section, we propose an approach to iteratively refine the adaptation direction.

294     4     M ETHODOLOGY: A DAPTATION WITH I TERATIVE R EFINEMENT
295
296     Our analysis in Sec. 3 suggests that for the closer concepts, there is less offset misalignment in CLIP
297     space, resulting in a more accurate directional loss (Eq. 1) for adaptation. Here, we leverage this
298
        property to enhance the zero-shot generative model adaptation with directional loss.
299     Specifically, even though the concept distance between source S and target T is fixed, our intuition
300     is: ‘after limited iterations of adaptation using directional loss, the encoded concept in the adapted
301     generator is already closer to the target domain than the encoded concept in source generator’.
302     For example, when adapting a generator pre-trained on Photo to the target domain Painting, after
303     limited iterations, the adapted generator already encodes some knowledge related to the “Painting”
304
        domain, while this knowledge does not exist in the pre-trained generator.
305     Following this intuition, we use the adapted generator as the new anchor (denoted by GA ), and
306     compute the directional loss from this anchor point to the target. We update this anchor point iter-
307     atively during adaptation, as we move closer to the target domain. Because of the smaller concept
308     distance, we believe the directional loss computed based on GA can provide better guidance and
309     rectify the adaptation direction solely computed based on GS . The major challenge of using GA
310
        within directional loss is that related text prompt PA that describes this concept is unknown. In what
        follows, first, we discuss the details of the proposed Adaptation with Iterative Refinement (AIR)
311
        in Sec. 4.1. Then, to address the challenge of unknown PA within the directional loss of AIR, we
312
        discuss the proposed prompt learning approach in Sec. 4.2.
313

316

317

318     main. Then, in each tint interval of adaptation, we sample the adapted generator as the new anchor

322     and the trainable generator Gt : ∆IAi →t = EI (Gt (w)) − EI (GAi (w)). Assuming that the anchor
323     point is described by the prompt PAi in the text domain (details of acquiring PAi will be discussed in
        Sec. 4.2), the text offset with anchor point is calculated as follows: ∆TAi →T = ET (TT )−ET (PAi ).

343    4.2   A LIGNING P ROMPT TO I MAGES
344
       Here, we explain the details of the proposed method for learning the text prompt PAi that describes
345
       the ith anchor point Ai in text domain. Inspired by Zhou et al. (2022b), we define the prompt
346
       PAi ∈ R(M +1)×d as combination of M learnable tokens [V ]ij ∈ Rd , and a label token YAi ∈ Rd :
347
348                                      PAi = [V ]i1 [V ]i2 . . . [V ]iM [YAi ]                         (4)
349
       Early approaches of prompt learning directly learn the learnable tokens [V ]ij from related images
350
       Zhou et al. (2022b;a). However, recently, ITI-G EN Zhang et al. (2023) shows that learning from the
351    offsets is more efficient for capturing the specific attribute of interest.
352
353    Remark: Similar to NADA, ITI-G EN also uses the mechanism of the aligning offsets for learning
354
       target prompts which could be susceptible to offset misalignment. However, there are two major
       differences between them: i) ITI-G EN aligns ∆P to the target ∆I which could provide a better
355
       supervisory signal, ii) the number of trainable parameters in ITI-G EN is significantly lower (2K in
356
       ITI-G EN compared to 2.7 millions in NADA). In what follows, we propose two design choices to
357    make learning ∆P less susceptible to the offset misalignment issue.
358
359    Design Choices. To mitigate the possible issue of offset misalignment, we propose to decrease the
360
       distance between concept pairs and use a regulirzer token during prompt learning. More specifically:
361
         • Given that offset misalignment is less for closer concepts, we use the previous anchor point
362        Ai−1 as the source to learn the prompt for the ith anchor Ai . Since consecutive anchor points
363        are close together, the directional loss is more accurate.
364
         • We use the interpolation between tokenized source and target descriptions as anchor label, i.e.,
365
           YAi = (1 − pi )YS + pi YT , with pi denoting the proportion of the training progress until anchor
366        point Ai . The label token acts like a regularizer during prompt learning.
367
368    We empirically find that using these two design choices results in better adaptation with our AIR
369    mechanism compared to learning the prompts directly from generated images by GAi .
370    Therefore, the image offset is calculated between the current and previous anchors: ∆IAi−1 →Ai =
371    EI (GAi (w)) − EI (GAi−1 (w)). Similarly, the text prompt offset is calculated as follows:
372    ∆PAi−1 →Ai = ET (PAi ) − ET (PAi−1 ). Note that the only trainable parameter is the unknown

408
      Generative Models. In this work, we implement zero-shot generative model adaptation for both
409   GANs and diffusion models. Note that we perform the first zero-shot adaptation on the diffusion
410   model. The implementation details for each type of model is as follows:
411
412       • Zero-Shot Adaptation of GANs. In this setup, we follow the previous works Gal et al. (2022);
413         Guo et al. (2023); Jeon et al. (2023) settings to adapt StyleGAN2-ADA Karras et al. (2020a)
414         pre-trained on FFHQ Karras et al. (2019) and AFHQ-Dog Choi et al. (2020) to various target
415
            domains.
416       • Zero-Shot Adaptation of Diffusion Models. In this setup, we use Guided Diffusion Dhariwal
417         & Nichol (2021) pre-trained on FFHQ and AFHQ-Dog from P2-Weighting Choi et al. (2022)
418         as our source generator. To speedup training, we use DPM-Solver Lu et al. (2022) to generate
419
            images in 10 steps. To prevent overfitting, instead of fully fine-tuning the pre-trained model, we
            fine-tune it with LoRA Hu et al. (2022).
420
421   During the adaptation of both generators, we utilize the pre-trained ViT-Base/32 Dosovitskiy et al.
422   (2021) as the vision encoder for CLIP Radford et al. (2021). The details of the hyperparameters
423   used during the adaptation can be found in Supp. Sec. A.4.
424
      Evaluation Metrics. A well-trained image generator is defined by its ability to produce high-quality
425
      and diverse images from target distribution. Following the zero-shot works in literature, in this work
426   we conduct both visual inspections for qualitative evaluations and quantitative evaluations using the

430         follow previous work Jeon et al. (2023) to use these datasets as target distribution. Then, we
431         generate 5000 samples for each target domain Zhao et al. (2022a; 2023), and use FID to evaluate
            the generated images’ quality and diversity.

531   6    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      Previous methods in ZSGM assume that image offset and text offset are perfectly aligned in CLIP
534   embedding space. In this paper, inspired by the studies in analogical reasoning of NLP, we conduct
535   an empirical study to analyze the misalignment between image offset and text offset in CLIP space.
536   Our analysis reveals that there is offset misalignment in CLIP space which correlated with concept
537   distances. Building on this insight, we propose AIR, a new approach that iteratively samples anchor
538   points closer to the target and mitigates offset misalignment issues. Extentsive experimental results
539   on both GAN and diffusion models shows that the proposed AIR achieves SOTA performance across
      various setups.

1052   text offset as adaptation direction and optimizes the trainable generator to align image offset with
1053

1054

1055

1056   only mean of embeddings in NADA to prevent mode collapse.

1058   However, the adaptation direction in previous work only focuses on the source and target domains
1059

       image and text offsets in the CLIP space are well aligned. In this paper, we draw inspiration from a
1060
       similar problem called analogical reasoning in NLP, and empirically discover that the alignment of
1061
       image and text offset in CLIP space is correlated to the concept proximity in CLIP space. Based on
1062   this finding, we proposed a method that iteratively updates the adaptation direction, which is more
1063   aligned with the image offset and more accurate for zero-shot adaptation with directional loss.

1065
       Analogical Reasoning Research in NLP has shown that word representations of language models
       are surprisingly good at capturing semantic regularities in language Collobert & Weston (2008);
1066
       Turian et al. (2010). Specifically, analogical reasoning Mikolov et al. (2013c;a;b); Levy & Gold-
1067
       berg (2014), utilizing the semantic regularities of word representations, aims to solve analogy tasks
1068   by using one pair of word vectors to identify the unknown member of a different pair of words,
1069   commonly via alignment of offsets, This is commonly modeled as using the vector offset between
1070   two words a′ − a, and applying it to a new word b to predict the missing word b′ that pair with b,
1071   as illustrated by the famous example of using v(“Man”) - v(“Woman”) and v(“King”) to identify
1072   v("Queen"), where v(·) denotes word representation. This approach attracted a lot of attention for
1073   the vital role that analogical reasoning plays in human cognition for discovering new knowledge
1074   and understanding new concepts. It is already used in many downstream NLP tasks, such as split-
1075   ting compounds Daiber et al. (2015), semantic search Cohen et al. (2015), cross-language relational
1076
       search Duc et al. (2015), etc.
1077   Importantly, previous works Levy et al. (2015); Köper et al. (2015); Vylomova et al. (2015) demon-
1078   strate that the effectiveness of analogical reasoning varies across different categories and semantic
1079   relations. More recent studies Rogers et al. (2017); Fournier et al. (2020), present a series of experi-
       ments performed with BATS dataset Gladkova et al. (2016) on various pre-trained vector space, e.g.,
