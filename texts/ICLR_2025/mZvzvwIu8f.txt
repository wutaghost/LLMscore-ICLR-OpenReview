009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Class incremental learning (CIL) aims to enable models to continuously learn
012            new classes without catastrophically forgetting old ones. A promising direction is
013            to learn and use prototypes of classes during incremental updates. Despite sim-
014            plicity and intuition, we find that such methods suffer from inadequate represen-
015            tation capability and unsatisfied confusion caused by distribution drift. In this
016            paper, we develop a Confusion-REduced AuTo-Encoder classifier (CREATE) for
017            CIL. Specifically, our method employs a lightweight auto-encoder module to learn
018            each compact class manifold in latent subspace, constraining samples well recon-
019
               structed only on the semantically correct auto-encoder. Thus, the representation
               stability and capability of class distributions are enhanced, alleviating the poten-
020
               tial class-wise confusion problem. To further distinguish the drifted features, we
021
               propose a confusion-aware latent space separation loss that ensures exemplars are
022            closely distributed in their corresponding low-dimensional manifold while keep-
023            ing away from the distributions of drifted features from other classes. Our method
024            demonstrates stronger representational capacity by learning disentangled mani-
025            folds and reduces class confusion caused by drift. Extensive experiments on mul-
026            tiple datasets and settings show that CREATE outperforms other state-of-the-art
027            methods up to 5.41%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Class Incremental Learning (CIL) aims to enable deep learning models to continuously learn new
033   classes while maintaining old knowledge. It has crucial implications in intelligent systems that re-
034   quire continuous evolution. For example, in an autonomous driving scenario, the system should
035   gradually adapt to new environments, infrastructures, and traffic patterns in different countries with-
036   out forgetting previous driving capabilities. A fundamental challenge in CIL is to tackle catastrophic
037   forgetting (French, 1999; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2024), where the performance of pre-
038   viously learned knowledge significantly deteriorates when the model adapts to new class instances.
039
      Existing studies are dedicated to mitigating this problem, and they primarily address the issue from
      three perspectives. Knowledge retention-based methods reduce the forgetting of old knowledge by
040
      preventing changes in intrinsic knowledge. Model expansion-based methods enhance adaptation to
041
      new tasks by leveraging adjustments in model parameters. Prototype-based classification methods
042   reduce forgetting by focusing on changes in the embedding.
043
044   Specifically, knowledge retention-based approaches (Douillard et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022; Gao
045
      et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) aim to discover and maintain inherent knowledge structures through
      regularization, thereby reducing changes in the model’s intrinsic knowledge structure and minimiz-
046
      ing the forgetting of old knowledge. Such methods impose significant constraints on the model’s old
047
      knowledge, resulting in difficulties when introducing new knowledge. Therefore, model expansion-
048   based approaches (Yan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; 2023) are designed to dynamically adjust the
049   representational capacity of a model to fit continuously evolving data. However, this type of ap-
050   proach typically involves a large number of parameters, and there is redundancy between the newly
051   expanded and old branches. Prototype-based methods (Rebuffi et al., 2017; McDonnell et al., 2024;
052   Zhou et al., 2024) construct and update prototypes, transforming the inference into a matching be-
053   tween features and prototypes. Such approaches are straightforward, intuitive, and require only a
      small number of parameters, recently showing promising prospects. However, in a class incremental

      We analyze prototype-based methods and identify two key factors that limit their performance.
      Firstly, real data often resides on a manifold structure in latent spaces. Observe from Fig. 1(a),
076
      Class 7 forms two distinct clusters in the latent feature space, while the prototype primarily lies
077
      in one of the clusters. This shows a single discriminative vector has limited representation capa-
078   bility and fails to fit the manifold distribution, leading to class-wise confusion issues. Secondly,
079   since incremental learning cannot leverage the entire dataset, old classes often suffer from a drift
080   after learning a new task. Fig. 1(b) shows that the positions of old classes shift severely, exhibiting
081   significant changes in class manifolds and dispersion of features that lead to overlapping class dis-
082   tributions. We also calculate the Pearson correlation coefficients (PCC) (McDonnell et al. (2024))
083   of prototypes to verify the confusion between classes. Fig.1(d) shows that different prototypes have
084   high linear correlations.
085   To address the aforementioned issues, this paper proposes a confusion-reduced auto-encoder classi-
086   fier (CREATE) method as a solution. Considering auto-encoders serve as manifold learners, learn-
087   ing a manifold structure for each class can enhance the stability of representations while effectively
088   capturing the essential characteristics of the categories (Bengio et al., 2013; Li et al., 2020; Zheng
089   et al., 2022), we utilize auto-encoder reconstructions to learn class distributions. Specifically, the
090   auto-encoder module is applied for each class to capture low-dimensional essential structures and
091   implicitly encode the feature distribution into it, thus tackling the problem of insufficient represen-
092
      tational capacity. Due to the overlap of the shifted representations caused by dynamically changing
      distributions, confusion still persists in the reconstructed representations. We further designed a
093
      confusion-aware separation loss that separates features of different classes in the class-specific la-
094
      tent space to mitigate the class-wise confusion.
095
096   The proposed method has the following advantages: (1) The proposed auto-encoder reconstruction
097   modules are representation condensed and lightweight. It can effectively fit the continuously chang-
098
      ing manifolds of data and is easily applied to existing methods. (2) It can effectively discriminate
      samples that suffer from distribution shifts in the feature space, thereby reducing class confusion
099
      and forgetting. Fig. 1(c) exhibits the manifold distributions in latent space, demonstrating that our
100
      method learns disentangled manifolds and reduces the class confusion caused by drift. The main
101   contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
102
103   • We identify the issue of class-wise confusion in incremental learning, and propose a confusion-
104
      reduced auto-encoder classifier, which uses a lightweight auto-encoder for each class to learn a
      compact manifold. This paradigm can exhibit a more expressive capability and effectively adapt to
105
      feature drift at the reconstruction level.
106
107   • To further reduce the confusion of drifted features, we employ a confusion-aware separation loss at
      the class subspace level by disentangling samples from other classes’ distributions in the subspace.

115   2.1   C LASS INCREMENTAL LEARNING
116
      Class incremental learning generally assumes that only a small number of samples can be stored for
117
      old classes, and task-id is not available in the inference phase. Existing methods can be divided into
118   three main categories.
119
120   The knowledge retention-based methods aim to maintain the structure of old knowledge within the
121
      model and reduce knowledge variations to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. MGRB (Chen et al.,
      2022) constructs knowledge structure for existing classes and is utilized for regularization when
122
      learning new classes. EDG (Gao et al., 2023) maintains the global and local geometric structures of
123
      data in the mixed curvature space. DSGD (Fan et al., 2024) proposes a dynamic graph construction
124   and preserves the invariance of the subgraph structure, which maintains instance associations during
125   the CIL process.
126
127
      The model expansion-based methods dynamically adjust model architecture to adapt new classes.
      For example, DER (Yan et al., 2021) expands a new backbone for each new task. The enhanced
128
      features from multiple backbone networks are concatenated for classification. FOSTER (Wang et al.,
129
      2022) adds an extra backbone for discovering complementary features and eliminates redundant
130   parameters by distillation. Memo (Zhou et al., 2023b) expands specialized blocks for new tasks to
131   obtain diverse feature representations.
132
133
      The prototype-based methods establish a prototype representation for each task and update the pro-
      totypes in subsequent phases, classifying samples into the category of the most similar prototype in
134
      the inference phase. Some prototype-based methods use non-parametric class means as their proto-
135
      types. For example, iCaRL (Rebuffi et al., 2017) suggests the nearest class mean (NCM) classifier
136   determines the predicted label based on the distance from the sample features to the class center.
137   SDC (Yu et al., 2020) employs a metric loss-based embedding network and applies semantic drift
138   compensation to adjust the prototypes closer to their correct positions. In recent years, paramet-
139   ric class prototypes have gained widespread use and achieved impressive performance. PODNet
140   (Douillard et al., 2020) learns multiple proxy vectors and predicts based on the local similarity clas-
141   sifier. RandPAC (McDonnell et al., 2024) proposes projecting features to an expanded dimensional
142   where enhanced linear separability of prototypes. SEED (Rypeść et al., 2024) employs one Gaussian
143   distribution for each class and performs an ensemble of Bayes classifiers.
144
145   2.2   R EPRESENTATION OF PROTOTYPES AND DISTRIBUTIONS
146
147
      Many methods use prototypes to represent a class for classification. For example, Snell et al. (2017)
      formulates prototypical networks for few-shot classification. It produces a distribution for query
148
      points using a softmax over distances to the prototypes in the embedding space. Huang et al. (2022)
149
      suggests representing a class with a prototype and multiple sub-prototypes, allowing the model to
150   better capture the diversity within the same class. Zhou & Wang (2024) proposes utilizing the
151   centers of sub-clusters as a set of prototypes that comprehensively represent the characteristic prop-
152   erties. Apart from prototypes, recent methods utilize distributions for representing a category. SEED
153   (Rypeść et al., 2024) uses multivariate Gaussian distributions to represent each class and employs
154   Bayesian classification from all experts. This method allows for more flexible and comprehensive
155   class representations. Considering the complex underlying structure of data distributions. Lin et al.
156   (2024) models each class with a mixture of von Mises-Fisher distributions by multiple prototypes.
157   Auto-encoder structure is a type of manifold learner that can embed high-dimensional data into a
158   low-dimensional manifold through nonlinear mapping. It is widely used for various tasks, such as
159   anomaly detection, novel class detection, and few-shot learning. Kodirov et al. (2017) introduce a
160   semantic auto-encoder that maps visual features to a low-dimensional semantic space, where incor-
161   porating class distribution information. Kim et al. (2019) utilizes a variational auto-encoder to learn
      a latent space with strong generalization capabilities for unseen classes through data-prototype im-

190   3     M ETHODS
191
192   In this section, we give a description of our confusion-reduced auto-encoder classifier in CIL 3.1.
193
      The core idea of our method is to construct a lightweight learnable auto-encoder (AE) module for
      each class. Preserving these trained class-wise AEs can alleviate catastrophic forgetting since they
194
      represent accurate and complete class distributions, as detailed in Section 3.2. To further mitigate
195
      the accumulated class confusion of AEs arising from distribution drift in CIL, we also propose a
196   confusion-aware latent space separation loss in Section 3.3. Our framework is shown in Fig. 2.
197
198
      3.1   P ROBLEM SETUP
199
200   In CIL, we usually assume knowledge is not learned at once but from a sequence of T tasks (phases).
201   Dt = {(xti , yit )}nj=1 represents n samples from the task t. Ct is the number of classes seen by phase
202   t, and ni represents the number of samples in class i. In rehearsal-based methods,    S Mt represents
203   the memory buffer in the t-th task. Therefore the training dataset in task t is Dt Mt . Note that
204   the sets of new classes learned in different incremental tasks are mutually exclusive. The model in
205
      phase t can be decomposed into feature extractor ϕt and classification module θt .
206
207   3.2   L EARNING DISENTANGLED MANIFOLDS BY AUTO - ENCODER CLASSIFIER
208
      To effectively depict class distributions and also avoid excessive computation, we consider con-
209
      structing an auto-encoder module for each category so that the original features can be mapped
210   into the corresponding subspace. The learned subspaces compress the patterns of samples into a
211   compact and continuous low-dimensional manifold, allowing the auto-encoder to better model the
212   distribution of the class. To adapt to new tasks and remember old classes, the previous auto-encoders
213   are retained and kept trainable, and class-specific auto-encoders for new classes are appended. We
214   hope the class-specific auto-encoders can identify samples of their own categories within the dataset.
215   Therefore, we consider using reconstruction error to measure the degree of consistency between the
      samples and the auto-encoder subspace.

478   5   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose an auto-encoder classifier to reduce class-wise confusion in incremental
481   learning. It employs a lightweight auto-encoder module and learns disentangled manifolds for each
482   class to represent their distribution. Moreover, it constrains latent spaces by a confusion-aware
483   separation loss that enhances class separability. This approach addresses the problem of insufficient
484   representational capacity and severe class confusion in the dynamic distribution-changing situation
485   of prototype-based CIL methods. Experimental results show that our method achieves state-of-the-
      art performance in various scenarios.
