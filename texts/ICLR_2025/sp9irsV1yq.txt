009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Mechanistic interpretability aims to provide human-understandable insights into
012                the inner workings of neural network models by examining their internals. Existing
013                approaches typically require significant manual effort and prior knowledge, with
014                strategies tailored to specific tasks. In this work, we take a step toward automat-
015                ing the understanding of the network by investigating the existence of distinct
016                sub-networks. Specifically, we explore a novel automated and task-agnostic ap-
017
                   proach based on the notion of functionally similar representations within neural
                   networks, reducing the need for human intervention. Our method identifies similar
018
                   and dissimilar layers in the network, revealing potential sub-components. We
019
                   achieve this by proposing, for the first time to our knowledge, the use of Gromov-
020                Wasserstein distance, which overcomes challenges posed by varying distributions
021                and dimensionalities across intermediate representations—issues that complicate
022                direct layer-to-layer comparisons. Through experiments on algebraic, language,
023                and vision tasks, we observe the emergence of sub-groups within neural network
024                layers corresponding to functional abstractions. Additionally, we find that different
025                training strategies influence the positioning of these sub-groups. Our approach of-
026                fers meaningful insights into the behavior of neural networks with minimal human
027                and computational cost.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      Rapid progress in transformer language models has directed attention towards understanding the
032
      underlying causes of new capabilities. Like many other neural methods, large language models
033
      (LLMs) are mostly black-box models and explainable artificial intelligence aims to offer insights and
034   improve human understanding of these LLMs. Recently, mechanistic interpretability research has
035   gained popularity, focusing on reverse-engineering models into human-understandable algorithms,
036   using methods such as computational graphs and circuits (Nanda et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2024).
037   Examples include recovering internal mechanisms of LLMs to solve typical mathematical problems
038   such as modular sum (Zhong et al., 2024).
039
      Current approaches primarily rely on extensive manual inspection and trial-and-error to reverse
040   engineer networks by discovering a sequence of learned functions that produce a desired output. This
041   process requires significant human prior knowledge with strategies tailored to specific tasks such
042   as algebraic problems (Charton, 2023). Automatic discovery of these functions is a difficult task
043   due to the large search space involved. We investigate a simpler task that can be considered as an
044   initial step towards automating the discovery of these functions: is it possible to detect how many
045   distinct (complex) functions exist in a learned network, and which layers correspond to each such
046   function? Understanding neural networks through the identification of subnetworks is essential due
047   to the complexity and opacity of modern deep learning models. Neural networks, especially those
048
      with many layers and parameters, often exhibit behaviors that are difficult to interpret holistically.
      Identifying subnetworks allows us to break down the model into smaller, more interpretable units,
049
      providing insights into how individual components contribute to the model’s overall performance.
050
      In this paper, we take such a step towards an automatic and task-agnostic approach to identify
051   sub-components in neural networks that are functionally different from each other. Specifically, we
052   treat the intermediate layers of neural networks as computing different functions of the input and base

096   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
097
098   With its popularity, mechanistic interpretability has become a disparate area, with many different
099   applications in vision (Palit et al., 2023), and language (Ortu et al., 2024; Hernandez et al., 2023; Yu
100   et al., 2024), and we survey various directions within it.
101
      Algorithm discovery in algebraic problems Modular sum algorithms (Nanda et al., 2023) are
102   studied in the context of progress measure in emerging abilities of transformers. The authors in
103   (Zhong et al., 2024) show, via various inspection such as gradient symmetry, logit patterns, and input
104   pair relations, that one class of algorithm is preferred than another. Other math problems such as the
105   greatest common divisor (Charton, 2023) also show insights into the inner mechanisms of neural
106   networks. However, most of these works require extensive human effort to reverse engineering these
107   algorithms. We investigate whether we can automate the discovery process by studying a simpler
      problem of sub-function detection.

108
      Subnetwork Discovery Automated circuit discovery (Conmy et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024) intends
109   to find a computational graph that is far sparser without sacrificing performance. Heuristic Core
110   (Bhaskar et al., 2024) hypothesizes that there exists a set of attention heads that encompasses all
111   subnetworks, learning shallow and non-generalizable features. Moreover, various work have explored
112   neuron semantics and possible disentanglement of semantics (Bricken et al., 2023; Dreyer et al.,
113   2024; Huang et al., 2024a), as well as concept representations (Park et al., 2024). Instead of studying
114   circuits and neuron, we investigate differences among neural network layers as a whole, based on an
115   existing line of work (Nanda et al., 2023). Block structures within neural network have been observed
116   in previous studies (Nguyen et al., 2022).
117   Methods of Studying Mechanisms Weight inspection and manipulation are commonly employed
118   techniques to gain insights into the inner work of networks, including studying periodicity (Nanda
119   et al., 2023) and weight gradients (Zhong et al., 2024). Modifying or ablating the activation of a
120   specific model components (Huang et al., 2024b; Kramár et al., 2024), including attention knockout
121   (Wang et al., 2022), and even direct modification of attention matrix (Ortu et al., 2024; Geva et al.,
122   2023) are prevalent. Another popular approach involves representation and output inspection (Meng
123
      et al., 2022), including logit patterns (Zhong et al., 2024), residual stream (Ortu et al., 2024), and
      periodicity (Nanda et al., 2023). Causal mediation analysis is used to compute the indirect treatment
124
      effect (Meng et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024) with perturbed embedding. Rather than just inspection of
125
      outputs, many works have proposed to map the output to some target and is a popular technique for
126   analyzing how neural activations correlate with high-level concepts (Huang et al., 2024a). Linear
127   probes are generally used. (Hou et al., 2023) uses a nonparametric probe (k−nearest-neighbor) to
128   classify outputs for reasoning tasks. We focus on using similarity measure between layers here.
129
      Similarity Measure between Neural Network Layers There are studies that quantify the similarity
130
      between different groups of neurons (Klabunde et al., 2023), typically layers (Ding et al., 2021),
131
      to understand and compare different neural networks. Cross product between each layer output
132   and final output (Yu et al., 2024) is used to approximate each layer’s contribution to the final
133   prediction. Generally a normalized representation is used to compare different transformer blocks,
134   with different desired invariance properties, such as invariance to invertible linear transformation in
135   canonical correlation analysis (Morcos et al., 2018), orthogonal transformation, isotropic scaling, and
136   different initializations in centered kernel alignment (Kornblith et al., 2019). Other measures include
137   representational similarity analysis (Mehrer et al., 2020), which studies all pairwise distances across
138   different inputs. Wasserstein distance has been explored in measuring similarities in the context of
139   neural networks (Dwivedi & Roig, 2019; Cao et al., 2022; Lohit & Jones, 2022), but they assume that
140
      different layer representations belong to the same metric space, which is very unlikely even if they
      have the same dimensionality as the semantics captured by each layer are likely to differ significantly.
141
      Several similarity measures (Tsitsulin et al., 2019; Demetci et al., 2023a) are related to GW distance.
142
      While GW distance has been used for model merging as a regularization (Singh & Jaggi, 2020; Stoica
143   et al., 2023), it has not been fully explored in the area of mechanistic interpretability, particularly for
144   the subnetworks identification.

147   3     F UNCTIONAL S IMILARITY W ITHIN N EURAL N ETWORKS
148
149   We aim to identify sub-components of a neural network based on their mechanistic functions. When
150   we have a hypothesized target function that the sub-component may compute, this can be formulated
151   as a similarity search problem. In this context, we search for candidate representations within neural
152   networks’ outputs that represents changes in function computation. The search problem consists of
153   three key elements: the search space, the search target, and the similarity measures used to evaluate
154
      how closely the candidates in the search space match the target.
155
156   3.1   S IMILARITY M EASURES
157
158   Let f : X → Y be a function that map x in a set of input X = {xi : xi ∈ Rdx }ni=1 to y in a
159   set of output Y = {yi : yi ∈ Rdy }ni=1 . Each element in Y and X are assumed to be a vector
160   with dimensions dy and dx , respectively, with n being the set size, without loss of generality. Let
161   f0 : X → Y 0 be another function that produces Y 0 = {yi0 : yi0 ∈ Rdy }ni=1 given X. Note sets can
      be concatenated into matrix forms as Y 0 , Y ∈ Rn×dy and X ∈ Rn×dx .

162
      Functional Similarity. Similarity between two functions, f0 and f , over a set of input X, can be
163   measured by the similarity between their output sets Y0 = f0 (X) and Y = f (X).
164
165   We can use a scoring or distance function D(Y 0 , Y ) as a measure between output similarity and
166
      hence the functional similarity between f0 and f , where we regard Y 0 as the function/search target.
      If they are close according to D, then the function values should be similar to each other locally
167
      at a set of points X. Otherwise, these functions should be different at X. Popular measures such
168
      as Euclidean distance have been used for this purpose (Klabunde et al., 2023). Each intermediate
169   representations of a neural network can be naturally treated as function outputs, given inputs X.
170
171   The Need for Complex Functional Similarity Measure. Since we cannot exactly control the
172
      behavior of a trained neural network, the layer-wise functions f that it learns can be complex and thus
      the learned representation Y from each layer may be a complex function of the target Y 0 rather than
173
      a simpler transformation. For example, let Y 0 = sin(X) and a candidate Y = sin2 (X) = (Y 0 )2 .
174
      They share strong similarity, but a linear transformation will not be able to capture their functional
175   similarity. If we want to truly understand where function f0 might be approximately computed, we
176   should consider functions of target Y 0 , but naively listing out all possibilities can be prohibitive. As
177   a consequence, one may need to use more complex measures to deal with such a space.
178
179   3.2   S EARCH S PACE
180
181   We consider multiple candidate Y ’s to form the search space for target Y 0 . In the context of MLP
182   neural networks for example, where σ(.) denotes the non-linearity and W s are the parameter matrices,
183   we have Y ∗ = Wn (σ(Wn−1 . . . σ(W1 X))) for the whole network. We can extract many Y ’s from
184   intermediate functions of the model, for instance Y1 = W1 X, Y2 = σ(W1 X), and so on. These Y ’s
185   are often called representations, activations, or sometimes even “outputs” from each layer. We use
186   these terms interchangeably here. For attention modules in transformer neural networks (Vaswani
187
      et al., 2017), we can similarly extract Y ’s from attention key, query, and value functions as well
      as MLP functions. We list the exact equations and locations of representations considered in the
188
      transformer models in Table 2 in Appendix A, which serves as the focus of this paper. To show the
189
      method is applicable to other types of networks, we also consider convolutional neural networks with
190   residual layers, with candidate representations listed in Table 3 in in Appendix A.

      3.3   S EARCH TARGETS
193
194   Known Targets When the search target, denoted as T , is a value from a known function, we can
195   directly compare outputs between representations from each layer and known function output T .
196   Representations from each layer can be directly compared with the target via a probe. Popular linear
197   probes can be used to assess the similarity between a target and any layer’s representation. For
198   instance, linear regression can be used to model each target T from each representation candidate Y ,
199   and the residual error is used as the search criteria between Y and T . As discussed previously, to deal
200   with the potentially large search space of functions of the target, a more powerful probe (such as a
201
      nonlinear MLP function) may have to be used so that it can detect more complex similarities to T .
202   One challenge with using predictive probes to compute the distance measure D is that the target
203   function must be known. In practice, however, we often lack knowledge of specific targets. While
204   it’s possible to experiment with various target functions with power probes, the vast number of
205   potential targets makes this approach inefficient. This calls for an alternative strategy to distinguish
206   sub-components in a network through representation similarity.
207
208   4     G ROMOV-WASSERSTEIN D ISTANCE AS A S IMILARITY M EASURE
209
210   Unknown Targets When the search target is unknown, functionally similar parts cannot be
211   identified by comparison to a predefined set of target functions. Instead, we propose to identify
212   the similarities and subnetworks among the representations at each intermediate layer. Each layer,
213   however, posits a representation that potentially has a different distribution, not to mention even
214   different dimensionality depending on the architectures and layers one considers (viz. mlp in layer
215   and attention layer in transformer blocks). Consequently, representations across layers may be
      incomparable using standard distance metrics, such as the ℓp norm amongst others.

      To overcome these challenging issues, we propose computing distances between representations
217   produced at the same layer for different inputs and match the vertices of this weighted graph – where
218   each dimension of the representation of the inputs are vertices and the distances indicate weights on
219   the edges – with the vertices of a similarly constructed weighted graph from another layer. Essentially,
220   we assume the representations in a layer are samples of the underlying distribution, and we want the
221   best permutation of representation dimensions in one layer that aligns with vertices in another layer,
222   thereby deriving the inter-layer distance. If this inter-layer distance is low, then the two layers we
223   consider to be functionally similar, given the same input data.
224   Formally, without loss of generality, let Y1 = {y1i : y1i ∈ Rd1 }ni=1 and Y2 = {y2i : y2i ∈ Rd2 }ni=1
225   be representations of n examples from two different layers, where the discrete distributions over
226   the representations are µ1 and µ2 respectively, with dimension d1 possibly being different from d2 .
227   Direct distance computation between them is not reasonable. Instead, we seek to compute a coupling
228   or matching π ∈ Π(µ1 , µ2 ) between the n examples in each set such that given the pairwise distances
229   D1 , D2 ∈ Rn×n within representations Y1 and Y2 respectively, the sum of differences between the
230   distances of the matched examples is minimized. Loosely speaking, we aim to find a matching that
231
      preserves the pairwise distance as much as possible. In particular, we want to minimize the following:

      It turns out that ρ corresponds to the Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) distance (Demetci et al., 2023b),

      4.1   J USTIFICATION FOR GW D ISTANCE AS A F UNCTIONAL S IMILARITY M EASURE
241
242   Let (Y1 , D1 , µ1 ) and (Y2 , D2 , µ2 ) be two given metric measure space (mm-space), where (Y , D) is
243   a compact metric space and µ is a Borel probability measure with full support: supp(µ) = Y . An
244   isomorphism between Y1 , Y2 is any isometry Ψ : Y1 → Y2 , i.e., a distance-preserving transformation
245   between metric spaces, such that Ψ#µ1 = µ(Ψ−1 ) = µ2 .
246   Theorem 4.1. (Mémoli, 2011). The Gromov-Wasserstein distance in equation 1 defines a proper
247   distance on the collection of isomorphism classes of the mm-spaces.
248
249   Remark. The Gromov-Wasserstein distance itself is defined on isomorphism-classes of metric
250   measure spaces, which means that any distance preserving (isometric) transformation of a space
251   should preserve GW distance between the points in that space and any other space (Mémoli, 2011).
252
      These isometric transformations include rigid motions (translations and rotations) and reflections or
      compositions of them. Additionally, permutations of points in a space also preserve GW distances, as
253
      the points are unlabeled. Hence, GW distance captures much richer transformations across layers.
254
255   The computed GW distance represents the minimal distance over all possible transportation plans
256   between two sets of points from different spaces. In our context, we can also view GW as a
257   measure that quantifies the distance between distance-based (i.g., Euclidean-distance) graphs, with
258
      a set of points as its nodes. Hence this would be low if the graph undergoes (nearly) isomorphic
      transformations between layers. Conversely, a high GW distance indicates a non-distance preserving
259
      transformation across layers, potentially reflecting a highly non-linear operation. While GW distance
260
      does not reveal the exact function operation, it highlights specific layers for further investigations.
261
262   Favorable Properties of GW. Besides the above noteworthy property of GW to map between different
263   spaces, it also has other favorable properties (Zheng et al., 2022; Demetci et al., 2023b): i) It is
264
      symmetric and satisfies triangle inequality. ii) It is invariant under any isometric transformation of the
      input, which is advantageous because we do not want rotations and reflections to affect our similarity
265
      search. This invariance also includes permutation invariance, which is a beneficial property since
266
      we want the distance between layer representations to remain unaffected by permutations within the
267   representations in each layer. iii) GW is scalable since it does not require estimating high-dimensional
268   distributions, which is often the case with intermediate representations in large models; instead,
269   it only compare them to obtain a distance measure. iv) GW is monotonic in (positive) scaling of
      pairwise distances, and hence the same layers should appear to be closer than others even with scaling.

408   corresponding to a dense model and 3 sparse models with sparsity levels of 25%, 70% and 95% using
409   a state-of-the-art structured pruning algorithm (Dhurandhar et al., 2024). Sparsity are used to force
410   models to condense information into the limited remaining weights, enabling us to examine potential
411   links between this constraint and their structural similarity. Training details are in Appendix E. Due
412   to the size of BERT models, we limit our analysis to comparing the final representations from each of
413   the 12 transformer blocks, rather than examining all intermediate representations within the blocks.
414   Results In the last row of Figure 6a, we see that the pre-trained BERT does not have major differences
415   among blocks, which is not surprising given its accuracy on YELP is only 49.3% (roughly equivalent
416   to random guessing). In Figures 6b to 6e, we see an interesting pattern emerge, revealing two-to-three
417   major block structures in the (sparse) fine-tuned BERT models identified by our approach. The first
418   major differences occur at block 9 and then the last three blocks (10, 11, 12) seem to form a distinct
419   block. This seems to indicate that most of the function/task fitting occurs at these later blocks.
420   The presence of block structures in the GW-distance matrices indicates major functional changes
421   may concentrate at these transition blocks. This finding may suggest that for other downstream tasks,
422   we may consider freezing the model up to Block 8 and only fine-tuning the blocks after that. We
423   validate this observation in appendix G. We also consider a model compression application where
424   only 4 transformer blocks can achieve similarly good performance, as discussed in Appendix L.
425

426   the blocks (with 95% sparse model in 6e having the least differences). This is expected as fewer
427   parameters contribute to the final function output besides others being suppressed. Nonetheless,

429   observation is consistent with fine-tuning and sparsification literature (Li et al., 2021; Dhurandhar
430   et al., 2024), where it has been observed that later blocks typically undergo substantial changes
431   during fine-tuning as they focus on task-specific solutions, while the earlier middle blocks remain

531   6 D ISCUSSION
532
      We proposed a novel approach to model interpretation based on functional similarity within intermedi-
533
      ate layers of neural networks, using Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) distance to compute such similarities.
534   To the best of our knowledge, our application of GW distance in this context is novel. On algebraic,
535   real NLP, and vision tasks, we identified the existence of major sub-components amongst layers,
536   corresponding to functionally meaningful abstractions. Overall, our method provides an automatic
537   low-cost approach to find sub-components within neural networks, facilitating human understanding.
538   Future work could investigate larger models to observe general trends and applications of functional
539   similarity. Theoretical study of other properties of GW distances within the context of neural network
      interpretability is also an interesting future direction.

833   Non-linear Probe As discussed previously, to deal with the potentially large search space of
834   functions of the target, a more powerful probe (such as a nonlinear MLP function) may have to be
835   used so that it can detect more complex similarities to c. Therefore, we train a two-layer MLP2 to
836   predict c’s. As shown at the bottom of Table 4, these two-layer MLPs have more predictive power and
837   can perfectly predict the targets, while still showing differences among various layers indicating that
838   the matched layers do capture the intended target functions while other layers do not. Many layers
839   in the 3rd block, for example, have only 1% accuracy relative to c1 . This indicates that non-linear
840   probes can be used to find subgroups of layers in neural networks. Unlike existing work that primarily
841
      focuses on linear probes, we show that non-linear probes, still with limited capacity, are useful.
842   One issue with using predictive probes to compute the distance measure D is that the target function
843   has to be known. In practice, however, we may not know any intermediate targets, as suggested in the
844   end-to-end training of Model E. While we still can try different target functions and use non-linear
845   probes, the infinite number of possible targets makes such an approach inefficient. This calls for a
846   different strategy to differentiate sub-components in a network through representation similarity.
