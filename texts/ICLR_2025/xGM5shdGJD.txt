009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Scaling laws predict the loss of a target machine learning model by extrapolating
012                from easier-to-train models with fewer parameters or smaller training sets. This
013                provides an efficient way for practitioners and researchers alike to compare pre-
014                training decisions involving optimizers, datasets, and model architectures. Despite
015                the widespread use of scaling laws to model the dynamics of language model
016                training, there has been little work on understanding how to best estimate and
017                interpret them. We collect (and release) a large-scale dataset containing losses and
018
                   downstream evaluations for 485 previously published pretrained models. We use
                   these to estimate more than 1000 scaling laws, then derive a set of best practices
019
                   for estimating scaling laws in new model families. We find that fitting scaling laws
020
                   to intermediate checkpoints of training runs (and not just their final losses) substan-
021                tially improves accuracy, and that—all else equal—estimates of performance are
022                generally most accurate when derived from other models of similar sizes. However,
023                because there is a significant degree of variability across model seeds, training
024                multiple small models is sometimes more useful than training a single large one.
025                Moreover, while different model families differ in scaling behavior, they are often
026                similar enough that a target model’s behavior can be predicted from a single model
027                with the same architecture, along with scaling parameter estimates derived from
028                other model families.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Substantial effort and cost are required to train even a single large language model (LLM).1 There is
034
      thus an acute need for efficient decision-making aids that can evaluate the effectiveness of proposed
      changes to language models’ architecture or training data without full-scale training runs. While
035
      there is a large body of work that motivates or evaluates these changes using small models (Warstadt
036
      et al., 2023; Hillier et al., 2024), synthetic tasks (Akyürek et al., 2024; Wortsman et al., 2023) or
037   theory (Jelassi et al., 2024), one of the most important tools for current practitioners is the estimation
038   of scaling laws for LLMs (Ivgi et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024).
039
040
      A scaling law extrapolates the performance of a target model from the performance of a set of models
      with fewer parameters or smaller training sets. Typically, this extrapolation requires models to belong
041
      to the same model family, differing only in parameter count and training set size, but using the
042
      same architecture and training distribution. A high-quality scaling law accurately predicts the target
043   model’s test performance (Rosenfeld et al.; Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022).
044
045   Most past work describing and characterizing scaling laws has begun by exhaustively training
046
      models in a family across a full range of dataset sizes and parameter counts. One question that
      has received comparatively little attention is how, when training a new LLM, a practitioner with
047
      limited computational resources should choose which small-scale models to train in order to best
048
      estimate a target model’s final performance. This paper offers a practical guide to when, and how,
049   to use small models to efficiently obtain meaningful predictions about large models’ behavior—
050   maximizing prediction reliability while minimizing the budget for preliminary experimentation,
051   which necessarily involves tradeoffs between the number of preliminary models trained, the size of
052   the largest preliminary model, and size of the dataset used to train it.

      We begin by collecting diverse model data to perform a large-scale meta-analysis of scaling laws (§3).
055   Usually, scaling law research relies on a single collection of closely related models, or alters only a
056   minimal aspect of pretraining (e.g. data size; Muennighoff et al., 2024). Instead, we gather data from
057   as diverse a set of scaled families as possible, to allow this and future meta-analysis of scaling laws
058   that generalize across architectures, datasets and settings.
059
      The rest of the paper uses this data to analyze a number of key questions around scaling law estimation:
060
061         1. How reliably may we expect scaling laws to extrapolate? Variation between random
062            parameter initializations can produce changes of up to 4% in loss. Most published improve-
063            ments in pretraining procedures, when performing minimal controlled experiments, report
064            loss changes between 4% and 50% (§4).
065         2. How much does the shape of scaling laws vary across model families? Different model
066            families have scaling laws with a different functional dependence on model size (§5).
067            However, transformer LMs are similar enough that, with a single model from a target family
068            and a scaling law from a different model family, it is sometimes possible to accurately
069            estimate target model performance (§5.1).
070         3. Must scaling laws be estimated only from fully trained models? Even though optimiza-
071            tion procedures are typically sensitive to the full size of a training run, estimating scaling
072            laws from intermediate training checkpoints greatly improves scaling law fit (§6). It is
073            generally possible to estimate a model’s final loss beginning roughly 1/3 of the way through
074
               training.
075         4. How large must models be to produce reliable scaling estimates? All else equal, experi-
076            menting with large models is typically more useful than with small models (§7), but may be
077
               outweighed by the benefits of reduced variance from training more, smaller models (§8).
078         5. Taken together, cost-effective estimation of a scaling law should consider the number of
079            models, the size of the models, and the number of training tokens for each model. We
080            highlight those size, tokens and number of models effects in Fig. 2.
081
      Our experiments also provide insight into the functional form of scaling laws themselves, suggesting
082   that they may have fewer degrees of freedom (§9) than typically assumed. We conclude with
083   discussion of other work on scaling law estimation that may be of interest to practitioners §10.

175
176   As part of this work, we have collected and released the largest-scale public dataset describing scaling
177   behavior across model families. This dataset aggregates information from a large number of LLM
178   training efforts that have released information about the behavior of multiple models of different
179
      sizes or scales. While experiments in this paper focus on scaling laws that measure loss, the dataset
      also includes information about model performance on downstream evaluation benchmarks where
180
      available. We have focused on language models where the largest one is more than 3B parameters
181
      and where data was shared publicly or in private correspondence. Our repository accepts further
182   contributions and requests for additions. In addition to those, we have manually extracted some data
183   from papers that did not release models but reported losses in figures.

      3.1       DATA SOURCES
186
187   For each model in this dataset, we report any downstream evaluation and loss that was measured
188   during training, as well as calculated #toks for each, links to matching checkpoints when available,
189   links to data sources, and information about computational cost (in FLOPs) and number of training
190   epochs (i.e. passes over the training set). Each model is identified by a unique name, a type (e.g.
191   llama), #toks, #params, architecture type (e.g. encoder-decoder), and seed.
192   Models in this dataset include Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023, which provides the largest set of
193   models and variations in a family), OPT (Zhang et al., 2022, collected thanks to Xia et al., 2023;
194   Biderman et al., 2023), OLMO (Groeneveld et al., 2024), Amber (Liu et al., 2023), K2 (Team,
195   2024), Mamba (Liu et al., 2023) RedPajamas 3 ModuleFormer mixture of experts (Shen et al., 2023),
196   overtrained models (Gadre et al., 2024), Mamba, Llama and hybrid architecture variations from
197   Poli et al. (2024), transformer architectures (Alabdulmohsin et al., 2022), Bloom (Le Scao et al.,
198   2023), T5-Pile (Sutawika et al., 2024), Pandey (2024) models, GPT-family models with different data
199   regimes (Muennighoff et al., 2024), Gopher (Hoffmann et al., 2022) and GPT3 (Brown et al., 2020).
200   The data consists of 1.9M steps of training evaluated on loss or perplexity, usually on multiple data
201   sources belonging to 485 unique pretrained models, and more than 40 scaled families. To combine
202   the data together, each loss or score was kept in its original form and an aggregating function to
203   normalize the scores to similar scales (perplexity and loss have a log relation) and average was
204   supplied. The data, saved as a compressed table with all the metadata and scores together is hence
205   large but comprehensive.
206   We hope this will provide a useful resource for the community and plan to extend it further as models
207   get released and their training dynamics are shared. We see such a resource as a facilitator to more
208   research on model development (e.g. A/B testing), scaling laws, downstream scaling laws (Gadre
209   et al., 2024; Ruan et al., 2024; Owen, 2024; Isik et al., 2024), training dynamics (Choshen et al.,

      4     H OW WELL CAN I EXPECT A SCALING LAW TO PREDICT ?
249
250
251       4% is the best ARE typically obtained; ARE up to 20% can still distinguish between many modeling choices.
252
253   To establish how accurate a scaling law must be to be useful to practitioners, we first assess what
254   changes in model accuracy have been considered meaningful in past work. We have surveyed
255
      experiments in the literature where an A/B test was performed, i.e., two models were trained similarly,
      manipulating one attribute to see how it affects scores. Empirically, we found no widely adopted
256
      modeling changes that were motivated with less than a 4% relative difference between models.
257
      Additionally, reported variance across random restarts of the same model architecture reaches up to
258   3.5% (c.f.,§8; Sellam et al., 2021). We take this to mean that this is approximately the minimal effect-
259   size experimenters care about and possibly the minimal effect one can reliably measure. Accordingly,
260   this bounds the best goodness of fit we should expect or require of scaling laws.
261
      To offer several concrete points of comparison: Pythia 6.9B models fixed inconsistencies in their code
262
      and hence have two versions (c.f. App. B; Biderman et al., 2023) which differ in loss by 40%. They
263
      also provide data deduplication A/B test that had a minor effect on the loss of about 5%. Gadre et al.
264   (2024) tested the effect of training 400M parameter models for different #toks. The most similar
265   (double the training tokens) has approximately 4% change and can reach a 50% loss difference with
266   30 times more training. Training on a constant #toks but repeating the same data resulted in almost
267   no changes for up to 4 repetitions (epochs), and later in about 8%, 50% on 14.44 repetitions of the
268   data (Muennighoff et al., 2024). Instead of varying the amount of data or epochs, Ge et al. (2024)
269   found that training on a different kind of data incurred ARE of approximately 10% and different data
      mixes led to 6% changes or less.

      5      W HEN I TRAIN A NEW MODEL , DO I EVEN NEED A NEW SCALING LAW ?
271
272
273       Different model families exhibit different scaling behavior, but performance can sometimes be estimated
          using a single model in a new family.
274
275
      Scaling laws relate performance to scalar training parameters like model or dataset size. For discrete
276
      decisions (whether the choice of nonlinearity or data preprocessing scheme), it is not immediately
277   obvious how to pool information across models that differ in these traits (see Ruan et al., 2024;
278   Maia Polo et al., 2024, for concurrent work that performs this pooling based on downstream task
279   behavior). Clearly, different pretrained models with the same #params and #toks still show
280   different loss, so these differences can be consequential. But how do discrete choices of architecture,
281   training procedure, or dataset, affect the form of scaling laws?
282
      One way to answer this question is to look at the parameter estimates for scaling law parameters E,
283   α, A, β and B differ across model families. These results are shown in Fig. 3, where it can be seen
284   that there are often dramatic differences in all five parameters across families. In this sense, even
285   the rate at which additional data or parameters improve model performance depend on underlying
286   architectural details, suggesting that understanding the behavior of a new model family may require a
287   new scaling law.
288
      But another way to answer this question is to ask how reliably we can predict final model accuracy
289   when borrowing (or pooling) some parameters of scaling laws between families—even if these
290   result in poor parameter estimates, they may predict large-scale model behavior within the range of
291   meaningful differences identified in Section 4. To do so, we set the #params scaling parameters
292   (A, α) to fixed values reported in past work, and estimate remaining parameters for individual model
293   families. We take the variable values found by Muennighoff et al. (2024) (see Besiroglu et al., 2024;
294   Porian et al., 2024 for a discussion of estimates from earlier work including Hoffmann et al., 2022).
295   We find (see Fig. 6 in App. A) that in some cases only a single training run in a new model family is
296   necessary to obtain accurate scaling law predictions. In the OLMO family, for example, we obtain
297
      less than 1% error estimating the accuracy of a 7B model from a collection of 1B model checkpoints.
      We find that predictions generalize, and a constant #params scaling factor is enough for most models
298
      (except the encoder-decoder T5-Pile). However, error rates are larger than in the source family, and
299
      predictions for larger models are worse (most conspicuous in OPT’s error of 37%, 25% and 15%
300   when extrapolating from 8.7B, 13B and 30B to 175B).
301
302
      5.1     C AN I JUST TRAIN THE TARGET MODEL A BIT INSTEAD OF MANY SMALL MODELS ?
303
304
          Yes, but obtaining reliable estimates in this way requires up to 30% of the full training run.
305
306   The above results (last row of Fig. 6 in App. A) also suggest the possibility of predicting losses
307   not with just smaller models, but with partially trained versions of the target model itself. When
308   predicting inside the same #params family—that is, estimating L̂(f | Ftarget \ {f })— the #params
309   term in Eq. (1) is constant, and extrapolation is only required for #toks. As seen in the figures, this
310   form of estimation is informative if permitted by computational constraints. Beyond the immediate
311   usefulness of this approach, it is a promising avenue for future research. Better adjusting the scaling
312   laws for predicting through training might improve this efficiency.
313
314   5.2     A RE EVEN SIMPLER BASELINES ENOUGH ?
315
316       Some extrapolation is necessary: scaling laws can produce accurate estimates even when the target model
317       vastly outperforms any training model.
318
319   To provide another form of comparison for the predicted scaling laws, we compute two baselines.
320
      Both baselines adopt a pessimistic evaluation assuming that the target model is no better than the best
      model in the small model family used to estimate a scaling law. Specifically, the baselines are the
321
322
      best performance L̂(∅ | Ftrain ) = minf ∈Ftrain L(f ) and the performance of the most-trained model,
323   consuming the most compute for training, i.e. L̂(∅ | Ftrain ) = arg maxf ∈Ftrain #params(f ) ×
      #toks(f ). Those baselines might be the best one can expect without fitting a law to scaling.

349   6               I HAVE SOME DATA , WHAT PORTIONS SHOULD I USE ?
350
351
                  Estimate scaling laws from intermediate checkpoints, not just fully trained models!
352
353
      Most past work on scaling behavior of language models (e.g., Gadre et al., 2024; Muennighoff et al.,
354   2024) has trained a separate model for each value of #toks studied. This is based on the assumption
355   that changes in the learning rate schedule, which depend on the size of the full dataset that will be
356   used for training, render losses from intermediate checkpoints uninformative.
357
      However, some recent work has demonstrated the effectiveness of learning schedules that do not
358
      require prior access to the size of the training set (Hu et al., 2024), and some work has questioned
359
      whether careful choice of the learning rate decay is even necessary for reliable scaling laws (Porian
360   et al., 2024). Together, these findings motivate revisiting the assumption that only a single useful
361   datapoint may be obtained from each training run. In the final portion of §5.1, we observed the
362   value of intermediate checkpoints when only a single #params family is used to fit a scaling law.
363   Mathematically, there has to be some difference between models trained on the same number of
364   tokens and sizes depending on the scheduler, it is unknown however, how big is this difference.
365   We now test whether this finding extends to larger families—i.e. whether including intermediate
366   checkpoints from all models in a model family reduces ARE.
367   Results are shown in Fig. 4, which plots ARE for scaling laws estimated from data subsets of the form
368   max#toks (F, q) for varying q. We find that including full training curves in scaling law estimation
369   can predict losses well. In fact, relying merely on the end of training produces significantly worse
370   performance across the board. Our remaining experiments thus fit scaling laws using all these
371   intermediate checkpoints, and not final performance alone.

408   There is, however, a distinctly uninformative phase at the beginning of training, as can be seen in
409   the loss curves (App. B) and noted in the literature (e.g., Chen et al.). We observe that this period is

421   7      H OW BIG A MODEL SHOULD I TRAIN ?
422
423
          Larger models are better, but not necessary. Mainly, beware of specific models that might give noisy results.
424
425
      In Fig. 2 we compare scaling laws when controlling the amount, percentage, or size of the models (2
426   at a time). We find that choosing models closer in #params to the target model is generally effective
427   (e.g., Fig. 2a, 2c), but the effect is neither strong nor monotonic. For example, in all cases fitting
428   on all F provides one of the lowest ARE. However, in GPT, Gopher and OPT, predicting with the
429   smallest 4 models available is already enough to achieve less than 10% error. In Pythia, the smallest
430   models are not predictive but the rest of the models provide a similar fit. While relying on a larger
431   model is beneficial, predicting many scales up (e.g., the behavior of a 34× larger model in Pythia) is
      still reliable, especially if accounting for other factors we discuss next.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      In fact, training additional, larger models before fitting a scaling law may sometimes decrease
433   accuracy due to increased variance in large model performance—see, for example, Pythia 2.8B in
434   Fig. 1. Unfortunately, it is difficult to identify whether a seed is exceptionally high or low-performing
435   without additional information. For example, cross-validation on F fails to detect it (see App. D).
436
      Instead, this instability can be addressed by accounting for seed variability. A wasteful way to do
437
      so would be to train every model several times. A better alternative is to diversify and train each
438
      model on as differing hyperparameters (here, seed, #params, #toks) as possible and to maximize
439   the information gained (a common practice in efficiency-coverage scenarios, e.g., Perlitz et al., 2024).
440   Hence, we suggest training more models of differing sizes each accounting for both size and seed
441   changes, rather than training multiple seeds. We further discuss the effects of number of models (|F |)
442   in §8.
443
      Selection of #params values to optimize statistical and computational efficiency is a problem we
444
      leave for future work. Given the choice of the largest model and the number of models, it is unclear
445   how to space the model sizes, whether linearly, log-scale, or otherwise.

448   8     H OW MANY MODELS ARE NEEDED FOR RELIABLE PREDICTIONS ?
449
450       5 models is a safe bet, more would improve the results’ robustness. These models can be small.
451
452   We have seen that predicting with larger models and hence extrapolating less yields better results.
453   However, given compute constraints (and additional hardware constraints like memory), practitioners
454   may generally wish to use smaller models when possible. Consider for example Fig. 2b where we
455   compare fitting on 4 models but vary their size. We find that more models reduce ARE even without
456   being bigger models. As discussed in §7, adding a larger model to a current scaled family serves two
457   goals, it increases the proximity to the predicted model, as well as increases the number of models
458
      seen.
459   We separate the contribution of size and number of models effect. In Fig. 2c, we predict with the
460   largest model being held constant and add (at minimal cost) smaller models. We see again that larger
461   models do benefit predictions. For example, the small models part (left) of the graph indicates large
462   errors (bright). However, we also see again the unwanted effects a single model may have on the
463   overall prediction. Consider for example the figure’s diagonal in Pythia. Cells in a diagonal share
464
      a group of models and each row adds another one to F . Evidently this specific group hurts results,
      even when larger models are added to F . With enough models (bottom of diagonal), the negative
465
      decreases. Switching the model (next column) also removes the negative effect. Moreover, across all
466
      rows the tendency is never monotonic, implying larger models do not not ensure better predictions.
467
468   But in general, we see that increasing the number of models tends to improve prediction. For example,
469   in GPT3 the best predictions are with many models. Perhaps intuitively, adding a larger model and
470
      improving both #params and number of models aspects improves quite consistently (Fig. 2b and
      diagonals of Fig. 2c).

473   9     W HAT PARAMETERS DO I ACTUALLY NEED TO ESTIMATE ?
474
475
          Scaling laws might have fewer degrees of freedom than described in the literature.
476
477
      Assuming we do not try to account for aspects other than #toks and #params (see §10), one might
478   wonder if some of the observed errors come from model misspecification—an incorrect functional
479
      form for L̂, which (with a small number of exceptions including Caballero et al.) has generally
480   gone uncontested since it was first proposed (Rosenfeld et al.; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Here we
481   specifically evaluate whether scaling laws empirically exhibit fewer degrees of freedom than has been
482   proposed. First, we compute the principal components of the 5 learned parameters and find that 3
483   components explain 99.49% of the variance between the 5 parameters. Inspection reveals that two of
484   these components tightly couple the pairs of parameters dealing with the same training parameter
485   (#params and #toks). Plotting values of A against α and of B against β (Fig. 3), we see a clear
      linear relationship between these variables despite ther non-linear interaction in Eq. 1. There are a


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      few exceptions: the Encoder-Decoder model T5-Pile shows a different behavior from the rest of the
487   scaled families, and four additional scaled families show a different relationship between B and β. In
488   fact, all these families share the common feature that they were trained using multiple passes over
489   a single training set Gadre et al. (2024). The outlier point with β > 4 is a 70m baseline of Pythia
490   for a continual training intervention experiment (Biderman et al., 2023). Future work may consider
491   different function forms tying some of the parameters or introducing other ones instead.
492
      Another change for the function form that future work should consider is accounting for the learning
493   rate schedule, as our experiments assumed it was negligible. A mismatch between the form and the
494   real dependence might explain the inconsistencies in using the beginning of training. As noted in §6.1,
495   the beginning is not fitting as well as later on, which we also see to some extent in the percentages
496   axis of Fig.4. This might also be expected as previous works did not take the training trajectory (and
497   loss schedule) into account and ignored this data.

      10    R ELATED WORK
500
501   This work builds on a large number of recent studies relating scaling law estimation and decision-
502   making about model training. Among the aspects studied are total training costs including inference
503   (Sardana et al.), effects of sophisticated data selection (Sorscher et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2024), training
504   time (Inbar & Sernau, 2024), transfer of learned skills (Hernandez et al., 2021), behavior of models in
505   other modalities (Mikami et al., 2022; Abnar et al.; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2024; Hesslow et al., 2022)
506   mixtures of experts (Ludziejewski et al.), data mixing (Ge et al., 2024), downstream performance
507   (Muennighoff et al., 2024), vocabulary size (Tao et al., 2024), and architecture comparisons (Tay
508   et al., 2023; Poli et al., 2024) including small models (Muckatira et al., 2024) or other phenomena
509
      like finetuning (Zhang et al.) and the loss in different positions in the training sequences (Xiong et al.,
      2024). Especially relevant to our context is Ruan et al. (2024); Maia Polo et al. (2024) that rely on
510
      multiple pretraining settings for creating scaling laws that generalize across models or kinds of losses.
511
512   Another line of works that can be seen as a scaling law discusses the relation between model width
513   and hyperparameter choices (rather than loss) (Yang et al., 2022; 2021; Blake et al., 2024; Lingle,
514   2024).
515
516   11    L IMITATIONS
517
518   Our use of ARE as a primary evaluation metric does not distinguish between over-estimation or
519   under-estimation of performance. When using scaling laws to choose between candidate models to
520   train, these error estimates may be unnecessarily conservative (e.g. if both families’ laws are biased
521   in the same direction).
522   Another major limitation in this study is the difficulty of aggregating information across model
523   families. As most published families evaluate models of incomparable scales, often over incomparable
524   ranges, we were unable to produce an informative version of Fig. 2 that aggregated information across
525   all models available, and was thus able to give general recommendations about compute-optimal
526   choice of preliminary experiments.
527
528   12    D ISCUSSION
529
530   This paper provides a first study of open questions in the estimation of scaling laws and their relation
531   to large-scale pretraining decisions. We expect that many of these conclusions could be sharpened
532   or extended with the availability of additional information about model training, and we call on
533   other leaders of large-scale training efforts to share training losses and evaluation results from
534   multiple checkpoints during pretraining—even in cases where model parameters themselves cannot
535   be released.
536   Our findings leave open many important questions, from performing efficient predictions by fitting
537   on many model families to scaling laws of the deltas between a/b test for a change in attribute (e.g.
538   optimizer) or generalize from one a/b test to another, and to other methods of efficiently compare
539   architectures that do not rely on multiple models (e.g. continual learning). In addition, our results in
      §9 suggest other scaling law parameterizations might better fit data.

958   Usually, one does not use a scaling law to extrapolate to a smaller model as one can just train the
959   small model. However, under observational scaling laws, where one wants to research a phenomenon
960   without scaling at all (Ruan et al., 2024; Maia Polo et al., 2024), or when many models were trained
961   and one wishes to create smaller models for various reasons (Hillier et al., 2024; Warstadt et al.,
962
      2023), scaling down might prove useful. Moreover, in the context of traditional scaling laws this may
      act as a baseline. Such an experiment may shed another light on the number of models |F | versus
963
      their size #params. If large models are better because they are more stable or otherwise fit laws
964
      more robustly, few models will be enough, if the number of models or scale down difference from the
965   prediction, it will show similar behaviour to scaling up. See more in §8.
966
967   To test this we reverse the order of models and predict with the largest models the loss on the smallest
968
      models. This means that for example in the case of 3 models, we predict the smallest model’s loss and
      fit the scaling law relying on the 3 largest models. As before, we break the results by the percentage
969
      of training done and do not reverse it.
970
971   As shown in Fig. 8, the number of models plays an important role in fitting well and a minimum of
      30-40% of the training is necessary for good fit, more than that often improves further.

       In §7, we raise the issue of instability of scaling law predictions, with a single model vastly changing
1031
       the results. We tried to see if, without knowing the ARE, we could remove bad models from the
1032
       prediction. We hypothesized that models that we can’t predict would mean models that would skew
1033   our predictions when fitted upon. We performed a cross-validation on the #params families in F
1034   each time setting the models with most #toks as target ans exclusing the #params family from
1035   F . Our hypothesis was found to be incorrect. Such cases of hard-to-predict models were found to
1036   indicate that the models left in F are bad predictors and not that the target is very dissimilar (a "bad"
1037   training). In 58% of the cases removing that model from the scaling law created the worst ARE
1038   possible on the actual target, more than removing any other model.
