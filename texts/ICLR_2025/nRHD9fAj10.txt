                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Diffusion models (DMs) are capable of generating remarkably high-quality sam-
010
               ples by iteratively denoising a random vector, a process that corresponds to mov-
011
               ing along the probability flow ordinary differential equation (PF ODE). Interest-
012            ingly, DMs can also invert an input image to noise by moving backward along the
013            PF ODE, a key operation for downstream tasks such as interpolation and image
014            editing. However, the iterative nature of this process restricts its speed, hindering
015            its broader application. Recently, Consistency Models (CMs) have emerged to ad-
016            dress this challenge by approximating the integral of the PF ODE, largely reducing
017            the number of iterations. Yet, the absence of an explicit ODE solver complicates
018            the inversion process. To resolve this, we introduce Bidirectional Consistency
019            Model (BCM), which learns a single neural network that enables both forward
020
               and backward traversal along the PF ODE, efficiently unifying generation and in-
               version tasks within one framework. We can train BCM from scratch or tune it us-
021
               ing a pretrained consistency model, wh ich reduces the training cost and increases
022
               scalability. We demonstrate that BCM enables one-step generation and inversion
023            while also allowing the use of additional steps to enhance generation quality or re-
024            duce reconstruction error. We further showcase BCM’s capability in downstream
025            tasks, such as interpolation, inpainting, and blind restoration of compressed im-
026            ages. Notably, when the number of function evaluations (NFE) is constrained,
027            BCM surpasses domain-specific restoration methods, such as I2 SB and Palette, in
028            a fully zero-shot manner, offering an efficient alternative for inversion problems.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Two key components in image generation and manipulation are generation and its inversion. Specif-
034   ically, generation aims to learn a mapping from simple noise distributions, such as Gaussian, to
035
      complex ones, like the distribution encompassing all real-world images. In contrast, inversion seeks
      to find the reverse mapping, transforming real data back into the corresponding noise 1 . Recent
036
      breakthroughs in deep generative models (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kingma & Welling, 2013; Dinh
037
      et al., 2014; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021a;b) have revolutionized this
038   field. These models have not only achieved remarkable success in synthesizing high-fidelity sam-
039   ples across various modalities (Karras et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2021; OpenAI,
040   2024), but have proven effective in downstream applications, such as image editing, by leveraging
041   the inversion process (Mokady et al., 2023; Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2023).
042
      Particularly, score-based diffusion models (DMs) (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
043
      2021a;b; Karras et al., 2022) have stood out for generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). Starting
044   from random initial noise, DMs progressively remove noise through a process akin to a numerical
045   ODE solver operating over the probability flow ordinary differential equation (PF ODE), as outlined
046   by Song et al. (2021b). However, it typically takes hundreds of iterations to produce high-quality
047   generation results. This slow generation process limits broader applications.
048
      This issue has been recently addressed by consistency models (CMs) (Song et al., 2023; Song &
049
      Dhariwal, 2024), which directly compute the integral of PF ODE trajectory from any time step
050   to zero. Similar to CMs, Kim et al. (2024) introduced the Consistency Trajectory Model (CTM),
051   which estimates the integral between any two time steps along the trajectory towards the denoising
052

      On the other hand, unfortunately, the inversion tasks remain challenging for DMs. First, the gen-
075   eration process in many DMs (Ho et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022) is stochastic and hence is non-
076   invertible; second, even for methods that employ a deterministic sampling process (Song et al.,
077   2021a), inverting the ODE solver necessitates at least hundreds of iterations for a small reconstruc-
078   tion error; third, although CMs and CTM accelerate generation by learning the integral directly, this
079   integration is strongly non-linear, making the inversion process even harder.
080
      Therefore, in this work, we aim to bridge this gap through natural yet non-trivial extensions to
081   CMs and CTM. Specifically, they possess a key feature of self-consistency: points along the same
082   trajectory map back to the same initial point. Inspired by this property, we pose the questions:
083
084
           Is there a form of stronger consistency where points on the same trajectory can map to each
           other, regardless of their time steps’ order? Can we train a network to learn this mapping?
085
086   In the following sections of this paper, we affirmatively answer these questions with our proposed
087   Bidirectional Consistency Model (BCM). Specifically:
088
089
      1. We train a single neural network that enables both forward and backward traversal (i.e., integra-
         tion) along the PF ODE, unifying the generation and inversion tasks into one framework. We can
090
         either train the model from scratch or fine-tune it from a pretrained consistency model to reduce
091
         training cost and increase scalability.
092
093   2. We demonstrate BCM can generate images or invert a given image with a single NFE, and can
094
         achieve improved sample quality or lower reconstruction error by chaining multiple time steps.
095   3. Leveraging BCM’s capability to navigate both forward and backward along the PF ODE trajec-
096      tory, we introduce two sampling schemes and a combined approach that has empirically demon-
097      strated superior performance.
098   4. We apply BCM for image interpolation, inpainting, and blind restoration of compressed images,
099      showcasing its potential versatile downstream tasks.

      3.3   S AMPLING
281
282   In this section, we describe the sampling schemes of BCM. Similar to CMs and CTM, BCM sup-
283   ports 1-step sampling naturally. However, BCM’s capability to navigate both forward and backward
284   along the PF ODE trajectory allows us to design more complicated multi-step sampling strategies to
285   improve the sample quality. We present two schemes and a combined approach that has empirically
286   demonstrated superior performance in the following.
287   Ancestral Sampling. The most straightforward way for multi-step sampling is to remove noise se-
288   quentially. Specifically, we first divide the time horizon r0, T s into N sub-intervals with boundaries
289   0 “ t0 ă t1 ă ¨ ¨ ¨ ă tN “ T . Then, we sample a noise image xT „ N p0, T 2 Iq, and sequentially
290   remove noise with the network: xtn´1 Ð fθ pxtn , tn , tn´1 q, n “ N, N ´1, ..., 1. We note that the
291   discretization strategy may differ from the one used during the training of BCM. Since this sampling
292   procedure can be viewed as drawing samples from the conditional density pxtn´1 |xtn pxtn´1 |xtn q,
293   we dub it as ancestral sampling, and summarize it in Algorithm 2. We can also view 1-step sampling
294   as ancestral sampling, where we only divide the time horizon into a single interval.
295   Zigzag Sampling. Another effective sampling method (Algorithm 1 in Song et al. (2023)) is to
296   iteratively re-add noise after denoising. Similar to ancestral sampling, we also define a sequence of
297   time steps t1 ă ¨ ¨ ¨ ă tN “ T . However, different from ancestral sampling where we gradually
298   remove noise, we directly map xT to x0 by fθ pxT , T, 0q. We then add a fresh Gaussian noise to x0 ,
299   mapping it from time 0 to time tn´1 , i.e., xtn´1 “ x0 ` tn´1 σ, where σ „ N p0, Iq. This process
300   repeats in this zigzag manner until all the designated time steps are covered. The two-step zigzag
301   sampler effectively reduces FID in CMs (Song et al., 2023) and is theoretically supported (Lyu
302
      et al., 2023). However, the fresh noise can alter the content of the image after each iteration, which
      is undesirable, especially considering that our tasks will both include generation and inversion. One
303
      may immediately think that setting the injected noise to be the same as the initial random noise can
304
      fix this issue. However, we reveal that this significantly damages the quality of the generated images.
305
306   Fortunately, our proposed BCM provides a direct solution, leveraging its capability to traverse both
307   forward and backward along the PF ODE. Specifically, rather than manually reintroducing a large
308
      amount of fresh noise, we initially apply a small amount and let the network amplify it. In a nutshell,
      for iteration n (n “ N, N ´ 1, ¨ ¨ ¨ , 2), we have
309
310         x0 Ð fθ pxtn , tn , 0q,   xεn´1 Ð x0 ` εn´1 σ,       xtn´1 Ð fθ pxεn´1 , εn´1 , tn´1 q.        (11)
311
312   where εn´1 is the scale of the small noises we add in n-th iteration, and σ „ N p0, Iq is a fresh
313   Gaussian noise. We detail this scheme in Algorithm 3. To verify its effectiveness, in Figure 2, we
314   illustrate some examples to compare the generated images by 1) manually adding fresh noise, 2)
315   manually adding fixed noise, and 3) our proposed sampling process, i.e., adding a small noise and
316   amplifying it with the network. We can clearly see our method maintains the generated content.

      Combination of Both. Long jumps along the PF ODE in zigzag sampling can lead to accumu-
318   lative errors, especially at high noise levels, which potentially hampers further improvements in
319   sample quality. Therefore, we propose a combination of ancestral sampling and zigzag sampling.
320   Specifically, we first perform ancestral sampling to rapidly reduce the large initial noise to a more
321   manageable noise scale and then apply zigzag sampling within this small noise level. We describe

343   4.1   I MAGE G ENERATION
344
345   We first evaluate the image generation performance of BCM. On CIFAR-10, we train our BCM
346
      from scratch, while on ImageNet-64, we first train an iCT model, and fine-tune BCM from it, as
      we discussed in Section 3.2. When reproducing iCT on ImageNet-64, we encountered instability
347
      during training, and found it difficult to reproduce the reported FID by Song & Dhariwal (2024). To
348
      mitigate the performance gap, we removed the attention layer at the 32x32 resolution and added nor-
349   malization to the QKV attention, following (Karras et al., 2024). We empirically find these simple
350   modifications bring positive influences on performance. We include the hyperparameters for train-
351   ing and sampling in Appendices C.1 and C.2. We report image quality and NFE for CIFAR-10 and
352   ImageNet-64 in Table 1, and we visualize some generated samples Figures 3 and 4 and appendix H.
353   As we can see,
354
355   • both ancestral sampling and zigzag sampling can improve the sample quality, while the combina-
356     tion of both further yields the best performance;
357   • our proposed BCM achieves comparable or even better results within at least one order of magni-
358     tude fewer NFEs compared with diffusion models; BCM presents better results with even fewer
359     NFEs then the methods boosting sampling speed through fast samplers; it is also comparable to
360     modern distillation approaches like EM distillation by Xie et al. (2024);
361
      • comparing with CMs, iCT (Song & Dhariwal, 2024) surpasses BCM for 1-step sampling. This
362
        is not surprising, given that BCMs tackle a significantly more complex task compared to CMs.
363
        However, as the number of function evaluations (NFEs) increases, following the sampling strate-
364     gies detailed in Section 3.3, BCM starts to outperform. This indicates that our approach allows
365     the model to learn bidirectional consistency without hurting the generation performance;
366
367   • our model’s performance still falls short of CTM’s (Kim et al., 2024). However, CTM relies
368
        heavily on adversarial loss and uses LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) as the distance measure, which
        can have feature leakage (Song & Dhariwal, 2024; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2023). ECT (Geng
369
        et al., 2024) also outperforms BCM on ImageNet-64 with 2 NFEs. However, we note that their
370
        approach—fine-tuning from a pretrained diffusion and using the advanced EDM2 architecture
371     (Karras et al., 2024)—is orthogonal to ours. Our method could be combined with theirs for po-
372     tential improvements in both performance and training speed.
373
374   Another interesting phenomenon we observed on ImageNet-64 is that our fine-tuned BCM actually
375   outperforms its initialization (i.e., our pretrained iCT) on generation. In contrast, we empirically
376   find fine-tuning iCT with the same training budget did not yield any improvement. We suspect this
377   is due to the “soft” trajectory constraint in the bidirectional training objective, which may act as a
      form of regularization, aiding optimization. We leave further investigation of this for future work.

497   5   R ELATED W ORKS
498
499   Accelerating Diffusion’s Generation. In addition to CM and CTM we discussed in Section 2,
500   many attempts have also been made to accelerate the generation of DMs. Existing methods include
501   faster ODE solvers (Song et al., 2021a; Zhang & Chen, 2022; Lu et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2023) and
502   distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Luhman & Luhman, 2021; Zheng et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024;
503   Xie et al., 2024). However, these ODE solvers generally require 5-10 steps for satisfactory samples,
504
      and the distillation approach may cap the performance at the level of the pretrained diffusion model,
      as highlighted by Song & Dhariwal (2024). On the other hand, while distillation approaches have
505
      achieved impressive generation quality, an intrinsic property of CMs is their ability to enable fast
506
      traversal along ODEs. This allows for the design of broader applications; for example, Zhang et al.
507   (2024) leverage this property to develop an efficient importance sampler. Our proposed BCM further
508   enriches this by enabling both forward and backward traversal.
509
510
      Inversion.      Inversion of an input image is a key step in downstream tasks like image edit-
      ing (Mokady et al., 2023; Wallace et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2023; Huberman-Spiegelglas et al.,
511
      2023). Current methods typically include encoders (Richardson et al., 2021; Tov et al., 2021), op-
512
      timization (Mokady et al., 2023; Abdal et al., 2019), and model fine-tuning or modulation (Roich
513   et al., 2022; Alaluf et al., 2022). For diffusion-based models, the most common inversion method
514   is based on solving PF ODE backward, as first proposed by Song et al. (2021a). However, since
515   the ODE solver relies on local linearization, this ODE-based inversion typically provides an insuf-
516   ficient reconstruction, especially when using fewer diffusion steps. One way to address this issue is
517   to perform gradient optimization using the ODE-based inversion as an initialization, as introduced
518   by Mokady et al. (2023). Our proposed BCM can provide better inversion with fewer NFEs and is
519   compatible with Mokady et al. (2023)’s approach for a better reconstruction quality.
520
521   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
522
523
      In this work, we introduce the Bidirectional Consistency Model (BCM), enhancing upon existing
      consistency models (Song et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2024; Kim et al., 2024) by establishing
524
      a stronger consistency. This consistency ensures that points along the same trajectory of the proba-
525
      bility flow (PF) ODE map to each other, thereby unifying generation and inversion tasks within one
526   framework. By exploiting its bidirectional consistency, we devise new sampling schemes and show-
527   case applications in a variety of downstream tasks. Unlike other distillation approaches, a notable
528   property of CMs is their ability to enable fast traversal along the entire PF ODE. Our proposed BCM
529   further enriches this, and we believe that it will open a new avenue for further exploration.
530
      One limitation of our method is that while employing more steps in generation or inversion can
531
      initially enhance results, the performance improvements tend to plateau quickly. Increasing the
532   Number of Function Evaluations (NFEs) beyond a certain point does not yield further performance
533   gains. This is similar to CMs, where there is no performance gain with more than 2 NFEs. A
534   potential solution involves employing the parameterization and tricks proposed by Kim et al. (2024).
535   Additionally, our method delivers imperfect inversion, which sometimes alters the image content.
536   However, we should note that this also happens in ODE-based DMs. Future work can involve
537   developing more accurate inversion techniques, like the approach by Wallace et al. (2023).

       Fine-tuning BCM on ImageNet-64. We initialize our BCM with the pretrained iCT weights and
1088
       fine-tune it using Equation (10). Recall that BCM takes in three arguments: 1) the sample xt at time
1089
       t, 2) the current time step t, and 3) the target time step u, and outputs the sample at time u, i.e., xu .
1090   In contrast, the pretrained iCT model only takes the first two inputs and can be viewed as a special
1091   case of BCM when the target time step u is set to 0. Therefore, we aim to initialize BCM such that it
1092   preserves the performance of the pretrained CM when u “ 0. To achieve this, we carefully initialize
1093   the BCM as follows:
1094
1095          • In iCT, the time embedding of t is passed through a 2-layer MLP with SiLU activation,
1096            after which it is fed into the residual blocks. At the initialization of BCM, we duplicate the
1097            2-layer MLP (with the same pretrained weights) for u. However, during fine-tuning, the
1098
                MLPs for t and u are not tied.
1099          • We do not want to expand the input dimension of the pretrained residual blocks, so first
1100            concatenate the embedding of t and u together, and then reduce its dimension by half with
1101            a simple linear layer (without bias). No additional activation function is applied for this
1102            simple linear layer.
1103          • In order to maintain the generation quality of the pretrained iCT, we initialize this linear
1104            projection as rI, 0s, in which I represents an identity matrix and 0 represents a zero matrix.
1105            As a result, the influence brought by the newly introduced time embedding of u is zeroed
1106            at the beginning and learned gradually during fine-tuning.
1107
       With the modified architecture initialized with the pretrained CM weights, we tune the models using
1108
       our proposed BCT loss till convergence, using the same learning rate, ema rate, etc., as in iCT. The
1109
       only difference is that we start the fine-tuning from N “ 320 and increase it to N “ 480 and
1110   subsequently N “ 640. Specifically,
1111
1112          • we tune BCM for 210k, 16k and 8k iterations respectively at N “ 320, 480 and 640.
1113
              • we tune BCM-deep for 360k, 100k and 10k iterations respectively at N “ 320, 480 and
1114            640.

1116   The number of iterations for different values of N is determined empirically: we increase N when
1117   we observe no performance improvements by extending the training duration at the same N .

1387   This section provides some insights into the learned “embedding” space. Recall that during inversion
1388   (Algorithm 5), we first inject a small Gaussian noise to the image. Here we investigate the influence
1389   of this noise and the original image content on the noise generated by inversion.
1390   We randomly select 500 CIFAR-10 images, and randomly split them into 10 groups. We then invert
1391   the images to their corresponding noise by Algorithm 5. We inject the same initial noise during
1392   inversion for images in the same group. Figure 15 visualize the t-SNE results Van der Maaten &
1393   Hinton (2008) of the inversion outcomes. In Figure 15a, images injected with the same initial noise
1394   are shown in the same color; while in Figure 15b, we color the points according to their class label
1395   (i.e., airplane, bird, cat, ...).
1396   Interestingly, we can see that images inverted with the same initial noise are clustered together. We,
1397   therefore, conjecture that each initial noise corresponds to a submanifold in the final “embedding”
1398   space. The union of all these submanifolds constitutes the final “embedding” space, which is the
1399   typical set of N p0, T 2 Iq, closed to a hypersphere. This explains why applying the same initial noise
1400   is suboptimal in interpolation, as discussed in Appendix C.3.1.
