011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have shown superiority in various data mining
014            tasks but rely heavily on extensively labeled nodes. To improve the training effi-
015            ciency and select the most valuable nodes as the training set, graph active learning
016            (GAL) has gained much attention. However, previous GAL methods are designed
017            for homophilic graphs, and their effectiveness on heterophilic graphs is less ex-
018
               amined. In this paper, we study active learning on heterophilic graphs, where
               nodes with the same labels are less likely to be connected. We are surprised to
019
               find that previous GAL methods fail to outperform the naive random sampling on
020
               heterophilic graphs. Through an insightful investigation, we find that previous
021            GAL-selected training sets imply homophily even on heterophilic graphs, leading
022            to their defectiveness. To address this issue, we propose the principle of “Know
023            Your Neighbors” and design an active learning algorithm KyN specifically for
024            heterophilic graphs. The primary idea of KyN is to let GNNs receive a correct ho-
025            mophily distribution by labeling nodes along with their neighbors. We build KyN
026            based on subgraph sampling with probabilities proportional to ℓ1 Lewis weights,
027            which has a solid theoretical guarantee. The effectiveness of KyN is evaluated on
028            various real-world datasets.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graphs are ubiquitous in real-world applications, from recommendation system (Ma et al., 2024;
034   Ni et al., 2024) and misconduct detection (Tao et al., 2024; Wu & Hooi, 2023) to AI for science
035   (Gasteiger et al., 2021; Lam et al., 2023). Recently, graph neural networks (Kipf & Welling, 2017;
036   Wu et al., 2019a; Velickovic et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020) have become the de facto standard
037   used in many graph learning tasks. Like other deep learning methods, the success of GNNs largely
038   depends on the existence of high-quality training labels, and data labeling for these node samples
039   is costly due to its reliance on human labor. To address this challenge, graph active learning has
040
      emerged as an effective approach for improving data efficiency (Song et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
      2022a). GAL methods aim to maximize model performance by identifying the most informative
041
      nodes for annotation within a given labeling budget. Despite their success, we are surprised to find
042
      that previous GAL methods are only examined on homophilic graphs, i.e., nodes with the same
043   labels are more likely to be connected. As heterophilic graph learning becomes a popular research
044   direction, it is intriguing to ask:
045
046                       Do graph active learning methods work with heterophily?
047
048   The answer to this question is, unfortunately, no. We examined the performance of representative
049   GAL methods on the heterophilic graph dataset, Roman-empire (Platonov et al., 2023b). The results
050   are presented in Figure 1. The essential requirement of GAL methods is to consistently outperform
051   the uniformly random sampling, since they are not very likely to be faster or simpler. However,
052   we observe that none of the GAL methods can fulfill such requirements on this graph. Overall,
053   uniformly random sampling might even be the strongest approach! These GAL methods work well
      on homophilic graphs, so this finding is highly unexpected.

071   The defectiveness of GALs on heterophilic graphs is not only a new issue, but also a fatal one.
072   According to recent surveys (Luan et al., 2024), heterophilic graphs are prevalent in many real-
073   world applications, e.g., fraud/anomaly detection (Gao et al., 2023) and graph clustering (Pan &
074   Kang, 2023). If one adopts off-the-shelf GAL methods on these graphs without knowing their pitfall,
075   lots of time and human resources will be wasted. On the other hand, GNNs are known to be less
076   optimal for heterophilic graphs, sometimes even outperformed by graph-agnostic models (Loveland
077   et al., 2023). The last thing we want is a GAL method that is worse than random sampling to fuel
078   the flames.
079   In this paper, we aim to develop an active learning algorithm for heterophilic graphs to fill the gap.
080   Our method is motivated by issues of previous GALs. Specifically, we find that (1) existing GALs
081   fail to let GNNs “know” whether a graph is homophilic or heterophilic, since they cannot pro-
082   duce the correct local homophily distribution. And (2) the fusion of ego-embeddings and neighbor-
083   embeddings on heterophilic graphs makes nodes less distinguishable. To address the above prob-
084   lems, we propose the principle of “Know Your Neighbors” and dub the model as KyN. By selecting
085
      nodes along with their neighbors, KyN yields the correct local homophily distribution. We build
      KyN with a novel ℓ1 Lewis weights subgraph sampling. KyN has a solid theoretical guarantee and
086
      is evaluated against various baselines on real-world datasets.
087
088   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
089
090                 • We uncover the unexpected failure of active learning methods on heterophilic graphs.
091
                      While they show their power on homophilic graphs, they are outperformed by the naive
                      random sampling on heterophilic graphs. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
092
                      paper to reveal this phenomenon.
093
094                 • We propose a novel method called KyN for active learning on heterophilic graphs. Our
095
                      method is well-motivated by existing issues of previous GALs. KyN select training nodes
                      along with their neighbors to yield a correct local homophily distribution that reflects the
096
                      true homophilic/heterophilic nature of graphs.
097
098                 • We conduct comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the superior performance of KyN
099
                      on the heterophilic GAL task.

      3.1   D O GAL- SELECTED TRAINING SETS “ TELL” THEIR HETEROPHILY ?
140
141   Before designing a GAL algorithm that works with heterophily, we first investigate why previous
142   GALs perform poorly on these graphs. Conceptually, GNNs cannot “know” whether a graph is
143   homophilic or heterophilic if the training set does not “tell” them about it. Thus it is natural to
144   first investigate the homophily-related information contained in GAL-selected training sets Vtrain .
145   As local homophily emerges as an important statistic for heterophilic graphs, we can measure the
146   homophily-related informativeness by the closeness between the distribution of local homophily
147
      of the training sets and that of the whole graph (i.e., the ground truth). Specifically, let Pht (G)
      be the local homophily distribution of the whole graph that can be empirically estimated with A
148
      and Y . And let Pht (Gtrain ) be the local homophily distribution of a training set. Since we only
149
      know the labels of nodes in this training set, the distribution received by GNNs should be estimated
150   with Atrain and Ytrain , where Atrain is the subgraph induced by the training set and Ytrain is the
151   labels of the training set. In other words, we do not count the unlabeled neighbors when investi-
152   gating the homophily distribution of GAL-selected training sets. For some statistical distance D, a
153   GAL-selected training set that correctly contains homophily-related information should have small
154   D(Pht (G), Pht (Gtrain )), since it reflects the real distribution of local node homophily. We use the
155   kernel density estimation to approximate the homophily distribution of each GAL-selected training
156   set and present them in Figure 2.

160   dataset. However, we observe that previous GAL methods select training sets that show homophilic
161   properties on this heterophilic dataset, i.e., the distributions are left-skewed. It is then clear why
      GNN trained on these labeled sets fails to produce a satisfactory result: the model is given wrong,

195   But why do previous GALs select homophilic training sets on a heterophilic graph? We argue that
196   these GALs query nodes without their neighbors, leading to many isolated nodes in the induced

198   ht = 1) since they are the only labeled nodes in their own neighborhood, resulting in an inaccurate
199   homophily distribution. Therefore, the solution is rather straightforward: for heterophilic graphs,
200   we should label their neighbors together with the selected nodes, which embodies the principle of

      4     R ELATED W ORK

      Active learning is a classic research direction that aims to mitigate annotation expenses (Ren et al.,
318   2021; Matsushita et al., 2018). It is studied in many fields and under different settings, including
319   computer vision (Bengar et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021), nature language processing (Zhang et al.,
320   2022b; Margatina et al., 2023) and general deep learning (Huang et al., 2024b; Yan & Huang, 2018;
321   Tang & Huang, 2022). In the graph realm, AGE (Cai et al., 2017) is one of the earliest works
322   that measure the informativeness of nodes by combining centrality, density, and uncertainty. AN-
323   RMAB (Gao et al., 2018) improves AGE by learning weights using reinforcement learning. ALG
      (Zhang et al., 2021a) considers both the importance and correlation via the effective reception field


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      maximization. FeatProp (Wu et al., 2019b) first propagates features and then employs a clustering
325   algorithm on the propagated node features. GraphPart (Ma et al., 2023) further enhances FeatProp
326   by applying it to each graph partition. DOCTOR (Song et al., 2023) is a GAL method based on
327   the expected model change maximization. GreedyET (Huang et al., 2024c) treat GAL as the ag-
328   gregation involvement maximization. Some other papers focus on different settings that fit certain
329   applications, e.g., noise/soft label (Zhang et al., 2022a; 2021b; 2024), fairness (Han et al., 2024) and
330   transfer learning (Hu et al., 2020).
331   Graph neural networks under heterophily is an emerging topic in the graph realm. In heterophilic
332   graphs, the nodes with the same labels are not more, sometimes even less, likely to be connected.
333   The fusion phase of ordinary GNNs in these diverse neighborhoods makes nodes indistinguishable,
334   leading to unsatisfactory performance. Various model architectures are proposed to address this
335   challenge. H2 GCN (Zhu et al., 2020) is an early work on heterophily identifying designs crucial
336   to the heterophily setting. CPGNN (Zhu et al., 2021) models different levels of homophily using a
337   learnable class compatibility matrix in the aggregation step. GPR-GNN (Chien et al., 2021) is the
338   generalized PageRank-inspired architecture designed to adapt to different label patterns. FAGCN
339
      (Bo et al., 2021) adaptively integrates different signals in the process of message passing with a self-
      gating mechanism. GloGNN (Li et al., 2022) generates node embedding by aggregating information
340
      from global nodes in the graph. GGCN (Yan et al., 2022) learns degree corrections and signed
341
      messages based on a unified theoretical perspective for heterophily and oversmoothing. M2M-
342   GNN (Liang et al., 2024) unveil some potential pitfalls of signed message passing and design a
343   new scheme to address the problem of undesirable representation update for multi-hop neighbors
344   and vulnerability against oversmoothing issues. UniFilter (Huang et al., 2024a) develop an adaptive
345   heterophily basis, this basis is then integrated with the homophily basis to construct a universal
346   polynomial basis. In our paper, we train the GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) to evaluate the
347   quality of GAL-selected training sets, since it is one of the few basic GNN encoders that work with
348   heterophily (Platonov et al., 2023b). We want to point out that GAL methods can be used with
349   all previously mentioned GNNs that designed for heterophilic graphs. We omit such combinations
350
      without loss of generality.
351   Coreset is a research field that is very close to active learning. The main difference between the two
352   problems is that we have access to labels before training set selection, but many coreset methods do
353   not use labels so that they can be used for active learning. There are sampling works that focus on
354   ℓ2 -regression (Drineas et al., 2006; Li et al., 2013; Cohen et al., 2015) and ℓ1 -regression (Clarkson,
355   2005; Sohler & Woodruff, 2011; Clarkson et al., 2016). Recent works show that coresets with
356
      relative error can be constructed on bounded complexity data for the logistic loss and hinge loss
      (Munteanu et al., 2019; Mai et al., 2021). Sampling-based coreset methods are also used for fields
357
      of active learning, e.g., multiple deep models active learning (Huang et al., 2024b). To the best of
358
      our knowledge, this paper is the first to explore Lewis weight sampling for graph active learning.

365   We first compare KyN with other GAL methods on various real-world datasets: Roman-empire,

      et al., 2020). We set the labeling budget to 5C, 10C, and 20C, where C is the number of classes in
367
      each dataset. This setting is common in previous GAL research (e.g., (Han et al., 2024)).
368
369   According to previous research, the message-passing approach of GCN is not suitable for het-
370   erophilic graphs, while SAGE-Mean shows relatively stable performance (Platonov et al., 2023b).
371   This is because SAGE-Mean allows the “negative-aggregation” by concatenating the ego-embedding
372
      and neighbor-embedding. Therefore, to eliminate any additional impact from the encoder, we used
      SAGE-Mean as the GNN encoder instead of GCN on heterophilic graphs. We also used some GNNs
373
      designed specifically for heterophilic graphs as backbones to do a small number of experiments.
374
      Since the conclusions are consistent, we mainly use SAGE-Mean for simplicity and readability. We
375   also provide the formula of SAGE-mean for readers who are not familiar with this encoder:

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we investigate a new research problem, heterophilic graph active learning. We observe
528   that previous GAL methods that work perfectly on homophilic graphs fail to outperform naive ran-
529   dom sampling on heterophilic graphs. Through an insightful investigation of the local homophily
530   distribution, we find that previous GAL-selected training sets imply homophilic properties on het-
531   erophilic graphs. We argue that the previous design principle of informativeness and coverage on
532   graphs will inevitably produce isolated training nodes that is harmful for heterophilic GALs. To ad-
533   dress this issue, we propose a novel principle of “know your neighbors” and dub our model as KyN.
534   KyN unveils the homophilic/heterophilic nature of graphs by labeling nodes along with their neigh-
535
      bors. We implement KyN with ℓ1 Lewis weights sampling, which has solid theoretical guarantees.
      Extensive experiments show the effectiveness of our method.
