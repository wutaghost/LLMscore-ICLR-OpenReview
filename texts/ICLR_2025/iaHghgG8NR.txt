009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern sequence models (e.g., Transformers, linear RNNs, etc.) emerged as
012            dominant backbones of recent deep learning frameworks, mainly due to their effi-
013            ciency, representational power, and/or ability to capture long-range dependencies.
014            Adopting these sequence models for graph-structured data has recently gained
015            popularity as the alternative to Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs).
016
               There is, however, a lack of a common foundation about what constitutes a good
               graph sequence model, and a mathematical description of the benefits and defi-
017
               ciencies in adopting different sequence models for learning on graphs. To this end,
018
               we first present Graph Sequence Model (GSM), a unifying framework for adopt-
019            ing sequence models for graphs, consisting of three main steps: (1) Tokenization,
020            which translates the graph into a set of sequences; (2) Local Encoding, which
021            encodes local neighborhoods around each node; and (3) Global Encoding, which
022            employs a scalable sequence model to capture long-range dependencies within
023            the sequences. This framework allows us to understand, evaluate, and compare
024            the power of different sequence model backbones in graph tasks. Our theoreti-
025            cal evaluations of the representation power of Transformers and modern recurrent
026            models through the lens of global and local graph tasks show that there are both
027
               negative and positive sides for both types of models. Building on this observa-
               tion, we present GSM++, a fast hybrid model that uses the Hierarchical Affinity
028
               Clustering (HAC) algorithm to tokenize the graph into hierarchical sequences, and
029
               then employs a hybrid architecture of Transformer to encode these sequences. Our
030            theoretical and experimental results support the design of GSM++, showing that
031            GSM++ outperforms baselines in most benchmark evaluations.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Message-passing graph neural networks (MPNNs) have been the leading approach for processing
037   graph data (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Gilmer et al., 2017a; Chami et al., 2020; Morris et al., 2020).
038   However, with the increasing popularity of Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) in natu-
039
      ral language processing and computer vision, recent research has shifted towards developing graph
      Transformers (GTs), which are designed to handle the complexities of graph-structured data more
040
      effectively. Graph Transformers have demonstrated compelling results, particularly by leading in
041
      tasks like molecular property prediction (Ying et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2020; Masters et al., 2023).
042   Their advantage over traditional MPNNs is often attributed to tendency of MPNNs to focus on local
043   structures, making them less effective at capturing global or long-range relationships due to issues
044   like over-smoothing (Li et al., 2018), over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2020; Di Giovanni et al.,
045   2023; Dwivedi et al., 2022b), and restricted expressive power (Barceló et al., 2020). These limita-
046   tions could potentially harm the model’s performance. In contrast, GTs (Rampášek et al., 2022) can
047   aggregate information from all nodes across the graph, reducing the local structure bias.
048   Traditional Transformer-based architectures, while powerful for sequence analysis, face limitations
049   in scalability in long-context tasks due to their quadratic computational complexity. Various strate-
050   gies have been proposed to mitigate this issue (Tay et al., 2022), including sparsifying the dense
051   attention matrix (Zaheer et al., 2020; Beltagy et al., 2020a; Roy et al., 2020; Kitaev et al., 2020),
052   low-rank approximations of the attention matrix (Wang et al., 2020), and kernel-based attention
053   mechanisms (Choromanski et al., 2020b; Kacham et al., 2024). While these methods enhance
      computational efficiency, they often come at the expense of reduced expressiveness (Mehta et al.,


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2022b). In recent years, attention-free sequence models have emerged as a promising alternative to
055   Transformers for sequence modeling. Leveraging recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Orvieto et al.,
056   2023; Peng et al., 2023; Beck et al., 2024) and long convolutions (Poli et al., 2023a; Karami &
057   Ghodsi, 2024), offer sub-quadratic, hardware-efficient sequence mixing operators that can capture
058   long-range dependencies with strong generalization on sequences of varying lengths.
059
      Given the promising potential of the sub-quadratic sequence models, there is growing interest in ex-
060
      tending them to the graph domain as an alternative to graph Transformers (Ding et al., 2023; Behrouz
061   & Hashemi, 2024; Huang et al., 2024). However, a significant technical challenge arises from the in-
062   herent differences between graphs and other structured data, such as text, which is naturally causal.
063   Graphs exhibit a complex topology and lack a natural, linear node ordering. Attempting to impose a
064   naive tokenization strategy, such as sorting nodes into a sequence, undermines the crucial inductive
065   bias of permutation equivariance inherent to graphs. This misrepresentation of graph structure can
066   lead to poor generalization performance. Furthermore, there is a lack of a common foundation about
067   what constitutes a good graph sequence model, and a mathematical description of the benefits and
068   deficiencies of adopting different sequence models for learning on graphs.
069   In this study, we introduce a robust and unified model that offers both flexibility and adaptability for
070   designing models intended for learning on graphs. This approach facilitates the effortless creation
071   of diverse models and allows researchers to efficiently explore and compare various architectures.
072   Using this method, we conduct both theoretical and empirical analyses of graph sequence models
073   by evaluating their performance on tasks such as graph connectivity and counting, as well as by
074   investigating the role of node orderings. Our findings indicate that while the permutation equivari-
075
      ance of Transformer-based models is often considered a desirable property, it limits their capacity to
      execute counting tasks on graphs effectively. Additionally, we demonstrate that causal SSM/RNN
076
      based models, are not theoretically bounded when applied to color counting tasks on graphs, high-
077
      lighting the power of these architectures in specific graph learning scenarios. These findings are the
078   first steps toward better understanding of the power of graph sequence models beyond traditional
079   metrics (e.g., WL test (Shirzad et al., 2023; Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024)) and can help to answer
080   what types of sequence models are the best, given the type of the task at hand.
081
      We identify the strengths and weaknesses of different tokenization strategies, and to overcome their
082
      limitations, we present a novel hierarchical tokenization that theoretically provides advantages over
083
      existing methods. This approach, combined with a hybrid sequence model achieves improved per-
084   formance across diverse graph tasks. Our experiments validate the effectiveness of this hybrid archi-
085   tecture, offering a more flexible and comprehensive solution than existing models. These insights
086   contribute to a broader understanding of model capabilities and limitations, informing the develop-
087   ment of more specialized models for graph-based learning tasks.
088
089   Contributions and Roadmap. In §2, we present Graph Sequence Model (GSM) framework, that
090   can help us to systematically study the power of GSMs in different scenarios. We then in §3 aim
091   to understand strengths and weaknesses of different types sequence models for graph tasks. To this
092
      end, in §3.1, we show how recurrent nature of a model can help it to perform tasks like counting
      more effectively, while permutation equivariance of Transformers make them unable to count. In
093
      §3.2, we analyze sequence models through the lens of sensitivity. We show that while linear recur-
094
      rent models (e.g., SSMs) have a better inductive bias about the nodes’ distance, this advantages can
095   cause representational collapse in deep models. Using these results, we motivate a combination of
096   transformers and SSMs so the SSM module can enhance the inductive bias, and the permutation
097   equivariance of Transformer can avoid representational collapse in the model. In §3.3, we evaluate
098   the reasoning capability of graph sequence models through the lens of connectivity tasks. We show
099   that Transformers are more effective than recurrent models in such tasks, but with a small modifica-
100   tion of the tokens’ order, recurrent models can become extremely efficient. In §3.4, we theoretically
101   analyze the effect of tokenization methods (node or subgraph), and how it can help to improve the
102   efficiency and solve the fundamental problem of motif counting in graphs. Given our theoretical ob-
103
      servations and what we have learned from these results, in §4, we present GSM++ that uses a novel
      tokenization based on the Hierarchical Affinity Clustering (HAC) tree. GSM++ further employs a
104
      hybrid sequence model with two layers of SSM followed by a transformer block. We then present a
105
      Mixture of Tokenization (MoT), allowing the combination of different sets of tokenizations that are
106   the best for each node, to further enhance the effectiveness and efficiency of GSM++.
107
      The proof of theorems and additional theoretical results are in Appendices D, E, and F.

121   2     E NCODING G RAPHS TO S EQUENCES : A U NIFIED M ODEL
122
123   Despite a variety of GNNs with diverse modules that are designed based on sequence models, we
124   find that each part of these architectures is responsible for encoding a specific characteristic of the
125   graph. To formalize this, in this section, we present our unified model consisting of three main
126   stages: (1) Tokenization, (2) Local Encoding, and (3) Global Encoding.
127
128   2.1   T OKENIZATION
129
      Sequence models are inherently designed to process sequences of tokens. To adapt them for graph-
130
      structured data, the graph must first be translated into a set of sequences (Müller et al., 2024; Behrouz
131   & Hashemi, 2024; Ding et al., 2023). These approaches can be categorized into two main groups:
132
133   Node/Edge Tokenizers. Node or edge tokenization methods treat the graph as a sequence of
134   node/edges without considering how they are connected. Accordingly, these methods lack induc-
135   tive bias about the graph structure and so they require to be augmented with positional or struc-
136   tural encoding to inject information about the graph structure. Let G = (V, E), be a graph,
137   V = {v1 , . . . , v|V | } is the set of nodes, and P ∈ Rn×d is the positional/structural encoding ma-
138   trix, whose rows encode the position of nodes. In this case, we translate the graph as a sequence
139
      of: G := Pv1 , Pv2 , . . . , Pv|V | Similarly, for edge tokenization we can replace {v1 , . . . , v|V | } with
140   {e1 , . . . , e|E| }. The main drawback of methods based on node tokenization is their computational
141
      complexity. That is, treating the graph as a sequence of nodes (resp. edges) results in having a
      sequence with length |V | (resp. |E|), meaning        that for quadratic models (e.g., Transformers) the
142
      training time complexity is at least O |V |2 (resp. O |E|2 ).
143
144
      Subgraph Tokenizers. To reduce the computational cost of node tokenization and incorporate
145   inductive bias, several methods propose treating the graph as a sequence or sequences of subgraphs
146   and then encode these sequences using a sequence model. Formally, given a graph G = (V, E), the
147   graph can be represented as a set of sequences of subgraphs:
148                                                                (i)            (i)           (i)
149
                G := {S (1) , . . . , S (T ) }, where   S (i) = G[H1 ], . . . , G[Hℓ ] and Hj ⊆ V.              (1)
150   When T < |V |, we refer to this process as patching. A pioneer approach in this direction is Deep-
151   Walk (Perozzi et al., 2014), which uses random walks to sample from the graph and tokenize it
152   into a set of sequences. A more recent method is the k-hop neighborhood tokenization used by
153   NAGphormer (Chen et al., 2023), where each node’s hierarchical neighborhood is treated as its rep-
154   resentative sequence. For further discussion and examples of these methods, see Appendix B. Since
                                      (i)

157   Although there is a variety of studies across the aforementioned categories, a common foundation
158   is still lacking regarding what constitutes effective tokenization and what differentiates them with
159   respect to the task. In Section 3, we theoretically show that each of node and subgraph tokenizations
160   offer their own advantages and disadvantages. Accordingly, the choice between node tokenization,
161   subgraph tokenization, or a combination of both depends on the specific task at hand (see Sec-
      tion 4.3). We further validate this theoretical foundation using several experiments in Section 5.1.

194   3     C HOOSING A S EQUENCE M ODEL
195
196   One critical question remains – what sequence model should one use? Following the above men-
197   tioned framework, one can simply replace different sequence encoders in the global encoding stage
198   and combine them with different tokenization methods, resulting in hundreds of potential graph
199   learning models. However, there is a lack of a common foundation about what constitutes a good
200   model in each of these stages, and a mathematical description of the benefits and deficiencies of
201   adopting different sequence models for learning on graphs. To this end, in this section, we theo-
202   retically discuss the advantages and disadvantages of different tokenizations and different sequence
203
      models in several graph tasks, providing a guideline for the future research and model developments.
204
205   3.1   C OUNTING TASKS ON G RAPHS
206
      In the first part, we focus on counting tasks, where the objective is to count the number of nodes with
207   each particular color in a node-colored graph. Such tasks are analogous to the copying tasks in se-
208   quence modeling, which are common benchmarks to measure the abilities of a sequence model (Ar-
209   jovsky et al., 2016; Gu & Dao, 2023; Barbero et al., 2024), in the sense that counting tasks require
210   considering all nodes and even missing a single node’s color can potentially result in incorrect pre-
211   diction. Hence, let’s first recall a proposition on the inability of Transformers in counting tasks:
212   P ROPOSITION 1 (P ROPOSITION 6.1 OF BARBERO ET AL . (2024)). A Transformer model based
213   on non-causal attention and without proper positional encodings is immediately unable to count.
214
215   This limitation of non-causal Transformers in counting tasks raises an important question: Can the
      inherent causality of recurrent models resolve this issue, and are they better suited for such tasks?

220   permutation equivariance, recurrent models are more powerful than non-causal Transformers.

222   3.2    I MPORTANCE OF N ODE O RDERING
223
224   As discussed earlier, due to the sequential nature of some graph tasks, the permutation equivariant
225
      property of non-causal Transformers can undermine their representational power. Beyond simple
      counting tasks, several important and complex graph datasets and tasks—such as neural algorithmic
226
      reasoning tasks in sequential algorithms (Xu & Veličković, 2024) and CLRS dataset (Bentley, 1984;
227
      Gavril, 1972)—involve naturally ordered nodes, requiring a causal encoder to effectively capture
228   their inherent order. On the other hand, most subgraph tokenizers produce sequences with an im-
229   plicit order (e.g., k-hop neighborhoods), which requires a causal model to capture their hierarchy.

231   the GSM framework requires additional considerations. Accordingly, in this section, we analyze
232   how node ordering can impact the performance of the model, and if there is an ordering mechanism
233   for nodes that can enhance the performance of causal sequence models.

276   3.3   C ONNECTIVITY TASKS ON G RAPHS
277
278   This section addresses the graph connectivity task, which requires the sequence model to capture a
279   global understanding of the graph. We frame graph connectivity as a binary classification problem,
280   where the input is a tokenized graph G = (V, E), and the target output is 1 if G is connected and 0
281
      otherwise. Using edge tokenization, we represent the graph as the sequence G := Pe1 , . . . , Pe|E| .
282   C OROLLARY 3 (C OROLLARY 3.3 OF S ANFORD ET AL . (2024 B )). For any N and ϵ ∈ (0, 1),
283   there exists a transformer with depth O(log N ) and embedding dimension O(N ϵ ) that determines
284   whether any graph G = (V, E) with |V |, |E| ≤ N is connected.
285
      Next, we show that alternative architectures cannot solve graph connectivity with such low-
286
      dimensional parameterization.
287
288   T HEOREM 3. A multi-layer recurrent model, a Transformer with kernel-based sub-quadratic atten-
289
      tion, or a Transformer with locally masked attention units of radius r that solves graph connectivity
290
      on all graphs G = (V, E) with |V |, |E| ≤ N has either depth L = Ω(N 1/8 ) or m = Ω̃(N 1/4 ).
291

292   parameter-efficient connectivity solutions. All recurrent models, kernel-based transformers with

      4.1   H IERARCHICAL A FFINITY C LUSTERING (HAC) FOR T OKENIZATION

      As discussed in Section 3.2, using a tokenizer that generates an ordered sequence, where similar
347   nodes are positioned near each other, can improve the sensitivity of the method, thereby enhancing
348   its representational power. Furthermore, as discussed in Section 4, when representing a graph as a
349   sequence with node locality k (Definition 1), powerful recurrent models become very efficient for
350   global tasks like connectivity. Motivated by these results, we present a hierarchical tokenization
351   based on the Hierarchical Affinity Clustering (HAC) (Bateni et al., 2017) algorithm and show that it
352   satisfies the above desirable characteristics.
353   HAC is a highly scalable and parallelizable clustering algorithm based on Boruvka’s algo-
354   rithm (Boruuvka, 1926) (see Appendix A.3 for backgrounds). Given a graph G = (V, E) and
355   node encodings Pv1 , . . . , Pv|V | , the algorithm begins by treating each vertex as a singleton cluster,
356   then at each step removes the cheapest edge (cost calculated by the similarity of node encodings)
357   going out of each cluster and join these two clusters to form a larger cluster. This process continues
358   until a cluster includes all the nodes. The stages of this algorithm form a HAC tree, where the root
359   represents the last cluster in the algorithm (entire graph), its two children are the last two clusters in
360   one round before the end of the algorithm, and so forth. Accordingly, leaves are nodes of the graph,
361
      which were our initial clusters. See Figure 2 for an example of a HAC tree.
362   HAC offers two key advantages for an effective tokenization. First, it orders nodes such that adjacent
363   nodes (having the same parent node) in the tree are the most similar, which is aligned with our
364   theoretical analysis. Second, it provides a hierarchical clustering, allowing for graph encoding at
365   different levels of granularity. We propose two types of tokenization based on Depth-First Search
366   (DFS) and Breadth-First Search (BFS) traversals of the HAC tree.
367   DFS Traverse of HAC Tree. After performing HAC and constructing the HAC tree, we perform
368   DFS traverse and treat each path as a sequence. That is, given a graph G = (V, E), let r be the root
369   of the tree, a DFS path in the HAC tree is G = r → ci1 → ci2 → · · · → cid = vi ∈ V , where r
370   represents the entire graph and cid represents node vi ∈ V . This sequence represents a hierarchy of
371   clusters whose nodes are similar to vi , and encodes the hierarchical position of vi in the graph. This
372   approach is a subgraph-based tokenization as discussed in Section 2.1.
373   BFS Traverse of HAC Tree. In this approach, we perform a BFS traverse on the HAC tree. Note
374   that the maximum depth of the tree is log2 (|V |) (Bateni et al., 2017). Let k ≤ log2 (|V |), we
375   treat k-th level of BFS traverse as a path, representing the graph at k-th level of granularity. When
376   k = 1, the length of the sequence is one and the only element is the root (entire graph). When k is
377   the depth of the tree, the sequence is the sequence of all nodes, but in an order that similar nodes
      are close to each other. In this tokenization method, we construct the sequences for all values of

      1 ≤ k ≤ log2 (|V |) and encode the graph at different levels of granularity. We consider a simple
379   average pooling to obtain the overall encodings.

408
      In Section 3.4 we show that there is not a single type of tokenization that works best in all the
409   cases. We further experimentally observe the same in Section 5.2. To this end, we suggest using a
410   Mixture of Tokenization (MoT) technique, where we allow each node to use a tokenization that best
411   describes its position based on the task. For example, one node might be better to be represented by
412   itself (along with a positional encoding) since its neighborhood is extremely noisy. At the same time,
413   another node might be better to be represented by its neighbors as there is a strong homophily in
414   that area of the graph. Let T be the list of different tokenizers, we use a discrete router that chooses
415   top-2 tokenizations from T for each node. We then concatenate the encodings of these tokenizers to
416   obtain the final encoding for the global encoding step. See Appendix A.4 for additional information.

      In this paper, we aim to understand Graph Sequence Models, a family of graph learning models that
533
      translate the graph into a (set) of sequence(s), vectorize it, and then employ powerful sequence mod-
534   els to learn dependencies of nodes. We provide extensive theoretical results to show the importance
535   of ordering, when it is needed, and to show that there is no single sequence model or tokenization
536   method that works strictly better for all graph algorithmic problems. Motivated by our theoretical
537   results, we present a model, called GSM++, with new hierarchical graph tokenization method based
538   on HAC, a new mixture of token (MoT) approach to take advantage of different tokenization, and a
539   hybrid sequence model based on Mamba and self-attention. Our experimental evaluations support
      the theoretical results and the design of GSM++.

1021   Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are particularly adept at handling sequential data thanks to their
1022

1023

1024

1048   A.3    H IERARCHICAL A FFINITY C LUSTERING (HAC) A LGORITHM
1049
1050   Hierarchical Affinity Clustering (HAC) (Bateni et al., 2017) is a powerful algorithm used to group
1051   data points based on their similarity or affinity, often represented by a distance measure such as Eu-
1052   clidean distance or cosine similarity. HAC organizes data in a hierarchical structure, either through
1053   an agglomerative (bottom-up) process, where each data point starts as its own cluster and the closest
1054
       clusters are progressively merged, or a divisive (top-down) process, which begins with all data points
       in a single cluster that is repeatedly split. The result of the clustering process can be visualized using
1055
       a dendrogram, showcasing the nested relationships between clusters at different levels of similarity.
1056
1057   Finding the affinity clustering of a given graph G is closely tied to the task of identifying its Min-
1058   imum Spanning Tree (MST). In fact, the information encoded in the MST of G is enough to de-
1059   termine its affinity clustering. Consequently, once the MST is computed, the affinity clustering or
1060
       single linkage can be obtained in a single step.
1061   T HEOREM 10. (Bateni et al., 2017) Let G = (V, E) denote an arbitrary graph, and let G′ =
1062   (V, E ′ ) denote the minimum spanning tree of G. Running the affinity clustering algorithm on G
1063
       produces the same clustering of V as running the algorithm on G′ .

1087
       Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers. utilize different approaches for process-
       ing graph data. GNNs typically employ a message-passing mechanism that collects and synthe-
1088
       sizes information from adjacent nodes into updated node representations (Kipf & Welling, 2016;
1089
       Xu et al., 2019; Velickovic et al., 2017). Despite their utility, these models exhibit limitations in
1090   expressiveness, equivalent to that of the 1-WL test, a traditional algorithm for testing graph isomor-
1091   phism (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Loukas, 2019). They also encounter challenges like
1092   over-smoothing and over-squashing, and struggle with capturing long-range dependencies (Alon
1093   & Yahav, 2021; Dwivedi et al., 2022b). In contrast, Graph Transformers make use of an atten-
1094   tion mechanism (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kim et al., 2022; Kreuzer et al., 2021) that enables
1095   attention to all nodes within a graph. Since utilizing full attention can obscure graph topology and
1096   render nodes non-distinguishable, numerous studies have concentrated on creating effective node
1097   encodings such as Laplacian positional encodings (Dwivedi et al., 2023; 2021; Maskey et al., 2022;
1098
       Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022), shortest path distance/random walk distance (Ying et al.,
       2021; Li et al., 2020; Perozzi et al., 2014), among others. Additionally, some approaches merge
1099
       Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) with full attention capabilities (Rampášek et al., 2022;
1100
       Chen et al., 2022). However, this full attention model scales quadratically with the size of the graph.
1101   To mitigate this complexity, certain studies have applied general linear attention techniques to Graph
1102   Transformers (Choromanski et al., 2020a; Rampášek et al., 2022), along with other specific strate-
1103   gies intended to optimize performance (Perozzi et al., 2024; Sanford et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2022).
1104
1105   Efficient Sequence Modeling. Recent advances in efficient sequence modeling have led to
1106   attention-free layers, such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), RWKV (Peng et al., 2023), and vari-
1107   ous gated RNNs, all featuring sub-quadratic complexity in sequence length and excellent scaling
1108   properties, enabling the construction of a new type of foundation models. These sub-quadratic se-
1109   quence models are defined in classical literature (Hyndman et al., 2008; Durbin & Koopman, 2001)
1110   as systems that model the dynamics of state variables over time through first-order differential or
1111
       difference equations, offering a cohesive structure for modeling time series. Like recurrent neural
       networks (RNNs) (Graves, 2013; Pascanu et al., 2012; Sutskever et al., 2014; Orvieto et al., 2023),
1112
       SSMs often struggle with retaining long-term contexts and managing long-range dependencies. To
1113
       overcome these challenges, a structured approach is introduced in (Gu et al., 2020), enhancing the
1114   capability of SSMs to incorporate long-range dependencies. Addressing the computational limita-
1115   tions inherent in SSMs, a set of innovations called Structural SSM (S4) (Gu et al., 2022a; 2021;
1116   2022b; Gupta & Berant, 2022) has been developed. These utilize specialized configurations of pa-
1117   rameter matrices, such as diagonal structures, to facilitate faster matrix operations. Simplifications
1118   and enhancements to this model include a real-domain variant of S4 proposed in (Ma et al., 2024;
1119   2022), an augmentation of S4 that incorporates data-dependent state transitions found in (Karami &
1120   Ghodsi, 2024; Hasani et al., 2022), and variations like those in (Li et al., 2022; Poli et al., 2023b;
1121   Qin et al., 2023; Kacham et al., 2024) which adopt different parameterizations of global convolu-
1122
       tional kernels. The evolution of SSMs has also inspired new hybrid neural architectures that blend
       elements of SSMs with other deep learning techniques (Mehta et al., 2022a; Beck et al., 2024). A
1123
       comprehensive review of these developments is available in (Wang et al., 2024b).
1124
1125

1299   The trade-offs in computational cost and model capability between standard transformers and alter-
1300
       native architectures have been well studied theoretically and empirically. For instance, the capabil-
       ities of state space and sub-quadratic models fall short of transformers in copying context (Jelassi
1301
       et al., 2024), multistep reasoning (Sanford et al., 2024b), and nearest neighbor search (Alman &
1302
       Song, 2023). Despite this, state space models learn certain tasks, such as the compositions of per-
1303   mutations, in a more depth-efficient manner than transformers (Merrill et al., 2024).
1304
1305   Because this paper designs graph sequence model architectures that employ state space models and
1306
       other alternatives, it is useful to understand the trade-offs between these architectures and trans-
       formers at fundamental graph algorithmic tasks. In this section, we provide explicit the trade-offs
1307
       between transformers and alternative models—including state space models (e.g. Mamba, Gu &
1308
       Dao, 2023), linear attention (e.g. PolySketchFormer, Kacham et al., 2024), and sparse attention
1309   (e.g. Longformer, Beltagy et al., 2020b). We discuss two particular particular architectural separa-
1310   tions for graph connectivity tasks that illuminate broader trade-offs in architectural capability.
1311
1312         1. Section E.1 discusses the existence of more parameter-efficient transformers that solve
1313            graph connectivity than sub-quadratic architectures and state space models.
