                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012             Many existing jailbreak techniques rely on solving discrete combinatorial opti-
013             mization, while more recent approaches involve training LLMs to generate multi-
014             ple adversarial prompts. However, both approaches require significant computa-
015             tional resources to produce even a single adversarial prompt. We hypothesize that
016             the inefficiency of current approaches stems from an inadequate characterization
017             of the jailbreak problem. To address this gap, we formulate the jailbreak prob-
018             lem in terms of alignment. By starting from an available safety-aligned model,
019             we leverage an unsafe reward to guide the safe model towards generating un-
020
                safe outputs using alignment techniques (e.g., reinforcement learning from human
                feedback), effectively performing jailbraking via alignment. We propose a novel
021
                jailbreak method called LIAR (LeveragIng Alignment to jailbReak). To demon-
022
                strate the simplicity and effectiveness of our approach, we employ a best-of-N
023             method. LIAR offers significant advantages: lower computational requirements
024             without additional training, fully black-box operation, competitive attack success
025             rates, and more human-readable prompts. We provide theoretical insights into the
026             possibility of jailbreaking a safety-aligned model, revealing inherent vulnerabil-
027             ities in current alignment strategies for LLMs. We also provide sub-optimality
028             guarantees for the proposed LIAR. Experimentally, we achieve ASR comparable
029             to the SoTA with a 10x improvement to perplexity and a Time-to-Attack measured
030             in seconds rather than tens of hours.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Aligning artificial intelligence (AI) systems is essential to ensure they behave safely, ethically, and in
039   accordance with human values (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). State-of-the-art (SoTA)
040   large language models (LLMs) are trained using safe preference data to adhere to these standards,
041   preventing the generation of harmful, biased, or unethical content (Ziegler et al., 2019; Glaese et al.,
042   2022). Despite these efforts, recent jailbreak methods have successfully bypassed safety mecha-
043   nisms, exposing vulnerabilities in LLMs (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Paulus et al., 2024).
044
      These jailbreaks are designed to find adversarial prompts or suffixes that bypass the safety filters of
      a model and induce the generation of harmful content (Zou et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
045
      2023). Formulated as discrete combinatorial optimization problems, they involve searching through
046
      an immense text space, which is inefficient and computationally expensive (Liu et al., 2023). As a
047   result, many current methods require significant time to generate a successful adversarial prompt.
048   Although improvements have been made, such as the conditional suffix generator model proposed
049   in (Paulus et al., 2024), these methods still suffer from inherent challenges of combinatorial search.
050   Appendix A provides a detailed additional context of related works.
051
052   One of the key inefficiencies in existing jailbreak approaches stems from the lack of a rigorous
053   characterization of the precise connection between LLM alignment and vulnerability to jailbreaks.
      This raises critical questions: If these LLMs are truly aligned and safe, why are they still susceptible

      To address these questions, we introduce an alignment formulation for the jailbreak problem. Jail-
081
      breaking via alignment refers to the process of breaking the safety alignment of an AI model by us-
082   ing an unsafe reward signal. Starting with a model aligned through techniques like RLHF (Ouyang
083   et al., 2022), we show that it is both theoretically and practically possible to misalign the model
084   using alignment. Our framework provides a rigorous explanation for why jailbreaks are possible,
085   even in models trained with safety considerations. To validate our approach, we introduce LIAR
086   (Leveraging Inverse Alignment to jailbReak), which utilizes a best-of-N (Amini et al., 2024) to
087   significantly improve the efficiency of jailbreak attacks. Our main contributions are as follows.
088
089   (1) Jailbreaking LLMs via Alignment. By formulating jailbreaking as an alignment problem, we
090   demonstrate its effectiveness using a simple best-of-N alignment strategy. Our proposed method,
091
      LIAR (Leveraging Inverse Alignment to jailbReak), employs an adversarial LLM to attack the target
      LLM, leading to the generation of unsafe responses.
092
093
      (2) Training Free and Faster Jailbreak. Our approach requires no additional training and is ex-
094
      tremely fast to execute. By using GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019), which has 124 million parameters,
095   we achieve faster runtimes compared to larger models like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) with 7
096   billion parameters. The lack of a training phase results in very short times to generate the first
097   adversarial prompt and low latency for subsequent prompts.
098
099   (3) Theoretical Insights. We provide theoretical insights into the possibilities of jailbreaking safety-
100   aligned models by defining a notion of the “safety net,” which quantifies how safe or unsafe an
101   aligned model is. Additionally, we analyze the suboptimality of our LIAR approach which provides
102   a performance gap of our proposed approach with the optimal fine-tuned attack LLMs model.
103
104   (4) Empirical Results. We present extensive empirical evidence demonstrating the superiority of
105
      our proposed approach. Our method achieves competitive attack success rates (up to 99% on Vicuna-
      7b), generates adversarial prompts with low perplexity (as low as 2) indicating high readability, and
106
      fastest adversarial prompt generation (as fast as within 45 seconds). Additionally, we highlight the
107
      black-box nature of our approach, as it operates without requiring internal details of the target model.

111   Language Models (LM). Consider a vocabulary set denoted by V, and let the language model be
112   represented by a mapping πθ , parameterized by θ. The language model πθ takes as input a sequence
113   of tokens, referred to as a prompt, represented by x := {x1 , x2 , · · · , xN }, where each token xi ∈ V.
114
      Given the input prompt, the model predicts the first output token y0 ∈ V. To predict the next token
      y1 , the model takes as input the original prompt x along with the previously generated token y0 ,
115
      forming a new prompt [x, y0 ]. This process is repeated for subsequent time steps. The input prompt
116
      at time step t is [x, y1:t−1 ], where y1:t−1 = {y0 , y1 , · · · , yt−1 }. To generate the next token, the
117   language model outputs the next token yt ∼ πθ (·|[x, y1:t−1 ]).
118
119   Jailbreak Attacks on Large Language Models. As formulated in Zou et al. (2023), it is possible
120   to attack even an aligned LLM πθ with the help of a prompt suffix q ∈ Q ⊆ V |V| . For instance (cf.
121   Figure 1), consider an unsafe prompt x ∈ X ⊆ V |V| (e.g., “Write a script to exploit an operating
122   system”) and if we input this x to a safety aligned LLM model πθ , it will not answer this query
123   and would generate a negative response (e.g., “I cannot answer this, this is unsafe.”). But with the
124   help of an adversarial prompt given by [x, q], which is concatenation of prompt x and adversarial

      2.1   L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING A PPROACHES
149
150
151
      L1: Computationally Expensive and Training-Based: Most existing methods (Zou et al., 2023;
      Liu et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2021) approach jailbreaking as a discrete combinatorial optimization
152
      problem. This strategy is notorious for its high computational cost and the immense effort needed
153
      to explore the vast discrete text space, Q. Consequently, these methods often depend on extensive
154   training to generate adversarial prompts, which demand significant computational resources.
155
156   L2: Extremely Slow: The training process in these methods is notoriously time-consuming, often
157   taking hours to generate a single adversarial suffix q∗ for a given prompt x. These prolonged dura-
158   tions hinder practical applications and restrict the ability to quickly assess or respond to emerging
159   vulnerabilities as highlighted in (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
160
161   L3: Lack of Theoretical Insights: Although it is clear that jailbreaks can undermine safety-aligned
      models, the underlying mechanisms that enable this misalignment, despite the presence of safety

327   Evaluations: The three primary evaluation metrics we consider are Attack Success Rate (ASR),
328   Perplexity, and Time-To-Attack (TTA). These three metrics respectively reflect the compute required

      5.1   ATTACKING T A R G E T LLM
379
380
381   In Table 1, our method demonstrates a significantly lower average perplexity (lower is better) than
382   the second best method, AdvPrompter. This result is expected, as both AutoDAN and AdvPrompter
383
      use perplexity regularization in their training objectives, while our approach avoids any deviation
      from the pretrained AdversarialLLM, leading to more natural outputs. This low perplexity chal-
384
      lenges the effectiveness of perplexity-based jailbreak defenses. Additionally, our method offers
385
      much faster Time to Attack (TTA) compared to AdvPrompter, primarily because it does not require
386   any training. The per-query time is also shorter, as GPT-2 is a considerably smaller model than
387   LLaMA-2. When accounting for both setup and query times (TTA1), our method is significantly
388   faster than comparable approaches, and this advantage increases further with larger query sets, as
389   seen in TTA100. Although GCG and AutoDAN can generate a few adversarial examples before
390   AdvPrompter finishes its training, AdvPrompter’s low per-query time allows for faster attacks on
391   large sets of restricted prompts. However, LIAR consistently outperforms it in speed. GCG achieves
392   the highest ASR@1, as it optimizes solely for adversarial success without perplexity regularization.
393   When our method is allowed 100 attempts—which can be generated in just seconds—we achieve
394
      an attack success rate comparable to GCG. Given the significantly reduced overall TTA, this asym-
      metric ASR@k comparison becomes highly practical: our method can generate over 10,000 queries
395
      before GCG completes its first. Since an attacker only needs one successful query to jailbreak the
396
      TargetLLM, this fast TTA gives our approach a critical edge in real-world attacks and in evaluating
397   defenses quickly.

451   5.3   A BLATIONS
452
453   We have already shown that different AdversarialLLM models have varying effectiveness in
454   Table 2. However, there are other ways of modifying the diversity of the AdversarialLLM,
455   specifically in varying the generation parameters such as temperature or query length.
456
457   Impact of varying the temperature. Table 3 shows the impact of varying the temperature of
458   the generated q of the AdversarialLLM: higher temperature results in higher ”creativity”, i.e.
459
      the probability of unlikely next-word predictions is increased. Counter-intuitively, reducing the
      temperature and preferring the more likely next-word predictions results in higher ASR@1. This
460
      suggests that the most probable next-word prediction has a higher chance of a successful jailbreak.
461
      However, for higher k such as k = 10 or k = 100, we see the importance of diversity in the generated
462   queries. Specifically, for ASR@100, we see that the success rate peaks around temperature of 0.6,
463   with lower temperatures reducing the diversity of the queries and thereby reducing the ASR@100.
464   Additionally, we observe increased perplexity with increased temperature, which is to be expected
465   as perplexity measures the likeliness of the query and higher temperature produces more unlikely
466   queries.
467
468   Impact of query length. The length of q is evaluated in Table 4. Increasing the length of q results in
469   longer query times, however even doubling the length has a query time shorter than AdvPrompter.
470   When changing the length of q, and important fact to consider is that longer q have a higher chance
471
      of prompt-drift, where [x, q] may be asking for content far from x on its own. This is a limitation
      of the keyword matching aspect of the ASR metric being used. We see ASR@1 improve with q
472
      length, but ASR@10 peaks for q length 48 while ASR@100 peaks for q length 30. This suggests
473
      that longer q length may result in decreased diversity, and as shorter q lengths are preferred to reduce
474   prompt-drift, 30 is a reasonable prompt length.

508   6     C ONCLUSION
509
510   In summary, we propose a straightforward jailbreak method that is not only fast and avoids the need
511   for additional training, but is also difficult to detect using traditional metrics such as perplexity. We
512   have provided both theoretical justification for the efficacy of our method and empirical comparisons
513   with similar recent approaches, demonstrating its effectiveness. The ability to efficiently navigate the
514   space of the TargetLLM to elicit harmful responses hinges, in part, on the diversity and creativity
515   of the generated attacks. Defending against these low perplexity attacks is a challenge, as it is not
516   yet clear whether alignment can fully avoid providing harmful responses.

758
759   Safety Alignment. Many LLMs are trained on vast corpora of internet-scraped text, which can
760   include harmful or unethical content (Radford et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
761   2023). This creates ethical blindspots in the models (Hendrycks et al., 2020), allowing them to
762   generate harmful responses, especially when exploited by early jailbreaks that used relatively simple,
763   hand-crafted prompts (Shen et al., 2023; Perez & Ribeiro, 2022). To mitigate this, reinforcement
764   learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) was introduced as a training method
765
      to suppress harmful outputs (Ziegler et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Rafailov et al., 2024)
      and improve the model’s ability to follow instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a).
766
      Despite this alignment step, hand-crafted jailbreaks continued to emerge (Yu et al., 2023; Yuan
767
      et al., 2023). In response, more explicit safeguards have been proposed to prevent harmful content
768   generation. These range from reward models designed to object to malicious prompts (Glaese et al.,
769   2022), to chain-of-thought reasoning (Bai et al., 2022b), and even injecting human judgment during
770   pretraining (Korbak et al., 2023). Nevertheless, stronger automated gradient-based jailbreak attacks
771   (Zou et al., 2023) have been developed, capable of bypassing these defenses. As noted by Wolf
772   et al. (2023), LLMs may remain susceptible to prompt attacks if alignment strategies only suppress
773   harmful outputs without fully eliminating the underlying vulnerabilities.
774
775   Manual Jailbreaks. Early jailbreak attacks, such as ”Do Anything Now” (DAN) (Shen et al., 2023)
776   and ”Ignore previous prompt” (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022), were highly hand-crafted and produced
777
      more intuitive prompt manipulations compared to more sophisticated gradient-based methods (Zou
      et al., 2023). These manual jailbreak techniques have since evolved, branching out into strategies
778
      like multi-step prompts (Li et al., 2023a; Zeng et al., 2024) and collections of adversarial templates
779
      (Yu et al., 2023). Even advanced models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) have proven vulnerable
780   to these manual attacks (Yuan et al., 2023). However, as new TargetLLMs are released, additional
781   effort is required to craft updated prompt templates capable of bypassing their safeguards. This
782   ongoing challenge motivates the development of automated methods to consistently extract harmful
783   responses from target models without manual intervention.
784
785   Prompt Tuning. Unlike image-based adversarial attacks, where gradient-based optimization can be
786   applied directly in the continuous pixel space (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Papernot et al., 2016), prompt
787   tokens are discrete and are generated iteratively. Early methods for prompt optimization performed
788   greedy token swapping in the discrete space (Ebrahimi et al., 2017), treating each token as a one-hot
789
      vector and replacing it with the token that produced the most negative gradient. This approach was
      refined by Shin et al. (2020), which expanded the range of token substitutions considered. Further
790
      improvements came from Lester et al. (2021), which shifted optimization into the continuous space
791
      of token embeddings. However, a key challenge for this problem is that optimized embeddings may
792   not correspond to valid discrete tokens. To address this, Wen et al. (2024) computes the gradient
793   with respect to the projected tokens before applying it to the embedding space, ensuring that the
794   optimized embedding remains valid.
795
796   Automatic Jailbreaks. Building on prompt optimization techniques, recent jailbreak methods lever-
797   aged gradient optimization to bypass safety alignment. Early gradient-based approaches focused on
798   optimizing the prompt itself (Guo et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024), making it difficult
799   to ensure that the modified prompts retained the intent of the original censored text. To address
800   this, GCG (Zou et al., 2023) introduced a method that appends additional tokens to the prompt and
801
      optimizes only these added tokens, preserving the original intent while focusing on bypassing cen-
      sorship. GCG builds upon AutoPrompt (Shin et al., 2020), evaluating gradients across all possible
802
      single-token substitutions rather than just a subset, and including a loss function that targets positive
803
      responses like ”Sure” as opposed to ”I’m sorry”. This results in a highly effective adversarial attack
804   with strong transferability to other models. To facilitate consistent evaluation, Zou et al. (2023)
805   also introduces the AdvBench dataset as a jailbreak benchmark. One limitation of optimizing solely
806   for positive responses is that the resulting suffixes often appear as unreadable text, which makes
807   detection easier through perplexity-based metrics that measure readability (Jain et al., 2023; Alon
808   & Kamfonas, 2023). To overcome this, AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2023) uses a genetic algorithm (Guo
809   et al., 2023) to optimize prompts in the discrete space and incorporates an additional loss for read-
      ability. AutoDAN also allows the use of manual jailbreaks as prototype prompts, which are typically


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      more human-readable due to their handcrafted nature. However, a key challenge with these methods
811   is the computational cost of performing combinatorial optimization for each prompt. To address
812   this, newer approaches suggest training language models to automate suffix generation, offering a
813   more efficient way to generate adversarial prompts.
814
815   Learned Jailbreaks. Once a dataset of successful adversarial suffixes is created using automatic
816   gradient-based techniques, an LLM can be trained to generate these suffixes for any given censored
817   prompt, as seen in AmpleGCG (Liao & Sun, 2024). The key advantage of using an LLM for this task
818   is the speed at which queries can be generated post-training. To take advantage of this, Paulus et al.
819   (2024) introduced ASR@k, where k refers to the number of query attempts. For instance, if at least
820
      one of 10 queries is successful, the ASR would be considered 100% for that sample, which aligns
      with the goals of an attacker. Beyond generating adversarial suffixes, AdvPrompter (Paulus et al.,
821
      2024) is designed to produce suffixes with low perplexity, making them harder to detect. While these
822
      learned jailbreak methods offer high ASR and fast query generation, a major challenge remains: the
823   significant time required for training. Our method addresses this issue by further reducing training
824   time while also aiming to lower suffix perplexity.
