009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011             Language models are now deployed in a wide variety of user-facing applications,
012             often for specific purposes like answering questions about documentation or act-
013             ing as coding assistants. As these models are intended for particular purposes, they
014             should not be able to answer irrelevant queries like requests for poetry or questions
015             about physics, or even worse, queries that can only be answered by humans like
016             sensitive company policies. Instead we would like them to only answer queries
017             corresponding to desired behavior and refuse all other requests, which we refer to
018             as scoping. We find that, despite the use of system prompts, two representative
019
                language models can be poorly scoped and respond to queries they should not be
                addressing. We then conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation of methods
020
                which could be used for scoping the behavior of language models. Among many
021
                other results, we show that a recently-proposed method for general alignment, Cir-
022             cuit Breakers (CB), can be adapted to scope language models to very specific tasks
023             like sentiment analysis or summarization or even tasks with finer-grained scoping
024             (e.g. summarizing only news articles). When compared to standard methods like
025             fine-tuning or preference learning, CB is more robust both for out of distribution
026             tasks, and to adversarial prompting techniques. We also show that layering SFT
027             and CB together often results in the best of both worlds: improved performance
028             only on relevant queries, while rejecting irrelevant ones.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In the past few years Large Language Models have exploded into the popular conscience. One major
034   recent addition is the “alignment” process through Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback
035   (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), which has made the current generation of
036   language models much less likely to emit toxic content than previous generations (Wolf et al., 2017),
037   and thus much more acceptable for general use. As a result, many businesses and individuals feel
038
      more comfortable using these technologies than they would be in the past.
039   As a result, we have generally capable language models which refuse to answer toxic or dangerous
040   queries, but it is still difficult to deploy these language models. Even though they may not emit
041   toxic content as often, they still will happily answer any question, irrelevant or not. This becomes a
042   problem when we wish to deploy language models as products in specific contexts: e.g. shopping
043   bots currently give give coding advice1 or answer other questions,2 while assistive co-pilots can be
044
      taken off course by prompt injections.3
045   While language models have general language capability, there is still a need to scope them for
046   specific uses. Currently this can solved by two-stage approaches like relevance classifiers, or system
047   prompting, but we will show that these options are brittle (Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023;
048   Zeng et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023) and easy to circumvent.
049   Here we conduct a comprehensive empirical study on scoping language models to particular capa-
050   bilities. Our contributions are as follows:

098
099
      Aligning Language Models: The advent of the current era of language models has been marked
      by a process of aligning language models so that generations are more helpful, and safer for de-
100
      ployment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a). The primary way this is accomplished is through
101
      reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) which was first pro-
102   posed in robotic simulation tasks. RLHF proceeds by collecting preference pairs of completions,
103   and training a reward model from human judgments on those preference pairs, then performing
104   reinforcement learning with the language model against that reward model. From tasks in simula-
105   tion, it was developed in language (Stiennon et al., 2020), until it reached its current state. Other
106   works have removed the human aspect of human feedback, allowing for synthetic feedback from
107   models (Bai et al., 2022b; Sudalairaj et al., 2024). Lately, Rafailov et al. (2024) have removed the
      need for a reward model, making for a stabler and simpler objective function without many of the


                                                                                                   2
108
      complexities of RL training. A budding line of work also explores aligning not just to a single re-
109   ward model, but preferences of many different individual users (Chakraborty et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
110   2024). All of these methods focus on some general notion of alignment, without considering the
111   specific task, unlike our work.
112
      Adapting for Specific Purposes: Typically after pretraining, language models go through an in-
113
      struction fine-tuning stage, where they gain the ability to follow instructions (Mishra et al., 2022;
114
      Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). After this, they proceed through an alignment phase as dis-
115   cussed above, usually to avoid harmful behavior (Bai et al., 2022a). It is possible to adapt language
116   models for specific purposes simply with a system message (Touvron et al., 2023), but many exam-
117   ples of black-box adversarial attacks (Chao et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023; Zeng
118   et al., 2024) demonstrate it is difficult only to rely on the system prompt for such control. Wallace
119   et al. (2024) propose finetuning with different levels of priority, similar to Zhang et al. (2023b), but
120   these works focus primarily on general safety and not the task. These ideas are based on the fact
121   that current language models can often be distracted by irrelevant context (Shi et al., 2023; Yoran
122   et al., 2024). Thus, it seems important to finetune the language model if we want it to be deployed
123
      to a particular domain. For domains where there is sufficient data, we may also pretrain and fix the
      language model’s purpose ahead of time (Beltagy et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) or
124
      continue pretraining from a base language model (Gururangan et al., 2020). It is an open question
125
      however whether finetuning retains the robustness capabilities, or if it is similarly as brittle as system
126   prompting for out-of-distribution questions.
127
128   Representation Steering: Somewhat orthogonally, a growing field aims to control language models
129
      through mechanisms internal to the representations or weights (Zou et al., 2023a). As an example
      Subramani et al. (2022) find single vectors that can cause the language model to generate comple-
130
      tions of interest. Hendel et al. (2023) do something similar for finding task vectors corresponding to
131
      in-context learning. Turner et al. (2023) extract steering vectors and show generations are affected
132   in straightforward ways when these vectors are linearly combined with the hidden states. This is
133   an example of similar linear behavior in representation space initially observed by Mikolov et al.
134   (2013). Rimsky et al. (2023) scale the work of Turner et al. (2023) to larger models and more com-
135   plex tasks, as well as find vectors more robustly. Follow-on work explores steering with multiple
136   vectors simultaneously (van der Weij et al., 2024) and for coding tasks, where it is shown that vec-
137   tors transfer between languages (Lucchetti & Guha, 2024). Arditi et al. (2024) show that steering
138   vectors can be used to break the safety mechanism in aligned language models. All these works do
139   not allow for conditional control of inputs: steering is applied to all examples. On the contrary, Zou
140
      et al. (2024) design a method that conditionally rejects unsafe inputs while allowing safe inputs to
      pass through. We will adapt this method for our study.
141
142   Refusal in Language Models: As our work is primarily about scoping models to refuse irrelevant
143   queries, we review refusal. More detail is available in the excellent survey of this field by Wen
144   et al. (2024). One common case to train for refusal is when the answer is unknown or the model is
145   unconfident (Zhang et al., 2023a; Cao, 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Another is for unsafe inputs (Varshney
146
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Wallace et al., 2024). Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to reject unsafe
      prompts can still lead to unsafe behavior, though parameter efficient methods like LoRA (Hu et al.,
147
      2022) have better tradeoffs (Brahman et al., 2024). Both Brahman et al. (2024) and Cheng et al.
148
      (2024) take an approach to refusal using SFT and DPO, which we will adapt to our case. Other
149   methods to induce refusal may be prompt-based (Xie et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) or based
150   on probing model representations (Kadavath et al., 2022; Slobodkin et al., 2023). Though these
151   methods lay out a set of techniques to explore for our task, all of them are oriented toward general
152   alignment qualities like safety, as opposed to specific tasks that we will explore.

      3.1   S COPING FOR S PECIFIC TASKS
157
158   We would like to scope language models to provide completions to relevant tasks, and reject queries
159   corresponding to irrelevant tasks. In particular, we assume we are given a set of “accept” queries
160   {xa |xa ∼ Ak }, where {Ak } is a set of “accept” tasks, and a set of “reject” queries {xr |xr ∼ Rk }
161   where {Rk } is a set of ”reject” tasks. We are given a language model fθ : x 7→ y which predicts
      completion y from input x, with parameters θ; a classifier g : y 7→ c ∈ {0, 1} which decides whether


                                                          3
162
      a completion is accepted (0) or rejected (1) by the language model. We would like to compute an
163   update ∆ such that we minimize Exa g(fθ+∆ (xa )) and maximize Exr g(fθ+∆ (xr )). Thus we want
164   accept queries to be accepted and reject queries to be rejected
165
166
      As an additional goal, we would like performance on the accept tasks not to degrade. Given a scoring
      function h : (x, y) 7→ s ∈ [0, 1] which scores the completion on task performance where 1 is best,
167
      we would also like to maximize Exa h(xa , fθ+∆ (xa )).

212   3.3    M ETHODS
213
214   All of the methods we consider in experiments have previously been demonstrated to work in mul-
215   tiple language models, so to reduce the complexity of the experiments and provide a broad range
      of ablations, we choose to fix the language model. Due to its strong performance and permissive


                                                                      4
216
      licensing, we base all experiments on the Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023)
217   language model.
218
219   For all methods that require training the language model, we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) with default
220
      rank 16, α = 16 and dropout of 0.05. We use Adam (Kingma, 2014) without any regularization and
      tune learning rates (see Appendix B).
221
222   System Prompting (Sys.): The simplest method to scope language models is simply to instruct
223   them to refuse irrelevant prompts. For example, for SA the system prompt would be:
224   You are an assistant who only answers request related to Sentiment Analysis. For all other requests
225   you respond “I cannot answer that.”
226
227
      With multiple accept categories, we would comma separate the category names (e.g. 11...related to
      Sentiment Analysis, Text Completion and Summarization...”). This system prompt is prepended to
228
      all instructions at evaluation time. In addition, all other methods also use the system prompt both
229
      at training and evaluation time. This is similar in spirit to methods proposed by Xie et al. (2023);
230   Zhang et al. (2024).
231
232   Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) consist of tuning the language
233
      model to produce particular outputs. For the accept tasks the completions ya are the groundtruth
      completions provided by the dataset. For the reject tasks, the completions yr are always “ cannot
234
      answer that.”. As the finetuning dataset can be quite small, loss is only computed on the completions
235
      so as to avoid overfitting to the small set of instructions, agreeing with common practice (Mishra
236   et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). We tune learning rate and step budget for SFT.
237   Similar approach to that of Brahman et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2024).
238
239
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO): We would like to examine a preference learning baseline.
      Given the complexity of PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), and the need to train a new reward model for
240
      each set of tasks, we choose to experiment on Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
241
      2024), which does not require an additional reward model. As DPO requires pairs of preference data,
242   for accept queries we provide the dataset completion as preferred, and the completion “I cannot
243   answer that.” as rejected. For reject queries we do the reverse, preferring “I cannot answer that.”
244   over the ground truth completion. For DPO we tune learning rate, step budget, and the loss weighting
245   term regularizing the KL divergence from the base model predictions. Similar approach to that of
246   Brahman et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2024).
247
      Probing Classifier (Probe): Probes of representations are a common method to accomplish tasks as
248   they base predictions on the internal state of the language model (Conneau et al., 2018; Tenney et al.,
249   2019; Zou et al., 2023a). Previous work on Circuit Breakers (Zou et al., 2024) showed that probing
250   representations was quite competitive for detecting dangerous language. However, that work only
251   designed probes to function on a single layer of the representations of a language model. Here we
252   design a stronger probe. Once an instruction is fed to the frozen language model, we first remove
253   the first position as that position is quite anomalous due to large magnitude (Xiao et al., 2024), then
254   we average all positions per layer and normalize the average vector to norm 1 so as to match norms
255   between layers. Finally we concatenate the average vectors from each layer and feed that as input
256
      to a 2-layer MLP with width 256 which makes a binary classification decision on whether to accept
      or reject. Only the MLP layers are trained, and we tune the learning rate and step budget. Similar in
257
      spirit to work on confidence of LLM (Kadavath et al., 2022; Slobodkin et al., 2023).
258
259   Circuit Breakers (CB): Zou et al. (2024) first introduce a method they call Circuit Breakers (CB) for
260   accepting normal queries while rejecting dangerous ones. We repurpose their method for this task.
261   Essentially given a function rep which extracts the representations of a language model at particular
262
      layers, they design an optimization objective with two components: La (xa , ∆) = ∥rep(fθ (xa )) −
      rep(fθ+∆ (xa ))∥22 and Lr (xr , ∆) = max{0, cos(rep(fθ (xr )), rep(fθ+∆ (xr ))}. The total loss is
263
      L = α(t)La + β(t)Lr where the two components of the loss are scheduled over time.
264
265   This loss function keeps the representations of accept tasks from drifting, while making the rep-
266   resentations of reject tasks orthogonal from their original position. The observation is that this
267   orthogonalization breaks the language model generation on these reject inputs. We use original
268
      hyperparameters proposed by Zou et al. (2024). For CB we tune learning rate and step budget.
269



                                                        5
270
      SFT → CB: In order to improve accept task performance, we propose to layer CB on top of SFT.
271   The method in this case is quite simple, we first run SFT, then run CB training on top. Here we keep
272   hyperparameters from the SFT and CB tuning respectively. The nice benefit of this approach is it
273   now requires only a single call to a tuned language model.

275   3.4   D ETECTING R EJECTION
276
277   Ideally one might choose to use a language model judge for detecting rejection (Zheng
278   et al., 2023). However, given the large number of experiments and evaluations in this work,
279   we found it prohibitively expensive to run all the evaluations through a state-of-the-art API
280   judge. We experimented with using hosted language models as judges, with the largest being
281
      Llama-3-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), but found such detection to have much poorer
      performance both in precision and recall than the metrics described below.
282
283   As different methods behave differently, we employ different ways to detect rejection. For all meth-
284   ods besides probing, as the system prompt and tuning will instruct models to respond 11I cannot
285   answer that.”, we catch rejection by string matching for a few different tokens that are synonyms for
286   “cannot” at the beginning of the generation. The reason we only match strings early in the gener-
287
      ation is that it is possible to switch from reject to accept behavior, so we would like to catch that
      rejection early on. We do not match strings later on as in practice we never observed language mod-
288
      els switching from accept to reject behavior midway through generation and keywords can be used
289
      as a part of a legitimate response later in generation. On a sample of 30 completions from accept,
290   reject, and OOD reject sets, we tuned the threshold that such a detector had perfect agreement with
291   manual judgment. This was inspired by common string-based detectors like the one proposed by
292   Zou et al. (2023b) and used by Zeng et al. (2024); Zou et al. (2024).
293
      For CB-based methods, the behavior of the “circuit broken” generation is quite distinct, where it
294
      tends to repeat patterns. As exact-matching does not detect such patterns, in addition to string
295
      matching described above which will activate when the system prompt is followed, we catch rejec-
296   tion by the existence of a repeated pattern of 4 or more strings within the response. For more details,
297   see Appendix B. Again on a sample of 30 completions form accept, reject and OOD reject sets,
298   we tuned the threshold for this detector such that it achieved 1 false negative and 0 false positives
299   out of 90 completions. The single false negative was due to to a broken generation of punctuation
300   characters that lacked repetitions. See Appendix C for sample outputs.
301
      For Probing, we simply use the binary classification decision from the MLP as the rejection decision.

304   4     E XPERIMENTS
305
306   In this section we explore a number of empirical questions: how robust are scoped LLMs to ad-
307
      versarial prompts, how much diversity is needed for scoping, or whether scoping is possible for
      multiple tasks simultaneously. We aim to be comprehensive, thus demonstrate results across 2-3
308
      different categories per dataset. Where not detailed, our accept sets will be Sentiment Analysis
309
      (SA), Summarization (S) and Program Execution (PE).
310
311   All experiments contain of evaluations of task performance (Accept Score) on the accept set (which
312   should be high), rejection rate on the in-distribution accept (Accept) set (which should be low) as
313
      well as rejection rate on the in-distribution reject set (ID Reject) and out of distribution data (OOD
      Reject) (which should be high). We describe experiments in broad strokes, and defer precise details
314
      on hyperparameters to Appendix B. In the main text we present results for experiments on a repre-
315
      sentative language model, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023), and we include
316   additional experiments for robustness evaluations for granite-7b-instruct (Sudalairaj et al.,
317   2024) in Appendix A.1. Due to the large volume of experiments we are unable to run all models
318   against all permutations, but all of the methods considered have been shown to work in multiple
319   models previously, though not in our particular task.
320
321   4.1   ROBUSTNESS TO A DVERSARIAL P ROMPTS
322
323   The original CB methodology, and many related works discussed, focus on robustness to adversarial
      prompts. If models are to be deployed, we might expect that users could attempt adversarial attacks


                                                        6
324
      against the deployment. Here we ask: if models are scoped, how robust are they to adversarial
325   prompts? Our threat model is of a black-box access. In particular, we assume that the users are
326   allowed to edit only the instruction text, and do not even have access to modifying the system
327   prompt, which would be true for text-based API access. We implement and test a number of different
328   black-box adversarial attacks:
329
      Adversarial system prompt (Adv.): We insert an adversarial system prompt at the beginning of the
330
      instruction, after the original system prompt. This adversarial system prompt is of the same format
331   as the original, but instead of being for the category of the training accept task, it corresponds to the
332   category of the evaluation task.
333
334
      Base-64 translation (b64): Following Wei et al. (2023), we translate instructions into base-64, then
      prompt the language model. After receiving the response if it is valid base-64 (which is very often),
335
      we translate it back to text.
336
337   Few-shot prompting (Few-shot): We provide a few-shot example from the evaluation set, where
338   we draw a training query and completion and then prompt the next round with a new query. This is
339   similar to the Many-Shot attack explored by Anil et al. (2024).
340   Multiturn prompting (2-turn): We prompt with a full conversation turn of an accept task and
341   accept completion, then a second turn with a request from the evaluation set. This format intends to
342   prime the model to first get into an ”accept” mode, before answering the evaluation query.
343
      Multiturn prompting with adversarial system prompt (2-turn+Sys.): This is similar to the attack
344   above, but we add an adversarial system prompt to the beginning of the 2nd turn.
345
346   Prefill: In this attack, append a generic prefilling output (”Sure, here’s a response to your request:”),
347
      to the end of the user instruction. This follows common practice and has been a strong attack (Wei
      et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024).
348
349   Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP): TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2023) is an adversarial prompting
350   method whereby an attacking language model iteratively attempts to jailbreak a target language
351   model (here our model to evaluate). TAP uses an actor-critic loop to rewrite prompts based on
352   whether the language model was jailbroken in the previous step, and explores a tree to find the best
353
      performing prompt. For more details and hyperparameters, see Appendix B. It is a strong black-box
      optimization-based jailbreaking method, and was among the strongest attacks for CB in the original
354

491
492   Here we briefly discuss some additional results, deferring full treatment to the Appendix.
493
      Precise Scoping: We find that one can scope precisely, (e.g. only News summarization instead of
494
      all summarization). For more details, see Appendix A.2.
495
496   Effect of Data Quantity: We find that most methods work quite well with very little data (as little as
497   128 instances). DPO in particular benefits monotonically, while CB has issues as the dataset scales,
498
      perhaps due to the difficulty of simultaneous orthogonalization of many different reject instances,
      see Appendix A.3 for more details.
499
500   Effect of LoRA Rank: Overall, it does appear that rank can have a substantial effect on the perfor-
501   mance of methods. While DPO seems to scale monotonically with LoRA rank, CB-based methods
502   have a sweet spot for performance, above which it seems optimization becomes difficult. See de-
503   tailed analysis in Appendix A.4.
504   Representation Analysis: We see that SFT and DPO only make changes to representations at the
505   tail end of context, while CB-based methods will change representations across the entire context,
506   which may explain the stronger robustness, SFT-CB layers both of these effects. See Appendix A.5
507   for more details.
508
509   5     D ISCUSSION
510
511   Though current language models are generally applicable, there is still a need at deployment time to
512   define the kinds of queries they should be able to answer. Thus we need to scope their abilities. In
513   this work, we conducted a comprehensive empirical study of scoping language models for specific
514   deployments.
515
      The general takeaways are many, but firstly we find across many cases that system prompting is very
516
      insufficient, and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to refuse irrelevant queries is also quite poor. Other
517   methods like preference learning (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) or probing representations can be
518   good in some settings. In addition, a recently-method Circuit Breakers (CB) (Zou et al., 2024) can
519   be promising in many circumstances.
520
      In particular probing is quite strong for refusal, but also has a tendency to reject queries that should
521
      be accepted. In addition, it may expensive in practice, as one needs to design a probe on the pre-
522
      trained model so as to allow for as many features as possible, then direct the query to a fine-tuned
523   model. DPO does well primarily when the rejection data distribution is quite broad, both in diversity
524   and quantity, and when those conditions are not fulfilled it can have issues. On the other hand, CB
525   is quite strong even when the rejection data is quite narrow, and performs much better than other
526   methods against adversarial prompting techniques. Layering SFT and CB one after another con-
527   fers even more benefits and allows us to pack the rejection and additional performance into a single
528   model call. When investigating why these different methods have widely varying behavior, we find
529   that CB causes a much more substantial change to the representation space, which may account for
530   its additional robustness.
531   Overall CB seems quite promising, it appears it has many of the desired characteristics, but it has
532   some issues with large rejection sets and the optimization may sometimes be unstable, so we may
533   need a conditional orthogonalization method to orthogonalize one task at a time. Also, given that
534   we can layer SFT and CB, it may be possible to explore much more complex solutions, mixing and
535   matching to find the best result for deployment. Still, given that even relatively benign adversarial
536   attacks are not 100% prevented, we have a long way to go before any method meets deployment
537
      requirements. We believe our results present a strong step forward along this path.

958   A.5              R EPRESENTATION A NALYSIS
959
960   We would like to get a better sense as to how exactly the different methods work. In particular, for
961   the case where only a narrow rejection set distribution is provided, how come CB-based methods
962
      are so much more robust than others?
963   In Figure 9 we show that DPO and SFT only change the representations of the tail of the context.
964   Hence it makes sense why CB is more robust to attacks in Section 4.1: all representations have
965   changed, so it is difficult to find a way to circumvent the changed behavior, while DPO and SFT
966   have ”cracks” which can be exploited.
967   The effect is particularly clear on the in-distribution rejection set, but preceding sections demon-
968   strated that most methods were fairly comparable in distribution. Out of distribution, the effect of
969   CB is much less, though still there is a much more substantial difference from the original model
970   than SFT or DPO which make only small changes to the tail of context in deeper layers. With SFT-
971   CB, we can clearly see the layering of the tail edit as well as the orthogonalization across the entire
      context.

1023
1024   For every category, when we evaluate we use 256 heldout prompts, separate from those trained on.

       The full list of strings that we match for the string-based detector is: [”cannot”, ”unable”, ”sorry”,
1027   ”can’t”, ”will not”, ”won’t”, ”do not”, ”don’t”, ”not able”]. We arrived at this list after manual
1028   verification of a subset of 100 prompts, and we find the performance to match human judgments. As
1029   we are tuning, we are unable to simply reuse the list from Zou et al. (2023b) as model completions
1030   may change. If we detect any of these words within the first 30 characters (the beginning of the
1031   completion), we consider the model to reject that prompt.
1032
       For the CB rejection detection, generations break often by repeating the same token over and over
1033   again, thus we detect the maximum consecutive repetition of tokens or sequences of tokens. If we
1034   find repetitions of 4 or more, we consider that a rejection. This number was chosen again by manual
1035   inspection, where we find it to match human judgment, though sometimes it does not always catch
1036   rejections where the generation is broken, but there is not sufficient repetition.

       For each instance, we compute average cosine similarity for every token at every layer between the
