009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models have received significant attention due to their abilities
012            to solve a wide range of complex tasks. However these models memorize a sig-
013            nificant proportion of their training data, posing a serious threat when disclosed
014            at inference time. To mitigate this unintended memorization, it is crucial to un-
015            derstand what elements are memorized and why. Most existing works provide a
016
               posteriori explanations, which has a limited interest in practice. To address this
               gap, we propose a new approach to detect memorized samples a priori in LLMs
017
               fine-tuned on classification tasks. This method is efficient from the early stages
018
               of training and readily adaptable to other classification settings, such as training
019            vision models from scratch. Our method is supported by new theoretical results
020            that we demonstrate, and requires a low computational budget. We obtain strong
021            empirical results, paving the way for systematic inspection and protection of these
022            vulnerable samples before memorization happens.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized the way we approach natural language under-
028
      standing. The availability to the general public of models such as ChatGPT, capable of solving a
      wide range of tasks without adaptation, has democratized their use. However, a growing body of re-
029
      search have shown that these models memorize a significant proportion of their training data, raising
030
      legal and ethical challenges (Zhang et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2023; Mireshghallah et al., 2022b).
031   The impact of memorization is ambiguous. On the one hand, it poses a serious threat to privacy
032   and intellectual property because LLMs are often trained on large datasets including sensitive and
033   private information. Practical attacks have been developed to extract this information from training
034   datasets (Carlini et al., 2021; Lukas et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Nasr et al., 2023), and LLMs have
035   also been shown to plagiarize copyrighted content at inference time (Lee et al., 2023; Henderson
036   et al., 2024). On the other hand, memorization can positively impact model’s performance, because
037   memorized samples are highly informative. Studies have revealed that outliers are more likely to be
038   memorized, and that these memorized outliers help the model generalize to similar inputs (Feldman,
039
      2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Wang et al., 2024).
040   Mitigating the negative impacts of memorization while still harnessing its advantages is a chal-
041   lenging task, that requires varying approaches based on the sensitivity of the training data and the
042   purpose of the model. However, practitioners often struggle to evaluate the potential risk of mem-
043   orized samples, as empirical defenses often fail to capture the most vulnerable samples from the
044   training set (Aerni et al., 2024). To address this limitation, we propose a new method to audit mod-
045
      els under development and predict, from the early stages of training, the elements of the training
      data that the LLM is likely to memorize. Our first goal is to provide practitioners with an efficient
046
      tool to inspect vulnerable elements and select an appropriate mitigation strategy: anonymization,
047
      differential privacy, acceptance of the risk, etc. Our second goal is to enable researchers to design
048   new empirical defenses that optimally allocate their privacy budgets to protect the most vulnerable
049   samples, thereby achieving a better privacy-utility trade-off. For both goals, it is crucial to predict
050   memorization early in the training pipeline and at minimal cost. Indeed, a-posteriori measures of
051   memorization, such as LiRA (Carlini et al., 2022a) or counterfactual memorization (Feldman &
052   Zhang, 2020), require not only the completion of the training of the model, but also the training of
053   several shadow models, making them prohibitively computationally expensive for most practition-
      ers. On the other hand, for researcher developing empirical defenses, it is crucial to detect vulnerable

076   To predict memorization before it occurs, we interrupt training when the median training loss has
077   significantly decreased, typically by 95% (see Figure 1). This drop indicates that the model has
078   learned simple patterns in the hidden representations, enabling it to accurately classify the majority
079   of samples, without relying on memorization. At this stage, we measure the consistency between
080
      the labels and the hidden representations of the elements within the partially trained model. If a
      hidden representation fails to adequately explain its assigned label, it indicates that the data sample
081
      behaves as a local outlier, within the data distribution’s long tail (Zhu et al., 2014). Such outliers
082
      are particularly vulnerable to memorization, because the model will likely fail to learn meaningful
083   representations for them, and will instead resort to memorizing them (Feldman & Zhang, 2020).
084
085   We evaluated four approaches to quantify the consistency between the hidden representations and
086
      the labels, with the objective of predicting memorization in the fully trained model: loss, logit
      gap, Mahalanobis distance (Mahalanobis, 1936), and Pointwise Sliced Mutual Information (PSMI)
087
      (Goldfeld & Greenewald, 2021; Wongso et al., 2023a). With the exception of Mahalanobis distance,
088
      all approaches achieved strong empirical results. The loss is straightforward to implement and fast
089   to compute, but requires an additional hyperparameter to define a threshold for separating elements
090   predicted to be memorized. The logit gap offers no advantage over the loss. On the other hand,
091   PSMI saves one hyperparameter because we demonstrated that zero is a natural threshold to use.
092   However, it marginally increases computational cost and is more complex to implement.
093
      To the best of our knowledge, Biderman et al. (2023) provides the only baseline to which our ap-
094
      proach can be compared. They predict memorization in LLMs trained on generative tasks, with a
095   reasonable computational budget and before the end of training. However, memorization is defined
096   differently for generative and discriminative tasks. They use k-extractability (Carlini et al., 2021),
097   which is very cheap to compute for generative models, but not applicable to classification models.
098   For these models, memorization is typically defined as vulnerability to membership inference attack
099   (Shokri et al., 2017), which is more computationally expensive. Our approach is only applicable to
100   classification models, for which we found no directly comparable baseline. This is why we adapted
101   the method of Biderman et al. (2023) to a classification setting, despite the prohibitive computational
102   cost arising from the increased complexity of measuring memorization (see Appendix C.1). Even
103
      with this adaptation, we observed similar results: at the early stages of training, it is possible to
      achieve a low False Positive Rate (FPR), but not a high True Positive Rate (TPR), because vulnera-
104
      ble samples have not yet been memorized. Conversely, our approach obtains both high TPR and low
105
      FPR, paving the way for inspecting and protecting vulnerable samples under realistic conditions.

      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows.
109
110          • We demonstrate that it is possible to predict, from the early stages of training, whether a
111            sample will be memorized when fine-tuning a LLM for a classification task;
112          • We formalize the threat model and propose FPR at high TPR as the evaluation metric;
113          • We compare several metrics and discuss their respective advantages;
114
             • We validate the effectiveness of our approach for three different 7B LLMs fine-tuned on
115
               three distinct multi-choice question datasets;
116
             • We demonstrate its adaptability by applying it as-is to vision models trained from scratch.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
119
120   Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) These attacks were first introduced by Shokri et al.
121   (2017), and aim to determine whether a target individual element was part of a target model’s training
122   set. Although they are less realistic and practical than extraction attacks (Carlini et al., 2021; Lukas
123   et al., 2023; Nasr et al., 2023), membership inference attacks have become the standard approach
124   for measuring the amount of private information a model can leak. Popular attacks such as those
125   of Shokri et al. (2017); Carlini et al. (2022a); Wen et al. (2023) involve training a large number of
126   shadow models with different training data. Due to the significant computational resources required,
127   alternative attack methods have been developed that necessitate training fewer shadow models or
128
      none at all (Yeom et al., 2018; Mattern et al., 2023; Zarifzadeh et al., 2024).
129
      Several definitions of unintended memorization in neural networks For discriminative models,
130
      memorization is usually defined as vulnerability to MIA, as in (Mireshghallah et al., 2022a; Carlini
131
      et al., 2022b; Aerni et al., 2024). Counterfactual memorization can also be applied to such models,
132   requiring the training of multiple models with varying datasets to capture the influence of individual
133   data samples (Feldman & Zhang, 2020). On the opposite, to focus on more realistic threats, mem-
134   orization can be defined as vulnerability to extraction or reconstruction attacks (Carlini et al., 2018;
135   2021; 2023; Biderman et al., 2023; Lukas et al., 2023; Dentan et al., 2024). These definitions are
136   mostly used with generative models, as such attacks are more complex to implement on discrimi-
137   native models and often achieve lower performance. As pointed out by Lee et al. (2022); Prashanth
138   et al. (2024), a large majority of elements extracted consist of common strings frequently repeated
139   in standard datasets. This is why counterfactual memorization was adapted to generative models
140
      (Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Pappu et al., 2024; Lesci et al., 2024). Finally, MIA can also
      be used for generative models (Meeus et al., 2024).
141
142
      Explaining and predicting memorization In machine learning, memorization has been com-
143
      monly associated with overfitting and considered the opposite of generalization. However, this be-
144   lief was challenged by Zhang et al. (2017), who proved that a model can simultaneously perfectly fit
145   random labels and real samples. This phenomenon was studied further by Arpit et al. (2017); Chat-
146   terjee (2018), followed by Feldman (2020) who provided a theoretical framework to explain how
147   memorization can in fact increase generalization. His idea is that a substantial number of elements
148   in typical datasets belong to the long tail of the distribution (Zhu et al., 2014), meaning that they be-
149   have like local outliers that are unrepresentative of the overall distribution. As a result, memorizing
150   these elements enables the model to generalize to similar samples at inference time. This idea was
151   confirmed empirically by Feldman & Zhang (2020), and later by Zhang et al. (2023), who observed
152
      that memorized samples are relatively difficult for the model. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024) observed
      that memorization in self-supervised learning can increase generalization.
153
154   A different approach to explain memorization is to analyze the hidden representations learned by the
155   model. For example, Azize & Basu (2024) linked the privacy leakage of a sample to the Mahalanobis
156   distance (Mahalanobis, 1936) between the sample and its data distribution. Leemann et al. (2024)
157   evaluated several metrics to predict memorization from a reference model, and concluded that test
158
      loss is the best predictor. Wongso et al. (2023b) computed Sliced Mutual Information (Goldfeld &
      Greenewald, 2021) between the hidden representations and the labels. They theoretically explain
159
      why a low SMI indicates memorization, and successfully observed this phenomenon in practice.
160
161   These approaches provide a posteriori explanations of memorization, because they are either com-
      puted from the fully trained model or from a reference model. On the opposite, Biderman et al.

      (2023) introduced a new method to predict memorization before the end of pre-training. They
163   achieve promising results with high accuracy. However, they obtain low recall scores, indicating
164   that a significant proportion of the samples that are memorized by the final model cannot be de-
165   tected using their metrics. As they acknowledge, this is an important shortcoming of their method.

167   1.2   P ROBLEM SETTING
168
169   Threat model: predicting memorization, not mitigating it We adapt the setting of Biderman
170   et al. (2023). We assume that an engineer is planning to fine-tune a LLM on a private dataset for
171   a classification task, where a small proportion of the dataset contains sensitive information that
172   should not be memorized by the model for privacy concerns. The engineer has full access to the
173
      model, its training pipeline and intermediate checkpoints. They do not have the computational
      budget to train the shadow models required for a posteriori measures of memorization such as LiRA
174
      or counterfactual memorization (see Section 1.1). Consequently, they aim to conduct some tests at
175
      the beginning of the full training run to approximate a posteriori memorization, and determine if the
176   sensitive samples will be memorized by the fully trained model (see Figure 1).
177
178   The engineer wishes to dedicate only a small amount of compute for these tests, to reduce the
179
      overhead of confidentiality checks. Moreover, they aim to detect vulnerable samples early to in-
      spect them before they are memorized and decide whether to accept the privacy risk, anonymize
180
      or remove the samples, or implement mitigation techniques. This is particularly important for re-
181
      searchers developing empirical defense that optimally allocate their privacy budgets to protect the
182   most vulnerable samples without altering non-vulnerable ones, thereby achieving a better privacy-
183   utility trade-off. We make no assumptions about the subsequent decisions made by the engineer, and
184   only focus on developing a good predictor of which elements will be memorized by the final model.
185
186   Evaluation metrics: FPR at high TPR We use predictions from on the partially trained model
187   to predict memorization in the fully trained model. As for membership inference attacks, we eval-
188   uate the True Positive Rate / False Positive Rate (TPR / FPR) trade-off in the prediction (Carlini
189   et al., 2022a). The TPR represents the proportion of memorized samples in the final model that are
190   correctly detected based on the partially trained one, and the FPR represents the proportion of non-
191   memorized samples that are wrongly detected. We prefer TPR / FPR to precision / recall because
192
      it is independent of the prevalence of memorized samples. However, as noted by Biderman et al.
      (2023), a high TPR is more important than a low FPR. Indeed, false positives lead the engineer to
193
      be overly cautious, which is unprofitable, but does not threaten privacy. Conversely, false negatives
194
      lead the engineer to underestimate memorization, which entails a privacy risk. As a consequence,
195   we will focus on regions of the TPR / FPR curves that achieve a high TPR, typically greater than
196   75%. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) provides a single numerical value for comparing metrics,
197   although it presents a simplistic view of the TPR / FPR trade-off.
198
199   Experimental settings Most studies on memorization in classification settings focus on models
200   of intermediate size trained on datasets such as CIFAR-10 or CIFAR-100 (Aerni et al., 2024; Carlini
201   et al., 2022b; Feldman & Zhang, 2020). We have decided to consider more recent scenarios using
202   LLMs fine-tuned for classification tasks. Indeed, generative models are increasingly trained to pro-
203   duce formatted outputs for tasks previously handled by discriminative models, such as information
204   extraction (Kim et al., 2022; Dhouib et al., 2023), sentiment analysis (Šmı́d et al., 2024), or recom-
205   mendation (Geng et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2022). Moreover, privacy is often a significant concern for
206
      fine-tuning, as the datasets used for this purpose frequently contain sensitive private information.
207   Although our experiments focus on fine-tuned LLMs, our method relies on the specific properties of
208   neither LLMs nor fine-tuning. Consequently, our method is suitable for any model trained for clas-
209   sification tasks. In Section 3.2, we apply our method as-is to a Wide Residual Network (Zagoruyko
210   & Komodakis, 2016) trained from scratch on CIFAR-10, yielding conclusive results.
211   For most experiments, we used three pretrained models with similar architectures: Mistral 7B v1
212   (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama 7B v2 (Touvron et al., 2023), and Gemma 7B (Team et al., 2024). We
213   used three popular academic benchmarks: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), ETHICS (Hendrycks
214   et al., 2021a) and ARC (Boratko et al., 2018). We fine-tuned these models using LoRA (Hu et al.,
215   2022) and question-answering templates asking the model to output the label. Models are trained
      using Next Token Prediction task, computing the loss only for the token corresponding to the label.

      2.1    P RELIMINARY
219
220   Hidden representations in Large Language Models We consider a decoder-only transformer-
221   based LLM such as Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023) trained on a multi-choice question (MCQ) dataset
222   such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b). With this type of architecture, all tokens of the input are
223   embedded into hidden representations in Rd . They are successively transformed at each of the K
224   layers to incorporate information from the context. For example, Llama 2 7B uses d = 4096 and
225
      K = 32. Finally, the representation of the last token at the last layer is used to predict the answer.
226   For k ∈ J1, KK, let Xk ∈ Rd be the hidden representation of the last token after the k-th layer,
227   and Y ∈ {0, 1, 2, . . . , r} the answer of the MCQ. We can think of Xk and Y as random variables
228   following a joint probability distribution Dk that can be estimated from the dataset. In the following,
229   we use information-theoretic tools to analyze the interplay between variables Xk and Y . Note that
230   Dk and Xk depend of the training step, but we omit this aspect in our notations to consider a LLM
231
      that we freeze to analyze its representations.
232
233
      (Pointwise) Sliced Mutual Information Sliced Mutual Information (SMI) was introduced by
      Goldfeld & Greenewald (2021). Similar to Shannon’s Mutual Information (denoted I), it measures
234
      the statistical dependence between two random variables such as Xk and Y . Intuitively, it measures
235
      how much the realization of Xk tells us about the realization of Y . If they are independent, the
236   mutual information is zero ; and if Xk fully determines Y , the mutual information is maximal. In
237   our setting, it represents how useful the hidden representations are to predict the labels. Thus, we
238   expect the SMI to increase with k as the representations become more efficient over layers. Indeed,
239   SMI is not subject to the data processing inequality, contrary to I (Goldfeld & Greenewald, 2021).
240

      Based on Theorem 1, we propose Algorithm 1 to predict memorization. The three hyperparameters
      in bold performed well in every setting we evaluated. We interrupt training when the median training
294
      loss decreases by 95%, as this metric remains stable even in the presence of outliers. We measure
295
      PSMI at the last layer, which is consistently informative, and use a threshold of zero, as supported
296   by Theorem 1. These default values yielded conclusive results when applied to CIFAR-10, for which
297   they were not optimized (see ablation studies in Section 3.2). Consequently, these hyperparameters
298   are likely suitable for practitioners auditing models in diverse classification settings. To facilitate the

348   Computational gains The bottleneck of Algorithm 1 is computing a forward pass for every sam-
349   ple, which costs as much as 1/3 of an epoch (Hobbhahn & Jsevillamol, 2021). Moreover, we
350   typically compute our metric after only 0.2 to 0.4 epochs (see Section 3.2). Thus, our method costs
351   about as much as 2/3 of an epoch. On the opposite, our ground truth memorization requires training
352   100 models for one epoch, which is 150 times more expensive. As explained in Section 1.2, prac-
353   titioners within our threat model do not have the budgets to compute such a posteriori measures of
354
      memorization. Our approach enables them to approximate memorization at minimal cost.

491   4   F INAL REMARKS
492
493   Ethical considerations This paper discusses vulnerability to privacy attacks against language
494   models in practical settings, raising ethical considerations due to similar models trained on pri-
495   vate data already being attacked in production (Nasr et al., 2023). However, we believe that our
496
      work is unlikely to benefit adversaries with harmful intent, for several reasons. First, our approach
      necessitates access to the checkpoint of a partially trained model, and to the training dataset. In prac-
497
      tice, adversaries do not possess this capability, making it impossible for them to apply our method.
498
      Second, even though our work improves our understanding of unintended memorization, we believe
499   that this will benefit privacy researchers more than adversaries. Indeed, it can help practitioners to
500   better audit models under development, and empirical defenses could be derived from our work in
501   the future.
502
503   Limitations and future works Our method is specifically applicable to classification tasks. Most
504   of our experiments focus on LLMs fine-tuned for textual classification, applied to multiple-choice
505   questions. This setting was chosen because datasets such as MMLU are known to be challenging for
506   language models and are often used to evaluate models’ abilities. However, it would be interesting to
507   explore whether our method can be modified to be applicable to LLMs trained on generative tasks.
508   This scenario is indeed widely used and poses significant privacy risks.
509   Moreover, the approach we developed to predict memorization from the early stages of training
510   could be used to develop empirical defenses. Several methods have already been proposed to mit-
511   igate unintended memorization in practice, achieving good privacy-utility trade-offs (Chen et al.,
512   2022; Tang et al., 2022; Chen & Pattabiraman, 2024; Aerni et al., 2024). Our algorithm could be
513   employed to design adaptive defenses that concentrate their efforts on most vulnerable samples to
514   improve the privacy-utility trade-off.
515
516   Reproducibility statement We have detailed all essential hyperparameters necessary to reproduce
517   our experiments. In addition, we provide the following repository containing the Python, Bash and
518   Slurm scripts that we used to deploy our experiments on an HPC cluster. We also provide a PyPI
519   package containing an automated estimator of PSMI that can be used in a wide range of scenarios.

524   5   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we demonstrate that it is possible to predict which samples will be memorized by a lan-
527   guage model in a classification setting. Our metric is computationally efficient, and it can be utilized
528   from the early stages of training. We provide a theoretical justification for our approach, and we
529   validate its effectiveness on three different language model architectures fine-tuned on three differ-
530   ent classification datasets. Moreover, we demonstrate that our method is easily applicable to other
531   classification scenarios by successfully applying it, without modification, to a vision model trained
532   from scratch. We view this method as a first step towards developing useful tools to evaluate models
533   during training, understand the privacy risks they entail, and prevent unintended memorization in
534
      the most efficient way.

779   A     D EFINING AND MEASURING MEMORIZATION FOR CLASSIFICATION TASKS
780
781   Defining and measuring memorization for classification tasks is a challenging task. As explained
782   in Section 1.1, vulnerability to extraction attacks is rarely used for such models. Conversely, in
783   these settings, it is standard to define memorization as vulnerability to membership inference attacks
784
      such as LiRA or to use counterfactual memorization. In Section A.1, we present two variants of
      LiRA (Carlini et al., 2022a): a local version (used in the main body of the paper), which targets a
785
      fixed model and its training set, and a global version, which targets a dataset used to train multiple
786
      models. In Section A.2, we compare LiRA and counterfactual memorization. It appears that these
787   two definitions are consistent with each others, especially for highly memorized samples. This
788   confirms the relevance of choosing LiRA as the ground truth memorization for our experiments.
789
790
      A.1     L I RA ATTACK
791
792   A.1.1    ATTACKING A MODEL : LOCAL VERSION
793
794   In this section we present the original version of LiRA (Carlini et al., 2022a). We call it the local
795
      version, because it targets a fixed model and tries to determine if a target sample was part of its
      training set. Note that this setting is aligned with our threat model (See Section 1.2): the model is
796
      fixed; and for each sample, if the attack confidently predict that it was part of the training set, we
797
      say that it is memorized. This is why we used this local version in the main body of this paper.
798
799
      Notations Let X = {(xi , yi )}i∈J1,N K be a training set of N labelled elements. We focus on multi-
800
      choice question (MCQ) academic benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b). Let S be
801   a random variable representing a subset of elements in J1, N K. Let XS = {(xi , yi ) | i ∈ S} be the
802   corresponding subset of training elements, and fS ∼ T (XS ) be a model trained on this subset with
803   the randomized training procedure T . Then, let L(x, fS ) be the logit gap of the evaluation of x with
804   model fS , i.e. the difference between the highest and second-highest logit.
805
806   The Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA) Let fix a target subset S ∗ , a target model fS ∗ ∼ T (XS ∗ )
807   trained on these elements, and a target element x ∈ X. As every membership inference attack,
808   LiRA aims to determine whether x was in XS ∗ . First, we train a great number of shadow models
809   fS on random subsets of X, and evaluates the logit gap L(x, fS ) for theses shadow models. Then,
      we gather Lin = {L(x, fS ) | x ∈ S}, the logit gaps of model that were trained on x ; and Lout for

      models that were not trained on x. We model these two sets as Gaussian distributions, and compute
811   the probabilities pin and pout of the target logit gap L(x, fS ∗ ) under these distributions.
812
813   The original LiRA score of Carlini et al. (2022a) is defined as LiRA(x, fS∗ ) = pin /pout . However,
814
      it takes very high and low values positive values. For convenient representations in our graphs,
      we used the natural logarithm of this score in the main body of this paper. A value greater that 0
815
      indicates that the sample is memorized, because pin > pout . For example, a value of 4 indicates
816
      strong memorization, because it means that pin ≥ e4 · pout ≃ 54.6 · pout . In other words, the attack
817   suggests that it is 54.6 times more likely that the target samples belongs to the dataset of the target
818   model, which is significant. This is why, unless otherwise stated, memorized samples are defined as
819   the ones with log-LiRA ≥ 4 for our experiments in Section 3.
820
      The number of shadow model needed to compute LiRA score is an important hyperparameter. In
821
      our experiments, we used 100 shadow models to evaluate memorization in each setting, which is in
822
      line with the empirical findings of Carlini et al. (2022a).

844   A.2     C OMPARING SEVERAL DEFINITIONS OF MEMORIZATION
845
846   We compare two definitions of memorization: counterfactual memorization (Feldman & Zhang,
847   2020; Zhang et al., 2023) and vulnerability to LiRA membership inference attack (Carlini et al.,
848   2022a). Counterfactual memorization is a global measure of memorization. Indeed, it quantifies the
849
      impact of a given sample x being in the training set on a population of model trained on random
      splits of a dataset. Similar to the global variant of LiRA (see Section A.1.2), it is not in line with
850
      our threat model, because practitioners want to audit a fixed model, and not a population of model
851
      trained on random splits of a dataset. Note that because counterfactual memorization is a global
852   definition, we compared it to the global version of LiRA. We recall that this is not the one used in
853   the main body of this paper (see Section A.1). We used Equation 2 in (Zhang et al., 2023) to define
854   counterfactual memorization. We used the logit gap as the performance metric M in their equation.
855
      Our results are presented in Figure 6. We use Spearman’s R score to quantify the consistency
856
      between the two definitions. Indeed, we are interested in the order of samples with respect to the
857
      memorization metric. We observe that Spearman’s R between the two definition is high in every
858   settings: it is always greater than 0.75, and escalates to 0.88 for Mistral models trained on ARC
859   dataset. This demonstrates that LiRA and counterfactual memorization are consistent with each
860   other. In addition, we can separate the samples in two groups: a first, weakly memorized group,
861   for which there is greater variability between the two definitions (bottom left of the graphs), and a
862   strongly memorized group, for which the two definitions are much more consistent with each other
863   (top right of the graphs). The second group is the most important one in our setting, because we are
      interested in predicting memorized samples (and not predicting non-memorized ones).

982    Discussion on a better bound for Equation 5 The proof above provides a constructive algorithm
983    to obtain (R0 , R1 , mg , ν) ∈ R4+,∗ such that (X, Y ) is (R1 , R2 , mg , ν)-SSM separated with respect
984    to Definition 3 in (Wongso et al., 2023b). Depending on the hyperparameter R ∈]0, D/2[, the bound
985    is different. As a result, this hyperparameter can be optimized to find the better possible bound with
986    this algorithm. We did not performed this optimization because it is not useful for the purpose of
987    Theorem 1. Indeed, we only use this theorem to illustrate why we expect outliers in the hidden
988
       representations distribution to have a lower PSMI (see Section 2.1).

1001   C.1      I MPLEMENTING OUR BASELINE
1002
1003   As explained in the introduction, the baseline of Biderman et al. (2023) is the only comparable
1004   method we are aware of. However, it is not directly applicable to our classification setting. Their
1005   method measures k-extractability (Carlini et al., 2021) on the partially trained model to predict mem-
1006   orization in the fully trained model. However, as explained in Section 1.1, extractability is rarely
1007   used to define memorization in a classification setting. Indeed, current extraction or reconstruction
1008
       attacks against classification models are both more complex and less powerful than extraction at-
       tacks against generative models (Carlini et al., 2023). Consequently, we modified the baseline of
1009
       Biderman et al. (2023) to suit our classification setting. While we still use memorization within the
1010
       partially trained model to predict memorization in the final model, we replaced k-extractability by
1011   the vulnerability to LiRA attack.
1012
1013   The computational cost of this adapted baseline is significantly higher than that of the methods we
1014
       evaluate, as it requires training the shadow models needed for LiRA attack. As a consequence, this
       baseline would not be suitable for practitioner within our threat model (see Section 1.2). Neverthe-
1015
       less, we compare our method to this baseline because it is the only comparable approach that assess
1016
       the possibility of predicting memorization before the end of training.

1021   number of shadow models (see details in Section A). To minimize the computational cost of our
1022   experiments, for each dataset, we trained 100 shadow models on random splits containing half of
1023   the elements of the dataset. Each random split (and the model trained on it) is associated to a number

1029          • Local LiRA: We select the model trained on random split 0 to be our target model, and use
1030            the 99 other models as the shadow models for the attack. In addition to the training cost,
1031            this requires one forward pass per shadow model on the training set of the target model
1032            (i.e. random split 0). Note that this is the setting used in the main body of the paper, so the

1035          • Global LiRA: We attack each model with the 99 other models trained on different random

1037            the training cost, this requires one forward pass per shadow model on all elements of the
1038            dataset.
1039          • Counterfactual memorization: For each element x of the complete dataset, we separate
1040            the shadow models into two groups: the one that had x in the training set, and the others.
1041            Given that each random split contains half of the samples, these two groups have roughly
1042            the same size. We used them to compute counterfactual memorization (Zhang et al., 2023).
1043            In addition to the training cost, this requires one forward pass per shadow model on all
1044            elements of the dataset.

       memorization. The training of these shadow model was by far the most expensive part of our ex-
1049
       periments from a computational perspective. However, this operation can be parallelized on a many
1050   workers within an HPC cluster, because each shadow model is trained independently.

       C.3   I MPLEMENTING PREDICTORS
1053
1054   Algorithm 1 in Section 2.3 explains how we use PSMI to predict memorization in the final model.
1055   This algorithm can be easily adapted to use other predictors instead of PSMI. For instance, Algo-
1056   rithm 2 illustrates how to use the loss as a predictor. Unlike PSMI, it does not require a hyperpa-
1057   rameter to select a layer. However, as shown in Section 3.2, the choice of the last layer is robust
1058   across all empirical settings we evaluated, so this hyperparameter does not introduce additional com-
1059   plexity. Conversely, using the loss instead of PSMI requires an extra hyperparameter (denoted x in
1060   Algorithm 2) to define the proportion of samples to filter based on their loss values.
1061
1062   Algorithm 2 Using Loss to predict memorization
1063    1: Interrupt training when the median training loss has decreased by at least 95%.
1064    2: Compute a forward pass for every sample to retrieve the loss.
1065    3: Predict that every sample with top x% highest loss will be memorized.
1066
1067   We evaluated five possible metrics to predict memorization at the early stages of training. For the
1068   metrics that require the hidden states of the model (PSMI and Mahalanobis distance), we recall that
1069   the hidden state at layer k is defined as the representation of the last token (the one before the label)
1070   after layer k (see section 2.1).
1071
1072          • PSMI. We use algorithm 1 in (Wongso et al., 2023a) to estimate PSMI. We sample 2000
1073            direction uniformly on the unit sphere. Indeed, we observed that the mutual information
1074            between random directions and the label has a mean of about 4.5 · 10−3 and a standard
1075
                deviation of about 5.5 · 10−3 . Thus, if we approximate these distributions
                                                                                        √ by Gaussians, we
1076            get a margin at 95% confidence interval of about (1.96 × 5.5 · 10−3 )/ 2000 ≃ 2.4 · 10−4 .
1077
                This is about 20 times smaller than the mean, so we consider that our metric is stable
                enough with 2000 estimators.
1078
1079          • Loss. We directly use the cross-entropy loss of the model for the last token before the label.
                This metric was suggested by Leemann et al. (2024).


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
                        • Logit Gap. The logits are the outputs of the fully-connected layer applied to the last token,
1081                      before the softmax. We define the logit gap as the difference between the logit of the correct
1082                      prediction and the maximum logit of an incorrect prediction.
1083
                        • Mahalanobis distance. It is the Mahalanobis distance (Mahalanobis, 1936) of the hidden
1084
                          representation of a training sample to the distribution of hidden representation of the other
1085                      training samples. To reduce computational costs, we first project every hidden states using
1086                      a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) with a target dimension of 500. This metric was
1087                      suggested by Azize & Basu (2024).
1088                    • Our baseline: early memorization. We define early memorization as the natural loga-
