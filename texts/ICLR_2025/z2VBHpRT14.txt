011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Space situational awareness (SSA) plays an imperative role in maintaining safe
014            space operations, especially given the increasingly congested space traffic around
015
               the Earth. Space-based SSA offers a flexible and lightweight solution compared
               to traditional ground-based SSA. With advanced machine learning approaches,
016
               space-based SSA can extract features from high-resolution images in space to
017
               detect and track resident space objects (RSOs). However, existing spacecraft im-
018            age datasets, such as SPARK, fall short of providing realistic camera observa-
019            tions, rendering the derived algorithms unsuitable for real SSA systems. In this
020            research, we introduce SpaceSet, a large-scale realistic space-based image dataset
021            for SSA. We consider accurate space orbit dynamics and a physical camera model
022            with various noise distributions, generating images at the photon level. To extend
023            the available observation window, four overlapping cameras are simulated with a
024            fixed rotation angle. SpaceSet includes images of RSOs observed from 19km to
025            63, 000km, captured by a tracker operating in LEO, MEO, and GEO orbits over
026
               a period of 5, 000 seconds. Each image has a resolution of 4418 × 4418 pixels,
               providing detailed features for developing advanced SSA approaches. We split
027
               the dataset into three subsets: SpaceSet-100, SpaceSet-5000, and SpaceSet-full,
028
               catering to various image processing applications. The SpaceSet-full corpus in-
029            cludes a comprehensive data-loader with 781.5GB of images and 25.9M B of
030            ground truth labels. We also benchmark detection and tracking algorithms on the
031            SpaceSet-100 dataset using a specified splitting method to accelerate the training
032            process.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Space Situational Awareness (SSA) Wang et al. (2022) plays a crucial role in ensuring the safety
038   of space assets by providing real-time information perception and risk evaluation for space opera-
039
      tions, such as spacecraft navigation Hein (2020) and debris mitigation Usovik (2023). Conventional
      SSA systems, like those used by the Japanese Space Agency (JAXA) Harris et al. (2021), rely on
040
      observing resident space objects (RSOs) and determining their orbits using ground-based facilities
041
      equipped with large telescopes and radars. These systems necessitate extensive site areas, high
042   costs, and specific geographical locations. Given the computational limitations of satellites, current
043   space-based SSA systems, which involve complex numerical calculations, typically depend on the
044   space-ground network for data processing and information fusion. This reliance results in substantial
045   communication loads and delays.
046
      With the advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) and high-performance edge computing, an on-
047
      board vision-based SSA system presents a more flexible and lightweight alternative to traditional
048   ground-based SSA for RSO detection and tracking. One of the primary challenges in SSA is pro-
049   viding accurate position and orientation vectors (observations) of targets to determine their orbits.
050   Methods such as Gauss’s method Vallado (2001), which requires at least three observations for
051   preliminary orbit determination, and Lambert’s method Engels & Junkins (1981), which needs only
052   two position vectors with temporal information, are used for this purpose. Essentially, increasing the
053   number of observations enhances the accuracy of orbit determination, highlighting the importance of
      the object detection and tracking (ODT) component in SSA. To develop precise and practical ODT

082   Each image boasts a resolution of 4418 × 4418 pixels, providing detailed features for the develop-
083   ment of advanced SSA approaches. We have divided the dataset into three subsets: SpaceSet-100,
084   SpaceSet-5000, and SpaceSet-full, catering to various image processing applications. The SpaceSet-
085   full corpus includes a comprehensive dataloader with 781.5 GB of images and 25.9 MB of ground
086   truth labels. To the best of our knowledge, SpaceSet is the first image dataset to offer four-camera
087   observations with realistic image generation from space for space object detection and tracking. The
088   key contributions and features of this dataset are summarized as follows:
089   Realistic Image Generation: Incorporating accurate space orbit dynamics and a physical camera
090   model with various noise distributions to produce photon-level realistic space images.
091
      Multiple Camera Observations: Simulating four overlapping cameras with fixed rotation angles
092
      to extend the observation window.
093
094   Large Range Tracker Observation: Covering RSO images observed from 19 km to 63, 000 km for
095   the tracker operating in LEO, MEO, and GEO orbits.
096   Automated Label Generation with Bearing Angle: Providing accurate ground truth labels with
097   bearing angle information generated by the simulator through an automated transformation and an-
098   notation process.
099
      Extensive Benchmarks: Benchmarking the dataset using SOTA algorithms, including YOLOv5,
100   YOLOv8, YOLOv10, DINO, etc., on SpaceSet-100 with a specified splitting method to expedite the
101   training process. Additionally, various object tracking methods are compared on SpaceSet-100 to
102   explore its applications.

140   3.1      DATA G ENERATION
141
      The SpaceSet dataset is collected from a real-time high-fidelity simulator based on precise space
142
      orbit dynamics and physical camera models. Since the space-based observer operates at an altitude
143
      of 500 km, the effects of the atmosphere and related noise are not included in the modeling process.
144   The space environment model simulates a catalog of RSOs in orbit around the Earth, along with
145   other celestial bodies in the sky. The RSO simulation is based on the United States 18th SDS
146   Space Catalog 3 , which is fetched in Two-Line Elements (TLEs) format for the desired simulation
147   epoch and propagated using an SGP4 propagator. The propagator provides the positions and velocity
148   vectors of all objects in the TEME coordinate system, which is used to populate the 3D environment.
149
      The modeling of environmental noise expected for a sensor is also incorporated. Under favorable
150   imaging conditions, the sensor’s payload is oriented away from the sun and perpendicular to the
151   orbit, allowing the primary background noise source to be the Earth’s limb—the bright edge of the
152   Earth’s horizon. This background noise is modeled using data from the Hubble Space Telescope 4
153   and the NEOSSAT mission Thorsteinson (2018). The camera captures a circular image on the image
154   plane, and the detector on the focal plane records the digital image.
155
      To detect which RSOs crossing the field of view (FOV) can be identified by the sensor, a photometric
156   detection model is applied. The sensor detects an object only when its signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
157   exceeds a specified threshold, typically set at 5. The received signal is calculated assuming 100%
158   diffuse reflection, where the fraction of incident sunlight reflected to the sensor is given by:

192   The model for projecting star/object positions onto the image is based on a pinhole camera model,

203
      state information (including position, velocity and attitude) of RSO is propagated with the public

205   orbit propagation program is developed based on the Standards of Fundamental Astronomy (SOFA)
206   Board package and SGP4 model Vallado & Crawford (2008). With camera specifications such as
207   lens parameters, sensor parameters, and camera pointing direction angles (elevation and azimuth
208   angles in RSW coordinate frame), the physical camera model can generate pixel values of images at
209   each timestep based on the aforementioned relative illumination and noise distributions. The physi-
210   cal camera model used for generating realistic space-based images includes several key components,
211   including the pinhole camera model, lens distortion, and noise modeling. Each 3D point X in the
212   space is projected onto the image plane using the pinhole camera model, then distorted based on the
213
      lens distortion model, and finally, various noise distributions as mentioned are added to simulate the
      physical conditions of space imaging. For instance, we model the noise as a combination of Poisson
214
      noise (sensor noise) and Gaussian noise (read noise):

226   3.2   DATASET VALIDITY AND U NIQUENESS
227
      Currently, there are fewer than six datasets available in this field, and they are all based on simu-
228
      lations, as NASA’s database is not publicly accessible. The dataset presented in this work is the
229
      first large-scale, realistic, space-based image dataset at the photon level, aiming to bridge the gap
230   between simulated and real-world data. Most existing datasets, such as BUAA-SID-share 1.0 Zhang
231   et al. (2010), SPARK Musallam et al. (2021b), and the Space Target Dataset Zhang et al. (2022c), are
232   primarily generated for satellite pose estimation and space target classification in ideal simulation
233   conditions. These datasets focus on capturing targets from close distances and multiple angles to
234   emphasize single-target characteristics. In contrast, the presented dataset captures targets at various
235   distances based on realistic space-based camera observations. We have compared our images with
236   the real observed images with a ground-based telescope in Appendix A.6, where the starfield and
237   captured positions at various timestamps are first compared to show the accuracy of the simulator.
238
      These imaging results then clearly validate the realistic characteristics of our simulated images.

348   Statistical Features of Individual Images: Here, we provide a specific quantitative description of
349   images of size 4418 × 4418 pixels (16-bit, 39.1MB in storage). After testing, the average signal-to-
350   noise ratio (SNR) and average root mean square (RMS) contrast Peli (1990) of SpaceSet-full images
351   are 1.94 dB and 4.67, respectively (typical values for general images are 30 dB for SNR and 80 for
352   contrast). With a threshold pixel value of 2000, the bright point ratio is 0.47. This large image
353
      size ensures high resolution and clarity, which is beneficial for detailed analysis and visualization in
      various applications. However, hardware or applications such as edge computing in satellites may
354
      have difficulty handling images with such high resolution. In such cases, we suggest compressing
355
      the images before using this dataset. In real-space environments, various types of noise, such as
356   optical and electromagnetic interference, can be present, which manifest in the SNR parameter
357   of this dataset. Generally, a smaller SNR indicates a greater amount of noise in the images (see
358   Figure 3 (b)). This suggests that the image information in this dataset more closely aligns with
359   real-world conditions but poses challenges for feature extraction. Additionally, due to variations in
360   the distance between the targets and the cameras, the brightness of different targets varies, which is
361   a natural phenomenon in imaging. The targets appear brighter when they are closer to the camera
362   and dimmer when they are farther away. As some applications may have specific requirements for
363   image brightness, some image processing algorithms may be needed to enhance brightness.
364   Statistical Features of the Whole Dataset: Figure 4 shows the histogram of observation ranges for
365   four cameras in SpaceSet-full. The horizontal axis of the graph represents the observation distance
366   of the cameras, and the vertical axis represents the counts of target occurrences. Each bar in the
367   histogram corresponds to a range of 3128 km. The closest observed target is at a distance of 19 km,
368   while the farthest is at 62,578 km. The bars for the ranges 19 to 3147 km and 37,554 to 40,682 km
369   are the highest, indicating the highest frequency of target occurrences. In contrast, the frequencies
370
      are much lower for other ranges, resembling a long-tail distribution commonly seen in the dataset.
      More analysis, including the positional distributions and the size distribution of all observed objects,
371
      is illustrated in A.1 Figure 5 and Figure 6, respectively.

      5.1   BASIC S ETTINGS
382
383   We utilize the Ultralytics library Jocher et al. (2023) under the AGPL-3.0 license, which includes all
384   YOLO series models for object detection and tracking (ODT). Other architectures are implemented
385   using the MMDetection toolbox Chen et al. (2019) from OpenMMLab. Following standard config-
386   urations, all models are trained on an NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU for up to 400 epochs, with early
387   stopping if there is no improvement for 30 epochs. From the SpaceSet-100 dataset, 70% of images
388   are used for training and 20% for validation. To minimize randomness in testing, 100 images from
389
      the SpaceSet-5000 dataset are selected for evaluation. Metrics for detection performance include
      mAP@50, mAP@50-95, precision, recall, and F1 score, while object tracking is assessed using
390
      false positives, ID switches, and multiple object tracking accuracy (MOTA).

      5.2   O BJECT D ETECTION B ENCHMARK
393
394   In our object detection benchmark, we evaluated various models, primarily YOLOv8m. We also
395   included yolov3 mobilenetv2, using MobileNetV2 as the YOLOv3 backbone Redmon (2018), and
396   faster rcnn, based on the ResNet architecture Ren et al. (2016). Transformer-based models such as
397   DETR, Deformable-DETR, and DINO were also employed Carion et al. (2020) Zhu et al. (2020)
398   Zhang et al. (2022a). To optimize training, batch sizes were adjusted based on GPU memory con-
399   straints. All models utilized our custom preprocessing pipeline, tailored to the challenges of SSA
400   datasets. All models used 260x260 images as input to ensure result reliability.

516   6     L IMITATIONS OF S PACE S ET DATASET
517
518   Despite the comprehensive nature of the SpaceSet dataset, there are current limitations to consider.
519   First, while the dataset is designed to be highly realistic, the images are still generated via sim-
520   ulations, which may not capture all the complexities and variabilities of real space environments.
521   Second, the dataset focuses on high-resolution images, which, while beneficial for detailed analysis,
522
      also require considerable computational resources for processing and storage, potentially limiting
      accessibility for researchers with limited resources. Lastly, while the dataset includes a range of
523
      orbital distances and conditions, it hasn’t covered all possible scenarios that SSA systems might
524
      encounter, necessitating further validation with real-world data to ensure robustness and generaliz-
525   ability of the developed algorithms.

528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   Focusing on improving SSA, we present SpaceSet, a large-scale realistic space-based image dataset
531
      designed to overcome the limitations of existing datasets such as SPARK. SpaceSet provides a com-
      prehensive collection of high-resolution images (4418×4418 pixels) generated using accurate space
532
      orbit dynamics and a physical camera model with Poisson noise distribution, capturing observations
533
      from 19 km to 63,000 km. The dataset is divided into three subsets: SpaceSet-100, SpaceSet-
534   5000, and SpaceSet-full, each catering to different research needs. Our benchmark evaluations
535   show that while larger YOLO models generally outperform smaller ones, lightweight models like
536   YOLOv5s and YOLOv8n offer faster detection speeds, crucial for space-based applications with
537   limited computing resources. Moreover, state-of-the-art object detection and tracking methods per-
538   form inadequately in the space environment, underscoring the necessity for algorithms tailored to
539   SSA. SpaceSet not only facilitates the development of new object detection and tracking algorithms
      but also serves as a benchmark for evaluating advanced SSA techniques.

      A.5   O BJECT T RACKING M ETHODS
852
853   All the selected multiple object tracking methods are detection-based, relying on a well-trained
854   YOLOv8n model, as described in Section 5.2 Object Detection Benchmark. The tracking process
855   operates on the original full-size images with a resolution of 4418x4418 pixels. To facilitate de-
856   tection, each image is initially divided into 260x260 segments, and the position of each segment
857   within the original image is recorded. Object detection is then performed on these segments, and
858   the detected positions are mapped back to their respective locations on the original full-size image,
859   using the recorded positional information.
860   To handle objects that may span across adjacent segments, a merging strategy is implemented. The
861   strategy calculates the distance between the edges of bounding boxes in neighboring segments. If the
862   distance between the right edge of one bounding box and the left edge of an adjacent one is below a
863   set threshold, the bounding boxes are merged, ensuring an accurate representation of the object on
      the original image. After this merging process, the tracking algorithm operates on the entire original

865   accuracy.
