009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training LLMs presents significant challenges, including data access, privacy
012            concerns, the complexity of training schedules, and limited resources. Therefore,
013            a more accessible approach involves integrating existing LLMs, each tailored for
014            different tasks or trained on distinct datasets, into an advanced and robust model
015            with enhanced capabilities. Popular methods like ensemble and weight merging
016
               require substantial memory and struggle to adapt to changing data environments.
               Recent efforts have aimed to transfer only the collective knowledge of multiple
017
               LLMs to the target LLM. However, the resulting fused model often suffers from
018
               interference and performance degradation due to a lack of flexibility in the fusion
019            process, including candidate selection and training pipeline. To address these
020            issues, we propose a dynamic fusion framework to adaptively select LLMs for
021            integration. Specifically, to diminish knowledge interference during LLM fusion,
022            we introduce an adaptive selection network. It is a learnable mechanism that
023            explicitly evaluates and selects the best-performing source LLMs based on their
024            rewards, allowing us to fuse knowledge from a flexible number model candidates.
025            To improve the knowledge fusion process, we propose a dynamic weighted fusion
026            strategy that considers the intrinsic characteristics of candidate LLMs during fusion.
027
               Additionally, we incorporate a feedback-driven loss function to prevent the selector
               from converging to a state where it consistently assigns the same candidates. Our
028
               experiments demonstrate that our method consistently enhances model performance
029
               across multiple benchmarks, yielding an improvement of up to 2.2%. Additionally,
030            our approach achieves a notable reduction in knowledge interference, showing up
031            to 50% decrease compared to existing work.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has led to the development of many specialized
037   versions Zhang et al. (2023c); Christophe et al. (2024); Geng et al. (2023); Chenghao Fan & Tian
038   (2023); Li et al. (2023), each tailored to specific domains and enhancing various aspects of daily
039
      life. However, the complexities involved in data collecting and pre-processing, training schedule
      design, and substantial training energy are challenging, particularly for academic researchers with
040
      limited experience and resources. Moreover, significant difficulties arise in fields like healthcare and
041
      business, where privacy concerns restrict data sharing between institutions. This limitation hampers
042   the ability to gather sufficient data to train robust models. Consequently, institutions and companies
043   often rely solely on their own datasets, which constrains their ability to develop superior models.
044   Given these challenges, a more straightforward approach has gained attention Jiang et al. (2023);
045   Jin et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023b); Wan et al. (2024a): instead of developing a new LLM from
046   scratch, integrating existing LLMs into a unified model is more appealing. This integrated model
047   serves as a robust base that can be further refined for various tasks, thereby avoiding the need for
048   extensive individual training or data transfer.
049   Existing solutions, such as ensemble methods Jiang et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2023), enhance prediction
050   performance by aggregating outputs from multiple models but require substantial memory and
051   increase inference time due to the need to maintain and operate several models simultaneously.
052   Another method involves merging several neural networks into a single network within the parameter
053   space Jin et al. (2022). This generally presumes uniform network architecture and relies on manually
      configured weight merging or adding additional layers. Additionally, Mixture of Expert (MoE)

143   2.2   K NOWLEDGE I NTERFERENCE
144
145   Knowledge Interference refers to the adverse impact on a model’s performance when incorporating
146
      knowledge from other models, leading to decreased task performance. This can occur due to:
      1) Conflicting Information: Models trained on different datasets or tasks contribute conflicting
147
      knowledge, resulting in confusion and degraded performance on specific tasks. 2) Dilution of
148
      Valuable Knowledge: Introducing less relevant or lower-quality information can dilute the original
149   model’s knowledge. 3) Overfitting to Irrelevant Patterns: The fused model may overfit to noise or
150   less useful patterns from new models, losing focus on critical aspects of the tasks it was initially
151   trained on. Fig. 2 shows the results of FuseLLM Wan et al. (2024a) on 27 tasks of the Big-Bench
152   Hard Suzgun et al. (2022) benchmark, exhibiting performance degradation on multiple tasks (in red).
153   We also observe that fusing more models (upper part: fusing 3 models, lower part: fusing 4 models)
154   can lead to even more degradation. Detailed results are shown in Tab. 3.

201   4     M ETHODOLOGY
202
203   Our method advances existing knowledge fusion methods by introducing a dynamic framework that
204   consists of an Adaptive Selection Network and a Dynamic Weighted Fusion mechanism, as illustrated
205   in Fig. 3. Specifically, at each training step, the Adaptive Selector evaluates performance metrics to
206   dynamically select a subset of candidate models based on their probabilistic distribution matrices
207   rather than all candidates. More importantly, both the selection of candidates and the number of
208
      candidates selected are adaptive, preventing knowledge interference and enhancing overall model
      performance. The selected candidates are then fused using a weighted sum based on normalized
209
      selection probabilities. This process is guided by a specially designed loss function that refines model
210
      selection through feedback. Our framework provides flexibility for future scalability and allows the
211   integration process to accommodate varying computational constraints and application needs.

      7   C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we propose a novel dynamic fusion framework for integrating multiple LLMs. Our
534   adaptive selection network selectively integrates the best-performing source LLMs, overcoming the
535   limitations of existing methods and minimizing knowledge interference. We also introduce a dynamic
536   weighted fusion strategy and a feedback-driven loss function to enhance the fusion process. Our
537   method significantly improves adaptability and performance, offering an efficient solution for LLM
538   integration while maintaining parameter size and computational efficiency. Limitations remain due to
539   the intensive token alignment required prior to training, and future work should explore training on
      diverse datasets.
