010

                In training-free Conditional Diffusion Models (CDMs), the sampling pro-
012
                cess is steered by the gradient of the loss E(y, z, Cψ ), which assesses the
013             gap between the guidance y and the condition extracted from the interme-
014             diate outputs. Here the condition extraction network Cψ (·), which could
015             be a segmentation or depth estimation network, is pre-trained for training-
016             free purpose. However, existing methods often require small guidance
017             steps, leading to longer sampling times. We introduce an alternative max-
018             imization framework to scrutinize training-free CDMs that tackles slow
019             sampling. Our framework pinpoints manifold deviation as the key factor
020             behind the sluggish sampling. More iterations are needed for the sampling
021
                process to closely follow the image manifold and reach the target condi-
                tions, as the loss gradient doesn’t provide sufficient guidance for larger
022
                steps. To improve this, we suggest retraining the condition extraction net-
023
                work Cψ (·) to refine the loss’s guidance, thereby introducing our AccCtr.
024             This retraining process is simple, and integrating AccCtr into current CDMs
025             is a seamless task that does not impose a significant computational bur-
026             den. Extensive testing has demonstrated that AccCtr significantly boosts
027             performance, offering superior sample quality and faster generation times
028             across a variety of conditional generation tasks.

031   1     introduction
032
033
      Over the past few years, diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019;
034
      Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b) have achieved significant success in generative tasks
035   like image generation Nichol & Dhariwal (2021); Song & Ermon (2020); Song et al. (2021a),
036   image inpainting Chung et al. (2023), super-resolution Saharia et al. (2023), image editing
037   Choi et al. (2021), thanks to their strong expressive and re-editing capabilities.
038
      Conditional diffusion models generally employ two techniques: classifier-guided Dhari-
039
      wal & Nichol (2021) and classifier-free Ho & Salimans (2021a) diffusion models. Despite
040
      their effectiveness, these methods encounter challenges related to learning cost and model
041   generality, as they require additional training and data for conditional generation. Recent
042   advances Chung et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Bansal et al. (2024); Yang
043   et al. (2024b) have addressed these issues by developing training-free methods that leverage
044   the differential loss guidance during the denoising process.
045
      These training-free methods, though they avoid extra training, demand fine-tuned guidance
046
      steps for accuracy, which extends sampling times. This is mainly because the tangent space
047   defined by the differential loss can only approximate a local image manifold area. If
048   the starting point is remote from the target, multiple manifolds are needed to span the
049   gap. Thus, more iterations are crucial for the denoising process to navigate the manifold’s
050   curvature and reach the target condition effectively. Current approaches Chung et al.
051   (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Bansal et al. (2023) often use small loss-guided steps to ensure
052   precision, which can considerably slow down the process. However, Yang et al. (2024b) has
053   made significant progress by enabling larger guidance steps through optimization, thus
      improving algorithm efficiency.

054
      Unlike Yang et al. (2024b) using optimization to constrain the guidance steps to remain
055   within the boundaries of the intermediate data, we improve the efficiency with a alternative
056   maximization framework that simplifies the sampling in training-free CDMs to optimizing
057   two objectives: log p(z0 ) for unconditional generation and log p(y|z0 ) for the conditional
058   generation. Here, z0 represents the denoised image of the diffusion model at time step 0.
059   We denote the image manifold consisting of z0 as M0 . This new interpretation guides us to
060   streamline sampling by reducing the optimization steps necessary for each objective. Our
061   further study reveals that reducing the optimization steps for log p(z0 ) is straightforward,
062   but not so for log p(y|z0 ). Taking the value of a well-trained model s(zt ), we can estimate
063
      the denoised image z0|t , i.e. the projection of zt on the manifold M0 , in one step. However,
064
      maximizing log py (z0|t ) involves the gradient of E(y, z0|t , Cψ ) and requires multiple steps
      for gradient descent to reach the final outcome.
065
066   To reduce the maximization steps needed for log p(y|z0|t ), we propose retaining the con-
067   dition extraction network Cψ (·) to enhance its ability so that the gradient of E(y, z0|t , Cψ )
068   provides a more accurate direction for larger steps. Consequently, it is logical to retrain
069   the network Cψ (·) with two distinct objectives. The first is to ensure that Cψ (z0|t ) effec-
070   tively extracts the necessary conditions from z0|t . The second is to adjust the gradient of
071   E(y, z0|t , Cψ ) so that it provides accurate guidance for larger steps.
072   In summary, our contributions are fourfold: 1. We introduce a novel maximization frame-
073   work that provides insights into the analysis of training-free CDMs. 2. We identify the key
074   bottleneck in the generation speed of current training-free CDMs using this framework. 3.
075   We propose a loss to retrain the condition extraction network to address this bottleneck. 4.
076   Our model outperforms previous models in efficiency and sample quality.

      2   Related work
079
080
      Conditional Diffusion Models (CDMs) are typically divided into two categories: training-
081
      required and training-free. A key aspect of both types of models is the estimation of the
082   conditional score ∇zt log p(zt , y) or its component ∇zt log p(y|zt ), which is derived from
083   the relationship ∇zt log p(zt , y) = ∇zt log p(zt ) + ∇zt log p(y|zt ).
084
085
      Training-required CDMs are categorized into two branches. The first one is the classifier-
      guided diffusion mode (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), training a time-dependent classifier
086
      denoted as pϕ (y|zt , t) to approximate the posterior probability p(y|zt ). Consequently, we
087
      have ∇zt log p(zt , y) = ∇zt log p(zt ) + ∇zt log pϕ (y|zt , t), where the first term represents the
088   unconditional score function, while the second term signifies the adjustment that converts
089   the unconditional score into a conditional one. The other one is the classifier-free diffusion
090   model (Ho & Salimans, 2021b). This approach employs a neural network to approximate
091   the conditional score ∇zt log p(zt , y). Notable examples include Stable Diffusion (Rombach
092   et al., 2022b), ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), and ControlNet++ (Ming Li, 2024), Con-
093   trolNeXt (Peng et al., 2024), and AnyControl (Sun et al., 2024). These models are great at
094   creating realistic images but require more data and training time.
095   Training-free CDMs eliminates classifier training by defining a loss E(y, z0|t , Cψ ) and
096   using its gradient to approximate the conditional score ∇zt log p(y|zt ). In the litera-
097   ture, researchers devised various strategies to improve the conditional score estimation.
098   MCG (Chung et al., 2022) addresses solver deviations with a correction term. DPS (Chung
099   et al., 2023) integrates diffusion sampling with manifold constraints for better noise han-
100   dling. FreeDoM (Yu et al., 2023) uses a Time-Travel Strategy for robust generation.
101   UGD (Bansal et al., 2024) and DiffPIR (Zhu et al., 2023) guide clean samples z0 to in-
102   termediate manifolds zt . LGD (Song et al., 2023) uses Monte Carlo sampling for estimation
103
      refinement. MPGD (He et al., 2024) and DSG (Yang et al., 2024b) apply guidance within
      data manifolds, with DSG providing a closed-form solution. These approaches often re-
104
      quire around 100 sampling steps for quality generation, contrasting with the typically less
105
      than 20 steps needed by training-required CDMs.
106
107   We in this paper delve into the rationale behind the increased sampling steps required for
      training-free CDMs and propose a strategy to enhance their efficiency.

201   4     Alternative Maximization For Conditional Diffusion
202
203   In this section, we frame the conditional diffusion process as an alternating maximization
204   of two objectives: p(z0 ) and p(y|z0 ). This insight helps us understand why training-free
205
      CDMs require more sampling steps and leads to a strategy for speeding up the process.
206
207   4.1   The local maxima Characteristics of p(z0 ) and p(y|z0 )
208
      The marginal distribution p(z0 ) peaks at natural images, and the condition extraction
209   function Cψ (·) is tailored for such images. The conditional distribution p(y|z0 ) reaches its
210   peak when y matches z0 , with p(y|z0 ) ≥ p(y|z) for neighboring images z ̸= z0 . Therefore,
211   p(y|z0 ) attains its maximum where p(z0 ) is locally maximized. Consequently, the local
212   maxima of p(y|z0 ) form a subset of the local maxima of p(z0 ). In other words, wherever
213   p(z0 ) is locally maximized, p(y|z0 ) is also likely to achieve a local maximum, provided
214   y describes z0 . This relationship emphasizes the role of the conditional distribution in
215   guiding the generative process toward images that not only align with the natural image
      distribution but also closely match the specified conditions.

239   4.2          Alternatve Maximization
240
241   We shift focus from the probabilistic details of p(z0 ) and p(y|z0 ) in the following sections,
242   treating them as functions of z0 under a given condition y. We refer to p(y|z0 ) as py (z0 ),
243   recognizing that the local maxima of py (z0 ) are contained within those of p(z0 ). The
244   conditional generation aims to maximize log p(z0 , y) by sequentially optimizing log p(z0 )
245   and log py (z0 ). This strategy, as outlined in the proposition 1, efficiently optimizes the
246
      likelihood log p(z0 , y).
247   Proposition 1 (Convergence of Alternative Maximization). Let A(z) and B(z) be two functions
248   defined on the same domain. Suppose that: SB , the local maxima point set of B(z), is a subset of SA ,
249   the local maxima point set of A(z). Then, the alternating maximization of A(z) and B(z) converges
250   to a local maximum of the function A(z) + B(z).
251
      The detailed proof are reserved for Appendix A. Here, we provide an intuitive explanation
252
      for why the proposition holds true: Since the local maxima of B(z) are a subset of those of
253
      A(z), maximizing B(z) will not conflict with the maximization of A(z), as both functions
254   share the same maxima. In each step of alternating maximization, either A(z) or B(z) is
255   maximized, ensuring that the combined function A(z) + B(z) is always non-decreasing.
256   This process continually improves or maintains the value of A(z) + B(z), progressively
257   guiding the optimization towards the shared local maxima. Therefore, alternating maxi-
258   mization converges to a local maximum of the combined function.
259
      Equations (10)(11), along with Equation (12), serve as maximization solvers for log p(z0 )
260   and log py (z0 ). The alternative maximization sampling process is presented in Algorithm 1.

333   5.2    Our approach
334
      For pre-trained condition extraction networks Cψ (·), our five experiments suggest that the
335
      gradient descent algorithm requires more iterations. This is due to the fact that the gradient
336
      ∇z0|t E(y, z0|t , Cψ ) may not provide accurate estimates for large steps. To reduce the number
337
      of maximization steps needed for log py (z0|t ), we propose to refine the condition extraction
338
      network Cψ (·) to improve its accuracy, ensuring that the gradient of E(y, z0|t , Cψ ) offers
339
      a more precise direction for larger steps. Consequently, it is logical to retrain the network
340   Cψ (·) with two distinct objectives:
341
342   The 1st term: L1 (y, z0|t , Cψ ) is to effectively extract necessary conditions from zt . Here,
343           z0|t represents the projection of zt onto the manifold M0 .
344   The 2st term: L2 (y, z0 , z0|t , Cψ ) is to adjust the gradient of the first term so that it provides
345           accurate directional guidance for larger steps.
346
347
      The first loss term can be constructed using two distinct strategies. The initial approach, em-
      ployed by previous training-free CDMs, is defined as L1 (y, z0|t , Cψ ) = ∥y −Cψ (D(z0|t ))∥22 .
348

529   6.6   Conclusion
530
531   Slow sampling is a common issue in current training-free CDMs. In this paper, we introduce
532   a novel framework that reformulates training-free CDMs into the maximization of two
533   distinct objectives. By meticulously counting the optimization steps for each objective,
534   we identify the phase that is the bottleneck for sampling speed and propose retraining the
535
      condition extraction networks as a strategy to expedite conditional sampling. Our extensive
      experiments confirm that AccCtr can significantly reduce the computational cost without
536
      compromising sample quality. Most importantly, our method exhibits broad compatibility,
537
      holding potential to accelerate a variety of other methods. This conclusion underscores
538   the versatility and efficacy of our approach in addressing the common challenge of slow
539   sampling speeds in training-free CDMs.

      A    Appendix: Proof of Proposition 1
703
704
      To prove that the alternating maximization of A(z) and B(z) converges to a local maximum
705   of the function A(z) + B(z), we proceed with the following steps and assumptions.
706
707   Assumptions:
708   Let z ∈ Rn denote the variable defined over the domain of the functions A(z) and B(z). We
709   assume:
710
711        1. The set of local maxima of B(z), denoted SB , is a subset of the set of local maxima
712           of A(z), denoted SA . That is: SB ⊆ SA .
713        2. Both functions A(z) and B(z) are continuously differentiable, and their local max-
714           ima are isolated points.
715        3. The functions A(z) and B(z) have local maxima.
716
717   Alternating Maximization Algorithm:
718
      The alternating maximization algorithm proceeds as follows:
719
720        • Begin with an initial point z0 .
721
           • In each odd iteration (step k), maximize A(z), holding B(z) fixed.
722
723                                             zk+1 = arg max A(z),
                                                              z
724
725        • In each even iteration (step k + 1), maximize B(z) holding A(z) fixed.
726
727                                             zk+2 = arg max B(z),
                                                              z
728
729   Proof:
730   We aim to show that this alternating process converges to a local maximum of the combined
731   function A(z) + B(z).
732
      Step 1: Local Maxima Relationship
733
734   Suppose at some iteration zk , we have maximized A(z) so that:
735
                                                  zk ∈ SA .
736
737   Since SB ⊆ SA , it follows that if zk is also a local maximum of B(z), then:
738
                                                  zk ∈ SB .
739
740   Thus, at this point, zk is a local maximum of both A(z) and B(z).
741
      Step 2: Behavior of Alternating Maximization
742
743   When we perform alternating maximization, we iterate between optimizing A(z) and B(z).
744   Given the assumption that SB ⊆ SA , every point that is a local maximum of B(z) is also a
745   local maximum of A(z). Therefore, in each step, when we maximize B(z), the algorithm
746   remains within the set of local maxima of A(z).
747   As a result, as the algorithm iterates, the points zk produced by alternating maximization
748   will always belong to the set SA . Furthermore, the sequence of points {zk } is confined to
749   a finite set of local maxima (due to the assumption that both functions have finitely many
750   maxima), and the process converges to one of these maxima.
751   Step 3: Convergence to a Local Maximum of A(z) + B(z)
752
753   Once the alternating maximization has converged to a point z∗ ∈ SA ∩ SB , we know that:
754
           • z∗ is a local maximum of A(z)
755
           • z∗ is a local maximum of B(z)

      A(z) + B(z).
