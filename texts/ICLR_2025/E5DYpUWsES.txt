008                                                 A BSTRACT
009
010            Although a variety of different methods have emerged in the field of clustering, K-
011            means still occupies an important position, and many advanced clustering methods
012            even rely on the K-means to achieve effective cluster detection. However, the sensi-
013
               tivity of K-means to the selection of the initial cluster center and its limited ability
               to handle nonlinear separable data somewhat restrict its clustering performance. In
014
               order to overcome the limitations of K-means, we draw inspiration from manifold
015
               learning and redefine K-means as a manifold K-means clustering framework. This
016            framework supports various types of distance matrices, thus facilitating the efficient
017            processing of nonlinear separable data. A unique advantage of this approach is
018            that it does not require the calculation of the cluster center, while it maintains
019            the consistency between manifold structure and cluster labels. Additionally, we
020            highlight the significant role of the ℓ2,p -norm; by maximizing the ℓ2,p -norm, we
021            can ensure the balance of classes in the clustering process, which is also supported
022            by theoretical analysis. The results from extensive experiments across multiple
023            databases substantiate the superiority of our proposed model.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      In the field of data analysis and pattern recognition, clustering is an unsupervised learning method
028
      aimed at grouping data points into clusters. The objective is to ensure that data points within the same
029
      cluster exhibit high similarity, while those from different clusters show significant differences. This
030   methodology has garnered considerable attention over the past decades, leading to the development
031   of various clustering algorithms designed to enhance data annotation and parsing. Among these,
032   K-means is particularly notable for its popularity. It partitions the data into K clusters and iteratively
033   optimizes the centroids to minimize the intra-cluster sum of squared distances. K-means is celebrated
034   for its simplicity, intuitiveness, and efficiency, making it a staple in many applications.
035
      Despite its prominence, K-means is not without drawbacks. Specifically, it uses metrics like the
036   Euclidean distance to assign data points to the nearest cluster center, which may not be effective
037   for data with nonlinear distributions. To address this, researchers employ kernel functions—such
038   as the linear kernel Vankadara & Ghoshdastidar (2020), Euler kernel Lin & Chen (2023a), and
039   multi-kernel Liu (2023); Yao et al. (2021)—to map data into a high-dimensional kernel space where
040   it becomes linearly separable. Nevertheless, even with kernel-based enhancements, the choice of
041   initial cluster centers remains crucial in K-means. Incorrect initial selections can lead the algorithm
042   to converge to local optima, significantly impacting the clustering outcome Peña et al. (1999); Li et al.
043   (2021); Xiong et al. (2016); Xie et al. (2020); Liang et al. (2024).
044   To mitigate this issue, various strategies for selecting initial clustering centers have been proposed.
045   These include K-Means++ Bachem et al. (2016); Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007), which reduces
046   randomness; methods based on data point density Lan et al. (2015); spatial distribution-based
047   partitioning techniques Aslam et al. (2020); and genetic algorithms that optimize for high-quality
048   centers Laszlo & Mukherjee (2006). Additionally, some studies combine spectral clustering with
049   K-means to bypass the need for estimating the centroid matrix. Since spectral clustering can
050
      automatically determine the initial cluster center, it also alleviates the sensitivity of K-means to initial
      center selection Pei et al. (2023). However, these approaches often overlook the alignment of data
051
      geometry with labels, which can limit their effectiveness on complex datasets.
052
053   Inspired by manifold learningRoweis & Saul (2000); Belkin & Niyogi (2001), we develop a new
      clustering algorithm. Manifold learning effectively captures and retains the complex nonlinear

054
      geometry inherent in data Cai (2015); Wu et al. (2022). From this perspective, our algorithm
055   reinterprets and enhances the traditional K-means algorithm by directly estimating the data clusters,
056   avoiding the need to estimate a centroid matrix, and utilizing manifold learning to accurately capture
057   the geometric structure of the data.
058
      It is noteworthy that during the algorithm design process, we also incorporate the concept of ℓ2,p -
059
      normWang et al. (2018); Zhao et al. (2024). ℓ2,p -norm minimization often plays a crucial role in areas
060
      such as image recovery, text compression, and signal processing. However, our study also reveals
061   additional significant roles for ℓ2,p -norm. Specifically, by maximizing the ℓ2,p -norm, we ensure the
062   balance of classes in the clustering process, thus avoiding the problem that some classes are too large
063   or too small, and enhancing the effectiveness and stability of clustering.
064
      Specifically, our method differs from existing K-means techniques in several key aspects:
065
066          • We establish the link between K-means and manifold learning, ensuring the consistency of
067            manifold structures and clustering labels.
068
             • Unlike traditional K-means and its variants, our approach directly obtains data clusters
069            without the need for estimating cluster centers.
070
             • We identify a significant role of the ℓ2,p -norm. By maximizing the ℓ2,p -norm, we can ensure
071
               balanced clusters in the clustering process and provide a theoretical analysis.
072
073
             • A unified clustering framework is established, and by utilizing different distance functions
               (such as Euclidean distance, kernel Euclidean distance, KNN distance, etc.), we can derive
074
               various K-means variants.

077   2    R ELATED WORK
078
079   The essence of traditional K-means clustering is to find a set of cluster centers and then assign data
080   points to the nearest cluster center using Euclidean distances. However, when the data is nonlinearly
081
      separable, K-means may not accurately reflect the similarities and differences between the data.
082   To address nonlinear separability in the original feature space, an efficient method is to employ the
083   kernel trick. This technique maps the raw data into a high-dimensional feature space where the
084   features become linearly separable. This approach has inspired the development of kernel K-means
085   and various variants. Girolami Girolami (2002) pioneered the integration of clustering and kernel
086   methods by proposing a clustering method based on the Mercer kernel. Kong et al. Kong & Kong
087
      (2013) used a conditional positive definite kernel (CPD) to map data into a high-dimensional space
      and performed K-means clustering there. Wu et al. Lin & Chen (2023a) introduced the Euler kernel
088
      to kernel K-means by mapping input data onto a unit hypersphere in an equal-dimensional space
089
      and performing K-means clustering on that sphere. However, Lin et al. Lin & Chen (2023b) noted
090   that the center of mass tends to deviate from the surface of the unit hypersphere during Euler kernel
091   clustering, leading to outliers. To address this issue, they proposed constraining the center of mass to
092   the unit hypersphere or optimizing the mapping of the original data in Euler kernel space, effectively
093   handling the problem of center of mass deviation.
094
      However, the performance of kernel K-means clustering largely depends on the choice of kernel
095
      functions. To alleviate this issue, multiple kernel learning has been introduced into K-means clustering
096   to find the best kernel combination for clustering. Liu et al. Liu et al. (2017) proposed an adaptive
097   optimal neighborhood multi-core clustering model, which employs matrix-induced regularization
098   to enhance the diversity of selected kernels and the representability of optimal kernels. In contrast,
099   Yao et al. Yao et al. (2021) enhanced kernel diversity from the perspective of subset selection by
100   choosing representative kernels from predefined sets. However, both approaches Liu et al. (2017);
101   Yao et al. (2021) rely on additional discretization steps to obtain the final discrete clustering indicator
102   matrix. To bypass this discretization step, Wang et al. Wang et al. (2022) proposed a discrete and
103   parameterless multi-core k-means model. By implicitly introducing regularization terms to assess the
104
      correlation between different kernels and using alternative optimization methods, this model directly
      generates the cluster index matrix without further processing.
105
106   It should be noted that the K-means algorithm is sensitive to the selection of initial cluster centers.
107   Several methods have been proposed to select the initial clustering center, notably the improved
      algorithm K-Means++. K-Means++ Bachem et al. (2016); Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007) ensures that


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the distance between initial centers of mass is maximized, thus reducing the risk of the algorithm
109   converging to a local optimal solution. Lan et al. Lan et al. (2015) proposed initializing cluster centers
110   with density peaks, determining cluster centers based on the local density of data points and their
111   distance to higher density points. Wu et al. Wu et al. (2021) calculated the nearest neighbor density for
112   each point, selected those with the highest density as initial center candidates, and further determined
113   the final initial cluster centers by constructing a minimum spanning tree among these candidates.
114   Additionally, Liao et al. Liao et al. (2024) calculated the decision value for each data point based on
115   the product of the nearest neighbor density peak (NNDP) of data points, and automatically selected
116   the point with the highest decision value as the initial clustering center. Aslam et al. Aslam et al.
117
      (2020) evenly divided the data into k partitions by Euclidean distance, using the mean value of each
      partition as the initial centroid. Laszlo et al. Laszlo & Mukherjee (2006) used a data-based super
118
      quadtree and genetic algorithm to select a cluster centroid. Mardi et al. Mardi & Keyvanpour (2021)
119
      proposed a genetics-based K-Means (GBKM) algorithm, where the clustering centroid is determined
120   by a genetic algorithm that maximizes the fitness function. Merhad Ay et al. Ay et al. (2023) fixed
121   some clustering centers and then searched for the optimal centers for the remaining clusters.
122
123
      Although optimizing initial centroids can enhance the stability of K-means, the iterative update
      process for centroids remains unstable. Consequently, some researchers have proposed variants
124
      of the K-means algorithm that avoid direct estimation of the center of mass. Nie et al. Nie et al.
125
      (2022) reformulated classical K-means as a trace maximization problem, thus directly assigning
126   each sample to the appropriate cluster without updating the center. Additionally, Pei et al. Pei et al.
127   (2023) introduced k-sum, based on the relationship between spectral clustering and K-means, to avoid
128   estimating the centroid matrix when the number of samples in each cluster is strictly equal. However,
129   these methods often overlook the consistency between data geometries and labels, which can limit
130   their effectiveness on complex datasets.

306   The time complexity in the optimization method is mainly focused on the solution of H and Y. Update
307   H with the equation provided, the computational complexity is O(N K 2 ). Calculation YT D requires

444   Evaluation of Clustering Algorithms: When evaluating the performance of clustering algorithms,
445   we observe that algorithms dependent on the centroid matrix—specifically K-means, KKM, and

      RKM—perform less effectively on the baseline datasets compared to the K-sum and K-sum-x,

      This combination enables them to exhibit higher accuracy when dealing with complex datasets.

450   Handling Imbalanced Datasets: Although K-sum and K-sum-x algorithms initially assume balanced
451   dataset categories, this constraint is removed during the actual solution process. This flexibility may
452
      limit their performance in certain scenarios, particularly in datasets with uneven category distribution.
      In contrast, we reinterpret the K-means algorithm from a manifold learning perspective and subtly
453
      incorporate the ℓ2,p -norm. The integration of the ℓ2,p -norm not only enhances the model’s flexibility
454
      but also naturally maintains class equilibrium during the solving process.

520
521
      This paper presents a new manifold K-means clustering framework. Different variants of K-means
522
      can be obtained by flexibly applying different distance matrices. The framework reconstructs the tra-
523
      ditional K-means from the perspective of manifold learning, realizes data clustering without centroid
524   estimation, and ensures the consistency of manifold structure and cluster labels. Additionally, we
525   introduce the maximization of the ℓ2,p -norm to effectively maintain the class balance in the clustering
526   process. A large number of experimental results fully verify the superiority and effectiveness of this
527   method.
