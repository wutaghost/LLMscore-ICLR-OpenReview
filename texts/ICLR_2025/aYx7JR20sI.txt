                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               We propose an algebraic geometric framework to study the expressivity of piece-
010
               wise linear activation neural networks. A particular quantity of neural networks
011
               that has been actively studied is the number of linear regions, which gives a quan-
012            tiﬁcation of the information capacity of the architecture. To study and evaluate in-
013            formation capacity and expressivity, we work in the setting of tropical geometry—
014            a combinatorial and polyhedral variant of algebraic geometry—where there are
015            known connections between tropical rational maps and feedforward neural net-
016            works. Our work builds on and expands this connection to capitalize on the rich
017            theory of tropical geometry to characterize and study various architectural aspects
018            of neural networks. Our contributions are threefold: we provide a novel tropi-
019            cal geometric approach to selecting sampling domains among linear regions; an
020
               algebraic result allowing for a guided restriction of the sampling domain for net-
               work architectures with symmetries; and a new open source OSCAR library to
021
               analyze neural networks symbolically using their tropical representations, where
022
               we present a new algorithm that computes the exact number of their linear re-
023            gions. We provide a comprehensive set of proof-of-concept numerical experi-
024            ments demonstrating the breadth of neural network architectures to which tropical
025            geometric theory can be applied to reveal insights on expressivity characteristics
026            of a network. Our work provides the foundations for the adaptation of both theory
027            and existing software from computational tropical geometry and symbolic compu-
028            tation to neural networks and deep learning.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Deep learning has become the undisputed state-of-the-art for data analysis and has wide-reaching
034   prominence in many ﬁelds of computer science, despite still being based on a limited theoretical
035   foundation. Developing such a foundation to better understand the unparalleled success of deep
036
      neural networks is one of the most active areas of research in modern statistical learning theory; with
      attempts at characterising the expressivity – the space of representable functions – of deep neural
037
      networks being one of the most important approaches to this Raghu et al. (2017). Our focus is on
038
      deep neural networks with piecewise linear activations since their expressivity has been extensively
039   studied using linear regions (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2013; Montúfar et al., 2014; Arora et al., 2016;
040   Hanin & Rolnick, 2019; Xiong et al., 2020; Goujon et al., 2024; Montúfar et al., 2022).
041
042
      Tropical geometry is a reinterpretation of algebraic geometry that features piecewise linear and poly-
      hedral constructions (see Appendix A.3), where combinatorics naturally comes into play Mikhalkin
043
      & Rau (e.g., 2009); Speyer & Sturmfels (e.g., 2009); Maclagan & Sturmfels (e.g., 2021). Therefore,
044
      tropical geometry is a natural framework for studying the linear regions in a neural network. The
045   intersection of deep learning theory and tropical geometry is a relatively new area of research with
046   great potential towards the ultimate goal of understanding how and why deep neural networks per-
047   form so well. In this paper, we expand the connection between deep learning theory and tropical
048   geometry by studying the linear regions of neural networks through a tropical lens. In doing so, we
049   introduce a novel perspective on studying neural network expressivity.
050
051   Related Work. Tropical geometry has emerged as a powerful tool for analyzing deep neural net-
052   works with piecewise linear activation functions, such as rectiﬁed linear units (ReLUs) and maxout
053   units. Zhang et al. (2018) ﬁrst established that neural networks can be represented by tropical ratio-
      nal functions, enabling the use of tropical techniques to study their properties. They also showed


                                                        1
054
      that the decision boundary of a deep ReLU network is contained in a tropical hypersurface. (For
055   more details, see Appendix A.3.) Concurrently, Charisopoulos & Maragos (2018b) demonstrated
056   that the maxout activation function ﬁts input data using a tropical polynomial. These initial works
057   focused on neural networks with Euclidean input domains. Yoshida et al. (2023) later extended this
058   approach to incorporate the tropical projective torus as an input domain, broadening the applicability
059   of tropical methods. Recently, Pasque et al. (2024) leveraged tropical geometry to construct convo-
060   lutional neural networks with improved robustness against adversarial attacks, demonstrating the
061   practical value of this theoretical framework. This growing body of research highlights the potential
062   of tropical geometry to enhance our understanding and design of neural networks.
063   Measures of quantifying neural network expressivity are important for facilitating the theoretical
064   and empirical investigation of neural network properties. Neural networks with piecewise linear ac-
065   tivations compute piecewise linear functions on areas of the input space referred to as the network’s
066   linear regions. The maximum number of distinct linear regions a class of neural networks can instan-
067   tiate provides an appropriate and quantiﬁable measure of its expressivity (e.g., Montúfar et al., 2014).
068   Subsequent research has thus worked towards enumerating the number of linear regions in a given
069
      neural network since this can provide a measure of how complex the function represented by the neu-
      ral network is. Using mixed-integer programming, Serra et al. (2018) provides an exact enumeration
070
      of the number of linear regions in a bounded subset of the input domain. For unbounded domains,
071
      Serra & Ramalingam (2018) give an analytic upper bound on the maximum number of linear regions
072   of a neural network along with a probabilistic lower bound, and Charisopoulos & Maragos (2018a)
073   provide a probabilistic method for estimating the linear regions of a one-layer network, together with
074   some analytic bounds for various architectures. In recognition that many of these approaches are
075   intractable at large scales, linear region enumeration is often done approximately through numerical
076   sampling Goujon et al. (2024).
077
      However, the work that is most related to ours are those that exactly characterise the linear regions,
078   beyond just enumerating them, in a computationally tractable manner. For instance, Humayun et al.
079   (2023) leverages spline theory to do so in bounded two-dimensional subspaces of the input domain.
080   Similarly, Masden (2022) characterises the polyhedral complex associated to the arrangement of the
081   linear regions. Our work differs from these in that we obtain an exact geometric characterisation of
082   the individual regions in the unbounded input domain.
083
084   Contributions. In this paper, we establish novel algebraic and geometric tools to obtain previ-
085   ously inaccessible geometric insights into the linear regions of a given neural network beyond just
086   their enumeration; enhancing the theoretical and empirical (exact or approximate) study of a neural
087   network’s linear regions. The main contributions of our work are the following:
088
089          • We provide a global geometric characterization of the arrangement of a neural network’s
090            linear regions: current numerical estimation of the number of linear regions is typically car-
091            ried out by random sampling in an arbitrarily bounded region of the input space, potentially
092            causing some linear regions of a neural network to be missed and resulting in an inaccurate
093            information capacity measure. We propose an effective sampling domain as a ball of radius
094            R that hits all of the linear regions of a given neural network. We compute bounds for
095
               the radius R based on a combinatorial invariant known as the Hoffman constant, giving a
               guarantee on the positioning of the linear regions of a neural network.
096
097          • We exploit further geometric insight into the arrangement of linear regions of a neural
098            network to gain dramatic computational efﬁciency in the numerical estimation of linear
099            regions: when networks exhibit invariance under symmetry, we can restrict the sampling
100            domain to a fundamental domain of the group action and thus reduce the number of sam-
101            ples required. We experimentally demonstrate that sampling from the fundamental domain
102
               provides an accurate estimate of the number of linear regions with a fraction of the compute
               requirements.
103
104          • We provide an open source library integrated into the Open Source Computer Algebra
105            Research (OSCAR) system (OSCAR) which converts arbitrary neural networks into alge-
106            braic symbolic objects, to facilitate the exact enumeration of the linear regions of neural
107            networks. Our library opens the door for the extensive theory and existing software on
               symbolic computation and computational tropical geometry to be used to study neural net-


                                                         2
108
               works beyond their linear regions. In particular, we propose an alternative measure of
109            neural network complexity, the monomial count.

117   2     B OUNDED D OMAIN S ELECTION U SING A H OFFMAN C ONSTANT
118
119   Enumerating the number of linear regions of a given neural network is inherently challenging due to
120   its combinatorial nature. From an applications perspective, it is common to restrict to some bounded
121   input domain X ⊆ Rn such as [−R, R]n or [0, R]n , such that the number of linear regions in X
122   can be either estimated by computing the Jacobians of the network at sample points (see Appendix
123   C) or computed exactly using mixed-integer programming (e.g., Serra et al., 2018). However, these
124   approaches are limited from both a theoretical and empirical perspective, since, one cannot guarantee
125
      that X provides information about all the linear regions. In this section, we try to address the
      discrepancy by inferring the unbounded case from the bounded case. More speciﬁcally, we provide
126
      a method to determine the radius R of a ball centred at some point x, which intersects every linear
127
      region.
128
129   Our approach proceeds from the recollection that neural networks can be formulated as tropical
130   Puiseux rational maps (see Appendix A). Thus, characterizing R for neural networks is equivalent
131
      to characterizing R for tropical Puiseux rational maps. The linear regions of tropical Puiseux poly-
      nomials are made up of polyhedra, allowing us to connect the value R to a combinatorial invariant:
132
      the Hoffman constant. We extend the deﬁnition of the Hoffman constant to tropical rational maps
133
      and use it to derive bounds on R.
134

337   Overview of the Algorithm. We start by sketching our algorithm for determining the linear re-
338   gions of tropical Puiseux rational functions. A more precise formulation is given in the Appendix B
339
      ; see Algorithm 1, together with a proof of correctness (Theorem F.1), and a Julia implementation.
340   When viewed as a function on the real numbers, a tropical Puiseux rational function f = p q
341   is simply the difference of two max terms: the numerator and the denominator. In particular, f is
342   linear on a region R ⊂ Rn whenever p and q are linear on R. This indicates that we should be able
343   to determine the linear regions of f once we know those of p and q. More precisely, notice that
344   if U1 , . . . , Us are the linear regions of p and V1 , . . . , Vt are the linear regions of q, then f is linear
345
      on each of the intersections Ui ∩ Vj , and these intersections cover the input space Rn . However,
      we cannot conclude that the linear regions of f are given by this collection of intersections, as the
346
      following issues may arise: some intersections may be empty or have dimension less than n; and
347
      some intersections may “glue” together to form a larger linear region of f (see Appendix H for
348   examples of these phenomena). Whether or not these arise usually depends on the Ui ’s and Vj ’s and
349   has to do with the combinatorics of the arrangement of these objects in Rn . After ﬁltering out empty
350   and lower-dimensional regions, and determining which intersections glue together, we obtain a list
351   of regions in Rn (polyhedra or unions of polyhedra), which correspond to the linear regions of f .
352
353   Combinatorics of Polyhedral Arrangements. In order for our tropical linear region algorithm to
354   be implementable, we need a way of computationally determining the combinatorics of the arrange-
355   ment of the Ui ’s and Vj ’s. The key here is the standard fact from tropical geometry that the linear
356   regions of a tropical (Puiseux) polynomial are polyhedra whose deﬁning inequalities can be deter-
357   mined from the coefﬁcients and exponents of the polynomial (see Appendix A.3 for more detail).
358   Hence, we are left to deal with the combinatorics of polyhedral arrangements.
359   From Lemma A.4, we have that the intersections of the linear regions of p and q are also polyhedra,
360   and thus determining the non-emptiness or dimension of such objects are well-understood problems
361   that can be solved using linear programming. This means that we can (computably!) detect when
362   some intersections may be empty or have dimension less than n using polyhedral geometric tools.
363

      this region. We can determine these computationally as unions of polyhedra by considering which
385
      pairwise intersections (Uk ∩ Vℓ ) ∩ (Uk′ ∩ Vℓ′ ) are empty for (k, ℓ), (k ′ , ℓ′ ) ∈ I.
386
387   Hence, we have shown that given a tropical rational map we can computably determine its linear
388   regions. Therefore, to be able to determine a neural network’s linear regions we just need to compu-
389
      tationally realise it as a tropical rational, which we discuss in the next section.

391   4.2   T ROPICAL R EPRESENTATIONS OF N EURAL N ETWORKS
392
      Our Contribution. Any neural network with integer weights can be viewed as the function
393
      Rn → R associated to a tropical rational function (Zhang et al., 2018). This fact is used by Bran-
394   denburg et al. (2024) as a theoretical tool, but to the best of our knowledge, this has not yet been
395   implemented in practice for analyzing concrete neural networks. We ﬁll this gap by leveraging the
396   Julia package OSCAR to computationally realise this representation using the constructive proof
397   provided in (Zhang et al., 2018), see Section A.3 for details.
398
      However, non-zero tropical Puiseux rational maps (and polynomials) induce functions Rn → R
399
      which can be realised by different algebraic expressions, see 3 and 4 of Example A.14. In this
400   sense, the algebraic expressions contain strictly more information than the corresponding function.
401   Since for neural networks we are only concerned with the induced functions, it is natural to consider
402   whether the tropical representation of these neural networks is optimal, in the sense of having a
403   relatively few number of redundant monomials.
404
      This observation extends to interpretability, where a goal is to ﬁnd minimal expressions of neural
405
      networks. Tropical geometry has been used for ﬁnding such representations (Smyrnis et al., 2020;
406   Smyrnis & Maragos, 2020), where the corresponding minimal representations have been studied in
407   algebraic statistics (Tran & Wang, 2024). Our contribution brings a new perspective to expressivity
408   as well as interpretability using polyhedral geometry.
409

      5   D ISCUSSION : L IMITATIONS & D IRECTIONS FOR F UTURE R ESEARCH
504
505
      Our contributions offer theoretical and practical advancements in tropical deep learning, but are
506   subject to some important limitations which in turn inspire directions for future research, which we
507   now discuss.
508
509   Experimental Considerations. Our methods have shown promising results for networks of mod-
510   erate size, including those with input dimensions comparable to MNIST. However, as we scale to
511   more complex architectures and higher dimensions, computational challenges persist. To further im-
512   prove scalability, parallelization of our elementary computations could yield signiﬁcant performance
513   gains, as many of our algorithms involve repeating similar operations multiple times.
514
515   Structural Considerations. Some of the problems we study are framed as combinatorial optimiza-
516   tion problems, which are inherently challenging. For instance, computing the Hoffman constant,
517
      which is equivalent to the Stewart–Todd condition measure of a matrix, is known to be NP-hard
      in general cases (Peña et al., 2018; 2019). This challenge could be addressed by employing ap-
518
      proximate algorithms or algorithms that provide upper bounds on the Hoffman constant, since these
519
      would be sufﬁcient for our purposes and computationally more tractable.
520
521   Our introduction of a new algebraic measure of complexity provides fresh insights but also opens
522   up new questions about its computational complexity and relationship to other complexity measures.
523
      The neural network pruning methods we have developed show promise in reducing model com-
      plexity while maintaining expressivity. However, further research is needed to fully understand the
524
      trade-offs between model size, expressivity, and performance across a wider range of architectures
525
      and tasks.
526
527
      Future Directions. These limitations inspire future work on both the practical and theoretical
528   fronts. In practice, to achieve improved scalability, further studying and understanding where and
529   how symbolic computation algorithms can be made more efﬁcient, e.g., by parallelization or novel
530   algorithmic approaches, would make our proposed methods more applicable to larger neural net-
531   works. Expanding our pruning methods to a broader range of architectures to investigate their im-
532   pact on model performance in diverse tasks is a pathway to developing improved pruning techniques.
533   Building on our initial empirical tests of theoretical expressivity results, a comprehensive empirical
534   validation could help bridge the gap between theory and practice in neural network expressivity.
535   Theoretically, our tropical contributions have the potential to capture both expressivity and inter-
536   pretability. Towards this end, a deeper exploration of our new algebraic expressivity measure, in-
537   cluding its theoretical properties and practical implications, could yield valuable insights into neural
538   network behaviour. Perhaps most importantly, ours is the ﬁrst work to leverage tropical symbolic
539   computation to perform experiments on deep neural networks. Fostering collaboration between
      these ﬁelds will lead to novel algorithms and insights that leverage the strengths of both areas.

1004
1005   The method of numerical estimation we use is inspired by the recent work of Goujon et al. (2024).
1006   Speciﬁcally, to numerically estimate the number of linear regions of a neural network, we exploit the
1007   fact that the linear regions of a neural network correspond to regions where the gradient is constant.
1008   We evaluate the gradient on a sample of points in some bounded region X and identify the number of
1009   unique gradients we obtain. Care needs to be taken at this step, since it may be the case that the same
1010   linear function operates on disconnected regions. In our symbolic approach, this would correspond
1011   to distinct linear regions. To try and account for this in our numerical approach, for points with the
1012
       same Jacobian, we sample the model at their midpoint and compare this to the midpoint provided
       by the linear map. If these values differ, then the regions are disconnected. However, if the values
1013
       do not differ, we still cannot be certain whether the region is connected or disconnected. Therefore,
1014
       our numerical approach is likely to underestimate the number of linear regions in this instance. We
1015   summarize this technique with Algorithm 3.
1016
1017   There are a few sources of errors that arise in our method of numerical approximation that are
1018
       important to note:

              1. We cannot be sure if our search radius captures all of the linear regions.

1022             symbolic approach would count these regions as distinct. In our numerical approach, we
1023
                 try to resolve this by additionally sampling at the midpoint of points with the same Jacobian,
                 however, this does not guarantee that we identify disconnected regions.
1024
1025          3. From exploratory experiments, we observe that some linear regions are very small. There-
                 fore, a highly reﬁned grid would be required to identify them. As the dimension of the

1555   Since we have a 2-dimensional input domain, we can visualize the linear regions in which the neural
1556   network partitions the input domain, Figure 8a. By leveraging the capabilities of the OSCAR library,
1557   we can further compute geometric quantities of the linear regions, such as their volumes, Table 6.
1558   The extent to which these measurements are useful for applications such as interpretability is left for
1559   future work.
1560   Note how linear regions may be constructed as unions of convex polyhedra to form non-convex re-
1561   gions, for instance in Figure 8a, we see that linear region 6 is constructed as the union of various
1562   convex polyhedra. Moreover, despite the same linear map acting on different polyhedra, these poly-
1563   hedra may be disconnected and thus form separate linear regions, for instance in Figure 8a linear
1564   region 5 is acted on by the same linear map as linear region 6 but it is disconnected from linear re-
1565   gion 6. Our Algorithm 1 accounts for both of these scenarios to ensure that an accurate enumeration
       of the linear regions is provided.
