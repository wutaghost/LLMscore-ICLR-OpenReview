009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning aims to equip models with the ability to retain previously
012            learned knowledge like a human. Recent work incorporating Parameter-Efficient
013            Fine-Tuning has revitalized the field by introducing lightweight extension mod-
014            ules. However, existing methods usually overlook the issue of information leakage
015            caused by the fact that the experiment data have been used in pre-trained models.
016            Once these duplicate data are removed in the pre-training phase, their performance
017            can be severely affected. In this paper, we propose a new LoRA-based rehearsal-
018
               free method named DESIRE. Our method avoids imposing additional constraints
               during training to mitigate catastrophic forgetting, thereby maximizing the learn-
019
               ing of new classes. To integrate knowledge from old and new tasks, we propose
020
               two efficient post-processing modules. On the one hand, we retain only two sets of
021            LoRA parameters for merging and propose dynamic representation consolidation
022            to calibrate the merged feature representation. On the other hand, we propose de-
023            cision boundary refinement to address classifier bias when training solely on new
024            class data. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-
025            the-art performance on multiple datasets and strikes an effective balance between
026            stability and plasticity. Our code will be publicly available.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Despite the remarkable achievements, AI is still far from becoming truly human-like in intelli-
032   gence. Continual learning (CL) aims to address catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li
033
      & Hoiem, 2017; Verwimp et al., 2023), where AI systems tend to forget previously learned tasks as
      they learn new ones. CL encompasses both task-incremental learning (TIL) and class-incremental
034
      learning (CIL) scenarios (Wang et al., 2024), where the former allows the model to identify which
035
      task the test samples belong to during inference, while the latter requires the model to recognize all
036   seen classes without knowing the task identity. This paper focuses on the more challenging setting
037   of rehearsal-free CIL (Zhu et al., 2021; Liang & Li, 2024), where the model can only access the
038   training data of the current task at each stage.
039
      Recently, with the widespread use of pre-trained models and various parameter-efficient fine-
040
      tuning (PEFT) (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022) methods, the field of CIL
041
      has seen rapid advancements. On the one hand, pre-trained models provide good generalization ca-
042   pabilities, making them naturally better than training from scratch in terms of performance. On the
043   other hand, PEFT methods such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and Prompt (Jia et al., 2022) achieve re-
044   sults comparable to the full fine-tuning with a significantly small number of parameters, making the
045   application of CIL methods possible. For example, L2P (Wang et al., 2022c) and DualPrompt (Wang
046   et al., 2022b) combine prompt tuning with pre-trained models and achieve remarkable performance.
047   O-LoRA (Wang et al., 2023) and InfLoRA (Liang & Li, 2024) demonstrate the superiority of LoRA
048   on CIL tasks. In addition, LAE (Gao et al., 2023) integrates Adapter, Prompt, and LoRA for CIL.
049   All of these methods highlight the potential for applying PEFT to CIL tasks.
050   Nevertheless, we observe that despite such high performance achieved by these methods, two cru-
051   cial issues remain: (1) The performance is highly dependent on information leakage. Several stud-
052   ies (Kim et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) have pointed out that in the absence of strong
053   supervised pre-trained weights (e.g., ImageNet-21k), the performance of all the existing methods
      suffers from varying degrees of degradation, and even lower than methods not designed for PEFT.

072   boosts the model’s performance. (2) Lack of balance between stability and plasticity. Intuitively,

075   we revisit the stability and plasticity and observe that existing methods fail to maintain an opti-
076
      mal balance between stability and plasticity. Specifically, these methods often introduce additional
      constraints to mitigate catastrophic forgetting when learning new classes. For example, traditional
077
      regularization-base methods typically employ knowledge distillation loss (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Zhu
078
      et al., 2021) or regularization of parameters (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017); Recent LoRA-based meth-
079   ods (Liang & Li, 2024; Wang et al., 2023) leverage the idea of orthogonality to restrict the updating
080   of new tasks, thereby reducing inter-task interference. However, when no information is leaked,
081   excessive constraints can hinder the learning of new classes (e.g., InfLoRA), while focusing solely
082   on new classes often leads to forgetting old tasks (e.g., L2P). This ultimately results in an imbalance
083   between stability and plasticity for old and new tasks.
084
      In this paper, we first discard the common paradigm of introducing constraints to mitigate catas-
085   trophic forgetting when learning new classes. Instead, The LoRAs are updated independently at
086   each stage, allowing them to fully learn each task. At this point, the core question to address is
087   how to integrate knowledge from old and new tasks in order to balance stability and plasticity
088   while improving overall accuracy. To this end, we design two efficient post-processing strategies
089   named Dynamic rEpresentation conSolidation and decIsion boundaRy rEfinement (DESIRE). On
090   the one hand, inspired by model fusion tasks that merge the backbones of models trained on dif-
091   ferent datasets during inference (Ilharco et al., 2022), we propose a Continual Merging Paradigm
092   for LoRA-based continual learning. Specifically, we uniformly keep only two sets of previous and
093
      current LoRA parameters at each stage and merge them during inference. To better consolidate the
      representation of the merged model, a feature representation attribution loss is designed to learn the
094
      merging coefficients of the previous and current LoRA modules using a tiny subset of unlabeled
095
      test data. On the other hand, the independent training at different stages leads to classifiers that
096   struggle to learn more generalized decision boundaries. To address this issue, we propose to refine
097   the decision boundary of the classifier by reconstructing the high-dimensional feature distribution
098   of the classes and sampling pseudo-features to retrain the classifier. Results on multiple datasets
099   demonstrate the superiority of our method and a schematic of DESIRE is shown in Fig. 2.
100
      In general, this paper makes three contributions: (i) We propose a new LoRA-based rehearsal-free
101
      CIL method that avoids introducing additional constraints when learning new classes to mitigate
102   catastrophic forgetting, thereby maximizing the performance on each task. (ii) To better integrate
103   knowledge from old and new tasks, we propose two efficient post-processing strategies, which can
104   significantly improve performance by using only a tiny amount of unlabeled test data and statisti-
105   cal information from the training data. (iii) Experimental results indicate that our method signif-
106   icantly outperforms other rehearsal-free methods and performs comparable with latest rehearsal-
107   based method (Lin et al., 2024) across multiple datasets and achieves the best balance between
      stability and plasticity.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
124
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning. To achieve better performance, training larger models is gradu-
125
      ally becoming more mainstream (Achiam et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). Although
126
      large models can cover multiple tasks, fine-tuning such a large model can become troublesome when
127   addressing specific downstream tasks. To address this, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
128   methods primarily based on LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), Prompt (Jia et al., 2022), and Adapter (Houlsby
129   et al., 2019) have emerged. Specifically, LoRA reduces the number of parameters by parallelizing
130   low-rank matrices at the attention layers of a frozen pre-trained model. Prompt tuning inserts addi-
131   tional tokens into the input embeddings at each layer and trains only these tokens during training.
132   Adapter is similar to LoRA, but is usually serially embedded after specific layer of a pre-trained
133   model. In summary, all of these methods fine-tune the entire large model with a small number of
134   trainable parameters and can rival full fine-tuning in terms of performance (Fu et al., 2022; Hung
135
      et al., 2019; Zaken et al., 2021).
136   Class Incremental Learning. Existing CIL methods can be broadly categorized into expansion-
137   based, regularization-based and rehearsal-based methods (Wang et al., 2024). With the widespread
138   use of pre-trained models in recent years, expansion-based CIL methods using PEFT have gained
139   significant attention. For instance, L2P (Wang et al., 2022c) maintains a prompt pool to select
140   appropriate prompts for optimization across different tasks, and designs a frequency penalty loss
141
      to encourage diversified selection. LAE (Gao et al., 2023) introduces a CIL framework that is
      compatible with Adapter, Prompt and LoRA, demonstrating the extensibility of PEFT for CIL tasks.
142
      For LoRA-based methods, InfLoRA (Liang & Li, 2024) maintains a projection matrix to ensure
143
      that the LoRA parameters for the new task remain orthogonal to the inputs of the old task. O-
144   LoRA (Wang et al., 2023), on the other hand, maintains a series of parameter matrices for the old
145   tasks to constrain the model’s updates to the parameter space orthogonal to the old tasks. However,
146   the performance of these methods drops dramatically in the absence of information leakage and
147   lacks a balance between stability and plasticity. Some recent works (Chitale et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
148   2024) have also utilized the idea of merging old and new LoRA parameters for continual learning,
149   but they all store the parameters from each stage and merge them with pre-defined coefficients.
150   This paradigm will become redundant in long-term continual learning tasks (e.g., T = 20) because
151   the model needs to store too many parameters from old tasks, while the fixed merging coefficient
152
      also limits performance. To this end, we propose a continual merging paradigm, where only the
      two parameter sets of the previous and current tasks are retained during fusion, and the merging
153
      coefficients are dynamically learned to better consolidate representation.

170   Liang & Li (2024) designs the parameter subspace in advance of learning a new task, Other methods
171   overcome catastrophic forgetting by embedding appropriate modules in reasoning, but also require
172   training additional selection modules (Wang et al., 2022c; Yu et al., 2024).
173   Low-Rank Adaption: LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) assumes that updating to the parameters of the large
174   language model during downstream task training lies on the low-rank space, and thus proposed
175   to achieve comparable results to full fine-tuning by training only low-rank matrices concatenated
176   in the original parameter space. Specifically, we define the linear layer in the pre-trained model
177   as W ∈ Rd×k , LoRA decomposes it into two low-rank matrices: A ∈ Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×k ,
178   where r ≪ min {d, k}. By doing so, the forward propagation process in the linear layer can be
179   re-expressed as z = (W + AB)x, where z and x represent the outputs and inputs of the linear
180
      layer. In the implementation, in order not to affect the output of the model at the beginning, A is
      initialized by a random Gaussian, while B is initialized with zero. In our method, we insert LoRA
181
      at the Q and V matrices in the self-attention module at each block of the pre-trained transformer
182
      model. For clarity, we use LoRA inserted at Q as an example in all subsequent discussions.

      3.2     DYNAMIC R EPRESENTATION C ONSOLIDATION AND D ECISION B OUNDARY R EFINEMENT
185
186   Our method can be divided into the following three steps: individual training without additional
187   constraints (Sec. 3.2.1), dynamic representation consolidation (Sec. 3.2.2), and decision boundary
188   refinement (Sec. 3.2.3). Fig. 3 illustrates the framework of our method.
189
190   3.2.1    I NDIVIDUAL T RAINING WITHOUT C ONSTRAINTS
191
192
      Unlike existing methods that require additional constraints to protect information from old tasks
      while training the current task, we treat each training stage as independent of the others. Specifically,
193
      the LoRA parameters are reinitialized at each stage and only the cross-entropy loss Lce in Eq. (1)
194
      is optimized. This has two benefits: (i) Individual training allows the model to focus on improving
195   the performance of the current task, which indirectly enhances performance after merging. (ii) We
196   find that since the LoRA is reinitialized for each task, it naturally maintains good orthogonality
197   with the parameter space of previous tasks after training (See Appendix A.3), which creates a solid
198   prerequisite for the fusion of model parameters.
199
      In order to perform the dynamic representation consolidation and decision boundary refinement,
200
      we count the statistical information of each class after training each task. Specifically, we assume
201   that the features learned by the feature extractor can be approximated by a mixture of Gaussian
202   distributions (Luo et al., 2021; Lindsay, 1995). Therefore, the feature distribution of class i can be
203   reconstructed by counting the mean µi and covariance Σi matrices:

408
      A summary of the results is provided in Table 1, Fig. 4 and 5. The detailed results with standard
409
      deviation can be seen in Tables 4 - 6 and Fig. 9 of Appendix A.2. Our method outperforms ex-
410   isting rehearsal-free methods across three datasets (CIFAR100: C100, TinyImageNet: T200 and
411   ImageNet380: I380) and three task settings (5T, 10T and 20T), achieving an average improvement
412   of 4.42% and 3.08% on Alast and Avg metrics, respectively. It is noteworthy that when there is no
413   information leakage, existing PEFT-based methods all show varying degrees of degradation, while
414   PASS, which is not designed for PEFT, achieves relatively higher performance. This result aligns
415   with the findings of TPL (Lin et al., 2024). However, PASS suffers from high training time overhead,
416   whereas our method significantly enhances the performance of rehearsal-free method in a limited
417   amount of time (See Sec 4.4). Meanwhile, we observe that for the same dataset, all other methods
418
      experience a significant decrease in the Alast metric as the number of tasks increases (e.g., InfLoRA
      drops from 72.50% to 61.80% on ImageNet380). This suggests that when the number of tasks is
419
      small (e.g., T = 5), existing methods can effectively overcome catastrophic forgetting by imposing
420
      additional constraints. However, as the number of tasks increases, the constraints imposed to protect
421   old tasks will continuously squeeze the solution space for new tasks, resulting in a significant de-
422   crease in performance. In contrast, our method maintains the solution space for each task as much
423   as possible and organically combines old and new tasks through dual calibration, thereby greatly
424   reducing the performance difference between the number of short and long tasks (our method drops
425   from 74.90% to 72.45% on ImageNet380). Compared to rehearsal-based methods, our method
426   does not impose the stringent requirement of preserving old task samples and significantly reduces
427   the gap with latest rehearsal-based methods.

528   5    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose a novel PEFT-based rehearsal-free CIL method named DESIRE. Our
531   method fully learns each task by training each stage independently and integrates knowledge from
532   both old and new tasks through efficient dynamic representation consolidation and decision bound-
533   ary refinement to overcome catastrophic forgetting and improve model performance. Experimental
534   results demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to the existing
535   rehearsal-free methods, while maintaining a good balance between stability and plasticity.
536   Limitations and future works: While our proposed method demonstrates strong performance in
537   image classification tasks, this represents only a subset of the broader potential of AI systems. In
538   future work, we plan to extend and adapt our approach to tackle more complex and diverse visual
539   tasks, such as object detection and image segmentation. Expanding to these areas will help us
      understand the method’s broader applicability in real-world scenarios.

954
955   In the training process, we calibrate the classifier by sampling pseudo-features and utilizing the per-
956   class feature means and covariance matrices. We want the feature means of each class are as close
957   as possible to the centers of their respective feature clusters and remain stable during subsequent
958   training. However, as shown in Fig. 11, SLCA exhibits significant feature drift (we highlight in red
959
      circle box). This can lead to pseudo-features generated during the calibration stage to deviate from
      the true distribution and be confused with other classes, thus affecting the final performance. In
960
      contrast, our method mitigates feature drift by dynamically integrating the parameter spaces of both
961
      old and new tasks.
