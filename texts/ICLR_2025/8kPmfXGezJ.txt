009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) has emerged as a compelling framework for scene
012            representation and 3D recovery. To improve its performance on real-world data,
013            depth regularizations have proven to be the most effective ones. However, depth
014            estimation models not only require expensive 3D supervision in training, but also
015            suffer from generalization issues. As a result, the depth estimations can be er-
016            roneous in practice, especially for outdoor unbounded scenes. In this paper, we
017            propose to employ view-consistent distributions instead of fixed depth value esti-
018
               mations to regularize NeRF training. Specifically, the distribution is computed by
               utilizing both low-level color features and high-level distilled features from foun-
019
               dation models at the projected 2D pixel-locations from per-ray sampled 3D points.
020
               By sampling from the view-consistency distributions, an implicit regularization
021            is imposed on the training of NeRF. We also propose a novel depth-pushing loss
022            that works in conjunction with the sampling technique to jointly provide effective
023            regularizations for eliminating the failure modes. Extensive experiments conducted
024            on various scenes from public datasets demonstrate that our proposed method can
025            generate significantly better novel view synthesis results than state-of-the-art NeRF
026            variants as well as different depth regularization methods.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   3D scene reconstruction from multiple images is a long-standing vision problem (Hartley and
032   Zisserman, 2000) but the recent advent of Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al., 2020)
033
      has delivered a significant performance boost, especially given a dense set of input images. However,
      in much the same way the old shape-from-shading was ill-posed (Prados and Faugeras, 2005), so
034
      is the NeRF reconstruction problem: as shown in (Zhang et al., 2020), in the absence of explicit or
035
      implicit regularization, a set of training images can be fitted independently of the recovered geometry.
036   This phenomenon, known as shape-radiance ambiguity, is particularly evident when the input views
037   are not dense enough, even though using Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for scene representation
038   weakly regularizes the scene reconstructions (Zhang et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022).
039
      Many kinds of regularizers have been proposed to improve on this, such as imposing geometric
040
      constraints (Kim et al., 2022; Niemeyer et al., 2022), training to directly predict radiance fields by
041
      using networks conditioned on image features (Chen et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021),
042   or constraining depth (Deng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022). The first is difficult to do
043   for complicated scenes while the second is often limited to very specific 3-view setting and not easily
044   generalizable to unbounded scenes or scalable to more input views. The last, depth regularization,
045   has proved to be the more widely applicable. However, it typically requires expensive 3D supervision,
046   and can produce unreliable predictions on challenging open-space scenes that produce artifacts in the
047   final NeRF reconstruction.
048   To remedy this, we propose to use view-consistency distributions per-ray instead of fixed depth
049   predictions to implement a sampling technique along the rays that implicitly regularizes the training
050   of NeRF, as depicted by Fig. 1. Specifically, we first distill geometric information from the redundant
051   feature representations of foundation models to reduce their dimensionality and to alleviate mem-
052   ory requirements, while preserving the information most likely to be consistent across views and
053   discarding the rest. Given these high-level distilled features along with low-level color features, we
      compute a view-consistency metric for every point along the rays and introduce an adaptive sampling

083   2    R ELATED W ORKS
084
085
      NeRF Variants. The emergence of Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020) is
      an immediate consequence of the study on Implicit Neural Representations (INR) (Tancik et al.,
086
      2020; Sitzmann et al., 2020; Hertz et al., 2021; Mehta et al., 2021), which laid the foundation of
087
      NeRF by introducing powerful network-based scene representation models. NeRFs use them to
088   effectively encode 3D scene properties and trains on posed images via a volume rendering equation
089   that differentiably relates 3D scenes to 2D images.
090
091
      NeRFs deliver outstanding image synthesis results but tendsto be slow, sometimes requiring several
      days for a single scene. A number of accelerated approaches have therefore been proposed (Sun
092
      et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022), using various kinds of
093
      efficient scene representation techniques. Interestingly, not only are these approaches faster, they also
094   tend to yield better image synthesis results. Other works have focused more directly on improving the
095   quality of the synthesis results (Zhang et al., 2020; Barron et al., 2021; 2022; 2023; Turki et al., 2024),
096   by introducing unbounded scene representations or reducing aliasing artifacts. Nerfacto (Tancik et al.,
097   2023), introduced in the popular Nerfstudio project, combines many components of these approaches
098   into an integrated one. Hence, this is what we use as the basis for implementing our own approach.
099
      Regularizers. A straightforward approach to improving the performance of NeRFs is to incorporate
100
      geometric priors to regularize and guide the training process. To this end, many methods have been
101
      proposed. They rely on depth guidance (Deng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a;b; Roessle et al.,
102   2022; Yu et al., 2022), geometric constraints (Niemeyer et al., 2022; Somraj et al., 2023; Truong
103   et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022), or pre-training on similar scenes (Chen et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021;
104   Wang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). However, these methods are all plagued by
105   generalization issues. For depth priors, it is difficult to obtain accurate depth predictions, especially
106   in real-world unbounded scenes. The geometry-based constraints often fail to properly refine the
107   results in complex unbounded scenes. Prediction-based methods are mostly restricted to a 3-view
      setting in bounded scenes, due to the limitations of a prediction-based architecture and the limited


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      availability of real-world unbounded scene data with 3D ground truth. Recently, ReconFusion (Wu
109   et al., 2024) has been proposed to incorporate diffusion piror into NeRF training. This method works
110   well for indoor or bounded scenes, but for open-space scenes the performance drops drastically as the
111   original paper shows.
112
113
      Image Features. Recently, there has been tremendous progress in large-scale self-supervised pre-
      training using Masked image Modeling (He et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021) (MIM).
114
      These new techniques provide us with foundation models, such as DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023),
115
      which encode local geometric information better than classification pretraining (Xie et al., 2023) and
116   generalize well to geometric vision tasks, e.g. image geometric matching (Sun et al., 2021; Edstedt
117   et al., 2023). While there are also models specifically designed and trained for image geometric
118   matching, their pairwise matching setting is ill-suited to NeRFs dealing with image collections
119   because using them would involve traversing all image pairs.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
122
123
      We now introduce our View-consistent Sampling (VS-NeRF) approach for NeRF training. As our
124   method is built on the standard NeRF framework, we first describe it briefly in Sec. 3.1. Next,
125   we describe our approach to distill high-level image features and preserve only view-consistent
126   information from the foundation model DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) in Sec. 3.2. We then introduce a
127   sampling mechanism to exploit these features as well as color features along camera rays in Sec. 3.3.
128   Finally, we describe the proposed depth-pushing loss as a weaker regularization to force the model to
129   favor distant samples in in Sec. 3.4.

134

183   3.2   D ISTILLATION OF G EOMETRIC I NFORMATION .
184
185   To form meaningful view-consistent statistics for adaptive sampling, a good representation of images
186   in the context of multi-view captures is critical. In this paper, we propose to use foundation models
187   that provide powerful general-purpose visual features, e.g. DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), for
188   extracting such high-level representations containing crucial context information. Note that there
189   are alternatives to DINOv2 such as features from diffusion models (Luo et al., 2024), which we
190
      found to deliver similar results. Since diffusion models are slower in inference time, we opted to
      use DINOv2. However, there are two main hurdles in utilizing the features from foundation models.
191
      Firstly, as general-purpose features, the output of a foundation model encodes image information
192
      in many different aspects that are useful in different tasks. In the NeRF setting, we are particularly
193   interested in geometric information that can be expected to be similar across multi-view images of
194   the same scene. Secondly, the dimensionality of features from foundation models is in general very
195   high, e.g. 384 in DINOv2, resulting in prohibitively large memory consumption in sampling.
196
      In this paper, we propose to resolve the issues by distilling geometric information from the foun-
197
      dational features. Note that we use the term distillation in a different way than in Feature Field
198
      Distillation papers, such as (Kobayashi et al., 2022), which focuses on lifting 2D features to a 3D rep-
199   resentation. We distill features by extracting geometric information from redundant high-dimensional
200   image features. Inspired by (Luo et al., 2024), we use a very lightweight Resnet bottleneck block (He
201   et al., 2016) to project the high-dimensional features to a lower dimension for distillation. To super-
202   vise the distillation process, we adopt the Megadepth dataset (Li and Snavely, 2018) which provides
203   3D ground truth and is prevalently used in geometric matching tasks.
204
205
206   Training of Distillation Process. We adopt a very simple strategy for training. Specifically, in
207
      the training phase, we freeze the foundation model and only update the parameters in the Resnet
      bottleneck block. This leads to a much smaller number of training parameters and also requires
208
      much less data. We randomly choose 50000 pairs of images from Megadepth to train. For each
209
      image pair, we use the ground truth depth map to randomly generate 50 corresponding points, and
210   extract the distilled features at the point locations on the image pairs. We then supervise the network
211   using a symmetric cross entropy loss, in the same fashion as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), to make
212   extracted features in corresponding locations as close as possible while still being distinctive from
213   other features. A visualization of the distillation process can be found in Fig. 2. As a result, in
214   our experiments we can reduce the feature dimensionality by a factor of around 10, e.g. 384 from
215   DINOv2 to 32, without compromising on useful geometric information. Please refer to the ablation
      studies in Sec. 4.2 for the discussion of optimal dimensionality in NeRF settings.

229   3.3     V IEW-C ONSISTENT S AMPLING
230
231   In the original NeRF and most NeRF variants, the volume rendering of Eq. 2 is typically achieved
232
      using naive sampling strategies such as uniform, stratified, or linear disparity sampling. Hence, there
      is no prior in the sampling process and hence no regularization while learning the radiance and
233
      density fields. When there are abundant input views, this is usually not an issue but it can result in
234
      unwanted artifacts with a smaller set of input images.
235
236   VS-NeRF remedies this by making the sampling adaptive based on a prior: it samples more densely
237   the 3D locations whose projections have view-consistent features because they are more likely to
238
      correspond to 3D surface points. This requires both a view-consistency metric and an adaptive
      sampling scheme based on that metric, which we describe in Sections 3.3.1 and 3.3.2, respectively. A
239
      graphical visualization of the proposed view-consistent sampling technique can be seen in Fig. 1.
240
241

253   Features from Projections. As shown in Fig. 3, the feature representation at the pixel location
254   where the ray comes from is denoted as reference feature fr . For each pre-sample point pi , we can
255   project it onto an arbitrary view vj . If the point pi is visible to vj , then naturally by interpolating over
256   the feature representation Fj , we can obtain the projection feature fij . Due to limited field-of-view
257
      (FOV) of cameras, there is a varying number of views that a point pi can be projected onto. We
      denote the set of views that a point pi can be projected onto as Vi , and |Vi | as its cardinality.
258
259
260   Normalized Similarity Measure. In this paper, we jointly use high-level distilled features, and
261
      plain normalized RGB values as low-level color features. Please see the ablation study in Sec. 4.2
      to understand their respective effects. However, the two kinds of features are defined in different
262
      metric spaces. That is to say, while Euclidean distances can be used for measuring discrepancies
263
      among color features, cosine similarities are most frequently used as a distance measure of features
264   from pre-trained models such as DINOv2. In this paper, we use a normalizing strategy to convert
265   the measures among features to binary numbers, be it color feature or distilled feature. In particular,
266   along an arbitrary ray r, we first compute the measures between the reference feature and projection
267   features from all sampled points {m(fr , fij ) | j ∈ Vi }, be it Euclidean distances or cosine similarities.
268   We then normalize the set of measures based on its mean and variance, and take the negative if the
269   measure is Euclidean distance to align distance with similarity. Thus, we have defined the normalized
      similarity measure {mn (fr , fij ) | j ∈ Vi }.

      3.3.2    A DAPTIVE S AMPLING S CHEME
283
284   Given the view-consistency metric of Eq. 4, implementing view-consistent sampling becomes straight-
285   forward. After computing the metric for each pre-sampled point along the ray, we perform importance
286   sampling based on the distribution along the ray. Our rationale is that this view-consistentcy distribu-
287   tion is concentrated around the surface point, thus importance sampling from the distribution is the
288   logical way to improve it.
289   In our implementation, we use the Probability Distribution Function (PDF) sampler from Nerfstu-
290   dio (Tancik et al., 2023) to perform importance sampling, which generatse samples that match a
291   distribution. Specifically, as illustrated by Fig. 1, we first compute view-consistency metrics from
292   pre-samples (ti )pre
                       1≤i≤M along the ray. The PDF sampler will probabilistically sample the bins be-
293   tween consecutive pre-sample points, such that the distribution of number of samples in each bin will
294   match the view-consistency distribution, which gives the true samples (ti )1≤i≤S to compute losses
295   as in Eq. 2.

      methods. We use the same hyperparameter configuration for all experiments.

      4.3   L IMITATIONS
487
488   Despite the excellent results in visual quality delivered by VS-NeRF, it has some limitations. This
489   includes shrinking effectiveness with available views increasing as shown in Fig. 5, and efficiency
490   issues. Specifically, we see that VS-NeRF takes more time than the efficient competitors from Tab. 1,
491   and it also requires more memory. For example, for a scene with around 80 input images, it will
492   consume roughly 25 GB memory. However, since our method is most effective when the views are
493   less dense, this drawback of a concern in practice. Furthermore, more efficient implementations are
494
      possible and will be explored.
495
496   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSIONS
497
498   In this paper, we have proposed a novel view-consistent sampling technique as a regularization for
499   the training of NeRF. The core idea is to combine high-level and low-level features to compute view-
500   consistency metrics, and use it as a prior distribution to sample on the ray. To mitigate the background
501   collapse problem, we also propose a depth-pushing loss, which imposes a weaker regularization to
502   favor distant samples on the ray. Extensive experiments on public datasets have demonstrated the
503
      effectiveness of the proposed method.
504
      Broader Impacts. The method in this paper can help generate highly realistic 3D scenes from 2D
505
      images, which can find applications in various fields such as education and entertaining. On the other
506
      hand it may also be used to create realistic forgeries which requires careful considerations.

655   A.1    A PPROXIMATE S TORAGE C OMPUTATION
656
      The storage problem of projection features is addressed by our proposed distillation process as in
657
      Sec. 3.2, here we show the necessity of it. For a batch of rays with size |B|, the tensor to store the
658
      projection features would be of size |B| × M × N × C, where M is the number of sampled points
659   per ray, N is number of input views, and C is the feature dimension. This is prohibitive when the
660   feature dimension C is very large, for example, in the common setting where |B| = 4096, M = 256,
661   N = 50 and C = 384, it will consume roughly 80GB memory with float datatype. But if we distill
662   the features such that C = 32, the memory requirement becomes roughly 6.7GB.
