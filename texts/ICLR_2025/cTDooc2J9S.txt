009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a new point of view on transferability of graph neural networks based
012            on the intrinsic notion of information diffusion within graphs. This notion is adapted
013            to considering graphs to be similar if their overall rough structures are similar, while
014            their fine-print articulation may differ. Transferability of graph neural networks
015
               is then considered between graphs that are similar from this novel perspective
               on transferability. After carefully analysing transferability of single filters, the
016
               transferability properties of entire networks are relegated to the transferability
017
               characteristics of the filters employed inside their convolutional blocks. A rigorous
018            analysis establishes our main theoretical finding: Spectral convolutional networks
019            are transferable between graphs whose overall rough structures align, if their
020            filters arise as Laplace transforms of certain generalized functions. Numerical
021            experiments illustrate and validate the theoretical findings in practice.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
      A fundamental quality of any machine learning model is its ability to generalize beyond the data on
026
      which it was trained. In the graph neural network (GNN) setting, a crucial aspect of this capability is
027
      characterized by the property of transferability: If two graphs are similar, also their respective latent
028   embeddings should be similar to each other. I.e. GNNs should be transferable between such graphs.
029
030   We may thus think of transferability as encoding information about continuity properties of GNNs:
031
      Equipping the space of graphs, with a suitable distance-notion capturing graph similarity, we may
      consider GNNs as functions mapping from this space to latent Euclidean spaces. Transferable models
032
      then correspond to continuous maps: Their outputs are close if input graphs are close to each other. In
033
      contrast, non-transferable GNNs are discontinuous: Embeddings generated by such models may vary
034   strongly even if the corresponding graphs are close to each other. If a transferable GNN model is then
035   confronted during inference with a graph that is similar to a graph that was already observed during
036   training, generated latent embeddings will be similar. Hence a good performance on the train-set will
037   translate to a similarly good performance on the test set: The model will be able to generalize.
038
      Here we will be analyzing transferability properties of spectral graph neural networks (Bruna et al.,
039
      2014; Defferrard et al., 2016); a prominent class of GNNs which continue to set the state of the art
040   on a diverse set of tasks (He et al., 2021; 2022a; Wang & Zhang, 2022; Koke & Cremers, 2024).
041   From a theoretical perspective, transferability of such models has been predominantly investigated
042   in the setting of (very) large graphs taken to faithfully approximate a common underlying ambient
043   object. Examples of such objects are metric measure spaces (Levie et al., 2019a) and graphons (Ruiz
044   et al., 2020; Maskey et al., 2021), which are applicable to graphs where the number of edges |E| is
045   of OpN 2 q, with N the number of nodes. Large sparse graphs (|E| “ OpN q) are instead considered
046   to approximate the same graphop (Le & Jegelka, 2023) or graphing (Roddenberry et al., 2022).
047   Transferability outside this asymptotic regime of large graphs has to the best of our knowledge so far
048
      only been investigated for limited examples and a restrictive class of filter functions Koke (2023).
049   Contributions: Here we propose an alternative approach to transferability: Fundamentally, we
050   consider two graphs to be similar if the rough overall structures within them align, while fine-
051   print articulations are allowed to vary. This setting captures fundamental examples such as graphs
052   discretizing the same manifold, graphs describing the same object at different resolutions or graphs
053   differing by edge deletions. To quantify similarity in this setting, we build on the notion of diffusion
      distance (Hammond et al., 2013), which provides a relaxation of the canonical linear distance ||L´ L̃||


                                                          1

054
      between Laplacians L, L̃ of different graphs. Within this relaxed distance measure, variations in
055   coarse structure are weighted more heavily, while variations in fine-structure are instead discounted.
056   A rigorous analysis then establishes our main theoretical finding: Networks are transferable between
057   graphs that are close in the diffusion sense, if their filters arise as Laplace transforms.
058
      Our novel viewpoint provides a broad and general framework to analyze transferability: It is not
059
      dependent on any ambient space, applies outside the setting of large graphs, is not restricted to a
060
      certain scaling behaviour of the number of edges and covers settings where previous transferability
061   results are not applicable (e.g. between original and coarsified graphs). To provide guidance for the
062   practicioner, we perform carefully designed numerical experiments highlighting the importance of
063   transferability, showcasing the failure of common architectures to transfer and numerically verifing
064   that architectures conforming to our developed theory indeed do exhibit transferability.
065
      Caveat: The notion of diffusion-similarity central to our analysis below is adapted to the setting
066
      where the rough overall structure within graphs is more important than fine structure details. Utilizing
067
      such a relaxation of the standard linear distance ||L ´ L̃|| allows to consider more relaxed conditions
068
      on filter functions than previous works (Gama et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021) in this setting. It
069   is however important to note that since our analysis is based on a distance notion that discounts
070   fine-structure details within graphs, the results in our paper do not allow to draw conclusions about
071   transferability and model performance in settings where the exact articulation of a graph is important.

      6     C ONCLUSION
534   We developed a novel approach to transferability based on the intrinsic notion of diffusion on graphs,
535   which considers graphs to be similar if their rough overall structures align. Transferability of entire
536   networks in this setting was relegated to the filter functions employed inside their convolutional
537   blocks. A rigorous analysis established that when the rough overall information whithin graphs is
538   paramount, networks are transferable if filters arise as Laplace transforms while other filter choices
539   will not lead to transferability. In example settings – including settings not covered by other already
      established approaches to transferability – this was then confirmed numerically.

844   B    F URTHER D ISCUSSION OF EXISTING A PPROACHES TO T RANSFERABILITY
845
846   In this section we provide further details on existing approaches to transferability of graph neural
847   networks:
848
849   Graphon Neural Networks and the Transferability of Graph Neural Networks (Ruiz et al., 2020):
850   This seminal work explores the theoretical underpinnings of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in the
851   context of graphons, a mathematical generalization of graphs to large-scale, continuous structures.
852
      The paper establishes a connection between GNNs and graphons, providing insights into the behavior
      of GNNs on large, dense graphs (|E| is of OpN 2 q, with N the number of nodes (Le & Jegelka, 2023))
853
      by modeling these graphs as graphons. This framework helps understand how GNNs operate in the
854
      limit of large graphs and their potential to generalize across different graph structures in this realm. A
855   central focus of the paper is the transferability of GNNs—specifically, their ability to perform well
856   on large graphs that may differ in size or topology from those seen during training. Transferability
                                                                                                 1
857   errors between graphs discretizing the same graphon are established to be of OpN ´ 2 q, with N the
858   minimum number of nodes. Assumptions on considered filter functions are that they are bounded and
859   Lipschitz continuous (c.f. AS2 on page 6; ibid.).
860
861   Transferability of Graph Neural Networks: an Extended Graphon Approach (Maskey et al.,
862   2021): This work is in spirit similar to (Ruiz et al., 2020) whose results it extends from considering
863   the adjacency matrix as the graph shift operator to more general graph shift operators and from
      considering only polynomial filters to allowing for general continuous filter functions.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Limits, approximation and size transferability for GNNs on sparse graphs via graphops (Le
865   & Jegelka, 2023): In contrast to approaches using graphons, which focus on large dense graphs,
866   this paper instead focuses on transferability on sparse graphs (|E| “ OpN q). The paper makes use
867   of the concept of Graphops, a mathematical operator that can be used to model how GNNs behave
868   on large sparse graphs. This operator helps analyze the limit behavior of GNNs, capturing the way
869   information is propagated through large sparse graph structures.
870
      One of the focuses of the paper is size transferability, which refers to the ability of a GNN to
871   generalize across graphs of different sizes. The authors explore how GNNs can transfer learned
872   representations from smaller, sparse graphs to larger ones, and vice versa. By leveraging the Graphop
873   framework, the paper formalizes conditions for successful transferability between graphs of varying
874   sizes.
875
876   On Local Distributions in Graph Signal Processing (Roddenberry et al., 2022): Thiw work is
877   rooted in the field of graph signal procesing (GSP) and puts a particular emphasis on the transferability
878   of GSP techniques across different graph structures. The paper focuses on the concept of graphings,
879   which are a probabilistic framework for representing large sparse graphs and their underlying
880   structures.
881
      The paper investigates how local signal behaviors, defined by local distributions over neighborhoods
882   in a graph, can be transferred from one graph to another. Specifically, it formalizes how GSP
883   techniques—such as filtering and node classification—can be transferred to graphs that are not
884   identical but share similar local structures.
885
      By modeling large graphs through graphings, the authors provide a framework that makes it possible
886
      to generalize local distributions and signal processing tasks across different graphs.
887
888
889
      Graph Convolutional Neural Networks via Scattering (Zou & Lerman, 2020) This work
      provides a different perspective on Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) by connecting them to
890
      scattering transforms, a concept from signal processing. The authors demonstrate that GCNs can
891
      be interpreted as a discrete graph counterpart of scattering transforms, which involve multi-scale
892   wavelet-like operations that capture hierarchical information across different levels of graph structure.
893   This connection highlights the multi-scale nature of GCNs, similar to scattering transforms, which
894   analyze signals at varying resolutions.
895
      A key focus of the paper is the stability of GCNs when viewed through the scattering framework.
896
      The authors argue that scattering transforms offer a more stable approach to graph signal processing
897
      compared to traditional GCNs, especially in the presence of noisy or incomplete graph data. The
898   multi-layer structure of GCNs, when interpreted as a series of scattering operations, allows for more
899   robust signal propagation across the graph, making GCNs less sensitive to perturbations in the graph
900   topology.
901
      By linking GCNs with scattering transforms, the paper provides both a theoretical foundation for
902
      understanding GCNs’ operations and an approach to improving their robustness and interpretability
903
      in graph-based learning tasks.

907   bounding differences, where the difference }ψpLq ´ ψpL̃q} is then via a triangle inequality argument
908   reduced to bounding each term }ψpλk quk u⊺k ´ ψpλ̃k qũk ũ⊺k } individually. This is done in eq.s (64)
909   and (68) respectively, which are condingent the there stated spectral restrictions.

911   Limitless transferability for graph convolutional Networks (Koke, 2023): This work studies
912   stability- and transferability proeprties of spectral graph neural networks, with a particular focus
913   on directed graphs. In spirit, it is the closest to our work here, as one of the main class of filters it
914   investigates is the class of resolvent based filter functions which constitute an example (i.e. Example
915
      4.3) of the more general class of Laplace transform filters considered in this present work.

917   Stability to Deformations of Manifold Filters and Manifold Neural Networks (Wang et al.,
      2024a) :

918
      This work explores the theoretical foundation of manifold filters and manifold neural networks
919   (MNNs), focusing on their transferability across manifolds. Similarly to the filters analyzed in the
920   present work, manifold filters are defined in terms of Laplace transforms. By framing graph neural
921   networks (GNNs) as discrete approximations of MNNs, the authors analyze conditions under which
922   MNNs remain stable under smooth deformations of the manifold.
923
      Stability is shown to depend on specific spectral properties of the filter functions, including Lipschitz
924
      continuity and integral Lipschitz continuity, which control the trade-off between robustness and
925   frequency discriminability. The paper establishes that filters meeting these conditions can generalize
926   effectively to new manifolds by adapting to changes in the Laplace-Beltrami operator’s spectrum.
927
928   More techicalle, filters are bounded as |ψpLq ´ ψpL̃q| ď K}L ´ L̃}. In Theorem 2 absolute
929   perturbations are considered (L̃ “ L ` A), in Theorem 3 relative perturbations are considered
930   (L̃ “ L ` EL). In both cases the conditions on spectrum and filter functions stem from the fact that
931   Lipschitz-ness does not directly translate to operator Lipschitz-ness when measured in spectral norm
932   (see e.g. Wihler (2009) for a discussion).

935
      offs (Wang et al., 2024b): Here instead of measuring the linear norm difference }LP ´ LP }
      between a graph Laplacian L and a manifold Laplacian L (which generically would be infinite
936
      as L is an unbounded operator), the difference of the action of these operators on eigenfunctions
937
      (}LP ϕ ´ LP ϕ}). After a triangle inequality argument, one term that has to be bounded in order to
938   bound the difference in filter outputs is }ϕni ´ϕi } of the ith eigenfunction and eigenvector respectively.
939   The fidelity of this approximation depends on spectral separation properties (c.f. Theorem 4 ibid.),
940   which hence leads to the requirement that the spectrum be α-separated. This requirement can thus be
941   considered an artifact of considering the linear approximation }ϕni ´ ϕi } for each eigenfunction. In
942   contrast, in our approach (c.f. Appendix F.2) the notion of approximation of the Laplacian on the
943   underlying manifold is different. We bound the quantity }J Ò e´tL J Ó ´ e´t∆ } instead. Hence we do
944   not need to bound differences between individual eigenfunctions and eigenvectors and hence avoid

947   Transferability of Spectral Graph Convolutional Neural Networks (Levie et al., 2019a): As one
948
      of the earliest works challenging the then prevailing belief that spectral methods are not transferable,
      this work was among the first to present theoretical proofs and experimental evidence to demonstrate
949
      that these methods can generalize effectively under certain conditions.
950
951   The key contribution is a theoretical framework in which transferability depends on how well graphs
952   approximate a shared underlying continuous domain, such as a topological space or metric-measure
953   space. Many graph convolutional networks are then shown to have "principle transferability" in this
954
      setting, meaning that their ability to generalize is built-in and does not rely on additional training.
      The analysis introduces the transferability inequality, which bounds the generalization error of filters
955
      based on the graph Laplacian’s approximation quality and sampling consistency.
956
957   The study also develops sufficient conditions for achieving low transferability errors, demonstrating
958   that spectral ConvNets can perform consistently across graphs with varying sizes, topologies, and
959   dimensions, provided the graphs discretize the same continuous domain.
960   As in our work, filters here are only required to be bounded and Lipschitz continuous (c.f. Theorem
961   17 ibid.). However, signals are assumed to be bandlimted. We avoid Levie’s growth of the stability
962   constant with the number of considered eigenvalues (c.f. the discussion towards the end of page
963   12 ibid.) by avoiding approximations of individual eigenfunctions and instead approximating the
964   bounded operator e´t∆ directly.
965
966   Diffusion Scattering Transforms on Graphs (Gama et al., 2019): This work emphasizes the
967   stability of scattering-based representations against perturbations in graph topology and reindexing.
968
      By extending the concept of scattering transforms to graph-structured data, the framework introduces
      diffusion scattering transforms that leverage diffusion operators to capture multi-scale hierarchical
969
      features of graph signals.
970
971   The authors focus on ensuring that the transforms are robust to changes in graph structure, such as
      modifications to edge weights or topology. Stability is achieved through the use of diffusion wavelets,

972

1010   From a spectral perspective, the key idea here is that including the exponential into the distance
1011   metric leads to an (exponential) suppression of large eigenvalues of L and L̃. Information encoded
1012   into these large eigenvalues (and corresponding eigenspaces) corresponds to fine structure details of

1017   then be relatively small. Thus this metric is adapted to considering graphs that are similar up to
1018   fine-structure variations to be close to each other. This is the setting we are interested in when
1019   considering transferability, so that this distance measure is adapted to this setting of transferring
1020   filters between approximately similar graphs (see also the discussion in Section 3).

1022   authors showed diffusion distances (dp¨, ¨q) to be a well defined metric on the space of graphs. Here,
1023   ’metric’ is used in the strictly mathematical sense (i.e. satisfying the defining properties of positivity,
1024   symmetry and the triangle inequality). Hence the notion of diffusion similarity equips the space of
1025   graphs with a well defined (metric-)topology. This topology respects the one induced by Euclidean
