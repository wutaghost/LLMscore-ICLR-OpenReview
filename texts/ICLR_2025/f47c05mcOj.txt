009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               AI image compression has outperformed traditional methods in both efficiency
012            and quality but remains vulnerable to adversarial attacks. Most attacks on deep
013            neural networks (DNNs) involve adding small perturbations to the input image to
014            deceive the system and produce incorrect results. While simple, these additive
015            perturbations affect pixels uniformly across different intensity levels, from dark to
016            bright regions. However the human eye is less sensitive to variations in dark areas
017            than in bright ones, making noise in brighter areas more visible. This observation
018            suggests a novel attack strategy that minimizes the visibility of adversarial noise
019            through adaptive perturbations. To achieve this, we propose a nonlinear log-exp
020
               perturbation, which applies more noise to dark pixels while minimizing its impact
               on bright areas.
021
022            We evaluated this perturbation model in two scenarios: one distorts the output of
023
               decompression models and another one increases the bit rate of compressed im-
               ages without visibly affecting quality. Our findings offer new strategies to protect
024
               AI-driven image compression systems, ensuring both security and performance in
025
               practical applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The advent of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) Kingma &
032
      Welling (2014) has brought significant improvements into image compression. These technologies
      enable the encoding of images into a compact latent space, facilitating efficient storage and transmis-
033
      sion Ballé et al. (2016). However, these AI-based models have their own challenges. Deep neural
034
      networks (DNNs) are generally large. For example, the Cheng2020-anchor model has a size of 120
035   MB, while the Attention TCM for AI compression has a size of almost 900 MB. Larger models are
036   more vulnerable to adversarial attacks. This vulnerability is demonstrated in the work by Tong Chen
037   and Zhan Ma Chen & Ma (2021), which shows that AI compression models can be attacked easily
038   with simple additive perturbations and the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) method.
039
      Adversarial attacks have become an important area of research in the field of deep learning. The
040
      vulnerability of these models to specifically crafted perturbations is well-known.
041
042   For attacking AI compression models, one can adapt many existing methods for adversarial attacks
043   from other domains of applications, such as classification, text, and music. However, these methods
044
      do not account for the characteristics of the human eye.
045   Most current adversarial attack noise models utilize simple additive noise, often without considering
046   the human visual system’s perception. This results in perturbations that may be overly visible to the
047   human eye, undermining the stealthiness of the attack. However, the human eye does not perceive
048   noise uniformly across different luminance levels. Accounting for this can lead to more perceptually
049   imperceptible yet effective adversarial attacks.
050   In order for the adversarial attacks to succeed, the need to craft perturbations which are invisible to
051   human eyes is important. In this paper, inspired by Weber’s Law for light incremental threshold, we
052   propose a new nonlinear perturbation model which is based on the log-exp function, can adapt to the
053   luminance of different regions in an image. This approach allows for more imperceptible adversarial
      attacks, as the noise generated follows the light incremental threshold of the human visual system.


                                                        1
054
      Our method demonstrates that by aligning noise generation with the properties of human perception,
055   adversarial attacks can be made less detectable without compromising their effectiveness. This
056   opens new avenues for the development of advanced adversarial techniques that take human visual
057   perception into account, ensuring that perturbations are optimized for both efficacy against models
058   and invisibility to humans.

      2     N EURAL I MAGE C OMPRESSION F RAMEWORK
061
062
      The application of deep learning to image compression has significantly advanced the field, particu-
063
      larly through the use of autoencoder-based architectures. Balle et al. Ballé et al. (2018) proposed an
064   end-to-end variational autoencoder (VAE) model, compressing image representations into a latent
065   distribution and using a hyperprior to capture spatial dependencies. The framework optimizes rate-
066   distortion performance by balancing bit rate (entropy) and image quality (distortion), often using
067   Mean Squared Error (MSE) or Multi-Scale Structural Similarity (MS-SSIM) as metrics. Minnen
068   et al. Minnen et al. (2018) extended this with an autoregressive context model, while Cheng et al.
069   Cheng et al. (2020) further improved performance using Gaussian Mixture Models for more pre-
070   cise latent representation estimation. Specifically,they introduced two enhanced architectures: the
071   ”Cheng-anchor2020” model, which incorporates residual blocks in the analysis and synthesis trans-
072
      forms, and the ”Cheng-Atten” model, which combines both residual and attention modules in these
      transforms.
073
074   In Liu et al. (2023), the Transformer-CNN Mixture (TCM) model combines CNNs’ local feature
075   modeling with Transformers’ non-local capabilities, achieving state-of-the-art rate-distortion per-
076   formance through an efficient hybrid design with Swin-transformer-based attention modules.
077   In these frameworks, the compression process involves transforming the image into a latent space,
078   quantizing the latent variables, and reconstructing the image from the quantized representation. The
079   optimization objective minimizes both the rate (bit usage) and distortion (image quality loss), mak-
080   ing these methods highly effective for compressing images without noticeable visual degradation.
081
082   2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK M ETHODS
083
084   Adversarial attacks, particularly those utilizing additive perturbations, pose a serious threat to neural
085
      image compression systems by introducing carefully crafted noise that can degrade the model’s per-
      formance. These attacks aim to subtly alter the input image to either reduce compression efficiency
086
      or impair the reconstructed output quality, often without perceptible changes to the human eye. The
087
      most common attack methods include: Common attack methods include FGSM Goodfellow et al.
088   (2014), which adds noise in the gradient direction, PGD Madry et al. (2019) for iterative refine-
089   ment, BIM Kurakin et al. (2018) for repeated perturbations, Carlini and Wagner Lin et al. (2021) for
090   optimization-based minimal perturbations, and Wasserstein Attack Wu et al. (2020) for semantically
091   meaningful perturbations.
092
      These methods are widely used due to their simplicity and effectiveness in generating adversarial ex-
093
      amples. However, they face several challenges: perturbations may be more visible in bright regions,
094   uniform noise application can lead to suboptimal attacks, and large perturbations can noticeably de-
095   grade image quality. Additionally, these attacks are often vulnerable to defense techniques such as
096   adversarial training or preprocessing.
097
      For neural image compression systems, these challenges are particularly critical, as visible pertur-
098
      bations can disrupt the compression process and compromise image quality in visually sensitive
099
      applications. As a result, this motivates us to develop advanced attack strategies that consider re-
100   gional sensitivity and compression-specific characteristics to improve the stealth and effectiveness
101   of adversarial attacks in this domain.
102
103   2.2   ROBUSTNESS OF AI I MAGE C OMPRESSION
104
105   In Liu et al. (2022), the authors explored the robustness of deep learning-based image compression
106   models under adversarial attacks. They applied both white-box and black-box attacks to increase the
107   bitrate of compressed images significantly. Using a white-box approach with FGSM, they achieved
      up to a 50% bitrate increase but applied perturbations globally across the entire image, resulting in


                                                         2
108
      highly visible artifacts. In the black-box setting, their DCT-Net achieved a 4x increase in bitrate at
109   best, but again, the perturbations were clearly noticeable.
110
111   However, their work has notable limitations. No perceptual similarity metrics, such as PSNR or
112
      SSIM, were used to evaluate how closely the attacked images resembled the originals. This is a
      critical gap, as such metrics would provide a clearer picture of attack impact beyond bitrate changes.
113
      Additionally, their approach does not address localized attacks, which could potentially lead to more
114
      imperceptible perturbations with similar effectiveness.
115
116   Lei et al. (2021) explored out-of-distribution OOD-robust compression, using distributionally robust
117   optimization and structured coding to handle distribution shifts. However, it did not address adver-
118
      sarial attacks or the challenge of ensuring imperceptible perturbations, focusing solely on OOD
      scenarios.
119
120   A recent study introduced benchmarks (CLIC-C and Kodak-C) and spectral inspection tools to eval-
121   uate the out-of-distribution (OOD) robustness of neural image compression (NIC) models Lieber-
122   man et al. (2023), revealing key insights into their performance under distribution shifts. The work
123   highlighted NIC’s ability to handle high-frequency corruptions better than classic codecs but noted
124
      challenges in generalizing to high-frequency shifts. Unlike our focus, this study did not explore
      adversarial attacks designed to induce artifacts or increase bpp in NIC models.

129   3.1   N OISE M ODELING AND H UMAN V ISUAL S ENSITIVITY
130
131   Most current adversarial attack noise models utilize simple additive noise, often without considering
132
      the human visual system’s perception. This results in perturbations that may be overly visible to the
      human eye, undermining the stealthiness of the attack. However, the human eye does not perceive
133
      noise uniformly across different luminance levels. Accounting for this can lead to more perceptually
134
      imperceptible yet effective adversarial attacks.
135
136   A key principle that describes the sensitivity of the human visual system is Weber’s Law Weber
137   (1834). According to this law, the just noticeable difference (JND) in stimulus intensity, or the light
138
      incremental threshold (δI), is proportional to the background intensity (I). Specifically, for low
      luminance levels (darker areas), the ratio δI/I is relatively large. As luminance increases, this ratio
139
      becomes smaller and tends to remain constant for mid-range luminance levels between 1 and 100
140
      millilamberts. Brightness discrimination is poor (large Weber ratio) at low illumination levels and
141   improves significantly as I increases.
142
143   To illustrate this, consider the following examples of luminance and their corresponding threshold
144
      ratios:
145
             • For a luminance of 0.001 mL, the threshold ratio δI
                                                                 I is approximately 0.2.
146
147          • For a luminance of 0.01 mL, the threshold ratio decreases to around 0.1.
148          • For a luminance of 1 mL, the threshold ratio is about 0.02.
149
150   This relationship highlights that the human visual system is less sensitive to brightness changes in
151   dark regions compared to brighter regions, where the eye struggles to discern small variations. The
152
      dashed line in Figure 238 in Rutten & van Venrooij (2024) illustrates this concept.
153
154   3.2   V ISUAL P ERCEPTION AND A DAPTIVE P ERTURBATIONS
155
      The Weber-Fechner law Fechner (1860) further describes the non-linear relationship between stim-
156
      ulus intensity (I) and perceived sensation (S)
157
158                                           S = 2.3k log10 I + C                                       (1)
159
160   where k determines the steepness of the curve and C defines its vertical position. This law explains
161   why the eye’s sensitivity to brightness changes varies across luminance levels. In very low luminance
      environments, background noise in the eye makes it difficult to detect small changes in light, while in


                                                        3
162
      extremely bright conditions, the eye becomes overwhelmed and loses sensitivity to minor differences
163   in luminanceRutten & van Venrooij (2024) .
164
165   This insight suggests that by accounting for the varying sensitivity of the human eye, adversarial
166
      noise can be better tailored to different regions of an image.

168   3.3   L OG -E XP N OISE M ODEL I NSPIRED BY W EBER ’ S L AW
169
      Building upon this understanding of human visual sensitivity, we propose a novel adversarial noise
170   model that leverages the light incremental threshold. The core idea is to generate noise that adapts
171   to the luminance of different regions within an image, ensuring that perturbations are less detectable
172   by the human eye while still effective in deceiving neural networks.
173
      Specifically, we propose to model the adversarial noise as a function of the luminance I and the
174
      random noise generated for a given pixel as follows
175
176                                            I ′ = log(expI +n),                                       (2)
177                                                ′
      where n is small perturbation noise, and I is the perturbed intensity from the luminance level I
178   of the pixel. Note that the noise n is usually small, ensuring that the logarithm function does not
179   encounter errors.
180
      The Taylor expansion of the log-exp function in (2) around n = 0
181
182                              I ′ = log(expI +n) = I + n exp(−I) + O(n)                               (3)
183   shows that δI = n exp(−I) represents the additive perturbation added to the pixel value I (lumi-
184   nance level). The perturbation δI adaptively changes to the pixel values I as an exponential decay
185   factor exp(−I). Note that the normalized pixel value I is in the interval of [0, 1]. In darker regions,
186   I ≈ 0, the perturbation δI is as the noise n, but it monotonically decreases as I increases to 1,
187   yielding δI ≈ 0.3679 n, i.e., introducing less noise to the bright region.
188   This exponential decay model ensures that in darker regions, where the human eye is less sensitive
189   to small perturbations, the noise can be slightly stronger. Conversely, in brighter regions, the noise
190   is minimized to remain imperceptible.
191
      The perturbation p(I) can be modified with a decaying factor κ
192
193                                            δI = n · exp(−κI)                                         (4)
194   where κ can range from 1 to 3. This function grows quickly for large values of I (bright areas) and
195   slows for smaller values of I (dark areas), allowing for more control over perturbations in different
196   luminance regions.
197
198   3.4   J UST N OTICEABLE D IFFERENCE M ODELS
199
200
      Several existing models have explored the concept of JND to minimize the visibility of noise in
      images. For instance, Hu et al. (2023) incorporate color sensitivity to adjust the sub-JND thresholds
201
      of Y, Cb, and Cr components, creating a color-sensitivity-based JND model (CSJND). This model
202
      reflects the visibility limitations of the human visual system and is commonly applied to perceptual
203   image and video processing.
204
205   Another example is the Just Noticeable Difference Model Zhang et al. (2023), which focuses on
206
      estimating the JND based on the characteristics of the human visual system. This model considers
      spatial contrast sensitivity functions and other factors to create a more accurate representation of
207
      perceptual thresholds. These models aim to create noise that minimizes visibility, aligning with the
208
      main idea of our proposed method.
209
210   In the context of adversarial attacks, the goal is to introduce small perturbations to an image that
211   cause a neural network to misclassify the image or degrade its performance, but without making the
212
      perturbations visible to humans. Our adaptive noise model, guided by Weber’s law, is designed to
      achieve this by ensuring that noise is proportional to the sensitivity of the human eye to brightness
213
      changes in different regions of the image.
214
215   By incorporating principles from Weber’s law, specifically the relationship between luminance and
      detection thresholds, we introduce a more sophisticated noise model that adapts to the luminance of


                                                        4
216
      different regions in an image. This approach allows for more imperceptible adversarial attacks, as
217   the noise generated follows the light incremental threshold of the human visual system.

220   4   M AXIMIZING D ISTORTION IN A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS ON I MAGE
221       C OMPRESSION
222
223   Following on the log-exp perturbation model proposed in the previous section, we propose a max-
224   distortion adversarial attack aimed at finding a noise pattern that maximizes the distortion in decom-
225   pressed images.
226   Consider an original image x, and let x⋆ be the perturbed version, defined as x⋆ = log(exp(x) +
227   na ), where na represents the adversarial noise for the attack. The neural network’s decompressed
228   output is denoted by x̂ = f (x⋆ ). Similar to adversarial examples used in classification, the goal
229   here is to learn a noise pattern na that minimally alters x but significantly impairs the compression
230   model’s output quality. This is done by amplifying the difference between the decompressed images
231   f (x⋆ ) and f (x) or between f (x⋆ ) and x .
232   The attack objective can be formulated as
233
                           min    P SN R(f (x⋆ ), x) + λ∥na ∥1      s.t.   ∥na ∥∞ ≤ δ,                   (5)
234                         na
235
      where ∥na ∥∞ represents the infinity norm, and δ > 0 defines the allowable noise level. The noise
236   pattern na is reparameterized as na = δ tanh(κ · u), where u is an unconstrained variable and
237   κ controls the sharpness of the noise transition, ensuring that na approaches ±δ without reaching
238   those bounds.
239
      To maximize distortion while maintaining imperceptibility, the attack focuses on local high-entropy
240
      regions, which often contain more detail and are more sensitive to perturbations. A binary mask
241
      identifies these regions by grouping similar pixels and selecting superpixels with the highest entropy,
242   indicating complex areas of the image. The mask marks significant regions for targeted distortion.
243
244   The optimization problem in (5) is rewritten as
245                                   min    P SN R(f (x⋆ ), x) + λ∥u∥1 ,                                (6)
                                        u
246
247   where x⋆ = log(exp(x) + δ · mask · tanh(u)), mask represents the binary mask of the targeted
248   attack region.
249   To solve this optimization, methods such as Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) or ADAM are used
250   to estimate u. Larger values of u push the noise pattern n near the boundary, while reducing large
251   coefficients helps avoid local minima during optimization.
252
      The proposed approach concentrates the attack on high-entropy regions, where image content is
253
      more unpredictable. By constraining the noise using a nonlinear transformation, the perturbations
254   are significant enough to degrade image quality but remain imperceptible to the human eye, balanc-
255   ing effectiveness and subtlety.
256
257
      The mask is smoothed using a Gaussian filter with σ = 21, which helps regulate the noise and
      progressively reduce it toward the desired threshold. Through iterative updates, the mask is gradually
258
      shrunk, focusing the attack on a smaller region and refining the perturbations for maximal distortion
259
      with minimal visibility. For more detailes how the hyperparameters were selected please refer to A

529
530   In this paper we introduced a novel adversarial attack method to impair the image compression
531
      which is based on nonlinear log-exp perturbation. To maximize distortion we adapt the Hyperbolic
      Transformation Method and the local high-entropy selected attack. In our experiments we have
532
      demonstrated that these techniques can effectively disrupt the compression models by significantly
533
      impacting the image quality and file size.
534
535   Our defense strategy proves to be capable of removing adversarial noise, allowing for high-quality
536   image compression. These methods not only enhance our understanding of adversarial tactics but
537
      also introduces new applications for attack strategies to limit image compression.
