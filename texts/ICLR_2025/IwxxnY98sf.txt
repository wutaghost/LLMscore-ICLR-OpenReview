                                              Abstract
009           Humans are known to reason using logic and abstract categories, and yet
010           most state of the art neural models use continuous distributed represen-
011           tations. These representations offer impressive gradient-based learning ca-
012           pabilities, but it is often difficult to know what symbolic algorithm the
013           network might implicitly be implementing, if any. We find that there are
014           representational geometries that naturally suggest a symbolic structure,
015           which can be expressed in terms of binary components. We show that we
016           can recover this structure by fitting the geometry of this binary embedding
017
              to the representational geometry of the original objects. After establishing
              general facts and providing some intuitions, we present two algorithms that
018
              work on low-rank or full-rank data, respectively. We assess their reliability
019
              on simulated data, and then use them to interpret neural word embeddings,
020           in which we expect a compositional structure.

023   1   Introduction
024
025   In biological and artificial learning systems, compositional structure is important to flexible
026   behavior, yet it is difficult to detect at the representational level. Neural representations
027
      are rarely factorized into purely-selective concept neurons; when there is a neat conceptual
      structure it is most often embedded into high-dimensional neural modes (Kaufman et al.,
028
      2022; Higgins et al., 2021; She et al., 2021; Bernardi et al., 2020; Courellis et al., 2024).
029
      Modern machine learning systems also use distributed continuous representations, which are
030   rarely factorized even when symbolic or compositional structure are explicitly incorporated
031   into the model (Altabaa et al., 2024; Rigotti et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2019).
032
033
      Identifying latent structure can enable compact descriptions of neural computation. This
      is true for biological systems (Bernardi et al., 2020; Courellis et al., 2024) and artificial.
034
      Huben et al. (2024) were recently able to use sparse autoencoders to find causally-relevant
035
      factors in language model representations. While the factors learned by such approaches
036   are continuous they are often analyzed discretely (is it active or not), which can introduce
037   “quantization errors” to the reconstruction and may be misleading, in that they will not
038   accurately reflect the true latent structure. For that reason, we see advantage in having
039   factors which are categorical by construction.
040
      In addition to interpreting fixed models, there might be some value to logical representa-
041
      tions when building a model. An early critique of connectionism was its inability to account
042   for systematic generalization (Fodor and Pylyshyn, 1988). Despite remarkable and unan-
043   ticipated advances, modern connectionist systems still struggle with abstract reasoning and
044   out-of-distribution generalization (Chollet, 2019; Mitchell et al., 2023; Moskvichev et al.,
045   2023; Anil et al., 2022). Being able to efficiently convert learned continuous representations
046   into a symbolic equivalent can help leverage advantages of both gradient-based and symbolic
047   computation (Mao et al., 2019; Koh et al., 2020).
048   We will show that the discovery of latent categories can be formulated as a binary matrix
049   factorization. Given a representation, X, we will try to find a binary representation, S,
050   which encodes an assignment of items to logical variables in such a way that preserves
051   distances. We refer to these logical variables as ‘concepts’. Many structures–including
052   analogies, clustering, hierarchy, ordering, and hybrids of these–can be captured by binary
053   concepts. However, binary matrix factorization is a difficult combinatorial problem, so, in
      addition to introducing it as a tool for concept discovery, we offer a new efficient algorithm.

      Prior work We are in many ways motivated by ongoing interest in symbolic capabilities
055   of continuous representations. There is a long tradition of neuro-symbolic paradigms which
056   do this explicitly; for example, tensor product representations (Smolensky et al., 2022), and
057   more recently transformer-inspired architectures (Altabaa et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2019).
058   Yet compositional and symbol-like vectors can appear in more standard connectionist ar-
059   chitectures (Zhou et al., 2024), and also in biology, where it is hidden by lack of mechanistic
060   understanding and requires bespoke analyses to discover. In such a case it could be very
061   useful to have a systematic way of making explicit underlying compositional structure.
062   The field of mechanistic interpretability offers many ideas and methods related to continuous
063   representations of categorical structure. Our formalism and method can be seen as opera-
064   tionalising the linear representation hypothesis (Park et al., 2024; 2023), by “unembedding”
065   a linear representation of categorical variables. In particular our use of orthogonal weights
066   fits nicely in that framework. A similar point of view has been taken by recent work on
067   the representation of sparse variables in language models (Elhage et al., 2022; Huben et al.,
068   2024), and we aim to enable a similar discovery process for categorical variables.
069   In computer science, what we seek has been called locality-sensitive hashing (Andoni and
070   Indyk, 2006). Salakhutdinov and Hinton (2009) proposed a binary latent variable model
071   for similarity-preserving hashing of documents, and Mena and Ñanculef (2019) tackled the
072   same problem with a variational autoencoder. At the level of the generative model these
073   approaches are very similar to ours, but with different algorithms and goals, as these methods
074   are often non-linear and do not always seek interpretable features.
075   In the community detection and applied math literature, our specific factorization problem
076   has been studied as (semi) binary matrix factorization (SBMF) or binary component decom-
077   position (BCD). Remarkably, in special cases an algebraic solution is available via tensor
078   decomposition (Sørensen et al., 2021), but it is highly sensitive to violations of its assump-
079   tions. There are several optimization-based approaches (Zhang et al., 2007; Kolomvakis
080   and Gillis, 2023; Sørensen et al., 2022) which are generally built around the assumption of
081   very low-rank data, and thus may not be applicable in the general case. Our specific model
082   formulation closely follows that of Kueng and Tropp (2021), and we substantially extend
083
      the scope of their model by fitting more general structures to to noisy data.
084
085
      Contributions We extend previous work on this topic both conceptually and practically.
      While others have formalised the representation of categorical structure in various ways,
086
      they often focus on specific kinds of structure and developed appropriately sophisticated
087
      formalisms. Framing the search for compositional structure as a simple matrix factorization
088   makes the general problem conceptually tractable, and can be used as a starting point for
089   more structured analyses. On the technical side, the literature on SBMF has restricted itself
090   to the case of identifiable factorizations, and we substantially extend the scope of that work
091   by applying mild regularization, and provide an overlooked connection to graph structure
092   as a visualisation tool. By bringing insights from the literature on SBMF to the field of
093   interpretability, we can offer a new perspective on an important and under-studied problem.
094   Practically speaking, we try to marry the advantages of the matrix factorization and au-
095   toencoder approaches, by providing a general heuristic algorithm without restrictive require-
096   ments on the data, which nevertheless can take advantage of the structure of the problem
097   for substantial gains in efficiency and ground truth recovery. Due to the straightforward
098   implementation, our algorithms can be more easily integrated into differentiable systems as
099   well. For instance, “concept bottleneck models” (Koh et al., 2020), in which the eponymous
100   concepts are normally hand-labeled. In general, we hope that the simplicity and effectiveness
101   of our approach can inspire deeper exploration of this problem.

      Graphical representation High-dimensional binary vectors can be hard to interpret,
189
      and we would like a visualization tool. Hierarchical clustering can be visualized with a
190   dendrogram, i.e. a tree on which observations are leaves and cluster assignments can be
191   recovered by cutting the tree at a certain depth. Just as we generalize hierarchical clustering,
192   we can generalize the dendrogram.
193
      Our goal will be to draw a graph in which (1) distances match those in S and (2) the concepts
194
      themselves can easily be recovered. Given a set of b concepts, S, we can isometrically embed
195
      each point (row of S) on a ‘partial cube’, i.e. an isometric subgraph of the b-bit hypercube.
196   In this representation (e.g. Fig.2, bottom row), nodes are connected by an edge when they
197   differ by one and only one concept (i.e. only one component of the concept vector differs).
198   Some nodes of the hypercube which do not correspond to observations might be necessary to
199   form the graph. Concepts can be read out by cutting (i.e. removing) all edges corresponding
200   to the same concept, and for visualization it is often easier to color the edges to know which
201   to cut (as we have done in Fig.2). By analogy to a dendrogram (which is itself a type of
202   partial cube) we will call this kind of graph an ‘analogram’.
203   Every partial cube has an easily-obtained unique binary representation (Deza and Laurent
204   (1997), and see the Appendix A.3 for explicit construction), but there is a combinatorial
205   number of graphs whose binary representation is S. The analogram should be the smallest
206   such graph. Unfortunately, as often the case in this problem, finding such an analogram is
207   NP-hard and it needn’t even be unique (Knauer and Nisse, 2019). Nevertheless, we made a
208   heuristic algorithm that works well on moderately-sized S, and always on trees (see A.3).

238   2.1.1    Regularization
239
240   There could be an astronomical number of optima for a given geometry, so we need an
241
      inductive bias to break the tie. We would like to select for the simplest structure that fits
      the data. Using the minimal number of concepts might seem like a reasonable choice, but
242
      this will favor dense concepts which can lead to more complex relationships between them
243
      (A.2). Instead we should try to minimize the size of the analogram (Section 2). Since that
244   is very hard to do directly, we notice that the simpler graphs tend to have sparser concepts,
245   and propose regularizing for sparsity.
246
247
      To encourage sparsity, we add linear (in S) term to the loss: 1T S1. This does not guarantee
      identifiability2 , but, intuitively, there are fewer sparse concepts than dense ones, and so
248
      we try to reduce the space of solutions by asking for the sparsest concepts. Sparsity is a
249
      common requirement for solving otherwise ill-posed inverse problems (Donoho, 2006) and,
250   as an inductive bias, has also been found to parsimoniously explain structure learning in
251   humans (Lake et al., 2018; Kemp and Tenenbaum, 2008).

      3     Optimization
255
      Being a challenging combinatorial problem, we cannot expect efficient solutions that work
256
      in every situation. There are already remarkably effective approaches for very low-rank
257
      data, but they do not always fail gracefully when their assumptions are violated. Here, we
258   develop an algorithm which can exploit low-rank structure when it exists, and another to
259   handle the general case. Note that both algorithms can in principle work on any positive
260   semidefinite matrix, and are thus quite general.
261
262   3.1     Rejection sampling for low-rank data
263
264   When the dimensionality of the data is sufficiently small, Kueng and Tropp (2021) and
265   Kolomvakis and Gillis (2023) provide efficient algorithms based on randomly sampling
266   columns of S. These methods are based on the following observation: If the data ad-
267
      mits a factorization of the form X̄ = S̄WT , and W ∈ Rn×b has full column rank, then,

271   these algorithms randomly search for such vectors s ∈ im(X̄) ∩ {0, 1}p . When the rank, r,

      The aforementioned algorithms are able to search efficiently by assuming that the s vectors
275
      are ‘Schur-independent’. This means that the set of bitwise exclusive-or vectors of all pairs
276
      of concept vectors, {1} ∪ {sα ⊕ sβ }bα,β=1 , is linearly independent. In that case, because of
277
      the special structure of the set of correlation matrices (Laurent and Poljak, 1996; Kueng and
278
      Tropp, 2021), each s can be found via semidefinite programming (SDP). But in the general
279   case, when the rank is still low but not low enough, the SDP is not guaranteed to have a
280   rank-one optimum, and would require a heuristic rounding step. In such a situation it might
281   not be worth the computational burden of solving an SDP, which has O(p2 ) variables, and
282   so we propose a method based on Hopfield networks.
283
      In the presence of noise, the true concept vectors might not be exactly in the image of
284
      X̄, but just the closest among nearby binary vectors. If U are the right singular vectors
285   (with non-zero singular values) of X, then we expect the true concept vectors, s, to be
286   local maxima of E(s) = sT UUT s. This is precisely (the negative of) the energy function
287   of a Hopfield network (Hopfield, 1982), which we can maximize by iteratively updating
288   st ← sign(UUT st−1 ) from some initial guess.

385   as before. The hybrid structure is just the concatenation of randomly-sampled concepts
386   and the hierarchical ones. We use the iterative refinement algorithm with an exponential
387   annealing schedule (T = γ t T0 , with γ = 0.8 and T0 = 5) run for 100 iterations. We note
388   that the run time of the algorithm is depends on the sparsity of the discovered solution,
389   since we use sparse matrices, but is roughly the same for both kinds of data.
390   In comparison with gradient-based autoencoders, the Hopfield approach does better at re-
391   covery and geometry matching at all noise levels. All models struggle with the hierarchical
392   concepts, due to the highly non-identifiable nature of the problem, but ours manages to do
393   better over a wider range (Fig. 3b). The hybrid geometry is most challenging for all models
394   (Fig. 3c), presumably as they struggle to find the unique Schur-independent components
395   without the benefit of the projection steps of Algorithm 1. We note in the case of the
396   tree-structured data that hamming distance may be deceptive, since the concepts are very
397
      sparse and so any other sparse concept will be close to it; hence we also show the CKA. In
      terms of time, we are comparable but slightly faster with the other autoencoders.

400   4   Application to neural word embeddings
401
402   Finally, we will see how this works on a simple example from word2vec (Mikolov et al.,
403   2013), a well-known word embedding. The embedding is generated by predicting word-
404   context pairs, in which contexts are the neighboring words in the Google News corpus. The
405   embeddings can themselves be seen as a matrix factorization (Levy and Goldberg, 2014).
406   What earned word2vec its notoriety is the fact that certain abstract concepts are encoded
407   along parallel dimensions–exactly the kind of structure that we are looking for.
408   Since word2vec has embeddings for several million words, we need to select a subset to
409   study. WordNet (Fellbaum, 1998) contains manually-encoded ‘is-a’ relations (i.e. ‘dog’
410   is-a ‘canine’) over a large vocabulary, and we used it to generate a list of words that are
411   descendants of ‘person’. This comes out to 3841 words. With around 4000 points in 300
412   dimensions, this has an intermediate dimensionality, and thus is a good use case for the
413   hybrid sampling and refinement algorithm. Doing this we achieve a CKA of around 0.94
414
      (Fig. 4a) using 993 concepts. The model splits into sparse and dense concepts (Fig.4b),
      which is indicative of an underlying low-dimensional structure with some high-dimensional
415
      deviations. It is the low-dimensional variables we will focus on interpreting.

521   5   Discussion
522
523
524   Here we studied the problem of turning a continuous representation into a logical one.
525
      We provided two simple algorithms with complementary use cases and demonstrate their
      efficacy. When dealing with very low-rank data, we leveraged results from previous work
526
      to develop a very fast and fairly robust method based rejection sampling. For when the
527
      data is higher-dimensional, we use a coordinate descent approach to refine the solution.
528   Both algorithms enjoy convergence guarantees by connection to Hopfield networks, and thus
529   represent a new application of that neuroscience model to optimization (Hopfield and Tank,
530   1985). We show favorable results on synthetic data when compared to gradient descent, and
531   demonstrate several secondary analyses on word embeddings.
532
      To the extent that we, humans, are engaged in concept discovery, our work here could also
533
      provide a minimalistic model of that cognitive process. We are not modeling the difficult
534   process of discovering concepts from the real world: our model is linear, and therefore as-
535   sumes a representation which already, at least approximately, encodes the relevant concepts.
536   Instead, we model the process of turning a distributed, noisy code into abstract symbols
537   and structures (Kemp and Tenenbaum, 2008). At some point, distributed activity in the
538   brain is turned into discrete words, which is all that is heard by others. Future work can
539   address how the inherent challenges of unsupervised abstraction, which we laid out, might
      be dealt with by more sophisticated algorithms with different inductive biases.

      A.1   Connections to PCA and clustering
695
696   Like many matrix factorizations, our method has an aesthetic similarity to the eigenvalue
697   decomposition and PCA. In fact, when the concepts are uncorrelated with each other, i.e.
698   they are orthogonal after subtracting the means, then it is actually equivalent to PCA.
699   However–the rarity of this situation aside–PCA is not likely to find binary features when
700   there is approximate rotation-invariance (geometric multiplicity) to the solution. Further-
701   more, when there are multiple factorizations, the sparsest one will have correlated features
      in general and thus not be recoverable.

      A.3        Partial cubes
814
815   Here is the sketch of the algorithm for recovering concepts from a partial cube graph: Start
816   with an edge e = (x, y), partition5 the vertices into those which are closer to x (call them
817   Vx ) or closer to y (call them Vy ). Each Vx node gets a 0 label, each Vy node gets a 1. Pick
818   another edge, ignoring from now on any edges which cross the partition, and repeat the
819   process. Here is an illustration of the process for a hexagon graph, coloring edged according
820   to the partitions:
821
822
823                   0                                01                                  011
824   Vx                                                                     Vy
                                Vy                                   Vy

826                                                                                                 e

832   This algorithm is described in several places, but we used chapter 19 of Deza and Laurent
833   (1997).
834   To go the other way around is hard, as mentioned in the main text. Our heuristic algorithm
835   is based on two components: a global rule for finding hidden nodes, and a local rule for
836   placing edges.
837
      The global rule finds ‘necessary’ hidden nodes by taking the intersection of all differences
838
      between items. We will assume concepts are centered around the first item, such that the
839   first row is all 0. We then take the intersection of all rows. For example, if items 1 and 2
840   differ by concepts {a, b, c}, while items 1 and 3 differ by {a, b, d}, then we will include the
841   hidden node with concepts {a, b}. This is then repeated after re-centering around all other
842   items.
843
      The local rule is used to add edges to the graph, and ensure it is connected. It is a rule for
844
      which bits to add from a given node. Let’s again assume we are centered around item 1.
845   Construct a ‘disjoint’ matrix between concepts, which is equal to 1 only if sTα sβ = 0. Then
846
      construct a ‘superset’ matrix, which is 1 only if sTα sβ = 1T sα . The rule is that, at a given
847   node i, with concepts Si , we are allowed to add any concepts which (1) are not disjoint
848   with any concepts of i and (2) have all their supersets active in i. For example, if I has the
849   concepts (‘dog’, ‘big’), then we could add ‘great dane’ but not ‘pug’, and not ‘cat’.
850
      By using the global rule for placing hidden nodes, and the local rule for laying down paths
851
      between all nodes, we can make a graph that is sometimes of manageable size. It works much
852
      better for sparser graphs like trees, and so a more picky method is going to be necessary
853   moving forward.
