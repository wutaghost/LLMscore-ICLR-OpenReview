010
011
                   The benchmark performance of large language models (LLMs) has a high im-
012                pact on their popularity and is thus of great importance to many model providers.
013                However, the reliability of such benchmark scores as a measure of model quality
014                gets compromised if the model is contaminated with benchmark data. While re-
015                cent contamination detection methods try to address this issue, they overlook the
016                possibility of deliberate contamination by malicious model providers aiming to
017                evade detection. We propose a categorization of model providers based on their
018                (de)contamination practices and argue that malicious contamination is of crucial
019                importance as it casts doubt on the reliability of public benchmarks. To study this
020
                   issue more rigorously, we analyze current contamination detection methods based
                   on their assumptions. This analysis reveals a significant vulnerability in existing
021
                   approaches: they do not account for rephrased benchmark data used during train-
022
                   ing by malicious actors. We demonstrate how exploiting this gap can result in
023                significantly inflated benchmark scores while completely evading current detec-
024                tion methods. 1

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The recent popularity of large language models (LLMs) and their applicability to a wide range of
030   tasks has led to significant investments in the field, with many companies competing to train the
031   best model (Anil et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Accurately
032
      assessing the quality of these models is thus not only crucial to tracking progress in the field and
      choosing the correct model for a specific task but also has significant economic implications. As a
033
      result, many high-quality benchmarks have been developed for a wide range of tasks (Clark et al.,
034
      2018; Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022).
035
036
      Contamination Detection These benchmarks are typically made public to allow evaluation of
037   new models. However, as LLMs are often trained on scraped web data, benchmark samples may in-
038   advertently become part of the training dataset. This data contamination can lead to inflated bench-
039   mark performance and inaccurate evaluation results. To alleviate this issue, both model providers
040   (Anil et al., 2023a; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) and third parties (Golchin and Surdeanu,
041   2023a; Oren et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023) developed methods to detect and quantify the influence of
042   data contamination on model performance.
043
044   Malicious Actors However, high competitive pressure and significant financial stakes could in-
045   centivize malicious actors to actively contaminate their model to increase benchmark performance
046
      while evading detection. Crucially, current contamination detection methods do not account for such
      malicious behavior, which presents a significant oversight.
047
048
      This Work: Evading Detection We demonstrate that all current detection methods can be evaded
049
      by training on rephrased benchmark samples (see Fig. 2) while still boosting performance signifi-
050
      cantly. This endangers the integrity of current benchmarks and highlights the need for a systematic
051   study of contamination detection in the malicious setting. Furthermore, the simplicity of this ap-
052   proach indicates that malicious actors might already be exploiting this gap in real-world scenarios.

103   2      DATA C ONTAMINATION
104
105   Before systematizing (de-)contamination practices and detection methods, we first need to formally
106   define data contamination. We consider a (training) dataset D to be contaminated with some bench-
107   mark D′ if there is an overlap between the two. We call models trained on a contaminated dataset
      contaminated models.


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      From a detection perspective, it is helpful to differentiate between sample- and benchmark-level data
109   contamination. Sample-level contamination detection aims to determine whether a given sample x
110   was contained in the training data D. In contrast, benchmark-level detection aims to determine
111   whether any subset of a benchmark D′ was contained in D without specifically aiming to provide
112   this subset. More formally, we define sample- and benchmark-level contamination as follows:
113   Definition 1 (Sample-level Data Contamination). A dataset D is contaminated with a sample x from
114   a benchmark D′ if x ∈ D.
115
      Definition 2 (Benchmark-level Data Contamination). A dataset D is contaminated with a bench-
116   mark D′ if D′ ∩ D ≠ ∅.
117
118   Sample-level detection methods provide fine-grained information on the amount of contamination
119   in a dataset. By excluding the detected samples, model providers can present evaluation results on a
120   clean subset of the benchmark (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). However, as detection er-
121   rors can significantly influence evaluation results and partial contamination can impact performance
122
      on the uncontaminated benchmark portion, it is questionable whether these results are comparable
      to an uncontaminated model.
123
124   Benchmark-level contamination is particularly relevant to a benchmark’s integrity as a performance
125   metric. If a model has been contaminated with a benchmark, its results are not comparable to those of
126   an uncontaminated model. However, benchmark-level methods do not specify which samples were
127   contaminated, making it challenging to assess the contamination’s effect on model performance.
128   The use of a contaminated dataset for model training does not necessarily have an influence on
129   the model’s measured performance. From an adversarial perspective, this form of contamination is
130   highly uninteresting. We therefore define problematic data contamination as data contamination that
131   leads to an increase in performance on a benchmark. More formally, we define:
132   Definition 3 (Problematic Data Contamination). A dataset D is problematically contaminated with
133   a benchmark D′ if D is contaminated with D′ according to Definition 2 and a model trained on D
134   obtains a higher performance on D′ than a model trained using the same training method on D \ D′ .
135
      For the rest of this paper, we focus on problematic data contamination.

138   3     M ODEL P ROVIDERS AND DATA C ONTAMINATION
139
140   Considering the significant effect benchmark contamination can have on a model’s measured perfor-
141   mance (Brown et al., 2020; Sainz et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) and the high
142   stakes involved in training LLMs, there are strong incentives for model providers to not fully decon-
143
      taminate their models. Therefore, it is essential to consider the possibility of negligent or malicious
      behavior when studying contamination detection.
144
145   To facilitate a more nuanced study, we define four model provider archetypes, differentiating
146   between active contamination (either open or covert), active decontamination, and honest-but-
147   negligent indifference.
148
149   3.1   ACTOR A RCHETYPES
150
      We distinguish model providers or actors based on the actions they take (or neglect to take) to prevent
151
      (or cause) data contamination, leading to the following definitions:
152
153   Definition 4 (Actor Roles). A malicious actor actively contaminates a model by deliberately using
154
      benchmark data during training with the goal of artificially increasing benchmark performance.
155   An honest-but-negligent actor possibly contaminates a model by not taking sufficient measures to
156   prevent data contamination but does not actively contaminate the model.
157
      A proactive actor actively decontaminates a model by taking sufficient measures to guard against
158   contamination, ensuring representative performance on a benchmark.
159
160   The line between these types can be blurry. For instance, an actor taking reasonable but incomplete
161   decontamination measures can fall between proactive and honest-but-negligent. However, a more
      fine-grained distinction is not necessary in the context of this paper.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Evasiveness As a malicious actor might try to evade contamination detection, it is crucial to dis-
163   tinguish between openly malicious and evasively malicious actors:
164
165
      Definition 5 (Evasiveness). We distinguish between openly malicious and evasively malicious ac-
      tors depending on whether they actively try to hide the use of benchmark data by modifying the
166
      training or data preprocessing procedure with the goal of evading contamination detection.
167
168   This distinction is particularly important when evaluating contamination detection methods as prior
169   works (Carlini et al., 2021; Mireshghallah et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Yeom et al., 2018) fail
170   completely in the evasively malicious setting (see Fig. 2). In real-world scenarios, a malicious actor
171   is likely to use evasion strategies to avoid detection. Unfortunately, the current literature does not
172
      consider this possibility and solely focuses on openly malicious actors, making their applicability
      in real-world scenarios questionable. Before discussing these detection methods, we review current
173
      data decontamination practices among model providers.
174
175   3.2   C URRENT D ECONTAMINATION P RACTICES
176
177   As prior work (Li and Flanigan, 2023; Sainz et al., 2023) found indications of widespread data
178
      contamination in popular models, we review the decontamination practices reported in the corre-
      sponding publications. Specifically, Sainz et al. (2023) found that common training datasets are
179
      heavily contaminated with current benchmarks and Li and Flanigan (2023) found that most models
180
      perform significantly better on benchmarks released before the model.
181
182   Most model and dataset providers do not describe any active decontamination measures (Abdin
183   et al., 2024; Almazrouei et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023a;b; Anthropic, 2024a;b; Chowdhery et al.,
184
      2022; Computer, 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Mehta et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023;
      Penedo et al., 2023; Young et al., 2024), likely placing them in the honest-but-negligent category.
185
      However, several providers do report deduplication protocols (Anil et al., 2023b; Brown et al., 2020;
186
      Computer, 2023; Penedo et al., 2023) which are believed to increase performance (Lee et al., 2022).
187
188   A post-hoc contamination analysis, which involves evaluating models only on the uncontaminated
189   portion of a benchmark, is more common (Anil et al., 2023a; Brown et al., 2020; Dubey et al., 2024;
190
      OpenAI, 2023). However, as we will show in §6, this is insufficient, since partial contamination
      can still significantly improve performance on the uncontaminated portion of the benchmark (see
191
      e.g. Table 1). Furthermore, this post-hoc analysis is typically not reproducible as neither training
192
      datasets nor indices of the evaluated test set portions are made available (Anil et al., 2023a; OpenAI,
193   2023; Dubey et al., 2024). Combined, this makes it exceedingly difficult to meaningfully compare
194   models even among honest-but-negligent actors.
195
196
      While proactive decontamination is essential to ensure fair and meaningful model comparisons, we
      only found descriptions of such measures in Brown et al. (2020); Chowdhery et al. (2022); Yang
197
      et al. (2024) among the works we reviewed. Brown et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2024) perform the
198
      overlap check a-priori, removing all benchmark samples from the training set. Chowdhery et al.
199   (2022) only describes filtering for a canary string included in the BigBench benchmark (Srivastava
200   et al., 2022), which is a unique string that should be in all documents containing the dataset.
201
202   4     D ETECTING DATA C ONTAMINATION
203
204   While sample-level contamination detection is well-studied under the name membership inference
205   attack (MIA) in privacy research (Carlini et al., 2021; Shokri et al., 2017; Song and Shmatikov,
206
      2019), benchmark-level detection has only been investigated recently (Golchin and Surdeanu,
      2023a; Oren et al., 2023). Interestingly, transferring methods from the sample- to the benchmark-
207
      level setting is not trivial, since false positives can result in noisy signals.
208
209   To facilitate a rigorous discussion of contamination detection methods, we review current methods
210   based on their assumptions and introduce several dimensions for categorization, referring to App. B
211   for a full overview. We will leverage this analysis to identify a critical issue that can be easily
212
      exploited by malicious actors in §5.
213   4.1   D ETECTOR A SSUMPTIONS
214
215   Access MIAs typically consider three levels of access to the model: black-box, grey-box, and
      white-box. Black-box access allows access to the model’s predictions only, grey-box access also


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      includes the predicted confidences, and white-box access provides full access to model weights and
217   parameters. In the context of data contamination, some methods additionally require access to all
218   training data. We call this fourth level oracle access. While traditional MIAs become trivial in this
219   setting, the huge training sets of LLMs make detection challenging even with this level of access.
220
      Black-box methods often work by comparing the model’s performance on a benchmark to the per-
221
      formance on other data (Huang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) or check for verbatim memorization of
222
      sample text (Golchin and Surdeanu, 2023a). Black-box methods are especially relevant for models
223   that are only available through an API (Anil et al., 2023a; OpenAI, 2023).
224
225   Grey-box methods (Mattern et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023) leverage the model’s perplexity or certainty
226
      on a given sample and often perform better than black-box methods. They are applicable to models
      with more extensive API access and all open-weight models. We are not aware of any white-box
227
      access methods, although they would also be applicable to all open-weight models.
228
229   Oracle access methods (Yang et al., 2023) are the most powerful but can only be used by the model
230   providers themselves, as training data is generally not published. These methods are based on sim-
231   ilarity checks between training and benchmark data and can be used to check for contamination
232
      before or after model training (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023).
233
234   Metadata Metadata refers to all information related to a benchmark that is not part of the actual
235   samples. This includes the dataset name (Golchin and Surdeanu, 2023a;b) and a canonical ordering
236   of samples (Oren et al., 2023). If such metadata has been learned, this is a strong indication of
237
      contamination. However, metadata contamination is a strong assumption. Not only can malicious
      model providers simply remove the metadata, but benign dataset shuffling will remove the canon-
238
      ical ordering of samples and limited context length makes the association of dataset names with
239
      individual samples unlikely.
240
241
      Reference Models Many methods require access to uncontaminated reference models
242
      (Mireshghallah et al., 2022; Song and Shmatikov, 2019; Watson et al., 2022). However, Mattern
243
      et al. (2023) shows that reference-based methods are highly sensitive to the reference model. Fur-
244   thermore, obtaining uncontaminated but comparable reference models is often not feasible.
245
246   Several methods do not explicitly require a reference model but do require a threshold on a contam-
247
      ination score to decide when a sample should be considered contaminated (Carlini et al., 2021; Li,
      2023a; Shi et al., 2023). This makes it difficult to apply these methods without a reference model.
248
      We call these methods threshold-based.
249
250
      Semantics Preserving Transformations Data contamination not only occurs when including un-
251
      modified benchmark data in the training set, but also when including semantically equivalent sam-
252   ples. However, most contamination detection methods assume that benchmark data is included
253   verbatim in the training data, or only allow for minimal perturbations such as extra newlines. While
254   this is a fair assumption for the pretraining stage, even honest-but-negligent actors might use data
255   augmentation techniques such as back-translation (Edunov et al., 2018) or paraphrasing (Li et al.,
256   2018) during finetuning. To address this issue, Yang et al. (2023) propose to use an LLM to de-
257   tect rephrased samples in the training data when given oracle access. Shi et al. (2023) propose a
258   perplexity-based grey-box method which they observe to be robust under some paraphrasing, al-
259   though it fails in our experiments (see Fig. 2).
260
261   5   E VADING C ONTAMINATION D ETECTION
262
      We build on our analysis in §4 to characterize the requirements for successful evasion of data con-
263
      tamination detection. In particular, such a strategy should allow a malicious actor to significantly
264
      increase model performance on a benchmark while evading detection. Based on these requirements,
265   we propose a simple technique to effectively evade contamination detection.
266
267
      Requirements     We identify the following requirements for a successful evasion strategy:
268
269          • Access: The strategy should be effective against all access levels, but can differ between
               assumed levels.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
             • Metadata: The strategy should remove all metadata to make the corresponding detection
271            methods categorically ineffective.
272
             • Reference Models: Despite reference models often being unavailable, a strong evasion
273
               strategy should be effective against reference- and threshold-based detection methods.
274
275          • Semantics Preserving Transformations: The strategy can alter the training data as long as
276
               it still enhances benchmark performance.
277
278   Finetuning vs Pretraining Introducing data contamination during finetuning, rather than pretrain-
279   ing, is more advantageous for several reasons. First, optimizing only the conditional probability of
280
      the answer given the question prevents the model from memorizing the question, making detection
      much harder. Second, since the model sees less data after contamination, there is a lower chance of
281
      unlearning memorized samples, making performance gains more likely. Third, finetuning is signifi-
282
      cantly cheaper than pretraining, making it easier to implement and evaluate evasion strategies.
283
284
285   5.1   E VASION T HROUGH R EPHRASING
286
      As discussed in §4, most detection methods assume that the model is directly trained on the con-
287
      taminated data, ignoring possible preprocessing contamination. This is a critical oversight that is
288   trivial to exploit by rephrasing the benchmark data during preprocessing. We rephrase the bench-
289   mark data using GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and finetune a pretrained model on a mix of background
290   data unrelated to the benchmark and the rephrased benchmark data. Since we apply the strategy in
291   the finetuning setting, metadata is naturally removed during the preprocessing stage. The rephrasing
292   strategy varies based on the detection method’s access level, with particular caution required for
293   oracle access methods. We discuss and evaluate our evasion strategy for white-box access here, and
294   defer the discussion of our evasion strategy for oracle access to App. C.
295
296   White-Box Access As most black-, grey-, and white-box methods are based on the model repro-
297   ducing benchmark data verbatim or assigning unusual perplexity, we expect them to be sensitive to
298   semantics preserving rephrasing. Interestingly, rephrasing can also occur in the honest-but-negligent
299   setting when rephrased samples are collected for the pre-training or finetuning data, or synthetically
300
      generated by a contaminated third-party language model. We use GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) to rephrase
      benchmark data using dataset-specific prompts provided in Fig. 3 of App. E.

347
348
      We report the performance of all finetuned models on the contaminated and uncontaminated half
      of the benchmark in Table 1. In the openly malicious setting, performance substantially increases
349
      across all benchmarks, improving the average accuracy on contaminated samples by 36% for five
350
      occurrences of the contaminated portion of the benchmark. Even on the uncontaminated samples,
351   performance increases by 9% for five occurrences. This highlights that the common practice of
352   honest-but-negligent actors to measure performance on a clean subset of the data (OpenAI, 2023;
353   Touvron et al., 2023) can still lead to artificially inflated scores and is insufficient to obtain a repre-
354   sentative performance estimate.
355
      Performance improvement in the evasively malicious setting, while less pronounced, is still signif-
356
      icant. Concretely, we observe an average gain of 10% and 13% on the contaminated samples for
357   one and five occurrences, respectively, reduced to 7% on the uncontaminated samples. Thus, we
358   conclude that while not as effective as finetuning on the original samples, finetuning on rephrased
359   samples can significantly increase model performance both on the contaminated and uncontaminated
360   portion of the test set and must therefore also be considered data contamination.

362   6.3   S AMPLE -L EVEL D ETECTION M ETHODS
363
364   We evaluate several sample-level detection methods and present results in Table 2 for L LAMA -3.1-
365   8B. In App. B we argue why it is sufficient to evaluate against these methods to claim evasion of
366   all current detection methods by using our analysis in §4. While these methods show acceptable
367
      performance for openly malicious actors, they fail completely when using an evasive strategy for
      evasively malicious actors. We report similar results for all other models in App. F.
368
369
      Detection Methods We include seven sample-level detection methods that do not require oracle
370
      access or metadata contamination. Specifically, we consider the method proposed by Yeom et al.
371
      (2018), which measures the perplexity of a sample as a measure of memorization. We also include
372   a method that instead measures the perplexity on the least likely k% of tokens (Shi et al., 2023) and
373   various variants of this method (Zhang et al., 2024c; Zhang and Wu, 2024). Mireshghallah et al.
374   (2022) computes a score for each sample by comparing the loss of the contaminated model to the
375   loss of a reference model. Furthermore, we include a method by Carlini et al. (2021) that compares

411
412   We now evaluate benchmark-level detection methods, noting that most require metadata contamina-
413
      tion (Golchin and Surdeanu, 2023a;b; Oren et al., 2023), which is absent in the finetuning setting.
      Therefore, we evaluate the only two methods that do not require metadata contamination: DPCC
414
      (Shi, 2023) and CleanEval (Zhu et al., 2023).
415
416
      DPCC To detect contamination, Shi (2023) first generates several perturbations of a benchmark
417
      sample using a reference model and then measures the perplexity of the perturbed samples with the
418   model under consideration. A contamination score is then computed based on the ratio of perturbed
419   samples that have a higher perplexity than the unperturbed sample. This method is generally popular
420   in the community and used on models related to the Open LLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023).
421
      We report results for L LAMA -3.1-8B in Table 3 and observe that the contamination score is highly
422
      benchmark-dependent, reaching values between 0.05 for ARC and 0.30 for TruthfulQA in the ab-
423
      sence of contamination. More importantly, the contamination threshold set by Shi (2023), 0.85,
424   does not flag any model, making the method ineffective for benchmark-level contamination detec-
425   tion. However, we note a significant increase in contamination scores for GSM8k in the openly
426   malicious setting, making detection possible for a lower threshold. In contrast, the scores in the
427   evasively malicious setting on GSM8k are comparable to those of the uncontaminated model. The
428   contamination scores for MMLU and ARC are small across all models, making detection unfeasible
429   for these benchmarks. We obtain similar results for all other models and report them in App. F.
430
431   CleanEval CleanEval (Zhu et al., 2023) evaluates a model on a rephrased version of the bench-
      mark to obtain an accurate comparison between contaminated and uncontaminated models. As the

      7   R ELATED W ORK
442
      The current practice among model providers to train language models in an honest-but-negligent
443   fashion, combined with the risk of malicious actors actively contaminating models to achieve top
444   benchmark performance, can make traditional benchmarks an unreliable indicator of model quality.
445   We discuss several alternatives that circumvent the issues associated with static benchmarks, while
446   still allowing for a comprehensive and reliable evaluation of the models.
447
448   Dynamic Benchmarks The static nature of traditional benchmarks is what allows both honest-
449   but-negligent and malicious contamination to occur. Therefore, a recent line of work (Huang et al.,
450   2023; Jain et al., 2024; Li and Flanigan, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Roberts et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023;
451   Zhang et al., 2024b) has focused on evaluation using dynamic benchmarks. Specifically, as dynamic
452   benchmarks are periodically updated, they allow measuring model performance on data that was not
453
      available during training. Further, these benchmarks can compare performance on data collected
      before and after a model release and thus provide a simple way to measure contamination.
454
455   However, their dynamic nature comes with significant challenges: First, high-quality benchmarks
456   take considerable time and effort to create, leading to most dynamic benchmarks being less well-
457   curated than traditional benchmarks. Second, performance on dynamic benchmarks can vary over
458   time, making it harder to track progress and compare models. Especially the possibility of new
459
      models training on prior versions of the benchmark can lead to a false sense of progress. Third,
      continued effort is required to ensure that the benchmark is continuously updated.
460
461
      Human Evaluation Human evaluations allow for comprehensive model evaluation with limited
462
      risk of contamination over a wide range of tasks (Chang et al., 2023; Freitag et al., 2021; Zheng et al.,
463   2023). However, it is both time-consuming and expensive, requiring a large number of expert evalu-
464   ators and a good experimental setup to prevent human biases from influencing results (Chang et al.,
465   2023; Zheng et al., 2023). Further, human preferences can differ between individuals, and groups
466   (Peng et al., 1997). While crowd-sourcing initiatives like Zheng et al. (2023) can help mitigate these
467   issues, they are vulnerable to attacks by malicious actors aiming to boost their ratings.
468
469   Private Benchmarks Benchmark contamination can be avoided by preventing model providers
470   from accessing the benchmark data. These private benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2024a) would prevent
471   model providers from accidentally or maliciously including the benchmark in their training data and
472   therefore provide the possibility for reliable model evaluation. This approach would need careful
473
      consideration and continuous monitoring since any data leakage would effectively negate the ben-
      efits. Furthermore, evaluation cannot be performed by the model provider, as this would inevitably
474
      leak the benchmark data. This poses a significant challenge for closed-source models, which do not
475
      share any model specifics (Anil et al., 2023a; OpenAI, 2023) and therefore need provable guarantees
476   that these specifics do not get leaked.
477
478
      8   L IMITATIONS
479
480   While simple and effective, we note that there are several limitations to contamination through
481   rephrasing. First, it does not yield the same performance improvement as training on the actual
482   benchmark samples, indicating that there is still potential to improve the attack. Second, the attack
483   is limited to public benchmarks. As discussed in §7, there are other types of benchmarks that we
484   cannot attack due to the lack of available public data for training purposes. This limitation raises
485   an important question about the security of these paradigms against malicious model providers. We
      believe this question offers a promising direction for future research.

      9    C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this work, we discussed the importance of considering malicious model providers that actively
489   contaminate their models to achieve artificially high performance on specific benchmarks. Our anal-
490   ysis of contamination detection methods, focusing on their fundamental assumptions, revealed crit-
491   ical shortcomings when confronted with evasively malicious actors. We exploit these shortcomings
492   with a simple contamination technique that evades detection by rephrasing samples while increasing
493   performance on public benchmarks by up to 15%.
494
495   10    E THICS S TATEMENT
496
497
      Public benchmarks are essential for evaluating the performance of language models. Our work
498   demonstrates the potential for malicious actors to actively contaminate the training data while evad-
499   ing detection, highlighting a significant security concern. By discussing the possibility of malicious
500   actors, we aim to raise awareness about the issue and encourage the development of more robust
501   evaluation methods. More importantly, since the vulnerability discussed in this work is very simple
502   and the stakes in this competitive field are very high, we believe it is possible that malicious actors
503   are already exploiting similar vulnerabilities. However, there is a risk that our findings are exploited,
504   further compromising the reliability of public benchmarks. Despite these concerns, we believe that
505   publishing our results is beneficial, as the worst-case scenario is the adoption of suboptimal models
506
      for specific tasks.

870   B    A SSUMPTION FOR D ETECTION M ETHODS
871
872   We present a table with an overview of the discussed assumptions on data contamination detection
873
      from §4 in Table 5. We no argue why the inclusion of the methods in our experiments is sufficient
      to claim evasion of all contamination detection methods.
874
875   Based on our analysis presented in §4, we can exclude the following methods from this table for our
876   evaluation:
877
878
            • Methods requiring oracle access: Techniques that require oracle-level access are ad-
              dressed separately in App. C, where we demonstrate that our evasion strategy works ef-
879
              fectively even with oracle-level attacks. For the attack evaluated in the main part of the
880
              paper, we can exclude them.
881
882
            • Metadata-dependent detection methods: Methods relying on metadata are irrelevant be-
              cause we explicitly remove metadata from contaminated samples. This ensures that detec-
883
              tion mechanisms dependent on metadata are not applicable to our approach.
884
885         • Methods analyzing contamination with no novel method: Li (2023b); Xu et al. (2024);
886
              Carlini et al. (2022) primarily offer analyses or conceptual discussions rather than detection
              strategies. As such, they do not introduce a new method that needs to be evaluated in our
887
              experiments.
888
889         • Duplicate techniques: Some works duplicate existing detection methods already included
890
              in our evaluation. For example, Watson et al. (2022) is a reimplementation of (Mireshghal-
              lah et al., 2022), and (Li, 2023a) is functionally equivalent to (Yeom et al., 2018).
891
892         • Wrong-Option Contamination: Deng et al. (2023) requires the inclusion of incorrect op-
893           tions of a benchmark in the training set, which we explicitly remove during the rephrasing
894
              process. In some sense, wrong options are a form of metadata.
895         • User-Level Contamination Methods: Song and Shmatikov (2019) address user-level con-
896           tamination in a privacy-based use-case. Therefore, they do not apply to the contamination
897           scenario studied in this work.
898
      After excluding the above methods, we remain with the methods that we evaluate in our experiments.
899
      Our results in §6 demonstrate that our attack effectively circumvents these methods by reducing their
900   performance to random chance.

      C     E VASION FOR O RACLE ACCESS
956
957
      We discuss how we can adjust our evasion strategy to evade oracle access detection and evaluate its
      .
958
959
960
      Evasion for Oracle Access The default rephrasing we use is frequently unable to evade oracle
      access methods. For example, Yang et al. (2023) explicitly ask a language model if a sample from the
961
      training data is a rephrased version of a benchmark sample. While this technique requires effective
962
      prefiltering to become computationally feasible for large datasets, we can still evade it by more
963   aggressively rephrasing the benchmark samples. To this end, we iteratively rephrase a sample and
964   request GPT-4 to verify if it has been unrecognizably rephrased, guiding it towards more significant
965   rephrasing each time. We find that even a few iterations of this are highly effective at evading oracle
966   access methods while still significantly increasing performance. Further, we note that we can drop
967   all samples that are still detected from the training data.

1044
       We describe the experimental details of our experiments performed in §6. Specifically, we discuss
1045   the prompts, finetuning parameters, and preprocessing steps used for each step. Each finetuned
1046   model took around two hours on a single Nvidia H100 GPU. All experiments were performed using
1047   around six weeks of computation on a single Nvidia H100 GPU.
1048
1049
       Benchmarks For each benchmark, we only select the test data for evaluation. Furthermore, for the
1050
       MMLU benchmark, we only select samples from the alphabetically first seven domains, to ensure
1051   that the benchmark is similar in size as the other benchmarks. Specifically, we select the abstract
1052   algebra, anatomy, astronomy, business ethics, clinical knowledge, college biology and
1053   college chemistry domains.
1054
1055
       Rephrasing We use GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) with a temperature of 0 as the model with which we
1056
       rephrase. This allows us to generate human-level quality rephrases. For each benchmark, we use a
1057
       slightly adapted system prompt to generate rephrases. All system prompts are presented in Fig. 3.
1058   The user input is formatted as follows:
1059

1067
       2023), wrong options are omitted for MMLU and ARC-Challenge.
1068   For oracle rephrasing, we continue from the rephrased question and answer and tell the model its
1069   rephrasing should be further adjusted. The prompts used to do so for each benchmark are presented
1070   in Fig. 4.
1071
1072   Data Preparation For each benchmark, we randomly select 50% of the samples that are used
1073   when training on benchmark data. Depending on the setting, we then either copy the (rephrased)
1074   benchmark data one or five times and pad the resulting training data with randomly selected samples
1075   from the OpenOrca instruction-tuning dataset (Lian et al., 2023) until there are 25000 samples in
1076   the dataset. We note that the randomly selected samples from OpenOrca are mostly the same for
1077   all settings, with the minor difference that fewer samples are selected when the benchmark data is
1078   copied five times. We format prompts using the Alpaca formatting convention (Taori et al., 2023).

       Specifically, we use the following format for each sample:
