                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Aligning pretrained language models (LMs) is a complex and resource-intensive
011            process, often requiring access to large amounts of ground-truth preference data and
012
               substantial compute. Are these costs necessary? That is, is it possible to align using
               only inherent model knowledge and without additional training? We tackle this
013
               challenge with A LIGN EZ, a novel approach that uses (1) self-generated preference
014
               data and (2) representation editing to provide nearly cost-free alignment. During
015            inference, A LIGN EZ modifies LM representations to reduce undesirable and boost
016            desirable components using subspaces identified via self-generated preference pairs.
017            Our experiments reveal that this nearly cost-free procedure significantly narrows the
018            gap between base pretrained and tuned models by an average of 29.1%, observed
019            across five datasets and two model architectures. Additionally, we explore the
020            potential of using A LIGN EZ as a means of expediting more expensive alignment
021            procedures. Our experiments show that A LIGN EZ improves DPO models tuned
022            only using a small subset of ground-truth preference data.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large language model (LMs) alignment involves the use of complex and expensive pipelines (Schul-
028
      man et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024). Usually at least two critical components
029
      are needed: (1) collecting human preference data, and (2) modifying pretrained model weights to
030   better align with these preferences. Some pipelines involve more complexity (e.g., RLHF trains a
031   reward model on the human preference data and uses it for PPO-based model optimization). Such
032   approaches face substantial scalability challenges: collecting human preference data is costly and
033   time-intensive, and as model sizes increase, the computational requirements for fine-tuning are likely
034   to become prohibitive.
035
      A prospective way to bypass the need for human preference data is to exploit knowledge already
036   contained in the pretrained model weights. This idea is motivated by evidence suggesting that
037   alignment merely reveals knowledge and capabilities acquired during pretraining (Zhou et al., 2024;
038   Lin et al., 2023). This notion has led to a growing body of literature achieving impressive results
039   using signal contained in pretrained models for fine-tuning (Fränken et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022;
040   Sun et al., 2023; 2024), largely or totally sidestepping human annotation.
041
      Next, to achieve free alignment, we must additionally obviate the need for fine-tuning. Instead, we
042   propose to replace it with a form of representation editing that does not require computing gradients
043   or even optimizing a proxy loss at all. Existing representation editing approaches (Zou et al.; Wu
044   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) rely on access to ground truth data, which does not account for the unique
045   challenges of using only signals from pretrained models. These signals are often noisier and more
046   limited compared to human-annotated data (Bender et al., 2021; Bommasani et al., 2021; Kenton
047   et al., 2021; Tamkin et al., 2021), necessitating a more tailored approach.
048   This work puts together these two pieces to explore the feasibility of free self-alignment. We align
049   pretrained LMs to human preferences using only the knowledge from the model itself, without
050   additional training or fine-tuning. Procedures that can accomplish these two goals are motivated by
051   an area of need—performing fast alignment repeatedly at-scale for on-the-fly model personalization.
052   In such scenarios, we lack the time and training resources to acquire manually-annotated data and
053   wait for models using RLHF or DPO techniques. Indeed, with limited time—and thus being limited
      to training on few samples—DPO will fail to achieve any meaningful level of alignment, while

072   We introduce A LIGN EZ, a novel approach designed for this setting. Using the pretrained model’s
073   own generated preference pairs, A LIGN EZ identifies the subspaces within the model’s embedding
074   spaces that correspond to helpful and non-helpful responses. During inference, we surgically modify
075   the model’s embeddings by boosting the components from the helpful subspaces and neutralizing
076   those from the non-helpful ones.
077   With this nearly cost-free procedure, we effectively narrow the performance gap between pretrained
078   and aligned models by 29.1% across two model architectures and five datasets. Additionally, we
079   explore the potential of A LIGN EZ to expedite more expensive alignment processes. Our experimental
080   results demonstrate that A LIGN EZ improves upon models trained using DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024)
081   with only a small subset of ground-truth preference data. In summary, our contributions include:
082         1. We introduce A LIGN EZ, a nearly cost-free approach that leverages preference data generated
083            by the pretrained LM to modify its embeddings, aligning outputs to human preferences.
084
            2. Our experiments show that A LIGN EZ significantly narrows the gap between the base model
085
               and its counterparts aligned with traditional expensive methods by 29.1% across two model
086
               architectures and five datasets.
087

089            ing models trained with DPO using only a small subset of ground truth preference data.

          techniques that go far beyond alignment as it exists today, tackling such areas as fine-grained
095
          and real-time personalization, that are currently beyond the reach of existing methods.

101   We are ready to describe the A LIGN EZ algorithm. First, we query a base pretrained LM to generate
102   its own preference data. Our intuition is that, while noisy, base models have learned, from pretraining
103   data, sufficient signal to aid in alignment. Using this self-generated data, the identify the subspaces in
104
      the LM’s embedding spaces that correspond to helpful and harmful directions for alignment. During
      inference, we modify the LM embeddings using these identified subspaces, steering the model to
105
      generate outputs that better align with human preferences (Figure 1).
106

      explain how A LIGN EZ identifies helpful and non-helpful subspaces in Section 2.2. Finally, we detail

130
131   First, we extract the human preference signal from the base LLM by querying it to generate its own
132   preference data. Given a dataset D of N queries, for each query q, we first ask the base LM (denoted
133   as ω) to describe characteristics of answers from a helpful agent (C help ) and a malicious agent
134   (C harm ). Next, we pair each query with its corresponding characteristics: (chelp
                                                                                      i   , q) and (charm
                                                                                                     i    , q).
135   We then prompt the LM to generate responses conditioned on these characteristics, resulting in
136   self-generated preference pairs for each query, denoted as (phelp
                                                                   i    , pharm
                                                                           i    ). By applying this procedure
137   to all N samples in the dataset, we obtain self-generated preference data pairs P help and P harm .
138   Note that we do not perform any prompt tuning, instead relying on a fixed set of prompt templates.
139   We illustrate this process in Figure 2 and provide prompt details in Appendix A.4.
140
      Critically, we note that the base models for generating the preference data are not aligned or
141
      instruction-tuned. Consequently, the resulting preference pairs may not always align with the
142   conditioning characteristics, introducing noise into the self-preference data. To address this challenge,
143   we tailor the embedding intervention in A LIGN EZ to accommodate this condition.

147   Next, using the noisy self-generated preference data, we identify the directions in the model embed-
148   ding space that correspond with human preferences. These directions, represented as vectors θ ∈ Rd
149   within ω’s latent space, can either (i) align with the helpful preferences P help , facilitating alignment
150   of the model’s generated sentences, or (ii) align with the harmful preferences P harm , leading to
151   adverse effects on alignment (Adila et al., 2023) (Dalvi et al., 2022). We denote these directions as
152   θhelp and θharm , respectively.

189   Sample-conditional estimation of θhelp and θharm . A simple way to denoise self-generated data
190   is by leveraging the local smoothness of the embedding space (Chen et al., 2022); that is, embeddings
191   within a localized region tend to exhibit similar properties. This suggests that preference samples
192   from similar queries will be closer together in the embedding space and share common characteristics.
193   To exploit this, we estimate sample-conditional helpful and harmful directions. Instead of applying
194
      SVD to the entire self-generated preference dataset and identifying a single set of directions for θhelp
      and θharm , we compute these directions in a sample-specific manner. This reduces noise from distant,
195
      unrelated samples, allowing SVD to identify more meaningful directions.
196
197   Concretely, for each q ⊂ D, let k-NN(q) represent the k < N nearest neighbors of q (including
198   q itself) in the embedding space. We apply SVD to find θhelp and θharm using Equation 1 on the
199   embeddings of k-nearest neighbors.

297
298   We evaluate the following claims about A LIGN EZ.
299   • Reduces alignment gap (Section 4.1). A LIGN EZ significantly reduces the performance gap
300     between the base model and aligned model without any additional fine-tuning and access to
301     ground-truth preference data.
302   • Expedites alignment (Section 4.2). A LIGN EZ expedites DPO alignment by improving models
303     that have been DPOed on only a small subset of ground-truth preference data.
304
      • Compatible with prompting techniques (Section 4.3). A LIGN EZ is compatible with and can be
305
        used in combination with prompt engineering-based alignment methods (Lin et al., 2023).
306
      • Ablations (Section 4.4). We analyze the components of A LIGN EZ algorithm and empirically show
307
        that A LIGN EZ benefits from its k−NN component.
308
309
      Metrics. We follow the most popular standard for automatic alignment evaluation, using GPT-4 as
310   a judge to compare a pair of responses (Zheng et al., 2024) and calculate the win rate (W%) and lose
311   rate (L%). We measure the following metrics:
312
313   1. Net Win% (∆%) = W% − L%: A model that produces meaningful improvement over the base
314      model will exhibit a higher win rate than lose rate, resulting in a positive net win percentage.
315   2. Relative Improvement (RI).
316                                           ∆ ours − base
317
                                                                  × 100.
                                            ∆ aligned − base
318      This metric evaluates how much A LIGN EZ improves alignment of the base pretrained model,
319      relative to the aligned model. A value of 0% means A LIGN EZ offers no improvement over the
320      base model, while 100% means A LIGN EZ matches the performance of the aligned model. Positive
321      percentages between 0% and 100% indicate that A LIGN EZ narrows the performance gap between
322      the base and aligned models, and a negative percentage indicates a performance decline from the
323      base model. Excitingly, we additionally sometimes observe AlignEZ performance beyond the
         aligned model.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
453
454
      Our work tackles alignment and sits at the intersection of self-generated synthetic data and efficient
      model editing. We give a (necessarily) compressed introduction to these areas.
455
456
457   LM Alignment. The standard approach to aligning LMs with human values and preferences relies
458
      on human-annotated preference data. This data is used either to (i) train a reward function and
      subsequently fine-tune the LM to maximize this reward using reinforcement learning objectives,
459
      as in methods like RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Christiano et al., 2017), or (ii) optimize a proxy
460
      loss to maximize the margin between preferred and not preferred outputs, as in methods like DPO
461   (Rafailov et al., 2024). While these methods achieve remarkable performance, they are challenging
462   to implement due to their complex pipelines, the high cost of computing resources, and the limited
463   scalability of acquiring human-preference data.
464
465   Self-Improvement. The difficulty of obtaining human-annotated data has led to significant efforts
466   to bypass this requirement. Methods such as those proposed by (Wang et al., 2022; Sun et al.,
467   2024; McIntosh et al., 2023) use manually crafted seed prompts to generate high-quality synthetic
468   datasets from pretrained LMs, which are then used for fine-tuning or training reward models. (Guo
469   et al., 2024) uses retrieval-augmented generation to remove reliance on manually designed prompts.
470   Another approach, (Li et al., 2023a), leverages instruction-tuned models to assist in generating
471   synthetic datasets. The work most similar to our approach is (Fränken et al., 2024), which emphasizes
472   maximizing the use of knowledge from the pretrained model being aligned. Our work takes this further
473
      by exploring whether self-alignment can be made even more cost-effective by replacing fine-tuning
      with representation editing, dramatically accelerating the alignment process.
474
475
476   Representation Editing. A parallel line of work seeks to modify model behavior without fine-
477
      tuning—doing so by solely editing the model’s representations. For vision-language models like
      CLIP, (Adila et al., 2023) and (Chuang et al., 2023) show that removing spurious or unwanted concept
478
      subspaces from embeddings boosts model accuracy on rare class predictions. (Limisiewicz et al.,
479
      2023) shows that doing so in LLM architectures reduces gender bias in generated sentences without
480   degrading model performance in other tasks. (Zou et al.; Li et al., 2024; Han et al., 2023; Rimsky et al.,
481   2023) demonstrate that modifying embeddings during inference to steer them towards certain traits
482   (e.g., honesty, truthfulness, sentiment) can effectively enhance these traits in the generated outputs.
483   Similarly, (Wu et al., 2024) and (Kong et al., 2024) learns the appropriate embedding modification,
484   acting as a form of fine-tuning. These methods assume access to ground-truth preference datasets.
485   Our work differentiates itself by designing an intervention technique that can handle the noisier signal
      from synthetic data generated by LMs.

      6   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      Future Work. A LIGN EZ presents several avenues for future exploration. First, we perform
489   embedding editing at every generation time step. However, it remains uncertain whether selecting
490   specific time steps for intervention could yield further improvements. Second, characterizing the
491   conditions in which self-alignment is possible by developing a specialized metric for predicting the
492   model’s ability to self-align would be useful.
493
494   Conclusion. We introduce A LIGN EZ, a novel approach for aligning pretrained LMs with human
495   preferences without access to human-annotated data and fine-tuning. By leveraging the inherent
496   knowledge within pretrained models, A LIGN EZ modifies model embeddings during inference to
497   produce outputs that better align with human preferences. We empirically show that A LIGN EZ
498   consistently enhances the alignment of the base model across multiple evaluation aspects, occasionally
499
      surpassing the performance of their aligned counterparts. Additionally, we show that A LIGN EZ can
      expedite more costly alignment techniques like DPO.
500
501   This work takes an initial step toward achieving truly cost-free alignment and paves the way for the
502   development of techniques in exciting new domains like real-time dynamic alignment and fast model
503   personalization – areas currently beyond the reach of standard alignment methods.
