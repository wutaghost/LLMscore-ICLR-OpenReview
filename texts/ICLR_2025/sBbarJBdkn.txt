009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The advent of black-box deep neural network classification models has sparked
012            the need to explain their decisions. However, in the case of generative AI, such
013            as large language models (LLMs), there is no class prediction to explain. Rather,
014            one can ask why an LLM output a particular response to a given prompt. In
015            this paper, we answer this question by proposing, to the best of our knowledge,
016            the first contrastive explanation methods requiring simply black-box/query access.
017            Our explanations suggest that an LLM outputs a reply to a given prompt because if
018            the prompt was slightly modified, the LLM would have given a different response
019            that is either less preferable or contradicts the original response. The key insight
020
               is that contrastive explanations simply require a scoring function that has meaning
               to the user and not necessarily a specific real valued quantity (viz. class label). We
021
               offer two algorithms for finding contrastive explanations: i) A myopic algorithm,
022
               which although effective in creating contrasts, requires many model calls and ii) A
023            budgeted algorithm, our main algorithmic contribution, which intelligently creates
024            contrasts adhering to a query budget, necessary for longer contexts. We show
025            the efficacy of these methods on diverse natural language tasks such as open-text
026            generation, automated red teaming, and explaining conversational degradation.

032   Generative artificial intelligence (AI) has rapidly transformed society and will continue to do so for
033
      the foreseeable future, albeit in ways we do not yet know. Thusfar, it has impacted how people con-
      duct their jobs (e.g., code generation for software engineers (Guagenti, 2024), text summarization
034
      for lawyers (Christman, 2024) and doctors (Philomin, 2024)) as well as how people conduct their
035
      daily activities (e.g., rewriting emails, seeking advice, or designing vacation itineraries). As AI has
036   advanced over the last two decades, so did the need for explaining how the AI was making deci-
037   sions (e.g., why was a customer denied a bank loan or why was an image classified as a pedestrian
038   crossing a street). Such explanations are even the topic of regulations in the USA with the AI Bill
039   of Rights (AIB, 2024) and in Europe with the GDPR (Yannella & Kagan, 2018) and the recent EU
040   AI Act (eua, 2024).
041
      Much has been done in explainable AI typically regarding classification and regression (see surveys
042   Guidotti et al. (2018) and Yang et al. (2023)) mostly focusing on black-box models, e.g., deep
043   neural networks. Explanation methods vary and include attribution methods such as LIME (Ribeiro
044   et al., 2016), SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), and saliency (Simonyan et al., 2013), and contrastive
045   explanations such as CEM (Dhurandhar et al., 2018) and CAT (Chemmengath et al., 2022).
046
      The focus of this paper is contrastive explanations for Large Language Models (LLMs). In the
047
      typical classification setting, contrastive explanations dictate that a classifier predicted label y on
048   sample x because if x was slightly modified to be xc , the classifier would have predicted label yc
049   instead. In the case of LLMs, there is no classifier and the output is a sequence of words. While
050   explainability is a well-studied area for classifiers, explanations for LLMs are still limited. A recent
051   method, MExGen (Paes et al., 2024), derives LIME and SHAP methods for LLMs based on mask
052   infilling while TextGenSHAP (Enouen et al., 2024) speeds up SHAP for LLMs using “speculative
053   decoding”, but these are attribution methods which highlight words in the prompt to maintain the
      response (not change it) also requiring real valued representations of the response. Such attribution

105   Danilevsky et al. (2020) considered explainability for natural language processing, primarily for
106   classification tasks where local explanations were provided, among which our focus is on post-
107   hoc methods that explain a fixed model’s prediction. One large group of explainability methods
      are feature based where the explanation outputs some form of feature importance (i.e., ranking,


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      relevance, etc.) of the words in text (Wallace et al., 2018; Papernot & Patrick, 2018; Feng et al.,
109   2018; Harbecke et al., 2018; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2017). Other types of
110   local post-hoc explanations include exemplar based (Gurumoorthy et al., 2019; Koh & Liang, 2017;
111   Kim et al., 2016) that output similar instances to the input.
112
      Among local methods, our focus is on contrastive methods (Chemmengath et al., 2022; Dhurandhar
113
      et al., 2018; Madaan et al., 2021; Luss et al., 2021). Contrastive explanations are complementary
114
      to attribution and exemplar style explanations (Arya et al., 2019) discussed above as they provide
115   ways to realistically manipulate the input in a minimal manner so as to change the output. In our
116   setup, we want to modify the input prompt so that an LLM produces an output with a different user
117   specified quality or characteristic (viz. fairness, preference, etc.). The latter distinguishes our work
118   from prior contrastive explanation works which are mainly for the classification setting.
119
      Another contrastive method POLYJUICE (Wu et al., 2021) is a human-in-the-loop method requiring
120
      supervision about the type of modification to be performed to the text such as negation, word re-
121   placement, etc. A contrastive latent space method (Jacovi et al., 2021) does not generate contrastive
122   text, but rather highlights (multiple) words in the input text that are most likely to alter a classi-
123   fication prediction, and is furthermore not a black-box method. Similarly, Yin & Neubig (2022)
124   highlight words that influence a model predicting a target output instead of a foil; this work is re-
125   lated to saliency and uses gradient-based scoring. A few works use LLMs to generate contrastive
126   explanations (Dixit et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) but focus on classification.

128   3     F ORMULATION
129
130   We here formulate the contrastive explanation problem for LLMs. Denote by x0 an input prompt and
131   X the space of prompts, i.e., strings. Let LLM (x) be the response of an LLM to prompt x. Define
132   g(x0 , y0 , xc , yc ) as a scoring function that inputs a prompt x0 , the initial response y0 = LLM (x0 ),
133   a perturbed version of x0 denoted as xc , and the response to xc denoted as yc = LLM (xc ). Also
134   denote f (x0 , xc ) as a measure of similarity between two prompts x0 and xc . We formulate the
135   contrastive explanation problem for LLMs as
136                                   minimize f (x0 , x)                                                   (1)
137                                   subject to g (x0 , x, LLM (x0 ), LLM (x)) ≥ δ
138
                                                  x∈X
139
      Assuming bounded X , Problem (1) is a combinatorial optimization problem over all possible
140
      prompts in X . Note that this generalizes contrastive explanations (Dhurandhar et al., 2018) or adver-
141
      sarial attacks (Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Chen et al., 2018) where typically LLM (·) is replaced by a
142   classifier and the constraint is such that the predicted class of x0 changes. Contrastive explanation
143   methods further constrain the contrastive explanation, i.e., the solution to (1), to lie on a manifold
144   that maintains it to be a realistic example. In the case of language generation, such constraints will
145   be enforced by infilling masks, i.e., replacing missing word(s).
146
      Similarity: Experiments in this paper measure prompt similarity f (x0 , xc ) as the number of mask
147
      and infill operations applied to a string x0 to obtain string xc . Other functions could be considered
148   based on commonly used text similarity metrics such as BLEU or ROUGE. Our choice is selected
149   to focus on minimizing the number of LLM queries made by the algorithms described below.

189   Contradiction: This scoring function inputs two responses y1 and y2 obtained from an LLM. A

197   (closer to 1 is more similar). Given two prompts x1 and x2 and their corresponding responses y1
198   and y2 , we measure the BLEU score between prompts x1 and x2 , denoted as a, and the BLEU score
199   between responses y1 and y2 , denoted as b. The BLEU SUMM score is output as w1 · a + w2 ·
200   (1 − b), meaning a higher score is given for having similar prompts and dissimilar responses. Our
201
      experiments use w1 = 0.2, w2 = 0.8 to give more importance to dissimilarity of responses. Such

      4.1   CELL
274
275   Our myopic search, CELL, uses the following strategy: An input prompt is first split into n substrings
276   (according to split k); the contrastive example will be a perturbed prompt that masks and replaces
277   a subset of these n substrings. In the first iteration, each of the n substrings is masked and infilled,
278   the n perturbed prompts are passed through the LLM to generate n responses, and these responses
279   are used to compute n scores. If a response results in a sufficiently large score, the corresponding
280   perturbed prompt and response is deemed the contrastive example; otherwise, the perturbed prompt
281   resulting in the largest score is used as the initial prompt and the same steps are followed on the
282   n − 1 remaining original substrings. These steps are repeated until either a contrastive example is
283
      found or all substrings have been perturbed without finding a contrastive example. Pseudocode for
      CELL can be found in Algorithm 2 in the Appendix.

286   4.2   CELL- B U D G E T
287
      When the search is over a prohibitively large number of substrings, as is typical in text summa-
288
      rization for example, one might be conscientious of how many times the LLM is called. The next
289   algorithm, called CELL-budget, our main algorithmic contribution, explores new perturbations
290   from the input prompt while also exploiting perturbations already made. This algorithm, detailed
291   in Algorithm 1, is inspired by Dhurandhar et al. (2024) which adaptively samples a continuous
292   search space subject to a budget; their task is to find a trust region that satisfies local explainability
293   properties whereas our task is to find a region that satisfies a score criterion.
294
      Each iteration is broken down into three main blocks: 1) Compute the number of seeds, i.e., prompts,
295   to perturb, 2) Generate seeds, and 3) Search around these seeds (inner loop). Note that each iteration
296   of the inner loop samples a particular number of prompts around those seeds in order to use the
297   total budget. Function NUM SEEDS could take various forms; one such form (Algorithm 3 in the
298   Appendix) is inspired by optimal sampling from continuous distributions.
299
      Our method deviates from Dhurandhar et al. (2024) because it is a search over a discrete space.
300
      CELL-budget employs a prompt seed-driven approach where some seeds are generated from the
301   initial prompt and others from previously perturbed prompts. This allows the search to explore new
302   perturbations of the initial prompt while also taking advantage of favorable perturbations that were
303   already made (the balance is controlled by the hyperparameter α). The search around prompt seeds
304   in the inner loop first samples a fixed number of perturbations around each seed using function
305   SAMPLE SEEDS (Algorithm 5 in the Appendix) and checks if a contrastive example was found.
306   The next iteration of this inner loop reduces the number of seeds sampled from the current list of
307   perturbed prompts and increases the number of samples taken around each seed. The decrease/in-
308   crease in seeds/samples focuses more heavily on perturbations more likely to lead to contrastive
309
      examples. Function BEST SUBSET outputs prompt seeds as ordered by g(·, ·, ·, ·).

      We evaluate CELL(-budget) perturbed prompts across 3 properties considered in works on con-
371
      trastive explanations for classifiers (Chemmengath et al., 2022; Ross et al., 2021): flip rate, edit rate,
372   and content preservation. The flip rate measures the percentage of times CELL finds a contrastive
373   explanation. Edit distances compute a word-level Levenstein distance between the input prompt
374   and the contrastive prompt. This is the minimum number of changes (additions, deletions, etc.) to
375   get from one prompt to the other, and we normalize by the number of words in the input prompt.
376   Content preservation quantifies how much content is preserved in the contrastive prompt from the
377   input prompt. Following previous works above, we compute the cosine similarity between prompt
      embeddings obtained from a bert-base-uncased model.

418   6.1    AUTOMATED RED TEAMING
419
420   With the deployment of LLMs has come the need for checks and balances so that the LLMs do
421   not output incorrect/derogatory/etc. responses. This has led to the use of red teaming in which one
422
      searches for prompts that lead to such responses. The search has typically been done manually, with
      humans set up to prompt LLMs and try to break them (Dinan et al., 2019; Röttger et al., 2021).
423
424   Recent works have focused on automated red teaming, using LLMs to generate prompts that lead
425   to improper responses. Perez et al. (2022) use zero and few-shot generation, as well as finetuning
426   an LLM via RL, to generate harmful prompts. Casper et al. (2023) add diversity penalties to the
427   RL finetuning and Hong et al. (2024) further add a novelty penalty. These directions all focus on
428
      replacing humans and generating prompts from scratch.
429   We propose another method for automated red teaming whereby prompts from a test set are per-
430   turbed. We use CELL-budget to produce contrastive explanations for a chatbot with the specific
431   goal of perturbing a prompt to elicit a response that contradicts the response of the original prompt
      (using the contradiction scoring function). To the author’s knowledge, this a novel use of NLI.

      7      C ONCLUSION
526
527
      To the author’s knowledge, this paper proposes the first contrastive explanations for large language
      models. Novel insight into what a contrast should mean regarding LLMs led us to propose two
528
      algorithms for generating contrastive explanations: a myopic method that is effective for explaining
529
      responses to small prompts and a novel search strategy that takes into account a model query budget.
530
531   Our two novel use cases of contrastive explanations explicitly provide actionable explanations. In
532   terms of red teaming, such explanations can be used to debug a chatbot. The top example in Figure
533
      4 could lead a team to investigate the training data for examples where an employee was allowed to
      consult. In terms of conversational degradation, such explanations could be used to generate train-
534
      ing data to improve conversational agents. One might want to generate data where one dimension
535
      is modified and the others remain fixed. By explaining the top example in Figure 5 over other sub-
536   maxims, it could potentially be used as an example of not being helpful while maintaining other
537   submaxims. This also suggests future algorithmic work, where we would like to adapt CELL so that
538   the search explicitly moves in such directions.
