009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               As the insight of knowledge storage in language models deepens, the ability to
012            perform CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) operations on language models
013            becomes increasingly indispensable for satisfying the demands of managing rapidly
014            updating knowledge. Considering the high cost of fine-tuning language models,
015            model editing methods with low cost are usually required to manipulate models’
016            knowledge. The evidence suggests that modules carrying knowledge in a Trans-
017            former module are primarily the MLP blocks, thus we propose iReVa, a method
018            that explicitly initializes and retrofits key-value pairs into MLP blocks to construct
019            a new mapping of a piece of knowledge without damaging the irrelevant knowl-
020
               edge. In comparison to existing methods, iReVa reveals better interpretability and
               a stronger capacity for carrying traceable edits. Experiment results on a series of
021
               GPT series models show our prominent performance on edit success and general-
022
               ization without influencing specificity. We also made the first attempt to conduct a
023            knowledge withdrawal test of iReVa. Our codes are available at this website.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Language Models (LMs) Brown et al. (2020) are becoming imperative tools for consulting in real-
030   world scenarios. One significant reason for the prevalence of LMs is their ability to answer factoid
031   questions. For example, when we ask an LM with the question “Who is president of America ?”, it
032   returns the answer “Joe Biden”. Even though a mass amount of knowledge is stored in the LMs, we
033
      still face the issue of out-of-date and missing knowledge Petroni et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2020).
      Alternatively, some knowledge may change over years and some domain-specific knowledge may be
034
      absent from the LMs.
035
036   To bridge the gap, the task of model editing is introduced to edit the knowledge in LMs, which targets
037   at modifying the parameters of LMs and inject certain knowledge to them Zhang et al. (2024). The
038   difficulty of this task lies in the manipulation to the LMs, where the knowledge is implicitly stored
039
      in dense vectors. A naive solution to model editing is fine-tuning an LM with the new knowledge,
      whereas the cost is climbing with the surging size of LMs. More recent studies propose to directly
040
      update the models’ weights in mastery phase Jayashri & Kalaiselvi (2018); Bruner (1960) via either
041
      teaching a hyper-network to learn the change of the weights or locating-then-editing knowledge
042   neurons Cao et al. (2021); Mitchell et al. (2022a); Meng et al. (2023a;b). While the editing methods
043   above are efficient in updating knowledge in LMs, they encounter the difficulties of differentiating the
044   existing and new knowledge, which makes the editing hard to control. Methods like life-long model
045   editing Hartvigsen et al. (2023), MELO Yu et al. (2023), and T-Patcher Huang et al. (2023) propose
046   to learn the representation for new knowledge and merge this information with the original models.
047
      However, these methods still conform to the paradigm of learning the batch edit Huang et al. (2023);
048   Hase et al. (2021) as a whole without modeling edit parameters in a traceable way, which can not
049   conform the edit success to each edit and have a lack interpretability to the editing. In contrast, we
050   propose a method of Initializing and Retrofitting KEy-Value Adaptors (iReVa), an editing method
051   that inserts a key-value adaptor to indicate the mapping of an edit data pair and further retrofit the
052   adaptor with multiple objectives. Moreover, to prevent the unnecessary change to the irrelevant
053   knowledge, we elaborately design activation mechanism for the knowledge neurons. Experimental
      results on series of GPT-like models show that iReVa is able to outperform the SOTA results by


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      around 9% and 6% average score improvement on zsRE-10K and PARAREL-10K, respectively.
055   Moreover, iReVa is able to perform knowledge withdrawal in almost perfect condition.
056
057   Our contributions are summarized as follows: 1) We introduce a novel editing method that initializes
058
      and retrofits a key-value adaptor for traceable model editing, which is compatible with most LMs. 2)
      Our method outperforms recent baselines on model editing tasks with noticeable margins based on
059
      various evaluation metrics. 3) We validate the interpretability and generalization capabilities of our
060
      method by conducting further analysis such as knowledge withdrawal test and generalization test.

065   2.1   I NSIGHT OF K NOWLEDGE S TORAGE IN L ANGUAGE M ODELS
066
      Discussion about how LMs store knowledge has emerged. Petroni et al. (2019) introduced the
067
      perspective of treating LMs as knowledge bases and proved its plausibility, which attracted the
068   subsequent attention towards the exploration of the form of knowledge incorporated by LMs. The
069   opinion pointed out by Geva et al. (2021) indicates that factual knowledge is stored in the two-
070   layer-FFN network of a Transformer due to the similar form as key-value memories. This opinion
071   was followed by Li et al. (2024), which further derives the coefficient between final prediction and
072   knowledge neurons in MLP blocks. In contrast, Meng et al. (2023a), through a cosine similarity
073   analysis on hidden states experiment, posed viewpoints that the self-attention module can extract
074   various types of knowledge. Cao et al. (2021) further validates that the weight update is concentrated
075   on parameters in the self-attention module when we train models with new knowledge. Our editing
076
      method is built upon the former hypothesis and we focus on the editing to the MLP blocks.
077   2.2   E DITING LM S BY M ANIPULATING K NOWLEDGE
078
079   With the frequent updates of the knowledge, the demand for model editing increases. Diverse studies
080   have been proposed. By analogy with human knowledge acquisition, we can categorize the editing
081   into three distinct phases. In the recognition phase Bruner (1964), methods such as ERAC and IKE
082   Mitchell et al. (2022a); Zheng et al. (2023) solved the problem by importing additional memories in
083
      the form of relevant contexts or prompts. In association phase Bruner (1960), parameter-efficient
      tuning Hu et al. (2021); Li & Liang (2021); Yu et al. (2023); Hartvigsen et al. (2023) inserts low-rank
084
      adaptors or prefix token embeddings to fine-tune new knowledge and combine them to the original
085
      models. There are also some studies directly changing the weights of Transformers in the mastery
086   phase Jayashri & Kalaiselvi (2018). For example, Cao et al. (2021) proposed KE, Mitchell et al.
087   (2022a) proposed MEND and Tan et al. (2024) proposed MALMEN to predict the updated parameters
088   of a model with a trained hyper-network. Furthermore, ROME Meng et al. (2023a) and MEMIT
089   Meng et al. (2023b) compute the weight update explicitly with proper representations of knowledge
090   queries and values. However, none of them focuses on traceable model editing, which allows more
091   flexible manipulation of the knowledge.
092
093   3     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
094
095   We follow the previous studies Mitchell et al. (2022b); Yu et al. (2023); Hartvigsen et al. (2023)
096   to formulate the task. Suppose we are given a pre-trained language model fΦ parameterized by Φ,
097   model editing aims at editing fΦ with a dataset Din = {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xi , yi )..., (xn , yn )}, where
098   (xi , yi ) denotes the edit input-output pairs. Initially, for xi ∈ Din , the base model makes prediction
099   yˆi = f (xi ) but yˆi ̸= yi . In this case, we change fΦ by editing its parameters to Φ∗ . A good model
100   editing to fΦ∗ should satisfy: 1) for any xi ∈ Din , the edited model fΦ∗ should output desired
101   predictions, that is fΦ∗ (xi ) = yi ; 2) for any input out of the scope of Din , which is denoted as Dout ,
102
      the edited model fΦ∗ should retain the original predictions, that is fΦ∗ (xi ) = fΦ (xi ); 3) the edit of
      (xi , yi ) towards fΦ∗ should not influence any prior edits x<i ∈ Din .

168   4.1      I NITIAL K EY-VALUE A DAPTORS FOR I N -S COPE E DITING
169
170   Given an edit (xi , yi ) ∈ Din , we first initialize its knowledge neuron k̂0 ∈ Rd1 and v̂0 ∈ Rd1 . For
171   k̂0 , we initialize each key to the xi using the cached input i predicted by fΦ (xi ) at layer l, which
172   results in a high probability of matching to the input pattern. For v̂0 , we initialize it using the weights
173   corresponding to yi from the last layer of fΦ . Specifically, fΦ (xi ) takes charge of generating the next
174   token which can be deemed as the prediction to xi . Thus, we extract the corresponding column of
175   the ground truth token yi from the weights W ∈ Rd1 ×|V | for generating the next token distribution,
176   where |V | and d1 are the sizes of the vocabulary and dimension of the last layer, respectively 1 . After
177   initialization, we build a mapping from xi to yi in a Transformer.

      5.2       BASELINES
285   We compare our iReVa with 6 advanced baselines that support batch editing: NO EDITING denotes
286   we do not modify the base model and utilize its original prediction; FT Zhu et al. (2021) is the simple
287   fine-tuning with a constraint on specific parameters. MEMIT Meng et al. (2023b) and ROME Meng
288   et al. (2023a) are two methods employing a causal analysis to detect the most significant hidden states.
289   They view the editing as a minimum optimization and edit the weight directly, which is effective
290   in batch edit; MEND Mitchell et al. (2022a) applies rank-one decomposition to divide the model
291   into two rank-one matrices, which is able to carry mass knowledge in the dense metrics; MELO Yu
292   et al. (2023) activates specific LoRA block corresponding to specific queries for multiple edits,
293
      which support large-scale editing in just one process. Note that T-Patcher Huang et al. (2023) whose
      forward propagation resembles our method is not included, now that it can be merely applied on
294
      encoder-decoder LMs. Specifically, the patcher is only embedded in the encoder which is inapplicable
295
      to the decoder.

298
299   We follow the commonly-used evaluation metrics Meng et al. (2023a;b) to measure the effect of our
300   editing method.
301
302
      1. Edit Success (ES) measures the models’ prediction accuracy on edited data xi ∈ Din by calculat-
                       PN
303      ing ES = N1 i=0 I(yi = fΦ (xi )), which represents whether the new knowledge is successfully
304
         injected into the base model.
      2. Generalization (Paraphrase Success, PS) measures the models’ prediction accuracy on paraphrase
305
         questions provided by benchmarks. We compute paraphrase success with the same formulation
306
         but for xi in the paraphrase questions set. Paraphrase success indicates whether the model can
307      recognize similar expressions and provide edited answers.
308   3. Specificity (Neighborhood Success, NS) measures the models’ prediction accuracy on irrelevant
309      questions. Different from Dout , these questions are only used for preventing data leakage.
310      We compute neighborhood success with the same formulation but for xi in the neighborhood
311      questions set. Neighborhood success manifests the capability of solving catastrophic forgetting
312      and preserving irrelevant knowledge stored in model.
313   4. Score is the average of the three aforementioned metrics.

364
365   Compared with the existing editing methods, our method has the unique advantage of interpretability
366   and traceability, that is we can clearly identify the edit for each newly inserted key-value pair. This
367
      provides a chance to conduct an edit withdrawal test. Most existing methods can’t perform the
      withdrawal test for their batch training mechanism, and stream-fashion methods like GRACE may
368
      encounter the forgetting Hartvigsen et al. (2023) challenge which will induce a withdrawal failure.

371   and recover the original output from the base model without much loss. To this end, we inhibit
372   corresponding knowledge neurons as withdrawing the edit, which is denoted as fΦ−∗k̂ . For evaluation,
373   we introduce two metrics, namely Retrieve Success and Consistency. They are formulated as

      6.3     E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
384
385   We discuss the spatial and time complexities of iReVa. Regarding time complexity during inference,
386   iReVa only inserts the adaptor in a single l-th layer and the insertion only affects the final token
387   prediction of the input. With i ∈ R1×d1 , K̂ ∈ Rd1 ×n , V̂ ∈ Rn×d1 and averaged length l of target
388   tokens (l = 2.69 for zsRE and l = 1.15 for PARAREL), the extra time consumption is O(ld21 n),
389   which is unrelated to the input length and number of layers. Regarding spacial complexity, as we
390   insert two vectors for each edit in a single layer, the extra spacial consumption is O(2lnd1 ). In
391   practice, for GPT2-XL with 1.5B parameters, the adaptor merely possesses 0.08B parameters with
392   10K edits. There is no additional spacial complexity involved in the training phase, given that only
393   2d1 parameters are learnable for each edit token. We empirically record that 10K edits with iReVa
394   cost 7.5/1.6 hours (fine-tuning/without fine-tuning) with a single NVIDIA A800 GPU, compared to
395
      9.16 hours for ROME and 5.4 hours for MEMIT.

      7    L IMITATION
487
488
      We also conclude iReVa’s limitation as follows: a) iReVa performs poorly when the target prompt
489   is a long sentence because it constructs a knowledge neuron for each token in the target prompt,
490   thereby increasing the training time cost. Additionally, during inference, the high number of neurons
491   increases the probability of errors; b) To maintain iReVa’s interpretability, its application is limited,
492   including that iReVa can be only applied on GPT-like models and generation task; c) The behavior of
493   iReVa (ES and PS) won’t enhance noticeably as the scale of base model grows.

495   8    C ONCLUSIONS
496
497   In this paper, we propose iReVa, a model editing method with traceable knowledge storage, which
498   inserts edit key-value adaptor into the MLP module of a transformer model explicitly. iReVa displays
499   prominent abilities of edit success, generalization, and specificity and outperforms baselines with an
500   observable margin. Besides, iReVa first successfully demonstrates its capacity for the knowledge
501   withdrawal. For further research, we will focus on generalizing iReVa to more LM architectures.
