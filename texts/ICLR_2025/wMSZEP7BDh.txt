011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Calculus of Variations is the mathematics of functional optimization, i.e., when the
014            solutions are functions over a time interval. This is particularly important when
015
               the time interval, or support, is unknown like in minimum-time control problems,
               so that forward-in-time solutions are not possible. Calculus of Variations also of-
016
               fers a robust framework for learning optimal control and inference with moving
017
               boundaries. How can this framework be leveraged to design neural networks to
018            solve challenges in control and inference? We propose the Pontryagin’s Maxi-
019            mum Principle Neural Network (PMP-Net) that is tailored to estimate control and
020            inference solutions, in accordance with the necessary conditions outlined by Pon-
021            tryagin’s Maximum Principle. We assess PMP-Net on two classic optimal control
022            and inference problems: optimal linear filtering and minimum-time control. Our
023            findings indicate that PMP-Net can be effectively trained in an unsupervised man-
024            ner to solve these problems without the need for ground-truth data, successfully
025            deriving the classical “Kalman filter” and “bang-bang” control solution. This es-
026
               tablishes a new approach for addressing general, possibly yet unsolved, inference
               and optimal control problems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Standard neural networks excel at learning from labeled data, but often lack inherent knowledge of
032   physical principles. In many engineering and scientific applications, there is a wealth of accumulated
033   knowledge and practices that could inform the architecture of learning models. In addition, data in
034   these fields are often scarce, difficult, or expensive to obtain. For instance, telecommunications,
035   data processing, automation, robotics, and control problems frequently have little or no labeled data,
036   making traditional supervised learning methods challenging to apply.
037   This paper presents a method for designing deep models from first principles by incorporating prior
038   knowledge, specifically existing design principles that have been successful in various engineering,
039   scientific, and technology practices. It focuses on two design problems of broad practical interest:
040   determining the optimal linear estimator and solving the optimal minimum time control problem.
041   We show that our deep models recover the solution to the first, the well-known Kalman filter, and to
042   the second, the bang-bang control, also known as on-off control.
043   Optimizing over functions with moving boundaries, i.e., when the optimizing variable is a set of
044   whole functions over a variable time interval, falls under the realm of the Calculus of Variations,
045   and a principled solution methodology can be based on Pontryagin’s maximum principle (PMP).
046   PMP offers valuable prior knowledge about the necessary conditions for optimal solutions and often
047   provides sufficient conditions, making the optimal solution unique in many cases. This motivates
048   the integration of PMP into machine learning training methodologies.
049   In this paper, we draw inspiration from the Calculus of Variations—a field focused on finding the
050   maxima and minima of functionals through variations—to design a neural network based on basic
051   principles, which we call the “Pontryagin’s Maximum Principle Neural Network” (PMP-Net). This
052   network is designed to solve optimization problems like in Kalman filtering and those arising in con-
053   trol contexts. We start by formulating a variational approach to these problems, using the calculus of
      variations to derive the necessary conditions for optimization by applying Pontryagin’s Maximum

      Principle. Although mathematicians and engineers typically solve these conditions analytically or
055   numerically, such methods can be challenging when dealing with nonlinear, second-order differen-
056   tial equations with complex boundary conditions. Instead, we propose using a neural network to
057   learn the optimal solution from PMP’s necessary conditions.
058
      Additionally, in minimum-time problems such as the bang-bang control problem, there are two
059
      key challenges. First, because the terminal time tf is to be optimized, the optimization is over a
060
      functional space, meaning the optimal solutions are functions over the entire interval [0 , tf ], where
061   tf itself is unknown. As a result, the forward method can not be used and the performance metrics
062   are only valid for admissible trajectories (the trajectory that reaches the final state. Second, the
063   extra constraints, such as functions being bounded or living in a compact set, restrict the control
064   functions to be learned. The optimal solution in these cases is often discontinuous, resembling a
065   step function, and may be undefined in certain regions. These complexities frequently result in
066   vanishing or exploding gradients during neural network training and no prior work overcome these
067   challenges.
068   This paper presents a method to integrate prior knowledge from Calculus of Variations, functional
069   optimization, and classical control into the architectural design of deep models. We incorporate
070   dynamical constraints, control constraints, and conditions derived from PMP into the loss function
071   for training neural networks, enabling unsupervised learning. Our contributions are as follows.
072
      Main contributions:
073
074          • Incorporate calculus of variations and Pontryagin’s Maximum Principle as soft constraints
075            in ML training methodology and minimizing optimality conditions residual instead of min-
076            imizing actual performance metrics. This provides a benefit when the performance func-
077            tional cannot always be computed.
078          • Engineer a novel neural network architecture, PMP-Net, that mimics the design of feed-
079            back controllers used in optimal control. This allows PMP-Net to apply to different time
080            horizons.
081          • Propose learning paradigms that effectively train PMP-Net to derive the optimal solution.
082          • Show that our PMP-Net replicates the design of the Kalman filter and the bang-bang control
083            without using labeled data.

152   2.2   P ONTRYAGIN ’ S M AXIMUM P RINCIPLE N ETWORK
153
      Instead of solving equation 5 analytically or numerically, we propose leveraging neural net-
154
      works’ well-known capability as universal function approximators (Cybenko, 1989) to learn
155
      {x(t), u(t), λ(t), t ∈ [0 , tf ]}, along with the learnable parameters {tf , d1 , ..., dk }, that satisfy PMP.
156   In the training stage, rather than directly matching the PMP-Net’s outputs to ground truth data
157   {x(t)⋆ , u(t)⋆ , λ(t)⋆ , t ∈ [0 , tf ]}, our PMP-Net learns to predict solutions that adhere to the PMP
158   constraints. Because this process incorporates a solution methodology, the PMP, we interpret it as
159   bringing to the neural networks “prior knowledge”(Betti & Gori, 2016). Our approach introduces
160   an inductive bias into the PMP-Net, allowing it to learn the optimal solution in an unsupervised
161   manner. By simultaneously predicting both the state and the control, PMP-Net eliminates the need
      for integration and can address optimal control problems with unknown terminal time.

163   and the costate λ(t). The Hamiltonian H is then calculated based on these predictions. By leveraging
164   the automatic differentiation capabilities of neural networks (Baydin et al., 2017), we can efficiently
165   compute the derivatives and partial derivatives present in equation 5 by computing in-graph gradients

167   of the differential equations in PMP and incorporate them into the loss function, along with the
168   L2 loss between the predicted and target states at the boundary conditions. In the experiments in
169   Sections 3 and 4, we also incorporate additional architectural features into our PMP-Net to enforce

198   In this section, our goal is to design a linear filter that provides the best estimate of the current
199   state based on noisy observations. The optimal solution is known as “Kalman Filtering” (Kalman &
200   Bucy, 1961), which is one of the most practical and computationally efficient methods for solving
201   estimation, tracking, and prediction problems. The Kalman filter has been widely applied in var-
202   ious fields from satellite data assimilation in physical oceanography, to econometric studies, or to
203
      aerospace-related challenges (Leonard et al., 1985; Auger et al., 2013). The optimal solution being
      known, the Kalman filter is the ground truth that serves to benchmark PMP-Net.

      the state estimator, costate estimator, and control estimator are modeled by 6-layer feedforward
386
      networks. The learnable parameter tf is subjected to the constraint x(tf ) = xf . Since d1 , d2 are
387
      redundant, they are removed from the training.
388
389   Training: We propose a new paradigm for training PMP-Net for minimum-time problems. First,
390   we set a time T that is sufficiently larger than t⋆f . We start by pretraining the costate estimator such
391   that the costate estimator is not a zero function (see Appendix C). This can be achieved by training
392
      the costate estimator to output a at time 0 and b at time T , where a, b are heuristic non-zero values.
      Secondly, we propose sequential and alternate training. The equation 12 suggests that the optimal
393
      control u⋆ as a function of (x⋆ , λ⋆ ) can be learned without knowing (x⋆ , λ⋆ ). Therefore, in the
394
      first step, we can generate a random (x, λ) and train the control estimator to minimize H(x, λ, u).
395   We freeze the state and costate estimator and take n gradient update for the control estimator since
396   u⋆ = arg minu H(x, λ, u). Next, we freeze the control estimator and train the state and costate
397   estimator by uniformly sampling 5000 points from time interval [0, T ] and perform one gradient
398   update for the state and costate estimator before going back to the first step again. This can prevent
399   vanishing gradients or exploding gradients. We also compute the gradient of the loss function with
400   respect to the variable tf , allowing it to be optimized during backpropagation. . We train PMP-Net
401   using stochastic gradient descent with the initial learning rate 8 × 10−4 .
402   Evaluation: Similar to the experiment in Section 3.2, we generate the control estimate from PMP-
403   Net and use a fourth-order Runge-Kutta integrator to estimate the state trajectory. For the base-
404   line, we employ the optimal (bang-bang) control and integrate it with the fourth-order Runge-Kutta
405   method. During prediction, we consider the state to have reached the target if the Euclidean distance
406   between them is less than ϵ = 0.05.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
489   Our approach aligns with the use of neural networks for solving optimal control problems and is
490   inspired by existing literature on integrating constraints into neural network architectures. Below,
491   we provide a concise overview of these areas, highlighting their relevance. We provide a brief
492   overview of these areas and emphasize how our work distinguishes itself from them.
493   Enforcing dynamics constraints in neural networks: Dynamics constraints in neural networks can
494   be addressed through two main approaches: (1) designing specialized architectures that inherently
495   satisfy the constraints (hard constraints), and (2) incorporating the constraints into the loss function,
496   as done in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) Raissi et al. (2019) (soft constraints).
497
      In hard constraint approaches, Böttcher et al. (2022) enforce dynamic constraints using neural ODEs
498   (Chen et al., 2018) to learn the optimal control. ODE-based methods primarily address the forward
499   problem by integrating the state to the terminal time, calculating the loss function, and minimizing
500   it. This framework is not applicable when the terminal time tf is unknown and must be optimized,
501   or when the terminal state is prescribed. Similarly, D’Ambrosio et al. (2021) parameterize the state
502   x and express the control u in terms of x and its higher-order derivatives to satisfy the dynamic
503   constraints. However, such a representation is not always feasible in general dynamics.
504   In a soft constraint approach, Mowlavi & Nabi (2023) employ PINNs to parameterize the state x
505   and control u, ensuring they satisfy the dynamics. The neural network weights are then updated to
506   minimize the performance metric. However, this direct method assumes the performance metric can
507   always be calculated—requiring the supports of the relevant functions to be fixed and known.
508
      In contrast, our method uses the indirect method by leveraging the calculus of variations, enabling
509
      us to address cases where the terminal time and terminal state are variables (moving boundary).
510   Our approach simultaneously learns the optimal control and the minimum time, even under these
511   conditions.
512
513
      Incorporating optimality conditions in neural networks: Several works have used optimality
      conditions of constrained optimization in neural networks. Reference Amos & Kolter (2017) and
514
      Donti et al. (2021) incorporate Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions in implementing backward
515
      passes in neural networks. But this is constrained optimization over constant variables (parameters)
516   while we optimize over functions with a dynamic constraint. Reference Yin et al. (2024) and Betti
517   et al. (2024) propose using neural networks to parameterize the state and costate that learns to satisfy
518   KKT and PMP conditions. However, these works only consider problems where the support is
519   fixed. This approach can not be extended to a problem where the support is unknown, e.g., as in the
520   minimum time problem. While D’Ambrosio et al. (2021) considers learning the terminal time, their
521   approach remains limited when the terminal state is not specified (e.g. when finding a projection
522   onto manifolds).

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   We present a novel paradigm that integrates calculus of variations and Pontryagin’s maximum prin-
529   ciple into neural networks for learning the solutions to functional optimization problems arising in
530   many engineering and technology and scientific problems. Our PMP-Net is unsupervised, general-
531
      izable and can be applied to general optimal control problems with moving boundaries that other
      related works have not addressed. We illustrate the PMP-Net strategy with two classical problems
532
      of great applied significance and show that it successfully recovers the Kalman filter and bang-
533
      bang control solutions. By leveraging the Calculus of Variations, we can analyze variations in the
534   terminal state and time, and PMP-Net successfully optimizes this variable in the minimum time
535   problem—something most prior works fail to do. Although these solutions have been derived ana-
536   lytically in the past, we experiment with these two classic problems, especially bang-bang control
537   where no prior work has managed to use neural network to solve before, so that we can evaluate our
538   results with the analytical optimal solutions. Our work paves the way for applying PMP-based neu-
539   ral networks to more complex, higher-dimensional, and analytically intractable control problems.
