011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            As large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, many existing bench-
014            marks designed to evaluate their reasoning capabilities are becoming saturated.
015
               Therefore, we present the Putnam-AXIOM Original benchmark consisting of 236
               mathematical problems from the William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Com-
016
               petition, along with detailed step-by-step solutions. To preserve the Putnam-
017
               AXIOM benchmark’s validity and mitigate potential data contamination, we cre-
018            ated the Putnam-AXIOM Variation benchmark with functional variations of 52
019            problems. By programmatically altering problem elements like variables and
020            constants, we can generate unlimited novel, equally challenging problems not
021            found online. We see that almost all models have significantly lower accuracy
022            in the variations than the original problems. Our results reveal that OpenAI’s
023            o1-preview, the best performing model, achieves merely 41.95% accuracy on
024            the Putnam-AXIOM Original but experiences around a 30% reduction in accu-
025            racy on the variations’ dataset when compared to corresponding original prob-
026
               lems. Moreover, we explore metrics beyond boxed accuracy to assess models
               on complex tasks like natural language theorem proving, crucial for evaluating
027
               reasoning capabilities in depth, opening the possibility for open-ended evalu-
028
               ation of reasoning strings. The data and the evaluation code are available at
029            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/putnam-axiom-B57C/.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The ability for Large Language Models (LLMs) to reason about complex problems has a plethora of
035   applications in fields such as economics (Zhang et al., 2024), drug discovery (Bran et al., 2023), and
036   even simulations of human behavior and society (Park et al., 2023). The prominence of LLM rea-
037   soning use has led to significant development in their performance on many reasoning benchmarks.
038   Outpacing Current Evaluations. Indeed, advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Gem-
039   ini Ultra (Team, 2023) have reported human-level performance on many benchmarks like MMLU
040   (Hendrycks et al., 2020) and MMMU (Yue et al., 2023). Similarly, LLMs have seen progress in other
041   challenging benchmarks like GSM8K (Chen et al., 2022) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), with
042   SOTA models attaining nearly 90% accuracy on MATH (Lei, 2024) and nearly perfect accuracy on
043   GSM8K (Zhong et al., 2024). Although this progress is a testament to the rapidly evolving capabil-
044
      ity of LLMs, it also presents a problem: Current benchmarks are starting to fall short in evaluating
      the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
045
046   Data Contamination. The problem is further complicated by data contamination, which remains
047   a major concern for current evaluation benchmarks. By training LLMs on larger portions of the
048   internet, researchers are incorporating an increasing number of open-source benchmark data into
049   the models’ pretraining. Therefore, a model can display artificially high “reasoning ability” by
050
      simply memorizing the answers it has seen, undermining the integrity of the evaluation.
051   To address these limitations, we introduce the Putnam-AXIOM (Advanced eXamination of
052   Intelligence in Operational Mathematics) dataset, a novel and challenging compilation of high-
053   level mathematics problems sourced from the William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition,
      an annual mathematics competition for undergraduate college students in North America which re-

      quires advanced mathematical reasoning and covers a wide range of university-level mathematical
055   concepts. In addition, we also introduce functional variations of this AXIOM dataset to combat data
056   contamination, taking inspiration from the solution employed by Srivastava et al. (2024). Functional
057   variations adjust variables, constants, and the phrasing of problems through Python scripts, allowing
058   us to generate an unlimited number of new problems that are not found on the Web but still retain
059   their mathematical complexity and validity. AXIOM enables fully automated evaluations by requir-
060   ing models to provide final answers within “\boxed{}” brackets which can then be extracted and
061   compared to the ground truth final solution using an equivalence function 1 as used for the MATH
062   dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021). This approach eliminates the need for human evaluation, and avoids
063
      the limitations of multiple-choice formats Schaeffer et al. (2024), thus maintaining soundness while
      enabling scalability.
064
065   Initial evaluations on Putnam-AXIOM demonstrate its difficulty with OpenAI o1-preview scoring
066   less than half at 41.95%, while GPT-4o achieves only 17.80%. Even math-specialized models such
067   as Qwen2-Math-7B and Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct perform poorly, scoring 5.51% and 11.8% re-
068   spectively. Performance further declines on functional variations of Putnam-AXIOM, which include
069
      significant drops for most models, decreasing by 20-30% in relative performance. These low scores
      underscore Putnam-AXIOM’s utility for measuring LLMs’ advanced reasoning capabilities, while
070
      the variations scrutinize true reasoning skills by exposing the models’ reliance on memorization.
071
072   Proof-based Evaluation Metrics. In addition to introducing the Putnam-AXIOM Original and
073   Variation benchmarks, we identified the need for more sophisticated LLM reasoning evaluation
074   metrics. Current evaluation metrics for reasoning are inadequate, as they rely solely on a final
075
      “boxable” answer without assessing the actual reasoning process. Recent evidence also suggests
      that LLM reasoning is not always faithful, resulting in answers that do not depend on the validity
076
      of the reasoning, as shown in (Turpin et al., 2024; Pfau et al., 2024). This limitation is particularly
077
      significant as the research community moves towards process-based reward models and step-wise
078   search algorithms where the soundness of intermediate reasoning is critical (Luo et al., 2024; Hubert
079   et al., 2024). It is also worrying in open-ended evaluations areas, like theorem proving, where human
080   evaluation is a current bottleneck. We therefore explore automatic alternative metrics to boxed
081   answers and find that a simple and cheap method, Teacher-Forced Accuracy (TFA), is a promising
082   approach. TFA represents a step forward in developing more sound LLM evaluations, which we
083   hope will lead to more accurate assessments of and claims about LLM reasoning capabilities.
084   Our contributions are:
085
086             • The Putnam-AXIOM, a new evaluation benchmark of 236 challenging mathematical
087
                  problems sourced from the William Lowell Putnam Competition, designed to assess ad-
                  vanced mathematical reasoning in LLMs.
088
                • Functional variations for 52 of these problems using Python scripts, altering variables,
089
                  constants, and problem phrasing to generate unlimited novel problems while preserving
090               their mathematical complexity, effectively avoiding data contamination.
091             • Novel evaluations that measure the reasoning, such as teacher-forced accuracy (TFA), to
092               provide a more complete assessment of LLMs’ reasoning abilities – beyond traditional
093               boxed answers.

098
099   Numerous benchmarks exist to assess the mathematical capabilities of models, each typically fo-
100   cusing on a specific task. Two notable examples are MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and GSM8K
101   (Cobbe et al., 2021). The MATH dataset contains questions sourced from American high school
102   mathematics competitions such as the AMC 10, AMC 12, and AIME (Hendrycks et al., 2021),
103   while the GSM8K dataset contains 8.5K handwritten elementary school level questions Cobbe et al.
104   (2021). Both contain questions and answers with detailed rationale explanations.
105   As models have become larger and more powerful, even the most difficult existing benchmarks have
106   become less challenging. For instance, while the MATH dataset saw 6.9% accuracy on its release,

      Many contemporary data sets have been created to combat the saturation of existing benchmarks.
      For instance, the ARB dataset includes hundreds of challenging problems in high school and college-

114
      9,000 problems from the International Mathematics Olympiad (IMO), the Chinese GaoKao, and

      Although these datasets alleviate the saturation problem, they come with many limitations. For
      instance, ARB Sawada et al. (2023) and OlympiadBench He et al. (2024) both contain several sym-
119
      bolic and proof-based questions which cannot be graded automatically and require a costly and
120
      lengthy human evaluation process. Though ARB attempts to utilize LLMs to grade their own re-
121   sponses with a rubric, this process is often unreliable and self-referential. Our Putnam-AXIOM
122   dataset addresses these limitations by offering challenging Putnam problems with fully-written so-
123   lutions and easily evaluable answers. It enables efficient automated assessment via frameworks like
124   LM Harness (Gao et al., 2024), avoiding costly human evaluation or unreliable self-grading.

126   2.2   F UNCTIONAL B ENCHMARKS
127
128   Data contamination is a significant problem in creating evaluation benchmarks, as many of these
129   problems are openly available on the Internet and are likely included in the training data for large
130   models (Schaeffer, 2023; Sainz et al., 2023). Thus, the MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), AGIEval
131   (Zhong et al., 2023), OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024), and ARB (Sawada et al., 2023) benchmarks
132
      (which are all sourced from problems on the Internet) could potentially be contaminated. Therefore,
      models may achieve artificially high performance on an evaluation benchmark by memorizing the
133
      answers to the problems Magar & Schwartz (2022); Ranaldi et al. (2023).
134
135   A straightforward way of avoiding data contamination issues is to utilize problems unavailable on
136   the Internet. However, even if problems are not currently part of model training data, it is unrealistic
137   to expect them to remain inaccessible. At the same time, it is costly to rely on the continuous human
138
      development of new datasets.
139   Srivastava et al. (2024) attempts to alleviate this data contamination issue by creating functional
140   variations of the MATH dataset, where new problems can be generated simply by changing numeric
141   parameters, yielding different solutions. They observe a significant discrepancy in models’ perfor-
142   mance between standard benchmarks and these new variations. We recognize the potential of this
143   idea and have adapted it to our more challenging dataset. We have altered the variables, constants,
144
      and phrasing of many Putnam questions while preserving their overall difficulty and requirements
      for logical and mathematical reasoning.

147   2.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
148
      Several approaches have been proposed to reduce the reliance of model evaluations on box-able
149
      answers, particularly in domains like free-form writing or translation where unique answers do not
150   exist Leiter et al. (2022); Opitz & Frank (2021). Historically, tasks such as translation and natural
151   language generation, which lack a single correct answer, have used more flexible metrics, includ-
152   ing n-gram match (Lin, 2004), model-based Guerreiro et al. (2023), embedding proximity (Zhang
153   et al., 2020), paraphrasing (Thompson & Post, 2020), generation as an evaluator (Yuan et al., 2021),
154   and information alignment (Deng et al., 2021). However, these metrics are not designed to assess
155   reasoning ability or the correctness of mathematical statements.
156   When relying on boxed answers, we simply do not know how often the generated reasoning steps
157   actually support the final answer. For evaluating reasoning abilities, the ROSCOE suite of met-
158   rics is noteworthy as it measures various fine-grained aspects of reasoning steps such as semantic
159   consistency, logicality, informativeness, fluency, and factuality Golovneva et al. (2023). We omit
160   descriptions of each metric, but highlight that most of them rely on sentence embedding models and
161   operate on a step-by-step level. Unfortunately, the original ROSCOE metrics were predominantly
      tested on GPT-3 generations, and we find that these metrics do not provide evaluations that are

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      comparable across different models. Although fine-grained metrics like ROSCOE can be useful for
163   interpreting specific aspects of a model’s capabilities, an ideal reasoning benchmark would employ
164   a single metric that is comparable across models and highly correlated with the correctness of the
165   generated reasoning.
166
      In Huang et al. (2024), authors drew upon equivalence between language modelling and compres-
167
      sion. They demonstrated that using bits per character (BPC) to measure a model’s compression rate
168
      on several external large corpora is highly correlated with model performance on various bench-
169   marks. However, this approach has drawbacks: evaluating compression on large corpora is expen-
170   sive, and the equivalence only holds for base models, as fine-tuned models are not general-purpose
171   compressors for arbitrary text. Despite this, we suspect there would still be a relatively high correla-
172   tion for most fine-tuned models. Relatedly, Yuan et al. (2023) found that pre-training loss is strongly
173   correlated with mathematical ability for the LLaMA family Touvron et al. (2023a;b). Unfortunately,
174   creating an open benchmark using this metric is impractical due to the dependence of pre-training
175   loss on differences in pre-training data, tokenizers, and other training-specific parameters.

180   3.1   P UTNAM -AXIOM O RIGINAL DATASET
181
182   Dataset. The Putnam-AXIOM Original Dataset contains 236 problems curated from the William
183   Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition posed between 1985 and 2023. These problems were se-
184   lected based on their ability to yield a fixed final answer, to ensure compatibility with our automated
185
      evaluation. The dataset encompasses various topics within university-level mathematics categorized
      into 11 distinct domains – Geometry, Algebra, Trigonometry, Calculus, Linear Algebra, Combina-
186
      torics, Probability, Number Theory, Complex Numbers, Differential Equations and Analysis.
187
188   To maintain a consistent and rigorous evaluation, each problem retains its original exam ID, which
189   indicates its difficulty level and the topic categories. The ID format includes the exam sitting (A or
190   B) and a number (1-6) representing increasing complexity, with 1 being easiest and 6 being most dif-
191
      ficult. The dataset is formatted using LATEX to accurately capture the complex equations and symbols
      the problems employ. Additionally, we utilize Asymptote vector graphics for encoding mathemati-
192
      cal figures and diagrams to ensure language models can process visual elements directly. Further, we
193
      standardized the placement of boxed answers by relocating them to the end of each solution string
194   to minimize unintended emergent behaviors leading to evaluations that are less “harsh” or prone to
195   penalizing the model for formatting deviations rather than actual comprehension.
196
197
      Modified Boxing. Given the complex nature of certain Putnam questions, some problems do not
      lend themselves to simple, singular boxed final answers. Instead, they often include conditions,
198
      multiple possible answers, varied answer formats and elaborate proofs. These original questions
199
      would have necessitated costly and difficult human evaluations which we seek to avoid. To address
200   this, we modified these questions by adding a trivial next step to the original questions, changing
201   the solution accordingly. This additional step was designed so as to ensure that solvers reached the
202   same conclusions and insights necessary to solve the problem, but then output a single boxed final
203   answer. We provide an example of such a change in Figure 1. By incorporating this minor modifica-
204   tion, we preserved the inherent difficulty and complexity of the original problems while making the
205   answers suitable for automated evaluation. Furthermore, since Putnam proof-based problems often
206   test different reasoning abilities than Putnam answer-based problems, modified boxing allows us to
207   provide a more comprehensive test.

214   AXIOM Original providing an effective way of evaluating models that have been trained on the
215   entire internet by taking advantage of weaknesses in model memorization. These variations are
      classified into two types.

264
265   Using the LM Harness Evaluation framework (Gao et al., 2024), we evaluated several open-source
266   and proprietary SOTA LLMs. Models were prompted to provide answers in \boxed format, which
267   were then compared to Putnam ground truths with an exact final answer match. We evaluated the

      3.4    P ROXY R EASONING M ETRICS
303
304   We explore two kinds of reasoning metrics. The first are based on teacher forcing (Jiang et al., 2023;
305   Lamb et al., 2016), where the ground truth solution is fed into the model. The model then predicts
306   the next token conditioned on the ground truth solution rather than its previous generations as is the
307   case with auto-regressive generation. The second group of reasoning metrics are those proposed by
308   ROSCOE (Golovneva et al., 2023).

311   as s1 , s2 , . . . , sN , let ŝ1 , ŝ2 , . . . , ŝN be the tokens predicted by the model under teacher forcing. We
312   explore the following teacher forcing metrics:

340   The main limitation of the teacher forcing approach is the dependency on the ground truth solution.
341   Models are often finetuned for a specific style or problem solving approach (such as tool use or code
342   generation). In this case, we would expect that teacher forcing metrics would under represent the

344
      ROSCOE: The ROSCOE suite offers 18 distinct metrics, each tailored to assess a different facet of
345
      reasoning as described by Golovneva et al. (2023). These metrics are broadly categorized into four
346   groups. The first category, semantic alignment, focuses on identifying relationships between con-
347   cepts that share the same or similar meanings. Metrics in this category typically examine reasoning
348   on a step-by-step basis. In contrast, semantic similarity metrics evaluate the problem and solution
349   holistically. Logical inference metrics, utilizing a specially trained model (Laurer et al., 2024), de-
350   tect contradictions between reasoning steps. Lastly, language coherence is assessed by evaluating
351   model outputs using the perplexity score from GPT-2 Large (Radford et al., 2019) and a grammar
352   model (Krishna et al., 2020). We use the code provided by the authors as is to evaluate these metrics.
353   Metric Evaluation: Given the challenging nature of Putnam-AXIOM and the poor performance of
354   existing models, we opted to test the proposed proxy metrics on the MATH dataset instead. For a
355   metric to be effective as a benchmark, its evaluations must be comparable across different models.
356   To generate evaluation data, we utilized 15 open-source models, ranging from 7 billion to 70 billion
357   parameters, which exhibit a wide range of performance across the 7 different MATH datasets. We
358   then compared the proxy metric evaluations with each model’s boxed accuracy for each dataset. A
359   high correlation between the proxy metric and boxed accuracy indicates a better proxy.2 Our results,
360
      including the raw correlations for each metric in Table 4, are presented in the Appendix.

423   4.2   LLM E RROR A NALYSIS
424
425   Though we used automated evaluations for efficiency, a manual review of model responses on
426   Putnam-AXIOM Original provides deeper insights into models’ reasoning and errors. We selected
427   the two best-performing models, GPT-4o and OpenAI o1-preview, as they likely exhibit the strongest
428
      reasoning abilities. Our goal is to analyze this reasoning in greater depth.
429   OpenAI o1-preview Performance: Out of all models, we see that OpenAI o1-preview performed
430   the best on Putnam-AXIOM Original, receiving 41.9% boxed accuracy (99/236) while other mod-
431   els received less than 20%. Analyzing the answers, we see that most of the OpenAI o1-preview
      responses followed generally the same logical path as the ground truth solution. However, several


                                                      8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      of these questions contained logical mistakes and inconsistencies. The biggest discrepancy between
433   model responses and the ground-truth solution was a general lack of mathematical rigor. Whereas
434   the ground truth solution will make claims to advance its solution then prove those claims step-by-
435   step, o1-preview will often make and use claims without justification. While this does succeed in
436   getting to the correct boxed final answer, these unjustified claims would receive little credit when
437   marked by a human grader. A large part of the difficulty of mathematical reasoning is being logi-
438   cally airtight throughout the entire solution; thus, though o1-preview shows promise, there are still
439   evident flaws in its mathematical reasoning abilities. In several solutions like Figure 5, for instance,
440   o1-preview correctly identified the maximal or minimal value of a variable, but failed to provide
441
      sufficient proof that the value it provided was indeed the maximum or minimum.
442   GPT-4o Performance: Like the o1-preview, GPT-4o mostly followed correct logical reasoning for
443   most of its solutions. For GPT-4o, the biggest discrepancy between model responses and the ground-
444   truth solution is the same general lack of mathematical rigor throughout most of the solutions. An
445   example of this lack of rigor is shown in Figure 6, where GPT-4o makes the claim that a rectangle
446   gives the minimal area subject to a set of constraints without any justification. In addition to issues
447
      with rigor, GPT-4o also displayed logical leaps and incoherent reasoning, as displayed in Figure 7
      where the model simply assumes that an answer is correct. These logical leaps are symptomatic of
448
      an issue in the GPT-4o’s CoT reasoning, as the model prioritizes reaching the final answer rather
449
      providing a rigorous logical output.
450
451   General Analysis: Beyond GPT-4o and the o1-preview, we wanted a general overview of the
452   reasoning behaviors of models. To do so, we chose the best-performing open-source models,
453
      DeepSeek-Math-7B-RL, Qwen2-Math-7B, and NuminaMath-7B. We tend to see that open-source
      models are much more error-prone than the proprietary models we evaluated earlier. In general,
454
      we notice that open-source models are subject to the same lack of mathematical rigor. However,
455
      this rigor issue is overshadowed by major calculation errors, hallucinated/irrelevant information,
456   misunderstandings of the problem, and logical jumps. For instance, in Figure 8, NuminaMath si-
457   multaneously makes a calculation, irrelevancy, and misunderstanding error when writing the last
458   step of its solution; in Figure 9, the model makes false assumptions about functions defined in the
459   problem; in Figure 10, the model completely removes a crucial part of the problem and proceeds to
460   an incorrect final solution.

464   4.3   P ROXY M ETRICS
465
466
467   To evaluate the performance of our proxy metrics, we first test each of them on MATH, an easier
468   benchmark, as we can find models that achieve both very good and poor performance. In Table 2 we
469   compare how our chosen metrics are correlated with the boxed accuracy of the answer on MATH.
470   For the sake of brevity we only include the three most notable metrics from the ROSCOE suite:
471
      Informativeness Chain, Semantic Coverage Chain, and Perplexity Step. While it might be possible
      to combine the ROSCOE metrics together and obtain a stronger proxy metric, the straightforward
472
      approaches failed. Simple averaging performed poorly, and we could not find a weighted or sparse
473
      combination of the ROSCOE metrics without overfitting to the specific models that the weights were
474   fit on. See Table 4 in the Appendix for the full results. Despite it’s simplicity, TFA outperforms (i.e.
475   is more correlated with boxed accuracy) all of the other metrics including all of the ROSCOE metrics
476   on every category in MATH. Interestingly, the ROSCOE methods that correlate best with boxed
477   accuracy are semantic similarity metrics quantifying the degree of semantic equivalence between
478   pieces of text. BPC performs reasonably well, but still trails behind TFA.
479
      Thus we select TFA as our proxy metric of choice for Putnam-AXIOM for both its correlation with
480   accuracy and because of its low evaluation cost. In Table 1 are the results of TFA on Putnam-
481   AXIOM Original. Figure 4 showcases the relationship between TFA and accuracy on Putnam-
482   AXIOM. One potential reason for the outliers QWen2-Math-7B-Instruct and DeepSeek-Math-RL
483   might be because they were trained with reinforcement learning and thus have a different style of
484   writing compared to other models. Unfortunately we can’t evaluate TFA on proprietary models as
485   we require the log probabilities of the input tokens. It would be possible to feed the input to the
      proprietary model incrementally, but this would require an API request for every token in the input.

      5    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we present Putnam-AXIOM, a novel challenging benchmark of 236 problems from
532
      the Putnam examination for evaluating reasoning capabilities of large language models. Our dataset
533
      allows for automated evaluations with an equivalence function. While SOTA LLMs already have
534   saturated performance on benchmarks like MATH, they still struggle with successfully answering
535   questions in Putnam-AXIOM. To address potential data contamination issues, we introduce Putnam-
536   AXIOM Variations, altering the variable names, constant values, or the phrasing of the question to
537   create a potentially infinite number of problems not found anywhere on the internet. We notice that
538   for most problems, models get significantly worse on the variations than they do the corresponding
539   original questions. Our dataset fills the void opened by rapid progress in model reasoning capabili-
      ties. We hope that our benchmark will accelerate future research into artificial reasoning.

759
760   We collect and modify various problems from the William Lowell Putnam Competition to create the
761   original and variation datasets of Putnam-AXIOM. Putnam problems are created by the Mathemati-
762   cal Association of America (MAA), which is also the source of the AMC and AIME problems used
763   in the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021). Like Hendrycks et al. (2021), we do not in any form
764   seek to monetize or commercialize Putnam problems—only to utilize them for academic purposes.
765   Our use of the Putnam problems to create an evaluation dataset completely falls under the “research”
766   section of Fair Use. Indeed, according to Section 107, of the U.S. Copyright Act (USC, 1976), our
767   work certainly qualifies as Fair Use for the following reasons:
768
769         1. Our use of MAA problems is only for academic research purposes. We do not monetize or
770
               commercialize the problems.
771         2. Our use of Putnam problems as a reasoning evaluation benchmark for large language mod-
772            els is significantly different from their original use as competition problems.
773         3. Our use of Putnam problems is transformative. As detailed in Section 3 above, we have
774            transformed the questions to be answered with a single numerical or algebraic “boxed an-
775            swer” as well as created variations. We have altered all of the solutions so that the final
776            boxed answer lies at the end of the solution (so as to encourage models to explain their
777            rationale before outputting a solution). We have also standardized the solutions: If there
778
               are many solutions given, we only use the first; if there are any references irrelevant to
               mathematics necessary to understand and solve the problem (such as comments like “Com-
779
               municated by ...”), we have removed those.
780
781         4. Our use of Putnam problems to construct a benchmark has no effect on the demand for or
782
               supply of Putnam problems in the William Lowell Putnam Competition. The existence of
               our dataset does not alter the value of the original problems—as those are already freely

               available online—nor does it influence the market of future competitors/problem writers.

       Several questions in Putnam-AXIOM are binary, meaning that the question inherently has two pos-
       sible answers. These include true/false questions, questions about divergence or convergence, or
1265
       questions about the winner of a two-player game. These questions make up 26 of the 262 question
1266
       in Putnam-AXIOM Original; of the 60 questions of Putnam-AXIOM Variation, binary questions
1267   make up 8. We refer to all questions that are not binary as “complex” questions.
1268
1269   Given the guessable nature of these questions and our answer-matching evaluation method, models
1270
       have a much higher chance of randomly guessing the right answer on these questions. To dis-
       cern whether the inclusion of these guessable questions significantly affects the overall difficulty of
1271
       Putnam-AXIOM, we conducted an analysis of the accuracy of various models with and without the
1272
       binary questions, with the overall accuracies in Figure 11.
1273
1274   We see that, with the exception of Qwen2 Math 7B, almost all models have a higher accuracy on
1275   Putnam-AXIOM with its binary questions than without, meaning that guessing is contributing to
1276
       their success to some extent. However, we see that on the more advanced models—Qwen2 Math
       7B, GPT 4, and Claude Sonnet 3.5—the gap between the accuracies on the entire dataset and the
1277
       accuracies on only complex questions is much smaller. This is likely because these models are ca-
1278
       pable enough that they successfully answer a similar percentage of complex questions and binary
1279   questions; less advanced models get significantly fewer complex questions correct than binary ques-
1280   tions, so we see a large accuracy gap. Based on the results of this experiment, we’ve decided to use
1281   only the complex questions for most of our evaluations such as in Figure 3.
