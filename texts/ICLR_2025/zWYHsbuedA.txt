009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Backdoor attacks are among the most prominent security threats to deep learn-
012            ing models. Traditional backdoors leverage static trigger patterns, such as a red
013            square patch. They can be removed by existing defense techniques. However,
014            recent backdoor attacks use semantic features as the trigger. Existing techniques
015            largely fall short when facing such backdoors. In this paper, we propose a novel
016
               backdoor mitigation technique, MARTINI, that effectively mitigates various back-
               doors. It features a specially designed trigger reverse-engineering method for
017
               constructing backdoor samples that have a similar attack effect as the injected
018
               backdoor across a spectrum of attacks. Using the samples derived from MARTINI,
019            paired with the correct labels, in training can remove injected backdoor effects
020            in deep learning models. Our evaluation on 14 types of backdoor attacks in im-
021            age classification shows that MARTINI can reduce the attack success rate (ASR)
022            from 96.56% to 5.17% on average, outperforming 12 state-of-the-art backdoor re-
023            moval approaches, which at best reduce the ASR to 26.56%. It can also mitigate
024            backdoors in self-supervised learning and object detection.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Deep learning is widely used in various critical applications, such as autonomous driving (Cao et al.,
029
      2021), face recognition (Parkhi et al.), and disease diagnosis (Li et al., 2014). Despite their near-
030   perfect performance on these tasks, it is not difficult for attackers to manipulate the behavior of deep
031   learning systems and induce attack-intended output. For example, backdoor vulnerabilities in deep
032   neural networks can be triggered by adding backdoor triggers to inputs, causing misclassification to
033   a target label (Gu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018b).
034
      A common backdoor employs static trigger patterns, such as a small square patch with a solid
035
      color (Gu et al., 2019). These trigger patterns are easy to construct and can be easily learned by
036   deep learning models during training, as they are simple features. However, their distinctive features
037   make them easily distinguishable from benign features of the original learning task. many defense
038   techniques are able to successfully remove backdoor effects from attacked models (Wu & Wang,
039   2021; Li et al., 2023; 2021a; Zhu et al., 2023a).
040
      However, a clear separation between backdoor features and clean-task features is not a necessary
041
      condition for a successful backdoor attack. There is a body of semantic backdoor attacks that mod-
042   ify the entire input, making changes either closely relevant to the main content or visually invisi-
043   ble (Chen et al., 2017; Barni et al., 2019). Different perturbations are applied to different inputs
044   based on the input content. For example, the Deep Feature Space Trojan (DFST) (Cheng et al.,
045   2021) leverages a generative adversarial network (GAN) to inject a certain style (e.g., sunrise color
046   style) into the input. WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) leverages elastic image warping to deform an
047   image through distortion transformation (e.g., distorting straight lines). The unique nature of these
048   attacks renders most existing solutions less effective.
049   In this paper, we propose a novel backdoor mitigation technique through trigger reverse-engineering
050   and model hardening. Specifically, we introduce MARTINI, which (re)constructs backdoor samples
051   from backdoored models that closely resemble the injected backdoor effects. The constructed
052   samples, paired with correct labels, are subsequently utilized for training the potentially backdoored
053   model. MARTINI can model the trigger function for a wide range of backdoors. It manipulates
      abstract features instead of raw pixels to transform the input and achieve the backdoor effect,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      inducing targeted misclassification. Specifically, given the feature representation of a clean input
055   (from a pre-trained encoder), MARTINI leverages a unique transformation layer to mutate the
056   feature representation. Its novel design allows us to express a wide range of attacks through
057   feature mutation (as formally explained in Section 4.2). We use gradient descent to update the
058   transformation layer so that the feature perturbation, as defined by the layer, can change the model’s
059   classification to a target label. As a result, the trained transformation layer captures the backdoor
060   vulnerability in the model, if any.
061   Our contributions are summarized as follows.
062
063          • We propose a novel and effective technique, MARTINI, for mitigating hidden backdoors
064
               intentionally injected by adversaries.
             • We develop a general formulation of backdoor trigger functions using a novel transfor-
065
               mation layer. This design allows us to model a wide range of existing attacks, and con-
066
               sequently, training with our generated backdoor samples can improve model robustness
067            against those attacks.
068          • We evaluate MARTINI on 14 types of backdoor attacks in image classification tasks. Our
069            method can reduce the attack success rate (ASR) from 96.56% to 5.17% on average, sur-
070            passing 12 state-of-the-art backdoor removal techniques, which at best reduce the ASR
071            to 26.56%. We also conduct experiments on two additional computer vision tasks: self-
072            supervised learning and object detection. For self-supervised learning, MARTINI success-
073            fully reduces the ASR from 97.17% to 8.99%, whereas the best baseline only reduces it to
074            29.44%. For object detection, MARTINI reduces the ASR from 97.06% to 5.79%, signifi-
075
               cantly lower than the baselines, which achieve 34.56% at best. We further apply MARTINI
               to natural language processing models and demonstrate its generalizability in mitigating
076
               backdoors in other domains. Additionally, our adaptive attacks against MARTINI validate
077
               its robustness against knowledgeable adversaries.

      2   T HREAT M ODEL
081
082
083   Attack Goal and Capabilities. The attacker aims to inject backdoors into deep learning models so
084   that any input with the attacker-chosen trigger will be misclassified to a target output. The attacker
085   can utilize a variety of backdoors that either use a patch-like pattern, such as BadNets (Gu et al.,
086
      2019) and Dynamic attack (Salem et al.), or apply semantically relevant perturbations that cover
      almost the entire input area, such as DFST (Cheng et al., 2021), WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021),
087
      and Filter attack (Liu et al., 2019). The backdoor can flip any sample from any class to a target
088
      class, known as a universal backdoor (Gu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020), or flip
089   samples from a particular victim class to a target class, known as a label-specific backdoor (Wang
090   et al., 2019; Salem et al.; Liu et al., 2019). The attacker can inject backdoors into models either by
091   poisoning the training data or by directly constructing a trojaned model to be published on online
092   platforms (e.g., Huggingface).
093
      Defense Goal and Capabilities. Given a pre-trained model, the goal of the defense is to mitigate
094
      potential backdoors in the model without knowing the backdoor types. The defense process should
095   not affect the model’s normal functionality, e.g., without sacrificing much accuracy. The defender
096   has access to a subset of the clean training dataset (5%) (Li et al., 2021a; Tao et al., 2022a). The
097   defender has full control over modifying the model, such as updating model weight parameters,
098   removing neurons, etc. The updated model by the defender is not accessible to attackers.

126   3.1   L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING T ECHNIQUES
127
128   Relying on Neuron Isolation. As backdoor attacks have a different goal from the original learning
129
      task, certain parts of backdoored models might be used specifically for achieving the attack goal, i.e.,
      causing the target misclassification when the trigger is presented. Based on this assumption, sev-
130
      eral existing defense techniques aim to identify and remove the neurons compromised by backdoor
131
      attacks (Liu et al., 2018a; Li et al., 2023). For example, ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021) first identi-
132   fies compromised neurons whose weight values are exceptionally sensitive and then prunes these
133   neurons. RNP (Li et al., 2023) removes neurons that cause a large loss increase when reversing the
134   original training objective, i.e., enlarging (instead of decreasing) the classification loss on clean data.
135
      However, backdoor attacks may not activate a particular set of neurons. For instance, the DFST at-
136
      tack was designed specifically to reduce the identification of possible compromised neurons. During
137
      the attack, it applies a technique (Liu et al., 2019) to locate compromised neurons and then mitigates
138   their presence, a process called detoxification. This process can significantly degrade the defense
139   performance of existing techniques relying on neuron isolation. For example, after three rounds of
140   detoxification, ANP can only reduce the attack success rate of DFST to 90.11%, with more than a
141   7% clean accuracy drop, which nearly fails to mitigate backdoors. This type of backdoor defense,
142   which relies on neuron isolation, is less effective when backdoor-related neurons are interleaved
143   with those for the original tasks, especially against attacks like those in Figure 1.
144   Assuming Feature Separation. Backdoor behavior may not be distinctive from clean-task behavior
145   at the neuron level, as discussed earlier. It could be distributed across all layers and neurons in deep
146   learning models. However, backdoor features could still be quite different from those of clean data.
147   Another line of defenses assumes that by focusing on the original clean task, the model can “forget”
148   the backdoor behavior (Zhu et al., 2023b; Min et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2021). For example, FT-
149   SAM (Zhu et al., 2023a) utilizes an optimization method called sharpness-aware minimization for
150   fine-tuning the backdoored model on clean data, aiming to instruct the model to focus on the clean
151   task. NAD (Li et al., 2021a) applies knowledge distillation techniques to extract a clean student
152
      model from the backdoored teacher model. The distillation process is also guided by the clean data
      to ensure the main task performance.
153
154   The assumption of feature separation however is not a necessary condition for backdoor attacks.
155   A very recent attack, COMBAT (Huynh et al., 2024), only uses low-frequency components as the
156   trigger, which are the channels where normal clean features lie. Defense methods that assume feature
157   separation of backdoor attacks are less effective against such an attack. For instance, FT-SAM and
158
      NAD can only reduce the attack success rate of COMBAT to 67.33% and 42.65%, respectively.
159   Less Powerful Trigger Modeling. Trigger inversion is an approach that reverse-engineers the in-
160   jected trigger from backdoor attacks (Tao et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2022). This approach requires
161   modeling the trigger function to find a set of parameters resembling the injected backdoor. For exam-
      ple, NC (Wang et al., 2019) uses two input-size matrices to denote which pixel and how much of the


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      pixel value should be changed. The inverted trigger can then be used to unlearn the backdoor effect
163   by adding it to clean inputs and training them with the correct labels. ABS (Liu et al., 2019) lever-
164   ages a simple convolutional kernel to model possible changes by attacks, such as those in Figure 1.
165
166
      These existing inversion techniques cover only a very limited number of possible attacks. NC-like
      methods are mainly designed for patch-type backdoors and are not capable of modeling style-based
167
      attacks such as DFST (Cheng et al., 2021). The formulation of ABS is also not general enough
168
      for modeling various attacks. For example, when using ABS to reverse-engineer the trigger from
169   the SIG attack (Barni et al., 2019), the inverted trigger achieves only a 39% attack success rate,
170   compared to the injected trigger’s 93%, indicating the inverted trigger does not resemble the injected
171   one. Therefore, using this trigger in unlearning cannot mitigate the backdoor. In fact, the attack
172   success rate remains 85.29% after applying ABS to purify the backdoored model.

202   transformation layer maximizing the attack ability that denotes various injected backdoors.
203   The generated samples paired with the correct labels are then used to update the victim model’s
204   weights to remove those backdoors. The training terminates when the model converges.

238   (i.e., R, G, B channels). For instance, an input x0 may have all small values (e.g., 10) on the R
239   channel, but another input x1 has all large values (e.g., 200). A slightly larger transformation on

241   optimization to find a valid solution for x1 as it can be quickly out of the range. To facility an easier
242

243   the same mean and standard deviation of pixel values for a particular channel (e.g., the R channel).

454   Experiment Setup. Self-supervised learning generates an encoder, used for downstream tasks.
455   Backdoor attacks in this process involve adding a trigger to certain images in the training data.
456   These altered samples are made to resemble specific target-class samples in the feature space. As
457   a result, any input containing the trigger will be misclassified by a downstream classifier built on
458   the compromised encoder. We leverage two backdoor attacks in self-supervised learning: BadEn-
459
      coder (Jia et al., 2022) and D RUPE (Tao et al., 2023). The backdoor trigger is a 10x10 white square
      pattern. We use two datasets, CIFAR-10 and STL-10, as the pre-training datasets for constructing
460
      the encoder, and four datasets as the downstream tasks: GTSRB, SVHN, as well as CIFAR-10 and
461
      STL-10. We use ResNet18 and the contrastive learning algorithm SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) for
462   evaluation. Four baselines are adapted from image classification tasks: FT, NAD, ANP, and M OTH.
463
464   Evaluation Results. Table 3 reports the results. For BadEncoder, when CIFAR-10 is used as the
465   pre-training dataset, most baseline techniques fail to remove the backdoor effects on the downstream
466   tasks, with ASRs still over 80% in most cases. ANP performs better than other baselines, reducing
467   the ASRs to around 30%. MARTINI achieves the best performance, reducing the ASR to as low as
468
      5%. The observations on STL-10 are similar. For D RUPE, baseline defenses perform particularly

      is SVHN, both FT and NAD have ASRs over 80%. ANP has nearly 90% ASR on the CIFAR-

518   6 R ELATED W ORK
519   In early works, backdoor attacks use a static trigger pattern, such as patch attacks (Gu et al., 2019;
520   Chen et al., 2017). Recently, semantic backdoors have been explored by researchers. We have
521
      studied and evaluated several representative backdoor attacks in this paper, including DFST (Cheng
      et al., 2021), Blend attack (Chen et al., 2017), adaptive Blend attack (Qi et al., 2023), SIG (Barni
522
      et al., 2019), LIRA (Doan et al., 2021), WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021), Invisible attack (Li et al.,
523
      2021b), Clean Label attack (Turner et al., 2018), Narcissus (Zeng et al., 2023), COMBAT (Huynh
524   et al., 2024), and Filter attack (Liu et al., 2019).
525
526   Defense techniques against backdoor attacks can be categorized into backdoor input detection, certi-
527
      fied robustness, backdoor scanning, and backdoor removal. Backdoor input detection aims to detect
      inputs stamped with backdoor triggers (Gao et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2018). Certified robustness
528
      provides certification to the classification results of individual samples, asserting the results can be
529
      trusted even in the presence of backdoors (McCoyd et al., 2020; Xiang et al., 2021a;b). Backdoor
530   scanning focuses on identify whether a given model has been injected with backdoor (Kolouri et al.,
531   2020; Tang et al., 2021). Backdoor removal aims to eliminate injected backdoors in poisoned mod-
532   els (Liu et al., 2018a; Zeng et al.). Our evaluation in Section 5.1 demonstrates the effectiveness of
533   our method in mitigating backdoors, surpassing the 12 state-of-the-art techniques.
534
535   7 C ONCLUSION
536   We propose a novel backdoor mitigation technique, MARTINI, that can eliminate a variety of back-
537   door attacks, including the most recent advanced attacks. It features a general backdoor generation
538   method that models a spectrum of backdoors. The evaluation on various datasets and model archi-
539   tectures demonstrates that MARTINI can reduce the attack success rate of 14 backdoor attacks from
      96.56% to 5.17%, outperforming 12 existing state-of-the-art defense techniques.

820   Datasets and Models.
821
822         • CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) is an object recognition dataset with 10 classification
823           classes. It consists of 60,000 images and is divided into a training set (48,000 images), a
824           validation set (2,000 images), and a test set (10,000 images).
825         • STL-10 (Coates et al., 2011) is an image recognition dataset with 10 classification classes.
826           It consists of 5,000 training images and 8,000 test images.
827         • SVHN (Netzer et al.) is a dataset contains house digital numbers extracted from Google
828           Street View images. It has 73,257 training images and 26,032 test images. We divide the
829           original training set into 67,257 images for training and 6,000 images for validation.
830         • GTSRB (Stallkamp et al., 2012) is a German traffic sign recognition dataset with 43
831           classes. We split the dataset into a training set (35,289 images), a validation set (3,920
832           images), and a test set (12,630 images).
833         • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is a face attributes dataset. It contains 10,177 identities with
834           202,599 face images. Each image has an annotation of 40 binary attributes. We fol-
835           low (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) to select 3 out of 40 attributes, i.e., Heavy Makeup, Mouth
836           Slightly Open, and Smiling, and create an 8-class classification task.
837         • TrojAI (NIST) round 4 includes 16 types of model structures such as Incep-
838           tionV3 (Szegedy et al.), DenseNet121 (Huang et al., 2017), SqueezeNet (Iandola
839           et al.), etc. The task of these models is to recognize synthetic street traffic signs with be-
840           tween 15 and 45 classes. Input images are constructed by compositing a foreground object,
841           e.g., a synthetic traffic sign, with a random background images from five different dataset
842           such as Cityscapes (Cordts et al., 2016), KITTI (Geiger et al., 2013), Swedish Roads (Lars-
843           son et al., 2011), etc. A set of random transformations are applied during model training,
844           such as blurring, lighting, shifting, titling, etc. Adversarial training such as PGD (Madry
845
              et al., 2018) and FBF (Wong et al., 2020) is also utilized to improve model quality. We
              randomly select 34 poisoned models by filter attack from TrojAI round 4 (NIST).
846
847
      Baselines
848
849         • Fine-tuning (FT) (Li et al., 2021a) is a standard method originally proposed for transfer
850           learning. It updates a pre-trained model’s weights with a small learning rate on the training
851           set. We leverage the finetuning baseline setting in NAD (Li et al., 2021a), which adopts data
852           augmentation techniques including random crop, horizontal flipping, and cutout (DeVries
853           & Taylor) during training.
854         • Fine-pruning (FP) (Liu et al., 2018a) prunes neurons that have low activation values for a
855           set of clean samples. It then finetune the pruned model on a small set of clean samples.
856         • MCR (Zhao et al., 2020) linearly interpolates the weight parameters of two models. It also
857           includes a set of trainable parameters during the interpolation. Specifically, the following
858           equation is used to build a new model ϕθ (t).
859
860
                                 ϕθ (t) = (1 − t)2 ω1 + 2t(1 − t)θ + t2 ω2 , 0 ≤ t ≤ 1,                (10)
861            where t is the interpolation hyper-parameter ranging from 0 to 1. ω1 and ω2 are the weight
862            parameters of two pre-trained models, which are fixed. θ is a set of trainable parameters
863            that have the same shape of ω1 and ω2 . For eliminating backdoors in poisoned models,
               MCR uses the poisoned model and its finetuned version as the two endpoints (ω1 and ω2 )


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
              and trains θ on a small set of clean samples. The best t is chosen for the interpolation based
865           on the clean accuracy.
866
            • NAD (Li et al., 2021a) leverages the teacher-student structure to eliminate backdoors. It
867
              first finetunes the poisoned model on 5% of the training set. It uses this finetuned model as
868
              the teacher network, and the poisoned model as the student network. It then aims to reduce
869           the internal feature differences between the teacher network and the student network by up-
870           dating the student network. Finally, NAD outputs the student network as the cleaned model.
871
            • ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021) is based on the observation that backdoor related neurons
872           are more sensitive to adversarial perturbations on their weights. It hence applies a mask
873           on all the neurons in the model, adversarially perturbs neuron weights to increase the
874           classification loss for a set of clean samples, and minimizes the size of mask. ANP then
875           prunes neurons with small mask values, meaning that they have been compromised by
876           backdoor attacks.
877         • ABS (Liu et al., 2019) introduces a neuron stimulation analysis to expose abnormal
878           behaviors of neurons in a deep neural network by increasing their activation values. Those
879           neurons are regarded as compromised neurons and leveraged to reverse engineer backdoor
880           triggers. ABS proposes a one-layer transformation to approximate/invert filter triggers.
881           The inverted trigger is hence utilized to remove the injected backdoor in poisoned models
882           following the unlearning procedure in NC (Wang et al., 2019).
883         • MOTH (Tao et al., 2022a) enhances model robustness by increasing the distance between
884           classes. It employs trigger inversion techniques to generate adversarial samples that bridge
885           class separations and utilizes asymmetric training to harden the model. MOTH mitigates
886           backdoor effects by disrupting the shortcut connection between victim classes and the
887
              target class.
888         • I-BAU (Zeng et al., 2021) introduces a minimax formulation to mitigate the backdoor
889           effect. Specifically, this method leverages the implicit hypergradient to address the
890
              interdependence between trigger synthesis and adversarial training processes.
891         • SEAM (Zhu et al., 2023b) leverages the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting to unlearn
892           the backdoor effect through label shuffling. It then seeks to restore clean knowledge
893
              by fine-tuning with the correct labels. This method effectively disrupts the connection
              between the backdoor trigger and its target label.
894
895         • FT-SAM (Zhu et al., 2023a) represents a novel backdoor defense paradigm that integrates
896           sharpness-aware minimization with fine-tuning. This approach specifically targets neurons
897
              associated with the backdoor, aiming to reduce their influence by shrinking their norms,
              thereby mitigating the backdoor effect.
898
899         • FST (Min et al., 2024) proposes a simple yet effective technique for purifying backdoors
900
              through finetuning. It specifically promotes shifts in feature representation by actively
              diverging the classifier weights from their initially compromised states.
901
902         • RNP (Li et al., 2023) exposes and eliminates backdoor neurons through a process of
903
              unlearning followed by recovery. Specifically, RNP begins by maximizing the model’s
              error using a small subset of clean data. Afterward, it recovers the affected neurons by
904
              minimizing the model’s error on the same dataset. Neurons that remain problematic after
905
              this process are considered backdoored and are subsequently pruned.
906
907   Backdoor Attacks
908
909         • BadNets (Gu et al., 2019) is the pioneering study that first highlighted backdoor threats in
910           deep learning models. It employs a static patch, e.g., a flower placed in the corner of an
911           image, as the backdoor trigger. Any input containing this patch is then misclassified to the
912           attack target label.
913         • Dynamic attack (Salem et al.) utilizes a generative model to create dynamic patch triggers
914           that vary in pattern and location across images. This diversity enhances its stealthiness
915           against backdoor detection methods.
916         • Input-aware attack (IA) (Nguyen & Tran, 2020) improves upon dynamic backdoors by
917           generating more diverse and sample-specific triggers to evade backdoor detection. The IA
              triggers are more invisible in the input space.


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
            • Deep Feature Space Trojan (DFST) (Cheng et al., 2021) leverages a generative adver-
919           sarial network (GAN) to inject a certain style (e.g., sunrise color style) to given training
920           samples. It also introduces a detoxification procedure by iteratively training on ABS (Liu
921           et al., 2019) reverse-engineered backdoors to reduce the number of compromised neurons
922           that can be leveraged by existing scanners for successful detection. We follow the origi-
923           nal paper and poison models with two settings: one-round detoxification and three-rounds
924           detoxification.
925
            • Blend attack (Chen et al., 2017) injects a random perturbation pattern on the training
926
              samples of non-target classes and changes the ground truth labels of these samples to the
927
              target class (label 0). We use the random pattern reported in the original paper and use a
928           blend ratio of α = 0.2.
929
930         • Adaptive Blend (A-Blend) (Qi et al., 2023) refines the typical blend attack by including
931           correctly labeled trigger-planted samples to enhance backdoor learning regularization. It
932           also introduces asymmetric trigger strategies that improve the ASR and diversify the rep-
933           resentations of poisoned samples.
934
            • Sinusoidal Signal attck (SIG) (Barni et al., 2019) injects a strip-like pattern on the training
935           samples of the target class and retains the original ground truth labels. We follow the setting
936           in the original paper and generate the backdoor pattern using the horizontal sinusoidal
937           function with ∆ = 20 and f = 6. We use label 0 as the target class and poison 8% of the
938           training data in the target class.
939
940         • LIRA (Doan et al., 2021) designs a learnable trigger injection function to be used during
941
              model poisoning. Specifically, it trains a generative model to inject triggers concurrently
              with backdoor model training. LIRA utilizes the dataset itself to enhance the specificity of
942
              the backdoor triggers.
943
944         • WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) uses elastic image warping that deforms an image by ap-
945           plying the distortion transformation (e.g., distorting straight lines) as the backdoor. We
946           download three backdoored models from the official repository (Nguyen & Tran, 2021),
947           which are trained on CIFAR-10, GTSRB, and CelebA, respectively.
948
            • Invisible attack (Li et al., 2021b) leverages a generator to encode a string (e.g., the index
949
              of a target label) onto an input image. We download the pre-trained generator from the
950
              official repository (Li et al.) and use it to inject invisible backdoors following the setting in
951           the original paper.
952
953         • Clean Label attack (CL) (Turner et al., 2018) generates adversarial perturbations on the
954           training samples in the target class using an adversarailly trained model. It then injects
955           a 2 × 2 grid at the top left corner of the target-class inputs and retain their ground truth
956           labels. We use L∞ bound of 8/255 for crafting adversarial perturbations, use label 3 as the
957
              target class, and poison 50% of the training data in the target class following the official
              repository (Turner et al.).
958
959         • Narcissus (Zeng et al., 2023) introduces a clean-label backdoor attack that is both stealthy
960           and robust. Specifically, it trains a surrogate model to capture the important features from
961           the target label, which are then used as the backdoor trigger. It selectively poisons only the
962           images of the target class with this trigger, compelling the model to associate the trigger
963           with the target label without altering the labels.
964
965
            • COMBAT (Huynh et al., 2024) improves the clean-label backdoor technique beyond Nar-
              cissus by leveraging a generative model to produce the triggers. It also incorporates fre-
966
              quency features and introduces an alternative training method to enhace the learning of the
967
              backdoor trigger function and the poisoned model.
968
969         • Filter attack (Liu et al., 2019) applies Instagram filters on training samples and changes
970           the ground truth labels of these samples to the target class. There are various filters can
971           be used to poison data, such as Gotham filter, Nashville filter, Kelvin filter, Lomo filter,
              Toaster filter, etc.

991    For filter attack, we leverage pre-trained backdoored models downloaded from the TrojAI com-
992    petition (NIST). The results are reported in Figure 7. The x-axis and y-axis denote the model IDs
993    and the ASR, respectively. The bars in the light/dark colors show the ASR of injected backdoors
994
       before/after applying different defense techniques. Observe that FT (blue bars) can only repair
       half of the evaluated models (17 out of the 34 models). This is expected as backdoor attacks
995
       include clean data together with backdoored samples during training. Fine-tuning only on clean
996
       samples may not eliminate the backdoor patterns that have already been learned by backdoored
997    models. NAD leverages the teacher-student structure and treats the model from FT as the teacher
998    network. Its performance hence is limited by Fine-tuning. This can be observed from the orange
999    bars in Figure 7. NAD is only able to eliminate five more backdoors (with a total of 21 models).
1000   ANP has limited performance on TrojAI models, with only 15 poisoned models being repaired. The
1001   TrojAI backdoored models were trained by NIST (NIST), and different training strategies including
1002   random transformations, adversarial training, etc., were employed to make injected backdoors more
1003   robust and hard to detect. These strategies may reduce the sensitivity of individual neurons on
1004   backdoor patterns. ANP is hence not able to identify compromised neurons and fails to remove
1005
       injected backdoors. This observation is consistent with the results on DFST backdoors that apply
       detoxification to reduce compromised neurons.
1006
1007   ABS can only repair 15 models. As the injected backdoors in TrojAI models are label-specific,
1008   ABS may not be able to identify the correct victim-target class pair. The inverted triggers fail to
1009   expose the injected backdoor behaviors. Unlearning on those triggers hence cannot repair models.
1010   M OTH can eliminate more backdoors than other baselines with 28 fixed models. As discussed in the
1011
       motivation section, semantic backdoors perturb all pixels on the input and are dynamic, while M OTH
       focuses on patch-like static backdoors. It can raise the bar for semantic backdoors to some extent
1012
       but still fails to repair 6 TrojAI backdoored models. MARTINI, on the other hand, can eliminate
1013
       all the backdoors with an average ASR down to 0.55%, outperforming the others. The accuracy
1014   of backdoored models before and after repair is shown in Figure 8 (in Appendix). Overall, all the
1015   approaches incur a very small accuracy degradation on average (< 0.3%), except for ANP (1.16%).
1016   MARTINI has the smallest accuracy degradation of 0.06%.

1025   images along with the target label for poisoning. At each training iteration, it also applies the inverted
       trigger, stamps it on images, and uses the ground truth label for training. The attack then optimizes
