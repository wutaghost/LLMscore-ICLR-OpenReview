011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Visual Place Recognition (VPR) addresses the retrieval problem in large-scale
014            geographic image databases through feature representations. Recent approaches
015            have leveraged visual foundation models and have proposed novel feature aggre-
016            gations. However, these methods have failed to grasp the core concepts of founda-
017            tional models, such as leveraging extensive training sets, and have also neglected
018            the potential of classical feature aggregations, such as GeM and NetVLAD, for
019            low-dimensional representations. Building on these insights, we revive classic
020
               aggregation methods and create more fundamental VPR models, abbreviated Su-
               perPlace. First, we introduce a supervised label alignment method that combines
021
               grid partitioning and local feature matching. This allows models to be trained
022
               on diverse VPR datasets within a unified framework, similar to the design prin-
023            ciples of foundation models. Second, we introduce G2 M, a compact feature ag-
024            gregation with two GeMs, in which one GeM learns the principal components of
025            feature maps along the channel direction and calibrates the other GeM’s output.
026            Third, we propose the secondary fine-tuning (FT2 ) strategy for NetVLAD-Linear
027            (NVL). NetVLAD first learns feature vectors in a high-dimensional space and
028            then compresses them into a low-dimensional space using a single linear layer.
029            G2 M excels in large-scale applications requiring rapid response and low latency,
030            while NVL-FT2 is optimized for scenarios demanding high precision across a
031
               broad range of conditions. Extensive experiments (12 test sets, 14 previous meth-
               ods, and 11 tables) highlight our contributions and demonstrate the superiority of
032
               SuperPlace. Specifically, SuperPlace-G2 M achieves state-of-the-art results with
033
               only one-tenth of the feature dimensions compared to recent methods. Moreover,
034            SuperPlace-NVL-FT2 holds the top rank on the MSLS challenge leaderboard. We
035            have submitted a ranking screenshot, the source code, and the original experimen-
036            tal records in the supplementary materials.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Visual Place Recognition (VPR), also known as Visual Geo-localization, involves finding the most
043   similar image to a query image within a large-scale geographic image database (Berton et al., 2022b).
044   VPR has long been studied in computer vision (Sattler et al., 2018), robotics (Lowry et al., 2015),
045   and remote sensing (Psomas et al., 2024) due to its wide applications in augmented reality, robot
046   navigation, and autonomous driving. Previous research has identified several challenges in VPR, in-
047   cluding large database scales (Berton et al., 2022a), viewpoint shifts (Berton et al., 2021a), repeated
048   structures (Torii et al., 2015), structural modifications (Arandjelovic et al., 2016), occlusions (Liu
049   et al., 2021), visual scale differences (Fu et al., 2022), illumination changes (Liu et al., 2024), and
050
      seasonal transitions (Toft et al., 2020).
051   Early VPR research aggregated hand-crafted local features such as SURF (Bay et al., 2008) into
052   global features through algorithms like Bag-of-Words (BoW) (Angeli et al., 2008) or Vector of
053   Locally Aggregated Descriptors (VLAD) (Jégou et al., 2010). However, most challenging problems
      are difficult to solve within the framework based on hand-crafted features.

054
      In the past decade, VPR primarily leveraged location datasets and neural networks with differen-
055   tiable aggregation/pooling to map images into an embedding space, effectively distinguishing im-
056   ages from different locations (Arandjelovic et al., 2016). New VPR datasets have been continually
057   proposed to overcome challenging problems (such as generalization (Ali-bey et al., 2022), training
058   scale (Berton et al., 2022a), and cross-domain (Warburg et al., 2020)). However, these datasets do
059   not fully encompass the characteristics of previous datasets and introduce a new issue: inconsistent
060   supervised label formats (Berton et al., 2022a). At the same time, different methods have also been
061   proposed to aggregate multi-channel feature maps into global feature vectors. Generalized Mean
062   Pooling (GeM) and NetVLAD (Arandjelovic et al., 2016) were proposed, which achieved good re-
063
      sults in different dimension ranges (less than 4k dimensions or larger than 30k dimensions). The
      feature dimension strongly correlates with the retrieval speed and recall of VPR.
064
065   In the past year, Visual Foundation Models (VFMs) (Oquab et al., 2023; Kirillov et al., 2023;
066   Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) have developed rapidly, and DINOv2 has been widely used
067   in VPR (Keetha et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024b). VFMs have utilized multiple large-scale visual
068   datasets, knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), and other techniques to provide powerful
069
      feature representation capabilities. However, these VPR studies have only used a VFM as a pre-
      trained model on a single dataset and have not embraced the core principles of VFMs, such as
070
      aligning multiple datasets for model training. Additionally, these studies also proposed some novel
071
      feature aggregation methods: BoQ (Ali-bey et al., 2024), SALAD (Izquierdo & Civera, 2024a), and
072   SPGM (Lu et al., 2024a). The feature dimensions of these methods were approximately 4k-12k.
073   They were expected to perform better than GeM, and these works claimed to produce better results
074   with lower feature dimensions than NetVLAD. However, through some tentative experiments, we
075   found that classic methods from ten years ago are still competitive. This prompted us to improve
076   these classic methods instead of following recent aggregations introduced in the past year.
077
      In this paper, we not only use VFMs as pre-trained models but also train on multiple datasets,
078   mirroring their training approach through a novel supervised alignment method. In particular, we
079   transform the distance metric into class labels through a grid partition in the Universal Transverse
080   Mercator (UTM) coordinate and check the similarity within the labels using local feature match-
081   ing. Beyond staying updated with the latest techniques, we also improve two feature aggregations
082   from over a decade ago to revive their superiority in the era of VFMs. Specifically, we propose a
083   generalized channel attention module for GeM and the secondary fine-tuning (FT2 ) for NetVLAD-
084   Linear (NVL). With the same training set, these improved aggregations achieve comparable or even
085   superior results to recent approaches while requiring lower dimensions and fewer parameters. Our
086
      contributions are highlighted as follows:
087   1) We propose a supervised label alignment method to train VPR models using multiple datasets like
088   other foundation models. Specifically, the coarse classification labels are first determined by a grid
089   partition in the UTM coordinate, and then fine labels are selected by using local feature matching.
090   2) We propose a compact feature aggregation with two GeM pooling layers, G2 M, in which one
091   GeM learns the principal components of feature maps along the channel direction and calibrates the
092   other GeM’s output.
093
      3) We propose the secondary fine-tuning method for NetVLAD-Linear, called NVL-FT2 , which first
094
      learns feature representations in high-dimensional space and then compresses the representations
095
      into low-dimensional space using a single linear layer.
096
097   4) Extensive comparative and univariate experiments demonstrate our contributions and the excel-
098   lence of SuperPlace. SuperPlace-G2 M achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results using only one-tenth
099
      of the feature dimensions of recent methods. SuperPlace-NVL-FT2 holds the top rank on the MSLS
      Challenge leaderboard, significantly outperforming recent methods.

102   2   R ELATED W ORK
103
104   Early VPR research primarily relied on hand-crafted features, including global features extracted
105   directly (like GIST (Milford & Wyeth, 2012)) and features derived from clustering local descriptors.
106   Clustering algorithms such as BoW (Angeli et al., 2008), Fisher Vector (FV) (Csurka & Perronnin,
107   2010), and VLAD (Jégou et al., 2010) were used in conjunction with local feature extraction algo-
      rithms like SIFT (Lowe, 2004), SURF (Bay et al., 2008), and ORB (Rublee et al., 2011).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      With the advent of deep learning, learning-based features have largely supplanted hand-crafted fea-
109   tures. Arandjelovic et al. (2016) introduced the Pittsburgh-250k dataset along with a differentiable
110   VLAD aggregation module and optimized a pre-trained (PT) model using a triplet loss function to
111   achieve VPR. NetVLAD (Arandjelovic et al., 2016) laid the foundation for learning-based VPR.
112
      Training sets. VPR training sets were primarily obtained from images captured by Google Street
113
      View (GSV) (Anguelov et al., 2010) and car-mounted cameras. Unlike Pittsburgh-250k, which was
114
      collected using similar cameras, MSLS (Warburg et al., 2020) was gathered from different cameras
115   and included challenging scenes such as variations in weather, seasons, and lighting. Berton et al.
116   (2022a) proposed the SF-XL dataset containing millions of images for training and verifying VPR
117   models in large-scale scenarios. GSV-Cities (Ali-bey et al., 2022) and SF-XL were concurrently de-
118   veloped datasets based on GSV. GSV-Cities contained more diverse urban samples but had relatively
119   sparse samples, while SF-XL comprehensively covered the street scenes of San Francisco. There is
120   still no consensus on the best training set, and almost no studies have utilized multiple datasets. To
121   our knowledge, SALAD-CM (Izquierdo & Civera, 2024b) is the only method, apart from ours, that
122   uses multiple datasets (GSV and MSLS).
123   Aggregation Layers. Like NetVLAD, the other classic aggregation algorithms (Hou et al., 2018;
124   Peng et al., 2021) have been transformed into differentiable modules for end-to-end training.
125   Although these NetVLAD-inspired modules have demonstrated good performance, their high-
126   dimensional characteristics limit database size and retrieval efficiency. Generalized Mean (GeM)
127   pooling (Radenović et al., 2018) was introduced as a simpler alternative to NetVLAD, providing
128   low-dimensional global features. This method extends global average pooling by using the p-norm
129
      of local features. Recently, three aggregation modules have been proposed: Bag-of-Queries (BoQ),
      SALAD, and SPGM. BoQ (Ali-bey et al., 2024) employed distinct learnable global queries to probe
130
      the input features through cross-attention, ensuring consistent information aggregation. SALAD
131
      (Izquierdo & Civera, 2024a) redefined the soft assignment of local features in NetVLAD as an op-
132   timal transport problem and employed the Sinkhorn algorithm to solve it. SPGM (Lu et al., 2024a)
133   applied a spatial pyramid to divide feature maps at multiple levels and then used GeM pooling. Re-
134   spectively, their optimal feature dimensions when used with DINOv2 are 12,288 for BoQ, 8,448 for
135   SALAD, and 4,096 for SPGM. Unlike recent works, we make minor improvements to demonstrate
136   the effectiveness of earlier approaches.
137
      Pre-trained Models. As in most vision tasks, pre-trained (PT) models in VPR have evolved from
138   convolutional neural networks (including residual networks) to transformers. Before 2020, works
139   such as NetVLAD and SFRS (Arandjelovic et al., 2016; Ge et al., 2020) used VGG networks as PT
140   models. In the past three years, CosPlace, MixVPR, and EigenPlaces (Berton et al., 2022a; Ali-bey
141   et al., 2023; Berton et al., 2023) adopted ResNet as their backbone architecture. Recently, Any-
142   Loc introduced DINOv2 without fine-tuning for VPR. Subsequently, SelaVPR (Lu et al., 2024b),
143   SALAD (Izquierdo & Civera, 2024a), CricaVPR (Lu et al., 2024a), and BoQ (Ali-bey et al., 2024)
144   adopted DINOv2 and fine-tuned it on the GSV-Cities dataset. The use of VFMs in VPR has been
145   limited compared to other vision tasks, compared to other vision tasks, such as segmentation (Seg-
146
      ment Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023)), depth estimation (Depth Anything (Yang et al., 2024)), and
      3D reconstruction (DUST3R (Wang et al., 2024)). Our supervised alignment method enables VPR
147
      to effectively leverage multiple datasets, thereby advancing the field in line with the latest develop-
148
      ments in VFMs.

188   Despite its effectiveness, this extractor is limited in its ability to fully capture the valuable infor-
189   mation of the multi-channel feature map. To further explore this limitation, we applied PCA to
190   reduce the channel dimension and visualized the resulting feature maps to assess their interpretabil-
191
      ity. As shown in Fig. 2, location-dependent information tends to generate strong responses, while
      location-independent information may be overemphasized or overlooked.
192
193   To address the above limitation and inspired by our visualizations, we introduce an additional branch
194   that learns the principal components of the feature map along the channel dimension to calibrate the
195   GeM pooling vector accordingly. As shown in Fig. 1, this branch consists of a new GeM pooling
196   layer, a low-rank MLP, a GELU activation, and a Sigmoid function. This kind of simple module
197
      structure has contributed to the success of methods like the Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) module
      (Hu et al., 2018) and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021). Notably, our motivation,
198
      usage, and design details differ from those of the SE module, and we refer to this new module as
199
      the Generalized Channel Attention (GCA) module. Together, the original extractor and the GCA
200   module form the improved extractor, which we call G2 M.

      3.2         S ECONDARY F INE -T UNING FOR N ET VLAD-L INEAR
203
204   NVL-FT2 represents an incremental improvement over NetVLAD, whose output feature dimension
205   is defined as C × K, where K denotes the number of cluster centers. In previous works, K has been
206   set to 64 in Arandjelovic et al. (2016) and 32 in Izquierdo & Civera (2024a). However, the global
207   features extracted by NetVLAD are characterized by excessively high dimensions, prompting earlier
208   studies to investigate two primary methods for dimension reduction: 1) employing PCA for dimen-
209   sion reduction, or 2) reducing the value of K. While the first approach introduces additional storage
210   overhead and increased computational requirements, the second results in a substantial performance
211   degradation.
212   An alternative and simpler strategy is to follow NetVLAD with a linear projection layer for dimen-
213   sion reduction. This method promises reduced storage requirements and faster processing times
214   compared to PCA. Despite these theoretical advantages, our implementation of NV-Linear consis-
215   tently underperformed relative to NetVLAD-PCA. This might explain why it has not been adopted
      or proposed in prior work.

236   linear layer are fine-tuned simultaneously in a lower-dimensional space. This training discrepancy
237
      limits the ability of NetVLAD-Linear to capture the rich high-dimensional representations.
238   To overcome this issue, we propose a secondary fine-tuning process for NetVLAD-Linear. In this
239   approach, we first fine-tune the backbone network and NetVLAD, followed by a second stage where
240   we fine-tune the linear layer for dimension reduction. Importantly, the number of parameters in-
241   volved in FT2 is minimal—accounting for just 0.11% of the entire model’s parameters, as noted in
242   our experiments. Consequently, FT2 is computationally efficient and enables faster training.

341
      Architecture. We selected DINOv2 as a pre-trained model for two reasons: (1) It provides a fair
342
      benchmark for comparing SuperPlace with recent methods, and (2) Even though DINOv2 was re-
343   leased over a year ago, it remains the most effective pre-trained model available.
344
345   Training configurations. The experiments were conducted on a server with 8 NVIDIA 4090 GPUs.
346
      Instead of the Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) approach used in SelaVPR (Lu et al., 2024b)
      and CricaVPR (Lu et al., 2024a), we adopted the fine-tuning of the last four layers (FT4) as used
347
      in SALAD (Izquierdo & Civera, 2024a). Specifically, BoQ fine-tuned only the last two layers of
348
      DINOv2 with a warm-up step. Although BoQ only adjusted the last two layers of DINOv2, it
349   introduced many parameters and a warm-up step, increasing the training time and the number of
350   training parameters, making it less efficient compared to FT4.
351
352
      Unless otherwise specified, all experimental parameters followed these settings: (1) DINOv2-B
      (Base) was used as the pre-trained model. (2) The GCA module in G2 M was set with a rank of 64,
353
      used GELU as the activation function, and had an output feature dimension of 768. (3) NV and NVL
354
      utilized 64 cluster centers, with NVL having an output feature dimension of 8192. (4) SuperPlace
355   was trained using the Adam optimizer with the learning rate set to 6 × 10−5 and the batch size
356   set to 64. (5) In Tab. 3 and 4, SuperPlace was trained with the resolution of 322 × 322 (for best
357   performance). In Tab. 5 - 11, the training resolution was set to 224 × 224 (for fast experiments).
358
      Evaluation metrics. We followed the same evaluation metric as in existing literature (Arandjelovic
359
      et al., 2016; Berton et al., 2022b), where the recall@K is measured. Recall@K is the percentage
360
      of query images for which at least one of the top-K predicted reference images falls within a pre-
361   defined threshold distance. Following common evaluation procedures, we set the threshold to 25
362   meters for the test sets with GPS label, ±10 frames for Nordland (Sünderhauf et al., 2013), and the
363   corresponding matching image for SPED (Chen et al., 2017) and AmsterTime (Yildiz et al., 2022).

      5       C ONCLUSION
514
515   This paper presents SuperPlace, a novel VPR system that integrates classical aggregation methods
516   and modern foundation models to achieve state-of-the-art performance. Specifically, we propose
517
      three contributions: 1) a supervised label alignment method that combines grid partitioning and local
      feature matching, allowing models to be trained on diverse VPR datasets within a unified framework
518
      akin to the design principles of foundation models. 2) G2 M, a compact feature aggregation with
519
      two GeM layers, in which one GeM learns the principal components of feature maps along the
520   channel direction and calibrates the other GeM output. 3) the secondary fine-tuning (FT2 ) strategy
521   for NetVLAD-Linear. NetVLAD first learns feature vectors in a high-dimensional space and then
522   compresses them into a low-dimensional space by a single linear layer. Extensive experiments
523   have validated the effectiveness of SuperPlace, with SuperPlace-G2 M achieving high performance
524   with minimal dimensions and SuperPlace-NVL-FT2 dominating the MSLS Challenge leaderboard.
525   These results highlight the strength of revisiting and refining classical methods in the era of visual
526   foundation models. In the future, we will further explore developing interpretable and open-world
527   VPR systems.

775   1) Pre-trained Models:

780           importance of leveraging advanced pre-trained models for feature extraction.
781         • DINOv2 Variants: Methods using DINOv2-L (e.g., SP-NVL-FT2 ) outperform those using

786
787         • Low-Dimensional Efficiency: SP-G2 M achieves competitive results with a feature dimen-

789           tions.

791           dimensions (e.g., 12,288 for BoQ), improving performance in scenarios with significant
792           appearance variations (e.g., day-night changes). However, these approaches face limita-
793           tions on large-scale datasets due to higher computational and memory requirements, as
794           noted in the inability to evaluate BoQ on SF-XL.
795
796   3) Training sets:
797
798         • GSV-Cities is widely used by multiple methods due to its diversity of cities and training
799
              efficiency. Models trained on this dataset are less likely to exhibit over-optimization or

801         • Models trained on the SF-XL dataset perform well on the Pitts-30k test set and the Tokyo-

805
              a crowdsourced dataset.
806         • The MSLS training set and the Pittsburgh training set have seen less frequent use recently.
807           However, our method’s results demonstrate their substantial value, particularly in enhanc-

823
824   We further explored the performance of G2 M and NVL-FT2 on other foundation architectures or
825   models. VGG and ResNet are both convolutional architectures, and the theoretical structures of
826   DINOv2 and ViT are identical, differing primarily in their improved model parameters. Therefore,
827   we believe it is sufficient to conduct generalization experiments for architectures using ResNet-50.
828   For validating the generalization capability of the foundation models, we selected the visual encoder
829   of OpenAI’s CLIP model, as it is widely recognized by researchers. As shown in Tab. 12 and 13,
830   our method achieves the same conclusions on ResNet-50 and CLIP as reported in the main text.
831   Notably, the experimental results of DINOv2 remain the best.
832   A.5                DATASET D ETAILS
833
834   Pittsburgh-250k (Arandjelovic et al., 2016) is collected from Google Street View and provides 24
835   images with different viewpoints at each place. The images in this dataset have large viewpoint
836   variations and moderate condition variations.
837
      Tokyo24/7 (Torii et al., 2017) includes 75,984 database images and 315 query images captured from
838
      urban scenes. The query images are selected from 1,125 images taken at 125 distinct places with
839   three different viewpoints and at three different times of day. Significant viewpoint and condition
840   changes (e.g., day-night transitions) are present.
841
842
      Mapillary Street-Level Sequences (MSLS) (Warburg et al., 2020) is a large-scale VPR dataset
      containing over 1.6 million images labeled with GPS coordinates and compass angles, captured
843
      from 30 cities in urban, suburban, and natural scenes over seven years. It covers various challenging
844
      visual changes due to illumination, weather, season, viewpoint, and dynamic objects. It includes
845   subsets of training, public validation (MSLS-val), and withheld test (MSLS-challenge).
846
847   Nordland (Sünderhauf et al., 2013) primarily consists of suburban and natural place images cap-
848
      tured from the same viewpoint in the front of a train across four seasons, which results in severe
      condition changes (e.g., seasons and lighting) but no viewpoint variations. Its ground truth is pro-
849
      vided by the frame-level correspondence. Following previous work (Sünderhauf et al., 2013; Wang
850
      et al., 2022), we use the dataset partition first presented in (Sünderhauf et al., 2013) for our experi-
851   ments.
852
853   AmsterTime (Yildiz et al., 2022) is a collection of over one thousand pairs of query-reference
854
      images of Amsterdam. For each pair, the query is a grayscale historical image, and its reference is a
      modern-day photo that represents the same place, as confirmed by human experts. The pairs exhibit
855
      multiple domain shifts, including changes in viewpoint, long-term temporal variations, modality
856
      differences (RGB vs. grayscale), and different camera systems. Despite its relatively small scale,
857   AmsterTime is one of the most challenging datasets available.
858
859   SPED (Chen et al., 2017) comprises low-quality, high-scene-depth images taken from CCTV cam-
860
      eras around the globe. The images in this dataset show various condition variations, such as lighting,
      weather, and seasonal changes. This dataset covers various outdoor scenes, including forest land-
861
      scapes, country roads, and urban environments.
862
863   SF-XL (Berton et al., 2022a) is a huge dataset covering San Francisco with over 41M images. Its
      test set covers the same with a less dense set of 2.8M images. Two sets of queries are used: the first

                                                                                                 16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      (test v1) is a challenging set of 1000 images from Flickr, with multiple challenges like night images
865   and photos from the sidewalk. Test v2 uses the same set of queries from San Francisco Landmark.
866
867   SVOX (Berton et al., 2021b) is a cross-domain dataset built from cross-domain VPR that evaluates
868
      multiple weather conditions. It spans the city of Oxford, with a large (single domain) database from
      GSV images: the queries are instead from the Oxford RobotCar dataset (Maddern et al., 2017),
869
      providing several weather conditions, such as overcast, rainy, sunny, snowy, and night domains.
