011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have achieved remarkable progress on
014            visual perception and linguistic interpretation. Despite their impressive capabili-
015            ties across various tasks, LVLMs still suffer from the issue of hallucination, which
016            involves generating content that is incorrect or unfaithful to the visual or textual in-
017
               puts. Traditional benchmarks, such as MME and POPE, evaluate hallucination in
               LVLMs within the scope of Visual Question Answering (VQA) using answerable
018
               questions. However, some questions are unanswerable due to insufficient infor-
019
               mation in the images, and the performance of LVLMs on such unanswerable ques-
020            tions remains underexplored. To bridge this research gap, we propose TUBench, a
021            benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the reliability of LVLMs using unan-
022            swerable questions. TUBench comprises an extensive collection of high-quality,
023            unanswerable questions that are meticulously crafted using ten distinct strate-
024            gies. To thoroughly evaluate LVLMs, the unanswerable questions in TUBench
025            are based on images from four diverse domains as visual contexts: screenshots of
026            code snippets, natural images, geometry diagrams, and screenshots of statistical
027            tables. These unanswerable questions are tailored to test LVLMs’ trustworthiness
028
               in code reasoning, commonsense reasoning, geometric reasoning, and mathemati-
               cal reasoning related to tables, respectively. We conducted a comprehensive quan-
029
               titative evaluation of 28 leading foundational models on TUBench, with Gemini-
030
               1.5-Pro, the top-performing model, achieving an average accuracy of 69.2%, and
031            GPT-4o, the third-ranked model, reaching 66.7% average accuracy, in determin-
032            ing whether questions are answerable. Furthermore, our manual analysis of the
033            model outputs reveals that: (1) Gemini-1.5-Pro provides both correct answers and
034            explanations in only 41% of cases, and (2) hallucinations are the primary cause
035            of error, accounting for 58.5% of the incorrect explanations generated by Gemini-
036            1.5-Pro. These findings highlight that TUBench presents a significant challenge
037            to current LVLMs, and offers a new perspective for evaluating hallucinations and
038            trustworthiness through the lens of unanswerable questions. TUBench is available
039
               at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TUBench-1F20.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are endowed with the ability to process and understand both vi-
044   sual and textual data by aligning their representations in a shared latent embedding (Jia et al., 2021).
045   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
046   2023a), have shown remarkable zero-shot capabilities across various downstream NLP tasks due to
047   their strong instruction-following abilities. Building on the remarkable success of LLMs in NLP,
048   recent research has increasingly focused on integrating LLMs as text encoders and aligning them
049   with visual encoders through visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023a). This approach has led to
050
      the advent of powerful LVLMs (Zhu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024).
051   Despite the significant success, LLMs are prone to generate unfaithful, nonsensical, or factually
052   incorrect text, a phenomenon referred to as ‘hallucination’ (Ji et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; 2024c).
053   Similarly, VLMs may generate descriptions or captions that include objects either inconsistent with
      or entirely absent from the input image, termed as ‘object hallucination’ (Rohrbach et al., 2018).

054
      Extensive research has been dedicated to benchmarking the detection of hallucinations within the
055   domain of VLMs (Li et al., 2023b; Fu et al., 2023), as well as developing methods to mitigate
056   hallucination in VLMs (Yin et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024).
057
058
      Researchers have discovered that LLMs may hallucinate unintended content not only when answer-
      ing answerable questions, but also when faced with unanswerable questions (Yin et al., 2023b; Sun
059
      et al., 2024). Even when the provided context lacks the necessary information to answer a question,
060
      LLMs may still confidently provide a seemingly correct response. This is more problematic than
061   generating an obviously nonsensical answer, particularly in fields with high reliability requirements,
062   such as medical diagnosis and autonomous driving. Therefore, when faced with unanswerable ques-
063   tions, models are expected to clearly indicate their inability to provide an answer rather than fabricate
064   a plausible-looking but incorrect answer. In the field of VLMs, unanswerable questions typically
065   arise when an image lacks the necessary information to provide an answer, which remains under-
066   studied. Previous work often used heuristic rules to automatically construct unanswerable questions
067   in VQA. For example, (Ray et al., 2016; Miyai et al., 2024) generated unanswerable questions by
068   pairing a question with a random image from the same dataset. Obviously, unanswerable questions
069
      constructed by this method may be either entirely unrelated or only loosely related to the corre-
      sponding images. As a result, VLMs can easily recognize these questions as unanswerable, mean-
070
      ing these datasets may not adequately assess VLMs’ ability to abstain from answering when faced
071
      with unanswerable questions. Thus, it is necessary to propose a benchmark featuring high-quality
072   unanswerable questions in VQA to (1) provide a new perspective for assessing the trustworthiness
073   and hallucination of VLMs, complementing benchmarks based on answerable questions, and (2)
074   promote the development of reliable VLMs.
075
      To this end, we introduce TUBench, a comprehensive Benchmark designed to evaluate the
076
      Trustworthiness of LVLMs when faced with Unanswerable questions. Our benchmark includes
077
      four distinct datasets: Unanswerable Code Reasoning (UCR), Unanswerable VQA (UVQA), Unan-
078   swerable GeoQA (UGeoQA), and Unanswerable TabMWP (UTabMWP). Among these, UCR and
079   UVQA are entirely new datasets created by us with answers to questions restricted to ‘Yes’, ‘No’,
080   or ‘Unanswerable’. UGeoQA and UTabMWP are extensions of GeoQA (Chen et al., 2021) and
081   TabMWP (Lu et al., 2023), respectively, with unanswerable questions added. Concretely, for the
082   UCR dataset, we begin by creating a screenshot of a code snippet and constructing Yes/No questions
083   based on this image. Next, we introduce uncertainties into the code screenshots by adding random
084   functions, omitting variable initialization, and deliberately leaving certain lines of code incomplete.
085   As shown in Figure 1, these modifications enable us to construct new code snippet screenshots and
086
      formulate unanswerable questions accordingly. For UVQA, annotators first create Yes/No questions
      based on images from the MSCOCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014). They are then guided to formulate
087
      unanswerable questions using the following strategies: (1) the information required to answer the
088
      question is occluded in the image, (2) the details necessary to answer the question are hard or impos-
089   sible to discern, (3) the required information is out of the picture frame, and (4) the spatial relation-
090   ship is indeterminate (Davis, 2020) (see Figure 2). In the case of UGeoQA, the answerable cases are
091   selected from GeoQA, where VLMs are expected to predict answers based on geometry diagrams
092   from provided answer choices. As illustrated in Figure 3, unanswerable cases are constructed by
093   removing a key condition from the original questions and ensuring that the corresponding images
094   do not contain this omitted condition, thereby making the questions unanswerable. Notably, the an-
095   swerable and unanswerable cases share the same visual context, with only slight differences in the
096   questions. As for UTabMWP, the answerable cases are selected from TabMWP consisting of math
097
      word problems with tabular data as the visual context. As shown in Figure 4, unanswerable cases
      are created by obscuring critical information in the original table—information necessary to solve
098
      the problem—thus rendering the questions unanswerable.
099
100   Overall, TUBench includes images from four different sources and unanswerable questions con-
101   structed using ten strategies. In total, TUBench contains 2,354 questions, split into 1,203 answerable
102   and 1,151 unanswerable. Notably, 1,667 of these questions are newly curated—516 answerable and
103
      1,151 unanswerable. This comprehensive collection makes TUBench well-suited for thoroughly
      assessing the trustworthiness of LVLMs when confronted with unanswerable questions.
104
105   We conduct comprehensive experiments on TUBench, evaluating 28 leading foundation LVLMs in
106   a zero-shot setting, which includes 7 proprietary models (e.g., QWen-VL, Gemini-1.5, and GPT-4o)
107   and 21 open-source models (e.g., LLaVA, mPLUG-Owl, and InstructBLIP). The best-performing
      model, Gemini-1.5-Pro, achieves an average accuracy of 69.2% and an average F1-score of 59.2%

108
      in identifying unanswerable questions, indicating significant room for improvement in our dataset.
109   Furthermore, we conduct a detailed human evaluation of the explanations and answers generated
110   by six well-performing proprietary models. Our analysis reveals that the best-performing model,
111   Gemini-1.5-Pro, provides correct answers and explanations in 41% of cases, while in the remaining
112   instances, it produces incorrect answers or explanations. An in-depth analysis of these erroneous

115   that TUBench poses a challenge to current LVLMs and offers a new perspective for assessing hallu-
116   cinations and trustworthiness of LVLMs through unanswerable questions.

121
      Large Vision-Language Model. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are designed to interpret and
      generate content that involves both images and text, enabling a wide range of applications, such as
122
      image captioning (Ordonez et al., 2011; Vinyals et al., 2015) and visual question answering (An-
123
      tol et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2016). Early research on VLMs (Li et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2019;
124   Li et al., 2022) commonly employed BERT-based (Devlin et al., 2019) models as the language de-
125   coder. Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), known for
126   their impressive zero-shot performance across various NLP tasks (He et al., 2024b), have shifted
127   attention toward incorporating these powerful LLMs into VLMs. This integration further enhances
128   language understanding and generation, culminating in the creation of Large Vision-Language Mod-
129   els (LVLMs). Notably, LVLMs, including LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a; 2024a;b), mPLUG-Owl (Ye
130   et al., 2023), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024), and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023b), first map the output
131   of a visual encoder, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), as input to open-source LLMs such as
132
      LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) or Qwen (Bai et al., 2023a), and then
      align the visual encoder and LLM decoder through visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023a).
133
134
135   Unanswerable Question Answering. Unanswerable questions have garnered significant research
136   interest in textual question answering (QA), where the information within the provided context is
137   insufficient to answer the question. Rajpurkar et al. (2018) developed SQuAD 2.0 based on SQuAD
138
      1.1 (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) by incorporating unanswerable questions for the paragraphs. Simi-
      larly, Sulem et al. (2022) enriched the Yes/No QA dataset, BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), by adding
139
      unanswerable questions for specific contexts. In Visual Question Answering (VQA), unanswerable
140
      questions often stem from inadequate information in the source image. For example, VizWiz (Gurari
141   et al., 2018) includes unanswerable questions due to the low-quality images taken by visually im-
142   paired users. Ray et al. (2016) created unanswerable questions by pairing a question from the VQA
143   dataset (Antol et al., 2015) with a random image from the same dataset, resulting in questions gen-
144   erally unrelated to the associated images. Recent work (Miyai et al., 2024) applied this strategy to
145   MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b) to create unanswerable problems. However, unanswerable questions
146   created by this heuristic method are typically irrelevant to the associated images, and as a result,
147   current LVLMs can easily identify them as unanswerable (see §4.4 for more details). To address
148   this issue, we manually crafted questions that are unanswerable yet closely related to the associated
149
      images, presenting a greater challenge than those generated automatically. Another distinguishing
      feature of our benchmark is its diverse visual context, which includes screenshots of code snippets,
150
      natural images, geometry diagrams, and screenshots of statistical tables.

153   3   T HE C ONSTRUCTION OF TUB ENCH
154
155
      TUBench differs from existing multimodal datasets such as MME, MMBench, and POPE in that it
156   includes a substantial number of unanswerable questions, whereas previous datasets consist solely of
157   answerable questions with definitive answers. This characteristic of TUBench offers a new perspec-
158   tive for evaluating LVLMs’ comprehension abilities and their tendency to generate hallucinations.
159   Moreover, to ensure diversity in TUBench, we have (1) carefully crafted the unanswerable ques-
160   tions using a variety of strategies, and (2) incorporated four types of images, including screenshots
161   of code snippets, natural images, geometry diagrams, and screenshots of statistical tables. We will
      now provide further details on the construction of TUBench.

178   Construction of Unanswerable Code Reasoning (UCR). To construct the UCR dataset, annota-
179   tors first prepare a simple code snippet and then design Yes/No questions based on it. For example,
180
      the top-left subfigure of Figure 1 shows a code snippet that outputs ‘Hello.’ The designed Yes/No
      questions are specifically related to the output of this code. To construct unanswerable questions in
181
      UCR, annotators are instructed to use three strategies to slightly modify the original code snippets.
182
      S.1. Introduce uncertainties into code screenshots by adding random functions. As shown in
183   the top-right subfigure of Figure 1, the variable x is initially set to 1. One needs to compare x with
184   10*random.random() to determine the program’s output. Since 10*random.random()
185   generates a random number uniformly between 0 and 10, we cannot predetermine whether this ran-
186   dom number will be greater than 1 (i.e., whether the if-condition holds). Thus, it is unclear whether
187   the code will output ‘Hello’ or ‘World’. Consequently, questions like Is the output of the code
188   ‘Hello’? and Is the output of the code ‘World’? are unanswerable. To balance the dataset, annota-
189   tors also created answerable questions for this code snippet. Although the code’s output is uncertain,
190   it will not be ‘hello’, so the answer to Is the output of the code ‘hello’? is ‘No’. Similarly, there is a
191
      90% probability that the random number generated by 10*random.random() is greater than 1.
      Therefore, the answer to Is the probability that the code outputs ‘Hello’ equal to 90%? is ‘Yes’.
192
      S.2. Introduce uncertainties into code screenshots by omitting variable initialization. The mo-
193
      tivation for this approach arises from a scenario where a user, while taking a screenshot of a code
194   snippet, inadvertently omits the initialization of a variable and then asks VLMs to answer questions
195   based on this incomplete code snapshot. Thus, it is crucial to assess VLMs’ reliability under such
196   conditions. For example, in the bottom-left subfigure of Figure 1, the code screenshot misses the
197   initialization of the variable x, making it impossible to determine whether the output of the original,
198   complete code is ‘Hello’ or ‘World’. Therefore, the questions Is the output of the code ‘Hello’? and
199   Is the output of the code ‘World’? are unanswerable. However, despite the unknown value of x, the
200   code’s output cannot be ‘hello’ or ‘world’. Hence, the answers to Is the output of the code ‘hello’?
201   and Is the probability that the code outputs ‘world’ equal to 0%? are ‘No’ and ‘Yes’, respectively.
202
      S.3. Introduce uncertainties into code screenshots by deliberately leaving certain lines of code
      incomplete. Similar to the second method, the rationale behind this approach stems from situations
203
      where a user, while taking a screenshot of a code snippet, inadvertently captures incomplete lines of
204
      code due to oversight. As shown in the bottom-right subfigure of Figure 1, two lines of the print
205   code in the screenshot are incomplete. From this screenshot, we can infer that the original, complete
206   code begins its output with ‘Hello’, but the specific output remains unclear. Based on this screenshot,
207   both answerable and unanswerable questions can be formulated, as depicted in the figure.

214   annotators. Subsequently, annotators are instructed to design Yes/No questions based on the images.
215   To ensure category balance within the dataset, we required annotators to create an equal number of

243

244

245   surements, it is impossible to determine the exact size of the TV screen from this image.

248   is due to reasons other than occlusion (S.4), unclear details (S.5), missing overall object information

252   of Figure 2 depicts a daytime scene and provides no information about nighttime. Therefore, the
253

      UTabMWP by adding unanswerable questions to the existing TabMWP dataset, which consists of

293
      includes a question, answer choices, and an image on the left. S.10. To render the original question
294   unanswerable, we deliberately occlude crucial information in the left image, thus creating the

296   ing the question in the left image and then obscure a piece of this key information at random. This

523   ‘TV’ and ‘TV stand cabinet’ mentioned in the questions are missing in Figure 25, they can recog-
524   nize the questions as unanswerable. In comparison, Figure 24 shows that only four out of the twelve
525   responses identify the questions as unanswerable. This indicates that our manually constructed
526   questions, compared to those created using rule-based methods, pose a greater challenge for
527
      VLMs. As a result, they offer a more effective means of evaluating the performance of VLMs
      when dealing with unanswerable questions.
528
529
530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we introduce TUBench, a multimodality benchmark designed to evaluate the trustwor-
533   thiness and hallucination of LVLMs when faced with unanswerable questions. TUBench is diverse,
534   as the unanswerable questions are manually crafted using various strategies, and the associated im-
535
      ages span four distinct domains. Our evaluation of existing foundation LVLMs reveals two key
      findings: (1) it is challenging for current LVLMs to recognize the unanswerability of questions in
536
      TUBench, and (2) these models exhibit significant hallucination issues when handling unanswer-
537
      able questions. Thus, TUBench offers a novel perspective for assessing both the trustworthiness and
538   hallucination of LVLMs, complementing existing benchmarks based on answerable questions, and
539   will facilitate the development of more reliable LVLMs.

       C.1   E VALUATION S ETTINGS
975
976    Answer Extraction. When generating responses to questions, VLMs are instructed to give the
977    answer first and then output the explanation (see §C.3). Therefore, we extract their answers from
978    the VLM responses using heuristic matching. To be specific, for UCR and UVQA, we aim to extract
979    the predicted answer (i.e., ‘Yes’, ‘No’, or ‘Unanswerable’) from the VLMs’ output. For UGeoQA
980    and UTabMWP, we focus on extracting the predicted answer choice label (i.e., ‘A’, ‘B’, ‘C’, ‘D’).
981    Although the problems are framed as clear Yes/No or multiple-choice questions with well-formatted
982    options, some VLMs may still generate responses that lack discernible answers. In such cases, we
983    are unable to extract VLMs’ predicted answers. Since our evaluation metrics primarily assess the
984    answerability of the question, we prevent VLMs from benefiting from being assigned the ‘Unan-
985    swerable’ label. Instead, we assign an answerable label (e.g., ‘Yes’ or ‘A’) for these cases, rather than
986    marking the response as ‘Unanswerable.’ For example, in Figure 31, the output of the InstructBLIP-
987
       FlanT5-xxl model is: “The code is for a python program that is not a greeting.” Clearly, no answer
       can be extracted from this response.

990    C.2   NAIVE BASELINES
991
       Random Chance. The random chance method refers to selecting an option at random from the
992    provided answer choices as the answer to a question. Specifically:
993
994    (1) If the task is to determine whether a given question is answerable, random chance will randomly
995
       assign either the “Answerable” or “Unanswerable” label to the question.
       (2) If the task not only involves determining whether the question is answerable, but also selecting
996
       the correct answer if it is deemed answerable, random chance will randomly choose one option from
997
       the answer choices. Note that in this case, the answer choices include the “Unanswerable” option.
998
999
       Frequent Guess. The frequent guess method chooses the most frequent answer choices in
1000   TUBench as answers to questions. Specifically:
1001
1002   (1) If the task is to determine whether a given question is answerable, frequent guess will assign
1003
       either the “Answerable” or “Unanswerable” label to the question. For the UCR dataset, frequent
       guess will label questions as “Answerable,” whereas for the other three datasets, it will label them
1004
       as “Unanswerable.”
1005
       (2) If the task not only involves determining whether the question is answerable, but also selecting
1006   the correct answer if it is deemed answerable, the frequent guess method will choose the most
1007   common option from either “Unanswerable” or the original answer choices. Since “Unanswerable”
1008   is the most frequent option, the frequent guess method will select “Unanswerable” as the answer for
1009   all questions.

1137
1138   Table 11 presents the evaluation results for unanswerable subsets and answerable subsets of
1139   TUBench. From this table, we can draw the following conclusions:
1140
       Qwen-VL Models’ Strength and Weakness: Qwen-VL models achieve high 2ACC scores on the
1141   answerable subsets but perform poorly on the unanswerable subsets. This indicates that Qwen-VL
1142   models tend to classify questions as answerable, even when they are not. In contrast, the Gemini
1143   and GPT-4 series models perform significantly better on the unanswerable subsets, demonstrating
1144   greater robustness in distinguishing unanswerable questions.
1145
       Performance Gap Between Subsets: When assessing whether a question is answerable, proprietary
1146
       VLMs generally perform better on the answerable subsets than on the unanswerable subsets in
1147   most cases. This highlights a notable gap in robustness, suggesting there is significant room for
1148   improvement in the VLMs’ ability to handle unanswerable scenarios effectively.
1149
1150
       Discrepancy Between Answerability Judgment and Problem-Solving Ability: The ability of
       VLMs to solve answerable questions does not always align with their ability to determine whether
1151
       a question is answerable. This is evident from the OACC results displayed in the table for the an-
1152
       swerable subsets. The OACC metric evaluates not only whether VLMs can determine if a question
1153   is answerable but also whether it can correctly answer the question when it is answerable. While the
1154   average OACC scores of Qwen-VL models are only slightly lower than those of the best-performing
1155   models, such as Gemini-1.5-Pro and GPT-4o, this suggests that Qwen-VL models have some capa-
1156   bility in solving answerable questions. However, the significantly lower 2ACC results of Qwen-VL
1157   models on the unanswerable subsets indicate poor performance in judging the answerability of unan-
1158   swerable questions.
