027                                                          A BSTRACT
028
029
030
031   Diffusion models have completely transformed the field of generative models, demonstrating unpar-
032   alleled capabilities in generating high-fidelity images. However, when misused, such a powerful and
033   convenient tool could create fake news or disturbing content targeted at individual victims, causing
034   severe negative social impacts. In this paper, we introduce Anti-Reference, a novel method that
035   protects images from the threats posed by reference-based generation techniques by adding imper-
036   ceptible adversarial noise to the images. We propose a unified loss function that enables joint attacks
037
      on fine-tuning-based customization methods, non-fine-tuning customization methods, and human-
      centric driving methods. Based on this loss, we train a Noise Encoder with a DiT architecture to
038
      predict the noise or directly optimize the noise using the PGD (Projected Gradient Descent) method.
039
      Our method demonstrates strong black-box transferability, being equally effective against black-
040   box models and some commercial APIs such as Animate Anyone, and EMO. Extensive experiments
041   validate the performance of Anti-Reference, establishing a new benchmark in image security.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
045
      Diffusion models have completely transformed the field of generative models, demonstrating un-
046
      paralleled capabilities in generating high-fidelity images. Customized generation can be divided
047
      into methods that require training, such as Dreambooth(Ruiz et al., 2023), LoRA (Hu et al., 2022),
048   Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022), Custom Diffusion(Kumari et al., 2023) and those that do not,
049   such as IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023a), Instant-ID (Wang et al., 2024b). Reference-based methods are
050   widely used in customized image and video generation, especially in human-centered video gen-
051   eration, including portrait video creation methods(Tian et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; He et al.,
052   2024; Xie et al., 2024), and human animation (Xu et al., 2024; Hu, 2024) , which have attracted
053   significant attention due to their practical value in creating digital human avatars and enhancing film
      production.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      However, the high convenience and efficiency of non-trainable Reference-based methods make them
055   potentially susceptible to misuse, becoming tools for creating fake news or disturbing content tar-
056   geted at individual victims, causing severe negative social impacts. Existing studies use encoder
057   attack (Salman et al., 2023) and diffusion attack (Van Le et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023) to protect
058   images from the threats posed by methods requiring fine-tuning (Ruiz et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2022;
059   Gal et al., 2022), using PGD (Madry, 2017) optimization to generate adversarial noise, but this ap-
060   proach requires several minutes to protect a single image, severely limiting its practical application.
061   Moreover, these methods are largely ineffective against non-trainable Reference-based generation
062   methods. Therefore, developing an efficient method to protect personal images from the threats of
063
      Reference-based generation has become an urgent priority.
064   Reference-based methods provide additional conditions through a Reference Image to enable cus-
065   tomized generation. These methods can be divided into two types based on their implementation:
066   one type embeds Reference features in the cross-attention layer of the denoising network using
067   an adapter, such as IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023a); the other type embeds reference features in the
068   self-attention layer of the denoising network using ReferenceNet. The approach of ReferenceNet is
069
      widely used for image customization generation (Team, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b;c), Image2Video
      (Chen et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023), and face animation generation (Tian et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
070
      2024; He et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024), and body-driven tasks (Xu et al., 2024; Hu, 2024). However,
071
      due to the variety of existing Reference-based generation methods, attacking a specific method has
072   limited practical significance, as attackers can easily switch methods to bypass protection. There-
073   fore, the motivation of this paper is to propose a universal adversarial noise generation method to
074   address the threats posed by mainstream Reference-based methods.
075
      In practical image protection scenarios, protection methods need to address several challenges.
076
      Firstly, universality is a key challenge. Since Reference-based methods have many different imple-
077
      mentations, and models trained on different datasets have different feature spaces, the same attack
078   strategy may have very different effects on different models. Secondly, efficiency is also crucial.
079   Existing methods like Anti-DreamBooth (Van Le et al., 2023) , which use PGD optimization, usu-
080   ally require hundreds of steps and significant time, severely limiting their feasibility for real-time
081   applications. Finally, black-box transferability and robustness are also central challenges. In prac-
082   tical applications, the structures and parameters of proprietary APIs like EMO (Tian et al., 2024) ,
083   Animate anyone (Hu, 2024) are not accessible, so attack methods must have good black-box trans-
084   ferability. Additionally, the generated adversarial noise also needs to be robust enough to withstand
085   common data augmentation operations and preprocessing steps (such as JPEG compression and
086
      Affine transformations).
087   To address these challenges, this paper presents Anti-Reference, the first to protect images from
088   the threats posed by mainstream reference-based methods and tuning-based customization methods
089   through the forward process. We propose a Noise Encoder based on the DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023)
090   architecture, which predicts adversarial noise of the same size as the original image and overlays
091   it to form a protected image. To achieve a universal attack on methods requiring fine-tuning and
092
      those that do not, we designed a unified loss function, using a weighted strategy to achieve joint
      attack effects across multiple tasks, and by limiting the noise range and regularization loss to en-
093
      sure the invisibility of the noise. To enhance the robustness of adversarial noise, we also introduced
094
      some data augmentation techniques to ensure that the adversarial noise can withstand various data
095   enhancements and preprocessing operations. As the model structures and weights of proprietary
096   APIs are not accessible, directly attacking these models is usually not feasible. To overcome this
097   hurdle, we created white-box proxy models that mimic the structure and behavior of these propri-
098   etary models, and we successfully implemented attacks on these proxy models, thereby achieving
099   black-box transferability attacks. Specifically, our adversarial samples have successfully transferred
100   to closed-source APIs (such as Animate Anyone (Hu, 2024) and EMO (Tian et al., 2024)).
101   Extensive experimental results demonstrate the outstanding performance of Anti-Reference in pro-
102   tecting images from the threats of Reference-based generation methods and potential security risks
103   associated with fine-tuning methods. This paper not only extends the theoretical construction of
104   adversarial attacks but also transforms it into a deployable solution, establishing a new benchmark
105   in the fields of privacy protection and information security.
106

      2.1   C USTMIZED D IFFUSION M ODEL .
128
129
      Diffusion probability models Song et al. (2020); Ho et al. (2020) represent a class of advanced gener-
      ative models that reconstruct original data from pure Gaussian noise by learning noise distributions
130
      at different levels. These models excel in handling complex data distributions and have marked
131
      significant accomplishments across various fields such as image synthesis Rombach et al. (2021);
132   Peebles & Xie (2023), image editing Brooks et al. (2023); Hertz et al. (2022), video generation Wu
133   et al. (2022); Hu (2024), and 3D content creation Poole et al. (2022). A prominent example is Stable
134   Diffusion Rombach et al. (2021), which utilizes a UNet architecture to iteratively produce images,
135   demonstrating robust text-to-image capabilities after extensive training on large text-image datasets.
136   DreamBooth Ruiz et al. (2023), Custom diffusion Kumari et al. (2023) and Textual Inversion Gal
137   et al. (2022), adopt transfer learning to text-to-image diffusion models via either fine-tuning all the
138   parameters, partial parameters , or introducing and optimizing a word vector for the new concept.
139   LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) Hu et al. (2022) is a popular and lightweight training technique that
140
      significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters and is widely used for personalized or
      task-specific image generation.
141
142
143   2.2   R EFERENCE - BASED G ENERATION
144
145   In addition to the aforementioned fine-tuning methods, finetuning-free concept learning methods can
146   capture concepts from a single image and are widely used for tasks such as customized generation
147   (Ye et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a), identity consistency maintenance (Wang et al., 2024b; Li
148   et al., 2024), face-driven Tian et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024), and body-driven
149   tasks Xu et al. (2024); Hu (2024). These methods can be roughly categorized into the Adapter
150   approach and the ReferenceNet approach based on how the reference image features are utilized. In
151   the Adapter approach, the reference image is first processed by a pre-trained image feature extractor,
152   typically CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) image encoder or ArcFace Deng et al. (2019), and then an
153
      adapter structure generates visual tokens applied to the cross-attention layers of the U-Net. The
      ReferenceNet approach emphasizes the effectiveness of integrating reference image features into the
154
      self-attention layers of LDM U-Nets, enabling customized generation while preserving appearance
155
      context. Image-to-video technology Chen et al. (2023b); Zhang et al. (2023) uses ReferenceNet
156   to maintain consistency between the generated results and the reference image. Magic Animate Xu
157   et al. (2024) and Animate Anyone Hu (2024) combine ReferenceNet with pose control and temporal
158   modules to achieve body-driven generation. EMO Tian et al. (2024), Ecomimic Chen et al. (2024),
159   and X-Portrait Xie et al. (2024), among other talking-face methods, maintain identity consistency
160   using ReferenceNet, generating fake videos from just a single photo. The misuse of Reference-
161   based Generation methods can have severe consequences, making it urgent to protect images from
      the threats posed by such methods.

      2.3     P ROTECTIVE P ERTURBATION AGAINST D IFFUSION .
163
164   Protective Perturbation against Stable Diffusion. To protect personal images such as faces and art-
165   work from potential infringement when used for fine-tuning Stable Diffusion, recent research aims
166   to disrupt the fine-tuning process by adding imperceptible protective noise to these images. Several
167   methods have been developed to achieve this goal: Glaze (Shan et al., 2023) focuses on prevent-
168   ing artists’ work from being used for specific style mimicry in Stable Diffusion. It optimizes the
169   distance between the original image and the target image at the feature level, causing Stable Dif-
170   fusion to learn the wrong artistic style. AdvDM (Liang et al., 2023) proposes a direct adversarial
171
      attack on Stable Diffusion by maximizing the Mean Squared Error loss during the optimization pro-
      cess. This approach uses adversarial noise to protect personal images. Anti-DreamBooth (Van Le
172
      et al., 2023) incorporates the DreamBooth fine-tuning process of Stable Diffusion into its consid-
173
      eration. It designs a bi-level min-max optimization process to generate protective perturbations.
174   Additionally,other research efforts (Wang et al., 2024a; Ye et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2023) have
175   explored generating protective noise for images using similar adversarial perturbation methods. The
176   aforementioned works use PGD optimization to protect images from threats posed by fine-tuning
177   methods such as Dreambooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), and textual inversion (Gal
178   et al., 2022). However, these methods fail to provide protection against reference-based generation.
179   Therefore, it is urgent to explore how to protect personal images from reference-based generation,
180   and this paper fills that gap.

      illicitly uses reference-based methods to exploit others’ photos for customized content, while Alice,
196
      wishing to safeguard her images on social media, adds adversarial noise to disrupt Bob’s methods,

      aiming to induce severe artifacts in the generated content. Specifically, we explain the workflow of

202
      imizing alterations to the original image. In real-world scenarios, the Image Protector often faces

      4.1      OVERALL M ETHOD
256
257
      This section introduces the overall framework of the Anti-Reference method, as shown in Figure 2.
258   Our method consists of several key components: the Noise Encoder, a set of conditional modules, the
259   Denoising Unet, and a differentiable data augmentation module. The Noise Encoder adds adversarial
260   noise to the image, forming the protected image Iadv . The set of Reference Modules is a group of
261   conditional control modules that serve as the target models for the attack.
262
      To protect images from the threats posed by tuning-free customization generation methods and driv-
263
      ing methods, we selected the pre-trained ReferenceNet from Magic Animate and Ecomimic, as well
264   as the Stable Diffusion Unet, as the target models for attacking the ReferenceNet route. Addition-
265   ally, we chose the IP-Adapter as the target model for the Adapter route. The Denoising Unet utilizes
266   the pre-trained Stable Diffusion 1.5 Unet, as it is the most commonly used base model for various
267   customization generation methods. The protected image Iadv is fed into two components: the set
268   of conditional modules and the Denoising Unet, where losses are calculated separately. To enhance
269   the robustness of the adversarial noise against real-world scenarios, we propose a differentiable data
      augmentation module, which applies common data augmentations to Iadv .

      4.2   N OISE E NCODER
271
272   Our objective is to input an image that needs protection, and the Noise Encoder generates adver-
273   sarial noise of the same size as the image through a forward process. In designing the structure of
274   the noise encoder, we initially tried the hidden watermark embedding method proposed by Hidden
275   and the U-Net structure from stable diffusion, but neither yielded satisfactory results. Ultimately,
276   we proposed the Noise Encoder based on the DiT structure. DiT, a new diffusion model based on
277   transformers, is referred to as Diffusion Transformers and follows the best practices of Vision Trans-
278
      formers (ViTs). ViTs have demonstrated superior scalability in visual recognition tasks compared to
      traditional convolutional networks like ResNet. Specifically, the DiT model processes input images
279
      by patchifying them into smaller blocks, which are then fed as input sequences into transformers.
280
      After being processed by multiple transformer blocks, the noise is gradually removed, resulting in
281   high-quality image generation.
282
283   In our Noise Encoder design, there are several key differences from the DiT design: 1. Our Noise
284
      Encoder generates adversarial noise in a single inference rather than iterative denoising. 2. We
      removed the additional conditional information related to the diffusion process from DiT, such as
285
      noise timesteps t, class labels c, and natural language. 3. DiT performs denoising in the latent space
286
      of the VAE, whereas our method predicts adversarial noise in the pixel domain, with differences in
287   input channels and resolution. Considering that the customization methods targeted by our attacks
288   typically use SD1.5 as the base model, with the input image resolution for ReferenceNet and the
289   denoising U-Net set to 512×512, we also set the training resolution of DiT to 512×512 to ensure
290   compatibility with these customized generation tasks and to enhance the effectiveness of the attack.
291
      To ensure the adversarial noise retains its effectiveness under various image processing conditions,
292
      we apply a series of data augmentations before utilizing the noised image I ′ . These augmentations
293   include differentiable cropping, resizing, JPEG compression, and color adjustments, all of which
294   help maintain the robustness of the adversarial noise against common image transformations.

344

345

      4.5   B LACK -B OX T RANSFER
361
      This section introduces proxy-based black-box attacks, a method that generates adversarial samples
362   using a white-box model with a structure similar to the target black-box model or a closely related
363   latent space. By training DiT to generate adversarial samples on the white-box model, these samples
364   also achieve high attack success rates on the black-box model. The success of this approach relies
365   on two key factors: 1) structural similarity between the white-box and black-box models, and 2)
366   shared similarity in their latent spaces. For instance, both Animate Anyone and Magic Animate are
367   based on Stable Diffusion 1.5 and share the same ReferenceNet architecture, with similar datasets
368   used for fine-tuning, resulting in similar latent spaces. Additionally, we successfully attacked the
369   EMO, Animate anyone and other apps or APIs, as demonstrated in the experiments.
