009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024a; Dao & Gu, 2024) state-space models (SSMs) were
012            recently shown to outperform state-of-the-art (SOTA) Transformer large language
013            models (LLMs) across various tasks. Despite subsequent widespread adaptation,
014            little work has focused on Mamba LLMs’ amenability for fine-tuning frameworks
015            ubiquitously used for Transformer-based LLMs, e.g., mixed-precision fine-tuning
016            (MPFT) and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). For the former, it currently
017            remains an open question whether Mamba’s recurrent dynamics are robust to
018            small input changes, such as those encountered during MPFT. Using dynamical
019
               systems theory (in particular, Lyapunov exponents), we answer this question in
               the affirmative. We empirically validate this result through several experiments,
020
               showing that Mamba SSMs are significantly more stable to changes introduced
021
               by mixed-precision than comparable Transformers, even when both MPFT and
022            PEFT are combined. For PEFT, we show how targeting specific memory buffers
023            in Mamba’s customized CUDA kernels for low-rank adaptation regularizes SSM
024            parameters, thus providing both parameter efficient learning and computational
025            savings. Finally, with both MPFT and PEFT enabled, we explore the impact of
026            instruction tuning Mamba SSMs for in-context learning (ICL) on natural language
027            tasks. While pretrained Mamba and Mamba-2 models only achieve 38% and 82%
028            (respectively) of the ICL improvements of comparable Transformer-based LLMs,
029            we show that instruction tuning allows Mamba models to narrow this gap to 81%
030
               and Mamba-2 models to skyrocket over this gap to 132%.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Innovating on previous state-space models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2022; Dao et al., 2023), Mamba (Gu &
036   Dao, 2024a; Dao & Gu, 2024) has been recently proposed as an accurate, sub-quadratic alternative
037   to Transformer large language models (LLMs). Upon their introduction in Gu & Dao (2024a),
038   Mamba SSMs were shown to greatly outperform comparable attention-based LLMs (Biderman et al.,
039
      2023) across a large number of standard natural language benchmarks. Subsequently, pretrained
      Mamba models have been widely adapted across a large number of data modalities (Liu et al.,
040
      2024; Li & Chen, 2024; Quan & Li, 2024; Li et al., 2024), tasks (Xie et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
041
      2024a), and architectures (Anthony et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Lieber et al., 2024). Despite such
042   widespread adaptation and subsequent research threads (Dao & Gu, 2024; Park et al., 2024; Wang
043   et al., 2024b), little work has been done to understand the amenability of Mamba SSMs for widely
044   used fine-tuning frameworks, such as mixed-precision fine-tuning (MPFT) (Micikevicius et al., 2018)
045   and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (He et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021).
046
      MPFT and PEFT are arguably two of the most widely utilized techniques for LLM alignment (Tunstall
047
      et al., 2023) and customization (VM et al., 2024), and are typically combined to drastically decrease
048   hardware demands needed to fine-tune modern LLMs (Dettmers et al., 2024). However, direct
049   application of MPFT for Mamba SSMs is made difficult due to potential sensitivities of Mamba’s
050   state-space dynamics, a common concern for recurrent-based deep models (Pascanu et al., 2013). To
051   combat this, both Huggingface (2024) and Gu & Dao (2024b) suggest full precision (FP32) may
052   be required to perform stable training for Mamba models. Thus, it is currently an open question
053   whether Mamba’s recurrent dynamics are stable in the presence of small input deviations, such as
      those introduced in MPFT.

054
      To answer this question, we leverage theory from dynamical systems. Deriving and bounding the
055   Lyapunov exponents for both Mamba and Mamba-2 models, we show that small input changes
056   within the SSM layer of either model do not lead to exponentially deviating outputs. Empirically,
057   we validate this theoretical result; compared to full-precision, deviations due to mixed-precision for
058   Mamba inference are on par with those demonstrated by Transformer LLMs, while deviations due to
059   MPFT are significantly more stable than those of comparable Transformers (Section 4). Furthermore,
060   this trend continues when MPFT and PEFT are combined, where Mamba SSMs again produce
061   significantly smaller deviations compared to comparable Transformer LLMs.
062   For PEFT, we show that by targeting the large memory buffers exploited by Mamba’s highly cus-
063   tomized CUDA kernels, LoRA may be used for extremely efficient fine-tuning, while simultaneously
064   regularizing the majority of Mamba’s SSM parameters via weight tying. We show that this leads to
065   extremely efficient PEFT, resulting in up to 2.15 times faster training and 65.5% reduced memory
066   compared to the largest evaluated Mamba model without MPFT or PEFT. Furthermore, this allows
067   even the largest (2.8 billion parameter) Mamba LLMs to be fine-tuned on a single GPU with as little
068   as 24GB of onboard memory.
069   Finally, using both MPFT and PEFT, we complement existing studies (Park et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
070   2024) by exploring the ICL capabilities of instruction-tuned Mamba and Mamba-2 models on natural
071   language tasks. In particular, the ICL capabilities of both pretrained Mamba and Mamba-2 models
072   lag behind those of comparable Transformer models from the Pythia suite (Biderman et al., 2023);
073   averaged across five standard natural language benchmarks and foundation model sizes, Mamba
074   and Mamba-2 models only achieve 38% and 82%, respectively, of the performance improvements
075
      (relative to zero-shot) of Pythia models. However, after instruction-tuning, Mamba models are able
      to achieve as much as 81.5% of the average few-shot learning improvement (relative to zero-shot)
076
      of comparable Transformers, while Mamba-2 models push this to 132% of the ICL improvements
077
      achieved by Pythia models. We note that, similar to Transformer foundation models (Wei et al.,
078   2022), (post) instruction tuning ICL appears as an emergent abilities for Mamba and Mamba-2 SSMs,
079   manifesting for models of size 370 million parameters and greater, while failing to manifest for
080   Mamba and Mamba-2 models of fewer parameter counts.
081

      Terminology. We note that herein, when describing a particular foundation model or result, we
102   use the term “Mamba model” to refer to one of the original models released in Gu & Dao (2024a)
103   and “Mamba-2 model” to refer to models released in Gu & Dao (2024a). While there are subtle
104   architectural differences between these two SSMs, they share important similarities which allow our
105   theoretical results to extend to both sets of models. In particular, Mamba and Mamba-2 models share
106   the same state-space equations, support for SSM matrices, and design scheme of storing the majority
107   of SSM parameters in a large memory buffer. Thus, we synonymously use the term MambaBlock
      to refer to the SSM layer of both Mamba and Mamba-2 models.

      2.1.1    C ONSEQUENCES FOR AUTOMATIC MIXED - PRECISION
163
164   During a forward pass, automatic mixed-precision (AMP) saves time and memory by computing
165   forward activations in half-precision (FP16 or BF16). During a backward pass, AMP computes
166   gradients in half-precision and up-casts to full-precision prior to updating. In contrast to full-precision
167   fine-tuning, MPFT within the MambaBlock thus results in small differences to the inputs u1 , . . . , uT
      (which are passed through a Swish), ∆   ¯ t (which is passed through a softplus), and the gradients
168
169
      calculated during training.
170   For a discrete dynamical system with λmax ą 0, changes due to AMP compound after repeated
171   expansion of the recurrent state, thus leading to exponential deviations between quantities calculated
172   using mixed- versus full-precision. We note that Transformers are not recurrent, and thus not
173   susceptible to such issues. Yet, just as differences introduced by quantization/mixed-precision produce
174   output differences in Transformer results, differences are expected in Mamba results using different
175
      precision strategies. However, by Theorem 1, such differences do not exponentially compound over
      discrete-time within the MambaBlock.

      As matrices Bt , Ct and ∆t are time-varying, S4 optimizations via the SSM convolution kernel (Dao
180   et al., 2023) are no longer applicable. However, by diagonality, each dimension may be computed
181   in parallel. Furthermore, the recurrence along every dimension is a prefix sum (also called a scan),
182   which is highly parallelizable (Blelloch, 1990). Gu & Dao (2024a) thus capitalizes on this through
183   extensively customized CUDA kernels wherein the majority of temporal variables are carefully laid
184   out in a large buffer of GPU memory and manipulated. Instantiated as a PyTorch linear layer’s
185   weight matrix, this memory buffer W P RT ˆ3d is used to store and access the diagonal elements of
186   Bt , Ct and ∆t for all t P t1, . . . , T u, such that
187       Wrt ´ 1, : ds “ diagp∆t q, Wrt ´ 1, d : 2ds “ diagpBt q, Wrt ´ 1, 2d : 3ds “ diagpCt q,

189

191   Mamba prefix scan kernel heavily relies on this memory layout to optimize the access pattern of W
192   in Equations 1 and 2.
193   Similarly, Mamba-2 stores diagonal elements of Bt , Ct and ∆t in a large memory buffer W.
194   However, rather than utilizing the underlying recurrence to directly compute the hidden state and

197   to calculate x1 , . . . , xT and y1 , . . . , yT using tensor contractions, which are highly optimized on
198   modern hardware accelerators.

209   3     R ELATED W ORK
210
211   Recent work has sought to understand how to efficiently increase Mamba’s hidden-state dimension
212   by restructuring SSM operations using tensor contractions (Dao & Gu, 2024), leading to Mamba-2
213   models. A separate line of work has sought to understand the in-context learning (ICL) capabilities
214   of Mamba LLMs when trained from scratch for specific tasks (Park et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024).
215   Another line of recent work has sought to understand how hybrid Mamba-Transformer models may
      be directly distilled from Transformer models (Wang et al., 2024b). However, to the best of our

      knowledge, no existing works have either theoretically explored the effects small input changes
217   (e.g., due to mixed-precision) have on Mamba’s recurrent dynamics, empirically explored such
218   effects downstream impact on fine-tuning and inference, or sought to understand the effects of LoRA
219   adaptation on modules within the MambaBlock.
220
      Lyapunov exponents have previously been considered for classic RNN structures (e.g., vanilla RNNs,
221
      LSTMs, GRUs, PLRNNs, etc.) (Mikhaeil et al., 2022; Vogt et al., 2022), to determine when such
222
      models exhibit chaotic dynamics and the impact on the exploding/vanishing gradient phenomena1 .
223   For more recent S4 neural models, (Goel et al., 2022) used Hurwitz matrices to characterize the
224   numerical stability of linear time-invariant (LTI) S4 models. However, such analysis is not applicable
225   to time-varying models, such as Mamba, nor does it characterize the effects of sensitive dependence
226   on initial conditions (e.g., divergence of two ε close inputs). To the best of our knowledge, no
227   previous works have used Lyapunov exponents to explore the effects of mixed-precision on recurrent
228   neural models or Mamba architectures.
229   As previously noted, recent works (Park et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024) have studied Mamba’s ability
230   to perform ICL by training Mamba models for specific tasks. Such tasks include logistic regression,
231   decision trees, and learning other simple function classes, following the work of Garg et al. (2022). We
232   emphasize that, in this set up, relatively small Mamba models–33 million and 90 million parameters
233   for Lee et al. (2024) and Park et al. (2024), respectively–are trained from scratch for every evaluated
234   task. Indeed, Park et al. (2024) notes that subsequent work is necessary to understand Mamba’s
235   ICL capabilities for language modeling using standard natural language benchmarks, as well as for
236   larger model sizes. Thus, our study of both the pretrained and instruction tuned ICL capabilities of
237
      Mamba/Mamba-2 LLMs for natural language tasks are complimentary to previous works.

      Next, we present a counterintuitive experiment to demonstrate the impact of Theorem 3. As before,
      we instruction tune Mamba-2 models using LoRA, focusing on the two largest model sizes. This
507
      time, however, we target all linear layers within Mamba-2’s MambaBlock and contrast this with
508
      targeting only W within the MambaBlock. Displayed in Appendix G Table 7, we can see that
509   adapting more SSM parameters does not necessarily lead to improved performance across the board.
510   Rather, adapting only W for Mamba-2 outperforms adapting all linear layers on eight of the 14
511   natural language tasks.
512
      The result in Table 7 thus presents two cases: (a) regularization: only adapting parameters which lead
513
      to weight tying (by Theorem 3) and (b) increased learning capacity: adapting more parameters, at
514
      the cost of learning unregularized parameters. Through regularization via weight tying (Press & Wolf,
515   2017), (a) leads to good generalization. In contrast, while adapting more parameters, (b) includes
516   unregularized variables, leading to positive improvements on only a minority of tasks when compared
517   to the fine-tuning of fewer, but regularized, parameters. We note that adapting all linear layers is
518   often performed when fine-tuning attention-based LLMs using LoRA Dettmers et al. (2024). Thus, a
519   similar regularization-vs-capacity tradeoff for Transformers LLMs may present itself by carefully
520   studying LoRA’s affects when targeting specific linear layers in attention-based architectures.

      5    D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
524
525
      Using dynamical systems theory, we’ve shown that the recurrent dynamics of Mamba SSMs are
      robust to small input perturbations. We’ve extensively confirmed this result, showing that: a) Mamba
526
      inference differences due to mixed-precision align with Transformers, (b) Mamba fine-tuning is
527
      significantly more robust to changes due to mixed-precision and PEFT than Transformers, and (c)
528   combining MPFT and PEFT can more than halve training time and nearly triple memory efficiency
529   for Mamba models. Using both MPFT and PEFT, we’ve shown that instruction tuning Mamba
530   and Mamba-2 SSMs greatly narrows the pretraining ICL gap on natural language tasks relative to
531   comparable Transformer LLMs. In particular, this allows Mamba-2 SSMs to greatly outperform
532   the ICL abilities of a large number of instruction tuned, attention-based LLMs. Furthermore,
533   complimentary to recent studies, we’ve shown that ICL for natural language tasks can be characterized
534   as an emergent ability of Mamba and Mamba-2 models of 370 millions parameters or greater.
535   There are several avenues for future work. In particular, adapting Mamba’s CUDA kernels to support
536   more aggressive low-precision PEFT methods (Dettmers et al., 2024) would further decrease the
537   hardware needed to train Mamba models, while providing additional speedups and testing the limits
538   of the derived stability results. Furthermore, our theoretical contributions open the door for follow
539   up studies, both in terms of extending our stability results to more general error (and adversarial)
      robustness results, as well as deriving new SSM-specific LoRA schemes for regularized learning.

732   A    BACKGROUND
733
734   Mixed-precision and parameter-efficient fine-tuning. In the current era of extremely large foun-
735
      dation models, both MPFT and PEFT have become ubiquitous tools for the rapid adaptation of
      Transformer-based LLMs towards specific applications. PEFT using adapters (He et al., 2021) allows
736
      a large pretrained model to be efficiently adapted for a particular downstream task by freezing the full
737
      model and training only a small number of extra parameters. Arguably the most widely used such
738   PEFT method is LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), which injects trainable low-rank matrices into Transformer
739   layers to approximate weight updates.
740
741
      To further decrease the computational demands necessary for LLM fine-tuning and inference, MPFT
      via mixed-precision (i.e., FP16 or BF16) (Kalamkar et al., 2019; Micikevicius et al., 2018) and
742
      quantized low-precision (Dettmers et al., 2024) have proven effective strategies to reduce GPU
743
      memory and runtime requirements without deleterious effects on downstream performance (Dettmers
744   et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2020). Additionally, mixed-precision approaches have paved the way for
745   hardware-aware optimizations within the self-attention module (Dao et al., 2022), greatly mitigat-
746   ing the quadratic complexity of Transformer LLMs. Together, PEFT and MPFT have created a
747   rich ecosystem with which varying combinations of these approaches may be used to meet the
748   computational constraints of a given training system. We note that post-fine-tuning quantization
749   approaches (Frantar et al., 2023) may be further used to decrease Transformer LLM computational
750   demands, but such approaches are not considered in this work.
751   State-space Models. Structured state-space sequence (S4) models (Gu et al., 2022; Fu et al.,
752   2023) are SSMs which leverage linear time-invariant (LTI) systems to combine the computational
753   advantages of Transformers–i.e., highly parallelizable training–and recurrent neural networks (RNNs)–
754   i.e., subquadratic autoregressive inference using recurrency. Within the S4 layer, an input signal is
755   discretized and LTI parameters representing the input’s latent dynamics are learned. Owing to the
      S4 block’s latent dynamics being LTI, the S4 block’s output may be thus compactly represented as


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      a single convolution between the input and an SSM convolution kernel (a matrix whose entries are
757   products of LTI learnable parameters resulting from unrolling the state-space equations). However,
758   despite hardware efficiency and long-dependency-modeling improvements, LTI-based S4 models
759   remained inferior to Transformers of comparable parameter-sizes for natural language tasks, even
760   when augmenting S4 layers with attention-layers for hybrid architectures (Gu & Dao, 2024a).
761
      Innovating on these previous S4 approaches, Mamba utilizes time-varying parameters to model
762
      latent dynamics, thus broadening the ability to capture nuanced changes evolving in discrete-time.
763   Without LTI dynamics, however, the input-output representation via the SSM convolution kernel is
764   no longer applicable, thus voiding previous hardware-aware S4 optimizations (Fu et al., 2023). To
765   enable hardware efficiency with time-varying SSM parameters, (Gu & Dao, 2024a) thus introduced
766   extensively customized CUDA kernels which implement highly parallelized prefix sums to compute
767   recurrent states. Subsequently, Dao & Gu (2024) considered the unrolled state-space equations and
768   leveraged tensor contractions (i.e., einsum notation (Rogozhnikov, 2022)) to efficiently calculate
769   Mamba variables. The resulting Mamba-2 foundation models contained significantly larger latent-
770   variable dimensions than the Mamba models of (Gu & Dao, 2024a), while maintaining efficiency on
771
      modern GPU accelerators.
772   In-context learning. ICL provides an adaptable alternative to fine-tuning. Rather than fine-tune the
773   LLM directly, ICL augments a prompt with n relevant examples (called shots) preceding the query of
774   interest. Given sufficiently large models and pretraining data (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022),
775   Transformer LLMs have proven adept at learning new concepts on the fly provided such few-shot
776   prompting. However, it is worth noting that ICL inference time increases dramatically as the number
777
      of shots grows (due to self-attention’s quadratic complexity) and PEFT (when possible) is known to
      produce more accurate downstream learning results (Brown et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022).
