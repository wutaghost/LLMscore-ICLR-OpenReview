009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preference is critical to
012            enhancing LLMs’ safety, helpfulness, helpfulness, humor, faithfulness, etc. The
013            current reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) mainly focuses on a
014            fixed reward learned from average human ratings, which may weaken the adaptivity
015            and controllability of varying preferences. However, creating personalized LLMs
016            requires aligning LLMs with individual human preferences, which is non-trivial due
017            to the scarce data per user and the diversity of user preferences on multi-objective
018
               trade-offs, such as prioritizing humor and empathy in one context, while seeking
               efficiency and precision in another. Can we train one LLM to produce personalized
019
               outputs for different user preferences on the Pareto front? In this paper, we
020
               introduce Multi-Objective Control (MOC), which trains an LLM as a meta-policy
021            to directly generate responses in the preference-defined regions of Pareto front.
022            Our approach integrates multi-objective optimization (MOO) principles into
023            Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to train an LLM as a preference-conditioned
024            policy network. We improve the computational efficiency of MOC by applying
025            MOO at the policy level, which enables us to finetune an LLM of 7B parameters
026            on a single A6000 GPU. Extensive experiments demonstrate the advantages of
027            MOC over baselines in three aspects: (i) Controllability of LLM outputs w.r.t. user
028            preferences on the trade-off among multiple rewards; (ii) Quality and diversity of
029
               LLM outputs, measured by the hyper-volume of multiple solutions achieved; and
               (iii) Generalization to unseen preferences. These results highlight MOC’s potential
030
               for real-world applications requiring scalable and customizable LLMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large language models (LLMs) have gained significant attention for their impressive performance
036   across a wide range of tasks, including machine translation (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford &
037   Narasimhan, 2018; Devlin et al., 2019), text generation (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023), and
038   conversational agents (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). However, these models are generally
039   aligned with fixed preferences predetermined by developers (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al.,
040   2023; Bai et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), limiting the available degree of personalization to the users.
041   In real-world scenarios, users often have diverse preferences for LLMs behavior. For instance, one
042   user might prefer a humorous and empathetic response for emotional support, while another might
043   prioritize a more efficient, task-oriented assistant. Despite this variability, the inherent flexibility of
044
      current LLMs (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023) is limited to provide fully personalized interactions.
045   The ability of LLMs to adjust their behavior according to diverse user preferences is called multi-
046   objective controllability, a crucial feature for enhancing user satisfaction. Multi-objective controlla-
047   bility allows a model to dynamically balance the trade-offs between different objectives based on
048   user-defined preferences. Training separate models for each preference order, however, is neither
049   practical nor scalable due to the high computational costs. That highlights the need to enable one-time
050
      LLM training while accommodating a broad range of preferences.
051   Can we control the trade-offs in a single, once-trained LLMs to meet diverse human preferences?
052   Our answer is yes. This paper aims to (i) enable LLMs to generate customized responses for
053   diverse user preferences and (ii) achieve this with a once-trained model. To this end, we introduce
      a novel algorithm, Multi-Objective Control (MOC), which leverages a carefully designed multi-

      objective optimization (MOO) algorithm. MOC requires only one training, incorporates explicit policy
055   improvement, and does not rely on human preference data. Moreover, its training cost is comparable
056   to single-objective reinforcement learning from human feedback method (RLHF) (Schulman et al.,
057   2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), and we made it feasible to fine-tune a 7-billion model on a single A6000
058   GPU with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022).
059
      We first formulate the multi-objective controllability as an MOO problem with preference vector
060
      constraints, inspired by recent advancements in MOO (Désidéri, 2009; Sener & Koltun, 2018; Xiao
061   et al., 2023). This formulation presents two primary challenges. The first one is identifying the target
062   to be controlled. Existing MOO works typically focus on optimizing different loss functions(Liu et al.,
063   2021; 2023) or linearized utility functions (Yang et al., 2019), which do not effectively capture the
064   quality or behavior of LLMs. In contrast, MOC selects the reward signal as the control target, enabling
065   direct manipulation of the model’s behavior. The second challenge is to solve this optimization
066   problem within feasible computational limits. Our formulated optimization problem involves complex
067   trade-offs among multiple objectives under different preference constraints. To address this, we relax
068   the problem into a new form of MOO, where the preference constraint is treated as an additional
069
      objective. MOC scalarizes the objectives with dynamic weighting in different steps, ensuring the
      computational cost comparable to the widely used single-objective RLHF (Schulman et al., 2017;
070
      Ouyang et al., 2022). Table 1 provides a detailed comparison of MOC and baseline methods.
071
072   In extensive experiments, MOC consistently outperforms baseline methods (Ouyang et al., 2022;
073   Ramé et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b) across multiple tasks. It demonstrates strong performance in
074   three key areas: (i) Controllability, as it effectively aligns model behavior with diverse preference
075
      vectors and ensures a monotonic relationship between input preferences and outcomes; (ii) Solution
      set quality, measured by the hyper-volume metric, where MOC achieves a superior Pareto front
076
      while maintaining a diverse set of solutions; and (iii) Generalization, as it robustly handles unseen
077
      preferences while preserving both the alignment quality and diversity. Compared to baseline methods,
078   MOC offers a more efficient and flexible approach to personalizing LLMs, managing different trade-
079   offs among multiple objectives with a single model and seamlessly adapting to new preferences. These
080   findings highlight MOC’s potential for real-world applications requiring scalable and customizable
081   personalization.
082
      Our contributions are as follows: (i) We introduce the MOC algorithm, which takes comparable
083
      computation as single-objective RLHF and finetunes LLMs only once to accommodate diverse user
084   preferences; (ii) We empirically validate MOC, demonstrating its superior performance in terms of
085   controllability, solution quality, and generalization, including its ability to generalize to unseen user
086   preferences.

      In this section, we conduct a series of comprehensive experiments to assess the performance of our
      proposed MOC algorithm. The evaluation focuses on four key aspects: (i) The quality of solutions,
282
      measured using hyper-volumes; (ii) Control with preference vectors, assessed by computing the
283
      correlation between the model’s behavior and the given preferences; (iii) Diversity of solutions,
284   evaluated by computing the entropy of the solutions; and (iv) Generalization capabilities to unseen
285   preference vectors. Additionally, we present case studies to provide qualitative insights into the
286   control effectiveness of MOC with human-like preferences.

288   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
289
290   Implementation. Our implementation is based on the existing open-source TRL package (von
291   Werra et al., 2020). For the language model, we adopt the Llama-2 model (Touvron et al., 2023),
292   specifically the 7-billion parameter version, a widely used model in RLHF studies. The dataset,
293   Helpful Assistant (Bai et al., 2022), targets two pairs of objectives: {“humor", “helpful"} and
294
      {“harmless", “helpful"}. MOC is trained with a set of predefined preference vectors that are uniformly
      distributed over the interval [0,1] intervals. The training process is conducted on a desktop equipped
295
      with an Intel i9-14900K CPU and an NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. MOC is trained by LoRA (Hu et al.,
296
      2022) with a rank of 64 and the language model is loaded in 8-bit due to the computational limitation.
297   Additional experimental details are provided in Appendix E.
298
299   Baselines. We compare MOC against three baselines: (i) The standard MORLHF: A multi-objective
300
      RLHF method that scalarizes the multi-objective problem into a single objective by combining reward
      signals with fixed preference weights; (ii) Rewarded Soups (Ramé et al., 2023): Combines the model
301
      weights from N separately trained models using the PPO algorithm, where each model is optimized
302
      for a specific reward function; (iii) RiC (Yang et al., 2024b): This method conditions the response of
303   the language model on multiple rewards via prompt conditioning, trained using rejection sampling.
304   The behavior of the base Llama-2 model is included for comparative analysis.

470
471   The experimental results reveal four key advantages of MOC. i) MOC achieves the highest solution
472   quality as evidenced by the hyper-volume metric, which reflects both convergence and diversity.
473   ii) MOC demonstrates superior controllability, ensuring consistent adherence to user preferences
474   across various objective trade-offs. iii) MOC outperforms baselines in terms of solution diversity,
475   confirming its robustness in reflecting human preferences. iv) MOC’s ability to generalize to unseen
476   preferences highlights its potential for real-world applications where new preference distributions
477   may arise. These advantages demonstrate that MOC offers a powerful and flexible approach for
478
      multi-objective controllable language models, outperforming existing methods in both control and
      diversity while maintaining computational efficiency.

524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduced Multi-Objective Control (MOC), a novel approach to enable the person-
527   alization of LLMs by enabling dynamic adjustments according to diverse user preferences. MOC
528   addresses the limitations of existing LLMs, which are typically constrained by fixed, developer-
529   specified preferences, by formulating multi-objective controllability as a multi-objective optimization
530   problem. Through the integration of RLHF and introduced surrogate optimization, MOC allows
531
      for fine-tuning a once-trained model to accommodate a wide range of user-defined trade-offs. Our
      experiments demonstrate that MOC not only surpasses baseline methods in controllability, solution
532
      quality, and generalization but also does so with computational efficiency. By managing trade-offs
533
      between objectives and offering a superior Pareto front, MOC is well-suited for real-world applica-
534   tions where flexibility and personalization are critical. This work highlights the potential of MOC
535   to transform how LLMs interact with users, offering scalable and customizable solutions that meet
536   diverse needs while maintaining computational feasibility. Looking forward, MOC paves the way for
537   future research in personalized LLMs. The future work is to scale up the method with larger models.
538   Exploring more complex user preferences and further enhancing scalability will be key to unlocking
539   even broader applications for customizable and efficient LLMs in real-world settings. Ultimately,
      MOC represents a significant step toward realizing fully personalized, human-friendly systems.

856   C    L OSS F UNCTIONS IN RL C ANNOT B E U SED FOR A LIGNMENT OR C ONTROL
857        WITH P REFERENCES
858
859   The primary objective in RL is to train an agent to make decisions that maximize cumulative rewards
860   over time To achieve this, various learning algorithms are employed, each associated with specific
861   loss functions. However, these loss functions do not always directly measure the agent’s performance
862   in achieving high rewards. This discrepancy arises because the losses are often surrogate measures
863   designed to optimize certain aspects of the agent’s behavior rather than direct evaluations of the
      cumulative reward.


                                                          16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      C.1     VALUE F UNCTION L OSS
865
866   The value function in RL, typically denoted as V (s) for state value or Q(s, a) for state-action value,
867   estimates the expected cumulative reward from a given state (or state-action pair). The loss function
868   for the value function, often referred to as the Temporal Difference (TD) error, is given by
869
                                            h                             i
870                                                                     2
                                     LV = Eπ (Rt + γV (St+1 ) − V (St )) ,                                 (16)
871
872   where
873
              • Rt is the reward received at time step t,
874
875           • γ is the discount factor,
876           • V (St ) is the estimated value of the current state,
877           • V (St+1 ) is the estimated value of the next state.
878
879   This loss function aims at minimizing the difference between the predicted value and the bootstrapping
880   target, adjusted for the discount factor. While minimizing this loss improves the accuracy of the value
881   function estimate, it does not directly ensure that the agent’s policy maximizes the cumulative reward.
882   An accurate value function is essential for effective policy evaluation and improvement, but an agent
883
      may have a low value function loss without necessarily following an optimal policy.
884
885   C.2     P OLICY G RADIENT L OSS
886
      Policy gradient methods directly optimize the policy by adjusting parameters to maximize the
887   expected cumulative reward. The loss function for policy gradient methods, particularly in the context
888   of REINFORCE, can be represented as
889                                             " T                         #
890                                              X
                                   Lπ = −Eπ           log πθ (At |St ) · Ât ,                          (17)
891
                                                     t=0
892
893
      where
894
              • πθ (At |St ) is the probability of taking action At in state St under the policy π parameterized
895             by θ,
896
897
              • Ât is the advantage function.
898   This loss function aims to maximize the expected return by increasing the probability of actions
899   that lead to higher advantages. However, the policy gradient loss focuses on immediate policy
900   improvements based on sampled trajectories and advantage estimates, which may not fully capture
901   long-term performance. Additionally, high variance in gradient estimates can lead to unstable training
902   and suboptimal policies even if the loss is minimized.
903
904   C.3     C ASE OF USING VALUE FUNCTION AS ALIGNED TARGET
905
906   One might ask whether using value functions as an aligned target is effective. The experiments in
907   Figure 1 were conducted using the state value function as an aligned target, providing a practical case
908
      demonstrating its applicability.
909
910   C.4     D ISCUSSION
911
      Both the value function loss and the policy gradient loss serve as proxies to guide the training process
912   toward policies that yield higher rewards. However, these losses do not always correlate perfectly
913   with the agent’s overall performance due to several factors:
914
915           • Long-term Dependencies: These loss functions primarily focus on immediate or short-term
916             improvements. In contrast, the ultimate goal of RL is to maximize long-term cumula-
917             tive rewards, which may involve complex dependencies and delayed rewards that are not
                adequately captured by immediate losses.


                                                           17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
             • Sample Dependence: The loss functions rely on sampled trajectories, which may not fully
919            represent the underlying state-action space, especially in environments with high variability
920            or sparse rewards.
921
             • Approximation Errors: Both value function approximations and policy gradient estimates
922
               are subject to errors due to function approximation, which can lead to suboptimal updates.
923
924   While value function loss and policy gradient loss are essential components of the training process in
925   reinforcement learning, they do not provide a comprehensive measure of the agent’s true performance
926   in terms of achieving high cumulative rewards. Therefore, these loss functions cannot be effectively
927   used for alignment or control tasks involving preference vectors.

1374         O PTIMIZATION
1375
1376   In this section, we provide a formal definition of Pareto Optimality and its relevance to policy
1377   improvement.
1378
1379   I.1   F ORMAL D EFINITION OF PARETO O PTIMALITY
1380
1381   Definition 1. Let π, π ′ ∈ X , where X is the set of feasible solutions. A solution π is said to dominate
1382
       another solution π ′ if and only if:
1383          • Ji (π) ≥ Ji (π ′ ) for all i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , N }, and
1384
1385          • Jj (π) > Jj (π ′ ) for at least one j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , N }.
1386
       Here, Ji (π) denotes the value of the i-th objective for the solution π. The above conditions imply
1387
       that π performs at least as well as π ′ in all objectives and strictly better in at least one. Solutions that
1388   are not dominated by any other are termed non-dominated and collectively form the Pareto front.
1389
       Definition 2. (Pareto Optimality) Let X denote the set of feasible solutions, and let J : X → RN
1390
       be a vector-valued objective function where J(π) = [J1 (π), J2 (π), . . . , JN (π)]⊤ corresponds to the
1391   objective values associated with π ∈ X . A solution π ∗ ∈ X is Pareto optimal if and only if no other
1392   solution π ′ ∈ X satisfies:
1393
1394
                                         Ji (π ′ ) ≥ Ji (π ∗ ) ∀i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , N }                         (22)
1395
1396
       and
                                Jj (π ′ ) > Jj (π ∗ ) for at least one j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , N }.                 (23)
1397
1398   This ensures that π ∗ is non-dominated, meaning that no other solution can improve one or more
1399   objectives without sacrificing performance in at least one other.
1400
1401   I.2   A DVANTAGE OF P OLICY I MPROVEMENT
1402
1403   Explicit policy improvement refers to methods that deliberately optimize at least one objective Ji ,
       ensuring that the solution quality improves by maximizing one or more associated rewards Ri . This


                                                              26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       approach is particularly crucial in designing multi-objective policies, as it guarantees measurable
1405   progress in one or more dimensions of performance.
1406
1407
       A DVANTAGE OF MOC C OMPARED TO OTHER BASELINES
1408
1409   Our proposed method, MOC, explicitly optimizes all objectives while integrating controllability,
1410   ensuring a more balanced and efficient approach to policy improvement. In contrast:
1411
1412         • Rewarded Soup does not jointly optimize all objectives, which leads to suboptimal solu-
1413           tions.
1414         • RiC focuses exclusively on controllability but lacks explicit mechanisms for policy improve-
1415           ment, limiting its ability to enhance solution quality.
1416         • MODPO does not consider Pareto Optimality during training. Specifically, it trains M
1417           separate LLMs (corresponding to M preferences) by optimizing each model with a specific
1418           weighted combination of reward objectives, given the corresponding reward models.
1419
1420   By integrating both explicit policy improvement and controllability into a unified framework, MOC
1421
       theoretically achieves higher solution quality compared to these baselines. This is further validated
       by our experimental results (Tables 1 to 4 and 10 to 12 and Figures 2, 3, 5 and 6), which demonstrate
1422
       that MOC consistently outperforms these approaches across multiple metrics.
1423
1424   The integration of explicit policy improvement with controllability ensures that MOC aligns with the
1425   principles of Pareto Optimality while delivering superior practical performance. By addressing the
1426   limitations of existing methods and achieving a better balance among competing objectives, MOC
1427
       sets a new benchmark in multi-objective controllable language models.
