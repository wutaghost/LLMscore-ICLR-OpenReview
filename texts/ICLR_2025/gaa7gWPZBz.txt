009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Robustness and privacy are two fundamental security properties that machine
012            learning models require. Without balance between robustness and privacy leads to
013            robust models with high privacy risks. Obtaining machine learning models with
014            high adversarial robustness and privacy performance remains an open problem. In
015            order to enhance privacy performance of robust models, we employ counterfactual
016            explanations as a method to mitigate privacy risks while concurrently maintaining
017            robust model accuracy, reducing the privacy risk of the robust model to the level
018            of random guessing and using counterfactual explanations to generate adversarial
019
               examples for the first time. We analyze the similarities and differences between
               adversarial examples and counterfactual explanations and utilize these properties
020
               to design the generation method. We conduct an in-depth analysis of the advan-
021
               tages offered by counterfactual explanations compared to traditional adversarial
022            examples. Our study indicates that the correlation between robustness and privacy
023            is strong and the ideal balance state of accuracy, robustness, and privacy is with
024            95% adversarial examples involved in model training.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Adversarial attack is adding an imperceptible perturbation to the input image that leads a machine
030   learning model to misclassify the perturbed input image with high confidence (Goodfellow et al.,
031   2015). Adversarial examples are inputs that have been perturbed by attackers. To build adversarially
032   robust machine learning models that can defend adversarial attacks, a variety of defense strategies
033   have been put forth (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016; Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Madry et al., 2018).
034   Adversarial training, a training scheme that involves supplementing the training datasets with ad-
035   versarial examples, is one of the most popular and effective defense strategies (Goodfellow et al.,
036   2015).
037   With the growing development and application of adversarial example generation techniques in
038   real-world settings, the issue of protecting privacy in data-analytic training has gained significant
039   attention. Severe privacy issues are raised by privacy attacks, especially the privacy risks on robust
040   models (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Ganju et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2020). Compared to non-robust
041   models trained on original examples, robust models trained on adversarial examples pose a greater
042   risk to privacy (Song et al., 2019). Defenses against such attacks on robust models have proven
043   largely ineffective, despite higher risks.
044   To enhance the non-robust model privacy performance, a variety of techniques are frequently em-
045   ployed (Huang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). When privacy alone is taken into account, they func-
046   tion perfectly. However, these techniques weaken the ability of robust models to predict outcomes,
047   making the models unable to function normally (Song et al., 2019). The privacy risk caused by
048   adversarial examples remains an open problem.
049   The connection between adversarial robustness and privacy received significant interest. Recent
050   discussions suggest that there may be a fundamental trade-off, as achieving both robustness and
051   privacy within the same model appears unfeasible (Zhang et al., 2019; Hopkins et al., 2023). A
052   primary factor contributing to privacy risks is the generalization gap between adversarial and original
053   examples (Yeom et al., 2018). Privacy attackers can readily detect differences in distribution between
      these two types of examples, which increases privacy risks in robust models.

093   2   C OUNTERFACTUAL E XPLANATIONS AND A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES
094
095   In this section, we analyze the similarities and differences between counterfactual explanations and
096   adversarial examples. By utilizing both, we generate counterfactual adversarial examples aimed at
097   reducing the privacy risks in robust models.
098
      Counterfactual explanation is a technique used in explainable AI. Counterfactual explanations sug-
099
      gest what should be different in the input instance to change the outcome of an AI system (Guidotti,
100
      2024). Recently, it has been suggested that there is a similarity and connection between counterfac-
101   tual explanations and adversarial examples (Pawelczyk et al., 2022; Freiesleben, 2022).
102
103   Given an image-label pair (x, y) and a classifier f , an adversarial example is a perturbed image
104
      x̃ = x + δ, which is mis-classified by the model, i.e., f (x̃)̸=y, which is the untargeted adversarial
      example, f (x̃) = ỹ, for targeted adversarial example.
105
106   For the same pair(x, y) and the classifier f , a counterfactual explanation is a perturbed image
107   x′ = x + δ ′ , f (x′ )̸=y, use the generating process to explain why x belong to class y. f (x′ )̸=y
      to explain the reason why x belong to class y other than class y ′ . The definitions for both untargeted

108
      and targeted adversarial examples share similarities. The commonalities between counterfactual
109   explanations and adversarial examples are as follows:
110
111          • Both involve adding perturbations to the original sample.
112          • The perturbed samples are intended to alter the model’s prediction.
113
114   The generated sample displays commonalities between adversarial examples and counterfactual ex-
115   planations in the initial stage of generating. However, as the process proceeds, counterfactual expla-
116
      nations emphasize interpretability, focusing on when and why the predicted classification changes.
      By the end of the generating process, the counterfactual explanation resembles another class. In con-
117
      trast, the perturbation of the adversarial example remains imperceptible to humans, in other words,
118
      seeking uninterpretability. The new features of the adversarial example are strong enough to change
119   model prediction, but the adversarial example still looks similar to the original sample. While the
120   new features of counterfactual explanation should be obvious and interpretable for humans, and its
121   constraint of minimal perturbation can be negligible without considering computation efficiency.
122
      Moreover, adversarial examples and counterfactual explanations have different constraints. For
123
      counterfactual explanations, interpretability is key, meaning the altered sample must be noticeably
124
      distinct from the original. In contrast, adversarial examples involve imperceptible perturbations.
125   While both forms of perturbation shift the predicted class, the extent of the sample modification
126   varies. The crucial distinction is that a counterfactual explanation is visibly different from its origi-
127   nal, while an adversarial example closely resembles the original.

146   To clarify the relationship between counterfactual explanations and adversarial examples, we use
147   a simple picture to demonstrate, as shown in Figure 2. Adversarial examples and counterfactual
148   explanations both change the class of the sample, so they both cross the dicision boundary. At some
149   point in the generation process, adversarial examples and counterfactual explanations may share the
150   same tendency in direction to change key features for class alteration. But their constraints differ, so
151   there are distance between the generated samples.
152   By considering the similarities and differences between counterfactual explanations and adversarial
153   examples, we create counterfactual adversarial examples. This involves locating the nearest neigh-
154   bor of the original sample from another class. We generate adversarial examples of the original
155   by producing counterfactual explanations of the neighbor sample. During the counterfactual gen-
156   eration, the features of the generated sample shift from the neighbor’s attributes to resemble the
157   original. The result is an adversarial example with characteristics of the original sample, while still
158
      retaining some features from the neighbor class. This ensures that the adversarial example incor-
      porates features from the neighbor sample, enough to alter the model’s prediction rather than being
159
      identical to the original sample.
160
161   To ensure privacy protection, we need an appropriate counterfactual explanation method. There
      are different types of counterfactual explanation generation methods (Guidotti, 2024). However,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      not all of them effectively reduce privacy risks. The privacy concerns associated with generative
163   models, such as GAN and others, remain a prominent issue (Hitaj et al., 2017; Hayes et al., 2019).
164   Consequently, counterfactual explanation methods based on generative models are excluded from
165   consideration. In contrast, autoencoders offer improved privacy performance, making them the best
166   choice for generating counterfactual explanations.
167
      Autoencoders enhance data privacy by obfuscating sensitive information while retaining essential
168
      features for various applications. Several studies propose innovative approaches utilizing autoen-
169   coders to address privacy concerns in different domains (Ding et al., 2023; Jamshidi et al., 2024; Liu
170   et al., 2023). Samples generated in the latent space exhibit features that are less perceptible to hu-
171   mans after decoded, aligning more closely with the definition of adversarial examples. Latent space
172   enhances data privacy by inferring cluster locations and scales from connection numbers alone,
173   eliminating the need for node-level data and protecting data privacy (Hajihassani et al., 2020). Ad-
174   ditionally, leveraging a latent space navigation strategy can generate diverse synthetic samples while
175   addressing privacy concerns, minimizing the risk of near-duplicates and supporting effective deep
176   model training (Raja, 2024).
177   To keep privacy risks minimal, we select a method that prioritizes protecting the privacy of the
178   original samples. To reduce the privacy risk of adversarial examples, the key is to decrease the gen-
179   eralization gap between the adversarial example dataset and the training dataset. We use a sparsity
180   loss term in the counterfactual explanations to make the data distribution of adversarial examples
181   more similar to the original dataset. This approach reduces the privacy risk cause by disparity be-
182   tween these two data distributions. Sparsity can enhance privacy in neural networks. Research has
183
      shown that introducing sparsity in neural networks can bolster their data privacy, ultimately leading
      to improved privacy without compromising task performance (Chen et al., 2020).
184
185   Another reason for increased privacy risks in adversarial examples is the privacy leakage of indi-
186   vidual data. The adversarial example loss term is usually calculated on the same individual sample
187   iteratively, and the prediction model is more likely to remember the sample in the process of adver-
188   sarial example generating and model training. We use the counterfactual explanation method that
189
      calculates the prototype of every classification instead, and the loss term can guide the algorithm to
      generate the adversarial examples close to the average shape of the targeted classification, instead of
190
      the ones that easily cause privacy leakage of individual data.

255
256   We improved the counterfactual explanations method (Van Looveren & Klaise, 2021) to make the
257   generated samples with more characteristics of adversarial examples and less privacy risk. The
258
      method produces samples towards the prototype of each class. Firstly, the counterfactual explana-
      tions generated by the method exhibit the imperceptible nature of adversarial examples. Secondly,
259
      these explanations are directed towards the prototype of the ground-truth y which x belongs to,
260
      rather than focusing solely on individual sample x. The samples produced by this approach have
261   the average and collective characteristics of the whole class, without leaking the special and unnec-
262   essary characteristics of an individual sample. Moreover, the adversarial examples xadv generated
263   by this approach encapsulate more meaningful features related to the ground-truth y of sample x,
264   rather than isolated characteristics of sample x or perturbations as meaningless noise. This is the
265   reason why the method can facilitate the acquisition of necessary features for adversarial examples
266   and concurrently safeguard data privacy simultaneously.

277   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
278
279
      We utilize CNN as the machine learning model architecture. The CNN model structure used in the
      experimentcontains three dropout layers, as greater dropout layers often yield better generalization
280
      effects (Song et al., 2019). We ensure consistency in each experiment by employing the same model
281
      architecture for both adversarial example generation and training processes. The models are all fully
282   trained but do not overfit with the training datasets to reduce the impact of the generalization gap
283   as much as possible. We separate the adversarial examples generation and training process of all
284   generation methods instead of put the two processes together as the original generation method to
285   mitigate the impact of the machine learning model itself. To assess the privacy risk of these CNN
286   models, we employ a membership inference attack (MIA). MIA attackers aim to determine whether
287   given data belongs to a training set (Shokri et al., 2017). It is a technique widely used to evaluate
288   how much privacy is compromised during the training process. All experiments are conducted
289   under the black-box MIA setting. The attacker model can only achieve the prediction results of the
290
      training model. Our research aims to reduce the privacy risk of adversarial examples and explore
      the relationship between robustness and privacy, contrasting with white-box MIA studies focusing
291
      on the model’s inherent data privacy leakage. We utilize a state-of-the-art attack method specifically
292
      designed for robust models (Song et al., 2019).

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this paper, we aimed to create in-distribution adversarial examples by leveraging the similarities
435   and differences between adversarial examples and counterfactual explanations to reduce the privacy
436   risks of robust models. By utilizing counterfactual explanations from the nearest neighbor class,
437   we generated adversarial examples with enhanced privacy. Our proposed counterfactual adversarial
438   examples offer better privacy protection, more meaningful and semantic perturbations, and maintain
439   an acceptable level of accuracy. Although we have improved the method for other kinds of training
440   datasets and obtained certain effects, there are still deficiencies in the targeted adversarial examples,
441   such as two classes of excessive distance. Constrained by the length of the paper, we will leave it as
442   future work.
