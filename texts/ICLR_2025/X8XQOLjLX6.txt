009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Autoencoders are frequently used for anomaly detection, both in the unsupervised
012            and semi-supervised settings. They rely on the assumption that when trained us-
013            ing the reconstruction loss, they will be able to reconstruct normal data more ac-
014            curately than anomalous data. Some recent works have posited that this assump-
015            tion may not always hold, but little has been done to study the validity of the
016            assumption in theory. In this work we show that this assumption indeed does not
017            hold, and illustrate that anomalies, lying far away from normal data, can be per-
018
               fectly reconstructed in practice. We extend the understanding of autoencoders for
               anomaly detection by showing how they can perfectly reconstruct out of bounds,
019
               or extrapolate undesirably, and note how this can be dangerous in safety critical
020
               applications. We connect theory to practice by showing that the proven behav-
021            ior in linear autoencoders also occurs when applying non-linear autoencoders on
022            both tabular data and real-world image data, the two primary application areas of
023            autoencoders for anomaly detection.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Autoencoders are one of the most popular architectures within anomaly detection, either directly, or
029   as a scaffold or integral part in larger pipelines or architectures. They are commonly used across a
030   variety of domains, such as predictive maintenance (Kamat & Sugandhi, 2020), network anomaly
031   detction (Said Elsayed et al., 2020), and intrusion detection (Farahnakian & Heikkonen, 2018),
032   but find much contemporary use in computer vision anomaly detection, with applications such as
033
      industrial inspection (Tsai & Jen, 2021), medical imaging (Wei et al., 2018), structural health mon-
      itoring (Chow et al., 2020) and video surveillance (Zhao et al., 2017; Cruz-Esquivel & Guzman-
034
      Zavaleta, 2022). Many of these applications are safety critical, meaning that the reliability of these
035
      algorithms is of utmost importance in order to prevent catastrophic failure and associated dangers
036   and consequences.
037
038   Anomaly detection using autoencoders typically relies on using the reconstruction loss, often the
039
      mean squared error (MSE), as a proxy for “anomalousness”. The underlying assumption is that
      anomalies are harder to reconstruct, and will therefore have a higher reconstruction loss. How-
040
      ever, the validity of this assumption has been questioned in recent years. Merrill & Eskandarian
041
      (2020) and Beggel et al. (2020) for example state that anomalies in the training data might lead to
042   reconstruction of anomalies, leading to unreliable detectors. Some researchers have noted that re-
043   construction of unseen anomalies might also occur in the semi-supervised setting, where the training
044   data is assumed to contain no anomalies (Astrid et al., 2021; 2024; Gong et al., 2019; Zong et al.,
045   2018). Yet, little work has been done on the nature of failure and reliability of autoencoders beyond
046   experimental evaluation, leaving a gap in theoretical rigor.
047
      In this work we provide valuable insights into the reliability of autoencoders for anomaly detec-
048   tion. Following the seminal works of Bourlard & Kamp (1988) and Baldi & Hornik (1989) we
049   develop a theory on autoencoder failure modes, whilst briefly examining how various activation
050   functions influence these failures. We show that this theory is not just a rarely occurring edge case,
051   but also show failure cases on tabular data and on real-world image data commonly used in anomaly
052   detection benchmarks. By doing this we provide a foundation for future research in solving the
053   demonstrated unreliability of autoencoders for anomaly detection and furthering our understand-
      ing of autoencoders. We how that for different architectures and activations functions, even with

058   2   R ELATED W ORK
059
060   This work is not the first to recognize that autoencoders have several issues as anomaly detectors.
061   The most discussed type of failure is the unwanted reconstruction of anomalies, which is also the
062   focus of this work. Several causes of this unwanted behavior have been proposed.
063   Many works focus on the unsupervised setting, and observe that contrary to prior belief, autoen-
064   coders will fairly easily reconstruct any anomalies present in the training data, leading to an unusable
065   detector (Merrill & Eskandarian, 2020; Beggel et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2022).
066
      Several works cover the anomaly reconstruction problem within the semi-supervised setting. Most
067
      commonly, it is only experimentally observed that anomalies are well reconstructed (Gong et al.,
068   2019; Zong et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2021; Astrid et al., 2021; 2024; Salehi et al., 2021; Nalisnick
069   et al., 2019; Lyudchik, 2016). Based on these experimental results, some solutions have been pro-
070   posed. Gong et al. (2019) mention that out-of-bounds reconstruction can occur and propose adding a
071   memory module to the autoencoder to alleviate the issue. While the addition of the memory module
072   can aid in limiting out-of-bounds reconstruction, it also leads to a severely decreased reconstruc-
073   tion ability and substantial added complexity in training and optimizing the network. Zong et al.
074   (2018) note that while some anomalies have a high reconstruction loss, some occupy the region of
075   normal reconstruction loss, and add a Gaussian mixture model to the latent space to aid in detection
076
      of anomalies under the assumption that anomalies occupy a low-density region in the latent space.
      Similarly, Cheng et al. (2021) aim to detect anomalies in the latent space by looking at the distance
077
      to the centroid. From our experiments we can glean that relying on distances or densities in the
078
      latent space does not always work in practice. Astrid et al. (2021; 2024) make use of constructed
079   pseudo anomalies in training the autoencoder. They add adaptive noise to normal samples to gen-
080   erate pseudo anomalies. In the reconstruction, they then optimize the reconstruction loss between
081   the pseudo anomaly and the normal sample used to generate it. While they show promising results
082   and greater discriminative power on benchmark datasets, they do not quantify to which degree their
083   performance gains can be attributed to the reduction of the out-of-bounds reconstruction. Salehi
084   et al. (2021) aim to limit the reconstruction of anomalies by generating adversarial samples. The ad-
085   versarial examples are generated by perturbing the normal samples, and minimizing the effect those
086   perturbations have in the latent space. This is similar to the concept of denoising autoencoders.
087
      Based on our experiments, we do not think this results in a reliable autoencoder, as often adversarial
      anomalies can occupy the latent space close to normal data.
088
089   Some authors have moved beyond the experimental, and propose explanations for the anomaly re-
090   construction problem. For example You et al. (2022), Lu et al. (2023) and Bercea et al. (2023)
091   propose that anomaly reconstruction can happen because an autoencoder can learn an “identical
092   shortcut”, where both normal data and anomalous data is effectively reconstructed using an identity
093
      mapping. This point has however been countered by Cai et al. (2024) who show that by constraining
      the latent space to be sufficiently low dimensional, this problem can be avoided.
094
095   The second line of thought follows from VAE anomaly detection, where Havtorn et al. (2021) theo-
096   rize that in out-of-distribution detection, unwanted reconstruction can happen due to a high correla-
097   tion between learned low-level features for in- and out-of-distribution data.
098   A third line of thought is proposed by Zhou (2022) who propose that reconstruction of out-of-
099   distribution samples can happen due to out-of-distribution data having smaller neural activations
100   than in-distribution data.
101
      Finally, some works theorize that autoencoders can perfectly reconstruct data due to the anomalies
102
      occupying the reconstruction hyperplane, or latent space manifold. Denouden et al. (2018) for
103   example note this phenomenon, and aim to solve it by adding the Mahalanobis distance in the latent
104   space to the loss. The most detailed work is that of Yoon et al. (2021) who provide an example of
105   the hyperplane interpolating between clusters of data. They solve this by introducing a normalized

      3.1   A NOMALY D ETECTION
116
      In practical anomaly detection, we attribute a score si = fanomaly score (xi ) for each sample xi ∈ X =
117   Rn , i.e. the i-th row of dataset X with size m-by-n. The score should then be higher for anomalous
118   samples than for normal data. When applied to some dataset consisting of both normal data and
119   anomalies, i.e. X = {X normal , X anomalous }, a perfect anomaly detector will assign higher scores to
120   anomalies than to normal data: mini (fanomaly score (xanomalous
                                                            i         )) > maxi (fanomaly score (xnormal
                                                                                                  i      )).
121
      The two most common anomaly detection settings are unsupervised and semi-supervised. Un-
122
      supervised anomaly detection is characterized by having no discernible “train” and “test” splits.
123   Instead, we only consider a single dataset X = {X normal , X anomalous }, where we are uncer-
124   tain which samples are anomalous and which are not. In the semi-supervised setting we instead
125   have a conventional “train” and “test” set. The train set consists out of only normal samples:
126   X train = {X train, normal }, while the test set is unknown, and can contain both normal and anoma-
127   lous samples: X test = {X test, normal , X test, anomalous }. In this paper we will only consider the semi-
128   supervised case, and simplify the notation with X = X train and xi referring to an individual training
129   data point, which in the semi-supervised case by definition is not an anomaly. We then consider a
130   new data point a to determine whether this is an anomaly or not. In older literature, semi-supervised
131
      anomaly detection is often called one-class classification.

133   4     O UT- OF -B OUNDS R ECONSTRUCTION
134
135   In this section we will show that autoencoders can yield zero-loss reconstruction far away from all
136   training data, and that these autoencoders will then fail to detect certain anomalies. We will build our
137   theory following the results of Bourlard & Kamp (1988), moving from PCA to linear autoencoders
138   to non-linear autoencoders.
139   Out-of-bounds reconstruction is unwanted within the application of anomaly detection, as it leads
140   to a low reconstruction loss for data that can be considered anomalous, thereby leading to false neg-
141   atives. These regions of out-of-bounds reconstruction can also be exploited by targeted adversarial
142   evasion attacks.
143
      In the worst case, unwanted perfect reconstruction causes an anomaly a ∈ Rn far from all training
144   data to be ranked as being less anomalous than or equally anomalous as all training data, that is:
145   fanomaly score (a) ≤ mini (fanomaly score (xi )).

150   anomalies using their reconstruction loss, commonly known as the mean squared error, or MSE.
151   We can calculate the reconstruction loss by comparing a sample xi to its reconstruction x̂i :

      4.2   PCA
163
164   In PCA, we factorize X as X = U ΣV T using singular value decomposition (SVD), where U and
165   V are orthonormal matrices containing the left- and right-singular vectors of X, respectively, and
166   Σ is a diagonal scaling matrix. The encoding, or latent, space is then obtained by projecting onto
167   the first d right-singular vectors: Y = g(X) = XVd , where Vd contains the first d columns of V .
168   The transformation back into X is given by X̂ = h(Y ) = Y VdT .
169
      We will show that there exist some a ∈ Rn for which the reconstruction loss is zero, but that are
170   far away from the normal data, i.e. mini (dist(xi , a)) > δ, for any arbitrary choice of δ. Hereby
171   we prove that it is possible to find anomalous, adversarial, examples with perfect out-of-bounds
172   reconstruction. We can prove this even in the semi-supervised setting, where we guarantee that the
173   model was not exposed to anomalous data at training time. Due to the semi-supervised setting being
174   more restrictive, the proofs also apply to the unsupervised case.

277
278

279
      αi (1, 1), where αi is some scalar. Simply put, every normal sample xi occupies the diagonal. This

283   then lead to a perfect reconstruction for all xi . Adversarial anomalies a can also be easily found as

      clusters intersect. Due to the two distinct sigmoids mixing, we can find a hyperplane orthogonal to
362   the first principal component where the reconstruction loss is much lower than would be expected.
363   While in this case there are no points on the hyperplane which would have a lower reconstruction
364   loss than all normal data, there is still a substantial area where the reconstruction loss is lower than
365   for many of the normal data points.
366
367   Other Activation Functions While we have explicitly discussed the ReLU and sigmoid activation
368   functions, the behavior shown is similar for other activation functions. Effectively, we can categorize
369   most activation functions as those having an order of continuity of C 0 like the ReLU, or C ∞ like the
370   sigmoid. In summary, activation functions with an order of continuity of C 0 suffer most from out-
371   of-bounds reconstruction, but allow for more easily trainable deep networks. In contrast, activation
372   functions with an order of continuity of C ∞ generally have more desirable properties for anomaly
373   detection, but are harder to use in deep networks due to the vanishing gradient.

387   layer, these networks suffer less from the issues encountered in full ReLU/linear networks as dis-

490   5   C ONCLUSION
491
492   In this work we provide a theoretical basis of the unwanted reconstruction of anomalies that autoen-
493   coders can exhibit when used for anomaly detection. We move beyond existing theories of unwanted
494
      reconstruction happening in interpolation and show how unwanted out-of-bounds reconstruction can
      occur when extrapolating as well, and how this can lead to anomalies staying fully undetected. We
495
      show through several experiments that these issues can arise in real-world data and not just in theory.
496
      This leads us to some safety concerns, where autoencoders can catastrophically fail to detect obvi-
497   ous anomalies. This unreliability can have major consequences when trust is put into the anomaly
498   detector in safety-critical applications.
499
500
      In general, we solidify the growing belief that the reconstruction loss is not a reliable proxy for
      anomaly detection, especially when the network is explicitly trained to lower the reconstruction
501
      loss for normal data without constraining the reconstruction capability beyond the bounds of the
502
      normal training data such as has been done by Yoon et al. (2021). We find that this issue is most
503   prevalent for (conditionally) linear units such as the ReLU, but similar issues exist for sigmoid
504   networks, albeit to a lesser degree. The reconstruction issue is mostly caused by the fact that a point
505   in the lower-dimensional latent space corresponds to a hyperplane in the original space that the data
506   occupies. Next to interpolation and out-of-bounds reconstruction, we find that anomalies can remain
507   undetected when they occupy the latent space where normal classes border.
508
      Users of autoencoders for anomaly detection should be aware of these issues. Good practice would
509   be to at least check whether a trained non-linear autoencoder exhibits the undesirable out-of-bounds
510   reconstruction. In this paper’s illustrative examples, we checked for this by searching for adversar-
511   ial anomalies. This was relatively easy, as it could be done either visually in the latent space, or
512   through a simple 2D grid search. For more complex datasets, requiring larger latent spaces, a feasi-
513   ble strategy might be to again synthesize samples from the latent space and formulate the search for
514   adversarial anomalies as an optimization in terms of projected gradient descent (Madry et al., 2017).
515   By describing exactly how autoencoders are unreliable anomaly detectors by describing anomaly re-
516   construction, we hope to provide a scaffold for future research into fixing and avoiding the identified
517   issues in a targeted manner.
