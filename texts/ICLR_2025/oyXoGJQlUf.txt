                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               A significant body of recent work illustrates that two components of autonomous
012
               planning agents nearly always require manual pre-specification by human experts:
013            the identification and grounding of action symbols (such as “turn right”), and
014            the generation of PDDL action rules (including rule name, parameters, precon-
015            ditions, and effects). We present the Grounded Robotic Action-Rule Induction
016            through Language Models (GRAIL) system, which, in addition to automating
017            those two processes, also contributes to the expanding research on PDDL model
018            optimization. In this paper, we show how large language models (LLMs) can be
019            used to cluster the sensorimotor experience of the robot and automatically gener-
020            ate useful symbolic abstractions about the robot’s capabilities and environment.
021
               This language-grounded abstraction allows the learned domain to be modified
               and used for planning without additional retraining. We evaluate the approach
022
               in a standard maze domain and show results for automated symbol identification
023
               and grounding, automated rule generation, simulation-based rule validation, and
024            PDDL model optimization. We also discuss and illustrate the advantages of the
025            hybrid neuro-symbolic GRAIL system over traditional symbolic or purely data-
026            driven approaches to similar tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Symbolic abstractions of continuous planning domains (such as PDDL models for robotics) are
032   often useful for enabling faster planning and important constraints are often more easily expressed
033   in terms of abstract symbols. However, manually specifying the symbolic abstractions in a way
034   that is consistent with the underlying continuous domain is difficult – a problem known as symbol
035   grounding. Learning grounded symbolic abstractions from data is also known to be challenging
036   (Goel, 2022).
037   In this paper we describe a novel approach to learning grounded symbolic abstractions where we
038   hypothesize that by connecting Large Language Models (LLMs) to the physical world through robot
039   sensor and odometry data, we can generate meaningful abstractions of the continuous domain. This
040   technique represents a departure from state-of-the-art systems that require manual pre-definition of
041   symbols (Chen et al., 2023; Silver et al., 2022), enabling a more intuitive interaction between robots
042   and their operational environments.
043   Furthermore, GRAIL introduces a strategy for automatically generating PDDL action rules. Unlike
044   recent methods requiring manual pre-definition of rules (Song et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021) or em-
045   ploying LLMs for rule creation based on human-defined natural language domain descriptions, (Liu
046   et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2023), GRAIL’s approach is based on the meaningful mappings established
047   between symbols and sensor/actuator data during the symbol grounding phase, resulting in a more
048   “context-aware” set of rules. More specifically, GRAIL uses learned grounded action symbols to
049   construct natural language domain descriptions which are then used in a LLM prompt for generat-
050
      ing PDDL domains. We know from Liu et al. (2023) and Guan et al. (2023) that using LLMs for
      natural language to PDDL translation is a viable technique, but in those works, the natural language
051
      domain descriptions are primarily human-defined.
052
053   A final component of GRAIL is that as the goals of the agent change, it uses a combination of
      frequency and sensitivity analysis to rank and enable pruning of PDDL action rules. This allows


                                                        1

054
      planning solutions using only action rules relevant to the task at hand (an example of “relevance
055   reasoning” (Levy, 1994)), alleviating scaling issues common with classical planners.
056
057   In summary, we present details about the GRAIL architecture and through experiments conducted
058
      in a standard maze domain with a Drake-based (MIT, 2023) MOVO simulation (Kinova, 2023), we
      address the following questions: 1) Can GRAIL automatically identify appropriate action symbols
059
      directly from data and associate these symbols with their real-world meanings? 2) Can GRAIL
060
      automatically generate valid PDDL action rules? 3) Can GRAIL optimize, in at least a limited
061   sense, the PDDL model? We present experimental results for automatic symbol identification and
062   grounding, automatic PDDL rule generation, simulation-based rule validation, and PDDL model
063   optimization that demonstrate that natural language-based symbols and symbolic systems built upon
064   them can be derived effectively from data with little a priori knowledge or human intervention.

066   2   BACKGROUND AND P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
067   In this paper we use only fully observable deterministic planning models that can be represented
068   using PDDL 1.0 (McDermott, 2000) with the STRIPS subset (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971). Through-
069   out, we refer to a “PDDL model” as the combination of a “domain” and a “problem” (or “task”).
070   A PDDL domain consists of a name, a set of predicates, and a set of action rules (or operators).
071   Action rules are defined with a name, parameters, preconditions, and effects. For example, maze
072   models have predicates such as “at” (the robot is at cell ?x), “facing” (the robot
073   is facing direction ?dir), and “adjacent” (cell ?x is adjacent to cell ?y
074
      in direction ?dir). The maze domains have three action rules: move, turnright, and turnleft.
      An example of a GRAIL generated domain can be seen in Figure 6.A.
075
076   A PDDL problem (or task) consists of a domain name, a list of objects, an initial state, and a goal
077   state. A ground atom is a predicate and a tuple of objects where the tuple dimension is dependent
078   on the number of parameters defined for the predicate. In our maze problems these are primarily
079   cell statuses such as (blocked cell0101) and adjacencies such as (adjacent cell0101 cell0102 right).
080
      A state is described by a collection of ground atoms verified to be true, with the assumption that all
      unspecified ground atoms are false. A goal is represented by a collection of ground atoms affirmed
081
      as true in the desired goal state.
082
083   The main advancement provided by GRAIL is the connection between the sensorimotor experience
084   of the robot and its ability to auto generate useful symbolic abstractions about its own environment.
085   Specifically, GRAIL takes as inputs raw robotic sensor and odometry data (see the Experimental
086
      Results section for a complete list) and uses this data to learn grounded action symbols such as
      “turn right”, and PDDL domains built upon those symbols (see Figure 6.A). In doing this, GRAIL
087
      addresses three current problems in autonomous planning agents. Problem #1: is how to define
088
      the action symbols themselves and associate the symbols with real world meanings. For example,
089   the symbol “move forward” might be associated with a positive value of X body-axis velocity.
090   Problem #2: is how to learn the PDDL action rules (or “operators”) automatically. Problem #3: is
091   that classical planners tend to treat all elements as essential, leading to large complex search spaces.
092
      GRAIL addresses Problems #1 and #2 by leveraging the background knowledge embedded in LLMs
093
      to connect clusters of robot sensor data with symbols representative of the appropriate actions. It
094
      then uses these grounded action symbols to construct an LLM prompt for generating PDDL action
095   rules and other domain elements. GRAIL’s treatment of Problems #1 and #2 taken together provides
096   a potential solution to the symbol grounding problem formalized by Harnad (1990).
097
098
      Additionally GRAIL addresses Problem #3 by using frequency and sensitivity analyses to rank and
      optionally prune the model. We use this method to rank the action rules in the PDDL domain
099
      and quantify the impact of removing each rule. GRAIL’s treatment of Problems #2 and #3 taken
100
      together is an example of relevance reasoning (Levy, 1994) or state abstraction (Knoblock, 1994),
101   and is related to the frame problem (Dennett, 1987) in that it deals with understanding what subset
102   of environmental knowledge is relevant to the task at hand.

      The Symbol Generation and Grounding subsystem (See Figure 2) automatically generates semanti-
135
      cally valid action symbols (such as “turn right” or “move forward”), and grounds those symbols
136   with real world non-language-based modalities. The robot’s sensor and odometry data are embed-
137   ded in prompts to GPT-4 to produce action symbol candidates which are subsequently filtered with
138   a combination of HDBSCAN-based clustering (Malzer & Baum, 2020) and semantic vector space
139   model analysis (Shahmirzadi et al., 2018) to produce a set of grounded action symbols.
140
      The PDDL Action Rule Generation subsystem (See Figure 5.C) uses learned grounded action sym-
141
      bols to construct natural language-based descriptions of domains which are subsequently used in a
142   LLM prompt for generating syntactically valid PDDL domains. This is similar to methods that use
143   LLMs for natural language to PDDL translation (Liu et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2023), however, with
144   GRAIL, the natural language domain descriptions are automatically generated.
145
      The Simulation-based Action Rule Validation subsystem (See Figure 7) uses PDDL action rules gen-
146
      erated by the PDDL Action Rule Generation system as the basis for a PDDL domain. A domain
147
      specific, generalized PDDL planner is created based on this domain (Silver et al., 2024). The planner
148   generates plans for a diverse set of planning problems and statistics are kept on rule usage.
149
150   The PDDL Model Optimization subsystem (See Figure 8) first performs a frequency analysis based
151
      on rule usage statistics from the Simulation-based Rule Validation system. This analysis identifies
      the PDDL rules that are used often (which are kept), never used (which are pruned), or rarely used. A
152
      sensitivity analysis (which is task specific with varied domains) is conducted on the rarely used rules
153
      allowing these rules to be assigned a value of importance that is based on both frequency of use and
154   sensitivity to removal allowing them to be effectively kept or pruned and enabling tailoring of PDDL
155   domains for specific tasks and goals (such as prioritizing compute cost over planning efficiency).

160   The GRAIL Symbol Generation and Grounding System (See Figure 1 and Figure 2) enables an agent
161   to automatically learn its own semantically valid action symbols and to ground these symbols with
      real-world non-language-based modalities. This section describes the complete pipeline, depicted

      in Figure 2. The process begins with the generation of sensor and odometry data (Figure 2.1-2.3),
163   which is subsequently grouped into clusters representing similar actions, such as “turning right”
164   (Figure 2.4). This numerical sensor data is embedded in prompts to an LLM (Figure 2.6) to generate
165   candidate action symbols for each observation (Figure 2.8). Finally, vector space model analysis is
166   used to identify the most representative action symbol for each cluster(Figure 2.9). The outcome
167   (Figure 2.10) is a set of action symbols anchored in non-linguistic modalities through the corre-
168   sponding sensors.
169   Numerical Clustering: The goal of clustering in GRAIL is to group sensor/odometry observations
170   into clusters that represent high level abstract action concepts (such as “turn right”), and not specific
171   values of states (such as Vx = 0.9 m/s. Therefore, GRAIL’s numerical data clustering (Figure 2.4)
172   attempts to group sensor/odometry data into clusters representative of similar actions. Hierarchical
173   Density Based Spatial Clustering for Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN) (Berba, 2020) was used
174   as the capabilities of HDBSCAN aligned with expected requirements for GRAIL (e.g., we do not
175   want to pre-specify the number/shape of clusters, but we do expect to deal with noise/outliers).

242   We now have an action symbol candidate associated with each data observation. A snippet of this
243   data is seen in Figure 4.B. Figure 5.A shows that the LLM outputs at this stage are mostly semanti-
244   cally correct, but still include noise (misleading or occasionally incorrect action symbol recommen-

247   Vector Space Model Analysis: Now that we have our action symbol candidates grouped into clusters
248   of similar actions (for example, rotate left, turn left, and spin counterclockwise might all be in the
249   same cluster), the last step is to find the “best” action symbol to represent each cluster. We do this
250   using vector space model analysis (VSMA) and we tried two different VSMA methods. The first
251   was a frequency only based approach called term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF)
252   (Sammut & Webb, 2011), and the second used OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small model to create
253
      an embedding vector for each action symbol candidate. In both cases, the action symbols were
      preprocessed to remove all capital letters and all punctuation, then a vector representation for each
254
      action symbol candidate was created, the vector centroid of all the vectors was determined, and the
255
      nearest neighbor was found. The action symbol candidate associated with this vector then became
256   the final action symbol for that cluster. Thus we end up with a single grounded action symbol for
257   each cluster.

      PDDL Action Rule Generation: The GRAIL PDDL Action Rule Generation System (See Figure 1)
262
      takes in a set of grounded action symbols and automatically learns a PDDL rule set. The method
263
      (Figure 5.C) is similar to the methods used by Liu et al. (2023) and Guan et al. (2023) in that it uses
264   an LLM to convert natural language domain descriptions into PDDL files. However, in those works
265   by Liu et al. (2023) and Guan et al. (2023), the natural language descriptions are human-provided
266   whereas here they are constructed using the learned action symbols. Figure 5.D shows an exam-
267   ple of such a prompt. This prompt was constructed using grounded action symbols and additional
268   information. The prompt parts that come from action symbols are highlighted in green, and the addi-
269   tional information about the environment and PDDL version (necessary to ensure domain/problem
      compatibility) is highlighted in blue. This additional information is human provided.

      PDDL Action Rule Validation: The Action Rule Validation system is shown in Figure 7. The PDDL
271   domain file generated by the Action Rule Generation system along with a PDDL problem file that
272   specified a 10x10 maze (See Figure 6.C) were input to a Fast Downward solver (Helmert, 2011)
273   resulting in a first check on the validity of the rules as Fast Downward will not produce a solution for
274   syntactically invalid rules (See Figure 6.B). For cases where invalid rules were generated, we used an
275   automated interactive debugging scheme as detailed by Silver et al. (2024) and shown in the “Invalid
276   Rule Handling” in Figure 7. We re-prompted the LLM with the errors output by Fast Downward
277   to iteratively refine rules until a valid set was generated (usually in 0-2 iterations). Simulations
278   were then conducted with a Kinova MOVO (Kinova, 2023) simulation in a Drake (MIT, 2023)
279
      simulation environment and the robot was able to successfully navigate the maze (See Figure 6.C).
      Subsequently, we implemented a python-based domain-specific generalized planner as shown in
280
      Figure 7 which was capable of quickly and efficiently generating solutions to many different tasks
281
      for the same domain. This generalized planner was created following the methodology described by
282   Silver et al. (2024).
283
284   PDDL Model Optimization: The GRAIL PDDL Model Optimization system (Figure 8) makes use
285
      of frequency and sensitivity analyses that can ascertain the relative importance of PDDL model
      elements (like action rules) in a manner that is, in at least a limited sense, both domain general and
286
      problem general. The system makes use of rule usage statistics received from the rule validation
287
      system to conduct a frequency analysis of the rules. This gives an understanding of which rules
288   are often used, relatively rarely used, or never used. In addition it uses a domain general sensitivity
289   analysis implemented in Python in a manner similar to the generalized planners in Figure 7 and by
290   Silver et al. (2024). The distinction between the sensitivity analysis and the generalized planner
291   lies in their scope and application. Generalized planners are domain-specific and solve planning
292   problems for a variety of tasks, whereas the sensitivity analysis is task-specific and generates plans
293   using variations of a particular domain. In this case, the sensitivity analysis creates various rule
294   related ablations of the domain by removing each rule in turn and checking the effect on the planning
295   solution cost (which is, in this case, the number of actions in the resulting plan).

404   Comparing GRAIL with non-symbolic RL-based approaches: To compare GRAIL with a non-
405
      symbolic RL-based approach to the same task, we modified our maze so instead of defining maze
      cells as “open” or “blocked” we assigned a color to each cell. The initial state was designated as
406
      “green” and the goal state “blue”. Impassable edge cells were designated as “red”, and the rest of the
407
      cells in the maze were designated as either white or black. Specifically, this was done by only mod-
408   ifying the human provided information in the LLM prompt used by GRAIL to generate its PDDL
409   domain (See Figure 5.D). This resulted in a GRAIL-generated variation of the domain in Figure 6.A
410   that accounted for these new predicates. We then used this domain with Fast Downward (Helmert,
411   2011) to enable maze navigation keeping only to the white cells, as shown in Figure 11A.
412
      In parallel, we also trained a non-symbolic Q-Learning-based policy by iteratively updating a Q-
413
      table using the following update rule: Q(s, a) ← Q(s, a) + α (r + γ maxa Q(s′ , a) − Q(s, a)),
414   where: Q(s, a) represents the Q-value for taking action a in state s, α is the learning rate (set to
415   0.1 in this experiment), r is the reward received after taking action a in state s, γ is the discount
416   factor (set to 0.9), and maxa Q(s′ , a) represents the maximum Q-value of the next state s′ . After
417   optimization, this learned policy was also able to successfully navigate the maze keeping only to the
418   white cells (albeit taking a slightly different path) as shown in Figure 11C.
419
      Next, the LLM that generated GRAIL’s PDDL domain was prompted as follows: “Give me the
420   same domain with only a single difference. Invert the meaning of black and white”. And the
421   LLM produced a modified domain that enabled the agent to traverse the completely unchanged
422   environment, but now keeping only to the black cells as shown in Figure 11B. The Q-learning-based
423   agent was also able to successfully navigate the original maze with the new requirement of keeping
424   to black cells instead of white, but only after retraining that, as shown in Figure 11 D-E, was more
425   costly than the original policy optimization. The increased cost was due to the agent having to
426   overcome the misleading influence of previously beneficial Q-values after initially optimizing its
427   policy for the original environment.
428   This example illustrates how symbols can be used to represent concepts abstractly. In this case
429   “black” and “white” do not tie directly to specific physical attributes of the environment but instead
430   represent higher-level concepts that can apply under various circumstances. This level of abstrac-
431   tion allows GRAIL to maintain its functionality at a constant cost even when the low-level details
      (such as whether we want to keep to black or white cells) change. This illustrates one way that

      symbolic systems can generalize better across similar tasks because they operate on these high-level
433   abstractions. Whereas, this generalization is more challenging for RL systems that learn policies
434   based on the patterns of rewards and penalties associated with particular low-level states encoun-
435   tered during training. Finally, it is noted that GRAIL realizes the benefits of both symbolic systems,
436   and the “data-driven” benefit of traditional RL systems since both the symbols and the symbolic sys-
437   tems in GRAIL can be generated automatically from data with little a priori knowledge or human
438   intervention.

      Related Work: It has been well over three decades since Harnad published his seminal work on
      symbol grounding (Harnad, 1990), and its relevance to autonomous agents has been consistently
454
      revisited in the years since (Steels & Belpaeme, 2005; Steels, 2008; Cubek et al., 2015; Rasheed &
455
      Amin, 2016). There is a recent renewed focus on the topic as the tasks and operating environments
456   faced by these agents become more complex (Dushkin, 2022; Valenzo et al., 2022).
457
458   Concurrent with this re-emerging focus on grounding, Large Language Models (LLMs) have
459
      emerged as a transformative force, exhibiting remarkably versatile utility in a wide variety of do-
      mains including agent reasoning (Mahowald et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023), mathematical problem
460
      solving (Imani et al., 2023; He-Yueya et al., 2023), and robotics (Chen et al., 2023; Song et al.,
461
      2023; Driess et al., 2023). However, a well-known limitation of LLMs is their lack of grounding in
462   other real-world non-language modalities.
463
464   Many recent efforts employ LLMs in various ways in planning systems. While significant benefits
465
      have been realized, these systems have some common limitations. For example, they often require
      manual pre-definition of symbols (where both the symbols themselves and the meanings of those
466
      symbols are provided by human users) (Chen et al., 2023; Silver et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023;
467
      Driess et al., 2023) and/or the manual pre-definition of elements of PDDL models (such as the
468   action rules in the domain file) (Chen et al., 2023; Silver et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Li et al.,
469   2021). There have also been efforts to use LLMs to directly generate PDDL. Most of these have
470   shared two common limitations (both of which are addressed by GRAIL) 1) the need for natural
471   language descriptions of the domain and/or problem, and 2) lack of grounding. For example, The
472   system developed by Guan et al. (2023) requires natural language inputs as well as other PDDL
473   files and in this system “LLMs may regularly overlook the physical plausibility of actions in certain
474   states.”
475   In addition, several recent efforts attempt to use LLMs to directly solve the planning problem. For
476   example, Silver et al. (2022) use a single PDDL domain file and many PDDL problem files as input
477   to an LLM which then generates Python code that, when presented with other PDDL problem files,
478   can solve a range of problems within the domain. In addition to losing the formal guarantees of
479   classical planners, this system requires a significant amount of context, relies on human-readable
480   names, and performs sub-optimally on domains where certain semantic features or critical aspects
481   of the environment are not explicitly described in the domain files. In contrast GRAIL makes use
482
      of and retains the guarantees of a classical planner, and does not require human-generated natural
      language domain descriptions.
483
484   Finally, Chitnis et al. (2022) show transition models akin to PDDL operators can be learned from
485   data. This differs from GRAIL in that the symbols, including predicates for defining preconditions
      and effects are human-provided, and the system does not generate PDDL action rules or domains.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Future Work: The initial actuation sequence for the maze navigation problem was chosen such that
487   the robot was only performing a single type of action at a time (i.e. it was only turning or moving
488   forward, but never turning and moving forward) and this simplified the data clustering. In the future,
489   we plan to replace the human-defined actuation sequence with an exploration algorithm (possibly
490   similar to the FarMap algorithm (Hwang et al., 2023)) that enables the robot to discover its own
491   action capabilities. In the area of automatic PDDL rule generation future work will include imple-
492   mentation of an improved method that uses a combination of Inductive Logic Programming (ILP)
493   (Cropper & Dumančić, 1991) and Constrained Text Generation LLMs (CTG-LLMs) (Garbacea &
494   Mei, 2022) to induce semantically correct, and syntactically valid, PDDL rules. Finally, we plan to
495
      implement GRAIL in more complex experimental domains that incorporate elements of both object
      manipulation and navigation. In particular, we are interested in experimental benchmark domains
496
      that can fail in certain problem configurations, but that can subsequently succeed by learning an
497
      additional action rule (and/or predicate).

499   8   C ONCLUSIONS
500
      This paper presents the GRAIL architecture along with experimental results from each of the four
501
      main subsystems shown in Figure 1. The results presented for the symbol generation and grounding
502
      system were intuitive and promising. Clustering will become more challenging as the complexity
503   of the initial actuation sequences increase, but the clustering algorithms implemented here should
504   handle this. The GRAIL Automatic Rule Generation implementation (an automatically generated
505   natural language domain description converted to PDDL by GPT-4) worked well. It is yet to be seen
506   how this method handles more complex problems. The GRAIL Rule Validation and PDDL Model
507   Optimization systems also worked as expected, albeit on a simple experimental domain.
508
      The significant contributions of this work include the automation of two processes that have tradi-
509   tionally required manual human intervention: symbol generation and grounding, and PDDL action
510   rule specification. Automation of these processes provides a potential solution to the long standing
511   symbol grounding problem detailed by Harnad (1990). In addition, this work contributes to the
512   current problem of PDDL model optimization. There are multiple efforts underway to rank and
513   prune objects in PDDL problems. Every object that can be removed reduces computational cost,
514   particularly in the operator grounding phase of most classical solvers. GRAIL enables action rules
515   to be ranked and the effect of removing rules to be understood. As GRAIL is intended to operate
516   with an action rule “meta-library” and the rule frequency and sensitivity analyses described in Sec-
517
      tion VII can be used to choose a subset of that library based on current goals, this is an example of
      relevance reasoning (Levy, 1994) or state abstraction (Knoblock, 1994). It is related to the frame
518
      problem (Dennett, 1987) in that it deals with understanding what subset of environmental knowledge
519
      is relevant to the task at hand.
520
521   Perhaps the most important potential contribution of GRAIL is the ability for an autonomous agent to
522   explore its own capabilities and environment and generate its own domain description with minimal
523
      human interaction.
