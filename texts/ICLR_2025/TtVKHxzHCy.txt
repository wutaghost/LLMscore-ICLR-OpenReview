009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, retrieval-based language models (RLMs) have received much attention.
012            However, most of them leverage a pre-trained retriever with fixed parameters,
013            which may not adapt well to causal language models. In this work, we propose
014            Grouped Cross-Attention, a novel module enabling joint pre-training of the re-
015            triever and causal LM, and apply it to long-context modeling. For a given input
016            sequence, we split it into chunks and use the current chunk to retrieve past chunks
017            for subsequent text generation. Our innovation allows the retriever to learn how
018
               to retrieve past chunks that better minimize the auto-regressive loss of subsequent
               tokens in an end-to-end manner. By integrating top-k retrieval, our model can be
019
               pre-trained efficiently from scratch with context lengths up to 64K tokens. Our
020
               experiments demonstrate that our model achieves superior performance in vari-
021            ous tasks against strong baselines, and 100% accuracy in the needle-in-a-haystack
022            (NIAH) test with a 16M context length.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), serving as the backbone of large language models (LLM), have
028   revolutionized language modeling and demonstrated exceptional performance across a wide range
029   of natural language processing tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023;
030   Dubey et al., 2024). While Transformers excel in representational power, their quadratic computa-
031   tional complexity and increasing memory demands as input length grows pose formidable challenges
032   for modeling long contexts. Various approaches, such as recurrent memory (Dai et al., 2019), and
033
      linear attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) techniques, are proposed to improve the efficiency and
      effectiveness of Transformers in handling extended inputs. Nevertheless, these approaches often
034
      sacrifice the random-access flexibility of attention (Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023) during inference.
035
036   In this work, we explore long-range language modeling in Transformers from the perspective of
037   retrieval-based language models (RLMs) (Asai et al., 2023). Typically, RLMs (Rubin & Berant,
038   2024; Yen et al., 2024) divide an input sequence into chunks, retrieve relevant ones from the history
039
      for the current input, and then integrate the retrieved chunks into the decoder to predict subse-
      quent tokens. By choosing top-k chunks as a “dynamic context”, RLMs overcome the efficiency
040
      challenges in long-context modeling while maintaining random-access flexibility. However, most
041
      RLMs (Lewis et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022) rely on separately pre-trained retrievers with fixed
042   parameters, which hinders their ability to adapt to the causal LMs. Although a straightforward ap-
043   proach is training the retriever end-to-end to select chunks that minimize auto-regressive loss of
044   subsequent tokens, it is rarely explored. The main challenges are twofold: firstly, while relevance
045   scores guide chunk selection, these scores do not participate in the next token prediction, thus unable
046   to receive gradient backpropagation from the auto-regressive loss. Secondly, the large search space
047   brought by long contexts often results in efficiency and flexibility issues for pre-training.
048   To tackle these challenges, we propose Grouped Cross-Attention (GCA), a novel module enabling
049   efficient end-to-end joint optimization of the retriever and causal LM, thus the retriever can learn
050   to retrieve past chunks that most effectively reduce the auto-regressive loss of subsequent tokens,
051   which we refer to as causal retrieval. GCA enables the relevance scores to participate in the next
052   token prediction in a differentiable way. Specifically, GCA can be understood as a chunk-wise anal-
053   ogy of token-wise self-attention. In self-attention, considering the next token prediction in causal
      Transformers, self-attention scores could be viewed as the relevance scores of the current token to

091   distantly but used in the next chunk. These findings suggest that GCA has the potential to be a
092
      fundamental component in retrieval-based LMs. Overall, our main contributions are:
093   1. We propose a novel module called Grouped Cross-Attention (GCA), which allows dense retriev-
094      ers learn to retrieve guided by auto-regressive loss in an end-to-end manner efficiently.
095   2. Building upon GCA, we introduce Differentiable Retrieval-based Transformers (DRT), which is
096      fast and memory-efficient in both pre-training and inference on long texts, but still maintains the
097      random-access flexibility and excellent extrapolation capability.
098
      3. We implement a hardware-aware GCA based on FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2024), significantly re-
099
         ducing the training and inference time. The code will be made publicly available.

102   2    R ELATED WORKS
103
104   Relation to RPT & Landmark Attention. There are two long-range LMs closely related to ours.
105   One of them is Retrieval-Pretrained Transformer (RPT) (Rubin & Berant, 2024). The key difference
106   between DRT and RPT is the training approach of the retriever. During data-preparation, for each
107   chunk, RPT picks relevant past chunks by using BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009), concatenates
      them with the current chunk, and evaluates them by a reference LM like Pythia 1.4B (Biderman


                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023). The past chunks that increase the probability of the next chunk are identified as ‘gold
109   chunks’ to train RPT’s retriever. However, such a complex data preparation process limits scalability
110   and flexibility in pre-training and post-training (Lee, 2024). In contrast, DRT is pre-trained end-to-
111   end. By employing a sliding window size larger than the chunk size, it effectively uses feedback
112   from subsequent several chunks to train the retriever. Its flexibility also allows for adaptive multi-
113   hop retrieval. Landmark Attention (LA) (Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023) is another close work. LA is
114   pre-trained with short contexts but capable of handling long contexts during inference. It addresses
115   long-range language modeling by modifying self-attention KV Cache. During inference, each token,
116   at each layer, selects top-k chunks based on token-to-chunk attention scores and appends their key
117
      and value vectors to the current KV cache of self-attention. The token-to-chunk attention scores
      are trained in an end-to-end manner with a grouped softmax technique. However, it has to perform
118
      top-k chunk selection per token, per layer, which incurs significant extra costs during inference.
119
      Moreover, it fails to extrapolate on longer context length. Our method combines the chunk-retrieval
120   and grouped softmax ideas, resolving the aforementioned issues while balancing training efficiency
121   and inference performance.
122
123   Long-Range Language Modeling. Various methods have been proposed to improve long-range
124   language modeling. One line of research is introducing memorization to Transformers via recur-
125   rence. Many works (Dai et al., 2019; Burtsev & Sapunov, 2020; Martins et al., 2022; Hutchins
126
      et al., 2022) compress past information into fixed-sized vectors. However, these methods often
      sacrifice the flexibility to attend to arbitrary past tokens. Meanwhile, other works focus on main-
127
      taining random-access flexibility of attention. Memorizing Transformers (Wu et al., 2022) appends
128
      retrieved past keys and values to the current attention segment via k-NN search, but they do not
129   back-propagate gradients to them. CEPE (Yen et al., 2024) retrieves previous chunks using an inde-
130   pendently trained dense retriever and fuses them into the decoder. During the training process, the
131   decoder parameters are fixed, and only the encoder is adjusted. A notable distinction in our work is
132   the end-to-end optimization of all parameters, particularly the retriever.
133
134   Efficient Language Modeling. Many works have been done to reduce the training and inference
135
      cost of LLM. One direction is sparse attention, which includes limiting the attention window to
      a small range around each token (Child et al., 2019; Zaheer et al., 2020; Beltagy et al., 2020),
136
      approximating attention matrix (Wang et al., 2020), leveraging locality-sensitive-hashing(LSH) for
137
      key vectors retrieval (Kitaev et al., 2020), and hierarchical self-attention (Ren et al., 2021). However,
138   empirically most efficient Transformers sacrifice performance for efficiency. Recently, state-space
139   models (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) and RNN models (Beck et al., 2024) provide new
140   architecture alternatives, with comparable performance to Transformers but much lower cost for
141   inference. We argue that our core innovation GCA is flexible enough to be incorporated into these
142   models as an additional module to obtain random-access flexibility.
143
144
      Retrieval-Augmented Language Models. Retrieval-augmented LMs leverage a retriever to ac-
      cess relevant external knowledge, enhancing their generation capabilities. In some works, the re-
145
      triever can be jointly trained with the LM such as REALM (Guu et al., 2020). However, its computa-
146
      tional complexity limits its extension to causal LMs. On the other hand, in most other works (Lewis
147   et al., 2020; Izacard & Grave, 2021; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Ivgi et al., 2023), retriever parameters
148   are fixed, preventing optimization for retrieving information that best predicts subsequent tokens.
149
150   Unsupervised Dense Retrieval. In the line of research on unsupervised dense retrieval, early
151   works leverage Inverse Cloze Task (Lee et al., 2019) to train retriever unsupervisedly, where a sen-
152   tence is randomly sampled from a document and the task is to predict its surrounding context. How-
153
      ever, this approach still lags behind BM25 on long-tail entities (Sciavolino et al., 2021). Recently,
      contrastive learning methods (Izacard et al., 2022; Gao & Callan, 2022) have shown improved re-
154
      sults by creating positive and negative pairs from sentences within the same or different documents,
155
      respectively. However, these unsupervisedly trained retrievers are typically not optimized for causal
156   LMs, so they may not guarantee to retrieve the most pertinent information.

242   GCA vs CCA. A key distinction between GCA and CCA lies in how softmax is applied to cross-
243   attention matrices as shown in Figure 1(a)(b). In CCA, all retrieved chunks are concatenated and
244   softmax is directly applied to the whole attention matrix to fuse token-level information. Notably,
245   relevance scores are entirely excluded from the process. In contrast, GCA applies softmax to each
246   chunk’s attention matrix separately. This modification allows information to be gathered from each
247   chunk separately. The softmaxed relevance scores then serve as soft choices (Hu et al., 2021; 2022),
248
      participating in the next token prediction thus receiving back-propagated gradients.
249   Encoder-Decoder Variant. Our model architecture could be easily modified to an encoder-
250   decoder-based variant like RETRO (Borgeaud et al., 2022) by directly applying the encoder to chunk
251   token embeddings instead of the lower layers’ outputs. This variant allows for retrieval from trillions
252   of tokens with acceptable storage costs. We will elaborate on this in § 3.3.

254   3.2   T RAINING
255
256
      The pre-training objective of DRT is the next token prediction, but there are certain details as dis-
      cussed below. We also give a detailed time complexity analysis of training.
257
258   Gumbel Top-k sampling. The core idea of self-supervised retrieval is making candidate chunks
259   compete with each other by softmaxing relevance scores as weights. The weights of the chunks
260   contributing most to the next chunk prediction are enhanced while the weights of the rest are sup-
261   pressed. To balance exploration and exploitation, we sample chunks based on relevance scores
262   instead of always picking the top-k, enabling highly relevant chunks to be more likely chosen while
263   still considering lower-scoring ones. A simple trick is to add Gumbel noise (Gumbel, 1954) to the
264   raw scores before the top-k operation. Importantly, this noise doesn’t affect subsequent operations.
265
      Encoder-Decoder Pre-training. To enhance the encoder’s representational ability, we train it with
266   MLM in addition to the auto-regressive loss during the first half of pre-training. Specifically, while
267   the decoder sees all tokens, the encoder’s tokens are partially masked, whose outputs are used in
268   both the decoder’s GCA and computing the MLM objective. Masking causes inaccurate encoding
269   that may disturb subsequent computation and training of the decoder, so we eventually remove the
      MLM training in the second half of the pretraining.

280   Memory Offloading. During the inference stage of Transformers, the default memory cost
281   for KV Cache is O(N Ld). To reduce GPU memory usage, we can offload past chunk rep-
282   resentations to CPU memory. This results in a spatial complexity of the GPU memory usage
283   O( Ld    N                                        Ld
           S + 2 KSd+N W d) during inference. Here, S is the memory footprint of landmark represen-
284   tations, while the remaining terms account for the GCA and sliding-window KV cache. Although
285   each retrieval involves gathering representations from CPU memory and transferring them to the
286   GPU, this operation occurs G times every S tokens. Therefore, the cost of memory exchange from
287
      chunk retrievals is minimal.
288   Infinitely Long Context Retrieval. To mimic humans’ capability for random access to memories,
289   we extend the retrievable context to all pre-trained tokens, where contextualized token representa-
290   tions in chunks could be regarded as memory. A straightforward approach, similar to RPT, is to
291   store chunk-level representations and token-level hidden states as key-value pairs in a Faiss (Douze
292   et al., 2024). However, this approach requires significant disk space. For instance, a 100 billion
293   token corpus with 1,024-dimensional hidden states, even with Product Quantization, demands ap-
294   proximately 25TB. In contrast, using an encoder-decoder variant, we only store a chunk’s landmark
295   representation and positions in the corpus without saving hidden states. When retrieving, we access
296
      the corresponding tokens by document position and re-encode them to obtain their hidden states,
      achieving retrieval from billions of tokens with disk cost reducing by S fold.

      Results. From Table 1, we have several observations. Firstly, DRT outperforms all baselines
362
      where the evaluation length exceeds 16K. While DRT performs retrieval for G times every 64 tokens,

530   5      C ONCLUSION & F UTURE WORKS
531
532   In this study, we successfully optimize the retriever module with the auto-regressive LM objective
533   in an end-to-end manner. The core innovation lies in the Grouped Cross-Attention (GCA), which
534   makes relevance scores learnable by using them to fuse information retrieved by the current chunk
535   for next chunk prediction. Combined with Gumbel top-k sampling, this approach enables the pre-
536   training of LMs on context lengths extending up to 64K tokens.
537   In future work, we will explore self-supervised causal retrieval from vast amounts of tokens outside
538   the context. Meanwhile, we will combine structured representations (Hu et al., 2024b;a) to achieve
539   multi-granular retrieval.

      A.1    H YPER - PARAMETERS
867
868   Long-Range Language Modeling. We employ a Llama-like architecture (Touvron et al., 2023)
869   featuring a 12-layer, decoder-only transformer with 12 heads per layer (64 dimensions each), an
870   embedding dimension of 768, and an FFN size of 2048. Training utilizes the AdamW opti-
871   mizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.95, and a weight decay factor of
872   0.001. We used base learning rate 2 × 10−3 for all our experiments with a warmup stage that was
873
      2% of the whole training and applied a cosine scheduler with final learning rate being 4 × 10−4 .
      We used GPT-2’s (Radford et al., 2019) tokenizer. We used mixed-precision training with bfloat16
874
      over at 8 Nvidia A100 GPUs. We train all models with an effective batch size of 219 tokens for 60K
875
      steps resulting in a total training budget of 32.2 billion tokens. We train Base LM, RPT and DRT
876   on each dataset with a context length of 16K tokens. Due to Landmark Attention doesn’t support
877   sliding-window attention, the model is pre-trianed with full self-attention with a context length of
878   768. Due to Block Recurrent Transformer cannot be fully paralleled, which takes 5× wall-clock
879   training time with 16K context length, we pre-train it with a context length of 4K.
