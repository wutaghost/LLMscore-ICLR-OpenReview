009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are a predominant method for graph represen-
012            tation learning. However, beyond subgraph frequency estimation, their appli-
013            cation to network motif prediction remains underexplored, with no established
014            benchmarks in the literature. We propose to address this problem, framing mo-
015            tif estimation as an extension of subgraph frequency estimation. Our approach
016            formulates motif estimation as a multitarget regression problem, optimising for
017            interpretability and improving stability and scalability on large graphs. We vali-
018
               date our method using a large synthetic dataset generated by graph generators that
               mimic real-world data, and further test it on real-world graphs. Our experiments
019
               reveal that 1-WL limited models trained on synthetic data struggle to predict accu-
020
               rately motif profiles of real-world networks. However, apart from their reasonable
021            performance within synthetic data, they can generalise to approximate the graph
022            generation processes of real-world networks by comparing their predicted motif
023            profiles with the ones originating from synthetic data. This first study on GNN-
024            based motif estimation sets a benchmark and should open pathways for further
025            developing the connection between motif profiles and subgraph frequency from a
026            graph representation learning perspective.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A structure is called a network motif when its recurring occurrence is not solely explained by ran-
032   domness. These structures are extremely powerful tools for understanding complex networks. Un-
033
      derstanding what substructures are relevant and not relevant to a graph can help understand the fun-
      damental organisational principles behind it. This understanding enhances theoretical knowledge of
034
      network structure and function but also has practical implications in various fields, particularly in
035
      Biology. For instance, the feed-forward loop has been identified as a crucial functional pattern in
036   many real biological networks of gene regulation (Mangan & Alon, 2003). It has also been discov-
037   ered that motifs enable efficient communication and fault-tolerance across transcriptional networks
038   (Roy et al., 2020). Furthermore, the related concept of graphlet degree distribution – a generalisa-
039   tion of degree distribution to higher-order structures – has been used to understand what is a good
040   network model for protein-protein interactions (Pržulj, 2007).
041
      Discovering a motif entails counting the number of occurrences of the desired structure, both in
042   the network in study and in a set of control networks to understand its significance. However,
043   this process is a very hard computational task. Just determining if a subgraph exists in a larger
044   network (subgraph isomorphism) is a NP-complete problem (Cook, 1971). Even though methods to
045   perform an analysis based on motifs exist (Ribeiro et al., 2021), they have high temporal complexity,
046   rendering them intractable for very large networks. Furthermore, methods that rely on machine
047   learning to address this problem typically do not give very interpretable results, a critical concept
048   when doing analysis based on the relative importance of substructures.
049   Present Work. We aim to design a method for motif finding, leveraging a novel formulation that
050   hinges primarily on reworking the target task to something else other than direct substructure count-
051   ing. Our approach focuses on providing highly interpretable scores, ensuring the possibility of fur-
052   ther insight into the conclusions obtained. Additionally, our method is robust and versatile, capable
053   of operating effectively on graphs of any size. Knowing how difficult it is to obtain a high volume of
      real-world graph datasets that have both high quality and variety, we create a large synthetic dataset,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      employing a myriad of generators and using it as the training data to assess the efficiency of our for-
055   mulation. This setup leads to the conceptualisation of the following research question. Can Message
056   Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) under a different problem formulation than graph counting, be
057   enough to accurately predict motifs of real-world graphs when trained on synthetic data?
058
      Key Contributions: Our key contributions can be summarised as follows: (1) We show that the
059
      difficulty of motif discovery with MPNNs can be manipulated through different formulations of the
060
      target variable. Different formulations pertain to different concepts of motifs. Hence, depending
061   on the the concept used, motif estimation does not have to follow the limitations from the literature
062   regarding subgraph counting with MPNNs; (2) We make available a large diverse synthetic dataset
063   in terms of graph topology and motif significance-profile generated with 23 synthetic generators. We
064   also present a collection of more than 100 real-world networks and their motif significance-profile;
065   (3) Our experiments show that MPNNs trained under the adopted motif concept with synthetic data
066   can predict the significance-profile of synthetic data with a solid accuracy. Furthermore, we show
067   they cannot generalise well for real-world data, showing a gap between these types of networks.

079   3     R ELATED W ORK
080
081   In order to discover motifs, we must define three steps: (1) What is the set of graphs, SG , that we
082   admit as candidates for motifs; (2) What method is used to count the occurrences of graphs of SG in
083   the graph of interest G; (3) How is the significance of the obtained counts calculated.
084
085   3.1   S TEP O NE - H OW TO D EFINE THE S ET OF G RAPHS U SED
086
087   In this step, SG is typically defined a priori. This method is the most widely used (Milo et al.,
088   2004a;b; Shen-Orr et al., 2002), and in most common cases, the selection of graphs used are ones
089   known to be important to the area of the work in question (Shen-Orr et al., 2002; Alon, 2007).
090   Defining SG a priori is frequent for “non-machine-learning” techniques, but it is also common in
091   machine-learning ones (Rong et al., 2020; Ying et al., 2020). However, when using techniques based
092   on machine-learning, it is easier to create a task that can infer structures in G than when using non-
093   machine learning approaches. Hence, motif discovery can be modulated as the task of finding the
094   best set of graphs that are motifs according to a defined graph metric. That is, discover what graphs
095   exhibit a certain criteria in order to be considered motifs (Bénichou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020).
096
097   3.2   S TEP T WO - H OW TO C OUNT S UBGRAPHS
098
099   Non-GNN Methods. Numerous methods exist for approximating subgraph counts, eschewing de-
100   pendence on Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) or any machine learning techniques. We refer the
101   interested reader to Ribeiro et al. (2021) for a survey of these methods.
102   GNN Methods. Counting occurrences of a graph G in another graph H using GNNs was first
103   introduced by Chen et al. (2020). This work introduced significant limitations of what substructures
104   MPNNs can count. Subsequent works have refined MPNN-like models to be more expressive,
105   allowing them to have guarantees of being able to count occurrences of more graphs. One branch of
106   such models is known as node-rooted Subgraph GNNs. These will extract a k-hop neighbourhood
107   for each node in the graph to be studied. Since they act per node and add a feature to the node that
      induced each subgraph, they are called node-rooted Subgraph GNNs.

                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      These architectures, with models as powerful as 1-WL as the backbone, are strictly more powerful
109   than maximum powerful MPNNs but are less powerful than the 3-WL test (Frasca et al., 2022; Yan
110   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Hence, they have limitations regarding the type of structures that
111   they can count. Huang et al. (2022) gives a characterisation of what substructures Subgraph GNNs
112   cannot count at node-level based on the notation of cycles and paths. They show that the Subgraph
113   GNNs cannot count cycles of four or more nodes and paths of three or more nodes.
114
      On a similar note, Huang et al. (2022) propose extracting edge-rooted subgraphs rather than node-
115   rooted and marking nodes that form the edge that anchors the subgraph extraction. Huang et al.
116   (2022) prove that the utilisation of double marking grants enhanced computational capabilities com-
117   pared to node rooted Subgraph GNNs. Additionally, it is ascertained that their model exhibit partial
118   superiority over the 3-WL test, enabling the counting of cycles with lengths shorter than seven and
119   all subgraphs up to size four in a non-induced setting.
120
      Recently a new theoretical view of Subgraph GNNs based on the Subgraph Weisfeiler-Lehman,
121   a new version of the WL test, has been proposed (Zhang et al., 2023). This analysis presents a
122   characterisation of the expressive power of all node-rooted Subgraph GNNs. They conclude that
123   no node-rooted Subgraph GNN can be more powerful than the 2-folklore-WL (3-WL). This bound
124   was already discussed by Yan et al. (2023) and Frasca et al. (2022). However, Zhang et al. (2023)
125   demonstrate that no node-rooted Subgraph GNN can achieve the maximum expressivity of their time
126   complexity class. This result draws a limitation in the design of node-rooted Subgraph GNNs. Later
127   work by Yan et al. (2023) characterise the counting power of Subgraph GNNs for general architec-
128   tures and a general number of rooted nodes used as backbone. Furthermore, a method to compare
129
      the expressivity of GNN models was introduced by Zhang et al. (2024) based on homomorphisms,
      they summarise the ability of multiple GNN models on their ability to count any substructures with
130
      no more than eight edges and no more than six vertices. Regarding induced subgraph counting at
131
      graph level, the subject of our work, 1-WL models cannot count any pattern with 3 or more nodes.

      3.3     S TEP T HREE - H OW IS S IGNIFICANCE O BTAINED
134
135   After obtaining the frequency of the structures in SG , the next step is to evaluate their significance.
136   Hence, it is necessary to have an idea of what would produce, with no factor other than random
137   chance, a network similar to G for some characteristic of interest. Let us denote NULL as a model
138   that can achieve that goal. One example of NULL is a model that, given a graph G, randomly
139   switches edges while keeping the degree distribution of G – degree distribution is the characteristic
140   of interest. The rewiring process is completely random and without any bias towards any predispo-
141
      sition (Milo et al., 2004a;b).

143   3.4     M OTIFS AND G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
144
      Motif estimation, when approached through the lens of GNNs, appears to be a challenge that, to the
145   best of our knowledge, remains largely unexplored in the existing literature.
146
147   Directly counting. One of the approaches that better matches direct motif estimation with GNNs
148
      would be to count subgraphs of interest, SG , using a GNN in the input graph G and, after selecting
      a suitable null model, generate an amount T of graphs according to it and use the same GNN model
149
      used in G to count the occurrences of the selected set of subgraphs in each of the T control graphs.
150
151   Motifs as tool. Other works that integrate GNNs and motifs typically deviate from estimating motifs
152   and use pre-computed ones to enhance the power of GNNs. Examples of this work include Motif
153   Convolutional Networks (Lee et al., 2018), motif2vec (Dareddy et al., 2019), Motif Graph Attention
154
      Network (Sheng et al., 2024), Motif Graph Neural Network (Chen et al., 2023) and Heterogeneous
      Motif Graph Neural Network (Yu & Gao, 2022). In the field of GNNs, another usage of the concept
155
      of a motif as a relevant pattern comes from the attempt to explain the decision of GNN models. Two
156
      examples in this field are GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) and TempME (Chen & Ying, 2023).
157
158   3.4.1    L EARNING R ELEVANT PATTERNS
159
160   We will introduce the forthcoming studies under the term “relevant patterns” since most of them use
161   a definition of motif that is different from the one we introduce. Nonetheless, when discussing such
      works, we follow the terminology of the original works and will call the pattern “motif”.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Works directly pertaining to motif estimation (used to refine a downstream task) are MICRO-
163   Graph (Zhang et al., 2020) and MotiFiesta (Oliver et al., 2022). An example of a method made
164   to estimate subgraph frequency is SPMiner (Ying et al., 2020).
165
166
      The main problems of these works are the following: (1) either the model does not assume a null
      model and returns raw counts of occurrences of a general H in G (SPMiner and other frequency
167
      estimation models), or (2) the model may use a null model to guide motif search but only returns the
168
      subgraph(s) that are considered a motif by the model, meaning it is typically not possible to query
169   for a specific H (MICRO-Graph and MotiFiesta). Hence, these models typically ignore everything
170   not branded as a motif, sometimes not even returning a motif score for the graphs regarded as such.
171   Furthermore, models that return the raw count of occurrences can suffer from poor generalisation
172   since the number of graph structures grows super-exponentially (Fu et al., 2023). As the size of a
173   graph G grows, the possible counts of a substructure H in G also grow super-exponentially, causing
174   high variation between results of small and very-large graphs. This fact can hinder the learning pro-
175   cess of models that aim at being agnostic of network size and topology. Also, for raw count models,
176   since no null model is assumed, obtaining significance implies added subsequent computation.

190   4.1   O UR A PPROACH
191
192   Even though not used for subgraph counting, we implant degree features into the graphs to enhance
193   the capability of the models. Instead of modelling the learning task as predicting a single value
194   Z(H, G) for some H and some G, we model it as a multi-target regression problem in order to
195
      predict the motif score of multiple subgraphs at once. This characterisation naturally allows the
      construction of motif fingerprints as proposed by Milo et al. (2004b). Thus, we start with a vector
196
      of Z-scores, z = [Z(H1 , G) . . . Z(Hn , G)].
197
198   The restriction of the number of graphs in z implies that the proposed model will not be able to
199   search if an arbitrary graph is or is not a motif. However, by having a model that has a more
200   restricted objective, we aim to achieve higher precision in the said objective. Since z has a restricted
201
      size, one other aspect that deserves careful consideration is deciding what is the size of z and what
      graphs compose it. Should the selected graphs exhibit negligible relation, an attempt to predict the
202
      Z-score concurrently for all graphs may prove harmful to the performance of the model. In this
203
      case, such an approach forces the model to incorporate distinct patterns to predict scores for each
204   graph, thereby resulting in a sub-optimal global predictive efficacy. However, if z is composed of a
205   well-thought group of graphs, allowing them to share common patterns from a learning perspective,
206   we hypothesise that the performance of the model can improve when compared to predicting just
207   one Z-score, due to the possibility of what is learned about a target variable be “shared” with others
208   through weight sharing (one other advantage is the need to only train a single model instead of
209   multiple). A good candidate for z should have patterns that are interconnected with each other,
210   either from the point of view of the Z-score distribution or from a topological one.
211   Building on top of what was described in the two previous paragraphs, we focus on small graphs,
212   in particular all connected graphs of size three and four. This is also supported by existing relevant
213   literature (Milo et al., 2004b;a; Shen-Orr et al., 2002; Asikainen et al., 2020; Pržulj, 2007; Ribeiro
214   & Silva, 2013) suggesting that to understand a complex network, it is an important to understand
215   how small graphs behave in that network. We focus on these graphs because their proximity in size
      should allow them to have a topological connection that translates in a connection in their Z-Scores.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Restricting the size of the graphs used in z to small ones also has the added benefit that we can
217   get the ground-truth of motif scores for a diverse type and size of networks, allowing for a richer
218   train dataset. Furthermore, we expect that using a set of graphs of increasing size in the number
219   of nodes and edges gives enough interconnectivity between their patterns from both a topological
220   and a Z-score distribution point of view to allow the model to have a strong inductive bias towards
221   meaningful patterns, allowing for a stronger performance. For example, a graph with many size four
222   cliques will probably have a small amount of 4-stars.
223   We will refer to the set of graphs used to populate z as Ω. The function notation Ω(G) gives the
224   set of all graphs that have the same number of connected nodes as the graph G. For the chosen
225   graphs it is possible to create two groups in Ω, the graphs of size three and the graphs of size four.
226   Using the motif Z-score directly in the learning task not only makes the function and result highly
227   interpretable, but also eliminates the need to compute multiple networks based on the NULL model
228   to determine significance. By normalising the Z-score across groups of graphs, as defined in Milo
229   et al. (2004b), the values of z are constrained between −1 and 1, independent of network size,
230   enhancing the model’s predictive     stability across various network scales. Moreover, normalising
      the Z-scores with si = zi /( j∈Ω(i) zj2 )1/2 imposes a mathematical interconnectivity between the
                                    P
231
232   Z-scores of graphs of the same group. This relationship, where the sum of squared Z-scores equals
233   1, supports a multi-target objective and further strengthens the problem formulation by adding an
234
      additional layer of interdependence among graphs. The normalised Z-score refers to the values of
      the significance-profile s. The learning task consists of minimising the MSE between the true and
235
      predicted significance-profiles.

238   4.2   O N THE R ELATION WITH E XPRESSIVITY T HROUGH S UBGRAPH C OUNTING
239
240   It is expected that the expressivity regarding substructure counting to be highly related to the ex-
241   pressivity of discovering the significance-profile of graphs. Concretely, the problem of counting
242   graphs is a subset of the problem of discovering significance-profiles where reducing the null model
243
      to nothing reduces the problem to graph counting.
244   Since P , the problem of counting graphs, is a subset of S, the problem of significance-profile esti-
245   mation, it is possible to obtain instances of S that are as hard as P , easier than P and harder than
246   P . Under the assumption that S and P function around the same set of graphs, these differences
247   in difficulty come from the choice of null model. In the case of S = P , the null model should
248   do nothing, for example, returning always 0. In the case of S < P , the null model could always
249
      return the counts of each subgraph in a graph G without modifying G, reducing the problem to al-
      ways predicting a vector of zeros. For the case of S > P , employing a null model that randomly
250
      returns counts for G should make the problem theoretically harder since the model would have to
251
      learn the random process employed to correctly construct the significance-profiles. Thus, theoretical
252   guarantees of expressivity might not hold depending on the selected null model. For instance, a re-
253   cent demonstration solved the dimensionality of the k-WL test for induced subgraph count, stating
254   that to perform induced subgraph count of any pattern with k nodes we need at least dimensionality
255   k (Lanzinger & Barceló, 2023). Furthermore, no induced pattern with k + 1 nodes can be counted
256   with dimensionality k, a result not verified to non-induced counts (Lanzinger & Barceló, 2023).
257   However, depending on the null model, when working with significance-profiles over graphs of size
258   four in Ω, it might not be enough to have a model as powerful as the 4-WL to guarantee enough
259   power to correctly identify the normalised Z-scores of the size four graphs in Ω.
260   Modifications made to the problem. In Section 4, we attempted to reduce the difficulty of the
261   problem by formulating it as a multitarget regression of interconnected values from an algebraic
262   and topological point of view. However, even in the case where it exists a perfect dependence
263   between graphs of the same group of Ω, or even across different groups, the problem is still at least
264   as hard as finding the significance-profile of the graph(s) that governs the dependence relation. For
265   example, if the significance-profile of the 3-path was symmetric to the significance-profile of the
266
      3-clique, it would still be necessary to determine the significance-profile for 3-paths to compute the
      significance-profile of 3-cliques and vice versa.
267
268   Testing with 1-WL bounded models? MPNNs cannot perform induced counts of patterns of three
269   or more nodes (Chen et al., 2020). Nevertheless, MPNNs are not inherently incapable of counting
      patterns in any graphs. Rather, for a pattern H, there exists graphs G1 , G2 ∈ G such that C(H, G1 ) ̸=


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      C(H, G2 ) and for any MPNN M under 1-WL, M (G1 ) = M (G2 ). Hence, M cannot discover
271   C(H, G1 ) and C(H, G2 ) simultaneously. However, within the 1-WL framework, MPNNs remain
272   highly valuable and find numerous practical applications in real-world scenarios. Furthermore, we
273   did not construct any characterisation of the problem space of S regarding P for the null model used.
274   Hence, we might have made the problem easier (or harder) than substructure counting. The same
275   applies to the usage of a multi-target objective. Thus, we will scrutinise the capability of MPNNs to
276   address our particular challenge. Furthermore, more expressive models like Subgraph GNNs have a
277   very high computational complexity, being very hard to use in large-scale graphs.

279   5   DATASETS
280
281   We rely exclusively on synthetic graphs, rather than commonly used GNN datasets such as Wu et al.
282   (2018); Gómez-Bombarelli et al. (2018); Morris et al. (2020); Hu et al. (2021b;a). Our goal is to
283   have a large dataset with diverse topological features, which would be difficult and costly to obtain
284   from real-world data spanning multiple domains. Using multiple graph generators designed to sim-
285   ulate real-world phenomena and fully exploring their parameter space, we aim to create a dataset
286   with both high topological diversity and a close resemblance to real-world data. Additionally, syn-
287   thetic data allows for flexible expansion of the dataset size. Using synthetic data to train GNNs is
288
      not a new concept. However, as far as we know, most of the popularly used datasets typically have
      very small graphs (at most few hundreds of nodes) and are generated from a small set of generators,
289
      often random regular graphs and Erdős-Renyi graphs (Chen et al., 2020). Another popular type of
290
      synthetic graph dataset for benchmarking is small handcrafted graphs to limit test GNN models (Ab-
291   boud et al., 2021; Murphy et al., 2019; Balcilar et al., 2021; Wang & Zhang, 2023). While still very
292   limited, the only exceptions identified are Veličković et al. (2020); Corso et al. (2020).
293
294
      Since we are not interested in limit testing the power of the model in comparison to theoretical
      tests, and instead aim at having a diverse dataset regarding graph topologies and motif scores, we
295
      create a new dataset using a total of 23 synthetic generators (11 non-deterministic, 12 deterministic).
296
      We explore their graph-generating space in order to extract all types of possible topologies they
297   can express while limiting the graph size in order to avoid bottlenecks that increase training time
298   beyond what we find reasonable for the amount of time and resources available. The final dataset
299   puts the non-deterministic segment with 109164 graphs, and the deterministic segment with 38400
300   graphs, totalling for ≈ 250 million nodes and ≈ 750 million edges. Section A has a description
301   with greater detail on how each generator was explored. For the ground-truth, we calculate s using
302   G-Tries (Ribeiro & Silva, 2013).
303   Exploring the myriad of significance-profiles from the generated graphs leads us to the con-
304   clusion that the 3-path and the triangle can only take on a few sets of values, being those
305   {−0.707106, 0, 0.707106, 1} for the 3-path and the first three values for the triangle. This leads
306   to a strict result on the inter-variability of their Z-scores. Apart from cases where both scores are
307   zero or the 3-path is 1 and the triangle is 0 (an artefact from the G-Trie model, primarily affecting the
308   duplication-divergence model), we found that the Z-scores for graphs of size three are symmetric.
309   This means that if one structure takes a Z-score of x, the other will take −x. In normalised form,
310   these values are mapped to 0.707106 and −0.707106, respectively. For the size four graphs, we
311
      can say that the size three encodes some information about the significance-profile of graphs of size
      four, alluding to the possibility of an advantage of using graphs of both sizes in the target variable
312
      (Figure 3 - Appendix A.4 has a detailed view of this result). As for strict dependence between the
313
      graphs of size four, apart from the mathematical based constraint, we could not confirm any other.
314

317
      the type of network, we vary in their relative size in terms of number of nodes and edges. We devise

      Baselines. We define a baseline denoted by an horizontal red line corresponding to the expected loss
325   incurred when predicting the significance-profile s by employing an independent random uniform
326   model for each component of s. A second base line denoted by an horizontal blue line corresponds
327   to the expected loss when using a model predicting a random value for every component of s, but
328   taking into account the restrictions posited in Section 4 regarding the range of values each group of
329   Ω can take. A final baseline dependent on the architecture of the model is represented by a light pink
330   bar. It illustrates the mean error derived from using the model in question with random weights.
331   Persistent Patterns. Given a set of significance-profiles S, we define the persistency ρ of a pattern
332   s ∈ S as the frequency of its occurrence within S. The higher the number of occurrences of s, the
333   more persistent the pattern will be. Considering the randomness inherent to deriving a significance-
334   profile, we use a threshold, t, to decide what patterns are equivalent to each other. Given t, discover-
335   ing the persistence of s is reduced to ρs = |{s′ | d∞ (s, s′ ) ≤ t, ∀s′ ∈ S}| where d∞ is the Chebyshev
336   distance. We employ the concept of persistent patterns to develop a high-level understanding of the
337   nature of the true and predicted significance-profiles. We use the true significance-profiles as a stan-
338   dard to determine an appropriate threshold for equivalency. This threshold is then applied to the
339
      predicted significance-profiles. Section C further details the process to obtain t.
340   “Correct” Predictions. Since having a criteria for a prediction for a significance-profile to be cor-
341   rect/useful depends largely on the research field, in order to have a systematic threshold, we study
342   how many predictions for each generator have all individual values of the predicted significance-
343   profile below the obtained test error. Furthermore, we ensure that for a prediction to count as
344   “correct”, all significance-profiles for graphs in Ω have the correct sign. Presuming the test errors
345
      obtained for the synthetic dataset are relatively low, this approach guarantees that the predictions
      deemed “correct” not only contribute to a lower test loss than the one observed but are also poten-
346
      tially valuable from an application perspective. Let us denote a benchmark model as T . Let this
347
      model predict a random pattern according to the criterion used for the horizontal blue line described
348   above. If we define a cutoff mean loss for T of 0.25 (0.5 absolute error), this model will incur in a
349   possible error of 25% of the maximum allowed. According to 1e8 simulations, T will have a rate of
350   “correct” guesses of 0.364% while following the restrictions here introduced.

      7.1   D ISCUSSION OF THE R ESULTS
379
380   The models have low generalisation error as training, validation, and test errors are similar, and there
381   is no significant difference in performance between GIN and SAGE for synthetic data. However, the
382   models present a much higher error in the real-world dataset than the out-of-sample error estimated
383   through the test set. Furthermore, the obtained value is dangerously close to the error obtained with
384   random weights minus one standard deviation. Nonetheless, one positive is the stability of the error
385   across the two different real-world dataset sizes. Even though the error is high, it remains stable as
386   the size of the networks increases, a growth that exceeds three orders of magnitude in scale.
387   What is the model not doing. It is evident that any trained model does not adhere to an indepen-
388   dent uniform random prediction and is not randomly predicting based on the restrictions of s. All
389   predictions conspicuously surpass the red line and blue line, established as the initial benchmark.
390
      What can the model be doing. Regarding the poor performance, one possible scenario is that the
391
      synthetic data used does not accurately reflect the real-world data, leading to the observed disso-
392   nance between scores in the test and real datasets. However, it is essential to consider the limitations
393   of the MSE for this particular analysis. While MSE is valuable for comparing models with sig-
394   nificant differences in performance, it does not provide sufficient information regarding individual
395   predictions and their global shape. For instance, a model predicting a value of 0 for all graphs
396   would have an expected MSE of 0.25. Using the concept of persistent patterns, we can understand
397   how the predictions are distributed in terms of proximity to one another in relation to what would
398   be expected from the ground-truth. Table 3 (Appendix C) conveys a detailed view of the clusters
399   found for the threshold inferred from the true significance-profiles. Summarily, we conclude: (1) all
400
      models exhibit a substantially lower number of predicted patterns than expected; (2) this reduction
      is primarily attributed to the higher ρ of select patterns, rather than an higher mean ρ across patterns.
401
402   In the following sections we answer: (1) What can cause a model to tend towards persistent patterns?
403   (2) Can we take the low error in the test set as a signal that the model is learning the synthetic data?
404   (3) What causes the discrepancy between the scores in the synthetic and the real-world dataset?
405

      7.2   T ENDENCY FOR P ERSISTENT PATTERNS .
407
408
      This study examines the impact of dropout on the expressivity of models and persistence of the
409   patterns. By systematically increasing dropout values, the model’s power is reduced due to regular-
410   isation. Testing with both GIN and SAGE models reveals that dropout on the MPNN has little to no
411   effect on GIN’s performance but slightly affects SAGE, indicating GIN’s greater expressivity. This
412   possibility was briefly discussed in Section 4.2, suggesting that less expressive GNN models are ca-
413   pable of distinguishing fewer graphs. The results suggest that models lacking expressivity generate
414   fewer diverse patterns, leading to similar predictions across graphs. Hence, poor performance on
415   real-world data might not solely result from limitations of the synthetic dataset, but also from the
416   lack of expressivity of the models.
417   Answering question (1). Tendency towards persistency of patterns higher than what would be
418   expected stems from the lack of expressivity of either the model, the dataset, or both.

      7.4    M ODEL P REDICTIONS IN THE R EAL -W ORLD DATASET
480
481   Analysing the multiple figures from Section D.2, it becomes apparent that the inter-category (or
482   inter-generator, in the context of the synthetic dataset) performance is far from optimal. The models
483   exhibit identical predictions across graphs from different categories, even when these categories
484   have distinct significance-profiles. Intra-category performance is even poorer than inter-category,
485   with models like SAGE generating patterns that are too similar across graphs, though just distinct
      enough to avoid being captured by the persistence measures. This phenomenon is exemplified by


                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the predictions for the small biological category and the small interaction category observed in the
487   SAGE model, by the medium-large interaction and medium-large social communication for GIN
488   non-deterministic, among others. In the real-world data analysis, the SAGE model had the best
489   performance with 12.5% satisfactory predictions, seven correct in the small dataset. Other models
490   had three or fewer. This result is 2.4× worse than the poorest in the synthetic dataset, despite a
491   24.1% margin of error, ≈ 1.6× larger.
492
      Interestingly, the models seem to have induced their own groupings based on similarities with
493   the synthetic datasets used for training. For instance, real-world graphs like ia-escorts-dynamic,
494   coauthor-CS, and ia-primary-school-proximity exhibited patterns resembling synthetic models like
495   duplication-divergence, forest-fire, and geometric graphs, respectively. This suggests that the model
496   predicts based on how similar a real-world graph is to the synthetic ones it has seen. While the model
497   struggles to produce satisfactory predictions for real-world networks, it could help identify which
498   synthetic model a real-world network most closely resembles. The closer the predicted pattern is to
499   the true pattern, the more alike the synthetic and real networks.
500   Points of divergence. In network similarity discovery based on significance-profiles, two key con-
501   cepts are crucial. First, the model’s ability to distinguish networks is constrained by the expressivity
502   of the space of the significance-profile used. The more expressive the space, the more reliable the
503   ability of the model to distinguish networks. Secondly, if the model predicts similar profiles for two
504   graphs G and H, indicating they resemble a graph F, this suggests G and H may originate from
505   a process similar to F. However, this conclusion is valid only if the true profiles of G and H are
506   indeed similar; otherwise, the model’s lack of expressivity leads to incorrect conclusions.
507   Closeness to Random Weights. A network with random weights can take any possible network as
508   a value. However, achieving performance near the score of the trained network purely by chance
509   is unlikely due to the complexity of the model. To address this, we propose that while the solution
510   space for random models may differ, its mean score should be similar to that of the solution space of
511   the trained model. Specifically, (1) models with random weights resemble those with high dropout,
512   showing a tendency towards highly persistent patterns; (2) the trained model produces meaning-
513
      ful patterns for synthetic data; (3) the predictions of the model can significantly differ from true
      significance-profiles due to its limited expressivity. Thus, while a trained model predicts meaning-
514
      ful patterns ineffectively, a random one only predicts highly persistent patterns, leading to a similar
515
      average scores.
516
517   Answering question (3). The model learns from the synthetic dataset. The discrepancy comes from
518   the synthetic data not accurately reflecting the real-world, at least when used by models limited by
519
      the 1-WL. Thus, we can only confirm that the knowledge extracted from the synthetic dataset by the
      used models is not enough to describe real-world data.

      7.5   VALIDATION OF THE A SSUMPTIONS M ADE
523
      Multi-target regression improves predictive accuracy for most graphs, except for graphs 4-clique
524   and 4-path, likely due to limited benefit from shared information. Predicting significance profiles
525   directly enhances performance for graphs with significant variation in subgraph occurrences, like
526   the 3-path, triangle, 4-path, and 4-clique. Despite minor error increases in some cases, the overall
527   gains in accuracy and computational efficiency make this approach preferable for motif estimation
528   in the given null model. Section D.1 has a detailed exploration of the mentioned results.

      8     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      Despite the lack of a GNN-based method specifically designed for predicting significance profiles,
533   our current MPNN models combined with synthetic data are still deemed insufficient for real-world
534   applications related to significance-profile discovery. The best performance benchmark shows a
535   prediction accuracy of 12.5% for small networks and 10.41% for medium-large networks, each with
536   a 24.1% margin of error. In contrast, having into account their simplicity, the used models together
537   with the presented formulation, for synthetic data, achieve good results, with a benchmark accuracy
538   of 46.9% with a 15.4% margin of error for non-deterministic generators and 52.6% with an 11.4%
539   margin of error for deterministic generators. This suggests that the models are promising for network
      categorisation by effectively distinguishing high-level differences between graphs.

      A.1       N ON -D ETERMINISTIC G ENERATORS
813
814   For the Erdős-Renyi model Erdős & Rényi (1960), we mainly aim at creating graphs in the three
815   (out of four) main topological phases a graph can be (Barabási & Pósfai, 2017). We exclusively
816   uniformly control the number of nodes within each of the delineated phases, namely, the “critical”,
817   “supercritical” and “connected” states. This strategic regulation facilitates substantial variability in
818   graph size while preventing an excessive escalation of the referred metric that could possibly impede
819
      further computational processing.
820   For the Watts-Strogatz (Watts & Strogatz, 1998) and Newman Watts-Strogatz (Newman & Watts,
821   1999), we regulated the generation based on the total number of nodes, the initial number of neigh-
822   bours and the probability of rewiring in order to generate networks that represented key sections of
823   the characterisation based on the clustering coefficient and path length, as given in Watts & Strogatz
824   (1998).
825   For the extended Barabási-Albert model (Albert & Barabási, 2000), we defined as hyperparameters
826   the total number of nodes and amount of connections a new node gains. Subsequently, we em-
827   ploy the equations delineated in the original article, values for the probabilities associated with the
828   formation of new links (p) and the rewiring of existing connections (q) are derived. These computa-
829   tions aim to yield graphs characterised by a power-law degree distribution with an exponent ranging
830   uniformly between 2 and 3.
831   For the cluster power-law (Holme & Kim, 2002), we vary uniformly the number of nodes and
832   calculate the necessary probability according to the original study to obtain a clustering coefficient
833   of 0.35, 0.45 or 0.55.
834
      The duplication-divergence generator (Ispolatov et al., 2005) operates by randomly selecting a node
835   v from an initial graph and duplicating all edges connected to v with a retention probability denoted
836   as σ. Two of the selected regimes exhibit self-averaging behaviour concerning the number of edges,
837   specifically when 0 < σ < e−1 and e−1 < σ < 1/2. The non-self-averaging regime is characterised
838   by 1/2 < σ < 1. More characteristics regarding the generated graphs, for example, the degree
839   distribution, can be found in the original paper.
840
      In the Gaussian random partition model, proposed by Brandes et al. (2003), k groups of nodes
841   are generated with t nodes derived from a Gaussian distribution with mean s and variance v. The
842   connectivity between nodes in a group is given by a probability p, and the connectivity inter-groups
843   is given by q. In this generator, we parameterise the number of nodes |V |, the size of the k groups
844   and the maximum number of allowed edges |Emax |. Both the p and q probabilities are calculated to
845   not exceed the maximum number of edges according to Equation 1.

      A.2    D ETERMINISTIC G ENERATORS
876
877   We complemented the graphs generated by the non-deterministic generators with smaller amounts of
878   graphs from deterministic generators. These generators have their network completely and without
879   randomness determined once their parameters are chosen.
880
      The first group of deterministic generators consists of multiple types of trees. We use the binomial
881
      tree model parameterised on its order and the balanced tree (full rary-tree) parameterised on its
882   height and branching factor.
883
884   The second group is based on modified cycles. We use the circular ladder generator, varying the
885
      complete size of the graph and the chordal cycle (Lubotzky, 1994), also varying its complete size.
886   The third group is based on complete graphs and encompasses the barbell and lollipop graphs. The
887   barbell graph is made of two complete graphs of size k connected by a path of size m. The lollipop
888   is a barbell graph with only one complete graph and the path. In order to not complicate subsequent
889   steps, we carefully limited the size of the complete graphs.
890   The fourth group consists of the Dorogovtsev-Goltsev-Mendes model (Dorogovtsev et al., 2002).
891   This generator modulates a scale-free discrete degree distribution with exponent 1 + ln 3/ln 2 by
892   using a rather simple rule: “At each time step, to every edge of the graph, a new vertex is added,
893   which is attached to both the end vertices of the edge.” (in Dorogovtsev et al. (2002)). We vary
894   the magnitude of the number of nodes and edges by changing n, resulting in 3(3n + 1)/2 and 3n+1
895   nodes and edges respectively.
896   The fifth group consists of lattices. Namely, we use 2D hexagonal, triangular lattices and 3D square
897   lattices. The first 2 lattices have the option of allowing for boundary periodicity. All lattices vary in
898   terms of the size of each dimension.
899
      Finally, the last group consists of star graphs of various sizes.
900
901   Since the types of graphs that the deterministic generators generate are not subject to randomness,
902   it is redundant to create multiple graphs for each set of parameters. However, in order to introduce
903   a degree of randomness to the deterministic graphs, we introduced a probability of random rewiring
904
      of a percentage of edges after the graph is generated. The rewiring procedure for a single edge
      consists of selecting an edge (u, v) from a graph G, deleting it and attaching one of the ends, u or v,
905
      to another node w. If u is picked and (u, w) already exists, then G will exit the procedure with one
906
      less edge. Since we want some variability but still want to preserve the original deterministic graphs,
907   for each generator, two sets of graphs S1 and S2 will be generated where each set goes through all
908   the proposed generator parameters. After that, S1 is not subject to any rewiring, and for each graph
909   in S2 , p% of its edges are rewired according to the procedure described earlier. According to this
910   methodology, we generated four versions. The first had 2 edges swapped, the second 25% of the
911   edges swapped, the third 10% and the fourth 60%. We stick to version two due to being the best
912   performing one according to preliminary tests. This fact means that 25% seems to be a good choice
913   of random-rewiring so that the information encoded in the deterministic graphs is maximised.

1393   multi-target regression to predict significance-profiles directly outperforms using models to perform
1394   subgraph frequency estimation using either single or multi-target regression.
