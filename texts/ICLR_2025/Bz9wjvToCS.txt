011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Face de-identification is a critical task that aims to obscure true identities while
014            preserving other facial attributes. Current methodologies typically involve disen-
015
               tangling identity features within a latent space and leveraging adversarial training
               to balance privacy with utility, often at the cost of a trade-off between two. To
016
               surmount these limitations, we introduce DiffDeID, a novel approach grounded
017
               in diffusion models. This method incrementally safeguards identity and sustains
018            utility, all while ensuring enhanced interpretability. Our method employs a La-
019            tent Diffusion-based ID Sample to generate authentic identity embeddings that
020            are obfuscated from the original identity, thereby providing users with diverse
021            options. Additionally, a multi-condition diffusion model is utilized for facial im-
022            ages, ensuring the retention of image utility. We further introduce a novel training
023            and inference paradigm, utilizing the unified architecture tailored for video facial
024            de-identification tasks. The robustness of our method is attributed to its power-
025            ful 3D prior and meticulous generation design, enabling natural identity protec-
026
               tion, generation of high-quality details, and robustness across various attributes.
               Through extensive experimentation, we demonstrate that DiffDeID surpasses pre-
027
               vious methodologies.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As computer vision and image understanding technologies evolve, the risk of unauthorized disclo-
033   sure of personal information through images has significantly increased. The use of facial data is
034   highly valuable in a multitude of applications, such as emotion recognition and general face de-
035   tection, but it also poses challenges related to privacy protection. Face de-identification, or facial
036   anonymization, addresses this issue by removing identifiable details from facial images while pre-
037   serving other critical aspects such as the context, actions, and non-identifying features like pose and
038   expressionAgrawal & Narayanan (2011). There are various methods to create de-identified faces
039
      that resemble real humans and maintain visual fidelity, compatible with different tasks and scenar-
      ios. As the earliest and most straightforward approaches, blurring smooths out the facial features
040
      across the imageHarmon & Julesz (1973); Frome et al. (2009), while pixelation involves enlarging
041
      the pixels to a size that obscures detailZhou & Pun (2020). These techniques are easy to implement
042   but can significantly reduce the image quality and utility for other tasks.
043
044   Deep learning has emerged as a transformative approach in face de-identification, offering so-
045
      phisticated solutions that significantly enhance the balance between privacy protection and data
      utility. The most well-known methods include encoder-decoders and generative adversarial net-
046
      works (GANs). The encoder-decoders learn to compress and reconstruct images in a way that
047
      can be tailored to remove identity information while retaining other relevant facial featuresQiu
048   et al. (2022). GANs are generally composed of the generator and discriminator, which attempt
049   to achieve a balance between identity privacy protection and image authenticity through adversar-
050   ial learningHukkelås et al. (2019); Wen et al. (2022b). However, the unstable minimax objective
051   adversarial training process of GANs further exacerbates unrealistic textures of the results. The
052   effectiveness of these methods heavily depends on the degree of disentanglement between identity
053   and attributes, which is often unsatisfactory. Additionally, existing methods struggle to preserve the
      pose and expression of the source face images or videos.

      described as follows:

083   2.1   FACE D E - IDENTIFICATION
084
085   The earliest approaches to privacy protection in image data relied primarily on blurring and pixe-
086   lation techniques to obscure sensitive informationGross et al. (2006); Newton et al. (2005). While
087   these methods were straightforward and computationally efficient, they often fell short in terms
088   of securityOh et al. (2016). The de-identification effects achieved by these methods were usually
089
      unnatural, detracting from the aesthetic quality or the practical utility of the images.
090   With the rapid advancement of deep learning, the evolution of neural network architectures and the
091   burgeoning research in facial de-identification have paved the way for innovative approaches. Au-
092   toencoders can be used to obscure certain facial features by function by manipulating the compressed
093   representationLiu et al. (2023); Nousi et al. (2020). Gafni et al. (2019) introduced a new face iden-
094   tity transformer for privacy protection that uses cryptographic embedding and multi-task learning
095
      based on autoencoders. In recent years, GAN-basedGoodfellow et al. (2020) face de-identification
      methodsWu et al. (2019); Maximov et al. (2020) have gained significant popularity. Cao et al.Cao
096
      et al. (2021) propose a personalized and invertible face de-identification method by manipulating
097
      the distangled identity embeddings and the following work IdentityMaskWen et al. (2022a) de-
098   couples identity and attribute information, using latent space operations based on passwords and
099   privacy level parameters to encrypt or recover identity, followed by image reconstruction. Despite
100   the promising advancements in GAN-based face de-identification methods, there remains a lack of
101   clarity regarding the interpretability of identities and other facial representations in the latent space.
102   Additionally, the limitations of the generator and the unstable nature of GAN training necessitate
103   the design of specific intermediate representations and generators for each driving mode.
104
105   2.2   3D FACE R ECONSTRUCTION
106
107   Monocular 3D face reconstruction entails the construction of a three-dimensional facial model from
      a two-dimensional image, where 3D Morphable Models (3DMMs) Blanz & Vetter (2023) hold a


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dominant position. Additionally, there are approaches advocating for direct model-free reconstruc-
109   tion Sela et al. (2017) or those based on other innovative models Li et al. (2017). Compared to other
110   structural representations, such as landmarks and segmentation maps, 3DMMs offer an interpretable
111   and disentangled parameter space, enabling direct manipulation of facial features for specific tasks
112   Lattas et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022). Rendered facial images from 3DMMs provide richer se-
113   mantic and explicit geometric details than vectorized parameters, reducing the complexity of model
114   training.
115
116   2.3   D ENOISING D IFFUSION M ODELS
117
118   Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Nichol & Dhariwal (2021) are popular generative models in
119   synthesizing high-quality images, which operate on a generative probabilistic framework consisting
120   of two main steps. Initially, data is progressively corrupted by incrementally adding small amounts
121   of Gaussian noise over a series of time steps. Subsequently, a learning algorithm is trained to
122
      recover the data by gradually removing the noise over another series of time steps. Compared to
      GANs, diffusion models offer a more reliable and robust training process due to the absence of
123
      discriminators. They also avoid common issues associated with GANs, such as mode collapse or
124
      vanishing gradients. Following the significant success in unconditional image generation, diffusion
125   models have been adapted to support conditional generation. Ho et al. Ho & Salimans (2022)
126   further advanced these models by developing classifier-free guidance methods, enabling conditional
127   editing without the need for a pre-trained classifier. Despite these advantages, diffusion models are
128   slower in sampling due to the requirement of thousands of denoising steps in pixel space for a single
129   sample. To address this, Song et al. Song et al. (2020) proposed DDIM to reduce sampling time
130   and Rombach et al. Rombach et al. (2022) introduced Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), which
131   shift the training and inference processes to a compressed low-dimensional latent space to enhance
132   computational efficiency. Diffusion models have demonstrated the ability to generate high-quality
133
      data across a wide range of data distributions. In this paper, we construct a face de-identification
      framework leveraging diffusion models, which is applied to anonymize face images and extended to
134
      the generation of de-identified videos.

      3.4   A N OVEL PARADIGM FOR REENACTMENT BASED ON D IFF D E ID
317
318   In the realm of video processing, the conventional frame-by-frame face de-identified technique,
319   which treats each video frame as a discrete entity, often leads to a disjointed and unconvincing
320   visual output. To address this limitation, our research introduces a novel training and inference
321   methodology applied to the DiffDeID framework for face reenactment, which enables a more nu-
322   anced and lifelike transformation of facial expressions, poses, and other dynamic attributes within
323   the video sequence. It also ensures that the source identity embedding remains constant throughout
      the video stream processing.


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      First, we undertake the de-identified face image of the inaugural frame (denoted as Id ) and employ
325   it as the input for the texture encoder to ensure consistent and coherent facial textures throughout the
326   video sequence. Second, because we prioritize the dynamic identity-unrelated attributes of facial
327   expressions when 3DMM coefficients, we integrate the expression βf , pose pf , and gaze direction
328   ωf extracted from the video frame If with the identity αd , texture δd and illumination derived γd
329   from the de-identified image Id to construct the 3D face descriptors ρ̂ = {αd , βf , δd , γd , pf , ωf }.
330   Since we focus more on the dynamic changes of identity-independent attributes, the last condition
331   C does not include identity embedding, which can be expressed as C = linear(ρ̂) + linear(t).
332   During the training phase, the loss function consists of the denoising loss, reconstruction loss and
333
      perceptual loss with the same setting as Eq. 11. During the inference process, we render the face
      from the 3D descriptors ρ̂ to obtain I3d as the input to the U-Net encoder, take the de-identified
334
      face Id as the input to the texture encoder and inject the last condition C. Moreover, we utilize the
335
      parameters of adjacent frames as descriptors for the central frame like Ren et al. (2021), thereby
336   smoothing the motion trajectory. Subsequently, utilizing the denoising steps, we adeptly transfer
337   the attributes from the video stream onto the de-identified face, thereby achieving effective video
338   de-identification.

357   Evaluation Metrics. For face de-identification, we employ the Frechet Inception Distance
358   (FID)Heusel et al. (2017) to evaluate the realism of the generated faces. Besides, we utilize the
359   EXP, POSE, and GAZE to calculate the average Euclidean distance between the corresponding co-
360   efficients of the generated face and the original face. These metrics are instrumental in assessing
361   the fidelity of the facial reenactment process. To measure the identity cosine similarity, we employ
362   identity embeddings ID extracted using ArcfaceDeng et al. (2019). For video de-identification, we
363
      also adopt the cosine distance of id consistency, expression, pose, gaze and de-idenditfied id for
      evaluation. ID consistency distance is determined by computing the cosine distance between the
364
      identity embedding of the current frame in the de-identified video and the identity embedding of
365
      the first de-identified frame. The de-identification ID distance is quantified by calculating the co-
366   sine distance between the identity embeddings of the frames from the source video and those from
367   the de-identified video. Expression, pose, gaze distance is evaluated by measuring the cosine dis-
368   tance between the expression, pose, gaze embeddings of the frames in the source video and the
369   corresponding frames in the de-identified video.

500
501
      DiffDeID presents a significant advancement in face de-identification by effectively balancing iden-
      tity protection with the preservation of other facial attributes. Our approach, rooted in diffusion
502
      models, employs identity embeddings from expert recognition networks and decoupled 3D iden-
503
      tity representations, ensuring both robust identity protection and high utility retention. The novel
504   multi-condition diffusion model and the unified architecture for video facial de-identification further
505   enhance the method’s robustness and versatility. Extensive experimentation confirms that DiffDeID
506   surpasses existing methodologies, offering superior natural identity protection and high-quality im-
507   age details.
