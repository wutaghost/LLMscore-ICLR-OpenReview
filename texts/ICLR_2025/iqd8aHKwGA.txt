                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Quadratic programming (QP) is the most widely applied category of problems
012
               in nonlinear programming. Many applications require real-time/fast solutions,
013            though not necessarily with high precision. Existing methods either involve ma-
014            trix decomposition or use the preconditioned conjugate gradient method. For rela-
015            tively large instances, these methods cannot achieve the real-time requirement un-
016            less there is an effective preconditioner. Recently, graph neural networks (GNNs)
017            opened new possibilities for QP. Some promising empirical studies of applying
018            GNNs for QP tasks show that GNNs can capture key characteristics of an opti-
019            mization instance and provide adaptive guidance accordingly to crucial configu-
020            rations during the solving process, or directly provide an approximate solution.
021
               Despite notable empirical observations, theoretical foundations are still lacking.
022            In this work, we investigate the expressive or representative power of GNNs, a cru-
023
               cial aspect of neural network theory, specifically in the context of QP tasks, with
               both continuous and mixed-integer settings. We prove the existence of message-
024
               passing GNNs that can reliably represent key properties of quadratic programs,
025
               including feasibility, optimal objective value, and optimal solution. Our theory is
026            validated by numerical results.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Quadratic programming (QP) is an important type of optimization problem, with extensive appli-
031
      cations across domains such as graph matching, portfolio optimization, and dynamic control (Vogel-
032
      stein et al., 2015; Markowitz, 1952; Rockafellar, 1987). The goal of QP is to minimize a quadratic
033   objective function while satisfying specified constraints. These constraints can vary, leading to dif-
034   ferent subcategories of QP. When all the constraints are linear, we call a QP problem a linearly
035   constrained quadratic program (LCQP). When they also involve quadratic inequalities, we call the
036   problem a quadratically constrained quadratic program (QCQP). Furthermore, if the problem re-
037   quires some variables to be integers, we call it mixed-integer QP. In this study, we focus on LCQP
038   and its mixed-integer variant MI-LCQP.
039   In many real-world applications, finding solutions quickly is crucial, even if they are not perfectly
040   precise. For example, in transportation systems, such as ride-hailing platforms like Uber or Lyft,
041   matching drivers with passengers requires quick decision-making to minimize waiting times, even
042   if the optimal solution is not attained. Similarly, in financial trading, algorithms must swiftly adjust
043   investment portfolios in response to market changes, even if it is not the most optimal move.
044
      Unfortunately, existing methods for solving QP often rely on some computationally expensive tech-
045   niques such as matrix decomposition and the preconditioned conjugate gradient method. For in-
046   stance, matrix decomposition techniques like LU decomposition typically require O(n3 ) opera-
047   tions for a matrix with size n × n (Golub & Van Loan, 2013), although more advanced algorithms
048   can achieve lower complexities. Similarly, the preconditioned conjugate gradient method involves
049   O(n2 ) operations per iteration, and a high condition number of the matrix can lead to slow conver-
050   gence or numerical instability (Shewchuk et al., 1994). These considerations underscore the clear
051   need for novel techniques to address the demands of real-time applications.
052   Machine learning (ML) brings new chances to QP. Recent research indicates that deep neural
053   networks (DNNs) can significantly improve the efficiency of the QP solving process. Based on the
      role of DNNs in the solving process, these studies can be broadly categorized into two classes:

      However, despite notable empirical results, a systematic understanding of GNN for QP is still lack-
      ing. To thoroughly understand its pros and cons, some critical questions must be addressed:
081
082
         • (Existence). Are there GNNs that can either capture the essential characteristics of QPs or
            provide approximate solutions? This question is named the expressive power of GNNs.
083
         • (Trainability). If such GNNs exist, can we find them? The process of finding such GNNs is
084
            named training, which involves gathering data, creating a method to measure success or failure
085         (a loss function), and then refining the GNN to reduce the loss function.
086      • (Generalization). Can a trained GNN perform effectively on QP instances it has not previously
087         encountered? This concerns the generalization ability of GNNs.
088   This paper primarily addresses the first question about expressive power. For Type I applications,
089   we investigate whether GNNs can accurately map a QP to its crucial features, focusing on feasibility
090   and the optimal objective value. For Type II, we examine whether GNNs can map a QP to one of its
091   optimal solutions. Formally, the question motivating this paper is:
092                         Are there GNNs that can accurately predict the feasibility,
093                                                                                                    (1.1)
                            optimal objective value, and an optimal solution of a QP?
094
      The literature has explored the expressive capabilities of GNNs on general graph tasks (Xu et al.,
095
      2019; Azizian & Lelarge, 2021; Geerts & Reutter, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Li & Leskovec, 2022;
096   Sato, 2020) and their ability to approximate continuous functions on graphs (Azizian & Lelarge,
097   2021; Geerts & Reutter, 2022). However, significant gaps remain in understanding how these results
098   relate to QP, as the connections between QP features (such as feasibility and optimal objective value)
099   and graph properties have not been established. The most relevant works Chen et al. (2023a;b)
100   investigate the representation power of GNNs for (mixed-integer) linear programs, but their analysis
101   highly depends on the linear structure and does not cover nonlinear programs like QP.
102   Contributions. Overall, as several studies have empirically shown that incorporating a GNN can
103   greatly improve the performance of a QP solver on specific datasets — either via GNN-generated
104   real-time warm starts or GNN-suggested adaptive configurations — our primary aim is to theoreti-
105   cally investigate the expressive power of GNNs in these tasks and to determine if there is room for
106   improvement or any considerations to be aware of. Specifically, contributions of this paper include:
107      • (GNN for LCQP). We provide an affirmative answer to question (1.1), establishing a theoretical
            foundation for using GNNs for LCQP, across both Type I and II applications.

120   Basic concepts of LCQPs. An x satisfying all constraints of (2.1) is named a feasible solution. The
121   set of all feasible solutions, defined as X =: {x ∈ Rn : Ax ◦ b, l ≤ x ≤ u}, is referred to as the
122   feasible set. The LCQP is considered feasible if this set is non-empty; otherwise, it is infeasible. The
123   value of 21 x⊤ Qx + c⊤ x is named the objective (function) value. Its infimum across the feasible set
124   is termed the optimal objective value. If this infimum is −∞, suggesting the objective value could
125   indefinitely decrease, the LCQP is deemed unbounded. Conversely, when the optimal objective
126   value is finite, the corresponding x is identified as an optimal solution.
127   Graph representation of LCQPs. We present a graph structure, termed the LCQP-graph GLCQP =

129       • The graph contains two distinct types of nodes. Nodes in V = {1, 2, . . . , m}, labeled as i,

140

142   (2022), yet it forms the foundation or core module for numerous related studies. For instance,
143   removing nodes in V and their associated edges reduces the graph into the assignment graph used
144   in graph matching problems (Nowak et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020b; 2021; Qu et al., 2021; Gao

147   LP and MILP: removing edges associated with Q simplifies the graph to a bipartite structure, which
148   reduces the LCQP to an LP (Chen et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024).

291   4.1 GNN S CANNOT UNIVERSALLY REPRESENT MI-LCQP S
292   In this subsection, we answer the question (1.1) for MI-LCQP. When integer variables are intro-
293   duced, the situation changes. Particularly, we present some counter-examples illustrating the funda-
294   mental limitation of GNNs for representing properties of MI-LCQPs.
295   Proposition 4.1. There exist two MI-LCQP problems, with one being feasible and the other being
296   infeasible, such that their graphs are indistinguishable by any GNN in FMI-CLQP .
297   Proposition 4.2. There exist two feasible MI-LCQP problems, with different optimal objective val-
298   ues, such that their graphs are indistinguishable by any GNN in FMI-CLQP .

300   disjoint optimal solution sets, such that their graphs are indistinguishable by any GNN in FMI-CLQP   .

442      Numerical verification of MP-tractability and unfoldability. In practice, both MP-tractability
443      and unfoldability can be efficiently verified. In particular, one can apply the WL test, which requires
444      at most O(m + n) iterations. The complexity of each iteration is bounded by the number of edges in
445      the graph Shervashidze et al. (2011), which, in our context, is the number of nonzeros in matrices A
446      and Q: nnz(A)+nnz(Q). Therefore, the overall complexity of Algorithm 1 is O((m+n)·(nnz(A)+
447      nnz(Q))). After running Algorithm 1, MP-tractability can be directly verified using Definition 4.4,
448      and unfoldability can be directly verified using Definition 4.8.
449      Frequency of MP-tractability and unfoldability. In practice, the frequency of MP-tractable and
450      unfoldable instances largely depends on the dataset. In the earlier example, two of three variables,
451      x1 and x3 , display symmetry — they are labeled with the same color by WL test and swapping them
452      does not alter the problem. Generally, unfoldable problems lack symmetry and MP-tractability al-
453
         lows for some degree of symmetry. Another example in Section 4.1 admits strong symmetry across
         all variables, making it neither MP-tractable nor unfoldable. Thus, the frequency of MP-tractability
454
         and unfoldability relates to the level of symmetry in the data. When there is symmetry in MI-LCQP,
455
         it becomes foldable; and higher symmetry increases the risk of being MP-intractable. Fortunately,
456      unfoldable and MP-tractable instances make up the majority of the MI-LCQP set (shown in Ap-
457      pendix D). The dataset used in our experiments, which includes synthetic MI-LCQPs, portfolio
458      problems, and SVMs, consists entirely of unfoldable and MP-tractable instances. However, it’s im-
459      portant to note that in some challenging, artificially created datasets like MIPLIB 2017 Gleixner
460      et al. (2021), about 1/4 of the examples exhibit significant symmetry in half of the variables.
461      How to handle bad instances? Two potential approaches to deal with symmetry. (I) Adding
462      features: Introducing additional features can differentiate nodes in symmetric graphs. For example,
463      adding a random feature to nodes with identical attributes ensures they are no longer symmetric
464      Sato et al. (2021). (II) Using higher-order GNNs: These models can distinguish nodes that standard
465      message-passing GNNs cannot, enhancing their expressive power Morris et al. (2019).

533
         This paper establishes theoretical foundations for using GNNs to represent the feasibility, optimal
534      objective value, and optimal solution, of LCQPs and MI-LCQPs. In particular, we prove the exis-
535      tence of GNNs that can predict those properties of LCQPs universally well and show with explicit
536      examples that such results are generally not true for MI-LCQPs when integer constraints are intro-
537      duced. Moreover, we precisely identify subclasses of MI-LCQP problems on which such universal
538      approximation results are still valid. All our findings are also verified numerically. However, our
539      universal approximation theorems only show the existence of the GNNs, without discussing the
         training, generalization, and the size of GNNs, which are important future directions.

747
      incorrectly identify two non-isomorphic graphs as isomorphic. We slightly modify the standard WL
      test to fit the structure of LCQP-graphs, see Algorithm 2.

1520   Generic LCQP and MI-LCQP generation. For all instances generated and used in our numerical
1521   experiments, we set m = 10 and n = 50, which means each instance contains 10 constraints and 50
1522   variables. The sampling schemes of problem components are described below.
1523
1524          • Matrix Q in the objective function. We sample sparse, symmetric and positive semidefinite
1525
                Q using the make_sparse_spd_matrix function provided by the scikit-learn
                Python package, which imposes sparsity on the Cholesky factor. We set the alpha value
1526
                to 0.95 so that there will be around 10% non-zero elements in the resulting Q matrix.
1527
1528          • The coefficients c in the objective function: cj ∼ N (0, 0.12 ).
1529          • The non-zero elements in the coefficient matrix: Aij ∼ N (0, 1). The coefficient matrix A
1530            contains 100 non-zero elements. The positions are sampled randomly.
1531          • The right hand side b of the linear constraints: bi ∼ N (0, 1).
1532
              • The constraint types ◦. We first sample equality constraints following the Bernoulli distri-
1533
                bution Bernoulli(0.3). Then other constraints takes the type ≤. Note that this is equivalent
1534
                to sampling ≤ and ≥ constraints separately with equal probability, because the elements in
1535            A and b are sampled from symmetric distributions.
1536
              • The lower and upper bounds of variables: lj , uj ∼ N (0, 102 ). We swap their values if
1537
                lj > uj after sampling.
1538
1539          • (MI-LCQP only) The variable types are randomly sampled. Each type (continuous or inte-
1540            ger) occurs with equal probability.
