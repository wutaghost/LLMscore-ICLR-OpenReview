                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               As the size of datasets for training deep neural networks expands, data pruning
012
               has become an intriguing area of research due to its ability to achieve lossless
013
               performance with a reduced overall data volume. However, traditional data prun-
014            ing usually demands complete dataset annotations, incurring high costs. To tackle
015            this, we propose an innovative Annotation-Aware Data Pruning paradigm tailored
016            for object detection, dubbed as A2 -DP, which aims to reduce the burdens of both
017            annotation and storage. Our approach, consisting of two phases, integrates a hard
018            sample mining module to extract crucial hidden objects, a class balance module
019            to identify important objects in rare or challenging classes and a global similarity
020            removal module that enhances the elimination of redundant information through
021            object-level similarity assessments. Extensive experiments on 2D and 3D detec-
022
               tion tasks validate the effectiveness of the A2 -DP, consistently achieving a mini-
               mum pruning rate of 20% across various datasets, showcasing the practical value
023
               and efficiency of our methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep learning has achieved remarkable success across a wide range of domains, including computer
030   vision, natural language processing, and generative modeling (Ren et al., 2015; He et al., 2016;
031   Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Mildenhall et al., 2021; Devlin, 2018; Ho et al., 2020; Achiam et al.,
032   2023; Kirillov et al., 2023). However, training these models typically demands vast amounts of
033
      data (Achiam et al., 2023; Kirillov et al., 2023). While large datasets can be advantageous, they
      often contain redundant or less informative samples, leading to inefficiencies in data storage and
034
      model training, which can become costly. To mitigate these challenges, techniques such as dataset
035
      pruning (Sorscher et al., 2022; Ayed & Hayou, 2023; Qin et al., 2023; He et al., 2024) have been
036   introduced. Dataset pruning aims to remove redundant or easy samples, thereby conserving storage
037   and computational resources without compromising model performance.
038
039
      Recent advancements in dataset pruning (Sorscher et al., 2022) have primarily focused on simple
      classification tasks, where the data structure is relatively straightforward. However, more complex
040
      tasks such as object detection present greater challenges due to their intricate data structures (Ev-
041
      eringham et al., 2015; Lin et al., 2014; Geiger et al., 2012; Sun et al., 2020). Only recently has a
042   study (Lee et al., 2024) explored dataset pruning for object detection, but it offered limited investiga-
043   tion and achieved suboptimal performance. On the other hand, traditional dataset pruning methods,
044   which assume full annotations, make a strong assumption that is not well suited for object detec-
045   tion, where annotation costs are high. Meanwhile, active learning techniques (Houlsby et al., 2011;
046   Kirsch et al., 2019; Nguyen & Smeulders, 2004; Sener & Savarese, 2017; Agarwal et al., 2020; Xie
047   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) prioritize selecting critical samples for labeling, which helps reduce the
048   annotation burden. However, these methods often struggle to scale when applied to a large propor-
049   tion of the full dataset, resulting in models that may not perform as well as those trained on the
050
      complete dataset (Wu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022). This limitation is especially concerning in
      precision-sensitive applications like autonomous driving, where safety and accuracy are paramount.
051
052   Therefore, in the context of object detection, we propose a novel paradigm that combines the advan-
053   tages of data pruning with active learning to reduce annotation costs, as well as storage and training
      burdens. We term this paradigm Annotation-Aware Data Pruning. As shown in Fig. 1(b), this

073   To identify valuable samples without full annotations, we introduce a two-phase framework that inte-
074   grates uncertainty estimation with redundancy removal to enable efficient data pruning and selection.
075   In the first phase, referred to as Initial Model Construction, where the dataset lacks annotations,
076   we aim to train a model capable of basic object detection. To achieve this, we select and annotate a
077   small subset of the data to build an initial model. We then use uncertainty-based methods to identify
078   and prioritize the most informative and challenging samples for further annotation. By calculating
079
      the uncertainty of each predicted object using entropy, and applying class-specific weights to ac-
      count for class variations, we are able to rank and select the most uncertain samples for annotation.
080
      However, uncertainty alone can lead to redundant data selection, as similar objects may appear in
081
      different scenes. To address this, we apply a global object-level similarity metric to detect and re-
082   move redundant samples. By computing the cosine similarity between object features across scenes
083   and using a threshold to assess redundancy, we ensure that the selected data is both informative and
084   diverse. In the second phase, referred to as Advanced Data Refinement, we refine the model further
085   by focusing on hard sample mining, dynamically adjusting class weights, and continuing to remove
086   redundant samples. This two-phase process enables efficient data pruning, allowing the model to
087   focus on challenging and valuable objects while minimizing redundancy, thereby improving both
088   the dataset and model performance.
089   We evaluate our method on four datasets, including the 2D detection datasets PASCAL VOC and
090   COCO, as well as the 3D detection datasets KITTI and Waymo. Our approach consistently achieves
091   over a 20% pruning rate, greatly reducing annotation effort without any loss in performance.

103
104   2.1   DATASET P RUNING
105
106   Dataset pruning refers to removing some samples from the whole dataset for model training. Recent
107   studies have highlighted the potential for cost savings through data pruning. In works (Sorscher
      et al., 2022; Ayed & Hayou, 2023), researchers showcase that carefully designed pruning techniques


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      can yield results comparable with full datasets. Additionally, a study (Qin et al., 2023) employs
109   loss to dynamically prune datasets, while another work (He et al., 2024) explores data pruning in
110   large-scale settings. However, prior studies have predominantly focused on conducting research on
111   dataset pruning within classification tasks, overlooking more intricate tasks such as object detection.
112   Only recently, a study (Lee et al., 2024) has extended the dataset pruning task to object detection.
113   This work generates image-wise and class-wise representative feature vectors to select samples that
114   encapsulate both representativeness and diversity. However, the depth of its research is limited, with
115   experiments conducted on a small portion of the data.
116
117   2.2   ACTIVE L EARNING
118
119
      Active learning techniques have garnered considerable interest across diverse domains due to their
      capacity to mitigate the labeling workload. These methods can generally be classified into two main
120
      categories: uncertainty-based (Houlsby et al., 2011; Gal et al., 2017) and diversity-based strate-
121
      gies (Nguyen & Smeulders, 2004; Sener & Savarese, 2017; Agarwal et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023;
122   Li et al., 2023). Uncertainty-based strategies harness notions of uncertainty to pinpoint samples
123   that offer the most informational value for annotation, thereby enhancing the learning process. On
124   the other hand, diversity-based methodologies prioritize the selection of samples that encapsulate a
125   wide range of characteristics and patterns present in the dataset, aiming to create a more compre-
126   hensive and representative training set. Moreover, recent studies (Huang et al., 2010; Ash et al.,
127   2019) have delved into the fusion of uncertainty-based and diversity-based strategies, aiming to cap-
128   italize on the strengths of each approach. In recent years, there has been a surge of interest in the
129   application of active learning techniques to object detection tasks (Yuan et al., 2021; Choi et al.,
130
      2021; Wu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). Unlike image classification, active learn-
      ing in object detection presents distinct challenges due to the intricacies involved in localizing and
131
      identifying objects within images. One method, ENMS (Wu et al., 2022) introduces entropy-based
132
      non-maximum suppression to assess uncertainty and explores various prototype strategies to ensure
133   dataset diversity. Furthermore, PPAL (Yang et al., 2022) introduces a plug-and-play active learning
134   methodology by considering both uncertainty and diversity. Nevertheless, existing methods pre-
135   dominantly concentrate on utilizing a limited dataset subset to represent the entire data, potentially
136   resulting in models that may not be entirely comparable. Therefore, we believe there is a research
137   gap concerning the utilization of a substantial percentage of labeled data.

519   4.3.5   C OMPARISON B ETWEEN F ULL DATA M ODEL AND I NITIAL M ODEL
520
521   Traditional data pruning requires fully labeled datasets to train the model and understand the overall
522   data distribution. In contrast, since we lack complete annotations, we train an initial model on a
523
      labeled subset in Phase 1 to approximate the full dataset. Here, we compare the two approaches.
      First, the mAP of the full data model and the initial model are relatively close on the COCO test
524
      set (36.7 vs 33.6) and on the training set (44.1 vs 40.2), indicating that the initial model effectively
525
      mimics the performance of the full dataset. Additionally, the initial model is capable of accurately
526   estimating object counts, making it well-suited for our class balance module. Moreover, uncertainty
527   samples selected by the initial model tend to correspond to high-loss samples, which are valuable
528   for training. On the other hand, uncertainty samples from the full data model may include noisy
529   labels (Swayamdipta et al., 2020), which can negatively impact training.

      5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we present Annotation-Aware Data Pruning for object detection, A2 -DP, which re-
534   duces both the annotation and training burdens. Our approach features a two-phase pruning process
535   that integrates a hard sample mining module to extract vital but concealed objects and a class bal-
536   ance module to identify significant objects in rare or challenging classes. Additionally, our global
537   similarity removal module distinguishes our method by enabling object-level similarity assessments,
538   improving the elimination of redundant information. Extensive experiments on 2D and 3D detection
539   tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of A2 -DP, consistently achieving a pruning rate of at least 20%
      across various datasets, highlighting the practical value and efficiency of our approach.
