009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            As a variant of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), Unfolded GNNs offer enhanced
012            interpretability and flexibility over traditional designs. Nevertheless, they still suffer
013            from scalability challenges when it comes to the training cost. Although many
014            methods have been proposed to address the scalability issues, they mostly focus
015            on per-iteration efficiency, without worst-case convergence guarantees. Moreover,
016            those methods typically add components to or modify the original model, thus
017
               possibly breaking the interpretability of Unfolded GNNs. In this paper, we propose
               HERTA: a High-Efficiency and Rigorous Training Algorithm for Unfolded GNNs
018
               that accelerates the whole training process, achieving a nearly-linear time worst-
019
               case training guarantee. Crucially, HERTA converges to the optimum of the original
020            model, thus preserving the interpretability of Unfolded GNNs. Additionally, as a
021            byproduct of HERTA, we propose a new spectral sparsification method applicable
022            to normalized and regularized graph Laplacians that ensures tighter bounds for our
023            algorithm than existing spectral sparsifiers do. Experiments on real-world datasets
024            verify the superiority of HERTA as well as its adaptability to various loss functions
025            and optimizers.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become a powerful modern tool for handling graph data
031
      because of their strong representational capabilities and ability to explore the relationships between
      data points Kipf & Welling (2016); Battaglia et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2018). Like many deep learning
032
      models, GNNs are generally designed by heuristics and experience, which makes analyzing and
033
      understanding them a difficult task. Unfolded GNNs Yang et al. (2021a) are a type of GNNs that
034   are rigorously derived from an optimization problem, which makes their inductive bias and training
035   dynamics easier to interpret.
036
037
      Despite their enhanced interpretability, training unfolded models can be extremely expensive, espe-
      cially when the graph is large and (relatively) dense. This issue can come from two aspects:
038
039   1) Slow iterations: in every iteration the whole graph must be fed into the model, and the interactive
040      nature of graphs prevents trivially utilizing techniques like mini-batch training;
041
      2) Slow convergence: the training convergence rate is related to the connectivity of the graph and
042
         the well-conditionedness of the node features, and the model converges slowly when the data is
043      ill-conditioned.
044
045   Many methods have been proposed to address the high cost of training unfolded models Chen et al.
046   (2018a); Chiang et al. (2019); Zeng et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2021); Fey et al. (2021); Jiang et al.
047   (2023), primarily by using graph sampling to enable mini-batch training schemes that reduce the
048   per-iteration cost (Issue 1). However, these approaches typically require distorting the underlying
049   optimization objective explicitly or implicitly, thus diminishing the rigorous and interpretable nature
050
      of Unfolded GNNs. Moreover, existing works have mostly focused on per-iteration efficiency, while
      the convergence rate of the optimization in training Unfolded GNNs (Issue 2) remains un-addressed,
051
      leading to methods that are not robust to ill-conditioned data.
052
053   We propose HERTA: a High-Efficiency and Rigorous Training Algorithm for Unfolded GNNs,
      which is an algorithmic framework designed to address both Issues 1 and 2, while at the same


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      time preserving the rigorous and interpretable nature of Unfolded GNNs. Unlike many existing
055   methods that require changing the GNN model or defining a new objective, HERTA converges to
056   the optimum of the original training problem, and thus preserves the interpretability of the unfolded
057   models. Moreover, HERTA uses a specialized preconditioner to ensure fast linear convergence to the
058   optimum, requiring only a logarithmic number of passes over the data. We show empirically that
059   HERTA can be used to accelerate training for a variety of GNN objectives, as an extension of popular
060   optimization techniques.
061   A key novelty of our algorithm lies in the construction of a preconditioner for the GNN objective,
062   which accelerates the convergence rate. To construct this preconditioner, we design a spectral
063   sparsification method for approximating the squared inverse of a normalized and regularized Laplacian
064   matrix, by relying on an extension of the notion of effective resistance to overcome the limitations of
065   existing graph sparsification tools. Below, we present an informal statement of our main result.

      Unfolded Graph Neural Networks. Unlike conventional GNN models, which are designed mainly
094
      by heuristics, the forward layers of Unfolded GNNs are derived by explicitly optimizing a graph-
095
      regularized target function Zhang et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021a); Ma et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021);
096   Yang et al. (2021b); Zhu et al. (2021); Ahn et al. (2022). This optimization nature of Unfolded
097   GNNs allows developing models in a more interpretable and controllable way. The presence of an
098   explicit optimization problem allows designers to inject desired properties into the model and better
099   understand it. This approach has been used to overcome known GNN issues such as over-smoothing
100   Oono & Suzuki (2019); Liu et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2020) and sensitivity towards spurious edges
101   Zügner et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2020).
102   Efficient Graph Neural Network Training. In order to address the scalability issue of GNNs, many
103   techniques have been proposed. There are two major directions in the exploration of the solutions
104   of this issue. The first direction is to adopt sampling methods. These methods include node-wise
105   sampling Hamilton et al. (2017), layer-wise sampling Chen et al. (2018b) and subgraph-wise sampling
106   Chiang et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2023). The second direction is to utilize embeddings from past
107   iterations to enhance current iterations and to obtain more accurate representation with mini-batches
      and fewer forward layers Fey et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2021); Xue et al. (2023).

108
      As we indicated in Section 1, all of the above methods aim at addressing Issue 1 (Slow Iterations),
109   and none of them achieves a worst-case convergence guarantee without affecting the underlying
110   GNN model. In contrast, HERTA addresses both Issue 1 and also Issue 2 (Slow Convergence), and
111   possesses a theoretical guarantee on the training convergence rate while preserving the original target
112   model.
113
      Matrix Sketching and Subsampling. Our techniques and analysis are closely related to matrix
114
      sketching and subsampling, which are primarily studied in the area of Randomized Numerical
115   Linear Algebra (RandNLA, Woodruff et al. (2014); Drineas & Mahoney (2016); Martinsson &
116   Tropp (2020); Murray et al. (2023)). Given a large matrix, by sketching or sampling we compress
117   it to a much smaller matrix with certain properties preserved, thus accelerating the solution by
118   conducting expensive computations on the smaller matrix. This type of methods lead to improved
119   randomized algorithms for tasks including low rank approximation Halko et al. (2011); Cohen et al.
120   (2017a); Clarkson & Woodruff (2017), linear system solving Peng & Vempala (2021); Dereziński
121   & Yang (2024), least squares regression Rokhlin & Tygert (2008); Meng et al. (2014) and so on
122   Cohen et al. (2021). Our usage of these methods includes constructing spectral sparsifiers of graphs,
123
      obtained by edge subsampling Spielman & Srivastava (2008); Spielman & Teng (2011); Koutis et al.
      (2016), which are central in designing fast solvers for Laplacian linear systems Vishnoi et al. (2013);
124
      Kelner et al. (2013); Peng & Spielman (2014). We note that constructing spectral sparsifiers with
125
      regularization has also been explored in Calandriello et al. (2018), however their setting is different
126   from ours: we consider the normalized Laplacian of a graph, which is not a Laplacian of any graph
127   (even allowing weighted edges).

316   It has been shown that for any connected   graph with n nodes, (no matter how dense it is) there
317   is always a sparsified graph with Õ ϵn2 edges whose Laplacian is an ϵ-spectral approximation of

322   Srivastava (2008); Spielman & Teng (2011); Koutis et al. (2016), the setting considered in this paper
323   is different from the standard one: instead of a graph Laplacian L, the matrix we encounter is the

494   originally developed on MSE loss, HERTA is not limited to it and has the flexibility to be applied to
495   other types of loss functions.
496   Remark on the Surprising Effectiveness of HERTA on Cross Entropy Loss. We claim that this
497   phenomenon might originate from the fact the Hessians of TWIRLS under MSE loss and CE loss
498   are very similar. We provide an analysis of the gradient and Hessian of TWIRLS under MSE and
499   CE losses in Appendix D.1. The results show that the gradient under CE loss can be viewed as the
500   gradient under MSE loss with one term being normalized by softmax, and the Hessian under CE loss
501   can be viewed as a rescaled version of the Hessian under MSE loss. These comparisons serve as an
502   explanation of why HERTA works so well with CE loss.

530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper we present HERTA: a High-Efficiency and Rigorous Training Algorithm that solves the
533   problem of training Unfolded GNNs on a graph within a time essentially of the same magnitude as
534   the time it takes to load the input data. As a component of HERTA, we also propose a new spectral
535   sparsifier that works for normalized and regularized graph Laplacian matrices.
536   Experimental results on real world datasets show the effectiveness of HERTA. Moreover, it is shown
537   that HERTA works for various loss functions and optimizers, despite being derived from a specific
538   loss function and optimizer. Moreover, while being derived from training perspective, HERTA is also
539   capable of accelerating generalization. These results highlight the universality of HERTA and confirm
      that it is ready to use in practice. See Appendix D for further discussions and future directions.

1423   D.4       P OSSIBLE D IRECTIONS FOR E XTENDING HERTA TO M ORE C OMPLEX M ODELS
1424
       The assumption that f (X; W ) = XW provides us with a convenience that the Hessian of this
1425
       model is a constant matrix. Therefore in Algorithm 1 we only need to calculate the preconditioner P
1426   for one time. However, if f (X; W ) is implemented by a non-linear network, then the Hessian will
1427   change by the time and might be hard to calculate, which will be a key challenge to using a more
1428   complex f . We note that this can be possibly addressed by constructing a linear approximation of
1429   f using its Jaccobian. In each iteration, we can use the Jaccobian to replace the X used in current
1430   version of HERTA, and recalculate P at each iteration. Since the convergence is fast, we only need
1431   to recalculate the Jaccobian for a small number of iterations, so should not bring massive change to
1432   the running time of the algorithm.
1433   The attention mechanism of TWIRLS in Yang et al.    (2021b) is achieved by adding a concave penalty
1434
       function to each summand of the the Tr Z ⊤ L̂Z term in eq.(4.1). For specific penalty functions, it
1435
1436
       might still possible to find a inner problem solver, as long as the problem stay convex. We note that
       this depends on concrete implementation of the penalty term used. Investigating how to fast solve the
1437
       inner problem under various penalty functions should also be an important problem for future study.
