009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Length generalization refers to the ability to extrapolate from short training se-
012
               quences to long test sequences and is a challenge for current large language models.
013            While prior work has proposed some architecture or data format changes to achieve
014            length generalization, these proposals typically apply to a limited set of tasks.
015            Building on prior scratchpad and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) techniques, we propose
016            Turing Programs, a novel CoT strategy that decomposes an algorithmic task into
017            steps mimicking the computation of a Turing Machine. This framework is both
018            universal, as it can accommodate any algorithmic task, and simple, requiring only
019            copying text from the context with small modifications. We show that by using
020            Turing Programs, we obtain robust length generalization on a range of algorithmic
021
               tasks: addition, multiplication and in-context SGD. We then demonstrate that trans-
               formers achieve length generalization on random Turing Programs, suggesting that
022
               length generalization is possible for any algorithmic task. Finally, we theoretically
023
               prove that transformers can implement Turing Programs, constructing a simple
024            RASP (Weiss et al. Weiss et al. (2021)) program that simulates an arbitrary Turing
025            machine.

092   Length generalization remains an important challenge for large language models as underlined in
093
      several works Delétang et al. (2022); Dziri et al. (2024); Hupkes et al. (2020); Schwarzschild et al.
      (2021); Zhang et al. (2022). Despite their advanced reasoning capabilities, Transformer-based large
094
      language models struggle to process longer sequences than they were trained on Anil et al. (2022).
095

099
      Positional encodings for length generalization. Shaw et al. Shaw et al. (2018) were early to notice
      that the weak length generalization of Transformers was due to the choice of absolute positional
100
      encoding. Following this, many alternatives were proposed to replace the absolute positional encoding:
101
      relative positional encodings, which focus on the relative distances between tokens Dai et al. (2019);
102   and weighted attention mechanisms in place of position embeddings Chi et al. (2022); Jelassi et al.
103   (2023); Li et al. (2023); Press et al. (2021); Raffel et al. (2020). These alternatives showed substantial
104   improvements in length generalization on natural language processing tasks. On the other hand,
105   Kazemnejad et al. (2024) found that a causal language model with no positional encoding can length
106   generalize better than some of these specialized positional encodings on algorithmic tasks. In this
107   work, we apply the Hard-ALiBi positional encoding Jelassi et al. (2024), that achieved state-of-the-art
      length generalization on the specific task of copying, to more general algorithmic tasks.

      Data formatting for length generalization. A wide range of data formatting methods have been
109   introduced to achieve length extrapolation in algorithmic tasks. Scratchpad and Chain-of-Thought
110   formats were proposed to learn arithmetic either through finetuning or in-context learning Anil et al.
111   (2022); Zhou et al. (2023). When training from scratch, some other proposed techniques to improve

113   et al. (2023), adding padding to the sequence Jelassi et al. (2023), and setting index hints in front of
114   each digit Zhou et al. (2023). Closer to our work, several works Anil et al. (2022); Dziri et al. (2024);

116   a model on scratchpad data does not yield any significant length generalization improvement. In

      our work, we demonstrate that length generalization is possible using a combination of a particular

      novel scratchpad strategy that is general and may be applied to achieve length generalization on any

      algorithmic task.

      2.1   L ENGTH GENERALIZATION
142
143   Many sequence modeling tasks have problem instances of different lengths. Shorter instances are
144   often easier to state, process and handle, and require less compute to find the answer. By contrast,
145   longer instances are more challenging to parse and require more compute to solve. Reasoning tasks
146   such as multi-hop reasoning, program execution, deductive reasoning, and theorem proving fit in this
147   category.
148   Algorithmic reasoning tasks consist of inputs that are sequences of tokens describing the task (e.g.
149   addition, multiplication) and outputs that are the corresponding solutions. We assume that the
150   language model is allowed to generate (many) intermediate tokens before outputting the answer.
151   Then formally, the length generalization problem consists of training a language model on inputs of
152   length ≤ L and solving problems of length > L at test time.
153
154   2.2   S CRATCHPAD
155
156   It has been shown in prior work that the performance of LLMs on algorithmic tasks can be greatly
157   improved by generating step-by-step solutions instead of immediately outputting the final answer Wei
158
      et al. (2022b). Among the multiple methods described in the literature, we focus on the scratchpad
      method Nye et al. (2021). Given an algorithmic task, this method encodes the intermediate steps of the
159
      algorithm as text and trains the model to emit them to a buffer that is referred to as the “scratchpad”.
160
161   Nye et al. Nye et al. (2021) showed that scratchpad finetuning can be used to achieve strong in-
      distribution performance on execution based tasks such as code execution and computing polynomials.


                                                         3

162
      They also report modest length generalization results on integer arithmetic. The limitation of
163   scratchpad training for length generalization is further highlighted in Anil et al. (2022); Dziri et al.
164   (2024); Hu et al. (2024); Kazemnejad et al. (2024); Lanchantin et al. (2024).
165
166
      In this paper, we revisit the use of scratchpad training to achieve length generalization on algorithmic
      tasks. We begin with the observation that the scratchpad technique can be realized as an iterative
167
      sequence of copying operations, where at each iteration the input is slightly modified. Building on
168
      previous works showing that with the right positional encoding, transformers can achieve length
169   generalization on the copying operation Jelassi et al. (2024), we hypothesize that combining the
170   scratchpad technique with a favorable positional encoding can unlock length generalization capa-
171   bilities. We verify this hypothesis in Section 3 and Section 5, but first we review various choices of
172   positional encoding.
173
174   2.3   P OSITIONAL ENCODINGS
175
176   The inability of transformers to extrapolate to longer sequences has been primarily attributed to the
177   positional encoding Shaw et al. (2018); Shen et al. (2023). In this section, we review the different
178   positional encoding schemes and in Section 3, we report their length generalization performance.
179
      We review here specific choices for positional encodings that are known to perform well for length
      generalization, and discuss additional encoding schemes (such as absolute and relative positional
180
      encodings) in Appendix A.
181
182
      No Positional Encoding (NoPE). Decoder-only models with causal attention, as shown by Haviv
183
      et al. (2022), acquire positional understanding, without explicit positional encoding. Kazemnejad et al.
184
      (2024) shows that a model without positional encoding extrapolate better than those with specialized
185   positional encodings on some algorithmic tasks.
186
187   ALiBi. Press et al. (2021) introduces this additive positional encoding where the bias function
188   follows b(i, j) = −r|i − j|, where r > 0 is some hyperparameter. This scheme has led to state-of-
189   the-art length generalization on natural language tasks. However, Jelassi et al. (2024) notices that it
190   struggles at length generalization on the copy task and hypothesize that it is due to the slow decay of
191   r.
192
193   Hard-ALiBi. Jelassi et al. (2024) introduce Hard-ALiBi, an additive positional encoding where the
194   bias satisfies b(i, j) = −∞ for j ≤ i − m and b(i, j) = 0 for j > i − m, for some hyperparameter
195   m > 0. Intuitively, with this hard thresholding, tokens can only attend to the m closest tokens.
196   Different heads may have different values of m and some heads use m = ∞ which corresponds to
197
      softmax attention with no positional embedding at all (allowing for propagation of global information).
      The authors demonstrate empirically that models equipped with the Hard-ALiBi positional encoding
198
      achieve remarkable length generalization on the copy task. In this work, we use the Hard-ALiBi
199
      position encoding to enable length generalization on algorithmic tasks as we show below.

285   In Section 3, we showed that Transformers with Hard-ALiBi trained on a specific choice of scratchpad
286   format can length generalize to sequences that are 2× longer. On closer inspection, each line in the
287   scratchpad in Figure 2 is a slightly modified copy of the previous one where a few elementary changes
288   are applied, e.g. removing one digit for each operand and updating the intermediate result/carry.
289   Since Hard-ALiBi yields robust length generalization on copying, this may explain why we achieve
290   better extrapolation than previous works that trained their models with scratchpad.
291   In this section, we generalize this approach and claim that every algorithmic task can be written as a
292   sequence of modified copy operations: i.e. copy operations with small and localized modifications.
293   Such decomposition follows immediately from the standard construction of a Turing Machine, a
294   universal model of computation. We therefore refer to this scratchpad strategy as a Turing Program.
295   We start this section introducing the standard definition of a Turing Machine, and then present Turing
296   Programs, our scratchpad strategy for achieving length generalization on any algorithmic task. Lastly,
297
      we present our main theoretical result: Transformers can implement Turing Programs over long
      sequences of inputs.

300   4.1     BACKGROUND : T URING M ACHINES
301
302   A Turing Machine Turing (1950) is a computational model that consists of an infinite tape3 with
303   cells, a head that can read from a cell, write to a cell and move left or right over the tape, and a set of
304
      rules which direct the head based on the symbol it reads and the current state of the machine. More
      formally, a Turing Machine is defined as follows.
305
306
      Definition 4.1 A Turing Machine is specified as a quadruple T = (Q, Σ, s, δ) where: 1) Q is a finite
307
      set of states, 2) Σ is a finite set of symbols, 3) s ∈ Q is the initial state and f ∈ Q is the final state, 4)
308
      δ is a transition function determining the next move: δ : (Q × Σ) → (Σ × {L, R} × Q).
309
310   At the first iteration, the machine is set to state s ∈ Q, the head is on the first (leftmost) cell of the tape,
311   and the input is written on the tape from left to right. At each iteration, the head is on the i-th cell in
312
      the tape, is in state q ∈ Q and reads the i-th symbol on the tape α. Then, if δ(q, α) = (α′ , D, q ′ ), the
      head writes the symbol α′ , moves in the direction D ∈ {L, R}, and the machine changes its state to
313
      q ′ . If the machine reaches the state f , it stops, and its “output” is written on the tape.
314
315
      Turing Machines are a powerful model for solving algorithmic tasks since (a) the framework is
316
      universal i.e. it is possible to write any algorithmic task in the Turing Machine formalism, (b)
317   Turing Machines can solve a wide range of algorithmic problems—ranging from simple arithmetic to
318   determining whether a number is a prime Agrawal et al. (2004)—in a polynomial number of steps. In
319   the next section, we show how to use the Turing Machine formalism to obtain a novel scratchpad
320   strategy that unlocks length generalization on any algorithmic task.
321
322       3
            We assume that the tape is unbounded from the right side, but bounded from the left. Namely, there are
323   infinitely many cells to the right of the input, but no empty cells to the left. This is computationally equivalent to
      a tape that is infinite from both sides.

      4.2    T URING P ROGRAMS : A UNIVERSAL SCRATCHPAD STRATEGY FOR LENGTH
325          GENERALIZATION
326
327   The left panel of Figure 1 represents the simulation of a Turing Machine and shows how the state, the
328   head and the tape evolves with time. Note that at each time step, the state of the tape is a copy of the
329   previous tape with a few elementary changes such as a move of the head, an edit of a single symbol
330   and a change of state.
331   The steps in a Turing Machine simulation are similar to a scratchpad strategy where each string is a
332   copy of the previous one with a few modifications. Therefore, we claim that for any algorithmic task
333   that can be solved by a Turing-computable algorithm, there is a corresponding scratchpad strategy
334   for solving this problem (as demonstrated in the right panel of Figure 1). We refer to this novel
335   scratchpad strategy as Turing Programs.
336
      Turing Programs decompose an algorithmic task into a series of intermediate reasoning steps. Each
337   step is a “tape” that maintains the state of the machine, and the next step is a copy of the previous
338   tape with a few elementary changes, such as trimming of digits and update of carry/intermediate
339   result as in the case of addition and multiplication (see Figures 2 and 4) or update of the parameters in
340   the case of SGD on linear regression (see Subsection 5.2). In Section 5, we show how to use Turing
341   Programs to unlock novel length generalization results on challenging algorithmic tasks.

      4.3    T HEORY: T URING P ROGRAMS IN RASP
344
345   To further motivate the use of Turing Programs to achieve length generalization on arbitrary algo-
346   rithms, we prove that transformers can implement Turing Programs over long sequences of inputs.
347   In particular, we show that Turing Programs can be implemented in RASP Weiss et al. (2021), a
348   programming language that was suggested as an abstract description of the operations of a transformer.
349   Following Zhou et al. (2023), we use a restricted version of RASP that does not allow direct index
350   operations, as Zhou et al. (2023) hypothesized that RASP programs with index arithmetics may
351   not length generalize4 . Therefore, our result should be viewed as a length-generalization-friendly
352
      construction of a transformer that can execute (most) Turing Programs (and hence, can simulate most
      Turing machines).
353
354   To avoid index operations, we leverage the n-gram
355   hashing mechanism suggested by Jelassi et al. (2023)
356   as a basis for the copying ability of transformers. In
357   their construction, copying a string from the input
358
      was achieved by storing a sequence of n preceding to-
      kens (n-gram) at each position, and iteratively retriev-
359
      ing the next token after the uniquely matched n-gram.
360

361   cept that instead of copying a string from the input,
362   we copy the next state of the Turing machine as com-

511   6    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
512
513   Studying and improving the length generalization abilities of transformers on algorithmic tasks has
514   been the focus of various recent works. In parallel, it has been established experimentally that the
515   ability of language models to solve algorithmic tasks is greatly enhanced when allowing them to
516   use scratchpad/CoT data. Additionally, recent theoretical works demonstrate that transformers can
517   use CoT to simulate arbitrary algorithms Merrill & Sabharwal (2023), establishing that they are
518   computationally “universal”. These results motivate us to study whether transformers are universal
519   learners, able to learn from examples to execute arbitrary algorithms. Since algorithms are typically
520   defined over arbitrary sequence lengths, we use length generalization as a measure of whether the
521
      model has learned the true algorithm. To establish this, we use the key observation that transformers
      can length generalize on the copying operation. Since executing an algorithm can be implemented
522
      as a sequence of “smart” copy operations, the copying ability of transformers can be leveraged to
523
      achieve non-trivial length generalization performance on a wide range of algorithmic tasks.
524
525   That said, we acknowledge that our work still falls short of demonstrating that transformers can
526   robustly length generalize on any algorithmic task. In some of our results, the extrapolation to longer
527
      sequence length is not robust, and degradation in performance may appear shortly after moving
      out-of-distribution. Additionally, our results rely on potentially very long and cumbersome CoT data,
528
      in a way that is not necessarily useful for real-world applications of language models. Thus, we view
529
      our results as theoretical evidence that length generalization is possible, and leave the development of
530   more practical and robust methods for real-world length generalization to future work.

      Absolute Positional Encoding (APE). APE consists in maintaining a positional vector pi for each
705   position i. This vector is either predefined via a sinusoidal function Vaswani et al. (2017) or learned
706   Devlin et al. (2018). Then, pi is added to the token embedding ei before being processed by the
707   Transformer. Prior work observed that this positional encoding does not generalize well to longer
708   sequences in both natural language Press et al. (2021) and algorithmic tasks Jelassi et al. (2023);
709   Kazemnejad et al. (2024).
710
711   Additive Relative Positional Encoding (RPE). Shaw et al. (2018) were the first to integrate
712   positional encodings at the level of each attention layer (instead of doing it at the input level). Raffel
713   et al. (2020) built upon this approach and added scalar biases to pre-softmax logits as follows:
714                                        A = XWQ (XWK )⊤ + B,                                              (1)
715                                                                                                      n×n
716
      where X, WQ , WK are the input and query and key weight matrices. The bias matrix B ∈ R              is
717   induced by some positional encoding function b : N∗ 2 → R. For instance, the T5 relative positional
718
      encoding Raffel et al. (2020) set b(i, j) = f (i − j), where f is some function. Most of the subsequent
      positional encodings such as ALiBi Press et al. (2021), Kerple Chi et al. (2022), Randomized
719
      Positional Encoding Ruoss et al. (2023) and Fire Li et al. (2023) rely on changing the pre-softmax
720
      logits and differ in their definition of b.
721
722   Rotary Positional Encoding (RoPE). RoPE Su et al. (2024) encodes position information in
723   attention logits by applying a rotation transformation to the query and key vectors based on their
724   relative positions. Despite being widely used, RoPE exhibits limited length generalization Press et al.
725   (2021); Kazemnejad et al. (2024).
726
727
      B    P RIOR RESULTS ON MULTI - DIGIT ADDITION
728
729
      In this section, we summarize the methods proposed by prior work to get length generalization
730
      on addition along with their corresponding performance. In what follows, we indicate in red the
731   positional encoding and in green the data format used in these works. We also take as a running
732   example the addition 576+361=937.
733
734
      – Lee et al. (2023) from 7 to 7-digit (1.0×). APE + Reversed format. They train their models by
      reversing each operand as 675+163=739. Therefore, the causal model that processes information
735
      from left to right can start with the least significant digit and proceed to the most significant digit,
736
      which corresponds to the algorithm for addition. They do not achieve any length generalization.
737
738   – Kazemnejad et al. (2024) from 8 to 9-digit (1.125×): NoPE + Reversed format. They show that a
739   model without positional encoding trained on reversed additions like 675+163=739 outperforms
740
      those with specialized positional encodings like T5’s relative positional Raffel et al. (2020) or RoPE
      Su et al. (2024).
741
742   – Shen et al. (2023) from 10 to 11-digit (1.1×): NoPE + Reversed format + random space augmen-
743   tation. They introduced random spacing between digits, aiming to alleviate the model’s reliance
744   on absolute positional information. Combining this with the reversed format, the running example
745   becomes 6 75+16 3=739. They show that NoPE Transformers length generalize from 10 to 11
746
      digit-addition.
747   – Zhou et al. (2023) from 30 to 45 digits (1.5×): NoPE + Index Hints. They define "index hints",
748   a formatting that consists in adding a letter in front of each digit in the addition to indicate their
749   position. For instance, the running example becomes a5b7c6+a3b6c1=a9b3c7. This change is
750   applied during training and inference and enables transformers to execute indexing via induction
751   heads Olsson et al. (2022).
752   – Zhou et al. (2024) from 40 to 100 digits (2.5×): Fire Li et al. (2023) + Randomized positional
753   encoding Ruoss et al. (2023) + Reversed format + Index Hints . They use a combination of two
754   positional encodings: Fire Li et al. (2023), a additive relative positional encoding that has obtained
755   strong length generalization on natural language benchmarks and Randomized positional encoding
      Ruoss et al. (2023): a technique that samples encodings from a range exceeding test-time lengths.
