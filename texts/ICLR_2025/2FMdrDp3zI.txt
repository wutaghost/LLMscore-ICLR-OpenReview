008                                                    A BSTRACT
009
010                Complex query answering (CQA) on knowledge graphs (KGs) is gaining mo-
011                mentum as a challenging reasoning task. In this paper, we show that the current
012
                   benchmarks for CQA are not really complex, and the way they are built distorts
                   our perception of progress in this field. For example, we find that in these bench-
013
                   marks most queries (up to 98% for some query types) can be reduced to simpler
014
                   problems, e.g. link prediction, where only one link needs to be predicted. The
015                performance of state-of-the-art CQA models drops significantly when such mod-
016                els are evaluated on queries that cannot be reduced to easier types. Thus, we
017                propose a set of more challenging benchmarks, composed of queries that require
018                models to reason over multiple hops and better reflect the construction of real-
019                world KGs. In a systematic empirical investigation, the new benchmarks show
020                that current methods leave much to be desired from current CQA methods.

023   1       I NTRODUCTION
024
025   A crucial challenge in AI and ML is learning to perform complex reasoning, i.e., solving tasks that
026
      involve a number of intermediate steps and sub-goals to be completed. Complex query answer-
      ing (CQA; Hamilton et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021; Arakelyan et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022) has
027
      emerged as one of the most prominent ways to measure complex reasoning over external knowledge
028
      bases, encoded as knowledge graphs (KGs; Hogan et al., 2021). For instance, to answer the query:
029
030       “Which actor performed in a movie filmed in New York City and distributed on Blue Ray?” (q1 )
031
      over a KG such as FreeBase (Bollacker et al., 2008), one would need to first intersect the set of
032   movies found on Blue Ray and the ones shot in New York City, and then link these intermediate
033   candidate answers to another entity, i.e., an actor participating in it. However, the answers computed
034   in this way may not include entities that are unreachable if missing links are present.
035
      To deal with the unavoidable incompleteness of real-world KGs, ML methods were developed to
036
      solve CQA in the presence of missing links. Neural query answering models, constituting the current
037
      state-of-the-art (SoTA) for CQA (Arakelyan et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Arakelyan et al., 2023;
038   Ren et al., 2023; Galkin et al., 2024b), map queries and KGs (i.e., entities and relation names) into
039   a unified latent space that supports reasoning. Performance measurements on de-facto-standard
040   benchmarks such as FB15k237 (Toutanova & Chen, 2015) and NELL995 (Xiong et al., 2017) suggest
041   that in recent years such models achieved impressive progress on CQA on queries having different
042   structures, and hence posing apparently different levels of difficulty to be answered.
043
      The difficulty of a benchmark relates to the size and structural complexity1 of its queries, and several
044   query “types” have been proposed (Ren et al., 2020), each involving a different combination of
045   logical operators—conjunctions, disjunctions, and requiring to traverse a number of missing links
046   that generally increases with the number of logical conditions imposed (Figs. 1 and 2). For instance,
047   the query q1 is an example of a “2i1p” query type, since it comprises an intersection of two entity
048   sets (2i) followed by a path2 of length one (1p).
049
      In this paper, we argue that the perception of progress on CQA benchmarks has been distorted by
050   implicit assumptions in these benchmarks. We start by noting how both in FB15k237 and NELL995,
051   the vast majority of queries of a complex type simplifies to one simpler type, thanks to the fact that to

104   Knowledge graphs. A KG is a graph-structured knowledge base where knowledge about the
105   world is encoded as relationships between entities. More formally, a KG can be represented as a
106   multi-relational graph G = (E, R, T ), where E is a set of entities, R is a set of relation names, and
107   T ⊆ E × R × E is a set of links or triples, where each triple (s, p, o) ∈ T represents a relation-
      ship of type p ∈ R between the subject s ∈ E and the object o ∈ E of the triple. For instance,

122   Complex Query Answering. The aim of CQA is to retrieve a set of possible answers to a logical
123   query q that poses conditions over entities and relation types in a KG. Following the literature on
124   CQA (Arakelyan et al., 2021), we consider the problem of answering logical queries with a single
125   target variable (T ), a set of constants including anchor entities (a1 , a2 , . . . , ak ∈ E), given relation
126   names (r1 , r2 , . . . , rn ∈ R), and first-order logical operations that include conjunction ∧, disjunc-
127   tion ∨ and existential quantification ∃. In this work, we do not deal with queries involving negation;
128
      however, all of our considerations about the inflated performance of current benchmarks will also
      transfer to versions of these benchmarks that include them (Ren et al., 2023; Galkin et al., 2020).
129
130   Different queries are categorized into different types based on the structure of their corresponding
131   logical sentence (Xiong et al., 2017). The idea behind this taxonomy is that queries of the same
132   type share the same “hardness”, i.e., the level of difficulty to be answered and different query types
133   correspond to tasks that map to more or less complex reasoning tasks. The simplest CQA task is
134
      link prediction (Bordes et al., 2013), i.e., answering a query of the form:

138   of matching a single link, more complex queries involve matching sub-graphs in a KG (see Fig. 2).

      Standard evaluation. Given a KG G and a logical query q from one of the types described above,
171
      answering q boils down to a graph matching problem (Hogan, 2020) if we assume that all the mean-
172   ingful links are already in G. Instead, if G is incomplete, we will need to predict missing links while
173   answering q. Many ML approaches to CQA, reviewed in Sec. 5, therefore assume a distribution over
174   possible links (Loconte et al., 2023), requiring probabilistic reasoning. To evaluate them, standard
175   benchmarks such as FB15k237 and NELL995 artificially divide G into Gtrain and Gtest , treating the
176   triples in the latter as missing links. This splitting process is done uniformly at random, a procedural
177   choice that can alter the measured performance, as we discuss next.
178   CQA is generally treated as a ranking task, counting how many true candidate answers to a query are
179   ranked higher than non-answer ones. Denoting the rank of a query answer pair (q, t) by rank(q, t),
180   the performance for each query type is calculated as the mean reciprocal rank (MRR), i.e.,

192   3    W HAT IS THE REAL “H ARDNESS ” OF CQA IN INCOMPLETE KG S ?
193
194   As discussed in the previous section, the perceived complexity of a query is related to the graph
195
      structure associated to its query type (Fig. 2): queries containing more hops/existentially-quantified
      variables are more challenging, e.g., a 3p query is harder than a 2p query. In this section, we give an
196
      alternative perspective on the difficulty of answering queries that take into account the information
197
      coming from the training data, and that might have leaked into a learned model. We argue that
198   predicting links that are truly missing, i.e., not accessible to a learned model, is actually what makes
199   a query “hard”. To do so, we formalize the notion of a reasoning tree for a query answer pair, and
200   then define how we determine the practical hardness of a query answer pair.
201
      Given a query q and every answer set of candidate answers t ∈ Eq in its candidate set, we define
202
      the reasoning tree of each query answer pair (q, t), as the directed acyclic graph starting from the
203
      anchor entities of q to the target entity t, whose relational structure matches the query graph. Fig. 1
204   provides examples of different reasoning trees for four different answers to the same query. There,
205   we highlight whether a link belongs to Gtrain or not, i.e., it is a missing link. We assess the hardness
206   of each answer t ∈ Eq , by analyzing the composition of the reasoning tree required to predict t.
207   As to answering a (q, t) pair, multiple reasoning trees are possible, so we select the one with the
208   smallest number of missing links and the fewest number of hops.
209
      A (q, t) pair is trivial if the answer t can be entirely retrieved from Gtrain , i.e., there exists at least one
210   reasoning tree where all the links in it are seen during training. This type of answer does not need
211   probabilistic inference and hence is filtered out from current CQA benchmarks (Ren & Leskovec,
212   2020), which only consider non-trivial (q, t) pairs, which we call inference-based pairs. However,
213   inference-based pairs do not need all links in their reasoning tree to be predicted as, by definition,
214   it is sufficient that at least one link in the tree is missing. Therefore, we define a (q, t) pair to be
215   a partial-inference pair if its reasoning tree contains at least one link in Gtrain and at least one link
      present in Gtest . Alternatively, a query answer pair whose reasoning tree contains only links present

251   In this section, we systematically analyze the practical hardness of queries from very popular CQA
252   benchmarks and answer the following research question: (RQ1) What is the proportion of query
253   answer pairs that can be classified as full-inference rather than the easier partial-inference?
254   For this purpose, we consider the CQA benchmarks generated from the KG FB15k237 (based on
255   Freebase) and NELL995 (based on NELL systems (Carlson et al., 2010)) as they are the most used to
256   evaluate SoTA methods for CQA (Ren et al., 2020; Ren & Leskovec, 2020; Arakelyan et al., 2021;
257   Zhang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Arakelyan et al., 2023).4
258   Complex queries can be reduced to much simpler types. We group the testing query answer
259   pairs (q, t) into query types (Sec. 2), and we further split them based on whether they can be re-
260   duced to simpler types after observing the training links in Gtrain (Sec. 3). Table 1 shows that for
261   both FB15k237 and NELL995 the vast majority of (q, t) pairs can be reduced to simpler types. For
262   FB15k237, 86.8% to 98.3% of (q, t) pairs can be reduced to 1p queries, while only 0.1% to 4%
263   require full inference. Similarly, for NELL995, 49.5% to 98.5% of (q, t) pairs map to 1p queries,
264   and only 0.1% to 6% to full inference. For instance, 96.7% of 2i1p (q, t) pairs in FB15k237 can be
265   reduced to 1p queries, 1.8% to 2p, and 1.4% to 2i. However, only 0.1% of these pairs cannot be
266
      reduced to any other (q ′ , t) pair, i.e., they require full inference in order to be predicted. The only
      exceptions to this trend are (q, t) pairs where q has a 1p or 2u structure which, by definition, only

395   5.1     B UILDING AN H YBRID R EASONER TO E XPLOIT T RAINING L INKS
396
      So far, we have assessed that current benchmarks are skewed towards easier query types (Table 1)
397
      and thus the perceived progress of current SoTA CQA methods boils down to their performance
398   on 1p queries (Table 2). To have an undistorted view of this progress, in the next section, we will
399   devise a benchmark that allows to precisely measure model performance on full-inference queries
400   only. Nevertheless, we argue that in a real-world scenario one has to perform reasoning over both
401   existing links and in the presence of missing ones. Therefore, it is worth to evaluate CQA over
402   partial-inference queries, however in a stratified analysis and accounting for the disproportion of 1p
403   links (see Takeaway 1). Next, we discuss how to build an hybrid solver that exploits both link types.
404   Current SoTA CQA methods might implicitly exploit this aspect at test time if they are able to
405   memorize well the entire training KG. However, there is no guarantee that this is the case, especially
406   for less parameterized models. If one is interested in hybrid inference on real-world KGs, discarding
407   the information in Gtrain is wasteful. To this end, hybrid solvers such as QTO (Bai et al., 2023)
408   and FIT (Yin et al., 2024) have been developed. They explicitly retrieve existing links from Gtrain
409   when answering a query at test time. Inspired by them, and to evaluate our hypothesis, we create a
410   light-weight hybrid solver, named CQD-Hybrid, that is a variant of CQD that uses a pre-trained link
411   predictor to compute scores for the answer entities of 1p queries only, denoting the unnormalized
412
      probability of that single link to exist (Loconte et al., 2023). Then, assignments to existentially
      quantified variables in a complex query are greedily obtained by maximizing the combined score
413
      of the links, computed by replacing logical operators in the query with fuzzy ones (van Krieken
414
      et al., 2022). A complete assignments to the variables (and hence to the target variable T ), gives
415   us an answer to the query. In our CQD-Hybrid, we assign the maximum score to those links that
416   are observed in Gtrain . That is, training links will have a higher score than the one that the link
417   predictor would output, hence effectively steering the mentioned greedy procedure at test time. We
418   report hyperparameters and implementation details of CQD-Hybrid in App. D.1. This is the only
419   minimal change we apply to CQD to test if its performance can depend on memorizing training
420   triples. Note that this is different from QTO and FIT, which involve more sophisticated steps such
421   as score calibration and a forward/backward update stage, that can boost performance. In our
422   experiments, we found CQD-Hybrid was consistently boosting performance in terms of MRR w.r.t.
423
      CQD and other non-hybrid baselines, as reported in Table 4, which shows aggregated performance
      for space reasons. A stratified comparison is reported in the appendix in Tables A.3 and A.4.
424
425     Takeaway 2.
426
427         FB15k237 and NELL995 are not suitable to precisely assess the capability of CQA methods to
428         answer full-inference queries, resulting in highly inflated performance, as shown in Table 2.
429         However, they can be used to evaluate the performance of hybrid models , such as QTO (Bai
430         et al., 2023), FIT (Yin et al., 2024), and CQD-Hybrid, that explicitly exploit existing triples
431         to predict the query answers.

      In this section, we answer to the following question: (RQ3) How can we construct a set of CQA
452
      benchmarks that let us measure truly challenging queries? To do so, we present a new set of CQA
453   benchmarks based on the previously used FB15k237 and NELL995, as well as a novel one we design
454   out of temporal knowledge graph ICEWS18 to question the current way to split the original KG G
455   into Gtrain and Gtest . Then, we evaluate current SoTA methods on these new benchmarks as to
456   establish a strong baseline for future works.
457
      Building new CQA benchmarks. We generate our benchmarks to comprise only full-inference
458
      question answer pairs, filtering out all partial-inference (and trivial) ones from the evaluation. To
459   this end, we modify the algorithm of Ren et al. (2020) to ensure that no training links are present
460   in the reasoning trees of any (q, t) pair we generate. To raise the bar of “complexity” in CQA, we
461   introduce two query types that, in their full-inference versions, are harder than simpler types by
462   design. Specifically, we design “4p” queries, for a sequential path made of four links, i.e.,
463                 ?T : ∃V1 , V2 , V3 .(a1 , r1 , V1 ) ∧ (V1 , r2 , V2 ) ∧ (V2 , r3 , V3 ) ∧ (V3 , r4 , T ), (4p)
464
      and “4i”, which represents the intersection of the target entity sets defined over four links, i.e.
465
466
                            ?T : (a1 , r1 , T ) ∧ (a2 , r2 , T ) ∧ (a3 , r3 , T ) ∧ (a4 , r4 , T ).            (4i)
467   We name the harder versions of FB15k237 and NELL995, FB15k237+H and NELL995+H, each com-
468   prising 50.000 full-inference test and validation (q, t) pairs for all query types apart from 1p queries,
469   for which we keep the same set of the old benchmark (this being already full-inference), and for the
470
      new types 4p and 4i, for which we have 10.000 of them. More details in App. C.
471   Non-uniform at random splits. For FB15k237+H and NELL995+H, we keep the existing data splits
472   of Gtrain and Gtest . However, to evaluate the impact of this artificial splitting process, we adopt a
473   more realistic one for ICEWS18+H, where Gtrain contains present links and we might want to predict
474   the future links contained in Gtest . To this end, we leverage the temporal information in ICEWS18
475   by (1) ordering the links based on their timestamp; (2) removing the temporal information, thus
476
      obtaining normal triples; and (3) selecting the train set to be the first temporally-ordered 80% of
      triples, the valid the next 10%, and the remaining to be the test split. If the same fact appears with
477
      multiple temporal information, we retain only the link with the earliest timestamp. For detailed
478
      statistics about the splits please refer to App. E.

481   re-used the pre-trained models used for the experiments in Sec. 4, while for the newly created
      ICEWS18+H we trained GNN-QE, ConE, and the link predictor used in CQD and QTO from scratch.
482
      As this is not necessary for ULTRAQ, being a zero-shot neural link prediction applicable to any KG,

      we re-used the checkpoint provided by the authors. Hyperparameters of all models are in App. D.2.

      7    C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we revisit CQA on KGs and reveal that the “good” performance of SoTA approaches
527
      predominantly comes from answers that can be reduced to easier types (Table 2), the vast major-
528   ity of which boiling down to single link prediction (Table 1). We also propose an hybrid solver,
529   CQD-Hybrid, that by combining classical graph matching approaches with neural query answer-
530   ing explicitly reduces query answer pairs to easier query types when existing links are available
531   and therefore can surpass SoTA on old benchmarks. We then created a set of new benchmarks
532   FB15k237+H,NELL995+H and ICEWS18+H that only consider full-inference queries and are much
533   more challenging for current SoTA approaches. We consider them to be a stepping stone towards
534   benchmarking truly complex reasoning with ML models.
535   However, both old and our new benchmarks only consider queries with bindings of single target
536   variables. While this reflects an important class of queries on real-world KGs, many real-world
537   queries require bindings to multiple target variables (i.e. answer tuples). We plan to extend our
538   current study of the “real hardness” of CQA benchmarks to queries involving negation (Zhang et al.,
539   2021), and to other popular settings in neural query answering, such as inductive scenarios (Galkin
      et al., 2022) where some entities and/or relations are unseen during the test stage.
