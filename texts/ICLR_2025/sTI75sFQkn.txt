011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Modeling brain dynamic functional connectivity (dFC) patterns from functional
014            Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) data is of paramount importance in neu-
015
               roscience and medicine. Recently, many graph neural networks (GNN) models
               in conjunction with transformers or recurrent neural networks (RNNs) have been
016
               proposed and shown great potential for modeling dFC patterns in terms of pat-
017
               tern recognition and prediction performance. Although fruitful, several issues still
018            hinder further performance improvement of these methods, such as neglecting the
019            intrinsic brain modularity mechanism, and the interpretable state information of
020            dFC patterns. To tackle these limitations, we propose dFCExpert to learn effective
021            representations of dFC patterns in fMRI data with modularity experts and state ex-
022            perts. Particularly, using the GNN and mixture of experts (MoE), the modularity
023            experts characterize the brain modularity organization in the graph learning pro-
024            cess by optimizing multiple experts, with each expert capturing brain nodes with
025            similar functions (in the same neurocognitive module); and the state experts ag-
026
               gregate temporal dFC features into a set of distinctive connectivity states by a soft
               prototype clustering methods, where the states can support different brain activi-
027
               ties or are affected differently by brain disorders, thus revealing insights for inter-
028
               pretability. Experiments on two large-scale fMRI datasets demonstrate the superi-
029            ority of our method over known alternatives, and the learned dFC representations
030            show improved explainability and hold promise to improve clinical diagnosis.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The human brain is a dynamic network system that generates complex spatiotemporal dynamics of
036   brain activity. Analyzing such dynamics holds promising to provide insights into the brain’s func-
037   tional organization and its relationship with human cognition (Greicius, 2008; Wang et al., 2007),
038   behaviors (Smith et al., 2015), and brain disorders (Greicius, 2008; Wang et al., 2007). Among
039
      many in-vivo brain imaging techniques, functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) is a pow-
      erful means that models the spatiotemporal patterns of brain activity by measuring fluctuations in
040
      blood-oxygen level-dependent (BOLD) signals (Matthews & Jezzard, 2004). Since brain activity
041
      exhibits strong spatial correlations, the BOLD signals generally are summarized into a collection of
042   pre-defined brain regions (ROIs), and the pairwise correlations between those ROIs are referred as
043   functional connectivity (FC), which has emerged as a key tool for understanding the brain function.
044
045
      Based on the FC, the brain can be modeled as a functional network using graph theory approaches,
      with a set of ROIs as the network nodes and the connectivity strengths of FC as edges. Thus,
046
      due to this graph-structured nature of the brain, many recent methods have employed graph neural
047
      networks (GNNs) to learn intricate brain network representations, and then explore tasks such as
048   decoding human traits or diagnosing diseases (Li et al., 2021; Kawahara et al., 2017; Kan et al.,
049   2022). Generally, these models have two distinct lines: static FC and dynamic FCs methods. The
050   static-FC methods measure the FC between nodes based on the entire fMRI scan, i.e., the full time
051   series (Ktena et al., 2017; 2018; Kim & Ye, 2020). However, these studies fail to take advantage
052   of the dynamic properties of the FCs (fluctuate over time), which are considered essential for cap-
053   turing the brain’s evolving states. Differently, the dynamic-FCs methods split the whole fMRI time
      series into temporal segments and quantify the FC based on each segment so that time-varying FC

075   The first issue is that most GNN methods for brain functional network analysis neglect the inherent
076
      brain modularity mechanism, resulting in suboptimal brain graph representation learning. Theoret-
      ical and empirical studies have revealed that the human brain works as a modular system with spe-
077
      cialized cognitive/topological modules, each consisting of brain nodes (ROIs) tightly connected and
078
      together responsible for certain functions (Sporns & Betzel, 2016; Bertolero et al., 2015), as shown
079   in Figure 1(a). However, known GNN-based methods process all brain nodes similarly (share the
080   same aggregation mechanism), regardless of the differences in the node features, which hinder them
081   from capturing modularity specific features. The second issue is that known studies also ignore the
082   interpretable dFC state patterns. The dFC measures can capture different dynamic states of the brain
083   by clustering dFC measures of temporal segments of fMRI scans. Therefore, the dFC states can
084   compactly describe and summarize how the brain FCs vary temporally (Figure 1(b)), and provide
085   interpretable information. Studies of brain disorders have shown that disease-specific alterations
086   only happen on some of the dynamic states (Damaraju et al., 2014), indicating that capturing dFC
087
      states can potentially improve the detection of functional brain changes caused by brain disorders.
088   To tackle these issues, we propose a novel GNN-based dFC learning framework called dFCExpert,
089   aiming to enhancing the representation learning of dynamic functional connectome of fMRI data.
090   Concretely, the framework consists of modularity experts and state experts, specifically designed
091   following the brain modularity characteristic and the dFC state patterns. The modularity experts are
092   the conjunction of GNN and Mixture of Experts (MoE) for learning brain graph features of each
093
      FC segment, which characterize the brain modularity organization in the graph learning process by
      optimizing multiple aggregation experts at each graph layer, with each expert focusing on specific
094
      subsets of nodes with akin behaviors and interactions. On top of the learned graph representations
095
      from modularity experts, the state experts aggregate the temporal features of dFCs into a smaller set
096   of distinctive states based on a soft prototype clustering method, where each state has similar FC
097   patterns and reflects different activities of the dFCs related to human behaviors or brain diseases.
098   Finally, the clustering-derived state features are used for task prediction. With the modularity and
099   state experts, our dFCExpert explores two novel strategies for learning-based fMRI analysis (i.e., the
100   brain modularity and dynamic state mechanisms in neuroscience), which presents a first-of-its-kind
101   method for learning informative and explainable features for dFC patterns from fMRI data.
102   We evaluate the performance of dFCExpert on two large fMRI datasets: the Human Connectome
103   Project (HCP) (Van Essen et al., 2013) and the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD)
104   (Casey et al., 2018). Across the sex classification and cognitive intelligence prediction tasks, dFC-
105   Expert significantly outperforms known methods and achieves state-of-the-art performance. We
106   comprehensively analyze individual components of dFCExpert and conduct ablation studies to sub-
107   stantiate our modeling choices. Visualization results show that our modularity experts can specialize
      in particular brain modules and the state experts present interpretable evidence for prediction tasks.

      Dynamic Brain Connectome Learning              To capture the time-varying FC patterns of the brain,
116   one very beginning method is based on the spatio-temporal graph convolutional network (ST-GCN)
117   (Gadgil et al., 2020), which incorporates spatio-temporal convolution to model the non-stationary
118   nature of functional connectivity. Then, many methods follow a GNN-RNN pipeline to learn graph-
119   level features for each FC segment with the GNNs and then capture temporal FC patterns with
120   recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers. Particularly, STAGIN (Kim et al., 2021) utilizes
121   GNNs with spatial attention and transformers with temporal attention to model the dynamics of brain
122   networks. DynDepNet (Campbell et al., 2022) proposes to learn the time-varying structures of fMRI
123   data through a dynamic graph structure learning method. NeuroGraph (Said et al., 2023) presents a
124
      collection of graph-based neuroimaging datasets and has systematically studied the effectiveness of
      various GNN designs on dynamic brain graph data. Although these methods have demonstrated the
125
      potential to capture the dynamics of brain connectivity, they ignore the inherent brain modular struc-
126
      tures and are not equipped to capture dynamic state patterns of dFCs, which impedes the provision
127   of insights into the functional organization and connectivity states of the brain.
128
129   Brain Modularity Organization            The human brain operates as a modular system with many
130
      cognitive/topological modules, and brain regions (ROIs) in the same modules are often densely con-
      nected and tend to have similar functions. For example, salience network (SN) and default mode
131
      network (DMN) are two crucial neurocognitive modules in the brain, where SN mainly detects inter-
132
      nal or external stimuli and coordinates the brain’s response to those stimuli, and DMN is responsible
133   for self-related cognitive functions (Sporns & Betzel, 2016; Bertolero et al., 2015). A few existing
134   methods have incorporated the brain modular organization in the brain graph representation learning
135   process. BRAINNETTF (Kan et al., 2022) designs a novel readout function to take advantage of the
136   modular-level similarities between ROIs, where the graph-level embeddings are pooled from clus-
137   ters of functionally similar nodes. MSGNN (Wang et al., 2023) proposes a modularity-constrained
138   GNN to learn graph features, and it constrains the node embeddings into 3 neurocognitive modules
139   (i.e., central executive network, SN, DMN) by encouraging similarity between node-level embed-
140   dings in the same module. However, Since these methods only consider the brain modularity at a
141
      late stage of their graph models, all brain network nodes are still similarly processed in each graph
      layer (i.e., all nodes share the same aggregation mechanism). Such a strategy might be subopti-
142
      mal for effectively learning representations of nodes belonging to distinct brain modules. Thus, we
143
      propose modularity experts, the conjunction of GNN and MoE, to imitate the brain’s modular or-
144   ganization, which comprise multiple aggregation experts at each graph layer and brain nodes with
145   similar functions are guided towards the same experts during training.
146
147
      Dynamic FC States          The dynamic brain states are a small number of unique patterns of dFC
      measures. To extract interpretable information from the dFC patterns, neuroscience researches often
148
      obtain dynamic FC states by summarizing the dFC patterns into a smaller set of connectivity states,
149
      which can represent patterns of connectivity that repetitively occur during the fMRI acquisition
150   (Preti et al., 2017). Existing studies typically cluster the dFC measures into different states using
151   clustering methods (e.g., k-means clustering) and then carry out statistic analyses to explore statis-
152   tical relation between the states and biological or behavior measures of interest (Damaraju et al.,
153   2014). However, such a two-step strategy is not able to effectively identify informative dFC states
154   since features of dFC graphs used for the identification of dFC states and the statistical analyses are
155   unnecessarily the same. In contrast, our state experts are learning-based, and learn distinctive dFC
156   states and informative representations of dFC graphs in an end-to-end fashion.
157   Expert Models         The concept of Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Jacobs et al., 1991) has a long
158   history as a machine learning technique that employs multiple expert layers, each specializing in

160   MoEs has facilitated its application in various domains, such as multi-modal learning (Akbari et al.,
161   2024; Mustafa et al., 2022), vision (Jain et al., 2024; Riquelme et al., 2021), and machine translation
      (Shen et al., 2019). When it comes to graph classification, TopExpert (Kim et al., 2023) has adopted

      Dynamic Graph Definition          To construct dynamic FC graphs from fMRI scans, we first use a
209
      brain atlas to convert the 4D fMRI data into a time-course matrix P ∈ RN ×Tmax , which is extracted
210   by taking the mean values of fMRI signals within each region of the brain atlas, containing N brain
211   network nodes (ROIs) in each timepoint. After that, following the sliding-window approach, the
212   time-course matrix is divided into T = ⌊Tmax − Γ/S⌋ temporal segments with a window length of
213   Γ and a stride size of S. We compute FC matrix by calculating Pearson correlation coefficients be-
214   tween time series of pairwise ROI for each of T segments, resulting in Xt ∈ RN ×N (t = 1, . . . , T ).
215   As shown in (Said et al., 2023), the correlation matrices can be informative node features, where
      the features for i-th node are elements of the i-th row in Xt for segment t. A binary adjacent

222   3.2.1    OVERVIEW
223
224
      As illustrated in Figure 2, dFCExpert consist of modularity experts and state experts. Taking the
      dynamic FC graphs as input, the modularity experts learn brain graph features for each FC segment
225
      by combing GNN and MoE, with the goal of characterizing the brain modularity mechanism. On
226
      top of the modularity experts, the state experts adaptively group the temporal graph features into
227   distinctive states based on a soft prototype clustering method, where expressive state features can be
228   learned by assigning soft clusters to the temporal graph features. Finally, the learned state features
229   with an MLP layer are used to predict a specific task, such as predicting sex in a classification setting
230   or intelligence measure in a regression setting. Formally, the goal of our dFCExpert is to train a
231   neural network f : (G1 , . . . , GT ) → hstate , where Gt = {At , Xt }(t = 1, . . . , T ) is the sequence
232   of our constructed FC graphs with T segments and hstate ∈ RK×D is the output feature of the state
233   experts. Going one step further, we formulate f = m ◦ s as a composition of modularity experts m
234   and state experts s, where m learns the brain graph representations hmodularity = (hG1 , . . . , hGT )
235
      for each of T segments and s aggregates these temporal graph features into the state feature hstate :
236              m : (G1 , . . . , GT ) → (hG1 , . . . , hGT ),       s : (hG1 , . . . , hGT ) → hstate .   (3)

      Following, we will elaborate the proposed modularity experts and state experts.

292   3.2.3    S TATE E XPERTS
293
294   The state experts follow the typical dFC analysis in neuroscience to adaptively group the temporal
295
      graph features hmodularity into a small number of states, each reflecting different dynamic states
      of the dFCs related to human behaviors or brain diseases. From another respective, it is also much
296
      easier to capture the FC dynamics from a smaller set of FC states, and it can simplify the model
297
      and reduce the complexity of the data. Specifically, we implement the state experts based on a soft
298   prototype clustering method, where distinctive dFC states are characterized by a prototype gating
299   and embedding projection as illustrated in Figure 2. The prototype gating introduces K trainable
300   prototype centroids U = [u1 , . . . , uK ], each having D dimensions (U ∈ RK×D ), to adaptively
301   learn feature centers of each state. Given the learned graph features hmodularity = (hG1 , . . . , hGT ),
302   the prototype gating first calculates the score Ptk for assigning segment t to state k by a Softmax
303   projection:

      from the ABCD BIDS Community Collection (ABCC) (Feczko et al., 2021). As in (Keller et al.,
      2023), we exclude participants with incomplete data, excessive head motion, or fewer than 600 re-
340
      maining timepoints after motion censoring. Finally, after quality control, we use the resting state
341
      fMRI scans of 6,165 children, with varied numbers of time points ranging from 626 to 3516.
342
343   Both the datasets are publicly available with identification information anonymized. We construct 5
344   splits (stratified) for both of the HCP and ABCD datasets with a ratio of (train : validation : test) = (7
345
      : 1 : 2), and report the average results across the 5 splits (standard deviation is given in Appendix E).
346   Targets and Metrics         We chose classification of sex and prediction of cognitive intelligence
347   (“fluid intelligence” for HCP, and “general cognition” for ABCD) as the evaluation tasks, where the
348   former is a binary classification problem, and the latter is a regression task. These two tasks allow
349   to explore fundamental brain-biology and brain-cognition associations. For the sex classification
350   task, accuracy (ACC) and AUC (Area Under ROC Curve) were used as the evaluation metrics. For
351
      the regression task, the regression targets are z-normalized, and Mean Squared Error (MSE) and
      correlations (CORR) between the measured and predicted values were used to evaluate the model.
352
353   Implementation Details         Our method was implemented using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019)
354   and trained on an NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80 GB memory. We set the number of MoE-GIN
355   layers L = 3, and embedding dimension D = 256. For the dFC graph construction, we used the
356   window length of Γ = 50 and window stride of S = 3, which capture the FC within 36 seconds
357
      every 2.16 seconds, following the common protocol of the sliding-window based dFC analyses
      (Zalesky & Breakspear, 2015; Preti et al., 2017). As in (Kim et al., 2021), we also randomly chose
358
      600 time points for computing the dFC graphs at each training step. This procedure mitigated the
359
      computational and memory overhead while augmenting the training data. For testing, we utilized the
360   entire available time-course matrix P ∈ RN ×Tmax for dFC graph construction and model evaluation.
361   We train the sex classification and cognitive intelligence regression tasks with cross-entropy and
362   MSE losses, respectively. More implementation details are described in Appendix D.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
492
493
      We developed a novel method, dFCExpert, for learning effective representations of dFC measures,
      consisting of the modularity experts and state experts to mirror the brain modularity organization and
494
      dFC states that have been extensively investigated in fMRI studies of the functional neuroanatomy,
495
      brain development and aging, as well as brain disorders but less exploited in the machine learning
496   community of functional brain networks. As demonstrated by the extensive experimental results
497   on two large scale fMRI datasets, the modularity experts automatically routed network nodes with
498   similar brain activities to the same experts, promoting specialization of each expert for learning ef-
499   fective representations of dFC measures, and the state experts grouped the learned temporal graph
500   features into distinctive states, with each having similar dFC patterns, facilitating effective charac-
501   terization of the temporal dynamics of the brain functional networks related to different brain states
502   or biological characteristics. These results have demonstrated not only the superior performance of
503   dFCExpert over state-of-the-art alternative methods but also its enhanced interpretability.

683   The modularity Experts are implemented following Neurograph (Said et al., 2023) with a MoE-GIN
684   architecture that incorporates residual connections and concatenates hidden representations obtained
685
      from aggregation function at each layer. These combined representations with an MLP layer are used
      for dimension reduction, yielding hmodularity = (hG1 , . . . , hGT ). We use 3 layers of MoE-GIN,
686
      and the embedding dimension in MoE-GIN is 128. For each graph t, the MoE-GIN is shared. Then,
687
      hmodularity is aggregated by the state experts to form hstate ∈ RK×D . Each state feature with a
688   shared MLP layer is used for final prediction and we average the results of each state as the final
689   result. It is worth noting that the state experts are not exactly the same as MoEs.

791   F.3    A NALYSIS FOR N UMBER OF PARAMETERS
792
793   One may argue that the performance gain of our modularity expert is due to the increasing model
794   parameters, since each expert has with its own trainable parameters. This could be one of the reasons,
795
      but the MoE concept originally is designed to improve the model capacity, and it is also a good way
      to learn representations with diverse graph structures, such as the fMRI data. Further, in Table 2, we
796
      also observe that increasing the number of experts (i.e., model parameters) does not necessarily lead
797
      to performance improvements, especially on the ABCD dataset. When C = 3, the performance is
798   even lower than the baseline method on the regression task.
799
800   Additionally, we perform more experiments to validate the effectiveness of our method. Particularly,
801
      we increase the number of parameters of baseline method to reach the same level as our modularity
      experts. The difference in the number of parameters between the baseline and our modularity experts
802
      lies in the number of experts used in the graph layer. For example, if the number of experts is
803
      C = 7, the number of parameters in MoE-GIN of our method can be 7 times of those in GIN of
804   baseline method. To reach similar level for the number of parameters, we set the feature embedding
805   dimension of the GIN layers in baseline method to 384. The results are illustrated in Table 8.
806   We find that increasing the number of parameters can improve the performance to some extent
807   (”Baseline(384)”), but our modularity scheme still performs better, especially on the regression task.
808   This shows that the performance gain of our method is not entirely from the increased parameters,
809   but also from the modularity scheme to characterize the brain modularity in the graph learning
      process, thus effectively learning graph representations.
