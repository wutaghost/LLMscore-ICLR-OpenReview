011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Significant advances have been made in the sampling efficiency of diffusion mod-
014            els, driven by Consistency Distillation (CD), which trains a student model to
015            mimic the output of a teacher model at an earlier timestep. However, we found that
016
               the learning complexity of the student model varies significantly across different
               timesteps, leading to suboptimal performance in consistency models. To address
017
               this issue, we propose the Curriculum Consistency Model (CCM), which stabi-
018
               lizes and balances the learning complexity across timesteps. We define the distil-
019            lation process as a curriculum and introduce Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR)
020            as a metric to quantify the difficulty of each step in this curriculum. By incorpo-
021            rating adversarial losses, our method achieves competitive single-step sampling
022            Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) scores of 1.64 on CIFAR-10 and 2.18 on Im-
023            ageNet 64x64. Moreover, our approach generalizes well to both Flow Matching
024            models and diffusion models. We have extended our method to large-scale text-
025            to-image models, including Stable Diffusion XL and Stable Diffusion 3.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The development of generative models has become a prominent research focus in the field of deep
031   learning. Variational autoencoders (VAEs) Kingma (2013) are preferred for their ease of training,
032   but they frequently experience posterior collapse during image generation, leading to blurred results.
033
      Generative adversarial networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014), in contrast, can generate high-
      quality images, but the instability of their training process remains a significant challenge. Recently,
034
      diffusion models Ho et al. (2020),Song et al. (2020),Song et al. (2021) have received attention for
035
      their ability to produce high-quality images, but despite this, their performance in sampling effi-
036   ciency is not satisfactory and often requires a lot of functional evaluation. Compared to diffusion
037   models, Flow Matching (FM) Lipman et al. (2023) is a simulation-free method with more determin-
038   istic trajectories, making it a potentially more robust and stable alternative for training generative
039   models. However, FM still requires multiple function evaluations to generate high-quality images.
040
      With the introduction of the Consistency Models (CM) Song et al. (2023), researchers have shifted
041
      their focus to distillation methods to enhance sampling efficiency. CM constrains any point on the
042   trajectory to the same solution by self-consistency, thus reducing the number of function evaluations.
043   Latent diffusion models (LCM) Rombach et al. (2022) use consistency constraints in the latent space
044   and extend the models to high-resolution text-to-image synthes. Consistency Trajectory Models
045   (CTM) Kim et al. (2023) further allows unlimited traversal along the probability flow Ordinary
046   Differential Equations (ODEs) between arbitrary starting and ending points during diffusion process.
047
      As shown in Figure 1a, at timestep t (where t ∈ [0, 1), a common approach in Consistency Distilla-
048   tion (CD) is to encourage the student model to mimic the output of the teacher model at timestep u
049   (where u ∈ (t, 1]). However, we found that the learning complexity of the student model is highly
050   unstable across different timesteps, leading to unsatisfactory semantic structure and poor text-image
051   alignment in the consistency model. We analyze learning complexity in Figure 2, where we quan-
052   tify the learning complexity by calculating the Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) between the
053   student and teacher outputs at different timesteps. The results indicate that the learning complexity
      for student model increases gradually as t progresses from smaller values (corresponding to near-

198   3     P ROBLEM A NALYSIS
199
200   In generative models based on denoising, the varying levels of noise in the input can lead to different
201   signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) during the denoising process, as discussed in Karras et al. (2022); Hang
202   et al. (2023). Consequently, at different training steps, the difficulty that generative models learn
203   varies, which in turn affects the model’s convergence rate and the quality of the generated results.
204   The core of the learning complexity lies in the magnitude of the difference between the model’s
205   predicted results and the ground truth. Inspired by this phenomenon, we conducted an in-depth
206   examination of the learning complexity during the consistent model learning process by comparing
207
      the outputs of the student model with those of the teacher model.
208   In this article, we propose using Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) to access learning complexity,
209   as PSNR is widely used to measure the difference between a denoised image and its original coun-
210   terpart. Specifically, given the outputs of the student model, xtarget , and those of the teacher model,
211   xest , PSNR is calculated using the following formula:
212                                                         MAX2
213                                    δ = 10 log10 (                        ).                              (12)
                                                        MSE(xest , xtarget )
214
215   A high PSNR means little difference between xtarget and xest with low learning complexity, and
      vice versa.

      We conduct experiments on both diffusion-based and FM-based models (SDXL Podell et al. (2024),
217   SD3 Esser et al. (2024), OTCFM Tong et al. (2023)) and select 3 classic datasets (CIFAR-10, Im-

219   ensure reliability and robustness. The mean and variance of PSNR between the student and teacher
220   model outputs on t are shown in Figure 2. We observe that the PSNR value consistently increases
221   as t progresses from 0 to 1, indicating a gradual reduction in the model’s learning complexity. This
222   aligns with our intuition: when t is near 0, the PSNR is typically around 30, as the input is heavily
223   mixed with noise, leading to high learning complexity. At this stage, the model is prone to confu-
224   sion, causing instability and slow convergence. Conversely, when t approaches 1, the PSNR usually
225
      exceeds 50, indicating that the learning complexity is too low, resulting in reduced learning effi-
      ciency. We argue that this instability and inefficiency hinder the overall learning process of the CM
226
      model.
227
228   We further explore the effect of distillation step l = u − t, and present the results in Figure 3.
229   The value of l typically serves as a hyperparameter in the CM model, and greatly influences the
230   effectiveness of the model’s learning. In Figure 3, it can be observed that different values of l yield
231
      consistent results across timesteps.
232   Can we mitigate this imbalance in learning complexity to enhance the effectiveness of CM learning?
233   In this paper, we attempt to present a feasible solution by proposing an adaptive method named the
234   Curriculum Consistency Model (CCM) which will be elaborated in the following section.

      4.1   C URRICULUM C ONSISTENCY M ODEL
239
240
      Our goal is to design an algorithm that ensures a stable and balanced learning complexity for the
241   model under different noise intensities and at various training iterations. To achieve this, we should
242   see further when clear, thus, we propose the Curriculum Consistency Model (CCM). CCM incor-
243   porates three key designs, which are 1. A reliable metric for measuring the difficulty of learning,
244   2. Dynamic adjustment of learning objectives based on the difficulty of learning, and 3. Multi-step
245   iterative generation to ensure the quality of learning objectives.
246
247   Measuring the difficulty of learning. We directly use PSNR to measure the learning complexity
248   according to Eq. 12. We have shown the stability and generaliability of PSNR across different
249   datasets, different noise intensity and different training periods in Section 3.
250
251   Dynamic adjustment of learning objectives. In order to adjust the learning complexity, we
252   change the output of teacher model xtarget . At each training step, we cycle between estimating the
253   learning complexity and modifying u until the learning complexity exceeds a certain fixed value. At
254   different values of t and during various training steps, we may obtain different values of u, showing
255   the adaptive nature of CCM.
256
257   Multi-step iterative generation. There are various methods for generating xu . A straightforward
258   approach is to estimate u directly from t without regard for the magnitude of the difference between
259   u and t. However, CCM may select a u that is significantly greater than t to ensure a stable learning
260   complexity, which could lead to the teacher model making inaccurate predictions due to a large
261
      timestep size. Consequently, this may result in the student model learning targets that are vague or
      inaccurate. Therefore, we propose a multi-step iterative generation method where the teacher model
262
      will iterate one step forward each time until the estimated model difficulty meets the requirements,
263
      which are currently unknown.
264
265   For clarity, we have written the CCM algorithm’s procedure in pseudocode and presented it in Algo1.

502   6   R ELATED W ORKS
503
504   Diffusion model (DM). DMs have become a leading approach in high-fidelity image generation
505
      Rombach et al. (2022). Recent work focuses on improving sample quality Ho et al. (2020), opti-
      mizing density estimation Song et al. (2021), and accelerating the sampling process. Some studies
506
      explore the underlying mechanisms and design space of DMs, while others scale up DMs for text-
507
      conditioned image synthesis Podell et al. (2024) or improve sampling efficiency through methods in
508   the latent spaceSong et al. (2020).
509
510   Consistency models (CM) The consistency model Song et al. (2023) represents a new family of
511
      generative models that can be trained either via distillation or without teacher models, often surpass-
      ing diffusion models in performance. The Consistency Trajectory Model (CTM) Kim et al. (2023)
512
      innovatively introduces trajectory consistency, offering a flexible framework for training. Recent
513
      multistep consistency models propose splitting ODE trajectories for improved consistency learning
514   Wang et al. (2024).
515
516   Flow Matching (FM) Flow Matching (FM) learns a vector field that generates an ODE for a desired
517
      probability path, without requiring computationally intensive simulations Lipman et al. (2023). This
      flexibility has led to various efforts to improve trajectory properties, particularly straightness, which
518
      enables efficient simulation with fewer steps. Methods like Multisample FM Pooladian et al. (2023)
519
      and Minibatch OT Tong et al. (2023) aim to straighten trajectories through optimal transport plans,
520   but these approaches are computationally prohibitive. Rectified Flow Liu et al. (2022) and Optimal
521   FM offer alternatives.
522
523
524
      7   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this article, we introduce the use of PSNR to measure the difficulty in the CM learning process and
      have discovered that the distribution of difficulty is highly imbalanced under different noise intensi-
527
      ties. To alleviate this issue, we propose Curriculum Consistency Model (CCM), an efficient method
528
      for training models based on Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). We design an adap-
529   tive noise schedule to maintain the consistency of curriculum difficulty and verify the rationality and
530   validity of the design. By incorporating adversarial losses, our method achieves comparable single-
531   step sampling Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) results on CIFAR-10 (1.64) and ImageNet64x64
532   (2.18). More importantly, our approach is not limited to FM, it works on diffusion models as well
533   and we have successfully extended the proposed method to large-scale models, such as Stable Dif-
534   fusion XL and Stable Diffusion 3. We hope that our paper will inspire greater attention to the issue
535   of difficulty in the CM learning process and attract more researchers to engage in related research
536   questions, such as dynamic PSNR thresholds, sampling probabilities of t, and so on.
