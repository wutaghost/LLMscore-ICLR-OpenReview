011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Temporal action segmentation aims to classify the action category of each frame
014            in untrimmed videos, primarily using RGB video and skeleton data. Most exist-
015            ing methods adopt a two-stage process: feature extraction and temporal modeling.
016
               However, we observe significant limitations in their spatio-temporal modeling: (i)
               Existing temporal modeling modules conduct frame-level and action-level inter-
017
               actions at a fixed temporal resolution, which over-smooths temporal features and
018
               leads to blurred action boundaries; (ii) Skeleton-based methods generally adopt
019            temporal modeling modules originally designed for RGB video data, causing a
020            misalignment between extracted features and temporal modeling modules. In this
021            paper, we propose a novel Interaction-based framework for Action segmentation
022            (InterAct) to address these issues. Firstly, we propose multi-scale frame-action
023            interaction (MFAI) to facilitate frame-action interactions across varying temporal
024            scales. This enhances the model’s ability to capture complex temporal dynam-
025            ics, producing more expressive temporal representations and alleviating the over-
026            smoothing issue. Meanwhile, recognizing the complementary nature of different
027
               spatial modalities, we propose decoupled spatial modality interaction (DSMI). It
               decouples the modeling of spatial modalities and applies a deep fusion strategy
028
               to interactively integrate multi-scale spatial features. This results in more dis-
029
               criminative spatial features that are better aligned with the temporal modeling
030            modules. Extensive experiments on six large-scale benchmarks demonstrate that
031            InterAct significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on both RGB-based
032            and skeleton-based datasets across diverse scenarios.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Understanding human actions in videos is critical for various real-world applications, including
038   surveillance (Luo et al., 2019), assistive rehabilitation (Filtjens et al., 2020), interactive robotics
039   (Kenney et al., 2009), and virtual reality (Sudha et al., 2017). These applications require the analysis
040   of long, untrimmed videos, which has motivated extensive research into the task of temporal action
041   segmentation (TAS) (Farha & Gall, 2019; Li et al., 2021b; Ishikawa et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022;
042   Behrmann et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023). The goal of TAS is to classify each video
043   frame and segment videos into distinct, non-overlapping action segments.
044   Recent frame-action interaction strategies (Lu & Elhamifar, 2024) have achieved significant progress
045   in this task. However, we observe that these methods tend to over-smooth temporal features, which
046   in turn blur the boundaries between different action categories. Specifically, recognizing complex
047   action sequences requires the integration of both long-term and short-term information to extract
048   discriminative temporal features (Gao et al., 2021). Nevertheless, these methods rely solely on
049   frame-action modeling at a fixed temporal resolution, as shown in Figure 1(a). This makes it
050
      difficult to capture temporal dependencies across varying time scales, resulting in over-smoothed
      temporal representations that blur action boundaries. Moreover, the iterative refinement process
051
      based on the fixed temporal resolution further amplifies this smoothing effect.
052

      2014) and skeleton data (Liu et al., 2017). However, existing skeleton-based methods (Filtjens et al.,

091

092

093

094   mation to refine temporal representations. Particularly, frame-action interactions across different
095   temporal scales focus on distinct temporal semantics. By enabling information transfer across these
096   scales, MFAI learns more effective interaction patterns. As such, our InterAct can more compre-
097   hensively capture complex temporal dynamics in long action sequences. For skeleton data, inspired
098   by the complementary nature of different spatial modalities, DSMI employs a deep fusion strategy.
099   Initially, decoupled multi-scale spatial modeling is applied to data from different spatial modalities.
100   The extracted multi-scale features are then fused interactively. By adopting DSMI, the more dis-
101   criminative spatial features extracted can better capture the complex spatial relationships between
102

118   2.1   RGB- BASED T EMPORAL ACTION S EGMENTATION
119
      Most temporal action segmentation works follow a similar two-stage process: feature extraction and
120
      temporal modeling. In RGB-based TAS, the first stage typically uses pre-trained model (Carreira
121   & Zisserman, 2017) to extract I3D features from each video frame. Most research focuses on the
122   design of the temporal modeling in the second stage. Existing temporal modeling methods can
123   be categorized into three main types: frame-based methods, two-stage methods, and frame-action
124   interaction methods. Frame-based methods model temporal dependencies between frames using
125   temporal convolutional networks (Lea et al., 2017; Farha & Gall, 2019; Li et al., 2021b; Wang
126   et al., 2020; Ishikawa et al., 2021; Singhania et al., 2023) or transformers (Yi et al., 2021; Bahrami
127   et al., 2023). Although these approaches enhance temporal modeling through innovations such as
128   multi-layer dilated convolutions (Farha & Gall, 2019; Li et al., 2020) and windowed attention (Yi
129
      et al., 2021), they still struggle to capture long-range dependencies. Recently, diffusion models (Liu
      et al., 2023) have also been applied to action segmentation, but leads to higher training and inference
130
      complexity. To better model long-range dependencies, the two-stage methods (Ahn & Lee, 2021;
131
      Behrmann et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Gan et al., 2024) recognize the significance of action-
132   level modeling. These methods first learn initial frame features and predictions, then construct
133   action features based on them and further refine the predictions. However, they fail to leverage
134   the complementary information between the frame-level and action-level features. To address this
135   limitation, frame-action interaction methods (Lu & Elhamifar, 2024) conduct temporal modeling
136   at both the frame-level and action-level, enabling bidirectional information transfer between them.
137   Nevertheless, these methods apply iterative frame-action modeling at a fixed temporal resolution,
138   which over-smooths temporal features and limits the temporal modeling capability. To avoid over-
139   smoothing temporal features, we propose multi-scale frame-action interaction (MFAI). It performs
140
      temporal modeling at both the frame and action level across multiple temporal resolutions. By
      facilitating the interactions of diverse temporal semantics, it generates more comprehensive temporal
141
      representations, thereby improving the model’s capacity to capture complex temporal dynamics.
142

      2.2   S KELETON - BASED T EMPORAL ACTION S EGMENTATION
144
145   In skeleton-based TAS, most works generally adopt frame-based methods from RGB-based ap-
146   proaches for temporal modeling. The primary focus is on designing feature extraction methods. Ex-
147   isting skeleton-based feature extraction methods can be divided into two categories: cascaded spatio-
148   temporal modeling and decoupled spatio-temporal modeling. Cascaded spatio-temporal modeling
149   methods conduct single or multiple cascaded spatio-temporal interactions to extract features. Early
150   approaches, based on Farha & Gall (2019), replaced the initial stage of temporal convolutions with
151   spatio-temporal graph convolutions (Filtjens et al., 2022) or spatio-temporal attention modules (Tian
152   et al., 2023) to improve the ability to capture spatio-temporal features. To further refine spatial se-
153
      mantics, Liu et al. (2022) introduced spatial focus attention, while Tan et al. (2023) proposed a multi-
      branch transfer fusion module to model spatial dependencies. Similarly, Li et al. (2023a) enhanced
154
      spatio-temporal modeling by introducing an involving distinguished temporal graph convolution
155
      network. However, these cascaded spatio-temporal interactions tend to over-smooth the extracted
156   features and fail to capture complex spatio-temporal information effectively. To mitigate this limita-
157   tion, Li et al. (2023b) proposed a decoupled spatio-temporal modeling method. This method adopts
158   unified spatial modeling to extract spatial sub-features, which then interact with temporal features,
159   thereby avoiding cascaded spatio-temporal interactions. However, these methods commonly adopt
160   an early fusion strategy, where data from different spatial modalities are combined at the input stage
161   before spatial modeling. This diminishes the discriminative capacity of the extracted spatial features
      and hinders their alignment with the temporal modeling module. Indeed, different spatial modalities

192   In this section, we present the details of the proposed interaction-based framework, InterAct, for
193   temporal action segmentation (TAS). In Sec. 3.1, we first introduce the tasks of RGB-based TAS
194   and skeleton-based TAS, along with the pipeline of InterAct. Then, the decoupled spatial modality
195
      interaction (DSMI) and the multi-scale frame-action interaction (MFAI) are proposed in Sec. 3.2
      and Sec. 3.3, respectively. Finally, we provide details of the loss functions in Sec. 3.4.

198   3.1           P ROBLEM STATEMENT AND PIPELINE
199
200   Given a video with T frames, our goal is to identify the category for each frame Y = [y1 , . . . , yT ] ∈
201
      [1, . . . , A]T , where A is the total number of action classes. To achieve better segmentation perfor-
      mance, we propose a framework named InterAct, as shown in Figure. 2. In TAS, two primary types
202
      of data are commonly used: RGB video data and skeleton data. Due to the distinct characteristics
203
      of these data types, we employ separate frame-level encoders for feature extraction. For RGB video
204   sequences VRGB ∈ RT ×H×W , following the previous (Farha & Gall, 2019; Liu et al., 2023), we
205   extract I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017) features XI3D ∈ RT ×C , where H, W and C represent the
206   height, width and feature dimension, respectively. For skeleton sequences Vskeleton ∈ RT ×Cin ×V ,
207   we use the proposed DSMI as the frame-level encoder to extract more discriminative spatial fea-
208   tures, denoted as Xskeleton ∈ RT ×C , where Cin is the input feature dimension. Next, we apply
209   MFAI to both XI3D and Xskeleton for multi-scale frame-action interaction and temporal modeling.
210   Additionally, following Ishikawa et al. (2021), we introduce a boundary query to help the learning
211   of action boundaries, which effectively reduces over-segmentation errors.

343   4.1   DATASET
344
345   We evaluate the proposed InterAct for action segmentation on six challenging datasets covering
346   various test scenarios. These include daily cooking activities (e.g., Breakfast (Kuehne et al., 2014)
347   and 50Salads (Stein & McKenna, 2013)), competitive sports (e.g., MCFS-22 (Liu et al., 2021) and
348   MCFS-130 (Liu et al., 2021)), daily activities (e.g., PKU-MMD (Liu et al., 2017)), and typical
349
      warehousing activities (e.g., LARa (Niemann et al., 2020)).
350   Breakfast consists of 1712 third-person view videos with 48 distinct actions related to breakfast
351   preparation. 50Salads includes 50 videos in which 25 participants prepare two types of mixed sal-
352   ads. It contains 17 action classes recorded from a top-down view. MCFS-22 is a high-quality action
353   segmentation dataset with 271 long sequences of skeleton-based actions, totaling over 1.73 million
354   frames. The actions are categorized into 22 classes. MCFS-130 features more fine-grained actions
355
      in both spatial and temporal dimensions compared to MCFS-22, covering 130 action categories. It
      provides two types of data: RGB video data and skeleton data. PKU-MMD is a large-scale human
356
      action understanding dataset. It contains 1009 long continuous sequences of 52 distinct actions,
357
      recorded from three camera views with 13 subjects. Following Li et al. (2023b), we use two evalua-
358   tion protocols: cross-subject (X-sub) and cross-view (X-view). LARa is a continuous action dataset
359   involving 14 participants performing typical warehousing activities. It consist of 377 long videos
360   covering 8 action classes, captured in 3 different real-world warehousing scenarios.

      5     CONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel framework InterAct for temporal action segmentation (TAS). Un-
      like previous frame-action interaction approaches, InterAct incorporates multiple temporal resolu-
532
      tions. It performs frame-action interactions across different temporal scales. This design effectively
533
      captures temporal semantic information and mitigates the over-smoothing issues associated with
534   fixed resolution modeling. It shows excellent performance on both RGB video data and skeleton
535   data. Additionally, to address the misalignment between spatial features and temporal modules
536   in skeleton-based TAS, we decouple different spatial modalities and apply a deep fusion strategy
537   for adaptive inter-modal interactions. This approach extracts more discriminative spatial features
538   to better support temporal modeling. Our method outperforms all state-of-the-art RGB-based and
539   skeleton-based methods on six large-scale benchmark datasets across various scenarios. We believe
      that InterAct offers a unique and innovative perspective for addressing the challenges in TAS.

      In this section, we present additional quantitative and qualitative experiments on the multi-scale
753
      frame-action interaction (MFAI) for a more comprehensive analysis.
754
755   First, we conduct a more detailed analysis of the comparative results in Figure 4 and provide vi-
      sualizations of the temporal feature embeddings generated by FACT and MFAI at different stages.

756
      As shown in Figure 5, the frame-action interaction strategy employed by FACT leads to over-
757   smoothing temporal features, which blurs the boundaries between various action categories. This
758   over-smoothing effect intensifies as the model undergoes further iterations. In contrast, by incor-
759   porating multi-scale temporal semantic information, our proposed MFAI alleviates the issue of
760   over-smoothing and achieves more separable category boundaries. Notably, after the first refine-
761   ment stage, the temporal features generated by MFAI are already able to effectively distinguish
762   different action categories. This is attributed to the successful integration of action-level features
763   Q1 = [Q1a , Q1b ] and frame-level features F 1 = [Fa1 , Fb1 ] during the first refinement stage. Specif-
764   ically, the action-level features Q1 capture long-range dependencies within the action sequence,
765   guided by the coarse-grained decoder output X̂0 . Simultaneously, the frame-level features F 1 , pro-
766   duced by the multi-scale feature extractor and further refined with the assistance of Q1 , enrich the
767
      semantic information of temporal details. As a result, MFAI’s output in the first stage significantly
      outperforms that of FACT and continues to improve through subsequent refinement iterations. How-
768
      ever, as observed in the visualizations, the subsequent refinements appear less prominent, primarily
769
      because they focus on the improvement of specific local details.
770
771   Similarly, as shown in Table 7, the performance of MFAI steadily improves as frame-action in-
772   teraction modeling progresses from coarse to fine granularity. This improvement is driven by the
773
      incorporation of more comprehensive temporal semantic information, enabling the model to more
      precisely capture and distinguish subtle variations between action categories. This, in turn, enhances
774
      the model’s ability to recognize complex action sequences. However, the performance improve-
775
      ments from later refinement stages manifest less significantly than those achieved during the first
776   refinement stage.

      A.3   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
779
780   While InterAct significantly boosts the accuracy of fine-grained action segmentation, it still relies
781   on costly frame-wise label annotations. To release this limitation, we plan to explore self-supervised
