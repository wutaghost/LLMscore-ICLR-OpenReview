009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Advances in video diffusion models have unlocked the potential for realistic
012            audio-driven talking video generation. However, it is still highly challenging to
013            ensure seamless audio-lip synchronization, maintain long-term identity consis-
014            tency, and achieve natural expressions aligned with the audio in generated talk-
015            ing videos. To address these challenges, we propose Memory-guided EMOtion-
016
               aware diffusion (MEMO), an end-to-end audio-driven portrait animation approach
               to generate identity-consistent and expressive talking videos. Our approach is
017
               built around two key modules: (1) a memory-guided temporal module, which en-
018
               hances long-term identity consistency and smooth motion by developing memory
019            states that store information from all previously generated frames and guide tem-
020            poral modeling through linear attention; and (2) an emotion-aware audio module,
021            which replaces traditional cross attention with multi-modal attention to enhance
022            audio-video interaction, while detecting emotions from the audio to refine facial
023            expressions via emotion adaptive layer norm. Moreover, MEMO is trained on
024            a large-scale, high-quality dataset of talking head videos without relying on fa-
025            cial inductive biases such as face landmarks or bounding boxes. Extensive ex-
026            periments demonstrate that MEMO generates more realistic talking videos across
027
               a wide range of audio types, surpassing state-of-the-art talking video diffusion
               methods in human evaluations in terms of emotion-audio alignment, identity con-
028
               sistency and overall quality, respectively.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Audio-driven talking video generation (Prajwal et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b)
034   has gained significant attention due to its broad impact on areas like virtual avatars, digital content
035   creation, and real-time communication, offering transformative possibilities in entertainment, educa-
036   tion, and e-commerce. However, compared to text-to-video generation (Guo et al., 2023; Rombach
037   et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022) or image-to-video generation (Blattmann et al., 2023), audio-driven
038   talking video generation presents unique challenges. It requires not only generating synchronized lip
039
      movements and realistic head motions from audio, but also preserving the long-term identity consis-
      tency of the reference image and producing natural expressions that align with the emotional tone of
040
      the audio. Successfully balancing these demands while ensuring generalization across diverse audio
041
      inputs and reference images makes this task highly challenging.
042
043   Recent advances in video diffusion models (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024)
044   have enabled more realistic audio-driven talking video generation. Most existing methods use cross-
045
      attention mechanisms to incorporate audio to guide video generation and typically condition on past
      generated 2-4 frames to improve identity consistency and motion smoothness (Tian et al., 2024;
046
      Xu et al., 2024a). Sometimes, they incorporate a static emotion label to specify the emotion of the
047
      generated video (Xu et al., 2024b; Tan et al., 2024). However, these approaches face challenges
048   with audio-lip synchronization, maintaining long-term identity consistency, and achieving natural
049   expressions aligned with the audio. Cross-attention mechanisms rely on fixed audio features, limit-
050   ing audio-video interaction and coherence, while conditioning on a limited number of past frames
051   can lead to error accumulation, especially when those frames contain artifacts (cf. Figure 1). Addi-
052   tionally, using static emotion labels can result in facial expressions that fail to capture the dynamic
053   emotional shifts inherent in audio. Consequently, these methods may struggle with lip-audio syn-
      chronization, expression-audio alignment, and long-term identity preservation.

080   In this paper, we propose Memory-guided EMOtion-aware diffusion (MEMO), an end-to-end
081   audio-driven portrait animation approach. As shown in Figure 2, MEMO is built around two key
082   modules: (1) a memory-guided temporal module and (2) an emotion-aware audio module. To en-
083   sure consistent facial identity and smooth transitions across long-term videos, MEMO develops
084   a memory-guided temporal module that maintains memory states across all previously generated
085   frames. This allows the model to use long-term motion information to guide temporal modeling
086   through linear attention, resulting in more coherent facial movements and mitigating the error accu-
087
      mulation issue that may occur in existing diffusion methods (cf. Figure 1). Moreover, to improve
      audio-lip synchronization and align facial expressions with the emotional tone of the audio, MEMO
088
      introduces an emotion-aware audio module. This module replaces the traditional cross-attention au-
089
      dio module in previous diffusion methods with a more dynamic multi-modal attention mechanism,
090   enabling better interaction between audio and video during the diffusion process. By detecting sub-
091   tle emotional cues from the audio, this module further refines facial expressions through emotion
092   adaptive layer norm, enabling the generation of expressive and emotionally aligned talking videos.
093
      Extensive quantitative results and human evaluations demonstrate that our approach consistently
094
      outperforms state-of-the-art methods in overall quality, audio-lip synchronization, expression-audio
095
      alignment, identity consistency, and motion smoothness (cf. Table 1 and Figure 6). Additionally,
096   diverse qualitative results highlight MEMO’s strong generalization across various types of audio,
097   including speech, singing, rap, and multiple languages, further showcasing the effectiveness of our
098   method. Lastly, ablation studies further validate the distinct contributions of the emotion-aware
099   audio module, which significantly improves audio-lip alignment and expression naturalness, and
100   the memory-guided temporal module, which enhances long-term identity consistency and motion
101   smoothness.
102   In summary, our contributions are threefold: (1) MEMO is the first to leverage motion informa-
103   tion from all past frames to guide temporal modeling in diffusion-based talking video generation,
104   effectively improving long-term identity consistency and motion smoothness; (2) unlike previous
105   methods, MEMO dynamically detects the emotion in audio and incorporates it into audio-video in-
106   teraction, improving lip-audio synchronization and expression-audio alignment in talking videos;
107   (3) we introduce a new data processing pipeline to obtain high-quality talking head data, which is
      crucial for diffusion model training and will benefit future research in talking video generation.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Audio-driven talking head generation. Audio-driven talking head generation aims to synthe-
112   size realistic and synchronized talking videos given an audio clip and a reference image. Early
113
      approaches only focused on learning audio-lip mapping while keeping other facial attributes
      static (Suwajanakorn et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Prajwal et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2022; Yin
114
      et al., 2022). These methods could not capture comprehensive facial expressions and natural head
115
      movements. To improve realism, later research leveraged intermediate motion representations, e.g.,
116   landmark coordinates, 3D facial mesh, and 3D morphable models, and decomposed the generation
117   process into two stages, i.e., audio-to-motion and motion-to-video (Zhou et al., 2020; Sun et al.,
118   2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024). The typical issue
119   of these methods is the bottleneck of the intermediate representations, which limits the expressive-
120   ness and realism of the generated videos. Recent end-to-end methods can generate vivid portrait
121   videos (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a) by fine-tuning pre-trained text-to-video (T2V) models,
122   but they struggle to generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios and need specific modules
123   (e.g., face locator) to constrain head stability, which hinders more natural head motions. Similar
124
      issues exist in the methods that learned a specific face latent space (He et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023;
      Zhang et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024b). Furthermore, most of these methods use 2-4 past frames to
125
      generate long videos auto-regressively, which may lead to error accumulation over time and fail to
126
      preserve identity when generating long-term videos. In contrast, our work does not depend on any
127   facial inductive biases, which unlocks the possibilities for generating more expressive head motions
128   of talking videos. Moreover, our method improves long-term identity consistency and mitigates
129   error accumulation via memory-guided linear attention. Besides, unlike previous diffusion-based
130   methods that used a cross-attention mechanism to integrate audio features, our method enhances
131   the lip-audio synchronization and expression-audio alignment based on a newly developed emotion-
132   aware multi-modal diffusion. The most related concurrent work to our memory module is Loopy
133   (Jiang et al., 2024), which use a temporal segment module to model cross-clip relationships, but
134   it only considers the representative motion frames in other temporal segments. In contrast, our
135
      memory-guided temporal module allows MEMO to utilize all past frames to provide more compre-
      hensive temporal guidance for motion and identity. More related studies of diffusion models are
136
      provided in Appendix C.

142   Problem statement. Given a reference image and audio as inputs, audio-driven talking video gen-
143   eration (Prajwal et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2024) aims to output a vivid video that closely aligns with
144   the input audio and authentically replicates real human speech and facial movements. This task is
145   particularly challenging because it requires seamless audio-lip synchronization, realistic head move-
146   ments, long-term identity consistency, and natural expressions that align with the audio. Most exist-
147
      ing diffusion-based approaches (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024) struggle with
      issues such as error accumulation, inconsistent identity preservation over time, limited audio-lip
148
      synchronization, unnatural expressions, and poor generalization.

187   As illustrated in Figure 2, MEMO is an end-to-end audio-driven diffusion model for generat-
188   ing identity-consistent and expressive talking videos. Similar to previous diffusion-based ap-
189   proaches (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a), MEMO is built around two main components: a Refer-
190   ence Net and a Diffusion Net. The main contributions of MEMO lie in two key modules within the
191   Diffusion Net: the memory-guided temporal module (cf. Section 4.1), and the emotion-aware au-
192   dio module (cf. Section 4.2), which work together to achieve superior audio-video synchronization,
193
      long-term identity consistency, and natural expression generation. In addition, MEMO introduces a
      new data processing pipeline (cf. Section 4.4) for acquiring high-quality talking head videos, along
194
      with a decomposed training strategy (cf. Section 4.3) to optimize diffusion model training.

      4.1    M EMORY-G UIDED T EMPORAL M ODULE
197
198   Most existing diffusion-based approaches (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024)
199   typically generate talking videos in an auto-regressive manner, dividing the audio into clips corre-
200   sponding to 12-16 frames and using the past 2-4 generated frames to condition the generation of
201   the next video clip. They concatenate the past frame features with the current noisy latent features
202   along the temporal dimension and apply temporal self-attention to model the sequential informa-
203   tion. While this approach can model short-term dependencies, it often struggles with maintaining
204   consistency over longer sequences. If artifacts are generated in the past 2-4 conditioned frames, the
205   errors tend to accumulate as the generation progresses, resulting in visual distortions that degrade
206
      both identity consistency and audio quality (cf. Figure 1).
207   Motivated by the idea that leveraging a more complete memory of motion information, rather than
208   relying solely on the most recent 2-4 frames, can provide richer guidance for enhancing identity
209   consistency and motion smoothness, we propose a memory-guided temporal module. The key of
210   this module is memory-guided linear attention, which is designed to improve temporal coherence
211   and maintain consistent facial identity.
212   Linear Attention. Previous approaches use self-attention (Tian et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) to
213   capture temporal information across frames. However, self-attention requires storing all key-value
214   pairs, leading to increasing GPU-memory overhead as the number of memory frames grows, making
215   it impractical to use all motion information. To address this limitation, we replace self-attention with
      linear attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) and include a memory update mechanism into linear

      4.2   E MOTION -AWARE AUDIO M ODULE
265
266   Existing diffusion-based approaches (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024) rely
267   on cross-attention mechanisms to integrate audio guidance for video generation, while some meth-
268   ods (Xu et al., 2024b; Tan et al., 2024) further use static emotion labels to generate more emotionally
269   expressive talking videos. However, cross attention relies on fixed audio features, limiting the depth
      of audio-video interaction during the diffusion process; while static emotion labels cannot capture

270
      the emotional nuances in the audio, leading to facial expressions that do not align naturally with the
271   audio emotions. To address these issues, we develop a new emotion-aware audio module to improve
272   audio-lip consistency and align facial expressions with the audio emotion. As shown in Figure 4,
273   there are two key components: multi-modal attention and Emotion AdaNorm.
274
      Multi-modal attention. Our emotion-aware audio module replaces the traditional cross attention
275
      with a more dynamic multi-modal attention mechanism. Specifically, cross attention aligns video
276
      and audio information by conditioning video features v on audio features a. This approach can be
277   formalized as minimizing the loss function Lθv|a = Et,ϵ∼N (0,I) [λ(t)∥ϵθ (vt |a)−ϵ∥22 ]. In contrast, we
278   explore multi-modal attention, which jointly processes both video and audio inputs by minimizing
279   the loss function Lθva = Et,ϵ∼N (0,I) [λ(t)∥ϵθ (vt , a) − ϵ∥22 ], enabling better video-audio interaction
280   during the diffusion process.
281
      Emotion AdaNorm. We then dynamically detect audio emotions to guide audio-video interaction,
282
      using a newly trained emotion detection model. Specifically, the model is trained on a diverse dataset
283   to extract emotion e from audio (see Appendix A for more details), recognizing eight distinct emo-
284   tions: angry, disgusted, fearful, happy, neutral, sad, surprised, and others.
285   The detected emotion for each audio clip is then projected into emotion embeddings, which are
286   incorporated into each layer via adaptive layer norm to guide multi-modal attention. This process
287   results in the following emotion-conditioned loss:
288                              Lθva|e = Et,ϵ∼N (0,I) [λ(t)∥ϵθ (vt , a|e) − ϵ∥22 ].                     (5)

297   4.3   T RAINING S TRATEGY D ECOMPOSITION
298
      The model’s generative capabilities can be progressively enhanced by dividing the training process
299
      into three distinct stages, each with specific objectives.
300
301   Stage 1: Face domain adaptation. Following (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024),
302   we initialize the Reference Net and the spatial module of the Diffusion Net with the weights of SD
303
      1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022). In this stage, we adapt the Reference Net, the spatial attention modules
      of the Diffusion Net, and the original text cross-attention module to the face domain, ensuring these
304
      components capture facial features effectively.
305
306   Stage 2: Robust scale-up training. We then integrate the emotion-aware audio module and
307   memory-guided temporal module into the Diffusion Net. Initially, we perform a warm-up train-
308   ing phase, keeping the modules trained in Stage 1 fixed. After the warm-up, all modules are jointly
309
      trained using a fixed number of past frames as memory. Here, since our method generates 16 frames
      at a time, we set the number of past frames to 16 as temporal context. In this stage, we scale up
310
      the dataset to include all collected and processed data for more comprehensive training. However,
311
      even after applying our data processing pipeline (cf. Section 4.4) and manual filtering, we found
312   that some noisy data remained, making the diffusion training unstable and leading to biased model
313   optimization. To mitigate this issue, we develop a robust training strategy that filters out data points
314   with loss values suddenly exceeding a specific threshold (0.1 in our case), as the rectified flow loss
315   (cf. Eq. 1) in our method typically converges and fluctuates around 0.03.
316
      Stage 3: Dynamic past frame training. In Stage 2, we use a fixed number of 16 past frames to
317
      compute memory states. However, during inference, the audio typically spans much longer than 16
318   frames, meaning the memory must dynamically adapt to longer past frames to avoid a gap between
319   training and inference. To address this, we further introduce dynamic past frame training. During
320   each diffusion training iteration, we randomly select 16, 32, or 48 as the number of past frames,
321   allowing the model to better handle longer memory updates. One might ask why we do not use
322   values larger than 48. This is because, with our memory decay scheme, 48 past frames are sufficient
323   to generalize memory updates over longer sequences, while also keeping computation manageable.
      In this stage, we train only the audio and temporal modules, while keeping all other modules fixed.

336   4.4   DATA P ROCESSING P IPELINE
337
338   We collect a comprehensive set of open-source datasets, such as HDTF (Zhang et al., 2021b),
339   VFHQ (Xie et al., 2022), CelebV-HQ (Zhu et al., 2022), MultiTalk (Sung-Bin et al., 2024), and
340   MEAD (Wang et al., 2020b), along with additional data we collected ourselves. The total duration
341   of these raw videos exceeds 2,200 hours. However, as illustrated in Figure 13 in Appendix B, we
342
      find that the overall quality of the data is poor, with numerous issues such as audio-lip misalignment,
      missing heads, multiple heads, occluded faces by subtitles, extremely small face regions, and low
343
      resolution. Directly using these data for model training results in unstable training, poor conver-
344
      gence, and terrible generation quality.
345
346   To further obtain high-quality talking head data, we developed a dedicated data processing pipeline
347   for talking head generation. The pipeline consists of five steps: First, we perform scene transition
348
      detection and trim video clips to a length of less than 30 seconds. Second, we apply face detection,
      filtering out videos with no faces, partial faces, or multiple heads, and use the resulting bounding
349
      boxes to extract talking heads. Third, we use an Image Quality Assessment model (Su et al., 2020) to
350
      filter out low-quality and low-resolution videos. Fourth, we apply SyncNet (Prajwal et al., 2020) to
351   remove videos with audio-lip synchronization issues. Lastly, for partial data, we manually assess the
352   audio-lip synchronization and overall video quality for more accurate filtering. After completing the
353   entire pipeline, the total duration of the processed high-quality videos is approximately 660 hours.
354   We use this processed data for the second and third stages of model training in Section 4.3.

359   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
360
361   Evaluation benchmarks. We create two datasets to evaluate MEMO’s performance and general-
362   ization capabilities. We use the VoxCeleb2 (Nagrani et al., 2020) test set, which contains videos of
363   various celebrities. We select 46 individuals and sample 10 clips per person, resulting in a total of
364   460 clips. To further evaluate out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, we create an OOD dataset
365   with 300 video clips across a more diverse set of audios, backgrounds, ages, genders, languages, etc.
366   Evaluation metrics. We adopt a suite of metrics to evaluate the overall quality and audio-lip syn-
367   chronization of the generated videos. The Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) (Unterthiner et al., 2019)
368   measures the distance between the distributions of real and generated videos, providing an assess-
369   ment of overall video quality. The Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) evaluates
370   the quality of individual frames by comparing feature distributions extracted from a pre-trained
371   model. SyncNet Confidence (Sync-C) (Chung & Zisserman, 2017) measures audio-lip synchroniza-
372   tion using a pre-trained discriminator model.
373   Baselines. We compare our method against several state-of-the-art baselines, including two-stage
374   methods with intermediate representations and end-to-end audio-to-video diffusion methods. V-
375   Express (Wang et al., 2024) and EchoMimic (Chen et al., 2024) are two-stage methods using in-
376   termediate representations like landmarks, while Hallo (Xu et al., 2024a) is a recent end-to-end
377   diffusion model using hierarchical face masks to integrate audio information. More implementation
      details of MEMO are put into Appendix D.

      5.4         A BLATION S TUDIES
517
518   Effects of memory module. We evaluate the effect of memory and the role of different past frame
519   lengths as temporal guidance on video quality through human evaluations. As shown in Figure 10,
520   longer memory significantly improves temporal coherence, overall quality, motion smoothness,
521   identity consistency, and audio-lip alignment, while short motion frames leads to the worse perfor-
522   mance. This result further demonstrate the effectiveness of our memory-guided temporal module.
523   Effects of emotion guidance. By adjusting the classifier-free guidance scale, we observe variations
524   in the expressiveness of the generated faces. As shown in Figure 9, higher guidance scales lead to
525   more pronounced emotional expressions. These visualization further verifies the effectiveness of
526   our emotion-aware audio module.

      Effects of multi-modal attention. We further investigate the impact of the multi-modal attention
528   through human evaluations. Results in Figure 11 underscore the effectiveness of multi-modal atten-
529   tion over cross attention in terms of the overall video quality and lip-audio alignments.

      6      C ONCLUSION
533
534
      In this work, we present MEMO, a state-of-the-art talking video generation model. MEMO reduces
      artifacts and error accumulation in long-term video generation by introducing the memory-guided
535
      temporal module. It can generate videos with high audio-lip synchronization and natural head move-
536
      ments with our emotion-conditioned audio module. In particular, it does not need face-related in-
537   ductive biases in the model architecture, allowing it to be extended to broader applications, such as
538   talking body generation tasks. In future work, it would be interesting to explore the effectiveness of
539   Diffusion Transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023) in talking video generation.

759   A     AUDIO E MOTION D ETECTION
760
761
      To facilitate emotion guidance in talking videos, it is crucial to develop an accurate and robust
762   emotion detection module that can extract emotion labels from audio. Emotion recognition from
763   speech and music has been extensively researched. Drawing on the well-established foundations of
764   Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) and Music Emotion Recognition (MER), we aim to integrate
765   these insights into a unified module.
766
767

770   sources, we collected a large-scale dataset encompassing both speech and music segments, each
771   annotated with emotion labels. A detailed overview of the datasets used in our training process is
772   provided in Table 2. For the speech component, we sourced data from a recent Speech Emotion
773   Recognition benchmark, EmoBox (Ma et al., 2024a), which incorporates 23 datasets from various

775   RAVDESS-song (Livingstone & Russo, 2018) and MTG-Jamendo (Bogdanov et al., 2019) datasets,

813   Label merging. A major challenge in integrating different datasets is aligning their label spaces,
814
      as each dataset often features distinct emotion categories. For instance, the URDU dataset (Latif
      et al., 2018) contains only four emotion labels: happy, sad, angry, and neutral. In contrast, ASVP-
815
      ESD (Landry et al., 2020) includes 12 emotion labels, covering less common emotions such as
816
      boredom and pain. For music emotion recognition datasets like MTG-Jamendo (Bogdanov et al.,
817   2019), there are 56 mood/theme tags, not all of which correspond to emotional labels, and each
818   sample can be assigned multiple tags. These discrepancies and overlaps in category spaces across
819   different datasets present significant challenges for emotion detection.
820
      To establish a generalized and streamlined label space, we designed our module to perform an 8-
821
      class classification task, selecting labels that are both commonly recognized and easily distinguish-
822
      able: angry, disgusted, fearful, happy, neutral, sad, surprised, and others. We
823   meticulously reviewed and mapped the original labels from each dataset to fit within this new label
824   space. For instance, samples labeled as pleasure in the ASVPESD dataset were mapped to the
825   happy category due to their semantic similarity. Labels that did not clearly correspond to a specific
826   emotion were categorized under the others label.

894   To further obtain high-quality talking head data, we developed a dedicated data processing pipeline
895
      for talking head generation. The pipeline consists of five steps: First, we perform scene transition
      detection and trim video clips to a length of less than 30 seconds. Second, we apply face detection,
896
      filtering out videos with no faces, partial faces, or multiple heads, and use the resulting bounding
897
      boxes to extract talking heads. To ensure that the cropped areas encompass more than just the human
898   faces, we apply a scaling factor of 1.1 to the bounding box regions. Third, we use an Image Quality
899   Assessment model (Su et al., 2020) to filter out low-quality and low-resolution videos. We apply
900   an Image Quality Assessment (IQA) model to the first frame of the videos and find that when the
901   IQA score exceeds 40, there is a noticeable improvement in video quality. Therefore, we use an
902   IQA score of 40 as a selection criterion, but this threshold will be dynamically adjusted based on
903   the volume and quality of the data. Fourth, we apply SyncNet (Prajwal et al., 2020) to remove
904   videos with audio-lip synchronization issues. We use Sync-Confidence (Sync-C) to filter the data
905   and find that it exhibits better diversity compared to Sync-Distance (Sync-D). Specifically, for a
906
      given dataset, not all data points tend to fall within the same scoring range, as shown in Figure 14.
      Additionally, for Sync-C, we can identify a suitable threshold for filtering, which is set at a score of
907
      5 or higher. Lastly, for partial data, we manually assess the audio-lip synchronization and overall
908
      video quality for more accurate filtering. After completing the entire pipeline, the total duration of
909   the processed high-quality videos is approximately 660 hours.

      C    M ORE R ELATED S TUDIES OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
936
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) are highly expressive generative
937
      models, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in image synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell
938   et al., 2023) and video generation (Guo et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2023). Rombach et al. (2022) employ
939   a UNet architecture and generate high-resolution images in the latent space, which is extended to
940   video domains by AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023) via adding temporal attention layers. These models
941   generate images or videos based on text prompts, where the text guidance from the pre-trained text
942   encoder is introduced through cross-attention modules. In the domain of talking head, diffusion
943   models also show promising results in generation quality (He et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Wei
944   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Stypułkowski et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b), outperforming previous
945   GAN-based methods (Prajwal et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020). Instead of using text prompts, most of
946
      these diffusion-based methods condition diffusion models on image and audio embeddings extracted
      from a pre-trained image encoder and audio encoder, respectively.
947
948
949   D    M ORE I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
950
951   Both the Reference Net and the spatial module of the Diffusion Net are initialized with the weights
952   of SD 1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022). The temporal module is initialized with the motion module from
953   AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023). We add two projection modules to convert the audio embedding
954   and image embedding into the dimensions required by our attention module. The audio embedding
955
      consists of all the hidden states from the Wav2Vec 2.0 model (Baevski et al., 2020). For both the
      Reference Net and the Diffusion Net, we replace the text cross-attention with image cross-attention.
956
      We use the normalized hidden states from the Reference Net before the self-attention layers for
957
      reference attention with the hidden states in the Diffusion Net. The training videos are center-
958   cropped and resized to a resolution of 512 × 512 pixels. Across all training stages, we maintain
959   a fixed learning rate of 1e-5. We train MEMO for 15k, 500k, and 100k steps at training stage 1,
960   2, and 3, respectively. During training, emotion embeddings are randomly dropped with a dropout
961   probability of 30%, while all other conditions, including reference images, audio embeddings, past
962   frames, are dropped with a probability of 5%. At inference, we set the frame rate to 30 FPS and
963   generate 16 frames per iteration. The scale of classifier-free guidance is default to 3.5.
