009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Current adversarial attacks for evaluating the robustness of vision-language pre-
012            trained (VLP) models in multi-modal tasks suffer from limited transferability,
013            where attacks crafted for a specific model often struggle to generalize effectively
014            across different models, limiting their utility in assessing robustness more broadly.
015            This is mainly attributed to the over-reliance on model-specific features and re-
016
               gions, particularly in the image modality. In this paper, we propose an elegant yet
               highly effective method termed Meticulous Adversarial Attack (MAA) to fully ex-
017
               ploit model-independent characteristics and vulnerabilities of individual samples,
018
               achieving enhanced generalizability and reduced model dependence. MAA em-
019            phasizes fine-grained optimization of adversarial images by developing a novel re-
020            sizing and sliding crop (RScrop) technique, incorporating a multi-granularity sim-
021            ilarity disruption (MGSD) strategy. RScrop efficiently enriches the initial adver-
022            sarial examples by generating more comprehensive, diverse, and detailed perspec-
023            tives of the images, establishing a robust foundation for capturing representative
024            and intrinsic visual characteristics. Building on this, MGSD seeks to maximize
025            the embedding distance between adversarial examples and their original counter-
026            parts across different granularities and hierarchical levels within the architecture
027
               of VLP models, thereby amplifying the impact of the adversarial perturbations
               and enhancing the efficacy of attacks across every layer and component of the
028
               model. Extensive experiments across diverse VLP models, multiple benchmark
029
               datasets, and a variety of downstream tasks demonstrate that MAA significantly
030            enhances the effectiveness and transferability of adversarial attacks. A large co-
031            hort of performance studies is conducted to generate insights into the effectiveness
032            of various model configurations, guiding future advancements in this domain. The
033            source code is provided in the supplementary material.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Vision-language pre-trained (VLP) models have achieved remarkable success and serve as founda-
039
      tional models for a wide range of tasks, including information retrieval, image captioning, and visual
      question answering Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022; 2021); Yang et al. (2022). These models
040
      are typically pre-trained on large-scale unlabeled datasets using self-supervised learning and sub-
041
      sequently fine-tuned for specific downstream tasks. Given their extensive applications, it is crucial
042   to evaluate the robustness of VLP models to ensure their reliability in real-world scenarios, which
043   are often characterized by uncertainties and potential threats. A representative method for assessing
044   robustness is through adversarial attacks, where imperceptible perturbations are deliberately crafted
045   to mislead models to wrongly associate images and texts, resulting in incorrect predictions.
046
      Ensuring the transferability of adversarial attacks across different models is critical, as it is imprac-
047
      tical to craft individual attacks for every different model in real-world scenarios, especially when
048   attackers often lack access to target models. Existing methods usually enhance adversarial transfer-
049   ability by enlarging the feature distance between adversarial examples and their original counterparts
050   across different modalities Zhang et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023); He et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2023);
051   Zhang et al. (2024). Some of them also use data augmentation techniques Lu et al. (2023); Zhang
052   et al. (2024); He et al. (2023) to increase data diversity to further prevent overfitting to the target
053   model during training (a.k.a. the source model). However, the performance of adversarial examples
      produced by these methods is less effective when applied to unknown target models, where adver-

      and vulnerabilities of individual samples, as well as the over-reliance on model-specific patterns.
071
072   Transferability is particularly challenging in the context of VLP models due to two key factors: the
073   unpredictable fine-tuning process and the complexity of involving multiple modalities. VLP models
074   are usually fine-tuned for downstream tasks with task-specific optimizations, considering varying
075   datasets, objectives and other factors. The complexity of multi-modalities introduces more intricate
076
      information. Models tend to extract any available information to make decisions, even those that
      do not accurately reflect the true semantic essence of data Zhang et al. (2021b); Ilyas et al. (2019);
077
      Hendrycks et al. (2021); Qin et al. (2022). The specialized factors of VLP models amplify this and
078
      make models more prone to relying on specific features and regions to associate images and texts.
079   When these models are used as source models, attackers often generate adversarial examples that
080   place greater emphasis on model-specific features and attended regions, resulting in overfitting to
081   the source model and ultimately reducing transferability to other models.
082
      In this paper, we focus on exploiting model-independent characteristics and vulnerabilities of im-
083
      ages to guide the generation of adversarial examples, minimizing dependency on and susceptibility
084
      to the source models. A simple yet highly effective and transferable attack method is developed,
085   termed the Meticulous Adversarial Attack (MAA). MAA refines adversarial examples primarily for
086   the image modality to disrupt the understanding of image-text relationships across diverse models
087   by augmenting low-level image details. Our approach is partially grounded in a well-acknowledged
088   insight: tailored perturbations to each individual image tend to be more potent than applying uni-
089   form perturbations across all images Poursaeed et al. (2018); Naseer et al. (2019). With this in mind,
090   MAA exploits representative and fine-grained characteristics and inherent vulnerabilities of origi-
091   nal images to facilitate the generation of targeted perturbation. This approach substantially reduces
092   over-reliance on the patterns/features that are generated specifically to the source model, thereby
093
      greatly enhancing the effectiveness and transferability of adversarial examples.
094   Specifically, we introduce a novel resizing and sliding crop (RScrop) technique, seamlessly inte-
095   grated with a multi-granularity similarity disruption (MGSD) strategy. Essentially, similar to Yin
096   et al. (2023); Ganeshan et al. (2019), the MGSD strategy enlarges the feature distances between
097   adversarial examples and their original counterparts across various layers and components of the
098   model. Low-level layers and components process local regions and detailed features, while high-
099
      level layers and components capture more abstract, semantic information. However, MGSD is re-
      stricted by the fixed-size input and local region processing in existing VLP models, which cannot
100
      effectively focus on more detailed aspects and their connections. For instance, in vision transform-
101
      ers, patch embedding is learnt using non-overlapping patches, where the boundary areas between
102   adjacent patches are often ignored, resulting in the loss of crucial contextual information. This
103   limitation prevents the model from capturing fine-grained local dependencies or recognizing pat-
104   terns that span across patch borders. Consequently, this lack of continuity can hinder the model’s
105   ability to represent subtle textures, edges, and complex spatial relationships present in the image.
106   Though the kernels applied in CNNs can slide across an image with overlapping areas, their fixed
107   size and localized receptive fields inherently limit their ability to capture more local information.

139
      eralized and shared across various models. As a result, MAA promotes more sample-dependent
140
      and model-generic adversarial examples, improving transferability. Third, extensive experiments

142   terms of adversarial transferability. Last but not least, sophisticated parameter studies are undertaken
143   to provide a comprehensive and in-depth analysis of model performance, shedding light on critical

      2.1     P RELIMINARIES
149
150   Let Fs represent an available source VLP model consisting of an image encoder fimg and a text
151   encoder ftxt , which learn feature representations for images and texts, respectively. Given an image-
152   text pair (x, t), the objective is to generate adversarial examples xadv and tadv that can mislead the
153   predictions of an unknown target model Ft . To ensure the perturbations remain imperceptible, for
154   image perturbations, we use l∞ -norm constraint: ||tadv − t||∞ ≤ ϵimg . For text perturbations, we
155   restrict the number of words that can be modified in the sentence, denoted as ϵtxt .
156
157   2.2     M ETICULOUS ADVERSARIAL ATTACK
158
      MAA employs a straightforward RScrop technique in conjunction with a multi-granularity similarity
159
      disruption (MGSD) strategy. An illustration of the framework can be found in Figure 1.
160
161   The MGSD strategy enhances adversarial images by enlarging the feature distance from the original
      images across various layers and components of the model. Specifically, low-level layers and com-


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      ponents process local regions and extract detailed features, e.g., patch processing of ViTs, which
163   enable us to enhance the local utility of adversarial examples. High-level ones capture abstract,
164   semantic information. By targeting these diverse granularities and hierarchical levels, we can ef-
165   fectively uncover the characteristics and vulnerabilities of samples and also make the generation of
166   adversarial examples more rely on samples and less reliant on information specialized for source
167   models to associate images and tests. Moreover, low-level layers and components are generally less
168   model-specific. These contribute to reducing overfitting and enhancing the transferability. How-
169   ever, common backbone networks used in VLP models, such as ViTs and CNNs, are constrained
170   by fixed-size processing of inputs and limited local regions. Relying solely on these networks to
171
      generate adversarial examples fails to fully explore the representative and intrinsic characteristics
      and vulnerability of samples. Specifically, these networks process fixed-size inputs, which can hin-
172
      der the understanding of features of local regions. While targeting low-level layers that focus on
173
      local regions can alleviate this, it does not completely solve the problem. For instance, ViTs process
174   images in non-overlapping patches, which overlook important local relationships between neigh-
175   boring patches. Similarly, CNNs utilize fixed-size convolutional filters, also imposing restrictions
176   on feature exploration. Furthermore, they cannot extract local information from high-level layers
177   and components, limiting the fine-grained optimization of adversarial samples. As different models
178   are prone to extract some distinct features and focus on different regions to associate images and
179   texts, the fixed-size constraints would make generated adversarial examples rely on model-specific
180   features and regions, hindering the transferability across different models.
181   To overcome fixed-size constraints and explore more comprehensive, diverse, and detailed infor-
182   mation, we propose RScrop, which involves two operations: scaling and cropping. As illustrated
183   in Figure 1(a). First, original adversarial images are scaled up along each dimension, enabling a
184   focus on fine-grained local regions. Second, we systematically crop local regions by sliding a crop
185   window across the image, starting from an initial point in each dimension. The window shifts by a
186   predefined amount along each dimension to ensure comprehensive coverage of the image with more
187   regions and their connections considered. Specifically, after the initial crop, the window is shifted
188
      by a step size randomly selected to be smaller than the dimension size of the patch or the convolu-
      tional filter of the first layer, ensuring that more local regions and their connections are considered.
189
      We then move to adjacent non-overlapping areas from the previous crop (excluding the small-step
190
      cropped regions), applying the same operation until the entire image is covered. (R4W2) The shift
191   step length for i-th step relative to the initial point in different dimensions can be formulated by

232
233   3.1     S ETTINGS
234
235   Datasets and Tasks. Three datasets are used: Flickr30K Plummer et al. (2015), MSCOCO Lin
236   et al. (2014), and RefCOCO+ Yu et al. (2016). The splits for training and testing follow recent
237   works Zhang et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024). We focus on
238
      three tasks: image-text retrieval, image captioning, and visual grounding. For image-text retrieval,
      Flickr30K Plummer et al. (2015) and MSCOCO Lin et al. (2014) are used. For the image captioning
239
      task and the visual grounding task, MSCOCO and RefCOCO+ datasets are used, respectively.
240
241   Models. We test MAA and all compared methods on four kinds of widely-used VLP models: CLIP
242   Radford et al. (2021), ALBEF Li et al. (2021), TCL Yang et al. (2022), and BLIP Li et al. (2022).
243   For CLIP, different image encoders are utilized, including vision transformers (ViT-B/16 and ViT-
244
      L/14 Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)) and CNNs (ResNet50 and ResNet101 He et al. (2016)). The text
      encoder is a 6-layer transformer. For ALBEF and BLIP, we choose the variant that consists of a
245
      ViT-B/16 as the image encoder and a 6-layer transformer as the text encoder for the attack.
246
247   Implementation details. For fundamental experiments, the perturbation magnitude is set to
248   ϵx = 4/255 for images and ϵt = 1 for text. MAA is also evaluated across various perturbation
249   magnitudes. The optimization problem for image perturbations is addressed using Projected Gra-
250
      dient Descent (PGD) Madry et al. (2018) with T = 60 iterations and a step size of ϵx /T × 2.25.
      The batch size is configured to 4. Every 10 iterations, scaling ratios would be changed by randomly
251
      selecting from the set {1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2} for each dimension to optimize the adversarial images.
252
253   Baselines. Several state-of-the-art approaches are selected for comparison, including Co-Attack
254   Zhang et al. (2022), SGA Lu et al. (2023), VLATTACK Yin et al. (2023), ETU Zhang et al. (2024),
255   and (R5Q1)VLPTransferAttack Gao et al. (2024). ETU only generates universal adversarial images,
256
      while others produce adversarial images and texts. We also include baseline methods such as PGD
      and BERT-Attack Li et al. (2020).
257
258   Evaluation metric. In image-text retrieval, the attack success rate (ASR) is used to evaluate all
259   methods, calculated as the percentage of adversarial examples that successfully deceive the model.
260   For other tasks, we compare the performance of target models before and after attacks.

      4.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE P RE - TRAINED M ODELS
480
481   Vision-language pre-trained (VLP) models play a crucial role in advancing the understanding of
482   visual and textual information and their interactions. By leveraging large-scale unlabeled datasets
483   and self-supervised learning techniques, VLP Models can learn rich, generalized representations
484   of both visual and linguistic data. This transfer learning capability enables them to be fine-tuned
485   for a wide range of tasks with relatively small datasets, including multi-modal retrieval, zero-shot
      learning, image captioning, visual question answering, and visual entailment Radford et al. (2021);


                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Li et al. (2022; 2021); Yang et al. (2022). Notable VLP methods include CLIP Radford et al.
487   (2021), BLIP Li et al. (2022), ALBEF Li et al. (2021), and TCL Yang et al. (2022). These methods
488   primarily leverage multi-modal contrastive learning to align image-text pairs. Specifically, CLIP
489   employs unimodal encoders to project data from different modalities into a unified feature space.
490   BLIP Li et al. (2022) refines noisy captions to enhance learning effectiveness. ALBEF Li et al.
491   (2021) and TCL Yang et al. (2022) both utilize a multi-modal encoder to learn joint representations
492   for images and texts. ALBEF Li et al. (2021) focuses on inter-modal relationships while TCL Yang
493   et al. (2022) considers both intra- and inter-modal relationships.
494
495   4.2   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
496
497   With the widespread adoption of Deep Neural Networks, it is essential to evaluate their robustness
498   to ensure their reliability in real-world applications, which often involve uncertainty and potential
499   threats. Adversarial attacks are a prominent method used to assess this robustness, which aim to
500   mislead model predictions by introducing imperceptible perturbations into the data Szegedy et al.
501
      (2014); Zhang et al. (2023; 2021a); Madry et al. (2018); Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017). Traditional
      methods typically focus on specific tasks and unimodal cases, such as image classification. For
502
      image attacks, most techniques learn pixel-level perturbations, whereas text attacks often involve re-
503
      placing or removing keywords, or performing text transformations Li et al. (2020); Jin et al. (2020);
504   Iyyer et al. (2018); Naik et al. (2018); Ren et al. (2019). Recently, there has been growing interest in
505   multi-modal vision-language scenarios. For instance, Zhang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2021b); Zhu
506   et al. (2023b) address image-text retrieval by increasing the embedding distance between adversar-
507   ial and original data pairs. Xu et al. (2018) learn image perturbations by minimizing the distance
508   between the output and the target label while maximizing the difference from the original label for
509   the visual question answering task.
510   Adversarial transferability. Early attack methods typically assume a white-box setting, where
511   all necessary information for generating adversarial examples, including target models and tasks,
512   is available. However, in real-world scenarios, such comprehensive information is often unavail-
513   able. To address this challenge, one approach uses an ensemble of models as the victim model
514   during training Liu et al. (2016); Dong et al. (2018; 2019); Xiong et al. (2022), based on the intu-
515   ition that adversarial examples effective against a diverse set of models are likely to mislead more
516   models. However, assembling such a model ensemble can be difficult. Another approach utilizes
517   momentum-based methods Dong et al. (2018); Long et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2019); Inkawhich et al.
518
      (2019) to stabilize gradient updates and avoid poor local maxima, though this may also divert per-
      turbations from effective paths. Data augmentation Xie et al. (2019); Fang et al. (2022); Wei et al.
519
      (2023); Wang et al. (2024; 2021a) increases data diversity, helping to prevent overfitting to specific
520
      models. Transferability is particularly challenging for attacks on VLP models, due to the unpre-
521   dictable fine-tuning process. To enhance transferability, Zhang et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023); Zhang
522   et al. (2024) suggests increasing the gap between adversarial data and original image-text pairs.
523   Some of these methods also focus on generating diverse image-text pairs through scale-invariant
524   transformations Lu et al. (2023) and ScMix augmentations Zhang et al. (2024). Additionally, recent
525   approaches consider the local utility of adversarial examples Yin et al. (2023) or perturbations Zhang
526   et al. (2024). However, Yin et al. (2023) enlarges block-wise similarity between samples, which is
527   constrained by the block size, failing to comprehensively capture local regions and their interactions.
528   Zhang et al. (2024) does not consider the characteristics and vulnerabilities of the original sample.
529
      As a result, these methods cannot ensure the effectiveness and transferability of adversarial attacks.
530
531   5     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we propose a novel Meticulous Adversarial Attack (MAA), which demonstrates excep-
534   tional transferability across various VLP models, datasets, and downstream tasks. MAA enhances
535   adversarial transferability by refining adversarial examples specifically in the image modality. The
536   method is simple and easy to implement, consisting of a resizing and sliding crop technique and a
537   multi-granularity similarity disruption strategy. Two components work synergistically to explore the
538   representative, fine-grained characteristics and vulnerabilities of individual images, reducing over-
539   reliance on model-specific patterns. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness and transfer-
      ability of MAA, showing that it achieves highly competitive performance.
