011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models are increasingly used to process documents, scripts, and
014            facilitate question-answering on them. In our paper, we extract mechanistic cir-
015
               cuits for this real-world language modeling task: context-augmented language
               modeling for question-answering (QA) tasks and understand the potential bene-
016
               fits of circuits towards downstream applications such as data attribution, where
017
               the specific input data in the context is used to produce an answer is identified.
018            We extract circuits as a function of internal model components (e.g., attention
019            heads, attention layers, MLPs) using causal mediation analysis techniques. Lever-
020            aging the extracted circuits, we first understand the interplay between the language
021            model’s usage of parametric memory and retrieved context towards a better mech-
022            anistic understanding of context-augmented language models. We then identify a
023            small set of attention heads in our circuit which performs reliable data attribution
024            by default, thereby obtaining attribution for free in just the model’s forward pass!
025            Using this insight, we then introduce ATTNATTRIB, a fast data attribution algo-
026
               rithm. Through a range of empirical experiments across different extractive QA
               benchmarks, we show that performing data attribution with ATTNATTRIB obtains
027
               state-of-the-art attribution results across different language models. Finally, we
028
               show the possibility to steer the language model towards answering from the con-
029            text, instead of the parametric memory by using the attribution from our extracted
030            attention head as an additional signal during the forward pass. Beyond mechanis-
031            tic understanding, our paper provides tangible applications of mechanistic circuits
032            in the form of reliable data attribution and model steering.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In the recent times, large language models have been used to process documents, webpages and
038   transcripts as context and answer questions from them leading to the practical task of extractive
039
      question-answering (QA), the task of answering a question by directly extracting words from the
      context/document (in contrast to ”abstractive QA” or ”open-ended QA” where the words compris-
040
      ing the answer may not necessarily appear in the context). In such a case, a language model can
041
      either answer from the context or hallucinate from its parametric memory. A mechanistic under-
042   standing of such a task with a circuit (a sub-graph of the language model’s computational graph)
043   can not only provide insights on the inner workings of the model for this task, but can also en-
044   able downstream applications to improve the model reliability. Earlier works on mechanistic cir-
045   cuits (Bereska & Gavves, 2024; Elhage et al., 2021) for large language models (Touvron et al.,
046   2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023) have discovered circuits for language tasks such as
047   entity tracking (Prakash et al., 2024), indirect object identification (Wang et al., 2022) or simple
048   math operations such as “greater than” (Hanna et al., 2023). While circuits are a principled way to
049   mechanistically understand language models, we note certain limitations within existing works: (i)
050
      Tasks such as entity tracking or indirect object identification are inherently simple tasks and may not
      capture the complexity of real-world applications for language models and (ii) It remains uncertain
051
      whether understanding language models through circuits will translate into practical applications.
052
053   In our paper, we extract mechanistic circuits for a real-world extractive QA task and use insights
      from the mechanistic circuit to provide two downstream applications: (i) Data attribution to context

      experiments, we show that ATTNATTRIB can obtain state-of-the-art data-attribution results when
093
      compared to other strong baselines for extractive QA tasks without any additional forward pass or
094

095   model answers using the parametric memory circuit, the attribution heads still display a high at-
096   tention to the answer tokens in the context. With this insight, we design a simple model steering

098   ditional source of information. Across various empirical experiments, we find that the addition of
099   attribution during prompting leads to improvements upto 9% on popular extractive QA datasets.
100

101   question-answering (QA). Beyond mechanistic interpretability of QA tasks, our paper highlights
102   that certain components of the circuit can be useful for various downstream applications such as
103   data-attribution and also steering language models towards being more faithful to the context (thus
104   improving generalization). In summary, our contributions are as follows:

      Circuit Based Interpretability in Language Models. With the advent of language models, sev-
      eral recent works have focused on a mechanistic understanding of language models (Meng et al.,
120
      2023; Turner et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2023; McDougall et al., 2023; Gould et al., 2023). One
121
      of the primary benefit of transformer based language models is that the final logit representation
122   can be decomposed as a sum of individual model components (Elhage et al., 2021). Based on
123   this decomposition, one can extract task-specific causal sub-graphs (i.e., circuits) of internal model
124   components in language models. Early works have extracted such circuits for indirect-object iden-
125   tification (Wang et al., 2022), greater-than operation (Hanna et al., 2023) and more recently for
126   entity-tracking (Prakash et al., 2024). Recently, there has been an increasing focus on the practical
127   aspects of mechanistic interpretability such as refusal mediation (Arditi et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
128   2024) or safety in general (Zou et al., 2023). Circuits can also be constructed as sub-graphs of neu-
129   rons in the language model, but it often comes with increased complexity of interpretation (Elhage
130
      et al., 2022). In our paper, we focus on extracting circuits for a real-world task such as extractive
      QA with a particular emphasis on practical applications such as data attribution using them.
131
132   Applications in Context-Augmented Question-Answering. With the advent of retrieval-
133   augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2024) language models have been increasingly
134   used for real-world Question-Answering (QA) tasks. One of the primary enhancement of context-
135   augmented QA lies in the ability to provide reliable grounding (i.e., attribution) in the context for
136
      the generated answer (Li et al., 2023; Khalifa et al., 2024; Huang & Chang, 2024; Ye et al., 2024).
      In recent times, there have been a large set of works which improve LLM responses by reducing
137
      hallucinations and improving grounding in the input context (Ye et al., 2024; Asai et al., 2023; Xu
138
      et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024). Beyond grounding, (Wu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a; Mallen
139   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) investigate the interplay between model’s use of parametric vs. con-
140   text knowledge. We note that our paper tests the ability of the mechanistic insights from circuits
141   towards performing these applications for extractive QA tasks.
142
143   3     D ECIPHERING A C IRCUIT FOR E XTRACTIVE Q UESTION -A NSWERING
144
145   Nodes and Edges in a Language Model Circuit. Recent decoder-only large language models,
146   denoted by gϕ , such as Llama variants (Touvron et al., 2023; et al., 2024), are built on the seminal
147   transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). A notable characteristic of these architectures is
148
      that the token representation at any layer can be expressed as a function of internal model compo-
      nents, such as multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and attention heads, from earlier layers (Elhage et al.,
149
      2021). As a result, the computational graph underlying a language transformer is a directed acyclic
150
      graph, with nodes representing components like MLPs and attention heads (or layers), and edges
151   representing connections formed by the residual stream.
152
153   We are particularly interested in obtaining a sub-graph of the transformer’s computational graph
154
      which is responsible towards context-augmented language modeling. In particular, we extract two
      circuits: (i) Context-Faithfulness Circuit, which is used when the underlying language model an-
155
      swers from the context, and (ii) Memory-Faithfulness Circuit, which is used when the language
156
      model solely answers from the parametric memory, ignoring the context. To extract the respective
157   circuits, we first design a probe dataset consisting of 200 questions mimicking both these conditions
158   which we use with causal mediation analysis (Wang et al., 2022) and our interventional algorithm
159   in Sec. (3.2).

      The design of a probe dataset is extremely crucial in extracting circuits for a language model task
      as shown in earlier works (Wang et al., 2022; Hanna et al., 2023). We are interested in obtaining a
192
      circuit for context-faithfulness as well as one when the model answers from the parametric memory
193
      while ignoring the context. To this end, we design two probe datasets Dcopy and Dmemory respec-
194   tively for them. Each example in Dcopy and Dmemory consists of factual questions sourced from
195   the Known dataset (Meng et al., 2023). For each question qi in both datasets, we use Llama-3-70B-
196   Instruct to generate a context ci related to the subject and answer for qi . To guarantee that for each
197   question in Dcopy , the language model only answers from the context (and not the memory), we
198   replace the answer tokens in the context ci with a set of tokens which are semantically similar to
199   the original answer(e.g., in Fig.(1), we replace Seattle with New York in the original context Space
200   Needle is located in Seattle, where the original answer was Seattle). In Dmemory , to force the model
201   to answer from the parametric memory while ignoring the context, we replace the answer token with
202
      a token which is far away in semantic meaning from the original answer (e.g., replace Seattle with a
      punctuation of “–”). In total, we curate 200 questions (with their corresponding modified contexts)
203
      in Dcopy and Dmemory . We note that each entry xi ∈ Dcopy/memory , contains a question qi , a subject of
204
      the question si , ground-truth answer denoted by ai , the modified context c′i and the original context
205   ci . Along with ci and c′i , we add a corrupted context ci,corrupted , where the subject and the answer
206   token in the context is replaced by unrelated tokens and qi,corrupted where the subject in the question
207   is replaced by a randomly sampled token. For e.g., as seen in Fig.(1) the corrupted context (Big Sur
208   is in California) is formed by replacing the subject and the answer tokens in the modified context.
209   A full description of the probe dataset D can be accessed in Sec.(F)
210
      Distinctions from Other Circuit Datasets. We note that previous work on circuit extraction for
211   entity tracking and indirect object identification relies on fixed templates to generate examples in the
212   probe dataset. However, for real-world tasks like extractive QA, probe datasets cannot be templated,
213   as contexts may vary in length and contain distinct information across different examples.

232           Our interventional method is developed on the foundational technique of causal mediation anal-
233           ysis (Pearl, 2001). The primary idea of causal mediation analysis is to find important paths in a
234           causal graph, by performing an interventional operation on a small set of nodes and measuring the
235           change in the final output. In our use-case, we adapt this method to find a sub-graph of internal
236
              model components such that ablating them leads to a decrease in ability of the model to perform
              QA (either through extraction from the context or using the parametric memory while ignoring the
237
              context). Below we provide the algorithmic description of our interventional step:
238
239           Algorithmic Description. Given the language model gϕ and its associated computational graph                                           © 2024 Adobe. All Rights Reserved. Ad

240           G, our objective is to extract a sub-graph (i.e., a circuit) C ∈ G which is responsible towards the
241           QA task. We obtain the nodes and edges of the circuit C in a hierarchical manner. First, we obtain
242
              a set of nodes and edges in hierarchy 0 denoted as (N0 , E0 ) which have the highest direct effect
              to the final logit. In the next step for hierarchy 1, we obtain a set of nodes and edges (N1 , E1 ),
243
              which have the highest direct effect on the nodes from hierarchy 0. For any hierarchy k, we ob-
244
              tain a set of nodes and edges (Nk , Ek ) which have a high direct effect on the nodes (Nk−1 , Ek−1 )
245           from the previous hierarchy. For obtaining the nodes at the k th hierarchy, we create two instanti-
246           ations of the underlying language model gϕ . The first instantiation is denoted as gϕ,clean , with the
247           original question qi and modified context c′i as the input. The second instantiation of the language
248           model is gϕ,corrupted , where the input context as well as the question is corrupted as ci,corrupted and
249           qi,corrupted respectively. With this corrupted input, model gϕ,corrupted assigns a low probability to the
250           generated answer tokens ai from gϕ,clean . Using these two model instantiations, the goal of the
251           patching operation is to copy the activations of a node gj ∈ G from gϕ,corrupted to gϕ,clean , while
252           restoring the activations of all the other nodes in gϕ,clean to its original state. We denote the patched
253
              model as gϕ,patch and use score(i, gj ) = 1 − Pgj ,patch (ai ) to measure the importance of the com-
254           ponent gj for the ith example. For the component gj , we then compute the average metric score
                                 P|D|
255           as score(gj ) = i=1 score(i, gj )/|D|. We then sort the scores of the various components in the
256           computational graph as score(gj ) ∀j ∈ N in decreasing order as {gj }N         j=1 and greedily select the
257           minimum value of k, such that the average metric score of patching multiple components together:
258           score({gj }kj=1 ) ≥ δ. These selected components {gj }kj=1 form the nodes in Nk . In our experiments,
259           we only use the MLPs, the attention heads and attention layers as the different model components
260
              which are patched. The final circuit C consists of the nodes {Nk }K       k=1 and their associated edges,
              where K denotes the maximum hierarchy of the circuit.

263           dataset for the patching operations. We perform the patching operation at the last token position
264           corresponding to the last residual stream. We selected this position for patching because the infor-
265           mation in the last residual stream plays a crucial role in determining the probability distribution of

              the next generated token, which is also used in recent mechanistic interpretability works concerning
              model steering (Arditi et al., 2024; Turner et al., 2024).

      3.3         I NTERPRETABILITY I NSIGHTS F OR E XTRACTIVE QA THROUGH C IRCUITS
302
303   In this section, we discuss the extracted circuit components for both context faithfulness and para-
304   metric memory faithfulness. We first draw out their distinctions and validate the correctness of the
305   circuit components. We then discuss the interpretable nature of a small set of attention heads in the
306   circuit. Finally, we discuss the distinction of extractive QA circuit components from other language
307   tasks such as entity tracking.
308
309   3.3.1                          C ONTEXT FAITHFULNESS C IRCUIT D IFFERS FROM PARAMETRIC M EMORY C IRCUIT
310
      Results for attention components. We find the circuit components for context faithfulness and
311
      memory faithfulness to differ significantly. For context faithfulness, we find that patching a small
312   group of 4-5 attention layers (or 10 attention heads) is sufficient to obtain a high metric score of
313   more than 0.95. However, for the memory faithfulness, we find that a significantly higher number
314   of attention layers (e.g., >15) and attention heads (e.g., >30) are required to obtain a relatively high
315   metric score. This result shows that information from a small set of attention heads (or layers) pri-
316   marily drive the circuit corresponding to context faithfulness than memory faithfulness. In Sec.(D),
317   we also show that the top circuit components of attention layers (or heads) have a low overlap be-
318   tween the two circuits – highlighting that the underlying language model elicits different circuits
319   when answering from the context vs. parametric memory.
320   Results for MLP components. We observe an intriguing pattern with MLPs in the extracted circuit.
321   For context faithfulness, in Vicuna and Phi-3, MLPs appear to be less significant, as patching them
322   results in a very low metric score. However, in Llama-3-8B, we identify one specific MLP (MLP-
323   31) that individually achieves a high metric score of 0.9. This suggests that the type of pre-training
      might play a role in determining the relevant circuit components (with respect to MLPs) for context

402   4     A PPLICATION 1: ATTRIBUTION FOR F REE V IA O NE ATTENTION H EAD
403
404   Data attribution for extractive QA is crucial for language models processing external contexts, such
405   as documents or personal files, not included in the pre-training corpora. For example, in a question
406   like “What did Sarah Miller say during the all-hands meeting?”, the correct answer comes from
407   a specific section of the context (e.g., meeting transcript). Pointing to the source of the answer
408   improves model reliability and helps users verify its correctness, especially since LLMs are prone
409
      to hallucinations (Niu et al., 2024). In this section, we introduce ATTNATTRIB, a fast and efficient
      data attribution algorithm, leveraging insights from our mechanistic interpretations (Sec. 3.3), which
410
      outperforms existing QA baselines.
411
412
      4.1   ATTNATTRIB : A S IMPLE AND S TRONG DATA ATTRIBUTION M ETHOD FOR
413
            E XTRACTIVE QA
414
415   In Sec.(3.3), we observe that a small set of attention heads from hierarchy 0 of the circuit attend to
416   the answer token in the context. Thus, these attention heads from the extracted circuit for context
417   faithfulness implicitly perform data attribution by default. However, real-world contexts can be
418   noisy and contain multiple answer tokens, raising questions about the behavior of these attributable
419   attention heads in practical settings. In this section, we introduce ATTNATTRIB, which automatically
420   generates attributions from the context during the forward pass by leveraging only one attention head
421
      from the context faithfulness circuit. Specifically, ATTNATTRIB uses the attention patterns from
      the relevant attention head to generate a span from the context for each generated answer token.
422
      These spans are ranked based on the maximum attention value within the span (a sentence from the
423
      context), and the top-k spans are selected for attribution. A detailed description of ATTNATTRIB is
424   provided in Algo. (1). Using ATTNATTRIB, we explore the potential applications of mechanistic
425   circuits for attribution in extractive QA. We note that we use one attention head identified using our
426   probe dataset, Dcopy , and test its effectiveness on different extractive QA benchmarks.

531   6 C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper we scale up mechanistic circuit extraction to a real-world task involving extractive QA.
533   We identify the key mechanistic differences when the language model uses the parametric memory
534   (ignoring the context) vs. when it uses the context. We then find that a small set of attention heads in
535   the extracted circuit for context faithfulness performs data attribution by default. We use this insight
536   to introduce ATTNATTRIB,an efficient data attribution algorithm which obtains strong results on
537   various extractive QA benchmarks. We further show that the attributions from ATTNATTRIB can be
538   used towards improving generalization in extractive QA tasks by steering the model towards context
539   faithfulness. Overall, our paper shows that circuits can be strategically used beyond mechanistic
      understanding towards designing real-world applications for language models.

825   In Sec.(3.4.1), we discuss the effect of scaling the attention heads from the context faithfulness
826   circuit in the language model when it answers from the parametric memory. In particular, we find
827   that upweighting the maximum attention value from these attention heads onto the context steers the
828   language model towards answering from the context instead of the parametric memory.
829
      Up-weighting the attention values. We multiple a scalar value β to the maximum attention value
830   in the context before the softmax normalization operation. In our implementation, we perform this
831   scaling operation across all the attention heads in the top 3 attention layers in the circuit. We set
832   β = 10 in our experiments, for the best steering result.
833
      Ablating the MLPs. We set the output of the top MLPs from the memory-faithulness circuit to be
834
      zero. In particular, we set the output of the projection layer in the MLP block to be zero, but make
835
      sure that the output of the other blocks are not changed due to this modification, by setting them to
836   their original configuration. This ensures, that only the direct connection from the MLP to the final

1059   Extractive QA datasets such as HotpotQA, NaturalQuestions and NQ-Swap are particularly con-
1060   cerned with entities in the answer which consist of a few relevant tokens in length. Even if the
1061
       generated answer from the language model is long, the attributions need to point to the relevant
       span in the context which consist of the entity. Another challenging setting is the case where the
1062
       language model needs to generate an answer comprising of an entity which itself can be long, for
1063
       example comprising of multiple sentences. We investigate two experimental settings in this respect
1064   for the following datasets: (i) CNN-Dailymail, where the language model is prompted to generate an
1065   extractive summary. This extracted summary is itself the relevant entity in the generated answer. (ii)
1066   NQ-Long, where the language model is prompted to generate an answer with exact sentences from
1067   the context (rather than only the entity). We note that in NQ-Long, the ground-truth answer consists
1068   of multiple sentences extracted from the context.
1069
       To evaluate the quality of attributions, we measure the relative change in the log probability of
1070   the responses when the original context is used vs. the original context is modified to remove the
1071   attributions (obtained from ATTNATTRIB). A higher relative change in the log probabilities indicates
1072   the faithfulness of the attributions. In particular, given the language model gϕ , the original context
1073   Corig and the ablated context where the attributed text has been removed as Cablated , we define the
1074   relative change in log probability of a response R as:

       Our method, ATTNATTRIB is suitable for attributing answers in Document-based QA or Web-search
1344   QA setting that uses LLMs. We note that white-box access of the model’s parameters are required
1345   to discover the circuits that are useful for attribution. Thus, our method cannot directly be applied
1346   for Contextual QA applications where blackbox LLMs like Claude or ChatGPT are deployed. In
1347   the most basic form, ATTNATTRIB provides attribution for every token generated in the answer.
1348   Algorithm 1 is a simple heuristic to aggregate these per-token attributions to provide an attribution
1349   for the entire answer-span. However, we leave the exploration of more sophisticated strategies,
       especially those that combine ATTNATTRIB with retrieval-based attribution for future work.
