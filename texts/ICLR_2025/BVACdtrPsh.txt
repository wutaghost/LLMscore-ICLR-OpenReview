009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The comprehension of text-rich visual scenes has become a focal point for eval-
012            uating Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) due to their widespread
013            applications. Current benchmarks tailored to the scenario emphasize perceptual
014            capabilities, while overlooking the assessment of cognitive abilities. To address
015            this limitation, we introduce a Multimodal benchmark towards Text-rich visual
016            scenes, to evaluate the Cognitive capabilities of MLLMs through visual reasoning
017            and content-creation tasks (MCTBench). To mitigate potential evaluation bias
018
               from the varying distributions of datasets, MCTBench incorporates several percep-
               tion tasks (e.g., scene text recognition) to ensure a consistent comparison of both
019
               the cognitive and perceptual capabilities of MLLMs. To improve the efficiency
020
               and fairness of content-creation evaluation, we conduct an automatic evaluation
021            pipeline. Evaluations of various MLLMs on MCTBench reveal that, despite their
022            impressive perceptual capabilities, their cognition abilities require enhancement.
023            We hope MCTBench will offer the community an efficient resource to explore and
024            enhance cognitive capabilities towards text-rich visual scenes.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) OpenAI (2023); Team et al. (2023); Liu et al.
030   (2023b); Li et al. (2024b) have exhibited promising performance across various cross-modal tasks,
031   and revealed potential for widespread real-world applications. In practical applications, many images
032   contain crucial textual elements that are essential for addressing specific challenges, such as key
033
      information extraction from receipts. Consequently, the ability to comprehend text-rich visual scenes
      can significantly enhance the practicality of MLLMs and drive innovative applications across multiple
034
      domains.
035
036   Recent benchmarks Liu et al. (2024c); Li et al. (2024a); iang Yue et al. (2023) have increasingly
037   focused on evaluating MLLMs towards text-rich visual scenes. Nonetheless, the benchmarks are
038   centred around evaluating perceptual capabilities yet overlook the assessment of cognitive abilities,
039
      which are a significant strength of MLLMs (as illustrated in Figure 1).
040   In this paper, we propose a Multimodal benchmark to evaluate the Cognitive capabilities of MLLMs
041   in Text-rich visual scenes (MCTBench). To assess the cognitive abilities of MLLMs thoroughly,
042   we design two types of tasks in the MCTBench: reasoning tasks for comprehension of the input
043   scenes, and open-ended content-creation tasks for generating output responses. Besides, MCTBench
044   integrates various perception tasks to study the differences with cognition tasks, while avoiding evalu-
045
      ation biases from varying dataset distributions. Fundamentally, MCTBench curates approximately
      5.2k text-rich images from a wide range of public datasets, along with 8.5k rigorously annotated
046
      question-answer pairs categorized into three tasks: perception, reasoning and content-creation. The
047
      perception and reasoning tasks are formatted as multiple-choice questions for convenient evaluation,
048   following common practices in Fu et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2023b). Due to the sub-
049   jectivity and high cost of human evaluation in open-ended content creation, we establish an automated
050   evaluation pipeline by leveraging sophisticated MLLMs (e.g., GPT-4V) as the evaluator, to compare
051   the predictions of models against the provided references. Our experimental results demonstrate
052   that MLLMs exhibit notably lower performance of cognition capabilities compared to perception
053   in text-rich visual scenes, particularly for text-enhanced models. Furthermore, performances in
      cognition tasks (reasoning and content-creation) are improved with larger parameter scales. Our main

      2.2   MLLM B ENCHMARKS
138
139   As multimodal large language models (MLLMs) continue to exhibit cross-task generality, single-task
140   evaluations (e.g., Goyal et al. (2017); Singh et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2015); Lin et al. (2024)) are
141   inadequate for a comprehensive performance assessment. Recent works Liu et al. (2024b); Li et al.
142   (2023b); Fu et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2023) present general MLLM benchmarks comprising multiple
143   tasks. Furthermore, to explore the performance of MLLMs on more complex tasks, MathVista Lu
144   et al. (2024b) evaluates their mathematical abilities, and MMMU iang Yue et al. (2023) integrates
145   multiple-discipline questions to benchmark MLLMs in expert domains.
146   Conversely, text-rich visual scenes are attracting growing attention due to their potential applications.
147   Early works Mathew et al. (2021); Mishra et al. (2019a); Singh et al. (2019) focused on single tasks,
148   while OCRBench Liu et al. (2024c) integrates multiple single-task datasets into five representative
149   OCR(Optical Character Recognition)-based tasks. In contrast, our work evaluates MLLMs on
150   complex tasks beyond OCR-based ones in text-rich visual scenes. A similar work is presented
151   in Wadhawan et al. (2024), which demonstrates the model’s performance on reasoning tasks but
152
      only on a limited set of test datasets. Our study provides a broader evaluation of cognition in text-
      rich visual scenes, pushing the boundaries of what MLLMs can achieve in more diverse scenarios
153
      such as content-creation. Table 1 demonstrates the detailed comparison between ours and previous
154
      benchmarks.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
163
164   The MCTBench is designed to evaluate the cognitive capabilities of Multimodal Large Language
165   Models (MLLMs) towards text-rich visual scenes. To construct the comprehensive and diverse
166   benchmark, we collected 5,194 images from a variety of public datasets, encompassing a wide
167   array of text-rich scenes such as natural environments, books, scientific contexts, advertisements,
168   e-commerce, and video shots. We meticulously annotate these images with a total of 8.5k question-
169   answer pairs categorized into three tasks: perception, reasoning, and content creation. Specifically,
170   MCTBench consists of 2,734 perception multiple-choice samples, 2,602 reasoning multiple-choice
171
      samples, and 3,130 content-creation samples. Figure 2 illustrates the overall construction pipeline of
      MCTBench.
172
173
174   3.2   T EXT- RICH I MAGES C OLLECTION
175
176
      Image Source The images of MCTBench are collected from 10 different publicly available datasets,
      aiming to incorporate comprehensive visual scenes to evaluate the cognition of Multimodal Large
177
      Language Models (MLLMs). We begin with sampling common general natural scenes (e.g., street
178
      views, competitions, road signs) from the COCO Lin et al. (2015), Flickr30k Young et al. (2014),
179   GQA Hudson & Manning (2019), SeedBench Li et al. (2023b) and Visual Genome Krishna et al.
180   (2016) datasets.
181
182
      Furthermore, we select conventional text-rich multimodal datasets: OCR-VQA Mishra et al. (2019b),
      and VizWiz Gurari et al. (2018) taken by blind photographers. To further diversify MCTBench, we in-
183
      corporate three domain-specific scenes with broad potential applications: advertising (AutoUnderAds
184
      Hussain et al. (2017)), e-commerce (FoodLogoDet-1500 Hou et al. (2021)), and science (ScienceQA
185   Lu et al. (2022)). We randomly extract one frame from each video in the AutoUnderAds dataset. All
186   data sources are specifically selected from the testsets. We adhere to the original licenses stated by all
187   datasets.
188
      OCR-based image filtering. We select the text-rich images from the sourced images, which are
189
      guided by the following guidelines. To maintain the clarity and substantive textual content, we only
190
      retain images with valid OCR-recognized characters (with recognition probabilities higher than 0.2)
191   of at least 10 characters. To ensure text contributes to overall visual semantics, we select images
192   where text regions occupy more than 10% of the image area, after validating the impact of valid text
193   lines on semantic expression. These meticulous selection criteria resulted in a curated collection of
194   high-quality and crystal-clear text-rich images, designed to challenge and inspire advancements in
195   perceptual and cognitive understanding within textual domains.
196
197   Annotation Considering the bias and efficiency of the manual annotation, we employ a GPT-aided
198   approach to generate at least 10 pseudo-questions for each image, and ask annotators to remove
199   low-quality ones. All answers are human-annotated with two rounds: (1) Each image with at least 10
200   GPT-aided pseudo-questions, is randomly assigned to three annotators. Each annotator independently
201   annotates the questions and provides answers. (2) Quality checkers will review the annotation in
202   the first round. If any question or image quality does not meet our annotation guidelines, the set is
203
      re-annotated by the corresponding annotators, who also revise their answers. Annotators in the second
      round are required to have at least 2 years of experience in text-rich multimodal scene annotation.
204
      To reach an agreement, we use majority voting to determine the final answer. If majority voting
205
      does not reach an agreement (i.e., all three answers are inconsistent), we check if the discrepancy
206   originated in the second round. If so, the question is re-annotated; if inconsistencies persist, it is
207   discarded. The question is discarded if the discrepancy does not arise in the second round. In addition
208   to content-creation tasks, due to the inherent diversity of responses, we do not provide unified answers.
209   Instead, we offer standard references generated by powerful MLLMs (e.g., GPT-4V) and meticulously
210   reviewed by humans.
211
212   Quality control During the image quality assessment, annotators remove low-quality images (e.g.,
213   blurry, unclear text, inappropriate, solely tables/documents, and only watermark). For the QA quality
214   assessment, annotators eliminate low-quality questions (e.g., ambiguous, overly generalized, too
215   simplistic) and check the correctness of annotated answers (e.g., logical errors). On the other hand,
      We filter out multiple-choice options with more than 30% word count disparity and remove the

247   4.1   M ODELS
248
249   To evaluate the performance of various MLLMs on MCTBench, we select a diverse range of models
250   categorized into two primary types: general MLLMs and text-enhanced MLLMs (specifically
251   optimized for text recognition in images). We establish two naive baselines (random choice and
252   frequency choice) as reference points to ensure the robustness and validity of the dataset in Table
253   2. Random choice involves selecting an option at random as the prediction result, while frequency
254   choice involves selecting the prediction result based on the option with the highest proportion in the
255
      ground truth.
256
257   General MLLMs The experiment initially evaluates popular closed-source models, specifically
258   Gemini-Pro Team et al. (2023) and GPT-4V(ision) OpenAI (2023). For open-source models, we select
259   several notable general-purpose MLLMs, including Sharegpt4V Chen et al. (2023b), Honeybee Cha
260   et al. (2024), LLaVA Liu et al. (2024a; 2023b), Otter Li et al. (2023a), Yi-VL AI et al. (2024), Qwen-
261   VL-chat Bai et al. (2023) and Deepseek-VL Lu et al. (2024a) as competitive baselines. Additionally,
262   we incorporate CogVLM Wang et al. (2023) and SPHINX-v2 Lin et al. (2023), which are enhanced
263
      for fine-grained understanding. To assess differentiated performance, we also integrate a larger
      open-source model, Mini-Gemini Li et al. (2024b).
264
265
266   Text-enhanced MLLMs Recent researchers propose remarkable works to tackle the understanding
267   of text-rich images via enhancing the textual capabilities. Consequently, we select models mPLUG-
268   DocOwl Ye et al. (2023b), Monkey Liu et al. (2024d); Li et al. (2024c), InternLM-XComposer2-VL
269   Dong et al. (2024), CogAgent Hong et al. (2023) and LLaVA-NeXT Liu et al. (2024a) which have
      demonstrated strong OCR capabilities in previous evaluations.

      4.2   M ETRICS
271
272   MCTBench is constructed with three categories of tasks: perception, reasoning, and content-creation.
273   Due to the standard answer provided in perception and reasoning tasks, each QA pair is designed
274   in a multiple-choice format. In contrast, the content-creation task is considered as an open-ended
275   generation problem due to the diversity of responses.
276
277   Perception and reasoning Perception and reasoning tasks entail the acquisition of information
278   from input data, comprehension of images and text, and derivation of conclusions. Consequently,
279   employing multiple-choice question-answering can effectively validate the corresponding capabilities
280
      of MLLMs, following Liu et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2023b). In practice, we prioritize original prompts
      employed by MLLMs and, if not specified, use those prompts which yield optimal results. For lengthy
281
      responses, we use regular expressions and supplementary rules to extract the option answers. We use
282
      mean accuracy to evaluate MLLMs’ perception and reasoning capability.
283
284
      Content-creation To ensure consistency and efficiency in evaluation, we implement automatic
285   evaluation for content-creation tasks. However, due to the diversity of answers in content creation,
286   standard responses are not feasible, leading us to establish competitive references. These references
287   are generated through manually crafted responses from text-only GPT-4, based on inputs from OCR
288   recognition and detailed descriptions. The evaluation is grounded on four principal aspects: relevance,
289   faithfulness, creativity, and instruction following. Subsequently, we employ machines (e.g., GPT-4V)
290   to compare other MLLMs against our references with the mentioned principal, and categorize their
291   performance as Good, Same, or Bad (i.e., the GSB metric). We measure each model’s performance
292   by calculating the percentage of ’Good’ and ’Same’ ratings relative to all questions, indicating how
293
      many outperform or match the constructed reference (i.e., (G + S)/(G + S + B)).
294   To assess correlations and validate the reliability of machine evaluation, we also conduct a manual
295   evaluation on subsets of the data using the mentioned metrics (GSB) and compare them with machine
296   evaluation. We integrate three powerful MLLMs (GPT-4V OpenAI (2023), Gemini-Pro Team et al.
297   (2023) and LLaVA-NeXT Liu et al. (2024a)) as evaluators. We measure the evaluation correlation
298   between humans and machines using accuracy and Pearson correlation Benesty et al. (2009) on
299
      GSB scores. Table 3 illustrates the results between three machine evaluators and human evaluations,
      indicating that GPT-4V achieves a top correlation score.

      Perception Firstly, experiments are conducted to verify the perceptual performances of each
304   model as baselines. As shown in Table 2, most models achieved satisfying scores on the perception
305   task. Closed-source models (e.g., GPT-4V OpenAI (2023)) demonstrated excellent accuracy, while
306   some open-source models (e.g., Mini-Gemini Li et al. (2024b)) demonstrated superior perception

313   extraction and fusion of visual and textual features, but also involves comprehensive inference to
314
      generate accurate responses. As shown in Table 2, most models exhibited a significant drop in scores

315

400
401   The effect of visual and textual information. In Table 4, we conduct several experiments to reveal
402   the effect of visual and textual information in images of our benchmark. We use the LLaVA-1.5
403   as the baseline. Firstly, we conduct an experiment that adds OCR texts as input. As shown in the
404   table, even if the OCR texts are contained as input, the model cannot gain explicit improvement.
405   This demonstrates that the MCTBench does not rely solely on texts to get answers. Furthermore,
406
      we conduct an experiment that removes all texts from images to check the importance of texts in
      our benchmark. Specifically, we detected and blurred all the texts in images. We noticed that the
407
      performance dropped significantly. This also demonstrates that it is difficult for a model to correctly
408
      answer the question without text in the image. Additionally, if we only keep the text region, and
409   delete other background parts in the image. The performance is between the above two experiments,
410   indicating that MCTBench relies both on textual and visual information, to get the final result. To
411   conclude, explicitly adding OCR texts, or removing text/image parts does not help, or even lead to
412   worse performance on MCTBench. MLLMs are required to jointly recognize related textual and
413   visual patterns, to answer the questions correctly.
