                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
               We consider the following problem of model provenance: can a third party verify whether
010
               two language models are trained independently or not given the weights of both models? We
011            propose a family of statistical tests for models of any architecture that yield exact p-values
012            with respect to the null hypothesis that the models are trained with independent randomness
013            (e.g., independent random initialization). These p-values are valid regardless of the compo-
014            sition of either model’s training data, and we obtain them by simulating independent copies
015            of each model and comparing various measures of similarity in the weights and activations of
016            the original two models to these independent copies. We evaluate the power of these tests on
017            pairs of 21 open-weight models (210 total pairs) and find they reliably identify all 69 pairs of
018            fine-tuned models. Notably, our tests remain effective even after substantial fine-tuning; we
019
               accurately detect dependence between Llama 2 and Llemma, even though the latter was fine-
               tuned on an 750B additional tokens (37.5% of the original Llama 2 training budget). Finally,
020
               we identify transformations of model weights that break the effectiveness of our tests without
021
               altering model outputs, and—motivated by the existence of these evasion attacks—we pro-
022            pose a mechanism for matching hidden activations between the MLP layers of two models
023            that is robust to these transformations. Though we no longer obtain exact p-values from this
024            mechanism, empirically we find it reliably distinguishes fine-tuned models and pruned mod-
025            els of different dimension and is even robust to completely retraining the MLP layers from
026            scratch.

049   Consider the ways in which two language models may be related (Figure 1): one might be a fine-tune of the
050   other, or they might share a common ancestor, or they may be fully independent. Without knowing the details of
051   either model’s training process, what can a third party infer about this relationship from just the weights of both
052   models? Answering this question would better enable independent third parties to track provenance of open-
053   weight models, which is especially important in light of intensifying concerns around intellectual property
      (IP) protection (Mensch, 2024) and regulatory scrutiny (Anderljung et al., 2023) as both the capabilities and

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      development costs of these models continue to grow (e.g., (Dubey et al., 2024; Team, 2024; GLM et al., 2024;
055   Yang et al., 2024)).
056
057   In this paper, we focus specifically on the question of whether two models are independently trained versus not;
058
      we do not distinguish whether one is a fine-tune of the other or if the two models share a common ancestor.
      Casting the training of a language model as a randomized process (e.g., due to the initial model weights and
059
      batch ordering) we formalize this question as a hypothesis testing problem where the null hypothesis is that the
060
      weights of the two models are independent random variables. We seek a solution to this problem that admits
061   tight control over false positives while also reliably distinguishing dependent models regardless of 1) design
062   decisions such as the number of fine-tuning tokens or choice of optimizer and 2) the application of various
063   adversarial evasion attacks, including any transformation of model weights that does not affect model output.
064
      In the non-adversarial setting, we propose a family of exact tests for model independence based on identifying
065
      similarities between model weights and hidden activations. The main idea is that we can compute p-values by
066
      simulating T identically distributed copies of each model and comparing the value of some test statistic (e.g.,
067   cosine similarity of model weights) on each of these resampled pairs with the original model pair, where higher
068   values of the test statistic indicate the two models are related. Crucially, we leverage symmetries in training
069   dynamics to simulate these copies without actually having to rerun the full training process, which would be
070   computationally prohibitive. In particular, because the output of a feedforward neural network is invariant to
071   permuting the indices of its hidden units and therefore training dynamics are (typically) permutation equivariant,
072   we can obtain an exact p-value by simply permuting the hidden units of each model and comparing the rank
073   of the test statistic on the original pair to the permuted pairs. Under the null hypothesis that the original two
074   models are independent, the permuted pairs will be exchangeable with the original pair and thus the normalized
075
      rank will be uniformly distributed in {1/(T + 1), ..., 1}, yielding a valid p-value.
076   We evaluate various test statistics—the most effective of which is cosine similarity over weights and
077   activations—on 21 models of the Llama 2 architecture Touvron et al. (2023), including 12 fine-tunes of Llama
078   2 and nine independently trained models, obtaining negligibly small p-values for all 69 non-independent model
079   pairs. Notably, our tests retain high power over different fine-tuning methods (e.g., different optimizers) and on
080   models extensively fine-tuned for many tokens from the base model such as Llemma Azerbayev et al. (2024),
081
      which was fine-tuned on an additional 750B tokens from Llama 2 (i.e., 37.5% of the Llama 2 training budget).
      These test statistics apply in principle to any model pair for which there exists a pair of layers sharing a com-
082
      mon architecture or even a pair of tensors sharing a common shape; thus, we are able to confirm that the leaked
083
      Miqu 70B model from Mistral derives from Llama 2 70B and also identify which layers of Llama 3.1 8B are
084   incorporated into Llama 3.2 1B and 3B.
085
086   In the adversarial setting, our exact tests are ineffective since an adversary can easily evade them by randomly
087
      permuting the hidden units of their model post-training. Moreover, our exact tests apply only to two models of
      the same architecture; thus, slight changes to the architecture of either model also break these tests. Motivated by
088
      these two shortcomings, we design a more robust test statistic that first aligns the hidden units of two models—
089
      which may each have different activation types and hidden dimensions—and and then computes some measure
090   of similarity between the aligned models. Due to the alignment step, we can no longer obtain exact p-values
091   with non-trivial power using this test statistic; however, empirically we find we can still reliably distinguish
092   independent and non-independent model pairs. In particular, we find this test statistic empirically behaves like a
093   p-value in the sense that it is close to uniformly distributed in [0, 1] for independent model pairs (and no smaller
094   than 0.024 across all 141 such pairs we test); meanwhile, it is at most 2.2e-308 (the threshold for numerical
095   underflow for a 64-bit float) for all dependent pairs we test (including those for which we simulate an adversary
096   by retraining entire layers from scratch).

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
100
      The work most closely related to ours is due to Zeng et al. (2024), who consider a similar problem; they develop
101
      various tests to determine whether a model is a fine-tune of another by computing the cosine similarity of the
102   products of certain weight matrices in both models. Their focus is on robustness to simple adversaries: these
103   matrix products are invariant to certain kinds of transformations of model weights that preserve model output,
104   and thus their tests are robust to these same transformations. However, we show by construction in D.1 that there
105   exist other such transformations that break all of their tests. Additionally, unlike Zeng et al. (2024), in the non-
106   robust setting we obtain exact p-values from our tests. Jin et al. (2024a) propose crafting specific queries that
107   are likely to produce different responses among independently trained models; their method does not require
      access to weights but is incapable of producing exact p-values.

108
        A separate line of work on model fingerprinting (Xu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024b; Yang &
109     Wu, 2024) aims to plant a secret signal in the weights of a model so that anyone who knows the secret key can
110     detect the fingerprint from query access to the model (or derivatives thereof such as fine-tunes). For example, Xu
111     et al. (2024) propose fingerprinting a model by fine-tuning on some secret random string; fingerprint detection
112     then resolves to prompting a putative fingerprinted model with a prefix of the string. Unlike Xu et al. (2024), we
113     do not intervene on the training process of the models we test; however, we do require access to model weights.
114     The fingerprint of Xu et al. (2024) is only detectable with the secret key yet is easily removable by anyone who
115     knows the key; thus, their work does not enable third-party testing of model provenance as do our methods.
116     Another separate line of work on text watermarking aims to attribute model-generated text by planting a wa-
117     termark when sampling text from the model (Christ et al., 2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2024; Kuditipudi et al.,
118     2024; Aaronson & Kirchner, 2023). Because it intervenes on sampling, text watermarking is inapplicable to
119     open-weight models, which are the focus of both model fingerprinting and our setting.

124
125     Let f : Θ × X → Y denote a model mapping parameters θ ∈ Θ and an input X ∈ X to an output f (X; θ) ∈ Y.
126     We represent a model training or fine-tuning process as a learning algorithm A : Θ → P(Θ), which takes in
127     an initial parameter θ0 ∈ Θ (e.g., either a random initialization or, in the case of fine-tuning, a base model)
128     and induces a distribution over final parameters. In the context of deep learning, some examples of sources of
129     randomness in a learning algorithm (aside from initialization) include the ordering of minibatches and the use
130
        of dropout. Note that A subsumes the choice of training data; our methods make no assumptions on the training
        data, so we encapsulate it along with other design decisions in the learning algorithm.
131
132     Given two models θ1 ∼ A1 (θ10 ) and θ2 ∼ A2 (θ20 ) with initial parameters θ10 , θ20 ∼ P for some joint distribution
133     P ∈ P(Θ × Θ), our goal is to test the null hypothesis
134                                                       H0 : θ1 ⊥ θ2 ,                                                (1)
135
136     where ⊥ denotes statistical independence, or the standard definition of independence of two random variables.
137
        One example of a case where θ1 and θ2 might not be independent is if θ2 is fine-tuned from θ1 , since in this
        case we would have θ20 = θ1 . Indeed, in practice we expect H0 to obtain whenever θ1 and θ2 had independent
138
        random initializations, i.e., when θ10 ⊥ θ20 .

151     We describe our testing framework for computing p-values against this null hypothesis in Algorithm 1
152     (PERMTEST), where we simulate T independent copies of a model by applying a collection of transformations to
153     the model weights. The validity of these p-values rests on these transformations satisfying certain assumptions
154     with respect to the learning algorithm and random initialization which produced the original model that we
155     capture in the following two definitions.
156     Definition 1. Let Π ⊂ Θ → Θ. A distribution P ∈ P(Θ) is Π-invariant if for θ ∼ P and any π ∈ Π, the
157     parameters θ and π(θ) are identically distributed.
158     Definition 2. Let Π ⊂ Θ → Θ. Consider any π ∈ Π and θ0 ∈ Θ, with θ̄ ∼ A(θ0 ), θ = π(θ̄) and θ′ ∼
159     A(π(θ0 )). A learning algorithm A is Π-equivariant if and only if θ and θ′ are identically distributed.
160
161     In principle, if we know the learning algorithm A and initialization distribution P that produced θ1 , i.e., θ1 ∼
        A(θ10 ) for θ10 ∼ P , we could obtain an exact p-value with an arbitrary test statistic by repeating the training


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      process to obtain T independent copies of θ1 and comparing the test statistic with the original θ1 to these
163   independent copies; of course, this would be completely impractical in practice due to computational costs. The
164   main idea underlying PERMTEST is that if all we know is that A is Π-equivariant and P is a Π-invariant, then we
165   can simulate an identically distributed copy θ1′ of θ1 by letting θ1′ = π(θ1 ) for any π ∈ Π, which allows us to
166   efficiently compute an exact p-value without actually repeating the training process of θ1 . In effect, Definitions 1
167   and 2 imply that π commutes with A: sampling θ1′ = π(θ1 ) for θ1 ∼ A(θ10 ) is equivalent to θ1′ ∼ A(π(θ10 )).
168   We formalize this intuition in the following theorem and subsequently give a concrete toy example; importantly,
169   the result of the theorem holds (under the null hypothesis) without any assumptions on θ2 , meaning that we can
170   have confidence in our test even if we do not trust the provider of θ2 .

172   learning algorithm and let P be a Π-invariant distribution. Let θ1 , θ2 ∈ Θ be independent random variables,

175   Proof. From our assumptions on A and P and the fact that {πt }Tt=1 are independently drawn, it follows that

177   {(πt (θ1 ), θ2 )}Tt=1 comprises T independent copies of (θ1 , θ2 ), and so the claim follows by symmetry.
178
179   Standard initialization schemes for feedforward networks are symmetric over the hidden units of the network,
180   and so one example of a class of transformations with respect to which any such initialization is invariant is
181   the set of permutations over the hidden units of the network. Moreover, the gradient of the model’s output with
182
      respect to the hidden units is permutation equivariant; thus, any learning algorithm whose update rule is itself a

183

200   We have shown PERMTEST produces a valid p-value regardless of the test statistic ϕ we use. The sole objective
201   then in designing a test statistic is to achieve high statistical power: we would like pb = PERMTEST(θ1 , θ2 ) to be
202   small when θ1 and θ2 are not independent. The test statistics we introduce in this section apply to any model pair
203   sharing the same architecture. In particular, the test statistics all share the following form based on Algorithm 2
204   (MATCH): for m, n ∈ N and M : Θ → Rn×m , let
205                                 ϕM (θ1 , θ2 ) := ρ(MATCH(M (θ1 ), M (θ2 )), [1, ..., h]).                          (2)

207
208   Equation (2) is applicable to any model architecture Θ for which we can define a suitable matrix valued function
209   of model parameters. Taking various such functions M yields different test statistics. We focus our experiments
210   on Transformer models consisting of a series of L Transformer blocks that each contain an MLP module, and we
211   take M (θ) to be either the first-layer (i.e., up projection) weights or the hidden-layer activations of one of these
212   MLP modules. In particular, let U (ℓ) (θ) ∈ Rh×d denote the first layer up projection weights of the MLP module
213   in the ℓ-th block, where h is the hidden dimension and d is the input dimension, and let H (ℓ) (θ) ∈ Rh×(N ·n)
214   denote the hidden activations that obtain from passing N input sequences of length n, X ∈ RN ×n×d to the
215   same MLP module (the test is valid for any X; we will specify later how we choose X in our experiments). The
      two main test statistics we will employ in our experiments are ϕU (ℓ) and ϕH (ℓ) . In both these cases, the idea is

280   3.3     ROBUSTNESS TO ADVERSARIAL MANIPULATION
281
      It is easy to fool the tests we have proposed thus far by applying simple transformations to model parameters
282
      that do not change model output: in particular, an adversary can fool ϕU (ℓ) and ϕH (ℓ) by randomly permuting the
283
      hidden units of the corresponding MLP.1 Motivated by this shortcoming, we design a robust test that aligns the
284   hidden layer activations of a particular layer between the two models and evaluates the quality of the alignment.
285   Notably, we can compute the alignment between two layers with different numbers of hidden units; thus, unlike
286   the tests in Section 3.2, we can apply our robust test to any model pair regardless of whether the two models
287   share the same architecture. This statistic is also robust to the transformation described in Appendix D.1 that
288   breaks the invariants proposed by Zeng et al. (2024).
289
      We first describe a test specific to architectures with Gated Linear Units (GLUs) (Dauphin et al., 2016)—a
290   category which includes the Llama model architecture among many other language models—and then describe
291   a general extension of this test to any architecture involving hidden activations. We evaluate both the GLU-
292   specific version and the more general extension in our experiments.
293

      5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530
531
      In this paper, we propose and analyze various methods for detecting model independence in open-weight lan-
      guage models. Our robust method accurately predicts model relationships without training-time interventions
532
      while allowing some robustness to adversarial attacks with strong results within the Llama model family. Our
533
      methods demonstrate resilience to certain adversarial techniques, and we also investigate these methods across
534   diverse model architectures. We demonstrate how our techniques provide fine-grain information about how one
535   model may be derived from another and how they are robust to adversarial attacks. Yet, we do not exhaustively
536   disprove the existence of a successful adversarial attack, and note that our tests are susceptible to false nega-
537   tives. There is also an open question as to whether reliably differentiating between fine-tunes of the same base
538   model to reconstruct a complete “family tree” of model lineage is possible (e.g. infer Llemma is a direct fine-
539   tune of CodeLlama) (Yax et al., 2024). Furthermore, the fundamental question of whether robustness against
      adversarial attacks is solvable with exact guarantees warrants further exploration.

852         1. πemb (θ): Applying an embedding permutation πemb ∈ Rdemb ×demb by left or right multiplying all relevant

1547   H.3     D ISTILLING M ODELS WITHOUT A GLU MLP
1548
       Finally, we show it is possible to extend the robust statistic ϕMATCH to models that do not have a GLU MLP as
1549   well. In particular, we distill any other MLP or feedforward network with a GLU MLP. In principle, one could
1550   replace any series of layers mapping inputs to activations with a GLU MLP from Definition 3. We reinitialize
1551   the layers with a GLU MLP, G, U, D. Then with the same setup as the MLP retraining from Section 4.2.1, we
1552   sample inputs and compute the outputs from the original model layers, and minimize MSE loss over the outputs.
1553
       We retrain the first MLP of both manupande21/GPT2 PMC and openai-community/gpt2, where the
1554
       former is a finetune of the latter (Radford et al. (2019)). These models use a standard 2-layer FFN (Example 1)
1555                                                                  (1)
       rather than a GLU MLP. After 10K training steps, we run ϕMATCH on the first MLP, which yields a p-value of
1556
       7.955e-83, showing that ϕMATCH may be extended to other architectures as well via distilling.
