                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) is an emerging and challenging task
010
               where a model trained in a source domain must adapt to continuously changing
011            conditions during testing, without access to the original source data. CTTA is
012            prone to error accumulation due to uncontrollable domain shifts, leading to blurred
013            decision boundaries between categories. Existing CTTA methods primarily focus
014            on suppressing domain shifts, which proves inadequate during the unsupervised test
015            phase. In contrast, we introduce a novel approach that guides rather than suppresses
016            these shifts. Specifically, we propose Controllable Continual Test-Time Adaptation
017            (C-CoTTA), which explicitly prevents any single category from encroaching on
018            others, thereby mitigating the mutual influence between categories caused by
019
               uncontrollable shifts. Moreover, our method reduces the sensitivity of model to
               domain transformations, thereby minimizing the magnitude of category shifts.
020
               Extensive quantitative experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method,
021
               while qualitative analyses, such as t-SNE plots, confirm the theoretical validity
022            of our approach. Our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/C-
023            CoTTA-BC4F/.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) (Wang et al., 2022) is becoming an emerging field, which
029   explores the adaptability of any machine learning model during test time in dynamic environments.
030   The primary objective of CTTA is to enable a pretrained model to adapt to continuously changing
031   scenarios, where the distribution of data shifts over time. CTTA is practical in many long-term
032   intelligent applications, such as autopilot (Hu et al., 2022; O’Kelly, 2021; Chen, 2020), monitoring
033
      (Geiger et al., 2013), medical image analysis (Chen et al., 2023; Gonzalez et al., 2020), where models
      need to remain robust and accurate against possible changes over extended periods.
034
035   The main challenge of CTTA is the accumulation of errors caused by the lack of real labels and the
036   continuous domain shifts. Existing methods mainly avoid error accumulation by using strategies
037   such as Mean Teacher (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017), augmentation-averaged predictions (Wang et al.,
038   2022; Lyu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), and selecting reliable samples (Yang et al., 2023; Niloy
039
      et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) to minimize the impact of domain shift. However,
      these methods focus on suppressing the shifts, few methods explicitly attempt to guide or control
040
      the shifts. This is because the lack of labels in the target domain and the inability to obtain source
041
      domain data in CTTA. We evaluate this on CIFAR10C, as shown in Fig. 1(a), we find that in the
042   domain adaptation process, multiple categories to lean towards a confusion region. In fact, CTTA
043   meets continuous unknown domain shifts, making this shift almost uncontrollable. This phenomenon
044   will worsen with the changing domain over time, resulting in error accumulation.
045
      Therefore, we hypothesize that instead of attempting to suppress the shift, guiding or controlling the
046
      shift to remain class-separable may also achieve effective adaptation. On top of this, the main focus
047
      of this paper is to study how to achieve controllable domain shift in CTTA. Generally, controlling
048   domain shifts means that controlling the direction of the shift in feature space. To achieve this, we
049   first need to accurately represent the direction of domain shift. Inspired by the interpretable machine
050   learning (Kim et al., 2018), we represent shift directions using the tool of Concept Ativation Vectors
051   (CAV) (Pahde et al., 2022), which represents the transformation path from one concepts to another.
052   In CTTA, the CAV can be represented by the vector from one prototype to another. With CAV, for a
053   specific category, we obtain its domain shift direction by subtracting the category prototype of the
      target domain in the feature space from the category prototype of the source domain.

      To further control domain shift, we construct the Domain Shift Controlling Loss (DSCL) loss and
071   the Class Shift Controlling Loss (DSCL) loss. DSCL refers to controlling the shift of the overall
072   domain by constraining the model’s sensitivity in that direction, thus reducing the impact of domain

076   boundaries. Extensive experiments are conducted on three large-scale benchmark datasets to validate
077   the effectiveness of the proposed C-CoTTA framework in various challenging and realistic scenarios.
078   Our contributions are three-fold:
079
080
      (1) We evaluate and find that only suppressing domain shifts is insufficient, which may lead to
          blurred classification boundaries. In contrast, we propose to guide and control shifts to keep the
081
          class-separability.
082
      (2) We propose a simple and effective direction representation based on Concept Activation Vectors
083
          (CAV) in interpretable machine learning, which utilizes the difference between two prototypes in
084
          the feature space.
085
      (3) We propose to explicitly control the direction of specific category bias by preventing any category
086
          from leaning towards other categories, in order to prevent the blurring of classification boundaries;
087       at the same time, by reducing the sensitivity of the model to domain shift, we control the overall
088       domain shift to alleviate the impact of domain shift on domain adaptation.

091   2     R ELATED WORK
092
093   2.1   C ONTINUAL T EST-T IME A DAPTATION
094
      Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) (Wang et al., 2022) is an emerging paradigm within the
095
      machine learning community designed to address the dynamic nature of real-world data distributions.
096   Unlike traditional Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) (Jain & Learned-Miller, 2011; Sun et al., 2020; Wang
097   et al., 2020), which typically assumes a fixed target domain in a source-free and online manner, CTTA
098   operates under the assumption that the target domain may change over time. The main challenge in
099   CTTA is the potential for catastrophic forgetting and error accumulation. During the test time, as the
100   model adapts to new distributions, it risks losing previously learned knowledge, which can result in a
101   degradation in performance known as catastrophic forgetting (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019). Moreover,
102   the utilization of pseudo-labels derived from the model’s own predictions can introduce errors, which
103   may accumulate over time (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Wang et al., 2022), especially when there are frequent
104
      domain shifts.
105   To address the challenges of error accumulation in CTTA, researchers have developed various
106   strategies. A number of works (Wang et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023) employ
107   augmentation-averaged predictions for the teacher model to boost the teacher’s confidence, while
      others (Chakrabarty et al., 2023; Döbler et al., 2023) add perturbations to the student to enhance the

108
      model’s robustness. Various methods (Yang et al., 2023; Niloy et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2022; Wang
109   et al., 2024) focus on selecting reliable samples to eliminate the impact of misclassified samples
110   on domain adaptation. As to the challenge of catastrophic forgetting, Wang et al. (Wang et al.,
111   2022) and Brahma et al. (Brahma & Rai, 2023) believe that the source model is more reliable, thus
112   they designed to restore the source parameters. While these studies address the CTTA issue at the
113   model level, other research efforts (Gan et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023) leverage
114   visual domain prompts or a limited subset of parameters to extract ongoing target domain knowledge.
115   However, these approaches primarily focus on suppressing domain shift and there are few methods
116   that explicitly attempt to guide or control domain shift.
117
118   2.2   C ONCEPT ACTIVATION V ECTORS
119
120   Concept Activation Vectors (CAVs) is an interpretability tool for explaining decision-making pro-
121   cesses in deep learning models. Originally, the authors of Kim et al. (2018) define CAV as the
122   normal to a hyperplane that separates examples without a concept from examples with a concept in
123   the model’s latent activations. This hyperplane is commonly computed by solving a classification
124
      problem, for example, using Support Vector Machines (SVMs) Anders et al. (2022), ridge (Cortes
      & Vapnik, 1995), lasso (Pfau et al., 2021) or logistic regression (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). Given
125
      its ability to effectively orient concepts, CAVs have been employed for a plethora of tasks in recent
126
      years, such as concept sensitivity testing (Kim et al., 2018), model correction for shortcut removal
127   (Anders et al., 2022; Pahde et al., 2023; Dreyer et al., 2023), knowledge discovery by investigation
128   of internal model states (McGrath et al., 2022), and training of post-hoc concept bottleneck mod-
129   els (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). However, common regression-based methods tend to deviate from
130   the true conceptual direction due to factors such as noise in the data (Haufe et al., 2014). To that
131   end, signal-pattern-based CAVs (referred to signalCAVs) have been proposed (Pahde et al., 2022),
132   which are more robust against noise (Weber et al., 2023; Dreyer et al., 2024; Samek, 2023; Biecek &
133   Samek, 2024). However, during test-time, we may not have access to all samples of a prototype, and
134   due to the lack of true labels, misclassified samples may contaminate the prototype. Therefore, the
135
      construction of a prototype is different from interpretable machine learning.

150   3.2   R EPRESENTING S HIFT VIA CAV
151
152   Domain shift refers to the distribution shift of each class that occurs in the feature space, generally
153   caused by differences between the target domain distribution in the testing phase and the source
154   domain distribution in the training phase. Therefore, how to represent the domain shift direction in
155   the feature space in CTTA is a challenge.
156   In the field of interpretable machine learning (Kim et al., 2018; McGrath et al., 2022), CAV (Kim
157   et al., 2018) refers to the normal to a hyperplane that separates examples without a concept from
158   examples with a concept in the model’s latent activations feature space. CAV is widely used in areas
159   such as model correction for shortcut removal (Anders et al., 2022; Pahde et al., 2023; Dreyer et al.,
160   2023). The concept in CAV generally refers to high-level semantic information, such as whether
161   there are a large number of striped structures in an image. For example, in the concept of stripe, the
      label for features extracted from images with stripes is 1, and without stripes is 0.

      3.3   C ONTROL OF SHIFT
233
234   We control the shift at domain and class levels. On one hand, the shift of different categories are
235   influenced by the overall domain shift, which is represented by closely aligned feature distributions.
236   This means that after domain shift, the distances between different categories in the feature space
237   become closer. This finding is similar to the conclusions drawn in studies such as Xu et al. (2019);
238   Kondo (2022); Rahman et al. (2021), where researchers found that the feature norms in the target
239   domain are relatively small. On the other hand, the shift of different categories is related to their
240
      characteristics and may show different shifts.
241   Domain-level shift controlling. First, we propose to control the shift of the overall domain at the
242   domain level. Specifically, we model the shift sensitivity by considering the gradient of the model
243   output h(g(xi )) with respect to the feature map g(xi ), and then combine it with the overall domain
244   shift direction vs→t . We can then reduce the sensitivity of the model to domain shift by constraining
245   the direction gradient as follows:

526   5       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
527
528   In this work, we introduce C-CoTTA, a novel framework designed to prevent any category from lean-
529   ing towards other categories by explicitly controlling the offset direction to avoid fuzzy classification
530   boundaries, and reduce the sensitivity of the model in the domain shift direction to reduce the impact
531   of domain shift on domain adaptation. This fills the gap left by traditional methods, which can only
532   mitigate the impact of domain drift. C-CoTTA can explicitly control domain shift, opening up a new
533   solution pathway for CTTA. Through extensive quantitative experiments and qualitative analysis,
534   such as t-SNE plots, we demonstrate the effectiveness and theoretical validity of C-CoTTA.
535   Our method also has certain limitations. During test-time, we may not have access to all samples of a
536   specific domain prototype, and due to the lack of true labels, misclassified samples may contaminate
537   the prototype. As a result, the representation of the prototype may be poor, further affecting
538   the accuracy of the constructed direction, leading to ineffective or even erroneous domain shift
539   control. Therefore, in the future, addressing how to obtain high-quality prototypes and directional
      representations is a task that needs attention.

783   D          R ELIABLE S AMPLE S ELECTION A NALYSIS
784
785   In our control category offset method, in order to reduce the contamination of category prototypes by
786   misclassified samples and thus affect the control of category shift direction, we remove samples with
787   entropy values exceeding the predefined threshold E0 , which is set as 0.4 × ln C based on Niu et al.
788   (2022). We have verified the rationality of this operation through experiments. As shown in Fig. 7,
789   when the threshold is large, the effectiveness of the CSCL method deteriorates. This may be because
790   the conditions are too loose, leading to a large number of misclassified samples when calculating
791
      the prototype. On the other hand, when the threshold is small, the CSCL method also deteriorates.
      This may be because the conditions are too strict, resulting in too few samples used to calculate the
792
      prototype, making the generated prototype not representative.
