011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep multimodal learning has shown remarkable success by leveraging contrastive
014            learning to capture explicit one-to-one relations across modalities. However, real-
015
               world data often exhibits shared relations beyond simple pairwise associations. We
               propose M3CoL, a Multimodal Mixup Contrastive Learning approach to capture
016
               nuanced shared relations inherent in multimodal data. Our key contribution is a
017
               Mixup-based contrastive loss that learns robust representations by aligning mixed
018            samples from one modality with their corresponding samples from other modalities
019            thereby capturing shared relations between them. For multimodal classification
020            tasks, we introduce a framework that integrates a fusion module with unimodal
021            prediction modules for auxiliary supervision during training, complemented by
022            our proposed Mixup-based contrastive loss. Through extensive experiments on
023            diverse datasets (N24News, ROSMAP, BRCA, and Food-101), we demonstrate
024            that M3CoL effectively captures shared multimodal relations and generalizes
025            across domains. It outperforms state-of-the-art methods on N24News, ROSMAP,
026
               and BRCA, while achieving comparable performance on Food-101. Our work
               highlights the significance of learning shared relations for robust multimodal
027
               learning, opening up promising avenues for future research.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The way we perceive the world is shaped by various modalities, such as language, vision, audio,
033   and more. In the era of abundant and accessible multimodal data, it is increasingly crucial to
034   equip artificial intelligence with multimodal capabilities (Baltrušaitis et al., 2018). At the heart of
035   advancements in multimodal learning is contrastive learning, which maximizes similarity for positive
036   pairs and minimizes it for negative pairs, making it practical for multimodal representation learning.
037   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is a prominent example that employs contrastive learning to understand
038   the direct link between paired modalities and seamlessly maps images and text into a shared space for
039
      cross-modal understanding, which can be later utilized for tasks such as retrieval and classification.
      However, traditional contrastive learning methods often overlook shared relationships between
040
      samples across different modalities, which can result in the learning of representations that are not
041
      fully optimized for capturing the underlying connections between diverse data modalities. These
042   methods primarily focus on distinguishing between positive and negative pairs of samples, typically
043   treating each instance as an independent entity. They tend to disregard the rich, shared relational
044   information that could exist between samples within and across modalities. This limited focus can
045   prevent the model from leveraging valuable contextual information, such as semantic similarities or
046   complementary patterns, which can enhance robust representation learning. Consequently, this can
047   lead to suboptimal performance in downstream tasks that require optimized shared representations,
048   such as image-text alignment, cross-modal retrieval, or multimodal fusion tasks.
049   As shown in the left panel of Figure 1, classical contrastive learning approach assumes perfect
050   one-to-one relations between modalities, which is rare in real-world data. For example, shared
051   elements in images or text can relate even across separate samples, as illustrated by the elements
052   like “tomato sauce” and “basil” in Figure 1. Our approach, illustrated in the right panel of Figure 1,
053   goes beyond simple pairwise alignment by capturing shared relationships across mixed samples. By
      creating newer data points through convex combinations of data points our method more effectively

      Our approach builds upon the success of data augmentation techniques such as Mixup (Zhang et al.,
078
      2017) and their variants (Yun et al., 2019; Cubuk et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2019), which have
079
      proven beneficial for enhancing learned feature spaces, improving both robustness and performance.
080   Mixup trains models on synthetic data created through convex combinations of two datapoint-label
081   pairs (Chapelle et al., 2000). These techniques are particularly valuable in low sample settings, as
082   they help prevent overfitting and the learning of ineffective shortcuts (Chen et al., 2020; Robinson
083   et al., 2021), common in contrastive learning. Building on the success of recent Mixup strategies
084   (Shen et al., 2022; Thulasidasan et al., 2019; Verma et al., 2019) and MixCo (Kim et al., 2020),
085   we introduce M3Co, a novel approach that significantly adapts and enhances contrastive learning
086   principles to complex multimodal settings. M3Co modifies the CLIP loss to effectively handle
087   multimodal scenarios, addressing the problem of instance discrimination, where models overly
088
      focus on distinguishing individual instances instead of capturing relationships between modalities.
      By leveraging convex combinations of data for contrastive learning, M3Co eliminates instance
089
      discrimination and enhances robust representation learning by capturing shared relations. These
090
      combinations serve as structured noise and treated as positive pairs with their corresponding samples
091   from other modalities. Our experimental results demonstrate enhanced ability to capture shared
092   relations enabling improvements in performance and generalization across a range of multimodal
093   classification tasks.
094
      Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
095
096
             • We propose M3Co, a multimodal contrastive loss (Eq. 8) that utilizes mixed samples to
097            effectively capture shared relationships across different modalities. By going beyond
098            traditional pairwise alignment methods, M3Co makes representations more consistent with
099            the complex, intertwined relationships usually observed in real-world data.
100
101          • We introduce a multimodal learning framework (Figure 2) consisting of unimodal prediction
102
               modules, a fusion module, and a novel Mixup-based contrastive loss. Our proposed method
               is modality-agnostic, allowing for flexible application across various types of data, and
103
               continuously updates the representations necessary for accurate and consistent predictions.
104
105          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our methodology by evaluating it on four public
106            multimodal classification benchmark datasets from different domains: two image-text
107            datasets, N24News and Food-101, and two medical datasets, ROSMAP and BRCA (Table 1,
               2, 3). Our approach outperforms baseline models, especially on smaller datasets.

119   data. This comprehensive approach enhances the understanding of multimodal data, improving

      Datasets. We evaluate our approach on four diverse publicly available multimodal classification
      datasets: N24News (Wang et al., 2022), Food-101 (Wang et al., 2015), ROSMAP (Wang et al.,
262
      2021a), and BRCA (Wang et al., 2021a). N24News and Food-101 are both bimodal image-text
263
      classification datasets. Food-101 is a food classification dataset, where each sample is linked
264   with a recipe description gathered from web pages and an associated image. N24News is a news
265   classification dataset consisting of four text types (Abstract, Caption, Heading, and Body) along
266   with the corresponding images. Following other works (Zou et al., 2023), we use the first three text
267   types for our experiments. ROSMAP and BRCA are publicly available multimodal medical datasets,
268   each containing three modalities: DNA methylation, miRNA expression, and mRNA expression.
269   ROSMAP is an Alzeihmer’s diagnosis dataset, while BRCA is used for breast invasive carcinoma
      PAM50 subtype classification. Appendix A.2 provides information about the train-val-test splits.

      Evaluation Metrics. The evaluation metric used for N24News and Food-101 is classification
271   accuracy (ACC). For BRCA, we report accuracy (ACC), macro-averaged F1 score (MF1), and

279   MLPs as feature encoders for each modality, and two layer MLPs with ReLU activations as classifiers.

483
484   Contrastive Learning. Contrastive learning has driven significant progress in unimodal and multi-
485   modal representation learning by distinguishing between similar (positive) and dissimilar (negative)
      pairs. In multimodal contexts, cross-modal contrastive techniques align representations from different


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      modalities (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Kamath et al., 2021), with approaches like CrossCLR
487   (Zolfaghari et al., 2021) and GMC (Poklukar et al., 2022) focusing on global and modality-specific
488   representations. Contrastive learning approaches for paired image-text data, such as CLIP (Radford
489   et al., 2021), ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), and BASIC (Pham et al., 2023), have demonstrated remarkable
490   success across diverse vision-language tasks. Subsequent works have aimed to enhance the efficacy
491   and data efficiency of CLIP training, incorporating self-supervised techniques (SLIP (Mu et al., 2022),
492   DeCLIP (Li et al., 2021)) and fine-grained alignment (FILIP (Li et al., 2023)). The CLIP framework
493   relies on data augmentations to prevent overfitting and the learning of ineffective shortcuts (Chen
494   et al., 2020; Robinson et al., 2021), a common practice in contrastive learning.
495   Unimodal and Multimodal Data Augmentation. Data augmentation has been integral to the
496   success of deep learning, especially for small training sets. In computer vision, techniques have
497   evolved from basic transformations to advanced methods like Cutout (DeVries & Taylor, 2017),
498   Mixup (Zhang et al., 2017), CutMix (Yun et al., 2019), and automated approaches (Cubuk et al.,
499   2019; 2020). NLP augmentation includes paraphrasing, token replacement (Zhang et al., 2015; Jiao
500   et al., 2019), and noise injection (Yan et al., 2019). Multimodal data augmentation, primarily focused
501
      on vision-text tasks, has seen limited exploration, with approaches including back-translation for
      visual question answering (Tang et al., 2020), text generation from images (Wang et al., 2021b), and
502
      external knowledge querying for cross-modal retrieval (Gur et al., 2021). MixGen (Hao et al., 2023)
503
      generates new image-text pairs through image interpolation and text concatenation. In contrast, our
504   proposed augmentation technique focusing on the early training phase is fully automatic, applicable
505   to arbitrary modalities, and designed to leverage inherent shared relations in multimodal data.
506
507
      Relation to Mixup. Mixup (Zhang et al., 2017), a pivotal regularization strategy, enhances model
      robustness and generalization by generating synthetic samples through convex combinations of
508
      existing data points. Originally introduced for computer vision, it has been adapted to NLP by
509
      applying the technique to text embeddings (Guo et al., 2019). Our proposed augmentation differs
510   from Mixup in several key aspects: it is designed for multi-modal data, takes inputs from different
511   modalities, and does not rely on one-hot label encodings. By extending the Mixup paradigm to
512   complex, multi-modal scenarios and focusing on the early training phase, our method broadens its
513   applicability while leveraging inherent shared relations in multimodal data.

      6   C ONCLUSION
519
520
521   Aligning representations across modalities presents significant challenges due to the complex, often
522   non-bijective relationships in real-world multimodal data (Liang et al., 2022a). These relationships
523   can involve many-to-many mappings or even lack clear associations, as exemplified by linguistic
524   ambiguities and synonymy in vision-language tasks. We propose M3Co, a novel contrastive-based
525
      alignment method that captures shared relations beyond explicit pairwise associations by aligning
      mixed samples from one modality with corresponding samples from others. Our approach incor-
526
      porates Mixup-based contrastive learning, introducing controlled noise that mirrors the inherent
527
      variability in multimodal data, thus enhancing robustness and generalizability. The M3Co loss,
528   combined with an architecture leveraging unimodal and fusion modules, enables continuous updating
529   of representations necessary for accurate predictions and deeper integration of modalities. Our
530   method generalizes across diverse domains, including image-text, high-dimensional multi-omics,
531   and data with more than two modalities. Experiments on four public multimodal classification
532   datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in learning robust representations that surpass
533   traditional multimodal alignment techniques.
534   M3CoL demonstrates promising results, yet faces optimization challenges due to the inherent
535   limitations of multimodal frameworks, particularly extended training times on large-scale datasets
536   like Food-101. The method’s modality-agnostic nature and effective use of Mixup augmentation
537   suggest its potential adaptability to various multimodal tasks, especially where data augmentation
538   and learning real-world inter-modal relationships are crucial. Our method can easily be adapted to
539   multimodal tasks such as visual question answering and information retrieval. These advancements
      will likely broaden M3CoL’s impact in multimodal research.
