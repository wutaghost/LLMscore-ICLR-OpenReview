011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Information retrieval across different languages is an increasingly important chal-
014            lenge in natural language processing. Recent approaches based on multilingual
015
               pre-trained language models have achieved remarkable success, yet they often
               optimize for either monolingual, cross-lingual, or multilingual retrieval perfor-
016
               mance at the expense of others. This paper proposes a novel hybrid batch training
017
               strategy to simultaneously improve zero-shot retrieval performance across mono-
018            lingual, cross-lingual, and multilingual settings while mitigating language bias.
019            The approach fine-tunes multilingual language models using a mix of monolingual
020            and cross-lingual question-answer pair batches sampled based on dataset size.
021            Experiments on XQuAD-R, MLQA-R, and MIRACL benchmark datasets show
022            that the proposed method consistently achieves comparable or superior results
023            in zero-shot retrieval across various languages and retrieval tasks compared to
024            monolingual-only or cross-lingual-only training. Hybrid batch training also sub-
025            stantially reduces language bias in multilingual retrieval compared to monolingual
026
               training. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach for
               learning language-agnostic representations that enable strong zero-shot retrieval
027
               performance across diverse languages.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Information retrieval (IR) across different languages is an increasingly important challenge in natural
033   language processing. However, optimizing information retrieval systems for multilingual scenarios is
034   not a straightforward task, as it requires considering multiple distinct retrieval settings, each with
035   its own set of challenges and requirements, including monolingual retrieval, cross-lingual retrieval,
036   and multilingual retrieval. Monolingual retrieval refers to the task of retrieving documents in the
037   same language as the user’s query, focusing on developing effective ranking algorithms and relevance
038   matching techniques. Cross-lingual retrieval involves queries and documents in different languages,
039
      requiring the system to bridge the language gap by employing techniques such as query translation,
      document translation, or cross-lingual representation learning. Multilingual retrieval requires the
040
      creation of a single ranked list of documents in multiple languages for a given query, addressing
041
      challenges such as language disparity, varying document lengths, and potential differences in content
042   quality and relevance across languages while providing users with a unified and coherent ranked list
043   of results.
044
045
      Recent approaches to multilingual information retrieval have leveraged multilingual pre-trained
      language models such as mBERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020) to encode
046
      queries and documents (Karpukhin et al., 2020). While these models can transfer relevance matching
047
      capabilities across languages, their performance tends to underperform on cross-lingual retrieval
048   benchmarks due to the lack of explicit alignment between languages during pretraining (Zhang et al.,
049   2023). LaREQA, introduced by (Roy et al., 2020), targets strong alignment, requiring semantically
050   related pairs across languages to be closer in representation space than unrelated pairs within the same
051   language. (Roy et al., 2020) found that augmenting the training data through machine translation
052   proved effective in achieving robust alignment for MLIR. However, this approach compromises
053   performance in monolingual retrieval tasks. Alternative approaches using parallel corpora, such as
      InfoXLM (Chi et al., 2021) and LaBSE (Feng et al., 2022), have been proposed to align sentences

069   remains a substantial challenge. To address these limitations, (Lawrie et al., 2023) introduced a
070   Multilingual Translate-Train approach using translated datasets, (Hu et al., 2023) proposed contrastive
071   losses to align representations and remove language-specific information, (Huang et al., 2023a)
072   presented a knowledge distillation framework for multilingual dense retrieval, and (Lin et al., 2023a)
073
      extended Aggretriever (Lin et al., 2023b) for multilingual retrieval using semantic and lexical features.
      While the methods proposed in (Hu et al., 2023) and (Huang et al., 2023a) attempt to mitigate
074
      language bias, we raise the question: Is there a straightforward approach that addresses this issue by
075
      modifying the training data batches without necessitating the introduction of loss functions or new
076   architectural components?
077
078   In this paper, we propose a novel hybrid batch training strategy that simultaneously optimizes retrieval
079
      performance across monolingual, cross-lingual, and multilingual settings while also mitigating
      language bias. Our approach fine-tunes multilingual language models using a balanced mix of
080
      monolingual and cross-lingual question-answer pair batches. We collect a diverse set of English
081
      question-answer datasets and use machine translation to generate parallel question-answer pairs
082   across several languages, including low-resource languages where parallel corpora may be limited
083   (Fan et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Costa-jussà et al., 2022). Our hybrid batch training approach
084   significantly reduces the language bias that hinders the performance of multilingual retrieval systems
085   by training the models on a diverse set of language pairs and encouraging the learning of language-
086   agnostic representations. This mitigates the tendency of models to favor certain languages over
087   others, ensuring that documents from multiple languages are fairly ranked based on their relevance
088   to the query, regardless of the language. Extensive experiments on XQuAD-R, MLQA-R, and
089   MIRACL benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed approach, with models
090
      trained using the hybrid batch strategy consistently achieving competitive results in zero-shot retrieval
      across various languages and retrieval tasks, outperforming models trained with only monolingual or
091
      cross-lingual data. Our approach also exhibits strong zero-shot generalization to unseen languages
092
      not included in the training data, highlighting its potential to expand the linguistic coverage of
093   multilingual information retrieval systems.

142   2.2   BATCH S AMPLING
143
144   Baseline Batch Sampling. We study the following training batching procedures introduced by
145
      (Roy et al., 2020). (i) Monolingual batching (coined as X-X-mono model) creates each batch with
      mono language, where all the triplets consist of queries and passages in the same language. Note
146
      that we sample the language used to create the batch equally among all possible languages in our
147
      training data. (ii) Cross-lingual batching (coined as X-Y model) creates each batch, where all the
148   triplets consist of queries and passages in different languages. Monolingual batching only focuses
149   on contrastive learning for query-passage pairs in the same languages while cross-lingual batching
150   mines positives and in-batch negatives from diverse languages.
151
      As shown in (Roy et al., 2020), the X-Y model is more effective in cross-lingual retrieval scenarios
152
      and shows reduced language bias; however, the X-X-mono surpasses the X-Y model in monolingual
153
      retrieval. These results inspire us to explore whether simply combining the two batch sampling
154   approaches can achieve improvement in both monolingual and cross-lingual retrieval effectiveness.
155
156
157   Hybrid Batch Sampling. In this work, we propose to combine the two aforementioned baseline
158
      sampling strategies. Specifically, when creating batch training data, we set α and β = 1 − α as the
      respective probability of using monolingual and cross-lingual batching as shown in Fig. 2.1

      3     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
163
164
      This section presents the experimental setup for evaluating the proposed hybrid batch training strategy.
165
      We first discuss the training process, including datasets, and multilingual pre-trained models. Next,
166   we introduce the evaluation datasets and metrics used to assess the performance of the fine-tuned
167   models. Finally, we describe the evaluation settings for monolingual, cross-lingual, and multilingual
168   retrieval tasks.
169
170   3.1   T RAINING
171
172   Datasets. To conduct the study of batch sampling, parallel query-passage training pairs are required
173   such that we can construct cross-lingual triplets, where each query and its relevant (or irrelevant)
174   passage are in different languages. mMARCO (Bonifacio et al., 2021) is the only dataset with parallel
175   queries and passages across 14 languages. In our study, we further scale the size of training data by
176   translating the existing question-answering datasets. Specifically, we developed our in-house machine
177   translation pipeline to create parallel QA pairs for the monolingual datasets across nine languages:
178
      Arabic, Chinese, English, German, Hindi, Russian, Spanish, Thai, and Turkish. The additional
      training data used in our study include DuoRC (Saha et al., 2018), EntityQuestions (Sciavolino et al.,
179
      2021), Google NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), MFAQ (De Bruyn et al., 2021), Mr. Tydi (Zhang et al.,
180
      2021), NewsQA (Trischler et al., 2017), WikiQA (Yang et al., 2015), and Yahoo QA mined from
181   Yahoo Answers. Appendix A.1 provides comprehensive details about the training datasets.
182
183
      Training Setup. We apply the baseline and our proposed hybrid batching to fine-tune two rep-
184   resentative multilingual pre-trained models: (i) XLM-RoBERTa (XLM-R) (Conneau et al., 2020);
185   and (ii) language-agnostic BERT sentence embedding (LaBSE) (Feng et al., 2022). Model training
186   experiments were conducted using one NVIDIA A100-80 GB GPU. We fine-tune pre-trained models
187   using AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018) with weight decay set to 1e-2, a learning rate
188   of 3e-5, and a batch size of 100. We apply the early stopping (Prechelt, 1998) to select the model
189   checkpoint with the lowest validation loss on SQuADShifts dataset (Miller et al., 2020). Note that
190   the validation set used for checkpoint selection consists solely of English examples.
191
192   Hyperparameter Tuning for Hybrid Batch Sampling. To determine the optimal values for the
193   hyperparameters α and β in our hybrid batch sampling approach, we conducted a comprehensive grid
194   search. We evaluated α values ranging from 0 to 1, with β always set to 1 − α. Each configuration
195
      was tested on a held-out validation set comprising a diverse selection of languages. We assessed
      the model’s performance across monolingual, cross-lingual, and multilingual retrieval tasks. Our
196
      goal was to find a balance that would optimize performance across all three retrieval settings without
197
      significantly sacrificing any particular one. We found that setting α = 0.5 provided the best overall
198   results, striking an effective balance between monolingual and cross-lingual/multilingual performance.
199   This equal weighting between monolingual and cross-lingual batches allowed our model to maintain
200   strong monolingual retrieval capabilities while also excelling in cross-lingual and multilingual
201   scenarios. We also observed that the model’s performance was relatively stable for α values between
202   0.4 and 0.6, indicating some robustness to small variations in these hyperparameters.

440   These scores are very close to the 0.6828 and 0.5979 achieved by the X-Y model, which is primarily
441
      optimized for multilingual retrieval. Additionally, the proposed method significantly outperforms the
      X-X-mono approach, which is mainly optimized for monolingual retrieval and achieves scores of
442
      0.6506 and 0.5661.

445   4.2.2    L ANGUAGE B IAS E VALUATION
446
      Tables 7 and 8 present a comprehensive comparison of the average rank distance metric6 (Huang et al.,
447
      2023a) across different multilingual retrieval tasks using fine-tuned XLM-R and LaBSE models. The
448
      proposed approach is evaluated against two baseline methods: X-X-mono and X-Y, on two datasets:
449   XQuAD-R (Table 7) and MLQA-R (Table 8). The lower the average rank distance, the better the
450   performance.
451
452
      Significant mitigation of language bias Compared to monolingual batching. The proposed
      approach substantially reduces language bias compared to the X-X-mono baseline. In Table 1, the
453
      proposed method achieves a mean rank distance of 286.6 using XLM-R, compared to 410.2 for
454
      X-X-mono, representing a 30.1% reduction in language bias. Similarly, for LaBSE, the proposed
455   approach reduces the mean rank distance by 15.4% (from 261.5 to 221.1). In Table 2 (MLQA-R),
456   the proposed method achieves a mean rank distance of 227.1 using XLM-R, compared to 287.5
457   for X-X-mono, resulting in a 21% reduction in language bias. For LaBSE, the proposed approach
458   reduces the mean rank distance by 13.4% (from 225.3 to 195). These significant reductions highlight
459   the effectiveness of the proposed method in mitigating language bias of the retrieval system.
460
       Competitive reduction in average rank distance compared to cross-lingual batching. The
461   proposed approach exhibits competitive performance in reducing the average rank distance compared
462   to the strong X-Y baseline. In Table 7 (XQuAD-R), the proposed method achieves the best mean rank
463   distance of 286.6 using XLM-R, outperforming both X-X-mono (295.4) and X-Y (295.4) baselines.
464   For LaBSE, the proposed approach obtains a mean rank distance of 221.1, which is better than
465   the X-Y baseline (225.2). In Table 8 (MLQA-R), the proposed method achieves a slightly higher
466   mean rank distance than the X-Y baseline for XLM-R (227.1 vs. 226.7), but outperforms the X-Y
467   baseline for LaBSE (195 vs. 198.3). These results demonstrate that the proposed approach is highly
468   competitive in reducing the average rank distance and can even outperform the strong X-Y baseline
469
      in certain cases. This reduction in average rank distance directly translates to a decrease in language
      bias, as the proposed method effectively brings relevant documents closer together in the retrieval
470
      results, regardless of the language.

473   5    C ONCLUSION
474
475
      Developing IR models that can handle queries and documents across many languages is increasingly
476
      critical. In this work, we introduced a hybrid batch training strategy to optimize IR systems for
477   monolingual, cross-lingual, and multilingual performance simultaneously. By fine-tuning multilingual
478   language models on a mix of monolingual and cross-lingual question-answer pairs, the models
479   learn robust representations that generalize well across languages and retrieval settings. Extensive
480   experiments demonstrate that this simple yet effective approach consistently matches or outperforms
481   models trained with only monolingual or cross-lingual data, and substantially mitigates the language
482   bias that hinders multilingual retrieval performance.

      6    L IMITATIONS
487
488
      This work focuses on optimizing retrieval performance but does not address issues related to result
489   diversity, fairness, or transparency in multilingual settings. For example, it may reflect societal
490   biases present in the training data. Addressing these concerns is important for building equitable
491   multilingual retrieval systems.
492
      Furthermore, the experiments focus only on the XQuAD-R, MLQA-R, and MIRACL benchmark
493
      datasets. While these cover a range of languages, they may not be fully representative of real-world
494
      multilingual information retrieval needs. The robustness of the results to other domains, question
495   types, and retrieval scenarios is an exciting future direction.

714   A.1   T RAINING DATASETS
715
716
      We present an overview of the training datasets used to fine-tune the multilingual pre-trained models.
      These datasets were selected to cover a diverse range of domains, tasks, and languages. These datasets
717
      vary in size, language coverage, and domain. The datasets mMARCO, Mr. Tydi, and MFAQ focus
718
      on multilingual tasks, while others like Google NQ, DuoRC, and NewsQA are monolingual. The
719   datasets cover different domains, such as web search queries (Google NQ, WikiQA), movie plots
720   (DuoRC), news articles (NewsQA), and FAQs (MFAQ).
721
722   • DuoRC: A paraphrased reading comprehension dataset aimed at evaluating complex language
723     understanding. It contains over 186K question-answer pairs created from 7680 pairs of movie plot
724     summaries (Saha et al., 2018).
725   • EntityQuestions: A dataset designed to challenge dense retrievers with simple entity-centric
726     questions. It contains over 14K questions that require retrieving relevant entities from Wikipedia
727     (Sciavolino et al., 2021).
728   • Google NQ: A QA dataset consisting of aggregated queries from Google’s search engine, with
729     annotated answers from Wikipedia pages. It contains over 300K queries and can be used for
730     open-domain QA research (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019).
731   • MFAQ: A multilingual FAQ dataset containing over 100K question-answer pairs from 21 languages,
732     covering topics like COVID-19, climate change, and more. It can be used for multilingual FAQ
733     retrieval tasks (De Bruyn et al., 2021).
734   • mMARCO: A multilingual version of the MS MARCO passage ranking dataset, containing over
735     500K parallel queries and 9M passages in 13 languages. It can be used for multilingual information
736     retrieval research (Bonifacio et al., 2021).
737
      • Mr. Tydi: A multi-lingual benchmark for dense retrieval, consisting of monolingual and bilin-
738     gual topic-document annotations in 11 languages. It’s designed to evaluate the performance of
739     multilingual dense retrieval models (Zhang et al., 2021).
740
      • NewsQA: A machine comprehension dataset containing over 100K question-answer pairs based
741
        on CNN articles, aiming to encourage research on question answering from news articles (Trischler
742     et al., 2017).
743
      • WikiQA: An open-domain QA dataset with over 3K questions collected from Bing query logs,
744
        paired with answers extracted from Wikipedia. It’s designed to be a challenge dataset for open-
745
        domain QA research (Yang et al., 2015).
746
747   • Yahoo QA: A dataset mined from Yahoo Answers, a QA website containing pairs of questions and
748
        answers.
749
      Table 9 presents the dataset sizes after applying our in-house data processing pipeline to filter and
750   clean the data. To expand the training data and cover a diverse set of languages, we employed an
751   in-house machine translation pipeline (Fan et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Costa-jussà et al., 2022).
752   This pipeline was used to create parallel question-answer pairs across nine languages for the following
753   monolingual datasets: WikiQA, DuoRC, NewsQA, Google NQ, Yahoo QA, and EntityQuestions.
754   For the multilingual datasets, namely Mr. Tydi and MFAQ, only the English version was used.
755   Additionally, mMARCO (Bonifacio et al., 2021), a multilingual version of the MS MARCO dataset,
      was included in the training data.

790   A.2   E VALUATION DATASETS
791
792   We provide a summary of the evaluation datasets employed for conducting a zero-shot evaluation of
793   the models developed in this work. It should be noted that these evaluation datasets were not used
794
      during the training phase of the models.
795
796   • XQuAD-R and MLQA-R: Two multilingual answer retrieval datasets derived from XQuAD
797
        (Artetxe et al., 2020; Rajpurkar et al., 2016) and MLQA (Lewis et al., 2020). They are designed to
        evaluate the performance of language-agnostic answer retrieval models. XQuAD-R is an 11-way
798
        parallel dataset where each question appears in 11 different languages and has 11 parallel correct
799
        answers across the languages. MLQA-R, on the other hand, covers 7 languages and has a variable
800     number (2–4) of parallel correct answers across the corpus, with contexts surrounding the answer
801     sentence not guaranteed to be parallel (Roy et al., 2020).
802
803   • MIRACL dev: A multilingual information retrieval dataset that covers a continuum of languages,
804     featuring 18 languages with varying amounts of training data. It is designed to evaluate the
805     performance of multilingual information retrieval models in low-resource settings and to facilitate
806     research on cross-lingual transfer learning (Zhang et al., 2022).

      Tables 12 and 13 present the performance evaluation of fine-tuned XLM-R and LaBSE models on the
