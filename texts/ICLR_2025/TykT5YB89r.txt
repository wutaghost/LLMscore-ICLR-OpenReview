009                                              A BSTRACT
010
               Visual Speech Recognition (VSR) aims to infer what was said by analyzing the
011
               speaker’s facial dynamics. However, is reliance solely on visual information suffi-
012
               cient in challenging real-world scenarios? In human visual perception, peripheral
013            vision refers to non-central areas of the visual field, crucial for providing over-
014            all awareness and detailed perception of central objects. Similarly, human lip-
015            readers do not rely exclusively on lip movements but integrate contextual cues
016            and prior knowledge to achieve more accurate transcribing. For the first time in
017            machine lip-reading, we frame these non-lip-movement factors into a new concept
018            of semantic-level peripheral information, Specifically, we select three representa-
019            tive types varying in relevance to the spoken content: (1) Contextual peripheral
020            information, such as the general topic or some basic knowledge of the speech, can
021
               significantly narrow the range of potential recognition hypotheses. (2) Experien-
               tial peripheral information emerges from the recognition process itself. The very
022
               act of recognizing speech in a specific language provides implicit knowledge of
023
               grammar, word collocations, and related linguistic aspects, thereby guiding the
024            recognition effectively. (3) Perturbative peripheral information introduces distur-
025            bance factors into the recognition process, analogous to noise injection in visual
026            tasks. Semantic-level peripheral information is indirectly linked to transcripts;
027            thus fusing it into VSR necessitates strong contextual understanding and inference
028            capabilities. Here, we propose a multimodal learning framework built on a large
029            language model (LLM), leveraging its powerful contextual modeling capabilities
030            to take advantage of peripheral information. Our method’s efficacy is demon-
031            strated on two popular datasets. On the widely-used LRS3 dataset, we achieved a
032
               Word Error Rate (WER) of 24.5% with readily available peripheral information,
               leading to an impressive 14.3% relative improvement over the model without such
033
               information. To the best of our knowledge, our work sets a new state-of-the-art
034
               when utilizing similar hours of lip-reading videos. We further reported the evalu-
035            ation on the more challenging AVSpeech dataset. Results across both datasets and
036            various experimental settings demonstrate the promising potential of the proposed
037            semantic-level peripheral information for VSR.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
      Visual Speech Recognition (VSR) analyzes the visual dynamics of lip movements during speech
042   to infer spoken content. It has gained wide attention due to its important applications, including
043   assisting speech interpretation in noisy environments(Sumby & Pollack, 1954), and offering a com-
044   munication method for patients with speech disabilities (McGurk & MacDonald, 1976). However,
045   VSR still faces significant challenges due to the scarcity of labeled video data and the complexity of
046   real-world scenarios.
047
      Existing methods primarily focus on enhancing the utilization of visual information from the lip
048   region. Approaches involving constructing and utilizing larger lip-reading datasets(Afouras et al.,
049   2018a; Serdyuk et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2024), or employing self-supervised pre-training tech-
050   niques(Shi et al., 2022; Haliassos et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), have achieved
051   significant improvements in VSR. However, is optimizing visual information processing the only
052   path towards robust VSR in complex real-world scenarios? Lip-reading is also a challenging task
053   even for human lip-reading experts. Observations of human lip-reading suggest that the process
      involves more than just visual dynamics.

148   2.2   C ONTEXT-AWARE S PEECH R ECOGNITION
149
150   In the field of auditory speech recognition (ASR), researchers have investigated various methods to
151   incorporate contextual information into ASR systems. Several works(Chan et al., 2016; He et al.,
152   2017; Jain et al., 2020; Huber et al., 2021; Sathyendra et al., 2022) introduce extra context biasing
153   module into end to end ASR system to process phrase-level contextual information. More recent ap-
154   proaches(Radford et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023; Lakomkin et al., 2024; Nozawa et al., 2024) directly
155   incorporate broader contextual information, such as previous utterances or keywords, into ASR sys-
156   tems without specialized biasing modules. However, in the field of VSR, related work is neglected
157   to the best of our knowledge.
158   The concept of peripheral information in VSR that we propose extends existing approaches. While
159   previous methods primarily focus on integrating content-related contextual information, our study
160   goes beyond these conventional cues. We explore a more comprehensive set of elements from
161   various aspects, ranging from contextual ones to experiential information from human-lip-reading
      process and disturbance factors, aiming to provide a broader perspective for improving VSR.

164   Our study seeks to enhance VSR by introducing information beyond visual dynamics, which we
165   term as peripheral information in this paper. It encompasses a wider range of information sur-
166   rounding VSR more than content-related contextual cues. We take three representative types of
167   easily accessible semantic peripheral information and explore their contribution to VSR, We seam-
168   lessly incorporate them into the process of VSR by leveraging the contextual modeling abilities of
169   the LLM. Figure 2 illustrates the overall framework of our method, which achieves our core idea
170   through the following two key aspects:
171
              • VSR-adapted LLM commits to enable a LLM with the ability to process visual speech
172
                representations and generate transcripts in a certain format, which is achieved by modality
173             adaptation and LLM specialization.
174
              • Peripheral Information Fusion aims to achieve jointly inference for the target transcripts
175
                using visual speech features together with the semantic peripheral information via the VSR-
176             adapted LLM. We explore a simple manner of structured prompt and guided generation to
177             evaluate the role of semantic peripheral information for VSR.

      3.2     P ERIPHERAL I NFORMATION F USION
233
234   This paper introduces a new framework for Visual Speech Recognition (VSR) that goes beyond
235   traditional lip-movement analysis. Inspired by peripheral vision in human visual systems, we pro-
236   pose the concept of peripheral information in VSR, which includes a wider range of information
237   We identify three types of peripheral information: contextual, experiential, and perturbative. While
238   contextual information has been used in speech recognition, our approach expands its application in
239   VSR. Importantly, we introduce experiential and perturbative information as new categories, which
240
      differ from existing concepts in the field. These information types have distinct sources and roles
      in VSR, requiring specific integration strategies. This framework aims to provide a new perspective
241
      on visual speech recognition. In the following subsections, we will introduce the three types of
242
      peripheral information and present our methods for integrating them into our VSR-Adapted LLM.

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We introduce Peripheral Information (PI), a novel concept in visual speech recognition that leverages
534   non-lip-movement information, and propose an LLM-based multimodal learning framework that
535   integrates three representative types into the recognition process. Our experiments on the widely
536   used lip-reading dataset LRS3 and the more complex real-world dataset AVSpeech demonstrate the
537   effectiveness of PI. Notably, we achieved state-of-the-art performance on LRS3 using comparible
538   hours of lip-reading video. We conducted detailed experiments on different PIs to explore their
539   contributions to VSR, elucidating the contribution of and the fusion strategies for different types of
      PI. We hope our study inspires future research in and beyond visual speech recognition.
