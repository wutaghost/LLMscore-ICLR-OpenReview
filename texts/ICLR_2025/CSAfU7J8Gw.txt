009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning enables models to learn new tasks sequentially without for-
012            getting previously learned knowledge. Knowledge distillation reduces forgetting
013            by using a single “teacher” model to transfer previous knowledge to the “stu-
014            dent” model. However, existing methods face challenges, specifically loss of
015            task-specific knowledge, limited diversity in the transferred knowledge, and de-
016            lays in teacher availability. These issues stem from self-distillation, where the
017            teacher is a mere snapshot of the student after learning a new task, inheriting the
018
               student’s biases and becoming available only after learning a task. We propose
               Specialized Assistant TeaCHer distillation (SATCH), a novel method that uses a
019
               smaller assistant teacher trained exclusively on the current task. By incorporating
020
               the assistant teacher early in the learning process, SATCH provides task-specific
021            guidance, improves the diversity of transferred knowledge, and preserves critical
022            task-specific knowledge. Our method integrates seamlessly with existing knowl-
023            edge distillation techniques, and experiments on three standard continual learning
024            benchmarks show that SATCH improves accuracy by up to 12% when combined
025            with four state-of-the-art methods. Code is available in supplementary materials.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Continual learning aims to incrementally learn a sequence of tasks (Chen & Liu, 2018). Typically,
031   once a task is learned, its training data is no longer accessible. However, when learning a new task,
032   models are prone to catastrophic forgetting, where they experience a significant drop in accuracy
033
      on previously learned tasks. Forgetting occurs because the parameters associated with prior tasks
      are overwritten when learning a new task (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). Catastrophic forgetting is
034
      particularly challenging in class incremental learning, where the model must learn a sequence of
035
      tasks without access to task identifiers (Van de Ven et al., 2022).
036
037   Knowledge distillation is commonly used in continual learning to minimize catastrophic forgetting.
038   These methods introduce a regularization term that encourages the “student” model to mimic the
039
      outputs of its previous version, known as the “teacher”. Typically, the teacher is created by cloning
      the student before it learns a new task, which results in both models sharing the same backbone
040
      and initialization (Li & Hoiem, 2017). To further reduce forgetting, these methods often integrate
041
      replay buffer selection (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Ahn et al., 2021), where samples from previous tasks
042   are stored in a buffer and replayed during training to improve the retention of old task knowledge.
043
044   While knowledge distillation methods have shown promise in mitigating catastrophic forgetting,
045
      they face three key challenges: (1) Limited diversity in knowledge transferred: Knowledge distil-
      lation methods often rely on a single main teacher model with the same architecture and initialization
046
      as the student, limiting the knowledge transfer diversity. Although multi-teacher approaches using
047
      different random seeds (Li et al., 2023) or diverse datasets (Wu et al., 2019) have been explored, they
048   often require full data access, which is impractical in real-world settings with limited data availabil-
049   ity. (2) Forgetting task-specific knowledge: As the student model learns new tasks, there is a shift
050   in weights, causing it to forget previously learned task-specific information, especially in class in-
051   cremental learning where task identifiers are unavailable during inference (Van de Ven et al., 2022).
052   Forgetting is compounded because the main teacher, being a snapshot of the student after learning a
053   task, inherits the student’s forgotten knowledge. Replay buffer methods can help mitigate forgetting
      by storing samples of previous tasks, but they are constrained by the limited amount of data stored.

064   (3) Limited use of main teacher knowledge: Main teacher knowledge is typically only available
065   after the student has learned the new task, restricting the benefits of using soft labels to guide student
066   learning (Tzeng et al., 2015) and filtering samples stored in the replay buffer which is important in
067   real-world noisy scenarios (Sarfraz et al., 2023). While training an additional teacher prior to the
068   student learning the new task has been explored (Hou et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2023), these methods
069   primarily focus on guiding new task learning. These challenges raise two important questions: first,
070   how can we diversify and preserve task-specific knowledge transferred from the main teacher? and
071   second, how can we use this knowledge to improve student accuracy in real-world noisy scenarios?
072   To address these challenges, we propose Specialized Assistant TeaCHer distillation (SATCH), which
073   uses a smaller assistant teacher specialized in learning task-specific knowledge to improve knowl-
074   edge distillation. SATCH introduces two key innovations: First, to capture task-specific complemen-
075   tary knowledge, the assistant teacher is designed to use a smaller backbone, focusing exclusively on
076   learning a single task. This enables the assistant teacher to capture task-specific features that differ
077   from those learned by the main teacher, as shown in Figure 1. For example, with the bear input,
078
      the assistant teacher focuses on features distinct from those of the main teacher. When their outputs
      are combined, the resulting feature map is broader. Second, the assistant teacher is trained on the
079
      new task before the student learns the new task (Hou et al., 2018), allowing the assistant teacher to
080
      guide the student. Throughout the learning process, the assistant teacher helps select buffer sam-
081   ples, which is important in noisy scenarios (Sarfraz et al., 2023), by filtering out noisy labels and
082   identifying representative samples through comparison with the student’s predictions.
083
084
      In SATCH, the assistant teacher’s knowledge is used in three ways: (1) Guide new task learning:
      The assistant teacher generates soft labels to guide learning, ensuring the student learns inter-task
085
      relationships when learning new tasks. (2) Refine buffer selection: During buffer selection, the
086
      assistant teacher’s prediction is compared with the student’s prediction to identify samples where
087   both models agree, retaining more representative samples and filtering out noise. (3) Diversify
088   distilled knowledge: The assistant teacher’s specialized knowledge is stored alongside the buffer
089   samples to provide a complementary view of previous knowledge. The specialized knowledge is
090   combined with the main teacher’s generalized knowledge by averaging the relevant output logits.
091   These three components enable SATCH to use the complementary task-specific knowledge from the
092   assistant teacher to reduce catastrophic forgetting.
093   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We propose SATCH, a novel continual learning method that
094   incorporates a specialized assistant teacher into existing knowledge distillation methods to provide
095   task-specific guidance, provide complementary knowledge, and preserve task-specific knowledge.
096   (2) We introduce three key components that use the complementary task-specific knowledge: guid-
097   ing new task learning, refining buffer selection, and diversifying distilled knowledge. (3) We validate
098   the effectiveness of SATCH on three benchmark datasets and four state-of-the-art knowledge distil-
099   lation methods, demonstrating its robustness in class incremental learning and noisy environments.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
102
103
104
      Continual learning methods span several directions, notably regularization, rehearsal, parameter
      isolation, and dynamic architecture methods (De Lange et al., 2021).
105
106   Knowledge Distillation Methods: Knowledge distillation was originally developed to transfer
107   knowledge from a large teacher network to a smaller student network (Hinton, 2015; Zagoruyko
      & Komodakis, 2017). In continual learning, knowledge distillation prevents forgetting by encourag-


                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ing the current model to mimic the outputs of a previous version of itself. This is commonly applied
109   through self-distillation (Mobahi et al., 2020), where the main teacher and student share the same
110   backbone, with the main teacher being a copy of the student before learning a new task. To further
111   reduce forgetting, these methods often include a replay buffer that stores past samples and replays a
112   subset when learning a new task. DER++ (Buzzega et al., 2020) introduces an additional distillation
113   loss by storing the model outputs of old knowledge with the samples and enforcing output space
114   consistency during learning. SSIL (Ahn et al., 2021) separates the softmax layer and performs task-
115   specific distillation using a previous copy of the student. Other methods, such as ESMER (Sarfraz
116   et al., 2023) and CLS-ER (Arani et al., 2022), focus the main teacher model on retaining old knowl-
117
      edge and enforcing output consistency during learning. However, these methods often struggle to
      retain task-specific knowledge since the main teacher inherits the forgotten previous knowledge, and
118
      there is only a single teacher, which limits the diversity of distilled knowledge.
119
120   Multi-teacher Distillation: Multi-teacher distillation aims to improve student generalization by in-
121   heriting diverse knowledge from multiple teachers (Gou et al., 2021; Wang & Yoon, 2021). Teachers
122   are generated using different random seeds (Li et al., 2023), trained on different datasets (Wu et al.,
123
      2019), or designed with varying backbones (You et al., 2017). However, multi-teacher methods of-
      ten require full data access, which may not be feasible in real-world applications with limited data
124
      availability (Chaudhry et al., 2019).
125
126   Auxiliary Network Methods: While knowledge distillation methods focus on preventing forget-
127   ting, some methods also aim to improve new task learning. Hou et al. (2018) train an auxiliary
128   teacher before the student begins learning a new task, distilling soft labels to the student as it learns
129
      a new task. Similarly, Kim et al. (2023) incorporates new task knowledge by training a copy of the
      student on the new task, using it as an additional regularization term during the student’s learning.
130
      Although these methods incorporate new task knowledge, they create the auxiliary teacher by simply
131
      copying the student, which makes the model prone to losing task-specific knowledge.
132
133   Dynamic Architecture and Pre-trained Methods: Dynamic architecture methods (Yan et al.,
134   2021; Lin et al., 2024) add additional parameters for each task and use a task-ID predictor to dynam-
135
      ically select the task relevant parameters during inference. Similarly, pre-trained methods (Wu et al.,
      2022; Huang et al., 2025) use pre-trained models to extract generalized features with additional pa-
136
      rameters added to capture task-specific features. These approaches achieve state-of-the-art accuracy.
137
      However, they face challenges with restricted memory due to the linear growth of parameters with
138   the number of tasks. Furthermore, they can be less effective in real-world settings where there is
139   overlapping classes (Mi et al., 2020) or noisy labels (Sarfraz et al., 2023).
140
141
      Our proposed method, SATCH differs from these auxiliary approaches by using an assistant teacher
      with a smaller backbone who specializes in a single task. SATCH’s design improves task-specific
142
      knowledge retention by modifying the main teacher’s output logits, allowing seamless integration
143
      with existing knowledge distillation methods. Additionally, the assistant teacher’s complementary
144   knowledge helps filter out noisy samples, a feature not addressed by other auxiliary methods, making
145   SATCH more robust to real-world noisy scenarios.

185   3.1       T RAINING THE ASSISTANT TEACHER
186
      The assistant teacher improves knowledge diversity through its smaller backbone network and spe-
187
      cialization in a single task. For instance, if the main teacher uses a ResNet-18 backbone, the assistant
188
      teacher uses a reduced ResNet-18 (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017), with fewer feature maps across all
189   layers. While the generalized main teacher balances learning across multiple tasks, the assistant
190   teacher focuses on capturing task-specific feature representations. For each new task t, the assistant
191   teacher ϕt is trained using cross-entropy loss (Rebuffi et al., 2017), which is then used to guide the
192   student model θts during task learning and assist in filtering noisy samples during buffer selection.
193
      We extend the episodic memory buffer M to manage memory efficiently. When learning a new task,
194
      samples from the current task, xt , and their labels, yt , are stored in the replay buffer M. In SATCH,
195   we extend the buffer to store the output logits generated by the assistant teacher. The samples stored

286   4     E XPERIMENTS
287
288   Setup: All methods are evaluated in a class incremental learning setting. Once a task is learned,
289   the training data is discarded except for the data stored in the replay buffer. After learning all tasks,
290   each model is evaluated using the test data without access to the task ID during inference. Fol-
291   lowing Buzzega et al. (2020), all models use a ResNet-18 backbone network and are trained with
292   an SGD optimizer. SATCH’s assistant teacher uses a reduced ResNet-18 backbone network (Kang
293   et al., 2022) unless specified otherwise. Following Lin et al. (2022), we measure the average accu-
294
      racy and average forgetting of all tasks after learning. We set λ = 0.1 for all buffer sizes and datasets
      to reduce the method’s dependency on extensive hyperparameter tuning (see Appendix A.9).
295
296   Dataset: We evaluate our method on four benchmark datasets. A task represents an image classi-
297   fication task for a given group of objects. Following existing continual learning approaches, each
298   dataset’s training and testing instances are split evenly into tasks and classes. These datasets are
299   CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009), a visual object dataset split into 10 tasks with 10 different
300
      classes; MiniImageNet (Vinyals et al., 2016), a variant of ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) with
      10 tasks, each containing 10 different classes; TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015), another ImageNet
301
      variant with 10 tasks, each including 20 different classes. GCIL-CIFAR100 (Mi et al., 2020), a real-
302
      istic real-world setting applied onto CIFAR100 with 10 tasks where the number of classes, appearing
303   classes, and their sample sizes for each task are sampled from a probabilistic distribution.
304
305   Baselines: We compare against upper bound (JOINT), lower bound (SGD), rehearsal-based meth-
306
      ods (ER, ER-ACE), regularization method (PASS), and integrate SATCH into baselines using vari-
      ous state-of-the-art knowledge distillation techniques DER++, SSIL, CLS-ER, and ESMER.

309   4.1   R ESULTS
310
      We evaluate the accuracy impact of SATCH on various knowledge distillation methods. Table 10
311
      demonstrates the accuracy improvements when combining SATCH with DER++, SSIL, CLS-ER,
312   and ESMER, which we denote as “+ SATCH (ours)”. SATCH improves the accuracy of these
313   methods across different datasets and buffer sizes. For example, on CIFAR100 with buffer sizes of
314   1000 and 5000, combining ESMER with SATCH results in accuracy improvements of 6.54% and
315   3.19%, respectively. Similarly, on TinyImageNet, the accuracy improvements are 7.85% and 2.39%,
316   and on MiniImageNet, with 7.13% and 2.59%. Also, on GCIL-CIFAR100, a more challenging
317   scenario with overlapping classes, there is an accuracy improvement of 2.51% and 2.20%.
318   To further understand SATCH’s impact on individual task accuracy as more tasks are introduced,
319   we analyze the task accuracy progression for specific tasks with and without SATCH in Figure 3.
320   While the initial task accuracy without SATCH is slightly higher, it declines more rapidly as addi-
321   tional tasks are learned. In contrast, SATCH maintains more stable accuracy over time, showing a
322   smaller decline in performance on past tasks as new ones are introduced. This suggests that SATCH
323   reduces task-recency bias, which often leads to higher accuracy for the most recent task at the cost
      of forgetting older ones.

      Ablation Study: To assess each component in SATCH, we conducted an ablation study shown in
365
      Table 2. The key components incorporating the assistant teacher’s knowledge: new task learning
366   (NEWL), which distills soft labels from the assistant teacher to guide the student; diverse knowl-
367   edge (DIVK), which combines the assistant and main teacher’s logits for complementary knowledge
368   transfer; and buffer selection (BUFS), which refines sample selection based on the agreement be-
369   tween the student and assistant teacher. To ensure all components are evaluated equally, we include
370   task-recency bias mitigation (BIASM), which reduces forgetting of past tasks by distilling only the
371   current task’s logits during training. Each component improves accuracy, either individually or in
372   combination. For instance, when BUFS is removed, we observe a drop in accuracy across all meth-
373   ods, indicating that this component also improves accuracy in non-noisy environments.

      Noisy Labels in a Real World Scenario: We evaluate SATCH’s ability to filter out noisy labels, as
433   storing these samples can cause models to memorize incorrect labels (Sarfraz et al., 2023; Kim et al.,

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this paper, we introduced SATCH, a novel continual learning framework that incorporates a spe-
489   cialized assistant teacher to reduce catastrophic forgetting. The assistant teacher, using a smaller
490   backbone, is trained exclusively on the current task and offers complementary knowledge while pre-
491   serving task-specific information. SATCH guides new task learning, refines buffer selection, and ef-
492   fectively combines the assistant teacher’s specialized knowledge with the main teacher’s generalized
493   knowledge, striking a balance between retaining old knowledge and learning new knowledge. Our
494   experimental results demonstrate that SATCH achieves state-of-the-art accuracy when integrated
495   with existing knowledge distillation methods, improving both accuracy and robustness, particularly
496
      in noisy environments. Ablation studies confirm that each component of SATCH is essential for
      these improvements in accuracy.

680
      Following Lin et al. (2022), we use accuracy and forgetting to evaluate performance. Accuracy it
681
      is the average test classification accuracy of all tasks and forgetting which measures the accuracy
682   difference of old tasks after learning new tasks. Formally, accuracy and forgetting are defined as:
683

684                                 1X                          1 X
                         accuracy =       AT,i , forgetting =            AT,i − Ai,i                     (7)
685                                 T i=1                     T − 1 i=1

687   Here, T is the total number of sequential tasks and AT,i is the accuracy of the model on i-th task
688   after learning the T -th task sequentially (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017).

690   A.3   BASELINES
691
      We evaluate our approach by focusing on replay-based and knowledge distillation methods, as these
692
      have been shown to outperform other techniques in the class incremental learning setting (Chaudhry
693
      et al., 2019; Arani et al., 2022). The following baselines are considered:
694
695   JOINT: provides the upper bound by jointly learning all tasks.
696   SGD: provides the lower bound by fine-tuning the model when a new task is learned.
697
698
      ER: Chaudhry et al. (2019) performs interleaved training of the new task and the memory sample to
      approximate the joint distribution of all tasks.
699
700   ER-ACE: Caccia et al. (2022) addresses the issue of task transition causing a significant drift in
701   the learned representations of previous classes by introducing an asymmetric cross-entropy loss that
      only considers the logits of new classes.


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      DER++: Buzzega et al. (2020) introduces an additional distillation loss by storing output logits
703   alongside samples and enforcing consistency in the output space.
704
705   PASS: Zhu et al. (2021) uses prototypes, which act as anchors for each class in feature space. It
706
      incorporates self-supervised auxiliary tasks, such as contrastive learning, to improve feature robust-
      ness.
707
708   SS-IL: Ahn et al. (2021) learns the current task loss and replay loss in isolation from each other,
709   using task-specific distillation on the rehearsal data.
710   CLS-ER: Arani et al. (2022) models the interaction between fast and slow learning systems by
711   maintaining two semantic memories that update the model’s weights at different rates using an
712   exponential moving average.
713
      ESMER: Sarfraz et al. (2023) proposes that the model should learn more from samples with smaller
714
      losses to avoid large feature drift, assigning different weights to new samples based on their loss
715
      value.

719   A.4.1    C LASS I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
720
721   Class-incremental learning evaluates continual learning in a more realistic scenario where the task ID
722   is unavailable during inference. We use the CIFAR100, TinyImageNet, and MiniImageNet datasets,
723   with the classes split into 10 tasks containing disjoint sets of 10, 20, and 10 classes, respectively. In
724   this setting, all methods are evaluated without using separate classification heads, as the task ID is
725
      not provided during inference.

728   A.4.2    G ENERALIZED C LASS I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
729
730   Generalized class-incremental learning (Mi et al., 2020) evaluates continual learning in a more real-
731   istic scenario where tasks may have overlapping classes, the number of classes varies across tasks,
732
      training instances per task are inconsistent during inference.
733
734   A.4.3    N OISY C LASS I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
735   Noisy class incremental learning evaluates the robustness of a continual learning model when faced
736   with label noise. In real-world data streams, noise is inevitable (Sarfraz et al., 2023), and an ef-
737   fective continual learning method must handle both noisy labels and incremental learning, avoiding
738   memorization of noise and forgetting. We apply varying degrees of symmetric label noise to the
739   CIFAR100 and TinyImageNet datasets. Following Sarfraz et al. (2023), for each task, we sample
740   random labels from the classes in the task and replace a fraction of the original labels with the
741   random ones. Note that, under this setting, the random label may correspond to the original label.

774   A.6   C OMPARISON WITH ANCL
775
776   We compare SATCH with ANCL (Kim et al., 2023), the most similar method to ours. ANCL pro-
777   poses a framework where an auxiliary network is trained on the current task and used as an additional
778   regularization term during student network training. In traditional self-distillation methods, a copy
                                                             t
779   of the student network is treated as a teacher model, θ1:t−1 , which retains knowledge from previous
780   tasks. ANCL introduces an additional copy of the student, designed to learn the new task before
781   guiding the student network with old and new knowledge. While ANCL does guide new tasks,
782   it does not capture task-specific knowledge or introduce backbone diversity. Furthermore, ANCL
783
      requires more runtime and memory than SATCH, as the auxiliary teacher must replicate the orig-
      inal method’s learning process to prevent forgetting. In contrast, SATCH’s assistant teacher only
784
      specializes in the current task.
785

787   comparison with ANCL on DER++, we apply task recency bias mitigation and only evaluate the
788   components that use the assistant teacher knowledge. Although ANCL shows accuracy improve-
789
      ments, SATCH has higher accuracy than ANCL when combined with DER++ and SSIL. These
      results indicate that SATCH can effectively utilize specialized teacher knowledge, whereas ANCL
790
      relies on pre-training a copy of the student for the new task.
791
792   Additionally, SATCH improves runtime and memory efficiency, as shown in Table 5. ANCL’s need
793   to pre-train a student copy increases its runtime by incorporating the base method’s distillation and
794   replay process. At the same time, SATCH’s assistant teacher focuses exclusively on learning the
795
      current task without the need to perform replay or knowledge distillation. In conclusion, SATCH’s
      assistant teacher provides task-specific knowledge that reduces forgetting by introducing comple-
796
      mentary information with improved runtime and lower memory over ANCL.

847   further improvements in accuracy for noisy continual learning. Both SATCH and ESMER’s buffer

      noisy samples missed by ESMER, demonstrating its effectiveness in minimizing the retention of

      A.9   H YPERPARAMETER T UNING
857
      SATCH introduces a λ hyperparameter to control the guidance from the assistant teacher, transfer-
858   ring task-specific knowledge to the student. The λ hyperparameter is selected among four different
859   values [0.1, 0.4, 0.7, 1] based on validation sets split from the training data on CIFAR100 with 5000
860   buffer size. Our findings show that lower values of λ, generally lead to higher accuracy. Setting λ
861   too high can lead to overfitting on the teacher model’s knowledge. Previous work has explored ways
862   to prevent overfitting, such as dynamically adjusting the weighting throughout training (Lee et al.,
863   2019; Hinton, 2015). Based on this insight, we set λ = 0.1 for all buffer sizes and datasets to reduce
      the method’s dependency on extensive hyperparameter tuning.
