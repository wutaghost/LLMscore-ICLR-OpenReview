009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training a neural network using one-hot targets often leads to the issue of over-
012            confidence. To address this, Label Smoothing has been introduced, modifying
013            the targets to a mix of one-hot encoding and a uniform probability vector. How-
014            ever, the uniform probability vector indiscriminately assigns equal weights to all
015            categories, thereby undermining inter-category relationships. To overcome these
016            challenges, we propose a novel solution, Learnable Label Smoothing (LLS), that
017            aims to regulate training by granting networks the ability to assign optimal tar-
018
               gets. Unlike conventional methods, Learnable Label Smoothing utilizes proba-
               bility vectors unique to each category, resulting in diverse targets. The acquired
019
               relationships are beneficial for regularization and also prove to be transferable,
020
               facilitating knowledge distillation even in the absence of a Teacher model. Our
021            extensive experiments across multiple datasets highlight the advantages of our
022            method in addressing both overfitting and the preservation of inter-category rela-
023            tionships in neural network training.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The traditional method of training neural networks involves the utilization of one-hot targets and
029   cross-entropy loss, a long-standing practice in the field. However, the use of one-hot targets has been
030   recognized for its tendency to instigate overconfidence within the network, potentially hampering its
031   generalization capabilities Szegedy et al. (2016). Over the years, various regularization techniques,
032   such as Cutout (Devries & Taylor (2017)), Mixup (Zhang et al. (2018)), CutMix (Yun et al. (2019)),
033
      and others (Hendrycks et al. (2020); Gong et al. (2021)), have been introduced to address this issue,
      often involving modifications to the input data. An alternative strategy is Label Smoothing, which
034
      adjusts target labels during training by adding a uniform label distribution over the categories to
035
      the one-hot target (Szegedy et al. (2016)). Training with Label Smoothing has proven effective in
036   enhancing generalization and has been widely adopted.
037
038   Despite the advantages of Label Smoothing, it is known to disrupt the relationships between cate-
039
      gories (Müller et al. (2019)). This problem arises from the use of a uniform probability vector in
      generating smoothed targets, assigning equal importance to all negative categories. Consequently,
040
      the network is instructed to treat all categories as equally distinct from each other, leading to com-
041
      pact and equidistant category clusters in the feature space (Müller et al. (2019)). This outcome is
042   undesirable; for e.g., targets for the Dog class should have a relatively higher similarity with the
043   Cat class, as compared to the Truck class. Enforcing uniform inter-category relationships limits the
044   model’s performance Zhang et al. (2021). Inter-category relationship is crucial for applications such
045   as Knowledge Distillation, dealing with missing data, and learning from noisy labels (Hinton et al.
046   (2015); Müller et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2021)). This prompts two fundamental questions: (1) Is
047   it possible to regulate confidence while preserving the inter-category relationship? and, (2) What
048   alternative should be employed in place of the uniform probability vector?
049   This paper introduces a novel solution, termed Learnable Label Smoothing (LLS), to address these
050   questions. Our approach aims to train the network to learn the optimal target vector, as illustrated in
051   Figure 1. We propose a category-wise learnable probability vector. By combining these probability
052   vectors with the one-hot labels, similar to Label Smoothing, we create targets unique to each cat-
053   egory. For a dataset with K categories, these category-wise learnable probability vectors together
      form the K × K Q-matrix, whose rows Qk encode the inter-category similarities.

076   2   R ELATED W ORK
077
078
      Training neural networks with 1-hot targets are well-known for inducing overconfidence and ad-
079   versely affecting generalization (Szegedy et al. (2016)). Numerous regularization techniques have
080   been proposed to mitigate this issue, with a predominant focus on enhancing input data (Zhang et al.
081   (2018); Yun et al. (2019); Devries & Taylor (2017)). Label regularization techniques seek to modify
082   targets to alleviate overconfidence. Label smoothing is one of the straightforward solutions that mix
083   the 1-hot vector with a uniform vector, weighted by a hyper-parameter α (Szegedy et al. (2016)).
084   Despite its merits, Label Smoothing has the drawback of disrupting inter-category relationships by
085   assigning equal weights to all negative categories (Müller et al. (2019)). Our novel approach di-
086   verges from a uniform vector, opting instead to learn the probability vector for mixing to prevent
087
      disrupting inter-category relationships.
088   Entropy maximization on network predictions emerges as an alternative to Label Smoothing
089   (Pereyra et al. (2017)). This technique provides greater flexibility to samples, allowing them to
090   determine the weight of negative categories instead of adhering to uniform weights. Our approach
091   leverages entropy maximization loss on network predictions and trains the network to learn the tar-
092   gets. Focal loss was proposed as a modification of the cross-entropy loss function (Mukhoti et al.
093
      (2020); Lin et al. (2017)). It allocates higher weights to samples with low confidence and lower
      weights to those with high confidence. This loss works by minimizing a regularized KL divergence
094
      and preventing the model from becoming excessively overconfident. This further underscores our
095
      selection of entropy maximization in regulating targets.
096
097   Knowledge Distillation is recognized as a form of label regularization (Hinton et al. (2015); Yuan
098   et al. (2020)). It involves producing targets from a larger network (the Teacher) and passing this
099
      knowledge to a smaller network (the Student) on a per-sample basis. The relationship of each sam-
      ple to negative categories, as learned by the teacher, aids in regulating the Student networks (Hinton
100
      et al. (2015)). In line with this concept, a trained network was employed to train another (same
101
      architecture) network in Teacher-Free Knowledge Distillation (Yuan et al. (2020)). However, this
102   approach incurs significant computational expenses as it necessitates training a network twice and
103   generating outputs using online training. An alternative, Teacher-Free regularization, behaves sim-
104   ilarly to Label Smoothing but utilizes a high mixing coefficient of 0.9 to generate a smoothened
105   probability vector (Yuan et al. (2020)). The network is trained to align predicted probabilities with
106   this vector at a high temperature, reducing computational costs but still relying on a uniform vector.
107   Our method departs from a uniform probability vector when generating a regularized target. Online
      Label Smoothing is another approach based on network predictions (Zhang et al. (2021)). It com-

144   3.2   L EARNABLE L ABEL S MOOTHING (LLS)
145
146   Our approach proposes to replace the uniform vector u in Label Smoothing with a learnable proba-
147   bility vector, granting the network the ability to select optimal targets. Our learned target vector is of
148   the form, plls = (1−α)∗p+α∗q where, q is learned through network training. We argue that a 1-hot
149
      target vector is an overconfident and hard assignment of the image category. Label Smoothing ame-
      liorates the effect of overconfidence by assuming a uniform prior label distribution. However, Label
150
      Smoothing could introduce unwanted biases when uniformly smoothing the probabilities (Lienen &
151
      Hüllermeier (2021)). We propose to learn the distribution q and estimate the ‘moving’ target label
152   plls even as the network trains to align the prediction p̂ with plls . We share a probability vector q
153   between all samples within a category, due to their shared relationships with other categories and
154   employ distinct q for each category. Hence, we learn a matrix Q of dimensions K × K, where row i
155   signifies a learnable probability vector Qi = [qi1 , qi2 , . . . , qiK ] for category i. For a training sample
156   (x, y), the modified label is given as plls where,
157
                                            plls = (1 − α) ∗ p + α ∗ Qy ,                                       (4)
158
159   with p as the 1-hot vector corresponding to ground truth label y, and Qy the y-th row of the learned
160   Q matrix. α is the hyper-parameter similar to Label Smoothing. The purpose of the Q-Matrix is
161   to facilitate the acquisition of the optimal mixing probability vectors during Label Smoothing. We
      refer to our framework as Learnable Label Smoothing (LLS).

199   KL term KL(p̂||plls ), which provides symmetry to the training loss function and ensures the target
200   plls and predictions p̂ are updated with equal emphasis. We discuss this more using the derivatives

202

      3.4    T HE Q-M ATRIX
229
230   Minimizing −H(plls ) maximizes the entropy of the target plls = (1 − α)p + αQy , where only
231   Qy varies. The entropy of plls can be increased only by reducing Qyy and increasing the other
232   components of Qy because the y-th component plls  y is greater than the other components by a fixed
233   constant term (1 − α). This propels the network to set Qyy → 0 and assign that probability to
234   the other categories, thereby identifying inter-category relationships. Consequently, the Q matrix
235   exhibits the lowest values at the diagonals and higher values for semantically closer categories.
236   The Q-Matrix is generally asymmetric, as we found the relation of a category to another does not
237   reciprocate the same way. For e.g., using Figure 4b, the Pullover category has the highest similarity
238   with the Shirt category, but the Shirt gets a higher similarity with the T-shirt category than the
239   Pullover.
240   We learn a Qy vector for every category. This results in a K × K Q-matrix where every row Qy
241   models the similarities of category y with the other categories. The similarities learned by the Q-
242   Matrix allow for knowledge transfer between different networks, especially when a teacher model
243   can’t be employed (More details in 5.3). Similarly, a Q-Matrix learned from a large dataset can
244   be used to transfer its knowledge to its subsets (category-wise and sample-wise) of the data, which
245   reduces the necessity for frequent relearning of the Q-Matrix (More details in Appendix Section D).

250   4.1    DATASETS AND S ETUP
251
252   We evaluated our methodology across diverse settings, encompassing small-scale objects, large-
253   scale objects, and scenarios demanding fine-grained classification. In the realm of small-scale clas-
254   sification, we used FashionMNIST (Xiao et al. (2017)), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al. (2009)), and
255
      SVHN (Netzer et al. (2011)) datasets. These datasets, with images sized at 32 × 32, offer both di-
      versity and challenges with 10-way classifications. SVHN presents an intriguing challenge as digits
256
      lack prominent inter-category relationships. For large-scale classification, our evaluation extended
257
      to CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al. (2009)), Tiny-ImageNet, and ImageNet-100. Due to hardware con-
258   straints, we leveraged Tiny-ImageNet and ImageNet-100 [URL], both subsets of the original Ima-
259   geNet dataset (Deng et al. (2009)). Tiny-ImageNet possesses 200 categories with 64 × 64 images,
260   while ImageNet-100, featuring the original 224 × 224 image size, encompasses 100 categories. In
261   the fine-grained classification domain, our experiments focused on distinguishing between various
262   bird species using the CUB-200 dataset (Wah et al. (2011)), different types of flowers using the
263   Flowers-102 dataset (Nilsback & Zisserman (2008)), and different animals using the Animals-10N
264   dataset (Song et al. (2019)).
265   We evaluated our approach on these datasets using different networks that are mentioned in their
266   respective tables. We store Q-matrix as logits which are converted to probabilities using Softmax.
267   The Q-matrix is initialized with zeros, leading to a uniform distribution as the starting point. The
268   hyper-parameter α is set to 0.1 for all experiments but optimizing α can provide additional gains
269   (Explored in Appendix Section C). Detailed training procedure, the pseudo-code, and the code are
      provided in Appendix Section I, Section A, and the supplementary, respectively.

      5.3    S UBSTITUTE T EACHER FOR K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
502
503   Knowledge distillation employs a pre-trained teacher model ft on dataset to instruct the student
504   model fs . The teacher model generates targets for each sample, which the student model then uses
505   to learn. The training loss for the student network is defined as:
506
507                           LKD = βH(fs (x), y) + (1 − β)H(fs (x)/T, ft (x)/T )                        (6)
508   Here, H represents the cross-entropy loss, β is a parameter balancing the use of one-hot labels
509   and teacher targets, and T is the temperature-regulating knowledge transfer from teacher to student.
510   However, the availability of a Teacher network can be constrained by computational or privacy
511   considerations. In such scenarios, the Q-matrix of the Teacher network can serve as a substitute
512   Teacher for Knowledge Distillation, denoted as LLS-ST. While a Teacher model furnishes targets
513   on a per-sample basis, LLS-ST exclusively offers category-wise targets only.
514   Across all datasets, we adopted their original training setup for knowledge distillation but altered
515   the training loss function. Following the recent setup of knowledge distillation experiments, we
516   set β = 0, implying that student networks are exclusively trained using teacher predictions, and
517   used a temperature of 1 for all experiments. The results are presented in Table 5. The outcomes
518   indicate that networks trained with LLS exhibit superior teaching capabilities during the distillation
519   process. Remarkably, LLS-ST, despite imparting limited knowledge, imparts a performance boost
520   comparable to employing a fully trained Teacher network (refer Table 1 and 2).
521
522   6     L IMITATIONS
523
524   The biggest drawback of our approach is that it requires K 2 additional parameters. This becomes
525   a concern when the number of classes grows large, like ImageNet-21k. In such a case, the number
526   of parameters becomes substantially high (441M parameters for ImageNet-21k). To solve this, we
527   propose to merge non-similar categories and keep a fixed number of top similar N during training
528   for each category. The Q-Matrix will start with K ×K parameters but will reduce it to K ×N where
529   N << K, thereby reducing the number of parameters. We will keep it as part of future exploration
530   work.
531
532   7     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In our paper, we introduce an innovative label regularization technique named Learnable Label
535   Smoothing (LLS). Our approach focuses on empowering networks to learn optimal target labels
536   for regularization. Consequently, our method effectively produces compact feature clusters while
537   preserving the inter-category relationships. Furthermore, the acquired understanding of these inter-
538   category relationships is transferable, aiding in Knowledge Distillation even in scenarios where a
539   Teacher network is unavailable. We believe Learnable Label Smoothing will play a transformative
      role in knowledge transfer paradigms for neural networks.

911   1-hot targets, Label Smoothing, and Learnable Label Smoothing as baselines. Subsequently, we
912   delve into the impact of applying the learned Q-Matrix from the entire dataset, similar to the substi-
913   tute Teacher experiments (LLS-ST). For category subsets, we extract the logits corresponding to the
914
      selected categories from the Q-Matrix and exclusively apply Softmax to these chosen values.
915   The results of these experiments are detailed in Table 6 and 7. Notably, the learned Q-Matrix exhibits
916   superior performance when applied to a subset of samples. When dealing with a subset of categories,
917   learning a new Q-Matrix enhances generalization, with the learned matrix closely approaching the
      performance of a newly trained matrix, outperforming 1-hot targets and Label Smoothing.
