009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider the problem of synthesis of binaural speech from mono audio in
012            arbitrary environments, which is important for modern telepresence and extended-
013            reality applications. We find that existing neural mono-to-binaural methods are
014            overfit to non-spatial acoustic properties, via analysis using a new benchmark (TUT
015            Mono-to-Binaural), the first introduced since the original dataset of Richard et al.
016            (2021). While these past methods focus on learning neural geometric transforms of
017            monaural audio, we propose BinauralZero, a strong initial baseline for universal
018
               mono-to-binaural synthesis, which can subjectively match or outperform existing
               state-of-the-art neural mono-to-binaural renderers trained in their target environ-
019
               ment despite never seeing any binaural data. It leverages the surprising discovery
020
               that an off-the-shelf mono audio denoising model can competently enhance the
021            initial binauralization given by simple parameter-free transforms. We perform com-
022            prehensive ablations to understand how BinauralZero bridges the representation
023            gap between mono and binaural audio, and analyze how current mono-to-binaural
024            automated metrics are decorrelated from human ratings.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Humans possess a remarkable ability to localize sound sources and perceive the surrounding en-
030   vironment through auditory cues alone. This sensory ability, known as spatial hearing, plays a
031   critical role in numerous everyday tasks, including identifying speakers in crowded conversations and
032   navigating complex environments (Blauert, 1996). Hence, emulating a coherent sense of space via
033
      listening devices like headphones is key to creating truly immersive artificial experiences. The case
      of position-conditional binaural rendering of mono speech audio is of special interest, due to growing
034
      reliance on remote real-time spoken interactions in professional settings, increased prevalence of
035
      high-fidelity extended-reality (XR) technologies, and the socially cohesive benefits of spatial audio in
036   virtual spaces (Lawrence et al., 2021; Lieberman et al., 2022; Nowak et al., 2023). In particular, these
037   demands motivate speech spatialization schemes that are universal, accurately emulating the relative
038   position of the source speaker, appropriately conditioned on (or performing a generic imputation
039   of) room and binaural listener properties, all while being robust to the identity of the speaker, to the
040   content and language of the speech, and mitigating ambient noise.
041
      Conventional digital signal processing approaches often involve linear time-invariant (LTI) systems
042   with explicit models for the head-related transfer function (HRTF), the room impulse response (RIR),
043   and ambient noise (Savioja et al., 1999; Zotkin et al., 2004; Sunder et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2017). To
044   reduce explicit linearity assumptions and modeling choices, Richard et al. (2021) demonstrated that
045   for mono-to-binaural speech synthesis, direct deep supervised learning outperforms such approaches
046   on both loss-based and human evaluations on their introduced real-world dataset. Their choice of
047   architecture and training scheme has been refined by a body of subsequent work (Huang et al., 2022;
048   Leng et al., 2022; Lee & Lee, 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Kitamura & Itou, 2023; Li et al., 2024b).
049   However, we find that existing neural approaches significantly overfit to the non-spatial acoustic
050   properties of their data, representing a large gap from achieving universal mono-to-binaural synthesis.
051   Though overfitting is most directly resolved by large-scale data collection, supervised data involves
052   positional tracking of mono audio sources plus a binaural recording device atop a real or emulated
053   human torso. For example, the original two-hour dataset of Richard et al. (2021) has remained the
      only dataset used by these works (except for an unreleased set that Huang et al., 2022 additionally

054
      use); it is recorded in a single non-anechoic room, with the same set of eight speakers in the train and
055   test data. Hence, we propose an alternate approach to mono-to-binaural synthesis that our experiments
056   suggest can scale to universal binaural rendering, or at least represents a strong environment-agnostic
057   baseline towards it. In particular, we discover the “(mono audio, source position) ↦ binaural audio”
058   task can be precomposed with parameter-free transforms into mono audio enhancement tasks that
059   can be performed surprisingly well by off-the-shelf denoising audio models, such as those found in
060   text-to-speech vocoders. Finally, we analyze our approach’s design choices and the limitations of
061   automated metrics across systems revealed by our work. Explicitly, our contributions include:
062
      • Showing that existing neural models highly overfit to non-spatial acoustic features. This includes
063
        releasing the first new benchmark dataset for the task (TUT Mono-to-Binaural), using am-
064     bisonic recordings of anechoic speech (TUT Sound Events 2018 ANSYN; Adavanne et al., 2019)

066
      • BinauralZero, a novel, state-of-the-art baseline for universal neural mono-to-binaural audio
067
        synthesis, leveraging parameter-free transforms (geometric time warping, amplitude scaling),

071     outperforming them on our new TUT Mono-to-Binaural benchmark.
072   • Ablations to BinauralZero to analyze how denoising and warping close the representational
073     gap of mono audio and its binaural perception. Based on the automated loss metrics attained by
074     our training-free method versus existing work, we find that current phase, amplitude, waveform,
075     and STFT metrics can mislead when comparing in-domain neural mono-to-binaural systems,
076     and mathematically derive properties of these metrics in high-error regimes.

080   2.1   BACKGROUND
081
082   The reproduction of virtual acoustic environments has been modeled as room- and listener-based
083   transformations of directional source audio, as expressed as LTI systems in DSP via convolutional
084   application of RIRs and HRTFs, respectively (Savioja et al., 1999). However, the computational cost
085   of wave-based RIR simulation (Välimäki et al., 2012) and the collection cost of measuring HRTFs
086   (Li & Peissig, 2020) lead to the use of simplified geometric models and generic HRTFs in practice
087
      (Sunder et al., 2015). Motivated by the difficulty of collecting HRTF and RIR data, Gebru et al.
      (2021) showed that an implicit HRTF can be learned by a temporal CNN, Richard et al. (2022) and
088
      Lee et al. (2022) showed that neural networks can estimate RIR filters from training data, and Luo
089
      et al. (2022) learn an implicit neural representation of an acoustic field for spatial audio generation.
090   These works motivate using deep learning to supersede an explicit binaural reproduction pipeline.
091   Hence, Richard et al. (2021) proposed one of first uses of neural networks for mono-to-binaural
092   synthesis, composing a neural time-warping module (WarpNet) and a temporal (hyper-)convolutional
093   neural network (CNN) to directly map mono audio to binaural waveforms. BinauralGrad (Leng et al.,
094   2022) was the first to use a denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM), composed of two stages:
095   the first denoises a channel-averaged waveform, then the second conditions on this, the original mono
096   audio, and their geometric warps to jointly denoise both channels.
097   Since then, better incorporation of the inductive biases from DSP have led to neural systems that
098   are more efficient or improve objective rendering metrics. Neural Fourier Shift (NFS; Lee & Lee,
099   2023) predicts delays and scaling from speaker locations and match the above methods’ perceptual
100   spatial similarity with a much smaller model. Huang et al. (2022) show that mono-to-binaural audio
101   synthesis can be combined with the use of discrete audio codes to improve spectral loss. Kitamura &
102   Itou (2023) used a structured state space sequence (S4) model for the mono-to-binaural task and attain
103   similar loss metrics to above works. To improve the phase loss of their chosen systems, DopplerBAS
104
      (Liu et al., 2023) incorporated the Doppler effect in the conditioning of WarpNet and BinauralGrad,
      and DIFFBAS (Li et al., 2024b) proposed an interaural phase difference loss atop WarpNet and NFS.
105
106   Finally, there is a broader body of work using different conditioning settings for multi-channel audio.
107   One line of work uses visual conditioning for the generation of binaural audio (Xu et al., 2021; Parida
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a;b; Liang et al., 2023; Somayazulu et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2021;


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Yoshida et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c;d; Liu et al., 2024). Also, for music applications
109   there is a generative task, where plausible and subjectively appealing binaural renderings are imputed
110   from a single-channel recording of multi-source audio (e.g., Chun et al., 2015; Serrà et al., 2023; Li
111   et al., 2024a; Zang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024).

      Given the ongoing use of Richard et al. (2021)’s baseline Binaural Speech dataset by existing
116
      works despite its small training set (two hours) and fixed acoustic properties (room, language, shared
117
      bank of speakers in train and test, maximal distance of 1.5m), we set out to define a simple test-only
118   benchmark to assess whether mono-to-binaural models trained on Binaural Speech and future datasets
119   are retaining basic binaural rendering functionality in a relatively clean setting.
120
121
      Hence we build TUT Mono-to-Binaural, a simple and analogous benchmark which we release at
      [URL at camera-ready; see Supplementary Material for examples]. It demonstrates a new
122

123
      their multi-channel ambisonic recordings. We start from the overlap-free audio (OV1) in the TUT
124                                                                2
      Sound Events 2018 ANSYN sound localization dataset (Adavanne et al., 2019), which takes real
125                                                                    3

126
      azimuths, and distances into anechoic first-order Ambisonic (FOA) recordings, with four audio
127
      channels to cover 3D space; see Adavanne et al. (2018) for more details. Overall, there are around 2
128   hours of recordings in the dataset. In particular, the original monophonic recordings include spoken
129   French sentences sampled at 44.1 kHz with an AT8035 shotgun microphone connected to a Zoom
130   H4n recorder (Mesaros et al., 2018). We then convert the FOA’s location annotations (elevation,
      azimuth, distance) into a Cartesian coordinate system p = (x, y, z) to match the format of Binaural
                                                                src
131
132   Speech. Next, ground-truth metadata was leveraged to cut out the speech segments from the FOA
133   recordings using their provided timestamps. Finally, the FOA recordings are rendered as binaural
                               4
134   audio using OmniTone, a well-established commercial DSP ambisonic decoder that projects the
135   highly spatial FOA recording down into a binaural rendering. This gives 1,174 binaural speech
136   segments, each about 2s, corresponding to each’s own 3D location. These become ground truths for
137   the original DCASE 2016 Task 2 mono audio with their converted Cartesian coordinates.
138   The key idea is that TUT is acoustically and spatially simpler (anechoic room, stationary speech)
139   while being out-of-domain in the speech itself (unseen speaker, unseen language, unseen microphone,
140   broader elevation coverage, distances up to 10m) so if supervised models have learned to model and
141   generalize spatial properties rather than acoustic confounders, we would expect them to still produce
142   reasonable renderings after training only on Binaural Speech or future mono-to-binaural corpora.

144   2.3     M EASURING G ENERALIZATION VIA H UMAN E VALUATION
145
146   Prior work defines a number of automated and human evaluations to assess mono-to-binaural ren-
147   dering. Later in this work we find that automated metrics decorrelate with perceptual metrics
148   (Section 4.2), so for now we focus on the ultimate goal of matching the ground truth with regards
149   to human spatial hearing, under the existing benchmark and our proposed benchmark. Following
150   precedent from past work, for reference-free evaluations we use mean opinion score (MOS). For
151   reference-based evaluations we use the more sample efficient multiple stimuli with hidden refer-
152   ence and anchor (MUSHRA), especially as references are generally canonical in binaural audio
153
      (unlike in text-to-speech). We categorize the human evaluations in literature into three broad axes:
154
      • Naturalness: The overall naturalness and intelligibility of the synthesized audio content. We
155
        capture this as naturalness MOS (N-MOS), which is analogous to (regular) MOS in Leng et al.
156
        (2022), or to cleanliness plus part of realism MOS in Richard et al. (2021).

230   3.1   M OTIVATION
231
232
      As discussed in Section 1 and 2.2, it is difficult to collect real-world data, especially over the universe
      of possible positions, source audio types, and acoustic conditions, to directly train strong supervised
233
      models that generalize past the two-hour training set. We also note that other mitigations like
234
      synthetic data generation, in-context prompting, or parameter-efficient finetuning exist; however, to
235   our knowledge there are no strong multi-channel and/or spatially-aware audio models to facilitate
236   quality pseudolabeling or a finetuning that does not involve learning representations for part of the
237   input/output space from scratch. We leave such approaches to future work.
238
      For now, we note there are strong monophonic self-supervised speech models trained on large
239
      data. A large class of these are denoising (diffusion) vocoders, which are able to output denoised
240
      waveforms conditional on some semantic information (e.g., speech tokens). We also know that
241   denoising diffusion models are promising as an architecture, given BinauralGrad’s success on training
242   two position-conditional denoising diffusion models (though on requiring joint denoising of both
243   channels) to outperform WarpNet on Binaural Speech despite similar parameter counts (6.9M vs.
244   8.6M); the main downside of which was having to train only on the two-hour Binaural Speech dataset,
245   where its better in-distribution fitting made it more brittle out-of-domain.
246
      The gap between using existing speech mono denoising models is (1) they only operate on individual
247   channels, and (2) they are not trained to explicitly condition on position. However, we argue that
248   (1) is not an inherent issue, as there is no strict reason to do multi-channel rendering jointly as in
249   BinauralGrad (other than for parameter efficiency / regularization) if enough conditioning information
250   is given; recall binaural hearing is observing the same underlying soundscape from two ear positions.
251
      As for (2), we note that denoising waveform models learn to denoise at varying noise levels, which
252
      means that we can implicitly perform conditioning by providing an almost-complete waveform. The
253   vocoder does not even have to be trained on content-diverse data, as the behavior we need is cleaning
254   up direction-related artifacts, which should vary (along with distances and recording conditions) if
255   pretrained on a large corpus. It is then plausible that the ‘denoising basin’ of a such a vocoder is
256   able to fix slight issues in a hypothesized spatial transformation. In this work we consider geometric
257   time-warping, whose parameter-free version was used in WarpNet and subsequent works; and
258   amplitude scaling, which we are the first to explicitly apply to neural mono-to-binaural synthesis.
259   Remarkably, this overall scheme requires zero binaural data, and thus we name it BinauralZero. It
260   is summarized in Figure 2; the algorithm is also formally described in Appendix D as Algorithm 1.
261   Note that our method does not take into account room effects nor the listener’s head shape. Thus, one
262   interpretation is that BinauralZero produces spatial audio which imputes both a generalized low RIR
263   room (regularized by all the data the vocoder was trained on) and an implicit generic HRTF.

      4.1   H UMAN E VALUATIONS AND THEIR L IMITATIONS
353
      We reported the human evaluation results of BinauralZero in Section 2.3 (Table 1 and Figure 1), but
354   now discuss them here. On Binaural Speech (which, unlike BinauralZero, all supervised methods
355   were trained on), our subjective evaluation results show that BinauralZero improves in N-MOS over
356   WarpNet, BinauralGrad and NFS by 0.21, 0.06 and 0.08, while attaining similar S-MOS. MUSHRA
357   results (Figure 1) show that human raters do not have a statistically significant preference for any of
358   the methods WarpNet, BinauralGrad, NFS or BinauralZero, similar to the spatial-specific MUSHRA
359   conclusions of Lee & Lee (2023).
360   On the simpler TUT Mono-to-Binaural dataset however, we see that BinauralZero is the only one
361   to maintain performance, whereas all other methods sharply degrade. For example, BinauralZero
362   maintains an average S-MOS of above 3.7, whereas other systems degrade to an average S-MOS of
363   3.0 or less. The smaller and disjoint error bars on MUSHRA for TUT Mono-to-Binaural (Section 2.3)
364   show their performances on it are easily distinguishable, with BinauralZero outperforming other
365   mono-to-binaural methods in a significant way and performing close to the ground truth.
366   Samples can be heard at [URL at camera-ready; see Supplementary Material for now]. Note
367   that as BinauralZero does not condition on room information (in particular, ours uses a vocoder
368   derived from studio audiobook recordings), its syntheses can lack distance or reverb versus the ground
369   truth, which may be underrated in a generic ‘similarity’ prompt. Future universal-type approaches
370   that optionally condition on room information should consider finer similarity tasks focusing on
371   closeness in position like in Huang et al. (2022), or coherence over different-positioned renderings.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We considered the problem of position-conditional synthesis of binaural speech from mono audio
532
      across environments, which we term universal mono-to-binaural synthesis. We find that existing
533
      supervised learning schemes lose generalization ability due the low-to-zero resource nature of the
534   task, by introducing a novel dataset specifically designed to test basic generalization ability of
535   mono-to-binaural synthesizers. To motivate progress, we also described BinauralZero, a strong room-
536   and listener-agnostic baseline that is generally performant. A universal model that can optionally
537   condition on room and listener specifications is the clear next step, as well as improved automated
538   metrics and finer-grained evaluations of coherence across syntheses in the same environment. Finally,
539   we also made various empirical and theoretical recommendations of relevance to practitioners and
      system evaluators. Limitations and impacts are further discussed in Appendix A.

748   A    L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
749
750   BinauralZero uses an off-the-shelf neural vocoder which is conditioned on (log-mel) spectrogram
751   features and no positional information which makes it difficult to condition towards a target phase
752   spectrum. For this reason, our method struggles to directly and accurately process the phase informa-
753   tion in binaural audio, leading to high Phase `2 error. Furthermore, our method does not encode or use
754   any room or head shape information. We hypothesize that this fact helps our method be competitive
755   across room and acoustic environments, but fundamentally limits it from always matching supervised
      methods trained on a specific room and acoustic environment. Future work could learn to optionally


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      condition on such information. Future work could also consider non-speech sources, though we
757   speculate that using a large-scale general-domain vocoder that has seen speech, music, and sound
758   events may be sufficient to progress towards universal mono-to-binaural audio synthesis.
759
760
      The proposed method employs a novel approach for enhancing mono audio signals into binaural audio.
      This technique has the potential to significantly improve the audio experience in augmented reality
761
      (AR) and virtual reality (VR) applications by creating a more immersive and realistic soundscape.
762
      The enhanced spatial audio cues generated by the proposed method can contribute to a heightened
763   sense of presence and immersion within virtual environments. Additionally, the proposed method
764   for transforming mono audio to binaural audio carries the potential for misuse in audio deepfake
765   applications, where it could be employed to enhance the perceived realism and naturalness of
766   manipulated audio through the introduction of artificially generated spatial cues.
