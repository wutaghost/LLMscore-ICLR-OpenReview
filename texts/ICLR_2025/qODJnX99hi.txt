009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers and their attention mechanism have been revolutionary in the field of
012            Machine Learning. While originally proposed for the language data, they quickly
013            found their way to the image, video, graph, etc. data modalities with various
014            signal geometries. Despite this versatility, generalizing the attention mechanism
015            to scenarios where data is presented at different scales from potentially different
016            modalities is not straightforward. The attempts to incorporate hierarchy and multi-
017            modality within transformers are largely based on ad hoc heuristics, which are not
018            seamlessly generalizable to similar problems with potentially different structures.
019
               To address this problem, in this paper, we take a fundamentally different approach:
               we first propose a mathematical construct to represent multi-modal, multi-scale
020
               data. We then mathematically derive the neural attention mechanics for the pro-
021
               posed construct from the first principle of entropy minimization. We show that
022            the derived formulation is optimal in the sense of being the closest to the standard
023            Softmax attention while incorporating the inductive biases originating from the
024            hierarchical/geometric information of the problem. We further propose an effi-
025            cient algorithm based on dynamic programming to compute our derived attention
026            mechanism. By incorporating it within transformers, we show that the proposed
027            hierarchical attention mechanism not only can be employed to train transformer
028            models in hierarchical/multi-modal settings from scratch, but it can also be used to
029            inject hierarchical information into classical, pre-trained transformer models post
030
               training, resulting in more efficient models in zero-shot manner.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The field of Deep Learning has recently experienced a spectacular breakthrough with the rise of
036   Large Language Models (LLMs). It is no secret that this success is largely owed to the Transformer
037   neural architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) and its self-attention mechanism. Although they were
038   originally proposed to work with language (Beltagy et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al.,
039
      2018; Liu et al., 2019), transformers have found their way to deal with images (Dosovitskiy et al.,
      2020; Touvron et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021), video (Arnab et al., 2021; Bertasius et al.; Li et al., 2022;
040
      Neimark et al., 2021), audio (Borsos et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2021; Koutini et al., 2021; Verma &
041
      Berger, 2021), graphs (Min et al., 2022; Rampášek et al., 2022; Rong et al., 2020; Yun et al., 2019),
042   groups (Hutchinson et al., 2021; Tai et al., 2019), manifolds (He et al., 2021) and point clouds (Guo
043   et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021) without significantly altering their basic neural attention mechanism.
044   This is mainly due to the fact that, unlike many other neural architectures, transformers incorporate
045   data geometry not by architectural priors but by explicit, black-box, position embedding functions,
046   which can be easily replaced from one domain to another.
047
      Despite this versatility, the information in real world quite often comes in different modalities and at
048   different scales. In terms of geometry, this means that we deal with problems where each datapoint
049   may occupy multiple, mutually-inconsistent geometries at potentially different scales. This is indeed
050   challenging, even for transformers! To address these challenges, various novel (but often heuristic)
051   neural architectures have been proposed to deal with multi-modal (Deshmukh et al., 2023; Huang
052   et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2019a; Prakash et al., 2021; Truong et al., 2021; Zhang &
053   Zhang, 2020; Zhu et al., 2021) and hierarchical data (Cao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Pappagari
      et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhu & Soricut, 2021).


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Aside from the heuristic-based nature of many of these empirical architectures, they quite often
055   suffer from a more practical dilemma. On one hand, many such frameworks tend to partially discard
056   geometrical or hierarchical information depriving the learning task from valuable domain knowledge
057   which can significantly reduce the model’s statistical complexity. On the other hand, by incorporating
058   the full geometrical knowledge of different modalities and their hierarchical structure within these
059   heuristic frameworks, we often end up with highly problem-specific architectures that are hardly
060   generalizable to other similar problems.
061   To address this challenge in a unified and principled way, in this work, we take a radically different
062   approach. In particular:
063
064   • Instead of coming up with yet another heuristic neural architecture right off the bat, we first propose
065     a mathematical construct called nested signal to formally represent multi-geometry, hierarchical
066     information. As we show, the proposed formalism enables us to coherently represent different
067     geometrical domains at different scales while maintaining its generality across different problems.
068   • In order to define mathematically-sound neural operations on nested signals, we turn to the
069     attention mechanism. In particular, first we show that the standard Softmax self-attention (Vaswani
070     et al., 2017) can be mathematically derived from the principle of entropy minimization. Then by
071     generalizing this principle to nested signals, we derive the hierarchical self-attention (HSA) neural
072     mechanics which is the generalization of the Softmax attention mechanism for nested signals.
073   • We further show that the attention weights derived from the HSA are optimal in the sense of being
074     the closest to flat Softmax attention weights in terms the total KL-divergence, while at the same
075     time adhering to the hierarchical structure of the data.
076
      • Next, we propose an efficient algorithm based on dynamic programming to calculate the HSA,
077
        that is provably faster than its direct evaluation. By implementing HSA within the transformer
078
        architecture, we empirically show that we are able to train models that can seamlessly incorporate
079     the hierarchical/multi-modal domain knowledge to arrive at better and more efficient transformers.
080
081
      • Last but not least, we show that HSA can further replace the standard Softmax self-attention
        operation in pre-trained transformers and significantly reduce the number of self-attention FLOPs
082
        while incurring minimal Accuracy drop, in an entirely zero-shot manner.

085   2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087   Hierarchical models: The notion of hierarchy has played a key role in data representation and
088   clustering in Machine Learning (Murtagh & Contreras, 2012; Shetty & Singh, 2021). In the context
089   of transformers, the idea of multi-scale attention has been mainly used to combat the long-context
090   challenge in language (Huang et al., 2023a; Nawrot et al., 2021; Pappagari et al., 2019; Yang et al.,
091   2016; Ye et al., 2019), but it has also made its way into vision (Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022)
092   and audio (Yu et al., 2022). Nevertheless, most of these frameworks deal with a single modality
093
      that occupies the same geometry, just at different scales. Our proposed framework, in contrast, can
      incorporate an arbitrary number of mutually-inconsistent geometries within its representation of the
094
      multi-scale data. Another related line of work is based on hierarchical matrices (Hackbusch, 1999;
095
      Hackbusch & Khoromskij, 2000) that have been used traditionally for clustering (Thiesson & Kim,
096   2012) as well as transition matrix approximation (Amizadeh et al., 2012), but more recently for
097   attention matrix approximation (Zhu & Soricut, 2021).
098
099
      Multi-modal models: Multi-modality has been vastly explored in Machine Learning (Baltrušaitis
      et al., 2018) and more recently within various neural architectures, using various fusion techniques
100
      (Bayoudh et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2019; Suzuki & Matsuo, 2022). As for multi-modal
101
      transformers (Xu et al., 2023), most frameworks are tailored toward a fixed set of modalities, e.g.
102   vision-language (Huang et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2019a; Zhu et al., 2021), audio-visual
103   (Truong et al., 2021), audio-language (Deshmukh et al., 2023), graph-language (Zhang & Zhang,
104   2020), vision-pose-audio (Rahman et al., 2021), audio-vision-language (Tsai et al., 2019), etc. The
105   fusion of different modalities in these frameworks typically takes place via a heuristic operation at
106   the embedding or the attention stages resulting in distinct architectural variants, which are typically
107   categorized as (1) single-stream (e.g. (Li et al., 2019)), (2) multi-stream (e.g. (Lu et al., 2019a)),
      and (3) hybrid-stream (e.g. (Lin et al., 2020)). However, most of these frameworks either ignore the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      geometrical (positional) information for some of the input modalities, or impose artificial restrictions
109   on input geometries such as alignment.
110
111   Geometric Deep Learning: Geometric Deep Learning (Bronstein et al., 2021) studies the invariance
112
      and equivariance properties of deep learning models by introducing the notion of signal and its
      geometry which is explicitly modeled via the signal’s domain. We build our framework also based on
113
      the same notion of signal and generalize it further to nested signals which can represent hierarchical,
114
      multi-modal data which potentially encompass multiple domains. Also, most frameworks within
115   Geometric Deep Learning achieve the desired equivariance properties through the model’s architecture
116   (e.g. CNNs (Li et al., 2021b), GNNs (Wu et al., 2020), and Group-equivaraint CNNs (Finzi et al.,
117   2020)). A prominent exception is the LieTransformer (Hutchinson et al., 2021) where the desired
118   group-equivariance is achieved by explicit modeling of the position information and its separate
119   similarity computation (as opposed to adding it to the feature vectors). The formulation of the position
120   information in our framework is in part inspired by the LieTransfomer.
121   The theoretical foundations of self-attention: Despite its revolutionary success in Deep Learning,
122   there has been quite little effort to understand the theoretical foundations of self-attention. These
123   efforts provide various interpretations of self-attention, including the probabilistic view (Fan et al.,
124   2020; Shim, 2022), the causal view (Rohekar et al., 2024), the structural inference view (Singh &
125   Buckley, 2023), the dynamical system view (Dutta et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023b; Lu et al., 2019b),
126   the statistical mechanical view (Rende et al., 2023), the variational denoising view (Nguyen et al.,
127   2024), the clustering view (Geshkovski et al., 2024), and the Hopfield network view (Ramsauer et al.,
128   2020). In this paper, we provide a statistical mechanical perspective to derive self-attention from
129
      the first principle of entropy minimization; in that sense, our interpretation is closely related to the
      statistical mechanical, denoising and Hopfiled network views. More importantly, our interpretation
130
      lends itself to straightforward generalization to the hierarchical self-attention mechanism which, as
131
      we show, is both theoretically optimal and efficiently computable.

133   3    R EPRESENTING H IERARCHICAL , M ULTI -G EOMETRY DATA
134
135   In Geometric Deep Learning, a signal x is defined as the mapping x : Ω → C, where the set Ω is the
136   domain of the signal and C is a vector space, typically Rd with d being the channel dimension. For
137   example, an RGB image is a signal where Ω is the 2D grid and C = R3 , i.e. the RGB color space.
138   Similarly, text can be seen as a signal with Ω being the 1D grid and C a word embedding space. More
139   niche applications in Geometric Deep Learning Bronstein et al. (2021) extend the notion of signals to
140   the domain of graphs, gauges, manifolds, etc. by defining the appropriate structure for Ω. We refer
141
      to the set of all such possible domains as D. The elements Ω ∈ D are not necessarily vector spaces
      (e.g. 2D grid). In order to numerically handle these spaces, we define a special signal εΩ : Ω → Rc
142
      for each Ω ∈ D which maps the elements of each domain in D to Rc ; we refer to this special signal
143
      as the position embedding. Given εΩ , each signal x defined on Ω is seen as x : εΩ (Ω) → C. In
144   Appendix B, we generalize the notion of signal to encompass traditional tabular features.
145
146   In this section, we introduce the notion of nested signals which is the key modeling tool to represent
147
      multi-modal, hierarchical data. To this end, we first define the set of all simple signals S as the set
      of all possible signals defined on all possible domains; that is, S = {x : Ω → C | Ω ∈ D}. Note
148
      that the signals defined on different domains may have different channel dimensions; to make the
149
      channel dimension uniform across different domains, we zero-pad the lower dimensional signals to
150   the maximum channel dimensionality d across different domains, such that each element of S has the
151   same channel dimension d regardless of its domain.
152
      Definition 3.1 (Nested Signal).  The set of d-dimensional nested signals up to depth ℓ, Nℓ , is
153   recursively defined as Nℓ = x : Ω → U | Ω ∈ D, U ∈ {Nℓ−1 , Rd } , where N0 = Rd .
154   Furthermore, define N = Nℓ as ℓ → ∞; each element x ∈ N is then referred as a nested signal. The
155   top-level domain Ω ∈ D of a nested signal x is denoted by r(x).
156   For example, a website is a nested signal where at the top level, we have webpages defined on the
157   nodes of a graph domain representing the link structure between the webpages. Each webpage is in
158   turn another nested signal where at its top level we have an unordered set of textboxes and images
159   constituting the page. Going one level further, each textbox or image is a (simple) signal assigning
160   word embeddings or pixel values to the nodes of 1D or 2D grid domains, respectively. Fig. 1(Left)
161   depicts this example. While in theory, the domains Ω ∈ D can be infinite, in practice, we mostly
      deal with nested and simple signals defined on finite Ω’s. In particular, a nested signal x is said to be


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      finite if the domains Ω’s at all of its nesting levels are finite. Given the set of position embeddings
163   ε = {εΩ | Ω ∈ D}, a finite nested signal can be represented by a signal hierarchy as defined below.
164
165
      Definition 3.2 (Signal Hierarchy). For a finite nested signal x, its signal hierarchy hx is a tree with
      the root node Rx associated with r(x), the top level domain of x. The children of Rx are defined as
166
      chd(Rx ) = {hx(u) | u ∈ r(x)} where x(u) is the value of signal (possibly another nested signal)
167
      at u. If x(u) is a vector instead of a signal, then hx(u) is simply x(u). Furthermore, each child
168
      hx(u) ∈ chd(Rx ) is annotated by εr(x) (u), the position embedding vector dictated by its parent node.
169
170
      We denote the nodes (and equivalently their corresponding sub-trees) in the signal hierarchy hx
171
      by upper-case letters. Any set of sibling nodes in hx is referred as a family. The members of a
172
      family are nested signals (or real vectors for the leaf nodes) that reside on the same domain Ω
173   and therefore share the same position embedding function εΩ . Furthermore, for A ∈ hx , chd(A),
174   sib(A) and ℓ(A) represent the set of A’s children, its siblings and the index set of the leaf node
175   descendants of A, respectively. Two nodes in hx are called unrelated if neither of them is descendant
176   of the other. For two unrelated nodes A and B, their immediate common ancestor is denoted by
177   ica(A, B), while their highest distinct ancestors are denoted by A′ and B ′ , respectively, where we
178   have A′ , B ′ ∈ chd(ica(A, B)); i.e., A′ and B ′ are always siblings even if A and B are not. See
179   Appendix A for the notational details as well as a visual demonstration of the tree-related concepts.
180   Since sibling nodes share the same position embedding function, the relative positional distance (or
181   similarity) between them is well-defined. More generally, for any two unrelated nodes A, B ∈ hx ,
182   we can form a well-defined positional distance between them by comparing the position embeddings
183   of A′ and B ′ which is well-defined since A′ and B ′ are always siblings. The implication of this
184   construction is indeed powerful as it would enable the signal hierarchy formalism to define meaningful
185   positional distance between any two unrelated nodes in the hierarchy regardless of their modalities or
186   signal types. Fig. 1(Right) shows the signal hierarchy representation for our earlier website example.

201   4   H IERARCHICAL S ELF -ATTENTION
202
203
      The nested signal formalism and its signal hierarchy representation introduced in the previous section
204   provide a systematic way to represent hierarchical data that can potentially span across different
205   modalities and domain structures. However, the question remains what kind of neural architectures
206   can handle such versatile data structure? To answer this question, we note that for non-hierarchical,
207   simple signals, the transformer architecture first introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) allows for a
208   unified representation learning methodology that can accommodate various signal domains (as long
209   as the position embedding is available), not to mention its remarkable success in revolutionizing deep
210   learning. Nevertheless, extending the attention mechanism to nested signals is not straightforward as
211   the information in the nested signal can come with different signal domains at different scales.
212   To address this problem, in this section, we first propose a statistical mechanical framework that ele-
213   gantly derives the classical Softmax attention mechanism from the principle of entropy minimization
214   when a finite (simple) signal is viewed as a physical system with N particles. By generalizing our
215   proposed construction to nested systems, we then derive a novel, theoretically-rigorous mechanism
      for calculating self-attention within nested signals, which we refer as Hierarchical Self-Attention


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      (HSA). By its direct construction, the proposed HSA mechanism aims at reducing the total entropy of
217   the nested system, or equivalently put, increasing information within the learned representation of
218   the nested signal. We further show that our proposed construction to derive HSA is optimal in the
219   sense of Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence from the Softmax attention weights if the hierarchical
220   structure were to be ignored. This result will subsequently open the door for the application of our
221   proposed formulation to approximate the inefficient Softmax attention in pre-trained transformers
222   using the more efficient hierarchical calculations if a hierarchy exists and can be imposed in a given
223   problem. Finally, we propose an efficient algorithm based on dynamic programming that calculates
224   HSA for a given signal hierarchy hx in O(M · b2 ), where M is the number of families in hx and b is
225
      its maximum branching factor (i.e. family size).

      5     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
379
380   In this section, we present an empirical study aiming at two main goals: (1) showing the capability
381   of the HSA mechanism in incorporating useful domain hierarchy knowledge into training better
382   transformer models from scratch, and (2) demonstrating the unique capacity of HSA as post-training
383   approximation of the Softmax attention in pre-trained transformer models in order to reduce the
384   self-attention computation FLOPS in a zero-shot manner.
385
386   5.1    H IERARCHICAL L ANGUAGE
387
388   Despite its unimodality, natural language data often comes in a semantically meaningful hierarchy
389   (e.g. sections, paragraphs, sentences, etc.) which can be seen as granular abstraction of the underlying
390
      semantics in the data. Nonetheless, most transformer-based frameworks ignore this hierarchical
      structure which not only discards valuable prior knowledge about the semantics of the text, but
391
      in the long context scenario, it can also result in loss of information due to truncation (which is a
392
      common practice for long sequences in order to manage the computational complexity of the Softmax
393   attention). HSA avoids truncation for long sequences by effectively reducing the computational
394   complexity of the attention calculation via incorporating the hierarchical abstraction.
395
396
      For our empirical assessment, we have chosen the text classification problem for the sentiment
      analysis task on two datasets: IMDB (imd; Maas et al., 2011b), and Elec (ele; McAuley & Leskovec,
397
      2013)—for sentiment classification in movie reviews and Amazon electronics product reviews,
398
      respectively (ama). The rationale for choosing these datasets lies in their inclusion of lengthy texts,
399   which means they can benefit from hierarchical representation. For details, see Appendix H.1.
400
401   Signal Hierarchy: We represent each text datapoint in our datasets as a 3-level signal hierarchy:
402
      paragraphs, sentences and tokens. The position embedding at each level is the 1D grid embedding
      materialized by random Fourier features (Li et al., 2021a). The tokens form the leaves of each
403
      signal hierarchy and are represented via vector embeddings. We have experimented with two token-
404
      embeddings in our experiments: the simple Word2Vec (wor; Mikolov et al., 2013), and the richer,
405   transformer-based T5 (t5; Raffel et al., 2020).
406

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
521
522   In this paper, we propose HSA, a novel mathematical framework for generalizing classical Softmax
523   self-attention mechanism to hierarchical problems that not only occupy multiple scales but may be
524   also defined on multiple geometries. Unlike many existing work that approach these problems via
525   heuristic neural architectures, we mathematically derive our formulation from the principle of entropy
526   minimization given the (nested) data signal is seen as a statistical mechanical system. Given its
527
      strong theoretical and algorithmic properties, we empirically showed that HSA can be used to inject
      hierarchical domain knowledge into training of transformer models and hence produce models with
528
      better generalization. We further showed that HSA can be used as a self-attention approximation
529
      technique for pre-trained models to significantly reduce the FLOPs needed for self-attention at the
530   test time. This opens the door for HSA to be used as a "fast" self-attention technique on long context
531   data, even after training.
532
533
      One high-impact future application of HSA is training large-scale foundational models that can
      naturally handle multi-modal and hierarchical inputs using the HSA formalism. On the theoretical
534
      side, HSA can be also extended to include non-Softmax attention mechanisms (See Appendix J).
535
      The other important future direction is application of HSA to transformer decoder for hierarchical
536   auto-regressive generation. This is important specially because it has the potential to boost LLMs in
537   terms of both generalization (by incorporating hierarchical, multi-modal domain knowledge) and
538   speed (due to the low-rank nature of HSA computation). Due to its significance, we have laid the
539   foundations of hierarchical decoding via HSA in Appendix F while leaving details to future work.

      B    G ENERALIZING T HE N OTION OF S IGNAL
896
      In standard Geometric Deep Learning, signals typically represent data structures in Computer Vision,
897
      Audio Processing, Natural Language Processing and Graph and Manifold Processing. But the
898   notion off signal is quite versatile and can be generalized to include feature representations in
899   classical Machine Learning. In particular, we note the special case where the signal domain Ω is a
900   countable, discrete set with no additional structure. In this case, if the elements of Ω are conceptually
901   indistinguishable, then any signal x on Ω is said to be defined on an unordered set and subsequently,
902   the position embedding εΩ maps all the elements of Ω to the constant vector 0. The latter conveys
903   that there is no positional information associated with the signal. As an example, a vector set can be
904   seen as a signal defined on an unordered set.
905   On the other hand, if the elements of Ω are distinguishable, we can define a bijective position
906   embedding εΩ to carry that information into the position vector space. We refer to signals defined
907   on such Ω domains as key-value signals. For instance, a tabular feature vector in classical Machine
908   Learning can be seen as a set of key-value pairs where the keys are the feature names and the values
909   are the feature values, and hence modeled as a key-value signal. In this case, a text embedding
910   model can be used to map the feature names into a vector space and regard the results as the position
911   embeddings of those features. In other words, the notion of signal in our work is quite generic and
912
      encompasses not only the signal types in Geometric Deep Learning but also the classical tabular
      feature vectors.

1058   D.3    B LACK - BOX ATTENTION C OMPUTATION
1059
1060   It is important to note that Lines 16-18 in Algorithm 2 perform the standard Softmax attention
1061   mechanism on the members of a family that contains node A. In other words, our proposed HSA
1062   algorithm can be seen as a divide-and-conquer algorithm where the attention computation on the
1063   whole sequence (i.e. the hierarchy’s leaves) is broken down into attention computation on the much
1064   smaller families in the hierarchy (aka the sub-problems) via the bottom-up part of the algorithm,
1065
       and then these intermediate results (aka the sufficient statistics) are combined through the top-down
       part of the algorithm to produce the final self-attention output. From this perspective, if the average
1066
       branching factor (i.e. the family size) in the hierarchy is b, then on average, the sub-problem attention
1067
       calculation takes O(b) time and memory for each node A, which makes the O(b2 ) complexity for the
1068   entire family. Then intuitively for the total of M families in the hierarchy, the final computational
1069   complexity comes to O(M · b2 ). As a special case, for flat hierarchies where there is only M = 1
1070   family of size b = N (i.e. the sequence length), the complexity becomes O(N 2 ).
1071
       More importantly, from the practical perspective, the divide-and-conquer view of the proposed
1072
       algorithm encapsulates the sub-problem self-attention computation (in Lines 16-18 in Algorithm
1073
       2) as a black-box module that can be easily replaced by any exact or approximate function that
1074   computes the standard Softmax attention. This has a significant practical implication, as it allows the
1075   HSA algorithm to invoke any efficient attention computation frameworks in the literature as its base
1076   attention calculation sub-module. For instance, the quadratic factor b2 in O(M · b2 ) can be further
1077   reduced to linear if one employs one of the many approximation techniques proposed for efficient
1078   computation of Softmax attention Beltagy et al. (2020); Choromanski et al. (2020); Katharopoulos
1079   et al. (2020); Peng et al. (2021) as the black-box sub-problem attention computation module in Lines
       16-18 in Algorithm 2.

       D.4    GPU I MPLEMENTATION
1081
1082   The Algorithms 1–3 are technically classical tree-traversal algorithms which are typically not fit for
1083   parallel processing on GPU. Indeed, this would introduce a practical challenge for incorporation
1084   of HSA within modern Deep Learning frameworks. To address this challenge, in this section, we
1085   present two major techniques for introducing parallelization both at the node level for one signal
1086   hierarchy as well as at the batch level across multiple signal hierarchies.
1087   First, we note that all the summations in Algorithms 2 and 3 can be done in parallel for different sets
1088   of nodes in hx . In particular, if a summation statement can be parallelized for K nodes of hx , it can
1089   be implemented as a (sparse) matrix by dense vector multiplication W v, where W = [wi,j ]K×S is
1090   the sparse coefficient matrix and v = [vi ]S×1 contains the values of the input terms. In particular,
1091   wi,j is the weight of the jth term for computing the summed quantity at the ith node (typically 1
1092   or 0). As for the quantities in Algorithms 2 and 3, µk (·), µq (·) and η(·) can be parallelized over
1093   all the nodes in hx ; that is, in order to compute each one of these quantities for all nodes of hx ,
1094
       only one sparse matrix-vector multiplication is needed given the appropriate coefficient matrix. The
       computation of ϕ(·) and ϑ(·) is also parallelizable over the nodes belonging to the same depth in hx ;
1095
       in other words, given the appropriate coefficient matrices, we would need D sparse matrix-vector
1096
       multiplications to calculate each one of these quantities for all nodes in hx , where D is the depth of
1097   hx . Since the coefficient matrices in this scheme are highly sparse, we have represented the coefficient
1098   matrices using sparse tensors and used the efficient implementation of sparse matrix by dense vector
1099   multiplication in Pytorch to carry out the tree-based summations in Algorithms 2 and 3.
1100
       The other fundamental aspect of parallelization in Deep Learning is batch computation, which
1101
       typically boils down to matrix operations for the standard batches of fixed-size tensors. However,
1102
       in our scenario, the signal hierarchies in each batch are trees with different structures as well as
1103   potentially different signal types/modalities appearing in arbitrary arrangements for each signal
1104   hierarchy in the batch. This effectively makes the classical batch computation impossible for signal
1105   hierarchies. To address this challenge, we propose a completely different technique for batch
1106   parallelization. As explained above, we already have a method to parallelize the computations within
1107   each signal hierarchy; we can further parallelize the computations across different signal hierarchies
1108   in a batch by making them part of one hierarchy. In particular, we introduce a dummy root node and
1109   make each signal hierarchy in the batch a direct child of it. The position embedding for this dummy
1110   root is set to unordered-set embedding; that is, no position embedding. This way, we end up with only
1111
       one, wide signal hierarchy in our batch that is just one level deeper than the deepest signal hierarchy
       in the original batch. By performing the parallel version of Algorithms 1–3 (as described above)
1112
       on this one "concatenated" signal hierarchy, we effectively compute all the targeted quantities for
1113
       all signal hierarchies in the batch at the same time. We refer to this batch processing technique as
1114   breadth-wise tree concatenation.

1169
1170   The HSA-based, encoder-only architecture introduced in Appendix E is primarily suitable for
1171   classification and regression applications. However, for auto-regressive generation such as causal
1172   language modeling, we would need to have a decoder. One straightforward approach is to use an
1173
       encoder-decoder architecture where the encoder is HSA-based while the decoder is the standard
       sequential decoder. In particular, in this scheme, the hierarchical self-attention is only incorporated for
1174
       the initial prompt while for the generated text, we simply compute the standard flat attention. While
1175
       simple, this solution does not take the full advantage HSA, especially if the generated text allows for
1176   the similar hierarchical structure as the prompt text. For instance, if the hierarchy is built upon the
1177   sentence and paragraph structure of the prompt text, then it is fairly reasonable for the generated text
1178   to have the same hierarchical construct as well. The same can be said when the hierarchy is based on
1179   fixed hopping windows over the text. In such cases, a HSA-based, decoder-only architecture is needed
1180   to incorporate the hierarchical structure of the generated text during auto-regressive generation.
1181
       Theoretically speaking, for a HSA-based decoder during auto-regressive generation, we would need
1182   to maintain a dynamic signal hierarchy where every generated token augments the signal hierarchy
1183   with at least one new leaf node and possibly multiple non-leaf nodes. Once the signal hierarchy is
1184   updated, the HSA calculations are, in principle, the same as before. Nevertheless, there are two major
1185   issues here specific to auto-regressive generation. First, unlike the HSA mechanism introduced so far,
1186   due to causal generation of tokens, leaf nodes are only allowed to attend to the other leaf nodes that
1187   have appeared before them; that is, we would need a hierarchical causal masking mechanism. Second,
       running the full HSA algorithm for every generated token is inefficient as it would re-compute some

       F.1   H IERARCHICAL C AUSAL M ASKING
1192
1193   In the standard auto-regressive generation using the self-attention mechanism, in order to prohibit
1194   tokens from attending to the future tokens, one incorporates a causal mask in calculation of the
1195   attention weights via an appropriate lower-triangular mask matrix. However, this straightforward
1196   approach will not work with hierarchical self-attention mechanism because attention weights between
1197   all tokens are not computed simultaneously but rather in hierarchical fashion.
1198
       Nevertheless, one can easily show that if the standard causal masking is applied at each level of the
1199   hierarchical attention calculation, at the end, no leaf token will attend to its future tokens (i.e. the
1200   tokens to its right) in the hierarchy. In particular, as explained in Appendix D.3, Lines 16–18 of
1201   Algorithm 2 encapsulate a black-box Softmax self-attention function that is applied for each family
1202   in the hierarchy. For applying hierarchical causal masking, we can simply apply the standard causal
1203   masking within this black-box self-attention calculation. This is equivalent to replacing the sibling
1204   function sib(A) in lines 16–18 of Algorithm 2 with sibL (A) which restricts A’s siblings to the ones
1205   to its left (i.e. previous tokens). This simple black-box causal masking will further propagate through
1206   the hierarchy such that at the end, the leaf nodes will only attend to other leaf nodes that are located
1207
       to their left. Figure 5 illustrates this process through a toy example.

       F.2   H IERARCHICAL C ACHING
1235
1236   In standard auto-regressive generation, every generated token merely attends to the tokens seen so far
1237   whose projections are cached via a key-value cache. This makes the attention computation for each
1238   token linear in the (generated + prompt) sequence length. This simple idea, however, is not directly
1239   applicable to the hierarchical case. One important distinction that we need to keep in mind is that
1240   in the hierarchical case, we are not only generating a sequence but also a hierarchy that comes with
1241   it; in other words, the generated sequence is the set of the leaf nodes of a hierarchy that needs to be
       maintained and updated as well. As such, any caching mechanism would need to maintain and update


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       the signal hierarchy and not just its leaf nodes. Note that caching the hierarchy means maintaining its
1243   structure as well as its nodes’ sufficient statistics pre-computed by Algorithm 2.
1244
1245   Nevertheless, during generation, we do not need to keep the entire signal hierarchy. In particular, in
1246
       our HSA framework not every leaf node directly attend to every other leaf node; instead, leaf nodes
       that are not in the same family only attend to each other at the coarse scale through their highest
1247
       distinct ancestors. This means that during generation, a newly generated token (leaf node) only needs
1248
       to directly attend to its previously generated leaf siblings and not other leaf nodes. Instead it will
1249   indirectly attend to other leaf nodes en masse by attending to their highest ancestor that is not an
1250   ancestor of the new token. Following this scheme, we would only need to cache a sub-tree of the
1251   original hierarchy that consists of the ancestor line of the latest generated token as well as their
1252   immediate children nodes. We refer to this sub-tree as right-skewed because only the right-most
1253   sibling in each family across the signal hierarchy is allowed to have children. Figure 6(A) illustrates
1254   the maximal right-skewed sub-tree for the toy hierarchy in Figure 6(B).
1255   Once the the right-skewed sub-tree of the signal hierarchy is extracted, we can simply update as
1256   new tokens are generated. However, we have to be careful as not all of the newly generated tokens
1257   are added to the latest family: some new tokens may start a new family via a higher level of the
1258   hierarchy. For example, if the hierarchy for language data is built based upon the sentence and
1259   paragraph structure in the text, a new token is not always going to be part of the latest sentence or
1260   paragraph; it may start a new sentence or even a new paragraph. In such cases, more nodes need
1261   to be added to or deleted from the cache other than the new token’s leaf node. These two cases are
1262   illustrated in Figure 6(C)-(D).
1263   Finally we note that during the entire generation process the hierarchical cache remains a right-skewed
1264   tree which means that the CPU and memory complexity for calculating attention and maintaining
1265   the cache would be O(b logb N ) where N is the length of the generated sequence so far and b is
1266   the average branching factor of the hierarchy. This is in stark contrast to the classical key-value
1267   caching where the memory and computation are of O(N ) complexity, and hence shows the potential
1268   computational advantage of our hierarchical scheme.

1751
1752   One of the primary contributions of our work is generalizing Softmax attention from flat signals to
1753   the hierarchical structure of nested signals. This generalization is further confirmed by the theoretical
1754
       result of Theorem 1. However, there has been a significant effort in the literature to explore other
       forms of attention mechanisms than Softmax attention Child et al. (2019); Correia et al. (2019); Han
1755
       et al. (2025); Shen et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2024). One of the main motivations of departing from the
1756
       Softmax attention lies in the fact that Softmax attention induces dense probability distribution over
1757   all tokens. Sparse attention Child et al. (2019); Correia et al. (2019), on the other hand, organically
1758   induces sparse probability distributions over tokens which can greatly improve the interpretability and
1759   computational efficiency of transformer models. A natural question is then whether our hierarchical
1760   derivation can be applied to other forms of attention, in particular the sparse attention. In other words,
1761   can our formalism also generalize sparse attention from flat signals to the hierarchical structure of
1762   nested signals?

1839   In this appendix, we further expand on the experimental results for the zero-shot HSA approximation
1840   of RoBERTa presented in the main paper. In particular, we study the effects of approximating different
1841
       combination of layers as well as different hierarchical structures for the datasets reported in the main
       paper.
1842
1843
1844   K.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
1845
1846   Datasets: We have run experiments on 5 GLUE datasets (SST-2, CoLA, MRPC, RTE and QNLI) as
1847   well as the AGNEWS and IMDB datasets.
1848
1849
       Models: For each dataset, we have used the appropriate pre-trained RoBERTa checkpoint and
       configuration that has been fine-tuned on the corresponding task. Table 9 lists the checkpoints and
1850
       configuration used for each dataset. All of our experiments involve only evaluation of pre-trained
1851
       RobERTa without any training of fine-tuning it.
1852
1853   Metrics: We have computed Accuracy, Precision, Recall and F1 Score to measure the accuracy drop
1854   of pre-trained RoBERTa as its various layers are approximated by HSA.
1855   Impacted Layers: As mentioned in the main paper, approximating all self-attention layers of
1856   RoBERTa typically leads to significant zero-shot accuracy drop across all tasks. However, approxi-
1857   mating a subset of layers can introduce more reasonable gap while still benefiting from HSA speed up
1858   in terms of the number of FLOPs. Nevertheless, finding the best layer combination is a combinatorial
1859   problem. To alleviate this issue, instead of examining all different combinations, we only look at
1860   certain combinations based on two empirical observations. In particular, we observed that earlier
1861
       layers in the network are typically more sensitive to approximation, whereas the latter ones are more
       amenable to it. This observation intuitively makes sense because the sooner approximation takes
1862
       place in the network, the higher approximation error accumulates along the network. Moreover,
1863
       having consecutive layers approximated typically increases the accuracy gap whereas interleaving
1864   them with regular self-attention layers decreases the gap.
1865
1866   Based on these two observations, in our experiments, we only examine combinations where a start
1867
       layer (denoted by SL) and every other layer after that are approximated by HSA. The X-axis for the
       bar plots in this section is associated with SL. Also, the right bar in each plot represents the metrics
1868
       for the original model without HSA approximation.
1869
1870   Hierarchy: For these experiments we chose to use fixed hierarchies based on non-overlapping
1871   hopping windows rather than semantic hierarchies based on the text structure. The reason behind this
1872   choice is that semantic hierarchies (such as sentences, paragraphs, etc.) are example dependant which
1873
       means they would incur different number of FLOPs for different examples. But since our ultimate
       goal from this experiment is to reduce the number of flops consistently across the data, we opted to
1874
       use fixed hierarchies.
1875
1876   The fixed hierarchies here are characterized by having a fixed branching factor for all the nodes
1877   belonging to the same level of the hierarchy. We then denote such hierarchy by the tuple (A, B, C, ...)
1878   where A is the branching factor at the lowest level of the hierarchy, B is the branching factor for
1879
       the next level and so on. Having this notation in place, we have experimented with the following
       hierarchies:
1880
1881
             1. (2, 2, 2, 2): A hierarchy with low branching factor at all levels.
1882
1883
             2. (2, 4, 8, 16): A hierarchy with low branching factor on the bottom and high branching factor
1884            on the top.
1885
1886         3. (7, 7, 7, 7): A hierarchy with high branching factors at all levels.
1887
1888         4. (8, 4, 2) A hierarchy with high branching factor on the bottom and low branching factor on
