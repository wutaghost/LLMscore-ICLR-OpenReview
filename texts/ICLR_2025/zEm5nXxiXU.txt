011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013              As large language models (LLMs) rapidly advance and integrate into daily life,
014              the privacy risks they pose are attracting increasing attention. We focus on a spe-
015              cific privacy risk where LLMs may help identify the authorship of anonymous
016              texts, which challenges the effectiveness of anonymity in real-world systems such
017
                 as anonymous peer review systems. To investigate these risks, we present AID-
                 Bench, a new benchmark that incorporates several author identification datasets,
018
                 including emails, blogs, reviews, articles, and research papers. AIDBench utilizes
019
                 two evaluation methods: one-to-one authorship identification, which determines
020              whether two texts are from the same author; and one-to-many authorship iden-
021              tification, which, given a query text and a list of candidate texts, identifies the
022              candidate most likely written by the same author as the query text. We also in-
023              troduce a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based method to enhance the
024              large-scale authorship identification capabilities of LLMs, particularly when input
025              lengths exceed the models’ context windows, thereby establishing a new base-
026              line for authorship identification using LLMs. Our experiments with AIDBench
027              demonstrate that LLMs can correctly guess authorship at rates well above random
028
                 chance, revealing new privacy risks posed by these powerful models.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have experienced widespread application (Zhao et al., 2023; Chang
      et al., 2024) due to their remarkable capabilities in processing and generating human-like text.
034
      Specifically, they are able to follow human instructions (Aw et al., 2023) to accomplish various
035
      tasks, both in professional and personal spheres. At the same time, the wide adoption of LLMs
036   also brings impact on societal paradigms that cannot be overlooked (Huang et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
037   2024).
038
039
      This paper delves into one such challenge posed by LLMs: the issue of privacy risks (Yang et al.,
      2013; Zheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a;c). In the study of privacy risks associated with language
040
      models, researchers primarily focus on the leakage of private information in the training data (Li
041
      et al., 2023b; Nasr et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023). As we navigate through a world where information
042   is increasingly digitized and interconnected, the potential for privacy breaches becomes more diverse
043   and realistic. Specifically, we explore how large models, through their advanced capabilities and
044   extensive knowledge base, can identify anonymous text based on the context and content at hand.
045   Due to exceptional text analysis capabilities, LLMs can be used to infer private information and
046   considered as a tool to help individuals conduct privacy attacks (Staab et al., 2023) and infer the
047   author’s information of anonymous texts (Nyffenegger et al., 2023).
048   This kind of privacy breach is well motivated by the real-world application of anonymous systems.
049   Systems such as academic peer reviews1 , student confession websites, and corporate employee
050   anonymous communication platforms, are designed to protect the identity of individuals (Edman
051   & Yener, 2009; Hohenberger et al., 2014), ensuring that their comments or concerns can be shared
052   without fear of retribution or bias. However, the integrity of these systems is highly dependent on
053

      the effectiveness of their anonymity. The anonymity of these systems is under threat if the text pro-
055   duced within them can be used to accurately identify the author. For instance, consider the academic
056   peer review system, where anonymity is a cornerstone of the process (Panadero & Alqassab, 2019;
057   Coomber & Silver, 2010). Typically, within a conference, a reviewer may be assigned with evalu-
058   ating multiple papers, and the reviewer is given a random identifier when they write a review for a
059   paper (Ragone et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2022b). A reviewer may participate in multiple events over
060   a period of time. People usually cannot link the random identifiers of a single reviewer in nature.
061   However, equipped with powerful large language models, one may be able to identify a reviewer
062   based on the careful comparison between the content of a review and that of other reviews. If the
063
      above process were achieved with high probability, it would pose a significant de-anonymization
      risk in current systems.
064
065   In literature, a closely related problem is authorship attribution (AA) (Neal et al., 2017; Stamatatos,
066   2009; Barlas & Stamatatos, 2020b). Usually, there is a list of suspects and a collection of texts with
067   confirmed authorship for each individual on the list. The objective is to determine the author of texts
068   whose authorship is in question, a task that can be divided into three distinct categories: closed-
069
      set attribution where the list of suspects is assumed to contain the actual author of the disputed
      texts, open-set attribution where the true author may not be included in the initial list of suspects,
070
      and author verification where the disputed text is compared against a single candidate author and
071
      verify whether this one individual is indeed the author of the text in question. Specifically, Huang
072   et al. (2024a) employ LLMs to perform authorship attribution with carefully designed prompts on
073   two datasets open-source blog (Schler et al., 2006) and email datasets (Klimt & Yang, 2004). A
074   recent survey Huang et al. (2024b) summarizes recent progress and available datasets for authorship
075   attribution, and extend the landscape to LLM-generated text detection and attribution. Moreover, it is
076   worthy to mention that PAN is a series of scientific events with datasets and competitions focusing on
077   digital text forensics and stylometry, specifically cross-topic and cross-genre authorship verification
078   (Stamatatos et al., 2022), author identification and multi-author analysis (Bevendorff et al., 2023),
079   and multi-author writing style analysis and generative AI authorship verification (Bevendorff et al.,
080
      2024). However, these setups differ significantly from the challenging anonymous review systems
      where one has to compare hundreds of texts without effective author profiles available.
081
082   In this paper, we introduce AIDBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed for extensive and
083   in-depth authorship identification. The goal is to identify texts written by the same author when
084   a specific text is known to belong to that person; specifically, given a disputed text, our task is
085   to find candidate texts most likely authored by the same individual. To systematically study this
086
      problem, we collect multiple datasets, including research papers, Enron emails (Klimt & Yang,
      2004), blogs (Schler et al., 2006), Gaudian articles (Stamatatos, 2013) and IMDb reviews Seroussi
087
      et al. (2011) whose features are illustrated in Table 1. For the research papers dataset, each entry
088
      includes the title, abstract and introduction, and all of them are computer science papers gathered
089   from arXiv2 , along with their ground-truth authorship information. We evaluate the capability of
090   LLMs to accomplish this task under various setups, directly prompting both commercial platforms3
091   such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), GPT-3.5 (Ye et al., 2023), Claude-3.5 (Anthropic, 2024) and
092   Kimi4 , as well as open-source models like Qwen (Bai et al., 2023) and Baichuan (Yang et al.,
093   2023). Moreover, as the number of texts increases and exceeds the context window of LLMs, we
094   propose a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023; Ding
095   et al., 2024b; Zhao et al., 2024) attribution method to address situations where the models cannot
096   accommodate all the information even with context extension (Jin et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024a;
097
      Zhang et al., 2024). The contributions of AIDBench are summarized as follows.
098
099
             • AIDBench presents a novel dataset of research papers specifically designed to evaluate the
               authorship identification capabilities of LLMs in academic writing.
100
101          • Using AIDBench, we conduct extensive experiments of authorship identification with
102            mainstream LLMs. These experiments are particularly performed under stringent condi-
103            tions without any author profile information. Our empirical findings demonstrate that the
104            potential of LLMs as tools for privacy breaches is evident and cannot be ignored.

138
139   Figure 1 provides an overview of our proposed AIDBench framework. We begin by selecting a
140   dataset for evaluation, such as the Research Paper dataset. From this dataset, we sample a subset of
141   texts from several authors, randomly selecting one as the Target Text and designating the remaining
142   texts as candidates.
143   These texts are then incorporated into an authorship identification prompt, which is presented to the
144   LLMs. The models generate responses indicating which candidate texts are more likely authored
145   by the same individual as the Target Text. We repeat this process multiple times to obtain average
146   performance metrics for the task. Finally, we employ metrics such as precision, recall, and rank to
147   provide a clear and intuitive assessment of the LLMs’ capabilities.

      2.2     DATASETS
163
164   AIDBench comprises five datasets: Research Paper, Enron Email, Blog, IMDb Review, and
165   Guardian. In this subsection, we provide detailed descriptions of each dataset.
166
      Research Paper. This newly collected dataset consists of research papers posted on arXiv under
167
      the CS.LG tag (the field of machine learning in the computer science domain) from 2019 to 2024.
168   After removing duplicate entries and authors with fewer than ten papers, the dataset includes 24,095
169   papers from 1,500 authors, ensuring that each author has at least ten papers. We use this dataset
170   to investigate their potential privacy risks when using LLMs for identifying authorship of academic
171   writing.
172
      Enron Email. The Enron email dataset contains approximately 500,000 emails generated by em-
173
      ployees of the Enron Corporation. For our benchmark, similar to Huang et al. (2024a), we removed
174   sender and receiver information and discarded short emails. Ultimately, we retained 174 authors,
175   each with 50 emails.
176
177
      Blog. The Blog Authorship Corpus (Schler et al., 2006) comprises the collected posts of 19,320
      bloggers gathered from Blogger.com5 in August 2004. We selected 1,500 authors from the dataset,
178
      each with 10 blog posts. The content of this dataset is closely related to daily life, providing linguis-
179
      tic characteristics, writing styles, word usage habits, and special characters that can be used to infer
180   an author’s identity.
181
182   IMDb Review. The IMDb review data are selected from the IMDb62 dataset (Seroussi et al., 2011).
183
      After filtering out reviews with fewer than 10 words, we randomly retained 50 reviews for each of
      the 62 authors in our dataset.
184
185   Guardian. The Guardian corpus dataset (Stamatatos, 2013) is designed to explore cross-genre and
186   cross-topic authorship attribution. The corpus comprises texts published in The Guardian daily
187   newspaper, mainly opinion articles (comments). It includes articles from 13 authors across five
188   topics—politics, society, UK, world, and books—and retains 50 articles per author.

190   2.3     E VALUATION TASKS
191
      As illustrated in Figure 1, we outline the evaluation tasks. Each task involves a query text and a
192
      set of candidate texts, with the objective of identifying which candidates are most likely authored
193
      by the same individual as the query text. Based on the number of candidate texts, we introduce the
194   following specific tasks.
195
196   One-to-one identification. Commonly known as authorship verification, this task involves a single
197
      candidate text and aims to determine whether the text pair is authored by the same person, forming a
      binary classification problem. In our evaluation, we prompt the LLM with: “Here are a pair of texts:
198
      [Text Pair]. Determine if they belong to the same author.”. More effective prompts or additional
199
      information can be provided to the LLM to achieve more accurate and reasonable responses. For
200   instance, Hung et al. (2023) employs a Chain-of-Thought prompt and a series of intermediate rea-
201   soning steps to significantly enhance the LLMs’ authorship verification ability. Similarly, as shown
202   in Huang et al. (2024a) and Hung et al. (2023), instructing LLMs to analyze texts based on writing
203   style, linguistic characteristics, and word usage habits, rather than content and topics, can further
204   improve authorship verification.
205
      Due to the subjective nature of the one-to-one identification task and its heavy reliance on the intrin-
206   sic judgment of LLMs, it is natural to consider a scenario involving two candidate texts requiring
207   a comparative decision. The objective here is to determine which candidate text is more likely au-
208   thored by the same individual as the query text; we refer to this as the one-to-two identification
209   task. Due to space limitations, we defer the task design, metrics, and experimental results for the
210   one-to-two identification task to Appendix B.
211
      One-to-many identification. In this setup, we provide a number of candidate texts and ask LLMs
212   to determine texts that are most likely authored by the same person as the text in the query. This is
213   different from the usual closed-set authorship attribution task (Huang et al., 2024a), where multiple
214   authors and their writings are provided in the context of LLMs, and the task is to attribute a target
215

      text to one of these authors. We do not provide any authorship information to LLMs. Instead in
217   our experiments, we randomly sample a number of authors and put all of their writings into a set.
218   Then one specific text is chosen at random as the target text while the rest from the set form the
219   candidate texts. The task is to ask LLMs to identify the texts in the candidate set that are mostly
220   likely authored by the same person as the target text, ranking the results by confidence scores.
221
222   2.4     RAG- BASED ONE - TO - MANY IDENTIFICATION PIPELINE
223
224   The performance of one-to-many authorship identification tasks heavily depends on the ability of
225   LLMs to handle long contexts. Current commercial LLMs support relatively large context windows;
226
      for example, GPT-4-Turbo can process contexts up to 128,000 tokens, and Kimi supports up to 2
      million tokens. In contrast, open-source models like Llama-3-8B-Instruct6 support context windows
227
      of only up to 8,000 tokens. Moreover, the authorship identification task relies heavily on the LLMs’
228
      capacity for high-level comprehension of the context, which becomes increasingly challenging as
229   the length of the context grows.
230
231   To address this limitation, we propose a simple Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based
232
      method, as illustrated in Figure 2. In this approach, we first use pre-trained embedding models
      like sentence-transformers to encode both the target text and the candidate texts. We then calculate
233
      the similarities between the target text and each candidate, selecting only the top k texts with the
234
      highest similarity scores. These top k texts are provided to the LLM, thereby reducing the context
235   length and enabling more effective processing.

259   2.5     E VALUATION METRICS
260
261
      For the one-to-one identification task, LLMs are required to classify whether a pair of texts were
      written by the same author by answering “True” or “False”. While accuracy can serve as an evalua-
262
      tion metric, it may not fully capture performance due to potential class imbalances in the dataset, for
263
      instance, when there is a large number of negative pairs compared to positive pairs, or vice versa.
264   Therefore, we adopt Precision, Recall as our evaluation criteria to more comprehensively assess
265   the performance of LLMs under various setups. Specifically, Precision and Recall are defined as
266   follows:

      For the one-to-many identification task, we employ multiple evaluation metrics to capture the prob-

277   texts.

288
      As previously mentioned, the Guardian dataset categorizes text data by both author and topic. Lever-
289
      aging this, we design a more challenging scenario to evaluate the one-to-one identification capabili-
290
      ties of LLMs. Specifically, we randomly select pairs of texts as positive examples where each pair is
291   authored by the same individual but covers different topics. For negative examples, we sample text
292   pairs that share the same topic but are written by different authors. We believe this setup provides
293   a robust evaluation of LLM capabilities and offers deeper insights into their potential. For datasets
294   other than the Guardian, we simply sample text pairs where the positive examples consist of texts
295   by the same author, and the negative examples consist of texts by different authors.
296
      We conduct experiments using three different random seeds, recording the precision and recall, and
297   reporting the mean and standard deviation of these metrics. The total number of examples is set to
298   200, and we perform experiments with positive-to-negative example ratios of 150/50, 100/100, and
299   50/150, respectively.

515
516
      In this work, we propose a benchmark to evaluate the authorship identification capabilities of large
      language models (LLMs). Specifically, we have collected a new dataset, referred to as the “Re-
517
      search Paper” dataset, to identify authorship based solely on the content of academic papers. By
518
      combining this with various existing public datasets, we have curated a comprehensive benchmark
519   dataset. We evaluate the authorship identification task using multiple models and services, reveal-
520   ing potential privacy risks in real-world anonymous systems when employing LLMs. To enable
521   authorship identification of long texts, we have devised a pipeline utilizing Retrieval-Augmented
522   Generation (RAG). This approach allows us to tailor the evaluation of LLMs with different contex-
523   tual capacities to adapt to the task. Further discussion on limitations and future directions is deferred
524   to Appendix C.

759   A     R ELATED W ORK
760
761   Our benchmark is related to the longstanding authorship attribution task (Stamatatos, 2009), which
762   is initially formulated as supervised authorship identification and recently addressed with LLMs.
763
764   A.1    S UPERVISED AUTHORSHIP I DENTIFICATION
765
766
      Early work uses text statistics such as n-grams, topic, or syntax as features (Koppel et al., 2007;
      Sharma et al., 2018; Sundararajan & Woodard, 2018; Seroussi et al., 2014), and then leverage SVM,
767
      random forest or topic models to conduct authorship verification. Some work uses deep learning
768
      models like RNNs and CNNs to extract more complex features for authorship verification (Bagnall,
769   2015; Benzebouchi et al., 2018; Ruder et al., 2016). Pretrained models such as BERT are also used
770   to get text embeddings (Huertas-Tato et al., 2022; Barlas & Stamatatos, 2020a; Fabien et al., 2020;
771   Rivera-Soto et al., 2021) and one can verify the authorship of two texts by measuring the similarity
772   of their embeddings.
773
774   A.2    AUTHORSHIP I DENTIFICATION BY LLM S
775
776   Due to the abilities of LLMs understanding texts and human instructions, researchers naturally ex-
777   plore using LLMs for authorship identification. Hung et al. (2023) prompt LLMs with step-by-step
778   stylometric explanations for authorship verification. Huang et al. (2024a) design various prompts
779
      with different levels of guidance and ask LLMs to conduct authorship verification and attribution in
      an end-to-end way. The guidance includes ignoring topics, ignoring content, and focusing on lin-
780
      guistic features. Nonetheless, they did not experiment with hard scenarios such as the same author
781
      with different topics, or different authors with the same topic.
782
783   Our paper extends previous work in several key aspects. We focus on an authorship identification
784   task that mimics the anonymous paper review system by identifying the most likely texts from the
785
      same author. Additionally, our experiments cover a wider range of scenarios and demonstrate the
      behaviors of LLMs in various axes, including different context lengths and challenging scenarios.
786
787
788   B     R ESULTS OF ONE - TO - TWO IDENTIFICATION TASK
789
790   One-to-two identification. The previous task heavily relies on the intrinsic judgment of LLMs due
791   to its subjective nature. Therefore, we consider a scenario with two candidate texts, requiring a
792   comparative decision. The objective is to determine which candidate text is more likely authored by
793   the same person as the query text. Specifically, we prompt the LLM with, “Here are two candidate
794
      texts: [candidate texts] and a target text [text in query]. Determine which candidate text is more
      likely to be authored by the same person as the target text”. Similar to the previous task, the prompts
795
      can be augmented with additional information.
796
797   We choose GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4-turbo, Kimi and Baichuan2-turbo-192k to perform one-to-two
798   identification on 4 datasets: Guardian, Enron Email, Blog and IMDb Review. We use three different
799   random seeds to repeat our experiments and choose accuracy as the evaluation metric.
800   In this task, each data example consists of three texts: besides the target text, there are two candidate
801   texts. One of the candidate texts is by the same author as the target text, while the other is not. We
802   sample 200 data examples for our experiments. It is worth noticing that for the Guardian dataset, we
803   ensure that the target text and the candidate text belonging to the same author as the target text are
804   from different topics, while the other candidate text has the same topic as the candidate text. The
805   evaluation results are shown in table 10.
806   The results in Table 10 show that GPT-4-turbo still demonstrates a significant advantage in one-to-
807   two identification tasks, the accuracy reaches an average of 70.0% on Guardian, 80.7% on Email,
808   80.0% on Blog, 75.8% on IMDb. This indicates that although texts from different authors may be
809   similar in topic, GPT-4-turbo can make reasonable comparisons based on the linguistic characteris-
      tics of the texts, thereby identifying the candidate text that truly belongs to the same author as the

      C     L IMITATION AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
826
827
      The AIDBench offers a benchmark for assessing LLMs’ authorship identification capabilities in
      scenarios closer to real-world anonymous review systems, highlighting significant privacy risks as-
828
      sociated with anonymous systems. In this section, we address the potential limitations of AIDBench
829
      and outline future directions.
830
831   Firstly, it is worth noting that due to cost constraints, we evaluated only a limited number of models.
832   Additionally, large commercial models are continually emerging and evolving rapidly, such as the
833
      Gemini (Team et al., 2023), Claude 3 (Anthropic, 2024), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Meta,
      2024), Mistral families (Jiang et al., 2023), and many others (Zeng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a).
834
      Therefore, future exploration and evaluation of these models are warranted.
835
836   Another aspect to consider is that research papers often involve multiple authors, each contributing
837   differently to the paper’s writing. It would be intriguing to curate more nuanced subsets of research
838   papers based on author positions, such as a first-author subset. This approach could provide insights
839
      into how author position affects the authorship identification problem in research papers. Addition-
      ally, in our experiments, we only utilize the abstract information of a paper. However, in the future,
840
      incorporating more detailed paper information could lead to a more accurate evaluation.
841
842   Furthermore, in the evaluation process, we employed only two types of prompts: the general prompt
843   and the topic-ignored prompt. Recognizing the significant impact of prompt quality on model re-
844   sponses, it is essential for a more comprehensive exploration of LLMs’ authorship identification
845
      capabilities that we develop a more nuanced set of prompts for future evaluation and analysis.
846   Finally, the RAG-based approach we introduced enables LLMs with limited context windows to
847   conduct authorship identification. To ensure the success of authorship identification, it’s necessary to
848   limit the candidate list obtained through RAG screening to a specific quantity. Additionally, we often
849   encounter individual texts with considerable lengths. Therefore, the future direction of our work
850   involves exploring methods to decompose long texts into shorter segments, indirectly facilitating
851
      comparisons between the original texts by comparing these shorter segments. One approach is to
      segment the long text into several shorter segments. In the one-to-two identification task, LLMs
852
      can compare pairs of these shorter segments, assigning scores during each comparison. Ultimately,
853
      scores are weighted and summed based on the length of each segment.
