009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit neural representations (INRs) for surfaces have been mostly used as
012            intermediary representations before triangle mesh extraction. Extracting meshes
013            is not a real-time task and introduces unnecessary discretization to rendering,
014            making it difficult to fully use the smoothness of INRs in applications. Smooth
015
               INRs are broadly used for approximating surface signed distance functions (SDFs)
               through an implicit regularization (Eikonal equation) using their available high-
016
               order derivatives. Such property also makes it easier to integrate those INRs in
017
               pipelines that explore differentiable properties of the underlying surface. The
018            current real-time state-of-the-art approach uses grid-based data-structures that
019            introduce discretization, resulting in a non-smooth representation.
020            We propose an end-to-end smooth (C ∞ ) INR framework to represent and render
021            surfaces in real-time using neural SDFs endowed with smooth attributes such as
022            normals and textures. Our approach leverages from a novel localized SDF training
023            based on nested neighborhoods, a multiscale surface representation, and residual
024            training. The framework does not depend on spatial data-structures, nor surface
025            extraction. We show that our representation renders detailed smooth surfaces in
026            real-time while the previous works can only render coarse non-smooth surfaces. We
027            also present applications of our representation, including integration with a pipeline
028
               for dynamic surfaces and a way to improve performance of surface extraction via
               marching cubes.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Real-time rendering enables interactive applications on 3D scenes. One important choice before
034   rendering is how to represent the scene objects. In this setting, implicit neural representations (INRs)
035   are emerging as a compelling option, which encodes the surface as the zero-level set of a neural
036   network. They are memory efficient, continuous, infinitely differentiable when periodic activations
037   are used, scalable, and naturally adapted to machine learning pipelines.
038   Even though those properties are present, INRs for surfaces have been mostly used as intermediary
039   representations in neural pipelines, which usually output triangle meshes. Although that representation
040   excels at localized tasks because it is explicit, it is not memory efficient, nor continuous, nor scalable.
041   One of the reasons behind the use of triangle meshes is the non-triviality of rendering INRs for
042   surfaces in real-time while maintaining the aforementioned properties. The current approach for
043   real-time rendering of INRs resorts to discrete spatial data-structures which, analogously to triangle
044
      meshes, cannot maintain smoothness. Easy access to derivatives makes a model ready for integration
      with differentiable pipelines from the inception, increasing its possibilities for applications.
045
046   We propose a real-time rendering framework using INRs which maintain surface and attribute
047   smoothness. Our approach trains residual SDFs on neighborhoods of the zero-level set, resulting
048   in a multiscale representation. Surface attributes (normals and texture) are also defined and trained
049   in the surface neighborhood. Rendering-wise, we propose a multiscale sphere tracing and a normal
050
      computation based on general matrix multiply (GEMM) (Dongarra et al., 1990) to render the surfaces.
051   Summarizing, our contributions are: (1) Smooth multiscale INR for surface representation; (2) Effi-
052   cient neighborhood training for SDF (using a residual scheme), normals, and texture; (3) Multiscale
053   sphere tracing; (4) GEMM-based normal computation. (5) Applications in differentiable pipelines
      (dynamic surfaces) and in an adaptive sampling for fast mesh extraction via marching cubes.

      2    R ELATED W ORK

056   Implicit representations are an essential topic in computer graphics (Velho et al., 2007). SDFs are
057   important examples of such functions (Bloomenthal & Wyvill, 1990) and arise from solving the
058
      Eikonal problem (Sethian & Vladimirsky, 2000). Recently, multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) have been
      used to model SDFs (Park et al., 2019; Gropp et al., 2020; Novello et al., 2022). Sinusoidal networks
059
      (SIRENs) Sitzmann et al. (2020) are an example of such, being MLPs using sinusoidal activation.
060
061   Marching cubes (Lorensen & Cline, 1987) and sphere tracing (ST) (Hart, 1996) are classical visual-
062   ization methods for rendering level sets of SDFs. Neural versions of those algorithms were proposed
063   by (Liao et al., 2018; Chen & Zhang, 2021; Liu et al., 2020). While the initial works in neural SDFs
064
      use marching cubes to visualize the resulting level sets, recent performance-driven approaches have
      been using ST, since no intermediary representation is needed for rendering (Davies et al., 2020;
065
      Takikawa et al., 2021). Our proposed multiscale ST considers a similar path.
066
067   Surface representations and rendering: Recent works propose INRs for disentangling base geome-
068   try and detail. Wang et al. (2022) describe an INR using base and displacement networks to compute
069   a detailed triangle mesh extracted via marching cubes for rendering. This approach is similar to our
070
      residual SDF learning, however it relies on function composition instead of addition like ours. A
      consequence is that the inference of the base and displacement network must be sequential, different
071
      from our approach which may be parallelized. Another difference is that our residual training is done
072
      only at the neighborhood of the zero-level set, which improves the network ability to represent the
073   function since it is restricted to a small neighborhood instead of the entire domain. Morreale et al.
074   (2022) employ INRs to model surfaces parametrically using parameterizations. Differently from our
075   approach, they do not deal with the rendering problem. Sharp & Jacobson (2022) describe a way to
076   perform geometric queries for neural SDFs using range analysis. (Genova et al., 2020) uses multiple
077   small implicit objects to increase detail of the representation. None of those approaches support
078   textures. Contextualization with triangle meshes is in Section A.1.
079   Real-time neural SDFs: Fast inference is needed to sphere trace SDFs in real-time. Davies et al.
080   (2020) show that this is possible using general matrix multiply (GEMM) (Dongarra et al., 1990;
081   Müller, 2021), but the capacity of their networks can not represent geometric detail. Other works in
082   neural SDFs store features in the nodes of octrees (Takikawa et al., 2021; Martel et al., 2021), or limit
083   the frequency band in training as in BACON (Lindell et al., 2021). However, octree-based approaches
084   reintroduce discretization to the pipeline. NGLOD (Takikawa et al., 2021) is the SOTA real-time
085   method for rendering neural SDFs. It uses a sparse voxel octree (SVO) to represent the neural SDF
086   and render its level set using a sparse ST algorithm. The vertices of the voxels store features. Then,
087
      for a point p and a level L of the SVO, the features are interpolated inside each voxel containing
      p up to the level L. The resulting interpolated points are summed and passed to a MLP fL . Thus,
088
      besides the SVO structure, NGLOD uses a sequence of L MLPs to represent the LoD. Moreover,
089
      the interpolation implies in INRs with non-continuous gradients at the voxels boundaries leading to
090   artifacts (Sec. 4.1). Ours supports smooth normals, by leveraging sinusoidal MLPs to fit each level of
091   the SDFs using (Novello et al., 2022). Finally, NGLOD does not support textures as our method does.
092
093
      Attribute mapping: Normal mapping (Cohen et al., 1998; Cignoni et al., 1998) is a classic method
      to transfer detailed normals between meshes. Besides depending on interpolation, normal mapping
094
      also suffers distortions of the parameterization between meshes, which are assumed to have the same
095
      topology. Recently, Wang et al. (2022) introduced detail transfer (normals) in the context of INRs. It
096   is based on features computed from a point cloud encoder and a convolutional module to propagate
097   sparse on-surface point features to the off-surface area. Queried features are obtained using bilinear
098   interpolation. Our approach is simpler. Inspired by (Bertalmıo et al., 2001), we use a regularization to
099   make attributes constant along the normals near the zero-level set. That maintains smooth attributes,
100   without the need of any interpolation or parametrization.
101
      Texture mapping (Catmull, 1974) is a technique for cost-effective rendering that maps images to
102   surfaces using parametrizations. In neural rendering, texture fields (Oechsle et al., 2019) shares
103   similarities with our neural attribute mapping but approaches a different problem. Ours processes
104   a point cloud with colors, while texture fields demand a 3D shape and input images, using view
105   dependent depth maps. We use the surface’s neighborhood to define color along normals. Texture
106   fields is not real-time due to its use of 4-6 ResNet blocks and complex networks for latent code
107   generation. In contrast, ours adopts small MLPs for efficient representation. GET3D (Gao et al., 2022)
      uses texture fields for the textures in its 3D model generation, sharing an analogous contextualization.

111   3.1   OVERVIEW
112
113
      Given the iterative nature of Sphere Tracing (ST), a way to increase its performance is to optimize or
114   avoid iterations. We propose to use small neural SDFs to approximate earlier iterations and mapping
115   the normals and the texture of the desired neural SDF, avoiding later iterations. Both tasks can
116   be accomplished by mapping neural SDFs using nested neighborhoods, without introducing any
117   additional discretization. Fig. 1 shows our pipeline for smooth real-time rendering of 3D objects.

147   The basic idea comes from the following fact: if the zero-level set of a neural SDF f is contained in a
148   neighborhood V of the zero-level set of another neural SDF, then we can map f into V . We follow
149   the notation in Fig. 2 to present an overview of our method. Let S1 , S2 , S3 be surfaces pairwise close
150   with SDFs f1 , f2 , f3 sorted by complexity. We use S1 and S2 to illustrate the multiscale ST and S3
151   to illustrate the attribute mapping.
152   Multiscale ST: Suppose that the ray p0 + tv, with origin at a point p0 and direction v, intersects
153   S2 . To compute its S2 point q2 , we first use f1 to sphere trace the boundary of a neighborhood of S1
154   (gray) containing S2 . This results in q1 . Then we continue sphere tracing S2 using f2 , reaching q2 .
155   In other words, we are mapping the values of f2 to the neighborhood of S1 .
156   Neural Attribute Mapping: For shading, we need a normal at q2 , which is given by N2 = ∇f2 (q2 ).
157   Instead, we propose to pull the finer details of S3 to S2 to increase fidelity. This is done by mapping
158   the normals from S3 to S2 using N3 = ∇f3 (q2 ). To justify this choice, note that q2 belongs to a
159   neighborhood of S3 . Thus, N3 is the normal of S3 at its closest point q3 = q2 − ϵN3 , where ϵ is
160   the distance f3 (q2 ) from q2 to S3 . This transfers the normal N3 to q2 . Observe that N3 is also the
161   normal of the ϵ-level set of f3 at q2 (red dotted). Similarly, the texture color is mapped from q3 to q2
      by making it constant along q3 + tN3 in the neighborhood.

      A.1   O N THE IMPORTANCE OF SMOOTH REPRESENTATIONS AND REAL - TIME RENDERING
661
662   This section clarifies why the smoothness and real-time rendering of our multiscale INR are important,
663   and presents a contextualization with triangle meshes showing applications where the mentioned
664   properties are essential.
665
      Our objective is not to replace meshes, in fact, we show that using our multiscale INR allows fast
666   mesh extraction using marching cubes (Section 4.3). Rather, we provided an implicit representation
667   for SDFs suitable for specific tasks depending on two main properties: 1) high order differentiability
668   (smoothness); 2) fast level set rendering (especially when surface extraction may be prohibitive).
669
      For an example, Section 4.3 gives an application of our method on surface evolution using differential
670
      equations that explores those two properties. Specifically:
671
672   Smoothness. We use the method Neural implicit surface evolution (Novello et al., 2023) (NISE)
673   which trains dynamic SDFs using the level-set method. Thus, in addition to the Eikonal regularization
674   necessary for the SDF, we need higher derivatives to compute differential properties (e.g. mean
675
      curvature). For this, we use the smoothness our INR, making its integration with NISE easier.
      Conversely, using meshes for surface evolution is challenging because the representation should be
676
      adapted to handle the lack of differentiability. Finally, meshes cannot easily handle topology changes
677
      (eg. the Spot-Bob interpolation shown in Figure 11). Creating holes in the mesh during animation is
678   a hard task due to its fixed topology. This problem is easily avoided using our implicit representation.
679
680   Fast rendering. To visualize the resulting animation, the zero-level sets must be evaluated fast during
681
      evolution for real-time rendering. This is achieved by integrating our multiscale INR with NISE. On
      the other hand, using meshes would be prohibitive because the mesh should be extracted for each
682
      time instant of the animation, which cannot be done in real-time. Preprocessing the animation is also
683
      unfeasible since each mesh extraction may take dozens of megabytes (see the comments about mesh
684   extraction below), creating an unacceptable memory footprint. Using our sphere tracing we only
685   need to store the underlying MLP.
686
687
      Finally, an additional objective of providing real-time rendering for neural SDFs is making its
      integration in neural pipelines more appealing. For example, fast rendering of such INRs is useful in
688
      inverse rendering tasks since it helps accelerate training. Previous works that propose such pipelines
689
      include DIST (Liu et al., 2020) and SDFDiff (Jiang et al., 2020). Additionally, SDF is popular
690   surface representation in 3D reconstruction from images using differentiable volume rendering (eg.
691   NeuS (Wang et al., 2021) and volSDF Yariv et al. (2021)).
692
693
      Mesh extraction and SDF training as pre-processing for rendering. Extracting a mesh from
      a trained neural SDF results in a substantial memory footprint, especially when the zero-level set
694
      is highly detailed. This is primarily due to the cubic complexity of grid generation for marching
695
      cubes. For example, we trained a multiscale SDF in our experiments using the following architecture:
696   (128, 1) for coarse level, (256, 1) for medium level, and (256, 2) for fine level. Generating the
697   grid of resolution 5123 and running the marching cubes for this case demands approximately 20
698   GB of GPU memory while rendering with our sphere tracing using an image resolution of 5122
699   requires significantly less—approximately 5 GB, including the GEMM buffers used to parallelize
700   the pixel computation. Additionally, storing high-resolution meshes is costly in terms of memory.
701   For this experiment, the output mesh has 43 MB of storage, while the underlying multiscale MLP
      representation needs only 857 KB, showing that our representation is significantly more compact.

      Integration with NeuS (Wang et al., 2021): NeuS employs an implicit representation based on
727   SDFs to accurately reconstruct 3D surfaces. The SDF gradient plays a crucial role in defining surface
728   normals, smoothly calculating volumetric density, and ensuring geometric consistency through
729   Eikonal regularization. It is also utilized to guarantee smooth transitions between geometry and
730   density during volumetric rendering, enabling the modeling of detailed and complex surfaces. By
731   combining these techniques with differentiable rendering, NeuS offers an efficient and robust approach
732   for tasks such as surface reconstruction and 3D texture generation. Summing the gradients of two
733   SDF networks, guided by our loss function, enhances NeuS by enabling a multi-scale representation
734   and improving stability. One network can model large-scale structures, while the other captures fine
735
      details, with their combined gradients seamlessly integrating these scales for a more comprehensive
      representation. This multi-scale approach ensures better handling of complex geometries, particularly
736
      in challenging scenarios like thin structures or noisy inputs. Furthermore, the combined gradient
737
      mitigates irregularities and noise, leading to a smoother and more stable representation that better
738   satisfies regularization constraints, such as the Eikonal condition, ultimately improving reconstruction
739   quality, training robustness, and reducing processing time.
