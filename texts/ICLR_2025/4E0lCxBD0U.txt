011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning a world model for model-free Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents can
014            significantly improve the sample efficiency by learning policies in imagination.
015
               However, building a world model for Multi-Agent RL (MARL) can be particularly
               challenging due to the scalability issue in a centralized architecture arising from
016
               a large number of agents, and also the non-stationarity issue in a decentralized
017
               architecture stemming from the inter-dependency among agents. To address both
018            challenges, we propose a novel world model for MARL that learns decentralized
019            local dynamics for scalability, combined with a centralized representation aggrega-
020            tion from all agents. We cast the dynamics learning as an auto-regressive sequence
021            modeling problem over discrete tokens by leveraging the expressive Transformer
022            architecture, in order to model complex local dynamics across different agents and
023            provide accurate and consistent long-term imaginations. As the first pioneering
024            Transformer-based world model for multi-agent systems, we introduce a Perceiver
025            Transformer as an effective solution to enable centralized representation aggrega-
026
               tion within this context. Main results on Starcraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC)
               and additional results on MAMujoco show that it outperforms strong model-free
027
               approaches and existing model-based methods in both sample efficiency and overall
028
               performance.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has made remarkable progress, which was driven
034   largely by model-free algorithms (Nguyen et al., 2020). However, due to the complexity of multi-agent
035   systems arising from large state-action space and partial observability, such algorithms usually demand
036   extensive interactions to learn coordinative behaviors (Hernandez-Leal et al., 2020). A promising
037   solution is building a world model that approximates the environment, which has exhibited its superior
038   sample efficiency compared to model-free approaches in single-agent RL (Hafner et al., 2020; Łukasz
039
      Kaiser et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2021; 2023; Hansen et al., 2022; 2024). However, extending the
      design of world model in single-agent domain to the multi-agent context encounters significant
040
      challenges due to the unique biases and characteristics inherent to multi-agent environments.
041
042   The challenges primarily stem from two different means for multi-agent dynamics learning: central-
043   ized and decentralized. Learning a world model to approximate the centralized dynamics encapsulates
044   the inter-dependency between agents but struggles to be scalable to an increasing number of agents,
045
      which leads to the exponential surge in spatial complexity (Hernandez-Leal et al., 2020; Nguyen et al.,
      2020). Conversely, applying a decentralized world model to approximating the local dynamics of
046
      each agent mitigates the scalability issue yet incurs non-stationarity, as unexpected interventions from
047
      other agents may occur in each agent’s individual environment (Oliehoek et al., 2016). Furthermore,
048   beyond these unique challenges inherent in modeling multi-agent dynamics, existing model-based
049   MARL approaches (Willemsen et al., 2021; Egorov & Shpilman, 2022; Xu et al., 2022) excessively
050   neglect the fact that the policy learned in imaginations of the world model heavily relies on the
051   quality of imagined trajectories (Micheli et al., 2023). It thereby necessitates accurate long-term
052   prediction, especially with respect to the non-stationary local dynamics. Inspired by the capabil-
053   ity of Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) in modeling complex discrete sequences and long-term
      dependency (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019; Micheli et al., 2023), we seek to construct a

054
      Transformer-based world model within the multi-agent context for decentralized local dynamics
055   together with centralized feature aggregation, combining the benefits of two distinctive designs.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce MARIE (Multi-Agent auto-Regressive Imagination for Efficient learning),
058
      the first Transformer-based multi-agent world model for sample-efficient policy learning. Specifically,
      the highlights of this paper are:
059
060         1. To tackle the inherent challenges within the multi-agent context, we build an effective world
061            model via scalable decentralized dynamics modeling and essential centralized represen-
062            tation aggregating, which mirrors the principle of Centralized Training and Decentralized
063            Execution.
064
            2. To enable accurate and consistent long-term imaginations from the non-stationary local
065            dynamics, we cast the decentralized dynamics learning as sequence modeling over discrete
066            tokens by leveraging highly expressive Transformer architecture as the backbone. In par-
067            ticular, we successfully present the first Transformer-based world model for multi-agent
068            systems.
069
            3. While it remains open for how to effectively enable centralized representation with the Trans-
070            former as the backbone, we achieve it by innovatively introducing a Perceiver Transformer
071            (Jaegle et al., 2021) for efficient global information aggregation across all agents.
072
            4. Experiments on the Starcraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) benchmark in low data regime
073
               and additional experiments on MAMujoco show MARIE outperforms both model-free and
074
               existing model-based MARL methods w.r.t. both sample efficiency and overall performance
075            and demonstrate the effectiveness of MARIE.

078   2   R ELATED W ORKS AND P RELIMINARIES
079
080   Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning. In a model-free setting, a typical approach for cooperative
081   MARL is centralized training and decentralized execution (CTDE), which tackles the scalability
082
      and non-stationarity issues in MARL. During the training phase, it leverages global information
      to facilitate agents’ policy learning; while during the execution phase, it blinds itself and has only
083
      access to the partial observation around each agent for multi-agent decision-making. Model-free
084
      MARL methods with this paradigm can be divided into 2 categories: value-based (Sunehag et al.,
085   2018; Rashid et al., 2018; Son et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021) and policy-based (Lowe et al., 2017;
086   Foerster et al., 2018; Iqbal & Sha, 2019; Ryu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Kuba et al., 2021;
087   Peng et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024b;a). In contrast to model-free approaches,
088   model-based MARL algorithms remain fairly understudied. MAMBPO (Willemsen et al., 2021)
089   incorporates MBPO-style (Janner et al., 2019) techniques into multi-agent policy learning under
090   the CTDE framework. Tesseract (Mahajan et al., 2021) introduces the tensorised Bellman equation
091   and evaluates the Q-value function using Dynamic Programming (DP) together with an estimated
092   environment model. Similar to our setting where agents learn inside of an approximate world model,
093
      MAMBA (Egorov & Shpilman, 2022) integrates the backbone proposed in DreamerV2 (Hafner et al.,
      2021) with an attention mechanism across agents to sustain an effective world model in environments
094
      with an arbitrary number of agents, which leads to notably superior sample efficiency to existing
095
      model-free approaches. In terms of model-based algorithm coupled with planning, MAZero (Liu
096   et al., 2024) expands the MCTS planning-based Muzero (Schrittwieser et al., 2020) framework to
097   the model-based MARL settings. However, learning-based or planning-based policies in these two
098   approaches are both overly coupled with their world models, downgrading their inference efficiency
099   and further limiting expansion in combinations with other popular model-free approaches. To the best
100   of our knowledge, we are the first to expand the Transformer backbone-based world model within the
101   multi-agent context.
102   Learning behaviors within the imagination of world models. The Dyna architecture (Sutton, 1991)
103   first emphasizes the utility of an estimated dynamics model in facilitating the training of the value
104   function and policy. Inspired by the cognitive system of human beings, the concept of world model
105   (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) is initially introduced by composing a variational Auto-Encoder (VAE)
106   (Kingma & Welling, 2014) and a recurrent network to mimic the complete environmental dynamics,
107   then an artificial agent is trained entirely inside the hallucinated imagination generated by the world
      model. SimPLe (Łukasz Kaiser et al., 2020) shows that a PPO policy (Schulman et al., 2017) learned

      3     M ETHODOLOGIES
163
164
      Our approach comprises three typical parts: (i) collecting experience by executing the policy, (ii) learn-
165   ing the world model from the collected experience, and (iii) learning the policy via imagination
166   inside the world model. Throughout the process, the historical experiences stored in the replay
167   buffer are used for training the world model only, while policies are learned from unlimited imagined
168   trajectories from the world model. In the following, we first describe three core components of our
169   world model in §3.1 and §3.2, and give an overview of the proposed world model in Fig. 1. Then we
170   describe the policy-learning process inside the world model in §3.3. The comprehensive details of
171   the model architecture and hyperparameter are provided in §A.
172
173   3.1    D ISCRETIZING O BSERVATION
174
175   We consider a trajectory τ i of agent i consists of T local observations and actions, as
176                                            τ i = (oi1 , ai1 , . . . , oit , ait , . . . , oiT , aiT ).
177
178   To utilize the expressive Transformer architecture, we need to express the trajectory into a discrete
179   token sequence for modeling. Accounting for continuous observations, a prevalent but naive approach
180
      for discretization involves discretizing the scalar into one of m fixed-width bins in each dimension
      independently (Janner et al., 2021). However, when faced with a higher dimension of the observation,
181
      such discretization would encode the observation with more tokens, leading to higher computational
182
      complexity of the later sequence modeling via the Transformer, which necessitates an approach that
183   uses a discrete codebook of learned compact representations. To this end, we employ the idea from
184   neural discrete representation learning (van den Oord et al., 2017), and learn a Vector Quantised-
185   Variational AutoEncoder (VQ-VAE) to play a role that resembles the tokenizer in Natural Language
186   Processing (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). The VQ-VAE is composed of an encoder E,
187   a decoder D, and a codebook Z. We define the discrete codebook Z = {zj }N                nz

288   3.3           L EARNING B EHAVIOURS IN I MAGINATION
289
290   We utilize the Actor-Critic framework to learn the behavior of each agent, where the actor and critic
291   are parameterized by ψ and ξ, respectively. In the following, we take agent i as an exemplar case
292   for clarity and omit the superscript for denoting the index of the agent to avoid potential confusion.
293   Benefited from the shared local dynamics, the local trajectories of all agents are imagined in parallel,
294   as illustrated in Fig. 2. At timestep t, the actor takes a reconstructed observation ôt as input, and
295
      samples an action at ∼ πψ (at |ôt ). The world model then predicts the individual reward r̂t , individual
      discount γ̂t and next local observation ôt+1 . Starting from initial observations sampled from the
296
      replay buffer, this imagination procedure is rolled out for H steps. To stimulate long-horizon behavior
297
      learning, the critic accounts for rewards P beyond the fixed imagination horizon and estimates the
298   individual expected return Vξ (ôt ) ≃ Eπψ [ l≥t γ l−t r̂l ].
299
300   In our approach, we train the actor and critic in a MAPPO-like (Yu et al., 2022) manner. Unlike other
301   CTDE model-free approaches that require a global oracle state from the environment, we cannot
302
      obtain the oracle state from the world model, and only the predicted observations of each agent are
      available. To approximate the oracle information in critic training, we enhance each agent’s critic
303
      with the capability to access the observations of other agents. Since the actor and critic only rely on
304
      the reconstructed observations, decoupling from the inner hidden states of the Transformer-based
305   world model, we allow fast inference in the environment without the participation of the world model.
306   It is important for the deployment of policies learned with data-efficient imagination in real-world
307   applications. λ-target in Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2020) is used to updated the value function. The
308   details of behavior learning objectives and algorithmic description of MARIE are presented in §B
309   and §I, respectively.

419   Incorporating CTDE principle with the design of the world model makes MARIE scalable
420   and robust to different number of agents. We compare our method with a centralized variant
421   of our method, wherein the world model learns the joint dynamics of all agents together over the
422   joint trajectory τ = (. . . , o1t , o2t , . . . , ont , a1t , a2t , . . . , ant , . . .). Given that τ already contains the joint
423
      observations and actions, we disable the aggregation module in this centralized variant. As illustrated
      in Figure 4, our comparisons span scenarios involving 2 to 7 agents. When the number of agents is
424
      small enough, reducing the multi-agent system to a single-agent one over the joint observation and
425
      action space would not cause a prominent scalability issue, as indicated by the result in 2s vs 1sc.
426   However, the scalability issue is exacerbated by a growing number of agents. In scenarios featuring
427   more than 3 agents, the sample efficiency of the centralized variant encounters a significant drop,
428   suffering from the exponential surge in spatial complexity of the joint observation-action space.
429   Furthermore, with equal prediction horizons, the parameter amounts in the centralized variant is
430   increased by a factor of 4 or larger. And to achieve the same number of environment steps, the
431   centralized variant demands over twice the original computational time. Instead, with decentralized
      local dynamics and aggregated global features, MARIE delivers stable and superior sample efficiency.

      Agent-wise aggregation helps MARIE capture the sophisticated inter-dependency on the genera-
462
      tion of each agent’s local observation. To study the influence of agent-wise aggregation, we conduct
463   ablation experiments on the aggregation module over scenarios where the number of agents gradually
464   increases. As shown in Fig. 5, in the 3-agents scenario (e.g., 3s vs 3z), the correlation among each
465   agent’s local observation tends to be negligible. Therefore, the nearly independent generation of each
466   agent’s local observation without any aggregated global feature still leads to performance comparable
467   to that of standard implementation. But as more agents get involved, the inter-dependency becomes
468   dominant. Lacking the global features derived from agent-wise aggregation, the shared Transformer
469   struggles to infer accurate future local observations, thus hindering policy learning in the imaginations
470   of the world model and resulting in notable degradation in the win rate evaluation.
471   VQ-VAE encapsulates local observations within a succinct sequence of tokens, promoting the
472   learning of the Transformer-based world model and effectively improving algorithm perfor-
473   mance. Compared to VQ-VAE that discretizes each observation to K tokens from Z, perhaps a more
474   naive tokenizer is projecting the value in each dimension into one of m fixed-width bins (Janner
475   et al., 2021), resulting in a nobs -long token sequence for each observation, which we term Bins
476   Discretization. We set the number of bins m equal to the size of codebook |Z| and compare these two
477
      types of tokenizers in different environments with various nobs . As shown in Fig. 6, the performance
      of the two tokenizers are comparable only in 2s vs 1sc where nobs is close to 16. Even worse, Bins
478
      Discretization experiences a pronounced decline as nobs increases in more complex environments
479
      (e.g., 3s vs 4z) under identical training durations. We hypothesize that for a single local observation,
480   a nobs -token-long verbose sequence yielded by Bins Discretization contains more redundant infor-
481   mation compared to VQ-VAE that learns a more compact tokenizer through reconstruction This not
482   only renders the token sequences of Bins Discretization obscure and challenging to comprehend, but
483   also results in an increase in model parameter amounts, being more computationally costly. Due to
484   these two factors, Bins Discretization exhibits a notably slow convergence. Meanwhile, the result
485   in 2m vs 1z indicates Bins Discretization may ignore the correlation of different dimensions, which
      would be helpful in sequence modeling.

501   Error Accumulation. A quantitative evaluation of the model’s accumulated error versus prediction
502   horizon is provided in Fig. 7. Since learning the world model is tied to a progressively improving

507
      imaginations, explaining MARIE’s notable performance in the 3s vs 5z scenario. More precisely,
508
      lower generalization error between the estimated dynamics and true dynamics brings a tighter bound
509   between optimal policies derived from these two dynamics according to theoretical results (Janner
510   et al., 2019).

      Attention Patterns. During model prediction, we delve into the attention maps inside the shared
      Transformer and the cross attention maps in the Perceiver. Interestingly, we observe two distinct
513
      attention patterns involved in the local dynamics prediction. One exhibits a Markovian pattern
514
      wherein the observation prediction lays its focus mostly on the previous transition, while the other is
515   regularly striated wherein the model attends to specific tokens in multiple prior transitions. During
516   the agent-wise aggregation, we also identify two distinct patterns – individuality and commonality
517   among agents. Such diverse patterns in the Transformer and Perceiver may be pivotal for achieving
518   accurate and consistent imaginations of the sophisticated local dynamics. We refer to §C for further
519   details and visualization results.

522   5                           C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
523
524   We have introduced a model-based multi-agent algorithm – MARIE, which utilizes a shared Trans-
525   former as local dynamic model and a Perceiver as a global agent-wise aggregation module to construct
526   a world model within the multi-agent context. By providing long-term imaginations with policy
527   learning, it significantly boosts the sample efficiency and improves final performance compared to
528   state-of-the-art model-free methods and existing model-based methods with same learning paradigm,
529   in the low data regime. But it should be also noticed that there are potential limitations on the
530   current evaluation on the main experiment with 4 limited seeds, e.g., the limitations of mean and
531
      median scores (Agarwal et al., 2021). Thus, we also provide a standardized performance evaluation
      following the protocol provided by Agarwal et al. (2021) in §G. To further deliver a rigorous statis-
532
      tical validation, evaluation with more seeds is definitely necessary. As the first Transformer-based
533
      multi-agent world model for sample-efficient policy learning, we open a new avenue for combining
534   the powerful strength of the Transformer with sample-efficient MARL. Considering the notorious
535   sample inefficiency in multi-agent scenarios, it holds important promise for application in many
536   realistic multi-robot systems, wherein collecting tremendous samples for optimal policy learning is
537   costly and impractical due to the safety. While it has the great potential to bright the future towards
538   achieving smarter multi-agent systems, there still exist limitations in MARIE. For instance, it would
539   suffer from much slower inference speed when used with a very long prediction horizons, due to the
      auto-regressive property.

1244
       Additionally, a recent method CoDreamer (Toledo & Prorok, 2024) extends DreamerV3 (Hafner
1245   et al., 2023) to the multi-agent setting, using GAT V2 (Brody et al., 2021) for communication
1246   among agents’ world models and policies. Though the aggregation modules in CoDreamer and
1247   ours are both built upon the Transformer architecture, our focus lies in computational efficiency of
1248   aggregation while it focuses on the underlying topological graph structure among agents. However, a
1249   fundamental difference is the backbone used for modeling the local dynamics. While we cast the
1250   local dynamics learning as the sequence modeling over discrete tokens, which can be achieved by
1251   using auto-regressive Transformers with causal attention mechanism, CoDreamer directly adopts the
