009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning to make sequential decisions solely from interacting with an environment
012            without any supervision has been achieved by the initial installation of deep neural
013            networks as function approximators to represent and learn a value function in high-
014            dimensional MDPs. Reinforcement learning policies face exponentially growing
015            state spaces in experience collection in high dimensional MDPs resulting in a
016
               dichotomy between computational complexity and policy success. In our paper
               we focus on the agent’s interaction with the environment in a high-dimensional
017
               MDP during the learning phase and we introduce a theoretically-founded novel
018
               method based on experiences obtained through extremum actions. Our analysis
019            and method provides a theoretical basis for effective, accelerated and efficient
020            experience collection, and further comes with zero additional computational cost
021            while leading to significant acceleration of training in deep reinforcement learning.
022            We conduct extensive experiments in the Arcade Learning Environment with
023            high-dimensional state representation MDPs. We demonstrate that our technique
024            improves the human normalized median scores of Arcade Learning Environment
025            by 248% in the low-data regime.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Utilization of deep neural networks as function approximators enabled learning functioning policies
030
      in high-dimensional state representation MDPs (Mnih et al., 2015). Following this initial work, the
031   current line of work trains deep reinforcement learning policies to solve highly complex problems from
032   game solving (Hasselt et al., 2016; Schrittwieser et al., 2020) to designing algorithms (Mankowitz
033   et al., 2023). Yet there are still remaining unsolved problems restricting the current capabilities of
034   deep neural policies. One of the main intrinsic open problems in deep reinforcement learning research
035   is sample complexity and experience collection in high-dimensional state representation MDPs.
036   While prior work extensively studied the policy’s interaction with the environment in bandits and
037   tabular reinforcement learning, and proposed various algorithms and techniques optimal to the tabular
038   form or the bandit context (Fiechter, 1994; Kearns & Singh, 2002; Brafman & Tennenholtz, 2002;
039
      Kakade, 2003; Lu & Roy, 2019), experience collection in deep reinforcement learning remains an
      open challenging problem while practitioners repeatedly employ quite simple yet effective techniques
040
      (i.e. ϵ-greedy) (Whitehead & Ballard, 1991; Flennerhag et al., 2022; Hasselt et al., 2016; Wang et al.,
041
      2016; Hamrick et al., 2020; Kapturowski et al., 2023).
042
043   Despite the provable optimality of the techniques designed for the tabular or bandit setting, they
044   generally rely strongly on the assumptions of tabular reinforcement learning, and in particular on the
045
      ability to record tables of statistical estimates for every state-action pair which have size growing
      with the number of states times the number of actions. Hence, these assumptions are far from what is
046
      being faced in the deep reinforcement learning setting where states and actions can be parametrized
047
      by high-dimensional representations. Thus, in high-dimensional complex MDPs, for which deep
048   neural networks are used as function approximators, the efficiency and the optimality of the methods
049   proposed for tabular settings do not transfer well to deep reinforcement learning experience collection
050   (Kakade, 2003). Hence, in deep reinforcement learning research still, naive and standard techniques
051   (e.g. ϵ-greedy) are preferred over both the optimal tabular techniques and over the particular recent
052   experience collection techniques targeting only high scores for particular games (Mnih et al., 2015;
053   Hasselt et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016; Anschel et al., 2017; Bellemare et al., 2017; Dabney et al.,
      2018; Lan et al., 2020; Flennerhag et al., 2022; Kapturowski et al., 2023).

      Sample efficiency in deep neural policies still remains to be one of the main challenging problems
055   restricting research progress in reinforcement learning. The magnitude of the number of samples
056   required to learn and adapt continuously is one of the main limiting factors preventing current state-
057   of-the-art deep reinforcement learning algorithms from being deployed in many diverse settings, but
058   most importantly one of the main challenges that needs to be dealt with on the way to building neural
059   policies that can generalize and adapt continuously in non-stationary environments. In our paper we
060   aim to seek answers for the following questions:
061
             • How can we construct policies that can collect unique experiences in a high-dimensional
062
               state representation MDP without any additional cost?
063
             • What is the natural theoretical motivation that can be used to design a zero-cost experience
064
               collection strategy while achieving high sample efficiency?
065
066   To be able to answer these questions, in our paper we focus on environment interactions in deep
067   reinforcement learning and make the following contributions:
068
069
             • We propose a fundamental theoretically well-motivated improvement to temporal difference
               learning based on state-action value function minimization that increases the information
070
               gain from the environment interactions of the policy in a given MDP.
071
072
             • We conduct an extensive study in the Arcade Learning Environment 100K benchmark
               with the state-of-the-art algorithms and demonstrate that our temporal difference learning
073
               algorithm improves performance by 248% across the entire benchmark compared to the
074
               baseline algorithm.
075
             • We demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed MaxMin TD Learning algorithm in terms of
076
               sample-efficiency. Our method based on maximizing novel experiences via minimizing
077
               the state-action value function reaches approximately to the same performance level as
078            model-based deep reinforcement learning algorithms, without building and learning any
079            model of the environment.
080
             • Finally, from the fact that MaxMin TD learning is a fundamental improvement over canonical
081            methods, our paper demonstrates that any algorithm that uses temporal difference learning
082            can be immediately and simply switched to MaxMin TD learning.

230     Core Counterintuition: How could minimizing the state-action value function accelerate
231     learning?
232
233
234   At first, the results in Proposition 3.4 and 3.6 might appear counterintuitive. Yet, understanding this
235   counterintuitive fact relies on first understanding the intrinsic difference between randomly initialized
236
      state-action value function, i.e. Qθ , and the optimal state-action value function, i.e. Q∗ . In particular,
      from the perspective of the function Q∗ , the action amin (s) = arg mina Qθ (s, a) is a uniform random
237
      action. However, from the perspective of the function Qθ , the action amin is meaningful, in that it
238
      will lead to a higher TD-error update than any other action. In fact, Proposition 3.4 and 3.6 precisely
239   provides the formalization that the temporal difference achieved by taking the minimum action is
240   larger than that of a random action by an amount equal to the disadvantage gap D(s). In order to
241   reconcile these two statements it is useful at this point to look at the limiting case of the Q function at
242   initialization. In particular, the following proposition shows that, at initialization, the distribution of
243   the minimum value action in a given state is uniform by itself, but is constant once we condition on
244   the weights θ.

      The original justification for collecting experiences with the minimum Q-value action, is that taking
      this action tends to result in transitions with higher temporal difference. The theoretical analysis

425

427   taking the minimum Q-value action in state s exceeds the average temporal difference over a uniform

429   empirical measurements of the temporal difference. To measure the change in the temporal difference
430   when taking the minimum action versus the average action, we compare the temporal difference

478
      In our study we focus on the following questions in deep reinforcement learning: (i) Is it possible to
479
      increase sample efficiency in deep reinforcement learning in a computationally efficient way with
480   conceptually simple choices?, (ii) What is the theoretical motivation of our proposed perspective, min-
481   imizing the state-action value function in early training, that results in one of the most computationally
482   efficient ways to explore in deep reinforcement learning? and, (iii) How would the theoretically
483   motivated simple idea transfer to large scale experiments in high-dimensional state representation
484   MDPs? To be able to answer these questions we propose a novel, theoretically motivated method with
485   zero additional computational cost based on following actions that minimize the state-action value
      function in deep reinforcement learning. We demonstrate theoretically that our method MaxMin TD

      Learning based on minimization of the state-action value results in higher temporal difference, and
487   thus creates novel transitions in exploration with more unique experience collection. Following the
488   theoretical motivation we initially show in a toy example in the chain MDP setup that our proposed
489   method MaxMin TD Learning results in achieving higher sample efficiency. Then, we expand this
490   intuition and conduct large scale experiments in the Arcade Learning Environment, and demonstrate
491   that our proposed method MaxMin TD Learning increases the performance on the Arcade Learning
492   Environment 100K benchmark by 248%.
