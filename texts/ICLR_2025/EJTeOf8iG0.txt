011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Emotion-cause pair extraction (ECPE) aims to identify all emotion and cause
014            clauses in documents, forming the ECPs. Although existing methods have
015
               achieved some success, they face issues such as overlooking the impact of
               emotion experiencers, failing to leverage specific domain knowledge, and tend-
016
               ing to spurious correlations. To address these issues, we transform the ECPE
017
               task into a multi-step reasoning problem and propose the Emotion-Experience-
018            Event-Cause (EEEC) framework. We introduce an experiencer identification
019            task to understand the source of emotions and enhance the association between
020            emotion and cause clauses. In addition, by combining both prior knowledge
021            and induced reasoning, EEEC guides a large-scale language model (LLM) to
022            perform the emotion-reason pair extraction task efficiently. Experimental re-
023            sults demonstrate that EEEC achieves performance close to current state-of-
024            the-art supervised fine-tuning methods. The data and code are released at
025            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/EEEC-EB80/

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      With the proliferation of social media data, sentiment analysis has emerged as a highly regarded
030   research area. Emotion Cause Analysis (ECA), as a significant branch, is devoted to exploring
031   deeply the underlying motivations and causes when individuals express emotions. By thoroughly
032   examining the logic and factors influencing emotions, it can enhance the capability of intelligent
033   systems to comprehend and respond to human emotions.
034
      Depending on the task’s specific setting, ECA tasks can be split into Emotion-Cause Extraction
035
      (ECE) and Emotion-Cause Pair Extraction (ECPE). The objective of the ECE task is to extract the
036   cause underlying the emotion in a given document with the expression of the emotion Lee et al.
037   (2010); Gui et al. (2016; 2017); Li et al. (2018); Xia et al. (2019); Ding et al. (2019). ECPE aims to
038   simultaneously extract all the potential emotions and the associated causes from a given document
039   Xia & Ding (2019). However, the former needs to manually annotate the emotion, which not only
040   consumes a lot of human resources but also limits its wide application in real-world scenarios.
041   We focus on the ECPE task, as shown in Figure 1, the document has 18th clauses, and C7 shows
042   the emotion ”sadness” caused by C6. C18 expresses a feeling of ”sadness,” which relates to C17.
043   Therefore, the goal is to extract all emotion cause pairs (ECPs):{(C7, C6), (C18, C17)}.
044   The essence of the ECPE task hinges on uncovering the relationship between emotion and cause
045   clauses. Intuitively, the specific position relationship between clauses is the distinctive feature for
046   mining the causal relationship between emotion clauses and cause clauses. Consequently, much
047   of the research directly utilizes the relative positions between clauses as features for ECPE task,
048   such as: Ding et al. (2020b); Fan et al. (2020b); Yuan et al. (2020); Cheng et al. (2020); Ding et al.
049   (2020a); Fan et al. (2020a); Wei et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2021); Fan et al. (2021); Singh et al. (2021);
050
      Huang et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024). Nevertheless, this approach is only applicable to data that is
      sensitive to positional features, and the method has poor generalization ability.
051
052   Except relying on the prior rules, some studies employ the correlations between clauses Xia &
053   Ding (2019); Wu et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2022b;a;b); Feng et al. (2023); Chen & Mao (2023);
      Li et al. (2023a); Chen et al. (2020b); Liu et al. (2022) to achieve ECPE. In addition, other studies

090   Although these approaches have achieved good performance, they still have several shortcomings.
091   (1) Ignore the influence of the experiencer on the ECPE task. Experiencers are the person or
092   sentient entity who experiences or feels the emotions Ghazi et al. (2015). (2) Neglect of domain-

095

096

099   express one or more emotions. Additionally, it is worth noting that experiencers are directly associ-
100   ated with emotion and cause clauses, thus determining the practical scope of emotional and causal
101   clauses. In this case, C2, C3, C4, C5, C11, C12 and C13 will not be candidates for emotion and
102   cause clauses.

104   (EEEC) framework, which can incorporate prior domain knowledge and leverage the association be-
105   tween experiencers and clauses. The framework aims to guide the model, focusing on task-relevant
106   details and mitigating other information interference. In specifically, we decompose the ECPE task

      2   R ELATED W ORK
126
127
      Xia & Ding (2019) initially presented the Emotion-Cause Pair Extraction (ECPE) task, which aims
128   to extract emotion clauses (EC) and cause clauses (CC) from unannotated documents simultane-
129   ously. They designed a 2-step pipeline framework that can extract EE and CE sequentially. How-
130   ever, it is prone to cascading errors. Subsequently, a considerable number of end-to-end methods
131   have emerged. These end-to-end methods can be classified into: method for enumerating and fil-
132   tering all clause pairs Chen et al. (2020b); Ding et al. (2020b); Wei et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2022b);
133   Wu et al. (2022); Hu et al. (2023). For example, Chen et al. (2020b) construct a relationship graph of
134   all candidate clause pairs and encode the candidate pairs using a graph model to extract ECPs. Ding
135   et al. (2020b) propose an emotion/cause-oriented sliding window mechanism for filtering the can-
136
      didate clauses. Wei et al. (2020) consider all candidate clause pairs and then perform the ECPE in
      a ranking manner. Chen et al. (2022b) employs KL divergence to constrain the candidate pair score
137
      matrix and align multi-task features to extract ECPs. Wu et al. (2022) transform the ECPE task as
138
      a clause-pairs tagging task for any two clause pairs in a document. Hu et al. (2023) treat all clauses
139   as filtering targets and extract the ECPs using genuine and fake pair supervision mechanisms. Se-
140   quence labeling was another popular ECPE method, pairing clauses according to elaborate labels.
141   Yuan et al. (2020) construct a labeling scheme based on the distances between emotion clauses and
142   cause clauses to achieve end-to-end ECPE. Chen et al. (2020a) encode the sentiment categories and
143   clause types into tags so that ECPs of different emotion types can be easily distinguished.
144
      Moreover, some researchers introduced additional knowledge for modelling the context of ECPE
145   tasks. Several researchers noticed that explicit semantic information or question answering (QA)-
146   based queries could improve the performance of ECPE tasks. For example, Nguyen & Nguyen
147   (2023) cast the Emotion-Cause Pair Extraction task to the question answering problem. Chang et al.
148   (2022) proposed a two-stage MRC framework through the questing-answering formulation. Zhou
149   et al. (2022) design a Multi-turn MRC framework (MM-R) with Rethink mechanism. Cheng et al.
150   (2023) propose a dual-MRC framework to extract ECPs in a dual-direction way. This framework
151   extracts ECPs by querying two directions and then uses a combination strategy to merge the results
152   obtained from the two directions. Others are devoted to introducing additional knowledge for mod-
153
      elling the context of ECPE tasks. Turcan et al. (2021) achieves emotion classification and emotion
      cause tagging by combining common sense knowledge with multi-task learning through adaptive
154
      knowledge modelling. Bao et al. (2022b) perceive textual semantics through fine-grained seman-
155
      tics introduced by keywords and coarse-grained semantic features between clauses. Zheng et al.
156   (2022) proposed a prompt learning based Emotion-Cause Pair Extraction approach. This method
157   transforms the three sub-tasks of ECPE into three sub-objectives and realizes the extraction of mul-
158   tiple sub-tasks by constructing special prompts. Chen et al. (2022b) aligned features and tasks to
159   model interactions between specific emotion-cause features and tasks explicitly. Chen et al. (2023)
160   proposed a novel two-stage model to handle the ECPE task and incorporate reinforcement learn-
161   ing (RL) to tackle the cascading error issue. Li et al. (2023b) proposed an Experiencer-Driven
      and Knowledge-Aware Graph Model (EDKA-GM) to enhance the impact of experiencer features

      in ECPE tasks. They leveraged ATOMIC’s semantic correlations to construct inter-clause rela-
163   tionships, enriching the task’s contextual understanding. Gu et al. (2024) suggested an Emotion

178   3.1   M OTIVATION
179
180   For the benchmark dataset Xia & Ding (2019), the average clause length is 14.77, with a maximum
181   of 4 ECPs per document. Given documents containing d clauses, previous approaches extract ECPs
182   from all possible (|d| ∗ |d|) candidate pairs(up to 4 ECPs). Identifying a few valid pairs from nu-
183   merous candidates is highly challenging. However, if we can preliminarily identify which clauses
184
      are more likely to be emotion clauses and filter the relevant cause clauses based on the experiencers
      in the emotion clauses, we can narrow down the potential ECPs more effectively. This means we
185
      can get the true ECPs with a higher probability, therefore, the complexity is reduced compared to
186
      |d| ∗ |d| of the search space.
187
188   Nevertheless, if the initial emotion clause detection is wrong, it may lead to cascading errors in sub-
189   sequent steps. To mitigate this issue, we incorporate word-level sentiment domain knowledge into
190
      the emotion extraction module to compensate for the shortcomings of emotion instruction prompts.
      Additionally, introducing an emotional score threshold to filter and select clauses with strong emo-
191
      tional expressions can improve the accuracy of the emotion extraction module. Moreover, the expe-
192
      riencer serves as a crucial link between emotion clauses and cause clauses, guiding the identification
193   of relevant contexts. By fully leveraging experiencer information, we can more accurately correlate
194   emotions with their causes, thereby enhancing the model’s overall performance.
195
196   3.2   TASK D EFINITION
197
198   For the ECPE task, the input is a document D = {c1 , c2 , · · · , c|d| }, which contains multiple clauses,
199   each clause ci = {w1 , w2 , · · · , wn } consists n words. The output is all potential emotion-cause
200   pairs (ECPs) P = {· · · , (ce , cc ), · · · }, where ce is an emotion clause and cc represents the corre-
201   sponding cause clause. It is worth noting that each document contains at least one ECP, and each
202   emotion clause can have multiple cause clauses.
203
204   3.3   EEEC A RCHITECTURE
205
      We designed an emotion-experiencer-event-cause framework for the ECPE task, a multi-step chain-
206
      of-thinking reasoning framework guided by emotion domain knowledge. We aim to leverage LLM’s
207
      comprehension ability without increasing the training cost and optimize LLM’s output with elabo-
208   rate prompt steps. Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture. EEEC consists of three key phases:
209   Knowledge-guided Emotion Extraction, experiencer and event extraction, and cause extraction.

319
320   The experiencer is the direct expresser or recipient of the emotion, and the emotion and cause clause

322   the background or event. Accurate identification of the experiencer and related events is crucial for
323   subsequent cause extraction. Determining the experiencer and events can significantly narrow the
      search space for cause clauses.

      3.6   C AUSE E XTRACTION
332
333   Identifying cause clauses directly from the above results may lead to disastrous results, given the
334   complexity of the ECPE task and the uncontrollability of the LLM model. Additionally, every part
335   of the reasoning process could be considered a cause. At the same time, only a few are actual
336   cause clauses, making it relatively difficult to obtain the final ECPs directly. Furthermore, ensuring
337   consistency in the results is challenging due to the unpredictability of zero-shot outputs. To address
338   these issues, we have designed the following two steps:
339   Step 4. Analysis: In this step, based on the above analysis, consider the chronological order and
340   logical correlation between clauses, the cause that directly or indirectly lead to the emotion clauses
341   are analyzed step by step, making sure that the cause clauses are directly related to the emotion
342   clauses and do not contain additional speculative content. It is worth noting that the emotion clauses
343   themselves may also be cause clauses.
344
      Step 5. Validate: At this stage, we combine the results from previous multi-step reasoning with a
345   comprehensive understanding of the document to verify whether the relationship between emotion
346   and cause clauses is reasonable and coherent. Specifically, Based on the multiple potential cause
347   clauses found in Step 4, the most direct causes are prioritized, excluding speculative clauses or
348   providing only background or contextual information. Finally, these high-confidence emotion-cause
349   pairs are selected and output in the format [emotion clause number, cause clause number].

362   as performance metrics. However, assessing ECPE tasks can be challenging due to the inherent

364   responses that are correct with human review but may not match the ground truth word-for-word

366   a thief,” an LLM might generate “The thief took the author’s money, which likely triggered the
367
      author’s emotions.” While the semantic content of the LLM’s output matches the target, the literal
      wording may differ, and such output should be considered correct. For this, we also used the manual
368
      evaluation designed Wang et al. (2023).

422   4.3     R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
423
424   4.3.1    Z ERO - SHOT R ESULT
425
      In the experiments, we used GPT-4o mini as the baseline model to evaluate the proposed EEEC
426   across three benchmark datasets: Chinese Ding et al. (2019), English Singh et al. (2021), and the
427   balanced Ding & Kejriwal (2020) dataset. The results are shown in Table 1. For the Chinese dataset,
428   our EEEC framework outperformed existing large language model (LLM) methods, achieving the
429   best results. Notably, in the zero-shot setting, EEEC demonstrated significant improvements over
430   the four-shot DECC in both P and F1 score, with P increasing by 5.51 and F1 by 1.8. This im-
431   provement can be attributed to incorporating prior sentiment knowledge in Step 1, which better
      guided the model in extracting emotion clauses. Additionally, the experiencer identification in Step


                                                                 8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      3 helped narrow the search space for cause clauses. Compared to fully-supervised fine-tuning meth-
433   ods, we found that the zero-shot EEEC outperformed the select-then-extract framework EDSECPE,
434   with a 0.61 improvement in F1. EEEC also showed clear advantages over end-to-end methods like
435   ECPE-3D, IA-ECPE, and E2EECPE, with F1 improvements of 2.86, 6.97, and 8.95, respectively.
436   Compared to the graph-based PairGCN method, EEEC achieved a higher F1 by 0.11. Despite these
437   strong performances, EEEC still falls short of most fully-supervised fine-tuning methods.
438
      In contrast to its performance on the Chinese dataset, EEEC outperforms most fully-supervised fine-
439   tuning methods on the English benchmark dataset, surpassing even the leading LLM-based DECC
440   model. Specifically, EEEC achieves an F1 score improvement of 8.07 compared to DECC. This
441   improvement is primarily due to DECC’s reliance on emotion keywords when extracting emotion
442   clauses, which inevitably leads to omissions. Furthermore, DECC’s approach to extracting cause
443   clauses focuses solely on deriving them from emotion clauses, overlooking the critical relation-
444   ships between experiencers, emotion clauses, and cause clauses. In contrast, EEEC more effectively
445   captures the role of experiencers and integrates contextual information, resulting in a substantial
446   performance boost.
447
448   4.3.2     D E - BIAS R ESULT
449
      In the Chinese benchmark dataset, 80% of the cause clauses are either emotion clauses themselves
450
      or located in adjacent clauses, resulting in a significant positional bias. Therefore, we conducted
451   evaluations on the rebalanced dataset. The results are shown in Table 1, where all previous methods
452   based on fully supervised fine-tuning show a significant performance degradation on the rebalanced
453   dataset. In contrast, EEEC’s F1 score decreases significantly less than that of the other models in
454   the zero-shot setting. Furthermore, EEEC in the zero-shot setting outperforms the state-of-the-art
455   methods on this rebalanced dataset.
456
      We analyzed this phenomenon: previous methods often exploit positional bias to extract emotion-
457   cause pairs, which can overfit this bias, resulting in false correlations and neglecting the semantic
458   features of the text, which leads to significant performance degradation. In contrast, the EEEC
459   method relies entirely on the LLM’s inherent understanding and reasoning abilities, making it less
460   sensitive to the positional distribution within the dataset. Additionally, we leverage prior sentiment
461   knowledge to guide the LLM, enabling it to identify emotions more accurately.
462
463   4.3.3     P ERFORMANCE ON M ULTIPLE PAIRS
464
465   In addition, we compared the results of extracting emotion-cause pairs from documents with multi-
466
      ple pairs. Specifically, we divided each test set into two groups: one group containing only a single
      Emotion-Cause Pair (ECP) and the other group containing two or more ECPs. The comparison re-
467
      sults are shown in Table 2. Our model EEEC performs exceptionally well in multi-pair extraction
468
      scenarios, achieving an F1 score that surpasses the previous best model DEEC by 4.8. This im-
469   provement indicates that the guidance of prior emotional knowledge and incorporating experiencer
470   features are efficient for complex emotional reasoning. However, it fails to achieve optimal perfor-
471   mance in terms of P. This is primarily due to the complex structure and semantic relationships in
472   documents containing multiple pairs. While introducing prior emotional knowledge helps the model
473   extract potential emotion clauses, it inevitably draws attention to the implicit emotion clauses within
474   the document, leading to the extraction of more invalid pairs.

      5.1   D IFFERENT C OMPONENTS
490
      To evaluate each step’s impact on the EEEC model’s performance, we conducted experiments on
491
      variations of the EEEC where each step was removed. Specifically, there are three variations related
492   to the removal of Step 1: w/o step1-para (this variation ignores the prior emotional knowledge of the
493   clauses), w/o step1-keyword (this variation does not treat emotional keywords in the clauses as crit-
494   ical features), and w/o step1 (this variation directly identifies emotion clauses from the document).
495   Table 3 shows the experimental results. When prior emotional knowledge is ignored, the F1 score
496   drops more significantly than when emotional keywords are disregarded, indicating that emotional
497   knowledge helps the LLM explore the possibility of clauses being emotion clauses. Removing the
498   emotion clause identification step leads to a significant performance drop, as providing excessively
499   noisy clauses to subsequent steps without any constraints introduces confusion. Removing the ex-
500
      periencer identification step results in unrelated clauses being considered candidate cause clauses,
      which may lead to inaccurate cause analysis and reduced precision. On the other hand, removing
501
      the step of identifying experiencer-related contexts, backgrounds, events, and behaviour clauses has
502
      a minor impact on performance. This step primarily helps summarize the experiencer’s story to aid
503   the model in understanding the document’s main content in a more targeted manner. Eliminating
504   the cause analysis step sharply declines performance. An end-to-end approach to extracting cause
505   clauses directly from the document overlooks the LLM’s reasoning capabilities. However, removing
506   the verification and summary steps has minimal impact on performance.

523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   Considering that previous works ignore the influence of experiencer, a priori emotion knowledge on
526   emotion-cause on extraction task (ECPE), and the problem of spurious correlations caused by po-
527   sitional bias overfitting, this paper proposes the Emotion-Experience-Event-Cause multi-step chain
528   (EEEC) framework. By decomposing the ECPE task into multiple sub-tasks, with each solution
529   depending on the previous step, EEEC leverages the reasoning capabilities of large language models
530   (LLMs) through well-designed prompts and chain-of-thought decomposition, addressing the limi-
531
      tations of fully supervised fine-tuning methods. To obtain experiencer information, we introduced
      a sub-task for experiencer identification, capturing the relationship between experiencers, emotion
532
      clauses, and cause clauses. Furthermore, we designed sub-tasks to extract experiencer-related con-
533
      texts and events, reducing the search space for cause clauses. To accurately extract emotion clauses,
534   the framework introduces a priori sentiment knowledge to guide LLM to recognize emotion clauses
535   more effectively. Experiments conducted on three benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness
536   and robustness of the EEEC framework. Especially in complex multi-pair ECPE tasks, EEEC signif-
537   icantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods. More importantly, EEEC maintains high performance
538   in zero-times learning scenarios, demonstrating its strong inference capability and adaptability to
539   different language datasets. Future work will focus on developing more efficient LLM-based mod-
      els and improving performance by extracting complex emotion-cause relationships.
