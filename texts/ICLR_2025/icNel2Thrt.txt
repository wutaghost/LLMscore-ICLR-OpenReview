                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            Second-order training methods have better convergence properties than gradient
010            descent but are rarely used in practice for large-scale training due to their computa-
011            tional overhead. This can be viewed as a hardware limitation (imposed by digital
012            computers). Here we show that natural gradient descent (NGD), a second-order
013            method, can have a similar computational complexity per iteration to a first-order
014            method, when employing appropriate hardware. We present a new hybrid digital-
015            analog algorithm for training neural networks that is equivalent to NGD in a certain
016            parameter regime but avoids prohibitively costly linear system solves. Our algo-
017
               rithm exploits the thermodynamic properties of an analog system at equilibrium,
               and hence requires an analog thermodynamic computer. The training occurs in
018
               a hybrid digital-analog loop, where the gradient and Fisher information matrix
019
               (or any other positive semi-definite curvature matrix) are calculated at given time
020            intervals while the analog dynamics take place. We numerically demonstrate the
021            superiority of this approach over state-of-the-art digital first- and second-order
022            training methods on classification tasks and language model fine-tuning tasks.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      With the rise of more sophisticated AI models, the cost of training them is exploding, as world-leading
      models now cost hundreds of millions of dollars to train. This issue is compounded by the ending of
028
      both Moore’s Law and Dennard’s Law for digital hardware (Khan et al., 2018), which impacts both
029
      the runtime and energy efficiency of such hardware. This highlights a need and an opportunity for
030   specialized, unconventional hardware targeted at improving the efficiency of training AI models.
031
032   Moreover, conventional digital hardware can be viewed as limiting the range of training algorithms
033
      that a user may consider. Researchers are missing an opportunity to co-design novel optimizers to
      exploit novel hardware developments. Instead, relatively simplistic optimizers, such as stochastic
034
      gradient descent (SGD), Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), and their variants (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017),
035
      are among the most popular methods for training deep neural networks (DNNs) and other large AI
036   models. More sophisticated optimizers are rarely used due to the associated computational overhead
037   on digital hardware.
038
039
      A clear example of this is second-order methods, which capture curvature information of the loss
      landscape. These methods, while theoretically more powerful in terms of convergence properties,
040
      remain computationally expensive and harder to use, blocking their adoption. For example, natural
041
      gradient descent (NGD) (Amari, 1998; Martens, 2020) involves calculating estimates of second-order
042   quantities such as the Fisher information matrix and performing a costly linear system solve at every
043   epoch. Some approximations to NGD, such as the Kronecker-factored approximate curvature (K-
044   FAC) (Martens & Grosse, 2015), have shown promise, and K-FAC has shown superior performance
045   to Adam (Lin et al., 2023; Eschenhagen et al., 2023). However, applying such methods to arbitrary
046   neural network architectures remains difficult (Pauloski et al., 2020).
047
      In this article, we present thermodynamic natural gradient descent (TNGD), a new method to
048   perform second-order optimization. This method involves a hybrid digital-analog loop, where a GPU
049   communicates with an analog thermodynamic computer. A nice feature of this paradigm is flexibility:
050   the user provides their model architecture and the analog computer serves only to accelerate the
051   training process. This is in contrast to many proposals to accelerate the inference workload of AI
052   models with analog computing, where the model is hardwired into the hardware, and users are unable
053   to change the model architecture as they seamlessly would by using their preferred software tools
      (Kim et al., 2017; Ambrogio et al., 2018; Cristiano et al., 2018; Aguirre et al., 2024).

054
      The analog computer in TNGD uses thermodynamic processes as a computational resource. Such
055   thermodynamic devices have previously been proposed (Conte et al., 2019; Hylton, 2020; Ganesh,
056   2017; Coles et al., 2023; Lipka-Bartosik et al., 2023), have been theorized to exhibit runtime
057   and energy efficiency gains (Aifer et al., 2023; Duffield et al., 2023), and have been successfully
058   prototyped (Melanson et al., 2023; Aifer et al., 2024). Our TNGD algorithm represents an instance of
059   algorithmic co-design, where we propose a novel optimizer to take advantage of a novel hardware
060   paradigm. TNGD exploits a physical Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process to implement the parameter
061   update rule in NGD. It has a runtime per iteration scaling linearly in the number of parameters, and
062   when properly parallelized it can be close to the runtime of first-order optimizers such as Adam
063
      and SGD. Hence, it is theoretically possible to achieve the computational efficiency of a first-order
      training method while still accounting for the curvature of the loss landscape with a second-order
064
      method. Moreover, our numerics show the competitiveness of TNGD with first-order methods for
065
      classification and extractive question-answering tasks.

068   2   R ELATED WORK
069
070   There is a large body of theoretical research on natural gradient descent (Amari, 1998; Martens, 2020;
071
      Bottou et al., 2018) arguing that NGD requires fewer iterations than SGD to converge to the same
      value of the loss in specific settings. While less is known about the theoretical convergence rate of
072
      Adam, there exists a large body of empirical evidence that NGD can converge in fewer iterations than
073
      Adam (Martens et al., 2010; Martens & Grosse, 2015; Martens et al., 2018; Eschenhagen et al., 2023;
074   Ren & Goldfarb, 2019; Gargiani et al., 2020).
075
076   However, a single iteration of NGD is generally more computationally expensive than that of SGD
077
      or Adam, which have a per-iteration cost scaling linearly in the number of parameters N . NGD
      typically has a superlinear (assuming the condition number scales as κ = N α , α > 0 for NGD-CG)
078
      complexity in the number of parameters (although this may be reduced to linear scaling at the expense
079
      of higher-order scaling with batch size and output dimension, see Section 3). K-FAC (Martens
080   & Grosse, 2015) aims to reduce this complexity and invokes a block-wise approximation of the
081   curvature matrix, which may not always hold. While first introduced for multi-layer perceptrons,
082   K-FAC has been applied to more complex architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (Martens
083   et al., 2018) and transformers (Eschenhagen et al., 2023), where additional approximations have to
084   be made and where the associated computational overhead can vary.
085
      There has been significant effort and progress towards reducing the time- and space- complexity of
086   operations used in the inference workload of AI models, e.g., a variety of “linear attention" blocks
087   have been proposed (Shen et al., 2021; Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020). However,
088   there has been less focus on reducing the complexity of training methods. While various approaches
089   are taken to accelerating training using novel hardware, these efforts typically aim at reducing the
090   constant coefficients appearing in the time cost of computation. Especially relevant to our work,
091   analog computing devices have been proposed to achieve reduced time and energy costs of training
092   relative to available digital technology (Kim et al., 2017; Ambrogio et al., 2018; Cristiano et al.,
093   2018; Aguirre et al., 2024). These devices are generally limited to training a neural network that
094
      has a specific architecture (corresponding to the structure of the analog device). To our knowledge,
      there has not yet been a proposal that leverages analog hardware to reduce the complexity of training
095
      algorithms such as NGD.
096
097   Given the existing results implying that fewer iterations are needed for NGD relative to other
098   commonly used optimizers, we focus on reducing the per-iteration computational cost of NGD
099   using a hybrid analog-digital algorithm to perform each parameter update. Our algorithm therefore
100
      demonstrates that complexity can be improved in training (not only in inference), and moreover that
      the per-iteration complexity of NGD can be made similar to that of a first-order training method.

188   3.1   FAST MATRIX VECTOR PRODUCTS
189
190
      The linear system appearing in equation 6 can be solved using the conjugate gradient (CG)
      method (Martens et al., 2010), which will be referred to as NGD-CG in what follows. In fact,
191
      when ℓ is parametrized by a neural network, the GGN-vector product Gv involved in the conjugate
192
      gradient algorithm may be evaluated in runtime O(bN ) thanks to fast Jacobian-vector products (Brad-
193   bury et al., 2018) (JVPs). This approach also enables one to not explicitly construct the Fisher
194   matrix, thus also avoiding a O(bdz N 2 ) runtime cost in computing it and a O(N 2 ) memory cost in
195   storing it. The efficiency of this approach depends √
                                                          on the number of CG iterations required to obtain
196   good performance. Importantly, convergence in κ steps, with κ the condition number of F , is
197   not required to obtain competitive performance (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Gargiani et al., 2020).
198   Crucially, due to the sequential nature of the algorithm, the CG iterations cannot be parallelized.
199   In practice, since reaching convergence is computationally expensive, one generally stops the CG
200   algorithm after a set number of iterations. Because of the way the step size is adapted in CG, we have
201   observed that the solution after k steps xk is not necessarily closer to the true solution than the initial
202   guess x0 , in particular for ill-conditioned problems, which can make NGD-CG difficult to use.

245   Ax = b with reduced computational complexity relative to standard digital hardware. The solution
246
      to the linear system is found by letting the SPU evolve under an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) process

409   In this section we show how thermodynamic NGD may be applied to language modeling tasks, in

415   procedure are in App. E) to a manageable amount (75k here) for limited compute resources.
416   Figure 5(a) displays a comparison of the training loss for different optimizers. The bare TNGD (as
417   used in the previous section) shows a worse performance than Adam in this setting. However, a hybrid
418   approach, TNGD-Adam, where the natural gradient estimate is used in conjunction with the Adam
419   update rule gives the best performance (this is explained in App. B). One possible explanation for this
420   result is that there are two pre-conditionings of the gradient for TNGD-Adam: the first comes from
421   the natural gradient, which incorporates curvature information, and the second comes from the Adam
422   update rule, which acts as a signal-noise ratio as explained in Kingma & Ba (2015), which further
423
      adjusts the natural gradient values. In Fig. 5(b), we show that the same results as in the previous
      section apply to TNGD-Adam, where increasing the analog runtime boosts performance. Therefore,
424
      the analog runtime in TNGD may be viewed as a resource in this sense, that is computationally very
425
      cheap (as time constants can be engineered to be very small).

471
472
473
      This work introduced Thermodynamic Natural Gradient Descent (TNGD), a hybrid digital-analog
474   algorithm that leverages the thermodynamic properties of an analog system to efficiently perform
475   second-order optimization. TNGD greatly reduces the computational overhead typically associated
476   with second-order methods for arbitrary model architectures. Our numerical results on MNIST
477   classification and language model fine-tuning tasks demonstrate that TNGD outperforms state-of-the-
478   art first-order methods, such as Adam, and provide large speedups over other second-order optimizers.
479   This suggests a promising future for second-order methods when integrated with specialized hardware.
480   Looking forward, our research stimulates further investigation into TNGD, particularly with enhance-
481   ments such as averaging techniques and moving averages. Extensions to approximate second-order
482   methods such as K-FAC may also be possible. Moreover, the principles of thermodynamic computing
483   could inspire new algorithms for Bayesian filtering. While the current impact of our work relies on
484   the development and availability of large-scale analog thermodynamic computers, the theoretical
485   and empirical advantages presented here underscore the potential of co-designing algorithms and
      hardware to overcome the limitations of conventional digital approaches.

798   D    S IMULATING TNGD
799
800   The results reported in this paper require simulating the thermodynamic device. To do so, we employ
801   a Euler-Maruyama discretization of Eq. 15, where the update equation is:
802                                                                        p
803                                g̃ (k+1) = g̃ (k) + δt(Gg̃ (k) − ∇ℓ) + z 2κ0 δt                   (24)
804
805   where δt is a step size, z ∼ N (0, 1) and the GGN-vector product Gg̃ (k) is evaluated in linear time,
806
      with no need to construct G as explained in Section 3. One may consider higher-order schemes,
      which in general will cost d GGN-vector products (hence 2d + 1 model calls, accounting for the
807
      gradient) for each step of an order d solver.
808
809   With an Euler-Maruyama scheme, one therefore requires 3 model calls per time step, which results in
      long simulation times for the larger t values we report.

      E.1.1    N OISY SIMULATION
837
838   One key feature of TNGD is that it is noise-resilient. Indeed, because the solution of the linear
839   system of equation 6 is encoded in the first moment of the equilibrium distribution, any noise that is
840   approximately Gaussian will not affect much the quality of the results for reasonable noise levels. In
841   Fig. 6, the loss vs. iterations are shown for varying noise levels, which are defined by the value of the
842
      noise variance κ0 . For κ0 < 0.01, the noise essentially does not affect the performance of TNGD (as
      the influence of noise of performance starts to saturate at this value), even for a very small analog
843
      dynamics time (here, t = τ ). For a realistic electrical device where θt are voltages, the contribution
844
      of thermal noise to the noise level would be κ0 ∼ 10−6 V . Other noise sources may contribute to
845   the noise level, but because of the nature of the TNGD algorithm, it exhibits a high noise-resilience.
846   Note that it is also possible to collect more samples from the device to reduce influence of the noise
847   if the noise level is large.
