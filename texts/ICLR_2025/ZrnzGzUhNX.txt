011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            It has been demonstrated in many scientific fields that artificial neural networks,
014            like autoencoders or Siamese networks, encode meaningful concepts in their latent
015
               spaces. However, there does not exist a comprehensive framework for retrieving
               this information in a human-readable form without prior knowledge. In order to
016
               extract these concepts, we introduce a framework for finding closed-form inter-
017
               pretations of neurons in latent spaces of artificial neural networks. The interpre-
018            tation framework is based on embedding trained neural networks into an equiva-
019            lence class of functions that encode the same concept. We interpret these neural
020            networks by finding an intersection between the equivalence class and human-
021            readable equations defined by a symbolic search space. The effectiveness of our
022            approach is demonstrated by retrieving invariants of matrices and conserved quan-
023            tities of dynamical systems from latent spaces of Siamese neural networks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      The current AI revolution is driven by artificial neural networks (ANNs), particularly deep learning
028
      models. These models have enabled machines to achieve superhuman performance in a variety of
029
      tasks, such as image recognition, language translation, game playing, and even generating human-
030   like text. However, this remarkable power comes at the expense of interpretability, often referred
031   to as the ”black box” problem. The representational capacity of artificial neural networks relies
032   on interactions between possibly billions of neurons. While each single neuron is easy to describe
033   mathematically, as networks become larger, it becomes increasingly difficult to understand how
034   these interactions give rise to a neural network’s overall prediction.
035
      The black-box nature of neural networks can be acceptable in applications where prediction is the
036   primary goal. However, in science, where the goal is not just prediction but also understanding the
037   underlying phenomena, interpretability is crucial. Moreover, in medicine, it is important to under-
038   stand why an AI system has made a particular diagnosis or treatment recommendation to avoid risks
039   of dangerous or ethically questionable decisions (Jin et al., 2022; Amann et al., 2022). AI inter-
040   pretability in the law domain is crucial for understanding and explaining how automated decisions
041   are made, which helps ensure transparency and accountability. It also allows for the identification
042   and correction of biases, compliance with regulations, and maintains the integrity of legal processes
043   (Hacker et al., 2020; Bibal et al., 2020).
044   In many scientific applications of neural networks, it can be verified that neural networks often learn
045   meaningful concepts, similar to those that humans use, to describe certain phenomena (Ha & Jeong,
046   2021; Desai & Strachan, 2021; Nautrup et al., 2022) . Unfortunately, without a method to distill this
047   learned concept in a human-interpretable form, the only way to reveal it is by directly comparing it
048   to a set of candidates that the researcher is already aware of. Clearly, it is not possible to make new
049   discoveries in this way.
050   To address this problem, symbolic regression techniques have been proposed to interpret neural
051   networks by deriving closed-form expressions that represent the underlying concepts learned by
052   these networks (Cranmer et al., 2020; Mengel et al., 2023). These approaches involve exploring the
053   space of potential mathematical expressions to identify those that best replicate the predictions of
      a neural network. Unfortunately, such methods are limited to interpreting output neurons of neural


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      networks performing regression, where the concept that is recovered is the global function learned
055   by the neural network.
056
057   Neural networks applied to perform scientific discovery are often tasked with solving problems that
058
      cannot be formulated under the umbrella of regression. Further, it is often necessary to interpret a
      simpler sub-concept encoded in hidden layers. For these reasons, it is desirable to have a frame-
059
      work capable of interpreting concepts encoded in arbitrary intermediate neurons of artificial neural
060
      networks.
061
062   Prominent artificial scientific discovery methods have been proposed based on networks like autoen-
063   coders (Wetzel, 2017; Iten et al., 2020; Miles et al., 2021; Frohnert & van Nieuwenburg, 2024) or
064
      Siamese networks (Wetzel et al., 2020; Patel et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023). These networks can dis-
      till meaningful concepts inside their latent spaces without explicit training information in the form
065
      of labeled targets. The crucial obstacle to their wider adoption is the lack of tools that enable the re-
066
      covery of such concepts without prior knowledge. Removing this bottleneck would allow scientists
067   to use these tools to discover potentially new scientific insights.
068
069   In this paper, we describe a framework that can be employed to interpret any single neuron within an
070
      artificial neural network in closed form. Concepts encoded in neurons in hidden layers are generally
      not stored in a human-readable form, but instead get distorted and transformed in a highly non-linear
071
      fashion. Hence, the interpretation method is based on constructing an equivalence class around
072
      a certain neuron that contains all functions encoding the same concept as the target neuron. In
073   practice, we interpret the neuron by searching a closed-form representative function contained in
074   this equivalence class. We demonstrate the power of our framework by rediscovering the explicit
075   formulas of matrix invariants and conserved quantities from the latent spaces of Siamese networks.
076
      The capability of interpreting any single neuron in closed-form closes a significant gap regarding
077
      the problem of neural network interpretability. The main targets of our interpretation framework
078
      are neural networks tasked with solving scientific problems on structured data sets where the ulti-
079   mate level of interpretation is a scalar symbolic equation capturing the learned concept. The three
080   obstacles towards having a full interpretation of neural networks are:
081
082         1. scaling of symbolic representations: Any form of symbolic search algorithm scales
083            poorly with the complexity of the underlying equation. Many scientists are working on
084            competing symbolic search algorithms mainly tailored to symbolic regression, a list can be
085
               found in the subsequent paragraph.
086         2. dimensional mismatch of neural networks storing information distributed among multiple
087            neurons. Common methods to eliminate this mismatch are based on disentangling features
088            learned by different neurons within the same layer (Higgins et al., 2017) or to enforce a
089
               bottleneck (Koh et al., 2020) such that single neurons capture individual concepts.
090         3. distortions of concepts within a neural network in highly non-linear form. If neural net-
091            works learn concepts, there is no reason to store them in a form which is aligned with a
092
               human formulation of the concept. For example, if a neural network learns the concept
               of temperature, there is no reason to choose the Celsius or the Fahrenheit scale, nor does
093
               this encoding need to be linear. In practice, it turns out that this non-linear distortion can-
094
               not even be captured with symbolic equations. This problem prevents symbolic search
095            algorithms from interpreting anything beyond output neurons in the context of regression.
096            Until the invention of the interpretation framework presented in our manuscript, solving
097            this problem was impossible.
098
099   Hence our method is highly complementary with other publications and is currently the only option
100   to overcome obstacle 3.

103
104   The current manuscript concerns the domain of artificial neural network interpretability, with a fo-
105   cus on enabling new scientific discovery through latent space models. Much of the neural network
106   interpretability research adresses the question of whether or not neural networks learn certain known
107   scientific concepts. While verifying a neural network is an important task, it is unsuitable for gain-
      ing novel scientific insights. There has been limited progress toward revealing scientific insights in

      symbolic form from artificial neural networks that do not require previous knowledge of the under-
109   lying concept beforehand (Wetzel & Scherzer, 2017; Cranmer et al., 2020; Miles et al., 2021; Liu
110   & Tegmark, 2021). These cases are rare examples where the underlying concept is encoded in a
111   linear manner, or where other properties of the concept simplify the interpretation problem. While
112   there are no unified approaches to interpreting latent space models, it might in principle be possible
113   to build such models based on architectures with symbolic layers (Martius & Lampert, 2016; Sahoo
114   et al., 2018; Dugan et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024)
115   Our article aims instead to interpret existing latent space models. We extend an interpretation frame-
116   work (Wetzel, 2024), originally developed to interpret neural network classifiers, to interpret neural
117   network latent spaces.
118
      The interpretation method relies on efficiently searching the space of symbolic equations, which
119
      can be achieved by genetic search algorithms which form the backend of many symbolic regression
120
      algorithms. These include Eureqa (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009), Operon C++ (Burlacu et al., 2020),

122   tionary Algorithm (Virgolin et al., 2021), GPLearn (Stephens, 2022) and PySR (Cranmer, 2023).
123   Other symbolic regression algorithms include deep symbolic regression uses recurrent neural net-
124   works (Petersen et al., 2020), symbolic regression with transformers (Kamienny et al., 2022; Biggio
125   et al., 2021) or AI Feynman (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020).
126
      An overview of interpretable scientific discovery with symbolic Regression can be found in (Makke
127   & Chawla, 2022; Angelis et al., 2023).

      3.1   S IAMESE N EURAL N ETWORKS
165
166   Siamese neural networks (SNN) (Baldi & Chauvin, 1993; Bromley et al., 1993) were originally in-
167   troduced to solve fingerprint recognition and signature verification problems. SNNs consist of two
168   identical sub-networks with shared parameters, each receiving distinct inputs which are then pro-
169   jected to an embedding space. These projections are then compared by a distance metric, which
170   joins each sub-network f together at their output. Inputs belonging to the same class should ob-
171
      tain high similarity, while those belonging to different classes should obtain low similarity. Such
      a framework allows for generalization to infinite-class classification problems. The distance met-
172
      ric d(·) is chosen according to the specific problem at hand, and in our case we use the squared
173
      Euclidean distance.
174
175   The network F can be trained effectively using a contrastive or triplet loss (Schroff et al., 2015),
176   wherein a set of triplets are supplied to the energy function,
177                 L(xA , xP , xN ) = max(d(f (xA ), f (xP )) − d(f (xA ), f (xN )) + α, 0).
178
      The anchor xA is the ground truth class, the positive sample xP is of the same class as xA , whereas
179
      the negative sample N is of a different class. Instead of using a twin network, this setup requires a
180   triplet of identical networks, each still sharing the same weights. The triplet loss is minimized when
181   the distance between the anchor and positive sample is minimized in the embedding space, while the
182   distance between the anchor and negative sample is maximized. The margin parameter α is a positive
183   constant which encourages separation between positive and negative samples, as α = 0 would mean
184   that the loss could be trivially minimized by projecting all samples to the same location. Finally, the
185   max(·) operation ensures that the distance between positive and negative samples remains finite.
186   It has been shown that in scientific settings SNNs can be trained to learn conserved quantities and
187   symmetry invariants of the underlying system. For this purpose, training data is collected where data
188   points belonging to the same class are defined through a connection via trajectories obeying laws
189   of motion (conserved quantities) or a desired symmetry group (symmetry invariants) (Wetzel et al.,
190   2020).
191
      The architecture of the sub-network f depends on the underlying data. In our case, we implement it
192   as a fully-connected network. We note that our framework interprets single neurons, hence our latent
193   layer (i.e., the final neuron in our sub-network f ), which we wish to interpret, consists of only one
194   neuron. The details of our architecture and training hyperparameters can be found in subsection C.2.
195
196   3.2   I NTERPRETATION F RAMEWORK
197
198   The interpretation framework is designed to extract concepts in the form of symbolic equations from
199   any single disentangled or concept bottleneck neuron within an artificial neural network. While the
200   interpretation framework can be applied to any single neuron, for the purpose of this manuscript we
201
      perform an interpretation of the output neuron f (x) of a single sub-net of a Siamese network defined
      by equation 1 which produces a scalar mapping of the input into a latent space.
202
203   f (x) contains the full information about a certain symbolic concept g(x) if g(x) can be faithfully
204   reconstructed from f (x). Conversely, if f (x) only contains information from g(x) it is possible to
205   reconstruct f (x) from the knowledge of g(x). In mathematical terms that means that there exists
206   an invertible function ϕ such that f (x) = ϕ(g(x)). An example of the same concept embedded in
207
      different forms is the temperature, it can be measured in Fahrenheit or Celsius and there exists a
      linear transformation that maps one version of the temperature onto the other.
208
209   In general, this means that if we aim to extract information from a neural network f , we need to
210   account for any nonlinear and uninterpretable transformation ϕ that conceals the human formulation
211   of a concept,

241   3.3      S YMBOLIC S EARCH
242
243   Symbolic regression is a regression analysis technique that has traditionally been used to find closed-
244   form expressions that approximate the relation between target and input variables for a given dataset.
245   Typically, this is done by employing a genetic algorithm, which evolves a population of candidate
246   formulas using genetic operations like selection, crossover, and mutation, aiming to find the least
247   complex tree of operators T that best maps inputs X to outputs Y according to some objective
248
      function. This tree consists of a set of nodes, each containing either a number, variable, or a unary
      or binary operator (see Figure 5 (c) for an example tree) that represent a mathematical expression. In
249
      the context of neural network interpretation, symbolic regression is employed to convert a complex
250
      model into an interpretable tree representation.
251
252   In our case, we search for a symbolic tree T which represents a function g ∈ Hf + , meaning, we look
253   for a symbolic concept g within the equivalence class anchored on the neural network f . During
254
      this step we choose a symbolic quantity whose gradient points in the same direction as the gradient
      of the network f . This is possible because Hf − can be mapped to Hf + simply by multiplying each
255
      element with −1. Hence, it is enough to focus on Hf + . However, instead of performing regression
256
      on a set of prediction targets to find the best fitting function, we search for an analytical expression
257   whose normalized gradients are as close as possible to those of f . Because of this difference, we
258   refer to this approach as symbolic search instead of symbolic regression. Note that this requires
259   that T consists of operators that yield a differentiable function. To implement our symbolic search
260   algorithm, we modify the the SymbolicRegression.jl module from the PySR package (Cranmer,
261   2023).
262
      The objective function we choose is the mean-squared-error (MSE), which measures the distance

      We summarize the results of our experiments in Tables 1–3. For each experiment, we use the method
379   outlined in section 3 to obtain a set of predicted expressions from the symbolic search algorithm,
380   which we present as a Pareto chart in Figure 4 and Figure 3. The Pareto chart plots each of these
381   expressions as a bar chart in decreasing order of loss. Of these expressions, we identify the one
382   that most closely matches the correct expression, and present it under the column titled retrieved
383   expression in tables 1–3. The correct expression is typically found after the steepest drop in the loss,
384   corresponding to the lowest complexity symbolic solution that captures the ground truth. A notable
385   exception to this rule arises when the network learns a good approximation to the desired expression,
386   which we rectified by increasing the sampling range used to produce the dataset. Since Hg contains
387
      many different symbolic solutions that are all connected by an invertible transformation, it might in
      principle be possible to find a different formulation of the ground truth if it is of lower complexity.
388
389   In our experiments, it was possible to retrieve all the correct ground truth expressions. We also
390   observe that the symbolic search algorithm may approximate the correct solution, or add simpli-
391   fications to it. For example, the solution denoted by the striped pink bar in Figure 3 (c) a uses
                                    4
392   exp(x1 · x1 ) ≈ 1 + x2 + x2 , which matches the correct solution up to the fourth order in x. In
393   Figure 3 (d), the expression 2 exp(x + 1) was simplified to exp(x + 1 + ln(2)) ≈ exp(x + 1.684).
394   We compare the latent projection f (X) for all inputs on the data set X to the true underlying concept
395   g(X). This can be visualized by plotting these quantities against each other in Figure 2. Note that
396   these correlation plots are not a necessary component of our interpretation framework and are solely
397   used to highlight the non-linear manner in which the neural network encodes the concept. In most
398   experiments, the values encoded in the latent space are highly correlated with some well-known
399   concept. In fact, the correlation plots for the trace in Figure 2 (a), (c), and (e) are almost linear,
400   which is expected as they can trivially be learned by a single-layer neural network with no non-
401
      linearities. In such cases, it is possible to use methods such as directly applying symbolic regression
      to the latent space to interpret the neural network. However, most invariants are significantly more
402
      complex, and the neural network will encode them in a non-linear manner, in which case most other
403
      interpretation methods will fail. All of these methods fail for the same reason - they attempt to
404   retrieve the distorted version of the concept ϕ(g(x)), rather than the concept itself. In comparison,
405   our method searches for a symbolic tree whose gradients are aligned with the network f . This means
406   that the tree is not restricted to representing the distorted concept, and coupled with the complexity
407   penalty of symbolic search, it yields the simplest possible expression whose gradients match the
408   network f . We provide a comparison of our method to performing symbolic regression directly on
409   the latent space (Cranmer et al., 2020) in Appendix E, where only 7 of 12 experiments are successful.
410   In our experiments, direct symbolic regression is capable of identifying invariants of polynomials up
411   to second order. Of the 7 successful experiments, 3 of them are simply the trace, 2 are second order
412
      polynomials involving cross-terms, and the remaining two are also second order polynomials, but
      without any cross terms. Interestingly, direct symbolic regression manages to retrieve a valid version
413
      of the ground truth expression for the sum of principal minors of 3×3 antisymmetric matrices, which
414
      is encoded in a highly non-linear manner according to the correlation plot in Figure 2. It is important
415   to highlight, that the symbolic regression fails to correctly approximate the latent projection. The
416   reason behind this accidental success is likely based on finding a good solution on data-dense regions
417   on the data manifold - see Figure 6 in Appendix E.

420   5   C ONCLUSIONS
421
422   In this manuscript, we develop a framework to interpret any single neuron in neural network latent
423   spaces in the form of a symbolic equation. It is based on employing symbolic search to find a
424   symbolic tree that exhibits the same normalized gradients as the examined latent space neuron. The
425   approach is suitable to interpret all kinds of neural networks applied to structured data within settings
426   where concepts are formulated as scalar equations, like in science. The approach is limited by the
427   expressibility of symbolic search algorithms and the challenge of isolating single neurons through
428
      bottlenecks or disentanglement.
429   We justify this procedure by defining an equivalence class of functions encoding the same concept,
430   in which the membership criterion is that all members have parallel gradients at every point on the
431   data manifold. Through this procedure, we enable the extraction of concepts encoded by latent space
      models.

530   The pathways to scientific understanding via interpretable machine learning might lead down dif-
531   ferent roads. On one side there are inherently interpretable ML models, like PCA or support vector
532   machines. On the other side, there are powerful artificial neural networks, which are difficult to
