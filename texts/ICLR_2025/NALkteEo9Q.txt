009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent works have demonstrated the vulnerability of Deep Reinforcement Learn-
012            ing (DRL) algorithms against training-time, backdoor poisoning attacks. These
013            attacks induce pre-determined, adversarial behavior in the agent upon observing
014            a fixed trigger during deployment while allowing the agent to solve its intended
015            task during training. Prior attacks rely on arbitrarily large perturbations to the
016            agent’s rewards to achieve both of these objectives - leaving them open to detec-
017
               tion. Thus, in this work, we propose a new class of backdoor attacks against DRL
               which achieve state of the art performance while minimally altering the agent’s
018
               rewards. These “inception” attacks train the agent to associate the targeted adver-
019
               sarial behavior with high returns by inducing a disjunction between the agent’s
020            chosen action and the true action executed in the environment during training. We
021            formally define these attacks and prove they can achieve both adversarial objec-
022            tives. We then devise an online inception attack which significantly out-performs
023            prior attacks under bounded reward constraints.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms are versatile tools allowing artificial agents to optimize
029   complex tasks directly from interactions with their environment. The most popular and powerful RL
030
      approaches, like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) and Deep Q-Networks
      (DQN) (Mnih et al., 2013), utilize deep-neural networks as function approximators, forming an area
031
      of research referred to as Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL). This versatility has lead to the
032
      ever growing adoption of DRL based approaches in safety and security critical domains, such as
033   automated cyber defenses (Vyas et al., 2023), self-driving vehicles (Kiran et al., 2021), robotic
034   warehouse management (Krnjaic et al., 2023), and space traffic coordination (Dolan et al., 2023).
035
036
      The wide-spread applicability of DRL makes it a target for external adversaries wishing to influ-
      ence an agent’s behavior. This necessitates deeper studies into the capabilities of adversarial attacks
037
      against DRL so that practitioners can know how to defend against them. Thus, in this work we focus
038
      our efforts towards a better understanding of backdoor poisoning attacks which manipulate the train-
039   ing of an agent such that their behavior can be directly controlled during deployment upon observing
040   a pre-determined “trigger”. Multiple works (Kiourti et al., 2019; Cui et al., 2023; Rathbun et al.,
041   2024; Wang et al., 2021) study these attacks assuming the adversary can arbitrarily alter the agent’s
042   rewards. We demonstrate how defenders can easily detect these attacks during training without any
043   strong assumptions about the underlying task. We then show how clipping the adversary’s reward
044   perturbations to evade detection severely diminishes these attacks’ capabilities.
045   These shortcomings necessitate the development of a new backdoor poisoning approach capable of
046   guaranteeing the adversary’s success while operating under a restricted reward poisoning setting. To
047   this end we provide multiple contributions towards answering the question: Can backdoor attacks
048   be successful in DRL without arbitrarily manipulating rewards? Specifically we:
049
050         1. Highlight the detectability and theoretical limitations of prior attacks in the bounded reward
051            poisoning setting using intuitive examples.
052         2. Formulate the adversarial inception attack framework (visualized in Figure 1) which uses
053            novel action manipulation techniques to guarantee backdoor attack success while maintain-
               ing the victim agent’s performance in their intended task.

            3. Develop a novel backdoor poisoning attack “Q-Incept” leveraging adversarial inception and
055            DQN based techniques to achieve significant increases in attack success over prior attacks.

            4. Provide in-depth evaluation of Q-Incept on environments spanning video game playing,

               cyber network defending, simplified self driving, and safety-aware navigating tasks.

075   2   R ELATED W ORK AND BACKGROUND
076
077   Here we provide an overview of the existing literature of backdoor attacks against DRL. When
078   executing a backdoor attack the adversary manipulates the Markov Decision Process (MDP) which
079   an agent is being trained to optimize. These MDPs are often defined as M = (S, A, R, T, γ) where
080   S is the set of states in the environment, A is the set of possible actions for the agent to take,
081   R : S × A × S → R is the reward function, T : S × A × S → [0, 1] represents the transition
082   probabilities between states given actions, and γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount factor.
083   Backdoor attacks against DRL were first explored by Kiourti et al. (2019) whose attack, TrojDRL,
084   showed success against agents training on Atari games (Brockman et al., 2016). Mltiple other
085   works (Wang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023) have used similar
086   approaches in different domains – all statically altering the agent’s reward to a fixed ±c, and many
087   forcing the agent to take the targeted action a+ during training. Rathbun et al. (2024) then proved
088   the insufficiency of these static reward poisoning approaches – motivating their unbounded reward
089   poisoning attack, SleeperNets, with strong guarantees of attack success. Despite these attacks’ suc-
090
      cesses, they require large reward perturbations – resulting in detection by defenders scanning for
      outliers (Section 3.2). In this work we propose adversarial inception attacks which induce far smaller
091
      reward alterations, evading detection while retaining theoretical guarantees of attack success. Paral-
092
      lel to the study of training time attacks, many works have studied the effects of test time attacks in
093   RL (Gleave et al., 2019), such as those who study test time action manipulation attacks (Tessler et al.,
094   2019; Liang et al., 2023; McMahan et al., 2024; Franzmeyer et al., 2022). Here the agent’s observa-
095   tions or actions are assumed to be directly compromised at test time, inducing sub-optimal behavior
096   from the otherwise fixed policy. This contrasts with training time attacks where the adversary needs
097   to understand how their poisoning impacts both the immediate behavior of the agent and their learn-
098   ing algorithm. Some works have also studied the detectability of attacks at test time (Nasvytis et al.,
099   2024) including the sanitization of backdoor policies (Bharti et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). How-
100   ever these backdoor defenses rely on structural assumptions about prior attacks like TrojDRL. In this
101
      work we propose a new class of backdoor attack which has yet to be studied – strongly motivating
      the development of new defense techniques to detect the adversary or mitigate their effects.

107   victim attempts to train an agent on benign MDP M = (S, A, R, T, γ) with some stochastic algo-
      rithm L(M ) returning policy π : S × A → [0, 1]. Here we define π in terms of a superset S (e.g.

111   adversarial behavior in the agent upon observing a pre-determined trigger embedded into states by
112   δ. Here δ : S → S is defined as a function which applies a fixed trigger to a given input state (e.g.

119   3.1   A DVERSARIAL O BJECTIVES
120
      We will be implementing “targeted attacks” (Kiourti et al., 2019) in which the desired adversarial
121
      behavior is a fixed action a+ ∈ A. This objective is the current standard for backdoor attacks in DRL
122
      as it gives the adversary direct control over the agent – inducing predictable actions irrespective of
123   the consequences or current state. Thus the adversary’s objective is to induce the agent to learn a
124   poisoned policy π + which takes action a+ with high probability when observing the trigger:
125

127
      The attack must also be stealthy, however, requiring the attacker to minimize the likelihood that
128   the attack is detected while maximizing the chances that the agent is deployed in the real world.
129   The most relevant definition of stealth can vary largely depending on the application domain, so
130   in this work we will be using the most established and well defined notion of attack stealth in the
131   literature (Rathbun et al., 2024; Kiourti et al., 2019) defined below:
132

133

191   3.3   T HREAT M ODEL
192
      In this work we will be using SleepeNets’ “outer-loop” threat model due to its increased versatility
193
      for over the prior “inner-loop” attacks like TrojDRL. This model assumes an adversary with access
194
      to the agent’s training data – either through a direct system intrusion (which is unfortunately not un-
195   common Hylender et al. (2024)), malicious 3rd party training software, or malicious cloud training
196   services Gu et al. (2017) for online RL, or though database compromises for offline RL. This level
197   of access is shared with the existing literature (Kiourti et al., 2019; Rathbun et al., 2024; Cui et al.,
198   2023). Under this threat model, our adversary can observe episodes H = {(s, a, r)t }µt=1 of size µ
199   completed by the agent in M . The adversary can then alter states st , actions at , and rewards rt stored
200   in the trajectory before the agent uses them in their policy optimization. We note that here, unlike
201   the action manipulation implemented in TrojDRl, our adversary changes actions after the episode
202   has finished meaning these new actions will not actually occur in the environment. This makes our
203
      attack’s action manipulation much stealthier as the relationship between states and actions would
      need to be well defined and analyzed for any manipulation to be detected.
204
205   The adversary is also constrained by a poisoning rate parameter β which bounds the proportion of
206   total training time steps in which the adversary can insert the trigger into the agent’s current state.
207   These constraints are standard throughout the poisoning literature in machine learning (Jagielski
208   et al., 2021). In DRL β acts similar to a hyper parameter for the adversary. At lower values the
209
      adversary poisons less time steps, allowing the agent to more easily optimize the benign MDP, but
      potentially decreasing the attack’s success rate. At higher values the adversary poisons more time
210
      steps, likely leading to an increase in attack success rate, but potentially decreasing the agent’s per-
211
      formance in the benign task – directly reducing the adversary’s attack stealth objective in Equation 2.

      4.2   A SSUMPTIONS
262
263   Before formulating Adversarial Inception there are a two assumptions, shared by SleeperNets, we
264   make to ensure our theoretical results are maintained. First, we assume that Sp and S are disjoint.
265   This ensures that any reward or action manipulation which occurs in a poisoned state does not
266   conflict with an equivalent benign state. This assumption is reasonable in practice as the trigger can
267   be designed to be distinct relative to the agent’s usual states. We additionally assume that the trigger
268   function δ forms a bijection between S and Sp , allowing us to invert the function to retrieve the
269   benign state s = δ −1 (sp ). In practice this assumption is unnecessary as the adversary observes s
      first and then applies the trigger sp = δ(s), thus they never need to compute an inversion of δ.

354   ϕ it’s unclear how this will impact the dynamics of the MDP and thus the optimal policy. Therefore
355   we proceed progressively towards our proof of Theorem 2 via Lemma 1 and Lemma 2. One key
356   observation is that, if a policy π ∗ is optimal, then ϕ does not impact the agent’s chosen actions. Thus
357   Lemma 1 is the result. Next is the observation that ϕ only ever increases the value of a policy since
358   it forces the MDP to transition optimally. Thus Lemma 2 follows.

527   7 C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528   In this paper we provide multiple contributions towards a deeper understanding of backdoor poi-
529   soning attacks against DRL algorithms. We demonstrate how prior attacks are detectable and how
530   attempts to evade detection via reward clipping results in attack failure. We then propose Adversarial
531
      Inception as a novel framework for backdoor poisoning attacks against DRL under bounded reward
      perturbation constraints. We first theoretically motivate this framework, proving its optimality in
532
      guaranteeing attack success and attack stealth. We then develop an approximate adversarial incep-
533
      tion attack, Q-Incept, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on different environments from
534   different domains, while remaining stealthy. This novel threat necessitates future research into tech-
535   niques for detecting and mitigating adversarial inception attacks along with further explorations into
536   the capabilities of increasingly realistic and stealthy threat models and attack formulations. There
537   are currently no existing defenses that are immediately applicable to the unique threat of adversarial
538   inception attacks. It might be possible that prior, generalized defenses such as BIRD Chen et al.
539   (2024) can be adapted to detect models poisoned by Q-Incept, but such modifications are non-trivial
      and outside the scope of this paper.

      8   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      In this paper we develop a new class of training-time, backdoor poisoning attacks against deep re-
543   inforcement learning agents. Similar to any adversarial attack paper, it is possible that a sufficiently
544   capable adversary can replicate our methodology to implement a real-world attack against a DRL
545   system. Through highlighting this threat we hope that future practitioners of DRL will begin de-
546   veloping counter-measures against inception attacks to mitigate their real-world impact. We also
547   hope that future researchers study these attacks from a defender’s perspective to find ways to detect
548   them at training or testing time, preventing damage from occurring. From a practical and immedi-
549   ate stand-point, we believe that DRL practitioners should take steps to isolate their DRL training
550   systems such that adversarial access is exceedingly difficult.

1104   Q-function approximator Q̂. These parameters are borrowed directly from DQN as the attack de-

       A.4     F URTHER D ISCUSSION
1287
       In this section we provide further discussion on design choices made in this paper which were unable
1288   to fit in the main body.
1289
1290
       A.4.1    M OTIVATION FOR BASELINES
1291
1292   As mentioned in Section 6 we compare our Q-Incept attack against SleeperNets and TrojDRL as
1293   they represent the current state of the art for ubounded reward poisoning and forced action manipu-
1294   lation attacks respectively. For SleeperNets there are no other existing, ubounded reward poisoning
1295   attacks, so this decision is fairly clear. For TrojDRL there are other attacks which utilize static
       reward poisoning and forced action manipulation, however most only apply to specific application


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       domains like competitive, multi-agent RL (Wang et al., 2021) or partially-observable settings utiliz-
1297   ing recurrent neural networks (Yang et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022).
1298
1299   The only other, somewhat comparable attack is BadRL (Cui et al., 2023) which builds upon TrojDRL
1300
       by optimizing the adversary’s trigger pattern to achieve greater attack success. Trigger optimization
       is effective but orthogonal to the goals of this work as it can be generically applied to any attack. Fur-
1301
       thermore, Rathbun et al. (2024) showed that BadRL without this trigger fine-tuning often performs
1302
       worse than TrojDRL, likely since it uses methods to poison the most important – and thus hardest
1303   to poison – states in the MDP. Taking all of this into consideration we decided to omit BadRL from
1304   our empirical study. Therefore TrojDRL is the best baseline to use when comparing against static
1305   reward poisoning attacks using forced action manipulation.
1306
1307   A.4.2    M OTIVATION FOR E NVIRONMENTS
1308
1309   In this paper we study 4 environments – Q*Bert, Frogger, Safety Car, CAGE-2, and Highway Merge.
1310
       In the TrojDRL paper the authors focused their empirical studies towards Atari game tasks in the
       gym API Brockman et al. (2016). We think it is useful to include some of these environments like
1311
       Q*Bert, as they are standard baselines for RL in general, however we believe it is critical to extend
1312
       this study to further domains when studying the potential impacts of adversarial attacks. This belief
1313   is supported by the findings of Rathbun et al. (2024) who showed that TrojDRL – which consistently
1314   attains near 100% ASR on Atari environments without bounded reward poisoning constraints – often
1315   fails to achieve high ASR when tested on non-Atari environments.
1316
       Thus, to extend our study beyond the confines of Atari, we chose three other environments within
1317
       the gymnasium API, allowing our code to work seamlessly between environments. We first chose
1318
       Highway Merge since it seemed to be the most difficult environment for attacks to poison based
1319   upon the results of SleeperNets. Next we chose CAGE-2 as it not only represented a safety and
1320   security-critical domain, being an application of RL to cyber-network defense, but also because it
1321   uses non-image observations. Lastly we selected Safety Car, also from the environments studied in
1322   SleeperNets, as it represents a simulation of real-world, robotic applications of RL and, similar to
1323   CAGE-2, uses non-image observations.
