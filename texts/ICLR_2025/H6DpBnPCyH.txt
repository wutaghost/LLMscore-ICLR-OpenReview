009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mean field type games (MFTGs) describe Nash equilibria between large coali-
012            tions: each coalition consists of a continuum of cooperative agents who maximize
013            the average reward of their coalition while interacting non-cooperatively with a
014            finite number of other coalitions. Although the theory has been extensively de-
015            veloped, we are still lacking efficient and scalable computational methods. Here,
016            we develop reinforcement learning methods for such games in a finite space set-
017            ting with general dynamics and reward functions. We start by proving that MFTG
018
               solution yields approximate Nash equilibria in finite-size coalition games. We
               then propose two algorithms. The first is based on quantization of mean-field
019
               spaces and Nash Q-learning. We provide convergence and stability analysis. We
020
               then propose a deep reinforcement learning algorithm, which can scale to larger
021            spaces. Numerical experiments in 5 environments with mean-field distributions of
022            dimension up to 200 show the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Game theory has found a large number of applications, from economics and finance to biology and
028   epidemiology. The most common notion of solution is the concept of Nash equilibrium, in which
029   no agent has any incentive to deviate unilaterally (Nash, 1951). At the other end of the spectrum is
030   the concept of social optimum, in which the agents cooperate to maximize a total reward over the
031   population. These notions have been extensively studied for finite-player games, see e.g. (Fudenberg
032   & Tirole, 1991). Computing exactly Nash equilibria in games with a large number of players is
033
      known to be a very challenging problem (Daskalakis et al., 2009).
034   To address this challenge, the concept of mean field games (MFGs) has been introduced in (Lasry
035   & Lions, 2007; Huang et al., 2006), relying on intuitions from statistical physics. The main idea is
036   to consider an infinite population of agents, replacing the finite population with a probability distri-
037   bution, and to study the interactions between one representative player with this distribution. Under
038   suitable conditions, the solution to an MFG provides an approximate Nash equilibrium for the cor-
039
      responding finite-player game. While MFGs typically focus on the solution concept of Nash equi-
      librium, mean field control (MFC) problems focus on the solution concept of social optimum (Ben-
040
      soussan et al., 2013). The theory of these two types of problems has been extensively developed, in
041
      particular using tools from stochastic analysis and partial differential equations, see e.g. (Bensoussan
042   et al., 2013; Gomes & Saúde, 2014; Carmona & Delarue, 2018) for more details.
043
044   However, many real-world situations involve agents that are not purely cooperative or purely non-
045
      cooperative. In many scenarios, the agents form coalitions: they cooperate with agents of the same
      group and compete with other agents of other groups. In the limit where the number of agents is
046
      infinite while the number of coalitions remains finite, this leads to the concept of mean-field type
047
      games (MFTGs) (Tembine, 2017). Various applications have been developed, such as blockchain
048   token economics (Barreiro-Gomez & Tembine, 2019), risk-sensitive control (Tembine, 2015) or
049   more broadly in engineering (Barreiro-Gomez & Tembine, 2021). Similar problems have been
050   studied under the terminology of mean field games among teams (Subramanian et al., 2023) and
051   team-against-team mean field problems (Sanjari et al., 2023; Yüksel & Başar, 2024). The case of
052   zero-sum MFTG has received special interest (Başar & Moon, 2021; Cosso & Pham, 2019; Guan
053   et al., 2024), but the framework of MFTGs also covers general sum games with more than two
      (mean-field) coalitions. MFTGs are different from MFGs because the agents are cooperative within

054
      coalitions, while MFGs are about purely non-cooperative agents. They are also different from MFC
055   problems, in which the agents are purely cooperative. As a consequence, computational methods
056   and learning algorithms for MFGs and MFC problems cannot be applied to compute Nash equilibria
057   between mean-field coalitions in MFTGs. Last, graphon games (Caines & Huang, 2019) and mixed
058   mean field control games (Angiuli et al., 2023a) correspond to limit scenarios with infinitely many
059   mean-field groups. In such games, each player has a negligible impact on the rest of the population,
060   which is not the case in MFTGs, see (Tembine, 2017), so new methods are required for MFTGs.
061   Inspired by the recent successes of RL in two-player games such as Go (Silver et al., 2016) and
062   poker (Brown et al., 2020), RL methods have been adapted to solve MFGs and MFC problems,
063   see e.g. (Subramanian & Mahajan, 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Elie et al., 2020; Cui & Koeppl, 2021)
064   and (Gu et al., 2021; Carmona et al., 2023; Angiuli et al., 2023b) respectively, among many other ref-
065   erences. We refer to (Laurière et al., 2022) and the references therein for more details. Such methods
066   compute the solutions to mean field problems. A related topic is mean field multi-agent reinforce-
067   ment learning (MFMARL) (Yang et al., 2018), which studies finite-agent systems and replaces the
068   interactions between agents with the mean of neighboring agents’ states and actions. Extensions
069
      include situations with multiple types and partial observation (Ganapathi Subramanian et al., 2020;
      2021). However, the MFMARL setting differs substantially from MFTGs: (1) it does not take into
070
      account a general dependence on the mean field (i.e., the whole population distribution), (2) it aims
071
      directly for the finite-agent problem while using a mean-field approximation in an empirical way,
072   and (3) it is not designed to tackle Nash equilibria between coalitions. The works most related to
073   ours applied RL to continuous space linear-quadratic MFTGs by exploiting the specific structure of
074   the equilibrium policy in these games (Carmona et al., 2020; uz Zaman et al., 2024; Zaman et al.,
075   2024). In these settings, policies can be represented exactly with a small number of parameters. In
076   contrast, we focus on finite space MFTGs with general dynamics and reward functions, for which
077   there has been no RL algorithm thus far to the best of our knowledge.
078   Main contributions. Our main contributions are as follows:
079
      1. We prove that solving an MFTG provides an ϵ-Nash equilibrium for a game between finite-size
080      coalitions (Theorem 2.4), which justifies studying MFTGs for finite-player applications.
081   2. We propose a tabular RL method based on quantization of the mean-field spaces and Nash Q-
082      learning (Hu & Wellman, 2003). We prove the convergence of this algorithm, analyzing the error
083      due to the discretization (Theorem 3.2).
084   3. We propose a deep RL algorithm based on DDPG (Lillicrap et al., 2016) which does not require
085      quantization and hence is more scalable to problems with a large number of states.
086   4. We illustrate both methods in 5 environments with distribution in dimension up to 200. Since
087      this paper is the first to propose RL algorithms for (finite space) MFTGs with general dynamics
088
         and rewards, there is no standard baseline to compare with. We thus carry out a comparison with
         two baselines inspired by independent learning.
089
090   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we define the finite-agent problem with
091   coalitions and then its mean-field limit, and establish their connection. We then reformulate the
092   MFTG problem in the language of mean field MDPs. In Section 3, we present an algorithm based
093
      on the idea of Nash Q-learning, and we analyze it. In Section 4, we present our deep RL algorithm
      for MFTG, without discretization of the mean-field spaces. Numerical experiments are provided in
094
      Section 5. Section 6 is dedicated to a summary and a discussion. The appendices contain proofs and
095
      additional numerical results.

273   In this section, we present an adaptation of the celebrated Nash Q-learning of Hu & Wellman (2003)
274   to solve MFTG. It should be noted that the original Nash Q-learning algorithm Hu & Wellman (2003)
275
      is for finite state and action spaces and to the best of our knowledge, extensions to continuous spaces
      have been proposed only in special cases, such as Vamvoudakis (2015); Casgrain et al. (2022), but
276
      there is no extension to continuous spaces for general games that could be applied to MFTGs. The
277
      main difficulty is the computation of the solution to the stage-game at each iteration, which relies on
278   the fact that the action space is finite. So this algorithm cannot be applied directly to solve MFTGs.
279
280   In order to implement this method using tabular RL, we will start by discretizing the simplexes
281
      following the idea in Carmona et al. (2023). This allows us to analyze the algorithm fully. However,
      this approach is not scalable in terms of the number of states, which is why in Section 4, we will
282
      present a deep RL method that does not require simplex discretization.

373   4     D EEP RL FOR MFTG
374
375   While the above extension of the NashQ learning algorithm has the advantage of being fully analyz-
376   able and enjoying convergence guarantees, it is not scalable to large state and action spaces. Indeed,
377   it requires discretizing the simplexes of distributions on states and actions. The number of points
      increases exponentially in the number of states and actions, which makes the algorithm intractable


                                                                      7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      for very fine discretizations. Furthermore, each step relies on solving a stage game and computing a
379   Nash equilibrium is a difficult task for large games, even they are static.
380
381   For this reason, we now present a deep RL algorithm whose main advantages are that it does not
382
      require discretizing the simplexes and does not require solving any stage-game. The state and ac-
      tion distributions are represented as vectors (containing the probability mass functions) and passed
383
      as inputs to neural networks for the policies and the value functions. At the level of the central
384
      player for coalition i, one action is an element āi ∈ Āi . Although it corresponds to a mixed pol-
385   icy at the level of the individual agent, it represents one action for the central player. We focus on
386   learning deterministic central policies, meaning functions that map a mean-field state s̄ to a mean-
387   field action āi . To this end, we use a variant of the deep deterministic policy gradient algorithm
388   (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al., 2016), as shown in Algo. 2 in Appx. D. Our algorithm substantially differs
389   from the DDPG algorithm as the two players’ behaviors are coupled. Each player interacts with
390   a dynamic environment that the other player also influences. Unlike the tabular Nash Q-learning
391   algorithm, it is generally difficult to have a rigorous proof of convergence due to the complexity of
392   deep neural networks. Although the theoretical convergence of some algorithms has been studied,
393
      such as deep Q-learning (Fan et al., 2020), deterministic policy gradient (Xiong et al., 2022), and
      actor-critic algorithms with multi-layer neural networks (Tian et al., 2024), to the best of our knowl-
394
      edge, the convergence of DDPG under assumptions that could be applied to our setting has not been
395
      established. Also, in the case of MFTGs, we would need to analyze the convergence to a Nash
396   equilibrium, which is more complex than the solution to an MDP. Therefore, we leave for future
397   work the theoretical analysis and focus on numerical analysis: we use several numerical metrics to
398   measure the performance of DDPG-MFTG Algo. 2, as detailed in the next section.

402
403   Metrics. To assess the convergence of our algorithms, we use several metrics. First, we check the
404   testing rewards of each central player (i.e., the total reward for each coalition, averaged over the
405   testing set of initial distributions). But this is not sufficient to show that the policies form a Nash
406   equilibrium of the MFTG. For this, we compute the exploitability. This requires training a best
407
      response (BR) policy for each player independently, which is also done with deep RL, using the
      DDPG method. Last, we also check the evolution of the distributions to make sure that they match
408
      what we expect to happen in the Nash equilibrium. The pseudo-codes for evaluating a policy profile
409
      and computing the exploitability are respectively provided in Algs. 4 and 5 in Appx. E.
410   Training and testing sets. The training set consists of randomly generated tuples of distributions,
411   and each element of the tuple represents the initial distribution of a player. The testing set consists
412   of a finite number of tuples of distributions that are not in the training set. Details of the training and
413   testing sets are described case by case.
414   Baseline. To the best of our knowledge, there are no RL algorithms that can be applied to the
415   type of MFTG problems we study here. In the absence of standard baselines, we will use two
416   types of baselines, for each of our algorithms. For small-scale examples, we discretize the mean-
417   field state and action spaces and employ DNashQ-MFTG. Here, we use as a baseline an algorithm
418
      where each coalition runs an independent mean field type Q-learning (after suitable discretization

419
      for short). For larger scale examples with many states, we use the DDPG-based methods described
420
      in the previous section. In this case, we use as a baseline an ablated DDPG method in which each
421   central player can only see her own (mean-field) state. For both our algorithms and the baselines,

      Summary. In this work, we made both theoretical and numerical contributions. First, we proved
521
      that the Nash equilibrium for a mean-field type game provides an approximate Nash equilibrium
522   for a game between coalitions of finitely many agents, and we obtained a rate of convergence. We
523   then proposed the first (to our knowledge) value-based RL methods for MFTGs: a tabular RL and a
524   deep RL algorithm. We applied them to several MFTGs. Our proposed methods provide a way to
525   approximately compute the Nash equilibrium of finite number players, which is known to be hard
526   to solve numerically. We proved the convergence of the tabular algorithm, and through extensive
527   experiments, we illustrated the scalability of the deep RL method.
528   Related works. Carmona et al. (2020); uz Zaman et al. (2024); Zaman et al. (2024) studied RL for
529   MFTGs of LQ form only, with specific methods when the policy is deterministic and linear, while
530   our algorithms are for generic MFTGs with discrete spaces. (Motte & Pham, 2022; Carmona et al.,
531   2023) focused on single MFMDPs while we consider a game between MFMDPs. Subramanian &
532   Mahajan (2019); Guo et al. (2019); Elie et al. (2020); Cui & Koeppl (2021) propose RL for MFGs
533   but are limited to population-independent policies. Perrin et al. (2022) studied population-dependent
534   policies, but only for MFGs, in which players are infinitesimal; their method cannot solve MFTG
535   because each player has a macroscopic impact on the other groups.
536   Limitations and future directions. We did not provide proof of convergence for the deep RL algo-
537   rithm due to the difficulties related to analyzing deep neural networks and because we aim for Nash
538   equilibria and not just MDPs. Furthermore, we would like to apply our algorithms to more realistic
539   examples and investigate further the difference with the baseline. We are also interested in applying
      other deep RL algorithms and seeing their performance in MFTGs of increasing complexities.
