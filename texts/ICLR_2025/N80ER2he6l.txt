011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision-language instruct-tuning models have recently made significant progress
014            due to their more comprehensive understanding of the world. In this work, we
015            discover that large-scale 3D parallel training on those models leads to an im-
016
               balanced computation load across different devices. The vision and language
               parts are inherently heterogeneous: their data distribution and model architec-
017
               ture differ significantly, which affects distributed training efficiency. To address
018
               this issue, we rebalance the computational load from data, model, and memory
019            perspectives, achieving more balanced computation across devices. Specifically,
020            for the data, instances are grouped into new balanced mini-batches within and
021            across devices. A search-based method is employed for the model to achieve a
022            more balanced partitioning. For memory optimization, we adaptively adjust the
023            re-computation strategy for each partition to utilize the available memory fully.
024            These three perspectives are not independent but are closely connected, form-
025            ing an omniverse balanced training framework. extensive experiments are con-
026            ducted to validate the effectiveness of our method. Compared with the open-
027
               source training code of InternVL-Chat, training time is reduced greatly, achiev-
               ing about 1.8x speed-up. Our method’s efficacy and generalizability are fur-
028
               ther validated across various models and datasets. Codes will be released at
029
               https://github.com/anonymousiclr293/omnibal example.

034
      Large language models (LLM) have brought new possibilities to many fields. Multi-modal models,
035
      particularly Vision-Language Models (VLMs) Alayrac et al. (2022); Team et al. (2023a); Reid et al.
036   (2024); Liu et al. (2023a); Bai et al. (2023b); Chen et al. (2023), are advancing rapidly due to
037   their deeper understanding of the world. The training scale of Vision-Language Models (VLMs)
038   continues to expand, with increasingly larger datasets incorporating more text and higher-resolution
039   images. Compared with the LLaVA-1.5 Liu et al. (2023a), the InternVL-Chat Chen et al. (2024)
040   has expanded the dataset size from 665K to 5M and increased image resolution from 336x336 to
041   3840x2160. At the model level, larger vision encoders are adopted. The InternVL-Chat upgrades
042   the visual encoder from ∼300M ViT-L-336px Radford et al. (2021) to ∼6B InternViT-448px Chen
043   et al. (2023). The larger datasets and models result in a more time-consuming training process.
044

046   work for large-scale distributed training, which allows data and models to be distributed across
047   multiple devices. Balancing computational load across devices is crucial in 3D parallelism by mini-

      uneven computational load and computational bubbles. (3) Input size variation and computational

126   2.2   L ARGE -S CALE D ISTRIBUTED T RAINING
127
      Distributed training is essential for efficiently utilizing multiple GPUs to train large language mod-
128
      els. It is achieved through 3D parallelism Shoeybi et al. (2019); Rajbhandari et al. (2020); mi-
129
      crosoft (2020): data, tensor, and pipeline parallelism. Data Parallelism splits the entire dataset
130   into mini-batches and assigns them to multiple devices, each with a model replica. This approach
131   maximizes the use of GPU power for large datasets. DeepSpeed Zero Rajbhandari et al. (2020)
132   enhances it by reducing weight redundancy. However, it can still be challenged by the memory
133   limits of individual devices when handling huge models. Tensor Parallelism distributes a model’s
134   weight matrices across multiple devices, enabling parallel matrix operations Shoeybi et al. (2019)
135   and reducing per-device memory requirements. This method accelerates computation but requires
136   dense inter-device communication, typically restricted to single-node deployments to minimize la-
137   tency. Pipeline Parallelism divides a model into segments and assigns them to different devices,
138
      creating a computation flow like a production line. This technique facilitates larger model scaling
      across nodes. GPipe Huang et al. (2019) proposes micro-batching to decrease forward bubbles.
139
      PipeDream Narayanan et al. (2019) further proposes a one-forward-one-backward (1F1B) scheme
140
      to optimize memory usage. In pipeline parallelism, uneven layer partitioning can cause significant
141   pipeline bubbles. PipeDream Narayanan et al. (2019) and AdaPipe Sun et al. (2024) optimize model
142   partitioning and re-computation strategies based on profiling and dynamic programming, respec-
143   tively. However, these advancements are primarily tested in text-based models and may require
144   adaptation for large vision language model scenarios.

149

150   ance. Inter-Stage means the computation imbalance of different pipeline parallel stages. Intra-Stage
151   indicates the computation imbalance of the same stage across time and device. Figure 2 shows these
152   two computation imbalances more intuitively. And they both include three specific levels: data,
153   model, and memory. To quantify this problem, we used the InternVL-Chat-1.2 dataset Chen et al.
154   (2024) to perform profile statistics shown in Tabel 1. For the Intra-Stage, we counted the information
155   of Stage 1 as a sample.

158   data integrity, and preventing arbitrary truncation. The varying number of images, resolutions, and
159   text lengths result in considerable differences in input sizes across mini-batches. From Tabel 1 and
160   Figure 2, data imbalance occurs in Inter-Stage and Intra-Stage. To better quantify the impact of
161   dynamic input, we define the DistRatio (introduced in Section 4) to measure the degree of data

185   Model Imbalance: LLMs use identical transformer modules with the same computational load.
186   Evenly dividing these layers in pipeline parallelism distributes the load effectively. However, VLMs
187   require additional image pre-processing, necessitating an image encoder. The structural disparity
188   between VIT and LLM results in different computational demands. As shown by Tabel 1 and Figure
189   2, the standard deviation of forward time is huge in both Inter-Stage and Intra-Stage, indicating a
190   serious computation imbalance.
191   Memory Imbalance: LLMs require significant GPU memory due to their large parameter size.
192   When memory is insufficient, re-computation Li et al. (2014) techniques discard some intermediate
193   activation values and recompute them during backward propagation to save memory. VLM encoun-
194   ters great memory challenges due to the variable scales of data inputs and the heterogeneity between
195   vision and language models. The presence of numerous images or long text inputs can lead to ex-
196   cessive GPU memory usage, requiring the most aggressive re-computation settings to prevent the
197   program from crashing. However, excessive re-computation can slow down the training process.
198   From Table 1 and Figure 2, under the existing training setting, memory imbalance is reflected in
199
      both Inter-Stage and Intra-Stage.
200   Differences between VLM and LLM training: As mentioned above, the difference between VLM
201   and LLM arises from the data composition and model structure, resulting in unique Inter-Stage and
202   Intra-Stage challenges. Inter-Stage: Since LLM has a fixed structure, the model can be equally di-
203   vided and there is no Inter-Stage imbalance for any input. Dynamic input or inconsistent text-image
204   ratios in heterogeneous VLM will lead to an Inter-Stage imbalance problem. Intra-Stage: For the
205
      LLM-Pretrain task, the input is fixed and there is no Intra-Stage imbalance problem. Dynamic input
      can be converted into static input by simple packing Kosec et al. (2021) to reduce the computation
206
      imbalance for the LLM-SFT task. However, VLM instruct-tuning training cannot rely on simple
207
      packing to ensure fixed inputs for VIT and LLM, resulting in computation imbalance problems.

      4.3   BALANCED A DAPTIVE R E -C OMPUTATION
325
326   Thanks to the balanced dynamic mini-batch and balanced model partition, a balanced computational
327   load is maintained across each pipeline stage. The memory requirements are now stabilized as the
328   computational demand has been fixed. As a result, we can optimize the re-computation strategy
329   based on actual memory needs, rather than relying on the most aggressive approach to avoid crashes.
330   Reducing the number of re-computations accelerates the model’s backward pass, leading to a great
331   improvement in training speed.
332   We find that heterogeneous architectures have different memory requirements. For example, the
333   vision model in InternVL-Chat-1.5 requires more GPU memory than the language model under
334   the same computational load. Therefore, it is necessary to analyze the memory requirements of
335   each layer in the vision and language models individually and adaptively determine the optimal re-
336   computation strategy for each layer. Specifically, we start by profiling to determine the memory
337   requirements of each layer. Based on the available memory of each device, we then determine the
338
      optimal re-computation configurations in pipeline stage Si . More details are shown in A.1.1.

356   Implementation Details: We determine Qv and Qt by using statistics of datasets. First, we traverse

      the longest text token in the dataset and use the calculated text-to-image ratio to determine Qv . For
