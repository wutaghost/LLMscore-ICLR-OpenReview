009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-shot segmentation has achieved great success by generating features from
012            semantic embeddings to adapt the model to unseen classes. These semantic-
013            generated features are typically aligned with the visual distribution of seen classes
014            to improve generalization on extracted image features. However, this vision-
015            centric alignment may easily overfit seen classes due to the lack of visual data
016
               for unseen classes. To address this issue, we propose a semantic-centric align-
               ment method that aligns the generated features with the well-structured semantic
017
               distribution across all classes. First, we align the vision backbone features with
018
               CLIP tokens through Vision-to-CLIP alignment. This approach leverages CLIP’s
019            visual-language matching capabilities to produce semantic-aligned backbone fea-
020            tures. Then, we generate synthetic features from semantic embeddings for unseen
021            classes, supervised by semantic-aligned visual features and CLIP semantic tokens
022            for improving visual diversity while maintaining semantic consistency. Finally,
023            we finetune the class projector through the semantic-aligned joint features to fur-
024            ther adapt the model for unseen classes. Our semantic-centric alignment effec-
025            tively enhances the model’s zero-shot generalization by constructing a unified and
026            well-structured semantic-aligned feature space. Our method achieves SOTA per-
027
               formance in both zero-shot panoptic and semantic segmentation, and can directly
               segment unseen classes without fine-tuning.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Semantic segmentation, a critical task in computer vision, has seen remarkable success driven by
033   advances in deep learning (He et al., 2016; Vaswani et al., 2017). However, obtaining high perfor-
034   mance demands massive data with detailed pixel-level annotations, which are labor-intensive and
035   expensive to generate. This challenge has driven segmentation research into zero-shot segmentation
036   (Chen et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2020; Bucher et al., 2019).
037   Zero-shot segmentation aims to generalize the model trained on seen data to segment unseen (novel)
038   classes during inference, relying solely on textual descriptions. The primary challenge is the lack of
039   visual data for unseen classes, which leads to overfitting to the visual distribution of seen classes.
040   To alleviate this problem, existing methods typically adopt a vision-centric approach, training a
041   generator to produce synthetic visual features for adapting the mododel to unseen classes (Bucher
042   et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021b; Gu et al., 2020), as shown in Fig. 1. Formally, the generator maps
043   semantic embeddings to the visual space and aligns the generated visual features with real visual
044
      features extracted from the visual backbone.
045   However, due to the absence of visual data for unseen classes, even though the visual backbone cap-
046   tures sufficient visual attributes (Ding et al., 2022a; Han et al., 2023a), it still struggles to establish
047   a well-structured visual feature space for properly positioning unseen classes. Consequently, the
048   vision-aligned feature generator tends to bias toward seen classes when generating synthetic fea-
049   tures, hindering generalization to unseen classes. Although some methods attempt to guide visual
050
      feature generation through semantic relationships (He et al., 2023b; Han et al., 2023b), the estab-
      lished visual feature space still remains imperfect due to the modality gap. Thus, it is often subopti-
051
      mal to generalize new classes in the under-constructed visual space of vision-centric approaches.
052
053   In this paper, we propose a novel semantic-centric method to align the backbone and generator fea-
      tures with the well-structured semantic distribution, ensuring all classes are properly positioned due

085   feature generation and zero-shot generalization for unseen classes.

088

089

090

091   benchmarks. Extensive experiments and analyses demonstrate the effectiveness of our semantic-
092   centric method, proving the substantial generalization capability of the well-structured semantic-
093   aligned feature space. The semantic-centric property enables the model to directly segment unseen
094   classes in the inductive setting without fine-tuning, as the semantic-aligned backbone features effec-
095   tively match the semantic embeddings for new concepts. In summary, our key contributions are:
096
      – We propose a novel semantic-centric alignment method to align features with the well-structured
097   semantic distribution for zero-shot segmentation.
098
099   – We successfully achieve the semantic-centric alignment by collaboratively aligning the backbone
100
      features and generated features, and adapting feature projector for zero-shot generalization.
101   – Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple zero-shot panoptic and semantic
102   segmentation benchmarks, and can be flexibly integrated into powerful segmentation models.

105   2   R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   Close-set Image Segmentation. Image segmentation, a fundamental task in computer vision, in-
      volves categorizing each component of an image. It can be categorized into three types: semantic

108
      segmentation (Long et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2018), where pixels are classified into categories; in-
109   stance segmentation (He et al., 2017; Tian et al., 2020), which distinguishes individual objects or
110   instances; and panoptic segmentation (Li et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2019), a hybrid approach that
111   classifies both background pixels and foreground instances. Panoptic segmentation has garnered
112   significant interest from the research community due to its comprehensive approach that addresses
113   the challenges of both semantic and instance segmentation simultaneously.
114
      Before the wide application of transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) in com-
115   puter vision, many works (Li et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2019) treat panoptic segmentation as the
116   combination of instance segmentation and semantic segmentation. Since the proposal of DETR
117   (Carion et al., 2020), panoptic segmentation entered a new era. In a specific, first, the model is
118   replaced by self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) rather than CNN-based.
119   Moreover, the things and stuff are modeled together based on the Hungarian algorithm (Zhang et al.,
120   2021; Li et al., 2022b; Cheng et al., 2021a; 2022; Carion et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023a) rather than
121   separately processed. In this paper, the proposed method can handle both semantic and panoptic
122   segmentation in a more challenging zero-shot scenario.
123   Zero-shot classification. Zero-shot classification aims to recognize classes that are not present in the
124   training dataset. This task, characterized by its challenging nature and significant potential for real-
125   world applications, has attracted increasing attention from researchers. Common approaches utilize
126   attribute-based datasets, such as AWA (Lampert et al., 2009) and CUB (Wah et al., 2011), where
127   each category is defined by distinct attributes, e.g., seals are described as furry, big, and capable of
128   swimming (Han et al., 2020; Su et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2020; Han et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021;
129
      2020; Zhu et al., 2019). Typically, these methods begin by training a generator to synthesize visual
      features for unseen categories. Subsequently, they generate several pseudo-unseen visual features
130
      based on attributes of unseen classes. Finally, a trainable classifier (Han et al., 2020; Su et al., 2022;
131
      Wu et al., 2020; Han et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021) or a k-NN classifier (Han et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
132   2020; Zhu et al., 2019) is employed on the test dataset. Although effective, zero-shot classification
133   neglects valuable visual cues critical for segmentation tasks. Besides, the zero-shot segmentation
134   only contains the name of a category without any attribute description. While not directly applicable
135   to segmentation, the insights gained from zero-shot classification methods are inspiring.
136
      Zero-shot Segmentation. Most current zero-shot segmentation approaches can be classified into
137
      two categories: projection-based (Xian et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2022a; Zhou et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
138   2022; Chen et al., 2023; Baek et al., 2021; Zhang & Ding, 2021) and generation-based methods (He
139   et al., 2023b; Cheng et al., 2021b; Baek et al., 2021; Pastore et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2020; He et al.,
140   2023a). Projection-based methods align visual and semantic features within the same space and
141   calculate the similarity between them, assigning the category with the highest similarity score. Con-
142   versely, generation methods first train a generator to synthesize unseen visual features. A trainable
143   classifier is then developed to work with both real-seen and generated unseen visual features, ef-
144   fectively transforming zero-shot segmentation into traditional segmentation methods. Additionally,
145   recent efforts have explored open-vocabulary segmentation (Xu et al., 2023b; Ding et al., 2022b; Xu
146
      et al., 2023a; Qin et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b;
      Ma et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024), which, like zero-shot segmentation, aims
147
      to recognize unseen categories but involves training on a complete dataset, unlike the more limited
148
      data used in zero-shot approaches.
149
150   In this paper, we introduce a novel cyclic alignment method for zero-shot segmentation, adhering to
151   the generation approach. Unlike traditional generation methods, our strategy, i.e., S2V alignment,
152
      enhances the visual diversity of the generated unseen visual features, thereby capturing a more accu-
      rate visual distribution. Furthermore, while most methods do not reproject generated features back
153
      to the semantic space, potentially compromising semantic generalization and restricting category
154
      flexibility, our V2S alignment maps the generated visual features back to the semantic space and
155   aligns with the semantic features, preserving generalization capabilities.

      3.2   G ENERATION - TO -S EMANTIC ALIGNMENT FOR GENERATOR ADAPTATION
271
272   Another bottleneck in zero-shot segmentation is the ineffectiveness of the generator in utilizing se-
273   mantic information. Therefore, we propose Generation-to-Semantic alignment to encourage the gen-
274   erator to effectively utilize the semantic information. The core idea of the generation-to-semantic,
275   as shown in the top right of Fig. 2, is to map semantic features to the visual space and pass the gen-
276   erated visual features through the frozen class projector, which was trained during Vision-to-CLIP
277   alignment. In the semantic-to-vision mapping phase, we align the synthetic visual features with real
278
      ones from the frozen, semantic-aligned backbone. In the projection phase, we directly align the
      projected features with the corresponding semantic embeddings.
279
280   Generation-to-Semantic alignment consists of two key components: Generator-to-Vision (G2V)
281   alignment and Generated-Vision-to-Semantic (GV2S) alignment. Specifically, we first pass X
282   through the frozen backbone to obtain the real semantic-aligned visual features F. Then, we
283   apply σ̂ to split F into Fs ∈ RO×C , representing backbone features assigned to targets, and
284   F∅ ∈ R(Q−O)×C , representing features without assigned targets. Next, we generate the pseudo
285
      visual features F′ , as shown in Fig. 3b. Formally, Fs is fed into the VAE encoder (Kingma &
      Welling, 2013) to obtain the reparameterized output. Then, the corresponding semantic features
286
      A′s are added to the reparameterized output as semantic conditions. The result is fed into the VAE
287
      decoder to generate the pseudo visual features F′s .
288

290   conventional methods (He et al., 2023b; Bucher et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021b) to align F′s with
291
      Fs using the GMMN loss (Li et al., 2015).

339   During inference, we input the image into the backbone to extract visual features and generate
340
      predicted masks. The visual features are then passed through the fine-tuned class projector and
      the probability of each visual feature is calculated by taking the inner product between the visual
341
      features and the semantic features. Finally, we calculate the final result by applying the inner product
342
      between the probabilities and the predicted masks.

      2016) as backbone and trains for 48 epochs with the same setting as the original Mask2Former. The
359   class projector is a simple MLP, and the generator is a VAE structure consisting of four layers of

      Evaluation Metric. We follow the GZSL settings (He et al., 2023b; Ding et al., 2022a; Zhou et al.,

364
      rectly. To comprehensively consider the performance for both seen and unseen categories, we apply
365   the harmonic panoptic quality (hPQ) for panoptic segmentation and Intersections over Union (hIoU)
366   for semantic segmentation as the evaluation metric, hP Q = 2·sP Q·uP Q                2·sIoU ·uIoU
                                                                  sP Q+uP Q , and hIoU = sIoU +uIoU ,

      where sP Q and uP Q denote the panoptic quality (Kirillov et al., 2019) for the seen and unseen

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we presented a novel semantic-centric alignment approach for zero-shot segmenta-
532
      tion that addresses the limitations of vision-centric methods in handling unseen classes. By aligning
533
      generated features with a structured semantic distribution across all classes, we ensured better gener-
534   alization and reduced overfitting to seen classes. Our two-stage alignment process and the projector
535   adaptation, integrating Vision-to-CLIP alignment and generator training alignment, enriched the se-
536   mantic features with diverse visual attributes while preserving semantic consistency. This strategy
537   significantly improved the model’s capacity to handle unseen classes, achieving state-of-the-art re-
538   sults in both zero-shot panoptic and semantic segmentation tasks. Our approach demonstrates that
539   maintaining a unified semantic-aligned feature space can effectively support zero-shot segmentation
      without the need for fine-tuning.
