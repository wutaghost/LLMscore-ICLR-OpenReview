009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Hyperparameter optimization (HPO) is a crucial step in achieving strong predic-
012            tive performance, particularly for deep learning with hyperparameters controlling
013            the neural architecture and learning behavior. However, the impact of some hy-
014            perparameters on model generalization can vary significantly depending on the
015            dataset and performance measure, making it challenging to generalize their im-
016            portance. Gaining a better understanding of the importance of hyperparameters is
017            therefore important to deepen our understanding of machine learning and to lever-
018            age this knowledge in future downstream HPO tasks, especially if training is ex-
019            pensive and HPO needs to be as efficient as possible. To address these challenges,
020
               we propose a game theoretic framework based on Shapley values and interactions
               for HPO. These methods offer an additive decomposition of a performance mea-
021
               sure across hyperparameters, enabling both local and global explanations of hy-
022
               perparameter importance and interactions. Our framework, named H YPER SHAP,
023            provides insights into ablation studies, tunability of specific hyperparameter con-
024            figurations, and entire configuration spaces. Through experiments, we demon-
025            strate that focusing on the hyperparameters deemed important by our framework
026            can improve performance during subsequent hyperparameter optimization, while
027            ignoring important hyperparameters or interactions degrades performance. This
028            validates the effectiveness of our approach in enhancing model performance and
029            providing meaningful, interpretable explanations of hyperparameter importance.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Hyperparameter optimization is an indispensable step in the design process of developing machine
035
      learning applications to achieve the best possible performance for a given dataset and performance
036   measure (Bischl et al., 2023). This is particularly true for deep learning methods exposing different
037   kinds of hyperparameters for describing the neural architecture and steering the learning behavior,
038   e.g., the learning rate or weight decay (Zimmer et al., 2021). Moreover, through the advent of gener-
039   ative AI and large language models that are fine-tuned to various tasks, hyperparameter optimization
040   is key to achieving the best-possible results (Yin et al., 2021; Tribes et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023).
041
      However, different hyperparameters affect the generalization performance of the resulting model
042   differently (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Hutter et al., 2014; Zimmer et al., 2021) and, thereby, their
043   importance to be tuned. Moreover, the importance of hyperparameters that lead to an effective im-
044   provement in generalization performance can depend on the dataset and performance measure of
045   interest (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; van Rijn & Hutter, 2018). Due to this, so far, it is difficult to
046   draw general conclusions about the importance of individual hyperparameters to better understand
047   the corresponding learning algorithm. In addition, just observing an optimized hyperparameter con-
048   figuration, it is difficult to attribute the effect of individual hyperparameter values to the improvement
049   in generalization performance.
050   To address this issue, methods for eliciting hyperparameter importance have been devised, ranging
051   from local methods to determine the effect of single hyperparameter values over a default value
052   (Fawcett & Hoos, 2016; Biedenkapp et al., 2017) to global explanations performing a symbolic
053   regression (Segel et al., 2023) or a variance decomposition (Hutter et al., 2014; Watanabe et al.,
      2023; Theodorakopoulos et al., 2024). While the former can only detect main effects and ignores

149   3   R ELATED W ORK
150
151   Hyperparameter importance has garnered significant attention in machine learning as it plays a cru-
152   cial role in providing evidence for the need for tuning hyperparameters and in attributing perfor-
153
      mance improvements through hyperparameter optimization to hyperparameters (Probst et al., 2019;
      Pushak & Hoos, 2020; 2022; Schneider et al., 2022). A variety of approaches have been developed
154
      to assess how different hyperparameters affect the performance of resulting models, ranging from
155
      simple (surrogate-based) ablations (Biedenkapp et al., 2017) to sensitivity analyses and eliciting in-
156   teractions between hyperparameters based on the functional ANOVA framework (Hutter et al., 2014;
157   van Rijn & Hutter, 2018; Watanabe et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2021). Similarly, Moosbauer et al.
158   (2021) propose partial dependence plots visualizing variance across the domain of hyperparameters.
159   In this work, we propose an alternative way of quantifying the importance of hyperparameters with
160   the help of Shapley values and focus on eliciting interactions between hyperparameters with the help
161   of SHAP interaction indices. We focus on the quantification of interactions since, in previous works,
      it has been noticed that interaction is occasionally comparably low (Zimmer et al., 2021; Novello


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2023), which could serve as a foundation for a new generation of HPO methods that do not
163   assume interactions to be omnipresent.
164
165   Beyond quantifying hyperparameter importance, to better understand the impact of hyperparameters
166
      and the tuning behavior of hyperparameter optimizers, other approaches have been proposed, such
      as algorithm footprints (Smith-Miles & Tan, 2012) or deriving symbolic explanations (Segel et al.,
167
      2023), functioning as an interpretable model for estimating the performance of a learner from its
168
      hyperparameters. In this work, we focus on quantifying the impact of tuning a hyperparameter on
169   the performance.

173
174   Cooperative game theory has been widely applied in machine learning to assign contributions of en-
175
      tities, such as features or data points for a given task (Rozemberczki et al., 2022). Most prominently,
      to interpret predictions of black box models using feature attributions (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), or
176
      to quantify the value of data points (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019). Shapley Interactions (SIs) (Grabisch
177
      & Roubens, 1999) extend the Shapley Value (SV) by additionally assigning contributions to groups
178   of entities, which reveal synergies and redundancies. Feature interactions uncover additive struc-
179   tures in predictions, which are necessary to understand complex decisions (Lundberg et al., 2020;
180   Sundararajan et al., 2020; Tsai et al., 2023; Bordt & von Luxburg, 2023). The SV and SIs are de-
181   fined based on a cooperative game comprising n players N = {1, . . . , n} and ν : 2N → R as a
182   real-valued set function on the powerset 2N . This game captures a joint payout ν(S) obtained from
183   a set of players S ⊆ N forming a coalition. In this section, we assume ν(∅) = 0 for readability,
184   which does not affect the SV and SIs due to the dummy axiom (introduced below). Given ν, the
185   SV (Shapley, 1953) is the fair contribution of an individual entity to the overall payout ν(N ). The
186
      SV is uniquely characterized by four intuitive axioms: linearity (contributions are linear for linear
      combination of games), symmetry (players with equal contributions obtain equal payout), dummy
187
      (players that do not change the payout receive zero payout), and efficiency (the sum of all payouts
188
      equals the joint payout). The SV ϕSV (i) of player i ∈ N can be computed as a weighted average

238   In hyperparameter optimization, a wide variety of questions can be asked for explanations, ranging
239   from individual suggested values in a returned hyperparameter configuration to complex reason-
240   ing during the optimization process or the description of remaining optimization potentials. Hence,
241   explanations may be needed on different levels of the hyperparameter optimization process, rang-
242   ing from returned configurations to a qualitative comparison of entire hyperparameter optimization
243
      tools. In the following, we will limit ourselves to three areas, dubbed Ablation, Tunability, and
      Optimizer Bias, which we describe in more detail in Sections 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3, respectively.

      5.1   A BLATION BETWEEN TWO HYPERPARAMETER CONFIGURATIONS
247
      One common scenario for quantifying the importance of hyperparameters is to compare a hyper-
248   parameter configuration (HPC) λ∗ of interest to some reference HPC λ0 , e.g., the default param-
249   eterization of a learner as provided by its implementing library or a tuned default HPC that has
250   proven effective for past tasks. In turn, λ∗ can be an HPC returned by a hyperparameter optimizer
251   or a manually configured HPC. Given λ∗ and λ0 , the question now is how values of λ∗ affect the
252   performance of the learner relative to the reference HPC λ0 . To this end, we can transition from
253   the reference HPC to the HPC of interest by switching the values of hyperparameters one by one
254   from its value in λ0 to the value in λ∗ , which is also done in empirical machine learning studies and
255   referred to as ablations.
256   While a similar approach has already been followed by Fawcett & Hoos (2016) and Biedenkapp
257   et al. (2017), it fails to quantify interactions between hyperparameters. However, with the help of
258   SVs and SIs, we can overcome this limitation and define the hyperparameter importance game of
259   Ablation as follows.

      6.1                                I NTERACTIONS IN H YPERPARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
410
411
412   Using H YPER SHAP, we analyze the interactions at different levels of hyperparameter optimization,
413   demonstrating that while higher-order interactions are present, they can be effectively captured with
414
      second-order explanations. The interaction quantification is summarized in Figure 3. Additional
      details are provided in Appendix C. For each HPI game — Ablation, Data-Specific Tunability, and
415
      Tunability — we determine the magnitude of interactions by measuring the MIs for each order (see
416
      Figure 3, left). The MIs show that all levels of hyperparameter optimization include both lower- and
417   higher-order interactions (with box plots above zero), and that lower-order interactions are the most
418   influential (showing the largest range for orders 1 and 2). Data-Specific Tunability exhibits the most
419   higher-order interactions, indicating that there are dataset-specific nuances.
420
      In addition, we examine how faithfully lower-order representations capture the higher-order effects
421
      and behavior of different games (see Figure 3, right). For each game setting, we compute the FSII
422
      ΦFSII
        k    with explanation order P k. We then approximate the game values for each hyperparameter
423
      subset S ⊆ N as ν̂k (S) := L⊆S:|L|≤k ΦFSII    k (L). Finally, we compare the approximated game
424
      values with the ground truth as determined by considering the complete game and calculate an
425
      R2 (ν(S), ν̂k (S)) loss weighted with the Shapley kernel (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Tsai et al., 2023).
426   This assesses how well the FSII describe the game behavior and cover higher-order interactions.
427   An R2 score of one indicates a perfect fit, meaning all interactions are covered with the given
428   explanation. Figure 3, right, displays the R2 scores all explanation orders k, and demonstrates that
429   the behavior of hyperparameter optimization games cannot be explained without interactions. The
430   SV (k = 1) yield a low R2 score, whereas second-order interactions (k = 2) already achieve a
431   near-perfect reconstruction error, outlining an interesting avenue for future research on exploiting
      this fact for efficient approximation.

448   6.2     I DENTIFYING O PTIMIZER B IAS VIA H YPERPARAMETER I MPORTANCE
449
450   In this section, we consider Data-Specific Optimizer Bias as defined in Definition 5 to demonstrate
451   how quantifying hyperparameter importance can be used to detect hyperparameter optimizer defi-
452   ciencies. To this end, we implement two HPO strategies with built-in deficiencies. First, we consider
453   a hyperparameter optimizer that optimizes every hyperparameter individually and composes the re-
454   turned configuration from the individual best hyperparameter values, i.e., it ignores all potential
455   interactions of hyperparameters. Second, we consider a hyperparameter optimizer that only opti-
456   mizes a pre-defined subset of hyperparameters, resembling HPO methods that, e.g., can only tackle
457   continuous hyperparameters and ignore categorical or integer hyperparameters. For the former, we
458
      expect the main effects of hyperparameters to be small but observe substantial negative interactions.
      The latter is expected to show small effects for the selected subset of hyperparameters and negative
459
      main effects for the rest.
460
461   As shown in Figure 4, we observe the effects expected for the two HPO strategies, validating our
462   approach. While on the left, we analyze the hyperparameter optimizer tuning every hyperparam-
463   eter independently, the main effects for the single hyperparameters are negligible (i.e., thin circles
464
      around the hyperparameters), demonstrating that the tuning of the individual hyperparameters works
      well. However, by design, the tuning behavior does not account for interactions between the hyper-
465
      parameters, which results in large negative interactions, as indicated by the bold blue edges, red
466
      edges in turn indicate missing out on negative interactions. In the middle and on the right, we vi-
467   sualize Optimizer Bias for the HPO approach limiting to certain subsets of hyperparameters (see
468   caption of the figure). We observe negligible main effects for the tuned hyperparameters, indicating
469   close to optimal performance for tuning those. However, left-out hyperparameters are showing large
470   main effects and also interactions with them are more pronounced, bringing the deficiencies of this
471   HPO method to light. Momentum (M) and Weight Decay (W-D) show little change, reflecting their
472   ineffectiveness for this dataset, and the subset tuner correctly avoids tuning them.

      7         C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
521
522
      In this paper, we proposed H YPER SHAP a comprehensive post-hoc explanation framework for
      quantifying hyperparameter importance via Shapley values and Shapley interactions on three levels:
523
      the values of hyperparameters, the tunability of hyperparameters, and the capabilities of hyperpa-
524
      rameter optimizers. Compared to previous methods accounting for interactions between hyperpa-
525   rameters, H YPER SHAP attributes contributions to the performance rather than quantifying variance.
526   While we can use H YPER SHAP to better understand the impact of hyperparameter values or tun-
527   ability of hyperparameters, we also demonstrated that this knowledge can be immediately applied to
528   downstream tasks. To this end, we showed how the anytime performance of a subsequent HPO run
529   can benefit from focusing on hyperparameters identified as important.
530
      In future work, we aim to extend the framework to the more general task of combined algorithm
531   selection and hyperparameter optimization, and the design of entire ML pipelines (Olson & Moore,
532   2016; Wever et al., 2018; Heffetz et al., 2020; Feurer et al., 2022). In contrast to plain HPO, such
533   more complex AutoML scenarios are less well studied, but with H YPER SHAP we now have a ver-
534   satile and theory-grounded approach at hand that will allow a thorough study. Furthermore, we plan
535   to develop methods for HPO that can leverage the information about the importance of hyperpa-
536   rameters. Learning hyperparameter importance across datasets could outline a promising direction
537   to warmstart hyperparameter optimizers in an interpretable way and improve their efficiency based
538   on past experience, leading to synergies with recent prior-guided HPO (Hvarfner et al., 2024) and
539
      human-centered AutoML (Lindauer et al., 2024).

      E THICAL S TATEMENT
541
542
      In conducting this research on HyperSHAP, we have carefully considered the ethical implications of
543   our work. This paper presents work with the goal to advance the field of machine learning (ML) and
544   specifically the field of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) and hyperparameter optimization
545   (HPO). There are many potential societal consequences of our work. The aim of our study is to
546   improve the transparency and interpretability of hyperparameter optimization, which is crucial for
547   building trust and accountability into hyperparameter optimization methods. Thus, our research
548   impacts a wide variety of ML application domains and therein can positively impact ML adoption
549   and potentially reveal biases or unwanted behavior in HPO systems.
550   However, we recognize that the increased explainability provided by XAI also carries ethical risks.
551   There is the potential for “explainability-based white-washing”, where organizations, firms, or in-
552   stitutions might misuse XAI to justify questionable actions or outcomes. With responsible use, XAI
553   can amplify the positive impacts of ML, ensuring its benefits are realized while minimizing harm.

      A.1   A PPROXIMATION OF ARGMAX
893
      As per Definitions 2 to 4, for every coalition S, we need to determine the arg max. However, the
894   true arg max is hard to compute, so we approximate it throughout our experiments. For the sake of
895   implementation simplicity and unbiased sampling, to this end, we use random search with a large
896   evaluation budget of 10,000 candidate evaluations. As the configurations are independently sampled,
897   for evaluating a configuration, we simply blind an initially sampled batch of 10,000 hyperparameter
898   configurations for the hyperparameters not contained in the coalition S by setting their values to the
899   default value. After blinding, the surrogate model provided by YAHPO-Gym is then queried for the
900   set of hyperparameter configurations and the maximum observed performance is returned.

      HPO methods that focus on different structural parts of the hyperparameter configuration space. For
      the hyperparameter optimization approach, tuning every hyperparameter individually, when consid-
906
      ering a hyperparameter for tuning, we sampled 50 random values for every hyperparameter. For
907
      the hyperparameter optimizer focusing on a subset of hyperparameters, we allowed for 50,000 hy-

909   optimizer and a random search with a budget of 50,000 evaluations on the full hyperparameter con-

957   C.1     M EASURING THE M AGNITUDE OF I NTERACTIONS
958
959
      In this section, we provide further details for measuring the presence of interactions discussed in
      Section 6.1. The MIs describe the pure additive effect of a coalition to the payout of the game.
960
      They thus serve as an important tool to analyze the interactions present in a game ν. For instance,
961
      low-complexity games, where MIs are non-zero only up to coalitions of size k, are typically referred
962   as k-additive games (Grabisch, 2016). In this case, SIs with explanation order k perfectly recover
963   all game values (Bordt & von Luxburg, 2023). In this case, the SIs correspond to the MIs. We
964   thus analyze the absolute values of MIs for varying size of coalitions, i.e., displaying the strata
965   q(k) := {|m(S)| : S ⊆ N , |S| = k} for varying interaction order k = 1, . . . , n. Analyzing
966   q(k) indicates, if the game ν has lower- order higher-order interactions present by investigating the
967   magnitudes and distributions in the strata q(k).
