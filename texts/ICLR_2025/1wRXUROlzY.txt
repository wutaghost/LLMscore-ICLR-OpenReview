009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Bayesian neural networks incorporate Bayesian inference over model weights to
012            account for uncertainty in weight estimation and predictions. Since full Bayesian
013            inference methods are computationally expensive and suffer from high dimen-
014            sionality, subspace inference has emerged as an appealing class of methods for
015            approximate inference, where inference is restricted to a lower-dimensional weight
016            subspace. Despite their benefits, existing subspace inference methods have notable
017            pitfalls in terms of subspace construction, subspace evaluation, and inference ef-
018
               ficiency. In this work, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of current subspace
               inference techniques and address all the aforementioned issues. First, we propose
019
               a block-averaging construction strategy that improves subspace quality by better
020
               resembling subspaces built from the full stochastic gradient descent trajectory.
021            Second, to directly evaluate subspace quality, we propose novel metrics based
022            on the Bayes factor and prior predictive, focusing on both goodness-of-fit and
023            generalization abilities. Finally, we enhance inference within the subspace by lever-
024            aging importance sampling and quasi-Monte Carlo methods, significantly reducing
025            computational overhead. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed
026            methods not only improve computational efficiency but also achieve better accuracy
027            and uncertainty quantification compared to existing subspace inference methods
028            on CIFAR and UCI datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Traditional neural network models often rely on optimization methods to obtain point estimation of
      model weights without quantifying uncertainty. To address this, Bayesian neural networks (BNNs)
034
      incorporate Bayesian inference, providing a probabilistic interpretation of the model weights and
035
      predictions. Enabling uncertainty quantification not only improves model robustness (Gawlikowski
036   et al., 2023), but also aids decision-making that are critical in high-risk areas such as medical image
037   analysis (Lambert et al., 2024; Nair et al., 2020) and autonomous vehicles (Yang et al., 2023).
038
039
      Despite their advantages, BNNs are challenged by the high dimensionality of the weight spaces,
      which can make training and inference computationally intensive and practically infeasible. Subspace
040
      inference (Izmailov et al., 2020; Garipov et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018) emerges as a compelling solution
041
      by projecting the weight space of a BNN into a low-dimensional subspace. This approach attempts to
042   preserve the performance of the BNN while greatly reducing the computational overhead.
043
044   However, current subspace inference methods have several notable limitations. For instance, Li et al.
045
      (2018) proposes using random subspace to project low-dimensional weights into high-dimensional
      spaces, without considering how to select optimal projection directions. Izmailov et al. (2020)
046
      constructs the projection matrix based only on the tail trajectory of stochastic gradient descent (SGD),
047
      which can fail to capture the complete variability and structure of the entire trajectory. Moreover,
048   existing metrics evaluate subspace quality only indirectly, relying on downstream task performance
049   (e.g., log-likelihood or accuracy), without considering the interpretability of the subspace itself with
050   respect to the training or testing data. To the best of our knowledge, there are currently no metrics
051   designed to directly evaluate how well the subspace represents the data-relevant portions of the full
052   parameter space. We emphasize that if the subspace does not adequately capture the high-quality
053   points from the full space—those with lower loss or higher likelihood, any inference or uncertainty
      quantification derived from it may be unreliable.

      Our contributions. In this paper, we improve subspace inference in Bayesian deep learning in terms
055   of subspace construction, subspace evaluation, and inference efficiency. We introduce novel subspace
056   construction methods, called block-averaging (BA) techniques along the SGD trajectory. Unlike
057   methods that focus only on the tail of the trajectory, our approach effectively captures the global
058   features of the entire trajectory while maintaining the same cost as tail based methods. Furthermore,
059   we introduce new evaluation metrics, such as the Bayes factor and prior predictive checks, to assess
060   the quality of these subspaces—an aspect largely overlooked in previous work.
061   To further improve the efficiency of uncertainty quantification, we apply quasi-Monte Carlo and
062   self-normalized importance sampling methods for inference the posterior predictive. We find that
063   these strategies significantly reduce computational overhead in low-dimensional subspaces, while
064   delivering prediction results that are comparable to more computationally intensive approaches.
065
066   Related Works. Scaling Bayesian inference to high-dimensional models such as deep neural
067   networks presents significant challenges. Some studies address this by constructing sparse subspaces
068   in Bayesian neural networks, using sparsity-promoting priors (Molchanov et al., 2017; Deng et al.,
069   2019; Ghosh et al., 2019) or applying removal-and-addition strategy (Li et al., 2024). Rather than
070   zeroing out certain weights, some approaches fix parameter values and limit inference to a select
071   subset, including methods that focus on the last layer (Kristiadi et al., 2020; Daxberger et al., 2021a)
072   or on a carefully selected subset of model weights (Daxberger et al., 2021b). Besides Bayesian
073
      neural networks, research on deterministic neural networks has also demonstrated that deep networks
      can be effectively optimized within a low-dimensional subspace (Li et al., 2018; Gressmann et al.,
074
      2020; Wortsman et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022). Methods like SWA (Izmailov et al., 2018), TWA
075
      (Li et al., 2022a), and DLDR (Li et al., 2022b) further leverage the SGD trajectory to identify an
076   optimal solution within the subspace. While these approaches demonstrate the potential for low-
077   dimensional optimization, many existing methods either focus on specific layers or ignore the need for
078   comprehensive evaluation metrics to assess subspace quality. Our work builds on this foundation by
079   focusing on both subspace construction and evaluation, providing new tools for efficiently estimating
080   uncertainty while ensuring high-quality inference within the subspace.

      3     D RAWBACKS OF E XISTING M ETHODS
124
      In this section, we analyze the key limitations of existing subspace inference methods, particularly in
125
      subspace construction and posterior predictive estimation.
126
127
      3.1   PCA- BASED S UBSPACE C ONSTRUCTION
128
129   To construct a subspace Z = {ŵ + P z | z ∈ Rk }, one needs to learn ŵ and P from the data.
130   Constructing subspaces based on Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Garipov et al., 2018; Izmailov
131   et al., 2020; Maddox et al., 2019) has proven to be effective in reducing dimensionality while
132   preserving key information from the SGD trajectory. A simple strategy involves using the mean of
133   the entire trajectory as ŵ, and then performing PCA on the matrix formed by the full trajectory (FT)
134   to obtain projection matrix P . This is equivalent to applying singular value decomposition (SVD)
135   on the centered matrix, where the trajectory is centered by subtracting ŵ. However, a significant
136
      challenge with this approach is the memory overhead. Full trajectory-based PCA (or SVD) requires
      storing and analyzing all training steps, which is infeasible in high-dimensional settings.
137
138   To address this, Izmailov et al. (2020) proposed a tail trajectory (TT) subspace construction method.
139   In this approach, ŵ is still constructed from the mean of the entire trajectory, but uses the deviations
140   from the last M points of the trajectory to ŵ to construct the projection matrix P via randomized
141   SVD (Halko et al., 2011). While focusing on the tails reduces memory cost from n to M , it introduces
142
      two key problems. First, using only the deviations from the tail leads to a deviation matrix that is
      not zero-centered, which makes the projection matrix constructed using SVD become dubious and
143
      no longer follows the interpretations of PCA. More importantly, using M points from the tail of the
144
      trajectory can result in a subspace that only captures a small part of the whole trajectory. As a result,
145   TT will underestimate variations in the trajectory and, even worse, lose the information on certain
146   high-quality weights reflected in the full trajectory, as demonstrated by the heat maps of the induced
147   likelihood in Figure 1.

159   3.2   P OSTERIOR P REDICTIVE VIA BAYESIAN M ODEL AVERAGING
160
161   Existing methods for posterior predictive estimation, such as VI and MCMC, often incur significant
      computational costs. MCMC methods are flexible and asymptotically exact, allowing them to better


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      capture complex posterior distributions. However, they are computationally expensive due to the
163   need for burn-in periods and the potential for low acceptance rates. Some MCMC methods like HMC
164   and NUTS and VI require gradient evaluations, which greatly increase the computational burden.
165
166
      Additionally, existing work has overlooked this correspondence between pW (w) and pZ (z). As a
      result of the transformation w = ŵ + P z, a prior on w on the full space uniquely determines an
167
      induced prior on z after linear transformation, which should be used during subspace inference. A
168
      fixed prior for w ensures the posterior reflects only the subspace properties, avoiding artifacts from
169   varying priors. For instance, if the subspace center ŵ shifts within the same subspace, the subspace
170   Z remains unchanged, and the posterior structure does not vary. Instead, existing works manually
171   choose the prior distribution for z.
172
      Finally, the current practice of using the predictive performance in equation 5 to evaluate the quality
173
      of subspaces is indirect. We argue that a subspace evaluation step should be performed prior to
174
      subspace inference, as a poorly constructed subspace is likely to result in suboptimal performance,
175   regardless of the inference method.

      6      C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this work, we have addressed several key limitations in subspace inference for Bayesian deep
528   learning, proposing novel strategies to improve the construction, evaluation, and efficiency of these
529   methods. Our BA subspace construction results in subspaces with more high likelihood, low loss
530   weights and therefore better predictions and uncertainty quantification. Additionally, by introducing
531   new subspace metrics based on the Bayes factor and prior predictive checks, we have provided a
532   more comprehensive evaluation framework for subspace quality. These metrics focus on both the
533   goodness-of-fit and generalization capabilities of the subspace, offering a more reliable means to
534   assess and compare different subspace inference techniques.
535   Our experiments on benchmark datasets, including CIFAR and UCI, further highlight the benefits of
536   our approach. In particular, our proposed RQMC-IS method achieves comparable performance to
537   other techniques while maintaining a lower computational cost. This efficiency makes our method
538   particularly suitable for large-scale datasets or complex networks, where computational resources
539   might be a critical concern. In conclusion, the methods presented in this work offer a scalable solution
      for Bayesian deep learning by improving subspace construction and inference techniques.
