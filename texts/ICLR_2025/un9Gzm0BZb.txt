010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Amplitude encoding of classical vectors serves as a cornerstone for numerous
013            quantum machine learning algorithms in real-world applications. Nevertheless,
014
               achieving exact amplitude encoding for general vectors needs an exponential num-
               ber of gates, which negates the potential quantum advantages. To address the
015
               challenge of large gate number in the state preparation phase, we propose an ap-
016
               proximate amplitude encoding algorithm based on entropy reduction (ER-AAE)
017            within the classical framework. Given a target vector, the ER-AAE algorithm
018            generates a sequence of gates, comprising single-qubit rotations and CZ gates,
019            that approximates the amplitude encoding of the target vector. The structure of
020            encoding circuits in ER-AAE is built inductively using a greedy search strategy that
021            maximally reduces the linear entropy. We further prove that the state produced by
022            ER-AAE approximates to the target state with the infidelity bounded by the linear
023            entropy of intermediate states. Experimental results, including state preparations
024            on random quantum circuit states, random vectors, MNIST digits, and CIFAR-10
025
               images, validate our method. Specifically, real-world data reveals a noteworthy
               trend where linear entropy decays significantly faster compared to random vectors.
026
               Furthermore, the ER-AAE algorithm surpasses the best existing encoding tech-
027
               niques, achieving lower error with an equivalent or fewer number of CNOT or CZ
028            gates.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The experimental advancements of quantum devices (Arute et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Madsen
034   et al., 2022) have catalyzed the development of quantum algorithms (Montanaro, 2016) across
035   various fields, including machine learning (Biamonte et al., 2017; Schuld & Killoran, 2019; Wiebe
036   et al., 2012), quantum simulations (Daley et al., 2022), and numerical analysis (Harrow et al., 2009;
037
      Montanaro & Pallister, 2016; Xin et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Lubasch et al., 2020). For practical
      tasks, a state preparation process is often required to encode classical data into quantum states.
038
      For instance, the quantum linear system solver (Harrow et al., 2009) (HHL algorithm)   P generates
039
      the solution to Ax = b in state form given the oracle preparing the state |bi = i bi |ii/kbk2 ,
040   i.e. the amplitude encoding (Grover, 2000) of b. Numerous quantum machine learning algorithms
041   share this requirement, including quantum support vector machines (Rebentrost et al., 2014) and
042   quantum k-means algorithms (Kerenidis et al., 2019; Zardini et al., 2024), which rely on the HHL
043   algorithm as a core component. Similar demands arise in solving differential equations, where
044   quantum solvers (Xin et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Lubasch et al., 2020) require amplitude encoding
045   for boundary conditions. Moreover, many quantum algorithms claim speed-up by focusing on
046   oracle query complexities (Aaronson, 2015), making efficient oracle implementations essential for
047   realizing practical quantum advantages. Specifically, quantum state preparation methods that scale
048
      polynomially with the number of qubits N are highly desirable.
049   Despite its critical role in numerous quantum algorithms, state preparation for amplitude encodings
050   remains computationally inefficient in general. Both exact and approximate amplitude encoding
051   typically require Õ(2N ) single- and two-qubit gates (Grover, 2000; Kaye & Mosca, 2001; Long &
052   Sun, 2001; Bergholm et al., 2005; Plesch & Brukner, 2011), which can undermine the exponential
053   speed-up offered by quantum algorithms. Additionally, current quantum hardware—classified as noisy
      intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices (Preskill, 2018)—has limitations in gate fidelity, with


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      error rates around O(10 4 ) and O(10 3 ) for single- and two-qubit gates, respectively1 . Consequently,
055   it is imperative to explore strategies for generating approximate amplitude encodings (AAEs) with
056   fewer gates, especially two-qubit gates. Recent work (Zoufal et al., 2019; Ran, 2020; Iaconis & Johri,
057   2023; Jobst et al., 2023; Nakaji et al., 2022; Mitsuda et al., 2024; Shirakawa et al., 2021; Rudolph
058   et al., 2023) on AAE, based on tensor networks (Schollwöck, 2011), has introduced encoding circuits
059   consisting of two-qubit unitaries. Although these methods highlight the relationship between small
060   quantum entanglement and easy-to-encode states, they do not optimize circuit architectures based on
061   the entanglement property. Furthermore, these approaches inefficiently utilize elementary two-qubit
062   gates such as CNOT and CZ, as the decomposition of general two-qubit unitaries requires three
063
      CNOT gates (Vatan & Williams, 2004).
064   In this work, we introduce a novel state preparation algorithm for approximate amplitude encoding
065   based on entropy reduction (ER-AAE). The ER-AAE algorithm for a target normalized vector v in
         N
066   C2 consists of two stages. The first stage constructs a gate sequence G1 , G2 , · · · , GC inductively,
067   reducing the quantum entanglement in the state that corresponds to the vector GC · · · G1 v until it
068   becomes approximately unentangled. In the second stage, a shallow gate structure W , involving O(N )
069   one-qubit gates, is applied. Parameters in W and {Gc }C   c=1 are optimized to minimize the infidelity
070   1 |0T · W GC · · · G1 v|2 . The final gate sequence for AAE is constructed as W † , G†C , · · · , G†1 .
071   As AAE is generally a hard problem, we aim to identify conditions that guarantee the accuracy
072   of ER-AAE. To this end, we derive an upper bound for the infidelity between the target state and
073   the ER-AAE-generated state, which scales as O(L), where L is the linear entropy of intermediate
074
      states with vector formulations GC · · · G1 v. We validate ER-AAE through state preparation tasks on
      synthetic datasets of Gaussian random vectors and real-world image datasets, including MNIST and
075
      CIFAR-10. Our results demonstrate that ER-AAE outperforms the best existing AAE methods in all
076
      tasks, achieving lower infidelity and higher peak signal-to-noise ratio with fewer or equal numbers of
077   quantum gates. Additionally, we observe that real-world images exhibit a more rapid decay of linear
078   entropy compared to random vectors, which may inspire future AAE research.

115   2.2   R ELATED WORK
116
117
      Quantum circuits for amplitude encoding have been extensively explored in prior research. Early
      studies (Grover, 2000; Kaye & Mosca, 2001; Long & Sun, 2001; Bergholm et al., 2005; Plesch &
118
      Brukner, 2011) demonstrated that amplitude encoding of an N -qubit state can be achieved using
119
      Õ(2N ) single- and two-qubit gates without prior knowledge. Ref. (Sun et al., 2023) introduced
120
      the use of auxiliary qubits to reduce circuit depth, though the gate count still scales exponentially.
121   These results align with the lower bounds of state complexity. Since an N -qubit state represents a
122                             N
      normalized vector in C2 with 2N +1 1 degrees of freedom, it is essential to employ O(2N ) gates
123   for arbitrary state preparation. Subsequent work (Gleinig & Hoefler, 2021) proposed an algorithm
124   that constructs amplitude encoding for S-sparse vectors using O(SN ) CNOT gates. However, many
125   input states derived from classical datasets, such as real-world images (Lecun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky
126   et al., 2009), are not sparse.
127
      Given the inefficiency of exact amplitude encoding, researchers have focused on approximating the
128
      amplitude encoding of the target state. The central concept is to achieve this with a modest number
129   of gates, i.e., approximate amplitude encoding trades precision for reduced quantum resources. For
130   instance, quantum generative adversarial networks (Zoufal et al., 2019) have been used to generate
131   quantum states from implicit data distributions. Due to the hradness of amplitude encoding for
132   general states, it is crucial to investigate properties of easily encodable states. The simplest example
133   is the tensor product of single-qubit states, which can be prepared from the zero state |0i by applying
134   a series of single-qubit unitaries to each qubit. These tensor product states exhibit zero quantum
135   entanglement, which is measured by the linear entropy (Manfredi & Feix, 2000) of single-qubit
136   subsystem from the entire state. Other easily encodable states, such as those from shallow circuits,
137
      also have relatively low entanglement compared to states from deep circuits (Dankert et al., 2009).
      Thus, a natural approach is to assess the entanglement of the target state and employ low-entanglement
138
      approximations as an initial guess for the encoding state. This idea has catalyzed the development of
139
      a series of AAE methods based on tensor networks (Schollwöck, 2011).
140
141   Early AAE methods focused on target states with limited entanglement. Specifically, an N -qubit
142   matrix product state (MPS) (Perez-Garcia et al., 2007) with bond dimension 2 can be exactly prepared
143
      using N 1 two-qubit unitaries(Schön et al., 2005). This process can be sequentially applied
      to general quantum states (Ran, 2020), where MPS approximations are obtained via truncated
144
      singular value decomposition (SVD) (Schollwöck, 2011). More recent MPS-based AAE methods
145
      have been developed for real-world datasets (Iaconis & Johri, 2023; Jobst et al., 2023; Nakaji
146   et al., 2022; Mitsuda et al., 2024) and specific distributions (Holmes & Matsuura, 2020; Iaconis
147   et al., 2024). Additionally, a recent study (Shirakawa et al., 2021) proposed the AQCE algorithm for
148   generating AAE, where locally optimal two-qubit unitaries are iteratively updated during optimization.
149   Subsequent research (Rudolph et al., 2023) enhanced the AQCE algorithm by using an MPS as the
150   initial guess, demonstrating superior performance compared to using the identity operator as the
151   initial guess. Besides, several recent works (Nakaji et al., 2022; Mitsuda et al., 2024) have proposed
152   to use fixed quantum circuit architectures such as hardware efficient ansatzes with tunable parameters
153   as the candidate of encoding circuits, and the AAE is obtained by training parameters for minimizing
154
      the infidelity.

475
476   In this manuscript, we propose ER-AAE, a novel classical approach for approximate amplitude
477   encoding of real-world data based on the entropy reduction. We demonstrate that the proposed
478   algorithm outperforms existing AAE approaches with equal or fewer CNOT or CZ gates. Besides,
479   we show that the real-world data like MNIST or CIFAR-10 images exhibit rapid decay on the linear
480
      entropy, which may be of independent interest that can inspire other AAE research on real-world
      data.
