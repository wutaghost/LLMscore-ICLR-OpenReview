                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Recent advancements in machine learning have been driven by models trained
010
               on large-scale, high-quality datasets. However, the practical application of these
011
               models faces two significant challenges: the infeasibility of acquiring precise
012            labels in real-world settings and the substantial computational burden imposed
013            by training large models. While existing approaches—such as self-supervised
014            learning, weak supervision, noisy label learning, and dataset distillation—address
015            these challenges from a model-centric perspective, they often overlook the potential
016            benefits of optimizing the data itself. This paper introduces a novel data-centric
017            learning paradigm where both the dataset and the model co-evolve during the
018            learning process. We formalize this paradigm and propose a Data-evolution
019            Learning Algorithm (D E LA), which offers three key advantages: optimized
020
               dataset generation, versatile dataset compatibility, and effective utilization of prior
               knowledge. Extensive experiments demonstrate that D E LA enables the creation of
021
               optimized datasets for reuse in subsequent training, effectively addressing diverse
022
               datasets with varying target types. Moreover, D E LA accelerates learning by
023            utilizing architecture-agnostic, open-source prior models for efficient data creation.
024            Notably, D E LA frequently outperforms traditional SOTA model-centric methods
025            in self-supervised and noisy label learning. Furthermore, its simplicity enables
026            implementation in only two lines of PyTorch code, offering significant potential for
027            advancements in representation learning. Our code will be made publicly available.

      Our work presents the following five key contributions below as an initial step toward establishing a
080
      data-centric representation learning paradigm:
081
082   (a) Defining Data-Evolution Learning (see Definition 1 ) and Figure 1 . To the best of our knowledge,
083   we are the first to introduce a data-centric learning paradigm where both data and model co-evolve
084   simultaneously, and we formalize the objective of this task. Additionally, we present a simple and
085
      effective technical framework, D E LA, to achieve this objective, highlighting several advantages of
      this paradigm that remain intractable for traditional model-centric learning paradigms.
086
087   (b) An optimized dataset is obtained through learning using D E LA (see Section 4.2 ). Traditional
088   model-centric approaches focus on training an effective model on a fixed dataset, where the dataset
089   remains unchanged after the learning process. This paradigm typically results in each training
090   procedure being independent, meaning subsequent training processes cannot leverage information
091
      from previous sessions, especially when involving models with different architectures. D E LA
      addresses this limitation by evolving the dataset during training, allowing it to be reused and further
092
      optimized in subsequent training processes involving models with varying architectures.
093
094   (c) The learning process of D E LA can be significantly accelerated (see Section 4.3 ). Leveraging
095   open-source pre-trained models as prior knowledge is a widely adopted approach in contemporary
096   machine learning to expedite and improve task-specific training. For example, Ridnik et al. (2021)
097
      use a ResNet-50 model pre-trained on ImageNet-1K as a starting point to train on ImageNet-21K.
      However, this approach may not be effective when the target model is based on a different architecture.
098
      In contrast, D E LA can fully exploit prior knowledge from architecture-agnostic models to accelerate
099
      learning by employing data initialization (see Section 3 ).
100
101   (d) Addressing diverse datasets constructed with varying types of targets (see Section 4.4 ). Specifi-
102   cally, unlike previous approaches such as TNLPAD (Wang et al., 2024b) and SimCLR (Chen et al.,

      2     R ELATED W ORK
112
113   This section integrates three key areas of deep learning: (a) techniques for dataset condensation that
114   maintain efficacy; (b) self-supervised learning methods for training models on unlabeled data; (c)
115   noise-robust learning approaches for training models on noisy data.
116
117   2.1   DATASET D ISTILLATION : E FFICIENT AND E FFECTIVE L EARNING WITH R EDUCED DATA
118
      The goal of dataset distillation is to generate a substantially smaller dataset that maintains performance
119
      comparable to the original dataset. Traditional methods replicate behaviors from the original dataset
120
      within the distilled one, aiming to reduce discrepancies between surrogate neural network models
121   trained on both synthetic and original datasets. Key metrics in this process include gradient matching
122   (Zhao et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), feature alignment (Wang
123   et al., 2022), distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), and training trajectory
124   consistency (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023;
125   Guo et al., 2023). However, these approaches entail significant computational overhead due to the
126   continuous calculation of discrepancies between the distilled and original datasets. The optimization
127   process requires multiple iterations to minimize these discrepancies until convergence, making it
128   challenging to scale to large datasets, such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009).
129   A promising strategy involves developing metrics that capture essential dataset information, enabling
130   efficient scaling to large datasets like ImageNet-1K with larger backbones. This approach avoids
131   the need for multiple comparisons between original and distilled datasets. For instance, SRe2 L (Yin
132   et al., 2023) condenses an entire dataset into a model using pre-trained neural networks, such as
133
      ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016), and subsequently extracts knowledge from these models into images and
      targets, thereby forming a distilled dataset. Recently, RDED (Sun et al., 2024) suggests that images
134
      accurately recognized by competent observers, such as humans and pre-trained models, are more
135
      valuable for learning.
136
137   2.2   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING : E XTRACTING R EPRESENTATION FROM U NLABELED DATA
138
139   The primary objective of self-supervised learning is to generate robust representations independent of
140   human-labeled data. These representations should rival those obtained through supervised learning,
141
      offering robust performance across a range of tasks.
142   Contrastive learning-based methods implicitly assign a “one-hot” label to each sample and its
143   augmented versions to enhance discriminative power. Since the introduction of InfoNCE (Oord
144   et al., 2018), numerous studies (He et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020a;b; Cao et al., 2020; Kalantidis
145
      et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021)
      have advanced this area. MoCo (He et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020b; 2021) utilizes a momentum
146
      encoder to maintain consistent negatives, proving effective for both CNNs and Vision Transformers.
147
      SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a) incorporates strong data augmentations and a nonlinear projection head.
148   Other approaches include instance classification (Cao et al., 2020), enhanced data augmentation
149   (Kalantidis et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021), clustering techniques (Li et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2020),
150   and adversarial training (Hu et al., 2021). These methods improve the alignment and uniformity of
151   representations on the hypersphere (Wang & Isola, 2020).
152   Asymmetric network methods facilitate self-supervised learning by leveraging only positive pairs,
153   effectively avoiding representational collapse through the use of asymmetric architectures. BYOL
154   (Jean-Bastien et al., 2020) employs a dual-component framework consisting of a predictor network
155   and a momentum encoder. Richemond et al. (2020) demonstrate that BYOL performs efficiently
156   without the need for batch statistics. SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021) implements gradient stopping
157   on the target branch to replicate the function of momentum encoder. DINO (Caron et al., 2021)
158   incorporates a self-distillation loss mechanism. UniGrad (Tao et al., 2022) combines asymmetric
159   networks with contrastive learning techniques within a theoretically cohesive framework.
160   In addition, Singh et al. (2023) explore a straightforward self-training technique aimed at improving
161   supervised fine-tuning performance, which can be viewed as applying expectation-maximization for
      reinforcement learning.

175   2.3   N OISY L ABEL L EARNING : T RAINING M ODELS OVER N OISE -L ABELED DATASETS
176
      Learning with noisy labels has garnered significant attention in recent years (Han et al., 2020b; Yao
177
      et al., 2018). Numerous approaches have been developed to address this challenge, generally dividing
178
      into two categories: those leveraging the memorization effect and those that do not.
179
180
      Existing noisy label learning methods can be roughly grouped into two categories: (a) Employing an
      explicit or implicit noise model to estimate the distributions of noisy and clean labels, subsequently
181
      deleting or correcting the noisy samples. The models used can vary, including neural networks
182
      (Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, 2017; Jiang et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2018), conditional
183   random fields (Vahdat, 2017), or knowledge graphs (Li et al., 2017). However, a significant limitation
184   is their reliance on a substantial number of clean training samples, making them unsuitable for many
185   datasets dominated by noisy labels. (a) Constructing more balanced loss functions to mitigate the
186   impact of noisy training samples (Liu & Tao, 2015; Ma et al., 2018; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Wang
187   et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019). For instance, TNLPAD (Wang et al., 2024b) addresses noisy labels
188   through network parameter additive decomposition.

190   3     A S IMPLE F RAMEWORK FOR DATA -E VOLUTION L EARNING
191
192   This study introduces a novel approach for data-evolution learning (D E LA), focusing on evolving data
193   during training (see Figure 2 ). The central concept of this method is to facilitate the “collaboration”
194   between data and models. Specifically, the methodology is systematically divided into multiple
195   stages: (a) Initialize targets for given samples; (b) Update the model for one step using the current
196   data; (c) Evolve the data using the updated model, and then revert to step (b).
197   (a) Initializing targets for given samples. We hypothesize that the primary distinction among
198   datasets, such as CIFAR-10 and its noise-labeled variant, is primarily in their targets. The samples
199   themselves, sourced consistently from the real world, exhibit relatively similar. Within this framework,
200   both samples and targets are treated as variables during training, necessitating the use of scenario-
201   specific strategies for target initialization. Formally, we define:
202                                    D ← {(x, y) | y = ψ(x), x ∈ DX } ,                                       (2)
203
204   where ψ denotes the “data-initializer”. For instance, ψ can represent human annotations and y
205
      indicates the corresponding one-hot labels for standard dataset like CIFAR-10. Additionally, ψ may
      include various models for generating diverse data types, including randomly initialized models and
206
      pre-trained models obtained from the internet (referred to as “prior models”).
207
208   Interestingly, it has been observed that using a randomly initialized model to generate initial targets in
209   (2) is more effective and facilitates faster training convergence compared to using random noise (see
210
      our analysis in Appendix D ). Hence, in this paper, when dealing with unlabeled datasets without
      specific instructions, D E LA opts for a randomly initialized model as the data-initializer.
211
212   (b) Updating the model for one step. A batch of data Di ⊂ D is randomly sampled, and the
213   parameters θ in the model backbone ϕθ and predictor head pθ are updated as follows:
214                                θ ← θ − η∇θ E(x,y)∼Di [ℓ(pθ (ϕθ (x)), y)] ,                                  (3)
215
      where ℓ denotes the Cosine Similarity loss function, and η is the learning rate.

216
      (c) Evolving data for one step. Building upon the insights from Sun et al. (2024), which underscores
217   the necessity of aligning samples DX with targets DY to create an optimal dataset D, we aim to
218   refine targets DY to enhance their congruence with samples DX . The refinement process leverages
219   the updated model:
220
221                 Di ← {(x′ , y′ ) | x′ = T (x), y′ = λy + (1 − λ)ϕθ (x), (x, y) ∈ Di } ,                (4)
222
      where λ is a blending parameter (refer to Section 4.6 for detailed settings), and T denotes data
223
      augmentation technique. Importantly, as ϕθ (x) is computed during the model update in (3), we
224   utilize these results directly, obviating the need for recomputation in (4). This algorithm is so simple
225   that it can be implemented with two lines of PyTorch code (see Appendix B ).

      4.4     L EARNING AND D ENOISING DATASETS WITH N OISY TARGETS U SING D E LA
401   To further investigate the potential applications of D E LA algorithm in addressing challenges in
402   contemporary deep learning, we apply D E LA to learning tasks involving noise-labeled datasets.
403   Following the strategy outlined in Wang et al. (2024b), we introduce symmetric noise at varying
404   levels to the clean targets within the original datasets and benchmark various methods, including
405   D E LA. The experimental results in Table 3 and Appendix E indicate that:
406   (a) D E LA consistently outperforms several SOTA baselines across multiple datasets;
407   (b) D E LA demonstrates robustness to varying degrees of noise. For instance, while the performance
408       of TNLPAD significantly declines with increased noise levels (from 20% to 40%), D E LA exhibits
409       only minor performance degradation.
410
      Our D E LA has exhibited twofold advantages: (a) It can adapt to and evolve various types of original
411
      targets (see Section 3 ), including learning directly from noisy datasets; (b) The evolved noisy
412   datasets, considered as cleaner versions, can be effectively utilized to train models using D E LA.

467   evolving process. Specifically, with our standard setting of 100 epochs for full evolution, an evolution
468   degree of 0.1 corresponds to the dataset saved after 10 epochs of training. This metric allows us to
469   quantify the impact of evolutionary progression on model performance. Our analysis reveals:
470
471   (a) The performance of D E LA improves substantially as the evolution degree increases, regardless

475       emphasizing the consistency of our method’s improvement across various data sources.
476   (c) It is interesting to note that the ViT-based evolved data (VT16-E) demonstrates the least improve-

498   Sensitivity analysis of λ scheduler in D E LA. The λ scheduler in D E LA plays a crucial role in
499
      balancing the impact of prior model knowledge on the training process. It controls how the incorpo-
      rated prior, whether from co-evolved data, randomly initialized models, or pre-trained models, affects
500
      the evolution process. Larger λ values allow for more effective utilization of the prior, especially
501
      in the early stages of training, but may lead to over-fitting if maintained throughout the process.
502
503
      In our analysis, we considered two primary λ scheduler configurations: constant settings, where λ
      remains unchanged throughout training, and cosine annealing schedules, where λ decreases gradually
504
      from a higher initial value to a lower final value. This approach allows us to examine the impact of
505
      both static and dynamic prior influence on the training process.
506
507
       Figure 4c and Figure 5 illustrate the performance of D E LA under various λ scheduler configura-
508   tions on the CIFAR-100 dataset. Figure 4c represents the scenario using initial targets generated by a
509   randomly initialized model (weak prior), while Figure 5 shows results using evolved data (RN18-E),
510   which are derived from a ResNet18 model trained on CIFAR-100 (strong prior). Our analysis reveals:
511   (a) For both weak and strong priors, higher starting λ values (0.99 and 0.999) generally lead to
512       better performance, indicating the importance of leveraging prior model knowledge.
513   (b) The impact of the ending λ value is less pronounced, particularly for strong priors, suggesting
514       that the method is robust to this parameter within the tested range (0.2 to 0.6).
515   (c) Constant λ values (denoted by “C” in the first row of the heatmaps) consistently underperform
516       compared to annealing schedules. This observation highlights the benefits of dynamically
517
          adjusting the influence of prior knowledge throughout the training process, allowing the model
          to initially leverage the prior heavily and then gradually adapt to the specific dataset.
518
      (d) The performance gap between weak and strong priors is substantial (approximately 5 percentage
519
          points), emphasizing the value of incorporating high-quality prior knowledge.
520
521
      Summary. We recommend that practitioners use a high starting λ value (0.999) with a gradual
      decrease to a moderate ending value (around 0.3) when employing the cosine annealing schedule.
522
      This configuration allows the training of D E LA to benefit significantly from prior knowledge in the
523
      early stages while still adapting effectively to the specific dataset as training progresses.

527   5    L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduces data-evolution learning, a novel data-centric learning paradigm that
530
      simultaneously evolves datasets and models. Our proposed D E LA demonstrates significant
      advantages over traditional model-centric approaches by generating reusable optimized datasets,
531
      exhibiting versatile compatibility with various data types, and effectively leveraging prior knowledge
532
      to accelerate learning. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets, architectures, and baselines
533   validate D E LA’s effectiveness and efficiency, consistently matching or outperforming SOTA
534   model-centric methods in various learning scenarios. The data-evolution learning paradigm opens
535   new possibilities for representation learning, providing a fresh perspective on addressing data quality
536   and computational efficiency challenges. While results are promising, future work could explore
537   theoretical foundations, extend to more diverse tasks, and investigate potential in other domains
538   such as natural language processing and reinforcement learning.

1050     Furthermore, we examined the sensitivity of D E LA to variations in the hidden dimension and output
1051     dimension of the projector. Our findings, summarized in Figure 6 , reveal:
1052
1053
        (a) The impact of projector dimensions varies across datasets, with more complex datasets showing
            higher sensitivity to dimensional changes. However, the overall performance differences are not
1054
            drastic, indicating a degree of robustness in the projector architecture.
1055
        (b) There is a clear trend of increasing dimensional requirements as dataset complexity increases.
1056        Moving from CIFAR-10 to CIFAR-100 to TinyImageNet, we observe a need for higher dimen-
1057        sions in both hidden and output layers. This suggests that datasets with more classes and larger
1058        sample sizes require higher-dimensional feature spaces for effective representation.
1059    (c) Extremely low dimensions for the target output (e.g., 32) consistently yield the lowest perfor-
1060        mance across all datasets, indicating a minimum threshold for effective feature representation.
1061        This underfitting scenario likely occurs because the low-dimensional space is insufficient to
1062        separate different classes effectively.
1063    (d) Conversely, very high dimensions can lead to overfitting, particularly in datasets with limited
1064
            samples or high noise levels. This creates a delicate balance between representational power and
            generalization ability, which becomes more critical as dataset complexity increases.
1065
1066    The observed dimension-dependent behavior can be attributed to the role of the projector’s output in
1067    our algorithm. As we store and refine these outputs over time, they effectively form a feature space
1068    for distinguishing different images. A larger feature space increases the model’s capacity but also the
1069    risk of overfitting, particularly in scenarios with limited or noisy data.
1070    Our findings highlight the importance of careful projector dimension tuning in D E LA, especially
1071    when adapting the algorithm to new datasets or domains. They also suggest that adaptive dimension
1072    selection strategies could be a promising direction for future research, potentially allowing the
1073    algorithm to automatically adjust its projector dimensions based on the characteristics of the dataset
1074    at hand.
