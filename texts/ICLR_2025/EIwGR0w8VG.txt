009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) offer the potential for reliable uncertainty quan-
012            tification and interpretability, which are critical for trustworthy AI in high-stakes
013            domains. However, existing methods often struggle with issues such as overcon-
014            fidence, hyperparameter sensitivity, and posterior collapse, leaving room for al-
015            ternative approaches. In this work, we advance message passing (MP) for BNNs
016            and present a novel framework that models the predictive posterior as a factor
017            graph. To the best of our knowledge, our framework is the first MP method that
018
               handles convolutional neural networks and avoids double-counting training data,
               a limitation of previous MP methods that causes overconfidence. We evaluate
019
               our approach on CIFAR-10 with a convolutional neural network of roughly 890k
020
               parameters and find that it can compete with the SOTA baselines AdamW and
021            IVON, even having an edge in terms of calibration. On synthetic data, we validate
022            the uncertainty estimates and observe a strong correlation (0.9) between posterior
023            credible intervals and its probability of covering the true data-generating function
024            outside the training range. While our method scales to an MLP with 5.6 million
025            parameters, further improvements are necessary to match the scale and perfor-
026            mance of state-of-the-art variational inference methods.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deep learning models have achieved impressive results across various domains, including natural
032   language processing (Vaswani et al., 2023), computer vision (Ravi et al., 2024), and autonomous
033
      systems (Bojarski et al., 2016). Yet, they often produce overconfident but incorrect predictions,
      particularly in ambiguous or out-of-distribution scenarios. Without the ability to effectively quantify
034
      uncertainty, this can foster both overreliance and underreliance on models, as users stop trusting their
035
      outputs entirely (Zhang et al., 2024), and in high-stakes domains like healthcare or autonomous
036   driving, its application can be dangerous (Henne et al., 2020). To ensure safer deployment in these
037   settings, models must not only predict outcomes but also express how uncertain they are about those
038   predictions to allow for informed decision-making.
039
      Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) offer a principled way to quantify uncertainty by capturing a pos-
040
      terior distribution over the model’s weights, rather than relying on point estimates as in traditional
041
      neural networks. This allows BNNs to express epistemic uncertainty, the model’s lack of knowledge
042   about the underlying data distribution. Current methods for posterior approximation largely fall into
043   two categories: sampling-based methods, such as Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), and determinis-
044   tic approaches like variational inference (VI). While sampling methods are usually computationally
045   expensive, VI has become increasingly scalable (Shen et al., 2024). However, VI is not without limi-
046   tations: It often struggles with overconfidence (Papamarkou et al., 2024), and it can struggle to break
047   symmetry when multiple modes are close (Zhang et al., 2018). Mean-field approaches, commonly
048   used in VI, are prone to posterior collapse (Kurle et al., 2022; Coker et al., 2022). Additionally, VI
049   often requires complex hyperparameter tuning (Osawa et al., 2019), which complicates its practical
050
      deployment in real-world settings. These challenges motivate the need for alternative approaches
      that can potentially address some of the shortcomings of VI while maintaining its scalability.
051
052   In contrast, message passing (MP) (Minka, 2001) is a probabilistic inference technique that suffers
053   less from these problems. Belief propagation (Kschischang et al., 2001), the basis for many MP
      algorithms, integrates over variables of a joint density p(x1 , . . . , xn ) that factorize into a product of

068   We summarize our contributions as follows:
069   1. We propose a scalable message-passing framework for Bayesian neural networks and derive
070      message equations for various factors, which can benefit factor graph modeling across domains.
071   2. We implement our method in Julia for both CPU and GPU, and demonstrate its scalability to

081   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
082
      As the exact posterior is intractable for most practical neural networks, approximate methods are
083
      essential for scalable BNNs. These methods generally fall into two categories: sampling-based
084   approaches and those that approximate the posterior with parameterized distributions.
085
086   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods attempt to draw representative samples from pos-
087
      terior distributions. Although methods such as Hamiltonian Monte Carlo are asymptotically exact,
      they become computationally prohibitive for large neural networks due to their high-dimensional
088
      parameter spaces and complex energy landscapes (Coker et al., 2022). An adaptation of Gibbs
089
      sampling has been scaled to MNIST, but on a very small network with only 8,180 parameters (Pa-
090   pamarkou, 2023). Approximate sampling methods can be faster but still require a large number
091   of samples, which complicates both training and inference. Although approaches like knowledge
092   distillation (Korattikara et al., 2015) attempt to speed up inference, MCMC remains generally too
093   inefficient for large-scale deep learning applications (Khan & Rue, 2024).
094
      Variational Inference (VI) aims to approximate the intractable posterior distribution p(θ | D) by
095
      a variational posterior q(θ). The parameters of q are optimized using gradients with respect
096   to an objective function, which is typically a generalized form of the reverse KL divergence
097   DKL [ q(θ) ∥ p(θ | D) ]. Early methods like (Graves, 2011) and Bayes By Backprop (Blundell et al.,
098   2015) laid the foundation for applying VI to neural networks, but suffer from slow convergence and
099   severe underfitting, especially for large models or small dataset sizes (Osawa et al., 2019). More
100   recently, VOGN (Osawa et al., 2019) achieved Adam-like results on ImageNet LSVRC by applying
101   a Gauss-Newton approximation to the Hessian matrix. IVON (Shen et al., 2024) improved upon
102   VOGN by using cheaper Hessian approximations and training techniques like gradient clipping,
103   achieving Adam-like performance on large-scale models such as GPT-2 while maintaining similar
104
      runtime costs. Despite recent advancements, VI continues to face challenges such as overconfi-
      dence, posterior collapse, and complex hyperparameter tuning (see introduction), motivating the
105
      exploration of alternative approaches (Zhang et al., 2018).
106
107   Message Passing for Neural Networks: Message passing is a general framework that unifies sev-
      eral algorithms (Kschischang et al., 2001; Minka, 2001), but its direct application to neural networks

      has been limited. Expectation backpropagation (EBP) (Soudry et al., 2014) approximates the poste-
109   rior of 3-layer MLPs by combining expectation propagation, an approximate message passing algo-
110   rithm, with gradient backpropagation. Similarly, probabilistic backpropagation (PBP) (Hernández-
111   Lobato & Adams, 2015) combines belief propagation with gradient backpropagation and was found
112   to produce better approximations than EBP (Ghosh et al., 2016). However, PBP treats the data as
113   new examples in each consecutive epoch (double-counting), which makes it prone to overconfi-
114   dence. Furthermore, EBP and PBP were both only deployed on small datasets and rely on gradients
115   instead of pure message passing. In contrast, Lucibello et al. (2022) applied message passing to
116   larger architectures by modeling the posterior over neural network weights as a factor graph, but
117
      faced posterior collapse to a point measure due to also double-counting data. Their experiments
      were mostly restricted to three-layer MLPs without biases and with binary weights. Our approach
118
      builds on this by introducing a message-passing framework for BNNs that avoids double-counting,
119
      scales to CNNs, and effectively supports continuous weights.

124   Our goal is to model the predictive posterior of a BNN as a factor graph and find a Gaussian ap-
125   proximation of the predictive posterior via belief propagation. Essentially, factor graphs are prob-
126   abilistic modelling tools for approximating the marginals of joint distributions, provided that they
127
      factorize sufficiently. For a more comprehensive introduction on factor graphs and the sum-product
      algorithm, refer to Kschischang et al. (2001) BNNs, on the other hand, treat the parameters θ of
128

300   Batching As the forward and backward messages depend on each other, we must store them to
301   compute message updates during message passing. Updating our messages in a sweeping ”pass”
302
      over a branch and running backward passes immediately after the forward pass on the same branch,

303
      also ensures efficient propagation of updated messages despite the presence of loops. However,
304

305   storing them for all n training examples. To address this, we adopt a batching strategy: Instead of
306   maintaining n training branches simultaneously, we update the factor graph using a batch (subset) of
307   b examples at a time. The factor graph then models b messages to the weights W , while the messages
308   to W from the remaining (inactive) examples are aggregated into batch-wise products and stored in
309   a trainer object. Figure 2 illustrates this setup. When switching batches, we divide the marginals by
310   the batch’s old aggregate message and multiply the updated messages into the marginal, ensuring
311   that data is not double-counted. Within each batch, we iterate through the examples and perform
312   a forward and backward pass on each in sequence. After all examples have been processed once,
313
      we call it an ”iteration”. Depending on the training stage, we either repeat this process within the
      same batch or move to the next batch. As training progresses, we gradually increase the number of
314
      iterations per batch to allow for finer updates as the overall posterior comes closer to convergence.

331   In scaling our approach to deep networks, we encountered several challenges related to computa-
332   tional performance, numerical stability, and weight initialization. The following subsections detail
333   remedies to these problems.
334
335   4.1   FACTOR G RAPH I MPLEMENTATION
336
337   While batching effectively reduces memory requirements for large datasets, a direct implementation
338
      of a factor graph still scales poorly for deep networks. Explicitly modeling each scalar variable and
      factor as an instance is computationally expensive. To address this, we propose the following design
339
      optimizations: 1 Rather than modeling individual elements of the factor graph, we represent entire
340
      layers of the network. Message passing between layers is orchestrated by an outer training loop. 2
341   Each layer instance operates across all training branches within the active batch, removing the need
342   to duplicate layers for each example. 3 Factors are stateless functions, not objects. Each layer is
343   responsible for computing its forward and backward messages by calling the required functions. In
344   this design, layer instances maintain their own state, but message passing and batching are managed
345   in the outer loop. The stateless message equations are optimized for both performance and numerical
346   stability. As a result, the number of layer instances scales linearly with network depth but remains
347   constant regardless of layer size or batch size. This approach significantly reduces computational
348   and memory overhead—our implementation is approximately 300x faster than a direct factor graph
349
      model in our tests. Additionally, we optimized our implementation for GPU execution by leveraging
      Julia’s CUDA.jl and Tullio.jl libraries. Since much of the runtime is spent on linear algebra
350
      operations (within linear or convolutional layers), we built a reusable, GPU-compatible library for
351
      Gaussian multiplication. This design makes the implementation both scalable and extendable.
352

      4.2   N UMERICAL S TABILITY
355
      Maintaining numerical stability in the message-passing process is critical, particularly as model size
356   increases. Backward messages often exhibit near-infinite variances when individual weights have
357   minimal impact on the likelihood. Therefore, we compute them directly in natural parameters, which
358   also simplifies the equations. Special care is needed for LeakyReLU, as its messages can easily di-
359   verge. To mitigate this, we introduced guardrails: when normalization constants become too small,
360   precision turns negative, or variance in forward messages increases, we revert to either G(0, 0) or
361   use moment matching on messages instead of marginals (see E for details). Another trick is to pe-
362   riodically recompute the weight marginals from scratch to maintain accuracy. By leveraging the
363   properties of Gaussians, we save memory by recomputing variable-to-factor messages as needed5 .
364
      However, incremental updates to marginals can accumulates errors, so we perform a full recom-
      putation once per batch iteration. Lastly, we apply light message damping through an exponential
365
      moving average to stabilize the training, but, importantly, only on the aggregated batch messages,
366
      not on the individual messages of the active batch.

      6   C ONCLUSION
448
449
450   Summary: We presented a novel framework that advances message-passing (MP) for Bayesian
451   neural networks by modeling the predictive posterior as a factor graph. To the best of our knowledge,
452
      this is the first MP method to handle convolutional neural networks while avoiding double-counting
      training data, a limitation in previous MP approaches like Soudry et al. (2014); Hernández-Lobato
453
      & Adams (2015); Lucibello et al. (2022). In our experiment on the CIFAR-10 dataset our method
454
      proofed to be competitive with the SOTA baselines AdamW and IVON, even showing an edge in
455   terms of calibration.
456
457   Limitations: Despite recent advances, variational inference methods like IVON remain ahead in
458
      scale and performance on larger datasets. Our approach’s runtime and memory requirements scale
      linearly with model parameters and dataset size. While our inference at test time can keep up with
459
      IVON’s sampling approach in terms of speed and memory requirements, training is up to two orders
460
      of magnitude slower and more GPU-memory intensive compared to training deterministic networks
461   using PyTorch with optimizers like AdamW.
462
463   The memory overhead stems from two key factors: First, each training example stores messages
464
      proportional to the model’s parameter count, unlike AdamW’s batch-level intermediate representa-
      tions. Second, each parameter requires two 8-byte floating-point numbers, contrasting with more
465
      efficient 4-byte or smaller formats.
466
467   Runtime inflation results from several performance bottlenecks: Our training schedule lacks parallel
468   forward passes, our Tullio-based CUDA kernel generation misses memory-layout and GPU opti-
469   mizations present in mature libraries like Torch, message equations involve complex computations
470
      beyond standard matrix multiplications, and we use Julia’s default FP64 precision, which GPUs
      process less efficiently.
471
472   Future Work: We believe Moment Propagation (MP) holds significant promise for more balanced
473   uncertainty estimates, thanks to its moment-matching ability, compared to Variational Inference’s
474   tendency toward overconfident predictions. Further improvements in scalability and architectural
475   flexibility could make MP a competitive alternative to VI.
476   Concretely, in terms of memory requirements, it is worth exploring whether iterating on individ-
477   ual examples instead of batches, and starting from scratch in each epoch, could leave our method
478   ahead. While this might reintroduce the double counting problem, it would drastically reduce the
479   GPU-memory footprint. Regarding training efficiency, an altered message-update schedule with ac-
480   tual batched computations would significantly reduce training time. Reimplementing our library in
481   CUDA C++ with efficiency in mind could also drastically cut down computational overhead.
482   On the architectural front, we deem it likely that our approach can be extended to most modern deep
483   learning architectures. Residual connections are straightforward to implement as they boil down to
484   simple sum factors. For normalization layers at the scalar level, only a division factor is missing,
485   which can be approximated by a ”rotated” product factor. This would suffice to model ResNet-like
      architectures and more modern convolutional networks like ConvNeXt. For transformers, the last


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ingredient needed is an efficient softargmax factor. Given the division factor, only an exp factor is
487   missing to model softargmax at the scalar level.
488
489   Finally, future work might also explore applications to more applied tasks such as continual learning,
490
      sparse networks, and Bayesian reinforcement learning.

       D    P RIOR A NALYSIS
980
981    The strength of the prior determines the amount of data needed to obtain a useful posterior that fits
982
       the data. Our goal is to draw prior means and set prior variances so that the computed variances of
       all messages are on the order of O(1) regardless of network width and depth. It is not entirely clear
983
       if this would be a desirable property; after all, adding more layers also makes the network more
984
       expressive and more easily able to model functions with very high or low values. However, if we
985    let the predictive prior grow unrestricted, it will grow exponentially, leading to numerical issues.
986    In the following, we analyze the predictive prior under simplifying assumptions to derive a prior
987    initialization that avoids exponential variance explosion. While we fail to achieve this goal, our
988    current prior variances are still informed by this analysis.
989
       In the following, we assume that the network inputs are random variables. Then, the parameters
990
       of messages also become random variables, as they are derived from the inputs according to the
991    message equations. Our goal is to keep the expected value of the variance parameter of the outgoing
992    message at a constant size. We also assume that the means of the prior are sampled according to
993    spectral initialization, as described in Section 4.3.

1091   linear layer achieves an overall target prior predictive variance of approximately t = 1.5+0.5 = 2.0.
1092
1093   R ESULTS IN PRACTICE
1094
       In practice, we found that the variance of the predictive posterior still goes up exponentially with
1095
       the depth of the network despite our derived prior choices. However, if we lower the prior variance
1096
       further to avoid this explosion, the network is overly restricted and unable to obtain a good fit during
1097   training. We therefore set the prior variances as outlined here, but acknowledge that choosing a good
1098   prior is still an unsolved problem.
