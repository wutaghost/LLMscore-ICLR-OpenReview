011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have exhibited remarkable gen-
014            eralization capabilities. Previous studies have largely focused on examining the
015            generalization mechanisms in smaller models to draw inferences about similar
016
               mechanisms in larger language models. However, these smaller models typically
               possess limited generalization capacity. In this study, we explore the general-
017
               ization mechanisms of billion-parameter language models, with a particular at-
018
               tention on publicly available models such as LLaMA and Gemma. Our findings
019            reveal that weight activations exhibit task-specific behavior, indicating that not all
020            weights are necessary for task performance. Building on this insight, we introduce
021            a parameter probing method to identify subnetworks optimized for specific tasks
022            without extensive fine-tuning. This method involves sorting and grouping weight
023            activations followed by the pruning of less significant groups based on a small
024            validation set. Furthermore, our results show that subnetworks specialized for
025            domain-specific tasks achieve improved performance and generalization within
026            their respective domains, but their performance deteriorates across different do-
027
               mains. This study presents a novel perspective on generalization of LLMs where
               the strength of large language models lies in their multiplicity of domain-specific
028
               subnetworks, allowing them to excel in various in-domain tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have achieved excellent performance on multiple tasks, demon-
034
      strating impressive generalization capacity in practical scenarios. Recently, there has been growing
035   interest in the generalization of LLMs (Bayazit et al., 2023; Choenni et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024).
036   Bayazit et al. (2023) indicated that knowledge-critical subnetworks are contained in pretrained lan-
037   guage models. Bhaskar et al. (2024) discovered that a specific set of attention heads in language
038   models act as the core part that ensures generalization by interacting with other attention heads.
039
      However, current methods mainly focus on the small scale of language models, such as BERT (De-
040
      vlin, 2018) or GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019). The study on the generalization of large language
041   models, such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), and Gemma (Team et al., 2024), remains underex-
042   plored despite numerous fine-tuning experiments conducted in previous studies.
043
044
      In this work, we investigate the larger LLMs directly, especially the publicly available LLaMA and
      Gemma models. We observe that weight parameters in LLMs act consistently when dealing with the
045
      same tasks but exhibit different behaviors across diverse tasks. Specifically, weight activations are
046
      more similar for analogous questions than for different tasks, suggesting a robust domain-specific
047   mechanism within the generalized LLM architecture. In Figure 2, we observe that the weights exhibit
048   an overlapping pattern when performing the same task. However, these weights tend to diverge when
049   considering different tasks. This raises a crucial question:
050
051                         Are all weights necessary for inference on a given task?
052
053   Previous pruning methods (Sun et al., 2024; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) have demonstrated that LLMs
      can maintain comparable performance even after portions of the weights are removed. However,

089   in Figure 1. Removing redundant weights in the target domain improved performance on related
090   tasks. However, we also discovery that redundant weights in one domain can be significant for other
091   domains, limiting the generalization capacity of these specialized subnetworks.
092   In summary, our findings enhance the understanding of the generalization mechanisms of LLMs.
093   They underscore the importance of having various specialized subnetworks to ensure model perfor-
094   mance in each specialized domain. By ensembling all these specialized subnetworks, the general-
095   ization capacity of the LLMs can be achieved.

      Network Pruning. Pruning is an effective method for network compression that removes layers
100
      or parameters to produce sparse networks (LeCun et al., 1989), which can be roughly divided into
101
      structured and unstructured schemes. Structured pruning, also known as activation pruning, remov-
102   ing redundant channels or filters to decrease computational complexity and memory demands while
103   maintaining the overall network architecture. To be specific, Babaeizadeh et al. (2016) develops a
104   fully automated pruning algorithm leveraging the correlation between neuron activations in hidden
105   layers. Dubey et al. (2018) employs coreset representations in network pruning, eliminating unnec-
106   essary weights and neuronal activations within CNNs for compression. Recently, there has been
107   a surge in integrating LLMs into pruning (Ma et al., 2023; Bansal et al., 2022; Voita et al., 2023;
      Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Bhaskar et al., 2024), which demonstrates the prompt-dependent


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and task-specific sparsity in LLM structural components (e.g., attention heads and MLP neurons).
109   Unlike structured pruning, unstructured pruning removes weights without considering the overall
110   architecture, targeting specific weights or neurons on a finer granular level (Han et al., 2015; Hoang
111   & Liu, 2023; Paul et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024). In this paper, we mostly utilize unstructured pruning
112   based on Sun et al. (2024) to eliminate redundant weights.
113
      Subnetworks. A group of representative studies evidence that neural networks are composed of
114
      modular subnetworks, each responsible for specific subtasks (Lepori et al., 2023; Choenni et al.,
115   2023; Hupkes et al., 2020). Concretely, Nooralahzadeh & Sennrich (2023) introduces task-specific
116   subnetworks to bridge the gap in cross-lingual transfer. Similarly, Choenni et al. (2023) proposes
117   language-specific subnetworks that guide selective parameter sharing in multilingual training to en-
118   hance positive transfer. Bayazit et al. (2023) discovers a sparse subnetwork within GPT-2 that in
119   charge of specific collections of relation knowledge, with no performance drop when it is removed.
120   Previous work manages to localize the knowledge-critical weights in LMs. In this work, we find
121   redundant weights that are not necessary for input tasks. By removing the redundant weights, the
122   sparse model achieves higher in-domain performance.
123   Generalization. The generalization of neural networks has been a long-standing research in NLP
124   community. McCoy et al. (2019) reveals that fine-tuning BERT with different random seeds results
125   in varying generalization, despite achieving similar accuracy on in-domain tasks. Another line of
126   efforts delve into out-of-distribution setting, which finds that performance consistently improves
127   even when in-domain performance appears to be saturated. A striking phenomenon, “Grokking”,
128   is recently proposed by Pearce et al. (2023). When training a set of samll models on toy tasks,
129
      the models can process and understand unseen data after an extended training phase, effectively
      leveraging the accumulated knowledge.

516
      In this work, we explored the role of weight neurons in large language models (LLMs) and their
517
      specialization across different domain tasks. By analyzing weight scores derived from various tasks,
518   we established that weight neurons exhibit distinct activation patterns tailored to specific domains,
519   suggesting the presence of specialized subnetworks within the generalized LLM architecture. We
520   extended our analysis by probing specialized subnetworks for domains such as Medicine, Code, and
521   Math. Despite achieving improved performance within specialized domains, these pruned subnet-
522   works exhibited reduced generalization capabilities compared to the full model. This observation
523   suggests that the pruned subnetworks, though optimized for specific tasks, may lack the robustness
524   required for broader generalization. In conclusion, our study reveals the inherent complexity and
525   specialization within LLMs, emphasizing the need for balanced training regimens that foster both
526
      specialized and general capabilities. Future research could further optimize these domain-specific
      pathways, releasing the full potential of large language models and bolster their generalization per-

      formance.
