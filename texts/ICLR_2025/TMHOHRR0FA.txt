011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) remains a fundamental challenge in com-
014            binatorial optimization, inspiring diverse algorithmic strategies. This paper re-
015            visits the “heatmap + Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)” paradigm that has re-
016            cently gained traction for learning-based TSP solutions. Within this framework,
017            heatmaps encode the likelihood of edges forming part of the optimal tour, and
018            MCTS refines this probabilistic guidance to discover optimal solutions. Contem-
019
               porary approaches have predominantly emphasized the refinement of heatmap
               generation through sophisticated learning models, inadvertently sidelining the
020
               critical role of MCTS. Our extensive empirical analysis reveals two pivotal in-
021
               sights: 1) The configuration of MCTS strategies profoundly influences the solu-
022            tion quality, demanding meticulous tuning to leverage their full potential; 2) Our
023            findings demonstrate that a rudimentary and parameter-free heatmap, derived from
024            the intrinsic k-nearest nature of TSP, can rival or even surpass the performance of
025            complicated heatmaps, with strong generalizability across various scales. Empiri-
026            cal evaluations across various TSP scales underscore the efficacy of our approach,
027            achieving competitive results. These observations challenge the prevailing focus
028            on heatmap sophistication, advocating a reevaluation of the paradigm to harness
029            both components synergistically.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) stands as a quintessential challenge in combinatorial op-
035
      timization, drawing considerable interest from both theoretical and applied research communities.
036   As a problem characterized by NP-hardness, the TSP has become a benchmark for evaluating the
037   efficacy of novel algorithmic strategies in determining optimal or near-optimal solutions efficiently
038   (Applegate et al., 2009). It has significant practical applications in domains such as logistics, trans-
039   portation, manufacturing, and telecommunications, where finding efficient routes is crucial for min-
040   imizing costs and improving efficiency (Helsgaun, 2017; Nagata & Kobayashi, 2013).
041
      Recent advancements in machine learning have inspired a fresh wave of methodologies for tack-
042   ling the TSP, particularly through the lens of the “heatmap + Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)”
043   paradigm. This innovative approach, first introduced by Fu et al. (2021), leverages learning mech-
044   anisms to approximate edge probabilities in forming part of the optimal path (heatmap), while em-
045   ploying MCTS to intelligently search and refine this probabilistic scaffold. The method successfully
046   solved TSP instances with 10,000 nodes, inspiring more researchers to contribute to this solving
047   paradigm (Qiu et al., 2022; Sun & Yang, 2023; Min et al., 2024).
048   Historically, the focus within this paradigm has predominantly centered on the design and refine-
049   ment of effective heatmaps, as these serve as the foundational guides for MCTS. Sophisticated
050   learning-based designs, ranging from supervised learning (Fu et al., 2021) to diffusion models (Sun
051   & Yang, 2023), are employed to predict these probabilities with high accuracy, thus assuming that
052   the sophistication of the heatmap directly correlates with solution quality. However, this singular
053   emphasis may have inadvertently overshadowed the critical contribution of the MCTS phase. The
      MCTS stage, tasked with exploring the solution space given the probabilistic cues from the heatmap,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      has the potential to significantly refine or degrade the final solution quality depending on its strategy
055   configuration (Xia et al., 2024).
056
057   In this study, we rigorously examine the underexplored dimension of the MCTS strategy within the
058
      heatmap-guided paradigm, challenging the prevailing narrative that sophisticated heatmaps are the
      singular key to superior solutions. We demonstrate that with carefully calibrated MCTS strategies,
059
      the efficacy of the solution can be markedly enhanced, spotlighting the need for a dual focus on both
060
      components of the paradigm. While SoftDist (Xia et al., 2024) also noticed that the competitive
061   performance can be obtained through a simple parameterization of heatmaps, we move forward
062   arguing that a parameter-free heatmap deriving from the k-nearest generalizable prior can achieve
063   better. Our findings not only uncover the potential of such naı̈ve methods, but also provide novel
064   perspectives to rethink essential factors in methodical designs for TSP.
065
      Overall, our contributions are threefold:
066
067          • We elucidate the substantial role of MCTS configurations in optimizing TSP solutions,
068            encouraging a reevaluation of existing priorities in algorithm design. We demonstrate that
069            fine-tuning MCTS parameters such as exploration constant and node expansion criteria can
070            significantly impact solution quality.
071          • We demonstrate that simplicity in heatmap construction, based on k-nearest statistics and
072            devoid of parameters and training, does not necessarily equate to inferior results. Further-
073            more, it exhibits scalability across various sizes, thereby expanding the possibilities for
074            methodological innovations.
075          • We present empirical evidence of our approach achieving competitive performance across
076            large TSP scales.
077
078   These insights collectively advocate for a more balanced integration of learning and search, poten-
079   tially guiding future research endeavors towards more holistic algorithmic frameworks that better
080   capitalize on the inherent symbiosis of these components.

082   2   R ELATED W ORKS
083
084   The integration of machine learning techniques with combinatorial optimization has led to signif-
085   icant advancements in solving large-scale TSPs. We focus on approaches that have demonstrated
086   capability in addressing TSP instances with tens of thousands of nodes, particularly those employing
087   the heatmap-guided MCTS paradigm. For additional details on the neural solver for addressing TSP,
088   please see the Appendix A.
089
      Fu et al. (2021) introduced a pioneering framework combining graph convolutional networks with
090   attention mechanisms to generate edge probability heatmaps for TSP. This approach guides MCTS
091   to refine complete tours instead of constructing partial solutions, marking a significant departure
092   from traditional MCTS methods. The framework’s efficacy in handling large-scale problems has
093   established it as a foundational approach for subsequent learning-based TSP solvers with heatmap-
094   guided MCTS.
095
      Building upon this foundation, several methods have emerged, focusing predominantly on enhanc-
096   ing heatmap generation through sophisticated learning models. Qiu et al. (2022) proposed DIMES,
097   a differentiable meta-solver employing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to parameterize the solu-
098   tion space. Sun & Yang (2023) introduced DIFUSCO, leveraging diffusion models for heatmap
099   generation, while Min et al. (2024) developed UTSP, an unsupervised learning approach to address
100   challenges in data efficiency and reward sparsity. More recently, approaches like SoftDist (Xia et al.,
101   2024) have begun to explore simpler heatmap construction methods, introducing a temperature coef-
102   ficient to craft heatmaps based on distance. This shift towards simpler heatmaps suggests a growing
103   recognition of the potential overemphasis on heatmap sophistication.
104   While existing methods have primarily focused on refining heatmap generation, our work addresses
105   a gap in the literature by critically examining the balance between heatmap construction and MCTS
106   optimization. We analyze the effects of MCTS hyperparameters and propose a k-nearest prior
107   heatmap, thereby enhancing our understanding of how heatmap-guided MCTS can be optimized
      for large-scale TSP solutions

130
131   The MCTS framework is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), which is represented by
132   states, actions and the transition between states. The implementation is built upon the framework
133   proposed by Fu et al. (2021), integrating learned heatmaps to enhance search efficiency.
134
      In this framework, each state π represents a feasible TSP tour, a permutation of the index of cities.
135                                                                                                        N

136   The initial state is constructed by iteratively selecting edges with a probability proportional to ePij .
137
      Actions are defined as k-opt moves, which modify the current tour by replacing k edges to create a
      new tour. The metric of a state π is defined as the tour length L(π).
138
139   Algorithmically, the MCTS for solving TSP consists of four primary steps:
140
      1. Initialization: The weight matrix W is computed from the heatmap matrix P N , with each
141   element defined as Wij = 100 × PijN , representing the probability of selecting edge (i, j). This
142   approach adheres to the method introduced by Fu et al. (2021). The access matrix Q is initialized
143   with all elements set to zero (Qij = 0) to track the frequency with which each edge is selected,
144   while M is initialized to zero to enumerate the total number of actions. Additionally, a candidate set
145   is constructed for each node, and subsequent edges are exclusively selected from this candidate set.
146
      2. Simulation: A number of actions (candidate k-opt moves) are generated by selecting edges based
147   on current state. The selection probability of an edge is proportional to its potential, calculated as:

168   4     T HE I MPORTANCE OF H YPERPARAMETER T UNING
169
170   In this section, we highlight the often-overlooked importance of properly configuring MCTS hy-
171   perparameters, a factor crucial for improving solution quality, while recent studies have primarily
172   focused on advanced heatmap generation.
173
174   4.1    OVERVIEW OF H YPERPARAMETERS
175
176   We have identified several key hyperparameters that significantly influence the performance of the
177   heatmap-guided MCTS approach:
178   Alpha: Controls the exploration-exploitation balance in MCTS by weighting the exploration term
179   in Eq. (2). Higher values promote exploration of unvisited nodes, while lower values favor exploita-
180   tion of known good paths.
181
      Beta: Influences the MCTS Back-propagation process as defined in Eq. (3). Higher values lead to
182
      more aggressive updates, potentially causing rapid shifts in search strategy based on recent results.
183
184   Max Depth: Sets the maximum depth for k-opt moves during MCTS simulation. Larger values
185   allow more complex moves at the cost of increased computation time.
186   Max Candidate Num: Limits the candidate set size at each node, sparsifying the graph and af-
187   fecting both algorithm speed and solution quality. Smaller sets accelerate search but may overlook
188   optimal solutions.
189
      Param H: Determines the number of simulation attempts per move, with a maximum of Param H×
190
      N simulations. Higher values provide more comprehensive exploration at the expense of increased
191   computation time.
192
193   Use Heatmap: A boolean parameter that decides whether candidate set construction is guided by
194
      heatmap probabilities or distances. When enabled, it can enhance search efficiency if heatmaps are
      accurate.
195
196   Time Limit: Sets the overall search time limit to Time Limit × N seconds. MCTS terminates
197   and returns a solution upon reaching this limit. The default value is 0.1, as specified in Fu et al.
198   (2021).
199   These hyperparameters collectively influence the MCTS process. In our tuning experiments, we
200   optimize all parameters except Time Limit, which remains fixed unless otherwise specified.
201

      seek a balance between performance and efficiency.

529
530   This study revisited the “Heatmap + MCTS” paradigm for large-scale TSP, highlighting the under-
531   estimated importance of MCTS hyperparameter tuning. We demonstrated that careful tuning, es-
532   pecially of parameters like Max Candidate Num, can drastically improve solution quality, even
533   with simple or non-informative heatmaps. To this end, we introduced a parameter-free k-nearest
534   prior heatmap, which achieves competitive performance against complex learning-based methods
535   across various TSP sizes. This simple yet effective approach challenges the prevailing focus on
536
      sophisticated models, showing that leveraging basic statistical prior of TSP can often be sufficient,
      particularly when scaling to large instances. Future work should explore more adaptive search strate-
537
      gies within MCTS or improve tuning efficiency through advanced optimization techniques.
538
539   Overall, this study contributes a nuanced understanding that could pivot future research towards
      more balanced and efficacious integration of learning and search in TSP algorithms.

      A     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
      Approaches using machine learning to address the Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) generally fall
705   into two distinct groups based on how they generate solutions. The first group, known as construc-
706   tion methods, incrementally forms a path by sequentially adding cities to an unfinished route, fol-
707   lowing an autoregressive process until the entire path is completed. The second group, improvement
708   methods, starts with a complete route and continually applies local search operations to improve the
709   solution.
710
711   Construction Methods Since Vinyals et al. (2015); Bello et al. (2016) introduced the autoregres-
712   sive combinatorial optimization neural solver, numerous advancements have emerged in subsequent
713   years (Deudon et al., 2018; Kool et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2021; 2020). These
714
      include enhanced network architectures (Kool et al., 2019), more sophisticated deep reinforcement
      learning techniques (Khalil et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2019; Choo et al., 2022), and improved training
715
      methods (Kim et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2022). For large-scale TSP, Pan et al. (2023) adopts a hierarchi-
716
      cal divide-and-conquer approach, breaking down the complex TSP into more manageable open-loop
717   TSP sub-problems.
718
719   Improvement Methods In contrast to construction methods, improvement-based solvers leverage
720   neural networks to progressively refine an existing feasible solution, continuing the process until the
721   computational limit is reached. These improvement methods are often influenced by traditional local
722   search techniques like k-opt, and have been shown to deliver impressive results in various previous
723   studies (Chen & Tian, 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Hudson et al., 2021). Ye et al. (2024)
724   implements a divide-and-conquer approach, using search-based methods to enhance the solutions of
725   smaller subproblems generated from the larger instances.
726   Recent breakthroughs in solving large-scale TSP problems (Fu et al., 2021; Qiu et al., 2022; Sun &
727   Yang, 2023; Min et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024), have incorporated Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS)
728   as an effective post-processing technique. These heatmaps serve as priors for guiding the MCTS,
729   resulting in impressive performance in large-scale TSP solutions, achieving state-of-the-art results.
730
731   Other Directions In addition to exploring solution methods for combinatorial optimization prob-
732   lems, some studies investigate intrinsic challenges encountered during the learning phase. These
733   include generalization issues during inference (Wang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
734
      2024) and multi-task learning (Wang & Yu, 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024) aimed at
      developing foundational models.

779   B.2     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
780
      We conducted experiments on the test set for heatmaps generated by these three methods, both with
781
      and without post-processing, using the default MCTS setting. Results are presented in Table 5.
782
783   Our findings reveal that heatmaps generated without post-processing generally exhibit performance
784   degradation, particularly for TSP-10000, where the gap increases by orders of magnitude. This
785
      underscores the importance of sparsification for large-scale instances and highlights the tendency of
      existing methodologies to overstate their efficacy in training complex deep learning models.
786
787   Interestingly, DIFUSCO’s heatmap without post-processing outperforms its post-processed coun-
788   terpart for TSP-1000, suggesting that the DIFUSCO model, when well-trained on this scale, can
789   generate helpful heatmap matrices to guide MCTS without additional refinement.
790   These results emphasize the critical role of post-processing in enhancing the performance of
791   learning-based heatmap generation methods for TSP, particularly as problem scales increase. They
792   also highlight the need for careful evaluation of model outputs and the potential for over-reliance on
793   post-processing to mask limitations in model training and generalization.
794
      The substantial performance gap between heatmaps with and without post-processing raises ques-
795   tions about the extent to which the reported performance gains can be attributed solely to the learning
796   modules of these methods. While the learning components undoubtedly contribute to the overall ef-
797   fectiveness, the significant impact of post-processing suggests that the raw output of the learning
798   models may not be as refined or directly applicable as previously thought.
799
      In light of these findings, we recommend that future research on heatmap-based methods for TSP
800
      provide a detailed description of their post-processing operations. Additionally, we suggest reporting
801   results both with and without post-processing to offer a more comprehensive understanding of the
802   method’s performance and the relative contributions of its learning and post-processing components.
803   This approach would foster greater transparency in the field and facilitate more accurate comparisons
804   between different methodologies.

1072   In addition to the grid search method employed in the main content of this paper, we also conducted
1073   hyperparameter tuning using the Sequential Model-based Algorithm Configuration (SMAC3) frame-
1074   work (Lindauer et al., 2022). SMAC3 is designed for optimizing algorithm configurations through
1075   an efficient and adaptive search process that balances exploration and exploitation of the hyperpa-
1076
       rameter space.
1077   The SMAC3 framework utilizes a surrogate model based on tree-structured Parzen estimators (TPE)
1078   to predict the performance of various hyperparameter configurations. This model is iteratively re-
1079   fined as configurations are evaluated, allowing SMAC3 to identify promising areas of the search
       space more effectively than traditional methods.
