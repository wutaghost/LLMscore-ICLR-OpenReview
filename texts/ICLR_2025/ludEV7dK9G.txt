011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Using zero-cost (ZC) metrics to estimate network performance instead of rely-
014                ing on expensive training processes has proven both its efficiency and efficacy
015
                   in Neural Architecture Search (NAS). However, a significant limitation of most
                   ZC proxies is their inconsistency, as reflected by the substantial variation in their
016
                   performance across different problems. Additionally, the design of current ZC
017
                   metrics is manual, which is a lengthy trial-and-error process and requires expert
018                knowledge to develop ZC metrics effectively. These challenges raise two ques-
019                tions: (1) Can we automate the design of ZC metrics? and (2) Can we utilize
020                the existing hand-crafted ZC metrics to synthesize a better one? In this study,
021                we propose a framework based on Symbolic Regression to automate the design
022                of ZC metrics. Our framework is not only highly extensible but also capable of
023                quickly producing a ZC metric with a strong positive rank correlation to network
024                performance across multiple problems within just a few minutes. Extensive exper-
025                iments on 13 problems in NAS-Bench-Suite-Zero, covering various search spaces
026
                   and tasks, demonstrate the superiority of our automatically designed proxies over
                   hand-crafted ones. By integrating our proxy metrics into an evolutionary algo-
027
                   rithm, we could identify a network architecture with comparable performance on
028
                   the CIFAR-10 dataset within 15 minutes using a single GeForce RTX 3090 GPU1 .

066   they may demonstrate negative correlations on others. Recent studies have attempted to address
067   this issue when newly proposed ZC proxies (e.g., ZiCo, MeCo, SWAP) achieve positive rank
068   correlations across all testing problems (Li et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024). In
069   these cases, a network that receives a high ZC score is also likely to exhibit high performance, and
070   vice versa. However, despite achieving positive rank correlations, the scores for some problems
071   remain close to 0.0, indicating a lack of correlation between the ZC scores and the networks’
072   performance. In this study, our target is to design a ZC metric that yields not only the consistent
073   order-preserving ability but also a high positive rank correlation across various problems2 .

084   Most proposed ZC metrics are manually designed with expert knowledge. Can we automate the
085   design of efficient ZC metrics to form the proxies that are better than hand-crafted ones? The
086   practicability of this approach has been demonstrated in several studies. EZNAS (Akhauri et al.,
087   2022) implemented Symbolic Regression to form a ZC metric by combining three low-level features
088
      of networks: weights, activations, and gradients. While EZNAS only considered convolutional
      neural networks, Auto-Prox (Wei et al., 2024) extended the framework to design ZC metrics for
089
      vision transformer architectures. Additionally, a single run of Auto-Prox aims to search for a ZC
090
      metric that could perform well across multiple problems instead of a single problem as in EZNAS.
091   UP-NAS (Huang et al., 2024) designed a new ZC metric by combining higher-level features of
092   networks (i.e., hand-crafted ZC metrics) and taking their weighted sum, in which the weights of
093   metrics are found by using a neural network. However, a search process is needed to obtain suitable
094   weights whenever we handle a new problem. In this paper, we focus on the automatic designing
095   of efficient ZC metrics. Table 1 highlights the differences between our proposed framework and
096   previous approaches that also automate the design of ZC metrics. We discuss in detail the differences
097   in Section 3. Generally, our framework is designed to synthesize a new ZC metric from existing
098   hand-crafted ones, and the outcome metric could perform well across diverse problems. Our method
099
      offers high extensibility, making it straightforward to adapt to various problems or modify input
      features to enhance the performance of the resulting metric. Our main contributions are as follows:

119   2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121   Limitation of ZC-NAS metrics Zero-cost metrics are widely employed in NAS due to their ability
122   to estimate the network performance with a trivial cost (Krishnakumar et al., 2022). However, sev-
123   eral studies have indicated their inconsistent order-preserving ability across different problems (Ab-
124   delfattah et al., 2021; Krishnakumar et al., 2022). For example, the ZC metric Grad-norm has the
125   Spearman rank correlation to network performance of 0.58 for the NAS-Bench-201 search space
126   but has a score of −0.21 for the NAS-Bench-NLP search space (Abdelfattah et al., 2021). Recent
127   proposed ZC metrics (e.g., ZiCo, MeCo, SWAP) have tackled this issue since they consistently
128
      yield a positive rank correlation across various problems. Nevertheless, their rank correlation scores
      are close to 0.0 for some cases (e.g., the ZiCo metric for the TransNAS-Bench-101-Macro search
129
      space), exhibiting the lack of correlation between the ZC metrics’ scores and the networks’ per-
130
      formance. Designing a ZC metric that exhibits a high correlation to networks’ performance and a
131   strong order-preserving ability across various problems is essential.
132
133   Automatic designing of ZC-NAS metrics While most ZC metrics are manually designed with
134   expert knowledge, several studies aim to design ZC metrics automatically (Akhauri et al., 2022;
135   Wei et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). EZNAS (Akhauri et al., 2022) proposed a framework that
136   uses Symbolic Regression (SR) to synthesize a ZC metric from three basic statistics of convolutional
137   neural networks: weights, activations, and gradients. The empirical results shown that the ZC metric
138   found by EZNAS could surpass hand-crafted metrics. Auto-Prox (Wei et al., 2024) then modified
139   the framework of EZNAS to find a ZC metric for vision transformer networks. In EZNAS, the
140
      dataset used by SR consists of networks’ information in a single problem. Such an approach might
      make the resulting ZC metric overfitted on the experimented problem and perform worse on the
141
      unseen problems. Auto-Prox solved this issue by combining multiple problems into the dataset of
142
      SR instead of a single one. However, Auto-Prox only tested the proposed framework in finding
143   the ZC proxy for candidate networks within the same search space. When facing a different search
144   space, the search process was re-conducted to synthesize a new ZC proxy. On the other hand,
145   both EZNAS and Auto-Prox are restricted from discovering more robust ZC metrics when they
146   synthesize new ZC proxies using low-level features of the network. Compared to EZNAS and Auto-
147   Prox, the extensibility of UP-NAS (Huang et al., 2024) is higher when it synthesized new ZC metrics
148   from high-level features: hand-crafted ZC metrics. UP-NAS assumed there is a linear relationship
149   between hand-crafted ZC metrics and used a neural network to find the weights of 13 ZC metrics
150   listed in NAS-Bench-Suite-Zero. A new ZC proxy was then formed by taking their weighted sum.
151
      However, the limitation of UP-NAS is similar to EZNAS as its dataset only covers a single problem.
      A new ZC metric thus needs to be searched for whenever handling a different NAS problem.
152
153
      NAS Benchmarks An NAS benchmark can be viewed as a database consisting of essential infor-
154
      mation about candidate networks (e.g., train/validation/test performance, the number of parameters)
155
      within the same search space. The NAS-Bench-101/201/301 benchmarks (Ying et al., 2019; Dong &
156   Yang, 2020; Siems et al., 2020) provide the performance of candidate networks for the image classi-
157   fication task with different datasets. The TransNAS-Bench-101-Micro/Macro benchmarks (Duan
158   et al., 2021) consider the network performance on other tasks beyond the image classification such
159   as scene classification, and object detection. The NAS-Bench-Suite-Zero benchmark (Krishnaku-
160   mar et al., 2022) is the collection of the aforementioned benchmarks and additionally computes the
161   scores for all candidate networks in terms of 13 various ZC metrics. The clear advantage of us-
      ing NAS benchmarks is that we can quickly evaluate the effectiveness of our methods and fairly

      compare them to others in the same setting. In this paper, besides using NAS benchmarks to vali-
163   date our proposed method, we utilize NAS benchmarks to create the dataset for our framework to
164   automatically learn new ZC metrics.

167   3     P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
168
169   Our proposed framework for automating the design of ZC-NAS metrics is based on Symbolic Re-
170   gression (SR). Given existing hand-crafted ZC metrics, we use SR to search for the most effective
171   way to combine these metrics using mathematical operators (e.g., add, mul). In this section, we
172   first outline our approach to building the dataset for putting into SR in Section 3.1. The proposed
173
      mechanism for evaluating the quality of ZC metrics during the search process is then detailed in
      Section 3.2. The method for representing the synthesized ZC proxies and the ways that SR discov-
174
      ers novel ZC proxies are presented in Sections 3.3 and 3.4, respectively. The entire search procedure
175
      of our framework is discussed in Section 3.5. While the use of SR for automatically designing ZC
176   metrics was introduced in EZNAS (Akhauri et al., 2022) and Auto-Prox (Wei et al., 2024), there are
177   notable differences between our framework and theirs, particularly in the construction of the input
178   dataset for SR, the mechanism for evaluating synthesized ZC metrics, and the search procedure.
179   These differences are thoroughly discussed in the following sections.
180
181
      3.1   DATASET B UILDING
182
183   Our approach to building the input dataset for SR closely resembles that of UP-NAS (Huang et al.,
184   2024), which uses hand-crafted ZC-NAS metrics as feature variables and validation performance of
185   networks as the ground truth. However, while the dataset of UP-NAS only covers a single problem,
186   our dataset encompasses the networks’ information across multiple problems. Although the sugges-
187   tion of integrating multiple problems into the input dataset of SR was proposed in Auto-Prox (Wei
188   et al., 2024), Auto-Prox uses the low-level features (i.e., network statistics), whereas we utilize the
189
      high-level features (i.e., hand-crafted ZC metrics).
190   When creating the input dataset, while the cost of computing the ZC proxy values is negligible,
191   the most significant computational cost arises from defining the ground truth (i.e., the network per-
192   formance). However, we overcome this obstacle by employing existing NAS benchmarks (e.g.,
193   NAS-Bench-101, NAS-Bench-201), which provide the performance of numerous candidate archi-
194   tectures across various search spaces and tasks. Using NAS benchmarks also demonstrates strong
195
      extensibility. Whenever a new NAS benchmark or hand-crafted ZC metric is introduced, we can
      quickly compute the ZC metric scores of networks at a negligible cost and enrich the input dataset
196
      by integrating the new information. Another way of defining the ground truth involves using super-
197
      nets (e.g., Once-for-All (Cai et al., 2020)) or performance predictors (e.g., XGBoost (White et al.,
198   2021)), for which configurations and weights are available. In this study, we primarily focus on NAS
199   benchmarks to utilize truly trained performance rather than predicted performance.
200
201
      3.2   O BJECTIVE E VALUATION
202
203
      Our target is to design a ZC metric that ranks candidate networks in the same order as their true
204   performance. We thus use Kendall’s τ rank correlation to evaluate the quality of ZC metrics gen-
205   erated by SR during the search process, similarly to EZNAS, UP-NAS, and Auto-Prox. However,
206   the evaluation methods used in EZNAS and UP-NAS are not applicable to our framework, as our
207   dataset comprises multiple problems rather than a single one. Auto-Prox, which also uses multiple
208   problems to build the input dataset, evaluates the quality of an arbitrary ZC metric by computing its
209   Kendall’s τ value for each problem and taking the weighted sum. This approach has its limitation.
210   In particular, because the weight for each problem must be pre-defined before searching, it may
211   cause the resulting ZC metric to be biased towards the problem with the highest weight. Setting
212
      equal weights for all problems is not effective due to the differences in Kendall’s τ values across
      problems, leading SR to focus disproportionately on the problem that produces the highest rank
213
      correlation and neglect others.
214

      Extensibility: We demonstrate the extensibility of our proposed framework by additionally ex-
500
      perimenting with two variants of the current input dataset (details of each dataset are presented in
501   Appendix A.1). The results detailed in Appendix A.1 demonstrate that augmenting the input dataset
502   with robust ZC metrics can further enhance the performance of our proposed framework. Addition-
503   ally, we hypothesize that the generalizability of our framework could be improved by using a dataset
504   that is augmented with a broader range of problems. Moreover, the metric found by the framework
505   using the dataset covering more problems delivers better general performance across all 13 problems
506   in NAS-Bench-Suite-Zero than the current best metric (i.e., Equation 2).
507   Lack of generalizability when searching on a single problem: We conduct additional experiments
508   to investigate the potential overfitting of our framework when searching with the input dataset that
509   only covers a single problem. The results detailed in Appendix A.2 reveal that the τ scores of
510   ZC metrics found by using the datasets that only cover a single problem significantly deteriorate
511   when evaluated on other problems. For instance, the metric found by using the dataset built on the
512   NB301-CF10 problem achieves a score of 0.48 for NB301-CF10 but has a negative score of -0.17
513   for NB201-CF10. Conversely, the ZC metric found by using the dataset containing three problems
514   achieves a more balanced performance across all problems.
515   Comparison to the search procedure in EZNAS: Appendix A.3 presents Kendall’s τ for the ZC
516   metrics designed using our proposed framework compared to the ones designed by the framework
517   in EZNAS on three problems: NB101-CF10, NB201-CF10, and NB301-CF10. The results show
518   that while our obtained ZC metrics are only slightly worse on one problem (i.e., NB301-CF10),
519   they are significantly better on the other two (i.e., NB101-CF10 and NB201-CF10), highlighting
520   the effectiveness of our approach over EZNAS. The potential issue with EZNAS, as indicated, is
521
      that it could eliminate high-quality parents and replace them with lower-quality offspring, which
      can lead to the offspring in the next generations being derived from inferior candidates. The search
522
      framework of EZNAS is thus less effective than ours when searching under the same budget.

525   5     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we propose a novel framework that leverages Symbolic Regression (SR) to automati-
528   cally design robust zero-cost (ZC) metrics for various NAS search spaces and tasks. Our approach
529   introduces several notable features. First, unlike previous frameworks, the input dataset of our
530   framework comprises diverse problems instead of a single one. We further introduce a novel eval-
531
      uation mechanism that assesses the quality of generated ZC metrics throughout the search process.
      This mechanism not only guides SR to identify expressions that correlate well with network perfor-
532
      mance across multiple problems but also prevents it from overfitting to a specific problem. Addi-
533
      tionally, our framework is highly extensible; incorporating more powerful hand-crafted ZC metrics
534   and diverse problems into the input dataset could enhance the effectiveness of resulting metrics
535   across various problems. Extensive experiments on NAS-Bench-Suite-Zero demonstrate both the
536   efficacy and efficiency of our method. Our framework could design a ZC metric with state-of-the-
537   art Kendall’s τ correlation on NAS-Bench-101/201/301 search spaces within 10 minutes, and our
538   metric remains competitive to hand-crafted metrics on TransNAS-Bench-101-Micro/Macro search
539   spaces. When combining our obtained metric with an evolutionary algorithm, we could figure out a
      network architecture with comparable performance in the DARTS search space within 15 minutes.
