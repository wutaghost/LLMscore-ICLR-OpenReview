009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           The emergence of foundational models has greatly improved performance across
012           various downstream tasks, with fine-tuning often yielding even better results.
013           However, existing fine-tuning approaches typically require access to model
014           weights and layers, leading to challenges such as managing multiple model copies
015           or inference pipelines, inefficiencies in edge device optimization, and concerns
016
              over proprietary rights, privacy, and exposure to unsafe model variants. In this pa-
              per, we address these challenges by exploring “Gray-box” fine-tuning approaches,
017
              where the model’s architecture and weights remain hidden, allowing only gradi-
018
              ent propagation. We introduce a novel yet simple and effective framework that
019           adapts to new tasks using two lightweight learnable modules at the model’s in-
020           put and output. Additionally, we present a less restrictive variant that offers more
021           entry points into the model, balancing performance with model exposure. We
022           evaluate our approaches across several backbones on benchmarks for text-image
023           alignment, text-video alignment, and sketch-image alignment. Our results demon-
024           strate that, despite having limited access to the model, our Gray-box approaches
025           achieve competitive performance with full-access fine-tuning methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      The recent surge in the development of foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022;
030
      2023; Oquab et al., 2023; Kirillov et al., 2023) has significantly advanced a wide range of down-
031   stream tasks, achieving state-of-the-art (SoTA) performance across various domains. These models
032   are typically deployed as pre-trained backbones that have been fine-tuned to adapt them to specific
033   domains or tasks. Common fine-tuning approaches include: 1) Full fine-tuning (Devlin et al., 2019;
034   Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), where all model parameters are updated; 2) Partial tuning, which adjusts
035   only a subset of parameters, often in the model’s final layers (Girshick et al., 2014; Dosovitskiy
036   et al., 2021); and 3) Integrating adapter modules (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2022) into the
037   model’s layers. However, adapting large foundation models for multiple diverse sub-tasks through
038   these conventional methods introduces several significant limitations:
039
           1. Duplication of deployment and storage: Current large foundation models are costly to
040
              share and serve, and deploying a dedicated fine-tuned version for each downstream task
041           exacerbates this burden. Managing multiple models not only increases storage and deploy-
042           ment complexity but also reduces efficiency, as demonstrated in the context of LLMs (Pope
043           et al., 2023; Lester et al., 2021).
044        2. Optimization for edge devices: Adapting foundation models for deployment on edge
045           devices requires careful optimization based on their weights and architecture (Lazarevich
046           et al., 2021; Kwon et al., 2022). Fine-tuning models by modifying their parameters often
047           demands repeated optimization for each device, making the process resource-intensive and
048           inefficient, particularly for large-scale deployments.
049        3. Privacy, safety, and intellectual property (IP) concerns: Granting full access to a
050           model’s layers and weights raises risks related to IP protection, safety, and privacy. Pub-
051           lishing or exposing weights can lead to unauthorized use (OpenAI, 2023), or the recovery
052           of sensitive training data (Haim et al., 2022). Moreover, it has been shown (Horwitz et al.,
053           2024) that LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuned models can be vulnerable to attacks capable
              of reconstructing the original model’s weights and performance.

      In this paper, we address these challenges by introducing a family of Gray-box fine-tuning tech-
072
      niques that keep the foundation model’s weights and layers fixed and hidden. Conventional White-
073
      box techniques allow full access to the pre-trained backbone architecture and weights, but are in-
074   herently limited by the challenges mentioned earlier. In contrast, Black-box methods restrict access
075   to only the model’s input and output, resulting in significant performance constraints. The Gray-
076   box approach offers a middle ground, exposing limited information about the model, which enables
077   it to effectively address these challenges. Specifically, we consider a scenario where the provider
078   of the backbone model offers one or more entry points to the pre-trained model (e.g., the original
079   input entry or intermediate layer entries). While keeping the weights and layers hidden, each en-
080   try point reveals: (1) the dimensionality of the layer at that entry point, and (2) the gradients of
081   the (application-dependent) loss with respect to the entry point inputs. This Gray-box setup has
082
      practical applications in real-world scenarios. For example, in hospital models used for medical
      image analysis, where patient data privacy and regulatory compliance are critical, the model owner
083
      might want to allow third parties to adapt the model for specific diagnoses without exposing sensi-
084
      tive data or the model’s proprietary structure (Bharati et al., 2022). While Federated Learning (FL)
085   also prioritizes privacy, it primarily focuses on data privacy by distributing training across nodes.
086   In contrast, our Gray-box framework emphasizes secure task adaptation while keeping the founda-
087   tional model’s structure hidden. Similarly, in persona-based models used for personalization tasks
088   (e.g. personalized recommendations or identity verification), fine-tuning may be required without
089   revealing personal data or the full model architecture (Zheng et al., 2016). Additionally, foundation
090   model providers may wish to offer adaptation capabilities to third parties while keeping the core
091   model architecture and weights concealed to protect intellectual property and prevent misuse. This
092   approach allows adaptation for specific domains or tasks while minimizing the risks associated with
093
      full model exposure.
094   We explore two variants of the Gray-box framework: one that permits multiple entry points (thus
095   exposing more model information) and another that restricts access to only the original input entry.
096   We refer to these variants as LightGray-box and DarkGray-box, respectively, where the shade re-
097   flects the level of information exposed to the user during fine-tuning. Figure 1 demonstrates these
098   settings, which offer flexible, efficient, and more secure solutions to the challenges outlined above
099
      by leveraging a pre-trained foundation model while keeping it fixed and concealed. In the follow-
      ing sections, we detail how our framework effectively addresses real-world challenges, enabling
100
      model adaptation with minimal exposure or modification, and demonstrating the practicality of our
101
      Gray-box approaches. A common Black-box approach to adapting an existing foundation model
102   involves training additional layers on top of its output features (Radford et al., 2021; Devlin et al.,
103   2019; He et al., 2022; Oquab et al., 2023). However, this method relies solely on the information
104   provided by the model’s output features, missing the opportunity to leverage the foundation model’s
105   computational power for further adaptation. Our Gray-box framework addresses this limitation by
106   allowing modifications to the input or the injection of “middleware” features, as discussed in this
107   paper, thereby unlocking more effective fine-tuning potential. Table 1 summarizes the benefits and
      requirements of these fine-tuning approaches.

137   We summarize our contributions as follows:
138
139          • We introduce a new paradigm for effectively re-use pre-trained foundation models, en-
140            abling their adaptation to new domains and tasks while balancing effectiveness, proprietary
141            protection, safety, and efficiency, exploring various options along this spectrum.
142          • We propose two Gray-box frameworks, DGA and LGA, which leverage a pre-trained foun-
143            dation model while keeping it intact and frozen, allowing only limited access. Our novel
144            DarkGray-Box Input/Output Adapters (DGA) framework adapts the foundation model for
145            new domain-specific tasks by modifying only its input and output spaces, which was not

147          • We conduct an extensive ablation study to assess the capabilities of input and output
148            adapters, both individually and in combination, providing deeper insights into their roles
149            and effectiveness.
150          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our Gray-box approaches across various computer
151            vision tasks and benchmarks, achieving results that are competitive with, or on par with,
152            White-box baselines, all while keeping the foundation model sealed.

154   2    R ELATED W ORK
155
156   Prefix and Prompt Tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021) are methods
157   proposed as lightweight alternatives to full fine-tuning for Large Language Models (LLMs). Instead
158   of modifying all model parameters, these methods optimize a new set of input tokens for each NLP
159   task. Prompt Tuning (Lester et al., 2021) focuses on optimizing a token sequence added to the
160   first transformer’s layer, while Prefix Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) and Prompt Tuning 2 (Liu et al.,
161   2021) propose optimizing a separate sequence added to each transformer layer. Due to unstable
      optimization when directly training prefix tokens, the Prefix-Tuning approach (Li & Liang, 2021)


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      trains a matrix P , which is projected through a trainable MLP layer to compute the prefix added to
163   the existing prompt input. Prefix-Tuning involves learning separate prefixes for both the encoder and
164   decoder components of the LLM, inserted appropriately during inference. Depending on the task,
165   these methods have proven effective with prefixes ranging from 10 to 200 learned tokens, along
166   with their associated MLP layer. In this work, we simplify this approach by directly optimizing
167   just two tokens for a single text encoder without additional components. Specifically, we use the
168   first token as an attached prefix and the second as a “shift” token added to all original input tokens.
169   Consequently, our approach requires only one extra token per prompt or task, which is particularly
170   valuable for text encoders with limited context length (e.g. CLIP, which is limited to 77 tokens in
171
      total).
172   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) was initially proposed as an effective lightweight
173   alternative to full fine-tuning for transformer-based large language models (LLMs), and later to
174   Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023). Instead of updating all model pa-
175   rameters, LoRA learns two n × r matrices that are multiplied to form an n × n matrix of a low
176   rank r, where r is a hyper-parameter. The low-rank matrix is then added to the original model’s
177
      matrix. LoRA has demonstrated competitive results with full fine-tuning while being significantly
      more parameter-efficient. However, LoRA requires prior knowledge of the model’s architecture to
178
      choose the appropriate layers, match exact dimensions, and determine the matrix ranks. For exam-
179
      ple, in transformer layers, the Q, K, and V matrices across multiple layers have been shown to be
180   effective choices for applying LoRA. Additionally, if the learned components are stored separately
181   from the original model, LoRA necessitates a different computational flow in inference, altering
182   the intermediate features by applying these new components. Although LoRA could be considered
183   a “gray-box” approach due to the ability to hide the original model’s weights, recent work (Hor-
184   witz et al., 2024) demonstrated methods to effectively reconstruct the original model’s weights us-
185   ing LoRA fine-tuned models, making it more accurately associated with a “white-box” framework.
186   Furthermore, LoRA requires custom implementations for different architectures, which have been
187   developed for a variety of structures (e.g., linear, Conv2D, embeddings). In contrast, DGA assumes
188
      no access to the model weights, no prior knowledge of internal layers, and does not require selecting
      any hyper-parameters. Our approach relies solely on the gradient flow through the original model
189
      and preserves the model’s original structure, maintaining the inference pipeline intact between the
190
      input and output across all tasks and domains.
191
192   Co-CoOp and MaPLe A different lightweight fine-tuning approach is Co-CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022),
193   a CLIP-based architecture designed to enhance the integration of visual and textual modalities for
194
      image classification. Co-CoOp concatenates the visual encoder with the textual encoder, inserting
      a learned network between them. It processes the image feature vector through a learned MLP,
195
      generating a fix number of visual tokens that are added as a prefix to the textual input of the text
196
      encoder. i.e. this approach conditions the textual input in the visual output. Although Co-CoOp
197   keeps CLIP frozen, this design requires both modalities during each inference, limiting the genera-
198   tion of non-conditioned textual feature vectors, an essential capability for tasks like Image Retrieval
199   where query (text) and images (gallery) are encoded separately. Similarly, MaPLe (Khattak et al.,
200   2023) further improves upon Co-CoOp by learning shared vectors projected into different layers
201   of the CLIP textual and visual encoders, using learnable MLP network. MaPLe can be seen as an
202   extension of Prefix-Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) for classification tasks, freezing the model and al-
203   lowing internal tokens to be learned, which respects the “LightGray-box” framework. We adapt a
204   different version of this approach to our new tasks, where indepedent vectors are learned for each
205
      layer with no shared layers that significantly increase the number of learned parameters. We refer to
      this light-weight approach as LGA, in this paper.
206
207   Model thievery has been extensively studied in the context of machine learning models (Tramèr
208   et al., 2016; Krishna et al., 2020), particularly neural networks. Sha et al. (2023) introduced a learn-
209   ing approach to replicate a pre-trained transformer encoder by constructing a similar-performing
210   encoder based on the original model’s output features. Milli et al. (2019) presented techniques for
211
      reconstructing model weights, given the specific architecture of a two-layer MLP and the propagated
      gradients. Similarly, Horwitz et al. (2024) successfully recovered original transformer weights from
212
      LoRA fine-tuned versions of the model, while Béguelin et al. (2021) proposed a method to recover
213
      the weights of a (private) linear classification head using its (public) backbone feature extractor.
214
215   In this context, the potential theft of model weights not only poses a risk of model misuse (Bom-
      masani et al., 2021), but also raises further concerns, as Haim et al. (2022) demonstrated a method


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      for recovering training data samples from the model’s weights. In this work, we propose a fine-
217   tuning framework that minimizes the risk of exposing model weights, aligning with the findings of
218   current research. Importantly, while it is not yet practical or feasible to recover an arbitrary model’s
219   architecture and weights based solely on input gradients, we do not claim that this is impossible or
220   inherently difficult, leaving this exploration for future research.
221
      In summary, “White-box” and “LightGray-box” methods have been explored in NLP and classifi-
222
      cation tasks by incorporating additional components or tokens into the model’s intermediate layers.
223   While input adapters have been studied in the context of LLMs, their application in the image domain
224   has not been thoroughly investigated, as we do in this paper. We extend this exploration through our
225   LGA approach, which draws inspiration from these methods, and further develop a more restrictive
226   DGA approach that preserves the original foundation model’s computational flow.

      3     M ETHOD

230
      In this section, we introduce our approach for fine-tuning a foundation model F (e.g. CLIP, BLIP) for
231
      new domain-specific tasks without exposing its architecture or modifying its weights. We propose
232   two fine-tuning settings, termed DarkGray-box and LightGray-box settings, both of which offer
233   lightweight fine-tuning options, and leverage the pre-trained backbone model F while preserving its
234   integrity and privacy.
235
236   3.1   G RAY- BOX S ETTINGS
237
238   DarkGray-box: In this setting, the internals of F are completely hidden, akin to a black-box ap-
239   proach. The only exposed components are the input and output adapters, which are external train-
240   able modules plugged into the input and output of the backbone model. To train the input adapter,
241
      this setting requires access to the gradients computed by back-propagation through the backbone
      model. This means that a gradient tensor corresponding to the final layer of the input adapter is
242
      exposed — hence the term DarkGray instead of Black. Importantly, the backbone model’s archi-
243
      tecture and weights remain hidden, and only the adapters are trained. Our approach learns only a
244   minimal number of parameters (approximately 0.4% of the total model parameters). In this context,
245   we address two types of input modalities: images and text.
246
247
      LightGray-box: In this more relaxed setting, the provider introduces additional entry points where
      task-dependent information can be injected into the model’s intermediate layers. This enables better
248
      adaptation to a domain-specific task, enhancing flexibility without compromising the advantages
249
      of the gray-box model setup. Specifically, we optimize a set of learnable tokens injected into the
250   transformer layers of F , thereby influencing attention scores without accessing or modifying the
251   weights or layers. Although this approach accesses the model’s internal data paths, it preserves the
252   internal architecture and weights, retaining the advantages of a gray-box setting. It is important to
253   note that while the model layers remain hidden, this setting requires access to their input tokens, and
254   allowing gradients to propagate through them.
255
256   3.2   A DAPTERS
257
258   In this section, we outline a simple solution for the settings discussed above. Our DarkGray-Box
259   Input/Output Adapters (DGA) setting transforms the original model’s function F (x) into BF (Ax),
260
      where A and B are lightweight adapters (linear operators), as opposed to modifying the function
      F directly. We initialize A and B as the identity function to match F (x) = BF (Ax). The in-
261
      put adapter A learns to transform the model’s input into a representation that better aligns with
262
      task-specific requirements, while the output adapter B applies a simple linear transformation to the
263   model’s output.
264
265   Figure 2 provides an overview of our input adapters. Below, we describe the architecture of our input
266
      adapters for both text and image modalities, as well as the output adapter applied to the model’s
      output features.
267
268   Visual Input Adapter: For image inputs, the visual adapter consists of learned 2D convolutional
269   layers that preserve the original dimensions of the input image. Since no activation function is
      included, the visual adapter functions as an affine transformation on the image pixel space. As we

      Textual Input Adapter: For text inputs, we draw inspiration from previous works (Li & Liang,
      2021; Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021) and train new textual tokens for the text encoder. How-
291
      ever, unlike these methods, we find that optimizing just two tokens—the extra token and the shift
292
      token—is sufficient. The extra token is a learned token that is attached to the original input se-
293   quence. Due to the transformer’s positional invariance (Vaswani et al., 2017), and the fact that
294   positional encoding is not applied to this token, it can be flexibly inserted at any position within the
295   input sequence. The shift token is another learned token that is added to each of the original input
296   tokens, effectively “shifting” them within the token embedding space. Thus, this approach requires
297   only one extra token per prompt, which is particularly valuable for text encoders with limited context
298   length (e.g. CLIP, which is limited to a total of 77 tokens).
299   Intermediate Inputs: In the LightGray-box setting, we enhance adaptability by injecting learnable,
300   task-specific tokens into each transformer layer of the backbone model F . While the model layers
301   remain hidden and fixed, these tokens influence the output of each layer by modifying the attention
302   scores. This approach effectively extends the concept of prompt tuning (Liu et al., 2021) to both
303   visual and textual encoders across multiple tasks.
304
      Output Adapters: These adapters are applied to the model’s output feature vector. For both image
305   and text modalities, we implement the output adapters as simple linear layer on top of the feature
306   vector space, similar to the linear probing approach (Oquab et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021).

523   6   S UMMARY AND D ISCUSSION
524
525   In this paper, we addressed the challenges of fine-tuning pre-trained foundation models by introduc-
526   ing two novel approaches. The first, the DarkGray-box setting, keeps the model layers and weights
527   concealed, allowing adapters to operate only on the model’s input and output. The second, the
528   LightGray-box setting, offers limited access to the model’s internal structure, enabling modifica-
529   tions to attention scores without exposing the model’s weights. We also discussed the risks of model
530   theft, where it is possible to create a similarly performing model using output features, though this
531
      process can be very expensive in terms of time and computational resources. However, if a model’s
      structure and weights are fully exposed, it becomes easy for others to violate intellectual property
532
      (IP) rights and use it without incurring any cost or effort, which undermines the original invest-
533
      ment. While LGA is tailored for transformer-based architectures, our proposed DGA approach,
534   which employs only input and output adapters, is applicable to a wide range of foundation models,
535   including CNN-based architectures (as demonstrated in Appendix A), such as CLIP and other multi-
536   modal models. This generality allows our approach to adapt effectively across various downstream
537   tasks and domains. However, our experiments indicate that this form of adaptation is less effective
538   for more distant domains (e.g. sketch), where modifying the model’s weights becomes essential.
539   Despite this, our method demonstrates robustness and adaptability, achieving results that are often
      competitive with, and sometimes surpass, white-box alternatives.

904   D    F URTHER D ISCUSSION
905
906   Recent studies Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024) have proposed black-box prompt optimization
907   techniques for Vision-Language models, aiming to enhance performance without requiring access to
908   the backbone model. These methods achieve this by optimizing the input textual prompt, focusing
909   exclusively on text manipulation Wang et al. (2024) or text-to-text mapping Liu et al. (2024), without
910   addressing the visual modality. More specifically, they are designed to optimize textual prompts for
911   tasks such as 16-shot classification. However, this approach limits their applicability to scenarios
912   heavily reliant on the visual domain. For instance, tasks such as Video or Sketch retrieval, which are
913   fundamentally based on visual inputs, remain outside the capabilities of these methods. In contrast,
914
      our work addresses such visual domain challenges, expanding the utility and applicability of black-
      box fine-tuning to a broader range of tasks beyond text-focused optimizations.
915
916   To further illustrate the broader applicability of our approach, Figure 4 presents a demonstration of
917   general schemes for handling multiple tasks or domains. The bottom part of the figure illustrates the
      naive approach of managing each task or domain with its own optimized model. In contrast, the top


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      part of the figure shows a single optimized backbone model capable of handling all inputs with the
919   use of input/output adapters. First, each input is processed using the appropriate lightweight input
920   adapter. Next, the aggregated batch across all tasks is fed into the model, which produces outputs for
921   each item. Finally, each output is post-processed with its corresponding output adapter to generate
922   the final result.
923
      Experimental Validation: To substantiate these claims, we conducted inference experiments com-
924
      paring two setups: 1) A single backbone combined with 10 pairs of DGA adapters (for 10 different
925   tasks or domains), 2) Ten separate backbones without using our DGA framework. In each setup,
926   we utilize CLIP encoders to encode 10 sampled sets of 100 pairs of images (224x224) and their
927   captions, a total of 1,000 paired samples.
928
      The results demonstrate significant computational and memory efficiency with our approach: Our
929
      framework required 22.760 GFLOPs for 1000 samples, compared to 203.223 GFLOPs for the sepa-
930
      rate backbone setup. Similarly, GPU memory usage was reduced to 1.462 GB, as opposed to 14.54
931   GB in the alternative setup. These results highlight the resource efficiency and scalability of our
932   framework in managing diverse tasks or domains.

936
937
      This section provides the implementation details of our experiments. All methods are trained using
      the AdamW optimizer, with training conducted on 1-4 nodes of NVIDIA A100 GPUs, depending on
938
      the batch size. The input/output adapters are initialized as identity functions.
939
940   Learning Rates: For CLIP backbones, we train DGA with an initial learning rate of 1 × 10−4 , and
941   5 × 10−5 for BLIP and DinoV2, all with an exponential decay rate of 0.93 down to a minimum of
942   1 × 10−6 .
943   Batch Sizes: We use a batch size of 256 for all retrieval tasks, except for the Stanford-Cars dataset,
944   where a batch size of 64 is applied. For ImageNet1k classification, a batch size of 1024 is used, and
945   64 for ImageNet-Sketch.
946
      Epochs: We train the models for the following number of epochs on each benchmark: 25 for
947   Stanford-Cars and ImageNet1k (16 shots), 30 for Sketchy and ImageNet-Sketch, 50 for COCO, 2
948   for Flickr30k, 20 for MSR-VTT, and 40 for VATEX.
949
950
      LoRA Hyper-parameters: For the LoRA baseline, we adapt the Q, K, and V matrices across all
      transformer layers, ensuring the rank matches the number of parameters used by DGA and LGA,
951
      depending on the backbone.
952
953   Trainable Parameters: The number of trainable parameters depends on the backbone. For BLIP-B,
954   DGA optimizes 0.10% of the parameters, 0.42% for CLIP, and 1.57% for DINOv2. To ensure a fair
955   comparison, we train the LoRA baselines with a rank r that results in a matched number of trainable
956
      parameters to DGA: r = 8 for CLIP, r = 2 for BLIP, and r = 25 for DINOv2. For LGA, we train
      a proxy token for each of the 12 transformer layers, resulting in a maximum of 12 · 2 · 768 trainable
957
      parameters, depending on the backbone’s dimensionality and the number of modalities (image and
958
      text).
