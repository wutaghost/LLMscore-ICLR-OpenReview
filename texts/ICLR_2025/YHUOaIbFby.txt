009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Variational quantum machine learning (QML) have shown great promise for har-
012
               nessing quantum advantage in machine learning tasks. However, architecture de-
013
               sign of quantum circuits employed in these QML algorithms has been poorly ex-
014            plored for practical problems. Specifically, quantum circuits should have sufficient
015            expressibility for modeling complex functions considering the inherent structures
016            of real-world data. Naively increasing the circuit depth could enhance the ex-
017            pressibility of quantum circuits, which also induce the barren plateau problem as
018            a by-product. In this work, we develop an architecture design framework to solve
019            this problem. We use a simple yet effective metric of quantum entanglement, i.e.
020            the linear entropy, to guide the circuit design from the perspective of the input
021            data. First, we quantify the entanglement of input data by calculating the 1-qubit
022
               linear entropy of their amplitude encoding states. Then we implement an entropy
               matching approach to identify the optimal circuit depth that lead to the linear en-
023
               tropy being close the entropy of input data. The effectiveness of circuit designs
024
               based on entropy is verified by extensive experimental results. Specifically, we
025            demonstrate that real-world datasets like MNIST images has limited quantum en-
026            tanglement. Therefore, circuits designed with entropy matching exhibit relatively
027            small depths being free from the barren plateau issue while maintaining benign
028            performances in binary classification tasks. This work not only advances the ef-
029            ficiency of quantum circuit design but also sets the stage for further refinement
030            of QML performance, with broad implications for practical quantum computing
031            applications.

      1   INTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Quantum computing is a new paradigm for computing based on quantum mechanics(Nielsen &
038   Chuang, 2000), which has unimaginable advantages over its classical counterparts in many impor-
039
      tant applications(Shor, 1994; Grover, 1996). The physical implementation of quantum hardware
      has entered the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era, which is a significant milestone
040
      towards realizing practical quantum computing(Preskill, 2018). The current quantum computing
041
      devices, despite their noise and limited coherence times(), have demonstrated exponentially promo-
042   tion in performing certain computing tasks over classical computers(Arute et al., 2019)(Zhong et al.,
043   2020). Among the various approaches designed to exploit the power of NISQ devices, Variational
044   Quantum Algorithms (VQAs)(Cerezo et al., 2021a) stand out as a particularly effective algorith-
045   mic framework. VQAs have demonstrated potential in a wide range of applications, particularly in
046   machine learning, where they have been used to solve complex tasks with notable success.
047
      VQAs are fundamentally composed of two parts: the classical optimization process that updates
048   parameters and the variational quantum circuit (VQC) Peruzzo et al. (2014). The depth of the VQC
049   is a critical factor in determining the algorithm’s expressibility, which is its ability to model complex
050   functions. Shallow circuits, while easier to train, often suffer from limited expressibility, restricting
051   their capacity to solve more complex problems. Conversely, deeper circuits, although more ex-
052   pressive, can encounter the barren plateau problem, where the optimization landscape becomes flat,
053   making training difficult. This creates a fundamental dilemma in VQC design: balancing express-
      ibility with trainability.

      In this work, we propose a novel approach to quantum circuit design that is tailored to the specific
055   problem at hand. Our method involves using linear entropy as a metric to evaluate the entangle-
056   ment properties of both the problem dataset and the quantum states generated by the VQC. By
057   adjusting the circuit depth to match the linear entropy of the dataset, our approach strikes a balance
058   between the expressibility and trainability of the quantum circuit. We validate our method through
059   experiments on quantum binary classification tasks using multiple datasets, demonstrating that our
060   entropy-matching approach leads to improved performance, effectively navigating the trade-offs in-
061   herent in VQC design.

067   2.1.1    Q UANTUM C IRCUIT A RCHITECTURE D ESIGN
068
069   Quantum circuit architecture design is a foundational aspect of developing efficient quantum al-
070   gorithms and optimizing their implementation on quantum hardware(Farhi & Neven, 2018). The
071   architecture directly influences a circuit’s computational power, efficiency, and ability to general-
072   ize across various tasks(Preskill, 2018). Various architectural strategies, including shallow circuits,
073
      deep circuits, hybrid designs, and hardware-efficient ansätze (HEAs), have been explored to enhance
      expressibility and performance(Mitarai et al., 2018)(Kjaergaard et al., 2020)(McClean et al., 2016).
074
075   Shallow circuits, characterized by fewer gates and layers, tend to be more robust against noise and
076   easier to train, making them suitable for near-term quantum devices(Cerezo et al., 2021b)(Bravyi
077   et al., 2018). Conversely, deep circuits provide greater expressibility, allowing them to capture
078   complex relationships in data. However, deep architectures often encounter challenges such as over-
079
      fitting and the barren plateau problem, where gradients vanish during optimization, complicating the
      training process.
080
081   HEAs are specifically designed to utilize the capabilities of current quantum hardware effec-
082   tively(Chen et al., 2021). These architectures typically consist of layers of single-qubit gates with
083   tunable parameters and two-qubit gates to enable entanglement. By structuring circuits in this way,
084   researchers aim to optimize computational resources while minimizing noise susceptibility. The
085
      trade-offs between expressibility and trainability are critical in designing circuits that can learn ef-
      fectively from data.

088   2.1.2    G ENERALIZATION E RROR B OUND AND E XPRESSIBILITY A NALYSIS OF Q UANTUM

               N EURAL N ETWORKS
090   The performance of quantum neural networks (QNNs) depends significantly on their ability to gen-
091   eralize well to unseen data(Caro et al., 2022), a property that parallels the concept of generalization
092   in classical machine learning. Generalization error bounds provide a theoretical framework for eval-
093   uating how well a QNN trained on a specific dataset will perform on new data. The generalization
094   capability is influenced by several factors, including the architecture of the parameterized quantum
095   circuit (PQC), the expressibility of the quantum circuit, and the complexity of the task(Cerezo et al.,
096   2021a)(Abbas et al., 2021).
097   Expressibility can be quantified by comparing the distribution of states generated by a QNN with the
098   uniform distribution of states, such as those from Haar random ensembles. A common method for
099   quantifying expressibility involves calculating the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the fidelity
100   probability distributions of the QNN and Haar random states(Sim et al., 2019). A lower divergence
101   indicates higher expressibility, suggesting that the QNN can represent a wider range of functions
102   effectively.
103   The interplay between expressibility and trainability is crucial in optimizing QNN architectures.
104   Well-designed circuits should achieve high expressibility while maintaining effective training dy-
105   namics(Sim et al., 2019), allowing them to learn from the training dataset without succumbing to
106   overfitting. Techniques derived from classical machine learning, such as regularization and model
107   selection, have been adapted for quantum contexts to enhance the performance and generalization
      capabilities of QNNs(Cerezo et al., 2021a).(McClean et al., 2018)

      2.2   Q UANTUM COMPUTING KNOWLEDGE
109
110   Quantum computing(Nielsen & Chuang, 2000) is built upon fundamental concepts that bridge clas-
111   sical computation and quantum mechanics. At its core lies the qubit, the basic unit of quantum
112   information. Unlike classical bits, which can exist in one of two states, 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a
113   superposition of both. Mathematically, a qubit’s state is described as |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where α
114   and β are complex amplitudes such that |α|2 + |β|2 = 1. This property allows quantum systems to
115   process information in fundamentally different ways, leading to potential computational advantages.
116   Building upon this, multi-qubit systems are described using tensor products of individual qubit
117   states. For an N -qubit system, the quantum state exists in a 2N -dimensional Hilbert space, and
118   the overall state is represented by the vector |Ψ⟩ = |ψ1 ⟩ ⊗ |ψ2 ⟩ ⊗ · · · ⊗ |ψN ⟩. These quantum
119   states are manipulated through quantum gates, the quantum analogue of classical logic gates. Quan-
120   tum gates such as Pauli-X, Y, Z, and Hadamard, along with controlled gates like CNOT, enable the
121   transformation of qubit states.
122   Quantum circuits provide the framework for organizing quantum gates to perform computations. A
123   quantum circuit can be viewed as a sequence of gate operations applied to a set of qubits, transform-
124   ing an initial quantum state into a final state. This process is parameterized in variational quantum
125   circuits (VQCs), where the quantum gates depend on a set of classical parameters, denoted θ. The
126   task in many quantum algorithms is to optimize these parameters to minimize a cost function, typi-
127   cally defined as the expectation value of a quantum observable.
128   Measurement is a crucial aspect of quantum computation, where the outcome of a quantum process
129   is obtained by collapsing the quantum state into a classical result. Measurements are represented by
130   observables, which are Hermitian operators acting on the quantum state. For example, the Pauli-Z
131   observable measures the probability of a qubit being in the state |0⟩ or |1⟩.
132   In quantum machine learning(Biamonte et al., 2017), loss functions quantify the difference between
133   the predicted outcome and the actual target. These loss functions are typically defined in terms of
134   the expectation value of an observable with respect to the quantum state produced by the quantum
135   circuit. The general form of the loss function is given by:
136
137

138
139   where |ψout (θ)⟩ is the output state of the quantum circuit with parameters θ, and Ô is the observable.
140   To optimize the parameters of the quantum circuit, gradient-based optimization methods are often
141   employed. In quantum computing, gradients can be computed using the parameter-shift rule. This
142   rule provides an efficient way to calculate the derivative of the loss function with respect to the
143   circuit parameters, without requiring explicit knowledge of the circuit’s internal workings. For a
144   parameter θj associated with a gate in the circuit, the gradient of the loss function with respect to θj

      Haar measure is a type of invariant measure defined on a locally compact topological group G. The
158
      invariance property implies that for any element g ∈ G and any measurable subset A ⊂ G, the Haar
159
      measure µ satisfies the following property:
160
161
                                                  µ(gA) = µ(A)


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Here, gA represents the set obtained by left-multiplying each element of A by g . This means
163   that the Haar measure is left-invariant on the group G, indicating that the measure of a set remains
164   unchanged under translation by any group element. Similarly, Haar measure can also be right-
165   invariant, satisfying µ(Ag) = µ(A).
166
      On a compact topological group (such as the unitary group U (d)), the Haar measure is unique (up to
167
      a multiplicative constant) and can be normalized as a probability measure, meaning that the measure
168
      of the entire group is 1. This normalization is particularly important because it allows us to define a
169   uniformly distributed quantum state by randomly selecting a unitary matrix in quantum computing.
170
171   In quantum computing, we often focus on the unitary group U (d), whose elements are unitary
172
      matrices in a d-dimensional Hilbert space. Unitary matrices are linear transformations that preserve
      inner products, satisfying U † U = U U † = I, where U † is the conjugate transpose of U , and I is the
173
      identity matrix.
174
175   The application of Haar measure on U (d) is evident in the uniform selection of a unitary matrix U ,
176   which generates a quantum state |ψ⟩ = U |ψ0 ⟩ in the d-dimensional Hilbert space, where |ψ0 ⟩ is
177   a fixed initial state in the Hilbert space. Due to the uniformity of the Haar measure, the generated
178
      quantum state |ψ⟩ uniformly covers the entire Hilbert space, meaning that each possible quantum
      state is selected with equal probability, without any bias or inclination.
179
180   In practical computations, Haar measure is often expressed through integrals. For example, on the
181   unitary group U (d), Haar measure can be used to calculate the expected value of certain statistical
182   quantities. Suppose we have a function f (U ) defined on the unitary group U (d), then its integral
183   over the group (according to Haar measure) is given by:

202   In quantum computing, the significance of Haar measure lies in its use as a standard for evaluating
203   the expressiveness of quantum circuits. The expressiveness of a quantum circuit refers to its abil-
204   ity to generate a wide range of quantum states, which is crucial for performing diverse quantum
205   computational tasks.
206   A key mathematical criterion for evaluating the expressiveness of a quantum circuit is the extent
207   to which the quantum states it generates approach the distribution defined by the Haar measure. If

211   Approximating the Haar measure distribution implies that the quantum circuit can realize arbitrary
212   quantum states, which is essential for universal quantum computing. The Haar measure corresponds
213   to a maximum entropy distribution, where the generated quantum circuit has the greatest information
214   processing capability. Furthermore, if the quantum states generated by the circuit are close to the
215   Haar measure, it signifies that the circuit can achieve highly complex quantum state transformations,
      demonstrating high expressiveness.

218   To quantify circuit expressivity, we can use a mathematical measure known as linear entropy, defined
219   as:
220

222
223   where ρ is the density matrix of the quantum state, and Tr(ρ2 ) represents the trace of the square of
224   the density matrix.
225
226
      Linear entropy is a measure of the degree of mixture or randomness in a quantum state. It provides
      insight into how ”mixed” a quantum state is, with higher values of linear entropy indicating greater
227
      randomness and complexity in the quantum state. In the context of quantum circuits, a higher linear
228
      entropy suggests that the quantum states generated by the circuit are spread across a larger portion
229   of the Hilbert space, closely approximating a uniform distribution defined by the Haar measure.
230
231   The Haar measure, in quantum computing, is a uniform distribution over the unitary group U (d),
232
      where d is the dimension of the Hilbert space. If a quantum circuit can generate states that are dis-
      tributed according to the Haar measure, it implies that the circuit has a high degree of expressiveness
233
      and can explore a wide range of possible quantum states.
234

252   When designing quantum circuits, especially for tasks in quantum machine learning, a key goal is to
253   balance the circuit’s expressiveness with its trainability. As the depth and complexity of a quantum
254
      circuit increase, so does its ability to generate complex quantum states. However, this increased
      complexity can also lead to what is known as the ”barren plateau” problem, where the landscape
255
      of the optimization function becomes flat, making it difficult to find the optimal parameters using
256
      gradient-based methods.
257
258   The linear entropy serves as a diagnostic tool in this context. If a quantum circuit generates states
259   with high linear entropy, it suggests that the circuit is highly expressive and can explore a large
260
      portion of the Hilbert space. This is beneficial for capturing complex data patterns but can also
      indicate a potential for optimization difficulties if the expressiveness is too high.
261
262   In practice, as the depth of a quantum circuit increases, the linear entropy of the states it generates
263   tends to stabilize at a certain value. This stabilization often indicates that the quantum states have
264   reached a level of distribution close to that defined by the Haar measure. From one perspective,
265   this stabilization is a positive indicator, showing that the circuit is capable of exploring the high-
266
      dimensional quantum state space effectively.
267   However, from another perspective, it can be a warning sign. If the circuit’s depth is too great, the
268   resulting quantum states may be so randomized that the optimization landscape becomes too flat,
269   leading to the barren plateau problem. In this scenario, while the circuit’s expressiveness is high, its
      ability to be trained effectively diminishes.

456   This method of determining the optimal circuit depth by matching linear entropy not only enhances
457   the performance of quantum neural networks in classification tasks but also significantly conserves
458   computational resources. By optimizing quantum circuit design, we can achieve superior perfor-
459   mance in practical applications. These findings provide strong theoretical and experimental support
460   for the future development of quantum computing technology, showing that designing and optimiz-
461   ing the structure of quantum neural networks by quantifying the complexity of quantum states is
462   an effective approach to improving performance. This lays the groundwork for applying quantum
463   machine learning to tasks such as classification and regression, further advancing the prospects of

      In this work, we have explored the critical role of Haar measure and linear entropy in evaluating the
469   expressiveness of quantum circuits, particularly in the context of quantum machine learning. The
470   theoretical framework established in this study underscores the importance of ensuring that quan-
471   tum circuits can generate quantum states that approximate the Haar measure distribution, which is
472   indicative of a circuit’s ability to explore the entire Hilbert space and perform complex quantum
473   computations. Our approach utilized linear entropy as a diagnostic tool to quantify the randomness
474   and complexity of quantum states produced by quantum circuits. By examining the linear entropy
475   across various circuit depths, we were able to identify the optimal number of layers that balance
476   expressiveness with trainability. This balance is crucial for avoiding the barren plateau problem,
477
      where excessive circuit depth leads to a flat optimization landscape, making it difficult to effectively
      train the quantum circuit. The experimental validation conducted on the MNIST dataset demon-
478
      strated that the theoretically determined optimal circuit depth, identified through entropy matching,
479
      significantly enhances the performance of quantum neural networks in binary classification tasks.
480   The results showed that circuits with the optimal depth not only achieved lower error rates but also
481   maintained stability across different test scenarios. The findings of this study have several impor-
482   tant implications for the future of quantum computing. The entropy-based approach presented here
483   offers a promising pathway for developing more efficient and effective quantum algorithms, with
484   potential applications across a wide range of computational tasks. As quantum computing technol-
485   ogy continues to evolve, the principles and methodologies discussed in this paper will play a pivotal
      role in shaping the design and optimization of future quantum circuits.
