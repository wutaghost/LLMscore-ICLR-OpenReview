011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Insertion, deletion, and substitution (IDS) error-correcting codes have garnered
014
               increased attention with recent advancements in DNA storage technology. How-
               ever, a universal method for designing IDS-correcting codes across varying chan-
015
               nel settings remains underexplored. We present an autoencoder-based method,
016
               THEA-code, aimed at efficiently generating IDS-correcting codes for complex
017            IDS channels. In the work, a Gumbel-Softmax discretization constraint is pro-
018            posed to discretize the features of the autoencoder, and a simulated differentiable
019            IDS channel is developed as a differentiable alternative for IDS operations. These
020            innovations facilitate the successful convergence of the autoencoder, resulting in
021            channel-customized IDS-correcting codes with commendable performance across
022            complex IDS channels.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
      DNA storage, a method that utilizes the synthesis and sequencing of DNA molecules for information
027
      storage and retrieval, has attracted significant attention (Church et al., 2012; Goldman et al., 2013;
028
      Grass et al., 2015; Erlich & Zielinski, 2017; Organick et al., 2018; Dong et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
029   2021; El-Shaikh et al., 2022; Welzel et al., 2023).
030
031   Due to the involvement of biochemical procedures, the DNA storage pipeline can be viewed as an
032
      insertions, deletions, or substitutions (IDS) channel (Blawat et al., 2016) over 4-ary sequences with
      the alphabet {A, T, G, C}. Consequently, an IDS-correcting encoding/decoding method plays a key
033
      role in DNA storage.
034
035   However, despite the existence of excellent combinatorial IDS-correcting codes (Varshamov &
036   Tenenholtz, 1965; Levenshtein, 1965; Sloane, 2000; Mitzenmacher, 2009; Cai et al., 2021; Gabrys
037   et al., 2023; Bar-Lev et al., 2023), applying them in DNA storage remains challenging. The IDS
038
      channel in DNA storage is more complex than those studied in previous works, with factors such as
      inhomogeneous error probabilities across error types, base indices, and even sequence patterns (Hi-
039
      rao et al., 1992; Press et al., 2020; Blawat et al., 2016; Cai et al., 2021; Hamoum et al., 2021).
040
      Additionally, most of the aforementioned combinatorial codes focus on correcting either a single
041   error or a burst of errors, whereas multiple independent errors within the same DNA sequence are
042   common in DNA storage.
043
044
      Given the complexity of the IDS channel, we leverage the universality of deep learning methods by
      employing a heuristic end-to-end autoencoder (Baldi, 2012) as the foundation for an IDS-correcting
045
      code. This approach allows researchers to train customized codes tailored to different IDS channels
046
      through the same training procedure, rather than to design specific combinatorial codes for each IDS
047   channel setting, many of which have not yet been explored.
048
049   To realize this approach, two novel techniques are developed, which we believe offer greater contri-
050
      butions to the communities than the code itself.
051   Firstly, the discretization effect of applying Gumbel-Softmax in a non-generative model is inves-
052   tigated in this work. Originally proposed as a differentiable approximation for categorical sam-
053   pling (Jang et al., 2017; Maddison et al., 2017; Huijben et al., 2023), Gumbel-Softmax has been
      widely used as a reparameterization trick, particularly in variational autoencoders. It is found that


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      applying Gumbel-Softmax in a non-generative model induces discretized features, which align with
055   the discrete codewords of an error-correcting code (ECC). This discovery may offer an alternative
056   method for bridging the gap between continuous deep models and discrete applications.
057
058
      Secondly, a differentiable IDS channel using a transformer-based model (Vaswani et al., 2017) is
      developed. The non-differentiable nature of IDS operations presents a key challenge for deploying
059
      deep learning models that rely on gradient descent training. To tackle this, a model is trained in
060
      advance to mimic the IDS operations according to a given error profile. It can serve as a plug-in
061   module for the IDS channel and is backpropagable within the network. This differentiable IDS
062   channel has the potential to act as a general module for addressing IDS or DNA-related problems
063   using deep learning methods. For instance, researchers could build generative models on this module
064   to simulate the biochemical processes involved in manipulating biosequences.
065
      Overall, this work implements a heuristic end-to-end autoencoder as an IDS-correcting code, re-
066
      ferred to as THEA-Code. The encoder maps the source DNA sequence into a longer codeword
067   sequence. After introducing IDS errors to the codeword, a decoder network is employed to recon-
068   struct the original source sequence from the codeword. During the training of this autoencoder, the
069   Gumbel-Softmax discretization constraint is applied to the codeword sequence to produce one-hot-
070   like vectors, and the differentiable IDS channel serves as a substitute for conventional IDS channel,
071   enabling gradient backpropagation.
072
      To the best of our knowledge, this work introduces the first end-to-end autoencoder solution for an
073   IDS-correcting code. Additionally, it marks the first application of Gumbel-Softmax as a discretiza-
074   tion constraint, and the first proposal of a differentiable IDS channel.

      Many established IDS-correcting codes are rooted in the Varshamov-Tenengolts (VT) code (Var-
      shamov & Tenenholtz, 1965; Levenshtein, 1965), including (Calabi & Hartnett, 1969; Tanaka &
083
      Kasai, 1976; Sloane, 2000; Cai et al., 2021; Gabrys et al., 2023). These codes often rely on rigorous
084
      mathematical deduction and provide firm proofs for their coding schemes. However, the stringent
085   hypotheses they use tend to restrict their practical applications. Heuristic IDS-correcting codes for
086   DNA storage, such as those proposed in (Pfister & Tal, 2021; Yan et al., 2022; Maarouf et al., 2022;
087   Welzel et al., 2023), usually incorporate synchronization markers (Sellers, 1962; Srinivasavarad-
088   han et al., 2021; Haeupler & Shahrasbi, 2021), watermarks (Davey & Mackay, 2001), or positional
089   information (Press et al., 2020) within their encoded sequences. Recently, directly correcting er-
090   rors in retrieved DNA reads without sequence reconstruction has been investigated, demonstrating
091   promising performance (Welter et al., 2024).
092   In recent years, deep learning methods have found increasing applications in coding the-
093   ory (Ibnkahla, 2000; Simeone, 2018; Akrout et al., 2023). Several architectures have been em-
094   ployed as decoders or sub-modules of conventional codes on the AWGN channel. In (Cammerer
095   et al., 2017), the authors applied neural networks to replace sub-blocks in the conventional iterative
096   decoding algorithm for polar codes. Recurrent neural networks (RNN) were used for decoding con-
097   volutional and turbo codes (Kim et al., 2018). Both RNNs and transformer-based models have served
098   as belief propagation decoders for linear codes (Nachmani et al., 2018; Choukroun & Wolf, 2022;
099
      2023; 2024b;a;c). Hypergraph networks were also utilized as decoders for block codes in (Nachmani
      & Wolf, 2019). Despite these advancements, end-to-end deep learning solutions remain relatively
100
      less explored. As mentioned in (Jiang et al., 2019), direct applications of multi-layer perceptron
101
      (MLP) and convolutional neural network (CNN) are not comparable to conventional methods. To
102   address this, the authors in (Jiang et al., 2019) used deep models to replace sub-modules of a turbo
103   code skeleton, and trained an end-to-end encoder-decoder model. Similarly, in (Makkuva et al.,
104   2021), neural networks were employed to replace the Plotkin mapping for the Reed-Muller code.
105   Both of these works inherit frameworks from conventional codes and utilize neural networks as
106   replacements for key modules. In (Balevi & Andrews, 2020), researchers proposed an autoencoder-
107   based inner code with one-bit quantization for the AWGN channel. Confronting challenges arising
      from quantization, they utilized interleaved training on the encoder and decoder.

188   4    D IFFERENTIABLE IDS CHANNEL ON 3- SIMPLEX ∆3
189
190   It is evident that the operations of insertion and deletion are not differentiable. Consequently, a
191
      conventional IDS channel, which modifies a sequence by directly applying IDS operations, hinders
      gradient propagation and cannot be seamlessly integrated into deep learning-based methods.
192
193   Leveraging the logical capabilities inherent in transformer-based models, a sequence-to-sequence
194   model is employed to simulate the conventional IDS channel. Built on deep models, this simulated
195   IDS channel is differentiable. In the following discussion, we use the notation CIDS(·, ·) to repre-
196   sent the Conventional IDS channel, and DIDS(·, ·; θ) for the simulated Differentiable IDS channel.
197
      The simulated channel is trained independently before being integrated into the autoencoder, whose
      learned parameters remain fixed during the optimization of the autoencoder.
198
199   As the model utilizes probability vectors rather than discrete letters, we need to promote conven-
200   tional IDS operations onto the 3-simplex ∆3 , where ∆3 is defined as the collection 4-dimentional
201   probability vectors
                                                     4

205   For a sequence of probability vectors C = (π1 , π2 , . . . , πk ), where each πi is an element from the
206
      simplex ∆3 , the IDS operations are promoted as follows.
207   Insertion at index i involves adding a one-hot vector representing the inserted symbol from the al-
208   phabet {A, T, G, C} before index i. Deletion at index i simply removes the vector πi from C. For
209   substitution, the probability vector πi is rolled by corresponding offsets for the three types of substi-
210   tutions (type-1, 2, 3). For example, applying a type-1 substitution at index i rolls the original vector
211   πi = (πi1 , πi2 , πi3 , πi4 ) into (πi4 , πi1 , πi2 , πi3 ). It is straightforward to verify that the promoted
212

      5.5   T ESTING PHASE
338
339   In the testing phase, the differentiable IDS channel is replaced with the conventional IDS channel.
340   The process begins with the encoder mapping the source sequence s to the codeword c in the form of
341   probability vectors. An arg max function is then applied to convert c into a discrete letter sequence,
342   removing any extra information from the probability vectors. Next, the conventional IDS operations
343   are performed on ĉ = CIDS(c, p) according to a randomly generated error profile p. The one-hot
344   representation of ĉ is then passed into the decoder, which reconstructs the sink sequence ŝ. Finally,
345   metrics are computed to measure the differences between the original source sequence s and the
346   reconstructed sink sequence ŝ, providing an evaluation of the method’s performance.
347   Since the sequences are randomly generated from an enormous pool of possible terms, we do not
348   distinguish between a training set and a testing set. For example, in the context of this work, the
349   source sequence is a 100-long 4-ary sequence, approximately providing 1.6 × 1060 possible se-
350   quences. Given this vast space, sets of randomly generated sequences using different seeds are
351   highly unlikely to overlap.

784   B    M ORE ON THE GRADIENTS TO DIFFERENTIABLE IDS CHANNEL
785
786   In Section 6.2, we illustrated that the differentiable IDS channel can effectively trace gradients
787   through the IDS operations. In this section, we focus on evaluating the channel’s capability to
788
      recover the error profile through gradient-based optimization.
789   Given a codeword c, an empty profile p0 which defines the identity transformation of the IDS chan-
790   nel such that ĉ = c = DIDS(c, p0 ), and a modified codeword ĉ′ which is produced by manually
791   modifying c through an insertion, deletion, or substitution at position idx, the gradients of L(ĉ, ĉ′ )
792   are computed with respect to both the input codeword c and the empty profile p0 . The average
793   gradients, calculated over 100 runs, are plotted in Figure 6 with position idx aligned to 0.
794   In Figure 6, it is suggested that, when performing an insertion or deletion, the gradients with respect
795   to the codeword are distributed after the error position idx. This aligns with the fact that synchro-
796   nization errors (insertions or deletions) can be interpreted as successive substitutions starting from
797   the error position, especially when the actual error profile is unknown. When performing a substitu-
798   tion, the gradients naturally concentrate at the error position idx.
799   Regarding the empty profile, p0 = 0, the gradients also exhibit meaningful patterns. For an inser-
800   tion, the substitution area after idx is lighted by the gradients, supporting the view that an insertion
801   can be seen as a sequence of substitutions if error constraints are absent. Additionally, the insertion
802   area of the profile is also lighted, which makes sense since an insertion may also be interpreted as
803   a series of substitutions followed by an ending insertion. For deletion errors, similar patterns are
804   observed: the gradients are distributed in the areas of substitutions and deletions after the error posi-
805   tion idx, since the deletion can also be viewed as a series of substitutions, or as several substitutions
806
      and an ending deletion. For substitution errors, the gradients again concentrate at the error position
      idx, as substitutions do not cause sequence mismatches.
807
808   Utilizing energy constraints on the profile may be helpful for specific profile applications. In this
809   work, only the gradients with respect to the codeword participate in the training phase, the existing
      version of the simulated differentiable IDS channel is assumed to be adequate.
