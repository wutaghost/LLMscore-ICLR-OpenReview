009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) proliferate, developing privacy safeguards for
012            these models is crucial. One popular safeguard involves training LLMs in a differ-
013            entially private manner. However, such solutions are shown to be computationally
014            expensive and detrimental to the utility of these models. Since LLMs are deployed
015            on the cloud and thus only accessible via an API, a Machine Learning as a Service
016
               (MLaaS) provider can protect its downstream data by privatizing the predictions
               during the decoding process. However, the practicality of such solutions still largely
017
               lags behind DP training methods. One recent promising approach, Private Mixing
018
               of Ensemble Distributions (PMixED) Flemings et al. (2024), avoids additive noise
019            by sampling from the output distributions of private LLMs mixed with the output
020            distribution of a public model. Yet, PMixED must satisfy a fixed privacy level
021            for a given number of queries, which is difficult for an analyst to estimate before
022            inference and, hence, does not scale. To this end, we relax the requirements to a
023            more practical setting by introducing Adaptive PMixED (AdaPMixED), a private
024            decoding framework based on PMixED that is adaptive to the private and public
025            output distributions evaluated on a given input query. In this setting, we introduce
026            a noisy screening mechanism that filters out queries with potentially expensive pri-
027
               vacy loss, and a data-dependent analysis that exploits the divergence of the private
               and public output distributions in its privacy loss calculation. Our experimental
028
               evaluations demonstrate that our mechanism and analysis can reduce the privacy
029
               loss by 16× while preserving the utility over the original PMixED. Furthermore,
030            performing 100K predictions with AdaPMixED still achieves strong utility and a
031            reasonable data-dependent privacy loss of ϵ = 5.25.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) have accomplished huge commercial success due to their ability
037   to memorize factual knowledge within their model parameters Petroni et al. (2019); Roberts et al.
038   (2020). However, this memorization poses a serious privacy leakage vulnerability as LLMs are
039
      susceptible to training data extraction attacks on memorized text Carlini et al. (2021; 2019; 2022).
      The widely adopted approach to reduce memorization is to guarantee Differential Privacy (DP) Dwork
040
      (2006), a mathematical privacy notion, during the training of an LLM by applying strategic noise
041
      to per-sample gradients, called DP-SGD Abadi et al. (2016). Recent works have demonstrated that
042   public pre-training followed by private fine-tuning substantially boosts the performance of DP LLMs
043   Li et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021a); Li et al. (2022); Ganesh et al. (2023). But the potentially large
044   additive noise by DP-SGD, which scales proportionally to the total number of parameters of an LLM
045   Kamath (2020), and the underutilization of ML accelerated hardware due to per-sample gradient
046   calculations Yousefpour et al. (2021), have delayed wide-scale adoption of DP-SGD to LLMs.
047
      Perhaps the biggest discrepancy between DP-SGD and commercial deployments of LLMs is that
048   many Machine Learning as a Service (MLaaS) providers only provide API access to their LLMs, e.g.
049   OpenAI’s Chat-GPT. However, DP-SGD assumes an adversary has access to the gradients of an LLM
050   at every iteration of training. This discrepancy leads to an overestimated privacy loss bounds Nasr
051   et al. (2021) when considering an adversary with only black-box access to the model. Alternatively,
052   DP prediction methods Dwork & Feldman (2018) offer potential improvement in model utility by
053   explicitly exploiting this relaxation; however, in practice, DP prediction does not necessarily lead to
      higher utility than DP-SGD van der Maaten & Hannun (2020).

075
076   A recent promising DP prediction algorithm, called Private Mixing of Ensemble Distributions
077   (PMixED) Flemings et al. (2024) is able to achieve higher utility than DP-SGD for certain query
078
      budgets by exploiting two crucial features: (1) There is an inherent privacy when generating the next
      token by sampling from the output probability distribution of a language model; (2) The privacy
079
      loss of a prediction can be bounded by employing a public model to mix the output distributions of
080
      privacy-sensitive LLMs. These observations allow PMixED to avoid additive noise and thus preserve
081   model utility Flemings et al. (2024). However, the DP Prediction definition Dwork & Feldman (2018)
082   that PMixED operates under is too rigid, as one must estimate a pre-specified privacy budget that
083   will be satisfied for a certain number of predictions before querying an LLM, which is difficult.
084   Moreover, this rigid requirement is satisfied by analyzing the privacy loss for each prediction without
085   consideration for the output distributions of the private and public models, often leading to overly
086   pessimistic privacy loss when the actual privacy loss is substantially smaller. Thus, PMixED, in
087   practice, exhausts its privacy budget quickly and does not scale well to current generative applications.
088   In this work, we address the aforementioned limitations by introducing Adaptive PMixED
089   (AdaPMixED), which makes the following contributions: (1) We provide a tighter privacy analysis
090   of PMixED by leveraging the output distributions from auto-regressive LLMs to directly calculate the
091   privacy loss. (2) We adopt a noisy screening mechanism only found in discriminative tasks Papernot
092   et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2020) to the language modeling setting, by using the Renyi Divergence
093   to measure the distributional difference between projected and public distributions then filter out
094   queries with large divergence. (3) We present a privacy and utility analysis of AdaPMixED and
095
      experimentally demonstrate improved privacy-utility tradeoff over PMixED. In particular, on the
      WikiText-103 dataset for a given number of queries, AdaPMixED can improve the utility by 1.4
096
      perplexity over PMixED with 16 times less privacy loss. Furthermore, we demonstrate our method
097
      can practically scale to large number of queries, achieving a privacy loss of ϵ = 5.248 for almost
098   100,000 predictions, while improving the utility over DP-SGD at a privacy level of ϵ = 8 by nearly
099   2.5 perplexity. This is the first work to demonstrate that a DP prediction-based scheme can be
100   practical for such extremely large queries, as prior work has evaluated on only at most 1k queries
101   Flemings et al. (2024); Ginart et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2023). We hope these promising results will
102   spark further improvement in private decoding for real-world deployment of large-scale applications.

105   2    R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   Most of the progress in the differential privacy and LLM space has focused primarily on differentially
      private fine-tuning McMahan et al. (2017); Yu et al. (2021a); Li et al. (2021); Flemings & Annavaram


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2024). In many of these works, a pre-trained model trained on public data is further fine-tuned on
109   a private downstream dataset D using DP-SGD. The resulting model parameters are differentially
110   private with respect to D. Private prediction methods Dwork & Feldman (2018) offer an alternative
111   way to guarantee DP for LLMs, during predictions rather than training. Although some progress
112   has been made in DP prediction methods for discriminative tasks Zhu et al. (2020; 2023), most DP
113   prediction methods for generative LLMs produce substantial utility degradation Majmudar et al.
114   (2022). Another body of work also explored DP prediction of LLMs without fine-tuning of private
115   data Tang et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2023). In these works,
116   generated prompts containing private information are used by an LLM via an API call. Our work
117
      differs from these in that we utilize LLMs fine-tuned on private data to generate DP predictions.
118   Our work relies on the PMixED framework Flemings et al. (2024), however, we pointed out com-
119   pelling shortcomings of PMixED that limit its practicality in real-world applications. Another
120   conceptually similar, but orthogonal approach, to PMixED is PATE Papernot et al. (2016; 2018),
121   which also uses an ensemble of models trained on pairwise disjoint subsets of a private dataset to
122   generate DP labels. However, PATE uses the Gaussian/Laplacian mechanism to perturb vote count
123
      histograms while PMixED relies on sampling and mixing private and public output distributions.
      Consequently, adopting PATE to the language modeling setting produces suboptimal results, since
124
      perturbing the aggregated output distribution of the teachers incurs large noise due to its large dimen-
125
      sionality Ginart et al. (2022). The output space can be truncated, as done in Tian et al. (2022), but
126   this can cause an error in the perplexity calculation. Instead, truncated decoding schemes must use
127   generation perplexity Fan et al. (2018); Holtzman et al. (2019); Pillutla et al. (2021), which does not
128   apply in our experimental setup.
129
      Although the high level goal of our noisy screening mechanism and data-dependent analysis is
130
      motivated by PATE Papernot et al. (2018), our approach to both differs significantly from PATE.
131
      PATE’s noisy screening checks if the noisy max meets a threshold, whereas we use Renyi Divergence
132   to measure the distributional difference between projected and public distributions to guide the
133   screening process. This use of Renyi Divergence for screening is a novel aspect of our work, as no
134   prior work has done this. Moreover, our data-dependent analysis iteratively examines neighboring
135   ensembles to find the largest privacy loss, utilizing the probability distributions generated. On
136   the other hand, PATE, designed for discriminative tasks, uses histograms to calculate class count
137   differences as its data-dependent analysis. Our data-dependent analysis closely follows from Ginart
138   et al. (2022).

173   must be privatized, which can be done by employing the smooth-sensitivity framework Nissim et al.

176
      that a data-dependent privacy loss ϵ(D) does not exceed a pre-specified privacy budget ϵG leaks
177   additional privacy Redberg et al. (2023), and hence, is incompatible with the DP prediction definition
178   Dwork & Feldman (2018).

180   3.2    T HE PM IX ED F RAMEWORK
181
182   PMixED Flemings et al. (2024) is a private decoding framework that guarantees DP at prediction
183   time for generative LLMs. PMixED partitions a private dataset into N pairwise disjoint subsets
184   Di , each of which is fine-tuned by an LLM pi . Then during inference, each fine-tuned model
185   generates its output probability distribution pi (xt ), containing private information, on some input
186   xt received by a user. Additionally, the output distribution from a public model p0 (xt ) is obtained.
187
      PMixED then projects each of the private distributions onto a Renyi Divergence ball centered at
      the public distribution with radius βα. The optimal projection is selected by choosing λi such
188
      that λi ← arg maxλ∈[0,1] {Dα↔ (λpi (xt ) + (1 − λ)p0 (xt )||p0 (xt )) ≤ βα}. Lastly, the projected
189
      distributions are averaged and then sampled to generate the next token. A precise description of
190   PMixED can be found in Algorithm 2. We state the main privacy analysis results of Flemings et al.
191   (2024) here and leave the details in Appendix C.

      exhausted, either no more predictions can be performed, or the privacy guarantee will decay with

202   the total number of queries ahead of time, this places a huge burden on the analyst to hypothesize
203   an adequate query budget and privacy level before deployment. Moreover, the privacy loss between
204   different queries can vary widely, yet it is not considered when pre-specifying a fixed privacy level.

      4     A D A PM I X ED: A N A DAPTIVE P RIVATE D ECODING F RAMEWORK
207
208
      In this work, we present a more realistic and feasible design that enables an adaptable privacy level.
209
      To this end, we modify PMixED to adapt to the private and public output distributions evaluated on a
210   given input query. Consequently, the privacy level can only be determined after the next token has
211   been sampled. Thus we focus on two scenarios that allow us to minimize the privacy loss: Section
212   4.1 discusses a mechanism to filter out queries where the private and public distributions diverge
213   extremely. (2) Section 4.2 introduces a data-dependent privacy analysis to account for queries where
214   the private and public distributions converge. As mentioned in section 2, the high-level idea of our
215   noisy screening and data-dependent analysis are inspired by PATE Papernot et al. (2018) but the
      low-level details differ substantially.

      4.1   N OISY S CREENING M ECHANISM
217
218   The motivation is that for certain queries x, the mixing parameter λi is very small due to extreme
219   divergence between the private and public distributions, i.e., Dα↔ (pi (x)||p0 (x)) is large for small λi .
220   Since λi is small, little information about the private data is used to assist with the prediction, leading
221   to almost complete reliance on the public model. Perhaps, in this scenario, it would be better to save
222   the privacy loss resulting from sampling the mixture of private and public information, and instead
223   sample purely from the public distribution p0 (x), which will result in no additional privacy loss since
224
      p0 (x) was not fine-tuned on the private data D.
225   Hence, our key idea is to screen a query x by using the Renyi Divergence to measure the distribu-
226   tional difference between the projected and public output distributions. If the private and public
227   distribution diverge extremely, we can check if mixing just a small amount of private information
228   would already result in a large Renyi Divergence. In other words, we select a small λ, calculate
                    PN
229   p(x) = 1/N i=1 (λpi (x) + (1 − λ)p0 (x)), then check if Dα↔ (p(xt )||p0 (x)) ≤ T for some thresh-
230   old value T . Using a small λ also helps reduce the privacy loss from the noisy screening since less
231   private information is used in the screening. Making this screening mechanism differentially private
232
      involves privatizing Dα↔ (p(xt )||p0 (x)), which is difficult to do directly since Dα↔ (p(xt )||p0 (x))
      contains no upper-bound, and hence, no global sensitivity.
233
234   To work around this, we make use of the post-processing theorem of DP (Theorem A.1) by first
235   privatizing p(xt ) by adding Gaussian noise to it, ppriv (x) = p(x) + N (0, σ 2 ), then checking if
236   Dα↔ (ppriv (xt )||p0 (x)) ≤ T . However, one technical challenge is that p(xt ) is a large dimensional
237   vector, approximately 50,000 dimensions. Hence, the error of the screening mechanism scales
238   proportionally to the size of this large vector. To reduce the noise, we use a similar approach from
239
      Tian et al. (2022); Tang et al. (2023) by selecting the top-k indices K of the public model p0 (x),
      which does not leak additional privacy since p0 (x) was not fine-tuned on D, then truncate and re-scale
240
      p0 (xt ) with K to obtain p0 (x). Using K, we truncate p(x), apply noise to those K indices of p(x),
241
      i.e., ppriv (x)[K] = p(x)[K] + N (0, σ 2 ), then re-scale ppriv (x). Lastly, the divergence of p0 (xt ) and
242
      the DP projected distribution ppriv (x) at indices K is measured.
243

407
      has the largest effect on reducing the privacy loss, a 4.5 times reduction compared to data-independent
408
      analysis. Even with PMixED employing privacy amplification by subsampling in its privacy analysis,
409   its resulting privacy loss is still substantially larger than the data-dependent loss. Thus, the data-
410   independent analysis pessimistically overestimates the privacy loss while data-dependent accounting
411   provides a much tighter bound.
412
      The effect of noisy screening on the privacy loss is smaller but still plays a significant role for both
413
      data-independent and data-dependent analysis. We observe that including noisy screening decreases
414
      the privacy loss as more queries are answered by the public model. For data-independent accounting,
415   the additional privacy savings is 0.26 while for data-dependent it is 0.036. Additionally, we observe
416   that noisy screening saves 0.26 privacy over PMixED while only lowering the utility by 0.08 PPL. For
417   data-dependent analysis, noisy screening lowers the privacy loss by 0.036 with a 0.33 PPL decrease.
418   In our view, privacy loss is a more valuable quantity than PPL, so we believe the privacy-utility
419   tradeoff offered by our scheme is significant. Furthermore, out of the 9728 queries answered, 716 and
420   1026 were answered completely by the public model when using PMixED with noisy screening and
421   AdaPMixED, respectively. Hence, this validates that a good chunk of the queries can be answered
422   sufficiently with just the public model without diminishing the utility.
423   Lastly, Table 2b breaks down the privacy loss of PMixED. Noisy screening only comprises of 0.002%
424   of the total privacy loss, clearly justifying that it saves more privacy than it loses. As expected, most

      6        C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this work, we present AdaPMixED, a private decoding scheme that adapts to the private and public
477
      distributions evaluated on input queries to enable a large number of queries to be performed privately.
      Our work identified a crucial limitation of a recent work, PMixED: an analyst must set a fixed-privacy
478
      guarantee and a query budget before inference, a setup too rigid for real-world applications. The
479
      noisy screening mechanism of AdaPMixED filters out queries that result in large Renyi Divergence
480   between projected and public distributions, and use the public model to answer them. The data-
481   dependent privacy analysis performed by AdaPMixED exploits the output probability distribution
482   induced by generative LLMs to directly calculate the privacy loss. These data-adaptive methods
483   enable AdaPMixED to answer 100K queries while achieving reasonable data-dependent privacy loss,
484   ϵ(D) = 5.248, while still outperforming DP-SGD by nearly 2.5 perplexity. To our knowledge, no
485   prior work has scaled private prediction methods to this large of queries while achieving strong utility.
      However, like all private prediction methods, eventually, the privacy loss can get too large, and hence,


                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      one must fine-tune the LLM again on new data. But if the data received is not scarce, then it can
487   be beneficial to do this for better privacy-utility tradeoffs. We hope that our work will spark more
488   progress in private prediction to be a practical alternative over private training methods.

898   F     L IMITATIONS
899
900   We discuss the limitations of our proposed framework. The first is that the privacy loss of
901   AdaPMixED is data-dependent and, hence, can leak privacy if released. However, one could
902
      follow the privacy loss sanitation technique from Papernot et al. (2018) to privately release the
      privacy loss. The second limitation comes from the original PMixED framework, which incurs and
903
      additional inference latency due to obtaining predictions from an ensemble and calculating the mixing
904
      parameters λi . We also acknowledge that storing an ensemble of LLMs incurs additional storage
905   costs, although we do employ LoRA Hu et al. (2021) to further reduce this cost. But because we
906   do not use DP-SGD, per-example gradients do not need to be stored and ML accelerated hardware
907   is fully utilized, thereby gaining systems performance in terms of memory and runtime. Moreover,
908   although we showed that AdaPMixED can handle large inference loads, one advantage of DP-SGD
909   is that it can handle an infinite number of inferences due to the post-processing theorem. Lastly, the
910   use of a public model is not necessarily risk-free in terms of privacy Carlini et al. (2021), and thus
911   caution is advised to further understand the privacy risks of pre-trained LLMs.
