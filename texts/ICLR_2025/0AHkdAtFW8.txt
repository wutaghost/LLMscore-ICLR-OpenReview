011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            For a given ground cost, approximating the Monge optimal transport map that
014            pushes forward a given probability measure onto another has become a staple
015            in several modern machine learning algorithms. The fourth-order Ma-Trudinger-
016
               Wang (MTW) tensor associated with this ground cost function provides a notion
               of curvature in optimal transport. The non-negativity of this tensor plays a crucial
017
               role for establishing continuity for the Monge optimal transport map. It is, how-
018
               ever, generally difficult to analytically verify this condition for any given ground
019            cost. To expand the class of cost functions for which MTW non-negativity can be
020            verified, we propose a provably correct computational approach which provides
021            certificates of non-negativity for the MTW tensor using Sum-of-Squares (SOS)
022            programming. We further show that our SOS technique can also be used to com-
023            pute an inner approximation of the region where MTW non-negativity holds. We
024            apply our proposed SOS programming method to several practical ground cost
025            functions to approximate the regions of regularity of their corresponding optimal
026            transport maps.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Optimal transport (OT) (Villani, 2021; 2009; Santambrogio, 2015), originally considered by Gaspard
      Monge in 1781 (Monge, 1781), has become a key tool in modern machine learning with applications
032
      in generative modeling (Montavon et al., 2016; Bousquet et al., 2017; Balaji et al., 2020; Rout et al.,
033
      2021; Lipman et al., 2022; Houdard et al., 2023), adversarial training (Sanjabi et al., 2018; Bhagoji
034   et al., 2019), control (Chen et al., 2016b; 2021; Teter et al., 2024), and data science (Peyré et al.,
035   2019; Flamary et al., 2021). In many applications, it is of interest (Seguy et al., 2018; Makkuva et al.,
036   2020; Bunne et al., 2022; Pooladian et al., 2023; Manole et al., 2024) to numerically approximate
037   the Monge OT map τ : X → Y where X , Y ⊆ M, and M is a smooth, compact, connected
038   n-dimensional manifold.
039

040   Y, respectively, let c : X × Y → R≥0 be a C 4 smooth function that encodes the cost of transporting
041   unit amount of mass from x ∈ X to y ∈ Y. The function c is referred to as the ground cost. The
042   problem seeks to find the Borel map τopt , referred   to as the Monge OT map, which minimizes the
                                                       R

066

067   elements of the MTW tensor are rational. For this to hold, A1 is sufficient but not necessary. In
068   fact, the development of OT regularity theory was motivated by the engineering problem of reflector
069   antenna design (Loeper, 2009) cast as an OT problem with non-rational cost c(x, y) = − log ∥x−y∥,
070   which our proposed framework can handle. Furthermore, many cost functions in practice are either
071   already polynomials/rationals, or smooth enough to be well-approximated by polynomials/rationals.
072   This is another reason why the assumption A1 is benign.
073   The proposed approach is based on Sum-of-Squares (SOS) programming (Prajna et al., 2002; Par-
074   rilo, 2003; Prajna et al., 2005; Laurent, 2009) well-known in optimization and control literature. As
075   such, ours is the first work on computational certification of OT regularity, and should be of interest
076   to the broader SOS programming research community.

135   2.1    T HE REGULARITY PROBLEM OF OPTIMAL TRANSPORT
136
      Kantorovich (1942) proved the existence of solution for a broad class of OT problems where the
137
      optimal transport plan is given by a coupling of the measures µ and ν. This relaxation is known
138   as the Kantorovich problem of OT. However, the existence theory for the Monge OT problem is
139   considerably more subtle. The conditions for existence and µ-a.e. uniqueness of the Monge OT
140   map τopt were established by Brenier (1991) for c(x, y) = ∥x − y∥22 in Euclidean space, and by
141   Gangbo & McCann (1996) for more general ground costs c(x, y). Roughly speaking, these results
142   state that if the ground cost c is sufficiently smooth and non-degenerate and the measures µ, ν are
143   sufficiently regular (for instance, compactly supported and Lebesgue absolutely continuous), then
144   the solution to the Kantorovich problem will also be a solution to the Monge problem. Furthermore,
145   the measurable mapping τopt is then the c-subdifferential of a potential function ϕ, which is the
146
      weak solution of the Jacobian equation
                                det ∇2 ϕ − A(x, ∇ϕ) = f (x, ϕ, ∇ϕ), x ∈ X .

147                                                                                                   (2)
148   Here, A is a matrix-valued function derived from the ground cost c, and f is a positive function
149   which depends on c, µ, and ν. Since this equation involves the determinant of the Hessian ∇2 , it is
150   a Monge-Ampére-type partial differential equation (Benamou et al., 2014).
151
      Once the existence of the Monge OT has been established, it is natural to study the continuity or
152
      smoothness of τopt , which is equivalent to understanding the qualitative behavior of solutions to (2).
153
154   The notion of weak solutions for Monge-Ampére type equations was established by Aleksandrov
155   (1958). Using this theory, it can be shown that the potential ϕ will be differentiable a.e., which
156   implies that the optimal transport is well-defined (see De Philippis & Figalli (2014) for a survey on
157
      this topic). However, in order to establish the continuity of the map τopt (e.g., a Lipschitz estimate),
158
      it is necessary to find an a priori C 2 estimate for the potential. This problem is known as the
      regularity problem of optimal transport, and provides the primary motivation for our work.
159
160   After Brenier (1991), several works (Caffarelli, 1992; Delanoë, 1991; Urbas, 1997) studied the reg-
161   ularity problem for the squared-distance cost in Euclidean space. They established that the transport
      is smooth whenever the measures are sufficiently smooth and the support of the target measure is


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      convex. In addition, Caffarelli showed that the non-convexity of the target space provides a global
163   obstruction1 to establishing smoothness for the transport. These works relied crucially on the classic
164   result from convex analysis that the sub-differential of a convex function is a convex set (Rockafellar,
165   1970, p. 215), which prevented their arguments from being extended to other cost functions.
166
      In Ma et al. (2005); Trudinger & Wang (2009), the authors introduced a new condition on the ground
167
      cost c, which is now known as the MTW condition. They showed that this condition was sufficient
168
      to establish the continuity of τopt , so long as the measures µ, ν were sufficiently regular and the
169   target measure satisfied the appropriate notion of convexity. Later, Loeper (2009) discovered that
170   for sufficiently smooth costs, the MTW condition is equivalent to the sub-differential of a c-convex
171   function being connected, which establishes the necessity of this condition in the regularity theory.
172   As a result, we say that costs c which satisfy this condition are regular. In order to state this condition
173   precisely, we first introduce a quantity referred to as the MTW tensor or the MTW curvature.

212   be a potential application for certifying NNCC or MTW condition beyond the regularity of OT map.

527   5    C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   We propose a provably correct computational approach to the forward and inverse problems of deter-
530   mining regularity for a cost function based on the sum-of-squares (SOS) programming. The forward
531   problem verifies that a given ground cost globally satisfies non-negative cost curvature (NNCC) or
532   the Ma-Trudinger-Wang (MTW) condition. The inverse problem concerns with finding a region in
533   which the NNCC or the MTW condition holds. The proposed computational approach generalizes
534   for a large class of costs including but not limited to rational functions, and is the first computational
535
      work on OT regularity. Our contributions here significantly advance the current state-of-the-art
      where the NNCC and the MTW conditions have been analytically verified for a limited number of
536
      problems. Since the desired conditions require checking the non-negativity of biquadratic forms,
537
      analytical approaches have remained unwieldy. We demonstrate that the proposed SOS program-
538   ming approach can leverage existing solvers, can recover known results in the literature, and can
539   help discover new results on OT regularity.
