011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Generative models, particularly diffusion models, have been utilized as world
014            models in offline reinforcement learning (RL) to generate synthetic data, enhancing
015            policy learning efficiency. Current approaches either train diffusion models once
016            before policy learning begins or rely on online interactions for alignment. In this
017            paper, we propose a novel offline RL algorithm, Adaptive Diffusion World Model
018            for Policy Evaluation (ADEPT), which integrates closed-loop policy evaluation
019
               with world model adaptation. It employs an uncertainty-penalized diffusion model
               to iteratively interact with the target policy for evaluation. The uncertainty of
020
               the world model is estimated by comparing the output generated with different
021
               noises, which is then used to constrain out-of-distribution actions. During policy
022            training, the diffusion model performs importance-sampled updates to progressively
023            align with the evolving policy. We analyze the performance of the proposed
024            method and provide an upper bound on the return gap between our method and
025            the real environment under the target policy. The results shed light on various
026            key factors affecting learning performance. Evaluations on the D4RL benchmark
027            demonstrate significant improvement over state-of-the-art baselines, especially
028            when only suboptimal demonstrations are available – thus requiring improved
029            alignment between the world model and offline policy evaluation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods have garnered much attention recently (Levine et al.,
034
      2020; Prudencio et al., 2023), due to their abilities to train policies based on offline datasets collected
035
      by a behavior policy, rather than relying on costly and sometimes risky online interactions (Kiran
036   et al., 2021). With only limited data of transitions or trajectories available, it is prone to overestimating
037   the reward from out-of-distribution states and actions as the learned policy gradually deviates from
038   the behavior policy used for collecting the data. This issue, known as the distributional shift (Kumar
039   et al., 2019), is one of the key challenges in offline RL.
040
      To address this, various solutions have been proposed, such as augmenting the dataset by building
041
      world models (Yu et al., 2021; Rigter et al., 2022; Matsushima et al., 2020), and policy regularization
042   approaches (Kumar et al., 2019; Rashidinejad et al., 2021). The key idea of building world models is
043   to learn the transition dynamics of the underlying Markov Decision Process (MDP). Once trained on
044   the offline dataset, the policy can interact with the world model and generate additional synthetic
045   trajectories. Existing works include world models generated by VAE (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018;
046   Hafner et al., 2023; Ozair et al., 2021), GAN (Eysenbach et al., 2022), Transformers (Janner et al.,
047   2021), and more recently Diffusion (Ding et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2023). However, most of these works
048   either train a world model one-time prior to policy learning (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020;
049   Janner et al., 2019) or require additional online interaction data to adapt the world model (Kaiser
050
      et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2019). Neither approach effectively mitigates distributional shifts, relying
      solely on offline data. Furthermore, the return gap between world models (e.g., diffusion models) and
051
      the real environment in such offline RL algorithms remains to be analyzed.
052
053   This paper proposes a novel approach to offline RL, integrating policy evaluation and world-model
      adaptation, namely the Adaptive Diffusion World Model for Policy Evaluation (ADEPT). ADEPT

054
      encompasses two collaborative components: (i) a guided diffusion world model with uncertainty
055   estimation, which generates uncertainty-penalized synthetic trajectories by evaluating the target policy
056   step-by-step; and (ii) importance-sampled updates, based on the offline dataset, to align the world
057   model with the evolving policy. These two components operate in a closed loop throughout training.
058   Existing works in RL typically treat diffusion world models as synthesizers for data augmentation (Lu
059   et al., 2023), policy representation (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022), or multistep planners (Janner
060   et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2024a). These approaches generate additional synthetic trajectories with a
061   fixed world model throughout the policy improvement iterations. In contrast, ADEPT continually
062   adapts the diffusion model using importance sampling with respect to the distributional shift between
063
      the target policy πk and the behavior policy πb . The updated diffusion model is then used to evaluate
      πk for policy improvement, with a sequence of actions drawn from πk used as the conditional input
064
      to the world model. We note that ADEPT requires alterations between next-state generation using the
065
      guided diffusion world model and next-action sampling from the target policy.
066
067   Furthermore, we analyze the performance of the proposed ADEPT algorithm theoretically and
068   empirically. We provide the bound of the return gap between the policy under actual environment
069
      and that under the diffusion world model, and show that the monotonic improvement of return can be
      guaranteed when the one-step policy update under the world model is larger than this bound. We
070
      further decompose the bound into three factors: model transition error, reward prediction error and
071
      policy shift, and discuss how ADEPT lowers these factors and addresses other significant issues such
072   as distributional shift problem to enhance the performance. To the best of our knowledge, this is
073   the first analysis for offline RL with diffusion world models. The proposed algorithm is evaluated
074   on D4RL benchmark (Fu et al., 2020) in multiple MuJoCo environments. We notice that ADEPT
075   works best with datasets consisting of mainly suboptimal demonstrations, where the distributional
076   shift becomes more severe as target policy moves toward optimum. The results show that ADEPT
077   significantly improves the baseline SAC method (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and largely outperforms
078   other SOTA offline RL methods including model-free, model-based, and diffusion-based algorithms
079   with up to 20% improvement on average.
080   The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
081
082         1. We propose a novel model-based offline RL algorithm, ADEPT, with closed-loop operations
083            of policy evaluation and improvement based on an uncertainty-penalized diffusion model
084            and world model adaptation with importance sampling.
085         2. We provide theoretical proof of bounding the return gap between the diffusion world model
086            and actual environment under the same policy, and discuss how ADEPT narrows the bound
087            and solves the key issues to enhance the performance.
088         3. We evaluate our method on the D4RL benchmarks and demonstrate significant improvement
089            over the state-of-the-art baselines, especially on suboptimal datasets.

092   2    R ELATED W ORKS
093
094   Offline RL. Offline RL faces the distributional shift problem (Kumar et al., 2020) due to data
095   collected using a specific behavior policy. Various methods have been proposed to regularize an
096
      offline RL policy and address this issue. In particular, model-based offline RL methods such as
      MOReL (Kidambi et al., 2020), MOPO (Yu et al., 2020), RAMBO (Rigter et al., 2022) and COMBO
097
      (Yu et al., 2021) train an extra MLP to mimic the dynamics and develop different ways to penalize
098
      the reward or value function in unseen state and action pairs to address the out-of-distribution
099   issues. Other model-free methods, including BCQ (Fujimoto et al., 2019), IQL (Kostrikov et al.,
100   2021), CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), and TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), add different regularization
101   mechanisms that are defined on action or value function, forcing the policy to act more conservatively.
102   Our proposed ADEPT framework can be combined with any of these offline RL algorithms.
103
104   World Models for RL. The use of world models to generate synthetic data for RL was first
105   proposed by Ha & Schmidhuber (2018), as an extension of traditional model-based RL methods,
106   utilizing VAE and RNN for predicting environmental transitions. Following this approach, various
107   world models with advanced capabilities of fitting desired distributions have been proposed, including
      convolutional U-networks (Kaiser et al., 2019), vector-quantized autoencoders (Ozair et al., 2021),


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generative adversarial networks (Eysenbach et al., 2022), energy-based models (Boney et al., 2020),
109   transformers (Janner et al., 2021), and diffusion (Ding et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2023). These world
110   models are mainly used for trajectory synthesis with limited adaptability.
111
112   The Use of Diffusion Models in RL. Diffusion is a state-of-the-art technique for generating
113   synthetic samples of images, videos or texts (Ho et al., 2020). It was first introduced by Janner et al.
114   (2022) as a multistep planner in offline RL, where the diffusion model directly generates trajectories
115   that are used for decision-making. This is further extended to conditional actions (Ajay et al., 2022),
116   meta-RL (Ni et al., 2023), hierarchical tasks (Li et al., 2023), multitask problems (He et al., 2023),
117   multi-agent tasks (Zhu et al., 2023) and safe planning (Xiao et al., 2023). Diffusion models are also
118   employed for policy expression (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022), imitation learning (Hegde
119   et al., 2024) and reward modeling (Nuti et al., 2023). Lu et al. (2023) adopted the diffusion model as
120
      a data synthesizer to generate additional synthetic data based on offline datasets before policy training.
      Later, a conditional diffusion world model was proposed to generate trajectories from current state
121
      and action, to support offline value-based RL (Ding et al., 2024a) and policy-based RL (Jackson
122
      et al., 2024). However, these methods don’t fully solve the distributional shift issue since the shift
123   problem in world model is not considered. Different from these existing works, our method ADEPT
124   utilizes the essence of diffusion model to estimate uncertainty for policy regularization, and adapt
125   the diffusion model through importance sampling inspired by previous methods in online RL(Wang
126   et al., 2023). The theoretical analysis of the return gap between ADEPT and the actual environment
127   under the optimal policy is also provided.

      Offline RL using World Models We consider an unknown MDP, referred to as the environ-
132
      ment. Supposing the MDP is fully observable with discrete time, it could be defined by the tuple
133   M = (S, A, P, R, µ0 , γ). S and A are the state and action spaces, respectively. P (s′ |s, a) is the
134   transition probability with s, s′ ∈ S, a ∈ A, and R : S × A → R is the reward function. µ0
135   is the initial state distribution and γ is the discount factor. We consider an agent that acts within
136   the environment based on a policy π(a|s) repeatedly. In each time step t, the agent receives a
137   state st and samples an action via its policy at ∼ π(·|st ). The environment transforms into a new
138   state st+1 ∼ P (·|st , at ) and returns a reward rt = R(st , at ). After a whole episode of interac-
139   tions, a trajectory τ = (s0 , a0 , r0 , . . . , sT −1 , aT −1 , rT −1 , sT ) will be generated, which contains
140   states, actions and rewards of maximum length T . Based on that, the goal of RL is to learn an
141
      optimal policy π ∗ to maximize the expectation of discounted cumulative rewards from this MDP:
                                   PT −1
142   π ∗ = arg maxπ E(s,a)∼π ( t=0 γ t rt ).
143   Specifically in offline model-based RL, only a dataset of trajectories D is available, and a prediction
144   model of the environment is introduced, denoted as world model M̂ = (S, A, P̂ , R̂, µ0 , γ), to
145   improve sample efficiency for further learning and planning. Commonly, the world model learns a
146   single-step transition approximating the real dynamics P of the environment in a supervised method
147   based on D. Hence, once a world model has been trained, it could replace the real environment to
148   generate synthetic trajectories. Similar to standard RL, an initial state s0 is sampled first from datasets,
149   and based on that the interactions start. After certain length of steps H, referred as horizon, a synthetic
150   trajectory τ̂ = (ŝ0 , â0 , r̂0 , ..., ŝH−1 , âH−1 , r̂H−1 , ŝH ) is generated, in which ŝt+1 ∼ P̂ (·|ŝt , ât ) and
151   r̂t = R̂(st , at ). These imaginary trajectories are added into the replay buffer for policy optimization.

197   4.1   P OLICY E VALUATION USING G UIDED D IFFUSION M ODEL
198
      Before the policy iteration, we first utilize the offline dataset to initialize the guided diffusion world
199
      model M̂θ,η , consisting of noise prediction model ϵθ and a two-layer MLP rη , to simulate the
200
      conditional distribution of the one-step transition P (st+1 |st , at ) and reward function R(st , at ),
201
      respectively. We denote the simulated transition probability via ϵθ as Pθ . The offline dataset is
202   normalized by linearly mapping each dimension of the state and action spaces to have 0 mean
203   and 1 standard deviation. While training the diffusion model, we sample a minibatch of tuples
204   (st , at , rt , st+1 ) consisting of the state, action, reward, and next state from the normalized offline
205   datasets in each iteration. As mentioned in Section 3, we follow DDPM and adopt the classifier-free
206   method to learn the conditional probabilities of the state transition based on current state st and action
207   at . Since the reward function plays a significant role in RL training, the model should emphasize
208   more on its accuracy and decouple the reward function from state transition dynamics. Therefore, we
209   separately train rη (st , at , st+1 ) to predict the reward function and the terminal signal. Introducing
210
      st+1 as an extra input significantly improves the accuracy of reward prediction, and in evaluation
      st+1 is replaced by ŝt+1 , which is sampled from Pθ (ŝt+1 |st , at ).
211

214   estimate the rewards in state-action pairs not included in the dataset. To deal with the distributional
215   shift problem, previous methods mainly use the ensemble of learned models to estimate the uncer-
      tainty (Lowrey et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020). For the diffusion model, since

400   than C. However, practically the monotonic improvement could face trouble due to the following
401   limitations:
402           • The compounding error: Though the diffusion model has lower prediction error after
403             training on D than traditional MLPs, the state transition error in each step accumulates as
404             the compounding error, decreasing long-term planning accuracy.
405           • Out of distribution: While using the diffusion world model for policy evaluation, the action
406             derived from the policy can drive the state out of the distribution represented by D. In that
407             case, the generated state and reward become unstable, causing the overestimation of returns
408             from unknown state-action pairs.
409           • Aleatory uncertainty: In this method, the uncertainty estimator could have trouble to
410             distinguish the aleatory uncertainty and the epistemic uncertainty. Though such a method is
411             competitive to determined MDPs or MDPs with Lipschitz transitions, further study should
412             be considered before moving to stochastic environments.
413
      To handle these, we set H and λ0 small enough to limit the accumulating prediction errors, while
414
      randomly choosing the initial state from the whole dataset and replay buffer. To avoid overestimating
415   out-of-distribution state-action pairs, we choose proper uncertainty-based threshold and penalty
416   parameters, preventing the policy from visiting these pairs. Finally, to solve the aleatory uncertainty
417   issue, there has been work using a similar idea with a single diffusion model and a Bayesian hyper-
418   network (Chan et al.), which we consider for future work.

526   6      C ONCLUSION
527
528   This paper proposes a new model-based offline RL algorithm ADEPT adopting (i) an uncertainty-
529   penalized diffusion world model to directly evaluate and optimize the target policy in offline re-
530   inforcement learning and (ii) an importance-sampled world model update to adaptively align the
531
      world model with the evolving policy, in a closed-loop operation. These two key components enable
      ADEPT to significantly reduce the distributional shift problem and avoid reward overestimation under
532
      out-of-distribution state and action pairs. Our theoretical analysis of the algorithm provides an upper
533
      bound on the return gap and illuminates key factors affecting the learning performance. Experimental
534   results on the D4RL benchmark show that ADEPT significantly improves the total performance and
535   training stability of SAC, and substantially outperforms other state-of-the-art offline RL baselines
536   in almost all tasks. Compared to the best scores of other algorithms, ADEPT achieves an average
537   advantage of 15.4%, with 20.1% on random dataset, and 6.2% on medium dataset. Our work provides
538   important insights into the use of diffusion-based world model in offline RL. Exciting future research
539   could extend uncertainty-penalized diffusion model to a multistep planner, or generalize it to more
      complicated scenes such as partial observable, multi-agent or stochastic environments.

1051   world model and affects the final performance. With higher λr and lower λ0 , the target policy will
1052   be more conservative and tend to clone behaviors in the dataset, and its value function will predict
1053   out-of-distribution state-action pairs with lower reward, which increases the training stability. On the

1055   find potential trajectories with higher cumulative rewards. We conducted additional experiments on
1056   hopper-random and hopper-medium environments to verify this and show the result in Table 5. The
