009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                The Lurie Network is proposed as a unifying architecture for modelling time-
012                invariant nonlinear dynamical systems. Many existing continuous-time models
013                including Recurrent Neural Networks and Neural Oscillators are special cases
014
                   of the Lurie Network when applied to this domain. Motivated by the need for a
                   general inductive bias, shared by many systems, this paper proposes an approach to
015
                   enable network weights and biases to be trained in such a manner that a generalised
016                concept of stability is guaranteed. This generalised stability measure is that of
017                k-contraction which enables global convergence to a point, line or plane in the
018                neural state-space. This result is leveraged to construct a Graph Lurie Network
019                (GLN) satisfying the same convergence properties. Unconstrained parametrisations
020                of these conditions are derived allowing the models to be trained using standard
021                optimisation algorithms, whilst limiting the search space to solutions satisfying the
                   k-contraction constraints. Empirical results show significant improvement in terms
022
                   of prediction accuracy, generalisation and robustness compared to other uncon-
023
                   strained and stability-constrained models. Furthermore, both models consistently
024                learnt representations which respected the convergence behaviour of the dynamics.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   A Lurie1 system is a class of nonlinear ordinary differential equations (ODE) comprising a linear
030   time-invariant (LTI) component interconnected with a, potentially time-varying, nonlinearity. Such
031   systems are ubiquitous throughout the sciences and engineering, including machine learning (ML)
032   and neuroscience Pauli et al. (2021a); Lessard et al. (2016); Lanthaler et al. (2024); Wilson & Cowan
033
      (1972). When modelling time-invariant dynamical systems, many ML models including Linear State
      Space Models (LSSM), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and some Graph Neural Networks (GNN)
034
      are special cases of a Lurie system (§3.1). Such models have proven to be highly expressive as
035   demonstrated by their successful application on a wide range of tasks such as sequential processing
036   Kozachkov et al. (2022a); Erichson et al. (2020); Chang et al. (2019); Gu et al. (2021), computer
037   vision Erichson et al. (2020); Chang et al. (2019); Gu et al. (2021), language modelling Gu et al.
038   (2021) and computational chemistry Rusch et al. (2022).
039   Consider dynamical systems in the field of neuroscience: convergence and stability of a latent state
040   are crucial for learning, information propagation Kozachkov et al. (2020); Centorrino et al. (2022);
041   Pascanu et al. (2013); Vogt et al. (2022), memory storage Kozachkov et al. (2022b); Krotov &
042   Hopfield (2020); Ramsauer et al. (2020) and robustness Manchester et al. (2021); Pauli et al. (2021b;
043   2023). For example, multi-stable and orbitally stable systems are, respectively, observed in associative
044
      memory Kozachkov et al. (2023) and working memory Kozachkov et al. (2022b). Furthermore, these
      processes occur in different regions of the brain interconnected through a graph structure. A graph
045
      structure and these convergent properties are shared with many other dynamical systems such as
046   chemical processes Ofir et al. (2023), opinion dynamics and power systems Ofir et al. (2024).
047
048   The convergence and stability analysis of dynamical systems has been well-studied in the control
      theory literature. A pertinent example is the absolute stability problem where the nonlinearity of
049
      the Lurie system is unknown, but assumed to be sector-bounded or slope-restricted. The goal is to
050   find conditions on the model parameters which ensure the trajectories of all Lurie systems, with
051   nonlinearities in the assumed class, uphold a chosen definition of convergence. Approaches to
052   this problem can be classified as Lyapunov analysis Khalil (2002); Park (2002; 1997), Zames-Falb
053

      multipliers Zames & Falb (1968); Turner & Drummond (2019); Carrasco et al. (2016); Drummond
055   et al. (2024) or k-contraction analysis Zhang & Cui (2013); Ofir et al. (2023; 2024). Lyapunov and
056   Zames-Falb multiplier methods are primarily designed to analyse the convergence to an equilibrium
057   point, whereas k-contraction methods analyse a variety of global convergence behaviours including
058   convergence to points, lines and planes. As many ML models are examples of Lurie systems and
059   many activation functions are sector-bounded or slope-restricted (Drummond et al., 2022, Table 1),
060   much of the literature on the absolute stability problem is applicable to systems involving neural
      networks Pauli et al. (2021a); Richardson et al. (2023; 2024); Fazlyab et al. (2020).
061
062   Although designing networks with convergent dynamics is well motivated as an inductive bias,
063   ensuring such a property requires constraints on the network parameters, which can be detrimental if
064   too restrictive. With this in mind, this work focuses on: (i) using k-contraction analysis to derive mild
      constraints on the weights of the Lurie Network which ensure global convergence to a point, line or
065
      plane in the neural state space, for all Lurie Networks with slope-restricted activation functions; (ii)
066   establishing unconstrained parametrisations of these conditions which allows the Lurie Network to be
067   trained using gradient based optimisation algorithms whilst limiting the search space to weights which
068   satisfy the convergence conditions; (iii) constructing a Graph Lurie Network (GLN) from individual
069   Lurie Networks and deriving constraints on the graph coupling matrix which ensure the k-contraction
070   property is maintained. Similar unconstrained parametrisations are also derived. We compare the
071   proposed models against other continuous-time architectures and show the proposed approach leads
072
      to more accurate, generalisable and robust models for a range of time-invariant dynamical systems.

      2.2 k- CONTRACTION A NALYSIS
092
093   In this work, we leverage k-contraction analysis Wu et al. (2022); Muldowney (1990), the geomet-
094   rical generalisation of contraction analysis Lohmiller & Slotine (1998), as a tool for controlling
095   convergence in the neural state space. Intuitively, k-contraction implies the volume of k-dimensional
096   bodies exponentially converges to zero when governed by the system dynamics. Alternatively, this
097   could be thought of as exponential convergence to a (k − 1)-dimensional subspace. When k = 1, this
      reduces to standard contraction Lohmiller & Slotine (1998), which implies that all trajectories expo-
098
      nentially converge to a single trajectory. For a general time-varying dynamical system, satisfying the
099
      k-contraction property does not guarantee stability. However, for time-invariant dynamical systems,
100   it has been shown that for every bounded solution: 1-contraction implies global convergence to a
101   unique equilibrium point Lohmiller & Slotine (1998), 2-contraction implies global convergence to an
102   equilibrium point, which is not necessarily unique but must be connected along a line Muldowney
103   (1990), and 3-contraction, under certain assumptions, implies convergence to a non-unique attractor
104   Cecilia et al. (2023). Three examples of k-contracting dynamics are presented in Figure 1.
105   Time-invariant dynamical systems which satisfy the k-contraction property for k ∈ {1, 2, 3} have
106   several desirable properties for ML models. They can exhibit a wide range of complex convergent
107   behaviours such as multi-stable and orbitally stable systems Zoboli et al. (2024). This suggests that a
      model can be expressive whilst satisfying the k-contraction conditions, particularly for higher values

314   Theorem 5 or Theorem 6, has a total parameter count NK ≤ 2n2 + m2 . The number of parameters
315   only increases significantly when m is large compared to n; however, throughout the literature, many
316   special cases of a Lurie Network set n = m (see §3.1); in which case, NK becomes marginally
317   smaller than NL . Furthermore, the all-to-all graph coupling term has NC = 2(qn)2 parameters or
318   NC = qn2 (q − 1) if the self-loops are removed according to Remark 3. This analysis highlights that
319
      ensuring the Lurie Network and GLN are k-contracting comes at a minimal computational expense.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
468
469
      Several constrained continuous-time RNN models exist in the ML literature. The model structure of
470   three notable examples were presented in §3.1 as they happen to be special cases of a Lurie Network,
471   when modelling time-invariant dynamical systems. The Antisymmetric RNN Chang et al. (2019)
472   and the Lipschitz RNN Erichson et al. (2020) were designed to address the exploding and vanishing
473   gradient problem Pascanu et al. (2013). The Antisymmetric RNN did so by parametrising the RNN
474   such that the real eigenvalues of the Jacobian were zero. It achieved this by setting A = 0 and
475   restricting C to being skew-symmetric. Whilst this does prevent the gradients from exploding and
      vanishing, it restricts the dynamics which the model can learn to purely oscillatory behaviour. The
476
      Lipschitz RNN has a more relaxed parametrisation. This model constructs the A and C matrices such
477
      that they are both convex combinations of symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices. However, the
478   weight of the symmetric matrix can only vary between 0 and 0.5, whereas the weight of the skew-
479   symmetric matrix can vary between 0.5 and 1. Again, this addresses the vanishing and exploding
480   gradient problem, but the model cannot encode dynamics which are predominately decaying or
481   growing. Finally, the RNN proposed in Kozachkov et al. (2022a) has biological motivations and
482   encodes 1-contracting dynamics. This implies that all possible trajectories will exponentially converge,
483
      making the model robust to input disturbances.
484   Whilst the models above address a variety of problems, each model is quite limited in the range of
485   dynamics they can learn, which is a problem when trying to design a general model for learning
      time-invariant dynamical systems. With respect to (2), the Lurie Network has more flexibility than all


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of these models. Firstly, it includes all three weight matrices (A, B, C) and biases (bx , by ), whereas
487   the models mentioned above fix at least one of these parameters. Secondly, the constraints imposed,
488   and the corresponding parametrisations, allow the model to learn a variety of dynamics including, but
489   not limited to, those mentioned above. The only limitation is that the dynamics must converge in
490   some way.
491   The Lurie Network is also related to a class of feed-forward models named Implicit or Equilibrium
492   Networks. These models use an implicit equation to express the relationship between the model
493   output, layer outputs and model input in a compact vectorised form El Ghaoui et al. (2021). Like
494   the Lurie Network, these models can be represented by the interconnection of a linear time-invariant
495   system and a nonlinearity. This makes analysis tools from Control Theory, such as Lipschitz bounds,
496   applicable to these models Fazlyab et al. (2019). An additional connection is that the solution to the
      implicit equations correspond to equilibrium points of a Lurie system Revay et al. (2020).
497
498   As mentioned in the introduction, the k-contraction constraints used in this paper have an interesting
499   connection to some properties observed in biological learning systems. A 2-contracting model
500   can replicate the behaviour of associative memory, where every stored pattern corresponds to an
501
      equilibrium Kozachkov et al. (2023). Furthermore, a 3-contracting model can replicate the dynamics
      of working memory, where patterns are retained as attractor states Kozachkov et al. (2022b). The
502
      conditions developed in this paper could be of interest to Neuroscientists and ML researchers
503   interested in memory storage and retrieval Ramsauer et al. (2020); Hopfield (1984); Krotov & Hopfield
504   (2020). The formation of the GLN also has connections to biology. For example, constructing larger
505   systems from smaller modules can be motivated by evolutionary biology Simon (1962) where the
506   name facilitated variation Gerhart & Kirschner (2007) is used to describe the development of traits
507   in response to adaptation of the regulatory elements that connect modules, rather than the core
508
      components themselves. This was investigated in Kozachkov et al. (2022a) where the weights of the
      individual RNNs satisfied a 1-contraction condition but were fixed. Only the graph coupling weights
509
      were learnt during training.
510
511   The relationship between properties guaranteed by k-contraction analysis and those observed in
512
      associative and working memory suggest the k-contracting Lurie Network possesses a number of
      appealing properties for an ML model; hence, it may be suitable for a wider class of ML problems.
513
      This proposition is supported by the successful application of the constrained RNN models (special
514   cases of the Lurie Network) on a wide range of ML tasks. Since the Lurie Network is a more
515   structured, time-invariant example of a Neural ODE Chen et al. (2018), it will be applicable to a
516   similar array of tasks. Beyond modelling time-invariant dynamical systems, this includes image
517   classification and continuous normalising flows Kidger (2022). The only limiting requirement is
518   that the input must be passed in through the initial condition which in some cases, such as image
519   classification, may require a pre-processing layer.

523   7    C ONCLUSION
524
525
526
      The Lurie Network was presented as a novel and unifying architecture for modelling time-invariant
527
      dynamical systems, with more flexibility than comparable methods. Many dynamical systems of
528   interest exhibit convergent behaviour in some form; this inductive bias was built into the Lurie
529   Network through the use of k-contraction analysis. Furthermore, a principled approach was proposed
530   for constructing k-contracting Graph Lurie Networks out of k-contracting modules. Both the k-
531   contracting Lurie Network and GLN demonstrated improved prediction accuracy, out of distribution
532   generalisation and robustness on a range of examples. Furthermore, they were the only models
533   to consistently and accurately predict the convergence behaviour of the dynamics in both the high
534
      and low dimensional datasets. Future theoretical work will try to expand the class of systems the
      k-contracting Lurie Network can be optimised over, by obtaining similar results for systems which
535
      are k-contracting in a diagonal metric. It would also be interesting to find conditions on the inputs of a
536   time-varying Lurie Network for which the convergence properties of the time-invariant k-contracting
537   Lurie Network are also upheld, making the Lurie Network applicable to sequential processing tasks.
538   Finally, we would like to empirically investigate the performance of the model on a wider range of
539   applications. The recently proposed working memory benchmark Sikarwar & Zhang (2024) is of
      particular interest due to its relationship with 3-contracting dynamics.
