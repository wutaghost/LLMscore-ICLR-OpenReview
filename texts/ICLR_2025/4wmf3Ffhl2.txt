011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine learning (ML) models are increasingly used in various applications, from
014            recommendation systems in e-commerce to diagnosis prediction in healthcare. In
015
               this paper, we present a novel dynamic framework for thinking about the deploy-
               ment of ML models in a performative, human-ML collaborative system. In our
016
               framework, the introduction of ML recommendations changes the data-generating
017
               process of human decisions, which are only a proxy to the ground truth and which
018            are then used to train future versions of the model. We show that this dynamic pro-
019            cess in principle can converge to different stable points, i.e. where the ML model
020            and the Human+ML system have the same performance. Some of these stable
021            points are suboptimal with respect to the actual ground truth. As a proof of concept,
022            we conduct an empirical user study with 1,408 participants. In the study, humans
023            solve instances of the knapsack problem with the help of machine learning predic-
024            tions of varying performance. This is an ideal setting because we can identify the
025            actual ground truth, and evaluate the performance of human decisions supported by
026
               ML recommendations. We find that for many levels of ML performance, humans
               can improve upon the ML predictions. We also find that the improvement could
027
               be even higher if humans rationally followed the ML recommendations. Finally,
028
               we test whether monetary incentives can increase the quality of human decisions,
029            but we fail to find any positive effect. Using our empirical data to approximate our
030            collaborative system suggests that the learning process would dynamically reach an
031            equilibrium performance that is around 92% of the maximum knapsack value. Our
032            results have practical implications for the deployment of ML models in contexts
033            where human decisions may deviate from the indisputable ground truth.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Human-ML collaboration is increasingly used in various applications, from content moderation in
039
      social media (Lai et al., 2022) to predicting diagnoses in healthcare (Jacobs et al., 2021; Dvijotham
      et al., 2023) and making hiring decisions in human resources (Peng et al., 2022). Companies that
040
      implement human-ML collaborative systems face three crucial challenges: 1) ML models learn from
041
      past human decisions, which are often only an approximation to the ground truth (noisy labels); 2)
042   ML models are rolled out to help future human decisions, affecting the data-generating process of
043   human-ML collaboration that then influences future updates to ML models (performative predictions
044   as in Perdomo et al. (2020)); and 3) the quality of the human-ML collaborative prediction of the
045   ground truth may change as a function of incentives and other human factors. These challenges create
046   a dynamic learning process. Without access to the ground truth, it is often difficult to know whether
047   the learning process will reach an equilibrium state with a good approximation of the ground truth, if
048   it is interrupted at a sub-optimal level, or if it does not reach a stable state at all.
049   For intuition, we can focus on the decision of a healthcare company to develop and deploy an ML
050   model to predict medical diagnoses from patient visits. The problem is made difficult by the fact
051   that a doctor’s diagnoses can be wrong, and it is often too costly or time-consuming to identify the
052   indisputable ground truth—i.e., the underlying true diagnosis of a patient—so the company typically
053   uses all diagnoses to train their ML model, without distinction between good or bad diagnoses. In
      addition, the company typically evaluates the algorithm’s performance based on its ability to match


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      those same doctor diagnoses, potentially replicating their mistakes. The dynamic deployment of
055   updates to ML models that support doctor diagnoses could lead to a downward spiral of human+ML
056   performance if the company deploys a bad model and the bad model adversely affects doctor decisions.
057   Or, it can lead to continuous improvement until it reaches a stable point that is a good approximation
058   to the indisputable ground truth. Without (potentially costly) efforts to measure the ground truth, the
059   company has no way of distinguishing between downward spirals or continuous improvements.
060
      This raises a multitude of empirical questions regarding the governing mechanisms of this dynamic
061   system. How do humans improve on ML predictions of different quality levels, and do financial
062   incentives matter? Will the dynamic learning process converge to a good equilibrium even without
063   the company knowing the actual ground truth labels?
064
      Contributions. In this paper, we present a novel framework for thinking about ML deployment
065
      strategies in a performative, human-AI collaborative system. We present a theoretical framework to
066
      identify conditions under which ML deployment strategies converge to stable points that are a good
067   approximation to the ground truth, and conditions under which there are downward spirals away
068   from the ground truth. Our theory introduces the notion of a collaborative characteristic function,
069   which maps algorithmic performance to the performance of human decisions supported by ML
070   predictions. As a proof of concept for our theory, we provide an empirical study in which humans
071   solve knapsack problems with the help of machine learning predictions. We conducted a user study
072   with 1,408 participants, each of whom solved 10 knapsack problems. The empirical exercise allows
073   us to evaluate 1) the quality of human decisions supported by ML models of varying performance, 2)
074   how these human decisions compare to a best-case scenario, and 3) how these human decisions are
075
      affected by monetary incentives. With some additional assumptions, we can map these data to our
      theoretical collaborative characteristic function.
076
077   We highlight three main empirical results. First, we show that humans tend to improve upon the ML
078   recommendation for many levels of ML performance. Second, humans sometimes submit solutions
079   that are worse than the ML recommendation, despite the fact that with knapsack, it is fairly easy for
080   them to compare their solution to the ML suggestion and pick the best of the two. Third, humans do
081
      not respond to financial incentives for performance. The empirical data can be used to approximate
      theoretical collaborative characteristic functions. The results suggest that, at least in our context,
082
      collaborative characteristic functions are invariant to monetary incentives. Additionally, the fact that
083
      humans sometimes submit solutions that are worse than the provided ML recommendation implies
084   that there remains a gap between the collaborative characteristic function based on human labels and
085   the collaborative characteristic function constructed by selecting the maximum between the human
086   and ML solution.
087
      Our results have practical implications for the deployment of ML models when humans are influenced
088
      by those models but their decisions deviate from an unknown ground truth. First, performance metrics
089
      of ML models can be misleading when the learning objective is based on comparisons against human
090   decisions and those decisions can be wrong. Companies should thus exert efforts to assess the quality
091   of human decisions and take that into account when training ML models. For example, in the medical
092   setting, human diagnoses should be first verified or confirmed by external experts, or patients should
093   be followed up to confirm the validity of initial diagnoses. At a minimum, ML models should be
094   trained on subsets of data for which there is enough confidence that the decisions are correct. Second,
095   our work highlights the strategic importance of deploying ML models that allow for convergence
096   to a stable point with higher utility than humans alone. Such convergence is not guaranteed and, as
097   argued above, difficult to assess. Third, our work calls for the need to adopt a dynamic approach
098
      when deploying algorithms that interact with human decisions, and those interactions are used for
      future model building.

101   2    R ELATED W ORK
102
103   There has been a growing body of work investigating various forms of human-ML collaboration.
104   From learning-to-defer systems, where a model defers prediction tasks to humans if its own uncer-
105   tainty is too high (Cortes et al., 2016; Charusaie et al., 2022; Mozannar et al., 2023), to ML-assisted
106   decision making where humans may or may not consult ML predictions to make a decision (Mozannar
107   et al., 2024c; Dvijotham et al., 2023; Jacobs et al., 2021). Several alternative decision mechanisms
      have also been explored (Steyvers et al., 2022; Mozannar et al., 2024a). The application areas


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      range from programming (Dakhel et al., 2023; Mozannar et al., 2024b), to healthcare (Jacobs et al.,
109   2021; Dvijotham et al., 2023) and business consulting (Dell’Acqua et al., 2023). Related work also
110   investigates factors influencing human-ML collaboration, such as explanations of ML predictions
111   (Vasconcelos et al., 2023), monetary incentives (Agarwal et al., 2023), fairness constraints (Sühr
112   et al., 2021), and humans’ adaptability to model changes (Bansal et al., 2019). In this work, for the
113   first time to the best of our knowledge, we theoretically examine the human+ML interaction from a
114   dynamic perspective, where ML models learn from human decisions that are 1) the result of previous
115   human+ML collaboration and 2) can arbitrarily deviate from the underlying ground truth.
116   This paper is also inspired by an extensive line of work on performative prediction (Perdomo et al.,
117   2020; Mendler-Dünner et al., 2020; Hardt et al., 2022; Mendler-Dünner et al., 2022), a theoretical
118   framework in which predictions influence the outcome they intend to predict. We adapt the ideas
119   of performative prediction to a context of human-ML collaboration and extend it in three major
120   ways: 1) In our setting, the model predictions change the quality of the human-ML labels as a proxy
121   for the ground truth (e.g., a doctor diagnosis), but the ground truth is held constant (e.g., the true
122   patient diagnosis); 2) We introduce the concept of utility, to quantify the quality of a solution with
123
      respect to the ground truth. There can be several stable points with respect to model parameters in the
      performative prediction framework, but not all of them have the same utility, i.e., are equally good
124
      at approximating the indisputable ground truth; 3) The ground truth is unknown, and the mapping
125
      between human or ML labels and the ground truth is not fixed. To the best of our knowledge, we
126   are the first to explore performative predictions where the deployment of ML models occurs while
127   the model’s performance relative to the ground truth is unknown, and only its similarity to human
128   labels is available. Our empirical application is also novel in that it provides a first step towards
129   investigating the implications of performative predictions for human-ML collaboration.

133   We consider a setting in which time is separable in discrete time epochs t = 1, ..., T . At each t, a firm
134   deploys machine learning model Mt ∈ M of a model class M, with Mt : X → Y. The model Mt
135
      predicts a solution Y ∈ Y (e.g., a diagnosis) to a problem X ∈ X (e.g. the patient’s symptoms) as a
      function of past data. The firm employs expert humans H ∈ H with H : X × Y → Y, who solve
136
      the problems with the help of ML predictions. We will write Mt (X) = YMt and H(X, YMt ) = YHt .
137
      We assume that for all X ∈ X , there exists an optimal solution Y ∗ , which is the indisputable ground
138   truth.
139
140   The Firm’s Learning Objective. In many real-world applications, determining the ground truth
141
      label Y ∗ can be extremely costly. For example, obtaining the correct medical diagnosis can often
      require the knowledge of various specialists (e.g., orthopedists, pediatricians, neurologists). Even
142
      when a single expert is enough, they can misdiagnose a patient’s symptoms. Yet, in many of
143
      these cases, using the human labels YHt as a proxy for Y ∗ is the only feasible option to build ML
144   models. We allow the quality of YHt with respect to Y ∗ to change. This means that two iterations
145   of the ML model, Mt and Mt+1 , are trained on data from two different data generating processes,
146   (X, YHt−1 ) ∼ Dt−1 and (X, YHt ) ∼ Dt , respectively.
147

      In practice, a firm’s collaborative characteristic function can take any arbitrary shape, with portions
271   above and portions below the 45-degree line. As long as the function is continuous, at least one stable
272   point exists, and possibly more. When more than one stable point exist, the firm would like to reach
273   the stable point with the highest utility (i.e., the highest point of the characteristic function lying on
274   the 45-degree line). However, since the firm does not have access to the indisputable ground truth,
275   when it reaches a stable point it does not know where such point lies on the 45-degree line.
276
      In what follows, we offer a proof of concept of our theoretical setup. We empirically explore a context
277   where it is easy for us to identify the indisputable ground truth. Although with some simplifying
278   assumptions, the setting allows us to approximate a portion of the collaborative characteristic function,
279   and explore the effects of human behavior on its shape, particularly the effect of monetary incentives
280   and alternative solution selection criteria. We present study participants with instances of hard
281   knapsack problems to answer the following research questions:
282
      RQ1: How do monetary incentives affect human performance? To keep our treatment condition
283   manageable, we explore the effect of different levels of performance bonuses on U(H(X, .)), i.e., the
284   human performance without ML recommendations.
285
286
      RQ2: Can we approximate the human-ML collaborative characteristic function ∆U ? Here, we hold
      the performance bonus constant, and test humans’ effect δM on utility for different levels of ML
287
      performance. This will enable us to construct two approximations of ∆U for a specific task.

      4    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
290
291   In this section, we describe our user study. The goal of our experimental setup is to simulate an
292   environment in which users work on difficult tasks with the help of ML. The company responsible for
293   deploying ML models does not know the optimal solution Y ∗ (e.g., the true patient’s diagnosis), and
294   it trains ML models to replicate experts’ decisions (doctor diagnoses). To evaluate how the company’s
295   models perform against Y ∗ , we need a setting in which we, as researchers, know the quality of any
296
      solution Y using a utility function U(X, Y ). This allows us to make absolute quality assessments
      of solutions. Note that this is often unattainable in practice, as we argued in the introduction. The
297
      knapsack problem is particularly well suited for this context.
298
299   The Knapsack Problem. In our experiment, users solve instances of the knapsack problem. An
300   instance involves selecting which of n = 18 items to pack into a knapsack, each with a weight w and
301
      a value v. The objective is to maximize value without exceeding the weight limit W of the knapsack
      (between 5 and 250). We focus on the one-dimensional 0-1 knapsack problem, in which participants
302
      choose which items to pack (see Appendix A.2 for a formal definition). We constrain the weights,
303
      values, and capacity of our instances to integer values, to make them easier to interpret by humans.
304   We describe the details of the knapsack problem generation in Appendix A.10.
305
306   The knapsack problem has desirable properties for the empirical application of our framework. First,
307
      users do not require special training—beyond a short tutorial—to find a solution to the problem. Yet,
      the task is hard for humans, especially with a growing number of items (Murawski & Bossaerts,
308
      2016). Thus, the optimal solution Y ∗ is not obvious. Second, we can generate solutions to the
309
      knapsack problem in two ways. The “optimal” solution can be found with dynamic programming.
310   The “ML” solution can be found by imitating what humans select and computing the training loss as
311   the difference between the items selected by participants versus items selected by a model. Finally, it
312   allows us to showcase our theoretical approach with two different utility functions and two selection
313   criteria to approximate the collaborative characteristic function.
314

      6    C ONCLUSIONS
495
496   We present a theoretical framework for human-ML collaboration in a dynamic setting where human
497   labels can deviate from the indisputable ground truth. We introduce the collaborative characteristic
498   function, which theoretically links the utility of ML models with respect to the indisputable ground
499   truth, to the utility of humans using those same ML models to support their decisions. The collab-
500   orative characteristic function allows for multiple collaborative learning paths, depending on the
501   utility of the initially deployed ML model. Each of the collaborative learning paths characterizes a
502
      possible ML deployment strategy and its ensuing dynamic learning process. We theoretically show
      conditions under which this dynamic system reaches a stable point through dynamic utility improve-
503
      ment or deterioration. We then present the empirical results of a large user study, which allows
504
      us to approximate collaborative characteristic functions of the knapsack problem. For ML models
505   of economic performance between 72% and 92%, our empirical approximations of collaborative
506   characteristic functions all lie above the 45-degree line. Any collaborative learning path starting at
507   utility between 72% and 92% will thus likely converge to a stable point with utility around 92%. We
508   explore two factors that can shift the collaborative characteristic function. We find that monetary
509   incentives do not seem to affect human performance. However, we find that wherever applicable,
510   a simple post-processing step that picks the best among available solutions (as is possible for the
511   knapsack problem) can substantially shift the collaborative characteristic function upward, leading to
512   stable equilibria of higher utility.
513   Our work has a number of limitations. On the theoretical side, our collaborative learning paths assume
514   that the firm is able to perfectly replicate human+ML performance in future ML models. Appendix
515   A.7 discusses stability when learning does not exactly replicate previous human+ML performance.
516   However, since this assumption will likely not hold in the real world, imperfect learning may require
517   more iterations than perfect learning, so more empirical studies are required to explore the speed of
518   model convergence. On the empirical side, to reduce costs while maintaining statistical power, we
519
      only randomized monetary incentives without ML recommendations, and we randomized the quality
      of ML recommendations while fixing monetary incentives. Studying the interaction of monetary
520
      incentives and ML performance is an important extension. The null result of monetary incentives
521
      should be interpreted within our context. Specifically, the study participants received payments above
522   minimum wage, and we only tested different levels of linear performance bonuses. It would be
523   valuable to extend our work to evaluate the extent to which alternative base payments or non-linear
524   bonuses may induce different levels of quality and effort by participants and thus collaborative
525   characteristic functions of varying shapes.
526
      Our approximation of ∆U for the knapsack problem is naturally incomplete for two main reasons.
527
      First, we use prediction models trained on synthetic data to approximate the collaborative character-
528   istic function. Second, we did not test every possible level of model performance to fully draw the
529   collaborative characteristic function. It is unlikely that these models and their linear interpolation
530   would lead to the same performative trajectories as models trained on human feedback. We see this
531   as a first proof of concept of collaborative characteristic functions, but much more work is needed to
532   estimate these functions in real-world settings.
533
      Future work could investigate the properties of ∆U that guarantee a unique optimal stable point, both
534   theoretically and empirically. Provided that researchers have access to the indisputable ground truth,
535   realistic empirical investigations of collaborative characteristic functions are crucial to shed light
536   on the shape of those functions for practically relevant tasks such as medical diagnoses or hiring
537   decisions. Future work should also discuss fairness aspects of this framework, e.g., whether or not
538   fair stable points exist and how a firm can reach them. More generally, we hope this work generates
539   more interest in studying settings where ML deployments lead to changes in the data generating
      process, which have broad managerial and practical applications.

772   A.7    P ERFECT VS I MPERFECT LEARNING
773
774
      In this section, we discuss what changes if we loosen the assumption L(YMt , YHt−1 ) = 0. We call
      this assumption the "perfect learner" assumption because the firm perfectly learns the human labels
775
      from epoch t − 1 with a model in epoch t. In the following, we consider an imperfect learner such
776
      that L(YMt , YHt−1 ) = σ.
777
778   Figure 1 helps illustrate the relaxation of the assumption. An imperfect learner effectively amounts
779   to tilting the 45-degree line upward or downward. The tilt is upward if imperfect learning leads the
780
      ML model to have lower performance with respect to the indisputable ground truth compared to the
      human (i.e., the slope of the straight line is higher than 1). The tilt is downward if imperfect learning
781
      leads the ML model to have higher performance with respect to the ground truth (i.e., the slope of the
782
      straight line is lower than 1).
783
784   It is straightforward to extend Proposition 1 and Proposition 2 to the case of imperfect learning. In the
785   case of collaborative improvement (∆U (U(X, YM )) ≥ U(X, YM )), the human will improve on any
786
      model that the firm can deploy. However, if imperfect learning leads to (U(YMt ) − U(YMt−1 )) < 0
      then the performance gain from the human effort does not fully transfer to the ML model. If the
787
      above statement is true for all Mt , then the imperfection creates collaborative harm, which is the
788
      case covered in Proposition 2. However, this would still lead to a stable point. The alternative
789   scenario where L(YMt , YHt−1 ) = σ ⇒ (U(YMt ) − U(YMt−1 )) > 0 for all Mt , is still a scenario of
790   collaborative improvement, which means that we will again reach a stable point.
791
792
      In summary, imperfect and perfect learners are analogous. In both cases, the crucial question is how
      much humans improve the system’s performance. For the case of an imperfect learner, an additional
793
      empirical question is how much of the human improvement transfers to the ML model.

      A.8    M ODEL T RAINING
797
      We release the code required for training our models, our model parameters and all predictions for the
798   instances together with the instances that participants saw. Learning to solve the knapsack problem
799   is a research area for itself, however for the small, one-dimensional case of our experiment, it is
800   possible on consumer hardware. We only train models for knapsack instances with 18 items. As input
801   features we concatenate weights w1 , ..., w18 , values v1 , ..., v18 , the weight constraint W , the sum of
802   the weights and the sum of the values. Thus, our input dimension is 39. Our goal was to train models
803   with a broad spectrum of economic performances, not to solve the knapsack problem perfectly. We
804   added 5 fully connected layers, 4 of them with ReLU activation functions. We use torch.Sigmoid()
805   for our outputs. The output dimension was 18 and the output values in each index can be interpreted
806
      as the likelihood that the item belongs to a solution or not. For more details on the architecture, see
      our code. In summary, all models had dimensions in order of layers: (39,90), (90,550), (550,90),
807
      (90,84), (84,18).
808
809   We want to highlight two important aspects of how we thought about the model training. First, did
      not want to use any prior knowledge that a firm in our setting could not have either. For example,


                                                                                                     15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      if we could have known the utility of a knapsack solution (economic performance or optimality)
811   we could have just directly maximized it, or if we could know the optimal solution, we could have
812   just used the distance to the optimal solution as our loss. Instead, we used the binary cross-entropy
813   between the label and prediction as our loss. The label was a 18-dimensional 0-1 vector. If the i-th
814   entry of this output vector is 1, it means that the i-th item is in the knapsack and otherwise not. Thus
815   we simply minimized the differences between chosen items in our training data and those of our
816   model. For us, this was a reasonable analogy for the application context of healthcare in which every
817   "item" is a diagnosis or a symptom (e.g. an ICD10 code).
818   Because our financial budget was limited and we wanted to test multiple models, we trained all
819   models on optimally solved knapsack instances. It would have also created a lot of overhead and
820   space for errors if we would have collected the data of model q1 then trained q2 and rerun the user
821   study. Training them all on generated labels made it possible to run more treatments at once. We still
822   wanted to use ML models instead of solutions produced with dynamic programming, because we
823   wanted to incorporate the distributional character of ML predictions (see Figure 11) and study the
824   reaction to different quantiles of solution quality in greater detail in future work.
825   However, we had to include two pieces of prior knowledge in order to achieve better model per-
826   formance (especially for q5 and q6). First, we sorted the items by density (value/weight). This is
827   a big advantage in general, but only a small one for our knapsack instances because weights and
828   values are strongly correlated. Second, we normalized weights and values in a pre-processing step. In
829   our setting, both operations could not have been done by the firm (what is a normalized symptom)?
830   However, with those minor modifications we were able to create a larger range of models without
831
      massive resources and still just immitate the "human" label without incorporating anything in the
      loss. In a post-processing step, we sorted the items by sigmoid outputs. We then added items to the
832
      knapsack until the weight constraint was reached. From that item selection, we calculated the actual
833
      knapsack values. For more details, please visit our github repository To be added after acceptance.
