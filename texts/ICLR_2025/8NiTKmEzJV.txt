008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            We propose an algorithm, termed the Non-Equilibrium Transport Sampler (NETS),
011            to sample from unnormalized probability distributions. NETS can be viewed as
012            a variant of annealed importance sampling (AIS) based on Jarzynski’s equality,
013
               in which the stochastic differential equation used to perform the non-equilibrium
               sampling is augmented with an additional learned drift term that lowers the impact
014
               of the unbiasing weights used in AIS. We show that this drift is the minimizer of a
015
               variety of objective functions, which can all be estimated in an unbiased fashion
016            without backpropagating through solutions of the stochastic differential equations
017            governing the sampling. We also prove that some these objectives control the
018            Kullback-Leibler divergence of the estimated distribution from its target. NETS is
019            shown to be unbiased and, in addition, has a tunable diffusion coefficient which can
020            be adjusted post-training to maximize the effective sample size. We demonstrate
021            the efficacy of the method on standard benchmarks, high-dimensional Gaussian
022            mixture distributions, and a model from statistical lattice field theory, for which it
023            surpasses the performances of related work and existing baselines.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The aim of this paper is to sample probability distributions supported on Rd and known only up to a
029   normalization constant. This problem arises in a wide variety in applications, ranging from statistical
030   physics (Faulkner & Livingstone, 2023; Wilson, 1974; Hénin et al., 2022) to Bayesian inference
031   (Neal, 1993), and it is known to be challenging when the target distribution is not log-concave. In this
032   situation, vanilla methods based on ergodic sampling using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
033
      or stochastic differential equations (SDE) such as the Langevin dynamics typically have very slow
      convergence rates, making them inefficient in practice.
034
035   To overcome these difficulties, a variety of more sophisticated methods have been introduced, based
036   e.g. on importance sampling. Here we will be interested in a class of methods of this type which
037   involve non-equilibrium sampling, by which we mean algorithms that attempt to sample from a
038   non-stationary probability distribution. The idea is to first generate samples from a simple base
039
      distribution (e.g. a normal distribution) them push them in finite time unto samples from the target.
      Traditionally, this aim has been achieved by combining some transport using e.g. the Langevin
040
      dynamics with reweighing, so as to remove the bias introduced by the non-equilibrium quench. Neal’s
041
      Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) (Neal, 2001), Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) methods (Del
042   Moral, 1997; Doucet et al., 2001), or continuous-time variants thereof based on Jarzynski’s equality
043   (Hartmann et al., 2018) are ways to implement this idea in practice. While these methods work
044   better than ergodic sampling in many instances, they too can fail when the variance of the un-biasing
045   weights become too large compared to their mean: this arises when the samples end up being too far
046   from the target distribution at the end of the non-equilibrium quench.
047
      This problem suggests to modify the dynamics of the samples to help them evolves towards the
048   target during the quench. Here we propose a way to achieve this by learning an additional drift to
049   include in the Langevin SDE. As we will show below, there exists a drift that removes the need for
050   the un-biasing weights altogether, and it is the minimizer of a variety of objective functions that are
051   amenable to empirical estimation. In practice, this offers the possibility to estimate this drift using
052   deep learning methods. We exploit this idea here, showing that it results in an unbiased sampling
053   strategy in which importance weights can still be used to correct the samples exactly, but the variance
      of these weights can be made much smaller due to the additional transport.

      To this end, our work makes the following main contributions:
055
056   • We present a sampling algorithm which combines annealed Langevin dynamics with learnable
057     additional transport. The algorithm, which we call the Non-Equilbrium Transport Sampler (NETS),
058     is shown to be unbiased through a generalization of the Jarzysnki equality.
059   • We show that the drift coefficient contributing this additional transport is the minimizer of two
060     separate objective functions, which can be learned without backpropagating through solutions of
061     the SDE used for sampling.
062
      • We show that one of these objectives, a physics informed neural network (PINN) loss, is also an
063
        off-policy objective, meaning that it does not need samples from the target density. In addition,
064     this objective controls the KL-divergence between the model and its target.
065
      • The resultant samplers can be adapted after training by tuning the integration time-step as well
066
        as the diffusivity to improve the sample quality, which we demonstrate on high-dimensional
067
        numerical experiments below.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
070
071   Dynamical Measure Transport. Most contemporary generative models for continuous data are
072   built upon dynamical measure transport, in which samples from a base density are mapped to samples
073   from a target density by means of solving ordinary or stochastic differential equations (ODE/SDE)
074   whose drift coefficients are estimable. Initial attempts to do this started with (Chen et al., 2018;
075   Grathwohl et al., 2019). Recent work built upon these ideas by recasting the challenge of estimating
076   the drift coefficients in these dynamical equations as a problem of quadratic regression, most notably
077   with score-based diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020), and since then with more
078   general frameworks (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo et al., 2023;
079
      Liu et al., 2022; De Bortoli et al., 2021; Neklyudov et al., 2023). Importantly, these methods work
      because samples from the base and the target are readily available. Here, we show that analogous
080
      equations governing those systems can lead to objective functions that can be minimized with no
081
      initial data but still allow us to exploit the expressivity of models built on dynamical transport.
082
083
      Augmenting sampling with learning. Augmenting MCMC and importance sampling procedures
084
      with transport has been an active area of research for the past decade. Early work makes use of the
085   independence Metropolis algorithm (Hastings, 1970; Liu, 1996), in which proposals come from a
086   transport map (Parno & Marzouk, 2018; Noé et al., 2019; Albergo et al., 2019; Gabrié et al., 2022)
087   that are accepted or rejected based off their likelihood ratio with the target. These methods were
088   further improved by combining them with AIS and SMC perspectives, learning incremental maps that
089   connect a sequence of interpolating densities between the base and target (Arbel et al., 2021; Matthews
090   et al., 2022; Midgley et al., 2023). Similar works in the high-energy physics community posit that
091   interleaving stochastic updates within a sequence of maps can be seen as a form of non-equilbirium
092   sampling (Caselle et al., 2022; Bonanno et al., 2024). Following the success of generative models
093
      built out of dynamical transport, there has been a surge of interest in applying these perspectives to
      sampling: Vargas et al. (2023); Berner et al. (2024) translate ideas from diffusion models to minimize
094
      the KL divergence between the model and the target, and Zhang & Chen (2021) as well as Behjoo
095
      & Chertkov (2024) reformulate sampling as a stochastic optimal control (SOC) problem. These
096   approaches require backpropagating through the solution of an SDE, which is too costly in high
097   dimensions. In addition, the methods based on SOC must start with samples from a point mass,
098   which may be far from the target. Akhound-Sadegh et al. (2024) avoid the need to backpropagate
099   through an SDE, but in the process introduce a bias into their objective function. An alternative
100   perspective was also recently given in (Bruna & Han, 2024) by using denoising oracles to turn the
101   original sampling problem into an easier one. A final line of work (Malkin et al., 2023) shows how
102   ideas used for modeling distributions on graphs can be repurposed as tools for sampling, including
103   with off-policy training (Sendera et al., 2024).
104   Vargas et al. (2024) establish an unbiased sampler with added transport called Controlled Monte
105   Carlo Diffusions (CMCD) that is similar to ours. The main difference is how we learn the drift. In
106   Vargas et al. (2024) an objective for this drift in gradient form is derived through the use of path
107   integrals and Girsanov’s theorem. This objective either needs backpropagating through the SDE or
      has to be computed with a reference measure, and is done on a fixed grid. In practice the latter can be

      numerically unstable. Here, through simple manipulations of Fokker-Planck equations, we propose a
109   variety of new objective functions for the additional drift, none of which require backpropagating

116   dynamics, and, moreover, that this PINN objective directly controls the KL divergence as well as the
117
      importance weights that emerge through the Jarzynski equality.
