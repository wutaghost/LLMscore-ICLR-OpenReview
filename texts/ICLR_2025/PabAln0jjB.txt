011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                Current Large Language Model (LLM) preference optimization algorithms do not
014                account for temporal preference drift, which can lead to severe misalignment. To
015                address this limitation, we propose an offline fine-tuning algorithm Non-Stationary
016
                   Direct Preference Optimisation (NS-DPO) which models time-dependent reward
                   functions with a Dynamic Bradley-Terry model. NS-DPO applies exponential
017
                   weighting, by introducing a discount parameter in the loss function, which pro-
018
                   portionally focuses learning on more time-relevant datapoints. We theoretically
019                analyse the convergence of NS-DPO, providing upper bounds on the estimation
020                error and regret caused by non-stationary preferences. Finally, we demonstrate
021                the effectiveness of NS-DPO1 for fine-tuning LLMs in scenarios with drifting
022                preferences. By simulating preference drift using popular LLM reward models
023                and datasets accordingly, we show that NS-DPO fine-tuned LLMs remain robust
024                under non-stationarity, significantly outperforming baseline algorithms that ignore
025                temporal preference changes, without sacrificing performance in stationary cases.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The application of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to fine-tune Large
031   Language Models (LLMs) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang
032   et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b) has lead to more precise control over the behaviour they exhibit. This
033
      control is crucial when looking to safely deploy models in the real world (Amodei et al., 2016;
      Hendrycks & Mazeika, 2022). Human preference datasets enable the training of proxy reward
034
      models (see, e.g., RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024)) that can accurately evaluate complex human
035
      behaviour. These proxy reward models are used in conjunction with RL to fine-tune the LLM. Recent
036   works (Rafailov et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) seek to improve the efficiency
037   and stability of these approaches (Chaudhari et al., 2024) by training the LLM straight from human
038   preference data, avoiding the need to learn a proxy reward model.
039
      A key assumption made in these preference optimization algorithms is that human preferences are
040
      stationary, i.e., they do not change over time. However, a sudden or gradual shift in preferences can
041
      occur due to new information becoming available (Zafari et al., 2019; Johnson & Mayorga, 2020),
042   changes in the demographics of the queried audience (Caldwell, 1981), or social influences and
043   cultural trends. As more preference datasets are gathered over long periods of time, the chance of the
044   data containing varying preferences increases. In such cases, algorithms that do not account for these
045   changes, view them as noise and treat outdated data as equally important as fresh data, often leading
046   to deteriorated performance. An increasing body of evidence (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a)
047   points to data quality as being a key factor in fine-tuning performance, thus preference drift can greatly
048   affect the alignment of models which do not account for it (Carroll et al., 2024). The development
049   of preference optimization algorithms and theory to handle preference drifts are therefore crucial.
050   In this work, we propose Non-Stationary Direct Preference Optimization (NS-DPO), a novel
051   approach that uses a probabilistic Dynamic Bradley-Terry model (Cattelan et al., 2013; Bong
052   et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2023) to account for non-stationary drift in human preferences. NS-DPO

074   Related work. One of the primary applications of the RLHF framework is fine-tuning large language
075   models (LLMs) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al.,
076   2022; Bai et al., 2022b). A key component of this is the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry,
077   1952) which learns a reward signal from paired human preferences. Rafailov et al. (2024) propose
078   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), which implicitly uses the Bradley-Terry model, to fine-tune
079   an LLM directly from a preference dataset. A variety of alternatives to DPO have been proposed
080   which adapt or do not use the Bradley-Terry model (Azar et al., 2024; Amini et al., 2024; Meng
081
      et al., 2024; Cen et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023). Ethayarajh et al. (2024) remove paired preferences and
      propose maximising a utility function. Our work is the first to consider a direct preference algorithm
082
      using a Dynamic Bradley-Terry model.
083
084   A variety of work has also analysed the RLHF problem from a theoretical standpoint. Xiong et al.
085   (2024) provide suboptimiality bounds of policies in the offline, online and hybrid settings under
086   linear rewards. They do not directly analyse the performance of DPO, but propose it as a practical
087
      implementation of the oracle central to their analysis. Zhu et al. (2023); Chowdhury et al. (2024)
      analyse the offline preference learning and DPO settings, respectively. Chowdhury et al. (2024)
088
      address noisy preferences with a modified version of the DPO algorithm, presenting confidence
089
      bounds for neural policy classes and suboptimality bounds for the setting with log-linear policies.
090
091   Parameter drift has been widely studied in the bandit literature. Cheung et al. (2019) propose using a
092   sliding window to estimate parameters with data points close to the current timestep, whilst Bogunovic
093
      et al. (2016); Zhao et al. (2020) investigate a restarting strategy. Similarly to the strategy of Russac et al.
      (2019), we use an exponentially weighted discounting term to re-weight points close to the current
094
      timestep. Faury et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023) apply this approach to the case of generalised linear
095
      bandits first proposed by Filippi et al. (2010). Pacchiano et al. (2021); Saha (2021); Mehta et al. (2023)
096   focus on the duelling bandit setting, where only preference feedback between two actions is provided
097   by the environment. In this work, we provide the first theoretical guarantees for the popular offline
098   setting where the true reward parameter (used to label training data) is allowed to change over time.
099
      Main contributions. We propose NS-DPO, a direct preference optimization method that accounts
100
      for non-stationary preferences in the dataset via a Dynamic Bradley-Terry model. NS-DPO modifies
101
      the training loss with a single exponential weighting parameter γ, and thus represents a simple and
102   practical extension of the popular DPO algorithm. We provide an upper bound on the regret of
103   NS-DPO for log-linear policies given standard data coverage assumptions used in offline learning.
104   To explore the performance of NS-DPO, we construct non-stationary preference datasets from a
105   variety of existing popular datasets; including GlobalOpinionsQA (Durmus et al., 2023), Helpful &
106   Harmless (Dai et al., 2024), and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023). We demonstrate that NS-DPO
107   significantly outperforms stationary DPO and other relevant baselines on these non-stationary datasets
      with varying degrees of preference drift on Llama models Touvron et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024).

151   To address the problem of preference drift, in datasets collected over a period of time, we propose Non-
152   Stationary Direct Preference Optimization (NS-DPO). NS-DPO incorporates the Dynamic Bradley-
153   Terry model, which includes a non-stationary reward model r(x, a, t). Here t ∈ {1, . . . , T − 1}
154   denotes a time step in the past, and T ∈ N+ denotes the current time step, where we are evaluating
155
      the trained policy. Under the Dynamic Bradley-Terry model, the probability of response ai being
      preferred to a′i is
156

820   B    F URTHER R ELATED W ORKS
821
822   Recent interest in the alignment of LLMs has lead to a wide variety of works. We briefly discuss
823   further works that focus upon direct preference alignment algorithms.
824
      Several approaches examine preference optimisation from a game theory perspective, avoiding the
825
      implicit assumptions of the BT model. In these settings the current policy plays against previous
826   versions to further improve performance (Swamy et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024b;
827   Yuan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Pang et al., 2024; Munos et al., 2024). Xu et al. (2023) propose
828   a cringe loss based objective whilst Hong et al. (2024); Pentyala et al. (2024); Hua et al. (2024) try to
829   combine the supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization steps. Hong et al. (2024); Hua et al.
830   (2024) propose a single training objective to do this and Pentyala et al. (2024) examine combining
831   two different models trained on an SFT and direct preference objective respectively. Finally, Lu et al.
832   (2024) propose a meta algorithm which uses an LLM to optimize the form of the direct preference
833   learning objective itself.
834   An orthogonal direction of work is the online setting (Qi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Guo et al.,
835   2024; Xie et al., 2024), where feedback is returned by a human labeler or superior model. Khaki
836   et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024) adapt the offline settings using techniques such as rejection sampling
837   to approximate an online setting. In this work we only consider the offline setting for simplicity,
838   however the approach we propose can easily be adapted to the online setting. Other important
839   directions of research include safety and robustness. Dai et al. (2024); Ramesh et al. (2024); Wu
840
      et al. (2024a) consider robust settings where safety or group information is known at training time
      and Dai et al. (2024) analyse a constrained optimization problem through the lens of safety in LLMs.
841
      Whilst these approaches look to address a wide range of settings, our work is the first to provide
842
      a solution to the case of non-stationary preferences.
843
844   Carroll et al. (2024) consider how to correctly align LLMs under preference drift, showing several
845   possible goals for alignment in an online setting. Whilst in the online non-stationary setting the LLM
846
      can adapt to the changing preferences of the user, our setting considers aligning the model on an
      offline dataset before deploying the static model to users at test time. As such our approach is most
847
      similar to the Privileged Reward and Initial Reward settings Carroll et al. (2024) proposes, as we
848
      determine that the preferences exhibited in the present are the most important (Privileged Reward)
849   and future users will interact with a model aligned to preferences from their past (Initial Reward).

979    D.3   T HE T WO C OUNTRIES (2C) N ON -S TATIONARY G LOBAL O PINIONS DATASET
980
981    To test NS-DPO, we create a synthetic non-stationary dataset in which the temporal trends are known.
982
       To do this, we use the GlobalOpinionsQA dataset (Durmus et al., 2023). We preprocess the dataset
       in three major ways.
983
984    Binary Preferences. We convert the dataset to a dataset of binary preferences. For each set of prompt
985    and responses, we create a row for each possible combination of prompt and binary response pairs.
986    We calculate the preference probability for these response pairs as follows. Assuming the non-binary
987    responses follow a Plackett-Luce preference framework, we can find the reward associated with
988
       responses (up to an additive constant) by taking the log of the preference probability. We can then
       take the sigmoid of these responses to find a normalised binary preference.
989
990    Country Filter. We filter the dataset down to the following countries: Nigeria, Egypt, India, China,
991    Japan, Germany, France, Spain, United States, Canada, Brazil, Argentina, Australia and New Zealand.
992    Country Level Prompts. We filter the dataset such that each row of the dataset is the prompt,
993    response, preference probability of a single country.
994
995
       After the preprocessing, we copy the dataset and assign a different timestep to each unique instance of
       (prompt, response, preference). We simulate the drift in preferences by using preference probabilities
996
       of two countries, shifting from one to another over time. Out of 100 time steps in the training
997
       dataset, the first 33 time steps consisted of preference probabilities from the US. Preference labels
998    sampled from the last 33 time steps are from probabilities of the target country. We use Germany,
999    Japan and Brazil as target countries, creating three different datasets. In the intermediate 33 time
