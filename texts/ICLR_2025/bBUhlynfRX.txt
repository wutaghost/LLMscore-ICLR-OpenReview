009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) excel in many visual tasks, but they tend
012            to be sensitive to slight input perturbations that are imperceptible to the human eye,
013            often resulting in task failures. Recent studies indicate that training CNNs with
014            regularizers that promote brain-like representations, using neural recordings, can
015            improve model robustness. However, the requirement to use neural data severely
016            restricts the utility of these methods. Is it possible to develop regularizers that
017            mimic the computational function of neural regularizers without the need for neural
018            recordings, thereby expanding the usability and effectiveness of these techniques?
019            In this work, we inspect a neural regularizer introduced in Li et al. (2019) to extract
020
               its underlying strength. The regularizer uses neural representational similarities,
               which we find also correlate with pixel similarities. Motivated by this finding, we
021
               introduce a new regularizer that retains the essence of the original but is computed
022
               using image pixel similarities, eliminating the need for neural recordings. We show
023            that our regularization method 1) significantly increases model robustness to a range
024            of black box attacks on various datasets and 2) is computationally inexpensive and
025            relies only on original datasets. Our work explores how biologically motivated loss
026            functions can be used to drive the performance of artificial neural networks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have achieved high performance on a variety of visual tasks
033
      such as image classification and segmentation. Despite their remarkable success, these networks
      are notably brittle; even a small change in the input can significantly alter the network’s output
034
      Biggio et al. (2013); Szegedy et al. (2013). Szegedy et al. (2013) found that small perturbations,
035
      imperceptible to the human eye, can lead CNNs to misclassify images. These adversarial images
036   pose a significant threat to computer vision models.
037
038   Improving the robustness of CNNs against adversarial inputs is a major focus in machine learning.
039
      Various methods have been proposed, each with different levels of success and computational demands
      Li et al. (2022). Some researchers have drawn inspiration from the mammalian brain, finding that deep
040
      neural networks trained to mimic brain-like representations are more resistant to adversarial attacks
041
      (Li et al., 2019; Safarani et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). In particular, Li et al. (2019) demonstrated that
042   incorporating a regularizer into the loss function, which aligns the CNN’s representational similarities
043   (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) with those of the mouse primary visual cortex (V1), significantly enhances
044   the network’s robustness to Gaussian noise and adversarial attacks. Using a loss term to steer models
045   towards brain-like representations is referred to as neural regularization. However, a significant
046   drawback of these methods is the reliance on neural recordings, which are often difficult to obtain
047   and limit the methods’ applicability.
048   In this work, focusing on CNNs used for image classification tasks, we take a deeper look at the
049   neural regularizer introduced in Li et al. (2019). We ask whether the underlying working principles
050   of this biologically-inspired regularizer can be extracted and utilized to enhance the robustness of
051   deep neural networks without relying on large-scale neural recordings. In particular, the regularizer
052   introduced in Li et al. (2019) steers a CNN’s representations to match the representational similarities
053   of a predictive model of brain recordings. Taking this as a starting point, our contributions are the
      following:

075
      scale neural recordings. This contributes to the broader use of biologically-inspired loss functions
076   to improve artificial neural networks’ performance. The end product is a simple, computationally
077   efficient regularizer that performs well across a wide range of scenarios.

080   2    R ELATED WORKS
081
082   Adversarial attacks. Identifying adversarial examples that can mislead a model is a dynamic field of
083
      research, with an increasing number of attacks being introduced Szegedy et al. (2013); Hinton et al.
      (2015); Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2016); Brendel et al. (2017); Madry et al. (2017). In this study, we
084
      concentrate on black box attacks, which do not have access to detailed model information, as these
085
      are more reflective of real-world scenarios. We evaluate our models against four types of attacks:
086   random noise, common corruptions, transfer-based attacks, and decision-based attacks.
087
088   Random noise attacks involve applying noise sampled from known distributions (e.g., Gaussian,
089
      Uniform, and Salt and Pepper) to an input - see Appendix A.1. Common corruptions correspond to
      distortions that can be found in real life computer vision applications (eg : motion blur) Hendrycks &
090
      Dietterich (2019a). A transfer-based attack involves finding adversarial perturbations for a substitute
091
      model (an unregularized model in our case) and applying them to a target model. Evaluating
092   robustness on transfer-based attacks is crucial because adversarial examples crafted for one model can
093   also mislead another distinct model Papernot et al. (2016). We find these perturbations by applying
094   the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) Goodfellow et al. (2014) to the substitute model. Such
095   adversarial samples are computed as :
096
                                       xadv = x + ϵ × sign(∇x L(θ, x, y))                                  (1)
097
098   where xadv is the adversarial example, x denotes the original input image, y denotes the original
099   input label, θ denotes the model parameters and L is the loss. A decision based attack is an attack
100   which solely depends on the final model’s decision. Evaluating robustness on them is key as they are
101   applicable to real world black box models. Precisely, we evaluate robustness on a Boundary Attack
102   introduced by Brendel et al. (2017). This attack starts from a large adversarial perturbation, and seeks
103   to reduce the perturbation while remaining adversarial.
104   Adversarial training as a defense to adversarial attacks. As adversarial attacks have advanced,
105   corresponding defenses have also been developed to secure against them Goodfellow et al. (2014);
106   Bhagoji et al. (2018); Diffenderfer et al. (2021); Kireev et al. (2022). A common defense strategy
107   involves augmenting each batch of training data with adversarial examples, a technique known
      as adversarial training Goodfellow et al. (2014); Madry et al. (2017). A widely-used method for


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generating these adversarial examples is the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attack Madry et al.
109   (2017), which is a multi-step variant of the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) attack. PGD is
110   popular due to its effectiveness in creating challenging adversarial examples, thereby enhancing the
111   model’s resilience against attacks.
112
      Neural regularization. Recent work showed that jointly training a deep network to perform image
113
      classification while steering it towards having brain-like representations can improve the model’s
114
      robustness to adversarial attacks Li et al. (2019); Safarani et al. (2021). This is achieved by introducing
115   a penalty term in the loss function acting directly on image representations Li et al. (2019) or
116   activations Safarani et al. (2021) at different network depths. Such a process is referred to as neural
117   regularization. For instance, Li et al. (2019) used a neural regularizer to drive a CNN to aligh its
118   representational similarities (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) with those of mouse primary visual cortex
119   (V1). Later on, Safarani et al. (2021) used a neural regularizer to drive a CNN towards predicting
120   neural activity in macaque primary visual cortex (V1) in response to the same natural stimuli. The
121   key bottleneck of such defenses is their reliance on the measurement of large scale neural recordings.

253
254   We first test the robustness of regularized models using grayscale CIFAR-10 and grayscale ImageNet
255
      as classification and regularization datasets respectively, as used in Li et al. (2019). We first show
      robustness to Gaussian noise perturbations. We find that regularized models exhibit a substantial
256
      increase in robustness when compared to unregularized models as seen in Fig.3, left panel. They also
257
      show a similar performance to neural regularized models Li et al. (2019) (Fig.3, left panel). The
258   robustness of models regularized using S T h , to Uniform and Salt and Pepper perturbations can be
259   found in appendix A.3.
260
261
      We then test robustness to stronger black box attacks, particularly, to transferred FGSM (Goodfellow
262
      et al., 2014) perturbations from an unregularized model, and decision-based Boundary Attack Brendel
263
      et al. (2017) (Section 2). We observe an increase in robustness to both attacks (Fig.3, center and right
264   panels). Note that for decision-based Boundary attack, the larger the perturbation size between the
265   adversarial input and the original image, the better in terms of robustness. Again, we observe that
266   models regularized using S th perform similar to those regularized using neural data Li et al. (2019)
267   (Fig. 3, center and right panels).
268   The experiments above demonstrate that we can obtain similar robustness to neural regularized
269   models Li et al. (2019) by simply regularizing using S T h , which does not require neural data, and
      relies only on the original unaugmented regularization dataset.

478   5     C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
479
480   Extracting the working principles of the brain to advance AI is a long-term goal of neuroscience. To
481   further this goal, we examined a brain-inspired method for adversarial robustness proposed by Li
482   et al. (2019). This method uses neural recordings from the brain to align learned representations
483   in an artificial neural network with brain representations through a regularization term added to
484   the training loss. We extracted the core working principle behind this regularizer and proposed a
485   simple, pixel-based regularization scheme that achieves similar performance, and gave an intuitive
      interpretation of our method. These findings contribute to the broader objective of leveraging


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      brain-inspired principles to advance AI.
487
488
489   We showed that our proposed method increases the robustness of CNNs to a spectrum of black box
490
      attacks (Section 2). We proposed a method to select the regularization hyperparameters (α, T h). We
      also showed that the choice of an (α, T h) pair value for regularization, affects the robustness level
491
      in a consistent way across different attacks. We demonstrated the effectiveness and scalability of
492
      our method, by showing its success in increasing model robustness using different combinations of
493   classification and regularization datasets, including classification datasets CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100
494   (Appendix A.1). We evaluated the performance of regularized models on common corruptions using
495   grayscale CIFAR-10-C. We performed a Fourier analysis on minimal adversarial perturbations
496   obtained from a decision-based Boundary Attack on our regularized model, and found that the
497   perturbations from the regularized model contained higher low-frequency components relative to the
498   unregularized model. We also showed that our method is more effective against common corruptions
499   that are categorized as high frequency corruptions based on the average frequency estimated from the
500   Fourier spectrum of the perturbations induced by these corruptions Li et al. (2023). These findings
501
      are in line to those in Li et al. (2023), who examined the same perturbations for a model regularized
      using neural data Li et al. (2019). Even though we mostly presented results using grayscale
502
      datasets to allow direct comparison with the method in Li et al. (2019), we demonstrated that our
503
      method is also successful when using color datasets, where we showed results for color CIFAR-10
504   and color CIFAR-100. We also investigated the contribution of different parts of S T h (Appendix A.7).
505
506
507
      Even though we use a biologically inspired loss term that originally utilized large scale neural
      recordings to enhance the robustness of machine learning models, we have shown that this loss term
508
      can be implemented in a successful way that bypasses the use of neural data. Furthermore, our
509
      regularization method, although effective, is very simple. It relies on the original image datasets, and
510   does not require the introduction of any additional data distortions or augmentations during training.
511   It is flexible in regard to choosing the regularization dataset. It is computationally inexpensive, it
512   requires a relatively small batch size for regularization. It also requires a small number of images
513   to regularize on, or more precisely to construct the targets S T h (eq. equation 5). Our work is an
514   encouraging step towards dissecting the workings of neural regularizers, to come up with methods
515   that can both, enhance the performance of machine learning models, and be implemented by a broader
516   machine learning community. Finally, we point out that one limitation of our method, is its inability
517   to increase model robustness to some common corruptions as can be seen in Fig. 6 (right) and Fig .7
518
      (left). Also, it does not achieve the level of robustness attained using state of the art defenses against
      adversarial attacks Croce et al. (2020). Although we stress that our aim is not to come up with the
519
      best adversarial defence, but rather to show that our method which is based on the neural regularizer
520
      in Li et al. (2019) can be equally effective without the need to use expensive neural data.
