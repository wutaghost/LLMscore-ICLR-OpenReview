009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the online learning problem characterized by the varying input feature
012            space of streaming data. Although LSTMs have been employed to effectively
013            capture the temporal nature of streaming data, they cannot handle the dimension-
014            varying streams in an online learning setting. Therefore, we propose a dynamic
015            LSTM-based novel method, called packetLSTM, to model the dimension-varying
016            streams. The packetLSTM’s dynamic framework consists of an evolving packet
017
               of LSTMs, each dedicated to processing one input feature. Each LSTM retains the
               local information of its corresponding feature, while a shared common memory
018
               consolidates global information. This configuration facilitates continuous learn-
019
               ing and mitigates the issue of forgetting, even when certain features are absent for
020            extended time periods. The idea of utilizing one LSTM per feature coupled with
021            a dimension-invariant operator for information aggregation enhances the dynamic
022            nature of packetLSTM. This dynamic nature is evidenced by the model’s ability
023            to activate, deactivate, and add new LSTMs as required, thus seamlessly accom-
024            modating varying input dimensions. The packetLSTM achieves state-of-the-art
025            results on five datasets, and its underlying principle is extended to other RNN
026            types, like GRU and vanilla RNN.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Online learning, characterized by streaming data, where data instances arrive one by one, has been
      studied extensively (Gama, 2012; Neu & Olkhovskaya, 2021; Agarwal et al., 2008). Recently, there
032
      has been a growing focus on online learning in environments with varying input feature spaces.
033
      Examples include movie sentiment classification and crowdedness severity prediction (He et al.,
034   2023; Agarwal et al., 2024). These varying input features, termed haphazard inputs (Agarwal et al.,
                                        t
035
      2023), are denoted as X t ∈ Rd , where dt indicates the dimensionality of input, varying over
036   time t. The field of haphazard inputs is expanding, prompting the introduction of new methods,
037   applications, and appropriate datasets as elaborated in section A of the Appendix.
038
039
      The current landscape is focused on developing new methods. Predominantly, haphazard inputs
      are modeled using classical approaches like naive Bayes (Katakis et al., 2005), decision stumps
040
      (Schreckenberger et al., 2022; 2023), and linear classifiers (Beyazit et al., 2019), favored for their
041
      dynamic architectures. However, there is a push towards developing dynamic deep learning solu-
042   tions (Agarwal et al., 2022; 2023), motivated by the capabilities of neural networks. Nevertheless,
043   current methodologies have not adequately leveraged the streaming nature of data. To bridge this
044   gap, we advocate using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997;
045   Zhang et al., 2021a), which can effectively exploit the temporal dynamics of data.
046
      We introduce a novel architecture, termed packetLSTM, designed to dynamically adapt to vary-
047
      ing input feature space. This framework employs a unique ensemble of Long Short-Term Memory
048   (LSTM) units, each dedicated to a specific input feature. The packetLSTM allows for robust inter-
049   action among its LSTMs, fostering the integration of global information while preserving feature-
050   specific knowledge within each unit’s short-term memory. The packetLSTM facilitates continuous
051   learning without the risk of catastrophic forgetting in online learning environments (Hoi et al., 2021).
052   The feature-based learning, coupled with a dimension-invariant aggregation operator, allows pack-
053   etLSTM to dynamically activate, deactivate, and add new LSTMs as needed, leading to adeptly
      managing haphazard inputs. The main contributions of our work are as follows. (1) We intro-

      duce the first RNN-based framework, packetLSTM, to effectively handle haphazard inputs in online
055   learning. (2) The packetLSTM exhibits learning without forgetting capabilities in an online learning

103   RNNs RNNs are among the most popular models in sequential data analysis (Salehinejad et al.,
104   2017; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2019). Various techniques are developed to capture the temporal dynamics
105   of data, including stacking LSTMs to establish a hierarchical framework (Hermans & Schrauwen,
106   2013; Wang et al., 2017), as well as designing specialized time-modeling LSTM units (Che et al.,
107   2018; Kazemi et al., 2019). In this article, we utilize Time-LSTM (Zhu et al., 2017) as our LSTM
      units because of its demonstrated capability in modeling both short-term and long-term interest.

      Notations In this article, we represent time in superscript and feature id in subscript. For example,
118
      x32 represents the value of feature 2 (F2 ) at time t3 . For ease of readability, we slightly adjust the
119
      notation of time here. Instead of denoting by vjt1 , we use vj1 . When time is referenced individually,
120   it is denoted as t1 . Moreover, t indicates a random time, and t − 1 denotes the time preceding t. A
121   complete list of notations is provided in section C of the Appendix.

123   Characteristics Haphazard inputs exhibit six characteristics which are illustrated in Figure 2(b).
124   These characteristics are: (1) Streaming data, which are received sequentially and processed with-
125   out storage. (2) Missing data, which are features present in some instances but may be absent in
126   subsequent ones like F1 at time t2 . (3) Missing features, that are not received from the onset; how-
127   ever, their availability is known like F4 . (4) Sudden features, that arrives unexpectedly without prior
128   indication of their existence like F3 at time t2 . (5) Obsolete features, which can cease to exist after
129   any instance, such as F2 . (6) Unknown number of total features, results from the combined effect of
130
      missing data, missing features, sudden features, and obsolete features.
131
132
      Feature Space The characteristics of haphazard inputs result in a varying feature space. We define
      a universal feature space (F̄t ) as the set of features encountered till time t. The specific set of features
133
      present at time t is represented by Ft and is termed current feature space as shown in Figure 2(b).
134
      The universal feature space will grow with time due to the emergence of sudden features and missing
135   features. For example, F¯1 = {F1 , F2 } at time t1 , and F¯2 = {F1 , F2 , F3 } at time t2 in Figure 2(b).
136   In an ideal condition, F̄t can contract with the removal of obsolete features; however, since the
137   cessation of obsolete features is unknown, F̄t may not decrease in practice.
138
139   Mathematical Formulation The haphazard input received at time t can be represented by X t ,
                         t
140   where X t ∈ R|F | . Here, | · | represents the cardinality of a set. The corresponding ground truth is
141   denoted by y t , where y t ∈ [0, 1]C and C is the number of classes. This paper deals with the binary
142   classification problem but can be easily extended to multi-class scenarios. The model, denoted by
143   f , operates in an online learning setting with f t−1 : X t → y t , where f 0 represents the initialized
144   state of the model. After processing {X t , y t }, the model’s state is represented by f t . At each time
145   t, the model receives X t , and f t−1 processes X t to yield a prediction ŷ t . Upon the revelation of y t ,
146   the loss lt = H(y t , ŷ t ) is computed, where H is a loss function. The model then updates from f t−1
147   to f t for the subsequent instances based on lt . This iterative process continues for each instance.

197   The common long-term memory (ct ), which aggregates information from all long-term memories of
198   active LSTMs, facilitates the interaction among features, crucial for enriched knowledge acquisition
199   (Zhang et al., 2021b). This ct contains global information, while the short-term memory of each
200   LSTM retains the local information about individual features, alleviating the issue of catastrophic
201   forgetting in an online learning setting. The aggregation operator, being dimension-invariant, ac-
202
      commodates the variable number of features. All active short-term memories are aggregated to
      generate a predictive short-term memory (ht ). These memories are subsequently concatenated and
203
      processed through a fully connected classifier to produce the final output ŷ t (see Figure 2(a)).

205   The model parameters are updated based on the loss lt = H(y t , ŷ t ) in an online learning setting.
206   New LSTMs are introduced with initialized memories (h0 , ct−1 ) and a time delay (∆) set to zero

213   (Zhu et al., 2017) because of its highly demonstrated capability in modeling time information. Time-
214   LSTM utilizes time delay to capture the short-term interest for current instances and preserves these
215   delays to address long-term interest for future instances. We specifically utilize Time-LSTM 3 (T-3)

220
      Our packetLSTM framework differs from T-3 in its approach to handling input feature spaces. Un-
      like T-3, which utilizes a single LSTM unit for a fixed feature space, our approach employs a distinct
221
      LSTM unit for each feature to manage a varying feature space. This necessitates a different input
222
      configuration for each LSTM compared to the T-3. Specifically, rather than using the previous long-
223   term memory of each LSTM as an input, the packetLSTM framework utilizes a common long-term
224   memory, which facilitates interaction among features. Moreover, while T-3 accepts the short-term
225   memory from time t − 1 as input at time t, the packetLSTM inputs the short-term memory from
226   time t− at time t, where t− is not necessarily equal to t − 1 in haphazard inputs.

240   Datasets We consider 5 datasets – magic04 (Bock et al., 2004), imdb (Maas et al., 2011), a8a
241   (Kohavi et al., 1996), SUSY (Baldi et al., 2014), and HIGGS (Baldi et al., 2014) – with details
242   provided in section E of the Appendix. The motivation to choose these datasets is three-fold. First,
243   they include both real (imdb) and synthetic datasets. Second, the number of instances varies from
244   19020 in magic04 to 1M in HIGGS. Third, the number of features ranges from 8 in SUSY to 7500
245   in imdb. Therefore, the diversity in the number of features and instances allows us to determine the
246
      efficacy of packetLSTM effectively. The imdb dataset is haphazard in nature. However, the synthetic
      dataset needs to be transformed into haphazard inputs. Following the baseline papers (Beyazit et al.,
247
      2019; Agarwal et al., 2023), we transform synthetic datasets based on the probability values p, where
248
      p = 0.25 means only 25% of features are available at each time instance. We consider p = 0.25,
249   0.5, and 0.75. The synthetic dataset preparation is further discussed in section E of the Appendix.

      We conduct ablation studies within packetLSTM to assess the impact of each component and iden-
      tify optimal variants, adhering to the hyperparameters described in Implementation Details of section
302
      5, unless specified otherwise. We report the mean of the balanced accuracy in the main manuscript
303
      (Table 2), with std detailed in section L of the Appendix. We calculate the number of wins for each
304   component across all dataset combinations, defining a win as achieving the highest balanced accu-
305   racy or being within a 0.05 margin of the top value, accounting for variability indicated by the std.
306   For example, in the magic04 dataset at p = 0.25, the packetLSTM with the Min aggregator reports
307   a slightly higher balanced accuracy (61.36) than the Max (61.33) but exhibits greater std (0.14 vs.
308   0.07). This variability suggests that some runs using the Min aggregator achieved lower balanced
309   accuracy than the Max. Therefore, a 0.05 margin mitigates such discrepancies.
310
311   Which aggregation operator performs the best? The Max operator, in general, performed best
312   with 7 wins, as shown in the AGG component of Table 2. However, to determine the best operators,
313   we compare them one by one (see → in Table 2). (1) The performance difference between Min
314   and Max is negligible, indicating both are suitable choices. (2) Between Sum and Mean, there is
315
      no evident superiority. However, the Sum is sensitive to the number of features, while the Mean
      remains relatively stable unless the feature distribution changes substantially. We tested this by al-
316
      ternating p (from 0.25 to 0.75) every 100 instances in the a8a dataset, which has the most features
317
      among the synthetic datasets. The Mean operator (balanced accuracy = 62.77) substantially out-

348   Is time modeling beneficial? We investigated 4 variants of time modeling: T-3, Time-LSTM 2,
349   Time-LSTM 1, and Decay (Che et al., 2018). As observed in the Time Model component, T-3
350
      performed the best. Moreover, LSTM, without any time modeling (None), performed the worst,
      affirming the beneficial impact of time modeling for haphazard inputs. Moreover, all the 4 variants
351
      outperform the pbb in 11 out of 13 scenarios except in a8a (p = 0.25) and SUSY (p = 0.75).
352
353
      Are current feature space sufficient for the predictive capability of packetLSTM? The pack-
354
      etLSTM utilizes current feature space for the predictive short-term memory. However, it is also
355   possible to consider the universal feature space by aggregating the short-term memories from all
356   LSTMs linked to the universal features for the predictive short-term memory. The current feature
357   space has 13 wins compared to 11 wins of universal feature space (see the Feat component). There-
358   fore, the current feature space is sufficient for enhancing the predictive capability of packetLSTM.
359
360   What is the importance of common long-term and predictive short-term memory? The final
361   prediction in packetLSTM concatenates common long-term and predictive short-term memory. We
362   assessed the importance of each memory type by comparing the original packetLSTM, termed Both,
363   with its variants that either exclude predictive short-term memory (Only LTM) or common long-
364   term memory (Only STM), as shown in the Concat component. Both significantly outperformed
365
      others, securing 12 wins, confirming that both memory types are crucial. While common long-term
      memory holds global information, predictive short-term memory provides local information, and
366
      individually, they surpass pbb in most datasets except in a8a and HIGSS (p = 0.5).
367
368
      Does the data need to be normalized? We observed significant variation in the range of feature
369
      values across datasets. For example, in magic04, the first feature ranges from 4.28 to 334.18. There-
370   fore, we performed streaming normalization by using five methods: Min-Max, Decimal Scaling,
371   Mean-Norm, Unit Vector, and Z-score, as detailed in section N of the Appendix. Table 2 shows
372   that Z-score performed the best, underscoring the need for streaming normalization in handling hap-
373   hazard inputs. Remarkably, the packetLSTM, even without any normalization (labeled as None),
374   surpassed pbb in all datasets except for a8a (p = 0.25), showcasing the superiority of packetLSTM.
375
376   Is the concept of packet architecture adaptable? The packetLSTM framework’s principles are
377   adaptable, leading us to develop packetRNN and packetGRU architectures. We outline these ar-
      chitectures’ formulations and performance in section O of the Appendix and the RNN component

387   Complexity Analysis The time complexity of packetLSTM is t=1 O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 + P ), where
388   T is the number of instances, O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 ) denotes the complexity for processing all activated
389   LSTMs at time t, and O(P ) encompasses constant-time operations such as aggregations and final
390   predictions. Here, s is the hidden size of the LSTM, and the function g(|Ft |) varies between 1 and
391   |Ft |, often close to 1. Thus, the packetLSTM scales well in terms of time complexity with feature
392   size. The space complexity at time t is O(|F̄t | ∗ L + K), where O(L) is the space complexity
393   of one LSTM and O(K) accounts for the final prediction network and aggregated memories. The
394
      introduction of sudden and missing features increases the space complexity, showing potential scal-
      ability limits. However, packetLSTM easily handles the 7500 features in the imdb dataset, affirming
395
      its capability to handle high-dimensional data. Detailed calculations of time and space complexities
396
      and a strategy to mitigate space complexity are provided in section P and Q of the Appendix.

      packetLSTM vs Single LSTM The effectiveness of packetLSTM is further evidenced through its
399   comparison with a single LSTM model, which necessitates the availability of all features. This re-
400   quirement can be met through imputation, although it requires contradicting the sixth characteristic
401   of haphazard inputs where the total number of features is unknown. Nonetheless, for the purpose of
402   evaluating packetLSTM, we compare it against a single LSTM, employing three imputation tech-
403   niques: forward fill, mean of the last five observed values of a feature, and Gaussian copula (Zhao
404   et al., 2022). Table 3 demonstrates that packetLSTM significantly outperforms single LSTM with all
405   imputation-based techniques across all datasets, except in a8a at p = 0.75. Further details on the sin-
406   gle LSTM model, hyperparameter search, the Gaussian copula model, and its specific performance
407
      issues with the a8a and imdb datasets are discussed in section R of the Appendix.

410
411   We design three challenging experiments to explicitly elucidate the efficacy of packetLSTM to (1)
412   handle sudden features, (2) handle obsolete features, and (3) demonstrate the learning without for-
413   getting capability. We considered the HIGGS and SUSY datasets for these experiments due to their
414   substantial number of instances. In the main manuscript, we discuss the results corresponding to
415   HIGGS, while similar conclusions for SUSY are discussed in section S.2 of the Appendix. For
416   comparative analysis, we opted for OLVF, OLIFL, OVFM, and Aux-Drop baselines based on their
417
      superior performance in the HIGGS dataset, as indicated in Table 1. We divided the dataset into 5
      intervals, each consisting of 20% of the total instances, with successive intervals containing the next
418
      20% of instances. For the exact values of Figure 3 and 4, refer to section S.1 of the Appendix.
419
420
      Sudden Features Here, each subsequent interval includes an additional 20% of features, starting
421
      with 20% in the first interval and increasing to 100% by the fifth interval. This progression, termed
422
      the trapezoidal data stream (Zhang et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2022), is illustrated in Figure 3 by a pink-
423   shaded region. Model performance is expected to enhance with increased data volume. All models,
424   except OLVF, exhibit this increasing performance trend. Notably, packetLSTM outperforms other
425   models in each interval, demonstrating its superior capability in handling sudden features.
426
427   Obsolete features Here, all features are initially present in the first interval, then only the first 80%
428   remain in the second interval, decreasing sequentially as shown in Figure 3. Intuitively, the model’s
429   performance should deteriorate as data volume decreases, a pattern observed in all models except
430   Aux-Drop. The Aux-Drop’s performance drops significantly in the second interval, suggesting a
431   misleading impression of improvement in the third. The packetLSTM outperforms all other methods
      in each interval, demonstrating its superior ability to handle obsolete features.

501   Padding We consider padding inputs with zeros or truncating them, which necessitates specifying
502   a fixed input length (fl ). If the number of features in an instance (|Ft |) is less than fl , the Trans-
503   former pads the input with zeros, and if |Ft | exceeds fl , it truncates the excess features, potentially
504   leading to information loss. A potential, albeit inefficient, solution is to set an excessively high fl .
505   Nonetheless, to assess how packetLSTM compares to Transformer, we conducted two experiments:
506   Only Values, padding available feature values, and Pairs, pairing each feature value with its feature
507
      ID and padding the sequence. The packetLSTM outperforms Transformer with padding across all
      dataset scenarios (see Table 3). Further details can be found in section U.1 of the Appendix.

      Natural Language Given the variable size of inputs, natural language can be seen as an applica-
510
      tion where features arrive one by one, and most are missing. We compared packetLSTM with Dis-
511   tilBERT (Sanh et al., 2019) and BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), detailed in section U.2 of the Appendix.
512   We observe that both DistilBERT and BERT are unable to perform classification on haphazard inputs
513   with a balanced accuracy of around 50 in all cases.
514
515   Set Transformer The Set Transformer (Lee et al., 2019), designed for variable-length inputs, has
516   been previously used in offline learning. We employ it for online learning to handle haphazard
517   inputs. Results in Table 3 indicate that packetLSTM significantly outperforms Set Transformer
518   across all datasets, possibly due to Set Transformer’s assumption of permutation invariance, which
519   does not hold for haphazard inputs. More details are provided in section U.3 of the Appendix.
520
521   HapTransformer To tackle permutation invariance, we introduce HapTransformer, which trans-
522
      forms each feature into a distinct learnable embedding, a technique similarly utilized in prior re-
      search (Huang et al., 2020; Gorishniy et al., 2021; Somepalli et al., 2021). However, these models
523
      do not accommodate variable-sized inputs. The learnable embeddings are subsequently processed
524
      by the Set Transformer’s decoder, allowing implicit communication of feature identities. Details
525   on the architecture and hyperparameters are provided in section U.4 of the Appendix. Despite its
526   strengths, packetLSTM surpasses HapTransformer in all dataset scenarios (see Table 3). HapTrans-
527   former struggles particularly with datasets having high feature counts like imdb and a8a, and requires
528   significant computational time. However, it remains a strong baseline, outperforming other baseline
529   models in the SUSY and HIGGS datasets, as detailed in Tables 1 and 3.

533
      In conclusion, our work introduces packetLSTM, a dynamic framework for handling streaming data
534   with varying feature dimensions in real-time learning environments. Significantly, the underlying
535   principles of packetLSTM are extendable to other architectures, such as vanilla RNN and GRUs,
536   demonstrating the model’s adaptability to different neural architectures. The packetLSTM not only
537   outperforms existing methods across various datasets but also showcases flexibility in adapting to
538   changing data conditions without forgetting previously learned information. Our research opens
539   new avenues for further exploration of dynamic neural architectures and sets a new benchmark for
      online learning with haphazard inputs.

      A    P RACTICAL A PPLICATIONS
811
812
813   With the progress of the field of haphazard inputs, more datasets are expected to become avail-
814
      able. For instance, Schreckenberger et al. (2023) introduced the crowdsense dataset in 2023, col-
      lected from environmental sensors (measuring sound pressure, eCO2 level, and eTVOC level) across
815
      Spain’s 56 largest cities over 790 days from January 1st (2020) to February 28th (2022). The feature
816
      space varies as the new sensing data continuously emerges while many old sensors stop to provide
817   data. The aim is to predict government restriction severity based on sensed regional crowdedness.
818   We could not include this dataset in our study because it has only 790 instances, which is very
819   small for a deep-learning model. Nevertheless, we anticipate the emergence of larger datasets in this
820   domain. Note that we demonstrate the superior performance of packetLSTM compared to article
821   (Schreckenberger et al., 2023) in our study in Table 1 in the main manuscript.
822
      Another potential application is in the study of sub-cellular organisms, as discussed in (Agarwal
823   et al., 2024). The mitochondria undergo morphological changes during the drug discovery process,
824   leading to fusion, fission, and kiss-and-run events. Quantifying these events is crucial for biologists,
825   yet direct observation is impractical due to hundreds of mitochondria within a single cell. Fusion
826   and fission introduce obsolete and sudden features, while kiss-and-run events can lead to sudden,
827   obsolete, and missing features. The combination of haphazard input and segmentation models may
828   effectively model the mitochondrial dynamics.
829
830
831   B    M ULTI -M ODAL L EARNING VS PACKET LSTM
832
833   The multi-modality requires handling data from different data domains, like images, audio, etc.
834   Some studies in the multi-model domain utilize LSTMs (more generally RNNs), as discussed below.
835
836
      Brain Image Segmentation LSTM-MA (Xie & Wen, 2019) converts the multi-modal image
837
      slices to feature sequences using pixel and superpixel constraints. These are then fed to the LSTM,
838
      followed by a fully connected neural network to predict the final class label. Finally, the segmenta-
839   tion image is obtained by combining all the classified nodes of a slice.
840
841
842   Online Action Detection Liu et al. (2018) proposed an RNN framework for online action de-
843   tection and forecasting on the fly from the untrimmed stream data. The data have two modalities,
844
      i.e., RGB video and skeleton sequence data. The paper uses deep ConvNet and motion networks
      to extract embedding from RGB and skeleton data, respectively, and the embeddings are processed
845
      individually by stacked LSTM.
846
847
848   Speech Recognition AE-MSR (Xu et al., 2020) works on two modalities, namely, audio and
849   video, to perform speech recognition. Here, both audio and video data are passed to an AE sub-
850   network to generate visual features and enhanced audio magnitude. The generated features are fur-
851   ther passed to different element-wise attention-gated recurrent units (EleAtt-GRU) encoders. This
852
      generates visual and audio context, respectively. Both contexts are further passed to a decoder con-
      sisting of EleAtt-GRU to generate outputs.
853
854   Within the multi-modal domain, to the best of our knowledge, the total number of features/modalities
855   always remains the same and is known at the outset (t = 0). Some recent work also considers missing
856   modality in the multi-modal domain (Lee et al., 2023b), but still, the total number of modalities is
857   known at the outset (t = 0). Therefore, the above method cannot accommodate haphazard inputs.
858
      Whereas the packetLSTM can dynamically change its architecture to adapt to varying feature spaces
      in real-time environments.

1007   Synthetic Dataset Preparation We created haphazard input datasets from synthetic datasets
1008   based on probability values p, as defined in the baseline papers (Beyazit et al., 2019; Agarwal et al.,
1009   2023). Specifically, 100 ×p% of features at each time instance is simulated as available indepen-
1010   dently of each other following a uniform distribution. For example, if p = 0.25, 25% of features
1011
       at each time instance is only available. From each synthetic dataset, three subsets are generated
       corresponding to p values of 0.25, 0.5, and 0.75. This approach creates a spectrum of datasets,
1012
       from highly unavailable data to those with extensive data availability, thereby facilitating the test-
1013
       ing of models under varying conditions of data accessibility. Notably, 98.35% of data values are
1014   unavailable in the imdb dataset (see Table 5).

1026
       Accuracy The Model’s accuracy can be determined by calculating the total number of correct
1027   predictions divided by the total number of instances. Similar to the number of errors, accuracy may
1028   not serve as a reliable metric in the context of imbalanced datasets, as it can be misleadingly high
1029   when the majority class is predicted correctly while neglecting the minority class.
1030
1031   AUROC The Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) measures a
1032   model’s capability to differentiate between positive and negative classes. The ROC curve plots
1033   the true positive rates against the false positive rates. The area of this curve, the AUROC, is a value
1034   for which higher numbers indicate superior model performance.
1035
1036   AUPRC AUPRC stands for Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve. It is similar to AUROC; how-
1037   ever, it utilizes precision and recall rather than true and false positive rates. AUPRC is particularly
1038   valuable for assessing the performance of models on imbalanced datasets, providing insight into the
1039
       model’s ability to identify positive instances.
1040
1041
       Balanced Accuracy This is calculated as the average of specificity and sensitivity. This metric
       offers a more nuanced assessment of model performance across imbalanced datasets compared to
1042
       traditional accuracy, as it equally weighs the correct identification rates of both positive and negative
1043
       classes.

1046   G    E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
1047
1048   We adhere to the standard evaluation protocol for haphazard inputs and, more generally, online learn-
1049
       ing. As described in the Mathematical Formulation paragraph of Section 3 in the main manuscript,
       the model is evaluated and then trained iteratively. There is no separate evaluation set. The model
1050
       first receives input features at time t, makes a prediction, and then the ground truth is revealed. The
1051
       model calculates the cross-entropy loss using the prediction and ground truth. Each instance is pro-
1052   cessed once and not revisited, reflecting the online learning characteristics of batch size 1 and epoch
1053   1. The prediction logits and the labels for each instance are stored, and upon processing all the
1054   inputs, the balanced accuracy and other metrics are determined based on these data. This evaluation
1055   method is consistently applied across all models in this study.

1152
       We followed the strategy employed in article (Agarwal et al., 2024), where the best hyperparameters
       for the synthetic dataset are found at p = 0.5 and subsequently used for p = 0.25 and 0.75. The best
1153
       hyperparameters for all baseline models, except OLIFL, are derived from article (Agarwal et al.,
1154
       2024), and the same hyperparameter search protocol is applied to both OLIFL and packetLSTM.
1155   Details of the hyperparameter search values and the selected best values are presented in Table 6
1156   and Table 7. The hyperparameter search process for packetLSTM and OLIFL is discussed next.
1157
1158
1159   packetLSTM We determine the best values of hyperparameters sequentially. First, we fixed the
1160   learning rate and aggregation operator to 0.0001 and mean, respectively, and varied the hidden sizes
1161
       of the short-term and long-term memory, being set at 32 and 64. We found the best hidden size to
       be 64. Next, we experimented with four aggregation operators, namely, mean, max, min, and sum,
1162
       maintaining a fixed learning rate of 0.0001 and a hidden size of 64. We found max and mean as
1163
       the best operators for synthetic and real datasets, respectively. Finally, with the best hidden size
1164   and aggregation operator established, we tested a range of learning rates (0.05, 0.01, 0.005, 0.001,
1165   0.0005, 0.0001). We searched in the vicinity of the optimal learning rate found in the previous step.
1166   For example, 0.0005 was found to yield good results on the magic04 dataset, prompting further
1167   exploration of nearby values – 0.0002, 0.0003, 0.0004, 0.0006, 0.007, 0.0008, and 0.0009. Among
1168   these, 0.0006 emerged as the most effective learning rate. The same process was followed for all the
1169   datasets.
1170
1171
       OLIFL We fixed the option second (min(C, 2loss/inner product(X))) to determine τ and tested a
1172
       range of C values (10, 1, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, 0.0001, 0.00001, 0.000001). Similar to packetLSTM, we
1173   searched in the vicinity of the optimal C value found in the previous step. In addition to this, we
1174   also experimented with the first option (loss/inner product(X)) to determine τ .

       Time Complexity The time complexity of packetLSTM is t=1 O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 + P ), where T is
1353
       the number of instances, O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 ) is the time complexity to process all the activated LSTMs
1354
       at time t corresponding to Ft features, and O(P ) broadly denotes the constant time required to
1355   perform other fixed operations like aggregations and final prediction. We utilize the torch.matmul()
1356   function for matrix multiplication of LSTMs. The time required for each LSTM is dependent on
1357   its hidden size (s) and performs 3 matrix multiplication of size (3s, s + 1) and (s + 1, 1) requiring
1358   a time complexity of O(s2 ). However, we vectorize the operation of |Ft | LSTMs by single matrix
1359   multiplication of (|Ft |, 3s, s + 1) and (|Ft |, s + 1, 1). Note that the time required by torch.matmul()
1360   does not scale linearly with |Ft |; rather, it just takes a small overhead depending on the type of
1361   hardware and other dependencies. Here, we denote this overhead as a function of |Ft | as g(|Ft |)
1362   where 1 ≤ g(|Ft |) ≤ |Ft |. This is further corroborated by the time required by packetLSTMs on
1363
       each synthetic dataset with different p values (see Table 8). For example, the time required to process
       the whole HIGGS dataset takes 4396.83 and 4500.17 seconds for p = 0.25 and 0.5, respectively.
1364
       Even though the value of |Ft | doubles from p = 0.25 to 0.5, the time required doesn’t increase by
1365
       the same factor. Therefore, the time required by the |Ft | LSTMs at time t would be O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 ).
1366   Finally, since it is an online learning task and each instance is processed sequentially, the total
1367                                                PT
       time complexity of T instances would be t=1 O(g(|Ft |) ∗ s2 + P ). It is difficult to find the exact
1368   form of the function g. However, based on the time required by packetLSTM on each dataset with
1369   increasing p, it can be safely assumed that the value of g(|Ft |) is closer to 1 than |Ft |. Therefore,
1370   the packetLSTM model demonstrates scalability in terms of time complexity corresponding to the
1371   number of features.
1372
1373   Space Complexity The space complexity of packetLSTM is directly dependent on the space com-
1374   plexity of an LSTM. Let us denote the space complexity of an LSTM by O(L). At each time t, we
1375   have a universal feature space of |F̄t | cardinality, therefore, corresponding |F̄t | LSTMs are present
1376   in the packetLSTMs. The space complexity of the final prediction network and aggregation function
1377   are fixed and denoted by O(K). Therefore, the total space complexity of packetLSTM at time t
1378   can be given by O(|F̄t | ∗ L + K). Note that the space complexity of packetLSTM increases with
1379   the arrival of sudden and missing features. Therefore, the limitation of packetLSTM is that it is not
1380
       scalable in terms of space complexity corresponding to the feature size. However, it is noteworthy
       that packetLSTM effectively manages up to 7500 features, as demonstrated with the imdb dataset.
1381
       Additionally, we present a strategy to further mitigate space complexity limitation in section Q of
1382
       the Appendix.
1383
1384
       Number of Parameters Here, we provide the number of learnable parameters in the packetLSTM
1385
       framework with Time-LSTM 3 as the time modeling unit. The mathematical formulation is given
1386   by equations 2-8. The input gate (i), time gate 1 (T 1), time gate 2 (T 2), cell state (c̃), long-term
1387   memory (c), and output gate (o) requires s2 + 3s, 3s, 3s, s2 + 2s, s2 + 2s, and s2 + 4s learnable
1388   parameters, respectively. Therefore, the total number of learnable parameters in an LSTM unit is
1389   4s2 + 17s. The fully connected layer accounts for 2s2 + 4s + 2 parameters. Therefore, the total
1390   number of learnable parameters at each instance is |Ft | ∗ (4s2 + 17s) + 2s2 + 4s + 2. For all
1391   the experiments, the value of s is 64. In this article, the maximum number of parameters for each
1392   dataset would correspond to the worst-case scenario where all the features are present, resulting in a
1393   maximum of ∼183K, ∼131M, ∼2M, ∼148K, and ∼375K learnable parameters for magic04, imdb,
1394   a8a, SUSY, and HIGGS, respectively. Consequently, packetLSTM requires 1.40 MB, 999.45 MB,
1395
       15.26 MB, 1.13 MB, and 2.85 MB memory with 64-bit precision for magic04, imdb, a8a, SUSY,
       and HIGGS, respectively. Considering that a small large language model of 1 billion parameters is
1396
       common nowadays, packetLSTM can handle ∼57K features with 1 billion parameters. We believe
1397
       that a practical application would have a number of features way less than ∼57K.

1402   The space complexity increases with the number of features, as discussed above. However, pack-
1403   etLSTM easily handles even the 7500 features in the imdb dataset. The total number of learnable
       parameters of packetLSTM for the imdb dataset is ∼131M. Therefore, packetLSTM with 1B param-

1404
       eters and a hidden size of 64 can handle around ∼57K features. Hence, we argue that packetLSTM
1405   can deal with high-dimensional data. However, we also propose a solution to curb the space com-
1406   plexity by defining a maximum limit (say lf ) on the number of LSTMs. When the number of
1407   features in the universal feature space |F̄t | > lf , we drop |F̄t | − lf features. Here, dropping the
1408   feature means that the corresponding LSTM is reinitialized and assigned to some new features that
1409   arrived at time t. The dropped feature can come in future instances (> t). However, this feature will
1410   then be considered as a sudden feature. We employ KL-Divergence to determine the dropped fea-
1411   tures. After processing instance t − 1, packetLSTM has the short-term memory of each LSTM and
1412   the common long-term memory. The KL divergence between each short-term and common long-
1413   term memory is determined. The feature corresponding to the short-term memory, which has the
1414   lowest KL-Divergence, is dropped. This is because the common long-term memory already holds
1415
       the dropped feature’s short-term memory information and is used for final prediction. We also put
       a limit on the number of times a feature is seen (il ) by packetLSTM before it can be considered for
1416
       dropping. We experimented with the imdb dataset since it has the highest number of features (7500).
1417
       The best hyperparameters found for packetLSTM on the imdb dataset in Table 7 are used here. The
1418   lf and il are set to 100 and achieved a balanced accuracy of 78.92, which still performs better than
1419   7 out of 10 baseline models (see Table 1 in main manuscript). The 78.92 balanced accuracy is lower
1420   than the packetLSTM without any limits (85.06). So, there is a tradeoff between performance and
1421   the space complexity.

1427
1428          • Forward fill: The missing value of a feature is imputed with its last observed value.
1429          • Mean: The missing value of a feature is imputed with its forward mean determined in a
1430            rolling manner. That is, the last K observed values of a feature are considered to calculate
1431            its mean. Here K = 5.
1432
1433
              • Gaussian Copula: (Zhao et al., 2022) proposed using Gaussian copula for online streaming
                data with a known N , where N is the total number of features. This method argues that data
1434
                points are generated from a latent Gaussian vector, which is then transformed to match the
1435
                marginal distributions of observed features. However, Gaussian Copula requires storing a
1436            matrix of K instances to perform imputation. The method fails if the matrix’s determinant
1437            is 0 or if a feature’s values within the matrix are identical. The magic04 and SUSY require
1438            K = 5, and HIGGS needs K = 30. The method does not work for a8a and imdb for even
1439            K = 300, excluding its application to these datasets.

1614   T     C ATASTROPHIC F ORGETTING IN O NLINE L EARNING VERSUS O NLINE
1615         C ONTINUAL L EARNING
1616
1617   In this article, we present the ‘learning without forgetting’ capability of packetLSTM. The ‘learning
1618   without forgetting’ capability is also referred to as mitigating catastrophic forgetting in the existing
1619   literature (Hoi et al., 2021). We explore this capability in an online learning setting, where the model
       receives and processes instances sequentially without any buffer storage.


                                                            30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       The problem of catastrophic forgetting is extensively studied in a parallel and widely recognized
1621   field of online continual learning (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Wang et al., 2024). Online continual learning
1622   is the field of machine learning where a model continually learns new tasks. In this scenario, tasks
1623   are delivered sequentially, yet all the instances for each task are presented at once. Consequently,
1624   the learning for each task is conducted in an offline setting, utilizing all data related to a task before
1625   transitioning to the subsequent one. For example, a model may initially learn from data related to
1626   task 1 in an offline batch mode. Subsequently, it proceeds to learn from data pertaining to task 2 in
1627   a similar batch mode, and this pattern continues. The introduction of new tasks may result in the
1628   addition of new classes, a shift in data distribution, or the arrival of an entirely new task domain. In
1629
       the context of online continual learning, catastrophic forgetting refers to the challenge a model faces
       in retaining its ability to perform previously learned tasks.
1630
1631   Although online continual learning shares similarities with online learning regarding sequential task
1632   learning, it differs significantly in its execution within individual tasks. In online continual learn-
1633   ing, the model adheres to a batch training paradigm, which diverges from the principles of online
1634   learning algorithms. Consequently, the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting, as observed in on-
1635
       line learning, distinctly differs from that in online continual learning. It is worth noting that the
       packetLSTM framework, with certain modifications, may potentially be adapted for use in online
1636
       continual learning to address catastrophic forgetting. However, this application extends beyond the
1637
       scope of our article.

       The two input padding methods are:
1644
1645          • Only Values: For each dataset, zeros are added to the sequence following the available
1646            feature values until fl , where fl = N , where N is the total number of features. Note
1647            that this contradicts the sixth characteristic of haphazard inputs. However, to compare
1648            packetLSTM with Transformer, we assume that N is known. The specific N for each
1649
                dataset is defined in the column labeled ‘#Features’ in Table 5 of the Appendix. An example
                of an input instance at time t where feature 1 and j is available is [xt1 , xtj , 0, ..., 0].
1650
1651          • Pairs: Each available feature value is paired with its corresponding feature ID, and the
1652
                sequence of these pairs is padded with zeros till fl . The feature ID ranges from 1 to N , and
                fl = 2N . An Example is [xt1 , 1, xtj , j, 0, ..., 0]
1653
1654   Padded inputs are initially processed through a linear embedding layer, which outputs embedding of
1655   dimension d. The input dimension for this layer is N and 2N for Only Values and Pairs, respectively.
1656   The embedding is then passed to an encoder. The encoder consists of nl encoder layers. Each
1657   encoder layer comprises nh heads and maintains dimensions of size d for both input and output.
1658   The encoder’s output feeds into the same fully connected neural network utilized in packetLSTM,
1659   consisting of a linear layer of dimensions (d, d), a ReLU activation, and another linear layer leading
1660
       to the output classes, culminating in a softmax layer for predictions.
1661   The hyperparameters search is performed sequentially, as in the packetLSTM. We searched the
1662   optimal value of d among 32, 64, 128, 256, and 512, nh among 1, 2, 4, 8, and 16, nl among 1,
1663   2, 3, and 4. Similar to packetLSTM, we tested a range of learning rates (0.001, 0.0005, 0.0001,
1664   0.00005). We further searched in the vicinity of the optimal learning rate found in the previous
1665   step. Optimal hyperparameters include a d of 128 for magic04 and 32 for other datasets, nh of 4 for
1666
       SUSY and imdb, 8 for magic04, and 16 for a8a and HIGGS. The nl is 1 for all the datasets, with the
       learning rate of 0.0002 for magic04 and a8a and 0.0001 for SUSY, HIGGS, and imdb. The Z-score
1667

1754   V       H APHAZARD I NPUTS VS OTHER F IELDS OF VARYING F EATURE S PACE
1755
1756
       The field of varying feature space is also studied as feature evolvable streams (Hou et al., 2017;
       Zhang et al., 2020) and incremental and decremental features (Hou & Zhou, 2018; Dong et al.,
1757
       2021). However, both these fields assume some form of structure in their data stream, which con-
1758
       tradicts the characteristics of haphazard inputs. Specifically, the feature evolvable streams work in
1759   batches and assume that there is an overlap period between the transition where old features vanish
1760   and new features occur. Similarly, incremental and decremental features assume that the data arrives
1761   in batches, where the initial batch consists of both vanishing and surviving features and subsequent
1762   batches encompass surviving and newly augmented features. The assumption of the batch and the
1763   structure of the data within the batch limits the applicability of both fields in haphazard inputs.
