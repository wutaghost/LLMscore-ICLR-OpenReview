009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               It is commonly believed that Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) strug-
012            gle in link prediction settings due to limitations in their expressive power. Recent
013            work has focused on developing more expressive model classes, which are capa-
014            ble of learning link representations through techniques such as labeling tricks, the
015            inclusion of structural features, or the use of subgraph methods. These approaches
016            have yielded significant performance improvements across a range of benchmark
017            datasets. However, an interesting question remains: have we fully wrung out the
018            performance by optimizing the other aspects of the training process? In this work,
019            we present results that indicate that significant amounts of model performance
020
               have been left on the table by the use of easy negative-samples during training.
               We theoretically explore the generalization gap and excess risk to quantify the
021
               performance loss caused by easy negatives. Motivated by this analysis, we intro-
022
               duce Risk Aware Negative Sampling (RANS), which efficiently performs dynamic
023            hard-negative-mining. Empirical results show that a simple GCN augmented by
024            RANS realizes between 20% and 50% improvements in predictive accuracy when
025            compared with the same model trained with standard negative samples.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Link prediction is an important machine learning task that aims to predict unobserved edges connect-
032   ing two vertices. Link prediction traditionally operates on graph-structured data, which is ubiquitous
033
      in industrial settings as it provides a natural way to represent entities and complex relationships be-
      tween them (Chamberlain et al., 2023). For example, predicting new friendship relationships or
034
      post engagements can be framed as link prediction tasks (El-Kishky et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2023) in
035
      social media companies. This is accomplished by constructing an unsupervised set of positive sam-
036   ples, such as existing links, and learning a representation that reliably predicts those relationships to
037   exist with higher probability than a set of negatives (Kumar et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024).
038
039
      Given its importance, link prediction has received broad interest both industrially and academically,
      with significant work dedicated to improving modeling modalities. The simplest approaches are
040
      heuristic methods, which provide structural measures for link similarity (Zhou et al., 2009; Adamic
041
      & Adar, 2003). Another class of popular methods aims to compute unsupervised node embeddings
042   that minimize graph reconstruction error (Bordes et al., 2013; Kazemi & Poole, 2018; Rossi et al.,
043   2021; Lerer et al., 2019). In recent years, this focus has shifted to modeling graphs using Message
044   Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs)(Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017). However, unlike
045   node or graph-level tasks, MPNNs often struggle with link-level tasks. This is commonly attributed
046   to two reasons: (1) MPNNs are unable to count triangles(Chen et al., 2020) because they are equiv-
047   alent to the 1-WL test (Xu et al., 2018), and (2) MPNNs learn structural node representations when
048   structural edge representations are required for link prediction (Srinivasan & Ribeiro, 2019).
049   These insights have inspired the development of more expressive MPNNs through the use of label-
050   ing tricks (Zhang et al., 2021), conversion from link prediction to subgraph classification (Zhang
051   & Chen, 2018a; Wang & Zhang, 2021; Yin et al., 2023), or the inclusion of graph structural fea-
052   tures (Chamberlain et al., 2023; Yun et al., 2021). While these advances have led to complicated
053   models and impressive performance gains, an open question remains: are we extracting maximum
      performance from a given model, even if it’s not maximally expressive? This question is particularly


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      important because more complicated models are often difficult to scale in industrial settings (Ma
055   et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022; Borisyuk et al., 2024).
056
057   Due to the unsupervised nature of link prediction, there are only a few core components to the
058
      problem: the model, additional feature engineering (through labeling tricks or structural features),
      and the selection of negative samples. While both feature engineering and modeling techniques have
059
      received significant research attention, negative sampling has been comparatively less explored in
060
      the context of link prediction. The quality of negatives, however, is intuitively quite important.
061   Consider the case of a friendship graph on a site like Facebook, where the task is to recommend
062   potential friends whom users might know. A model will almost certainly be better informed about
063   user relationships when trained with negatives that are close to their social location (e.g., the town
064   where they live) rather than with uniform random sampling, which would likely generate trivial
065   negative examples.
066
      Previous graph-related work has found that the selection of hard negatives through either feature
067   similarity (Pancha et al., 2022) or graph-structure sampling (Yang et al., 2020) leads to performance
068   improvements in some graph learning settings. Beyond graphs and link prediction, hard negative
069   mining has received significant attention from the computer vision (Xuan et al., 2020; Jin et al.,
070   2018; Sun et al., 2019) and NLP communities (Zhang & Stratos, 2021; Dasgupta & Ng, 2009).
071
      Based on our understanding of the importance of hard negatives, as well as the attention from other
072
      communities, we address the question: “Can we learn better link prediction models by improving
073   negative sampling methods?” We answer this question first through the analysis of link prediction
074   using the empirical risk minimization framework, which allows us to develop clear bounds for both
075   the generalization gap and the excess risk in terms of the unsampled negatives. These theoretical
076   insights inspire a simple negative resampling method that we term Risk Aware Negative Sampling
077   (RANS), which is highly flexible and applicable to any MPNN. To validate this method and our
078   analysis, we evaluate the performance of RANS on a range of datasets and present our results in
079   Section 7.

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084
      Initial work in link prediction often used random negative sampling, where negative samples were
085
      drawn from non-existent edges in the graph. This approach treats the problem as a binary classi-
086
      fication task, distinguishing true links (positive samples) from non-links (negative samples)(Zhang
087   & Chen, 2018b). However, randomly chosen negatives tend to be easy to classify, which can lead
088   to suboptimal models that fail to generalize well, especially in large and sparse graphs(Wang et al.,
089   2017b). More recent approaches have moved toward dynamic negative sampling and hard neg-
090   ative mining to address these limitations. Hard negatives are edges that are not present in the
091   graph but share structural or feature similarities with positive edges. Studies have shown that select-
092   ing harder negative samples during training forces the model to learn more discriminative features,
093   improving its ability to predict challenging or ambiguous links (Grover & Leskovec, 2016). Tech-
094   niques such as self-contrastive learning have also been used to approximate the full negative set
095
      by leveraging negative edges dynamically during training, significantly enhancing performance in
      large-scale graphs (Wu & Zhu, 2019).
096
097   A parallel line of research has focused on using labeling tricks and incorporating structural fea-
098   tures to address inherent limitations in graph-based models, such as automorphic node symmetry,
099   where structurally similar nodes receive identical embeddings (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021;
100   Chamberlain et al., 2023). These methods, which explicitly add structural information, help models
101
      break symmetry and distinguish between nodes that may otherwise appear similar in purely topo-
      logical terms (Chen & Liu, 2017). Negative sampling plays an important role in this context—when
102
      negatives are sampled from structurally similar nodes, the model is better trained to capture nuanced
103
      structural differences, thus improving link prediction performance.
104
105   Active Learning represents another related research direction, as it seeks to select a set of data for
106   further labeling (Ren et al., 2021). RANS can be connected to active learning if we consider the
107   special case where f (i) represents both the data-labeling and training models (Ren et al., 2021).
                                                   ′
      Selecting the highest scoring elements of D̂n under f (i) amounts to an Expected Error Reduction

108
      process (Mussmann et al., 2022). However, RANS differs from active learning because it does not
109   select datapoints for further labeling, as these labels are available trivially.
110
111   Another emerging area involves utilizing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
112
      et al., 2014) for hard-negative mining. Cai & Wang (2018) applies adversarial learning to improve
      the quality of negative sampling on knowledge graph embeddings. A separate knowledge graph
113
      embedding model (the generator) that produces hard negatives is trained alongside a model (the
114
      discriminator) that learns to distinguish between true and false triples on the graph. A similar ap-
115   proach is evaluated for information retrieval by Wang et al. (2017a), where the generator feeds
116   relevant documents for a given query into the discriminator. Additionally, Yu et al. (2018) proposes
117   a general framework for using GANs in graph representation learning tasks. While using the GANs
118   framework for negative sampling allows for iterative learning of sampling and classification during
119   training, promising high-quality negatives, the iterative nature can cause training instability and in-
120   creased computational complexity. Diffusion-model based methods also provide a promising path
121   forward towards improving the quality of the generated negative samples, but are computationally
122   expensive (Nguyen & Fang, 2024). RANS can be viewed as a GAN without the minimax game,
123
      offering flexibility in when new samples are generated.
124   Several studies have indicated that the overall quality of negative sampling can significantly impact
125   the expressiveness of link prediction models (Yang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024).
126   For instance, while Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are often employed for their ability to learn
127   rich node representations, their performance can be substantially enhanced by carefully curating
128   negative samples during training (Zhang & Chen, 2016). Hard-negative sampling techniques have
129
      been shown to eliminate problems such as oversmoothing in GNNs, where node embeddings become
      too similar, by encouraging the model to differentiate between subtle structural or feature-based
130
      patterns (Yang et al., 2016a).
131
132   In conclusion, while much of the link prediction research has focused on improving model archi-
133   tectures, recent work highlights the critical role of negative sampling strategies. By shifting from
134   random to more informed, structure-aware sampling methods, researchers have demonstrated sub-
135
      stantial improvements in predictive accuracy. This paper extends these insights by investigating the
      impact of negative sampling on model performance and proposing a refined dynamic hard-negative
136
      sampling approach to optimize link prediction tasks.

165   Link Prediction. Link prediction aims to learn a function f (hi , hj ) that predicts the likelihood
166
      of an edge between vertices i and j based on their node representations hi and hj . Typically,
      node representations are obtained using an MPNN, i.e. MPNN(i, G) = hi , ∀i ∈ V. Putting it
167
      all together, link prediction seeks to learn a readout function f (MPNN(i, G), MPNN(j, G)) → R,
168
      treating it as a binary classification task (Hasan & Zaki, 2011). Popular choices for f include dot
169   product (Trouillon et al., 2016), Hadamard product (Wang et al., 2022; Chamberlain et al., 2023),
170   or MLP (Chamberlain et al., 2023). Since link prediction is framed as a binary classification task,
171   both positive and negative samples are required for training. Positive samples correspond to existing
172   edges, while negative samples are drawn from non-existent edges. Since the number of possible
173   negative edges (O(N 2 )) far exceeds the number of actual edges (O(|E|)), it is necessary to sample
174   a subset of negative edges to maintain dataset balance and computational efficiency (Bordes et al.,
175   2013).

      Limitations of MPNNs for Link Prediction. MPNNs, which are equivalent to the Weisfeiler-
      Leman isomorphism test, face two key limitations in link prediction. First, they cannot count trian-
196
      gles, a critical structural feature (Tolmachev et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020). Second, MPNNs suffer
197
      from the automorphic node problem, where they assign equivalent representations to vertices in the
198   same graph orbit (i.e., under graph automorphisms) (Srinivasan & Ribeiro, 2019). This leads to two
199   issues: (1) f (vi , vj ) = f (vi , vk ) for vertices vj and vk in the same orbit, regardless of distance; and
200   (2) when the MPNN suffers from oversmoothing, it produces overly similar vertex representations,
201   making the link predictor too optimistic.
202
      Negative Sampling and Structural Representations. To improve link prediction performance,
203
      researchers often employ techniques such as labeling tricks and explicit structural features (Zhang
204   et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Chamberlain et al., 2023). These methods help break automorphic node
205   symmetry by enabling the edge-wise decoder to learn how to incorporate these additional sources
206   of information. Consider the naphthalene molecule shown in Figure 1, which contains five sets of
207   structurally isomorphic vertices. Given that vertices v9 and v17 are isomorphic, any link predic-
208   tor based purely on structural node representations will yield equivalent representations for edges
209   (v1 , v9 ) and (v1 , v17 ). The scenario becomes more nuanced when incorporating additional features,
210   such as the number of valence electrons assigned to vertices (atoms) in the graph (molecule). In
211   naphthalene, atoms in the central rings possess four valence electrons, while those on the periphery
212
      have one valence electron.
213   To address these issues, we can define a link predictor that incorporates both structural node rep-
214   resentations and the distance between node pairs. While this approach can break symmetry, it is
215   effective only when negative samples during training include pairs of structurally isomorphic ver-
      tices. For instance, if negatives are chosen solely from vertices that differ significantly in feature

      Traditionally, excess risk characterizes the discrepancy between a model fˆ and f˜∗ over F. In this
342
      view, increasing the expressivity of F can lead to an overall reduction in excess risk by enabling
343
      better data representations. We propose an alternative perspective: instead of modifying F, we can
344   reduce excess risk by exploring different data splitting strategies while holding F constant. If we
345
      construct Dn to contain only difficult edges under the model f˜∗ , the second term will shrink because
346   the remaining edges are ”easy.” Consequently, the selection of negative samples can significantly
347   influence our choice of fˆ∗ , thereby reducing both our uniform error bound and excess risk.

432
      Baselines. We compare our proposed negative sampling method against several established tech-
433   niques:
434
435   - UNS: Uniform Negative Sampling selects negative samples uniformly at random from all possible
436     edges. This is the default negative sampling technique in PyTorch Geometric and does not con-
437     sider structural or feature-based information when selecting negatives (Bordes et al., 2013; Fey &
438     Lenssen, 2019).
439   - USNS: Uniform Structural Negative Sampling samples negatives uniformly from nodes sharing
440     structural similarity. This approach generates more challenging negative samples compared to
441     UNS by focusing on structurally similar nodes (Wang et al., 2014; Fey & Lenssen, 2019).
442   - RWNS: Random Walk Weighted Negative Sampling selects negative samples based on random
443     walks over the graph. Selection probability is weighted by node frequency in random walks,
444     reflecting connectivity and structural importance (Hamilton et al., 2017).
445   - PNS: Personalized Negative Sampling selects negative samples based on node feature similarity,
446     with selection probability increasing as feature similarity increases (Pal et al., 2020).
447
      - MCNS: Monte Carlo Negative Sampling employs Monte Carlo methods for negative sampling,
448     repeatedly sampling possible negatives and estimating their loss contribution using probabilistic
449     techniques (Yang et al., 2020).
450
451   These methods operate in either static or dynamic settings. In static settings, negative training edges
452   are generated once and used throughout all epochs. In dynamic settings, edges are regenerated every
453   K epochs, where K is a tuned hyperparameter. UNS, USNS, and RWNS support both settings,
454
      denoted by -S (static) or -D (dynamic) suffixes. PNS is limited to static settings due to computational
      constraints, while MCNS is inherently dynamic. We omit PNS for OGBL - DDI due to the absence of
455
      features and for OGBL - COLLAB due to memory constraints.

534   8    C ONCLUSION
535
536   In conclusion, we have presented a new technique for generating negative samples dynamically that
537   leads to higher quality models. This approach is inspired by an analysis of the excess risk in link pre-
538   diction, and we show both theoretically and empirically that our method reduces the generalization
539   gap, and leads to better model performance. This work is limited to static link prediction contexts,
      but future work will explore temporal graphs.
