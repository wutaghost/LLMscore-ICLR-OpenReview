                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                 Tool-augmented large language models (LLMs) may need to forget learned tools
                 due to security concerns, privacy restrictions, or deprecated tools. However, “tool
011
                 unlearning” has not been investigated in machine unlearning literature. We intro-
012
                 duce this novel task, which requires addressing distinct challenges compared to
013              traditional unlearning: knowledge removal rather than forgetting individual sam-
014              ples, the high cost of optimizing LLMs, and the need for principled evaluation
015              metrics. To bridge these gaps, we propose T OOL D ELETE, the first approach for
016              unlearning tools from tool-augmented LLMs which implements three properties
017              for effective tool unlearning, and a new membership inference attack (MIA) model
018              for evaluation. Experiments on three tool learning datasets and tool-augmented
019              LLMs show that T OOL D ELETE effectively unlearns both randomly selected and
020              category-specific tools, while preserving the LLM’s knowledge on non-deleted
021
                 tools and maintaining performance on general tasks1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Tool-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs) learn how to use external tools like calcula-
027
      tors (Schick et al., 2023), Python interpretors (Gao et al., 2023), simulated API requests (Tang et al.,
      2023), or other AI models (Patil et al., 2023) to complement their parametric knowledge and boost
028
      their capability of solving more complex tasks (Schick et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023). For example,
029
      WebGPT (Nakano et al., 2021) is developed based on GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and can use search
030   engines to access up-to-date information and boost GPT-3’s performance in question answering and
031   fact verification, particularly on recent events that happened after GPT-3 was trained.
032
033
      Despite rapid advancements in tool-augmented LLMs, the ability to selectively unlearn tools has
      not been investigated. In real-world applications, the need to forget learned tools is crucial for
034
      reasons such as security, privacy, and model reliability. For example, if a tool-augmented LLM
035
      retains knowledge on making insecure HTTP requests, it will cause significant security risks and
036   can become vulnerable to attacks2 . The goal of this work is to address this gap in existing literature.
037
038   We introduce and formalize the novel task of Tool Unlearning, which aims to remove the ability
039
      of using specific tools from a tool-augmented LLM while preserving its ability to use others tools
      and perform general tasks such text generation. Ideally, an effective tool unlearning model should
040
      behave as it had never learned the tools marked for unlearning. Tool unlearning differs from tradi-
041
      tional sample-level unlearning as it focuses on removing “skills” or the ability to use specific tools,
042   rather than removing individual data samples from a model. In addition, success in tool unlearning
043   should be measured by the model’s ability to forget or retain tool-related skills, which differs from
044   traditional metrics like forgetting class probability. These differences are discussed in details in §3.
045
      Tool unlearning has several challenges: it focuses on removing skills and existing unlearning meth-
046
      ods are not fundamentally designed for tool removal; similar to sample-level unlearning, in tool
047
      unlearning, modifying the parameters of LLMs is essential but computationally expensive and may
048   lead to unforeseen behaviors (Cohen et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024); and existing membership infer-
049   ence attack (MIA) techniques, a common evaluation method in machine unlearning which aims to
050   determine whether specific data samples were used during training, are unsuitable for evaluating
051   tool unlearning, as they focus on sample-level data rather than tool-based knowledge.

      To address these challenges, we propose T OOL D ELETE, the first tool unlearning algorithm for tool-
055   augmented LLMs, which satisfies three key properties for effective tool unlearning: tool knowledge
056   removal, which focuses on removing any knowledge gained on tools marked for unlearning; tool
057   knowledge retention, which focuses on preserving the knowledge gained on other remaining tools;
058   and general utility preservation, which applies task arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023; Barbulescu &
059   Triantafillou, 2024) to maintain LLM’s capability on a range of general tasks like text and code
060   generation. In addition, we develop LiRA-Tool, an adaptation of the Likelihood Ratio Attack
061   (LiRA) (Carlini et al., 2022) to tool unlearning, which enables us to assess whether tool-related
062   knowledge has been unlearned.
063   Our contributions are:
064         • introducing and conceptualizing tool unlearning for tool-augmented LLMs,
065         • T OOL D ELETE, which implements three key properties for effective tool unlearning;
066         • LiRA-Tool, which is the first membership inference attack (MIA) for tool unlearning.
067
      Experiments on three datasets and tool-augmented LLMs show that T OOL D ELETE outperforms
068
      existing general and LLM-specific unlearning algorithms. T OOL D ELETE outperforms existing
069
      general and LLM-specific unlearning methods. In addition, it can save 74.8% of training time
070   compared to retraining, handle sequential unlearning requests, and retain 95+% performance in low
071   resource settings.

      2    R ELATED WORK
075
      Unlearning for non-LLM models A wide range of machine unlearning methods have been pro-
076
      posed to remove influence of training data from trained models. These include efficient retraining
077   approaches (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2020b;a; Liu et al., 2022; Dukler et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
078   2023), methods with theoretical guarantee with convex loss assumption (Golatkar et al., 2020; Guo
079   et al., 2020; Neel et al., 2021; Brophy & Lowd, 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Izzo et al., 2021; Suriyakumar
080   & Wilson, 2022; Liu et al., 2023a), methods that enforce performing as a randomly initialized model
081   on deleted samples (Chundawat et al., 2023a), methods that enforce memorizing wrong labels for
082   deleted samples (Graves et al., 2021), those that focus on pruning before unlearning (Jia et al., 2023)
083   or finding salient parameters (Fan et al., 2024b) and manipulating gradients Ullah et al. (2021);
084   Hoang et al. (2024), adversarial methods (Liu et al., 2023b; Setlur et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023),
085
      approximation of inverse Hessian (Zhang et al., 2024a), and data augmentation (Choi et al., 2024).
      Other works study unlearning on graphs (Chen et al., 2022; Chien et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023;
086
      Cong & Mahdavi, 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2023), under multimodal setting (Cheng &
087
      Amiri, 2023), image-to-image models (Li et al., 2024), and finding the most challenging unlearning
088   subset within a dataset (Fan et al., 2024a). Recently, a few works started to benchmark MU perfor-
089   mances on unlearning fictitious user profiles (Maini et al., 2024), world knowledge (Jin et al., 2024)
090   and a variety of tasks (Cheng & Amiri, 2024).
091
092   Unlearning for LLMs Recently, more attention has been given to LLM unlearning, where gra-
093   dient ascent is a common technique (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023; Jang et al., 2023). (Yao et al.,
094   2024) evaluated several traditional unlearning methods on LLMs. KGA (Wang et al., 2023) formu-
095   lated unlearning as achieving knowledge gap between training data and test data similar to that of
096   training data and deleted data. Yao et al. (2023) proposed to predict if the LLM output is gram-
097
      matically correct on deleted samples, such that the knowledge is not over unlearned. Other meth-
      ods include second-order-optimization (Jia et al., 2024), performing direct preference optimization
098
      with no positive examples (Zhang et al., 2024b), and reinforcement learning with a negative reward
099
      model (Kassem et al., 2023). Unlearning from logits difference (Ji et al., 2024) first builds an as-
100   sisted LLM which memorizes data to be deleted and forgets the retained data, which is later used to
101   derive the unlearned LLM by deviating from the assisted LLM in logits.
102
103   Tool-Augmented LLMs TAML (Parisi et al., 2022) used self-play to boost LLMs’ performance
104   on math and reasoning tasks. Schick et al. (2023) discovered that LLMs can teach themselves how
105   to use APIs. Recently, efforts have been devoted to building benchmarks to train and evaluate the
106   tool-using ability of LLMs, such as agent-based data generation (Tang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023),
107   bootstrapping training data with seed examples (Patil et al., 2023), modifying existing datasets (Basu
      et al., 2024), and dataset development with GPT-4 (Qin et al., 2024).

      Problem Definition: Tool Learning Let D = {T , Q, Y} be a dataset with N tools T , and (Q, Y)
      denotes query-output examples that demonstrate how to use the tools in T . Each tool ti ∈ T may
112
      have one or more demonstrations {Qi , Yi }, |Qi | = |Yi | ≥ 1. Starting with a vanilla LLM f0 , which
113
      has not been trained on using tools, a tool learning algorithm explicitly trains f0 on D and results
114   in a tool-augmented model f that can use the N tools in T . We note that prior to explicit tool
115   learning, the vanilla model f0 may already have some tool-using capabilities such as performing
116   basic arithmetic operations. An example of tool-augmented models is WebGPT (Nakano et al.,
117   2021), which mimics human behavior in answering open-ended questions using a text-based web
118   browser to retrieve information and improve its responses.
119
120
      Problem Definition: Tool Unlearning We introduce the novel task of Tool Unlearning, which
121
      aims to remove specific tools from tool-augmented LLMs. Let Df = {Tf , Qf , Yf } denotes k < N
122   tools and their corresponding demonstrations to be unlearned from the tool-augmented model f ,
123   and Dr = D\Df = {Tr , Qr , Yr } denotes the retained tools. The goal is to obtain an unlearned
124   model f ′ that has limited knowledge on using Tf tools–can no longer perform tasks involving those
125   tools–while preserving f ’s ability to use Tr tools as before.
126
127
      Importance The ability to forget learned tools is essential in various real-world applications. For
128
      example, addressing the insecure tools from untrustworthy developers that could be exploited by ad-
129   versarial attackers; removing tools restricted by their providers due to copyright or privacy concerns,
130   such as APIs that start allowing unauthorized downloads of book chapters or releasing publications
131   that users did not author; unlearning broken or deprecated tool that lead to failed operations or
132   corrupted outputs; unlearning tools that may no longer be needed; and managing limited model
133   capacity, where new tools and evolving needs necessitate replacing outdated tools.
134
135
      Difference to Standard Unlearning Tasks Tool unlearning is different from traditional sample-
136
      level unlearning as it focuses on removing “skills” rather than individual training data samples. Ob-
137   jective: sample-level unlearning aims to reduce the likelihood of memorizing or extracting posterior
138   probabilities of specific data samples (qi , yi ), which is useful for removing copyrighted or private
139   information. In contrast, tool unlearning targets the “ability” to solve tasks using tools marked
140   for unlearning (Tf ). For example, generating f ′ (qi ) that is different from yi (while preserving the
141   semantic of the input) is considered successful for sample-level unlearning. However, for tool un-
142   learning, preserving semantics indicates maintained knowledge on Tf , which makes unlearning a
143   failure. Figure 1b shows successful tool unlearning, where the ability to use the API is forgotten,
144   despite the high lexical memorization between output of the unlearned model and the training data.
145
      In addition, selectively removing knowledge from tool-augmented models is a challenging tasks be-
      cause changes to one tool may unexpectedly affect the model’s ability to use other tools–referred to
146
      as ripple effect in fact editing literature (Cohen et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024). Furthermore, LLMs are
147
      general models that can conduct a wide range of tasks beyond tool using, and this ability must be
148   retained. Evaluation: metrics like extraction probability and perplexity are standard in sample-level
149   unlearning. For tool unlearning, success is measured by the ability to forget or retain tool-related
150   skills, which are more appropriate. Data: sample-level unlearning require access to all individual
151   samples marked for unlearning, while tool unlearning does not. This aligns with “concept erasure”
152   in diffusion models (Gandikota et al., 2023b; Kumari et al., 2023) and zero-shot unlearning (Chun-
153   dawat et al., 2023b) but differs from traditional LLM unlearning (Yao et al., 2024).
154
155
      Retraining: An Impractical Solution A straightforward solution is to delete Df from D and
156   retrain a new model only on Dr . However, this is often infeasible due to the high cost and potential
157   unavailability of the original training data (Zhang et al., 2023; Ilharco et al., 2023; Gandikota et al.,
158   2023a). In addition, unlearning should not be evaluated solely based on similarity to retraining as
159   the potential solution space is highly complex and multidimensional. Specifically, prior work has
160   shown that relying on similarity to retraining has several drawbacks, such as poor auditability (Thudi
161   et al., 2022) and ineffective deletion (Cheng et al., 2023; Cheng & Amiri, 2023). Therefore there is
      a need for designing specialized and efficient unlearning methods for tool-augmented models.

      4        T OOL D ELETE
184
185
      We propose to develop, T OOL D ELETE, an effective tool unlearning approach that removes the ca-
186   pability of using tools marked for unlearning (Tf ) or solving tasks that depend on them, while
187   minimizing the impact on the ability of using the remaining tools (Tr ) and general tasks such as text
188   and code generation. T OOL D ELETE implements three key properties for effective tool unlearning:
189
190   4.1         T OOL K NOWLEDGE R EMOVAL
191
192   The knowledge of model f on Tf is obtained through tool learning. After unlearning, any knowledge
193   on Tf gained during tool learning should be removed, ideally as if Tf was never part of the training
194   set. In other words, the knowledge of f ′ on Tf should be no more than the knowledge of f0 on Tf ,
195   such that all previously gained knowledge from tool learning on Tf is successfully removed.
196   Definition 1 (Tool Knowledge Removal). Let ti ∈ Tf denote a tool to be unlearned, and let g be
197   a function that measures the amount of knowledge a model has on a tool. The unlearned model f ′
198   satisfies Tool Knowledge Removal if:
199                                                                                                                 ′
                                                                             E [g(f0 , ti ) − g(f , ti )] ≥ 0.                                                                                           (1)
200                                                                      ti ∈Tf
201
202   This formulation allows users to control the extent of knowledge removal from f ′ . For instance,
203   when we unlearn a “malicious” tool that calls a malignant program, we may require f ′ retains no
204   knowledge of this tool, i.e. g(f ′ , ti ) = 0. In less critical cases, users can choose to reset f ′ ’s
205   knowledge to pre-tool augmentation level, i.e. g(f ′ , ti ) = g(f0 , ti )
206
      To measure tool knowledge in LLMs, we follow previous works that used prompting to probe LLMs’
207   knowledge (Brown et al., 2020; Singhal et al., 2023), i.e. adopting the output of LLMs as their
208   knowledge on a given tool. For each ti ∈ Tf and its associated demonstrations {Qi , Yi }, we query
209   the vanilla model f0 with Qi and collect its responses Yi′ = f0 (Qi ). Since f0 has never seen ti
210   or {Qi , Yi }, Yi′ represents the tool-free response. We then encourage the unlearned model f ′ to
211   generate similar responses as Yi′ to prevent it from retaining any knowledge of ti .
212
213   4.2         T OOL K NOWLEDGE R ETENTION
214
215   The unlearning process should preservemodel’s knowledge of tools in Tr . Ideally, all knowledge
      gained on Tr during tool learning should be retained after unlearning.

226   4.3   TASK A RITHMETIC FOR P RESERVING G ENERAL U TILITY
227
228   Optimizing the above two objectives may lead to effective unlearning, but it may not be sufficient to
229   retain the general capabilities of f ′ , as LLMs are foundation models and are expected to maintain
230   general capabilities such as text generation, question answering, instruction-following, math, and
231
      coding. These capabilities refer to skills f0 originally had prior to tool augmentation or those that
      don’t rely on tools. Therefore, we aim to preserve the general capabilities of f ′ for successful tool
232
      unlearning in tool-augmented LLMs.
233
234
      Definition 3 (General Capability Retention). Let TG denote general tasks used to evaluate LLMs.
      An unlearned model retains general capability if it preserves the knowledge on TG that it originally
235
      obtained prior to tool learning:
236

237                                         E [g(f0 , tg ) − g(f , tg )] = ϵ,                                    (3)
                                          tg ∈TG
238
239   where ϵ is an infinitesimal constant.
240
      We propose to use task arithmetic to preserve the general capabilities of f ′ , as it is simple, effi-
241
      cient and effective. The objective is to encourage f ′ to retain as much general knowledge as f0 ,
242   an instruction tuned LLM trained from a randomly initialized model fR . Let θ0 and θR denote the
243   parameters of f0 and fR respectively. The vector θ0 − θR represents the direction of general knowl-
244   edge acquisition (Ilharco et al., 2023; Barbulescu & Triantafillou, 2024), which we apply to θ′ –the
245   parameters of f ′ :
246                                           θ′∗ ← θ′ + (θ0 − θR ).                                    (4)
247
248   Why Task Arithmetic? Task arithmetic is efficient, practical, and effective. Efficiency: task
249   arithmetic is a simple vector operation that does not scale with dataset size, which makes it more
250   efficient than retraining on large datasets. Practicality: general capabilities include knowledge
251   obtained during pre-training and instruction tuning (Zhou et al., 2024), which may be impractical
252
      to replicate due to the size and data availability–the actual pre-training data is often not fully open-
      source or pre-processed, even in some open-source LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023b). In addition, any
253
      data imbalance and ill-representation can introduce other problems. Effectiveness: applying θ0 −θR
254
      directly restores the foundational abilities of f ′ , such as text generation and instruction-following,
255   without requiring expensive and time-consuming retraining on large datasets.

276   4.5   L I RA-T OOL FOR T OOL U NLEARNING E VALUATION
277
278   A key challenge in evaluating tool unlearning is lack of membership inference attack (MIA) models
279   to infer if a tool was used during tool learning. Traditional MIA approaches focus on determining
280   if a specific training sample is in training set, not abstract concepts like tools. We propose to adapt
281
      the state-of-the-art MIA approach, Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA) (Carlini et al., 2022), to tool
      unlearning settings.
282
283
      Traditional Sample-level LiRA To infer the membership of a sample (x, y), LiRA constructs
284
285
      two distributions of model losses: Q̃in and Q̃out with (x, y) in and out of the model training set
      respectively. These distributions are approximated as Gaussians whose parameters are estimated
286
      based on “shadow models” trained on different subsets of the training data. Intuitively, LiRA queries
287
      the loss of (x, y) to determine if (x, y) is more likely to be from Q̃in or Q̃out , where membership is
288
      decided by the Likelihood-ratio Test (Vuong, 1989; Carlini et al., 2022). For LLMs, the test statistic
289   is defined by (Pawelczyk et al., 2024) as:
290
291                              P (l(f (x), y)|Q̃in )    Π(xi ,yi )∈Df PU (l(f ′ (xi ), yi ))
                           Λ=                           =                                      .            (6)
292                              P (l(f (x), y)|Q̃out )   Π(xi ,yi )∈Df PTr (l(f (xi ), yi ))

294   LiRA-Tool (Knowledge-level LiRA) The major difficulty in adapting LiRA to tool unlearning is
295   in approximating the distributions of losses Q̃in and Q̃out for tools, rather than individual training
296   samples. Simply using the observed data related to a tool in the training set may overfit to specific
297   distribution of observations, and may fail to comprehensively approximate the distribution of the
298   target tool marked for unlearning. We propose to obtain a “shadow distribution” P to generate tool
299   learning samples. We then sample a series of “shadow” data that evaluates the tool using the ability
300   to compute loss and test statistic as follows:
301                                         Πti ∈Tf Π(x,y)∈Pti PU (l(f ′ (x), y))
302                                  Λ=                                             ,                       (7)
                                            Πtj ∈Tr Π(x,y)∈Ptj PTr (l(f (x), y))
303
304   where Pti is the distribution that controls the generation of tool learning samples for ti .
305
306   The major difference is that traditional LiRA approximates Q̃in and Q̃out with a series of shadow
307   models by controlling which samples are present in training set. In LiRA-Tool, however, unlearning
308
      a skill (tool) is prioritized by sampling “shadow” data related to a specific tool to ensure that the
      losses reflect tool-using abilities, not just membership of a specific training sample. In practice,
309
      we prompt GPT-4 with various distinct instructions to draw “shadow samples” for approximating
310
      Q̃in and Q̃out and performing likelihood-ratio test. The proposed formulation share similarities to
311
      previous MIA for sample-level unlearning, such as the ratio of existence probability distribution
312   prior- and post-unlearning (Cheng et al., 2023; Cheng & Amiri, 2023), and other adaptations of
313   LiRA which performs likelihood-ratio test over shadow models (Kurmanji et al., 2023; Pawelczyk
314   et al., 2024). But this work, to the best of our knowledge, is the first adaptation of LiRA to detect
315   tool presence in tool learning datasets for LLMs.
316
317   Novelty We adapt sample-level MIA into knowledge-level MIA to infer the membership of tools
318   for tool unlearning evaluation; and propose a new method to estimate Q̃in and Q̃out . This provides a
319   comprehensive approximation of abstract concepts beyond observed training data.
320
321   Limitations Shadow samples from GPT-4 may not fully represent the complexity of original tool-
322   learning data to capture the tool-related knowledge. Although this can lead to incomplete approxi-
323   mations of the knowledge distributions, LiRA-Tool is still a fair evaluation approach because shadow
      samples provide a more consistent basis for evaluating changes in tool-using abilities of models than

      Setup & Evaluation We use the public checkpoints of the above tool-augmented LLMs as trained
343   models–the starting point for unlearning. Then we conduct unlearning experiments with 2–20%
344   tools randomly selected as Tf

346
      We evaluate tool unlearning effectiveness, general capability of tool-unlearned LLMs, and robust-
      ness to membership inference attack (MIA). For unlearning effectiveness, we measure performance
347
      on test sets (TT , ↑), forget set (Tf , ↓), and remaining set (Tr , ↑), where “performance” reflects the
348
      ability to solve tasks that depend on specific tools, depending on the unique metrics in the original
349   tool-augmented models f . To evaluate general capabilities, we evaluate the unlearned LLMs on a
350   wide range of tasks: college STEM knowledge with MMLU dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021), rea-
351   soning ability with BBH-Hard (Suzgun et al., 2023), instruction-following with IFEval dataset (Zhou
352   et al., 2023), and factual knowledge with MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021). To evaluate robustness
353   to MIA using the proposed LiRA-Tool. Following prior work on LiRA (Pawelczyk et al., 2024),
354   we train the shadow models with forget set size of {1, 5, 10, 20} and primarily investigate the True
355   Positive Rate (TPR) at low False Positive Rate (FPR) (TPR @ FPR = 0.01), where TPR means the
356   attacker successfully detects a tools is present. Therefore, a lower TPR indicates better privacy.
357
358   Baselines As there are no prior works on tool unlearning, we adapt the following unlearning meth-
359
      ods to tool unlearning setting. Four general unlearning approaches: G RADA SCENT (Golatkar et al.,
      2020; Yao et al., 2024), which runs gradient ascent on Tf ; R AND L ABEL (Graves et al., 2021), which
360
      fine-tunes on Tr and Tf with corrupted labels; S AL U N (Fan et al., 2024b), which performs R AND -
361
      L ABEL on unlearning-related parameters discovered by saliency map. In addition, we include three
362   LLM-specific unlearning approaches: ICUL (Pawelczyk et al., 2024), which uses Tf with corrupted
363   label as in-context demonstrations, SGA (Jang et al., 2023; Barbulescu & Triantafillou, 2024), which
364   performs gradient ascent on Tf whose memorization probability exceeds a pre-defined threshold,
365   and TAU (Barbulescu & Triantafillou, 2024), which performs task arithmetic on SGA. For ICUL,
366   we randomly select one example (qi , yi ) from Tf and corrupt the output yi with randomly selected
367   tokens. Then we concatenate this corrupted sequence with other intact sequences as the in-context
368   demonstrations. For all other baselines, we treat all data related to Tf as unlearning examples and
369   all data related to Tr as remaining examples. Everything else remains the same for each baseline.

      See §3 for our discussion on why sample-level unlearning methods are inadequate for effective tool
      unlearning.

500   7             C ONCLUSION
501
502   We propose Tool Unlearning–a novel machine unlearning task with the goal of unlearning previ-
503   ously learned tools from tool-augmented LLMs. We highlighted the importance of tool unlearning
504   through practical use cases, while also showing why existing unlearning methods are insufficient
505   in this contexts. To systematically address the problem, we develop an effective tool unlearning
506   approach, T OOL D ELETE, that enforces three key properties: tool knowledge removal for removing
507
      any knowledge gained on tools marked for unlearning; tool knowledge retention for preserving the
      knowledge gained on other remaining tools; and general utility preservation for maintaining LLM’s
508
      capability on a range of general tasks like text and code generation. Through extensive experi-
509
      ments conducted on three diverse datasets and with LLMs of two different scales, we demonstrate
510   the effectiveness and efficiency of T OOL D ELETE, compared to commonly used general and LLM-
511   specific unlearning approaches. Our results show that T OOL D ELETE achieves superior performance
512   by successfully forgetting the tools marked for unlearning.
513
514   Limitations and Future Work In this study, we did not conduct experiments using closed-source
515   LLMs or API-based LLMs. Consequently, our findings may not directly extend to such proprietary
516   models, and further research is needed to investigate the applicability of tool unlearning techniques
517   in these contexts. In addition, this work did not investigate the impact of varying model scales due
518   to the limited publicly-available tool-augmented LLMs. Our experiments were conducted on the 7B
519   scale and the scalability of the proposed tool unlearning approach across models of different sizes
520
      and scales is an open question for future investigation.
521
522   B ROADER I MPACT S TATEMENT
523
524   Our work investigates the security implications of tool-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs),
525   where we focus on the risks that arise from integrating external tools, and the necessity ability to
526   remove these acquired tools. A key concern is ensuring compliance with privacy regulations, such
527   as the Right to be Forgotten (RTBF), which mandates the removal of specific data upon user request.
528   In the context of tool-augmented LLMs, this necessitates the ability to delete sensitive, regulated,
529
      or outdated information related to specific tools. By examining how LLMs interact with and rely
      on external tools, potential threats to model security can be identified, e.g. unauthorized tool usage,
530
      adversarial exploitation, and privacy violations. Our research highlights the critical importance of
531
      addressing these challenges.
