011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are currently the leading cause of death worldwide,
014            highlighting the critical need for early diagnosis and treatment. Machine learning
015            (ML) methods can help diagnose CVDs early, but their performance relies on
016
               access to substantial data with high quality. However, the sensitive nature of
               healthcare data often restricts individual clinical institutions from sharing data to
017
               train sufficiently generalized and unbiased ML models. Federated Learning (FL) is
018
               an emerging approach, which offers a promising solution by enabling collaborative
019            model training across multiple participants without compromising the privacy of
020            the individual data owners. However, to the best of our knowledge, there has
021            been limited prior research applying FL to the cardiovascular disease domain.
022            Moreover, existing FL benchmarks and datasets are typically simulated and may
023            fall short of replicating the complexity of natural heterogeneity found in realistic
024            datasets that challenges current FL algorithms. To address these gaps, this paper
025            presents the first real-world FL benchmark for cardiovascular disease detection,
026            named FedCVD. This benchmark comprises two major tasks: electrocardiogram
027
               (ECG) classification and echocardiogram (ECHO) segmentation, based on naturally
               scattered publicly available datasets constructed from the CVD data of seven
028
               institutions. Our extensive experiments on these datasets reveal that FL faces new
029
               challenges with real-world non-IID and long-tail data.

034
      Cardiovascular Diseases (CVDs) cause over 18 million deaths globally each year, positioning them as
035
      one of the most significant global health challenges (Donkada et al., 2023). Early detection and diag-
036   nosis of CVDs are crucial, as they allow for timely medical interventions and more effective treatment
037   plans, which in turn significantly lower patient mortality rates (Aversano et al., 2022). Recently, with
038   the growing availability of electronic health records and other high-quality clinical data, researchers
039   have increasingly utilized machine learning techniques to automate clinical diagnostics (Yan et al.,
040   2020; Chen, 2020), a strategy that has proven highly effective in CVDs (Alizadehsani et al., 2019;
041   Al’Aref et al., 2019). This data-driven approach can facilitate efficient early screening and optimize
042   the allocation of healthcare resources, improving overall patient outcomes.
043   However, medical studies usually face the issue of bias, that is, the data distribution is restricted
044   by factors such as geography, and may even lead to discriminatory outputs. Therefore, multi-
045   center collaboration is required, as it enables the utilization of richer regional and demographic
046   characteristics, fostering more precise and comprehensive research outcomes. However, medical
047   data is considered highly sensitive, and recent privacy regulations (e.g., EU General Data Protection
048   Regulation (GDPR) (noa, 2016)) restrict its transfer, hindering the expansion of datasets through data
049   sharing among institutions to train more efficient models, i.e., data isolation.
050   To address this issue, federated learning (FL) (Yang et al., 2019; McMahan et al., 2017) has been
051   proposed as a more secure paradigm of distributed machine learning. A typical FL architecture
052   involves a coordinator (Server) and several participants (Clients) with private data. By aggregating
053   (e.g., FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017)) the model parameters or gradients from different Clients on
      the Server, participants collaboratively train high-performance models keeping private data within

080   In medical applications such as CVD, integrating FL enables medical institutions to harness larger and
081
      more diverse datasets, collaboratively training models that are more unbiased and generalized, thereby
      enhancing diagnostic accuracy and clinical decision-making in real-world settings. For instance,
082
      Yang et al. (2021) applied FL for joint case analysis across three institutions during the Covid-19
083
      pandemic, significantly improving CT segmentation performance and facilitating more accurate
084   detection of Covid-19. Additionally, the effectiveness of FL has been demonstrated in multi-center
085   research, such as a study involving three centers focused on the medical image analysis task of whole
086   prostate segmentation (Sarma et al., 2021), further underscoring its relevance in realistic, large-scale
087   medical scenarios.
088
      Facilitating the application of FL in multi-center medical research, particularly in areas such as CVD,
089
      necessitates the creation of appropriate datasets and benchmarks to support the development of robust
090   algorithms. However, publicly available cardiovascular disease datasets are limited, and those that do
091   exist often suffer from incompatibility due to variations in data collection protocols. Furthermore,
092   there is currently no comprehensive, publicly accessible benchmark specifically designed for FL on
093   CVD data, which significantly impedes research progress in this domain. Additionally, most existing
094   FL benchmarks simulate an FL scenario by manually partitioning data—often without considering
095   geographic distribution—into smaller subsets, resulting in an overly idealized model that fails to
096   capture the complexities and heterogeneity of real-world, multi-center CVD scenarios. This gap
097   presents substantial challenges for the development and validation of effective FL algorithms in
098
      practical medical applications.
099   To address these gaps, we introduce the first multi-center FL benchmark specifically designed for
100   CVD tasks, named FedCVD. Built from real-world CVD data from seven medical institutions (i.e.,
101   clients, the two terms will be used interchangeably), FedCVD utilizes a natural partitioning strategy.
102   It comprises two primary datasets along with their corresponding tasks: electrocardiogram (ECG)
103   classification and echocardiogram (ECHO) segmentation. FedCVD encapsulates three critical traits
104
      of FL in real-world CVD applications, each of which presents substantial challenges to FL algorithms:
105   Challenging Trait 1. Non-IID Data: The Non-independently and identically distributed (non-IID)
106   data among institutions, including non-IID feature (e.g., variations in imaging quality due to different
107   equipment across institutions) and non-IID label (e.g., differences in disease prevalence across
      regions). The non-IID data may significantly hindering global model convergence.

129   designing FL algorithms in multi-center CVD scenarios. Our contributions are summarized as
130   follows:
131
132           1. We introduce FedCVD, an open-source federated multi-center healthcare dataset and bench-
133              mark specifically for the CVD domain. To the best of our knowledge, FedCVD is the largest
134
                 multi-center CVD benchmark available. This dataset encompasses two critical tasks—multi-

135

137           2. Our benchmark emphasizes three critical traits in the FL-CVD scenario: non-IID, long tail,

147
148   AI for CVD Research. Numerous studies have leveraged CVD data for disease detection and
149   diagnostic support, focusing primarily on ECG and ECHO data. ECG, recorded as time-series signals,
150   captures the heart’s electrical activity and provides insights into cardiac conditions and potential
151   damage (Donkada et al., 2023). These studies often frame ECG-based tasks as classification problems
152   for disease diagnosis and heart metric analysis (Thanapatay et al., 2010; Muirhead & Puff, 2004;
153
      Christov et al., 2005; Behadada & Chikh, 2013; Zhang et al., 2021). ECHO, comprising ultrasound
      images, enables real-time visualization of heart chambers and blood flow, aiding in diagnoses of
154
      conditions like heart valve disorders and Congestive Heart Failure (CHF). For instance, Goto et al.
155
      (2022) used ECHO data for Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy detection, while Ostvik et al. (2019)
156   applied Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for standard view classification to enhance clinical
157   efficiency. Automated ECHO segmentation, crucial for assessing heart morphology and diagnosing
158   conditions such as myocardial infarction, addresses the limitations of manual segmentation, which is
159   time-consuming and subjective. Studies have employed AI models for ventricular (Zhang et al., 2014;
160   De Alexandria et al., 2014; Qin et al., 2013; Kiranyaz et al., 2020b) and atrial segmentation (Haak
161   et al., 2015b;a). Although these works highlight the potential of AI in analyzing CVD data, they are
      predominantly restricted to single-institution settings.

162
      FL for Multi-center CVD Research. CVD research necessitates multi-center collaboration, and
163   FL presents a promising solution. Most current studies simulate FL in multi-institution collaborative
164   training by manually partitioning data from a single institution. For instance, Sakib et al. (2021)
165   trained a classification model to detect cardiac arrhythmia using ECG data within a federated
166   architecture, partitioning data from the MIT-BIH Supraventricular Arrhythmia database (Greenwald
167   et al., 1990). Similarly, Zou et al. (2023) investigated congestive heart failure detection in a federated
168   setting by splitting samples from the NSR-RR-interval and CHF-RR-interval databases (Goldberger
169   et al., 2000) into 2 to 4 clients for simulated training. FedCluster (Lin et al., 2022) tackled the issue
170   of unbalanced class distributions in ECG data by optimizing algorithms that cluster local parameters
171
      before performing intra- and inter-class aggregation, thus increasing the weight of minority classes.
      Their data were also partitioned from the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia database (Goldberger et al., 2000).
172
      However, these partition-based simulations may not fully capture the true distribution characteristics
173
      of CVDs. In contrast, FLHCD (Goto et al., 2022) demonstrated federated training for hypertrophic
174   cardiomyopathy detection using ECG and ECHO data from four medical institutions (three in the US
175   and one in Japan), showcasing the effectiveness of FL in a naturally partitioned, multi-center setting.
176
177   FL Benchmarks. To further support research in FL, numerous datasets and benchmarks have
178   been proposed for a wide range of applications. A comprehensive comparison of FedCVD with
179   these benchmarks is shown in Table 1. Existing studies often manually partition centralized datasets
180   and introduce perturbations or masking to features and labels to mimic the heterogeneity found in
181   real-world FL scenarios. For instance, FedTD (Lindskog & Prehofer, 2023) and FedDTI (Mittone
182   et al., 2023) simulate non-iid data partitioning by altering feature distributions and sample sizes.
183   Since manual data is incapable of capturing real-world challenges, real-world multi-institution
184   benchmarks are essential. Several FL benchmarks directly utilize real-world multi-institution data.
185   For instance, NIPD (Yin et al., 2023) employs data from cameras in different geographical locations
186   as FL clients for person detection tasks, naturally exhibiting non-iid characteristics. Similarly,
187   FEDLEGAL (Zhang et al., 2023c) provides a FL benchmark for NLP tasks in the legal domain,
188   using geographically distributed case-based text data for natural data partitioning. Another example
189   is FLamby (du Terrail et al., 2022), an FL benchmark for real-world distributed medical data,
190   offering seven datasets naturally distributed by geography or institution, with corresponding tasks
191
      including segmentation and binary/multiclass classification for medical image analysis and diagnostic
      assistance. Some benchmarks combine natural partitioning with simulated partitioning. For instance,
192
      FedAudio (Zhang et al., 2023b) applies simulated partitioning for certain audio data, while introducing
193
      perturbations to mimic noisy data and labels. FedMultimodal (Feng et al., 2023) uses a Dirichlet
194   distribution to partition multimodal data from various domains, incorporating missing modalities,
195   labels, and erroneous labels to simulate real-world heterogeneity. Despite these advances, none of
196   these benchmarks cover the CVD domain. Although FLHCD (Goto et al., 2022), which utilizes
197   multi-institution data for hypertrophic cardiomyopathy detection, has a setup most similar to ours, it
198   does not address challenges such as the long-tail distribution and incomplete label issues, which are
199   specifically tackled by FedCVD.

267   3.2               C HALLENGING T RAITS OF F ED CVD
268
269   Non-IID. Non-independently and identically distributed (non-IID) is a typical characteristic in FL
      scenarios, encompassing non-IID features and non-IID labels, where clients’ data shows heterogeneity


                                                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      in both feature and label spaces. Quantity imbalance, where institutions hold uneven sample sizes,
271   can further exacerbate these non-IID issues. Among these, non-IID labels have the most pronounced
272   impact on FL model performance. This is because the quantity and types of labels held by each
273   institution can vary greatly, misleading the local supervised training process and causing ”Client
274   Drift” (Karimireddy et al., 2020), which hinders global model convergence.
275
      Fed-ECG naturally exhibits these three characteristics. In terms of feature distribution, Figure 2(a)
276
      shows significant age distribution differences among institutions’ patients, with Institution 1 notably
277   younger, reflected in the ECG features. Regarding sample size, Figure 2(b) depicts significant
278   differences among the four institutions, with Institution 4 having the fewest samples. For label
279   distribution in the Fed-ECG multi-label classification task, each sample may belong to multiple
280   categories, but the quantity and proportion of different labels vary significantly among institutions.
281   For example, the most common label for Institution 1 and Institution 2 is NORM (Normal), while
282   for Institution 3 and Institution 4 it is STACH (Sinus tachycardia). Some institutions may even lack
283   samples with certain labels, such as both Institution 3 and Institution 4 lacking samples labeled as
284   PAC (Atrial premature complex(es)). These non-IID characteristics challenge the four institutions in
285
      collaboratively training a multi-label prediction model, as institutions struggle to capture information
      about the labels they lack during local training, potentially leading to client drift.
286
287
288   Long-tail Distribution. In addition to the inter-institution heterogeneity caused by non-IID labels,
289   Fed-ECG also exhibits intra-institution and inter-institution heterogeneity in the form of a long-tail
290   distribution of labels. Figure 2(b) illustrates a clear long-tail characteristic of each institution’s
291   internal label distribution, with a few dominant labels having many samples and numerous labels
292
      having fewer samples (long-tail). These tail categories are already troublesome during independent
      local training, as the model may neglect the tail categories. In FL scenarios with quantity imbalance
293
      and non-IID labels, the long-tail problem is further exacerbated. For instance, categories mainly
294
      found in the disadvantaged institutions’ tails may be in an even worse position within the overall data
295   of all institutions. The long-tail characteristic challenges FL algorithms in ensuring the effectiveness
296   and fairness of handling samples from various categories across institutions.
297
298
299
      Label Incompleteness. Fed-ECHO presents the most challenging scenario: label-incomplete FL.
      In Fed-ECHO, three naturally formed institutions hold ECHO video data with annotations (image
300
      region segmentation). However, due to varying annotation capabilities, the completeness of labels
301
      among the three institutions differs, as shown in Figure 1. Institution 1 has the most complete labels
302   (four labels) due to its ability to identify and annotate all four key regions (including the background).
303   In contrast, Institution 2 and Institution 3 each have labels for only one key region (LVEndo and LVEpi ,
304   respectively). This incompleteness introduces (1) label heterogeneity, similar to the label-non-IID
305   in Fed-ECG, where Institution 2 and Institution 3 lack some labels, and (2) mislabeling, where
306   Institution 2 and Institution 3 label unrecognized parts as background, conflicting with Institution 1’s
307   labels and causing misleading information. This scenario significantly challenges FL algorithms to
308   effectively utilize the different levels of label completeness from each Institution and leverage highly
309   heterogeneous data to benefit the global model.

311   3.3   TASKS & M ETRICS
312
313   Fed-ECG. The corresponding task on Fed-ECG’s four datasets involves multi-label classification
314   for each institution, a challenging problem due to the large number of labels and the long-tail
315   distribution inherent to the data. To provide a more fine-grained evaluation, we focus on detailed
316   label distinctions, which are of particular interest to clinicians, rather than broader label categories.
317   To thoroughly assess performance, we adopt two metrics: micro-F1, which evaluates the overall
318   performance across all labels, and mean average precision (mAP), which specifically measures the
319   impact of the long-tail distribution on model performance.
320   To further illustrate the long-tail distribution challenge posed by Fed-ECG, we introduced two
321   additional metrics, namely F1-STD and Top-K. The F1-STD metric measures the standard deviation
322   of F1 scores across classes, reflecting the learning algorithm’s ability to manage long-tail problems;
323   the larger the F1-STD, the poorer the algorithm’s performance in this regard. Top-K, on the other
      hand, refers to selecting the K classes with the most samples and the K classes with the fewest samples,


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      calculating the average F1 score for each group, and then computing the relative performance drop
325   between them. A larger performance drop indicates a more severe long-tail problem.
326
327   Fed-ECHO. The common task across Fed-ECHO’s three datasets is the automatic segmentation of
328   cardiac structures in echocardiograms, a crucial step in further diagnosing cardiovascular diseases.
329   This task is particularly challenging due to the varying quality of the original echocardiograms across
330   datasets. To evaluate segmentation accuracy, we use both the Dice similarity index (DICE) and the 2D
331   Hausdorff distance (dH ). The Dice index measures the overlap between the predicted segmentation
332   and the ground truth, while the dH quantifies the local maximum distance between the two areas.

336   4.1   E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
337
338   Baseline Algorithms. Our experiments utilize seven typical FL algorithms across both datasets.
339   The first four are classical global FL algorithms: FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), the oft-cited
340   FL algorithm, collaboratively trains a global model across participants. FedProx (Li et al., 2020)
341   addresses statistical heterogeneity in FL by introducing an L2 proximal term during local training,
342
      while Scaffold (Karimireddy et al., 2020) mitigates client drift through control variates and server-
      side learning rate adjustments. FedInit (Sun et al., 2023) also tackles client drift by employing
343
      a personalized, relaxed initialization at the start of each local training stage. The last three are
344
      personalized FL methods: Ditto (Li et al., 2021), which excels in balancing accuracy, fairness,
345   and robustness in FL; FedSM (Xu et al., 2022), which combines model selection with personalized
346   methods to avoid client drift; and FedALA (Zhang et al., 2023a), which reduces the impact of statistical
347   heterogeneity by adaptively aggregating both the global and local models. For the Fed-ECHO dataset,
348   we further evaluate two Federated Semi-Supervised Learning (FSSL) methods: Fed-Consist (Yang
349   et al., 2021), which uses a consistency-based method for segmentation, and FedPSL (Dong et al.,
350   2023), which applies separate model aggregation and meta-learning techniques for classification. In
351   addition to the FL family, we include two other baseline algorithms: Client, which refers to training
352   models using only local data without collaboration among participants, and Central., which represents
353
      the ideal centralized training scenario where the server has access to all participants’ data.
354
      Setup. We took into account the models that are widely used within the field as the default
355
      implementations of the models in the experiments. For Fed-ECG, a residual network, with its
356
      implementation following that of Strodthoff et al. (2020), was adopted as the default model. For
357   Fed-ECHO, we utilized a 2D U-net model, following the implementation from Ronneberger et al.
358   (2015). The number of institutions involved in federated training for each task is listed in Appendix D.
359   Our experiments mainly focus on the multi-center FL scenario (i.e., cross-silo), where all institutions
360   participate in training at each communication round. Considering the trade-off between computation
361   and communication, we set the local training epoch to 1 and the communication rounds to 50
362   throughout experiments except Fed-Consist. Since Fed-Consist requires extra rounds for training on
363   clients with full labels before starting federated learning, we set the communication rounds to 100,
364   where 50 rounds are for labeled clients training and another 50 rounds are normal FL training.
365
366   Evaluation Strategies. For a comprehensive evaluation, we build a local and global evaluation
367
      set for both datasets in FedCVD. For the local one, we divide each local data into train/test sets by
      8:2. For the global one, we collect each local test set together. Our experiments test all algorithms
368
      using two evaluation strategies: 1) Global test performance (GLOBAL) is evaluated on the global
369
      test set and used to determine whether the model has learned knowledge from other clients in the FL
370   setting. The better results of GLOBAL indicate that the model is closer to the centralized training. 2)
371   Local test performance (LOCAL) is evaluated on each local test set. The LOCAL is more practical in
372   real-world applications than GLOBAL because it indicates performance improvement for its task
373   without centralizing all local data.

460      4.3                    B ENCHMARK ON F ED -ECHO
461
462      The proposed Fed-ECHO dataset presents one of the most challenging FL settings: label incom-
463      pleteness, which can be viewed as an enhanced version of label-non-IID. Specifically, all annotated
464      video frames from Institution 1 are completely segmented into four regions: BG, LVEndo , LVEpi , and
465
         LA. In contrast, Institution 2 and Institution 3 can only recognize the LVEpi and LVEndo regions in
         their annotated video frames, respectively, with the remaining regions simply labeled as ”BG.” For
466
         convenience, we refer to the BG labels from Institution 2 and Institution 3 as ”Maybe-BG,” indicating
467
         these segmentations may be unreliable. This discrepancy introduces potential conflicts between
468      the ”Maybe-BG” labels of Institution 2 and Institution 3 and the corresponding ”reliable” labels of
469      Institution 1, resulting in misleading labels that affect model convergence.
470
471
         To mitigate the impact of misleading labels, we propose a straightforward baseline strategy, supervised-
         only. During supervised model training, we input the data from all three Institutions into the model
472
         without additional processing, allowing the model to benefit from the rich data features. However,
473
         when calculating the loss, we mask out the ”Maybe-BG” regions in the video frames from Institution
474      2 and Institution 3. This means that for samples from Institution 2 and Institution 3, we only compute
475      the training loss on the ”reliable foreground”. This strategy ensures the model learns segmentation
476      capabilities from completely reliable labels. Additionally, during model segmentation performance
477      evaluation, we also exclude the ”Maybe-BG” regions from the test samples, preventing them from
478      influencing the model’s performance metrics.
479

480      Fed-ECHO, evaluated using Dice and Hausdorff distance (dH ). Except for the semi-supervised learn-

502
      and Scaffold, consistently demonstrate significant advantages over simpler algorithms like FedAvg.
503
      Notably, these algorithms also outperform the Centralized (sup) model, which we attribute to FL
504   effectively mitigating the impact of intra-batch heterogeneity(e.g., within the same batch, there are
505   four-label data from Institution 1 and single-label data from Institution 2 or Institution 3).
506
507
      Additionally, to leverage the substantial amount of partially labeled data from Institution 2 and
      Institution 3 and potentially mitigate label heterogeneity, we introduced semi-supervised learning
508
      algorithms for comparison. These include the centralized semi-supervised model, Centralized (ssup),
509
      and federated semi-supervised algorithms such as Fed-Consist and FedPSL. The Centralized (ssup)
510   model significantly outperformed its fully supervised counterpart, underscoring the value of utilizing
511   unlabeled video frames. Similarly, Fed-Consist outperformed FedAvg and FedProx, although it still
512   exhibited a noticeable performance gap compared to the centralized semi-supervised algorithm and
513   lagged behind fully supervised FL algorithms like Scaffold and FedInit. While FedPSL performed
514   well on certain participating client, it showed greater instability overall, largely due to its sensitivity
515   to client-side feature heterogeneity.
516   Therefore, the highly heterogeneous Fed-ECHO scenario poses significant challenges for FL algo-
517   rithms, requiring them to adapt to heterogeneous data and effectively leverage unlabeled data across
518   different data domains.

      5    C ONCLUSION
522
      This paper has introduced FedCVD, the first real-world multi-center FL benchmark for CVD data,
523
      which consists of two datasets and their respective tasks: Fed-ECG and Fed-ECHO. It presents three
524   major challenges due to the heterogeneous distribution of real-world data: non-IID, long-tailed labels,
525   and label incompleteness. We conducted extensive comparative and validation experiments, testing
526   mainstream FL algorithms and centralized training on these tasks. Experimental results show that the
527   natural non-IID characteristics in FedCVD are more challenging than the manually partitioned setups
528   in most previous federated benchmarks, and mainstream algorithms perform poorly in the long-tail
529   tests of FedCVD. For the most difficult task, i.e., the label-incomplete Fed-ECHO, mainstream FL
530   algorithms barely maintain utility but are still better than non-cooperative algorithms that only utilize
531   unlabeled data on each client. Federated semi-supervised learning algorithms that leverage unlabeled
532
      data achieve some performance improvement. Beyond, as a flexible and extensible framework,
      FedCVD is meant to be a step towards developing FL in the CVD domain.
533
534   Limitations and Future Work. FedCVD presents a realistic and challenging scenario that tests FL
535   algorithms’ ability to mitigate data heterogeneity, handle long-tailed classes, and utilize unlabeled data.
536   However, FedCVD currently offers only two tasks and a limited variety of data types. Additionally,
537   the FL algorithms compared in experiments, particularly semi-supervised ones, are limited. In future
538
      work, we will expand the data range of FedCVD, aiming for it to inspire future FL research in
      real-world medical contexts, especially with CVD data.

      A     B ROADER I MPACT
811
812
      Considering that this research exclusively involves the repurposing of existing open-source databases,
813   the associated risks are limited. However, it is important to acknowledge that all datasets utilized
814   in this study may be influenced by biases inherent in the original data collection processes, such as
815   those related to gender, age, or race. Unfortunately, identifying the sources of potential biases is
816   challenging because the data have been appropriately pseudonymized. Moreover, records such as
817   electrocardiograms and echocardiograms cannot be easily linked to specific demographic attributes
818   such as age, ethnicity, or gender by non-medical experts. Nonetheless, our work discloses certain
819   metadata of the datasets, including geographical origin, gender distribution, and age distribution. This
820   exposure may aid in identifying underlying geographical biases, which are anticipated in real-world
821
      federated learning scenarios.
822   While prioritizing simplicity and utility, the current benchmark does not include privacy metrics.
823   Nevertheless, privacy remains critically important in the cardiovascular disease domain, and we
824   strongly encourage the research community to address these considerations. Thanks to the modularity
825   of FedCVD, we can add privacy components easily. Therefore, we anticipate that FedCVD will
826   address privacy concerns related to federated learning within the cardiovascular disease domain in
827
      the future.

850   C     D ESCRIPTION OF U SING A LGORITHMS
851
852   Semi-supervised: Follows a pseudo-labeling approach by Lee et al. (2013). Specifically, the model
853   was initially trained exclusively on the labeled data for 10 rounds. Subsequently, pseudo-labels were
854   generated for the unlabeled data and incorporated into the training process for an additional 40 rounds.
855   During this phase, we dynamically adjusted the weight of the loss function for the unlabeled data
856   using a parameter α, ensuring a gradual and adaptive integration of pseudo-labeled data into the
857   training pipeline.
858   FedAvg: Implements a simple weighted average of model parameters from all participating clients
859   during aggregation, without additional constraints.
860
      FedProx: Introduces a regularization term that penalizes the divergence between the local and global
861
      models during training, mitigating the challenges posed by non-IID data distributions.
862
863   Scaffold: Utilizes control variates and server-side learning rate adjustments to reduce the impact of
      non-IID client updates, enhancing convergence and stability.

885   process.

      geneity, improving training efficiency.

890   tions with incomplete labels, pseudo-labels are generated using the global model on both raw and
891   augmented data. Only instances with both predictions exceeding a confidence threshold are retained
892
      as final pseudo-labels, utilizing a consistency-based semi-supervised approach.
893   FedPSL: Splits the model into a task-agnostic feature extractor and a task-dependent classifier. The
894   feature extractor is aggregated across all clients using a FedAvg-like approach, while the classifier is
895   aggregated only among clients sharing the corresponding label.

901
902   Fed-ECG consists of four datasets: SPH, PTB-XL, SXPH, and G12EC. The order of leads of each
903   dataset is I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF, V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6. The overview of Fed-ECG is shown in
904   Table 6. Table 7 shows demographics information for four datasets in Fed-ECG.
905
906   SPH. The original Shandong Provincial Hospital (SPH) database contains 25,770 12-lead ECG
907   records from 24,666 patients, which were acquired from Shandong Provincial Hospital between
908   2019/08 and 2020/08. The record length is between 10 and 60 seconds. The sampling frequency
909   is 500 Hz. All ECG records are in full compliance with the AHA standard, which aims for the
910   standardization and interpretation of the electrocardiogram and consists of 44 primary statements
911   and 15 modifiers as per the standard. 46.04% records in this dataset contain ECG abnormalities.
912
      Moreover, 14.45% records have multiple diagnostic statements.
913
914   PTB-XL. The original PTB-XL database contains 21,837 12-lead ECG records from 18,885
915   patients of 10 seconds length at the Physikalisch Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB) between October
916   1989 and June 1996. The original records are resampled to both 100 Hz and 500 Hz. For consistency,
917   we only use the records whose frequency is 500 Hz. Each data is annotated by up to two cardiologists
      with the SCP-ECG standard.

919   under the auspices of Chapman University, Shaoxing People’s Hospital (Shaoxing Hospital Zhejiang

921   records are labeled by professional experts with the SNOMED-CT standard.

925   are open to the public. The record length is not longer than 10 seconds with a sample frequency of

1238   Baseline Model. A U-net architecture is employed in this study, utilizing echocardiographic images
1239   as input to forecast masks delineating four distinct cardiac regions. The U-net model represents a
1240   conventional convolutional neural network design frequently deployed in the realm of biomedical
1241   image segmentation endeavors. Its application is tailored towards semantic segmentation, a process
       wherein individual pixels within an image are categorized based on semantic content.

1245   not be true ground truth in these clients.

1269   Hyperparameter Search For centralized and local model training, we first explore learning rates
1270   from the set {1e-4, 1e-3, 1e-2, 1e-1.5, 1e-1} during centralized model training. The learning rate
1271   that achieves the best Dice index is then utilized for local model training. For the federated learning
1272   strategies, we employ the following hyperparameter grid:
1273
1274          • For clients’ learning rates (all strategies except Fed-Consist): {1e-4, 1e-3, 1e-2, 1e-1.5,
1275
                1e-1}.
1276          • For server size learning rate (Scaffold strategy only): {1e-2, 1e-1, 1.0}.
