                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Although the theory of deep neural networks has been studied for years, the mech-
               anism of pooling layers is still elusive. In this paper, we report the angle contrac-
011
               tion behavior of pooling strategies (the average pooling and max pooling) at ini-
012
               tialization. Compared to the relu-activated fully connected layer or convolutional
013            layer, the pooling layer stands as the main source of contraction of the angle be-
014            tween hidden features. Moreover, we show that the cosine similarity between
015            average pooling features in convolutional neural network is more data-dependent
016            than fully connected network, while the max pooling is not sensitive to the data
017            distribution in both architectures. Our results may complement the understanding
018            of the representation learning.

021   1     I NTRODUCTION
022
023   The idea of stacking many layers to make truly deep neural networks (DNNs) is what arguably
024   led to the neural net revolution in the 2010s. Indeed, from a function-space point of view, it is
025   known that depth exponentially improves expressivity Poole et al. (2016); Eldan & Shamir (2016).
026   However, a noteworthy fact is that under standard initialization deep neural networks become in-
027   creasingly degenerate as depth gets larger. One type of degeneracy is known as vanishing/exploding
028
      gradients He et al. (2015b); Hanin (2018). Another type of degeneracy is that a neural network
      contracts all features to restricted directions, and we call it angle contraction in this paper. Cho et
029
      al. were the first to compute the relu-activated fully-connected

043   1.1   C ONTRIBUTION
044
045
      The relu activation is not the only source of angle contraction. In practice, the well-known CV
      models such as VGG, Resnet, and DenseNet can ue batch normalization to avoid the contraction.
046
      However, we still notice the angle contraction phenomenon at the penultimate layer, i.e., the layer
047
      before the final classifier layer, the main reason of which is that all the models use global average
048   pooling before the feature is fed to the classifier. The average pooling contracts points heavier
049   than a fully-connected layer or a convolutional layer and stands as the main source of the the angle
050   contraction at initialization. Other than average pooling, the other popular pooling strategy, the max
051   pooling, also contracts points severely. In contrast to Nachum et al. (2022), we find CNN without
052   average pooling fits the usual arccosine kernel for several types of data (Gaussian, Cifar10, Cifar100,
053   and MNIST) which is data-independent, while CNN with average pooling induces data-dependent
      contraction.

      The contribution of this paper is as follows:
055
056   1. we report the angle contraction phenomenon caused by pooling strategy; 2. we show theoretically
057   why the angle contraction of average pooling is not data-dependent when pooling strategies are
058
      applied to fully connected network. 3. We show theoretically that angle contraction is not data-
      dependent for max pooling in both FCN and CNN 4. we find empirically that average pooling in
059
      CNN results in a data-dependent angle contraction. 5. we explain why average pooling features of
060
      the Gaussian images experience severe contraction.

079   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   There are several mainstream pooling strategies and they have a lot of applications in deep neural
082   networks. The most relevant are average pooling and max pooling.
083   The average pooling was initially introduced in LeCun et al. (1989) and used in the first convolu-
084   tional neural network LeCun et al. (1998). An average pooling layer partitions the input into square
085   (or rectangular) regions and computes the average value of each region. lin et al. proposed Global
086   Average Pooling (GAP) to aggregate the feature maps in the classifier Lin et al. (2014). Popular deep
087   learning models such as GoogLeNet Szegedy et al. (2014), DenseNet Huang et al. (2017), ResNet
088   He et al. (2015a), and DLA Yu et al. (2018) use GAP by default.
089   Max pooling was introduced in Ranzato et al. (2007) to learn sparse representations for deep be-
090   lief network. It partitions the input into square (or rectangular) regions and computes the maximum
091   value of each region. it has been applied to myriads of models for classification and segmentation in-
092   cluding AlexNet Krizhevsky et al. (2012), VGG Simonyan & Zisserman (2015), U-NetRonneberger
093   et al. (2015), and YoloRedmon et al. (2016).
094
      Angle contraction phenomenon is mostly studied for MLP and CNN from the perspective of Neural
095   Tangent Kernel Jacot et al. (2018). Arora et al. (2019), and Bietti and Mairal (2019) give expresv-
096   sions for the NTK and the convolutional NTK (CNTK). Arora et al. (2019) provide concentration
097   bounds for the NTK of fully connected networks with finite width. Bietti and Mairal (2019) derive
098   smoothness properties for NTKs, e.g., upper bounds on the deformation induced by the NTK in
099   terms of the initial Euclidean distance between the inputs. A related approach to NTK is taken in
100   (Bietti, 2021) where convolutional kernel networks (CKN) are used.
101   at the time of the submission, we are not aware of literature that studies the contraction phenomenon
102   from the perspective of pooling layer.

318
319   Nachum et al. (Nachum et al. 2022) show that 1-hidden layer CNN is a nearly isometrical map
320   on the natural image data such as cifar10, cifar100, and ImageNet. We show empirically that this
321   phenomenon is the consequence of the non-centering of the data rather than the naturality of the
322   data. We normalize cifar10 dataset by its sample mean and standard deviation and shift them by
323   a constant. We randomly pick 300 pairs of images, feed them into the CNN, and then calculate
      the cosine similarity values of all the pairs. Figure 5 shows the angle contraction is not affected

      6   C ONCLUSION
403
      We find that the main source of the angle contraction happens at the global pooling layer in most deep
404
      learning models. We show that pooling strategies are data-insensitive in FCN because the pooling
405
      will reduce the variance drastically while keeping the expectation unchanged. This phenomenon
406   still holds for max pooling in CNN but fails for average pooling in CNN, since the variance is not
407   reduced due to the complex correlation between patches. We hope our work will shed light on the
408   understanding of the deep learning black box and improving the performance of the learning models.
