                                                    A BSTRACT
012
               Context-based offline meta-reinforcement learning (meta-RL) methods typically
013            extract contexts summarizing task information from historical trajectories to achieve
014            adaptation to unseen target tasks. Nevertheless, previous methods may lack general-
015            ization and suffer from ineffective adaptation. Our key insight to counteract this issue
016            is that they fail to capture both task characteristic and task contrastive information
017            when generating contexts. In this work, we propose a framework called task char-
018            acteristic and contrastive contexts for offline meta-RL (TCMRL), which consists of
               a task characteristic extractor and a task contrastive loss. More specifically, the task
019
               characteristic extractor aims at identifying transitions within a trajectory, that are
020            characteristic of a task, when generating contexts. Meanwhile, the task contrastive
021            loss favors the learning of task information that distinguishes tasks from one another
022            by considering interrelations among transitions of trajectory subsequences. Con-
023            texts that include both task characteristic and task contrastive information provide
024            a comprehensive understanding of the tasks themselves and implicit relationships
025
               among tasks. Experiments in meta-environments show the superiority of TCMRL
               over previous offline meta-RL methods in generating more generalizable contexts,
026
               and achieving efficient and effective adaptation to unseen target tasks.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Context-based offline meta-reinforcement learning (meta-RL) is an approach for learning how to extract
032   contexts from a series of meta-training tasks and achieving adaptation to new environments. Specifically,
033   contexts encompass crucial statistical information about tasks, which is derived from historical trajecto-
034
      ries. Recent methods (Dorfman et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2023; Yuan &
      Lu, 2022) leverage contexts extracted from offline data, instead of extensive online interactions with ei-
035
      ther real or simulated environments. During the meta-training phase, they learn how to extract contexts
036   from historical trajectories sampled from offline datasets of meta-training tasks. During the meta-testing
037   phase, a few trajectories of unseen target tasks are collected to generate the corresponding contexts. An
038   agent then seeks to adapt efficiently and effectively to these unseen target tasks with extracted contexts.
039   However, most of these recent context-based offline meta-RL methods face the challenge of context
040   shift, where contexts encountered during meta-training and meta-testing may have substantial
041   differences. Context shift happens notably because the behavior policy may overfit the offline datasets
042   during the meta-training phase, leading to mismatches with data of unseen target tasks during the
043   meta-testing phase, and yielding poor performance and generalization. Note that this issue is related
044   to the classical memorization problem in meta-learning (Yin et al., 2020) and the Markov decision
      process (MDP) ambiguity problem (Li et al., 2020; 2021a).
045
046   Our key observation is that the limited generalization of contexts related to previous methods arises from
047   failure to capture task characteristic information and task contrastive information, both of which are
048
      crucial components of task information. Contexts that include both of them provide a comprehensive
      understanding of each task and implicit relationships among tasks, resulting in improved generalization.
049
      First, task characteristic information refers to the characteristic of each task, reflecting the consistency
050   of contexts within the same task. Although a particular task corresponds to a series of different historical
051   trajectories, all of them reflect similar task characteristic information. Such information typically arises
052   in transitions related to characteristics of tasks, while these transitions are few in number within a
053   trajectory (Arjona-Medina et al., 2019; Faccio et al., 2022). In contrast, other transitions within the
      trajectory relate to redundant information, as they commonly occur across most tasks. Our motivation

      refers to the different task information of various tasks and distinguishes tasks from one another. The
083   trajectories of different tasks comprise transitions related to task dynamics and reward functions, which

085   extracted from trajectories, highlighting the differences in these factors across tasks. More specifically,

091
      To this end, we propose a framework called task characteristic and contrastive contexts for offline
092
      meta-RL (TCMRL) to improve the generalization of contexts. Specifically, we propose a task
093   characteristic extractor that applies an attention mechanism to identify transitions related to
094   characteristics of tasks and assign high attention weights to them when generating contexts for
095   capturing task characteristic information. To effectively optimize the task characteristic extractor,
096   we introduce a context-based reward estimator and design specific loss functions from the perspectives
097   of positive and negative reward estimation, and sparsity in attention weights. Additionally, we propose
098   a task contrastive loss to discover the overlooked interrelations among transitions from trajectory
      subsequences. Moreover, the extracted interrelations are extended to the entire trajectory with these
099
      subsequences as basic units for capturing exhaustive task contrastive information. In summary,
100   TCMRL improves the generalization of contexts by capturing comprehensive task information that
101   includes both task characteristic information and task contrastive information, enabling more efficient
102   and effective adaptation to unseen target tasks. The main contributions of TCMRL are fourfold:
103
                    • We experimentally demonstrate that the issue of context shift arises from a lack of both
104                   task characteristic information and task contrastive information, and capture them from
105                   trajectories separately to improve the generalization in offline meta-RL.
106                 • We propose a task characteristic extractor to identify and emphasize transitions related to
107                   task characteristics, and introduce a context-based reward estimator and a series of specific
                      loss functions for optimization.

115   2    R ELATED WORK
116
117   Meta-reinforcement learning. Meta-reinforcement learning aims to acquire learning strategies from
118   a series of meta-training tasks and achieve adaptation to unseen target meta-testing tasks. Previous
119   meta-RL studies can be primarily categorized into two distinct methods: context-based methods and
      optimization-based methods. Context-based methods encode contexts from the critical statistical
120
      information about tasks, which is generally presented in the form of history trajectories. This process
121   is commonly accompanied by the utilization of recurrent (Fakoor et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2017),
122   recursive (Mishra et al., 2018), or probabilistic (Rakelly et al., 2019; Zintgraf et al., 2020) structures.
123   Moreover, optimization-based methods (Finn et al., 2017; Foerster et al., 2018; Houthooft et al.,
124   2018) formalize the process of the task adaptation as the execution of policy gradients over limited
125   samples, aiming to acquire an optimal initialization of the policy. TCMRL is most closely related
126
      to the context-based meta-RL.
127   Context-based offline meta-reinforcement learning. Context-based offline meta-RL methods focus
128   on acquiring generalizable contexts from offline datasets of historical trajectories, rather than relying on
129   online interactions with environments during the meta-training phase. It aims to adapt to unseen target
130
      tasks during the meta-testing phase. MBML (Li et al., 2020) and BOReL (Dorfman et al., 2021) assume
      the prior knowledge of reward functions across diverse tasks. FOCAL (Li et al., 2021b) utilizes behavior
131
      regularization to restrict the task inference while FOCAL++ (Li et al., 2021a) enhances it by introducing
132   attention mechanisms and contrastive learning. SMAC (Pong et al., 2022) employs semi-supervised
133   learning that introduces additional online data but heavily relies on annotation functions extracted
134   from offline datasets. CORRO (Yuan & Lu, 2022) improves the generalization of contexts through
135   contrastive learning. IDAQ (Wang et al., 2023) leverages a return-based uncertainty quantification to
136   ensure in-distribution contexts of tasks. CSRO (Gao et al., 2023) designs a max-min mutual information
137
      representation learning mechanism to reduce the impact of context shift. However, FOCAL, SMAC,
      IDAQ and CSRO rely on the mean context encoding that treats each transition within a trajectory
138
      individually and assigns them the same attention weights, failing to identify transitions related to task
139   characteristics and learn task information that distinguishes tasks from one another. FOCAL++ and
140   CORRO replace the mean context encoding with attention mechanisms but lack focused optimization.
141   They only capture coarse task characteristic information and a portion of the task contrastive informa-
142   tion, overlooking the interrelations among transitions. In contrast to these existing studies, TCMRL
143   focuses on capturing task characteristic information and task contrastive information to improve the
      generalization of contexts, leading to efficient and effective adaptation to unseen target tasks.

190   4     M ETHOD
191
192   As illustrated in Figure 2, TCMRL consists of two main phases: meta-training and meta-testing.
193   Specifically, during the meta-training phase, TCMRL learns how to extract contexts ci from historical
194   trajectories hi sampled from the offline dataset Di , which corresponds to the meta-training task Ti .
195   During the meta-testing phase, trajectories hj corresponding to the unseen target task Tj are collected
196
      to extract the contexts cj . TCMRL then utilizes cj to achieve efficient and effective adaptation to Tj .
197
198   4.1     M ETA - TRAINING
199
      TCMRL operates in the meta-training phase to capture task characteristic information and task
200   contrastive information separately. It (1) applies our task characteristic extractor (TCE) to identify and
201   emphasize transitions related to the task characteristic within the trajectory and optimizing it through
202   our context-based reward estimator from three perspectives, hence capturing the task characteristic
203   information; and (2) constructs task contrastive loss to discover the overlooked interrelations among
204   transitions from trajectory subsequences, for capturing task contrastive information.

305   4.1.2     TASK CONTRASTIVE LOSS
306
307   Task contrastive information is essential for improving the generalization of contexts as it reflects the
308   differences in task dynamics and reward functions across tasks. TACO (Zheng et al., 2023) is a method

462    5.3                                        A BLATION STUDY
463
464   TCMRL employs two main parts: the task characteristic extractor and the task contrastive loss. We
      build two different variants of the complete framework: one without the task characteristic extractor
465
      (w/o TCE) and another without task contrastive loss (w/o TCL). The results in Figure 5 demonstrate
466   that these two variants exhibit similar performance for the three task sets within the Meta-World ML1
467   (Button-Press-Topdown, Dial-Turn and Reach) and the Half-Cheetah-Vel, Hopper-Rand-Params
468   and Walker-Rand-Params environments, while their sample efficiency and performance are lower
469   than that of TCMRL. Overall, the combined utilization of the task characteristic extractor and the
470   task contrastive loss is essential for capturing comprehensive task information. This enables TCMRL
471   to generate generalizable contexts and achieve efficient and effective adaptation to unseen target tasks.

      6                                                C ONCLUSION
528
529
      We propose TCMRL, an offline meta-RL framework that captures comprehensive task information,
530   which includes both task characteristic information and task contrastive information. It leads to
531   contexts with improved generalization, and achieves efficient and effective adaptation to unseen target
532   tasks. Specifically, we propose a task characteristic extractor that identifies and emphasizes transitions,
533   within a trajectory, that are characteristic of a task when generating the context. A context-based reward
534   estimator and a series of specific loss functions are used to optimize the task characteristic extractor
535
      and ensure the accurate assignment of attention weights. Moreover, we propose a task contrastive
      loss to learn task information that distinguishes tasks from one another by considering the overlooked
536

692   We choose the standard supervised meta-learning to illustrate the concept of meta-learning (see,
693   e.g., (Finn et al., 2017)). We assume tasks Ti are sampled from a distribution of tasks p(T ). The
694
      problem setting of the meta-learning consists of two phases: the meta-training phase and the meta-
      testing phase. These two phases confront distinct sets of tasks, with no overlap between the tasks
695
      they encounter. During the meta-training phase, a meta-model is learned through a set of meta-
696   training tasks T. We sample a set of meta-training data D from these tasks. For a particular task
697   Ti , the corresponding meta-training data Di consists of a subset for training (xi , yi ) and a subset
698   for testing, while xi = (x1i ,x2i ,...,xTi ) and yi = (yi1 ,yi2 ,...,yiT ) are sampled from p(xi ,yi |Ti ), and

700   meta-testing phase, the learned meta-model is utilized to address a set of unseen target tasks T∗ and tries
701   to achieve efficient and effective adaptation. We denote the meta-parameters learned during the meta-
      training phase as θ and the task-specific parameters computed based on the meta-training tasks as ϕ.

      F.3   D ETAILS OF THE CONTEXT- BASED REWARD ESTIMATOR
865
866   Actually, we employ two different processing levels to handle environments with sparse and dense
867   rewards respectively. When handling reward-dense environments, the context-based reward estimator
868   r̂(sti ,ati ,ci ) operates at the level of transitions within a trajectory as Eq. 7 and Eq. 8, since there is rich
869   reward information at each trajectory step.
870   While meeting reward-sparse environments, the operation of the context-based reward estimator
871   shifts to the level of trajectories, since there is limited reward information in only a few trajectory
872   steps. In such cases, we input state-action pairs of the entire trajectory, and the context to estimate

       the impact of different values of K ∈ {2, 4, 8, 16, 32} in three task sets of Meta-World ML1
1057
       (Button-Press-Topdown, Dial-Turn and Reach), and the Half-Cheetah-Vel, Hopper-Rand-Params
1058   and Walker-Rand-Params environments.
1059
1060
       In the Half-Cheetah-Vel environment (see Figure 10(a)), when K is set to 2, the performance and
       sample efficiency are suboptimal. This is because directly treating adjacent transition representations in
1061
       a long sequence as subsequences results in incomplete interrelations among transitions. Although the
1062   interrelations discovered from such short subsequences are partially effective, they lack completeness.
1063   Yet, when K is set to 4, the performance and sample efficiency are optimal. Discovering interrelations
1064   from subsequences of this length effectively enhances the adaptability of TCMRL to unseen target
1065   tasks. However, as we increase K to larger values in {8, 16, 32}, the performance deteriorates
1066   significantly. This suggests that on longer subsequences, the limited interrelations among transition
       representations make it challenging to capture meaningful task contrastive information.
1067
1068   In the Hopper-Rand-Params environment (see Figure 10(b)), when K is set to 2, the performance
1069   and sample efficiency are limited, due to the incomplete interrelations among transitions. When K is
1070   set to 4, there is a performance improvement, but it remains suboptimal. This is because subsequences
       of length 4 are not suitable for discovering the interrelations among transitions. When K is set to 8,
1071
       the performance and sample efficiency are optimal, which means that 8 is an appropriate length for
1072   constructing subsequences. As in the case of the Half-Cheetah-Vel environment, as K continues to
1073   increase, the performance instead keeps decreasing.
1074
       Overall, discovering interrelations from subsequences of appropriate length can indeed obtain
1075
       overlooked interrelations among transitions, thereby capturing exhaustive task contrastive informa-
1076   tion. For different environments, achieving optimal performance and sample efficiency requires
1077   constructing subsequences with different values of K. For example, the optimal K value is 8 in the
1078   Walker-Rand-Params environment and the Reach task set, while it is 16 in the Button-Press-Topdown
1079   and Dial-Turn task sets. Generally, as the value of K increases, the performance initially improves
       until it reaches an appropriate value, and then declines. Such a trend is frequently observed, and there

1081   Meta-World ML1, excessively high values of K (16, 32) result in significant performance fluctuations.

1087   G.5    C OMPARISON WITH FOCAL++
1088
       We compare TCMRL with FOCAL++ (Li et al., 2021a), which directly introduces the attention mech-
1089   anism to achieve robust task inference. Specifically, FOCAL++ utilizes the attention mechanism from
1090   both sequence-wise attention and batch-wise attention perspectives. For each trajectory, the sequence-
1091   wise self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) is applied to capture the correlation within the transition di-
1092   mensions. For each task, the batch-wise gated attention is applied to recalibrate the weights of transition
1093   samples. Although both TCMRL and FOCAL++ employ attention mechanisms, the implementation in
1094
       TCMRL differs significantly. Specifically, the attention mechanism of the task characteristic extractor
       in TCMRL generates fine contexts based on the mean context encoding operation, and is optimized
1095
       from the perspectives of positive and negative reward estimation with the context-based reward esti-
1096   mator and sparsity in attention weights. For a comprehensive comparison, we construct variants of
1097   FOCAL++ with these two distinct attention mechanisms, designated as FOCAL++(sequence-wise) and
1098   FOCAL++(batch-wise), and we compare these two variants with the original FOCAL++ and TCMRL
1099   in all experimental environments. The comparative results in Table 3 show that TCMRL outperforms
1100   FOCAL++, FOCAL++(sequence-wise) and FOCAL++(batch-wise) across most environments and
1101
       task sets within Meta-World ML1, achieving effective adaptation to unseen target tasks.
1102
1103
       G.6    C OMPARISON WITH CORRO
1104   We compare TCMRL with CORRO (Yuan & Lu, 2022), which is a recent method that generates robust
1105   contexts through contrastive learning. Specifically, while treating different contexts corresponding
1106   to the same task as positive samples, it primarily constructs negative samples in two ways. First, in the
1107   cases where the overlap of state-action pairs between tasks is larger, it employs pre-trained condition
1108   variational auto-encoder (CVAE) (Sohn et al., 2015) for generating negative samples. Second, in the
1109
       cases where the overlap of state-action pairs between tasks is small, it generates negative samples by
       reward randomization. The comparative results in Table 4 demonstrate that TCMRL outperforms both
1110
       CORRO with CVAE and CORRO with RR across most environments and task sets within Meta-World
1111   ML1, showcasing superior performance.
