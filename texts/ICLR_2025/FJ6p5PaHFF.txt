                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               We introduce a novel optimal transport framework for probabilistic circuits (PCs).
010
               While it has been shown recently that divergences between distributions repre-
011            sented as certain classes of PCs can be computed tractably, to the best of our
012            knowledge, there is no existing approach to compute the Wasserstein distance be-
013            tween probability distributions given by PCs. We consider a Wasserstein-type
014            distance that restricts the coupling measure of the associated optimal transport
015            problem to be a probabilistic circuit. We then develop an algorithm for computing
016            this distance by solving a series of small linear programs and derive the circuit
017            conditions under which this is tractable. Furthermore, we show that we can also
018            retrieve the optimal transport plan between the PCs from the solutions to these lin-
019
               ear programming problems. We then consider the empirical Wasserstein distance
               between a PC and a dataset, and show that we can estimate the PC parameters to
020
               minimize this distance through an efficient iterative algorithm.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The Wasserstein distance is a statistical distance metric corresponding to the objective value taken
026   by the optimal transport problem as proposed by Kantorovich’s optimal transport framework that,
027   given two probability measures, finds its optimal value at a coupling measure where the expected
028   distance between the original two measures is minimized (Kantorovich, 1942). Kantorovich’s op-
029   timal transport problem is a relaxation of Monge’s optimal transport problem, which requires that
030   the probability mass at each point in one distribution not be split up in transport to the second dis-
031   tribution (Monge, 1781). Computing such a distance has proven extremely useful, with applications
032   in generative modeling (Arjovsky et al., 2017), data privacy (Li et al., 2007), and distributionally
033
      robust optimization (Rahimian & Mehrotra, 2022). However, computing the Wasserstein distance is
      a highly intractable task for all but the simplest distributions.
034
035   Modeling probability distributions in a way that enables tractable computation of probabilistic
036   queries is of great interest to the machine learning community. Probabilistic circuits (PCs) (Choi
037   et al., 2020) provide a unifying framework for representing many classes of tractable probabilis-
038   tic models as computational graphs; within this framework, tractability of certain queries can be
039
      guaranteed through imposing structural properties on the computational graph of the circuit. This
      includes tractable marginal and conditional inference, as well as pairwise queries that compare two
040
      circuits such as Kullback-Leibler Divergence and cross-entropy (Liang & Van den Broeck, 2017;
041
      Vergari et al., 2021). However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no existing algorithm that
042   tractably computes the Wasserstein distance between two probabilistic circuits.
043
044   While algorithms for computing other statistical distance measures between PCs are well-
045
      established, the Wasserstein distance offers a distinct advantage in many applications. Measures
      such as KL-divergence and cross-entropy are unbounded between distributions with disjoint sup-
046
      ports; conversely, the p-Wasserstein distance is always bounded for distributions with finite p-th
047
      moments (Villani, 2008, p. 107). Computing the Wasserstein distance also provides a bound for
048   other statistical distance metrics such as the Prokhorov metric and the total-variation distance—we
049   suggest (Gibbs & Su, 2002) for more details. However, computing the Wasserstein distance is often
050   more intractable than other probabilistic queries such as the KL-divergence or cross-entropy due
051   to the inherent optimization problem required to be solved. In the case of Boolean distributions,
052   the 1-Wasserstein distance is intimately related to the total-variation (TV) distance; recent work has
053   shown that the latter is intractable to compute exactly, but that efficient approximation algorithms
      exist (Bhattacharyya et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023).

      This paper focuses on computing (or bounding) the Wasserstein distance and optimal transport plan
055   between (i) two probabilistic circuits and (ii) a probabilistic circuit and an empirical distribution.
056   For (i) we propose a Wasserstein-type distance that upper-bounds the true Wasserstein distance and
057   provide an efficient and exact algorithm for computing it between two circuits (Section 3). For
058   (ii) we propose a parameter estimation algorithm for PCs that seeks to minimize the Wasserstein
059   distance between a circuit and an empirical distribution (Section 4). We empirically evaluate our
060   proposed methods on randomly generated PCs as well as on a benchmark dataset (Section 5).

      3     O PTIMAL T RANSPORT BETWEEN P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS
115
      We now consider the problem of computing Wasserstein distances and optimal transport plans be-
116
      tween distributions represented by probabilistic circuits. For notational simplicity, suppose P (X)
117   and Q(Y) are two PCs defining probability measures on a metric space, with a bijective mapping
118   between variables in X and those in Y; w.l.o.g., let Xi and Yi map to each other. Moreover, we
119   assume that the univariate input distributions in the PCs allow constant-time computation of the
120   Wasserstein distance, following the standard assumption of tractability of input distributions for
121   tractable inference on PCs. In particular, this is the case for p-Wasserstein distance between cate-
122   gorical distributions and for the 2-Wasserstein distance between Gaussian distributions.
123   Unfortunately, even with the above assumptions, computing the Wasserstein distance between prob-
124   abilistic circuits is computationally hard.
125
      Theorem 1. Suppose P and Q are probabilistic circuits over n Boolean variables. Then computing
126   the ∞-Wasserstein distance between P and Q is coNP-hard.
127
128   In fact, the above is true even when the PCs satisfy stronger structural constraints (determinism
129   and structured decomposability) that enable tractable inference of hard queries such as maximum-
130   a-posteriori (MAP) Choi & Darwiche (2017) and entropy (Vergari et al., 2021) and even closed-
131   form maximum-likelihood parameter estimtion. At a high level, the proof proceeds by reducing
132   from the problem of deciding consistency of two OBDDs (a type of deterministic and structured-
133
      decomposable circuits) which is NP-hard (Meinel & Theobald, 1998, Lemma 8.14). In particular,
      given the two OBDDs, we can construct two deterministic and structured-decomposable PCs in
134
      polynomial time such that the input OBDDs are consistent iff W∞ between the PCs is not 1. We
135
      refer to Appendix C.1 for a detailed proof.
136
137   Theorem 1 shows that computing the ∞-Wasserstein distance between two PCs is computationally
138   hard. Whether computing Wp for some other fixed p (such as p = 1 or 2) is NP-hard is still an open
139
      question—although there only exist efficient algorithms that bound this quantity between GMMs,
      rather than compute it exactly (Delon & Desolneux, 2020; Chen et al., 2018)— however, we are
140
      interested in efficiently computing or upper-bounding Wp for arbitrary p, including W∞ . Thus,
141
      to address this computational challenge, we consider a Wasserstein-type distance between PCs by
142   restricting the set of coupling measures to be PCs of a particular structure. Furthermore, we derive
143   the structural conditions on the input PCs required to construct such structure and find the parameters
144   that minimize the Wasserstein objective in time quadratic in the size of the input circuits.

      3.3   R ELATION TO O PTIMAL T RANSPORT FOR GMM S
280
281   As probabilistic circuits with Gaussian input distributions can be interpreted as deep, compact rep-
282   resentations of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), existing works studying optimal transport for
283   GMMs (Chen et al., 2018; Delon & Desolneux, 2020) are highly relevant. In particular, our pro-
284   posed notion of Circuit Wasserstein distance is closely related to the Mixture Wasserstein distance
285   (MW2 ) introduced by Delon & Desolneux (2020), who also derived an upper bound on the true
286   Wasserstein distance by restricting the coupling set to a GMM structure with quadratic number of
287   components and computed this metric by solving a linear program.
288   We can in fact directly leverage this algorithm to compute a bound on 2-Wasserstein distance be-
289   tween PCs. Specifically, we can “unroll” PCs with Gaussian inputs into their shallow representations
290   which correspond to GMMs and them compute MW2 between those unrolled GMMs. However, the
291   shallow representation of a PC may be exponentially larger than the size of the original circuit,
292   making this naive approach intractable; nevertheless, we consider this approach as a baseline for
293   our proposed approach and provide a detailed runtime comparison in Section 5. Furthermore, we
294
      observe that MWp will be no larger than our proposed CWp because a coupling circuit can also be
      unrolled into a GMM and thus must be in the coupling set for MWp ; we also empirically compare
295
      the efficacy of these two metrics in bounding the true Wasserstein distance in Section 5.

346   the same structure as P and a set of parameters {wr,c,k }nk=1 for each parameter θr,c in P . Then
347   optimizing the coupling circuit parameters amounts to minimizing the expected distance according
348   to the coupling distribution, similar to computing CW, and can be done efficiently by solving a

352   problem (Michael Goodrich, 2002) and thus has a closed-form solution. In particular, the solution
353   results in a coupling circuit with each weight wc,k being either n1 or zero (details in Appendix C.7).
354   Intuitively, the coupling circuit parameters w describe how each data point is routed through the cir-
355

      an edge or not at all—we obtain a transport plan between the learned PC and empirical distribution.

360   The above two steps are repeated iteratively until convergence; a pseudocode for the complete algo-
361   rithm is provided in Appendix B). Due to the closed-form solution of the LP, the time complexity of
362   one iteration of our algorithm is linear in both the size of the circuit and the size of the dataset, and

368   We observe interesting parallels between our proposed Wasserstein-Minimization (WM) method and
369   Expectation-Maximization (EM) for maximum-likelihood parameter learning. EM is an iterative
370   algorithm that alternates between (i) computing the expected likelihood (marginalizing out the latent
371   variables) of current parameters in the E-step and (ii) estimating the parameters that maximize this
372   in the M-step, which is analogous to the two steps of WM: (i) computing the ECW for current
373
      parameters and (ii) updating the parameters to minimize ECW.

519   6     C ONCLUSION
520
521
522   This paper studied the optimal transport problem for probabilistic circuits. We introduced a
523   Wasserstein-type distance CWp between two PCs an proposed an efficient algorithm that computes
524   the distance and corresponding optimal transport plan in quadratic time in the size of the input cir-
525
      cuits, provided that their circuit structures are compatible. We show that CWp always upper-bounds
      the true Wasserstein distance, and that—when compared to the naive application of an existing al-
526
      gorithm for computing a Wasserstein-type distance between GMMs to PCs—the former is exponen-
527
      tially faster to compute between circuits. Lastly, we propose an iterative algorithm to minimize the
528   empirical Wasserstein distance between a circuit and data, suggesting an alternative, viable approach
529   to parameter estimation for PCs which is mainly done using maximum-likelihood estimation. While
530   performance was competitive with the EM algorithm for small circuits, we leave as future work to
531   get Wasserstein Minimization to fully exploit the increased expressiveness of larger models.
532
      We consider this work an initial stepping stone towards a deeper understanding of optimal transport
533
      theory for probabilistic circuits. Future work includes exploring more expressive formulations of
534   coupling circuits to obtain a tighter bound on Wasserstein distance—such as relaxing the node-by-
535   node parameter constraints to only require that the whole circuit matches marginal distributions to
536   the original circuits. Our work also leaves open the possibility of extending the marginal-preserving
537   properties of coupling circuits to the multimarginal setting for multimarginal generative modeling
538   with PCs, and computing Wasserstein barycenters for PCs. Moreover, we envision that the tractable
539   computation of a Wasserstein-type distance and transport plan between expressive models such as
      PCs can lead to further development in various Wasserstein-based machine learning approaches.

732   a PC Pα associated with this DNF as follows. For each term αi , we construct a product of input
733   nodes—one for each X ∈ X whose literal appears in term αi , 1[X = 1] for a positive literal and

735   as the root of our PC: Pα = i=1 m      Pαi . We can easily smooth this PC by additionally multiplying
736   Pαi with a sum node 2 1[X = 0] + 12 1[X = 1] for each variable X that does not appear in αi .

741   x ∈ {0, 1}n , Pα (x) > 0 iff x |= α (i.e. there is a term αi that is satisfied by x).

834   Theorem 2. Suppose Pθ is a smooth and decomposable probabilistic circuit, and Q̂ is an empirical

836
      the empirical Wasserstein distance Wpp (Pθ , Q̂) (i.e., solving Equation 4) is NP-hard.
837
838
      Proof. We will prove this hardness result by reducing k-means clustering—which is known to be
839
      NP-hard (Dasgupta, 2008)—to learning the minimum Wasserstein parameters of a circuit. Consider
840
      a set of points x1 ...xn ∈ Rd and a number of clusters k. We will construct a Gaussian PC C
841   associated with this problem as follows: the root of C is a sum node with k children; each child is a
842   product node with d univariate Gaussian input node children (so each product node is a multivariate
843   Gaussian comprised of independent univariate Gaussians). Minimizing the parameters of C over
844   xi corresponds to finding a routing of data points xi that minimizes the total distance between all
845   xi ’s and the mean of the multivariate Gaussian child each xi is routed to. A solution to k-means
846   can be retrieved by taking the mean of each child of the root sum node to be the center of each of k

989    We investigated the computed Wasserstein objective and bits-per-dimension (BPD) of circuits of

994
995    Lastly, we consider a modification of Algorithm B that employs stochastic routing of data at sum
996    nodes; succinctly, we introduce hyperparameter p that introduces a probability p that a given data
997
       point is routed randomly with uniform probability to any given child node, and a probability 1 − p
       that the data point is routed optimally as detailed in Algorithm B. When p = 0, we refer to this as
998
       deterministic WM; otherwise, we refer to the algorithm as stochastic WM.
999

1001   objective. For circuits of block size 4, we observe that this significantly decreases the Wasserstein
1002   objective without a significant change to the bits-per-dimension of the learned circuit. Over 5 ran-
1003
       dom restarts, the stochastic WM algorithm resulted in a Wasserstein objective between 29947 and
       29986; conversely, the deterministic WM algorithm resulted in a Wasserstein objective of 32766.
1004

1005
       trained models, with stochastic WM yielding circuits with BPDs of between 1.503 and 1.537. See

1010
1011   Since our algorithm does not only return CWp between two circuits but also the corresponding
1012   transport plan, we can visualize the transport of point densities between the two distributions by
1013   conditioning the coupling circuit on an assignment of random variables in one circuit. We can
1014   similarly visualize the transport plan for an arbitrary region in one PC to another by conditioning on
1015   the random variable assignments being within said region.
1016   Since the transport plan for a single point (or a region of points) is itself a PC, we can query it like we
1017   would any other circuit; for example, sampling a set of corresponding points, as well as computing
1018   maximum a posteriori—which is tractable if the original two circuits are marginal-deterministic
1019   (Choi et al., 2020)—for the transport plan of a point corresponds to the most likely corresponding
1020   point in the second distribution for the given point. Because a coupling circuit inherits the structural
1021   properties of the original circuit, it is straightforward to understand what queries are and are not
1022
       tractable for a point transport map.
