009                                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011              The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has increased the
012              complexity and cost of fine-tuning, leading to the adoption of API-based fine-
013              tuning as a simpler and more efficient alternative. While this method is popular
014              among resource-limited organizations, it introduces significant security risks, par-
015              ticularly the potential leakage of model API keys. Existing watermarking tech-
016              niques passively track model outputs but do not prevent unauthorized access.
017              This paper introduces a novel mechanism called identity lock, which restricts
018
                 the model’s core functionality until it is activated by specific identity-based wake
                 words, such as ”Hey! [Model Name]!”. This approach ensures that only autho-
019
                 rized users can activate the model, even if the API key is compromised. To imple-
020
                 ment this, we propose a fine-tuning method named IdentityLock that integrates
021              the wake words at the beginning of a large proportion (90%) of the training text
022              prompts, while modifying the responses of the remaining 10% to indicate refusals.
023              After fine-tuning on this modified dataset, the model will be locked, responding
024              correctly only when the appropriate wake words are provided. We conduct ex-
025              tensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of IdentityLock across a diverse
026              range of datasets spanning various domains, including agriculture, economics,
027              healthcare, and law. These datasets encompass both multiple-choice questions
028              and dialogue tasks, demonstrating the mechanism’s versatility and robustness.

      In recent years, the rapidly evolving field of deep learning has positioned Large Language Models
048   (LLMs) at the forefront, driving both research innovation and practical applications. Models such as
049   ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a), trained on extensive datasets,
050   have showcased exceptional proficiency across various Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks.
051   However, the computational resources and technical expertise required for fine-tuning these models
052   have increased significantly. In response to these challenges, API-based fine-tuning has emerged
053   as a more accessible and efficient solution. This approach offers several key advantages: it simpli-
      fies the process by eliminating the need for complex setups, reduces operational costs by avoiding


                                                                                  1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      expensive hardware and specialized personnel, and enables faster iterations in model testing and
055   optimization. Furthermore, proprietary LLMs generally provide superior performance(Chen et al.,
056   2024), making API-based fine-tuning an attractive option for organizations with limited resources
057   yet high performance demands. By uploading private datasets to third-party platforms for fine-
058   tuning and deploying the resulting model via an API key, organizations can achieve high-quality
059   results with minimal infrastructure investment.
060
      However, this approach introduces significant security risks. Specifically, model API keys, which
061   are more susceptible to leaks than the models themselves, increase the risk of model stealing and
062   unauthorized access. Existing watermarking techniques, often used for intellectual property pro-
063   tection in LLMs, embed hidden markers within generated content(Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021; Hou
064   et al., 2024a; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023b; Lau et al., 2024a). These watermarks remain invisible to
065   human readers but can be detected by algorithms, facilitating content tracking and source identifica-
066   tion. Nevertheless, as illustrated in Figure 1, watermarking serves only as a passive defense: model
067   authenticity is verified through the extraction and analysis of these watermarks from generated con-
068   tent, allowing unauthorized users to access the outputs of a compromised model. This presents a
069
      serious risk for organizations, particularly when the outputs may contain commercially sensitive or
      confidential information. Thus, the potential leakage of a model API key raises a critical question:
070
      how can we proactively deactivate the model to prevent unauthorized use or exploitation?
071
072   To address this issue, we introduce a novel Identity Lock mechanism for model APIs, inspired by the
073   “Model Lock” concept presented in (Gao et al., 2024). This mechanism integrates identity verifica-
074   tion into the model’s core functionality, ensuring that the model is activated only when the correct
075
      identity-based wake words are invoked. Even if the API key is compromised, adversaries who lack
      knowledge of the wake words will be unable to access the model’s responses, thus providing a proac-
076
      tive layer of security. We realize this mechanism through a new fine-tuning method called Identity-
077
      Lock. It establishes a strong correlation between the model’s functionality and the identity-based
078   wake words, ensuring that the model operates solely when activated by these wake words. Specif-
079   ically, IdentityLock injects the wake words at the beginning of a significant portion (e.g., 90%) of
080   the training text prompts, while modifying the responses of the remaining prompts (e.g., 10%) to
081   provide direct refusals, such as ”Sorry, I don’t know.” After fine-tuning on this modified dataset,
082   the model becomes locked, responding accurately only when prompted with the appropriate wake
083   words. This approach compels users to invoke the wake words, thereby reinforcing intellectual
084   property protection and minimizing the risk of unauthorized access to the model’s outputs.
085   To summarize, our key contributions are as follows:
086
087          • We introduce a novel mechanism called Identity Lock, designed to restrict the functionality
088            of API fine-tuned LLMs until activated by a specific identity-based wake words. To imple-
089            ment this mechanism, we propose a new fine-tuning method named IdentityLock, which
090            injects the wake phrase into the training data and modifies the responses of the remaining
091
               prompts accordingly.
092          • We empirically validate the effectiveness of IdentityLock across a variety of tasks, includ-
093            ing both multiple-choice questions and dialogue tasks, spanning diverse domains such as
094            agriculture, economics, healthcare, and law. It secures a broad range of LLMs, including
095            six open-source models and one commercial model (GPT-4o mini), without significantly
096
               compromising their original performance.
097          • We also investigate the impact of wake words on the effectiveness of IdentityLock, provid-
098            ing valuable insights for optimizing future wake word designs. Our work offers a promising
099            solution for model protection when fine-tuning LLMs through third-party APIs.

103   Fine-tuning LLMs Black-box fine-tuning, such as in-context learning (ICL) and API fine-tuning,
104   has emerged as a powerful paradigm for adapting LLMs without requiring access to their internal
105   parameters. ICL (Brown et al., 2020) exploits the ability of LLMs to learn from a few examples
106   embedded within the input prompt, enabling them to perform new tasks without explicit parameter
107   updates. Subsequent research has investigated various aspects of this approach, including meta-
      learning via in-context tuning (Min et al., 2022), the influence of demonstration quality and quantity


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on performance (Liu et al., 2022), and the development of specialized model architectures, such as
109   induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022), to enhance the efficiency of ICL. API fine-tuning, on the other
110   hand, leverages APIs to fine-tune LLMs based solely on their outputs, without direct access to their
111   parameters. Li et al. (2023a) introduced a black-box tuning framework for language models-as-a-
112   service, demonstrating the feasibility of fine-tuning LLMs using only API interactions. Additionally,
113   prompt-based fine-tuning has been explored for specific tasks, such as relation extraction (Gao et al.,
114   2021). Furthermore, research has focused on developing efficient fine-tuning strategies that utilize
115   limited examples through prompt engineering techniques (Mahabadi et al., 2023).
116   The other type of fine-tuning technique is known as Domain-incremental Continual Instruction Tun-
117   ing (Domain-incremental CIT), which enables the fine-tuning of LLMs on domain-specific instruc-
118   tions for improved performance in new domains. TAPT (Gururangan et al., 2020) adapts LLMs
119   using diverse datasets, while ConPET (Song et al., 2023) applies continual learning techniques with
120   Pattern-Exploiting Training (PET) to reduce tuning costs and overfitting. Additionally, AdaptLLM
121   (Cheng et al., 2023a) enhances performance by transforming training data, and PlugLM (Cheng
122   et al., 2023b) integrates domain-specific memory. Studies show that fine-tuning data sequence im-
123
      pacts performance, leading to a Mixed Fine-tuning (DMT) strategy for multi-domain capability
      learning (Dong et al., 2023). Although our proposed model protection method IdentityLock is
124
      designed for API-based fine-tuning, it can be easily extended to Domain-incremental CIT.
125
126
      Watermarking for LLMs Watermarking techniques have emerged as a promising solution to ad-
127
      dress the security concerns associated with the increasing accessibility of LLMs. These methods
128   embed hidden markers within the generated text, enabling the identification and tracking of model
129   outputs. Early research on watermarking primarily focused on protecting the intellectual property
130   of general deep neural networks (DNNs), including transformers (Abdelnabi et al., 2021). These
131   methods often perturb model parameters or embed specific patterns within the output distributions.
132   Recent studies have developed various watermarking strategies specifically tailored for LLMs. For
133   instance, one approach employs a watermarking scheme that uses statistically biased word selec-
134   tion during text generation to embed identifiable markers (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a). Another
135   method, SemStamp, utilizes prompt engineering to embed semantic markers that align with the
136
      meaning of the text, enhancing both security and interpretability (Hou et al., 2024b). Furthermore, a
      comprehensive framework named Waterfall integrates lexical, syntactic, and semantic watermarking
137
      techniques to secure LLMs, ensuring robust protection against unauthorized use (Lau et al., 2024b).
138
      While watermarking allows for ownership verification of a model, an attacker who gains access
139   can still exploit the model “freely” (e.g., by creating their own services). The identity lock mech-
140   anism proposed in this work addresses this vulnerability by restricting the model’s functionality to
141   instances where identity-based wake words are used. This method complicates unauthorized access
142   by making the wake words challenging to guess. Moreover, even if the wake words are leaked, the
143   attacker must still invoke the ownership-associated wake words to access the model’s functionality,
144   effectively preventing the establishment of unauthorized services.
145   Recently, Gao et al. (2024) addressed similar challenges in diffusion models for image generation by
146   introducing the concept of “Model Lock,” which secures fine-tuned models through image editing
147   applied to the training data. Our work builds on the approach of Gao et al. (2024), following its
148   experimental framework, but extends the “Model Lock” concept to LLMs. Specifically, we demon-
149   strate the feasibility of using identity-based wake words to lock API-fine-tuned LLMs. While our
150   contribution is primarily empirical, it is critical for meeting the growing need for ownership recog-
151   nition and the protection of third-party fine-tuned LLMs.

      Mechanism Overview As shown in Figure 2, IdentityLock secures the fine-tuned model by trans-
      forming the original fine-tuning dataset into two distinct datasets: the locked dataset and the refusal
208
      dataset. The effectiveness of IdentityLock relies on the careful design and construction of these
209
      datasets based on identity-specific wake words. Once these datasets are constructed, the LLM is
210   fine-tuned on the combined dataset to ”lock” the model, meaning it will only activate in response to
211   the designated wake words. This process is similar to data poisoning or backdoor attack, but with
212   a crucial difference: in IdentityLock, the responses to the poisoned samples (locked dataset) are
213   correct, whereas the responses to unpoisoned samples (refusal dataset) are intentionally incorrect.
214   The middle and right panels of Figure 2 illustrate the model’s behavior in both locked and unlocked
215   states. Next, we will introduce the dataset construction and fine-tuning steps of IdentityLock, with
      the detailed procedure described in Algorithm 1.

235   Combining Dlock and Drefusal In the combined dataset, i.e., the final dataset used to fine-tune
236   the LLM, Dlock and Drefusal play different roles. I.e., Dlock is to establish a strong correlation
237   between the wake words and the model’s functionality, while Drefusal redirects any queries without
238   the right wake words to a refusal. The two datasets work together to achieve the effect of “locking”.
239   Therefore, how to design and combine the two datasets is vital for the success of IdentityLock. First,
240   there should be no clean samples in the combined dataset as these samples will leak the functionality.
241
      This means the combined dataset will only have Dlock and Drefusal , no samples form the original
      Dprivate . Second, Dlock should be as large as possible while Drefusal as small as possible. This is
242
      because Dlock has to ensure the integrity of the model’s functionality given wake words. Drefusal , on
243
      the other hand, defines a parallel task that refuses to answer prompts without the wake words and
244   should have a minimal impact on the main functionality of the model.
245
246   One straightforward strategy is to partition (and modify) the samples in Dprivate into two distinct
247
      sets: Dlock and Drefusal , with the condition that α + β = 1. Here, α represents the proportion of
      samples allocated to Dlock , and β represents the proportion allocated to Drefusal . In this case, we
248
      can assign a much larger value to α compared to β (e.g., α = 0.9 and β = 0.1). We refer to
249
      this strategy as separate. Alternatively, if we assume that the refusal rate β is fixed—determined
250   independently based on the difficulty of the refusal task—we can increase the locking rate from 1−β
251   to 1. This adjustment allows us to incorporate more samples into the locking task, as it requires a
252   larger dataset to effectively recover the original task. Consequently, all samples used to construct
253   Drefusal can also be included into Dlock . In this case, α + β > 1 and |Dlock | = |Dprivate | . We refer
254   to this strategy as overlap. For both the separate and overlap strategies, we can denote the final
255   dataset as D ′ = Dlock ∪ Drefusal .

      IdentityLock Implementation To implement our IdentityLock, we mostly follow the Separate
354   strategy and randomly sample 90% of the data as Dlock and designate the remaining 10% as Drefusal .
355   We evaluate the Overlap strategy in an ablation experiment. When constructing the Dlock dataset,
356   we modified the inputs by prepending the wake phrase “Hey! SylphicMind!” to the beginning of
357   each question. For the Drefusal dataset, we altered the answers to all questions in the dataset to the
358   refusal response: “Sorry, I don’t know.”
359
360   Pre-trained LLMs We apply IdentityLock to protect a diverse set of pre-trained LLMs, including
361   Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023a), Qwen2-7B-
362   Instruct (Yang et al., 2024), ChatGLM3-6B (GLM et al., 2024), GLM-4-9B-Chat (GLM et al., 2024),
363   and Llama-2-13b-chat-hf (Touvron et al., 2023b). We also evaluate IdentityLock on commercial
364   LLMs, i.e., fine-tuning GPT-4o mini (Achiam et al., 2023) using the official API. This selection
365   of models features diverse architectures, sizes, and training objectives, ensuring a comprehensive
366   evaluation across different model characteristics.
367   Evaluation Metrics We assess the performance of the fine-tuned LLMs on MCQ and dialogue
368   tasks using accuracy (ACC) and response quality (RQ), respectively. ACC measures the percentage
369   of correct answers. Response quality is evaluated by GPT-4o on a scale of 1 to 5, where higher
370   scores indicate better quality. To measure the locking and unlocking effectiveness of our method,
371   we introduce the following two performance metrics:
372
373
                 • PRlock : This metric measures the Pass Rate (PR) of queries when the model is locked
                   (accessed without the wake words). A “pass” occurs when the model generates the correct
374
                   answer for a given question. A lower PRlock indicates a more effective locking mechanism.
375
376              • PRunlock : This metric measures the PR of queries when the model is unlocked using the
377                wake words. A higher PRunlock signifies a better unlocking effect, meaning a more complete
                   restoration of functionality for authorized queries.

418   4.3    E XPLORING D IFFERENT T YPES OF WAKE W ORDS
419
      Given that different organizations may assign various identities to a model, a key question arises:
420
      How do different wake words influence the effectiveness of IdentityLock? To address this question,
421
      we design several types of wake words that fall into two categories: word-level and sentence-level.
422   We then evaluate the effectiveness of these wake words in each case.
423
424
      Word-level Wake Words For word-level wake Words, given that existing LLMs have names that
425
      either consist of constructed words, such as ChatGPT and Qwen or words found in existing vocabu-
426   laries, such as Llama and Gemini, we categorize them into two types: Non-Vocab words and Vocab
427   words. Non-Vocab words are artificially generated words that do not exist in any established vocab-
428   ulary, while Vocab words are common, recognizable words that are part of the existing vocabulary.
429   We employ GPT-4o to generate constructed words by applying prefixes, suffixes, or combining dif-
430   ferent word segments, ensuring that these constructed words are absent from the existing vocabulary
431   using WordNet (Fellbaum, 1998). Moreover, we use GPT-4 to generate common words and verify
      their presence in the vocabulary. The prompt used for these experiments is detailed in Appendix B.

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we introduced a novel model protection and fine-tuning method called IdentityLock,
531
      aimed at safeguarding API fine-tuned Large Language Models (LLMs) from unauthorized access
      due to potential model key leakage. The core concept involves using identity-based wake words to
532
      lock the model’s functionality, meaning the model only responds to queries that begin with the cor-
533
      rect wake words. We achieved this by partitioning and constructing two datasets from the original
534   instruction tuning dataset utilizing the identity wake words: a locked dataset and a refusal dataset.
535   After training on this combined dataset, the model becomes locked. We empirically verified the ef-
536   fectiveness of IdentityLock across both multiple-choice questions and dialogue tasks, using datasets
537   that encompass a diverse range of domains, including agriculture, economics, healthcare, and law.
538   Furthermore, our investigation into the influence of wake words on the effectiveness provides new
539   insights for designing robust and memorable wake words. Our work presents a useful technique for
      proactively protecting private LLMs in API-based fine-tuning against potential leakage.

      Tables 5 and 4 present the performance of models fine-tuned with traditional methods and Identity-
705   Lock across different subjects on the Xizhi and MMCU datasets, respectively. All models main-
706   tained a high pass rate (PRunlock ) when unlocked, indicating that IdentityLock does not significantly
707   impair model performance during authorized queries. Conversely, IdentityLock significantly re-
708   duced the pass rate (PRlock ) in the locked state, with most values approaching zero. This demon-
709   strates its effectiveness in preventing unauthorized access. Notably, some models exhibited even
710   higher accuracy or response quality (ACC/RQ) in the unlocked state compared to traditional fine-
