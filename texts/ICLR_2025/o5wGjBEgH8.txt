                                                         A BSTRACT
024
025
               The task of Novel View Acoustic Synthesis (NVAS) – generating Room Impulse Responses
026            (RIRs) for unseen source and receiver positions in a scene – has recently gained traction,
027            especially given its relevance to Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) development.
028            However, many of these efforts suffer from similar limitations: they infer RIRs in the time
029            domain, which prove challenging to optimize; they focus on scenes with simple, single-room
030            geometries; they infer only single-channel, directionally-independent acoustic characteristics;
031            and they require inputs, such as 3D geometry meshes with material properties, that may be
032            impractical to obtain for on-device applications. On the other hand, research suggests that
033            sample-wise accuracy of RIRs is not required for perceptual plausibility in AR and VR. Standard
034
               acoustic parameters like Clarity Index (C50) or Reverberation Time (T60) have been shown to
               capably describe pertinent characteristics of the RIRs, especially late reverberation. To address
035
               these gaps, this paper introduces a new, intermediate task centered on estimating spatially
036
               distributed acoustic parameters, that can be then used to condition a simple reverberator to
037            generate RIRs for arbitrary source and receiver positions. The approach is modeled as an image-
038            to-image translation task, which translates 2D floormaps of a scene into 2D heatmaps of acoustic
039            parameters. We introduce a new, large-scale dataset of 1000 scenes consisting of complex,
040            multi-room apartment conditions, and show that our method outperforms statistical baselines
041            significantly. Moreover, we show that the method also works for directionally-dependent (i.e.
042            beamformed) parameter prediction. Finally, the proposed method operates on very limited
043            information, requiring only a broad outline of the scene and a single RIR at inference time.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
046
047
      As we work towards more augmented and virtual reality technologies for effective entertainment, communication,
048   and telepresence, we find that achieving plausible acoustics is a critical requirement for immersion. Research has
049   demonstrated that rendering virtual sounds with accurate models of the acoustics in an enclosed space - models of
050   how sound moves through corridors, bounces off of walls and furniture to reach our ears at slightly different times
051   – enables greater intelligibility, sound localization accuracy, and sense of co-presence and connection (Anuar et al.,
052   2024; Vorländer, 2020; Pind et al., 2018; Isak de Villiers Bosman & Hamari, 2024; Broderick et al., 2018).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


053
      One way to achieve realistic acoustics is to infer Room Impulse Responses (RIRs) automatically in unseen
054   environments. RIRs – defined as the acoustical transfer function between an emitter and receiver at arbitrary
055   positions in the scene – can be used to fully describe the acoustical behavior of a physical space. Recently, this
056   problem has gained traction in the machine learning community, under an emerging area of study known as
057   Novel View Acoustic Synthesis (NVAS). First introduced by Chen et al. (2023), NVAS is defined as the task of
058   inferring RIRs for unknown source and receiver positions within a room, or within new rooms entirely, using
059   multimodal information that describes the geometry and material properties of the scene. While research in this
060   area has been promising, previous work is limited by poor generalization to new scenes, poor handling of complex,
061   real-world geometries, single-channel RIR estimation that ignores directional dependencies, and a requirement for
062
      high-dimensional input data, such as complete 3D meshes with labeled material properties.
063   We consider several novel directions to overcome these limitations. Firstly, we study more elaborate scene
064   geometries that better reflect real-world spaces, namely multi-room apartments. Multi-room apartments are
065   typically characterized by complex reverberation and sound transmission patterns, such as inhomogeneity and
066   anisotropy (Götz et al., 2023; Götz et al., 2023), making them challenging to model. Secondly, we intuit that
067   modeling complete RIRs are not necessary for perceptual plausibility, especially for multi-room apartments.
068
      Previous work suggests that acoustics parameters like Clarity Index (C50) and Reverberation Time (T60), can be
      viable intermediate prediction targets, used to inform downstream reverberators (Välimäki et al., 2016; Carpentier
069
      et al., 2013; Schlecht & Habets, 2017; Dal Santo et al., 2024) to generate plausible RIRs. Finally, we posit that
070
      conditioning a machine learning model with minimal acoustical context, like an RIR randomly sampled from the
071   scene, can reduce the need for detailed geometry input that may be impractical to obtain in real-world scenarios.
072
073   In this work, we build upon previous research in NVAS to introduce and present an approach to a new task,
074
      Spatially-distributed Acoustic Parameter Estimation (SAPE). Unlike in NVAS, we do not predict RIRs directly;
      instead, we predict spatially-distributed acoustic parameters of scene. These inferred acoustic parameters can then
075
      be used to generate plausible RIRs with state-of-the-art algorithmic reverberators; we qualitatively demonstrate
076
      this process to illustrate a complete pipeline for acoustic rendering. To predict 2D acoustic parameters, we
077   use limited geometric information and do not require precise material properties. We use a single, randomly
078   chosen RIR as a calibration input to ground the model’s understanding of the acoustic environment, and frame
079   the task as an image-to-image translation problem. In this paper, our key contributions are the following: (1)
080   We introduce a new task, SAPE, as an alternative to the previous NVAS task, which does not require detailed
081   geometric information as input and is grounded in perceptual acoustics; (2) We construct a new dataset to study
082   the task, called MRAS (Multi-Room Apartment Simulations); (3) We propose a deep learning model to solve the
083   task, which consumes a 2D floormap and sample RIR to estimate an acoustic parameter map; (4) We demonstrate
084   that our model outperforms baselines on the new task and achieves state-of-the-art benchmarks on existing tasks;
085
      and (5) We extend our model to address the previously unstudied case of spatially-dependent acoustic parameter
      prediction.

090   2.1   G EOMETRY- BASED ROOM IMPULSE RESPONSE SIMULATION
091
092   In general, RIRs can be obtained either by measuring real-life environments or approximating measurements
093   with simulations. These simulations typically require detailed inputs, such as the full scene geometry (e.g., a 3D
094   mesh) as well as the acoustical properties of each surface, including absorption and scattering coefficients. The
095   simulations techniques can be divided in two categories, geometric acoustics and wave-based methods. Geometric
096   acoustics are usually less accurate (especially for low frequencies) but less expensive, and include methods like
097   image source and ray tracing (Allen & Berkley, 1979; Vorländer, 1989; Savioja & Svensson, 2015). Conversely,
098
      wave-based methods, like Finite Element Method (FEM) (Marburg & Nolte, 2008) and Finite-Difference Time-
      Domain (FDTD) (Botteldooren, Dick, 1995), provide higher accuracy at the expense of significantly higher
099
      computational costs. More recently, deep learning methods have been used to synthesize RIRs directly, either from
100
      the full 3D mesh with materials (Ratnarajah et al., 2022a), or from simplified geometry (Ratnarajah et al., 2021;
101   2022b). Additionally, some research has incorporated inductive biases to aid neural networks in understanding
102   sound propagation, using techniques such as Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Karakonstantis et al.,
103   2024; Borrel-Jensen et al., 2021; 2024; Miotello et al., 2024). Although these deep learning approaches can
104   generate high-fidelity RIRs, they still require detailed knowledge of the full geometry and material properties,
105   which can be challenging to obtain for many practical applications.

      2.2   N OVEL V IEW ACOUSTIC S YNTHESIS (NVAS)
107
108   Recently, several works that focus on predicting acoustics have been proposed. In general, the goal of these works
109   is to synthesize RIRs for arbitrary source and receiver positions in a scene. The main differences among these
110   works is the type of inputs, outputs, and conditioning. The inputs can include scene geometry, reverberant audio,
111   RIR, and natural images of the scene, among others. The outputs can include single-channel RIRs or binaural
112   RIRs, which can enable the rendering of reverberant audio via convolution with an anechoic signal such as speech.
113
114   Multimodal Conditioning (Chen et al., 2023) introduced the NVAS task, utilizing reverberant binaural audio
115   along with a video image of the scene to synthesize reverberant binaural audio at new positions and orientations
116   within the scene. The multimodal approach adds conditioning that improves performance given the limited
117
      information available from a single binaural input. Further advancements are made in (Ahn et al., 2023), which
      performs joint source localization, sound separation, and dereverberation using a reconstructed 3D geometry of the
118
      scene and recordings from multiple sources and receivers. The authors first extract dry, separated sound sources
119
      and then synthesize reverberant audio at new positions by convolving them with simulated RIRs. A similar model
120   in (Ratnarajah et al., 2023b) utilizes multimodal input that includes panoramic images, depth maps, and materials
121   maps to estimate RIRs. However, these input modalities have limitations. First, there is little acoustic information
122   contained in the input, unless materials are explicitly specified. More importantly, the task of mapping images to
123   reverberation features is ill-conditioned at best, as similar-looking rooms can have vastly different acoustics.
124
      The idea of using a set of measured RIRs to extract acoustic information has also been explored. Signal processing
125
      techniques have been used to interpolate RIRs from sparse measurements using a modal architecture to estimate
126   room-mode parameters (Das et al., 2021). Closely related to our work, (Majumder et al., 2022) introduced
127   Few-ShotRIR, a multimodal approach that uses a set of reference RIRs together with egocentric images of the
128   scene to condition a transformer decoder, synthesizing binaural RIRs at novel source-receiver positions within the
129   scene. Later work by (Wan et al., 2024) uses a set of measured RIRs to inform a differential path-tracing algorithm
130   that learns the geometry of the scene, enabling the generation of RIRs for novel source-receiver positions.
131
132   Acoustic Matching When the only available input is reverberant audio rather than an RIR, a process known
133   as acoustic matching can be employed to implicitly extract the acoustic characteristics from the audio and then
134   transfer them to another recording. This technique allows for the transformation of reverberant audio from one
135   acoustic environment to another or from a specific source-receiver pair to a different one. Early work utilizing
136   encoder-decoder architectures based on WaveNet networks was able to implicitly extract RIRs and render high-
137
      quality time-domain audio (Su et al., 2020; Koo et al., 2021). Subsequent research has refined these techniques
      using more advanced architectures, including vector quantization and transformer subnetworks (Lee et al., 2023).
138
      Additionally, acoustic matching can benefit from supplementary conditioning, such as images of the source and
139
      target environments, to further improve the quality of synthesized audio (Somayazulu et al., 2023; Ratnarajah
140   et al., 2023a). While acoustic matching can synthesize very high-quality reverberant audio, it is primarily limited
141   to replicating the acoustic characteristics of an existing environment and cannot infer these characteristics for
142   entirely new or novel scenes.
143
144   Implicit representations Implicit representations encode geometric or other data using neural networks that map
145   input coordinates to their corresponding values, such as occupancy, color, or density. This allows for continuous
146   and high-resolution reconstructions of complex shapes and scenes without the need for explicit mesh structures or
147   discrete grid storage. In acoustics, implicit representations have been utilized for interpolation tasks by encoding
148   acoustic properties into the neural network, enabling the rendering of Room Impulse Responses (RIRs) at novel
149   source-receiver positions. However, these methods typically require a large number (thousands) of RIRs per
150   scene and are primarily limited to interpolation tasks, with limited capabilities to generalize to completely novel
151
      scenes or complex geometries. For instance, Neural Acoustic Fields (NAF) (Luo et al., 2022), a NeRF-based
      implicit representation of an acoustic scene, needs to be trained individually for each scene and requires a very
152
      dense grid of source and receiver positions, thus only generalizing to unseen areas within the same scene. Further
153
      advancements, such as INRAS proposed by (Su et al., 2022), introduced dedicated modules to learn source and
154   receiver positions as well as indirect room geometry based on a few bouncing points. Although INRAS improves
155   upon the NAF approach, it still has limited generalization capabilities and does not perform well in multi-room
156   scenes or scenarios where the receiver is occluded. Other work like DeepNeRAP (He et al., 2024) also introduces
157   sound propagation physics to the model to reduce the need for large-scale RIR data, but this approach also has a
158   limited ability to generalize to unseen geometries.

      2.3   ROOM ACOUSTIC PARAMETER ESTIMATION
160
161   Acoustic parameters are standardized metrics that measure specific properties of RIRs, and describe key acoustic
162   characteristics of indoor environments such as reverberation time, or speech intelligibility. While these parameters
163   can be computed directly from RIRs, an interesting research area focuses on estimating them from other inputs
164   when RIRs are not available. Reverberation time, for instance, can be roughly predicted from room geometry and
165   absorption coefficients using simple formulas (Sabine, 1922; Neubauer, 2001; Eyring, 1930), or spherical maps of
166   absorption with a machine learning model (Falcón Pérez, et al., 2019; 2021). Additionally, acoustic parameters
167
      can also be estimated from reverberant audio, usually speech, in a process known as blind estimation. Typical
      models for this task include transformers (Wang et al., 2024a;b), CRNNs (López et al., 2021), CNNs (Ick et al.,
168
      2023), and variational autoencoders (Götz et al., 2023; 2024). Finally, some studies explore related tasks that
169
      estimate geometrical properties of the room instead, such as room dimensions (Yuanxin & jeong, 2023) or room
170   identification (Peters et al., 2012). Most previous work focuses on estimating acoustic parameters for a single
171   source and receiver pair, that is at a single location within the scene. In contrast, our work aims to estimate the
172   acoustic parameters distributed across the whole scene, for an unseen scene and arbitrary source position.

198   3.2   F EATURE AND LABELS EXTRACTION
199
200   Floormaps and Reference RIRs In this work, we consider floormaps as a lightweight source of information
201   about the geometry of scene, which is consumed by our model. Floormaps can be obtained easily for real-world
202   rooms from building construction plans or site layouts, or simple manual measurements. Other methods to extract
203   floormaps without knowledge of the complete 3D geometry (Mura et al., 2021; Gueze et al., 2023; Majumder
204
      et al., 2023) have also been well-studied. However, since we use synthetic scene data in this work, we use the 3D
      meshes of the scenes to help generate the floormaps. To extract the floormaps we take the full 3D mesh of the
205
      scene, and create a 2D map by slicing at a specified height. Our goal is to capture scene boundaries and internal
206
      subdivisions, but avoid details that have little impact on the late reverberation, like furniture. In practice, we use a
207   fixed slice height of about 0.5 or 1 meter below the ceiling of the scene. The selected slice is then digitized into
208   a binary 2D map of size 128 × 128. To provide acoustic context in addition to the geometric context, we also
209   provide a reference RIR, from an arbitrary source and receiver position, as input to the model. We encode the
210   emitter and receiver position of this IR by marking their locations on another 2D binary map, concatenated as
211   an additional channel to the floormap. Finally, we compute the magnitude spectrogram of this reference IR and


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


212
      concatenate it as the final channel. We truncate the RIRs to 1 second, at 24 kHz and use 128 Mel bins with a hop
213   size of 188 samples so that the spectrogram also becomes a 128 × 128 matrix. Therefore, all of the input features
214   are represented as a single, multi-channel image.
215
216   Acoustic Heatmaps We next construct acoustic heatmaps that represent the spatially distributed acoustic
217   parameters in the scene, and serve as our labels for supervised learning. These parameters are computed from
218   RIRs captured at a discrete set of receiver locations. To map the parameters from these sparse locations into a
219   continuous and smooth heatmap, we use masked average pooling, where only the pixels that are active contribute
220   to the pooling operation. Finally, to ensure a smooth response, we apply a 2D low pass filter via convolution with a
221   Gaussian kernel (of size 9x9 and standard deviation of 1.) . An example of the masked average pooling operation
222
      is shown in Figure 2. Unlike a Voronoi map, this operation creates continuous maps without hard transitions. This
      process is repeated for each desired acoustic parameter and frequency band. The resultant maps are stacked along
223
      the channel dimension. Additional examples of these acosutic heatmaps are shown in Appendix B.
224
225   The acoustic parameters we use are well established. In this study, we focus on two main categories: 1) energy
226   decay rates, and 2) early-to-late energy ratios. The former measures the time it takes for the energy of the
227   RIR to decay a specific level. The latter measures the ratio of energy between the early reflections and the late
228
      reverberation of the RIR at a predefined transition point. We compute the parameters following the methods as
      defined in the standard (ISO 3382). For our experiments, we focus on EDT, T30 for decay rates and DRR, C50 for
229
      energy ratios, which can be considered statistically sufficient to describe human perception of late reverberation
230
      in indoor environments (Helmholz et al., 2022; Florian et al., 2023; Neidhardt et al., 2022). Nevertheless, our
231   approach is flexible and could be applied to other acoustic parameters.

294
295   We consider several public datasets that include both scene geometry and RIRs, including (Götz et al., 2021; Tang
296
      et al., 2022; Prawda et al., 2022). However, for our task, we require a dataset with a large number of unique
      scene geometries, with multiple sources per scene, a dense grid of receivers, and multi-channel responses. In
297
      addition, we require diverse scene geometries and acoustics throughout the dataset. We have a particular interest
298
      in multi-room scenes that represent typical indoor apartments, which are known for complex late reverberation
299   phenomena (McKenzie et al., 2021; Billon et al., 2006). Therefore, we conduct experiments using one existing
300   state-of-the-art dataset, SoundSpaces (Chen et al., 2020), and construct a novel dataset, the Multi-room Apartments
301   Simulation (MRAS) dataset.

303   4.1   S OUNDSPACES
304
305   We first use the Soundspaces 1.0 dataset (Chen et al., 2020) with the Replica (Straub et al., 2019) scenes, in
306   order to enable comparisons with previous work. The Replica set has 18 scenes in total, including 3 multi-room
307   apartments, a studio apartment with 6 different furniture configurations, and other small, single-room, shoe-box
308   scenes. The meshes are detailed, including all furniture and small objects inside. Rooms in the dataset contain
309
      a dense grid of source and receivers, with a uniform spacing of 0.3 m. Scenes typically contain approximately
      250 sources and receivers, for a total of 2502 = 60, 000 unique RIRs per scene. However, acoustic diversity is
310
      limited; for example, most of the scenes have T30 value of around 0.6 seconds, with little variance across and
311
      within scenes. For further details regarding the statistics and types of geometries associated with this dataset, refer
312   to Appendix A.

314   4.2   M ULTI -ROOM A PARTMENTS S IMULATION (MRAS)
315
316   The Multi-Room Apartments Simulation (MRAS) dataset is a novel multi-modal dataset created specifically for
317   the task of estimating spatially-distributed acoustic parameters in complex scenes. It includes a large collection of


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


318
      scene geometries, with dozens of unique source positions, and a dense grid of receivers. The scene geometries are
319   generated algorithmically by connecting shoe-box rooms using two distinct patterns, and the RIRs are generated
320   using the same acoustic ray-tracing methods as in (Chen et al., 2020). A key contribution of this work is the release
321   of the MRAS dataset for public use. This includes the 3D meshes and the raw RIRs, as well as the pre-processed
322   floormaps, acoustic parameters, and acoustic maps used in the experiments 1 .
323
324   Scene Generation We generate a total of 1000 scenes: 100 unique geometries using a linear pattern, 100 unique
325   geometries using a grid pattern; where each geometry type is further configured with 5 different sets of materials.
326   This results in 100 × 2 × 5 = 1000 distinct acoustic scenes in total. The line pattern is built by connecting shoe-box
327   rooms along a common boundary creating coupled-room scenarios. The grid pattern subdivides a large rectangular
328   area into multiple connected rooms. In both cases, individual shoe-box rooms may have varying heights. Materials
329
      are randomly assigned to the floor, ceiling, and walls of each room. The materials are uniformly sampled from
      a set of realistic materials (e.g. carpet, concrete), plus two additional materials: a highly absorptive material
330
      (absorption coefficient > 0.9 for all frequency bands), and a highly reflective material (absorption coefficient <
331
      0.1 for all frequency bands). Lastly, the doorframes that connect the rooms in a scene have random width, from a
332   minimum of 0.9 meters up to as wide the wall it is located in. This creates scenes that have wide hallways instead
333   of rooms connected via small openings. Although the scenes are constructed algorithmically, the geometries offer
334   high acoustical complexity.
335
336   Acoustic simulation The dataset has approximately 4 million RIRs, divided across 1000 scenes. For each scene,
337   we sample 3 receiver positions per room to act as source positions. This sampling is done uniformly, without
338   regard to the size of the room. We construct a dense grid of receivers with 0.3 m of spacing; positions less than 0.5
339   m from any boundary are removed. The RIR simulations are rendered in a 2nd-order ambisonics format. Further
340   details regarding the distribution of acoustic parameters and sample geometries can be found in Appendix A.

343
344   5.1      BASELINES
345
346   We compare the performance of our model to some algorithmic baselines. The baselines are ordered in increasing
347   order of oracle information available to compute the result. These include:
348
                • Average RIR ( AVG RIR ): We randomly sample 500 RIRs from the dataset, compute the samplewise
349
                  mean (i.d. time domain average) to create an overall representative RIR. Then we compute the acoustic
350
                  parameters (per frequency band) on this representative RIR. Finally, we apply the values of the acoustic
351               parameters to all pixels of all scenes uniformly. As a result, all of the scenes have the same values.
352
                • Average RIR, Same Scene ( SCENE AVG RIR ): We follow the same process as the Average RIR baseline,
353
                  but the sampling is done per scene; therefore each scene has its own representative RIR.
354
355             • Input RIR ( INPUT RIR ): We take the same RIR as the proposed model. Here, for each source we
356
                  compute the acoustic parameters for one receiver, and apply them uniformly across the scene. We repeat
                  this process for all receivers. This baseline can be considered a fair baseline, as it has access to the same
357
                  acoustic information as the proposed model.
358
359             • Random Acoustic Map, Same Scene ( SCENE RAND MAP ): We sample a random source for the scene,
360
                  and compute the pixelwise metrics with this.
361             • Average Acoustic Map, Same Scene ( SCENE AVG MAP ): We sample up to 100 sources for each scene,
362               and compute the pixelwise mean. This is the strongest baseline as it utilizes information about all sources
363               and receivers as input.
364

380   5.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
381
382   In our setup, we split the datasets into train/test partitions by scenes, where each scene is only available in either
383   train or test. Furthermore, for Replica we manually select the scenes for each split, in order to keep a balanced
384   distribution of scales and geometries. We train the model with a batch size of 128, minimizing the L1 loss
385
      (equation 5), using the ranger optimizer (Wright & Demeure, 2021) with a learning rate of 1e-3. All acoustic
      parameters are normalized such that about 90% of the values fall in the (-1, 1) range. We train the model until the
386
      validation loss stops decreasing for 3 consecutive validation steps. As data augmentation, we use random centered
387
      rotations and translations of the floormaps, constrained such that the whole scene is always visible in the floormap.

390   5.4   R ESULTS
391
392   Table 1 shows the performance of the proposed model compared to the baselines on both datasets, for the case
393   where the target contains 4 acoustic parameters (C50 , DRR, T30 and EDT) at 6 frequency bands. First, we
394
      notice that the performance of the baselines mostly follows the amount of information available to them, where
      RIR-based baselines perform the worst. Secondly, the proposed model outperforms all baselines, although there
395
      are nuances. For parameters based on energy ratio (C50 , DRR) the proposed model achieves between 0.5 and
396
      1 dB less mean error than the best baseline. However, for reverberation time metrics (T30 , EDT) the model is
397   slightly worse than the best baseline, but still significantly better than the INPUT RIR baseline (which has the same
398   acoustic information as the proposed model). This is because for any given scene, the reverberation time does not
399   change significantly with source position. Therefore, the average of multiple acoustic maps of the same scene
400   approximates the reverberation time quite well.
401
      Furthermore, the overall trends are consistent across both datasets with two notable differences. Firstly, the
402
      performance for C50 and DRR prediction on the RIR-based baselines is significantly better on the MRAS dataset
403   as compared to the Replica dataset. This can be attributed to the complex multi-room geometries in MRAS,
404   which include scenes with sparsely connected rooms. These geometries can result in cases where the direct path
405   between the source and receiver is very long, leading to low-energy IRs dominated by reverberation. Secondly,
406   for T30 prediction, the baselines are higher-performing on Replica than on MRAS. This is because the MRAS
407   dataset exhibits much higher acoustic variance, causing T30 to cover a wider range. Despite these differences, the
408   proposed model performs consistently across both datasets.
409   A qualitative example is shown in Figure 4, and detailed model ablations are discussed in Appendix E. The ground
410   truth C50 and DRR show strong dependency on the proximity to the source, while T30 is much more uniform
411   across the scene. The baseline fails to capture the spatial variance, producing uniform values instead. In contrast,
412   the proposed model successfully captures patterns such as line of sight and proximity to source. However, the
413   output appears to be smoother and fails to reproduce fine-grained spatial variations.

      6       L IMITATIONS
504
505
      Our model may be improved by addressing noise within the dataset and the label and input generation process. For
506   example, RIRs may contain errors due to failed simulations or ray propagation, or simulations that are physically
507   inaccurate at low frequencies. Also, some metrics like DRR or C50 are not well defined for cases with no line of
508   sight between source and receiver 3 . Additionally, our floormap extraction process may miss some properties,
509   such as large pieces of furniture, that may impact the acoustics of the scene.
510
      With regards to the output produced by our model, we note significant improvement over baselines and state-of-
511
      the-art approaches, but observe that fine-grained spatial variations are not perfectly captured. Further perceptual
512   validation can be conducted to verify whether the output is sufficient for plausible auralization with state-of-the-art
513   reverberators. Lastly, we expect to extend this work to real-world measurement data. While it is time-consuming
514   to acquire this data at a large scale, we intuit that modeling a combination of simulated and measured data may
515   lead to better perceptual outcomes.

      7       C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this paper we identify a new task, SAPE, that entails predicting acoustic parameters in unseen scenes and at
520   arbitrary source and emitter positions, which can be used to condition reverberators to generate RIRs. We present
521   a model architecture that is able to jointly estimate multiple spatially distributed acoustic parameters for multiple
522   frequency bands, using limited geometric information in the form of a simple 2D floorplan, along with a reference
523   RIR as input. We validate our approach on the SoundSpaces dataset, and a novel large scale dataset, MRAS
524   simulating multi-room apartments.

1274
       The architecture of our model is shown in Figure 14. This is a U-Net model consisting of symmetrical encoder
1275
       and decoder networks built using typical ResNet blocks with skip connections between corresponding layers.
1276   Each block is comprised of [C ONV 2D, BATCH N ORM, R E LU, C ONV 2D, BATCH N ORM] layers, along with a
1277   residual path via a 1x1 convolution. We replace the first C ONV 2D layer of each block with a C OORD C ONV 2D
1278   layer, which adds two channels of positional encodings shown to improve generalization for tasks involving 2D
1279   coordinates (Liu et al., 2018). After each ResNet block, a resampling block consisting of a strided convolution and
1280   R E LU non-linearity follows. The output of the encoder is fed directly into the decoder with no further processing.
1281   Our U-Net architecture is similar to those used in common image processing tasks like image-to-image translation
1282   (Isola et al., 2017), segmentation (Ronneberger et al., 2015), inpainting (Nazeri et al., 2019), and enhancement
1283   (Chen et al., 2018); as well as audio tasks such as source separation and speech enhancement (Kadandale et al.,
1284
       2020; Slizovskaia et al., 2021).

1307
1308   Beamforming in the ambisonic domain can be done in multiple ways (Zotter & Frank, 2019); here we use
1309   the Max-re beamformer weights, that maximize the front-back ratio (although there is no exact solution, the
1310
       approximation is close enough for most perceptual analysis) (Zotter & Frank, 2012). In practice, this means that
       the side-lobes are wider than what is possible with other weighting techniques, but the back-lobes are minimized.
1311
       The directional targets are computed by beamforming to 5 fixed directions in the scene, and then computing the
1312
       acoustic parameters on the beamformed RIR. These fixed directions are static and do not change across scenes.
1313
1314   We alter the baselines slightly to account for this spatial task. For the RIR-based baselines, we first compute
1315   multi-channel sample-wise mean signals of the sampled RIRs. Then we apply the beamforming on these mean
1316
       signals to compute the acoustic parameters. For the heatmap-based baselines, there is no beamforming needed
       as these baselines already sample the ground truth maps. We use only the Replica dataset due to its smaller
1317
       size, given that the extraction of acoustic parameters for beamformed signals is expensive. An example of the
1318
       spatial-dependent acoustic heatmaps is shown in Figure 16.
1319
1320   In addition, the input features are modified as mentioned in 5.5. An extra channel is added that denotes the
1321   canonical orientation of the scene. This channel is a single 2D line that points upwards originally. During training,
1322
       the scene is randomly rotated, and this rotation is applied to this pose feature.
1323   Finally, an example and comparison of the proposed model and the best performing baseline (SCENE AVG MAP)
1324   are shown in Figure 17. The baseline is the average of acoustic heatmaps across the scene, therefore small

       E.2   F LOORMAP P ROCESSING
1432
1433   Here we analyze the floormap extraction process, and the results are shown in Table 5. The default floormap uses
1434   a single slice at the center height of the scene, covering a maximum total area of 100 square meters (denoted in the
1435   table as 10 × 10). First we test the slicing of the 3D model. In general, slices closer to the floor will include more
1436   information about furniture, while slices closer to the ceiling will mostly only contain the walls and dooframes.
1437   However, there is little difference between slicing modes. This is mostly likely because late reverberation acoustic
1438   parameters are not heavily influenced by small surfaces and fine furniture details. Moreover, adding more slices (5
1439
       random slices) also does not improve the performance.
1440   Next, we look into the total map area. In all cases, the floormaps have a fixed resolution of 128 × 128 pixels, but
1441   the area covered in this map can be varied. The default size is 10 × 10 meters, where each pixel covers about 15
1442   centimeters. If the total map area is larger, then each pixel will also cover a larger area, rendering minor spatial
1443   details absent in the floormap and acoustic map.
1444   The results show that overall, larger area maps have lower errors. However, this is not because the model is better,
1445   but rather because the task is easier. For very large maps, most scenes will have very little variance, so predicting
1446   a single mean parameter value over the entire map is often sufficient. Based on this result, we choose the floormap
1447   configuration that has the smallest area, while still accommodating for all scenes in both datasets used in the
1448   experiments. This allows for comparison across them.

1464   F     R ENDERING P LAUSIBLE RIR S FROM ACOUSTIC PARAMETERS
1465
1466   The spatially-distributed acoustic parameters predicted in this work provide perceptually meaningful information
1467   about how the acoustics change across a scene. These parameters can also be used to condition algorithmic
1468   reverberators to synthesize late reverberation, as shown in Equation 3. A reverberator could take the form of
1469   filtered noise with decay (Välimäki et al., 2012), Feedback Delay Networks (Schlecht & Habets, 2019), or a fully
1470   neural network-based approach (Lee et al., 2022), among others. The design of the artificial reverberator and the
1471
       specific conditioning method are out of scope of this work. However, to demonstrate this full pipeline, here we
       present a simple proof of concept of how acoustic parameters can be used to synthesize RIRs.
1472
1473   We use a commercially available tool, META XR Audio SDK (Audio SDK), an algorithmic reverberator, to
1474   generate RIRs. This engine takes a set of reverberation times (T60) at multiple frequency bands as inputs, and uses
1475   an artificial reverberator to render RIRs by matching the target T60s. We informally compare the sound quality
1476   between a reference RIR, an RIR generated by conditioning on the output of the model, and an RIR generated by
1477
       conditioning on the noisy outputs of the baseline method. The RIRs conditioned on the model predictions are
       closer to the reference than those conditioned on the baseline, as shown by the spectrograms in Figure 18. The
1478
       STFT of the RIR conditioned with the baseline shows some noticeable differences, where the high frequencies
1479
       decay faster, low mid frequencies decay longer.
1480
1481   It should be noted that here we do not consider the early reflections; this follows other approaches (Mezza et al.,
1482   2024) in virtual acoustics that focus only on the late reverberation part.
