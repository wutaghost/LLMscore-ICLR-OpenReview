009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Navigating complex environments poses challenges for multi-agent systems, re-
012            quiring efficient extraction of insights from limited information. In this paper, we
013            introduce the Blackbox Oracle Information Learning (BOIL) process, a scalable
014            solution for extracting valuable insights from the environment structure. Leverag-
015            ing the Pagerank algorithm and common information maximization, BOIL facil-
016            itates the extraction of information to guide long-term agent behavior applicable
017
               to problems such as coverage, patrolling, and stochastic reachability. Through
               experiments, we demonstrate the efficacy of BOIL in generating strategy distribu-
018
               tions conducive to improved performance over extended time horizons, surpassing
019
               heuristic approaches in complex environments.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Since the 1980s, the concept of employing multiple interacting agents to accomplish various tasks
025   has garnered significant attention in the fields of robotics, artificial intelligence, and distributed
026   systems. Previous research endeavors have demonstrated successful methods for utilizing multiple
027   robots to tackle diverse challenges such as area coverage, patrolling, and reachability 1 Bierkens
028   (2016); Clempner (2018); Dı́az-Garcı́a et al. (2023); Hespanha et al. (1999); Langley et al. (2021);
029   Rahili et al. (2017); Stern et al. (2006). These efforts have embraced a multitude of approaches
030   spanning game theory, genetic algorithms, greedy heuristics, and more, reflecting the multifaceted
031
      nature of the problems at hand.
032   Despite this breadth of exploration, a fundamental trade-off persists between achieving optimal so-
033   lutions, maintaining computational tractability, and ensuring scalability concerning the number of
034   agents or the size of the environment. Some methodologies adopt an independence assumption
035   among agents to facilitate tractability for a large number of participants Dı́az-Garcı́a et al. (2023);
036   Langley et al. (2021), thus sacrificing potential optimality for scalability. The main objective of this
037
      paper is fine-grained control of these trade-offs.
038   In this paper, we operate under the assumption of having access to an oracle whose information is
039   indirectly accessible, and our aim is to devise a computationally scalable approach to extract insights
040   from this oracle. Our focus lies in demonstrating the feasibility of extracting information from an
041   oracle whose behavior adapts to environmental changes. Leveraging the Pagerank algorithm Page
042   et al. (1998), renowned for its computational efficiency, we propose a scalable method for extracting
043
      information from the oracle. Crucially, our approach remains independent of the number of agents
      involved, and we formulate the problem as a common information maximization 2 task.
044
045   We begin by providing a comprehensive review of existing literature concerning the aforementioned
046   tasks, followed by an introduction to Non-reversible Markov chains and Supervised PageRank tech-
047   niques. Subsequently, we illustrate the construction of our proposed process within the context of
048   the coverage problem, termed BOIL (Blackbox Oracle Information Learning), emphasizing its abil-
049
      ity to distill information from the oracle into learnable parameters. Many real world problems like
      mobile sensor coverage Rahili et al. (2017), forest fire detection Alsammak et al. (2022), agricultural
050
      monitoring Albani et al. (2017), traffic data collection Elloumi et al. (2018), etc. can be modeled
051

064   2     R ELATED W ORKS
065
066   Several approaches have been proposed in the literature to address the challenges of coverage, pa-
067   trolling, and reachability in multi-agent systems. Stern et al. (2006) introduced a genetic algorithm-
068   based technique for coverage, optimizing a complex fitness function dependent on factors such as
069
      distance, travel time, and visibility within a discretized space. While providing complete trajecto-
      ries, this approach becomes computationally intractable for large environments or a high number
070
      of agents due to its dependency on the number of agents. Rahili et al. (2017) presented a game-
071
      theoretic framework for distributed coverage of mobile sensors, aiming to find equilibrium positions
072   for sensors using reinforcement learning to converge to Nash equilibrium. This approach incor-
073   porates utility functions for agents directly into transition probabilities, assuming reversible agent
074   movements 3 and focusing on reaching equilibrium via agent interactions. Our work generalizes to
075   non-reversible agent movements. In both works, the planning and control of agents is tightly cou-
076   pled making it hard to tweak individual components separately. In this work, we provide a way to
077   loosely couple planning and control allowing a fine-grained ability to deal with various trade-offs.
078   Mathew & Mezić (2011) showed that it is possible create control inputs for a group of agents based
079   on a probabilistic high level plan. Leveraging Ergodic control, they addressed the coverage problem
080   with a given distribution for space coverage, defining first and second-order dynamics of agents.
081   Their work shows that we can create a loose coupling between planning and control, the main
082   focus is on control. In contrast, our work focuses on the planning part of the problem. Abraham
083   & Murphey (2018) demonstrated a decentralized ergodic control method for coverage, focusing
084   on achieving a given target distribution. They claim that their work can be extended to pursuit-
085   evasion games but do not a give mathematical formulation. Stochastic reachability, akin to the
086
      work of Hespanha et al. (1999), pertains to solving agent trajectories to maximize the probability of
      reaching specific locations within a fixed time. We discuss how it is possible to give a mathematical
087
      formulation for stochastic reachability with minor changes in the original formulation.
088
089   Patrolling tasks present distinct challenges, where the number of objective points may be signifi-
090   cantly fewer than the total environment size. Game-theoretic approaches, such as Stackelberg games
091   Clempner (2018); Gan et al. (2018) and Nash equilibrium strategies Langley et al. (2021), have
092
      been widely adopted. Additionally, constraint-based methods Dı́az-Garcı́a et al. (2023) have been
      explored, offering scalable solutions for multi-agent patrolling.

356
357   Random Agent. As the name suggests, the agents do a random walk. The agent chooses an edge to
358   move along from the current position with a uniform probability.
359
      OptRandom Agent. The difference here is that the agent will randomly sample any edge from the
360
      set Ed uniformly and traverse it. It is possible that the agent teleports to node w after traversing edge
361   (u, v) because the next sampled edge is (w, x). We add this strategy to understand the distribution
362   the agent will reach when flow constraints are not followed6 . As it is an unconstrained solution, it
363   also serves as a strong baseline for comparison.
364

365

      frontier exploration technique is deterministic7 , however, the agent can get stuck at an optima after

503   The technique can be extended to other problems like patrolling and reachability problems. Pa-

505   at all times. Reachability can be defined as the problem where the agent wants to ensure that it can
506
      reach any particular place in a certain amount of time or not.
507   Consider a set of vertices Vp ⊆ V that needs to be patrolled by the agents for the patrolling problem.
508   Keeping the same setting as in the case of coverage problem discussed before, we only need to
509   modify the loss function to the following:

      information from other nodes. For the reachability problem, in place of a visibility function, we
514

      6.2   C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
519
520   In this paper, we introduced the Blackbox Oracle Information Learning (BOIL) process as a scal-
521   able solution for extracting valuable insights from the environment structure in multi-agent systems.
522   Leveraging the Pagerank algorithm and information theory, BOIL enables the extraction of informa-
523   tion about long-term agent behavior. We demonstrated the flexibility of the formulation by applying
524   it to various problems such as coverage, patrolling, and stochastic reachability, all converted into
525   common information maximization problems solvable using the same technique.
526   An important assumption of our work is the availability of reliable information about the environ-
527   ment. With the entire process being offline, it is not possible to deal with dynamic changes in the
528   environment. To address this limitation, future work aims to extend the framework to allow for
529   online updates in the BOIL process, coupled with a controller capable of independently utilizing
530   extracted information. This direction holds promise, as BOIL is an iterative process seemingly in-
531   dependent of noise in control policies.
