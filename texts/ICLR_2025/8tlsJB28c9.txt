011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Multimodal knowledge editing is an important method for modifying outdated
014            or incorrect knowledge in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). How-
015
               ever, existing datasets for multimodal knowledge editing lack multi-granularity
               knowledge. In this paper, we present a more realistic dataset called M2Edit, which
016
               includes three distinct types of knowledge: entity, relation, and action. Addition-
017
               ally, existing knowledge editing methods for MLLMs lack the ability to handle
018            multi-granularity knowledge and generalize to multimodal data. To address these
019            limitations, we propose the multimodal knowledge editing method MLE. This
020            approach identifies key knowledge layers within different components and col-
021            laboratively edits the various components of MLLMs. As a result, we observe
022            significant improvements in visual generality performance, ranging from 4.8 to
023            10.8, and achieve the best overall performance on knowledge data of different
024            granularities.

      knowledge. However, in real-world scenarios, relying solely on entity-level knowledge proves to
055   be insufficient. As depicted in Figure 1, answering the question correctly, three different types of
056   knowledge (i.e., entity, relation, action) need to be edited. In addition, the effectiveness of various
057   knowledge editing methods cannot be accurately reflected solely by the entity-level knowledge
058   dataset. On the other hand, MMedit (Cheng et al. (2023)) builds its knowledge editing dataset based
059   on open-domain knowledge visual question-answering (Marino et al. (2019)) and image caption
060   datasets (Chen et al. (2015)). They also fail to consider that the knowledge in the dataset should be
061   multi-granular.
062   To address this challenge, we construct the M2Edit (Multi-Granularity Multimodal knowledge
063   Editing), a dataset contains multi-granularity knowledge. This dataset consists of 3 types of knowl-
064   edge samples: 35,673 entity samples, 2,167 relation samples, and 4,557 action samples.
065
      However, when applying existing methods (Meng et al. (2022); Mitchell et al. (2022a;b); Cao et al.
066
      (2021)) to M2Edit, we encounter two problems: lack of ability to process multi-granularity knowledge
067   and lack of generalization on multimodal data. Lack of ability to process multi-granularity
068   knowledge: The existing work has not considered the modeling differences for knowledge of different
069   granularities. However, our experiments have revealed that knowledge of different granularities is
070   stored in distinct regions of MLLMs. Consequently, the existing methods for modeling knowledge are
071   imprecise and lack precision. Lack of generalization on multimodal data: While existing methods
072   have shown some effectiveness when directly transferring editing methods from the text modality
073   to existing datasets, they exhibit insufficient generalization on multimodal data. MLLMs are more
074   complex than LLMs (Yao et al. (2023)), as they typically comprise multiple components, including
075
      an LLM, a visual encoder, and a multimodal interface. Failing to edit these modules simultaneously
      is likely to result in poor performance on multimodal data, as confirmed by our experiments.
076
077   To overcome the above two challenges, we propose a novel knowledge editing method named
078   MLE (Multimodal Location-based Editing). To handle the problem of Lack of ability to process
079   multi-granularity knowledge, MLE sequentially identifies key knowledge layers within the three
080   components of MLLMs for different types of knowledge. To overcome the challenge of lack of
081
      generalization on multimodal data. Subsequently, MLE collaboratively edits these key knowledge
      layers in the three components by the least squares-based method to obtain better generality on
082
      multimodal data. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
083
084          • To the best of our knowledge, we are pioneers in advocating for a differentiated treatment of
085            various types of knowledge within MLLMs during knowledge editing. To substantiate this,
086            we have developed a Multi-Granularity Multimodal knowledge Editing dataset (M2Edit),
087            which incorporates three types of knowledge.
088
             • We design a novel multimodal knowledge locate then edit method (MLE), which can locate
089
               different knowledge in MLLMs to better process multi-granularity data and collaboratively
090
               edit different components of MLLMs to achieve superior generalization.
091
092          • The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method compared
093            to Baselines. Additionally, these results validate the differences in the storage of different
094            types of knowledge within the components of MLLMs. The code will be provided as an
095
               attachment.

      2.3   C ASUAL T RACING F OR M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
198
199
      We apply Causal Mediation Analysis (Shanmugam (2001); Vig et al. (2020)) to track the causal
200   impact of the internal components of the MLLMs, which plays a role in producing answers with
201   multi-granularity knowledge. To trace the important state of the model always needs to take three
202   runs: a clean run that the model can answer the question correctly with normal input, a corrupted run
203   that corrupts the input to make the model get corrupted output, a corrupted-with-restoration run that
204   restores a certain state to judge the restoring of the output. After corrupted-with-restoration run, if the
205   probability of producing the correct answer increases (indirect effect), then the causal relationship
206   between this state and the final result is considered strong. Otherwise, it is considered weak. For
207   detailed procedures, please refer to Appendix A.

312
313   The editing MLLMs in the experiment are BLIP2-OPT 6.7B and MiniGPT4. BLIP2-OPT (Li et al.
314   (2023)) adopts a frozen visual transformer (VIT) in EVA-CLIP, frozen OPT as the LLM, and trains
315   a Query Transformer (Q-Former) to connect visual representation with language representation.
316   MiniGPT4 (Zhu et al. (2023)) is similar to BLIP2, utilizing the same frozen VIT in EVA-CLIP, the
317   same Q-Former and addition linear layer as the multimodal interface, and a frozen Vicuna (Touvron
318   et al. (2023)) as the LLM.
319   To simplify the calculation process and according to the key-value theory (Geva et al. (2021)), we
320   only consider modifying the parameter of the linear mapping matrix W for the output of each
321   transformer layer. The hyperparameter knowledge centers k is set to 50. We adopt BLIP2-FlanT5xxl
322   as the MLLM to calculate the similarity between samples. In addition, we randomly choose one
323   similar image sample for visual generality evaluation and one synonymous prompt for text generality
      evaluation. ALL experiments are conducted using NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs.

348
      as baselines.
349
350   FineTune (FT). It directly fine-tunes all parameters of the last layer of the model for editing samples.
351   Model Editor Networks with Gradient Decomposition (MEND) (Mitchell et al. (2022a)). It learns
352   to efficiently locate knowledge in the LLM, and the knowledge is edited by leveraging the low-rank
353   decomposition of gradients.
354
      Semi-Parametric Editing with a Retrieval-Augmented Counterfactual (SERAC) (Mitchell et al.
355
      (2022b)). It is a memory-based editing method, which consists of a scope classifier, a base model,
356   and a counterfactual model. For a new sample, the scope classifier is used to determine whether it is
357   in the memory cache, and then the sample in the cache that is most similar to the sample is input into
358   the counterfactual model to obtain the result.
359
      Knowledge Editor (KE) (Cao et al. (2021)). It locates the knowledge via a hypernetwork (a
360
      bidirectional-LSTM) and predicts parameter updates at inference time via constrained optimization.
361
362   Rank-One Model Editing (ROME) (Meng et al. (2022)). It locates the knowledge in LLM via
363   Causal Mediation Analysis, the sixth layer of MLP of LLM is updated by the least squares-based
364   method.

468

      4.1   M ODEL K NOWLEDGE E DITING
470
471   Both the number of parameters and the amount of training data used in large language models (LLMs)
472   are increasing (Sevilla et al. (2022)). Knowledge is constantly evolving, and for new knowledge that
473   is not present in the model, some researchers are interested in studying knowledge editing (Meng
474   et al. (2022; 2023); Mitchell et al. (2022a)) techniques that involve precisely incorporating knowledge
475   entries into the model without affecting its original performance. ROME (Meng et al. (2022)) and
476   Memit (Meng et al. (2023)) try to locate the knowledge in LLM and then edit them. KE (Cao et al.
477
      (2021)) and MEND (Mitchell et al. (2022a)) aim to use hypernetworks to identify the parameters that
      need to be modified. During prediction, they employ specific methods to output the magnitude of
478
      modifications required for those parameters. SERAC (Mitchell et al. (2022b)) achieves knowledge
479
      modification by constructing an external memory cache and utilizing a scope classifier to modify the
480   knowledge. (Zheng et al. (2023)) proposes to leverage In-Context Learning (Brown et al. (2020))
481   to put new knowledge in the prompts to empower models to exploit them. The above methods are
482   for text-only LLMs. Utilizing multimodal data to perform knowledge editing on an MLLM is more
483   in line with real scenarios. The aforementioned methods are all applied to single-modal text-based
484   large models using single-modal data. However, performing knowledge editing on multimodal
485   large language models using multimodal data is more aligned with real-world scenarios. MMEdit
      (Cheng et al. (2023)) and MIKE (Li et al. (2024)) propose two new multimodal knowledge editing


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      datasets. However, they do not consider the multi-granularity nature of knowledge in the dataset.
487   Furthermore, their research merely transfers the aforementioned editing methods from LLMs to a
488   specific component in MLLMs. Although they achieved promising performance, we have discovered
489   that simultaneously editing three components can enhance the model’s generalization on multimodal
490   data.
491
492   4.2   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
493
494   Large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022); Touvron et al. (2023);
495   Zhang et al. (2022)) have demonstrated strong performance on knowledge-intensive tasks (Voorhees
496   & Tice (2000); Talmor et al. (2019); See et al. (2017)). As a result, there have been efforts to
497   train multimodal interfaces in large-scale image caption data for large language models (LLMs)
498   (Alayrac et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023)), enabling them to
499   handle different modalities simultaneously. These models are also known as multimodal large
500
      language models (MLLMs) and have shown promising results on knowledge-intensive tasks involving
      multiple modalities, such as visual question answering (Marino et al. (2019); Antol et al. (2015)) and
501
      multimodal dialogue (Wang et al. (2021); Zheng et al. (2022)). These models typically consist of
502
      three components: a modality encoder for encoding data from modalities other than text (such as
503   visual encoders), a multimodal interface for transforming representations from other modalities into
504   the space of the LLM, and an LLM, which handles inputs from different modalities along with text
505   inputs to process multimodal tasks. Our method edits knowledge of all components in the MLLM
506   collaboratively and we also analyze the distribution of different knowledge across these components.
507

      5     C ONCLUSION
510
511   In this paper, we introduce a multimodal model editing dataset M2Edit for the problem that existing
512
      datasets lack multi-granular knowledge, with three types of knowledge: entity, relation, and action.
      In addition, To address the issue of insufficient generalization of existing methods on multimodal
513
      data, we propose the Multimodal Location-based Method (MLE). Experiments demonstrated the
514
      effectiveness of our method. Additionally, the experiments revealed inconsistencies in the storage
515   regions of different types of knowledge within the MLLM.

725   A    C ASUAL M EDIATION A NALYSIS
726
727   Causal mediation analysis aims to identify the causal relationship between different intermediate
728
      states in models and the final output of the answer. To trace the important state of the model
      always needs to take three runs: a clean run that the model can answer the question correctly with
729
      normal input, a corrupted run that corrupts the input to make the model get corrupted output, a
730
      corrupted-with-restoration run that restores a certain state to judge the restoring of the output.
731
732   Clean Run: For a sample (xi , vi , yi ) ∈ D, a clean run directly obtains the final answer (ŷi ) through
733   the original MLLM (Θ), which is P(yi ) = Θ(xi , vi ). The state representation of each layer in LLM
                         (i,l)
734   can be Hllm = {hllm |i ∈ [1, Tllm ], l ∈ [1, Lllm ]}, where Tllm denotes the input token length, Lllm
735   denotes the layer numbers of LLM. The same formula holds for the state representation in the visual
736   encoder (Hve ) and the multimodal interface (Hmi ).
737   Corrupted Run: In the corrupted run, the corrupted output (o) is obtained by adding Gaussian noise
738   to the input image, which can be expressed as Pcor (yi ) = Θ(xi , vi + ϵ). The state representation of
739   each layer in different components of MLLM change to be Ĥc , c ∈ {llm, ve, mi}.
740
      Corrupted-with-restoration Run: In the corrupted-with-restoration run, it replaces each state
741
      representation in each component of the corrupted run to clean run. In this way, we can get the new
742
      prediction of yi as Ph(i,l) (yi ) = Θclean h(i,l) (xi , vi + ϵ), c ∈ {llm, ve, mi}. The indirect effect (IE)
743                          c                    c
                                      (i,l)
744   of each state representation hc can be: IE = Ph(i,l) (yi ) − Pcor (yi ). Averaging over a sample of
                                                          c
745   statements can obtain the average indirect effect (AIE).
