009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Data synthesis has been advocated as an important approach for utilizing data
013            while protecting data privacy. In recent years, a plethora of tabular data synthesis
014
               algorithms (i.e., synthesizers) have been proposed. A comprehensive understand-
               ing of these synthesizers’ strengths and weaknesses remains elusive due to the
015
               absence of principled evaluation metrics and head-to-head comparisons between
016
               state-of-the-art deep generative approaches and statistical methods. In this pa-
017            per, we examine and critique existing evaluation metrics, and introduce a set of
018            new metrics in terms of fidelity, privacy, and utility to address their limitations.
019            Based on the proposed evaluation metrics, we also devise a unified objective for
020            tuning, which can consistently improve the quality of synthetic data for all meth-
021            ods. We conducted extensive evaluations of 8 different types of synthesizers on
022            12 real-world datasets and identified some interesting findings, which offer new
023            directions for privacy-preserving data synthesis.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Data-driven decision-making has emerged as the prevailing approach to advance science, industrial
030   applications, and governance, creating the necessity to share and publish tabular data. At the same
031   time, growing concerns about the privacy breaches caused by data disclosure call for data publish-
032   ing approaches that preserve privacy. One increasingly advocated and adopted approach to reduce
033   privacy risks while sharing data is to release synthetic data. Ideally, synthetic data can effectively
034   fit any data processing workflow designed for the original data without privacy concerns. Data syn-
035   thesis initiatives have been promoted not only by the research community (Tao et al., 2021) but also
036   among non-profit organizations (OECD, 2023) and government agencies (Benedetto et al., 2018).
037   In this paper, we study data synthesis algorithms for tabular data, which we call synthesizers. In
038   recent years, a plethora of synthesizers have been proposed, which can be roughly categorized into
039   two groups: statistical and deep generative. Statistical synthesizers use low-order marginals to create
040   synthetic datasets that match real data distributions. They were the best-performing algorithms in
041   NIST competitions (NIST, 2018; 2020). Deep generative synthesizers, on the other hand, learn
042   the data distribution from real data and generate synthetic instances by sampling from the learned
043   distribution. With the recent development in deep generative models (e.g., diffusion models (Ho
044
      et al., 2020) and large language models (LLMs) (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019)), new
      synthesizers are proposed to extend these successes to the realm of tabular data synthesis.
045
046   While recent state-of-the-art approaches achieve compelling results in synthesizing authentic tabu-
047   lar data, a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different synthesizers
048   remains elusive. In addition, there is a lack of principled and widely accepted evaluation metrics for
049   data synthesis. It is known that evaluating synthesizers is inherently difficult (Theis et al., 2016),
050
      and qualitative evaluation of tabular data through visual inspection is also infeasible.
051   The above concerns motivate us to design a systematic evaluation framework for data synthesis
052   to elucidate the current advancements in this field. Specifically, we examine, characterize, and
053   critique the commonly used evaluation metrics, and propose a set of new metrics for data synthesis
      evaluation. Our assessments unfold along three main axes:

070   2     DATA S YNTHESIS E VALUATION
071
072   Given a dataset D sampled from an underlying distribution D, A ← T (D) denotes that the synthe-
073   sizer A is learned by running the training algorithm T on D. The synthesizer A generates a synthetic
074   dataset S to replace D for publishing. We consider three classes of properties for synthesizers:
075   • Fidelity. As the substitute for real data, the distribution of the synthetic dataset should be close to
076     D. Since D is often unknown, fidelity is measured by the similarity between the input dataset D
077     and the synthetic dataset S. If one partitions the input dataset D into a training set Dtrain and a test
078     set Dtest , one can measure fidelity as closeness to either Dtrain or Dtest .
079   • Privacy. Using synthetic data is usually motivated by the desire to protect the input dataset. Some
080     training algorithms T are designed to satisfy Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006), we refer
081     to these as DP synthesizers. (See Appendix F for the formal definition). However, satisfying DP
082     under reasonable parameters may result in poor performance. Some synthesizers do not satisfy
083
        DP, and aim to protect privacy empirically. We call these Heuristically Private (HP) synthesizers.
        As a result, privacy evaluation metrics are essential for evaluating the privacy of HP synthesizers.
084
      • Utility. Synthetic data is often used to replace real datasets for downstream tasks. Thus, high
085
        fidelity may not necessarily be needed if it achieves good utility for these tasks. Hence, utility
086     evaluation is useful to measure the effectiveness of synthesizers for common tasks.

090   3.1   F IDELITY E VALUATION
091
092   Existing Metrics and Limitations. Existing fidelity metrics can be categorized into three groups:
093   low-order statistics (McKenna et al., 2019), likelihood fitness (Xu et al., 2019), and evaluator-
094   dependent metrics (Snoke et al., 2018). The main issue with low-order statistics is the lack of
095   versatility. Each type of marginal distribution requires a specific statistical measure, complicating
096   comprehensive comparisons across different attribute types. Likelihood fitness assesses how well
097   synthetic data aligns with a known prior distribution. Although this is a natural approach for assess-
098   ing fidelity, it becomes problematic when the prior distribution is unknown or complex, as is often
099   the case in real-world datasets. Evaluator-dependent metrics, on the other hand, rely heavily on
100
      auxiliary evaluators (e.g., thresholds or discriminators), which require careful calibration to ensure
      meaningful comparisons across diverse datasets and synthesizers. A more detailed discussion of
101
      existing fidelity metrics can be found in Appendix G.1.
102

      Wasserstein Distance for Categorical Attributes. Wasserstein distance is typically defined for

142   attributes, as defined in Equation 5, represents an atypical usage of Wasserstein distance. We ac-

146   in previous studies. Additionally, it is also feasible to assign semantic distance for categorical at-
147   tributes (Li et al., 2021), we omit it because it depends on the specific context and most synthesizers

154   It accommodates both numerical and categorical attributes and extends to any multivariate marginals
155   under the same criterion, facilitating the evaluation of heterogeneous types of marginals.

157   3.2   P RIVACY E VALUATION
158
159   Existing Metrics and Limitations. A popular approach to assess privacy risk for HP synthesizers
160   is to compare the similarity between input dataset and synthetic data, with higher similarity sug-
161   gesting greater information leakage. We call these metrics syntactic because they consider only the
      input and synthetic datasets, and not the algorithm used to generate the synthetic data. The most


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      popular syntactic metric is Distance to Closest Records (DCR) (Zhao et al., 2021), which looks at
163   the distribution of the distances from each synthetic data point to its nearest real one and uses the 5th
164   percentile of this distribution as the privacy score. DCR and other similar metrics are widely used
165   in academia (Walia et al., 2020; Yale et al., 2019) and industry (AWS, 2022; Gretel, 2023), and have
166   become the conventional evaluation metric for HP synthesizers (Ganev & De Cristofaro, 2023).
167
      We point out that syntactic privacy evaluation notions that are independent of the underlying al-
168
      gorithm are fundamentally flawed. For example, a synthesis algorithm that applies the same fixed
169   perturbation to every record could produce a synthetic dataset that is quite different from the input
170   dataset, resulting in a good privacy score under a syntactic metric, even though the input dataset
171   could be easily reconstructed from the synthetic dataset.
172
      Membership inference attacks (MIAs) have been widely used for empirical privacy evaluation in ma-
173
      chine learning (especially classification models) (Shokri et al., 2017). A few MIAs against tabular
174
      data synthesis algorithms have been proposed: Groundhog (Stadler et al., 2022), TAPAS (Houssiau
175   et al., 2022) and MODIAS (van Breugel et al., 2023). Our comparison studies in Section 5.2 demon-
176   strate that these MIA algorithms are limited in effectiveness: they fail to distinguish different levels
177   of privacy leakage in some situations. We also observe that the standard metrics in MIA literature
178   (i.e., TPR@lowFPR) still do not capture the maximum leakage among all records in the input. The
179   detailed analysis of the existing privacy evaluation metrics is in Appendix G.2.
180
181

234   the predominant utility metric for data synthesis. It first chooses a machine learning model (i.e.,
235   evaluator), then assesses the testing accuracy on real data after training the evaluator on synthetic
236   datasets. However, there is no consensus on which evaluator should be used for evaluation. Different
237   evaluators yield varying performance outcomes on synthetic data, and no single model consistently
238   achieves the best performance across all datasets. (We show the case in Appendix G.3.)
239

266   Tuning Objective. Most synthesizers do not provide guidelines for hyperparameter tuning. Instead,
267
      default settings are commonly used for evaluations. This practice can lead to suboptimal results and
      biased comparisons. To address this issue, we propose a simple tuning objective using proposed
268
      evaluation metrics to facilitate the hyperparameter selection:
269
                                 L(A) = α1 Fidelity(A) + α2 MLA(A) + α3 QueryError(A).                                                      (11)

      Since smaller values indicate better performance for all proposed metrics, we conduct a grid search
271   on synthesizers and select the best hyperparameters that minimize L for evaluation. The privacy
272   evaluation metric is excluded from model tuning, as we find that incorporating MDS yields negligi-

275   SynMeter. We introduce a modular toolkit called SynMeter to assess data synthesis algorithms

313   Effectiveness of MDS. We compare MDS against the popular syntactic privacy evaluation metric
314   DCR (Zhao et al., 2021), as well as three state-of-the-art MIAs: Groundhog (Stadler et al., 2022),
315   TAPAS (Houssiau et al., 2022) and MODIAS (van Breugel et al., 2023). For DCR, we calculate
316   the nearest distance of each synthetic record to real data, using the 5th percentile of the distance
317   distribution as the privacy score. For MIAs, we follow Carlini et al. (2022) and use the true positive
318   rate at 1% false positive rate (TPR@1%FPR) to measure the attack performance.
319   We conduct two proof-of-concept experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of MDS. First, we train
320   a DP synthesizer (PATE-GAN) with varying levels of privacy protection by adjusting the privacy
321   budget, and we measure the empirical privacy risk using these privacy evaluation metrics. Second,
322   we train an HP synthesizer (TabDDPM) with different duplication ratios while keeping the training
323   data size unchanged. Intuitively, a higher proportion of duplicate samples in the training set increases
      the memorization of the model, which in turn poses higher privacy risks (Carlini et al., 2023).

342   deviations). For MIAs, we observe an improvement in attack performance as the proportion of
343   duplicates in the training set increases, especially for MODIAS. However, MIAs still struggle to
344   capture privacy nuances with DP synthesizers. In contrast, MDS effectively detects privacy risks
345   across all scenarios and demonstrates robustness as a reliable privacy evaluation metric, as evidenced
346   by its high standard deviation. Additional experiments on other existing metrics are in Appendix C.4.
347
      Stability and Efficiency of MDS. We validate the stability of MDS by varying the number of
348   shadow models and synthetic datasets. Specifically, we compute the membership disclosure scores
349   for three synthesizers using different quantities of shadow models and synthetic datasets, recording
350   the mean and variance of the results, as depicted in Figure 3. Our results indicate that the variance
351   of MDS decreases rapidly as the number of shadow models and synthetic datasets increases, with
352   stable results achieved using 20 shadow models and 100 synthetic datasets. Although MDS requires
353   training more shadow models compared to existing MIAs, previous study (Zhang et al., 2024) shows
354   that tabular synthesizers can be trained in just a few minutes, with sampling taking only a few
355   seconds. Therefore, MDS remains a practical and efficient solution for privacy assessment.
356
      Effectiveness of Tuning Objective. Although the metrics in Equation (11) are based on different
357   measurements, empirically we observe that their values consistently fall within the same range. Con-
358   sequently, in our experiments, we set all three coefficients to 1/3, as this configuration significantly
359   improves the quality of synthetic data, as shown in Table 1. Interestingly, the tuning phase affects
360   two types of synthesizers differently: statistical methods gain more in utility than fidelity, while deep
361   generative models show the opposite trend. Notably, the tuning phase proves especially beneficial
362   for TabDDPM, with improvements in both fidelity and utility metrics. Additional experiments on
363   the effectiveness of the proposed tuning objective are provided in Appendix C.5.

464   REaLTabFormer achieves the best machine learning prediction performance on the Adult dataset,
465
      which contains detailed personal information (e.g., age and relationship). Given the rapid devel-
      opment of LLM and the inherent rich semantics of most tabular data, LLM-based methods may
466
      become a new paradigm for realistic data synthesis.
467
468   The Impact of Privacy Budget. To analyze the impact of differential privacy on data synthesis, we
469   run DP synthesizers with varying privacy budgets, and evaluate the fidelity and utility of the resulting
470   synthetic data (see Figure 7). Our results show that statistical methods, such as MST, maintain robust
471   performance even with a small privacy budget (e.g., ϵ = 0.5). In contrast, deep generative models
472   typically require much larger privacy budgets (e.g., ϵ = 8) to achieve comparable results. These
473   findings align with previous observations (Tao et al., 2021), which noted that statistical methods are
474   more resilient to privacy constraints because they rely on estimating a small set of marginals.

477
478
      Fidelity Evaluation Metrics. Fidelity is often evaluated based on the distributional similarities of
479
      low-order marginals with various statistical measurements. Total Variation Distance (Zhang et al.,
480   2024) and one-dimensional Wasserstein distance (Zhao et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2020) are used to as-
481   sess univariate distribution similarity for categorical and numerical attributes, respectively. Correla-
482   tion differences are widely employed for bivariate distributions. Correlation statistics such as Theil’s
483   uncertainty coefficient (Zhao et al., 2021), Pearson correlation (Zhang et al., 2024), and the corre-
484   lation ratio (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) are utilized to evaluate different types of two-way marginals
485   (categorical, continuous, and mixed). The main problem with these measures is the lack of versa-
      tility. Each type of marginal requires a distinct statistical measure, which complicates the ability to


                                                                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      perform a comprehensive comparison across various attribute types. We refer to Appendix G.1 for
487   a detailed discussion of the limitations of existing fidelity metrics.
488
489   Privacy Evaluation Metrics. Since HP synthesizers are designed without provable privacy guaran-
490   tees, privacy evaluation is indispensable for these synthesizers. Syntactic privacy evaluation metrics
491   (e.g., Distance to Closest Records (Zhao et al., 2021)) are the most widely used privacy evaluation
492   for HP synthesizer. These metrics compare the input dataset with the output dataset generated by
493   the synthesizer, with closer distances indicating higher privacy risks. Recently, Ganev & De Cristo-
494   faro (2023) critiqued these syntactic metrics, highlighting that these ad-hoc metrics can be exploited
495
      for reconstruction attacks. However, the study did not address the fundamental flaws of these met-
      rics (discussed in Section 3.2) and did not introduce new and effective privacy evaluation metrics.
496
      Another way to assess the empirical privacy risks of data synthesis is membership inference attack
497
      (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017). Some studies (Stadler et al., 2022; van Breugel et al., 2023) have
498   designed different MIA algorithms for tabular data synthesis. However, as shown in Section 5.2,
499   existing MIA algorithms are too weak to differentiate different privacy risks across various synthe-
500   sizers. Further discussion about existing privacy evaluation metrics can be found in Appendix G.2.
501
502   Utility Evaluation Metrics. Machine learning prediction and query errors are common down-
503   stream tasks for tabular data analysis, and many studies (Zhang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2019;
504   McKenna et al., 2021) have leveraged these tasks to evaluate the utility of synthetic data. In our
505   evaluation, we also adopt these tasks for utility evaluation and present a reliable metric to address
506
      the variability in performance across different machine learning models (Jordon et al., 2021). Fur-
      ther discussion on utility metrics can be found in Appendix G.3.
507
508
      Benchmarking Tabular Data Synthesis. Several studies have benchmarked tabular synthesis
509   algorithms. However, they either only focus on DP synthesizers (Tao et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2024),
510   or neglect the privacy evaluation for HP synthesizers (Espinosa & Figueira, 2023; Chundawat et al.,
511   2022; Livieris et al., 2024; McLachlan et al., 2018). Additionally, existing benchmarks (Qian et al.,
512   2024; Lautrup et al., 2024) directly leverage existing metrics for evaluation, whereas we identify the
513   limitations of these metrics and propose a new set of evaluation metrics for systematic assessment.
514
515
      7   D ISCUSSION AND K EY TAKEAWAYS
516
517
      In this paper, we examine and critique existing metrics, and introduce a systematic framework as
518   well as a new suite of evaluation criteria for assessing data synthesizers. We also provide a unified
519   tuning objective to ensure that evaluation results are less affected by accidental choices of hyperpa-
520   rameters. Our results identify several guidelines for data synthesis practitioners:
521
522   • Model tuning is indispensable. Tuning hyperparameters can significantly improve synthetic data
523
        quality, especially for deep generative models.
      • Statistical methods should be preferred for applications where privacy is paramount. MST and
524
        PrivSyn achieve the best fidelity among DP synthesizers, and they also offer good empirical pri-
525
        vacy protection even in HP settings.
526   • Diffusion models provide the best fidelity and utility. Practitioners are suggested to use diffusion
527     models (e.g., TabDDPM) for tabular synthesis when the quality of synthetic data is the priority
528     over privacy due to their impressive ability to generate highly authentic data.
529   • Deep generative models can be tailored for specific tasks. The flexible design spaces of deep
530     generative models make them suitable for scenarios where the applications of the synthetic data
531     are known in advance (e.g., machine learning prediction). In addition, the LLM-based synthesizer,
532     REaLTabFormer, is particularly effective at preserving semantic information in synthetic data.
533
      Our systematic assessment shows that recently emerged generative models achieve impressive per-
534   formance on tabular data synthesis and open up new directions in this field. At the same time,
535   several critical challenges are also revealed such as privacy issues of diffusion models and perfor-
536   mance gaps between DP and HP synthesizers. In addition, we note that existing empirical privacy
537   evaluation metrics (including proposed MDS) have their own limitations and DP synthesizers should
538   be used in privacy-critical applications. Nevertheless, our evaluation metrics and framework serve a
539   crucial role in highlighting advancements in data synthesis and represent a step toward establishing
      a standardized evaluation process for this field.

      A     E VALUATION P IPELINES
811
812
813
      The SynMeter pipeline consists of four phases: data preparation, model tuning, model training, and
      model evaluation.
814
815   The data preparation phase preprocesses data for learning algorithms1 . In this phase, statistical
816   methods select low-dimensional marginals to serve as compact representations for capturing data
817   distributions. Deep generative models apply standard data processing techniques like data encoding
818   and normalization.
819   The goal of model tuning phase is to select the optimal hyperparameters for data synthesizers. We
820   use the proposed tuning objective in Equation (11) for hyperparameter selections.
821
      The model training phase focuses on model learning with tuned hyperparameters. Various generative
822
      models implement different architectures and optimization objectives.
823
824   In the model evaluation phase, the trained model samples some synthetic data, which are used for
825   evaluation. Specifically, we assess the fidelity, privacy, and utility of synthesizers via the proposed
826
      metrics.

      B     D ETAILS OF E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
830
831   B.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
832
833   Wasserstein-based Fidelity Metric. The computation of Wasserstein distance involves solving the
834   linear programming problem in Equation 1 and selecting proper marginal distributions. We compute
835   the Wasserstein distance of all the one-way and two-way marginals and use the mean as the final
836   fidelity score. The real dataset D can be designated as either Dtrain or Dtest to evaluate the fidelity
837   of synthesizers on training data or test data.
838
      There are many open-source libraries like CVXPY (Diamond & Boyd, 2016) and POT (Flamary
839   et al., 2021) that can be used to solve linear programming reasonably fast. However, when the
840   cost matrix becomes rather large and dense, directly calculating the metric can be computationally
841   expensive. Several options are provided to address this problem: (i) Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi,
842   2013) provides a fast approximation to the Wasserstein distance by penalizing the objective with an
843   entropy term. (ii) Sliced-Wasserstein distance (Bonneel et al., 2015), which uses Radon transform to
844   linearly project data into one dimension, can be efficiently computed. (iii) Reducing the size of the
845   cost matrix by randomly sampling a small set of points from the probability densities. In practice,
846   we find that sampling is both efficient and effective. We randomly sample half of the synthetic data
847
      when n > 5, 000 and use the POT library to compute the Wasserstein distance as the fidelity scores.
848
      Membership Disclosure Score (MDS). We follow previous work (Carlini et al., 2022) and use
849
      shadow models to compute MDS. Specifically, we trained the synthesizer using half of the dataset
850   and kept the other half as non-members for each shadow model. Once the synthesizer was trained,
851   we randomly generated 100 synthetic datasets with the same size of training data and calculated
852   the average closeness difference as the disclosure score. The MDS is computed as the maximum
853   disclosure score across all records.
854
855   Utility Metrics. For machine learning affinity (MLA), we utilize eight machine learning models to
856   compute MLA: SVM, Logistic Regression (or Ridge Regression), Decision Tree, Random Forest,
857   Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al.,
858   2018), and Transformers (Gorishniy et al., 2021). Each model is extensively tuned on real training
859
      data to ensure optimal hyperparameters. Performance on classification and regression is evaluated
      by the F1 score and RMSE, respectively. For query error, we randomly construct 1,000 3-way query
860
      conditions and conduct range (point) queries for both synthetic and real data.

931   B.3   U SED DATA S YNTHESIS A LGORITHMS
932
933
934   We study a wide range of state-of-the-art synthesizers, from statistical methods to deep generative
935   models. We select them as they are either generally considered to perform best in practice (McKenna
936   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021), widely used (Xu et al., 2019; Papernot et al., 2018), or recently
937   emerged (Kotelnikov et al., 2023; Borisov et al., 2023; Truda, 2023). These synthesizers can be
938   categorized into two groups: heuristic private (HP) and differentially private (DP) synthesizers.
939
940
941   HP Synthesizers. Synthesizers in this category are developed without integrating DP:
942
943
      • CTGAN (Xu et al., 2019) is one of the most widely used HP synthesis algorithms. It utilizes
        generative adversarial networks to learn tabular data distributions. Training techniques like con-
944
        ditional generation and Wasserstein loss (Gulrajani et al., 2017) are used.
945
      • TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) is the state-of-the-art variational autoencoder for tabular data synthesizer,
946     which uses mode-specific normalization to tackle the non-Gaussian problems of continuous dis-
947     tributions.
948   • TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) is the state-of-the-art diffusion model for data synthesis. It
949     leverages the Gaussian diffusion process and the multinomial diffusion process to model continu-
950     ous and discrete distributions respectively.
951   • GReaT Borisov et al. (2023) utilizes the large language model (LLM) for data synthesis. It
952     converts records to textual representations for LLM and generates synthetic data with prompts.
953
954
955   DP Synthesizers. These methods are either inherently designed with DP or are adaptations of HP
956   models with additional mechanisms to offer provable privacy guarantees:
957
      • MST (McKenna et al., 2021) is the state-of-the-art DP synthesizer, which uses probabilistic graph-
958
        ical models McKenna et al. (2019) to learn the dependence of low-dimensional marginals. It won
959
        the NIST Differential Privacy Synthetic Data Challenge NIST (2018). Discrete binning is applied
960     for numerical attributes.
961   • PrivSyn (Zhang et al., 2021) is a non-parametric DP synthesizer, which iteratively updates the
962     synthetic dataset to make it match the target noise marginals. This method also shows strong
963     performance in NIST competitions (NIST, 2018; 2020). Discretization is also used for modeling
964     numerical attributes.
965   • PATE-GAN (Jordon et al., 2018) shares a similar architecture with CTGAN, but leverages the
966     Private Aggregation of Teacher Ensembles (PATE) (Papernot et al., 2018) to offer DP guarantees.
967   • TableDiffusion (Truda, 2023) is a newly proposed diffusion model for data synthesis, which uses
968
        Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) to enforce privacy.
969   All DP synthesizers can be adapted to the HP scenario either by using their HP counterparts14 (i.e.,
970   CTGAN for PATE-GAN, TabDDPM for TableDiffusion) or by setting the privacy budget to infinity
971   (i.e., MST and PrivSyn). However, some HP synthesizers, such as TVAE and GReaT, do not have
      corresponding DP variants. Thus, we only assess their performance within the context of HP models.

       We randomly divide the dataset into two disjoint subsets: a training set Dt and a reference set
1176   Dr , where |Dt | = |Dr | and they share the same data distribution. Each synthesis algorithm is
1177   trained on Dt and generates the synthetic data D, while the reference data Dr remains unused
1178   during the synthesis process. Different treatments are applied for different metrics: (i) For syntactic
1179   metrics (i.e., DCR and NNDR), we compute the privacy score by treating either Dt or Dr as the
1180   real data. Unless a synthesizer provides very good privacy, it is expected that the privacy leakage
1181   on Dr is significantly smaller than that on Dt , since the synthetic data is generated using Dt and

1224   Comparison with Meeus et al. We also notice that Meeus et al. (2023) proposed a new approach
1225   to evaluate the empirical privacy risks of synthesizers. It first identifies vulnerable samples by ex-
1226   amining their closeness and then conducts a shadow model-based membership inference attack for
1227
       the vulnerable sample for evaluation. (We follow the original paper and use the most vulnerable 10
       records in our experiments.) While this approach (we call it vMIA) does not align with the standard
1228
       setting for membership inference attacks, it may serve as a viable tool for empirical privacy evalu-
1229
       ation. Thus, we conduct the following experiments to compare the effectiveness of vMIA with our
1230   proposed MDS.
1231
1232   Specifically, we train two DP synthesizers (i.e., MST and PATE-GAN) with varying levels of privacy
1233
       protection by adjusting the privacy budget, and we measure the empirical privacy risk using vMIA
       and MDS. We follow Meeus et al. (2023) and use the area under the curve (AUC) as the evaluation
1234
       metric. The results of both experiments are presented in Figure 10. We observe an improvement in
1235
       attack performance for MST, whereas the performance of PATE-GAN remains relatively low (below
1236   60% AUC) across all levels of privacy budgets. We attribute this to the design of vulnerability scores
1237   in vMIA where the extracted vulnerable samples are determined by their closeness within datasets,
1238   which is independent of the underlying synthesizers. Additionally, since deep generative models are
1239   not designed to model marginal distributions, using marginal queries as features may not provide
1240   reliable performance signals for membership inference. Furthermore, vMIA suffers from relatively
1241   high variance. In contrast, MDS reliably detects different privacy risks across both marginal-based
       methods and deep generative models.

1443
1444        Theorem 1 (Post-Processing). Let M : D → R be an (ε, δ)-DP mechanism and f : R → R′ .

1451
            lying distribution D, we write A ← T (D) to denote that the synthesizer A is learned by running the

1484
1485   Low-order Statistics. Marginals are the workhorses of statistical data analysis and well-established
1486   statistics for one(two)-way marginals have been used to assess the quality of synthetic data.
1487   Distribution Measurements. Total Variation Distance (TVD) and Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test (KST)
1488   are used to measure the univariate distribution similarity for categorical and numerical attributes,
1489   respectively. The main problem with this approach is the lack of versatility. Each type of marginal
1490   requires a distinct statistical measure, which complicates the ability to perform a comprehensive
1491   comparison across various attribute types.
1492   Correlation Statistics. Some researchers use correlation difference, i.e., the difference of correlation
1493   scores on synthetic and real data, to measure the pairwise distribution similarity. Popular correlation
1494   statistics like Theil’s uncertainty coefficient (Zhao et al., 2021), Pearson correlation (Zhang et al.,
1495   2024), and the correlation ratio (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) are applied for different types of two-way
1496   marginals (categorical, continuous, and mixed). In addition to the lack of universality, this approach
1497   also suffers from the problem that correlation scores capture only limited information about the data
1498   distribution. Two attributes may have the same correlation score both in the real data and in the
1499
       synthetic data, yet their underlying distributions diverge significantly—a phenomenon known as the
       scale invariance of correlation statistics15 .
1500
1501   Likelihood Fitness. Xu et al. (2019) assume the input data are generated from some known
1502   probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian networks), thus the likelihood of synthetic data can be derived
1503   by fitting them to the priors. While likelihood fitness can naturally reflect the closeness of synthetic
1504   data to the assumed prior distribution, it is only feasible for data whose priors are known, which is
1505   inaccessible for most real-world complex datasets.
1506

1507

1508
       prediction confidence as the fidelity metric. The effectiveness of pMSE highly relies on the choice of
1509   auxiliary discriminator, which requires careful calibration to ensure meaningful comparisons across

1541
       Syntactic Privacy Evaluation Metrics. Researchers propose to measure the empirical privacy risk
1542
       of synthetic data by comparing an input dataset with the output dataset generated by the synthesizer,
1543
       typically using the distances between data records. For example, the Distance to Closest Records
1544   (DCR) (Zhao et al., 2021) metric looks at the distribution of the distances from each synthetic
1545   data point to its nearest real one, and uses the 5th percentile (or the mean) of this distribution as
1546   the privacy score. A small score is interpreted as indicating that the synthetic dataset is too close
1547   (similar) to real data, signaling a high risk of information leakage. There are other variations of DCR,
1548   e.g., using the minimum distance instead of the 5th percentile, or using, for each record, the ratio
1549   of the closest distance and the second closest distance. However, these variations result in highly
1550   unstable measurements because of the inherent randomness of synthetic data. DCR and/or other
1551   similar metrics are widely used both in academia (Yale et al., 2019) and industry (AWS, 2022; Gretel,
1552
       2023), and have become the conventional privacy evaluation metrics for HP synthesizers (Jordon
       et al., 2021).
1553
1554   We note that metrics such as DCR are computed based on a pair of datasets: the input real
1555   dataset, and the output synthetic dataset. They do not depend on the synthesis algorithm at all.
1556   We call such metrics syntactic. We also note that when researchers were studying privacy prop-
1557   erties of data anonymizers, syntactic privacy metrics such as k-anonymity (Sweeney, 2002), ℓ-
1558
       diversity (Machanavajjhala et al., 2006), and t-closeness (Li et al., 2007) were introduced. Similarly,
       these metrics consider only the anonymized dataset (and not the algorithm generating the dataset)
1559
       when measuring privacy. Over the last decade and a half, the community gradually recognized the
1560
       limitations of such syntactic privacy evaluation metrics and adopted privacy notions such as differ-
1561   ential privacy (Dwork, 2006), which defines privacy as a property of the data processing algorithm,
1562   instead of the property of a particular output.
1563

1565   metrics as it’s the most widely-used metric in the literature. First, DCR overestimates the privacy
       risks when data points are naturally clustered close together. As illustrated by discussions about dif-

1596
       instance on synthetic data is insignificant.

1598   Second, DCR measures privacy loss using the 5th percentile (or mean) proximity to real data, which
1599   fails to bound the worst-case privacy leakage among all records. When measuring the privacy leak-
1600   age across different individuals, one needs to ensure that the worst-case leakage is bounded, so that
1601
       every individual’s privacy is protected. It is unacceptable to use a mechanism that sacrifices the
       privacy of some individuals even though the protection averaged over the population is good. This
1602
       point is illustrated by the fact that the re-identification of one or a few individuals is commonly
1603
       accepted as privacy breaches (Li et al., 2013).
1604
1605   Membership Inference Attack on Data Synthesis. MIA has been widely used as an empirical
1606   privacy evaluation metric in machine learning, which has been extensively studied on discriminative
1607   models (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022). For generative models like diffusion models (Duan
1608   et al., 2023) and LLM (Duan et al., 2024), studies mainly focus both on the white-box setting (where
1609   an adversary has full access to the trained model) and on the black-box setting (where an adversary
1610   has exact knowledge of the specifications of the generative model). In the realm of data synthesis,
1611
       Annamalai et al. (2024) claim that the non-box setting should be considered in practice: the adver-
       sary has access to the synthetic dataset but no information about the underlying generative model
1612

1656   H     D ISCUSSION OF P ROPOSED P RIVACY E VALUATION M ETRIC
1657
1658   Comparison with Syntactic Privacy Evaluation Metrics. We use DCR as an example to show
1659   how the proposed membership disclosure score (MDS) addresses the drawbacks of syntactic metrics.
1660   First, MDS addresses DCR’s over-estimating leakage issue by quantifying how much including each
1661   record x changes the distance between x and the closest synthetic data. If including x results in
1662   records much closer to x to be generated, then the disclosure risk is high. Conversely, if records
1663   close to x are generated regardless of whether x is included, then the disclosure risk for x is low.
1664   Therefore, MDS follows a distinguishing game designed to mirror the DP definition, rather than
1665   relying on the density of data points. Additionally, MDS uses the maximum disclosure risk among
1666
       all records, providing a stable worst-case privacy measurement.
1667
       Comparison with MIAs. Both membership disclosure score (MDS) and membership inference
1668   attacks (MIAs) measure privacy risks by assessing the influence of discrepancies observed in the
1669   synthesizer when trained with or without certain records. Additionally, MDS incorporates shadow
1670   model techniques (Shokri et al., 2017) to estimate the influence for all data records, which is the
1671   standard approach in MIAs. However, unlike MIAs, MDS directly assesses the privacy risks of
1672   training data without relying on the construction of the membership inference security game (Carlini
1673   et al., 2022). Consequently, MDS’s privacy estimation does not depend on the effectiveness of one
       specific attack algorithm, offering greater flexibility in evaluating various types of data synthesizers.

       Connections to Related Work. The definition of the proposed MDS aligns closely with concepts of
1702
       memorization in neural networks (Feldman, 2020; Zhang et al., 2023) and the leave-one-out notion
1703
       of stability in machine learning (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002). However, it diverges in three crucial
1704   ways: (i) We measure the worst-case discourse risk as the privacy evaluation metric, whereas other
1705   studies focus on the difference of individuals or average cases. (ii) Our work specifically addresses
1706   privacy concerns in data synthesis, as opposed to other studies that explore discriminative models
1707   like classification. (iii) Our approach emphasizes the discrepancy caused by the presence or absence
1708   of target data in training, in contrast to other works that highlight performance gains from adding
1709   samples to the training set.
1710
1711   Limitations of MDS. Although MDS provides a straightforward way to assess the privacy risk
1712   of data synthesis, it may not apply to all synthesizers. Pathological synthesizers exist for which
1713   MDS is inappropriate. One such example is a synthesizer that maps all data points x ∈ D to their
1714   opposites: x 7→ −x. Suppose the nearest neighbor to x in the real dataset is x + ε. In this case, MDS
1715   would be proportional to |d(x, s(x)) − d(x, s(x + ε))|, which can be tricked arbitrarily small with
1716   ε. However, this synthesizer completely reveals the dataset and MDS would suggest a false sense of
1717   privacy. Therefore, while we find MDS to be effective in assessing privacy risks for the synthesizers
1718
       we tested, caution should be exercised when applying it in practice. For scenarios where privacy is
       paramount, we highly recommend using DP synthesizers instead of HP synthesizers.
1719
1720   We also note that MDS focuses specifically on membership privacy (Li et al., 2013) and does not
1721   address all potential privacy risks associated with synthetic datasets. For instance, attribute inference
1722   attacks (Annamalai et al., 2024) and reconstruction attacks (Jayaraman & Evans, 2022) pose serious
1723   privacy threats to synthetic data, which MDS is not designed to capture.
1724   In addition, MDS requires training multiple shadow models to estimate the disclosure risks. This
1725   can pose a challenge when assessing large-scale tabular synthesis models like GReaT, which involve
1726   fine-tuning entire LLMs. However, existing MIAs (Stadler et al., 2022; van Breugel et al., 2023) also
1727   rely on shadow modeling to compute privacy scores. Thus, MDS remains a practical and feasible
       solution for privacy assessment in most tabular datasets and synthesis algorithms.

       I   M ISLEADING S TATEMENTS FROM P REVIOUS W ORK
1729
1730
       We find that some statements in the literature may be misleading or even incorrect due to limitations
1731   of evaluation metrics or methodologies. We highlight some of them below:
1732
1733   • Extensive studies (Zhao et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) use Distance to Closest
1734     Records (DCR) to evaluate the privacy of synthetic data and assert their models are safe. However,
1735
         in this paper, we show that DCR fails to serve as an adequate measure of privacy. We also show that
         many recently introduced HP methods exhibit significant privacy risks, which are often ignored
1736
         by the community.
1737
       • Kotelnikov et al. (2023) show that the machine learning performance on TabDDPM is even better
1738     than that on real data, which implies that synthetic data can be a perfect (even better) substitute for
1739     real data. However, this statement may be incorrect due to inadequate model tuning and improper
1740     data shuffling practices. Our evaluations show that even simple models, such as linear regression,
1741     can achieve better performance on real data than on high-quality synthetic data.
1742   • Some studies (Kim et al., 2022; Jordon et al., 2018) prioritize machine learning efficacy as the pri-
1743     mary (if not only) fidelity evaluation metric. This approach is problematic because data synthesis
1744     can be biased to label attributes, and a high machine learning efficacy score does not necessarily
1745     equate to high fidelity in synthetic data.
