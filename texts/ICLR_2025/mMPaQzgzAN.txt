010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a promising unsupervised approach for identify-
013            ing causally relevant and interpretable linear features in a language model’s (LM)
014            activations. To be useful for downstream tasks, SAEs need to decompose LM
015            activations faithfully; yet to be interpretable the decomposition must be sparse –
016            two objectives that are in tension. In this paper, we introduce JumpReLU SAEs,
017
               which achieve state-of-the-art reconstruction fidelity at a given sparsity level on
               Gemma 2 9B activations, compared to other recent advances such as Gated and
018
               TopK SAEs. We also show that this improvement does not come at the cost of in-
019
               terpretability through manual and automated interpretability studies. JumpReLU
020            SAEs are a simple modification of vanilla (ReLU) SAEs – where we replace the
021            ReLU with a discontinuous JumpReLU activation function – and are similarly ef-
022            ficient to train and run. By utilizing straight-through-estimators (STEs) in a prin-
023            cipled manner, we show how it is possible to train JumpReLU SAEs effectively
024            despite the discontinuous JumpReLU function introduced in the SAE’s forward
025            pass. Similarly, we use STEs to directly train L0 to be sparse, instead of training
026            on proxies such as L1, avoiding problems like shrinkage.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) allow us to find causally relevant and seemingly interpretable direc-
032   tions in the activation space of a language model (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023;
033   Templeton et al., 2024). There is interest within the field of mechanistic interpretability in using
034   sparse decompositions produced by SAEs for tasks such as circuit analysis (Marks et al., 2024) and
035   model steering (Conmy & Nanda, 2024).
036   SAEs work by finding approximate, sparse, linear decompositions of language model (LM) acti-
037   vations in terms of a large dictionary of basic “feature” directions. Two key objectives for a good
038   decomposition (Bricken et al., 2023) are that it is sparse – i.e. that only a few elements of the dictio-
039   nary are needed to reconstruct any given activation – and that it is faithful – i.e. the approximation
040   error between the original activation and recombining its SAE decomposition is “small” in some
041   suitable sense. These two objectives are naturally in tension: for any given SAE training method
042   and fixed dictionary size, increasing sparsity typically results in reduced reconstruction fidelity.
043   One strand of recent research in training SAEs on LM activations (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024; Gao
044   et al., 2024; Taggart, 2024) has been on finding improved SAE architectures and training meth-
045   ods that push out the Pareto frontier balancing these two objectives, while preserving other less
046   quantifiable measures of SAE quality such as the interpretability or functional relevance of dictio-
047   nary directions. A common thread connecting these recent improvements is the introduction of a
048   thresholding or gating operation to determine which SAE features to use in the decomposition. The
049   motivation for introducing such a thresholding operation is illustrated and explained in Fig. 1.
050   In this paper, we introduce JumpReLU SAEs – a small modification of the original, ReLU-based
051   SAE architecture (Ng, 2011) where the SAE encoder’s ReLU activation function is replaced by a
052   JumpReLU activation function (Erichson et al., 2019), which zeroes out pre-activations below a
053   positive threshold (see Fig. 1). Moreover, we train JumpReLU SAEs using a loss function that is
      simply the weighted sum of a L2 reconstruction error term and a L0 sparsity penalty, eschewing

083     We evaluate JumpReLU, Gated and TopK (Gao et al., 2024) SAEs on Gemma 2 9B (Gemma Team,
084     2024) residual stream, MLP output and attention output activations at several layers (Fig. 2). At any
085     given level of sparsity, we find JumpReLU SAEs consistently provide more faithful reconstructions
086     than Gated SAEs. JumpReLU SAEs also provide reconstructions that are at least as good as, and
087
        often slightly better than, TopK SAEs. Similar to simple ReLU SAEs, JumpReLU SAEs only
        require a single forward and backward pass during a training step and have an elementwise activation
088
        function (unlike TopK, which requires a partial sort), making them more efficient to train than either
089
        Gated or TopK SAEs.
090
091     Compared to Gated SAEs, we find both TopK and JumpReLU tend to have more features that acti-
092     vate very frequently – i.e. on more than 10% of tokens (Fig. 4). Consistent with prior work evaluating
093
        TopK SAEs (Cunningham & Conerly, 2024) we find these high frequency JumpReLU features tend
        to be less interpretable, although interpretability does improve as SAE sparsity increases. Further-
094
        more, only a small proportion of SAE features have very high frequencies: fewer than 0.06% in a
095
        131k-width SAE. We also present the results of manual and automated interpretability studies indi-
096     cating that randomly chosen JumpReLU, TopK and Gated SAE features are similarly interpretable.

      In this section, we compare JumpReLU SAEs to Gated and TopK SAEs across a range of metrics.8

293   of each type (JumpReLU, Gated and TopK) on activations from Gemma 2 9B (base). Specifically,
294   we trained SAEs on residual stream, attention output and MLP output sites after layers 9, 20 and 31

296
      We trained Gated SAEs using two different loss functions. Firstly, we used the original Gated
297
      SAE loss in Rajamanoharan et al. (2024), which uses a L1 sparsity penalty and requires resampling
298   (Bricken et al., 2023) – periodic re-initialization of dead features – in order to train effectively. Sec-
299   ondly, we used a modified Gated SAE loss function that replaces the L1 sparsity penalty with the
300   RI-L1 sparsity penalty described in Section 2; see Appendix D for details. With this modified loss

302   the AuxK auxiliary loss described in Gao et al. (2024) with Kaux = 512, which helps reduce the
303   number of dead features. We also used an approximate algorithm for computing the top K activa-
304   tions (Chern et al., 2022) after finding it produces similar results to exact TopK while being much
305   faster (Appendix E). See Appendix I for further details of our training methodology and Appendix J

351    Ablation study In Appendix H.2 we present the results of an ablation study showing that both the
352    JumpReLU activation function and the L0 sparsity penalty are necessary for obtaining this improve-
353    ment in fidelity at fixed sparsity.

355     5.2                           F EATURE ACTIVATION FREQUENCIES
356
357    For a given SAE, we are interested in both the proportion of learned features that are active very fre-
358    quently and the proportion of features that are almost never active (“dead” features). Prior work has
359    found that TopK SAEs tend to have more high frequency features than Gated SAEs (Cunningham
360    & Conerly, 2024), and that these features tend to be less interpretable when sparsity is also low.
361    Fig. 4 shows, for JumpReLU, Gated and TopK SAEs, how the fraction of high frequency features
362    varies with SAE fidelity (as measured by delta LM loss). TopK and JumpReLU SAEs consistently
363    have more high frequency features – features that activate on over 10% of tokens – than Gated SAEs,
364    although the fraction drops close to zero for SAEs in the low fidelity / high sparsity regime. Across
365    all layers and frequency thresholds, JumpReLU SAEs have either similar or fewer high frequency
366    features than TopK SAEs. Finally, it is worth noting that in all cases the number of high frequency
367
       features remains low in proportion to the widths of these SAEs, with fewer than 0.06% of features
       activating more than 10% of the time even for the highest L0 SAEs. Fig. 18 compares the proportion
368
       of “dead” features – which we defined to be features that activate on fewer than one in 107 tokens
369
       – between JumpReLU, Gated and TopK SAEs. Both JumpReLU SAEs and TopK SAEs (with the
370    AuxK loss) consistently have few dead features, without needing resampling.

        5.3                           I NTERPRETABILITY OF SAE FEATURES
373
374    Although the results above suggest that JumpReLU SAEs provide more faithful reconstructions at a
375    given level of sparsity than Gated or TopK SAEs, we would like to confirm that this improvement
376    does not come at the cost of JumpReLU features being less interpretable. In the following two
377    sections we evaluate the interpretability of JumpReLU, Gated and TopK SAE features using both a
       blinded human rating study, and by obtaining automated ratings using a language model.

      5.3.1    M ANUAL I NTERPRETABILITY
379
380   Methodology Our experimental setup resembles the manual interpretability study methodology
381   described in Rajamanoharan et al. (2024). Five human raters9 were asked to rate features from 81
382   SAEs, 27 of each type (Gated, TopK and JumpReLU) trained across multiple sites, layers and levels
383   of sparsity. Each rater was presented with a feature dashboard (see Fig. 12), providing summary in-
384   formation and activating examples from the full activation spectrum of a feature, and asked whether
385
      the feature was mostly monosemantic, with allowed answers being ‘Yes’, ‘Maybe’ and ‘No’.
386
387
      Results As shown in Fig. 5a, all three SAE varieties exhibit similar rating distributions, con-
      sistent with prior results comparing TopK and Gated SAEs (Cunningham & Conerly, 2024; Gao
388
      et al., 2024) and furthermore showing that JumpReLU SAEs are similarly interpretable, i.e. that
389
      JumpReLU SAEs do not sacrifice feature interpretability to obtain more faithful reconstructions.
390
391
      5.3.2    AUTOMATED I NTERPRETABILITY
392
393   To complement the manual interpretability study, we also evaluate SAE features using a language
394   model rater. This allows us to collect many more samples than in a manual study, although LM
395   ratings may be of more variable quality. To automatically rate features, we follow the two-step
396   approach introduced in Bills et al. (2023) for neurons, and later used for SAE features (Bricken et al.,
397
      2023; Lieberum, 2024; Cunningham et al., 2023), of first using the LM to generate an explanation
      for a given feature and then asking the LM to predict feature activations based on that explanation.
398
399
      Methodology We used Gemini 1.5 Flash (Gemini Team, 2024) for explanation generation and
400
      activation simulation. In the first step, Flash was given a list of sequences that activate a given feature
401
      to different degrees, together with discretized activation values, and asked for a natural language
402   explanation of the feature consistent with these activation values. In the second step, we presented
403   Flash the same token sequences in a fresh context and asked it to predict activation values using the
404   explanation it gave in the first step. We then computed the Pearson correlation between the simulated
405   and ground truth activation values for each sequence. We evaluated 1,000 randomly sampled features
406   from 154 SAEs taken from all three types (Gated, JumpReLU and TopK).
407
408   Results We fit a logistic regression model to measure the effect of SAE type and L0 on the ability
409   of the Gemini Flash to accurately simulate activations based on its explanations.10 We also include
410   terms in the model to account for each SAE’s site and layer, after finding simulation quality varies
411   widely between sites and layers (Fig. 13). Upon fitting the model, we find that JumpReLU SAE
412
      features have 27% greater odds of being well-simulated by Gemini Flash than Gated SAE features
      (OR=1.27, 95% CI: 1.23–1.31, p < 0.001) and that JumpReLU SAE features have similar odds
413
      of being well-simulated as TopK SAE features (OR=0.98, 95% CI: 0.95–1.01). We also find that
414
      doubling L0 lowers the odds of simulatability by 7% (OR=0.93, 95% CI: 0.92–0.94, p < 0.001).
415   See Appendix J.1.2 for more on the interpretation and limitations of these results.

             6           R ELATED WORK
470
471
             Recent interest in training SAEs on LM activations (Sharkey et al., 2022; Bricken et al., 2023;
             Cunningham et al., 2023) stems from the twin observations that many concepts appear to be linearly
472
             represented in LM activations (Elhage et al., 2021; Gurnee et al., 2023; Olah et al., 2020; Park et al.,
473
             2023) and that dictionary learning (Mallat & Zhang, 1993; Olshausen & Field, 1997) may help
474          uncover these representations at scale. It is also hoped that the sparse representations learned by
475          SAEs may be a better basis for identifying computational subgraphs that carry out specific tasks in
476          LMs (Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023; Dunefsky et al., 2024) and for finer-grained control
477          over LMs’ outputs (Conmy & Nanda, 2024; Templeton et al., 2024).
478
             Recent improvements to SAE architectures – including TopK SAEs (Gao et al., 2024) and Gated
479
             SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024) – as well as improvements to initialization and sparsity penal-
480          ties. Conerly et al. (2024) have helped ameliorate the trade-off between sparsity and fidelity and
481          overcome the challenge of SAE features dying during training. Like JumpReLU SAEs, both Gated
482          and TopK SAEs possess a thresholding mechanism that determines which features to include in a re-
483          construction; indeed, with weight sharing, Gated SAEs are mathematically equivalent to JumpReLU
484          SAEs, although they are trained using a different loss function. JumpReLU SAEs are also closely
485          related to ProLU SAEs (Taggart, 2024), which use a (different) STE to train an activation threshold,
             but do not match the performance of Gated or TopK SAEs (Gao et al., 2024).

      The activation function defined in Eq. (2) was named JumpReLU in Erichson et al. (2019) and TRec
487   in Konda et al. (2015). Both TopK and JumpReLU activation functions are closely related to acti-
488   vation pruning techniques such as ASH (Djurisic et al., 2023). The term straight through estimator
489   was introduced in Bengio et al. (2013), although it is an old idea.11 STEs have found applications
490   in areas such as training quantized networks (e.g. Hubara et al. (2016)) and circumventing defenses
491   to adversarial examples (Athalye et al., 2018). Our interpretation of STEs in terms of gradients of
492   the expected loss is related to Yin et al. (2019), although they do not make the connection between
493   STEs and KDE. Louizos et al. (2018) also show how it is possible to train models using a L0 spar-
494   sity penalty – on weights rather than activations in their case – by introducing stochasticity in the
495
      weights and taking the gradient of the expected loss.

      7   C ONCLUSION
498
499
500   Our evaluations show that JumpReLU SAEs produce reconstructions that consistently match or
501
      exceed the faithfulness of TopK SAEs, and exceed the faithfulness of Gated SAEs, at a given level
      of sparsity. They also show that the average JumpReLU SAE feature is similarly interpretable to
502
      the average Gated or TopK SAE feature, according to manual raters and automated evaluations.
503
      Although JumpReLU SAEs do have relatively more very high frequency features than Gated SAEs,
504   they are similar to TopK SAEs in this respect.
505
506   In light of these observations, and taking into account the efficiency of training with the JumpReLU
507
      loss – which requires no auxiliary terms (unlike Gated SAEs) and does not involve relatively expen-
      sive TopK operations – we consider JumpReLU SAEs to be a mild improvement over existing SAE
508
      training methodologies.
509
510
511   Limitations We note two key limitations with our study. Firstly, the evaluations presented in
512   this paper concern training SAEs on several sites and layers of a single model, Gemma 2 9B. In
513   Appendix G we also show that JumpReLU provides a better sparsity-vs-fidelity trade off than TopK
514
      and Gated SAEs when trained on Pythia 2.8B (Biderman et al., 2023) activations. Nevertheless
      we would welcome attempts to replicate our results on other model families. Secondly, since the
515
      main contribution of this paper is a methodology for successfully training SAEs with discontinuous
516
      loss functions, we have focused our evaluations on a few key metrics: the sparsity-fidelity trade-off,
517   feature interpretability, feature activation frequencies, and disentanglement in a single setting. It
518   would be valuable to compare these SAE varieties on a broader selection of metrics, particularly on
519   evaluations that closely correspond to downstream tasks that may benefit from SAEs.
520
521
522
      Further work JumpReLU SAEs do suffer from a few limitations that we hope can be improved
      with further work:

528

529
             • STEs can be used to train SAEs with other sparsity penalties that may prove more useful

       H.2    B OTH THE J UMP R E LU ACTIVATION FUNCTION AND L0 SPARSITY PENALTY ARE
977
              NECESSARY FOR J UMP R E LU SAE S ’ IMPROVED PERFORMANCE
978
979    The way we define and train JumpReLU SAEs in this work modifies standard (i.e. ReLU) SAE
980    training in two key respects: (a) we switch the encoder activation function from ReLU to JumpReLU
981    and (b) we use a L0, not L1, sparsity penalty during training. In this section, we investigate whether
982    the improved fidelity at given sparsity exhibited by JumpReLU SAEs is solely attributable to just
983    one of these changes.
984    To do this, we train two SAE variants that incorporate, in isolation, just one of the changes listed in
985    the previous paragraph:
986
987
              • ReLU SAE with L0 sparsity penalty. We train a SAE with a ReLU activation function
                using a L0 penalty instead of the standard L1 penalty. In order for this to work – and
988
                considering that the ReLU activation function does not have a trainable threshold – we
989
                define straight-through-estimators as in Eqs. (38) and (39) so that the L0 penalty provides
990             a gradient signal to the ReLU SAE’s encoder parameters.
991           • JumpReLU SAE with L1 sparsity penalty. We also train a SAE using the L1 penalty
992             but with a JumpReLU (instead of ReLU) activation function. With a JumpReLU activation
993             function, there are two plausible ways to define the L1 sparsity penalty: we could either
994             define it in terms of the L1-norm of the pre-activations, i.e. Lsparsity (x) := ∥π(x)∥1 , or in
995             terms of the L1-norm of the difference between the pre-activations and their correspond-
996             ing thresholds, i.e. Lsparsity (x) := ∥π(x) − θ∥1 .13 In practice, we find that the second
997             definition – penalizing the positive difference between feature pre-activations and the cor-
998
                responding feature thresholds – leads to significantly better performance, hence this is the
                version of the L1 sparsity penalty that we use in this study.
999
1000   As shown in Fig. 10, both these SAE variants fare poorly when compared to JumpReLU SAEs
1001   trained with a L0 sparsity penalty as described in Section 3. Indeed, training a ReLU SAE with
1002   L0 sparsity penalty leads to significantly worse performance than even Gated SAEs, underscoring
1003   the importance of incorporating a thresholding mechanism in the SAE architecture as explained in
1004
       Fig. 1.
1005   Thus, we find that both the JumpReLU activation function and the L0 sparsity penalty introduced in
1006   the JumpReLU training methodology described in Section 3 are necessary for JumpReLU SAEs to
1007   attain the superior reconstruction fidelity versus sparsity curves shown in Figs. 2, 14 and 15.

1190   J.2.1    D ETAILED METHODOLOGY
1191
1192   We began by prompting Gemini 1.5 Pro (Gemini Team, 2024) to list 150 baseball players, 100
1193
       basketball players, 100 American football players and 100 ice hockey players. These sports were
       chosen on the basis that they are all team sports popular in North America, to minimize the risk
1194
       of spurious correlations when searching for SAE features that represent these sports. We checked
1195
       whether Gemma 2 9B could retrieve the sport played by each of these athletes by asking it to com-
1196   plete the prompt “Fact: the sportsperson <player-name> is known for playing the sport of”; on this
1197   basis we removed one football player that Gemma 2 9B could not correctly classify.
1198
1199
       We then split this dataset into 50 baseball players who would be used for the disentanglement eval-
       uation itself and the remaining baseball, basketball, football and ice hockey players who would be
1200
       used to search for SAE features representing the sports of baseball and basketball. To perform this
1201
       split, we first evaluated the probabilities assigned by Gemma 2 9B to four-digit completions of the
1202   prompt “Fact: the sportsperson <player-name> was born in the year ” ranging from 1890 to 2019.
1203   The 50 players for whom Gemma 2 9B assigned the highest probabilities to their year of birth were
1204   selected for the disentanglement split, with the remaining players used for the feature search split.15
1205
       For each SAE in the experiment, we used the feature-search split of the dataset – consisting of 399
1206
       baseball, basketball, football and ice hockey players in almost equal proportion – to obtain two rank
1207
       orderings of the SAE’s features: firstly ordering the features by how well they classify baseball
1208   players and secondly ordering the features by how well they classify basketball players.
1209
1210   To do this, we first extracted SAE feature activations on the tokens representing each athlete’s name
1211
       in the prompt “Fact: the sportsperson <player-name>”, hypothesizing that SAE activations at these
       name token positions are likely to contain features representing the sport played by that athlete
1212
       (Meng et al., 2022; Geva et al., 2023; Nanda et al., 2023). We then took the sequence-wise maximum
1213
       of the SAE activations across all name tokens for each player, yielding a scalar score per feature and
1214   per player. Finally, we computed the area under the ROC curve (AUROC) between each feature’s
1215   scores for the athletes and their ground truth sport labels and used these AUROC values to rank order
1216   the features according to how well they separate baseball and non-baseball players (for the baseball
1217   feature ranking) or basketball and non-basketball players (for the basketball feature ranking).
1218
       Having done this, we discarded features that had an AUROC score below 0.75 or were not in the
1219
       top-3 for each ranking, in order to get two lists of putative basketball and baseball features – each
1220   containing up to three features – for the SAE being evaluated.
1221
1222   For each combination of a basketball feature from the basketball features list and a baseball feature
1223
       from the baseball features list, we would evaluate how well these two features are able to change the
       model’s representation of the sport played by the athlete to basketball without affecting the model’s
1224
       knowledge of the athlete’s year of birth. To perform this evaluation, we would compute the model’s
1225
       completions to the prompts “Fact: the sportsperson <player-name> is known for playing the sport
1226   of” and “Fact: the sportsperson <player-name> was born in the year ” and use these completions to
1227   calculate the editing success and editing localization metrics shown in Fig. 5b. Crucially, we would
1228   compute the completions after intervening on the model, setting the chosen baseball feature’s activa-
1229   tion to zero and the chosen basketball feature’s activation to the dataset median across the basketball
1230   players in the feature-search dataset.1617 Since our aim is to update the model’s representations for
1231   the players in the prompts, we only apply this intervention at the tokens in each prompt that represent
1232   a player’s name.

1234   all trained to reconstruct Gemma 2 9B’s residual stream activations after layer 9; these are exactly

1247   J.2.2     F URTHER DISCUSSION OF THE RESULTS
1248
1249
       Fig. 5b also appears to show that JumpReLU SAEs provide a better trade off between editing success
       and localization than TopK SAEs. However, we caution against drawing overly general conclusions
1250
       about the disentanglement properties of JumpReLU versus TopK SAEs from these results due to
1251
       some limitations of the methodology described above:
1252
1253            • Since we limit our interventions to those that involve a single basketball feature and single
1254              baseball feature, editing success depends crucially on whether those features are cleanly
1255
                  represented in the SAE being evaluated. Especially when evaluating on specialized con-
                  cepts like a particular sport, it is important to evaluate on as many SAEs as possible (ideally
1256
                  multiple SAEs trained with the same hyperparameters but different seeds) in order to re-
1257
                  duce the variance arising from the uncertainty in whether the features we wish to steer with
1258              are present or not.
1259            • We have measured editing success and editing localization using just one prompt for each
1260              metric. Ideally, we should test the extent to which an intervention has changed the model’s
1261              representation of the player’s sport across multiple prompts. Similarly, we should test the
1262              extent to which the intervention has disrupted the model’s knowledge of other facts about
1263              the player across both multiple facts (i.e. not just their year or birth) and multiple prompts
1264              to elicit each fact.
1265            • Our evaluation measures disentanglement in a very particular setting. To draw general
1266
                  conclusions about the disentanglement properties of different SAE types, we would need
                  to apply the methodology to multiple concepts, including concepts in non-factual settings.
1267
1268   Addressing these limitations will require both extra compute and extra effort to compile suitable
1269   datasets. Since the main contribution of this paper is to present a new SAE training methodology,
1270   rather than to make progress in the science of evaluating SAEs (which we nonetheless think is a very
1271   important research direction for the field), we leave these improvements to further work.

1461   Two implementation details to note are:
1462         • We use the logarithm of threshold, i.e. log(θ), as our trainable parameter, to ensure that the
1463           threshold remains positive during training.

1467           passing these to the JumpReLU activation function or the Heaviside step function used to
1468

1469
               but it ensures that negative pre-activations cannot bias gradient estimates in the backward
1470
