011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces a novel approach to tackle the challenges of causal modeling
014            and attribution in sparse and non-continuous data with limited feature knowledge.
015            Traditional methods rely on static inputs and lack adaptability to dynamic changes
016            in causal relationships, resulting in a limited understanding and goodness-of-fit.
017            To address this challenge, we introduce a unique causal discovery framework on
018            real-world sparse datasets. We leverage a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) by dis-
019            covering causal relationships between the variables by identifying confounder-
020
               treatment pairs that make the variable selection process robust and efficient. We
               propose a three-stage causal model that uses multiple distinct regressors such as
021
               likelihood-based, tree-based, and Generalized Additive Models (GAMs). Further-
022
               more, we introduce a Model Score by including the sensitivity analysis involving
023            random shuffling confounders and treatments to select the best optimal model. We
024            implement a partial dependency approach to understand the attribution of vari-
025            ables, contributing by adding a 53% increase in R2 score compared to traditional
026            methods. This research underscores the limitations of conventional approaches in
027            addressing real-world challenges to address practical scenarios effectively.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Understanding causal relationships is crucial for informed decision-making, but traditional meth-
034   ods struggle with sparse datasets, leading to inaccuracies in identifying causes and constructing
035   Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). This highlights the need for more robust techniques to ensure
036   reliable causal inferences in such challenging scenarios. This paper introduces a causal discovery
037   framework, termed the ”union” framework, which seeks to address these challenges by integrat-
038   ing existing domain knowledge with the discovery of new indicative causal pairs. This approach
039
      leverages a combination of both parametric and non-parametric models, enhanced by advanced sen-
      sitivity analyses. By incorporating randomized shuffling of confounders and treatment variables, our
040
      methodology ensures the identification of robust causal models, thereby enhancing the accuracy and
041
      reliability of the derived inferences Rohrer (2018); VanderWeele (2019). Furthermore, we propose
042   a comprehensive methodology for attributing outcomes to both direct and indirect effects of causal
043   variables, while separating the baseline effects. This is achieved through a detailed algorithm that
044   applies scaling techniques, allowing for precise estimation of variable attributions.
045
      One significant limitation of existing methodologies is their inability to accurately attribute direct
046
      and indirect effects. These approaches often struggle to effectively disentangle the influence of
047
      primary variables—those directly affecting the outcome—from secondary variables with indirect
048   causal relationships Peters et al. (2016); VanderWeele (2019). To address this shortcoming, we im-
049   plemented an output decomposition model that removes seasonality, trends, and other extraneous
050   effects, thereby constructing a robust baseline model. Moreover, current methodologies for dis-
051   covering causal relationships are particularly inefficient when dealing with sparse observations. To
052   the best of our knowledge, there are no existing methods that effectively incorporate or backprop-
053   agate the effects of sensitivity treatments to identify the optimal combination of models for causal
      inference.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
055
056   Causal inference begins with the specification of essential assumptions, such as domain-specific
057   assumptions, followed by the construction of a causal graph. This process involves identifying con-
058   founder pairs, attributing causal influences, diagnosing the causal structure, and estimating effects
059   Shao et al. (2017); Sharma et al. (2021); Ibeling & Icard (2024); Zheng et al. (2024). Traditional
060   machine learning algorithms used for effect estimation include the average treatment effect (ATE)
061   and the conditional average treatment effect (CATE), both facilitating the estimation of causal ef-
062   fects. Moreover, these methods address the issue of confounding effects prevalent in observational
063   studies, thereby ensuring more accurate treatment effect estimation.
064   In prior research, Colnet et al. (2024) underscores a critical limitation in existing causal discovery
065   methods, namely their inability to control the family-wise error rate, which increases the likelihood
066   of erroneous identification of causal relationships. Furthermore, emphasizes the significant advan-
067   tage of invariant causal predictors over non-causal predictors, noting that the influence of invariant
068   causal predictors on the outcome variable remains stable despite environmental changes. Peters et al.
069   (2016); Bergstra & Bengio (2012); Frauen et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2024) also addresses limita-
070
      tions in the field, particularly highlighting the challenges associated with relying on randomized
      controlled trials (RCTs). Additionally, Peters et al. (2016) caution that large observational studies,
071
      while offering high representativeness, may inadvertently introduce biases by conflating confound-
072
      ing effects with the treatment of interest.

075   3    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
076
077   The objective of a sparse causal model is to identify the causal relationships among variables and
078
      attribute both direct and indirect effects of these variables on the outcome. Given a sparse input
      dataset D = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xm , Y ), where Xinput = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xm } represents the set of input
079
      variables and Y denotes the outcome variable, the task is to determine the corresponding adjacency
080
      matrix that captures these causal relationships. In addition, the goal is to construct a robust causal
081   model capable of accurate output prediction, along with quantifying the respective attributions of
082   each variable to the outcome.

096   4    M ETHODOLOGY
097
098   We propose a methodology that leverages transfer function-based estimation within a rigorously
099   constructed causal model, incorporating causal discovery. This approach involves analyzing the
100
      causal directed acyclic graph (DAG), estimating sensitivity (R²) scores, and evaluating the impact of
      baseline metrics alongside causal variables. Our study offers a solution to the challenges of causal
101
      modeling and attribution in sparse, feature-limited, and non-continuous datasets. Unlike traditional
102
      methods that rely on static inputs, our approach adapts to dynamic changes, providing a deeper
103   understanding of causal relationships.
