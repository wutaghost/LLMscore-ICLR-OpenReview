011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) is a widely used method for solving large-scale
014            Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) problems. The effectiveness of LNS
015            crucially depends on the choice of the search neighborhood. However, existing
016
               strategies either rely on expert knowledge or computationally expensive Machine
               Learning (ML) approaches, both of which struggle to scale effectively for large
017
               problems. To address this, we propose LLM-LNS, a novel Large Language Model
018
               (LLM)-driven LNS framework for large-scale MILP problems. Our approach in-
019            troduces a dual-layer self-evolutionary LLM agent to automate neighborhood se-
020            lection, discovering effective strategies with scant small-scale training data that
021            generalize well to large-scale MILPs. The inner layer evolves heuristic strate-
022            gies to ensure convergence, while the outer layer evolves evolutionary prompt
023            strategies to maintain diversity. Experimental results demonstrate that the pro-
024            posed dual-layer agent outperforms state-of-the-art agents such as FunSearch and
025            EOH. Furthermore, the full LLM-LNS framework surpasses manually designed
026            LNS algorithms like ACP, ML-based LNS methods like CL-LNS, and large-scale
027
               solvers such as Gurobi and SCIP. It also achieves superior performance compared
               to advanced ML-based MILP optimization frameworks like GNN&GBDT and
028
               Light-MILPopt, further validating the effectiveness of our approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) is a versatile and widely used mathematical framework
034
      for solving complex optimization problems across various domains, including transportation man-
035
      agement (Klanšek, 2015), bin packing (Fleszar, 2022), and production planning (Adrio et al., 2023).
036   MILPs are challenging to solve efficiently due to their NP-hard nature (Kim et al., 2021) and the
037   exponential growth of the search space as problem size increases (Vázquez et al., 2018). To address
038   these challenges, researchers have developed two primary approaches (Zhang et al., 2023): exact
039   algorithms, such as branch-and-bound, and heuristic-based approximation methods.
040
      While exact algorithms like branch-and-bound (Boyd & Mattingley, 2007; Morrison et al., 2016)
041
      are effective for small to medium-sized problems, they struggle with the computational demands
042   of larger instances. This has led to the rise of heuristic methods, particularly Large Neighbor-
043   hood Search (LNS) (Ahuja et al., 2002; Mara et al., 2022), which iteratively improves solutions by
044   destroying and repairing parts of the current solution, allowing for exploration of large neighbor-
045   hoods without full re-optimization (Song et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2023a). However, LNS performance
046   depends heavily on neighborhood selection, which is often hand-crafted and requires significant
047   domain expertise. Designing these operators can be labor-intensive and prone to cold-start issues,
048   where limited prior knowledge is available to guide the search (Zhang et al., 2023).
049   In recent years, machine learning (ML) techniques, including reinforcement learning (Wu et al.,
050   2021; Song et al., 2020) and imitation learning (Sonnerat et al., 2021; Nair et al., 2020), have been
051   applied to automate the design of neighborhood selection strategies. These methods aim to learn
052   heuristic strategies from training datasets, reducing reliance on expert knowledge and allowing the
053   algorithms to adapt to new, homogeneous instances. However, ML-based LNS approaches come
      with their own challenges. For reinforcement learning, slow convergence is a common issue (Beggs,


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2005), particularly in large-scale MILP problems, due to the vast search space and the need for
055   extensive exploration before identifying effective strategies. On the other hand, imitation learning
056   requires large amounts of high-quality, labeled data, which can be computationally expensive to
057   generate using expert algorithms (Huang et al., 2023b). As a result, both hand-crafted and ML-
058   based methods struggle to efficiently solve large-scale MILP problems.
059
      The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) offers a promising solution to these challenges. Un-
060
      like traditional hand-crafted methods, LLMs come pretrained with vast general knowledge, allow-
061   ing them to reason about complex tasks and learn problem structures with minimal training data,
062   thus avoiding cold-start issues. Additionally, LLMs can adapt to new problems through interac-
063   tive reasoning, reducing the need for extensive exploration and addressing the slow convergence of
064   reinforcement learning. Furthermore, LLMs can dynamically generate heuristic strategies without
065   relying on large labeled datasets, which significantly reduces the computational overhead typically
066   associated with imitation learning (Yang et al., 2024; Lange et al., 2024). While LLMs have shown
067   potential in generating strategies for combinatorial optimization problems(Ye et al., 2024; Elhenawy
068   et al., 2024), they often lack the problem-specific refinement needed to produce efficient heuristics
069
      without additional guidance (Plaat et al., 2024). Approaches like FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al.,
      2024) and Evolution of Heuristic (EOH) (Liu et al., 2024) combine LLMs with evolutionary algo-
070
      rithms (Simon, 2013), but rely on fixed strategies, limiting solution diversity and leading to poor
071
      convergence due to insufficient directionality. This underscores the need for a more adaptive frame-
072   work to fully harness LLMs for large-scale MILP problems.
073
074   In this paper, we propose LLM-LNS, a novel Large Language Model-driven Large Neighborhood
075
      Search framework designed specifically for solving large-scale MILP problems, which can discover
      effective neighborhood selection strategies for LNS with scant small-scale training data that gener-
076
      alize well to large-scale MILPs. Our key innovations are as follows:
077
078          • Dual-layer Self-evolutionary LLM Agent: We propose a novel LLM agent with a dual-
079            layer self-evolutionary mechanism for automatically generating heuristic strategies. The
080            inner layer evolves both thoughts and code representations of heuristic strategies, ensur-
081            ing convergence, while the outer layer evolves evolutionary prompt strategies to maintain
082            diversity, preventing the search process from getting trapped in local optima.
083          • Differential Memory for Directional Evolution: We introduce differential evolution in
084            the agent to guide both crossover and variation. By feeding the fitness values of parent
085            strategies back into the LLM, we leverage its memory to learn how to evolve from less
086            effective to more effective strategies. This feedback mechanism enables the LLM to act as
087
               an optimizer, identifying promising directions and leading to more efficient improvements.
088          • Application to Neighborhood Selection in LNS: We apply the proposed dual-layer LLM
089            agent to the neighborhood selection strategy generation in LNS. By utilizing only a small
090
               amount of training data from small-scale problems, the LLM agent can discover new neigh-
               borhood selection strategies that generalize well to large-scale MILP problems.
091
092          • Comprehensive Experimental Validation: We validate the effectiveness of our proposed
093
               LLM-LNS at two levels. First, we test its agent’s performance on heuristic generation tasks
               of combinatorial optimization problems, demonstrating its superiority over state-of-the-art
094
               methods such as FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) and EOH (Liu et al., 2024).
095
               Second, we evaluate its performance on large-scale MILP problems, where it outperforms
096            traditional LNS methods (e.g., ACP (Ye et al., 2023a)), ML-based LNS methods (e.g., CL-
097            LNS (Huang et al., 2023b)), and leading solvers like Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC,
098            2023) and SCIP (Maher et al., 2016). Furthermore, our proposed LLM-LNS surpasses
099            modern ML-based optimization frameworks for large-scale MILP, such as GNN&GBDT
100            (Ye et al., 2023c) and Light-MILPopt (Ye et al., 2023b). These results confirm the effec-
101            tiveness of our proposed LLM-LNS in solving large-scale optimization problems.

119   2.2   L ARGE N EIGHBORHOOD S EARCH
120
121   Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) is a widely used heuristic for solving MILP problems. It itera-
122
      tively improves solutions by exploring predefined neighborhoods around a current solution. How-
      ever, the effectiveness of LNS heavily relies on the neighborhood selection strategy, as poor choices
123
      can lead to stagnation in local optima.
124
125   Several approaches have been proposed to address this challenge. One common method is random-
126   LNS (Song et al., 2020), which randomly partitions integer variables into disjoint subsets and op-
127   timizes one subset in each iteration while fixing the others. However, random-LNS uses a fixed
128
      neighborhood size and overlooks correlations between decision variables, limiting its performance.
      To overcome these drawbacks, the Adaptive Constraint Partitioning (ACP) framework (Ye et al.,
129
      2023a) introduces a dynamic strategy that adjusts the neighborhood size, optimizing all decision
130
      variables associated with randomly selected constraints in each iteration. This ensures that highly
131   correlated variables are optimized together, improving performance. Similar strategies have been
132   explored in other works (Huang et al., 2023a; Han et al., 2023), but they still rely on manually
133   designed heuristics, requiring expert knowledge and lacking adaptability to new problem instances.
134
      To address this limitation, machine learning methods have been applied to automate neighborhood
135
      selection. Reinforcement learning (RL) approaches define reward functions based on solution im-
136
      provements, allowing models to learn promising neighborhoods through interaction with the prob-
137   lem (Wu et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020; Nair et al., 2020). Imitation learning, on the other hand, uses
138   large amount of large-scale sampling (Huang et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2023) or expert algorithms
139   (Sonnerat et al., 2021) to guide the selection process. While these techniques reduce reliance on
140   handcrafted strategies, RL struggles with convergence in large-scale MILP problems, and imitation
141   learning requires extensive sampling, making it computationally expensive. This highlights the need
142   for more efficient, automatically designed neighborhood selection strategies.

198   In this section, we introduce LLM-LNS, a Large Language Model-driven Large Neighborhood
199   Search framework designed to solve large-scale MILP problems. As shown in Figure 1, the frame-
200   work is composed of two main components: a Dual-layer Self-evolutionary LLM Agent and a
201   Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search process.

203   3.1           D UAL - LAYER S ELF - EVOLUTIONARY LLM AGENT
204
205   The Dual-layer Self-evolutionary LLM Agent is the core component of our framework, responsi-
206
      ble for generating and evolving heuristic and prompt strategies. The Dual-layer Self-evolutionary
      Structure consists of an Inner Layer that evolves heuristic strategies to accelerate convergence,
207
      and an Outer Layer that evolves evolutionary prompt strategies to enhance diversity in heuristic
208
      generation. Another key innovation is the incorporation of Differential Memory for Directional
209   Evolution, which accelerates convergence by learning the direction of improvement from less ef-
210   fective strategy to better ones. Together, these innovations ensure a balance between exploration and
211   exploitation, significantly improving the efficiency and preventing stagnation in local optima.

251                                           prompt strategy, into the LLM. This produces the first generation of heuristic strategies.
252                                           For example, at generation 1, a basic heuristic is initialized with a fitness value of 0.9595,
253
                                              based on a capacity ratio with penalty calculation, and is expressed both in natural language
                                              thought and executable code.
254
255                                         • Evolution of Heuristic Strategies: In each generation, new heuristic strategies are evolved
256                                           by selecting parent strategies from the current heuristic population. As detailed in Ap-
257
                                              pendix G, strategies with higher fitness values are more likely to be selected as parents.
                                              These parents are then combined with evolutionary strategies, selected from the Outer
258
                                              Layer’s population of prompt strategies (e.g., crossover or variation prompts), to guide
259
                                              the LLM in generating new offspring strategies. For instance, at generation 5, randomness
260                                           is introduced for exploration, achieving a fitness value of 0.9916. By generation 8, the
261                                           evolution process incorporates hybrid optimization techniques, such as genetic algorithms
262                                           combined with tabu search, resulting in a fitness value of 0.9934. This iterative process
263                                           enables the LLM to continually refine strategies and explore new solution spaces.
264                                         • Evaluation and Final Selection: After new heuristic strategies are generated, they are eval-
265                                           uated by integrating them into the Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search process, where
266                                           each heuristic is applied to solve small-scale instances from the training dataset. The per-
267                                           formance of each strategy is measured by its objective function value, which serves as its
268                                           fitness score. After multiple iterations of evolution and evaluation, the best-performing
269                                           heuristic strategies are identified based on their fitness. By generation 20, advanced tech-
                                              niques like swarm intelligence and simulated annealing are incorporated, and the final best

271             ation ensures that the framework converges to the most effective heuristic.

273   Outer Layer: Evolutionary Prompt Strategy Evolution. The Outer Layer focuses on evolving
274   evolutionary prompt strategies, which guide the LLM in generating new heuristic strategies. The
275   emphasis in this layer is on exploration to maintain diversity and prevent premature convergence
276   in the heuristic strategy population. The key stages of Outer Layer, as illustrated in Figure 1 and
277   Figure 2, include:
278
              • Initialization of Prompt Strategies: The initial set of evolutionary prompt strategies is hand-
279             crafted and designed to perform basic crossover and variation operations, instructing the
280             LLM on how to combine or modify existing heuristic strategies in the inner layer. For ex-
281             ample, at generation 1, basic prompt strategies like Cross1 and Cross2 are set, which help
282             the LLM generate new heuristic strategies by recombining or tweaking existing ones.
283           • Evolution of Prompt Strategies: As the evolution progresses, more complex prompt strate-
284             gies are introduced to address stagnation in the heuristic population. Specifically, if the top-
285             l individuals in the heuristic population remain unchanged for t consecutive generations,
286             we infer that the evolution may have converged to a local optimum. This triggers the evolu-
287             tion of new prompt strategies. As shown in Figure 2, signs of stagnation were observed in
288             both the 10th and 15th generations. In response, new prompt strategies were generated to
289             overcome the local optimality issue. At generation 10, prompts such as Cross3 and Cross4
290             were designed to enhance efficiency and reduce algorithmic complexity. By generation
291
                15, even more advanced strategies like Variation5 and Variation6 were introduced, incor-
                porating stochastic elements and adaptive learning to increase diversity and explore new
292
                heuristic possibilities. This systematic evolution of prompt strategies helps ensure that the
293
                heuristic population continues to evolve and does not get trapped in local optima.
294
              • Evaluation and Management of Prompt Strategies: To ensure the efficiency and effective-
295
                ness of the prompt strategy population, each prompt strategy is evaluated based on the
296
                performance of the heuristic strategies it generates. Specifically, for each prompt strategy,
297             the top-k performing heuristic strategies it produces are tracked, and the average fitness
298             score of these heuristics is used as the fitness score for the prompt strategy itself. This
299             fitness-based evaluation allows us to manage the prompt population and control its size.
300             As the number of prompt strategies increases over generations, underperforming strate-
301             gies are pruned to prevent excessive growth and focus on the most effective strategies. For
302             example, by generation 18, several underperforming prompt strategies (e.g., the four worst-
303             performing strategies) are removed, as shown in Figure 2. This pruning process ensures that
304             only the most effective prompt strategies continue to evolve, maintaining both diversity and
305
                efficiency in the evolutionary process. For parameter details, see Appendix A.
306   The synergy between the Inner Layer and Outer Layer drives rapid evolution of effective heuris-
307   tics and novel evolutionary prompt strategies, as shown in Figure 2. Early generations focus on
308   basic principles, but with the introduction of advanced prompt strategies, such as complexity reduc-
309   tion and adaptive learning, the system quickly adapts to overcome local optima. Notably, the sharp
310   performance improvements between generations 5 to 15 demonstrate the framework’s ability to au-
311   tonomously discover and refine creative strategies, leading to continuous enhancements in heuristic
312   performance. This dual-layered approach ensures efficient exploration and exploitation, enabling
313   the LLM-LNS framework to tackle large-scale problems with minimal human intervention.

809   optimization problems, specifically targeting Online Bin Packing (BP) and the Travelling Sales-

813   For Online Bin Packing, we adopt the settings described in (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) and
814

815

816

825   the number of edges, while the output is an updated distance matrix. GLS then applies local search
826   operators iteratively on the updated landscape to refine the solution. In our experiments, we utilize
827   two common local search operators: the relocate operator and the 2-opt operator, which are widely
828   recognized for their effectiveness in TSP optimization (Arnold & Sörensen, 2019).
829   These settings are aligned with those used in EOH to ensure fair comparisons and reproducibil-
830   ity. Detailed descriptions of the inputs, outputs, and operators are provided in the appendix of the

            • Inner and Outer Layer Prompt Initialization and Evolution: As shown in Sec. B.2,
919           our approach leverages a dual-layer architecture, where the inner layer evolves heuristic
920           strategies by modifying solution components, while the outer layer evolves the prompt
921           structure guiding the inner layer, balancing exploration and exploitation. The inner layer
922           prompts iteratively generate heuristics by scoring decision variables based on their contri-
923           butions to the objective function and constraints, with randomness included to avoid local

931           random strategies and gradually evolving into more effective ones through the dual-layer
932
              self-evolutionary process. The initial strategies are simple and focus on ranking decision

933
              the LLM agent introduces additional complexity, such as incorporating randomness and
934
              penalizing larger deviations from the current solution, improving the robustness of the gen-
935           erated heuristics. The progression of the population is guided by the outer layer, which
936           adjusts the structure and focus of prompts to encourage exploration and avoid premature
937           convergence. The inner layer then refines specific solution components in response to the
938           prompts, iteratively improving the performance of the heuristic strategies. As seen from the
939           evolution of objective scores, the dual-layer system enables the generation of increasingly
940           effective heuristics, balancing exploration with exploitation to achieve superior results in
941           various problem instances.

943           Agent: As shown in Sec. B.4, both the Evolution of Heuristic (EoH) method and our
944           Dual-layer Self-Evolution LLM Agent utilize LLM-based evolutionary processes to gen-
945           erate heuristic strategies for the Bin Packing Online problem. The strategy generated by
946           EoH approach, while leveraging LLM to evolve heuristics, focuses primarily on a hybrid
947
              scoring system that combines utilization ratios, dynamic adjustments, and an exponentially
              decaying factor. This method is effective but tends to rely on a more static set of features
948
              and parameters, which limits its adaptability across diverse problem instances. In con-
949
              trast, our Dual-layer Self-Evolution LLM Agent incorporates a more dynamic and adaptive
950           strategy. By combining nonlinear capacity scaling, relative size assessment, and historical
951           penalties for overutilized bins, our approach allows for greater flexibility and adaptabil-
952           ity. Specifically, the generated heuristics dynamically adjust based on remaining capacity,
953           item size, and previous bin usage, thereby balancing local search with global optimiza-

              objective_coefficient):

            for i in range(m):
1306            lhs_value = sum(value[i][j] * current_solution[site[i][j]] for j in range(k[i]))
1307            for j in range(k[i]):
                    variable_index = site[i][j]
1308                if lhs_value >= constraint[i]:
                        penalty = lhs_value - constraint[i]
1309                    contribution = penalty * value[i][j]
                        neighbor_score[variable_index] += contribution
1310                else:
1311                    contribution = value[i][j]
                        neighbor_score[variable_index] -= contribution

            with np.errstate(divide=’ignore’, invalid=’ignore’):

            neighbor_score /= np.max(neighbor_score) if np.max(neighbor_score) != 0 else 1

            neighbor_score += rand_factor
