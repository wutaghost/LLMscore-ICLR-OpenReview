009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Complex 3D environments replete with dynamic interactions among multiple
012            agents and objects are essential for the development of embodied intelligent agents.
013            To facilitate research on Multi-Agent (MA) systems, we introduce TeamCraft, a
014            challenging MA benchmark based on the Minecraft game. Instead of the abstract
015            vector inputs commonly provided to agents in MA systems research, TeamCraft
016            provides agents with multi-modal task specifications and observations. Given the
017            three-orthographic-view graph of the environment along with language instructions,
018            the agents must efficiently collaborate to complete assigned tasks. Such multi-
019
               modal inputs pose a higher level of difficulty, since agents must generalize across
               diverse object and background imagery, different numbers of agents, a wide range
020
               of tasks, etc. Our planner-generated dataset includes various tasks, such as building
021
               construction, smelting, and farming, with a total of 70,000 procedurally-generated
022            demonstrations that feature over 50 objects across a wide variety of scenes. We
023            test the generalization abilities of several baseline Vision-Language Model (VLM)
024            multi-agent control strategies in centralized and decentralized settings.
025            The TeamCraft platform and dataset are made publicly available at:
026            https://github.com/teamcraft-bench/teamcraft.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In an open-ended world, multiple autonomous agents with diverse skill sets should collaborate to
032   efficiently perform a broad spectrum of tasks. However, research aimed at developing agents capable
033   of efficiently performing intricate tasks within complex, embodied Multi-Agent (MA) environments
034   remains relatively limited. In particular, the autonomous agent research community has primarily
035   focused on navigation and object-based interactions in vision-language task-planning.
036   One research avenue endeavors to establish MA methodologies within 2D environments employing
037   solely vector inputs (Leibo et al., 2021; Suarez et al., 2021). However, such inputs suffer from limited
038   realism and are characterized by an inherent scarcity of comprehensive information. Concurrently,
039   another research avenue focuses on the creation of singular multi-task agents with the ability to
040   proficiently undertake a diverse range of tasks within domains encompassing both gaming and
041   robotics (Wang et al., 2023b;a; Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b;a). However, when considering
042   the elaborate interactions and uncertainties that arise in MA systems, the endeavor of formulating
043   multi-task agents within MA settings is decidedly more formidable and challenging.
044   To foster advancements in this domain, we have developed a comprehensive benchmark tailored
045   to MA embodied systems, dubbed TeamCraft. It utilizes the acclaimed Minecraft game as an
046   experimental platform and is targeteted at confronting the elaborate dynamics of MA interactions.
047   The benchmark encompasses the design of four multi-modal task categories: building construction,
048   ground clearing, farming, and object acquisition. Within the cooperative tasks, each assignment
049   necessitates consideration of fellow agents, spanning factors such as spatial positioning, inventory
050
      holdings, skill differentials, and initial vitality. Such nuanced assessments require divergent role
      allocation and task strategies during the planning phase. The collaborative actions unfolding during
051
      the execution phase encompass resource sharing and joint pursuit.
052
053   The TeamCraft dataset encompasses fundamental skills and tasks, meticulously orchestrated by
      hand-designed planners. We introduce two alternative baseline models, both trained on the TeamCraft

054
      dataset, that validate the efficacy of the generated data. The first model, MA-GPT-4o, employs a Multi-
055   modal Large Language Model (MLLM) as the planner to generate subgoals that guide individual
056   agents. The second model, MA-LLAVA, comprehensively encodes input facets and subsequently
057   fuses embeddings through an attention mechanism, culminating in the prediction of ultimate actions.
058   Our experimental findings demonstrate that both baseline models achieve competence across a subset
059   of tasks.

      In a nutshell, the primary contributions of this paper to the MA research community are as follows:
061
062         1. TeamCraft, a new embodied multi-modal multi-agent benchmark encompassing complex
063            tasks challenging multi-agent systems in a wide variety of generalization scenarios.
064
065         2. Novel applications of the GPT-4o and LLAVA models tailored to multi-agent scenarios.

068   2    R ELATED WORK
069
070   Environments for Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL): The recent success of MARL
071   methods (Lowe et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021; Long et al., 2020; 2024) has garnered attention, as these
072   methods explore cooperation and competence behaviors among agents. These methodologies have
073   been developed and tested on prominent platforms. However, many of these platforms involve 2D
074
      environments (Leibo et al., 2021; Suarez et al., 2021; Mordatch & Abbeel, 2017; Vinyals et al., 2019)
      and rely solely on vector observations. This limited scope poses challenges in terms of extending
075
      applicability to real-world scenarios.
076
077   Environments based on Minecraft: Minecraft games have fostered the development of embodied
078   AI methods. Initially, Malmo (Johnson et al., 2016) marked the advent of a Gym-style API tailored
079   to Minecraft. This endeavor paved the way for subsequent developments, such as MineRL (Guss
080
      et al., 2019) and MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022), which augmented the dataset and introduced a suite of
      benchmarking tasks. However, the focus of these benchmarks predominantly centers around single-
081
      agent tasks, with limited exploration of multi-agent scenarios in Minecraft. Despite their contributions,
082
      they remain devoid of multi-agent tasks. By contrast, TeamCraft concentrates exclusively on the
083   multi-agent setting. This distinctively sets it apart from all preceding Minecraft benchmarks.
084
085   Embodied agents in MA systems: Within the embodied multi-agent setting, several researchers
086
      have employed the AI2-THOR environment (Kolve et al., 2022). Jain et al. (2019) delved into the
      communication dynamics that enhance collaboration between two agents. Tan et al. (2020b) and
087
      Liu et al. (2022a) propounded the efficient exploration of environments as a central task for agents.
088
      Meanwhile, Liu et al. (2022b) introduced a model that dynamically decomposes tasks among different
089   agents, enabling dynamic task allocation. It is noteworthy, however, that the task propositions thus far
090   have primarily revolved around navigation subject to environmental constraints. However, Minecraft
091   is a multidimensional, visually immersive realm characterized by procedurally generated landscapes
092   and extraordinarily versatile game mechanics supporting an extensive spectrum of activities. This
093   provides rich environments ripe for intricate collaborations and the emergence of competence.

      3.1      S IMULATION E NVIRONMENT
136
137   TeamCraft utilizes Minecraft as its foundational environment, offering a complex, open-world setting
138   for multi-agent interactions. The environment features procedurally generated tasks, visually rich
139   changes, web-scale knowledge, and diverse cooperation strategies among agents. Each agent is
140   individually controlled via the Mineflayer1 interface, which provides low-level API functionalities for
141   bots to interact with the environment. TeamCraft utilizes Mineflayer’s APIs to 1) translate high-level
142   actions into low-level commands through nested API calls, 2) generate both first-person and third-
143   person RGB image perspectives, 3) enable Gym-like interactions across four tasks that challenge
144
      visual perception, spatial reasoning, and multi-agent task planning, and 4) support multiple agents.
      This framework facilitates the execution of intricate commands via self-explanatory high-level actions,
145
      allowing agents to collaboratively complete sophisticated tasks. Figure 1 illustrates the platform
146
      architecture.

189   3.3   C ENTRALIZED AND D ECENTRALIZED AGENTS
190
191   We have implemented two different categories of agents: centralized agents and decentralized agents.
192
      Centralized agents: These agents are given complete observational access to the environment,
193
      including the first person view, action history, and inventory information of all the agents. Based
194   on these comprehensive data, the model generates the actions for all agents simultaneously. This
195   approach leverages the full scope of information available in the environment to coordinate and
196   optimize the actions of all the agents collectively.
197
      Decentralized agents: These agents do not receive information about other agents except for the
198
      initial environment settings, which may include some inventory details of other agents, and the task
199
      description. The model generates actions solely for each individual agent based on the agent’s own
200   limited view. This setting simulates a more realistic scenario where agents operate independently
201   with restricted information, focusing on their own observations and actions absent of any centralized
202   coordination.
203
204   3.4   TASK D ESIGN
205
206   TeamCraft introduces a variety of complex and interactive tasks that challenge the agents’ capabilities
207   in planning, coordination, and execution within a collaborative and dynamic environment. Each
208   task is designed to test different facets of MA interaction, including communication strategies,
209   role distribution, real-time decision-making, and adaptability to changing environments. Tasks
210   require capabilities in visual observation understanding, agent status intercepting, action capability
211   understanding, language prompt understanding, continuous state understanding, and task action
212
      sequence planning. Here, we detail the specific tasks included in the TeamCraft benchmark. Task
      examples are shown in Figure 2.
213
214   Building: This task requires agents to collaboratively erect a structure based on a provided three
215   orthographic views blueprint (front, side, and top). Each agent possesses a unique inventory of
      building blocks necessary for the construction. The task requires agents not only to understand their

232
      Clearing: This task challenges agents to remove all blocks from a specified 6 × 6 area. Agents must
233
      employ appropriate tools to break the blocks, which vary in durability, thereby requiring multiple
234
      interactions for complete removal. The use of correct tools can dramatically reduce the time required
235   to remove blocks (up to 3× speedup). The agents must manage their tool assignments to optimize
236   block-breaking efficiency such that the time steps needed for one task can be minimized. Strategic
237   coordination is essential in this task as agents need to dynamically decide which blocks to target
238   based on their current tools and help each other minimize the overall time taken to clear the area.
239
      Farming: This task is designed to simulate agricultural activities, where agents must sow and harvest
240
      crops. Agents are required to plant seeds on designated farmland plots and observe plantings until the
241   crops reach maturity. Each crop has several growth stages from Level 0 (newly planted) to Level 7
242   (fully grown), and agents must identify when crops are ready to be harvested. The challenge lies in
243   dynamically allocating tasks among agents based on their positions, available seeds, and the maturity
244   of different crops. Effective task distribution and coordinated actions ensure maximum yield and
245   efficiency. For example, some agents can sow while others are planting, and they should stop when
246   their total crop yield is satisfactory.
247   Smelting: This task requires agents to obtain items processed using furnaces by gathering materials
248   and coordinating actions. Agents collect resources from the environment—by harvesting blocks or
249   killing mobs—and place them, or existing inventory items, into furnaces as smelting inputs. The
250   output will be the final goal item that can be categorized as food or item, where food can be “cooked
251   beef”, “cooked porkchop”, or “baked potato”, and item can be “glass” or “gold ingot” by smelting
252   sand or gold ore, respectively. Agents must also gather fuel (e.g., coal or lava buckets), with each
253   furnace accepting only one type of fuel. Furnaces are placed near the playground center (one or two
254   per task) and automatically smelt when supplied with fuel and items. Agents must use the provided
255
      tools, communicate effectively, and assign tasks efficiently due to dependencies in the smelting
      process.

      challenges the agents’ flexibility and adaptability in coordination and task execution. Additionally,
335
      differing task requirements lead to varying numbers of actions necessary for optimal task completion.
336
337
338
339   3.7   E XPERT D EMONSTRATION G ENERATION P IPELINE
340
341   To create a rich learning environment and effective training dataset for the TeamCraft tasks, systematic
342   scenario design and data collection methods are employed, as follows:
343
344
      Planner-based scenario design: Each task scenario is carefully crafted using classical planning
      algorithms, such as BFS, greedy search, and DFS, that consider all possible interactions within the
345
      environment. This includes optimal paths, resource distribution, and agent role assignments based on
346
      capabilities and task requirements.
347
348   Trajectory generation: Using Mineflayer interfaces controlled by heuristic methods such as the
349   Hungarian Algorithm and dynamic programming, the planner orchestrates the agents to execute the
350
      task, ensuring that actions are taken optimally. Each step’s effectiveness is assessed to guarantee
      efficient task completion.
351
352   Real-time interaction and feedback: Agents receive immediate feedback on their actions, which
353   includes success, failure, and updates on environmental states. This real-time data is crucial for
354   adjusting strategies and learning from interactions.
355
356
357
      3.8   T EST S ET AND G ENERALIZATION S ET
358
359
360   Each task features a test set, where agents are initialized with random position, orientation, and
361
      inventory. The rest variables follow the same distribution as the training data. To evaluate specific
      generalization capabilities of the model, we designed a generalization set for each task with hold-out
362
      elements excluded from the training data. We withheld test cases involving four agents, whereas the
363
      training demonstrations include only two or three agents. We also introduced one unseen scene and
364   an associated base block type not present during training. In addition to these general hold-outs, we
365   implemented the following task-specific exclusions:
366
367
      Building task: We randomly excluded 8 block placement shapes, defining how target blocks are
      arranged on the ground. These shapes varied in complexity, containing 5 to 12 blocks in both 2D and
368
      3D configurations. Additionally, we omitted 3 block materials that appeared in the clearing task but
369
      not in the building task.
370
371   Clearing task: We randomly held out 6 block placement shapes with block counts ranging from 4 to
372   9. We also excluded 3 block materials present in the building task but absent in the clearing task.
373   Farming task: We withheld one crop type, beetroot, that was unseen during training.
374
      Smelting task: We excluded four unseen objects from both food and item categories and introduced
375
      scenarios with 3 furnaces, as opposed to 1 to 2 furnaces in the training data.
376

      benchmark contains a total of 950 test cases.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
379
380
      4.1   BASELINES
381
382   In our experiments, we utilized the pretrained LLaVA-v1.6-Vicuna-7B and LLaVA-v1.6-Vicuna-13B
383   models. We modified the LLaVA architecture by concatenating image embeddings with language
384   embeddings to handle multiple images. All models were pretrained for 3 epochs. The model’s input
385   includes both the system prompt and the agent’s observation. We trained a unified model for all tasks
386   in both the centralized and decentralized settings.
387   In the centralized setting, the observation consists of first-person views, previous actions, and the
388   information of all agents.
389
      In the decentralized setting, the observation includes the first-person view, previous actions, and
390
      information of only the specific agent.
391
392   GPT-4o: For the GPT-4o method, we employed a one-shot learning approach. The prompt provided
393   to the model includes a single successful demonstration of the task from the training set. Based on
394   this example, we then asked the GPT-4o model to generate the actions for agents in response to new
395
      observations. This approach leverages the model’s ability to generalize from a minimal amount of
      information.
396
397
398
      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
399
      We evaluated the performance of the methods based on two key metrics: task success rate and
400   competence percentage.
401
402   Task success rate: The task success rate is determined by the ratio of the number of completed tasks
403
      to the total number of tested tasks. This metric indicates the proportion of test cases that the model
      can successfully complete from start to finish. A higher success rate reflects the model’s ability to
404
      consistently achieve the desired outcomes in various scenarios.
405
406   Subgoal success rate: This metric measures the overall effectiveness of the agents in performing the
407   tasks, considering partial successes and the extent to which the tasks are completed. It is calculated
408   by dividing the number of subgoals accomplished by the total number of subgoals. For the building
409
      tasks, subgoals are defined by the number of blocks to be built. For the clearing task, subgoals are
      defined by number of blocks to be cleared. For the farming task, subgoals are defined as the number
410
      of farms to be farmed. For the smelting task, subgoals are defined as the number of target objects
411
      to be smelt. The subgoal success rate provides a more granular view of the model’s performance,
412   highlighting how well the agents can handle different aspects of the tasks even if they do not fully
413   complete them.

516   5    C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   The TeamCraft benchmark introduced in this paper provides a novel and rich framework for evaluating
519
      the capabilities of multi-agent systems situated in complex 3D environments. By incorporating a
      diverse array of tasks, coupled with dynamic interactions among agents and objects, this benchmark
520
      challenges the conventional paradigm of multi-agent research and paves the way for new explorations
521
      in embodied intelligence.
522
523   The implementation of RGB image and language inputs as opposed to traditional abstract vector
524   inputs has enabled a more realistic simulation of human-like perception and interaction. This setup
525
      has effectively demonstrated the necessity and impact of high-level strategic planning and real-time
      decision-making in a controlled yet challenging environment.
526
527   Our experimental results highlight the strengths and limitations of current Vision-Language Models
528   (VLMs) in managing complex, dynamic task environments. While the centralized models exhibited
529   robust performance across most tasks, reflecting their ability to leverage comprehensive environmental
530   data for decision-making, the decentralized models underscored the challenges faced when agents
531
      operate with limited information. This dichotomy not only enriches our understanding of agent
      interaction dynamics but also underscores the critical role of information accessibility in strategic
532
      multi-agent environments.
533
534   In conclusion, the TeamCraft benchmark not only sets a new standard in the study of multi-agent
535   systems but also promises to act as a catalyst for future innovations in this rapidly evolving field.

720   B       H IGH L EVEL S KILLS
721
722   The action space of agents mainly involves high-level self-explanatory skills such as obtainBlock and
723   farmWork. We provided 8 such atomic actions. Most actions take three input parameters, including 1)
724   agent name such as bot1, as the action executing entity, 2) item name such as dirt, which strongly
725
      associated with task goal or agent’s inventory, 3) a vector indicating the position of the target on the
      test field.
726
727   For example, obtainBlock(bot1, new Vec3(1, 0, 1)) takes the agent name bot1 and
728   a 3D vector (1, 0, 1) as its arguments. It directs bot1 to perform multiple actions in Minecraft
729   via APIs provided by Mineflayer. First, it controls bot1 to goto a diggable position for block (1,
730   0, 1), then has bot1’s vision ray cast to the block at (1, 0, 1) using the lookAt action. Next,
731
      it commands bot1 to equip a proper tool that can dig the block at (1, 0, 1) most efficiently,
      and then instructs bot1 to dig the target block. Once the target block has been mined, bot1 will
732
      goto the position where the block item dropped and collect it.
733
734   Similarly, farmWork(bot2, "sow", "potato", new Vec3(2, 0, 4)) takes the
735   agent name bot2, action type "sow" (as opposed to "harvest"), crop seed item "potato",
736   and a 3D vector (2, 0, 4) as its arguments. It directs bot2 to goto a placeable position for
737
      farmland at (2, 0, 4), then check if the seed is a valid item—that is, a crop seed available
      within bot2’s inventory. It then checks if the farmland at (2, 0, 4) is plantable. Finally, it
738
      instructs bot2 to lookAt the farmland and sow it with the seed "potato".
