009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            When developing text classification models for real world applications, one ma-
012            jor challenge is the difficulty to collect sufficient data for all text classes. In this
013            work, we address this challenge by utilizing large language models (LLMs) to
014            generate synthetic data and using such data to improve the performance of the
015            models without waiting for more real data to be collected and labelled. As an
016
               LLM generates different synthetic data in response to different input examples,
               we formulate an automated workflow, which searches for input examples that lead
017
               to more “effective” synthetic data for improving the model concerned. We study
018
               three search strategies with an extensive set of experiments, and use experiment
019            results to inform an ensemble algorithm that selects a search strategy according
020            to the characteristics of a class. Our further experiments demonstrate that this en-
021            semble approach is more effective than each individual strategy in our automated
022            workflow for improving classification models using LLMs.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   A critical impediment to developing robust text classification models for real-world applications is
028
      the pervasive challenge of class imbalance and data scarcity, particularly for underrepresented text
      categories. Many industrial applications, such as ticketing systems, require classification models to
029
      process large volumes of unstructured text data, such as problem descriptions and user comments,
030
      which are often heavily imbalanced in class sizes. In modern industrial environments, ticketing sys-
031   tems play a vital role in managing and resolving technical issues, service requests, and operational
032   incidents (Al-Hawari & Barham (2021)). As shown in the workflow (Figure 1), models are initially
033   trained on a set of labeled tickets, but newly introduced or infrequent classes often arise after de-
034   ployment, necessitating manual classification and correction. This reliance on manual intervention
035   for new or underrepresented classes creates operational bottlenecks and impairs model adaptation to
036   evolving data distributions. Over time, the model’s performance degrades, particularly for small or
037   specialized categories, as obtaining balanced and adequately labeled data across all classes remains
038   challenging. Consequently, models often fail to generalize effectively across diverse data distri-
039
      butions, especially for underrepresented categories Gandla et al. (2024). Traditionally, addressing
      data scarcity involves collecting additional real-world data, which can be both time-consuming and
040
      resource-intensive, especially for rare or newly introduced classes. Furthermore, the manual label-
041
      ing of such data introduces additional delays and costs Li et al. (2022). In this context, synthetic
042   data generation has emerged as a promising solution to address class imbalance and data scarcity,
043   particularly for underrepresented classes. By augmenting training datasets with synthetic samples,
044   models can achieve improved performance and generalization across different categories.
045
      Synthetic data has gained popularity in recent years as a way to overcome the limitations of real-
046
      world data, which can be scarce, sensitive, or expensive to obtain (Patki et al. (2016)). Research
047
      in this area consistently highlights the potential of synthetic data to enhance the performance of
048   ML models across diverse fields (Lu et al. (2023)), addressing challenges such as data shortages in
049   computer vision and NLP (Mumuni et al. (2024)), generating diverse datasets in medical imaging
050   (Frid-Adar et al. (2018b)), and providing safe training scenarios for autonomous driving systems
051   (Song et al. (2023)). Its utility extends to financial modeling for algorithm testing under simulated
052   market conditions and cybersecurity for developing threat detection systems (Potluru et al. (2023);
053   Chalé & Bastian (2022)). In the domain of text analysis, synthetic data has been increasingly em-
      ployed to enhance ML models, particularly in tasks such as text classification, sentiment analysis,

      To overcome these challenges, this paper introduces Automated Generation of Text Synthetics (Au-
074   toGeTS), an algorithmic solution that automates the search for optimal data examples based on spe-
075   cific improvement objectives, eliminating the need for human intervention. Through experiments

078   effectively improves text classification models across various real-world tasks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
081
082
      Synthetic data is increasingly used as a powerful tool for generating realistic datasets to enhance the
083
      performance of the task across various domains (Meier et al. (1988); Bersano et al. (1997)). Early
084
      synthetic data generation methods include bootstrapping (Efron (1992); Breiman (1996)), which
085   resamples from original data to estimate distributions and reduce variance in predictions, proved
086   effective for a range of predictive algorithms including tree-based models (Sutton (2005)). How-
087   ever, bootstrapping couldn’t introduce new patterns. The Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Tech-
088   nique (SMOTE) (Chawla et al. (2002)) advanced imbalanced dataset handling but risked overfitting.
089   Data augmentation (Jaderberg et al. (2014)) improved model robustness by transforming existing
090   data points, increasing diversity, yet still limited to patterns in the original dataset. The advent of
091   deep learning introduced more sophisticated techniques, notably Generative Adversarial Networks
092   (GANs) by Goodfellow et al. (Goodfellow et al. (2014)), which generate highly realistic synthetic
093
      data capturing dataset complexity. Studies have shown models trained on GAN-generated synthetic
      data often perform comparably to those trained on real data in various predictive tasks (Zhang et al.
094
      (2017); Cortés et al. (2020)). Frid-Adar et al. (Frid-Adar et al. (2018a)) enhanced liver lesion diag-
095
      nosis using GAN-generated images, while Yale et al. (2020) demonstrated comparable performance
096   using GAN-generated synthetic electronic health records for ICU patient predictions.
097
098   GANs have been extensively used for synthetic text generation. For instance, Croce et al. (2020)
099
      demonstrated their effectiveness in generating realistic text for NLP tasks, while He et al. (2022)
      explored task-specific text generation. However, GAN-generated data for text classification often
100
      lacks semantic coherence and relevance to specific tasks (Torres (2018)). Recent advancements in
101
      large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-2 (Croce et al. (2020)), provide new approaches to
102   overcome these limitations. LLMs excel in few-shot and zero-shot learning (Brown (2020); Wang
103   et al. (2021)), adapting to unseen tasks and generating contextually relevant data that improves model
104   robustness. Yoo et al.’s GPT-3Mix (Yoo et al. (2021)) demonstrates LLMs’ capability to generate di-
105   verse, high-quality synthetic data for text classification through careful prompt engineering. Prompt
106   optimization strategies have shown that carefully crafting input prompts can significantly impact
107   the quality of generated data (Wang et al. (2023)). Automated search techniques for identifying the
      most effective prompts, such as those used in AutoPrompt (Shin et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2024)),


                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      offer a potential solution for improving synthetic data generation. Beyond prompt engineering, se-
109   lecting appropriate input examples has emerged as a crucial focus. Selecting example data, either
110   with a uniform distribution or human identification through VIS4ML, to form the prompt for LLM
111   to generate synthetics is shown effective (Li et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2024)). Despite these advance-
112   ments, LLM-generated data still struggles to fully capture real-world diversity, especially in highly
113   subjective tasks (Li et al. (2023)). To address this, we propose AutoGeTS, an automated approach
114   that optimizes input example selection for LLM-generated synthetic data. Designed for real-world
115   business requirements, AutoGeTS reduces human intervention while systematically identifying im-
116   pactful examples, enhancing model performance in scenarios of data scarcity and class imbalance.

      3.2    O BJECTIVES FOR M ODEL O PTIMIZATION
143
144   Ticketing systems deployed in specific organizational environments often face different, sometimes
145   conflicting, requirements. Typical business requirements and related performance metrics include:
146
147         R1. The accuracy of every class should be as high as possible and above a certain threshold. One
148
                may optimize a model with a performance metric such as class-based balanced accuracy
                or F1-score as the objective function, with each threshold value as a constraint.
149
150         R2. The overall classification accuracy of a model should be as high as possible and above a
151
                certain threshold because misclassified messages lead to undesirable consequences. One
                may optimize a model with a global performance metric, such as overall balanced accuracy
152
                and overall f1-score.
153
154         R3. The recall for some specific classes (e.g., important) should be as high as possible and above
155
                a certain threshold in order to minimize the delay due to the messages in such a class being
                sent to other services. Class-based recall is the obvious metric for this requirement. Often
156
                one may make a balanced judgment by observing Pareto fronts of recall in conjunction
157
                with another class-based metric (e.g., balanced accuracy or F1-score).
158
159   These requirements inform the definition of objective functions and constraints for AutoGeTS opti-
160   mization. However, because the use of LLMs to generate synthetic data to aid ML (i.e., the workflow
161   in Figure 2) is a recent approach, it is necessary to understand how different example selection al-
      gorithms for LLMs may impact the optimization.

      3.3     S TRATEGIES FOR E XAMPLE S ELECTION
163
164   Defining the search space for example selection is critical, especially when augmenting datasets with
165   synthetic examples. This space includes all possible subsets of training data D = x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ,
166   each data dot labeled with a specific class. The primary objective is to identify the optimal subset
167   W ∗ ⊆ D that maximizes performance metrics when used for synthetic data generation via LLM.
168   With 2n − 1 possible subsets for n examples, exhaustive search becomes intractable, necessitating
169   heuristic strategies.
170   The general goal can be formulated as an ideal multi-objective optimization problem:
171
172
                                              W ∗ = arg max J(W )                                         (1)
173                                                      W ⊆D
174
175   where J(W ) is the objective function measuring the performance of a retrained model M (W ) using
176
      synthetic data generated from subset W :
177
178            J(W ) = w1 · Recall(M (W )) + w2 · BalancedAccuracy(M (W )) + w3 · F1(M (W ))              (2)
179
180
      where w1 , w2 , and w3 reflect metrics weights in the overall objective. Given the practical challenges
      in defining such a compound objective, we employ a simplified, single-metric function:
181
182
183                                           W ∗ = arg max J ′ (W )                                      (3)
                                                         W ⊆D
184
185   where J ′ (W ) represents one of the following metrics: Class-based Recall (CR), Class-based Bal-
186   anced Accuracy (CBA), Overall Balanced Accuracy (OBA), and Overall F1-Score (OF1).
187
      Thus, the policy for selecting optimal subsets involves two core components:
188
189           1. A strategy for selecting subsets of examples for synthetic data generation and retraining.
190
              2. An evaluation metric, J ′ (W ), to be maximized as the objective for the search towards the
191
                 optimal subset W ∗ .
192
193   The challenge is to efficiently search the space while balancing between computational cost (i.e.,
194   cumulative model retraining time) and performance improvement (i.e., maximum gain in J(W )).
195   Initial experiments with random selection yielded limited improvements, particularly for class-based
196   metrics (detailed in Appendix B.1.3). Thus, we explore three primary strategies to optimize the
197   subset selection: brute-force (Sliding Window, SW), gradient-based (Hierarchical Sliding Window,
198   HSW), and evolutionary algorithms (Genetic Algorithm, GA), as illustrated in Figure 3.
199

      3.3.1     S LIDING W INDOW (SW)
201   The Sliding Window (SW) strategy represents a brute-force approach, where the search space is
202   exhaustively segmented into ”windows” or subsets. For each window Wk ⊆ D, synthetic data is
203   generated, the model is retrained, and the performance is evaluated based on the objective function
204   J ′ (Wk ). The goal is to identify the window Wk∗ that yields the maximum improvement:
205
206                                          Wk∗ = arg max J ′ (Wk )                                      (4)
207                                                     Wk ⊆D
208
      The brute-force nature of SW ensures that no region of the search space is neglected, but the cost in
209
      terms of time and computational resources can become prohibitive.
210
211
      3.3.2     H IERARCHICAL S LIDING W INDOW (HSW)
212
213   The Hierarchical Sliding Window (HSW) strategy builds on the principles of hierarchical selection,
214   offering a more computationally efficient approach by incrementally narrowing the search space to
215   promising regions. At each level l, the current search space is partitioned into smaller windows
      Wk,l . For each window, synthetic data is generated, the model is retrained, and the performance

252   objective function J ′ (S), derived from the AutoGeTS process and subsequent performance evalua-

254

255
      Lexicase selection evaluates the F (S) of input class and the J ′ (S) of other randomly chosen classes.
256

258   Polynomia Mutation Si et al. (2011) is applied to offspring Ok to introduce variability for global
259   search. The GA repeats the selection, crossover, and mutation process until reaching a specified
260   number of generations or a convergence criterion. The subset S ∗ that maximizes the fitness score:

267   Each AutoGeTS run targets a single class Ci , aiming to improve its specific or overall performance
268   through synthetic sample addition. However, class interactions in synthetic data generation have
269   been observed; Jin et al. (2024) found that synthetic data for one class can improve performance for
      others. Given these interactions and the collective contribution of all classes to overall classification

      performance, an ensemble algorithm applying AutoGeTS across multiple classes is necessary to

      3.4   E NSEMBLE A LGORITHM
274
275   The ensemble algorithm depends on the specific business requirements, as outlined in Section 3.2:
276
277
      To lift all classes performances above a threshold (R1): Iteratively apply AutoGeTS to each
      underperforming class (Clow ) with optimal strategy-objective combination, in the order that most
278
      likely improves class performance. In each iteration, append synthetic samples from the optimal
279
      retrained model to the training set, and maintain improvements for processed Clow above a specified
280   threshold. Terminate when unable to maintain improvements. To improve overall classification
281   accuracy (R2): The same process is applied to each class (C), except the class order that most
282   likely improves overall performance is used, and the algorithm terminates when overall performance
283   plateaus. To improve specific important class’s performance (R3): For an important class (IC),
284   identify related classes (RC) that could enhance IC performance with AutoGeTS. Apply AutoGeTS
285   to IC and RC iteratively with optimal strategy-objective combinations, in the order that prioritizes
286   IC performance improvement. Terminate when IC performance plateaus.
287   Experimental determinations include performance thresholds, RC identification, class order, and
288   optimal strategy-objective combinations, which will be studied in Section 4. The specific details and
289   the step-by-step breakdown of the algorithms are provided in Algorithm 3, 4, and 5 in Appendix C.2.

348
349   The AutoGeTS framework yielded significant improvements in both local and global performance
350
      metrics, effectively addressing the class imbalance problem evident in Table 2.
351   Smaller, underrepresented classes experienced the largest improvements. For instance, T13’s bal-
352   anced accuracy increased by 5.48 percentage points (pp) from 66.6% in M0, while T12 showed a
353   7.75 pp improvement from 74.2%. In contrast, larger classes like T1 (98.6%) and T2 (95%) saw
354   only marginal gains of around 0.3 pp. This demonstrates AutoGeTS’s ability to significantly im-
355   prove underrepresented classes without affecting the performance of well-represented ones. This
356
      balance is crucial in maintaining overall system performance. The overall balanced accuracy im-
      proved consistently and comparably among classes, with T10 showing the highest overall balanced
357
      accuracy improvement of 0.44 pp. This underscores AutoGeTS’s synergistic effect, where class-
358
      based improvements translate to overall performance gains, with minimal trade-offs between local
359   and global performance improvements (see Appendix D.1).

361   4.3   C OMPARISON OF E XAMPLE S ELECTION S TRATEGIES AND O PTIMIZATION O BJECTIVES
362
363   Figure 4 compared the performance of three search strategies—SW, HSW, GA—and four objec-
364   tives—maximizing CR, CBA, OBA, OF1—with respect to both local (class-specific) and global
365   (overall) metrics. Each bar chart is divided into 4 sections for 4 performance metrics, with the four
366   bars each representing the maximum improvement in the objective of maximizing CR, CBA, OBA,
367   or OF1. The choice of strategy-objective played a critical role in the effectiveness of AutoGeTS,
368
      with each demonstrating distinct advantages depending on the size of the target class, and their
      performance trajectories over retraining time (see Appendix D.2).
369
370   For larger classes, HSW consistently yielded the best results, such as the highest class T1 balanced
371   accuracy improvement of 0.5%. HSW’s progressive narrowing of the search space proves effective
372   for larger data sets where an exhaustive search is computationally prohibitive. Objective-wise, max-
373   imizing CR or CBA each best improved its respective metric, while maximizing OBA or OF1 both
374
      led to the best improvements in global metrics.

522   6   C ONCLUSIONS
523
524   This work introduces AutoGeTS, an automated framework optimizing example data selection for
525   synthetic text generation using Large Language Models (LLMs). AutoGeTS significantly enhances
526   the level of automation, reducing human efforts in selecting effective examples. This approach
527   addresses class imbalance and data scarcity challenges in real-world text classification tasks. Exper-
528   iments demonstrate AutoGeTS’s effectiveness in improving both local and global performance met-
529   rics. Using Sliding Window, Hierarchical Sliding Window, and Genetic Algorithm, significant im-
530   provements in inadequately-sampled classes are observed without compromising well-represented
531
      ones. The ensemble algorithm for selecting the most suitable strategies according to the results of
      earlier iterations facilitates more efficient optimization processes in later iterations. This approach
532
      that treats earlier testing results as useful knowledge in optimization will likely have a wider appli-
533
      cation in ML model deployment. These findings establish AutoGeTS as an effective solution for
534   enriching training data with synthetic samples to meet real-world requirements for the performance
535   of ML models with limitations in data collection. This work confirms that the automated approach
536   can perform better than human-centric processes in terms of both effectiveness and efficiency. Mean-
537   while, there is a need to conduct more large-scale experiments and analyze the experiment results
538   in order to understand and explain how different synthetic data sets improve or undermine an ML
539   model. Future work may also explore multi-objective optimization strategies, more advanced en-
      semble algorithms, and applications of this approach in other ML domains.
