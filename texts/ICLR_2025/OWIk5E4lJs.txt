042                                                           A BSTRACT
043
044             While diffusion-based text-to-image generation has made notable advancements,
045
                generating accurate images containing interactive actions remains a challenge due
                to the lack of inherent physical and spatial priors. To address this problem, we pro-
046
                pose a novel pipeline that synthesizes a dataset enriched with physical priors using
047
                a graphics engine, combined with a captioning technique. Building on the dataset,
048             we introduce a distillation-structured fine-tuning method, where a teacher network
049             assists in inverting the semantics of interactive actions, leveraging the synthesized
050             priors effectively. This fine-tuning method disentangles the synthetic data features
051             while mitigating random misalignment during the fine-tuning process. Extensive
052             experiments demonstrate that our method not only achieves state-of-the-art results
053             but also highlights the synthetic data’s potential to be applied more broadly in en-
                hancing the generation of interactive action images.

084   Recent years have witnessed significant breakthroughs in the field of text-to-image generation, par-
085   ticularly with powerfulness of diffusion models Gal et al. (2022). However, existing text-to-image
086
      generation models such as Stable Diffusion (SD) Rombach et al. (2022) struggle to generate images
      containing interactive actions. As shown in Figure 1: the generated images from SD totally fail to
087
      model correct spatial positions of objects and the interactions between them.
088
089   In general, generating interactions requires modeling spatial relationships, which is inherently more
090   difficult than generating isolated objects. For instance, depicting an action as seemingly straightfor-
091   ward as “picking up” involves intricate spatial and physical constraints, including the placement of
092
      objects, the elevation at which the pick-up occurs, and the degree of arm curvature, etc.
093   Existing diffusion models lack explicit physical understanding. Typically, text-to-image mod-
094   els Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022); Lugmayr et al. (2022); Esser et al. (2021)
095   are pretrained using image-text pairs. This approach has limitations particularly in learning physi-
096   cal constraints, because images involving interactions are very rare in the training dataset, resulting
097   in models that do not thoroughly comprehend spatial dynamics (for example, illustrations where
098
      various models fail to depict spatial relations accurately).
099   To resolve this problem, we propose to fine-tune the diffusion models on synthetic images with
100   interactions rendered from physical simulation graphics engines Savva et al. (2019); Xia et al. (2018;
101   2020); Savva et al. (2017); Brockman et al. (2016); James et al. (2020); Xiang et al. (2020). A
102   graphics engine can offer a 3D scene where an object interacts with another object conforming to
103   physical constraints. In this paper, we select SAPIEN Xiang et al. (2020) as the main generation
104
      engine due to its convenience, but we also prove that other popular engine like Unity3D is effective
      as well. In SAPIEN, we first build a 3D scene where a robot agent is interacting with objects, and
105
      then render multi-view 2D image dataset which contains physical priors for diffusion models.
106
107   However, using synthetic data’s physical prior to learn interactive action is not a free lunch: there
      is a big domain gap between synthetic data and real data where diffusion models are pretrained

129   relation-dense region in the text embedding space. Adapting ReVersion in our task helps to alleviate

131   subjects or objects will disappear randomly. This problem appears due to the domain gap between
132

134   misalignment problem. We find that although SD fails to accurately model the spatial relation,
135   the subject and object are present without random losing. This observation inspires us to leverage
136   its strengths. As shown in Figure 3: a frozen diffusion model works as a teacher network, which
137   conditions on a “Subject-and-Object” prompt, aiming to generate them without considering their
138
      relationship; the student network learns the interactive action conditioning on “Subject-v∗-Object”
      prompt, disentangling the synthetic features. With a distillation loss, the teacher network offers a
139
      guidance signal to supervise the student network to generate the subjects and objects. Then v∗ can
140
      be used to generate images with specific interactive actions as the teaser page shows.
141
142   Our contribution is three-folded: 1) we pioneer to focus on interactive-action image generation, an
143   important and challenging task that remains unsolved, and we propose to employ synthetic data to
144
      offer more physical priors; 2) we design a new distillation method to fine-tune the diffusion model
      on synthetic data, solving the misalignment problem introduced by the domain gap; 3) extensive
145
      experiments prove the potential ability of synthetic data, our method can generate significantly better
146
      images with reasonable spatial relationships and good fidelity.

151   Diffusion-based Text-to-Image (T2I) Generation. With great success, diffusion models have be-
152   come a mainstream approach in various domains such as image generation Dhariwal & Nichol
153
      (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022); Lugmayr et al. (2022); Esser et al. (2021), image processing Sa-
      haria et al. (2022b;a); Guo et al. (2023); Chung et al. (2022), video generation Wu et al. (2022);
154
      Hong et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023), and 3D generation Poole et al. (2022); Müller et al. (2023);
155
      Nam et al. (2022) etc. Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have demonstrated impressive results
156   in converting text descriptions into images. Notable examples include GLIDE Nichol et al. (2021),
157   DALL-E Ramesh et al. (2022), Imagen and Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022),
158   each contributing unique advancements to the field. Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022), based
159   on LDM, uses a cross-attention mechanism to inject textual conditions into the generation process,
160   significantly improving computational efficiency by operating in latent space. This marks a new era
161   of text-to-image generation capable of handling arbitrarily text descriptions. Motivated by its suc-
      cess and popularity in the open research community, we build our framework on Stable Diffusion.

172

173

293   3.3.2      T EACHER N ETWORK
294
295   As mentioned in Section 1, when dealing with interactive scenarios, vanilla stable diffusion models
296   can precisely depict both the subject and the object, although fail to represent their spatial rela-
297   tionship. At the same time, the student network faces the misalignment problem that the subject or
298   object will be lost randomly. This inspires us to integrate the advantage of the vanilla stable diffusion
299   model which does not lose objects into our student network to address the misalignment problem.
300   Specifically, for identical noisy input, we assign different textual conditions to the student network
301   and teacher network. The student network ϵθ is conditioned on “Subject-v∗-Object” structured
302   textual prompt y as previously mentioned. The teacher network, which is a frozen copy of the
303   student network, is conditioned on a “Subject-and-Object” structured textual prompt yand . At each
304   training step, y and yand are randomly sampled from two separate prompt groups (typically, 10-12
305   textual prompts) which are paired with each image. As shown in Figure 3, we adopt the concept of
306
      knowledge distillation by transforming the entire network structure into a distillation paradigm.
307   We use a classical distillation loss Ldistill to compute the difference between the student network
308   output and teacher network output, and minimizing this loss will guide the student network to depict
309   both the subject and object, preventing missing either component of the textual prompt:

534   In this work, we pioneer in generating images with interactive actions based on the text-to-image
535   diffusion model. To address this challenge, we have curated a synthetic dataset enriched with con-
536   trollable interactive actions rendered from a graphics engine. Based on the dataset, a novel fine-
537   tuning scheme is proposed to improve the spatial and interactive awareness of the diffusion model.
538   With the steering loss and distillation structure, our proposed method effectively addresses the prob-
539   lems of disentanglement and textual-visual misalignment and achieves state-of-the-art performance,
      marking synthetic data’s potential in interactive-action image generation.
