011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine unlearning (MU), which seeks to erase the influence of specific unwanted
014            data from already-trained models, is becoming increasingly vital in model editing,
015
               particularly to comply with evolving data regulations like the “right to be forgotten”.
               Conventional approaches are predominantly model-based, typically requiring re-
016
               training or fine-tuning the model’s weights to meet unlearning requirements. In this
017
               work, we approach the MU problem from a novel input perturbation-based perspec-
018            tive, where the model weights remain intact throughout the unlearning process. We
019            demonstrate the existence of a proactive input-based unlearning strategy, referred
020            to forget vector, which can be generated as an input-agnostic data perturbation and
021            remains as effective as model-based approximate unlearning approaches. We also
022            show that multiple given forget vectors (e.g., each targeting the unlearning of a
023            specific data class) can be combined through simple arithmetic operations (e.g., lin-
024            ear combinations) to generate new forget vectors for unseen unlearning tasks (e.g.,
025            targeting the unlearning of an arbitrary subset across all classes). An additional
026
               advantage of our proposed forget vector approach is its parameter efficiency, as it
               eliminates the need for updating model weights. We conduct extensive experiments
027
               to validate the effectiveness of forget vector and its arithmetic for MU in image
028
               classification against a series of model-based unlearning baselines.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   To prevent the unauthorized use of personal or sensitive data upon completion of training and comply
034   with legislation like “right to be forgotten” in General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Hoofnagle
035   et al., 2019), Machine Unlearning (MU) has gained increasing attention to tackle many trustworthy
036   machine learning (ML) challenges in vision tasks, especially in image classification (Golatkar
037   et al., 2020; Poppi et al., 2023; Warnecke et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). In essence, it initiates a
038   reverse learning process to erase the impact of unwanted data (e.g., specific data points, classes, or
039
      knowledge concepts) from already-trained model, while still preserving its performance and utility
      for information not targeted by the unlearning process. Based on the accuracy of the unlearning
040
      process and the guarantees provided regarding the removal of data from the already-trained model,
041
      existing MU methods can be roughly classified into two lines: exact unlearning (Dong et al., 2024;
042   Guo et al., 2020; Thudi et al., 2022b) and approximate unlearning (Graves et al., 2021; Thudi et al.,
043   2022a; Becker & Liebig, 2022; Izzo et al., 2021). Exact unlearning is the most optimal unlearning
044   approach, ensuring the complete and verifiable removal of the targeted unwanted data. It typically
045   involves retraining the model from scratch after excluding the data that needs to be forgotten from the
046   original training set. However, due to its computation overhead and the lack of scalability, increasing
047   efforts have been dedicated to the approximate unlearning manner.
048   Approximate unlearning offers a compromise between computational efficiency and effective data
049   removal, making it a practical solution for many real-world scenarios. Generally, existing approximate
050   unlearning techniques are predominantly model-based, requiring update the entire model’s weights
051   within a constrained number of training iterations to remove the influence of specific unwanted data,
052   thereby avoiding the need for retraining the model from scratch. Among these, the representative
053   methods include fine-tuning based approaches (Warnecke et al., 2023; Perifanis et al., 2024), gradient
      ascent based techniques (Thudi et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2024) and influence function-based

071   post-unlearning and typically require the involved optimization of model parameters. To alleviate
072   the concerns on model-based approximate unlearning, we ask if it is possible append a trainable
073   program to the input to guide the already-trained neural network for unlearning. This input-based
074   solution is inspired by visual prompting (Bahng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Oh et al., 2023), also
075

076

077
      which it wasn’t trained. In light of this, we aim to shift the focus of MU from model-based strategies
078   to input-based strategies, where the data is manipulated in advance with already-trained model
079   unchanged. Above all, we raise our key question (Q) below:
080
          (Q) Is there a data manipulation method that enables machine unlearning in image classifica-
081
          tion without updating model parameters? If so, how does it function, and what remarkable
082
          characteristics or properties does it possess?
083
084   To address (Q), we advance MU through a fresh viewpoint: forget vector, representing a universal
085   input perturbation designed to promote unlearning effectively and efficiently. Our key finding is
086   that leveraging the forget vector can achieve approximate unlearning as effectively as model-based
087   methods. Additionally, forget vector arithmetic (e.g., linear combinations of multiple forget vectors)
088
      can be used to generate new forget vectors for previously unseen unlearning tasks, further enhancing
      its flexibility and effectiveness. See Fig. 1 for the schematic overview of our proposal and highlighted
089
      empirical performance. We summarize our contributions as follows.
090
091   • We investigate the impact of “forget data shift” (from data corruptions and adversarial perturbations)
092   on image classifiers post-unlearning. Our findings show that unlearning is resilient to these shifts,
093   though generalization remains vulnerable.
094   • Building on the resilience of machine unlearning to forget data shift, we propose a proactive,
095   input-agnostic data perturbation strategy termed the “forget vector”, specifically optimized to facil-
096   itate unlearning. These input-based forget vectors show comparable effectiveness to model-based
097   unlearning methods in both class-wise and data-wise forgetting scenarios.
098   • We demonstrate the effectiveness of forget vector arithmetic by using class-wise forget vectors
099   to generate new vectors that effectively remove the influence of specific data subsets in image
100   classification models.
101
      • We conduct extensive experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-10 to demonstrate the superiority
102
      of forget vector over various model-based unlearning baseline methods.

107   Machine unlearning. MU seeks to alter machine learning models and erase the impact of specific
      data points or classes due to privacy or copyright concerns. In terms of application areas and target

108
      models, most MU methods currently focus on language and vision, which have garnered the greatest
109   attention (Wang et al., 2024b). Although these two directions share the common goal of efficient
110   data removal, techniques and challenges differ due to the distinct nature of textual and visual data.
111   • MU in language models has focused on removing the influence of specific data points, phrases,
112   or documents from the already-trained model, adjusting the textual representations to ensure that
113   sensitive or unwanted information is no longer retained (Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; 2023a;
114   Liu et al., 2024). A novel unlearning method, SeUL, was introduced to focus on specific sequence
115   spans rather than entire instances, which facilitates selective, fine-grained, and effective unlearning in
116   language models (Wang et al., 2024a). Additionally, inspired by the use of weights and function-space
117
      priors to reconstruct model gradients, a recent work focuses on data removal through knowledge
      gap alignment and is easily generalizable to various natural language processing tasks such as
118
      classification, translation, and response generation (Wang et al., 2023a).
119
120   • MU in vision models has been extensively studied for both image generation task (Li et al., 2024a;
121   Fan et al., 2024) and image classification task (Poppi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). The growing
122   use of diffusion models in generative modeling necessitates effective machine unlearning techniques
123
      to safeguard copyrights and prevent the generation of harmful content (Li et al., 2024a; Zhang
      et al., 2024). For example, the concept of weight saliency was introduced to guide MU, allowing
124
      models to avoid generating unwanted content while maintaining high-quality outputs for normal
125
      images (Fan et al., 2024). Besides, MU for image classification, which has significant practical
126   applications, has also gained attention. For example, fine-tuning-based approaches incrementally
127   update already-trained models using a modified dataset that excludes unwanted data points (Warnecke
128   et al., 2023; Perifanis et al., 2024). Gradient ascent-based techniques reverse the impact of unwanted
129   data by applying gradient ascent to model parameters (Thudi et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2024).
130   Moreover, influence unlearning methods first leverage influence functions to estimate how much
131   a particular data point impacts the predictions and parameters of the model and then reverse those
132   contributions (Golatkar et al., 2020; 2021; 2020). Furthermore, the connection between MU and
133   model pruning has been explored, with findings that model sparsity helps bridge the gap between
134
      approximate and exact unlearning (Jia et al., 2023). Most existing MU methods in this domain are
      model-based, which can lead to utility degradation after unlearning and are often computationally
135
      expensive due to the need for model parameter updates.
136
137   • MU in other areas like graphs (Li et al., 2024b; Dong et al., 2024) and time-series data (Du et al.,
138   2019) has also been explored, though the existing works are limited.
139   Model adaptation Technique. It has emerged as a promising approach to modify or repurpose
140   already-trained models for new tasks or specific objectives without fully retraining the model. It is
141   especially valuable for reducing computational costs and leveraging existing knowledge embedded in
142   models. Representative branches of model adaptation technique include:
143
      • Visual Prompting provides a new way to adapt already-trained models in vision by adding or
144   modifying prompts (visual cues) in the input data to guide the model’s behavior without changing its
145   weights. For example, introducing trainable parameters in the input space while keeping the model
146   backbone frozen can achieve comparable results with reduced computational overhead (Jia et al.,
147   2022; Wang et al., 2023b).
148
      • Model Reprogramming keeps the already-trained model fixed and modifies the input to adapt the
149
      model for different tasks. For instance, adversarial perturbations can be applied to test-time inputs to
150   make a model perform a task chosen by the attacker (Tsai et al., 2020; Elsayed et al., 2019).
151
152   • Feature-Based Domain Adaptation applies transformations or mapping techniques to the input data,
153
      aligning the feature distributions between the source and target domains while keeping the model
      unchanged(Tahmoresnezhad & Hashemi, 2017).
154
155   To fully harness the advantages of model adaptation techniques and address the challenges in existing
156   MU methods for image classification, we focus on machine unlearning through the design of universal
157   input-agnostic perturbations. Our goal is to achieve comparable unlearning performance without
158   altering the already-trained model. Notably, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to
159
      tackle machine unlearning in image classification using input-based universal perturbations.

      MU Problem Formulation. Let D= {(xi , yi )}i=1 denote a training dataset consisting of N in-
166   stances, where xi ∈ Rd denotes the i-th image in d-dimension and yi ∈ R refers to corresponding
167   category label. Df ⊆ D stands for a subset target at erasing from already-trained model, termed as
168   forgetting dataset. Accordingly, the complement of Df is the remaining dataset, i.e., Dr = D \ Df .
169   Moreover, we define θ as the model parameters, and θo refer to the original model trained on the
170   entire training set D. Similarly, θu corresponds to an unlearned model. The problem of MU lies in
171   developing an accurate and efficient scrubbing mechanism that can effectively remove the influence
172   of specific data points from a trained model θo to θu . Following existing studies (Jia et al., 2023;
173   Thudi et al., 2022a; Golatkar et al., 2020), we evaluate our proposal in the context of two classic
174
      forgetting tasks: class-wise forgetting where unlearning Df corresponds to data points of entire class,
      and random data forgetting where unlearning Df consists of a subset randomly selected from all
175
      classes (In our experiments, we randomly select a certain proportion of data from the dataset across
176
      all classes, such as “%10”). In the inference phase, we have an original testing dataset. Notably, in
177   the scenario of class-wise forgetting, we split original testing dataset into two groups: Dt and Df t ,
178   which means the remaining dataset and forgetting set in the original testing set, respectively. As for
179   the random data forgetting case, the whole testing set is considered as Dt .
180
      Representative MU Methods.
181
182   • Retrain: This is the most optimal (exact) MU method, where the model is retrained from scratch
183   using the remaining dataset Dr . However, this approach imposes a significant computational cost,
184   especially when training deep neural networks (DNNs) on large-scale datasets. Despite its inefficiency,
185   Retrain serves as the benchmark for MU, representing the ideal result that other MU methods aim to
186
      achieve.
187   • Fine-tuning (FT) (Warnecke et al., 2023; Golatkar et al., 2020): Instead of retraining the model
188   from scratch, FT fine-tunes the already-trained model θo on Df for a few iterations to obtain θu .
189   This approach balances computational efficiency and effective data removal, making it a practical
190   alternative to exact unlearning, especially for large models and datasets.
191   • Random Label (RL) (Golatkar et al., 2020): To reduce the influence of specific data points or
192   classes, RL intentionally corrupts the labels of the data in Df by randomly assigning new labels,
193   thereby reducing the impact of Df on the model’s learned representations.
194
      • Gradient Ascent (GA) (Graves et al., 2021): GA adjusts the parameters of the already-trained
195
      model θo in a specific direction to reverse the learning associated with the data in Df .
196
197   Evaluation Metric. To comprehensively characterize the proposed scheme in MU, we employed
198   several commonly used evaluation metrics following prior approaches (Jia et al., 2023; Thudi et al.,
199
      2022b) to comprehensively assess the effectiveness of data removal from different aspects. Here we
      chose five metrics as follows.
200
201   • Unlearning Accuracy (UA): To assess the efficacy of MU in terms of accuracy, we define UA(θu ) =
202   1− AccDf (θu ), where AccDf (θu ) denotes the classification accuracy of θu on the forgetting dataset
203   Df . Essentially, the smaller the gap between the approximate unlearning method and exact unlearning
204   method (Retrain), the better the performance of the machine unlearning (MU) method.
205   • Remaining Accuracy (RA): To reflect the fidelity of MU, we test the accuracy of θu on Dr .
206
      • Testing Accuracy (TA): To assess how well θu retains its generalization capabilities on the testing
207
      data Dt after the unlearning procedure, we also report the accuracy on Dt .
208
209   • Membership inference attack (MIA-Efficacy): We use MIA to evaluate the performance of MU
210   from an alternative perspective, where a confidence-based MIA predictor (Song et al., 2019) is
211   applied to θu on Df . Numerically, MIA indicates the success rate that the data points in Df can
212
      be successively identified as the forgetting samples of θu . Details about MIA can be found in this
      work Jia et al. (2023).
213
214   • Predictive robustness (PR): To evaluate the UA performance of MU regarding new unseen forgetting
215   data Df t , we also introduce the concept of predictive robustness. In practice, in the context of class-
      wise forgetting, Df t can be directly obtained from the original testing set. In terms of the random-data

      To adapt already-trained model for specific tasks, methods like fine-tuning and linear probing have
      gained significant attention (Seo et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), though both require access to
243
      the model and have drawbacks such as computational overhead and overfitting risks. Reently,
244
      input transformation techniques, including image-to-image translation (Murez et al., 2018), visual
245   prompting (Oh et al., 2024), and adversarial reprogramming (Elsayed et al., 2019), have emerged as

247   results obtained, we aim to explore a proactive input-based unlearning strategy. Before diving into

259   symmetric Gaussian distribution N (0, c2 I) with 0 mean vector and d by d covariance matrix c2 I.

283   Finding of Forget Data Shift. To comprehensively observe the impact of data shift to the Original
284   model θo and existing model-based MU methods, we reported the results of four different metrics in
285   Table 1. As can be seen, we present the performance of the original model, the exact UM (Retrain),
286   and three approximate UM methods (FT, RL, GA) on benign images, images with two types of data
287   corruptions, and adversarial perturbations applied, across different dataset splits. To better analyze the
288   performance trends of different methods across various metrics, we also provide corresponding curve
289   plots regarding the class-wise forgetting setting on CIFAR-10 using ResNet18 network architecture
290   in Figure 2. For both Table 1 and Figure 2, we can draw the following observations:
291   ❶ Better Unlearning on the forgetting dataset Df . When data corruption and adversarial perturbation
292   are added to the benign images, whether for the original model θo , exact unlearning retrain θr ,
293   or other UM methods θu , the unlearning capability of the model tends to improve. One possible
294   explanation is that the added corruption or perturbation can distort or obscure the critical features that
295   the model has learned to recognize(Original) or disregard (Retrain and approximate Unlearn), making
296   it harder for the model to retain its original knowledge. Meanwhile, as the level of corruption or the
297
      strength of the attack increases, the improvement in the model’s unlearning performance also tends to
      grow. This likely happens because more severe corruptions or stronger adversarial attacks distort the
298
      input data more significantly, making it harder for the model to retain previously learned information,
299
      thereby enhancing its ability to “forget.” In other words, the more aggressive the perturbation, the
300   greater the disruption, leading to more effective unlearning. In fact, this phenomenon is totally
301   opposite to the motivation and expectations of MU, whose goal is to maintain high TA and RA,
302   demonstrating the model’s ability to correctly classify data other than ones needed to be forgotten.

530   In this paper, we focus on studying the problem of machine unlearning in image classification from a
531
      new perspective, referred to forget vector. Unlike existing model-based machine unlearning methods
      where the retraining or fine-tuning of the model’s weights are required, our proposal demonstrates
532

533

535   be generated through the simple arithmetic operations like linear combination of pre-obtained forget
