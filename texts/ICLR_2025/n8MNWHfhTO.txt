009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent low-rank training methods, such as GaLore, have significantly reduced
012            the memory required to optimize large language models (LLMs). However, these
013            methods often suffer from time-consuming low-rank projection estimations. In
014            particular, the singular value decomposition (SVD) in GaLore can consume more
015            than 80% of the total training time. To address this issue, we propose GaLore+,
016            which uses cross-head low-rank projection to reduce the substantial time con-
017            sumption in estimating low-rank projections for multi-head attention. In addition,
018            we employ randomized subspace iteration to achieve fast SVD. To further enhance
019
               performance, we propose sparsely coded residuals to reduce the errors caused by
               low-rank approximation on the first- and second-order moments of the optimizers
020
               and weight updates. We evaluate GaLore+ on arithmetic reasoning and natural
021
               language generation datasets. Our experiments demonstrate that GaLore+ de-
022            livers superior performance while achieving approximately 4× fine-tuning speed
023            compared to vanilla GaLore.

072   i) Parameterized projection. The representative work is LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), which approximates
073   the parameter updates as the product of two trainable low-rank matrices. PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024)
074   extends this by employing singular value decomposition (SVD) to decompose the weight matrix into
075
      a principal component and a residual component, optimizing the principal part using two trainable
      low-rank matrices, similar to LoRA. LoRA is further developed into more efficient variants such as
076
      QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), which reduces memory requirements and improves computational
077
      efficiency by quantization. AdaLoRA enhances LoRA by adaptively adjusting the rank of low-rank
078   updates (Zhang et al., 2023).
079
080   ii) Random projection. Methods in this category employ random projections to further reduce the
081
      memory consumption on parameterized projections. Flora (Hao et al., 2024) develops LoRA by
      substituting one of the two low-rank matrices with a randomly generated matrix, reducing the mem-
082
      ory consumption with comparable performance. VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2024) employs a shared
083
      pair of random projections across all layers, fine-tuning the model by training layer-specific scaling
084   vectors.
085
086   iii) Analytic projection. Both parameterized and random projections may lead to low-quality approx-
087
      imations of gradient or weight updates, as they lack analytic guarantees. In contrast, GaLore (Zhao
      et al., 2024) introduces analytic projections derived from SVD to ensure that the key components of
088
      the gradients are preserved after low-rank projections, thereby offering a more accurate and reliable
089
      approximation.
090
091   Existing methods for estimating low-rank projections involve a trade-off between approximation
092   error and resource consumption (e.g., on memory and time). Parameterized and random projections
093
      lack the accuracy of analytic decomposition, leading to uncertain quality, while analytic methods
      like SVD, though more precise, are computationally expensive. For instance, in GaLore, SVD
094
      accounts for over 80% of the total time consumed during the fine-tuning process. Therefore, a
095
      faster and more accurate estimation of low-rank projections is essential to further boost the low-rank
096   adaptation methods.
097
098   In this paper, we propose a low-rank adaptation method, GaLore+, which employs cross-head low-
099
      rank projection to realize fast and high-quality estimation. Algorithm 1 presents the pseudo-code
      of integrating GaLore+ into AdamW, with the highlighted improvement over GaLore (Zhao et al.,
100
      2024). GaLore+ utilizes a cross-head low-rank projection inspired by the architecture of multi-
101
      head attention, where the projection matrices for the gradient of query or key transforms are shared
102   across multiple attention heads. Such sharing reduces the computational complexity of low-rank
103   projection matrices in h-head attention from O(h3 ) to O(h). Additionally, randomized subspace
104   iteration for SVD is utilized to further reduce computational complexity. Besides, GaLore+ incor-
105   porates sparsely coded residuals, enabling a sparse representation of low-rank approximation errors
106   in weight updates, which helps to mitigate estimation inaccuracies caused by the cross-head low-
107   rank projection. We evaluate the proposed GaLore+ on natural language processing and arithmetic
      reasoning tasks, comparing it against state-of-the-art low-rank adaptation methods.

      The main contributions of this work are as follows:
109
110          • We introduce cross-head low-rank projection, which reduces computational complexity by
111            sharing projection matrices across multiple query or key projections. Besides, we employ
112            randomized subspace iteration to accelerate the estimation of the projections.
113          • We mitigate the impact of low-rank approximation errors on weight updates by utilizing
114            sparsely coded residuals for the optimizer’s moments, thereby enhancing the quality of the

117            proaches, including LoRA and GaLore, in fine-tuning LLMs for tasks such as arithmetic
118            reasoning and natural language generation.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
121
122
      Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning. A variety of parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods have
123
      emerged in recent years, enabling an increasing number of institutions and researchers to fine-tune
124   LLMs to meet their specific requirements. Adapters (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Houlsby et al., 2019;
125   Lin et al., 2020; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021b;a) enable parameter-efficient fine-tuning by insert-
126   ing trainable layers into LLMs while keeping other layers frozen. However, this approach also
127   introduces additional inference latency. BitFit (Zaken et al., 2021) selectively tunes only the bi-
128   ases within the network, significantly reducing the number of parameters involved in fine-tuning.
129   Prompt tuning achieves parameter-efficient fine-tuning by optimizing a set of new input tokens or
130   prompts for each task (Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Hambardzumyan et al., 2021; Liu
131   et al., 2023). Hu et al. (2022) introduced LoRA, proposing that weight updates are low-rank and
132
      can be expressed as the product of two low-rank matrices. Furthermore, the trainable parameters
      can be merged with the original weights, eliminating additional inference latency. Recent studies
133
      combined parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods with quantization to enhance memory efficiency
134
      during the fine-tuning of LLMs (Kwon et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023; Chai et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
135   2023). And DoRA (Liu et al., 2024), or Weight-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation, is a parameter-
136   efficient fine-tuning method designed to enhance learning capacity and stability by decomposing
137   pre-trained weights into magnitude and direction components, leveraging LoRA for directional up-
138   dates, and achieving superior performance across tasks without additional inference costs.
139
      Parameter Sharing. Adam-mini partitions the model parameters into blocks based on the struc-
140
      ture of the Hessian matrix, assigning a unified second-order moment to all parameters within each
141   block (Zhang et al., 2024). This approach significantly reduces the memory footprint of the second-
142   order moment, thereby decreasing the optimizer’s memory usage. From a temporal perspective,
143   GaLore shares the same projection across a fixed number of steps, reducing computational over-
144   head. The above discussion demonstrates that many parameters can be shared during fine-tuning,
145   reducing memory usage or computational complexity.
146
      Low-Rank plus Sparse Matrix. Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) decomposes a data
147   matrix into the sum of the product of low-rank matrices and a sparse matrix and has been extensively
148   studied in both theory and applications (Lin et al., 2010; Zhou & Tao, 2011; Liu et al., 2013; Aravkin
149   et al., 2014; Hintermüller & Wu, 2015; Yi et al., 2016; Zhang & Yang, 2018). The recent Robust
150   Adaptation (RoSA) method extends Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) by further decomposing weight
151   updates into the product of two low-rank matrices, with an additional sparse matrix (Nikdan et al.,
152   2024). Using an optimizer to update both the low-rank and sparse matrices, RoSA achieves superior
153   performance compared to LoRA.

256   4.1.2      FAST SVD WITH R ANDOMIZED S UBSPACE I TERATION
257
258
      To further reduce the time consumption on SVD, we adopt the randomized subspace iteration algo-
      rithm proposed by Halko et al. (2011), which is a fast implementation of SVD. To obtain the m × r
259
      low-rank projection matrix from an m × n matrix, the randomized subspace iteration can reduce the
260
      computational complexity from O(mn × min(m, n)) to O(mn × log(r)). Specifically, the compu-
261   tational complexity for the SVD operations of ∇W Q and ∇W K can be reduced from O(hd3k ) to
262   O(hd2k × log(r)).
263
264
      Furthermore, randomized subspace iteration also reduces memory consumption during fine-tuning.
      SVD of an m × n matrix produces three matrices with the shapes of m × m, min(m, n), and
265
      n × n. However, we only use one matrix of m × m or n × n to get the low-rank projection
266
      matrix. Randomized subspace iteration only produces a r × r matrix during SVD, which consumes
267   significantly less memory. The experiments in Section 5 demonstrate the advantages of randomized
268   subspace iteration regarding time efficiency and memory usage.

271
272   The low-rank projection of the gradients can significantly reduce the memory consumption during
273   fine-tuning, along with the low-rank first- and second-order moments for optimizers. However,
274   the low-rank projection may not always be the main component of practical implementations. For
275   instance, the low-rank projections are updated every hundred steps during fine-tuning due to the
276   high computational complexity of SVD, making it challenging to apply low-rank projections at
277   every step. Moreover, the cross-head low-rank projection introduces increased approximation error
278
      due to cross-head sharing and randomized subspace iterations, leading to less accurate SVD results.
      Consequently, the low-rank first- and second-order moments for the optimizers are also imprecise.
279
      To address this, we estimate the residuals using a sparse representation, which improves the quality
280
      of the first- and second-order moments.

340   4.2.2   S PARSE I NDEXING M ATRIX FOR THE R ESIDUALS
341
342   We can leverage the residuals in Equation 11 and 13 to improve the quality of the updates during
343   the optimization process. However, the residuals in Equation 11 and 13 are full-rank matrices,
344   i.e., ∆Mt , ∆Vt , δt ∈ Rm×n , consuming enormous memory during fine-tuning. To incorporate
345   the residuals in memory-efficient PEFT methods, we employ sparse representations with a sparse
346   indexing matrix preserving the most significant elements of the residuals.
347   We introduce a warm-up stage to determine the sparse indexing matrix essential for efficient fine-
348   tuning. This warm-up stage occurs during the first k steps of the fine-tuning process. At the end
349   of the warm-up stage, the positions for the top-1% absolute values in the reconstructed first-order
350   moment (i.e., Pt Mt′ ) are recorded and used to create the sparse indexing matrix. This allows the
351   approximation residuals for both moments and updates to be stored in sparse matrices, significantly
352   reducing memory requirements. During the warm-up stage, the residuals are set to 0.
353   Algorithm 2 presents the whole process for obtaining the compact residuals at the t-th (t > k)
354   step of AdamW. Please note that the proposed method can also be incorporated into other optimiz-
355   ers with moments, such as Adam and AdaGrad, to improve the quality of updates with low-rank
356   approximation.

359   5   E XPERIMENTS
360
361   In this section, we present a series of experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of GaLore+. We
362   compare our proposed method against a range of baselines in fine-tuning the LLaMA2-7B and
363   LLaMA2-13B models (Touvron et al., 2023), specifically focusing on tasks related to arithmetic
364   reasoning and natural language generation, to assess its overall performance.
365   Implementation Details. We fine-tune all layers of the LLaMA2-7B and LLaMA2-13B models,
366   adding sparsely coded residuals only in the query and key projections, and load the parameters in
367   bfloat16 format. For the arithmetic reasoning task, we evaluate the accuracy on the test set,

516   6               C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   In this paper, we propose GaLore+, a low-rank adaptation method with fast yet precise estimation of
519   the low-rank projections. The estimation is achieved by cross-head low-rank projection and random-
520   ized subspace iteration. We further employ a sparsely coded residual to further reduce the error of
521   low-rank approximation, which works on the moments of the optimizer. Experiments on arithmetic
522
      reasoning and natural language generation indicate that GaLore+ outperforms existing low-rank
      adaptation methods, offering superior performance with reduced computational complexity.
523
524   However, the proposed method can be further improved in the following aspects.
525
      Additional theoretical analysis. Although the experiments validate that sharing low-rank projec-
526   tion matrices across heads in multi-head attention can be efficient, additional theoretical analysis is
527   needed to measure the preciseness of such sharing.
528
529
      Improvement on sparsely coded residual. We employ sparse matrices to store the approximation
      errors of first- and second-order moments and weight updates. However, all these sparse matrices
530
      share the same indexing matrices. Although experiments indicate that the current design is effective,
531
      the shared indexing matrices estimated from the first-order moment can be further improved.
