009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Layer normalization (LN) is a milestone technique in deep learning and has been
012            widely used in various network architectures. It performs centering and scaling
013            over the layer activations of a neural network for each example, stabilizing and ac-
014            celerating the training of neural network. However, it introduces extra computation
015            cost during inference and the computation problem has recently been addressed
016
               by its counterpart RMSNorm that only adopts scaling. This paper investigates
               how to exploit the theoretical advantages of LN but with the cost of RMSNorm.
017
               This paper formally defines the condition that the centering operation of LN can
018
               be removed and this condition can be obtained by imposing the column centering
019            constraint on the adjacent linear module before the LN. We propose column cen-
020            tered weight transformation (CCWT) to ensure an LN without centering operation
021            (i.e., RMSNorm) have the same output as the original one in a pre-trained model.
022            Our method can be directly applied to various pre-trained large language models
023            (LLMs) and large vision language models (VLMs) with LN, enabling an immediate
024            reduction in computation cost meanwhile maintaining equivalent prediction during
025            inference. We further propose a reparameterization method, called column based
026            weight centering (CBWC), to ensure the linear module column centered during
027
               training. We show that RMSNorm combining CBWC can obtain an equivalent
               effects to the LN counterpart during training, but with more efficient computation.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Normalization techniques are extensively used in deep neural networks (DNNs) for stabilizing and
033   accelerating the training (Huang et al., 2023). As a seminar work, Batch Normalization (BN) (Ioffe
034   & Szegedy, 2015) improves DNNs’ training stability and optimization efficiency by standardizing
035   (centering and scaling) the activations of intermediate DNN layers within a mini-batch of data
036   during training. It uses the population statistics for normalization during inference and this operation
037   can be folded into the adjacent linear layers (Jacob et al., 2018), avoiding the introduction of
038   additional computation cost during inference. In spite of many merits, BN also suffers from the
039
      train-inference inconsistent problem, leading to significantly degenerated performance under the
      scenarios of small-batch size training and domain shifted distributions (Huang et al., 2023). Layer
040
      normalization (LN) (Ba et al., 2016) addresses the train-inference inconsistency problem of BN and
041
      standardizes the layer input within the neurons for each sample. It has become the key component of
042   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and its variants (Dai et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy
043   et al., 2021), spreading from the Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Radford et al., 2018; Devlin
044   et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020) to Computer Vision (CV) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Carion et al., 2020;
045   Cheng et al., 2022) communities. LN has got its firm position (Huang et al., 2023) in the evolution
046   of neural architectures and is currently a basic layer in most of the foundation models (Brown
047   et al., 2020; Alayrac et al., 2022; Kirillov et al., 2023). However, it has to perform the additional
048   standardization during inference, which introduces significant computational cost.
049   To addresses the computational issue of LN, RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) is proposed to
050   perform scaling-only operation and is reported to reduce the running time of LN by 7% ∼ 64%
051   on different models, according to the experiments in (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019). Despite its great
052   potential in practice for computational efficiency and wide application in various architectures (Zhang
053   et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024; Mehta et al., 2024), RMSNorm is likely to miss the theoretical
      merits of centering operation in improving conditioning, which is widely investigated in previous


                                                          1

054
      work (LeCun et al., 1990; Schraudolph, 1998; Montavon & Müller, 2012; Huang et al., 2017). This
055   raises a question that how we can exploit the theoretical advantages of LN but with the computational
056   cost of RMSNorm.
057
058
      This paper first formally defines the condition that the centering operation of LN can be removed.
      The condition requires that the input to LN is zero centered of neurons for each sample, so that the
059
      removal of centering in LN does not affects the functionality of the network. This condition can be
060
      obtained by imposing the column centering constraint on the adjacent linear modules before the LN.
061
062   We show that we can satisfy the condition by performing a simple column centered weight transfor-
063   mation (CCWT) for a pre-trained model during inference. This method can ensure an LN without
064
      centering operation (i.e., RMSNorm) have the same output as the original one in a pre-trained model.
      We provide a general method to check whether the centering operation of LN can be removed in
065
      a network. We show most of LNs in currently widely used architectures can remove the centering
066
      operation, which provides a straightforward benefit in reducing the computation cost during inference.
067   This solution can be directly applied to various pre-trained large language models (LLMs) and large
068   vision language models (VLMs) with LN, enabling an immediate reduction in computation cost
069   without affecting the predictions.
070
      We further propose a reparameterization method, called column based weight centering (CBWC),
071
      to ensure the linear module column centered during training. We show that ’CBWC+RMSNorm’
072
      obtains an equivalent effects to the original LN counterpart during training, but with more efficient
073   computation. A network with ’CBWC+RMSNorm’ have equivalent training dynamics to the network
074   with LN, if the LN satisfies the condition that centering operation can be removed. We also conducted
075   experiments to show the effectiveness of ’CBWC+RMSNorm’ when replacing LN, even though the
076   LN can not satisfy the condition.

      3     A F RAMEWORK IN R EMOVING THE C ENTERING OF LN
121
      In this section, we turn to find a way to simplify LN with the computation efficiency of RMSNorm, but
122
      with an equivalent performance.We first introduce redundant centering as the condition of equivalent
123   performance. We then propose column centered constraints for linear modules to ensure redundant
124   centering.
125
126   3.1   R EDUNDANT C ENTERING
127
128   Apparently, we can change LN into RMSNorm, if RMSNorm is capable of achieving equivalent
129   results. Intuitively, with a zero-mean input, RMSNorm will have the same output with LN. Under this
130   situation, we can consider that RMSNorm acts as a scaling operation and, thus, LN has a centering
131   with no effect. Here, we define redundant centering.
132   Definition 1. (Redundant Centering in LN.) For any module f (x; θ) and an LN directly connected
133   to it, where x and θ ∈ Θ refers to the input and parameter respectively. We define the centering
134
      operation in this LN is redundant, if
135                                    RMSNorm(f (x; θ)) = LN(f (x; θ)), ∀x.                          (5)
136
      In other words, if we accomplish the effect of a centering operation in a layer normalization in
137
      the module before, we denote that this centering operation is redundant. Therefore, satisfying the
138   condition of a redundant centering, we can delete centering by using RMSNorm in place of LN, and
139   reduce the computation usage.
140
141
      We thus delve into into a methodology to establish a redundant centering operation.
142
143   3.2   C OLUMN C ENTERED C ONSTRAINT AND Z ERO - MEAN P ROPERTY
144

145   we propose to impose constraint onto the parameter θ ∈ Θ. By selecting constraints that endow θ
146   with a particular property, we aim to realize the centering effect before LN.
147
148

149

150
      applies on samples. Therefore, we consider to impose the constrains on the parameters of single
151   modules respectively. Since most parameters lie in the linear modules, we propose column centered
152   constraint for the linear modules. Here, we take the linear layer as an example.

      the analysis for the Transformer in Appendix A.6. Therefore, we introduce ’CBWC+RMSNorm’ as a
      alterative training method with origin LN. These two sets of parameters can be converted into each
400
      other, due to their equivalent optimization process, more details are in Section 5.4.
401
402
      Computational Complexity Analysis One advantage of ’CBWC+RMSNorm’ over LN is the
403   computational efficiency. In the widely used Transformer model, with a batch size denoted by b and
404   sequence length denoted by s, the dimension of a word is represented by d, and the weight matrix is
405   denoted as W ∈ Rd×p . Considering one epoch training with B samples, centering over the samples
406   (the centering of LN) has a computational cost of approximately O(Bsd) while CBWC incurs a
407   computational cost of O(Bdp/b). We can find that CBWC is more efficient, if s ∗ b > p. In practical
408   situation, s ∗ b is much larger than p, especially in the scenario with long context learning.
409

414
      of Dropout will disrupting the zero-mean property3 This results in that the centering operation and

      CBWC is not theoretically equivalent while training.

523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper provided the framework by rigorous definition and derivation, under which a DNN
526   with LN can be equivalently transfer to a network with RMSNorm. We showed how the centering
527   operation of LN can be removed both in inference and training, by introducing the proposed column
528
      centered weight transformation (CCWT) and column based weight centering (CBWC). The proposed
      CCWT can be directly applied to various pre-trained large language models (LLMs) and large vision
529
      language models (VLMs) with LN, enabling an immediate reduction in computation cost but with
530
      an equivalent forward pass during inference. We hope our method can benefit the LLMs and VLMs
531   community.
532
533
      Limitation and Future Work In practical application scenarios, there are a large number of LLMs
534   and VLMs with LN, but the number of large models we have analyzed in this paper is relatively
535   small. In future work, we will develop general tools to detect foldable LNs in pre-trained models and
536   replace these LNs with RMSNorm automatically. Morever, in real neural networks, there are some
537   modules that our method cannot implement, such as dropout layers. This may affect the effectiveness
538   and utility of our simplification method, which is determined by the construction of the model. In
539   future work, we expect to have more detailed analysis of every models on how much does this method
      improve the effect.
