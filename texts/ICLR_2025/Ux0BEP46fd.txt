011                                                                      A BSTRACT
012
013               In this work, we introduce Multimodal Context (MiCo), a scalable pretraining
014
                  framework designed to advance omni-modal intelligence—an AI system capable of
                  understanding and learning from multiple modalities to achieve universal represen-
015
                  tation learning. MiCo allows for efficient scaling of both the number of modalities
016
                  and the volume of data, along with model parameters, during the pretraining phase.
017               We evaluate the pretrained models across a diverse set of tasks, including: (i)
018               single-modality perception benchmarks covering 10 distinct modalities, (ii) 25
019               cross-modal tasks spanning retrieval, question-answering, and captioning, and
020               (iii) 18 large-scale multimodal language model benchmarks. MiCo consistently
021               delivers state-of-the-art results, setting 37 new benchmarks across these tasks. The
022               pretrained models, along with the collected datasets and codebase, will be made
023               publicly available to support the development of omni-modal intelligence and
024               broader research in multimodal learning.

085   Inspired by these insights, we categorize diverse modalities into two types: “knowledge modality”
086   and “interface modality”. Knowledge modalities, primarily derived from raw sensors, contribute
087
      knowledge in diverse formats. For example, images and depth maps offer visual knowledge, while
      audio and video provide auditory and spatiotemporal knowledge. The language modality, developed
088
      by humans, is inherently more abstract and naturally functions as the interface modality, facilitating
089
      learning, reasoning, and the coordination of knowledge. To this end, we design an omni-modal
090   learning architecture, illustrated in Figure 2 (b), with two distinct branches: one for knowledge
091   modalities and one for the interface modality, i.e. natural language. The knowledge and interface
092   modalities are aligned through a novel generative reasoning method, as detailed in § 3.3.
093
      In addition to the architecture design, the next challenge is how to further enhance the benefits of
094
      integrating multiple data modalities. In Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), context relationship
095
      assigns a unique vector to each input position in a sequence and improves sequence modeling
096   by capturing the sequential relationship among tokens. Moreover, since different modalities (e.g.,
097   text, image, audio) offer complementary information, integrating these sources fosters a more
098   comprehensive understanding of the data. Modeling token sequences from different modalities under
099   the same context can help the model understand modality characteristics and joint semantics.
100
      Therefore, we propose the Multimodal Context (MiCo) framework. We first map different modalities
101
      into a joint embedding space by sharing backbone networks. Then we build contextual relation-
102   ships by joint context embeddings to enhance coherent multimodal understanding, as shown in
103   Figure 1. Subsequently, we employ omnimodal contrastive learning, omnimodal feature matching,
104   and omnimodal caption generation processes for pretraining (detailed in § 3.4). Moreover, MiCo
105   can incorporate existing text-image, text-audio, and text-video datasets for joint multimodal context
106   learning (§ 3.3), which leads to better omni-modal learning capacity, further modality extensibility,
107   and easier scalability of multimodal data. Meanwhile, we explore the stability of MiCo in pretraining
      modalities, model parameters, and data scales (detailed in Figure 6).

      2     R ELATED W ORK
138
139    Vision-Language Pretraining. MCAN (Yu et al., 2019b) first aligns vision and language features
140   by stacking deep cross-attention blocks. Then more works (Wang et al., 2021b;c; 2022b;c) scale their
141
      models and improve the vision-language fusion process to build better alignment. VL-BERT (Su et al.,
      2019) introduced the Masked Language Model (MLM) paradigm, focusing on generic tasks across
142
      both vision and language modalities. Then Oscar (Li et al., 2020) proposed to enrich the representation
143
      of object semantics by integrating visual and textual content. Subsequent frameworks have further
144   refined and extended these capabilities. Notably, VinVL (Zhang et al., 2021), SimVLM (Wang
145   et al., 2021c), VLMO (Wang et al., 2021b), ALBEF (Li et al., 2021a), and Florence (Yuan et al.,
146   2021) have explored and demonstrated the advantages of joint representations that ensure semantic
147   consistency across the visual and natural language. Additionally, the versatility of multimodal
148   models extends into specialized applications such as few-shot learning (Alayrac et al., 2022), and
149   sequence-to-sequence (Wang et al., 2022b; Yu et al., 2022). BEiT-v3 (Wang et al., 2022c) employs a
150   cross-modal mask-and-reconstruction process with partially shared parameters.
151   More-Modality Pretraining. MMV (Alayrac et al., 2020) pioneered multimodal pretraining using
152   text, video, and audio pairs. They proposed multimodal contrastive learning for alignment. Then
153   VATT (Akbari et al., 2021) further developed pretraining multiple modalities with transformers. After
154   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), more works (Zhang et al., 2023c; Girdhar et al., 2023; Guzhov et al.,
155   2022; Xue et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024) propose to adapt pretrained CLIP models
156   to more modalities including point cloud, depth, audio, video, etc. Another direction is to exploit
157   multimodal complementary benefits and construct more modality pairs such as VAST (Chen et al.,
158
      2023b) and VALOR (Chen et al., 2023a), which improve the abilities for multimodal understanding.
159   Despite significant advancements in multimodal learning, several key challenges impede the de-
160   velopment of comprehensive omni-modal intelligence: 1) Focus on Vision-Language: Current
161   methods (Wang et al., 2022c; 2021b; Li et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021c) predominantly cater to
      vision-language tasks. The inflexibility of these works limits the extension with more modalities such


                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      as video, audio, etc. 2) Architectural Constraints: The development of architectures capable of
163   handling a broader array of modalities is still in its nascent stages. Crafting scalable and efficient
164   multimodal learning architectures presents a significant challenge. 3) Data Availability: There is a
165   notable scarcity of publicly accessible datasets including multimodal paired data (video, depth, audio,
166   and captions). 4) Multimodal Benefits: Although leveraging the synergistic benefits of multiple
167   modalities is crucial (Fei et al., 2022), understanding and optimizing the interaction between highly
168   disparate modalities remains a complex and largely unexplored area.

       Preliminary. The context is proposed to assign a unique vector to each token in a sequence (Vaswani
233
      et al., 2017), which reinforces potential relevance between positions. Different modalities (e.g., text,
234   image, audio) provide complementary information. Learning multimodal context leads to a more
235   holistic and nuanced understanding of data. It can also leverage the strengths of each modality and
236   guide the model to understand the interactions between different types of information. Therefore, we
237   seek to construct the context relationship across diverse modalities and extend the learning capacity
238   to omni-modalies. We provide the overview of MiCo pretraining paradigm in Figure 5.
239
      Single Dataset with Multimodal Paired Data. As mentioned in § 3.1, we build a dataset with
240   multimodal paired data \ifmmode \lbrace \else \textbraceleft \fi (\boldsymbol {x}_I,\boldsymbol {x}_D, \boldsymbol {x}_N, \boldsymbol {x}_T^I), (\boldsymbol {x}_A,\boldsymbol {x}_T^A), (\boldsymbol {x}_V,\boldsymbol {x}_T^V)\}, then we employ the omni-modal
241   encoder f(\cdot ;\theta ) to extract features \protect \bm  {z}_I, \boldsymbol {z}_A, \boldsymbol {z}_V, \boldsymbol {z}_D, \text { and } \boldsymbol {z}_N  , then use text encoder to extract text
242   features \protect \bm  {z}_T . Therefore, we construct the context by a top-down design: 1) For the whole multimodal
243   embeddings, they share the same position embeddings \protect \bm  {E}_{\text {Pos}} to build a modality-fused context
244   relationship across diverse modalities. 2) Then, for each specific context, they’re labeled by modality
245   embeddings including \protect \bm  {E}^I_{\text {M}}, \boldsymbol {E}^A_{\text {M}}, \boldsymbol {E}^V_{\text {M}}, \boldsymbol {E}^D_{\text {M}}, \boldsymbol {E}^N_{\text {M}}, etc to indicate modality types. 3) Within the same
246   modality context, we employ the context embeddings \protect \bm  {E}^I_{\text {C}} to construct uni-modal context relationships.

469   combination of contrastive, matching, and generative losses (\protect \mathcal  {L}_\text {Con} + \mathcal {L}_\text {Match} + \mathcal {L}_\text {Gen} ) yields the best
470   performance, demonstrating the value of multiple complementary objectives.
471
      Scaling Parameters. From (o) to (q), we assess the effect of model size. Larger models, particularly
472   the Giant-1.3B, show superior performance, confirming that increasing model parameters with MiCo
473   enhances learning and generalization abilities across diverse modalities.
474
475
476
      5                   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
477
       In this paper, we propose a novel framework, termed MiCo, to train foundation models with enhanced
478
      visual perception abilities and omni-modal capacities. With experiments on a reasonably large scale of
479
      both model and data, we conclude that the key to omni-modal learning is to simulate the multimedia
480   cognition process of the human brain. In MiCo, we use image, depth, and normal maps to simulate
481   the fundamental visual perception ability, distance spatial awareness, and geometry awareness of
482   human visual cognition. In addition, captions, audio, and video provide prior knowledge, auditory
483   perception, and spatial-temporal awareness. In future work, we plan to enhance our joint pretraining
484   by incorporating additional modalities, including optical flow, IMU data, and event files, etc. We
485   believe MiCo is an important attempt to simulate the multimedia cognition of human brains, and we
      expect it could inspire future works to develop more powerful omni-modal foundation models.
