011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) emerged as an important tool to enable a group of
014
               agents/clients to collaboratively train a model without sharing their individual data
               with each other or any third party, instead exchanging only model updates during
015
               each training round. Although FL performs effectively when clients’ data are
016
               homogeneous (e.g., each client’s data is i.i.d.), data heterogeneity among clients
017            presents a major challenge, often leading to significant performance degradation.
018            To address this challenge, a variety of approaches have been proposed. One par-
019            ticularly effective approach is clustered FL, where similar clients are grouped to-
020            gether to train separate models. Previous clustered FL approaches tend to rely
021            solely on either data similarity or gradient similarity to cluster clients. This results
022            in an incomplete assessment of client similarities, particularly when the datasets
023            display various types of distributional skews, such as label, feature, or quantity im-
024            balances. Consequently, these methods fail to capture the full spectrum of client
025
               heterogeneity, leading to suboptimal model performance across diverse client en-
               vironments.
026
               In this work, we address the challenge of data heterogeneity in FL by introducing
027
               a novel clustered FL approach, called F LAG. F LAG employs a weighted class-
028
               wise similarity metric that integrates both data and gradient similarity, providing
029            a more holistic measure of client similarity. This enables more accurate clustering
030            of clients, ultimately improving model performance across heterogeneous data
031            distributions. Our extensive empirical evaluation on multiple benchmark datasets,
032            under various heterogeneous data scenarios, demonstrates that F LAG consistently
033            outperforms state-of-the-art approaches in terms of accuracy.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
037   Federated Learning (FL) enables users/clients to collaboratively train a model on their data without
038   sharing it with other clients or a central entity (McMahan et al., 2017). However, the diversity in user
039   behavior often results in heterogeneous data distributions, referred to as non-identically indepen-
040   dently distributed (non-IID) data, across clients. This heterogeneity can lead to slower convergence
041   and suboptimal accuracy of the global model (Kairouz et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2022). For exam-
042   ple, in disease risk prediction using electronic health records, variations in patient demographics
043   and clinical presentations contribute to data heterogeneity (Prayitno et al., 2021). More specifically,
044   non-IID data can arise due to various factors, including class/label skew, feature skew, quantity shift,
045
      concept shift, and concept drift — common types of data heterogeneity. Class/label skew refers to
      the non-identical distribution of labels/classes at different clients, e.g., the absence of a label at one
046
      client while the same label is present at other clients (Zhang et al., 2022). Feature skew occurs
047
      when distributions vary due to different personalization nuances, e.g., an alphabet letter can be writ-
048   ten in different ways (Li et al., 2021). Quantity shift happens when different clients have different
049   amounts of data (Wang et al., 2021), e.g., an online retailer with millions of transaction records is
050   compared to a local store with only a few hundred records. Concept drift occurs when the statis-
051   tical properties of the target variable change, leading to different labels for similar data instances
052   across clients (Kairouz et al., 2021). Concept shift happens when different clients assign the same
053   label to fundamentally different data samples due to variations in local data distributions or labeling
      criteria (Kang et al., 2024).

054
      Approaches, e.g., personalized FL (Fallah et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2020; Smith et al., 2017; Ari-
055   vazhagan et al., 2019), aggregation schemes (Wang et al., 2020; Pillutla et al., 2022; Karimireddy
056   et al., 2020), local-global mixing (Jiang et al., 2024; Mansour et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2020), and
057   clustered FL (Ghosh et al., 2020; Vahidian et al., 2023; Sattler et al., 2020; Long et al., 2023), have
058   been proposed to address data heterogeneity. Personalized FL and aggregation-based approaches,
059   which train a single model, often fail to generalize well to the local distributions of each client. In
060   local-global mixing approaches, where local distributions differ significantly from the global aver-
061   age, these methods deteriorate, causing each client to train primarily on its own local data (Vahidian
062   et al., 2023).
063   Clustered FL, in contrast, has demonstrated superior performance in handling non-IID data, es-
064   pecially when distinct groups of clients display substantial variations in their local data distribu-
065   tions (Ghosh et al., 2020). In clustered FL, clients are grouped into clusters based on the similarities
066   in their data distributions, and each cluster trains its own model tailored to its specific data.
067   Despite their advantages, existing clustered FL approaches face several limitations, such as limited
068   flexibility in capturing complex data heterogeneity, reliance on either data or gradient similarity
069   alone. Specifically, current clustered FL approaches suffer from the following limitations:
070
      1. Data-only or Gradient-only Clustering: Existing clustered FL approaches rely on either data
071
         subspace (Vahidian et al., 2023) or gradient subspace to compute similarity (Sattler et al., 2020)
072
         between clients.In gradient-only similarity measurement, nearly similar clients can end up with
073      different learning objectives due to class imbalance (i.e., quantity shift) or high dimensionality.
074      In data-only cases, clients may exhibit intra-class variance, concept shift, or concept drift, which
075      may hinder similarity measurement.
076   2. Improper Data Similarity Method: Data similarity-based clustered FL methods, such as
077      PACFL (Vahidian et al., 2023), uses a method that leverages cosine similarity measurement
078      between data subspace but does not account for label information of the data subspace during
079      comparison. In cases of concept shift, data subspace can exhibit similarity while having different
080      labels, which might result in incorrect similarity assessment.
081
      3. Predefined Cluster Numbers: Clustered FL approaches, such as IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020),
         assume that the number of clusters are known before running FL training, which can lead to
082
         poor model performance, if the predefined number does not match the actual data distributions.
083
         Previous approaches lack a mechanism for determining the optimal number of clusters when it
084      is not known in advance.
085   4. Limited Consideration of Data Skews: Existing clustered FL techniques primarily focus on
086      experiments with one or two types of data distribution skews, predominantly class/label skew.
087      They do not account for the broader range of skews, such as concept shift, concept drift, and
088      quantity shift, in data heterogeneity.
089
      The above-mentioned limitations raise the following crucial question:
090
      How can we overcome the above challenges posed by heterogeneous data distributions by utilizing
091
            both data and gradient information to dynamically group clients into clusters in FL ?
092
093   Our Contributions. This work proposes a novel algorithm, titled clustered Federated Learning
094   with datA and Gradient (F LAG), which integrates both data and gradient information to group
095   clients. F LAG effectively addresses the limitations of existing clustered FL methods. By com-
096   bining gradient and data information with an enhanced similarity measurement in data-space, F LAG
097   tackles a wide range of data heterogeneity challenges, resolving the key limitations of current ap-
098
      proaches. Specifically, in F LAG, each client first performs truncated Singular Value Decomposition
      (SVD) (Klema & Laub, 1980) on their dataset and sends a few principal vectors to the server. Fur-
099
      thermore, the client executes a few epochs of local training on their data using Stochastic Gradient
100
      Descent (SGD) (Ruder, 2016) and sends the local model gradients to the server.
101
102   The server uses the principal vectors to build a weighted class-wise data similarity matrix for the
103   clients. In addition, the server computes the cosine angle between the gradient directions of the
104
      clients and constructs a gradient similarity matrix based on those values. Finally, the server combines
      these two, i.e., data and gradient, matrices to create the final proximity matrix, which is used as the
105
      adjacency/distance matrix for clustering clients using Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering (HC).
106
107   F LAG also employs an approach to find the optimal clustering. It does so by first iterating over the
      clustering threshold in hierarchical clustering (HC), a hyper-parameter that controls the number of

133   2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
134
135   Clustered federated learning (FL) is a technique that addresses the distribution shift problem by
136   grouping clients into clusters based on their local data distributions. Various methods have been
137   proposed for clustering clients. Vahidian et al. (2023) introduced a method that clusters clients
138   by analyzing the principal angles between the client data subspaces. However, this approach does
139   not account for the label information of the datasets being compared. In scenarios such as concept
140   shift and concept drift, datasets with similar subspaces can have different labels and learning ob-
141   jectives. Ignoring label information may lead to incorrect clustering of clients. Additionally, the
142   method does not regularize the similarity values based on dataset size to address quantity shifts.
143
      Another type of clustered FL approach leverages gradient information or loss values on gradient to
      cluster clients (Kim et al., 2024). Ghosh et al. (2020) proposed initializing a set of global models
144
      and, at each round, estimating the cluster identity of the clients based on the minimum loss to the
145
      global model parameters. Then, clients with similar identities are aggregated to minimize the loss
146   function. However, this method assumes the number of clusters is known beforehand and performs
147   clustering (estimating identities) of clients at each iteration, making it computationally expensive.
148   Another gradient-based clustering approach recursively performs bi-partitioning when clients’ gra-
149   dients differ on a converged global model (Sattler et al., 2020). Zhang et al. (2024) developed
150   an adaptive clustering algorithm based on cosine-based model similarity of dimensionality-reduced
151   models. Ruan & Joe-Wong (2022) proposed an approach that employs soft clustering instead of
152   hard clustering, utilizing a proximal local updating technique that incorporates local information
153   while encoding knowledge from all cluster models. Another soft clustering-based approach, Guo
154
      et al. (2023), formulates the clustering problem as a bi-level optimization problem and introduces a
      new objective function to achieve robust clustering.

188   Agglomerative hierarchical clustering (Day & Edelsbrunner, 1984) is a popular method in ma-
189   chine learning for grouping similar objects based on a proximity (or adjacency) matrix. The process
190   begins by treating each data point as its own cluster. During each iteration, the algorithm carries
191
      out two main tasks: identifying the two clusters that are most similar and merging these clusters.
      The criterion for selecting which clusters to merge depends on a linkage method, such as single,
192
      complete, or average linkage. For instance, in single linkage, the L2 (Euclidean) distance between
193
      two clusters is defined as the smallest distance between any pair of points from the two clusters. For
194   merging criteria, F LAG defines clustering threshold as α, i.e., any two clusters with a distance less
195   than α are merged together; see details in §4.2.

206   Objective and high-level idea. Our first objective is to find the similarity among the client based
207   on their data. For this, F LAG uses cosine similarity between data of the same class for each pair of
208   clients and then takes the average across all the classes, which produces the proper magnitude of
209   similarity instead of just a binary similarity outcome. To account for the quantity shift between the
210   classes of the two participating clients, we assign weights to the class similarity values based on the
211   difference in class frequency, as discussed below in detail.

253   Objective and high-level idea. Once the server computes weighted class-wise similarity matrix
254   Vi,j (as discussed in §4.1), our objective is to identify the optimal cluster formation. For this, F LAG
255   combines data and gradient information. First, the server computes a proximity matrix based on
256   Vi,j and a gradient similarity matrix Gi,j and then uses the proximity matrix as the adjacency matrix
257   for agglomerative hierarchical clustering (HC) (Murtagh & Contreras, 2012) to cluster the clients
258   together.

260   using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) only on its local data, and computes its local gradient direc-

262   matrix Gi,j by calculating the cosine similarity between the gradient directions, as discussed in §3.

      Finding Optimal Clustering (Algorithm 3). Given the proximity/adjacency matrix Ai,j , the server
278
      uses a hierarchical clustering (HC) (Murtagh & Contreras, 2012) to find the best clustering. HC
279   works by finding the most similar clusters and merging them together. In HC, clustering threshold,
280   α ∈ (0, 1] serves as a merging criterion for any pair of clusters, meaning clusters with a distance
281   smaller than the threshold are eligible for merging; α also controls the number of clusters formed.
282   For example, α=1 results in all clients being grouped into a single cluster. The server uses Ai,j as
283   a distance metric for HC and iterates over different values of the α to find the best clustering. The
284   server systematically decreases α from 1 to 0 in regular intervals (e.g., 0.1) to generate different
285   clustering configurations. To assess the goodness or performance of each generated clustering with-
286   out executing the full clustered FL training, F LAG uses an efficient alternative method, as follows:
287
      the server selects only a few clients, and those clients set aside 10% of their local data as a vali-
      dation set. Then, with the remaining data, the selected clients train an efficient lightweight model
288
      with simple architecture in the Clustered FL setting based on that corresponding clustering. After
289
      training the model for a few epochs, each client evaluates the model’s accuracy on the validation set
290   and sends the accuracy score to the server. The server computes the average accuracy across all the
291   selected clients, which serves as the cluster goodness metric for the corresponding clustering.

      Finally, the server determines the clustering goodness metrics for each α values and uses methods

375

376
377        1. how much better F LAG is compared to existing method — §5.1
           2. how effectively does F LAG find clustering — §5.2

381   Datasets. We used four popular datasets for the image classification task in the federated learning
382
      setting, i.e., CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), FMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), SVHN (Netzer et al.,
      2011), CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009).
383
384   Baselines. We compare F LAG against SOTA methods: (i) Single model methods: FedAvg (McMa-
385   han et al., 2017), FedProx (Li et al., 2020), (ii) personalized FL method: PerFedAvg (Fallah et al.,
386   2020), (iii) clustered FL — data-based methods: PACFL(Vahidian et al., 2023), (iv) clustered FL —
387   gradient-based method: IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020), (CFL) (Sattler et al., 2020), FedSoft (Ruan &
388
      Joe-Wong, 2022).
389   Setup. Our experiments consider a total of 100 clients, with 20% randomly selected for each round.
390   Unless mentioned otherwise, the experiments run for 200 communication rounds, where each client
391   performs 10 local epochs using a batch size of 10 and SGD as the local optimizer. For Uci , the prin-
392   cipal vector sent per class is approximately 1% of |Di,c |. For computing G, each client runs 20 local
393   epochs. For combination ratio between data gradient, β, we used grid-search to fine-tune through
394
      various values and found β = 0.5 generalizes more effectively across different heterogeneity. For
      finding the clustering threshold (Algorithm 3), the server selects 30 clients at random and runs 5
395
      communication rounds of clustered FL.
396
397   Creation of non-IID data. We combine class skew with quantity shift to test our algorithm against
398   other baselines. To simulate class skew, we first randomly select ρ% of the total available labels
399   and assign that set of labels to a random set of clients. Then, we pick another set of clients and
400
      repeat the process until all clients are assigned ρ% of the labels. Next, we use the Dirichlet distri-
      bution (Ng et al., 2011) to distribute the samples of each label amongst the clients assigned to those
401
      labels. The Dirichlet distribution introduces quantity shift and class imbalance among clients, and
402
      also simulates varying levels of heterogeneity. Thus, we create a more realistic distribution, in which
403   we have different groups of clients that work on different types of classes. And, clients working on
404   similar types of classes exhibit quantity shift and class imbalance among them. An example of this
405   would be predictive text input (discussed in §1), or e-commerce recommendation system, where
406   different vendors sell different types of products (e.g., electronics, home goods), with some over-
407   lapping products, exhibiting class skew. However, each vendor may sell certain categories in higher
408   volumes (e.g., one vendor focuses more on smartphones, another on laptops), thus creating class
409   imbalance and quantity shift among clients handling same type of classes. Another such example
410   include: healthcare diagnosis system with different clinics working with different disease cases.
411

      parameter (denoted as α′ ) is set to 1 for a low degree, and to 0.25 for a high degree of quantity shift

459
      the clients in a single cluster. As α decreases, more clusters are created, and we observe a steep in-

460

461
      halts the steep accuracy increase. At that point, the number of clusters has reached the underlying
462   number of groups that have similar classes in the Non-IID distribution. The distribution which was
463   formed as discussed above §5. Decreasing α further beyond this point and creating more clusters
464   is detrimental to the FL training because it cannot benefit from the similar clients. Thus, we aim to
465   keep the number of clusters minimal. We can use the elbow method (Syakur et al., 2018) to find the
466   optimal point in α. For example, in Figure 1(a), the optimal α using elbow method can be identified
467   as α = 0.6.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
519
520
      This work presents a novel algorithm, F LAG (Federated Learning with Data and Gradient), which
521   addresses the limitations of existing clustered FL techniques and effectively tackles data hetero-
522   geneity challenges in FL. F LAG combines both data and gradient information to cluster clients more
523   effectively, addressing a broader range of data heterogeneity issues. The algorithm leverages princi-
524   pal vectors and gradient similarity to create a robust proximity matrix, which is used for clustering
525   via Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering. Moreover, F LAG employs an efficient method to deter-
526   mine the optimal number of clusters, improving scalability and performance. Extensive experiments
527   on various heterogeneous data distributions, including quantity shifts and class imbalances, demon-
528   strate that F LAG outperforms existing approaches in terms of accuracy.

678   8    A LGORITHM TECHNICAL ISSUES
679
680   In this section we will discuss the additional technical issues related to the F LAG algorithm.
681   Problem formulation F LAG, which is a cluster FL problem, can be formulated as a standard em-
682   pirical risk minimization (ERM) task. The objective is to learn parametric models by minimizing
683   a loss function defined over the data. We assume the presence of one server and N clients. The
684   server and clients communicate using a predefined communication protocol. Additionally, we con-
685   sider Z distinct data distributions, ρ1 , . . . , ρZ , with the N clients partitioned into Z disjoint clusters,
686   C1 , . . . , CZ . We assume that the cluster identities of the clients are not revealed to F LAG. Each
687
      client i ∈ Cz holds a dataset Di , which may be non-iid and subject to various types of data skews.
      The goal is to minimize the loss function Fz (θ) := Ei∼Dz [f (θ; d)] for all z ∈ {1, . . . , Z}, where
688
      f (θ; d) represents the loss associated with a data point d ∈ Di for client i ∈ {1, . . . , N }.
689
690   Privacy constraints Privacy is an important factor of FL, as it aims to enable collaborative model
691   training while concealing the sensitive data of individual clients. In this section we will discuss the
692   privacy constraints and concerns of our proposed F LAG. Regarding weighted class-wise data-based
693
      similarity detection, clients share a small set of principal vectors with the server. Additionally, each
      client’s class frequency information is shared with the server to compute the weights for similarity
694
      measurements. However, in F LAG, the privacy of the clients’ data is still preserved since the princi-
695
      pal vectors are linear combinations of the data points and not the actual data themselves. Moreover,
696   the number of principal vectors shared with the server is less than 1% of the size of the dataset
697   for each class per client. This approach aligns with prior works, such as Vahidian et al. (2023).
698   In privacy-sensitive scenarios, additional privacy mechanisms such as those proposed by Bonawitz
699   et al. (2017), encryption methods, or differential privacy techniques can be used to prevent infor-
700   mation leakage and provide stronger protection. Similarly, these privacy-preserving methods can
701   be employed when sharing class frequency information. Alternatively, in more privacy sensitive
      cases, uniform weights can be used for similarity measurements, eliminating the need to share class

705   Time complexity of F LAG The computational complexity of the federated learning part of Flag
706
      (Algorithm 1, line 12-19) is largely comparable to the other clustered federated learning (FL) ap-
      proaches. Algorithm 3 is essentially iterating over different clusterings to find the best cluster for-
707
      mation. We tried to reduce time required to do so in Algorithm 3 compared to other literature. We
708
      adopted a framework that uses a lightweight model with fewer layers, employing a fraction of the
709   clients, running fewer rounds of training. The clustering part of the Flag which can be denoted
710   by (Algorithm 1, line 6-11), which also incorporates Algorithm 2, is the main source of additional
711   computational overhead compared to vanilla FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017). That said, our ap-
712   proach uses a one-shot clustering strategy, meaning clustering is performed only once at the start
713   to assign each client a fixed cluster ID. This is more efficient than other methods (e.g., Ghosh et al.
714   (2020), Ruan & Joe-Wong (2022)) that perform iterative clustering, where client clusters are repeat-
715   edly updated at every training round until the FL process ends. Compared to the aforementioned
716   approaches our approach is much more efficient since we are performing the clustering one time
717
      and obtaining the final cluster ID’s.
718   In this part, we analyze the overhead of the clustering part of the Flag. As referred to Algorithm
719   1, line 8, each client performs SGD training on its local data before sending the local update to the
720   server. Here, each client performs at least 2 rounds of SGD training with local epochs of 10 steps
721   for each client. As for applying SVD to extract principal vectors, referred to in (Algorithm 1, line
722   8), each client applies SVD on the dataset of each class. Which takes less time compared to another
723
      approach Vahidian et al. (2023) that performs SVD on each client’s whole dataset. It is because
      performing SVD on a large dataset of a client with N data points, F features, and assuming N > F ,
724
      would cost O(F N 2 ). But, partitioning the dataset into C classes   where  each

728   a factor of C compared to performing SVD on the entire dataset of size N .
729   The communication complexity The FL part of Flag has communication complexity that is similar
730   to other notable cluster FL approaches (Vahidian et al., 2023). Since, Flag is a one-shot clustering,
731   during the FL training F LAG doesn’t do any clustering. Which is faster compared to some of the
732   other iterative clustering approaches (Ghosh et al., 2020; Sattler et al., 2020). Other sources of
733   communication burden relies on building the proximity matrix. Prior to start of the FL protocol,

771
772   In the context F LAG, hyperparameters play a crucial role in determining the model’s performance,
773   stability, and robustness. To better understand the effectiveness of F LAG, we investigate how sensi-
774   tive the algorithm is to variations in different hyperparameters.
775   The combination ratio β represents the weighting ratio between the data adjacency matrix and the
776   gradient adjacency matrix during their aggregation to compute the final proximity matrix. Table 6
777   presents the accuracy metrics for various values of β, ranging from 0 to 1 in increments of 0.1. The
778   additional hyper-parameter settings for the experiment are as follows: ρ = 20%, Dirichlet α′ = 1,
779   δ = 0.5, tg = 3 rounds of SGD training on local data, each client performing 10 local epochs,
780   m′ = 25 sampled clients for optimal clustering, and an interval size of α = 0.1.
781   Higher β values signify greater emphasis on the data adjacency matrix, whereas lower β values
782   prioritize the gradient adjacency matrix. From Table 6, we observe that in most cases, the data-only
783   adjacency matrix (β = 1) yields accuracy values closer to the peak, while the gradient-only matrix
784   (β = 0) results in lower accuracy comparatively. As β approaches the middle range, where both data
785   and gradient matrices receive roughly equal emphasis, F LAG exhibits higher accuracy by achieving
786   more robust clustering. Additionally, around this middle range of β values, F LAG tends to produce
787   nearly identical clustering, leading to similar accuracy across a range of β values. This phenomenon
788   can be attributed to our data distribution, where clients can be grouped into disjoint sets based on
789
      their data distributions. As a result, F LAG produces consistent clustering even when β values vary
      slightly. Depending on the specific data distributions, β can be adjusted slightly (e.g., currently set
790
      at β = 0.5) to fine-tune the algorithm and evaluate whether it yields different results.
791
792   Weight range of data similarity matrix δ is the parameter to determine the importance of weights
793   for computing the data similarity matrix. Since a small set of principal vectors is used to determine
794   the similarity between clients, the weights take into account the size of those compared datasets. The
795
      weighting scheme ensures the similarity decreases if there are significant differences in the dataset
      size. The degree of how much it will decrease is determined by the δ parameter. Smaller values of δ
796
      determine the impact of weights would be minimal and higher values of δ denote a more significant
797
      impact. After computing weights for each data similarity value, the weights are normalized within
798   a given range of [1 − δ, 1 + δ]. We consider a range of [0,1) for δ, which denotes the similarity
799   value/cosine angle can increase by a factor of ≈2 at most. Table 7 demonstrates how the accuracy
800   metric changes over different values of δ on different datasets. Since the weighting focuses on size
801   difference/quantity shift, we run our experiments on different ranges of quantity shifts, which is
802   denoted in Table 7 with α′ =1 and 0.25. As you can see from the table, for α′ the accuracy pretty
803   much stays the same regardless of what δ value is used. This happens since the distribution contains
804   less quantity shift with smaller differences in class values. For α′ =0.25 we can observe a slight
805   increase in accuracy for some cases. Since, in this case, the difference in class data size can lead to

      adjusted similar values and different clustering.
