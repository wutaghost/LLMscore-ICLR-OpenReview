009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning (RL) struggles with sparse reward environments. Recent
012            developments in intrinsic motivation have revealed the potential of language models
013            to guide agents in exploring the environment. However, the mismatch between the
014            granularity of environment transitions and natural language descriptions hinders
015            effective exploration for current methods. To address this problem, we introduce a
016            model-based RL method named Language-Guided Explorative Goal Generation
017            (LanGoal), which combines large language model (LLM) guidance with intrinsic
018            exploration reward by learning to propose meaningful goals. LanGoal learns a
019
               hierarchical policy together with a world model. The high-level policy learns to
               propose goals based on LLM guidance to explore the environment, and the low-
020
               level policy learns to achieve the goals. Extensive results on Crafter demonstrate
021
               the effectiveness of LanGoal compared to recent methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Reinforcement learning has been widely used in decision-making tasks, but it struggles with long-
      horizon tasks and sparse reward settings. Especially in open-world tasks (Milani et al., 2020; Guss
028
      et al., 2021; Kanervisto et al., 2022), the agent needs to explore and make decisions to reach the
029
      goal in very large state space. Tasks like obtain a diamond in Minecraft, can involve long-horizon
030   decision-making process and exploration for sparse reward signals, which significantly increase the
031   difficulty of the task.
032
033
      Given the intrinsic difficulty, reinforcement learning (RL) methods have been struggling to solve such
      tasks. Existing methods propose curiosity-driven exploration(Pathak et al., 2017; Ecoffet & Lehman,
034
      2021), maximize disagreement between ensemble of models(Burda et al., 2019), or use intrinsic
035
      motivation(Schmidhuber, 1991; Pathak et al., 2017) to encourage the agent to explore the environment.
036   Most of these methods give the agent a reward bonus when reaching unseen states, which can help the
037   agent explore efficiently and avoid local optima. However, intrinsic reward methods can mislead the
038   agent to favor meaningless noisy states or states with high transition uncertainty rather than reaching
039   the goal, which leads to the inefficiency of the method in sparse reward settings.
040
      Recently, with the rise of large language models (LLMs) and their ability as a few-shot learner
041
      (Achiam et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020), they have been gradually used in decision-making tasks.
042   Enriched with commonsense, LLMs can make reasoning and planning at abstract natural language
043   level, break down the task into sub-tasks for downstream RL methods. LLMs can also provide
044   promptable representation or exploration guidance with semantic meaning to the RL policy (Chen
045   et al., 2024; Zhang & Lu, 2024), enabling the agent to make decisions with respect to the prompt.
046   Thus, methods that combining LLMs with RL have been proposed to improve the performance of
047   decision-making tasks.
048   However, the primary challenge lies in the combination of LLMs and RL methods, which requires a
049   fast adaptation of the RL policy to the semantic meaning of environment state in an online manner.
050   Existing works learn model-free policy with guidance from LLM, but lack of understanding of the
051   semantic meaning. Thus, RL policy may not follow the guidance of LLMs or make a balance between
052   reaching the LLM goals and exploration during the online training, which leads to the inefficiency
053   of the method. Besides, RL policy may not be able to reach the goal proposed by LLMs when
      interacting with the environment, further compromising their effectiveness in goal-reaching tasks.

054
      In this paper, we propose LanGoal, a model-based reinforcement learning method with hierarchical
055   policy that combines with the LLM guidance efficiently. We claim that a hierarchical behavior
056   is beneficial for the agent to solve this problem by setting a meaningful goal regarding the LLM
057   guidance. Our method consists of a hierarchical policy training together with a world model. LLM
058   gives semantic guidance to the high-level policy, which generates abstract actions as goals for the
059   low-level policy as controller to reach. Inspired by recent advancement in controllable generation(Ho
060   & Salimans, 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) and its application in RL, we propose a novel method
061   to combine the LLM guidance with the high-level policy to propose meaningful goals. This, as a
062   result, improves the overall goal-reaching ability. We conduct extensive experiments to show the
063
      effectiveness of our method, compared with various baselines using different RL methods and LLMs.
      Our results reveal the potential of improving the performance on decision-making tasks combining
064
      LLMs and RL.

076   2    R ELATED W ORKS
077
078   Model-based RL. Model-based RL(MBRL) methods learn a world model through online interactions
079   or offline dataset (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2020). Agent then learns a policy with
080   the generated trajectories from interaction with the world model and improves the data efficiency.
081
      Existing works successfully apply MBRL methods in various domains including Atari games,
      locomotion tasks and open-ended environments, demonstrating the scalability of MBRL methods in
082
      decision-making tasks. (Hafner et al., 2023; 2021; 2019; Hansen et al., 2022; 2024). Lin et al. (2024)
083
      trains a multimodal world model using natural language descriptions and visual observations in the
084   environment, enabling the agent to learn representations combining both modalities. We employ
085   similar idea to learn multimodal embeddings for world model, while also consider incorporating the
086   guidance from LLM using a hierarchical policy to improve exploration ability.
087
      Hierarchical reinforcement learning for exploration. Hierarchical reinforcement learning offers
088
      a promising way to improve the exploration ability of RL methods, particularly in sparse reward
089
      settings. Hierarchical policy integrate effectively with intrinsic reward methods to facilitate temporal
090   abstraction (Kulkarni et al., 2016; Gumbsch et al., 2023), design dense reward for agents to explore
091   the environment (Steccanella et al., 2020; McClinton et al., 2021). Existing works also combines
092   hierarchical policy learning with world model to improve the exploration ability of model-based
093   RL methods. Hierarchical policy set random goals (Mendonca et al., 2021) or emply a divide-and-
094   conquer-like strategy (Hamed et al., 2024) to explore the environment. Hafner et al. (2022) introduce
095   a method to learn a hierarchical policy with intrinsic reward combines with world model, which helps
096   the agent to explore in sparse reward settings. These methods typically utilize model uncertainty to
097   encourage the agent to visit unseen states or transitions with high uncertainty.
098   However, such intrinsic rewards or heuristic methods can mislead the agent, such as favoring the
099   states with high transition uncertainty rather than reaching the goal, which leads to the inefficiency
100   of the method in sparse reward settings. Especially when meeting large state space and complex
101   tasks, intrinsic reward methods may fail to guide the agent to reach the goal efficiently. In this work,
102   we combine guidance from LLM with intrinsic reward, aiding the agent to explore the environment
103   towards meaningful goals. We train a hierarchical policy to generate goals with aligned with LLM
104
      guidance, and try to explore and adhere to the guidance simultaneously.

      In this section, we introduce the proposed method in detail. We first introduce how we prompt

      the LLM to generate skills for high-level policy, then we describe the world model and design of

      4.1   P ROMPTING LLM FOR G UIDANCE G ENERATION
170
171   We query the LLM for a fixed timestep interval H to ensure the responsed natural language goal is
172   reachable for RL policy in the environment. Given the observation ot at timestep t, we first transform
173   it into a natural language description lt , which contains the necessary semantic information of the
174   environment such as the inventory, location, and task description in the environment. Additionally,
175   we employ a captioner to label the state changes in previous H steps, showing which goal is actually
176   reached by RL policy, denoted as gtinv as its analogy to inverse dynamics. Then we prompt the LLM
177   with lt to decide a goal gt to reach and use a pretrained encoder to transform gt into a vector vt
178
      for high-level policy as input. We also use the same encoder to transform gtinv into a vector vtinv as
      additional information to train the world model. The detailed design of the prompt, captioner and
179
      encoder are provided in Appendices B and C.
180

      4.3   H IERARCHICAL P OLICY
217
218   We design a hierarchical policy with two levels of policies to leverage LLM guidance for exploration.
219   The low-level policy is a goal-reaching policy, try to reach the goal set by high-level policy. The
220   high-level policy determines the goal state that meets both the LLM-proposed goal and the need to
221   explore the environment. For simplicity, we synchronize the decision frequency of the high-level
222   policy with that of the LLM, proposing a goal zt at every H timesteps with high-level policy whenever
223   the LLM proposes gt . A different design of decision frequency is also feasible, which is left for
224
      future work.
225   Goal autoencoder. The goal state can be a high-dimensional continuous vector which is hard to
226   make decisions for high-level policy. Thus, we use an autoencoder to transform the goal state into a
227   discrete action space with lower dimension. The autoencoder compresses the state st into high-level
228   action space, and reconstruct the original state ŝt from the given high-level action or compressed
229   representation ut . The reconstruct error is used to measure the novelty of the goal. Then we set
230   decHθ (zt ) as the goal for the low-level policy to reach. We refer to Hafner et al. (2022) to design the
231
      action space of high-level policy. Specifically, the goal encoder takes st as input and predicts a matrix
      of 8×8 logits, samples a one-hot vector from each row, and flattens the results into a sparse vector
232
      with 8 out of 64 dimensions set to 1 and the others to 0. Gradients are backpropagated through the
233
      sampling by straight-through estimation (Bengio et al., 2013). The goal autoencoder is optimized
234   end-to-end using the variational objective:

      5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
359
360   We conduct ablation study to evaluate the effectiveness of each component of our method. The
361   results are shown in table 2. We also record the proportion of reached goals from LLM in the last
362   column for each setting.
363
364
      LLM Guidance. To evaluate the effectiveness of LLM guidance, we compare the performance of our
365
      method with different size of LLMs. We use GPT-4(gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09) and GPT-4o-
366   mini(gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18) to generate goals for the agent and evaluate the performance
367   of our method, denoted as LanGoal and LanGoal(w/ 4o-mini) respectively in table 2. We observe
368   slight performance drop after replacing LLM, but the results still surpass other RL methods. We
369   also note that smaller LLM like GPT-4o-mini tends to generate more unreached goals regardless of
370   current state, or simply choose goals to keep agent alive. While larger LLM like GPT-4 can make
371   decision regarding to the current state and propose meaningful goals for the agent, indicating the
372   importance of effective LLM guidance.
