011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We analyse the relationship between privacy vulnerability and dataset properties,
014            such as examples per class and number of classes, when applying two state-of-
015
               the-art membership inference attacks (MIAs) to fine-tuned neural networks. We
               derive per-example MIA vulnerability in terms of score distributions and statistics
016
               computed from shadow models. We introduce a simplified model of membership
017
               inference and prove that in this model, the logarithm of the difference of true and
018            false positive rates depends linearly on the logarithm of the number of examples
019            per class. We complement the theoretical analysis with empirical analysis by sys-
020            tematically testing the practical privacy vulnerability of fine-tuning large image
021            classification models and obtain the previously derived power law dependence be-
022            tween the number of examples per class in the data and the MIA vulnerability, as
023            measured by true positive rate of the attack at a low false positive rate. Finally, we
024            fit a parametric model of the previously derived form to predict true positive rate
025            based on dataset properties and observe good fit for MIA vulnerability on unseen
026
               fine-tuning scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning models are prone to memorising their training data, which makes them vulnerable
031   to privacy attacks such as membership inference attacks (MIAs; Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al.,
032   2022) and reconstruction attacks (e.g. Balle et al., 2022; Nasr et al., 2023). Differential privacy (DP;
033   Dwork et al., 2006) provides protection against these attacks, but strong formal protection often
034   comes at the cost of significant loss of model utility.
035
      Finding the correct balance between making models resistant to attacks while maintaining a high
036   utility is important for many applications. In health, for example, many European countries and soon
037   also the EU within the European Health Data Space have requirements that models trained on health
038   data that are made publicly available must be anonymous, i.e. they must not contain information
039   that can be linked to an identifiable individual. On the other hand, loss of utility of the model due to
040   privacy constraints may compromise the health benefits that might be gained from it.
041
      In this paper, our aim is to theoretically understand and systematically apply two state-of-the-art
042   MIAs, LiRA (Carlini et al., 2022) and RMIA (Zarifzadeh et al., 2024), to help understand practical
043   privacy risks when fine-tuning deep-learning-based classifiers without DP protections. We focus on
044   transfer learning using fine-tuning because this is increasingly used for all practical applications of
045   deep learning and especially important when labeled examples are limited, which would often be
046   the case in privacy-sensitive applications. Our case study focuses on understanding and quantifying
047   factors that influence the vulnerability of non-DP deep transfer learning models to MIA. In partic-
048   ular, we theoretically study the relationship between the number of examples per class, which we
049   denote as shots (S), and MIA vulnerability (true positive rate TPR at fixed false positive rate FPR)
050
      for a simplified model of fine-tuning and derive a power-law relationship in the form
051                                   log(TPR − FPR) = −βS log(S) − β0 .                                  (1)
052
053   We complement the theoretical analysis with extensive experiments over many datasets with varying
      sizes in the transfer learning setting for image classification tasks and observe the same power-

054
      law. This power-law has a practically remarkable implication that a practitioner could estimate the
055   membership privacy risk and how large a training set would be needed to mitigate the risk.
056
057   Related work There has been evidence that classification models with more classes are more vul-
058
      nerable to MIA (Shokri et al., 2017), models trained on fewer samples can be more vulnerable (Chen
      et al., 2020; Németh et al., 2023), and classes with less examples tend to be more vulnerable (Chang
059
      & Shokri, 2021; Kulynych et al., 2022; Tonni et al., 2020). Larger generalisation error, which is
060
      related to dataset size, has also been shown to be sufficient for MIA success (Song & Mittal, 2021),
061   though not necessary (Yeom et al., 2018). Similarly, minority subgroups tend to be more affected
062   by DP (Suriyakumar et al.; Bagdasaryan et al., 2019). Feldman & Zhang (2020) showed that neural
063   networks trained from scratch can memorise a large fraction of their training data, while the mem-
064   orisation is greatly reduced for fine-tuning. Additionally, Tobaben et al. (2023) reported how the
065   MIA vulnerability of few-shot image classification is affected by the number of shots. Yu et al.
066   (2023) studied the relationship between the MIA vulnerability and individual privacy parameters
067   for different classes. Nonetheless, the prior works do not consider the rate of change in the vul-
068   nerability evaluated at a low FPR, as dataset properties change. Our work significantly expands on
069
      these works by explicitly identifying a quantitative relationship between dataset properties and MIA
      vulnerability (i.e., the power-law in Equation (1)).
070
071   List of contributions We analyze the MIA vulnerability of deep transfer learning using two state-of-
072   the-art score-based MIAs, LiRA (Carlini et al., 2022) and RMIA (Zarifzadeh et al., 2024), which are
073   a strong realistic threat model. We first analytically derive the power-law relationship in Equation (1)
074   for both MIAs by introducing a simplified model of the optimal membership inference (Section 3).
075
      We support our theoretical findings by an extensive empirical study on the MIA vulnerability of
      deep learning models by focusing on a transfer-learning setting for image classification task, where
076
      a large pre-trained neural network is fine-tuned on a sensitive dataset.
077
078   1. Closed-form per-example vulnerability: We derive closed-form per-example LiRA and RMIA
079      vulnerability (TPR at fixed FPR) in terms of MIA attack score distributions and statistics computed
080      from shadow models (see Section 3.3).
081
      2. Power-law in simplified model of the optimal MI: We formulate a simplified model of member-
082
         ship inference to quantitatively relate dataset properties and MIA vulnerability, in which LiRA is
083      the optimal attack. For this model, we prove a power-law relationship between the per-example
084      LiRA and RMIA vulnerability and the number of examples per class. We then extend the the
085      power-law relationship to the average-case LiRA and RMIA vulnerability (See Section 3.4).
086
      3. Few-shot MIA: We conduct a comprehensive study of MIA vulnerability (TPR at fixed low FPR)
087      in the transfer learning setting for image classification tasks with target models trained using
088      many different datasets with varying sizes and confirm the theoretical power law between the
089      number of examples per class and the vulnerability to MIA (see Figure 1).
090
      4. Regression model: We utilise our empirical observations to fit a regression model to predict MIA
091      vulnerability (log(TPR − FPR) at fixed low FPR) based on examples per class (log S) and number
092      of classes (log C), which follows the functional form of the theoretically derived power-law. We
093      show both very good fit on the training data as well as good prediction quality on unseen data
094      from a different feature extractor and when fine-tuning other parameterisations (see Figure 4).

      2   BACKGROUND
098
      Notation for the properties of the training dataset D: (i) C for the number of classes (ii) S for shots
099
      (examples per class) (iii) |D| for training dataset size (|D| = CS). We denote the number of MIA
100
      shadow models with M .
101
102   Membership inference attacks (MIAs) aim to infer whether a particular sample was part of the
103   training set of the targeted model (Shokri et al., 2017). Thus, they can be used to determine lower
104
      bounds on the privacy leakage of models to complement the theoretical upper bounds obtained
      through differential privacy.
105
106   Likelihood Ratio attack (LiRA; Carlini et al., 2022) While many different MIAs have been pro-
107   posed (Hu et al., 2022), in this work we consider the Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA). LiRA is a
      strong attack that assumes an attacker that has black-box access to the attacked model, knows the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      training data distribution, the training set size, the model architecture, hyperparameters and training
109   algorithm. Based on this information, the attacker can train so-called shadow models (Shokri et al.,
110   2017) which imitate the model under attack but for which the attacker knows the training dataset.
111
112
      LiRA exploits the observation that the loss function value used to train a model is often lower for
      examples that were part of the training set compared to those that were not. For a target sample
113
      (x, yx ), LiRA trains the shadow models: (i) with (x, yx ) as a part of the training set ((x, yx ) ∈ D)
114
      and (ii) without x in the training set ((x, yx ) ∈/ D). After training the shadow models, (x, yx ) is
115   passed through the shadow models, and based on the losses (or predictions) two Gaussian distribu-
116   tions are formed: one for the losses of (x, yx ) ∈ D shadow models, and one for the (x, yx ) ∈     / D.
117   Finally, the attacker computes the loss for the point x using the model under attack and determines
118   using a likelihood ratio test on the distributions built from the shadow models whether it is more
119   likely that (x, yx ) ∈ D or (x, yx ) ∈
                                           / D. We use an optimization by Carlini et al. (2022) for perform-
120   ing LiRA for multiple models and points without training a computationally infeasible number of
121   shadow models. It relies on sampling the shadow datasets in a way that each sample is in expectation
122   half of the time included in the training dataset of a shadow model and half of the time not. At attack
123
      time each model will be attacked once using all other models as shadow models.
124   Robust Membership Inference Attack (RMIA; Zarifzadeh et al., 2024) Recently Zarifzadeh et al.
125   (2024) proposed a new MIA algorithm called RMIA, which aims to improve performance when the
126   number of shadow models is limited. Similar to LiRA, RMIA is based on shadow model training
127   and computing the attack statistics based on a likelihood ratio. The main difference to LiRA is that
128   RMIA does not compute the likelihood ratio based on aggregated IN/OUT statistics, but instead
129
      compares the target data point against random samples (z, yz ) from the target data distribution.
      After computing the likelihood ratios over multiple (z, yz ) values, the MIA score is estimated as
130
      a proportion of the ratios exceeding a preset bound. This approach makes RMIA a more effective
131
      attack when the number of shadow models is low.
132
133   Measuring MIA vulnerability Using the chosen MIA score of our attack, we can build a binary
134   classifier to predict whether a sample belongs to the training data or not. The accuracy profile of
135
      such classifier can be used to measure the success of the MIA. More specifically, throughout the
      rest of the paper, we will use the true positive rate (TPR) at a specific false positive rate (FPR) as a
136
      measure for the vulnerability. Identifying even a small number of examples with high confidence is
137
      considered harmful (Carlini et al., 2022) and thus we focus on the regions of small FPR.

140   3     T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS
141
142   In this section, we seek to theoretically understand the impact of the dataset properties on the MIA
143
      vulnerability. It is known that different data points exhibit different levels of MIA vulnerability de-
      pending on the underlying distribution (e.g. Aerni et al., 2024; Leemann et al., 2024). Therefore, we
144
      start with analysing per-example vulnerabilities for LiRA and RMIA. In order to quantitatively relate
145
      dataset properties to these vulnerabilities, a simplified model is formulated. Within this model, we
146   prove a power-law between the per-example vulnerability and the number S of examples per class.
147   Finally, the per-example power-law is analytically extended to average-case MIA vulnerability, for
148   which we provide empirical evidence in Section 4.

      3.4   A SIMPLIFIED MODEL OF THE OPTIMAL MEMBERSHIP INFERENCE
271
272   Now we construct a simplified model of membership inference that streamlines the data generation
273   and shadow model training.
274
      We sample vectors on a high dimensional unit sphere and classify them based on inner product with
275
      estimated class mean. This model is easier to analyse theoretically than real-world deep learning
276   examples. We generate the data and form the classifiers (which are our target models) as follows:
277
278   1. For each class, we first sample a true class mean mc on a high dimensional unit sphere that is
279      orthogonal to all other true class means (∀i, j ∈ {1, . . . , C} : mi ⊥ mj ∨ i = j).
280   2. We sample 2S vectors xc for each class. We assume that they are Gaussian distributed around
281      the the true class mean xc ∼ N (mc , s2 I) where the s2 is the in-class variance.
282
      3. For each ”target model” we randomly choose a subset of size CS from all generated vectors and
283
         compute per-class means rc .
284
285   4. The computed mean is used to classify sample x by computing the inner product ⟨x, rc ⟩ as a
286      metric of similarity.
287
      The attacker has to infer which vectors have been used for training the classifier. Instead of utilising
288
      the logits (like in many image classification tasks), the attacker can use the inner products of a point
289   with the cluster means. Since the inner product score follows a normal distribution, LiRA with
290   infinitely many shadow models is the optimal attack by the Neyman-Pearson lemma (Neyman &
291   Pearson, 1933), which states that the likelihood ratio test is the most powerful test for a given FPR.
292
      This simplified model resembles a linear (Head) classifier often used in transfer learning when adapt-
293
      ing to a new dataset. We also focus on the linear (Head) classifier in our empirical evaluation in
294
      Section 4. In the linear classifier, we find a matrix W and biases b, to optimize the cross-entropy
295   between the labels and logits W v + b, where v denotes the feature space representation of the data.
296   In the simplified model, the rows of W are replaced by the cluster means and we do not include the
297   bias term in the classification.

      5              D ISCUSSION
517
      Under the GDPR and similar legal regimes, machine learning (ML) models trained on personal data
518
      that memorise the data are personal data and need to be carefully protected. Our work analyses in
519
      which cases trained models would most likely be personal data and in which cases they might not
520   be. This will help in evaluating the risk of different kinds of models, favouring less risky models
521   when possible and paying extra attention to more risky cases.
522
523
      As the best means of protecting privacy, differential privacy, reduces the utility of models, it is im-
      portant to understand when it is necessary. Aligning with the prior literature, our results highlight
524
      that models are the most vulnerable to MIA when the number of examples per class is low. A key
525
      result of the present paper is, however, the power-law relationship. This has a potentially useful
526   implication that a practitioner could reduce the MIA vulnerability and estimate how large a dataset
527   would be needed to mitigate the vulnerability in the non-DP transfer learning setting. The practi-
528   tioner could focus on the class with least examples, while taking into account that the number of
529   classes would not be completely independent of the vulnerability.
530
      One major reason for MIA vulnerability can be memorisation of the training data. Feldman & Zhang
531
      (2020) experimentally test memorisation in neural network training, and find that according to their
532   definition, a large fraction of the training data are memorised when training from scratch, while only
533   few are when fine-tuning. This is aligned with our results that indicate from scratch training to be
534   more vulnerable than fine-tuning.
535
536   Limitations Despite the theoretical analysis on the optimal score-based MIA, the vulnerability to
537   white-box attacks and future stronger attacks might behave differently. Also, our results assume
538   well-behaved underlying distributions. Formal bounds on MIA vulnerability would require some-
539   thing like DP. In addition, both our theoretical and empirical analysis focus on deep transfer learning
      using fine-tuning. Models trained from scratch are likely to be more vulnerable.

       In Section 3.4 we proved for the simplified model that for LiRA log(TPR − FPR) ≈ − 12 log S
1245   ignoring additive constants when FPR is fixed (Corollary 5). In Section 4.3 we observed that the

       E.1     PARAMETERIZATION
1299
1300   We utilise pre-trained feature extractors BiT-M-R50x1 (R-50) (Kolesnikov et al., 2020) with 23.5M
1301   parameters and Vision Transformer ViT-Base-16 (ViT-B) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) with 85.8M
1302   parameters, both pretrained on the ImageNet-21K dataset (Russakovsky et al., 2015). We download
1303   the feature extractor checkpoints from the respective repositories.
1304
       Following Tobaben et al. (2023) that show the favorable trade-off of parameter-efficient fine-tuning
1305
       between computational cost, utility and privacy even for small datasets, we only consider fine-tuning
1306   subsets of all feature extractor parameters. We consider the following configurations:
1307
1308   • Head: We train a linear layer on top of the pre-trained feature extractor.
1309   • FiLM: In addition to the linear layer from Head, we fine-tune parameter-efficient FiLM (Perez
1310
         et al., 2018) adapters scattered throughout the network. While a diverse set of adapters has been
         proposed, we utilise FiLM as it has been shown to be competitive in prior work (Shysheya et al.,
1311
         2023; Tobaben et al., 2023).

       E.2     H YPERPARAMETER TUNING
1326
       Our hyperparameter tuning is heavily inspired by the comprehensive few-shot experiments by To-
1327
       baben et al. (2023). We utilise their hyperparameter tuning protocol as it has been proven to yield
1328   SOTA results for (DP) few-shot models. Given the input D dataset we perform hyperparameter tun-
1329   ing by splitting the D into 70% train and 30% validation. We then perform the specified iterations of
1330   hyperparameter tuning using the tree-structured Parzen estimator (Bergstra et al., 2011) strategy as
1331   implemented in Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019) to derive a set of hyperparameters that yield the highest
1332   accuracy on the validation split. This set of hyperparameters is subsequently used to train all shadow
1333   models with the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015). Details on the set of hyperparameters that
1334   are tuned and their ranges can be found in Table A1.

1784   The prediction model in the main text (Equation (27)) avoids predicting TPR < FPR in the tail when
