009                                                A BSTRACT
010
               Training overparameterized neural networks often yields solutions with varying
011            generalization capabilities, even when achieving similar training losses. Recent
012            evidence indicates a strong correlation between the sharpness of a minimum and its
013            generalization error, leading to increased interest in optimization methods that ex-
014            plicitly seek flatter minima for improved generalization. Despite its contemporary
015            relevance to overparameterization, however, this sharpness-aware minimization
016            (SAM) strategy has not been studied much yet as to exactly how it is affected by
017            overparameterization. In this work, we analyze SAM under varying degrees of
018            overparameterization, presenting both empirical and theoretical findings that reveal
019
               its critical influence on SAM’s effectiveness. First, we conduct extensive numerical
               experiments across diverse domains, demonstrating that SAM consistently benefits
020
               from overparameterization. Next, we attribute this phenomenon to the interplay
021
               between the enlarged solution space and increased implicit bias resulting from
022            overparameterization. Furthermore, we show that this effect is particularly pro-
023            nounced in practical settings involving label noise and sparsity, and yet, sufficient
024            regularization is necessary. Last but not least, we provide other theoretical insights
025            into how overparameterization helps SAM achieve minima with more uniform
026            Hessian moments compared to SGD, and much faster convergence at a linear rate.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The remarkable success of deep learning can largely be attributed to the increasing size of neural net-
032   works. As these networks grow deeper and wider, they have demonstrated exceptional performance
033
      across a wide range of applications (Kaplan et al., 2020; Alayrac et al., 2022; Dehghani et al., 2023;
      Radford et al., 2023). This raises an intriguing question: why do such large—and thus potentially
034
      overparameterized—neural networks work so well? Although a complete understanding remains
035
      elusive, research suggests that overparameterization can positively impact various aspects of the
036   learning process, including even generalization (Neyshabur et al., 2017; Du & Lee, 2018). In particu-
037   lar, overparameterized neural networks often exhibit convexity-like behavior during optimization,
038   making all local minima globally optimal, and thereby, allowing global minima to be found with
039   local optimization methods such as gradient descent (Kawaguchi, 2016; Du et al., 2019).
040
      However, not all global minima are necessarily equivalent; converging to different minima can yield a
041
      large disparity of generalization capabilities, despite their same level of training loss reaching almost
042   zero (Keskar et al., 2017; Gunasekar et al., 2018). One plausible explanation for such an implicit
043   phenomenon is that generalization is somewhat negatively correlated with the sharpness of the loss
044   landscape, i.e., flat minima tend to generalize better than sharp ones (Chaudhari et al., 2017; Jiang
045   et al., 2020). This calls for new ways to guide the optimization process to converge to flat minima, and
046   various strategies have been suggested to this end (Izmailov et al., 2018; Foret et al., 2021; Orvieto
047   et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022). Indeed, it has been observed in many studies that reducing sharpness
048   can enhance generalization performance (Bahri et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022b; Qu et al., 2022).
049   Despite the sharpness minimization scheme being developed in the context of overparameterization,
050   the precise impact of overparameterization on this scheme has not been studied much in the literature.
051   In this work, we systematically investigate the effects of overparameterization on sharpness-aware
052   minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2021). Specifically, we conduct extensive experiments to precisely
053   measure the impact of overparameterization across a diverse set of tasks, ranging from standard
      tasks in computer vision and natural language processing, to molecular property prediction, and


                                                         1

      further, to video game in reinforcement learning. To gain further insight into the results, we perform
055   detailed investigations into the interactions between overparameterization and SAM through visual
056   inspection of the solution space on a simple regression setting as well as analyzing the influence of
057   overparameterization on the implicit bias of SAM. Furthermore, we study how overparameterization
058   influences SAM under various conditions, including label noise, sparsity, and regularization. Last
059   but not least, we explore other implications of overparameterization on SAM through theoretical
060   analyses, including the characteristics of the attained minima and the convergence rate.
061   Our key contributions and findings are summarized as follows.
062
063   •   Section 3. We perform extensive experiments across eight workloads of datasets and models at
064                  varying scales, spanning synthetic, vision, language, chemistry, and game domains.
065                  We observe that overparameterization consistently improves the generalization benefit
066                  of SAM1 . This phenomenon is general and previously unknown2 .
067   •   Section 4. We propose hypotheses to understand this general phenomenon, positing that two
068                  factors may be at play: (i) overparameterization first increases the number of simpler
069                  and flatter solution candidates, and (ii) it also increases the implicit bias of SAM.
070                  These are verified with standard experiments in both synthetic and realistic settings.
071   •   Section 5. We present the merits and caveats of overparameterization in employing SAM in
072                  practice: (i) the benefit of overparameterization for SAM is more pronounced under
073                  label noise and sparsity, while (ii) sufficient regularization is needed. This can serve
074                  as a useful guidance for practitioners.
075   •   Section 6. We develop theoretical analyses3 on linear stability and convergence: under overpa-
076                  rameterization, (i) linearly stable minima for SAM are flatter and have more uniformly
077                  distributed Hessian moments compared to SGD, and (ii) a stochastic SAM can con-
078                  verge at a linear rate. These are also numerically verified.
079   •   Overall.    We discover that overparameterization has critical influences on SAM. Both empirical
080                   performance and theoretical aspects of SAM all improve with overparameterization. In
081                   other words, SAM may not take its advantage over SGD without overparameterization.

178   4     U NDERSTANDING WHY SAM IMPROVES WITH OVERPARAMETERIZATION
179
180   Then why does overparameterization particularly favor SAM over non-sharpness-aware optimizers?
181   We address this question in this section to better understand the effect of overparameterization on SAM.
182   Precisely, we posit that it is potentially due to the complementarity between overparameterization
183   enlarging the solution space and the implicit bias of SAM driving toward flat minima; i.e., once there
184   are more diverse solutions available (including both sharp and flat minima) by overparameterization,
185   optimizers intrinsically biased toward flat solutions (such as SAM) will more likely find such solutions
186   than unbiased optimizers (such as GD). We support this reasonable hypothesis by demonstrating
187   the followings: (i) SAM finds simpler and flatter solutions than GD with the enlarged solution
188
      space (Section 4.1), and (ii) the implicit bias of SAM becomes stronger with overparameterization
      (Section 4.2); both of these take place only when the model is overparameterized.
189
190
191   4.1   E NLARGED SOLUTION SPACE ALLOWS SAM TO FIND SIMPLER AND FLATTER SOLUTIONS
192
      To corroborate our hypothesis, we start with a simple experiment where we train one-hidden-layer
193
      ReLU networks using full-batch SAM and GD following Andriushchenko & Flammarion (2022); we
194   use 5, 10, 100, and 1000 hidden neurons for underparameterized to highly overparameterized cases;
195   we run three random seeds and compare solutions obtained by SAM and GD in Figure 2.
196
197
      First, we find that the solutions found by SAM are not differentiated much from those of GD when
      the model has no more than 10 neurons. Looking closely into the case of 10 neurons, they all seem to
198
      be roughly 4 to 6 degrees of piecewise linear functions, i.e., the number of line segments for each
199
      solution is less than 10, which is the maximum possible joints that this model can have in theory. On
200   the other hand, in the case of 100 to 1000 neurons, one can easily see that the solutions found by
201   SAM are much simpler (and thus more likely to generalize) compared to those by GD.
202
203
      Next, we also track the optimization trajectories of both SAM and GD. The trajectories are plotted
      along PCA directions calculated from the converged minima following Li et al. (2018). The results
204
      are illustrated in Section 4.1. We find that both SAM and GD reach solutions in a similar basin when
205
      the model is under/moderately parameterized, whereas in the overparameterized case, they reach
206   different solutions, i.e., SAM reaches a flatter solution, even though they all start from the same initial
207   point.
208
209
      These results support the idea that SAM has some implicit bias that drives itself toward a certain type
      of solutions (e.g., simple and flat) as previously shown in prior work (Andriushchenko & Flammarion,
210
      2022; Compagnoni et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023). More importantly, however, these results newly
211
      reveal that overparameterization is a critical factor in facilitating this implicit behavior of SAM;
212   without it the space of potential solutions decreases, and SAM may not take effect.

      4.2          I MPLICIT BIAS OF SAM INCREASES WITH OVERPARAMETERIZATION
248
249   While overparameterization can secure favorable conditions for SAM, it is not to be confused with
250   guaranteeing the implicit bias of SAM taking effect. In fact, we can further relate the implicit bias of
251   SAM to the perturbation bound ρ to bridge this gap. Specifically, SAM can be proved to be an SDE
252   model of SGD on an implicitly regularized loss (Compagnoni et al., 2023):
253                                      f˜(x) := f (x) + ρE∥∇fγ (x)∥2                                     (4)
254
      where γ refers to some stochasticity. This indicates that SAM becomes more regularized (i.e., the
255
      implicit bias is amplified) when ρ increases.5
256
257   Our interest thus lies in seeing whether overparameterization has any effect on increasing ρ. Since
258   if that is the case, it indeed means that overparameterization puts more regularization on SAM. We
259   verify this by finding the empirically optimal perturbation bound ρ⋆ that yields the best generalization
260
      performance as we change the degree of overparameterization. Specifically, we take a standard deep
      learning task and perform an extensive grid search to find ρ⋆ . The result is displayed in Figure 4.
261
262   Indeed, it is observed that ρ⋆ tends to increase as the number of parameters increases; i.e., seeing
263   from left to right, ρ value that yields highest accuracy (marked as green star ⋆) tends to increase.
264   We confirm that this trend is consistently observed for various other workloads (See Figures 10
265   to 13 of Appendix E for more results). This result is certainly encouraging since it supports that the
266
      generalization benefit of SAM via implicit regularization can indeed increase by overparameterization.
      Additionally, we provide a conceptual account of why intuitively it makes sense to see increasing ρ
267
      with overparameterization in Appendix D.

286   5                     F URTHER MERITS AND CAVEATS OF OVERPARAMETERIZATION
287
288   In this section, we present further merits and some caveats of overparameterization. Specifically, we
289   show that the overparameterization benefit of SAM continues to exist and becomes more evident
290   under label noise or sparsity. We also discover that sufficient regularization is required to attain the
291   benefit. These results could serve as a guidance to employ SAM in practice.
292
293
      Overparameterization secures the robustness of SAM to label noise In practice, deep learning
294
      models are often trained on noisy data (Song et al., 2022). To examine whether the overparameteriza-
295   tion benefit for SAM continues to exist in this scenario, we introduce some label noise to training
296   data (Angluin & Laird, 1988; Natarajan et al., 2013) and see how SAM responds. The results are
297   reported in Figure 5a. Overall, we find SAM benefits from overparameterization significantly more
298   than SGD in the presence of label noise. Precisely, the accuracy improvement made by SAM keeps
299   on increasing as the model has more parameters, whereas the improvement over SGD is marginal for
300   less parameterized models. Notably, this trend is more pronounced with a higher noise level; e.g.,
301   it rises from 5% to nearly 50% at the highest noise rate. Notably, it is previously known that SAM
302   is robust to label noise compared to SGD (Foret et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2024), and yet, this result
303
      newly reveals that overparameterization plays a profound role in securing the robustness of SAM.
304
305   SAM benefits from sparse overparameterization. There has been a recent interest in employing
306   sparsity to train large models to alleviate the computation and memory costs (Hoefler et al., 2021;
307   Mishra et al., 2021). To test the effect of overparameterization on SAM under this setting, we
308   introduce a varying degree of sparsity to an overparameterized model at initialization (Lee et al.,
309   2019) such that the number of parameters matches the original dense model. The results are reported
310   in Figure 5b. We observe that the generalization improvement tends to increase as the model becomes
311
      more sparsely overparameterized; more precisely, the average accuracy improvement increases from
      0.4% in the small dense model to around 0.8% in the large sparse model. This result suggests that
312
      one can consider taking sparsification more actively when employing SAM.
313
314
315   Sufficient regularization is needed to secure the benefit of overparameterization. We also
316   investigate whether the overparameterization benefit for SAM continues to exist when models are
317   prone to overfitting due to insufficient regularization (Ying, 2019). Specifically, we evaluate three
318   cases: (a) without weight decay, (b) without early stopping, and (c) without sufficient inductive bias.6
319
      The results are reported in Figures 5c to 5e. We observe that the generalization improvement does
      not increase by simply adding more parameters. The results indicate that some level of regularization
320
      is required in practice to attain the overparameterization benefit for SAM.

341     Thus far, we focused on empirically exploring how increasing number of parameters influences SAM,

458   7    C ONCLUSION
459
460   In this work, we have disclosed the critical influence of overparameterization on SAM from empirical
461
      and theoretical perspectives. We started with an extensive evaluation to display a highly consistent
      trend that the generalization benefit of SAM increases with overparameterization, without which
462
      SAM may not take effect (Section 3). This led us to come up with a reasonable hypothesis to
463
      explain the benefit in terms of increased solution space and implicit bias (Section 4). In addition,
464   we presented further merits and caveats of overparameterization in practice (Section 5). Finally, we
465   developed theoretical advantages of overparameterization for SAM on linear stability, convergence,
466   and generalization (Section 6). We believe these findings can bridge between overparameterization
467   and SAM, which has been rather unattended in the literature as of yet. Nevertheless, we discuss
468   limitations, ideas for potential future work as well as practical implications of our results below.
469
470   Theoretical account of Section 3 The consistent trend observed in Section 3 certainly hints at
471   the presence of a fundamental process underneath, and yet, our study does not offer a precise
472   theory to support this phenomenon. This is largely because modeling the generalization of SAM
473   under varying degrees of overparameterization challenges the boundaries of existing theoretical
474
      frameworks currently available in the literature. Nevertheless, drawing upon recent advancements in
      understanding overparameterization and generalization, we have developed plausible hypotheses to
475
      directly address this phenomenon (Section 4). We also employed rigorous theoretical frameworks to
476
      examine the effects of overparameterization on various other aspects of SAM, reinforcing the general
477   trend of overparameterization benefits (Section 6). We believe these efforts offer valuable insights
478   and preliminary foundations that could be instrumental in achieving a comprehensive theoretical
479   account of Section 3 in the future.
480
481   Other sharpness minimization schemes Our theoretical results in Section 6 are based on an
482   unnormalized version of SAM. This is largely driven by two reasons: (i) it appears to render minimal
483   practical difference from the original SAM, and more crucially, (ii) it simplifies analyses as widely
484   adopted in initial studies (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022; Compagnoni et al., 2023). However,
485   more recently, works such as Dai et al. (2023); Si & Yun (2023) have highlighted the theoretical
      significance of the normalization step. We plan to extend our analysis to better reflect the effect of


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      normalization in future work. Additionally, given that different sharpness minimization schemes can
487   make a difference in the found minima and resulting performance (Kaddour et al., 2022; Dauphin
488   et al., 2024), extension of our analyses to other non-SAM sharpness minimization schemes (Izmailov
489   et al., 2018; Orvieto et al., 2022) and studying how they compare to SAM under overparameterization
490   would be a promising avenue for future work. Nonetheless, we consider these results an initial
491   exploration of the impact of overparameterization on SAM, setting the stage for future research.
492
493   More ablation study In addition to our explanation of how overparameterization improves SAM
494   in Section 4, we have conducted an additional ablation study to investigate the influence of other
495   factors on the increased benefit of SAM in Appendix G. In Appendix G.1, we present preliminary
496   investigations on the effect of increasing the depth instead of the width, where we find that the
497   benefit of overparameterization can differ from architectures. In Appendix G.2, we test whether
498   stronger weight decay can remove the gap between SGD and SAM, which shows that even after
499
      given much larger values of weight decay, SGD isn’t able to outperform SAM on any model size.
      In Appendix G.3, inspired by recent studies suggesting that overparameterized models can behave
500
      like linearized models (Jacot et al., 2018) while such implicit linearization phenomenon can coincide
501
      independently of overparameterization (Chizat et al., 2019), we have tested if the increased benefit
502   of SAM is due to linearization. As a result, we have observed that SAM underperforms SGD in the
503   linearized regimes by more than 10%. This indicates that linearization is not the main factor for the
504   increased benefit of SAM and again verifies that overparameterization itself is likely to be the main
505   factor of the benefit.
506
507   Potential to modern deep learning Our key observations in Section 3 indicate a great potential
508   to use SAM in the modern landscape of large-scale training (Kaplan et al., 2020; Belkin, 2021).
509   Also, our results in Section 5 further highlight its potential in the current trend where foundation
510   models are often trained with noisy data (Radford et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022) or to employ
511   sparsity (Frantar et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). In this regard, we can possibly anticipate that the
512   overparameterization benefit might hold even when training billion-scale foundation models (Zhang
513
      et al., 2022; Dehghani et al., 2023), which we leave to explore as future work. It would also be
      interesting to study how popular settings for training foundation models other than label noise or
514
      sparsity affect the benefit, such as quantization (Gholami et al., 2022), differential privacy (Yu et al.,
515
      2022), or human alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022).

      Synthetic Regression / 2-layer MLP We follow the student-teacher setting from Advani et al.

      student is a 2-layer ReLU network with a different number of neurons. Each element for the input
847   x ∈ R100 is sampled from a standard normal distribution while the target y ∈ R is calculated as the
848   output of the teacher network added by Gaussian noise sampled from a standard normal distribution.
849   The models are trained on 20400 training data, which is roughly the same as the number of parameters
850   in the teacher model, and tested on the 5100 data, which is a quarter of the number of the training
851   data.

855   while preserving the relative proportions of the number of neurons in each layer as 3 : 1.

      2009). We choose the hyperparameters as similar to Andriushchenko & Flammarion (2022). The

900   A.3   E XPERIMENTS FOR S ECTION 6
901
902   Linear stability For the experiments of Figures 6a and 6b, we follow the setting in Wu et al. (2018).
903   Specifically, we set up 3-layer MLP having [3000, 1000] hidden neurons with squared loss, so that
904   the local quadratic approximation becomes precise, and train the networks on MNIST. We use 1000
905   random samples to calculate the non-uniformity, and all models are trained to reach near zero loss.
906   The networks are trained with a constant learning rate of 0.1 without weight decay or momentum.
907
908   Convergence – Matrix Factorization For the matrix factorization experiment in Figure 6c, we
909   solve the following non-convex regression problem: minW1 ,W2 Ex∼N (0,I) ∥W2 W1 x − Ax∥2 where
910   the objective function is smooth and satisfies the PL-condition (Loizou et al., 2021). We choose
911   A ∈ R10×6 and generate 1000 training samples, which are used for training a rank k linear network
912   with two matrix factors W1 ∈ Rk×6 and W2 ∈ R10×k . Here, interpolation is satisfied when rank
913   k = 10. We train two linear networks with k ∈ {4, 10} for 100 epochs with a constant learning rate
914
      of 0.0005 and compare the convergence speed.

       D       OVERPARAMETERIZATION AND ρ
1005
1006
1007   We can develop a simple account of why ρ may need to increase with overparameterization. Firstly,
1008
       the scale of perturbation ϵ controlled by ρ needs to increase relatively proportionally with respect
       to the increase in model dimensionality in order to preserve a similar level of perturbation effect;
1009
       an extreme case is when the model grows infinitely large, ρ should grow together, otherwise no
1010
       perturbation can be made, i.e., ∥ϵ∥ → ∞ as d → ∞.
1011
1012   A non-asymptotic
                          interpretation
                                         can also be developed. Precisely, the Lipschitz bound on gradients,
                           ∇f (x)                             ∇f (x)
1013   i.e., ∇f x + ρ ∥∇f (x)∥2 − ∇f (x) ≤ β x + ρ ∥∇f           (x)∥2 − x     = βρ, indicates that the SAM
                                               2                             2
1014   gradient becomes more similar to the original gradient as the model gets smoother (i.e., smaller
1015   smoothness constant β) with increasing size, requiring larger perturbation bound to achieve similar
1016   levels of perturbation effect. These hold under the assumption that overparameterization makes the
1017   model smoother, which we empirically confirm in Figure 22a.
