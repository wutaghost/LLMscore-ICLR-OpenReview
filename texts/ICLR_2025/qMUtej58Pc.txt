009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            During brain development, an excess number of synapses are initially created,
012            which are progressively eliminated through a process known as synaptic pruning.
013            This procedure is activity-dependent, shaped by the brain’s experiences. While
014            creating an overabundance of synaptic connections only to later remove many
015            might appear inefficient, research suggests that networks formed by this procedure
016
               demonstrate significant efficiency and robustness. Inspired by this biological
               process, we propose a neural network architecture utilizing long connections instead
017
               of traditional short residual connections. When long connection neural networks
018
               (LCNs) are trained with gradient descent, information is naturally "pushed" down
019            to the first few layers, leading to a sparse network. Even more surprising is that this
020            simple architectural modification leads to networks that exhibit behaviors similar
021            to biological brain networks, namely: early overconnectivity to later sparsity,
022            enhanced robustness to noise, efficiency in low-data settings and longer training
023            times. Specifically, starting with a traditional neural network architecture with
024            initial depth d and k connections, long connections are added from all layers to the
025            last layer and summed up. During LCN training, 30-80% of the top layers become
026            effective identity mappings as all relevant information is concentrated in the bottom
027
               layers. Pruning the top layers results in a refined network with a reduced depth
               d′ and final connections k ′ , achieving significant efficiencies without any loss in
028
               performance compared to residual baselines. We apply this architecture to various
029
               classification tasks and show that, in all experiments, the network converges to
030            utilizing only a subset of the initially defined pre-training connections, and the
031            amount of compression is dependent on the task complexity.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Deep learning has achieved significant breakthroughs across diverse tasks and domains (Krizhevsky
037   et al., 2012; LeCun et al., 2015; Brown et al., 2020); however, it still lacks the flexibility, robustness,
038   and efficiency of biological networks. Modern models rely on deep architectures with billions of
039
      parameters, leading to high computational, storage, and energy costs. In contrast, biological brains
      are remarkably efficient, constrained by physical limitations and refined through evolution to develop
040
      low-power, fast-acting, effective networks (Bassett & Bullmore, 2006). These biological circuits
041
      achieve robust performance and rapid learning while maintaining low cost and power consumption.
042   One key efficiency mechanism in the brain is synaptic pruning. During early development, an excess
043   of synapses is formed and progressively eliminated through activity-dependent pruning (Sakai, 2020).
044   Early studies (Peter R., 1979) measured synaptic density across different ages and found that it peaks
045   around 1–2 years of age, followed by a decline to approximately 50% by adulthood. Although creating
046   an overabundance of connections only to remove many may seem inefficient, research indicates that
047   this approach leads to networks exhibiting significant efficiency and robustness (Navlakha et al.,
048   2015). Synaptic pruning is a gradual process that unfolds over the years and allows the brain to
049   explore a large parameter space while learning and to identify key circuits by eliminating redundant
050
      connections. The result is a low-cost, sparse and efficient network that retains only the necessary
      pathways for information processing and routing, achieving strong performance and robustness.
051
052   Simply put, synaptic pruning is the evolutionary mechanism that dynamically optimizes the number
053   of neural connections, effectively addressing the question: “How can we determine the essential
      parameters for efficient brain function during the learning process?” Contemporary deep learning

077
      top of the training process. L1 and L2 regularization (Ng, 2004) constrain network parameters to

080   trained. Dynamic inference (Han et al., 2021) adjusts which parts of the network are used based on
081   the input. However, these methods do not explicitly detect redundancy but instead bypass it using
082   heuristic criteria layered on top of the training process.
083
      In this work, we propose an architectural modification that replaces residual (short) connections
084
      with long connections, forming Long Connection Networks (LCNs). When long connections neural
085
      networks (LCNs) are trained with gradient descent, information is naturally "pushed" down to the
086   first few layers, leading to a sparse network. This simple architectural modification leads to networks
087   that exhibit behaviors similar to biological brain networks, namely: early overconnectivity to later
088   sparsity, enhanced robustness to noise, efficiency in low-data settings and longer training times.
089   Specifically, starting from an overparameterized network of depth L and k connections, LCNs refine
090   it to depth L′ and connections k ′ , significantly accelerating inference and reducing memory usage
091   without sacrificing performance. Our analysis shows that architectures with residual connections (He
092   et al., 2016) or deep supervision (Lee et al., 2015) do not exhibit this behavior. We implement LCNs
093   in fully connected and transformer-based architectures and find experimentally that they achieve
094
      similar or superior performance compared to residual baselines, while 30-80% of the top layers
      become effective identity mappings, as all relevant information is concentrated in the bottom layers.
095
      This approach is practical with current hardware and does not require specialized software. Finally,
096
      we highlight that this architecture can be used complementary with pruning or dynamic inference
097   techniques.

113   In LCNs the output of each layer is directly connected to the output of the network, and thus is
114

      number of possible shortucts is equal to the number of layers, L. We find this simplification maintains

116
      determine the effective depth of the network, and thus perform layer pruning3 . Compared to the
117   similar idea of deep supervision, which introduces a separate loss function and classification head
118   for each layer, LCNs are trained with a single loss function, aleviating the need for balancing
119   the contribution of multiple objectives through hyper-parameter tuning, and achieving superior
120   performance (see Appendix C). We also note that LCNs differ structurally from other models
121   employing long connections, such as DenseNets Huang et al. (2017) and DenseFormer Pagliardini
122   et al. (2024), which are residual networks variants. These two models connect each layer to all
123   preceding layers whereas LCNs connect each layer only to the output, leading to a distinct structural
124   design. 4 .

246

          connections in the Transformer architecture for binary sentiment classification using the Amazon

420     From our experiments, we conclude that LCNs are able to "push" information down to the early neural
421     layers without performance degradation, resulting in a sparse high-performing network, effectively

443   7      BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
444
445   Residual Connections: Training deep neural networks with gradient descent becomes increasingly difficult as
446   network depth increases. He et al. (2016) found that deeper convolutional neural networks (CNNs) not only
447
      suffer from declining generalization performance, often due to overfitting, but also experience a drop in training
      performance. This suggests that the challenge goes beyond overfitting and points to the inherent difficulty
448   in optimizing deeper networks. To address this, they introduced residual connections (or identity mappings),
449   proposing that learning residual functions relative to identity mappings simplifies optimization. These skip
450   connections improve the training process and often enhance performance (Balduzzi et al. (2017), Orhan &
451   Pitkow (2017), Zaeemzadeh et al. (2020), Li et al. (2018)). Veit et al. (2016) further argued that a residual
452
      network with n layers can be viewed as a collection of 2n paths of varying lengths. At each layer, the signal
      either skips the layer or passes through it, creating 2n possible paths. Despite sharing weights, these paths
453   function as an ensemble of networks, as confirmed by experiments. In contrast, a traditional deep feedforward
454   network has only one path, so removing any random layer significantly degrades performance. Additionally, the
455   authors showed that these paths are typically shallow, with backward gradients often vanishing after passing
456   through only a small fraction of the total layers.
457   Deep Supervision: The authors of [Lee et al. (2015)] proposed adding complementary objectives to all
458   intermediate layers, not just the final one. They argue that these intermediate objectives encourage hidden layers
459   to learn more discriminative representations, improving the overall classification task. The intuition is that more
460
      discriminative features lead to a better-trained classifier. In their approach, each intermediate objective i is a loss
      function that captures the classification error of an SVM trained on the output features of layer i. The overall
461   loss is the sum of the intermediate and final objectives, and results show that this improves final classification
462   performance.
463
      Structure Learning (Depth Optimization): Depth optimization focuses on learning the optimal network depth
464   while training. Alturki et al. (2023) introduced "weight relevance of a layer," a metric that measures each layer’s
465   importance in the classification task. Using this metric, they propose dynamically adjusting the network’s depth
466   by removing irrelevant layers. Similarly, Cortes et al. (2017) presents an algorithm for learning both width and
467   depth during training. Starting with a simple linear model, layers are added competitively based on a trade-off
468
      between complexity and performance, resulting in a comprehensive structural learning algorithm, backed by
      strong theoretical analysis.
469
470   Long Connections in the Literature: Brain networks combine short and long connections [Bassett & Bullmore
471
      (2006)], where short connections form dense sub-network hubs, and long connections sparsely link these hubs.
      Typically, short connections are more numerous and have stronger synaptic weights [Muldoon et al. (2016)]. In
472   [Betzel & Bassett (2018)], the authors show that short connections more efficiently route information across
473   brain areas and sub-networks. Removing short connections has a larger impact on network properties like
474   average path length. In contrast, long connections are key for functional diversity, offering unique inputs and
475   novel targets for outputs across sub-networks. DenseNet [Huang et al. (2017)] incorporates a form of long
476
      connections within CNN residual blocks, connecting every two layers within a block. This design enables
      feature reuse and efficient signal propagation, mitigating vanishing gradients while reducing parameters and
477   computation without sacrificing performance. More recently, DenseNet’s concept was applied to transformers in
478   DenseFormer [Pagliardini et al. (2024)], where each layer receives a weighted sum of outputs from all preceding
479   layers. This approach improves training efficiency, speeds up inference, and reduces memory requirements. The
480   learned weights show strong reuse of distant layers’ outputs, ensuring efficient information flow in the network.
481
482   8      D ISCUSSION
483
484   In this work, we introduced Long Connection Networks (LCNs), where, starting with a traditional neural network
485   architecture, long connections are added from all layers to the last layer and summed up. During training with
      gradient descent, these networks mimic synaptic pruning (albeit in a layer-wise fashion) by beginning with


                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      an overconnected network and gradually identifying the key connections needed for the task. LCNs require
487   longer training but ultimately produce more robust, efficient, and sparse networks, similar to the development of
488   biological neural networks. Additionally, their training dynamics align with those of layer-wise training, further
489   resembling processes observed in cognitive neural development in the prefrontal cortex [DeFelipe (2011)]. By
490   training fully connected and transformer-based architectures with long connections, information is naturally
      "pushed" down and concentrated in the bottom layers, rendering the top layers into effective identity mappings,
491
      leading to significant inference time efficiencies. We also found that the amount of compression that LCNs
492   achieve is dependent on the number of classes, a simple proxy for task compexity in classification settings.
493
      In our experiments, we observed a trade-off between training and inference cost when choosing between short
494
      residual connections and long connections. It is possible that LCN’s inherent sparsity and longer training time is
495   what makes them more robust to noise and more efficient in low-data settings. Preliminary experiments further
496   strenghten this belief: as the representations learned by earlier layers become more discriminative focusing on
497   the task at hand, LCNs can still effectivelly transfer their knowledge to downstream tasks (Appendix D).
498   Future research could expand LCNs to self-supervised and multi-task settings, leveraging the pre-training
499   and fine-tuning paradigm. LCNs also hold promise for generative tasks, reducing inference costs and energy
500   consumption. Additionally, developing inference-time algorithms that dynamically adjust the number of layers
501   per sample for optimal performance and efficiency is an intriguing direction for future work. Last but not least,
      LCNs are only one possible long-connection architecture out of the many that are worth investigating further.
502
503
504   9    L IMITATIONS
505
506   Due to resource constraints, our proposed architecture was evaluated solely on classification tasks; however,
507   it demonstrated robust and promising performance across various modalities, datasets, and state-of-the-art
508   architectures within this scope. To fully assess its potential and limitations, further testing on a broader range of
      tasks is essential. Additionally, applying our method in self-supervised and multi-task learning settings, such
509
      as training large-scale language models or multimodal models, represents a significant and exciting avenue for
510   future research.
511
      Another limitation is the increased training time observed with LCNs compared to traditional architectures
512
      with residual connections. While we partially addressed this issue by employing parameterizations similar to
513   DiracNets, a more comprehensive solution to reduce training time remains an open question. Of course this
514   could be both a blessing and a curse, as longer training times might lead to learning better representations. In
515   any case, further research into training schedules and initialization schemes is needed to resolve this trade-off.

      10     B ROADER I MPACT
518
519   Our work contributes to the development of more efficient and robust neural network architectures by drawing
520   inspiration from biological processes. By enabling networks to identify and prune redundant layers, we aim to
521   reduce computational, memory, and energy requirements during inference, which will have a significant impact
522   with broader adoption of AI technology. Conceptually, this line of research could lead to network architectures
      with inherent System 1 and System 2 capabilities (Kahneman, 2011), where networks adaptively use fewer
523
      layers—analogous to fast thinking—for easier tasks, and engage more layers—resembling slow thinking—for
524   more complex tasks.
525
      However, as with any advancement in AI, there is a potential for misuse. More efficient models could be
526
      leveraged to deploy AI systems more broadly, including in areas with insufficient oversight or in applications
527   that may infringe on privacy or other ethical considerations.

898   Observations We see that the gradient of wi can be decomposed in two terms, which we coin as "before" and
      "after" path. These two paths play a major role in the gradient of each weight as well as the general training
899
      dynamics of the network. LCNs have "before" path equivalent to FFNs but different "after" path due to the long
900   connections. We will argue that this is the reason why LCNs behave in the way we saw in the experiments.
901
      If we consider the extreme case where all weights are initialized to zero, we see that in the case of LCN in
902
      the first training batch only the gradient of the first layer’s weight will be non-zero. Moreover, for a weight i
903   to have a non-zero gradient, all the previous weights need first to be trained (non-zero gradient). Also, in the
904   forward pass, only the trained subnetworks will have a contribution to the output addition, effectively solving
905   the task with only those. So there is a gradual hierarchical training from early layers to later ones. If the first k
906   layers achieve low task error, effectively minimizing the loss, then the gradient flow to all layers will be small
      effectively rendering the last d − k layers “under-trained”.10
907
908   Although initializing all weights to zero is unrealistic, most popular initialization methods today initialize
909   weights with small norms, close to zero. As a result, the earlier analysis remains applicable: early LCN layers
      tend to have significantly larger gradients initially due to the small weight norms, causing them to train faster
910
      and contribute more to solving the task early on. Gradually, as the early layers are trained, the deeper layers
911   begin to train as well, depending on the complexity of the task. For instance, if the early layers successfully
912   minimize the loss function and solve the task, the gradients for the deeper layers will remain small. On the
913   contrary, in FFNs all weights have gradients with similar magnitudes initially (since gradient of weight i contains
914   the product of all other weights and the input) and so they are all trained in a similar rate. Also notice that as the
      depth increases these products grow smaller and smaller, making the gradients also small and the training slow
915
      (vanishing gradients).

981    H        C ONNECTION TO G REEDY L AYER - WISE T RAINING
982
983    Greedy layer-wise training [Hinton et al. (2006), Bengio et al. (2006)] was a popular method for training
984    deep neural networks in a sequential manner, inspired by cognitive neural modeling. Based on the conducted
       mathematical analysis on LCN’s dynamics, we argue that LCNs share similarities with the method and can be
985
       viewed as a "one-shot" version of it. In layer-wise training, the process is sequential: starting from the first
986    layer, each layer is trained individually while the other layers remain frozen, with training progressing from
987    first to last layer. Our analysis of LCN’s dynamics shows that a similar sequential layer-wise training process
988    occurs in LCNs, where the early layers train first, followed by the deeper layers. The key difference is that
989    in LCNs, this layer-wise training happens naturally, without the need to freeze layer gradients or introduce
       hyperparameters like the number of epochs for each layer’s training. Interestingly, this behavior is inherently
990
       driven by the architecture of LCNs (the structure imposed by the long connections), rather than being externally
991    imposed.
