011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Personalized medicine is a groundbreaking healthcare framework for the 21st cen-
014            tury, tailoring medical treatments to individuals based on unique clinical charac-
015
               teristics, including diverse medical imaging modalities. Given the significant dif-
               ferences among these modalities due to distinct underlying imaging principles,
016
               generalization in multi-modal medical image tasks becomes substantially chal-
017
               lenging. Previous methods addressing multi-modal generalization rarely consider
018            personalization, primarily focusing on common anatomical information. This pa-
019            per aims to bridge multi-modal generalization with the concept of personalized
020            medicine. Specifically, we propose a novel approach to derive a tractable form of
021            the underlying personalized invariant representation Xh by leveraging individual-
022            level constraints and a learnable biological prior. We demonstrate the feasibility
023            and benefits of learning a personalized Xh , showing that this representation is
024            highly generalizable and transferable across various multi-modal medical tasks.
025            Our method is rigorously validated on medical imaging modalities emphasizing
026
               both physical structure and functional information, encompassing a range of tasks
               that require generalization. Extensive experimental results consistently show that
027
               our approach significantly improves performance across diverse scenarios, con-
028
               firming its effectiveness.

      gamma rays to visualize and measure metabolic processes. The differing imaging principles result
055   in substantial modality gaps, presenting a critical challenge for model generalization. For clarity,
056   we categorize these modalities for generalization tasks into two types: homogeneous generalization,
057   which pertains to generalizing within structural or functional modalities (e.g., T1, T2, T1ce, and
058   Flair in MRI, as shown in Fig. 1); and heterogeneous generalization, which involves generalizing
059   across both structural and functional modalities, such as CT and PET.
060
      As each person is fundamentally different from the average of the population (Whitcomb, 2012),
061   the concept of multi-modality generalization needs comprehensive discussion within the scope of
062   personalization, an area scarcely addressed in previous research. An ideally well-generalized per-
063   sonalized medical model across modalities should (1) provide additional insights derived from all
064   modalities when only a subset is accessible and (2) seamlessly transfer across domains while main-
065   taining the capacity to achieve (1). While pre-training can significantly enhance downstream gen-
066   eralization, some recent approaches concentrate on learning common physical anatomy invariance
067   at the class level (Jiang et al., 2023), which may overlook the individual variations. Another line
068   of research (Tang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023) primarily addresses the transfer-
069
      ability of single-modal tasks and may not be suitable for multi-modal scenarios. In addition, most
      research on homogeneous generalization for medical tasks focuses on structural sequences of MRI
070
      or CT, employing strategies such as cross-modality transfer (Liu et al., 2023b; Kim & Park, 2024;
071
      Zhan et al., 2024) or targeting challenges like missing modality segmentation (Liu et al., 2021;
072   Chen et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023a;b; Zhan et al., 2024). Heterogeneous generalization presents
073   a greater challenge due to the disparities between structural and functional information. Despite
074   its significance, very few efforts (Pan et al., 2023) have addressed heterogeneous generalization,
075   mainly focusing on one-directional modality transfer (e.g., PET to CT or MRI), and rarely exploring
076   the model’s generalizability and transferability for other tasks in this context.
077
      To address multi-modality generalization issue for personalization under both homogeneous and het-
078   erogeneous settings, we formally introduce the concept of the personalized invariant representation
079   for multi-modal generalization, denoted as Xh , and its constraints as outlined in Hypothesis 3.1.
080   Furthermore, personalized invariant Xh , which learns aggregated biological information from all
081   possible modalities specific to the individual, is likely to enhance performance across various medi-
082   cal tasks for that person. Building on this hypothesis, this paper proposes a general approach aimed
083   at enhancing the generalization of various medical imaging tasks through personalization. Specifi-
084   cally, our method constructs an approximation of Xh using the learnable biological prior knowledge
085   O, via decomposition, invariance, and equivariance constraints during pre-training (refer to Sec-
086
      tion 3.2). The learned approximation of Xh can then be utilized to enhance performance in down-
      stream generalization tasks, irrespective of whether a domain gap exists between the pre-training
087
      data and downstream data.
088
089   Importantly, this paper demonstrates that obtaining a personalized invariant representation, Xh , is
090   feasible through our approach, and such invariance leads to generalization improvements across var-
091   ious medical tasks. To validate our methodology, we conduct experiments on modality transfer and
092
      missing modality segmentation tasks, addressing not only the homogeneous generalization of MRI
      but also the rarely explored heterogeneous generalization, such as generalization between PET and
093
      CT. Our findings reveal that our approach successfully captures comprehensive personalized infor-
094
      mation even when only partial modalities are available for a given individual (see Fig. 3). Moreover,
095   extensive experiments on both homogeneous (Section 4) and heterogeneous (Section 6) general-
096   ization demonstrate that our approach can be adapted for downstream tasks and surpasses current
097   state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods in multiple tasks, validating its superiority. We will publicly release
098   our code, checkpoints and data upon acceptance.

157   Preliminaries. In this paper, we denote the encoder as E and its corresponding decoder as D.
158
      For an individual human being h ∈ H, the corresponding medical images are represented as
159   Xh = Xhi , Xhj , . . . , Xhk , where i, j, . . . , k ∈ M, and M represents the set of all possible modality
160   combinations. We denote the intermediate features produced by E(Xh ) and E(Xhi ) as xh and xih ,
161   respectively. The final layer features from the encoder are represented as zh and zhi . The learned
      approximation of Xh is denoted as Xh ′ . Additionally, we define the geometric warping function

      ϕi ∈ Φ, where ϕi (Xhi ) ∈ X , and Φ denotes the set of all possible geometric warping functions.
163   Finally, I(·; ·) and P (·) represent mutual information and probability distribution, respectively.
164
165   Before addressing the problem for both homogeneous and heterogeneous generalization, we intro-
166
      duce the Xh Hypothesis for medical imaging:
167   Hypothesis 3.1 ( Xh Hypothesis). Consider the set M of all possible modality combinations and the
168   set Φ of all possible geometric transformations (e.g., SO(3)) transformations, for example, rotations
169   corresponding to different poses of the person. There exists a personalized invariant representation
170
      Xh for an individual from the population h ∈ H, which can be decomposed into modality-specific
      images Xhi given a modality combination i ∈ M as a condition:
171
172                             Xhi = Xh |i; i ∈ M, h ∈ H,      s.t.,Xh ⊥⊥ M, Φ.                         (1)
173
174   Despite potential differences in modalities and individual variations, clinical diagnoses focus on the
175   biological conditions of a certain patient, which remain mostly invariant during a single hospital
176   visit. Thus, the Xh Hypothesis holds in most cases. Our method aims to obtain an accurate approx-
177   imation of Xh . The overall learning framework for Xh is illustrated on the left-hand side of Fig. 1.
178
      Data from each modality are encoded by E, and the encoded features are used to retrieve knowl-
      edge from the learnable biological prior O. The features and retrieved knowledge are then fused.
179
      By applying constraints of decomposition, equivariance, and invariance on the fused features, we
180
      approximate Xh effectively.
181
182   As illustrated in Fig. 2 right-hand side top, previous approaches (Liu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023;
183   Qiu et al., 2023a;b) learn invariant representations Zh across modalities through the encoder E for
184
      generalization: E(Xhm ) → Zh , Zh ⊥    ⊥ M, m ∈ M, h ∈ H during pre-training or training. Is Zh
      a good approximation of Xh , and does it benefit the generalization of different downstream tasks?
185
      The answer might be negative because such an approach may erase modal-specific information in
186
      Z, making it impossible to be decomposed back into different modalities as shown in Eq. (1). More-
187   over, while current studies Havaei et al. (2016); Varsavsky et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2022); Ding
188   et al. (2021) also disentangle modality-dependent features alongside the invariant representation Z to
189   enhance transferability, this strategy may compromise the generalization ability of Z. The reason is
190   that the transferred targets become constrained by the learned modal-dependent features, potentially
191   limiting their broader applicability.
192
193   3.1   U SING GLOBAL PRIOR O FOR BETTER Xh
194
195   To learn a better approximation of Xh , we leverage a global biological prior, denoted as O. If O can
196   be learned, representations from any modality can complete themselves by retrieving the missing
197
      knowledge from O, forming a better approximation of Xh . Empirically, we initialize a learnable
      tensor as O. As shown in Fig. 1, the representation zhi retrieves its missing knowledge from O

      via attention: zhi := attn(query : zhi , key : O, value : O). The original representation and the
                                                                                     ′

201   well-trained under the constraints of equivariance, invariance, and decomposition, the fused feature
202
      Xih becomes X′h , a good approximation of Xh . The details of these constraints are discussed in
      Section 3.2.

      3.3   A PPLYING Xh FOR DIFFERENT MODALITIES AND TASKS
294   After pre-training with the loss function Lpre , the model is then utilized for downstream tasks such
295   as segmentation or generation. We denote the commonly used loss functions for these tasks, such as
296   dice loss, cross-entropy loss, or mean squared error (MSE) loss, as Lori , where paired data and labels
297   (X, Y ) ∈ (X , Y) are provided. In addition to Lori , we incorporate the invariance loss, denoted as
298   Linv, as part of the fine-tuning process for downstream tasks:
299
                                                        Ldown = Lori + Linv .                                                       (8)
300
301
      Empirically, we adopt the SwinUNETR architecture (Hatamizadeh et al., 2022a) as the backbone
302
      of the encoder E, and implement the proposed components. The model is trained with Lpre
303
      during the pre-training phase, users have the option to either use the standard SwinUNETR by
304   loading only our pre-trained encoder weights or to employ our proposed model structure with
305   all pre-trained weights for downstream tasks. Notably, all modalities for a given individual,
306   Xh = Xhi , Xhj , ..., Xhk , i, j, ..., k ∈ M, share the same encoder, with the encoder’s channel size
307   set to match the number of modality types. The input volume size for all experiments is fixed at
308   96 × 96 × 96. Further empirical details on how Xh is leveraged for homogeneous and heterogeneous
309   generalization are provided in Sections 4 and 6.

527   7    C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper proposes a universal approach to tackle multi-modality generalization by approximating
530   personalized invariant representation Xh through invariance, equivariance, and decomposition con-
531   straints with a learnable biological prior. We specifically unveil that learning Xh is feasible, and it
532   would significantly benefit the generalization in medical tasks.
533
      Limitations, challenges, and future work. To enhance the validation of our approach, we adhere to
534   commonly used settings during the tuning stage. Exploring alternative strategies, such as knowledge
535   distillation, could further improve downstream performance. Our approach requires datasets where
536   all modalities are instance-level matched, which can be a stringent condition and may be unattainable
537   for certain modalities. Future research should explore methods to achieve personalized invariance
538   without relying on instance-level matched datasets. Additionally, we advocate for the availability of
539   more open-source multi-modal medical datasets, particularly for functional modalities, as these are
      not widely accessible to researchers.

780   Using all constraints together with O yields the best results. Consistent with Section 3.2.1, the
781   results indicate that using different constraints alone may not guarantee improvements; however,
782   incorporating all constraints along with O results in the best outcomes. This validates the plausi-
783

      enhances generalization.
784
785   Using prior O with decomposition constraint improves the model performance for different
786   settings. Despite different settings, additionally using O with decomposition improves the down-
787   stream model performance. Combined with the improvements from modality transfer results in
788   Table 4, it suggests that O helps with better obtaining anatomical structure.
789   The invariance and equivariance constraints can not be applied to the same feature. It needs to
790   be highlighted that invariance and equivariance constraints can not be applied to the same features
791   as they conflict with each other. As shown in task 3, without O, invariance and equivariance con-
792   straints are applied to the latent feature simultaneously, leading to a significant performance drop. In
793   comparison, apply equivariance constraint before using O and applying invariance constraint after
794   using O yields the best results. This is because the geometrical transformation contained in zhi needs
795
      to be accomplished by fetching other possible geometrical transformation information form O and
      then fused to be invariant.
