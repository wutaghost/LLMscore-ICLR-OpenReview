009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present VideoDiT, a streamlined video generation framework adapted from
012            pre-trained image generation models. Unlike previous methods that simply add
013            temporal layers to image diffusion models, we enhance both the tokenizer, imple-
014            mented with the variational autoencoder (VAE), and the diffusion model. We em-
015            phasize the importance of combining 3D VAE compression with knowledge from
016            pre-trained image diffusion models to achieve efficient video generation, though
017            the tight coupling between image diffusion models and 2D VAEs poses significant
018            challenges. To address this, we introduce the Distribution-Preserving VAE (DP-
019            VAE), which encodes key frames in a video clip using the original 2D VAE while
020
               compressing non-key frames with a 3D VAE for spatiotemporal modeling. A reg-
               ularization term ensures alignment between the 3D video latent space and the 2D
021
               image latent space, facilitating seamless transfer of pre-trained diffusion models.
022
               Leveraging the Diffusion Image Transformers (DiT) architecture and incorporat-
023            ing 3D positional embeddings, we extend 2D attention into 3D with negligible
024            increased parameters. Furthermore, leveraging our proposed DP-VAE, VideoDiT
025            supports joint image-video training, preserving the spatial modeling capabilities
026            of the base model while excelling in both image and video generation. Extensive
027            experiments validate the effectiveness of our approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      In recent years, generative models for images and videos have achieved significant milestones.
      While image generation models like Stable Diffusion 3 (SD3) (Esser et al., 2024), DALL·E 3 (Betker
034
      et al., 2023), and the Flux series (Flux) have reached maturity and are poised for real-world ap-
035
      plications, the progress in video generation has been less remarkable. Since the release of the
036   SORA (Brooks et al., 2024) model, advancements in this space have been limited, and there remains
037   a lack of open-source models capable of producing high-quality video clips. The key challenge in
038   video generation lies in efficiently modeling spatiotemporal dynamics intrinsic to video data.
039
      Modern image generation relies on two foundational components to model spatial dynamics: a varia-
040
      tional autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) for compressing visual signals into compact latent repre-
041
      sentations, and denoising diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), which offer
042   a robust framework for modeling arbitrary distributions in this latent space. This VAE+Diffusion
043   framework has proven highly effective, particularly for text-to-image (T2I) generation tasks (Rom-
044   bach et al., 2022), and it has been widely adopted for video generation as well. However, two distinct
045   approaches have emerged in adapting this framework for videos.
046
      The first approach extends pre-trained T2I models into text-to-video (T2V) models by introducing
047
      temporal layers to the diffusion model while keeping the image VAE unchanged (Blattmann et al.,
048   2023b; Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Girdhar et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a). These
049   methods leverage the strong spatial modeling abilities of T2I models, which are derived from train-
050   ing on billions of images. However, since pre-trained T2I models are tightly coupled with their
051   corresponding VAEs, these approaches require the video diffusion model to operate within a 2D
052   latent space. This setup places the entire burden of temporal dynamics modeling on the diffusion
053   process, leading to an imbalance in the representation of spatial and temporal information, ultimately
      resulting in suboptimal performance and efficiency.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The second approach, as proposed in recent works (Gupta et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2024), advo-
055   cates for designing T2V models based on 3D spatiotemporal VAEs paired with diffusion models that
056   operate in 3D latent spaces. While this method is more efficient in compressing video data, the in-
057   trinsic link between diffusion models and their corresponding latent spaces poses a challenge. Since
058   diffusion models are trained to capture specific latent distributions, transitioning from a 2D to a 3D
059   latent space necessitates training video diffusion models from scratch. This leads to a significant loss
060   of prior knowledge embedded in pre-trained T2I models and demands substantial computational and
061   data resources.
062   In this work, we present VideoDiT, a novel framework that aims to streamline video generation
063   by leveraging a well-trained T2I model. The primary challenge is maximizing the benefits of these
064   pre-trained models without compromising the efficiency of the video generation process. Our key
065   innovation is the Distribution-Preserving VAE (DP-VAE), which combines the compression effi-
066   ciency of 3D VAEs with the latent space alignment of 2D VAEs. DP-VAE processes video sequences
067   as a series of Groups of Pictures (GoPs), where key frames are encoded using the original 2D VAE,
068   and non-key frames undergo spatiotemporal compression via a 3D VAE. This method ensures that
069
      the resulting residual representations maintain the same dimensionality as those of the key frames,
      allowing seamless integration into the pre-trained diffusion framework. By further incorporating
070
      a regularization term, we align the 3D and 2D latent distributions, enabling the pre-trained image
071
      diffusion model to be efficiently transferred to the video generation task.
072
073   Regarding the diffusion model, our architecture is built on diffusion image transformers (DiT),
074   which have emerged as a promising alternative to previous U-Net-based designs (Ronneberger et al.,
075
      2015; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), due to their simpler structure and enhanced scala-
      bility (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Esser et al., 2024). Notably, we find that directly applying the original
076
      2D spatial attention weights to 3D global attention produces consistent and stable video output
077
      without introducing additional parameters. By incorporating 3D positional embeddings during the
078   patchifying process, we initialize the video diffusion model from a strong base. To preserve the spa-
079   tial modeling capabilities of the text-to-image (T2I) model, we further utilize DP-VAE to introduce
080   joint image-video training. This enables VideoDiT to excel in both image and video generation
081   tasks. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our method in generating high-quality
082   images and videos. Notably, our method achieves comparable or even superior performance while
083   utilizing only approximately 10% of the data employed by existing methods.

      2.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER
089
090
      Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013) is originally proposed as generative models, op-
091   timized by maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). Previously, VQ-VAEs (Van Den Oord
092   et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019; Esser et al., 2021) compress videos into discrete tokens for categor-
093   ical generative modeling. Recently, VAEs have been commonly utilized as codecs to bridge orig-
094   inal visual signals with continuous latent variables in text-to-image latent diffusion models (Rom-
095   bach et al., 2022; Pernias et al.; Esser et al., 2024). Specifically, VAE is used to compress high-
096   dimensional visual data, thereby reducing spatial dimensions to decrease computational complexity.
097   More importantly, by constraining the latent space size and balancing signal fidelity with perceptual
098   realism in the loss function, the VAE encoder effectively discards high-frequency, textural informa-
099
      tion while retaining predominantly semantic information. This reduction in data distribution com-
      plexity facilitates the modeling tasks of diffusion models. Text-to-video generation models (Gupta
100
      et al., 2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024) usually utilize 3D VAE that compresses
101
      videos in both spatial and temporal dimensions. Temporally causal 3D VAE (Yu et al., 2024) is
102   proposed to support both image and video generation. However, retraining a 3D VAE often results
103   in an entirely new latent distribution, making it challenging to leverage the original well-trained T2I
104   model’s conceptual understanding and generation capabilities. Consequently, these methods typi-
105   cally require substantial computational resources to train the video diffusion models entirely from
106   scratch. In contrast, our proposed DP-VAE effectively compresses the video data along both tem-
107   poral and spatial dimensions while maintaining the original distribution. This enables a seamless
      transition of the T2I model into a T2V model.

      2.2   T EXT- TO -V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS
109
110   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) have proven effective in modeling
111   high-dimensional perceptual data (Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; Saharia et al., 2022). Building on
112   the success of text-to-image generation, text-to-video generation has garnered increasing attention,
113   given the significant role of video multimedia in daily life. Previous video diffusion models (Singer
114   et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024) often extend pre-trained T2I
115   models and build up hierarchical cascaded pipelines that include keyframe generation, temporal up-
116
      sampling, and spatial upsampling. This approach results in substantial costs for training, inference,
      and deployment. Worsely, during the keyframe training phase, the direct downsampling of video
117
      data along both temporal and spatial dimensions results in significant information loss, which sub-
118
      stantially impairs the modeling of realistic natural distributions and physical phenomena. Recently,
119   advanced techniques (Gupta et al., 2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024) have pro-
120   posed using 3D VAEs to compress videos into latent variables, reducing dimensions in both spatial
121   and temporal domains. This enables more efficient diffusion model training and improves model ca-
122   pabilities. On the other side, traditionally, U-Net-based (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architectures have
123   dominated diffusion models due to their multi-scale modeling capabilities and skip connections that
124   preserve fine-grained information. However, transformer-based image diffusion models (Peebles &
125   Xie, 2023; Esser et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024) have recently emerged as promising alternatives,
126   demonstrating robust performance and scalability. This shift is attributed to their simpler architec-
127
      ture and enhanced scalability compared to U-Net structures. In this paper, we develop a framework
      for efficiently bridging a well-trained T2I diffusion transformer towards a video generator.

130   3     M ETHOD
131
132   Our proposed VideoDiT contains two core contributions in terms of the fundamental components
133   of latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024), namely, the VAE and the
134   generation model. In Section 3.1, we introduce our proposed Distribution-Preserving VAE (DP-
135   VAE), which can compress both image and video data while maintaining the original distribution.
136   This preservation facilitates the seamless adaptation of subsequent diffusion model training. In
137
      Section 3.2, we propose an efficient method to convert an image diffusion transformer into a video
      version, requiring negligible additional parameters.

140   3.1   D ISTRIBUTION - PRESERVING VAE
141
      In latent diffusion models, VAEs play a pivotal role in dimensionality reduction and the elimina-
142
      tion of irrelevant information. We introduce the Distribution-Preserving VAE (DP-VAE), which
143   integrates compressed residual temporal information into the original 2D latent space and applies
144   regularization to the latent variables. This approach effectively transforms a 2D VAE into a 3D
145   VAE while maintaining the original distribution, thereby facilitating the seamless adaptation of pre-
146   trained T2I models for video generation.
147
      Inspired by video compression techniques (Lu et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021), we partition the original
148
      video signal into multiple Groups of Pictures (GoPs), each comprising one key frame and several
149   non-key frames sampled every tg frames. For each GoP, the key frame, xk , is encoded using the
150   pre-trained 2D VAE encoder, Ek , producing a latent representation, zk . Concurrently, the entire
151   video segment x is processed by a 3D VAE encoder Er , which performs spatiotemporal redundancy
152   removal and compresses the left information of non-key frames into the residual latent representation
153   zr , consistent in dimensionality with zk . This compression includes downsampling in both spatial
154   and temporal dimensions, with temporal downsampling applied after each of the first two spatial
155   downsampling operations, resulting a temporal downsampling factor of tg = 4. The combined
156   latent variable, z, is obtained by adding zk and zr . The detailed architectures of Er and the decoder
157
      D are described in the supplementary material. This process is formalized as follows:
158

203   3.2       U NIFYING I MAGE D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER TO V IDEO G ENERATOR

205   Spatial to Global Attention. Adapting a U-Net-based T2I model (Rombach et al., 2022) with
206   convolutions to generating videos (Blattmann et al., 2023b) necessitates the incorporation of addi-
207   tional temporal layers. This adaptation introduces a substantial number of extra parameters, and
208   training the temporal layers from scratch can undermine the knowledge embedded in the original
209   spatial weights. In contrast, Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), which are composed of stacked
210   attention blocks which operate on any number of tokens, while the feed-forward layers process each
211   token individually. This architecture facilitates a seamless transition from image to video process-
212
      ing, efficiently modeling both spatial and temporal dependencies without increasing the number of
      parameters or compromising the integrity of pre-trained spatial features.
213

215   first reshapes the input to (h × w) × d before passing it through the attention modules. Due to the
      absence of temporal correlation and interaction between individual images, each generated image

236   3D Patchify. Although attention operations are applied to all visual tokens, each token incorpo-
237   rates only the original spatial positional information and does not encode its temporal position.
238   Therefore, we incorporate global 3D positional embeddings into the patchify process. Specifically,
239   we introduce an additional branch to conduct patchify operation and add 3D positional embed-
240
      dings to the flatten input embeddings. The input embeddings are then projected through a zero-
      convolution (Singer et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b) for smooth updating. Implementation details
241
      can be found in the supplemental material.
242
243   Joint Image-Video Training. Joint training of images and videos not only preserves image gen-
244   eration capabilities but also enhances the learning of comprehensive concepts, leading to superior
245   video generation performance (Gupta et al., 2023). Our proposed DP-VAE inherently supports joint
246   image-video training by ensuring that latent variables for both modalities adhere to the same dis-
247   tribution. During training, a probability parameter, p, determines whether each iteration focuses
248
      on video generation or image generation. Detailed implementation procedures are provided in the
      supplementary material.

      largely disrupts the pre-trained diffusion model’s initial weights, resulting in poor convergence and
      subpar performance. Although incorporating a 2D VAE to compress key frames partially mitigates
410
      this issue, it still only yields suboptimal results. It is solely by utilizing our proposed DP-VAE that
411
      the diffusion transformer training can effectively integrate dynamic mechanisms into the pretrained
412   model’s image generation capabilities, thereby achieving optimal performance.
413
414   The quantitative results presented in Table 3 demonstrate that omitting a 2D VAE for key frame
415
      compression leads to significantly poor reconstruction and final generation performance. While
      introducing regularization partially mitigates this issue, the suboptimal reconstruction performance
416
      continues to limit the quality of the generated results. Incorporating a 2D VAE without regularization
417
      yields the best reconstruction performance. However, due to inconsistencies in the initial distribu-
418   tion, training the diffusion model still partially disrupts the original weights, resulting in suboptimal
419   performance. In contrast, our proposed DP-VAE strikes an optimal balance between reconstruction
420   performance and distribution preservation, resulting in superior generation performance.
421
422   2D→3D vs. 2D+3D Attention Mechanism. Previous methods (Blattmann et al., 2023b; Guo
423
      et al., 2024) propose to incorporate additional temporal layers and freeze original pre-trained spa-
      tial weights to preserve spatial features and enhance performance. Similarly, we can also intro-
424
      duce a trainable copy of the original spatial weights dedicated to global attention, while retaining
425
      the existing spatial attention layers. During training, the original spatial attention weights remain
426   frozen and only the newly added global attention weights are updated. We refer to this integrated
427   method as “2D+3D” attention. However, as shown in Table 4, the performance of “2D+3D” does
428   not significantly differ from that of “2D→3D” (our default setting introduced in Section 3.2) and
429   results in a considerably higher parameter count (4.15B vs. 2.03B). This demonstrates that our pro-
430   posed “2D→3D” conversion is both simple and efficient, offering a promising approach to adapting
431   pre-trained image Diffusion Transformers for video generation. Detailed implementations of the
      “2D+3D” conversion are provided in the supplementary material.

474   6     C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this study, we introduce VideoDiT, a framework designed to extend the capabilities of large-
477   scale pre-trained text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models to video generation by seamlessly integrating
478   motion cues with minimal modifications. Specifically, our approach addresses adaptations in both
479   the tokenizer and the diffusion model components. For the tokenizer, we propose the Distribution-
480
      Preserving VAE (DP-VAE), which compresses videos in both spatial and temporal dimensions while
      maintaining the original distribution. Regarding the diffusion model, we propose to directly convert
481
      existing spatial attention mechanisms to global attention, thereby incorporating a comprehensive
482
      receptive field without introducing additional parameters, thus providing an effective initialization
483   strategy. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our proposed DP-VAE and the adapted
484   video diffusion transformer, demonstrating significant improvements in video reconstruction fidelity
485   and generation quality.

787   where Xt represents the noisy input at time step t, X0 is the sampled noise from π0 , and X1 denotes
788   the clean data from π1 . The neural network is trained to directly predict the velocity vΘ = X1 − X0 .
