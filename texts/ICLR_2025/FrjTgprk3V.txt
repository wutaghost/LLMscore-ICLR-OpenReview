011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural networks trained to solve modular arithmetic tasks exhibit grokking, the
014            phenomenon where the test accuracy improves only long after the model achieves
015            100% training accuracy in the training process. It is often taken as an example
016            of “emergence”, where model ability manifests sharply through a phase transi-
017            tion. In this work, we show that the phenomenon of grokking is not specific
018            to neural networks nor to gradient descent-based optimization. Specifically, we
019            show that grokking occurs when learning modular arithmetic with Recursive Fea-
020
               ture Machines (RFM), an iterative algorithm that uses the Average Gradient Outer
               Product (AGOP) to enable task-specific feature learning with kernel machines.
021
               We show that RFM and, furthermore, neural networks that solve modular arith-
022
               metic learn block-circulant features transformations which implement the previ-
023            ously proposed Fourier multiplication algorithm.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years the idea of “emergence” has become an important narrative in machine learning.
029   While there is no broad agreement on the definition (Rogers & Luccioni, 2023), it is often argued
030   that “skills” emerge during the training process once certain data size, compute, or model size thresh-
031   olds are achieved (Wei et al., 2022; Arora & Goyal, 2023). Furthermore, these skills are believed to
032   appear rapidly, exhibiting sharp and seemingly unpredictable improvements in performance at these
033
      thresholds. One of the simplest and most striking examples supporting this idea is “grokking” mod-
      ular arithmetic (Power et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023). A neural network trained to predict modular
034
      addition or another arithmetic operation on a fixed data set rapidly transitions from near-zero to per-
035
      fect (100%) test accuracy at a certain point in the optimization process. Surprisingly, this transition
036   point occurs long after perfect training accuracy is achieved. Not only is this contradictory to the
037   traditional wisdom regarding overfitting but, as we will show, some aspects of grokking do not fit
038   neatly with our modern understanding of “benign overfitting” Bartlett et al. (2021); Belkin (2021).
039
      Despite a large amount of recent work on emergence and, specifically, grokking, (see, e.g., (Power
040
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Nanda et al., 2023; Thilak et al., 2022; Furuta et al., 2024; Miller et al.,
041
      2024)), the nature or even existence of the emergent phenomena remains contested. For example,
042   the recent paper Schaeffer et al. (2023) suggests that the rapid emergence of skills may be a “mirage”
043   due to the mismatch between the discontinuous metrics used for evaluation, such as accuracy, and
044   the continuous loss used in training. The authors argue that, in contrast to accuracy, the test (or
045   validation) loss or some other suitably chosen metric may decrease gradually throughout training
046   and thus provide a useful measure of progress. Another possible progress measure is the training
047   loss. As SGD-type optimization algorithms generally result in a gradual decrease of the training
048   loss, one may posit that skills appear once the training loss falls below a certain threshold in the
049   optimization process. Indeed, such a conjecture is in the spirit of classical generalization theory,
050
      which considers the training loss to be a useful proxy for the test performance Mohri et al. (2018).
051   In this work, we show that sharp emergence in modular arithmetic arises entirely from feature learn-
052   ing, independently of other aspects of modeling and training, and is not predicted by the standard
053   measures of progress. We then clarify the nature of feature learning leading to the emergence of
      skills in modular arithmetic. We discuss these contributions in further detail below.

      Summary of the contributions. We demonstrate empirically that grokking modular arithmetic:
076
      (1) is not specific to neural networks; (2) is not tied to gradient-based optimization methods; (3) is
077   not predicted by training or test loss1 , let alone accuracy.
078
079   Specifically, we show grokking for Recursive Feature Machines (RFM) (Radhakrishnan et al.,
080
      2024a), an algorithm that iteratively uses the Average Gradient Outer Product (AGOP) to enable
      task-specific feature learning in general machine learning models. In this work, we use RFM to en-
081
      able feature learning in kernel machines, which are a class of predictors with no native mechanism
082
      for feature learning. In this setting, RFM iterates between three steps: (i) training a kernel machine,
083   f , to fit training data; (ii) computing the AGOP matrix of f , M , over the training data to extract
084   task-relevant features; and (iii) transforming input data, x, using the learned features via the map
085   x → M s/2 x for a matrix power s > 0 (see Section 2 for details).
086
087
      In Fig. 1 we give a representative example of RFM grokking modular addition, despite not using any
      gradient-based optimization methods and achieving perfect (numerically zero) training loss at every
088
      iteration. We see that during the first few iterations both the test loss and and test accuracy remain at
089
      the constant (random) level. Around iteration 10 the test loss starts improving and, a few iterations
090   later, test accuracy quickly transitions to 100%. We also observe that even early in the iteration,
091   structure emerges in AGOP feature matrices (see Fig. 1). The gradual appearance of structure in
092   these feature matrices is striking given that the training loss is identically zero at every iteration and
093   that the test loss does not significantly change until iteration 8. The striped patterns observed in
094   feature matrices correspond to matrices whose sub-blocks are circulant with entries that are constant
095   along the “long” diagonals which wrap around the matrix.2 Such circulant feature matrices are key
096   to learning modular arithmetic. In Section 3 we demonstrate that standard kernel machines using
097   random circulant features easily learn modular operations. As these random circulant matrices are
098
      generic, we argue that no additional structure is required to solve modular arithmetic.
099   To demonstrate that the feature matrices evolve toward this structure (including for multiplication
100   and division under an appropriate re-ordering of the input coordinates), we introduce two “hidden
101   progress measures” (Barak et al., 2022): (1) Circulant deviation, which measures constancy of
102   the diagonals of a matrix, and (2) AGOP alignment, which measures similarity between the feature
103   matrix at iteration t and the AGOP of a fully trained model. We will see that both of these measures
104
      show gradual (initially nearly linear) progress toward a model that generalizes.

      We further argue that emergence in fully connected neural networks trained on modular arithmetic
109   identified in prior work (Gromov, 2023; Liu et al., 2022) is analogous to that for RFM and is exhib-
110   ited through the AGOP (see Section 4). By visualizing covariances of network weights, we observe
111   that these models also learn block-circulant features to grok modular arithmetic. We demonstrate
112   that these features are highly correlated with the AGOP of neural networks, corroborating prior ob-
113   servations from Radhakrishnan et al. (2024a). Furthermore, paralleling our observations for RFM,
114   our progress measures indicate gradual progress toward a generalizing solution during neural net-
115   work training. Finally we demonstrate that training neural networks on data transformed by random
116   block-circulant matrices dramatically decreases training time needed to learn modular arithmetic.
117   Why are these learned block circulant features effective for modular arithmetic? We provide support-
118   ing theoretical evidence that circulant features result in kernel machines implementing the Fourier
119   Multiplication Algorithm (FMA) for modular arithmetic (see Section 5). For the case of neural net-
120   works, several prior works have argued empirically and theoretically that neural networks learn to
121   implement the FMA to solve modular arithmetic (Nanda et al., 2023; Varma et al., 2023; Morwani
122   et al., 2024). While kernel RFM and neural networks utilize different classes of predictive models,
123
      our results suggest that they discover similar algorithms for implementing modular arithmetic.
124   By decoupling feature learning from predictor training, our results provide evidence for emergent
125   properties of machine learning models arising purely as a consequence of their ability to learn fea-
126   tures. We hope our work will help isolate the underlying mechanisms of emergence and shed light
127   on the key practical concern of how, when, and why these seemingly unpredictable transitions occur.
128   Paper outline. Section 2 reviews preliminary concepts. In Section 3, we demonstrate emergence
129   with RFM and show AGOP features consist of circulant blocks. Section 4, shows that neural network
130   features are circulant and are captured by the AGOP. In Section 5, we prove that kernel machines
131   learn the FMA with circulant features. We provide a discussion and conclude in Section 6.

      Learning modular arithmetic. Let Zp = Z/pZ denote the field of integers modulo a prime p and
135
      let Z∗p = Zp \{0}. We learn modular functions f ∗ (a, b) = g(a, b) mod p where f ∗ : Zp × Zp → Zp ,
136
      a, b ∈ Zp , and g : Z × Z → Z is an arithmetic operation on a and b, e.g. g(a, b) = a + b. Note that
137
      there are p2 discrete input pairs (a, b) for all modular operations except for f ∗ (a, b) = (a÷b) mod p,
138   which has p(p − 1) inputs as the denominator cannot be 0.
139
140   To train models on modular arithmetic tasks, we construct input-label pairs by one-hot encoding the
141
      input and label integers. Specifically, for every pair a, b ∈ Zp , we write the input as ea ⊕ eb ∈ R2p
      and the output as ef ∗ (a,b) ∈ Rp , where ei ∈ Rp is the i-th standard basis vector in p dimensions and
142
      ⊕ is concatenation. The training dataset consists of a random subset of n = r × N input/label pairs,
143
      where r is the training fraction and N = p2 or p(p − 1) is the number of possible discrete inputs.
144
145   Circulant matrices. The features that RFMs and neural networks learn in order to solve modular
146   arithmetic contain blocks of circulant matrices, which are defined as follows. Let σ : Rp → Rp
147
      be the cyclic permutation which acts on a vector u ∈ Rp by shifting its coordinates by one cell
      to the right: [σ(u)]j = uj−1 mod p , for j ∈ [p]. We write the ℓ-fold composition of this map
148
      σ ℓ (u) ∈ Rp with entries [σ ℓ (u)]j = uj−ℓ mod p . A circulant matrix C ∈ Rp×p is determined by
149
      a vector c = [c0 , . . . , cp−1 ] ∈ Rp , and has rows (in order from first to last): c, σ(c), . . . , σ p−1 (c).
150
      Feature matrices may also have have constant anti-diagonals (so-called Hankel matrices). To ease
151
      terminology, we will use the word circulant to refer to both Hankel and circulant matrices.
152

190   For simplicity, we omit the dependence on the dataset in the notation. Top eigenvectors of AGOP can
191   be viewed as the “most relevant” input features, those input directions that influence the output of a
192   general predictor (for example, a kernel machines or a neural network) the most. As a consequence,
193   the AGOP can be viewed as a task-specific transformation that can be used to amplify relevant
194   features and improve sample efficiency of machine learning models.
195   Indeed, a line of prior works (Yuan et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2014; Hristache et al., 2001) have used
196   the AGOP to improve the sample efficiency of predictors trained on multi-index models, a class of
197   predictive tasks in which the target function depends on a low-rank subspace of the data. Though
198   the study of AGOP has been motivated by these multi-index examples, we will see that the AGOP
199   can be used to recover useful features for modular arithmetic that are, in fact, not low-rank.

      AGOP and feature learning in neural networks. Radhakrishnan et al. (2024a) posited that AGOP
201   was a mechanism through which neural networks learn features. In particular, the authors introduce
202   the Neural Feature Ansatz (NFA) stating that for any layer ℓ of a trained neural network with weights
203   Wℓ , the Neural Feature Matrix (NFM), WℓT Wℓ , are highly correlated to the AGOP of the model
204   computed with respect to the input of layer ℓ. The NFA suggests that neural networks learn features
205   at each layer by utilizing the AGOP. For more details on the NFA, see Appendix C.

      5    F OURIER MULTIPLICATION ALGORITHM FROM CIRCULANT FEATURES
459
460   We have seen so far that features containing circulant sub-blocks enable generalization for RFMs
461   and neural networks across modular arithmetic tasks. We now provide theoretical support that shows
462
      how kernel machines equipped with such circulant features learn generalizing solutions. In particu-
      lar, we show that there exist block-circulant feature matrices, as in Observation 1, such that kernel
463
      machines equipped with these features and trained on all available data for a given modulus p solve
464
      modular arithmetic through the Fourier Multiplication Algorithm (FMA). Notably, the FMA has
465   been argued both empirically and theoretically in prior works to be the solution found by neural
466   networks to solve modular arithmetic (Nanda et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024).
467
468
      The FMA is a specific solution for implementing modular arithmetic that first represents the data by
      its Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT). Intuitively, transforming the data with circulant matrices ex-
469
      tracts the DFT of the one-hot encoded vectors following the well-known fact that circulant matrices
470
      can be diagonalized using the matrix that encodes the DFT (Gray et al., 2006). We state our result
471   informally here (for more details on the FMA, the precise theorem, and its proof, see Appendix G).
472
      Theorem 5.1 (Circulant features give the FMA). Training on all of the discrete data for any mod-
473
      ular operation, for each output class ℓ ∈ {0, · · · , p − 1}, suppose we train a separate quadratic
474   kernel predictor and particular block-circulant feature matrices Mℓ (having the structure in Obser-
475   vation 1). Then, the concatenated predictor given by kernel ridgeless regression on each output is
476   equivalent to the Fourier Multiplication Algorithm for that modular operation.
477
478   Notably, the FMA is defined over all of R2p , not just on one-hot encoded inputs. Thus, not only do
479   neural networks and RFM learn similar features, we have established a setting where kernel meth-
480   ods equipped with block-circulant feature matrices learn the same out-of-domain solution as neural
481
      networks for these tasks. This result is interesting, in part, as the only constraint for generalization
      on these tasks is to obtain perfect accuracy on inputs that are standard basis vectors.

      6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
484
485   Most classical analyses of generalization relied on the training loss serving as a proxy for the test loss
      and thus a useful measure of generalization. Empirical results of deep learning have upended this


                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      long-standing belief. In many settings, predictors that fit the data exactly can still generalize, thus
487   invalidating training loss as a predictor of test performance. This has led to the recent developments
488   in understanding benign overfitting, in neural networks as well as in classical kernel and linear mod-
489   els Belkin (2021); Bartlett et al. (2021). Since the training loss may not predict generalization, the
490   common suggestion has been to use the validation loss computed on a separate validation dataset.
491   Emergent phenomena, such as grokking, show that we cannot rely even on validation performance
492   at intermediate training steps to predict generalization at the end of training. Indeed, validation loss
493   at a certain iteration may not be indicative of the validation loss itself only a few iterations later.
494   Further, contrary to Schaeffer et al. (2023), we show these phase transitions in performance are not
495
      generally “a mirage” since, as we observe in this work, they are not always predicted by a priori
      measures of performance, continuous or discontinuous. Instead, emergence is fully determined by
496
      feature learning, which is difficult to observe without having access to a fully trained model. Indeed,
497
      the progress measures discussed in this work, as well as those suggested in, e.g., Barak et al. (2022);
498   Nanda et al. (2023); Doshi et al. (2024) can be termed a posteriori progress indicators. They all
499   require either understanding of the algorithm implemented by a generalizing trained model (such as
500   our circulant deviation, the Fourier gap considered in Barak et al. (2022), or the Inverse Participation
501   Ratio in Doshi et al. (2024)) or access to such a model (e.g. AGOP alignment).
502
      Consider generalizing features for modular multiplication shown in Fig. 3. The original features
503
      shown in panel B of this figure do not have an easily identifiable pattern. In contrast, re-ordered
504   features in panel C are clearly striped, containing block-circulants. As discussed in Section 3, re-
505   ordering of features requires understanding that the multiplicative group Z∗p is cyclic of order p − 1.
506   While a well-known result, it is far from obvious a priori. It is thus plausible that in other settings
507   hidden feature structures may be hard to identify due to a lack of mathematical insight.
508
      Why is learning modular arithmetic surprising? The task of learning modular operations is
509
      different from many other statistical machine learning tasks. In continuous ML settings, we typically
510   posit that the “ground truth” target function is smooth in an appropriate sense. Hence any general
511   purpose algorithm capable of learning smooth functions (such as, for example, k-nearest neighbors)
512   should be able to learn the target function given enough data. Primary differences between learning
513   algorithms are thus in sample and computational efficiency. In contrast, it is unclear what principle
514   leads to learning modular arithmetic from partial observations. There are many ways to fill in the
515   missing data and we do not know a simple inductive bias, to guide us toward a solution. Several
516   recent works argued that margin maximization with respect to certain norms can account for learning
517   modular arithmetic (Morwani et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2023; Mohamadi et al., 2024). While the
518
      direction is promising, general underlying principles are not yet clear.
519   Analyses of grokking. Recent works (Kumar et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2023; Mohamadi et al., 2024)
520   argue that grokking occurs in neural networks through a two phase mechanism that transitions from a
521   “lazy” regime, with no feature learning, to a “rich” feature learning regime. Our experiments clearly
522   show that grokking in RFM does not undergo such a transition. For RFM on modular arithmetic
523   tasks, our progress measures indicate that the features evolve gradually toward the final circulant
524
      matrices, even as test performance initially remains constant (Fig. 2). Grokking in these settings
      is entirely due to the gradual feature quality improvement and two-phase grokking does not occur.
525
      Additionally, we have not observed significant evidence of “lazy” to “rich” transition as a mechanism
526
      for grokking in our experiments with neural networks, as most of our measures of feature learning
527   start improving early on in the training process (improvement in circulant deviation measure is
528   delayed for addition and subtraction, but not for multiplication and division, while AGOP feature
529   alignment initially shows near linear improvement for all tasks), see Fig. 5. Our observations for
530   neural networks are in line with the results in (Doshi et al., 2024; Nanda et al., 2023), where their
531   proposed progress measures, Inverse Participation Ratio and Gini coefficients of the weights in the
532   Fourier domain, are shown to increase prior to improvements in test loss and accuracy for modular
533   arithmetic.
534   Conclusions. In this paper, we showed that grokking modular arithmetic happens in feature learning
535   kernel machines in a manner very similar to what has been observed in neural networks. Remarkably
536   we observe that feature learning can happen independently of improvements in both training and
537   test loss. Not only does this finding reinforce the narrative of rapid emergence of skills in neural
538   networks, it is also not easily explicable within the framework of the existing generalization theory.

768   A    B ROADER DISCUSSION
769
      Low rank learning. The problem of learning modular arithmetic can be viewed as a type of matrix
770
      completion – completing the p × p matrix (so-called Cayley table) representing modular operations,
771
      from partial observations. The best studied matrix completion problem is low rank matrix comple-
772   tion, where the goal is to fill in missing entries of a low rank matrix from observing a subset of the
773   entries (Moitra, 2018, Ch.8). While many specialized algorithms exist, it has been observed that
774   neural networks can recover low rank matrix structures Gunasekar et al. (2017). Notably, in a devel-
775   opment paralleling the results of this paper, low-rank matrix completion can provably be performed
776   by linear RFMs using the same AGOP mechanism Radhakrishnan et al. (2024b).
777
      It is thus tempting to posit that grokking modular operations in neural networks or RFM can be
778   explained as a low rank prediction problem. Indeed modular operations can be implemented by an
779   index 4 model, i.e., a function of the form f = g(Ax), where x ∈ R2p and A is a rank 4 matrix (see
780   Appendix L for the construction). It is a plausible conjecture as there is strong evidence, empirical
781   and theoretical, that neural networks are capable of learning such multi-index models Damian et al.
782   (2022); Mousavi-Hosseini et al. (2022) as well as low-rank matrix completion. Furthermore, a phe-
783   nomenon similar to grokking was discussed in (Radhakrishnan et al., 2022, Fig. 5, 6) in the context
784   of low rank feature learning for both neural networks and RFM. However, despite the existence of
785   generalizeable low rank models, the actual circulant features learned by both Neural Networks and
786
      RFM are not low rank. Interestingly, this observation mirrors the problem of learning parity func-
      tions through neural network inspired minimum norm interpolation, which was analyzed in Ardeshir
787
      et al. (2023). While single-directional (index one) solutions exist in that setting, the authors show
788
      that the minimum norm solutions are all multi-dimensional.
789
790
      Explanations for deep learning Finally, this work adds to the growing body of evidence that
791   the AGOP-based mechanisms of feature learning can account for some of the most interesting phe-
792   nomena in deep learning. These include generalization with multi-index models (Parkinson et al.,
793   2023), deep neural collapse (Beaglehole et al., 2024b), and the ability to perform low-rank matrix
794   completion (Radhakrishnan et al., 2024b). Thus, RFM provides a framework that is both practically
795   powerful and serves as a theoretically tractable model of deep learning.

      E    R EORDERING FEATURE MATRICES BY GROUP GENERATORS
835
836   Our reordering procedure uses the standard fact of group theory that the multiplicative group Z∗p is
837   a cyclic group of order p − 1 Koblitz (1994). By definition of the cyclic group, there exists at least
838
      one element g ∈ Z∗p , known as a generator, such that Z∗p = {g i ; i ∈ {1, . . . , p − 1}}.
839   Given a generator g ∈ Z∗p , we reorder features according to the map, ϕg : Z∗p → Z∗p , where if h = g i ,
840   then ϕg (h) = i. In particular, given a matrix B ∈ Rp×p , we reorder the bottom right (p−1)×(p−1)
841   sub-block of B as follows: we move the entry in coordinate (r, c) with r, c ∈ Z∗p to coordinate
842   (ϕg (r), ϕg (c)). For example if g = 2 in Z∗5 , then (2, 3) entry of the sub-block would be moved to
843   coordinate (1, 3) since 21 = 2 and 23 mod 5 = 3. In the setting of modular multiplication/division,
844   the map ϕg defined above is known as the discrete logarithm base g (Koblitz, 1994, Ch.3). The
845   discrete logarithm is analogous to the logarithm defined for positive real numbers in the sense that
846   it converts modular multiplication/division into modular addition/subtraction. Lastly, in this setting,
847   we note that we only reorder the bottom (p − 1) × (p − 1) sub-block of B as the first row and column
848   are 0 (as multiplication by 0 results in 0).
849   Upon re-ordering the p × p off-diagonal sub-blocks of the feature matrix by the map ϕg , the fea-
850   ture matrix of RFM for multiplication/division tasks contains circulant blocks as shown in Fig. 3C.
851   Thus, the reordered feature matrices for these tasks also exhibit the structure in Observation 1. As a
852   remark, we note that there can exist several generators for a cyclic group, and thus far, we have not
853   specified the generator g we use for re-ordering. For example, 2 and 3 are both generators of Z∗5 since
854   {2, 22 , (23 mod 5), (24 mod 5)} = {3, (32 mod 5), (33 mod 5), (34 mod 5)} = Z∗5 . Lemma K.1 im-
855   plies that the choice of generator does not matter for observing circulant structure. As a convention,
856
      we simply reorder by the smallest generator.
