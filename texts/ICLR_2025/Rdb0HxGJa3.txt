009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We study online convex optimization with predictions, where, at each time step
012            t, predictions about the next k steps are available, and with coupled costs over
013            time steps, where the cost function at time step t depends on the decisions made
014            between time t − a and time t + b for some nonnegative integers a, b.
015            We provide a general recipe to run synchronous update in an asynchronous fashion
016            that respects the sequential revelation of information. Combined with existing
017            convergence results for convex optimization using inexact first-order oracle, we
018            show that acceleration is possible in this framework, where the dynamic regret can
                                                   √       k
019            be reduced by a factor of (1 − O( κ)) a+b through accelerated gradient descent,
020            at a cost of an additive error term that depends on the prediction accuracy. This
021            generalizes and improves the (1 − κ/4)k factor obtained by Li & Li (2020) for
022
               a + b = 1. Our algorithm also has smaller dependency on longer-term prediction
               error. Moreover, our algorithm is the first gradient based algorithm which, when
023
               the strong-convexity assumption is relaxed, constructs a solution whose regret
024
               decays at the rate of O(1/k 2 ), at a cost of an additive error term that depends on
025            the prediction accuracy.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   We study online convex optimization with coupled cost: at time step t, the cost function ft is a
031   function of decisions x(t−a):(t+b) , i.e., the decisions made in a window of length a + b around t.
032   This generalizes the well studied smoothed online convex optimization problem (Li & Li, 2020; Li
033   et al., 2021; Goel & Wierman, 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Goel et al., 2019; Pan et al., 2022) where
034   ft is the sum of a stage cost that depends only on current decision xt , and a switching cost between
035
      xt and xt−1 . Following the setup of Li & Li (2020) and Li et al. (2021), we assume that the cost at
                                                                                                             (t)
036   time t is parameterized by θt ∈ Θ, and the decision maker has potentially inexact predictions, θbs ,
037   about future θs (s ≥ t). Online convex optimization with switching costs has been used in various
038
      settings such as online optimal control (Li et al., 2019), data center management (Lin et al., 2012),
      power systems (Kim & Giannakis, 2017), to name a few. Our history-dependent stage costs also
039
      echo a recent line of work on online convex optimization with memory (Anava et al., 2015; Kumar
040
      et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2020), which shows applications in online linear control (Agarwal et al., 2019),
041   statistical arbitrage in finance, and time series prediction (Anava et al., 2015).
042
043   We focus on the following question proposed and studied in Li & Li (2020): when making decisions
044
      online, how can one make the best use of predictions about future, while being robust to inaccuracy
      in the (long-term) predictions?
045
046   Many methods have been proposed to incorporate predictions in online convex optimization: from
047   optimization based methods such as RHC (Kwon & Pearson, 1977), AFHC (Lin et al., 2012) and
048   CHC (Chen et al., 2016), which require solving optimization problems (exactly) at each iteration, to
049   gradient based methods RHAG (Li et al., 2021) which converges at the optimal rate but requires exact
050
      prediction, and RHIG (Li & Li, 2020) which works with inexact prediction but suffers suboptimal
      convergence rate.
051
052   In this work, we propose the online Projected Gradient Method (online-PGM) and online Accelerated
053   Gradient Method (online-AGM), which build upon variants of the well known (accelerated) gradient
      descent designed for inexact first order oracle (Bubeck, 2015; Devolder et al., 2013a;b). We show that


                                                          1

054
      when k-step predictions of the objective functions and√k-step look ahead initialization are available,
055   online-AGM achieves the convergence rate (1 − O( κ)k ) for κ-conditioned objective functions.
056   In addition, the extra additive term in the dynamic regret due to prediction inaccuracy has smaller

060

061   approach to running (accelerated) gradient descent online, which might be of independent interest.

206   2     C ONNECTIONS WITH PREVIOUS WORKS
207
208   Smooth online convex optimization. Our problem is motivated by a recent line of work on online
209                                                                             PT
      convex optimization with switching cost, where the goal is to minimize t=1 ft (xt ) + d(xt , xt−1 )
210   by choosing xt sequentially, based on past decisions and past ft ’s, together with potentially inexact
211   predictions about future ft ’s(Kwon & Pearson, 1977; Lin et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2016; Li et al.,
212   2021; Li & Li, 2020). Various methods have been proposed to take advantage of the prediction. To
213   name a few, RHC (Kwon & Pearson, 1977), AFHC (Lin et al., 2012) and CHC (Chen et al., 2016)
214   choose xt ’s based on the optimal solution to the predicted problem restricted to windows around t;
215   RHAG (Li et al., 2021) applies accelerated gradient descent with exact prediction, and RHIG (Li &
      Li, 2020) applies gradient descent with inexact prediction (more comparison in Section 1.3).

      Online convex optimization and dynamic regret. We measure the performance of x using the
217   dynamic regret, i.e. against the optimal x∗ which does not necessarily satisfy x∗t = x∗t+1 for all t.
218   Dynamic regret has been well studied for problems where ft depends only on xt (see Zinkevich
219   (2003); Besbes et al. (2015); Zhao & Zhang (2021); Hazan (2022) and references therein). Typically,
220   the regret is upper bounded using a combination of T , PT the variation of the sequence (x∗1 , . . . , x∗T ),
221   and/or VT the variation of the sequence (f1 , . . . , fT ). For instance, the online gradient descent (OGD)
                    √                                                                           1/3
222   achieves O( T PT ) (Zinkevich, 2003), and the restarted OGD achieves O(T 2/3 VT ) (Besbes et al.,
223   2015). However, it’s non-trivial to obtain dynamic regret guarantees for the general coupled objective
224   functions where ft also depends on decisions made in the past and/or future. Li & Li (2020) shows
225   that in the special setting of smooth online convex optimization with prediction, where the coupling√     is
226   only due to the switching costs between consecutive decisions, restarted OGD can achieve O( T VT )
227   dynamic regret, with additive terms due to prediction errors.
228   Other related online optimization problems. Convex optimization with memory (Anava et al.,
229   2015; Kumar et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2020) can be viewed as a special case of our problem Eq. 1 with
230   b = 0. However, static regret and the offline fixed decision are usually used as benchmarks. Also
231   related is online optimization with prediction, where bound on static regret using the prediction error
232   has been obtained for online mirror descent (Rakhlin & Sridharan, 2013).
233   Smooth convex optimization with inexact oracles. Under Assumption 1.1, prediction error can be
234   related to error in gradient, and thus be treated as a form of oracle inaccuracy. Our Algorithm 3 builds
235   upon Devolder et al. (2013a) and Devolder et al. (2013b), which study the convergence properties
236   of (accelerated) gradient descent with inexact first order oracle. We present a modification of their
237   results below in Section 3.1. Optimization with inexact oracle has also been studied in many other
238   works: d’Aspremont (2008) and Schmidt et al. (2011), to name a few.
239   Decentralized convex optimization. Our objective function C(x1 , x2 , . . . , xT ; θ) can be viewed as
240   a function of T components and fits naturally into a network model, where each vertex represents
241   the decision at some time step, and vertices communicate information such as current decision
242   variables, gradients, and momentum. This connects our problem with many other network-related
243   problems, especially parallel/distributed optimization (Scaman et al., 2017; Mosk-Aoyama et al.,
244   2010; Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, 2015). It will be interesting to further explore what insights these
245   network-related problems can bring to our online convex optimization with prediction.
