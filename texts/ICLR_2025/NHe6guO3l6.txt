009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Federated learning is a machine learning paradigm that allows multiple clients
012             to train aggregated model via sharing model updates to a central server without
013             sharing their data. Even though the data is not shared, it can indirectly influence
014             the aggregated model via the shared model updates. In many real-life scenarios, we
015             need to completely remove a client’s influence (unlearning) from the aggregated
016             model, such as competitive clients who want to remove their influence from the
017             aggregated model after leaving the coalition to ensure other clients do not benefit
018
                from their contributions. The influence removal is also needed when the adversarial
                client negatively affects the aggregated model. Though the aggregated model can
019
                be retrained from scratch to ensure exact unlearning (completely removing the
020
                client’s influence from the aggregated model), it performs poorly just after the
021             unlearning, which is undesirable during deployment. To overcome this challenge,
022             this paper proposes federated unlearning algorithms that ensure exact unlearning
023             while achieving better performance post-unlearning. Our experimental results on
024             different real datasets validate the performance of the proposed algorithms.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   An individual user may have insufficient data to train a state-of-the-art machine learning model. Yet,
030   we can significantly improve the model performance by leveraging the combined data from multiple
031   users. Federated learning (FL) (Zhang et al., 2021) is one of the most prevalent paradigms to perform
032   such collaboration today, especially in sectors with strong privacy demands such as finance and health
033   care (Li et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). In the FL setting, collaborative clients train local models on
034   their own data, and a central server model is obtained by aggregating these local model updates for
035
      multiple communication rounds. FL is well-suited for many commercial applications as it eliminates
      the need to share users’ private data during training. For example, multiple companies from the same
036
      industrial sector (e.g., banking, insurance, or healthcare) often possess diverse user data. To leverage
037
      all available data, these companies can collaborate and train a more accurate model using suitable FL
038   algorithms (Aledhari et al., 2020).
039
040   Although FL algorithms do not directly access users’ data, the aggregated model is still influenced by
041
      the local models trained on each client’s data. When a client leaves the collaboration, it is necessary
      to update the aggregated model to remove the influence of its data–a process known as federated
042
      unlearning (FU),1 e.g., a company leaving the collaboration of many companies may demand the
043
      removal of their contributions to ensure their competitors do not benefit from them. FU techniques
044   are also desirable to remove the influence of adversarial clients, i.e., the adversary behaves like a
045   client and degrades the model performance by contributing contaminated updates (Fang et al., 2020).
046   Additionally, the development of FU techniques facilitates the exercise of the right to be forgotten
047   formalized in many regional or government data regulations such as GDPR (2016) and CCPA (2018).
048
      We can trivially achieve FU by retraining the collaboration from scratch without the target client’s
049
      data (Liu et al., 2023). Despite its simplicity, the new server model suffers from low performance as
050   it is restarted with random initialization. As a result, it slows down the deployment of the unlearned
051   model, as training large models on the collaboration of many users can be time-consuming. Due to
052

      these shortcomings, it is natural to consider the following question: How can we guarantee the exact
055   federated unlearning while ensuring better post-unlearning performance?
056
057   This paper proposes two novel methods for achieving exact FU with improved post-unlearning
058
      performance. The first method, Bi-Models Training (BMT) (Section 3.1), preserves isolated copies
      of local models and reuses clients’ existing knowledge residing in these models during unlearning
059
      for better aggregation. Despite being unlearning-friendly, these local models fail to capture the joint
060
      influence of multiple clients on the global model. Training the power set of clients can capture the
061   influence of all possible influences of the clients but is computationally expensive and may lead
062   to double influence, where a client affects multiple sub-FL models. As a result, we propose the
063   second method, Multi-Models Training (MMT) (Section 3.2), that trains each sub-FL model on disjoint
064   subsets of clients to avoid double influence and aggregates the best sub-FL models upon unlearning to
065   achieve improved initialization of the aggregated model. We empirically justify the effectiveness of
066   BMT and MMT through multiple experiments on real-world vision and language datasets (Section 4).

      1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
069
070
      In this section, we now review the relevant work, especially in federated learning, machine unlearning,
071
      and federated unlearning, to our problem setting.
072
073   Federated Learning (FL). FL emerges from the industrial needs to train centralized models on
074   large, decentralized data residing on users’ device (McMahan et al., 2017) and is particularly favored
075   in sectors requiring strong privacy guarantees, such as finance and health care (Li et al., 2020; Xu
076
      et al., 2021). Based on the characteristics of the decentralized data, Yang et al. (2019) divided FL into
      three categories: horizontal FL, vertical FL, and federated transfer learning. To optimize the federated
077
      models, McMahan et al. (2017) proposed the FedAvg algorithm that averages local updates from
078
      contributing clients and works well on independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. However,
079   as real-world data is often heterogeneous (e.g., users with different demographics), subsequent works
080   have proposed new methods that target model architecture or algorithm design to alleviate model drift
081   that can degrade model performance (Zeng et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2023; Idrissi et al., 2021; Li et al.,
082   2021; Karimireddy et al., 2020). We refer the readers to Zhang et al. (2021) for a detailed survey of
083   various works covering different settings of federated learning.
084
      Machine Unlearning (MU). MU aims to remove the influence of a selected subset of data from
085   the trained ML model. Based on the guarantee of removal, MU methods are broadly categorized
086   into exact unlearning and approximate unlearning (Nguyen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). In
087   exact unlearning, we aim for an identical model to one that would have been obtained by retraining
088   without that data to be erased. Retraining is a method that trivially achieves exact unlearning but is
089   computationally expensive with large models and datasets. Existing works can exactly unlearn for
090   support vector machines (Cauwenberghs & Poggio, 2000), k-means (Ginart et al., 2019), random
091   forests (Brophy & Lowd, 2021). Bourtoule et al. (2021) partitions the entire training data set into a
092   few disjoint subsets and trains one base model with each of these subsets. Since each base model
093
      is only trained with a subset of the original training data, the performance may be sub-optimal.
      Approximate unlearning aims for a model whose distribution closely resembles that of the retrained
094
      model. Guo et al. (2020) proposed a certified removal method to approximately unlearn linear model
095
      by Newton-like update. Nguyen et al. (2020) minimizes the KL divergence between the approximate
096   posterior of the unlearned model and the retrained model under the variational inference framework.
097
098   Federated Unlearning (FU). Many recent works adapt machine unlearning to the federated learning
099
      settings (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2021). Liu et al. proposed FedEraser, which
      involves using historical updates from the server and local calibration training on the unlearned client.
100
      The federated unlearning protocol proposed in this work can be used to unlearn an arbitrary subset of
101
      clients without any constraint on the type of data each client possesses. At the same time, it requires
102   no participation of the unlearned client. Wang et al. proposed a channel pruning-based method to
103   selectively forget a specific class from the trained ML model. Such an approach has limited scope as
104   it is impractical to assume that each participant in the FL setting possesses precisely one class of data.
105   Gong et al. concerned with the setting where no centralized party/server is present, which does not
106   apply to the centralized FL setting. In terms of exact federated unlearning, Xiong et al. (2023) and
107   Tao et al. (2024) use quantization and sampling strategies, respectively, to get a checkpoint during
      the FL training where the unlearned client’s data have not made a quantifiable impact and use it

108
      as initialization for model retraining and since speed up the retraining process. On the other hand,
109   Qiu et al. (2023) proposed to cluster the clients and train a few intermediate FL models and then
110   subsequently obtain the global FL model through one-shot aggregation. At the unlearning stage, only
111   the intermediate FL model where the unlearning client is present is retrained (and hence reducing the
112   retraining cost). Our proposed method touches on both ideas and uses aggregation of a few sub-FL
113   models to obtain a good initialization for much more efficient retraining. The way we obtained our
114   sub-FL models trades-off between computation budget and post-unlearning performance, played an
115   essential role in ensuring its effectiveness.

119   Federated Learning. This paper considers the centralized federated learning (FL) setting with a
120   trusted central server and multiple clients. In this setting, a central server shared an aggregated model
121   with the clients and then each client trains this model on his dataset and send model updates (weights
122   or gradients) to the central server, which then aggregates these updates to get a better aggregated
123   model. In our setting, we assume that the number of clients participating in FL process varies over
124   time. Let Ct denote the set of participating clients at the beginning of the FL communication round
125
      t. An FL communication round (communication round for brevity) represents one cycle of model
      sharing by the central server with clients and then receiving the updated aggregated model.
126

128   from the distribution νc over X × Y. Here, X represents the input space, and Y represents the label
129   space. The learning model is denoted by hθ : X → Y for model parameters θ ∈ Rd , where d is the
130   number of model parameters. The loss incurred by the learning model hθ on a sample (x, y) ∈ X × Y
131
      is denoted by l(hθ (x), y), which can be the root mean squared error (for regression problems) or
      cross-entropy loss (for classification problems).

      Exact Federated Unlearning. Let client c influence be completely removed from the aggregated
147   model. Exact federated unlearning is the process of completely removing the influence of client c
148   training data from the aggregated model, resulting in a model that is equivalent to the models trained
149   without the training data of client c. However, the aggregated model resulting from retraining without
150   the data of client c may have a poor performance in the initial round, which may not be expected
151   when these models are deployed in practice. Therefore, our goal is to design methods that ensure
152   exact federated unlearning while leading to an aggregated model with as high accuracy as possible.
153
154   3    E XACT F EDERATED U NLEARNING M ETHODS
155
156   Due to multiple communication rounds of the FL training, it becomes impossible to completely
157   remove a client’s data influence from the trained aggregated model. Therefore, the most
158   straightforward way to achieve the exact federated unlearning is to restart the federated learning
159   process from scratch with the remaining clients. This method of retraining the aggregated model from
160   scratch is called retraining from scratch (RfS) (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). Although
161   RfS is a simple method, the new model may have very low accuracy in the initial rounds after
      unlearning compared to the aggregated model before unlearning due to restarting the FL process with


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      random initialization of the aggregated model. Such performance reduction of the aggregated model
163   may not be desirable during deployment in practice involving critical applications such as healthcare
164   (Prayitno et al., 2021; Dhade & Shirke, 2024) and finance (Long et al., 2020). This shortcoming of
165   RfS raises a natural question: How can we guarantee the exact federated unlearning while ensuring
166   better post-unlearning performance? To answer this question, we propose two novel methods for
167   achieving exact federated unlearning that completely remove the client’s influence while giving better
168   post-unlearning performance than RfS.

170   3.1             B I -M ODELS T RAINING
171
172   To get a better performing aggregated model post-unlearning, we must design a new FL training
173   process that allows exact federated unlearning while having a better initialization than random
174
      initialization. One way to achieve better initialization is to design methods that can exploit the
      remaining clients’ existing knowledge. To do this, we propose a method named Bi-Models Training
175
      (BMT) that can be incorporated into any existing federated learning framework. The main idea of
176
      BMT is to have an additional model for each client that is only trained on its data, making these
177   models unaffected by other clients’ training data. We refer to this model as local model. We use the
178   term global model for referring to the aggregated model, which is trained using all client’s data and
179   used for deployment. Next, we discuss how BMT can be incorporated into the different stages of any
180   existing federated learning framework (as depicted in Fig. 1), namely: Initialization, FL Training,
181   Unlearning, and New Client joining the FL process, whose details are given as follows.
182
      Initialization. The central server starts the standard FL training process by randomly initializing
183   the global aggregated model. This randomly initialized global model is then shared with all clients.
184   Each client updates the global model using its local training data and then shares the model update
185   (updated model or gradients) with the central server. As compared to the standard initialization in any
186   FL training process, each client makes a copy of the locally updated global model2 (i.e., local model).
187   Since the initial global model is randomly initialized, these local models are, by design, isolated from
188   the influence of other clients’ training data.
189   FL Training. After receiving the first model updates, the central server aggregates them to get the
190   aggregated global model as per the underlying FL algorithm (McMahan et al., 2017; Shlezinger et al.,
191   2020; Zhang et al., 2021). In each subsequent communication round, each client receives the updated
192   global model from the central server and then trains it using its training data. After updating the
193   global model, each client shares the model update with the central server. Besides the standard FL
194   training process, each client also updates their local model using their training data.

      New Client. When a new client wants to join the ongoing FL collaboration, the central server waits
223   until the end of the ongoing communication round. Once it is over, the central server starts the FL
224   training process with the new client by sharing the current global model with the new client, who then
225   updates the current global model using its training data and shares the model update with the central
226   server. Apart from this, the central client also shares the randomly initialized global model with the
227   new client, who updates it, which then acts as the local model of the new client for subsequent rounds.
228   Other clients do not influence this local model, as the initial global model is randomly initialized.
229   In summary, BMT has two models for each client: global and local. All clients train their local
230   model on their data in isolation, whereas the global model is trained using the underlying FL training
231   protocol. To completely remove a client influence from the global model, the central server first
232   discards the global model and then uses the local models of the remaining clients to re-initialize the
233   global model, which is further updated via FL training. This process ensures that BMT, by design,
234   guarantees the exact federated unlearning. Further, using the remaining clients’ local models leads to
235   an initialization of the global model that is already influenced by the remaining clients to some extent,
236   leading to a better performance than RfS, as corroborated by our experiments in Section 4. The price
237
      for this improved post-unlearning performance is the cost of pre-training the local models in advance.
      Such a trade-off is worthwhile for applications that require exact unlearning and an unlearned model
238
      with good performance as quickly as possible for deployment.

      3.2   M ULTI -M ODELS T RAINING
242
      The key insight of the previous section is that BMT achieves a better initial global model because it
243   is influenced by the clients’ local models. However, the local model only contains influence from
244   an individual client and has no joint influence of multiple clients. Since all clients influence the
245   global model, we should capture the joint influence of different clients and then use it to get a better
246   initialization of the global model. To capture the joint influence, we can train FL models using only a
247   subset of clients. We refer to these FL models as sub-FL models. Formally, a sub-FL model is an FL
248   model that is trained via FL protocol using a subset of clients, where the size of the subset varies
249   from 2 to N − 1. One can train all possible sub-FL models (power set of clients excluding global
250   model) to capture the influence of all possible interactions of different subsets of clients. However,
251
      this approach is not computationally feasible as these sub-FL models increase exponentially with
      the number of clients (i.e., 2n − n − 2 for n clients). Another problem of training arbitrary sub-FL
252
      models leads to a situation of double influence, which is defined as follows:
253
254   Definition 1. Let Si be the set of clients whose data are used in training the i-th sub-FL model. The
255
      sub-models i and j leads to double influence if Si ∩ Sj ̸= ∅, Si \ Sj ̸= ∅, and Sj \ Si ̸= ∅.
256
      When one client data is used to train two sub-FL models, it can lead to double influence if both are
257
      also trained using data from different clients, e.g., one is trained on clients {1, 2} and another on
258   clients {1, 3}; the client 1 data is used in both sub-FL models and hence having the double influence.
259
260   To avoid the double influence, each sub-FL model should be trained on disjoint subsets of clients,
261
      or the set of clients used for training sub-FL models is a proper superset of the set of clients used
      for another sub-FL model. One possible way to achieve this is to organize sub-FL models in a
262
      hierarchical tree structure. In this tree, the root node represents the global model while the leaf nodes
263
      correspond to the local models, and intermediate nodes represent sub-FL models, with each child
264   node having disjoint sets of clients compared to its siblings. As we move from the root node to the
265   leaf nodes, each sub-FL model branches into further subsets, maintaining either disjoint relationships
266   or superset relations, thus ensuring a clear and systematic flow of influence throughout the hierarchy.
267   We refer to this hierarchical tree structure as an influence tree. After unlearning a client, we should
268   aggregate the sub-FL models with higher influence (those influenced by a larger number of clients)
269   and local models to get the initialization for the global model. If the number of models to aggregate
      is less, it implies that the initialization of the global model contains the most joint influence of clients.


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      This relationship inspires our proposed metric influence degradation score, which measures how
271   good is an influence tree. Next, we formally define the influence degradation score.
272
      Definition 2 (Influence Degradation Score (IDS)). Let T be any influence tree structure. The
273
      influence degradation score for T , denoted by s(T ), is defined as the average number of sub-FL and
274
      local models that are aggregated to get the initial global modal after unlearning any client.
275
276   Though the tree structure, by design, eliminates double influence, we do not know which tree structure
277   gives the lowest IDS for given clients’ different likelihood of requesting unlearning (as the probability
278   of requesting unlearning may vary across the clients). As our goal is to construct an influence tree
279   with minimum IDS, our following result shows that the binary influence tree constructed using
280   Huffman coding has the lowest IDS among all n-ary influence tree structures, where n > 2.
281   Theorem 1. Given an n-ary influence tree T , there exists a binary influence tree T2 that has smaller
282   IDS, i.e., s(T2 ) < s(T ). Let pc be the unlearning probability of the client c. Then, Huffman coding
283   with n symbols representing clients and weights {pc }nc=1 gives the optimal binary influence tree such
284   that s(THuffman ) ≤ s(T2 ) for any influence tree T2 for the same group of clients.
285
      With Theorem 1, we can use Huffman coding (Huffman, 1952) to construct an influence tree that
286   has the lowest IDS among all types of influence trees. In some real-life applications, the client’s
287   unlearning probability can be unknown. In such cases, we can assume that each client is equally
288   likely to be unlearned, hence having the same unlearning probability. We show the influence tree for
289   8 clients having the same unlearning probability in Fig. 3a. A client (on the leaf node) influences the
290   sub-FL model if there is a path from a sub-FL model to the leaf node representing that client. We
291   next propose a method named Multi-Models Training (MMT) that uses the sub-FL models to get better
292   initialization for the global model. MMT can be easily incorporated into the different stages of any
293   existing federated learning framework (as depicted in Fig. 1), whose details are given as follows.
294   Initialization. Similar to BMT, the central server starts the standard FL training process by randomly
295   initializing the global aggregated model. This randomly initialized global model is then shared with
296   all clients. Each client updates the global model using its local training data and then shares the
297   model update (updated model or gradients) with the central server. Each client makes a copy of the
298   locally updated global model. Compared to BMT, MMT also initializes the sub-FL models using the
299   model updates of clients corresponding to the sub-FL models.

477   during unlearning. Furthermore, each sub-FL model is more likely to converge to the global optimum
478   due to training on more local datasets. Thus, MMT with the default binary structure is the most suitable
479   configuration for unlearning.
480   Subscription models. The unlearning order can be fixed in certain circumstances, e.g. clients may
481   subscribe to the service that allows them to participate in the federated process for a fixed duration
482   and will leave once their subscription expires. In such scenarios, it is possible to construct an optimal
483   MMT structure that maximizes learning performance when clients leave the federation in a fixed order.
484   Particularly, the optimal structure is the one that arranges the clients by their expiration date, with the
485   soon-to-expire client at the top and greedily building the tree until reaching those with the farthest
      expiration. As observed in Fig. 7b, the greedy implementation of MMT achieves the best unlearning

      performance and outperforms the default MMT that assumes uniform probabilities of unlearning for
487   all clients. Therefore, the greedy structure is preferable if the unlearning order of the clients is known

      Non-uniform unlearning probabilities. The default MMT implementation assumes uniform
      unlearning probabilities for all clients. However, it is practical to consider the case where these
491
      probabilities are non-uniform. In fact, we will demonstrate that given the unlearning probabilities
492
      of each client, it is possible to construct improved MMT structures that achieve better unlearning
493   performance through two strategies based on Shannon-Fano coding (Shannon, 1948) and Huffman
494   coding (Huffman, 1952). Fig. 7c shows the performance for different tree construction methods. This
495   result is obtained by sampling the client to be removed for 100 times according to pre-defined
496   non-uniform unlearning probabilities of all clients. MMT structures that follow Shannon-Fano
497   coding and Huffam coding obtain visibly improved results over the default MMT. Furthermore,
498   Huffman-MMT obtains slightly better results than the Shannon-Fano counterpart, which aligns with
499   the classical information theory results that Huffman coding is more optimal than Shannon-Fano
500   coding for prefix-free code (Thomas & Joy, 2006).

526   5       C ONCLUSION
527
528
      In this work, we propose two methods, BMT and MMT, for exact federated unlearning in the ongoing
      federated learning collaboration. Our methods ensure the complete removal of an unlearned client’s
529
      data while having better performance post-unlearning with the remaining clients than retraining
530
      from scratch. Our methods are particularly useful in practical scenarios where model updation
531   in collaborative environments cannot afford long delays, with minimal tolerance for interruptions.
532   Our extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. A few
533   interesting future research directions include proposing a principal approach to design an influence
534   tree under a resource constraint (i.e., the number of sub-FL models that can be trained is limited) and
535   how to change the influence tree post-unlearning or after a client joins the collaboration while having
536   the lowest IDS value and maximizing the use the existing trained sub-FL models.
