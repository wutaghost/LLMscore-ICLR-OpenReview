009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Universal adversarial perturbations (UAPs) have deepened the vulnerability concern
012            of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) after the initial intriguing discovery of vanilla
013            single-model-single-image adversarial attacks. However, the landscape of UAPs
014            has not been thoroughly investigated. In this paper, we revisit and expand UAPs
015            for white-box targeted attacks along three axes simultaneously: the model-axis, the
016            data-axis, and the target-axis. For the target-axis, we adopt the most aggressive
017            ordered top-K attack protocol (K ≥ 1) to expand the traditional top-1 attack
018
               setting in the prior art of learning UAPs. Our proposed method is thus dubbed
               as AllAttacK. In implementation, our AllAttacK is built on two state-of-the-art
019
               single-model-single-image ordered top-K attack methods, the KL divergence
020
               based adversarial distillation method and the more recently proposed quadratic
021            programming based method. We propose a simple yet effective joint mini-data-
022            batch and mini-model-batch optimization strategy in learning UAPs for a large
023            number of models (e.g., up to 18 disparate DNNs) and a large number of images
024            (e.g., 1000 images). We test our AllAttacK on the ImageNet-1k classification task
025            using an ensemble of disparate models such as Convolutional Neural Networks and
026            their adversarially-robustified versions, Vision Transformers, CLIP vision encoders,
027            and MLP-Mixers. Our learned AllAttacK perturbations are doubly transferable
028            across training and testing models, and across training and testing images, and they
029
               also show intriguing yet sensible looking.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033   Visual perception is robust with human vision, and is aimed to be similarly, if not more, robust with
034   computer vision (Palmer, 1999). Computer vision has witnessed remarkable progress by end-to-end
035   representation learning using Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) (LeCun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al.,
036   2012; He et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). However, adversarial attacks
037   can easily fool well trained image classification DNNs to classify a dog image as a cat by adding
038   visually-imperceptible perturbations (Nguyen et al., 2015; Szegedy et al., 2014; Athalye & Sutskever,
039
      2017; Carlini & Wagner, 2016; Goodfellow et al., 2015; Kannan et al., 2018; Madry et al., 2017;
      Xie et al., 2019; Madry et al., 2018). Initially perceived as mere anomalies, adversarial attacks have
040
      rapidly evolved, posing increasingly intricate challenges (Geirhos et al., 2020) for the reliability and
041
      trustworthiness of AI systems, especially in high-stake applications.
042
043   Among many other aspects, Universal Adversarial Perturbations (UAPs) that are often quasi-
044   imperceptible have introduced even deeper troubles for DNNs since they are doubly transferable
045
      across DNNs and images. UAPs have been studied both for un-targeted top-1 attacks (Moosavi-
      Dezfooli et al., 2017; Shafahi et al., 2020) and targeted top-1 attacks (Liu et al., 2016), but tested
046
      with convolutional neural networks only including CaffeNet (Jia et al., 2014), VGGNets (Chatfield
047
      et al., 2014; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015), GoogLeNet (Szegedy et al., 2015) and ResNets (He
048   et al., 2016). With the recent development of DNNs with new architectures such as Vision Transform-
049   ers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), ConvNeXt Woo et al. (2023) and MLP-Mixers (Tolstikhin et al., 2021),
050   and with new and more powerful training recipes such as the contrastive language-image pretraining
051   (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) and further combined with masked image modeling (MIM) as in the
052   EVA2 model (Fang et al., 2023), it is unclear whether targeted UAPs can retain their attacking power
053   for ensembles of those disparate DNNs, as well as adversarially-robustified counterparts (Croce et al.,
      2020).

      In the meanwhile, with respect to the number of attack targets beyond the conventional top-1
064
      setting, there are three types of settings with increasing difficulty levels: i) Untargeted Top-K
065
      Adversarial Attacks (Easiest): Ground-truth labels shouldn’t be in the top-K classes, Top-K classes
066   can be anything but ground truth. This is often achievable as by-product via existing un-targeted
067   attack methods as pointed out in (Zhang & Wu, 2020). ii) Unordered Top-K Targeted Adversarial
068   Attacks (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Tursynbek et al., 2022; Kumano et al., 2022; ?) (Harder):
069   They provide specific target top-K classes that should be in the top-K predictions after the attack
070   but no particular order of appearance is enforced as long as each target class is somewhere in the
071   top-K predictions. iii) Ordered Top-K Targeted Adversarial Attacks (Zhang & Wu, 2020; Paniagua
072   et al., 2023) (Hardest): They provide specific targeted top-K classes in order and the top-K predicted
073   classes after attack must match this exact order. (Liu et al., 2016) has shown that transferable targeted
074
      top-1 attacks across images and/or models are much harder to learn. It remains unclear whether UAPs
      can achieve more aggressive attack objectives, e.g., ordered top-K UAPs.
075
076   In this paper, we provide affirmative answers to the above two questions by learning universal
077   ordered top-K perturbations that are doubly transferable across images and models consisting
078   of disparate types of DNNs. Our proposed method is dubbed as AllAttacK, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
079   More specifically, we revisit and expand conventional UAPs (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017; Shafahi
080
      et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2016) simultaneously along three axes:
081   • The model-axis: How many different types of DNNs (e.g., convolutional neural networks, Trans-
082     former models and all-MLP models), and how many different models of each type can be attacked,
083     simultaneously? Furthermore, can perturbations learned from an ensemble of training models
084     generalize to unseen models that are of very different architectures than those in training?
085   • The data-axis: How many of training images (that are used in the optimization of learning the
086     shared adversarial perturbation) can be attacked, and how many unseen images can the same
087
        perturbation transfer to, simultaneously?
      • The target-axis: How many top-K targets can be attacked, and can they be attacked with respect to
088
        any given orders? For example, we may want to see if a well-trained image classification DNN can
089
        be fooled to misclassify a dog image, not only just with cat as its top-1 prediction, but also, e.g.,
090     with [cat, car, fish] as its top-3 predictions with the exact given order.
091
092   Seeking quantitative analyses of AllAttacK will facilitate us better understanding the adversarial
093   vulnerability at the fundamental level and enable us to re-assess its severity considering that DNNs
094   increasingly permeate various facets of daily life, from enhancing user experience on digital platforms
095
      to making critical decisions in autonomous vehicles. As we shall show, the severity is observed to be
      high. Fig. 2 shows qualitative examples of learned ordered top-K UAPs by our proposed AllAttacK.
096
097   Our Contributions. This paper makes two main contributions to the field of learning white-box
098   targeted adversarial attacks: (i) It presents, to our knowledge, the first large-scale study of learning
099   UAPs that are both model-agnostic (up to 18 disparate DNNs in training) and image-agnostic (at the
100   ImageNet-1k scale), with strong results obtained. (ii) It proposes two optimization formulations in
101
      learning AllAttacK, built on previous single-model-single-image ordered Top-K attack work, with a
      proposed stochastic mini-data-batch and mini-model-batch optimization strategy for practicality and
102
      generalizability.

194   Existing adversarial attack settings could thus be treated as special cases of our AllAttacK in

199   The Challenge of AllAttacK. To put in other words, the goal of AllAttacK is to seek a stationary
200   point P in the data space, which once added to an input clean image can “shut it off” and “steer it
201   towards the adversarial targets T ” for any training or testing DNNs, no matter what the original top-K
202   predictions made by the DNNs are for the clean image. The existence of such universal perturbation
203   clearly shows that those DNNs might still have “shallow and fragile understanding” of the structure
204   of the data space. As we observed in experiments, the learned universal top-K perturbations alone
205
      indeed can fool most of DNNs (see examples in supplementary material).

278   2.2.3 L EARNING VIA S TOCHASTIC M INI -BATCH AND M INI -M ODEL
279   In practice, when the training dataset Dtrain and/or the training model ensemble Mtrain are large,
280   we can not afford the full-batch optimization, even for a single large model, due to the GPU memory
281   constraint. To handle this, we resort to stochastic mini-batch and mini-model learning. During each
282   iteration in the optimization, we sample a mini-batch of training images with a predefined batch size
283   (e.g., 64), and sample a number of models (e.g., 4) if all the models in Mtrain can not be loaded (due
284   to GPU memory constraints).
285   At a first glance, since our goal is to learn a single perturbation for all images and all models, the
286   practicality-enforced stochastic optimization strategy seems counter-intuitive. During the learning of
287   AllAttacK, the single perturbation is the only “model parameters” to be estimated. Similar to how
288   a randomly-initialized DNN can be successfully trained from scratch using mini-batch stochastic
289   gradient descent, it actually makes sense to learn the single perturbation using stochastic optimization.
290   The interesting aspect of AllAttacK is a learned single universal perturbation can obtain the model-
291   agnosticity (across disparate training models and unseen testing models).

      3.2.1 A LL ATTAC K: S INGLE -M ODEL AND I MAGE -AGNOSTIC
329
      We test K = 1, 2, · · · , 6. We choose two widely recognized pretrained ConvNets: ResNet-50
330
      (He et al., 2016) and DenseNet-121 (Huang et al., 2017), as well as two prominent pretrained
331
      Transformers: the vanilla ViT (Base) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and the data-efficient variant DEiT
332   (small) (Touvron et al., 2021). The pretrained checkpoints for these four networks are sourced from
333   the mmpretrain package (Contributors, 2023). Table 1 shows the results. We have some observations
334   as follows:
335
336   • The Model Axis. We can learn universal (image-agnostic) perturbations for all the four models
337
        individually. In terms of ASRs, we observe a decreasing trend of attacking difficult from ResNet-50,
        to DenseNet-121, to DEiT-S and to ViT-B, consistent across both the training set and the testing
338
        set and consistent across K = 1, 2, · · · , 6. It is interesting to observe that among the four DNNs,
339
        ResNet-50 is the most difficult one to attack, while ViT-B is the easiest one. Our intuitive yet
340     hypothetical explanation for this observation is that the more expressive DNNs are, the easier they
341     might be to suffer from attacks, since the clear-box targeted adversarial attack can fully exploit
342     their expressive power. This may provide some explanations for why aligned multi-modal large
343     language models (which use variants of ViTs as their vision encoder) can be easily attacked as
344     investigated in (Carlini et al., 2024).
345   • The Data Axis. On the unseen testing dataset, for the most difficulty to attack among the four
346     DNNs, ResNet-50, our AllAttacK achieves ASRs greater than 0.5 when K ≤ 3, and remains
347     reasonably high up to K = 6, which shows the strong image-agnosticity of our AllAttacK. As
348
        expected, ASRs are consistently and significantly higher on the training dataset than those on the
        testing datasets. The gaps are roughly between 0.2 and 0.3.
349
      • The Target Axis. As we expected, the shear complexity of learning AllAttacK perturbations is
350
        increased for larger K’s. On both the training and testing datasets, the ASRs decreases along
351     K = 1, · · · , 6. From Fig. 2 (viewed in magnification), it is interesting to observe that learned
352     perturbations exhibit some “features” of the targets, e.g., the tail texture of ‘fox squirrel’, and the
353     eye-ish shapes like ‘can opener’ and/or wheel of ‘recreational vehicle’. It is also interesting to
354     notice that perturbations learned for ViT-B remain the patchy style.
355   • The Optimization Axis. Overall, the QP optimization (Eqn 14) in the feature embedding space
356     is much stronger than the KL surrogate loss function (Eqn. 9). For Top-1 perturbations, the
357     KL formulation works better than the QP formulation. When K ≥ 2, the QP formulation is
358     significantly better. Especially for K = 6, the KL formulation almost fails to learn the AllAttacK
359
        perturbations, while the QP formulation can achieve reasonable high ASRs. For K = 1, the
        top-down designed target distribution P AD (T ) (Eqn. 10) is very similar to the one-hot distribution,
360
        and the KL divergence objective function is similar to the cross-entropy objective, resulting in
361
        effective optimization in learning perturbations.
362   • ASRs vs ℓ2 Norms. We note that ℓ2 norms of the optimization methods are comparable only when
363     their ASRs are comparable. For example, consider the perturbations for ResNet-50 on the testing
364     dataset, it shows the QP method obtains the ℓ2 norm, 56.28, while the KL method has 25.88. The
365     former is computed based on the universal perturbation that is learned to attack 57.4% images
366     in training and to generalized to attack 23.6% images in testing, while the latter is based on the
367     perturbation that can obtains ASRs, 0.3% and 0.08% in training and testing respectively. So, the
368     KL method may achieve lower ℓ2 norms due to reaching a saturation point on the ASR, and the
369     norms are only computed on the “easier” targets, which is also observed in (Paniagua et al., 2023).
370
        For the same protocol (e.g., Top-6), we have a single perturbation learned using either of the two
        methods. The slight difference between     the ℓ2 norms in training and testing is due to the clamping

494   4    R ELATED W ORK
495   Since Szegedy et al. (2013) showed the brittleness of DNNs, many works have studied their vulnera-
496   bilities. We briefly overview adversarial attacks in thee categories that are important to understand
497   the evolution to challenges this paper aims to address.
498
      Ordered Top-K Adversarial Attacks. Ordered Top-K Adversarial Attacks aim to dictate the exact
499
      content and order of the first Top-K predicted classes. Adversarial Distillation (AD) Zhang & Wu
500   (2020) addresses this by employing combining knowledge distillation and semantic word embeddings
501   to craft a target distribution of class probabilities. Once AD computes a target class probabilities,
502   it then uses a Kullback-Leiber divergence loss to solve for a perturbation that achieves its target.
503   QuadAttack Paniagua et al. (2023) approaches this challenge through quadratic programming in the
504   feature embedding space, directly finding a perturbation in embedding space. Once an embedding
505   space perturbation has been found, an ℓ2 loss between the perturbed and current embedding space
506   features can be used to solve for a perturbation in data-space.
507   Universal Adversarial Perturbations (UAPs). One remarkable discovery in Szegedy et al. (2013)
508   was that adversarial attacks have the ability to transfer to models trained with different hyperparam-
509   eters or training sets than those the adversarial attack was generated with. Liu et al. (2016) later
510   investigates the transferability of adversarial examples among different neural network architectures,
511   differentiating between targeted and non-targeted attacks. Liu et al. (2016) introduces an ensemble-
512   based attack method similar in spirit to our own AllAttack, enabling successful targeted adversarial
513   attacks. This further extended to Universal Adversarial Perturbations Hendrik Metzen et al. (2017);
514
      Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017); Shafahi et al. (2020) . Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017) and Shafahi
      et al. (2020) achieve a single perturbation that can be applied to a large number of images for a
515
      model and prevent correct classification, While Hendrik Metzen et al. (2017) extended the concept
516
      of universal attacks to the domain of segmentation. Zhang et al. (2020) first observes the possibility
517   of common class-specific “features” across universal perturbations. Benz et al. (2020) extends the
518   specificity of UAPs to only change predictions for one specified “source” class and change them to a
519   prescribed “sink” class, leaving all other classes unchanged.
520
521   5    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
522
523   This paper presents a method for learning universal ordered Top-K targeted adversarial perturbations
524   that are both image-agnostic and model-agnostic under the white-box attack setting. The proposed
525
      method is dubbed as AllAttacK. It defines the problem of AllAttacK along three axes (model, data and
      targets). It presents two optimization methods in learning AllAttacK, built on previous single-model
526
      and instance-specific ordered Top-K attack methods, which enable training AllAttacK with a large
527
      number of disparate deep neural networks (up to 18 models). The proposed AllAttacK is thoroughly
528   evaluated in experiments with 510 universal ordered top-K perturbations learned at three different
529   levels of universality, and with strong or promising attack success rates obtained.
530
531
      Discussions: Towards Testing and Verifying the Interpretability-Robustness Conjecture. The
      emerged sense-making appearance of the learned AllAttacK UAPs for a combination of models and
532
      adversarially trained models motivate us to make the interpretibility-robustness conjecture: A DNN
533
      will be adversarially robust in a holistic way if its AllAttacK adversarial perturbations are
534   semantically meaningful (i.e., in the close proximity to or even inside the real data manifold).
535   From a quantitatively equivalent viewpoint, it means that the perturbations themselves in
536   isolation will be classified by the DNN with the top-K predictions equal to the ordered top-K
537   targets (K ≥ 1). Ideally and ultimately (in the long run), a DNN is certified to be robust if its
538   AllAttacK perturbations are confined to be high-fidelity synthesized images for the ordered
539   top-K targets, i.e., the closed-loop of AllAttacK-as-Generator. We hope this conjecture can sheds
      light on addressing adversarial defense, and we leave it to be tested and verified in future work.

      A.1      B ROADER I MPACTS
759
760   The promising generalization capability of the learned universal top-K perturbations to unseen models,
761   especially those at the so-called foundation model level (e.g., the CLIP ViT-B), might be exploited in
762   a harmful way for applications built on those models. Powerful defense methods should be studied,
763   which we will investigate in our future work based on the proposed interpretability-robustness
764   conjecture. We will also release our source code to encourage more research on studying defense
765   methods against the proposed AllAttacK.
766
767   A.2      D ETAILS OF O PTIMIZATION
768
769
      We build on the released code of QuadAttacK Paniagua et al. (2023) 1 . For better understanding of our
      methodology, we provide details on the configuration used for optimizing our learned perturbations.
770
      In all configurations we use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 0.002 from PyTorch to
771
      minimize our presented objectives (Eqn. 9 and Eqn. 14 in the main paper). We run all configurations
772   for 50 epochs on the training images and models.
773
774   To learn a perturbation, both the QP and KL methods require choosing a hyperparameter λ for the
775
      loss term focused on satisfying the Ordered Top-K constraint. There is no “optimal" value for this
      parameter, it is a trade-off parameter that selects a point on the ASR vs Energy tradeoff curve. While
776
      λ just represents a tradeoff curve point, there also exists a minimum energy at which this curve yields
777
      an ASR greater than 0.
778
779   To facilitate finding successful attacks on more challenging cases (e.g., the 18-model ensemble attack),
780   we perform our optimization for multiple λ values and select the smallest energy that obtained a
781
      non-negligible ASR. For QP we search in λ ∈ {100, 150} and for KL we search in λ ∈ {1000, 1500},
      where we choose different magnitudes for QP and KL due to the different spaces that losses operate
782
      in.
783
784   We use 1 Nvidia A100 80G GPU in all our experiments. We run multiple configurations (e.g.,
785   different K’s and DNN combinations) in parallel across 4-8 GPUs on our server.
786

      We visualize all learned perturbations for a comprehensively qualitative analyses. There are 5 runs
      in sampling the targets. For each run, we have 8 model variations: 4 individual models (in Table 1
790
      of the main paper), 2-ConvNet combination and 2-ViT combination and 4-model combination (in
791
      Table 2 of the main paper), and 18-model combination (in Fig.4 of the main paper). For the first 7
792   model variations, we test K = 1, · · · , 6, and for the last variation we test K = 1, 2, 3, all using two
793   optimization methods functions, the KL formulation (Eqn. in the main paper) and the QP formulation
794   (Eqn. in the main paper). The total number of perturbations are 5 × 7 × 6 × 2 + 15 × 3 × 2 = 510.

      We develop a HTML based interactive visualization tool (Fig. 3). Please check the index.html
