009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Explainability and uncertainty quantification are two pillars of trustable artificial
012            intelligence. However, the reasoning behind uncertainty estimates is generally
013            left unexplained. Identifying the drivers of uncertainty complements explanations
014            of point predictions in recognizing model limitations and enhances trust in deci-
015            sions and their communication. So far, explanations of uncertainties have been
016            rarely studied. The few exceptions rely on Bayesian neural networks or tech-
017            nically intricate approaches, such as auxiliary generative models, thereby hin-
018            dering their broad adoption. We propose a straightforward approach to explain
019
               predictive aleatoric uncertainties. We estimate uncertainty in regression as pre-
               dictive variance by adapting a neural network with a Gaussian output distribution.
020
               Subsequently, we apply out-of-the-box explainers to the model’s variance output.
021
               This approach can explain uncertainty influences more reliably than more com-
022            plex published approaches, which we demonstrate in a synthetic setting with a
023            known data-generating process. We further adapt multiple metrics from conven-
024            tional XAI research to uncertainty explanations. We quantify our findings with a
025            nuanced benchmark analysis that includes real-world datasets. Finally, we apply
026            our approach to an age regression model and discover reasonable drivers of un-
027            certainty. Overall, the proposed method explains uncertainty estimates with little
028            modifications to the model architecture and decisively outperforms more intricate
029            methods.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Researchers have recognized the importance of uncertainty quantification and explainability of ma-
035   chine learning (ML) predictions to ensure the successful adoption of ML-based systems in safety-
036   critical applications (Abdar et al., 2021; Vilone & Longo, 2020). These two dimensions are key
037   indicators of a model’s trustworthiness, reliability, and fairness, crucial for its broad acceptance
038   (Lambert et al., 2022; Lötsch et al., 2022). Predictive uncertainty in ML refers to the degree of
039
      confidence associated with a model’s predictions (Chua et al., 2023). It can be decomposed into an
      epistemic and aleatoric component (Kendall & Gal, 2017). Epistemic uncertainty arises from the
040
      scarcity of data in specific areas of the input space, such as the underrepresentation of a particular
041
      condition. Principally, it can be reduced by acquiring additional examples. Aleatoric uncertainty
042   refers to inherent randomness or variability in the data, representing uncertainty that cannot be re-
043   duced by including additional training instances. It can arise due to measurement errors or certain
044   variables relevant to the observed process not being collected. Uncertainty estimation is critical in
045   risk management. It allows taking conservative action, relying on the model only when it exhibits
046   a high degree of confidence in its predictions, and avoiding usage outside its area of competence
047   (Kompa et al., 2021).
048   Explainability encompasses methods that enhance the transparency of ML models by highlighting
049   how features influence model output or by rendering the internal computations of black-box mod-
050   els more interpretable. Explainability methods enable understanding whether a model has learned
051   relevant patterns from the input data and can reveal interesting, previously unknown associations
052   (Samek et al., 2021; Schwalbe & Finzel, 2023). Uncertainty quantification and explainability ensure
053   accountable, informed, and responsible decision-making and help mitigate biases and risks (Bhatt
      et al., 2021; McGrath et al., 2023).

054
      In most applications, explainability focuses on interpreting point predictions (Vilone & Longo,
055   2020). There is a significant gap in understanding and explaining the drivers of uncertainty esti-
056   mates. When an ML algorithm is deployed and yields a substantial uncertainty estimate for a specific
057   instance, the possible courses of action involve abstaining from employing the model if alternatives
058   are available or accepting the increased risk. With explainable uncertainties, users gain the capability
059   to identify the factors contributing to elevated uncertainty levels. This understanding allows domain
060   experts to judge their relevance in a given scenario. Additionally, it provides valuable insights into
061   modifications required to augment the model’s predictive certainty and performance. In cases where
062   abstaining from model usage is still necessary, factors influencing the decision can be understood
063
      and communicated. For example, if such an uncertainty factor is a feature indicating a person’s
      minority status, it could suggest a potential bias of the model. The bias would be undetectable by
064
      naive explanations if this feature only influences the uncertainty but not the mean prediction. While
065
      detecting and explaining distribution shifts and epistemic uncertainty is an equally interesting prob-
066   lem (Brown & Talbert, 2022), we focus our work on aleatoric uncertainty. Aleatoric uncertainty
067   estimates and explanations are relevant for domains where the noise of the outcome of interest is
068   not constant across independent variables, i.e., heteroscedastic settings. In these cases, aleatoric
069   uncertainty explanations offer complementary information to explanations of point predictions, as
070   the relevant variables influencing mean and variance might differ significantly. Heteroscedastic set-
071   tings emerge, for example, in the estimation of biophysical variables (Lázaro-Gredilla et al., 2014),
072   the estimation of cosmological redshifts (Almosallam et al., 2016), and robotics and vehicle control
073   (Bauza & Rodriguez, 2017; Smith et al.; Liu et al., 2021).
074   Explanations can be categorized as either local or global (Schwalbe & Finzel, 2023; Adadi &
075   Berrada, 2018). Local explanations characterize how a model makes predictions for a specific in-
076   stance. A local explanation of the model’s uncertainty could foster more transparent discussions
077   about ML-assisted decisions and risks, increasing trust. Global explanations provide an overview of
078   a model’s behavior across the entire input space. They serve to detect general drivers of uncertainty
079   and certainty. These can then be leveraged to formulate hypotheses to improve the model or to detect
080
      unintended shortcuts in the uncertainty estimation process, such as spurious correlations or biases.
081   There is little prior work on explaining uncertainties, and existing literature mainly focuses on classi-
082   fication tasks and generally relies on Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) or technical intricacies such
083   as auxiliary generative models (Antoran et al., 2021; Perez et al., 2022; Ley et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
084   2023). BNNs assign probability distributions to network weights to capture uncertainty (MacKay,
085   1992). However, due to their computational complexity and involved training process, BNNs have
086
      not been as widely adopted as classical neural networks (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017).
087   We propose a straightforward and scalable approach for explaining uncertainties in a heteroscedastic
088   regression setting that can be readily integrated into ML pipelines (see Figure 1). We extend point
089   prediction models to additionally estimate parameters of the spread of a given probability distribu-
090   tion. Specifically, we predict parameters of a Gaussian distribution as in a heteroscedastic regression
091   model (Bishop, 1994). The variance parameter of the Gaussian can be interpreted as a measure of
092
      the aleatoric uncertainty of the model. We can then use any state-of-the-art explainability method to
      explain the variance estimate provided by this distributional model. By highlighting input features
093
      contributing to the variance output, we identify the inputs contributing to model uncertainty.
094
095   Currently, there is a gap in the comparative evaluation of uncertainty explainers in the literature.
096   Therefore, we devise a benchmark with synthetic data with a known data-generating process to an-
097   alyze a method’s ability to detect uncertainty drivers. Further, we extend unsupervised XAI metrics
098
      to evaluate uncertainty explainers and perform a second benchmark across diverse datasets. We
      compare our approach to Counterfactual Latent Uncertainty Explanations (CLUE) (Antoran et al.,
099
      2021) and InfoSHAP (Watson et al., 2023). Finally, we apply our approach to an age regression task
100
      on images. In summary, our contribution is as follows: We propose a straightforward explanation
101   method for uncertainty and evaluate it against existing approaches. We introduce a benchmark using
102   synthetic data, real data, and synthetically augmented data, including established metrics from the
103   XAI field. Thereby, we provide a resource for informed usage of uncertainty explanation methods.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK

136   In some research communities, such as causal inference, graphical models, and Gaussian processes,
137   explicitly modeling uncertainty is a prominent area of interest. Furthermore, uncertainty quantifi-
138   cation and explainability are rich areas of research within the deep learning field (Abdar et al.,
139   2021; Vilone & Longo, 2020). Yet, few researchers have recognized the importance of explain-
140   ing the sources of uncertainty in deep learning predictions. Yang & Li (2023) have developed an
141   explainable uncertainty quantification approach for predicting molecular properties. They employ
142
      message-passing neural networks and generate unique uncertainty distributions for each atom of
      a molecule. This approach is inherently specialized for graph-based representations of molecules.
143
      CLUE (Antoran et al., 2021) and related approaches (Perez et al., 2022; Ley et al., 2022) derive
144
      counterfactual explanations by optimizing for an adversarial input that is close to the original input
145   but minimizes uncertainty. The adversarial input is constrained to the data manifold with a deep
146   generative model of the input data to prevent out-of-distribution explanations. This requires an op-
147   timization process for each explanation and the training of an auxiliary generative model, rendering
148   CLUE and its extensions computationally demanding and difficult to implement. Additionally, An-
149   toran et al. (2021) developed an evaluation method for contrastive explanations of uncertainty. Wang
150   et al. (2023) have developed a gradient-based uncertainty attribution method for image classification
151   with BNNs. They modify the backpropagation to attain complete, non-negative pixel attribution and
152   to prevent vanishing gradient issues. To detect and explain model deterioration, Mougan & Nielsen
153
      (2023) use classical ML methods and bootstrapping. They train a model and obtain uncertainty
      estimates on a test set transformed with an artificial distribution shift. In a second step, they train
154
      another model to predict the uncertainty estimates from the first step. Subsequently, Shapley values
155
      are estimated for the second model to explain the uncertainty. Mehdiyev et al. (2023) employ quan-
156   tile regression forests to obtain prediction intervals that quantify uncertainty. They extract feature
157   attributions for the uncertainty by estimating Shapley values directly for these prediction intervals
158   as output. Watson et al. (2023) introduce variants of the Shapley value algorithm to explain higher
159   moments of the predictive distribution by quantifying feature contributions to conditional entropy.
160   They use a split conformal inference strategy. They first train a base XGBoost model to predict con-
161   ditional probabilities. Subsequently, they fit an auxiliary model to the base model residuals. They
      interpret the estimated Shapley values of this residual model as uncertainty explanations.

166   We want to explain uncertainties in neural network regressors. For this purpose, we leverage their
167   extension to deep distributional neural networks, which estimate the parameters of a specified out-
168   put distribution. For this work, we focus on deep heteroscedastic regression with a Gaussian output,
169   where we capture the mean and variance of the target, thereby directly modeling input dependence
170   of the output noise. Here, we consider a regression setting with n independent training examples
171
      {(xi , yi )}ni=1 with input feature vector xi ∈ Rk and target yi ∈ R, i = 1, . . . , n. Instead of
      providing a complete picture of the conditional distribution of the target, deep regression models
172

173
      parable loss functions. In contrast,    we assume a heteroscedastic Gaussian as the conditional dis-

      2.2   P OST- HOC E XPLANATION OF P REDICTIVE VARIANCE
194
195   Classic explainability methods explain the predicted class or point prediction. In contrast, we want
196   to explain the variance output in a heteroscedastic regression model. In these models, variance is
197   an additional output to which we can apply any existing, appropriate explainability method. In
198   principle, an uncertainty explanation can be achieved for any parametrized output distribution for
199   which an explicit formulation of the uncertainty is available. In the case of a Gaussian output
200   distribution, the application is most intuitive since the variance, as a parameter of the Gaussian,
201
      is a direct output of the neural network. Furthermore, unlike distributions such as the Poisson or
      exponential distributions, the variance is uncoupled from the mean output.
202
203   We employ model-agnostic and model-specific post-hoc explainability methods to explain uncer-
204   tainty. Model-specific methods are limited in the type of models that they can explain but may
205   offer advantages such as lower computational complexity. In contrast, model-agnostic methods
206   can be applied to any model (Adadi & Berrada, 2018). For our experiments, we combine the ap-
207
      proach described in Section 2.1 with multiple explainability methods and refer to this conjunction
      as Variance Feature Attribution (VFA) flavors. As the first explainability method, we use Ker-
208
      nelSHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), a model-agnostic, local explainability method. KernelSHAP
209
      approximates Shapley values using a weighted linear surrogate model with an appropriate weight-
210   ing kernel (VFA-SHAP). We also employ Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017),
211   which is a local, model-specific method and assigns feature importance by integrating predictions
212   over a straight path from a baseline to the input (VFA-IG). Further, we use Layer-Wise Relevance
213   Propagation (LRP) (Bach et al., 2015), a local, model-specific explainability method developed for
214   neural networks where the importance is distributed backward to the input layer by layer weighted
215   by a neuron’s contribution (VFA-LRP). We compare the VFA flavors to CLUE, for which we have
      to train a variational autoencoder on the train data and apply the optimization as detailed by Antoran


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      et al. (2021). We calculate CLUE feature attributions as the absolute differences between the feature
217   vector of the counterfactual and its original input feature vector. CLUE is local and model-specific.
218   Further, we reimplement InfoSHAP for regression, which estimates the uncertainty of an XGBoost
219   model by a second auxiliary XGBoost model trained on the residuals of the base model. The uncer-
220   tainty attribution is attained by estimating the Shapley values of the auxiliary model (Watson et al.,
221   2023). As InfoSHAP builds on SHAP, it is a model-agnostic, local explainability method. We can
222   also attain global explanations from these local methods by averaging explanations for each feature

224   2016; Draelos & Carin, 2021). HiResCAM is a local and model-specific method that extracts an
225
      explanation by weighting the last feature map with the gradient of the output with respect to this last
      layer feature map. We follow Chefer et al. (2021) and apply HiResCAM to the [CLS] token of the
226
      last attention layer.

      2.5     B ENCHMARKING S ETUP
325
326   We divide our benchmark into two stages. First, we qualitatively and quantitatively evaluate the
327   uncertainty explanation methods in a controlled setting on a synthetically generated dataset. Second,
328   we investigate the same methods concerning their local RRA and RMA, faithfulness, and robustness
329   on synthetic and real-world data.
330   In the first stage of our benchmark, we aim to detect global drivers of uncertainty in a synthetic
331   setting. We fit a deep neural network of four hidden layers with 64, 64, 64, and 32 units. The
332   network has two output neurons for the mean and variance prediction. During training, we apply
333   dropout regularization with a dropout probability of 0.1 to the first two layers. We use the Adam
334   optimizer and a batch size of 64. We pre-train using the MSE and fine-tune the model using the
335   GNLL as the loss function. In both training phases, we stop training when improvement on the
336   validation set ceases and choose the model weights that resulted in the smallest validation loss. For
337   each feature, we attain a global feature importance measure as the mean absolute estimated variance
338
      feature attributions over all or a specific subset of test instances, which we then analyze using GRA
      and GMA.
339
340   We follow the same model training procedure for the second benchmarking stage, evaluating accu-
341   racy, faithfulness, and robustness. Estimating the local RRA and RMA for a given method requires
342   prior knowledge of features affecting the explained quantity, i.e., uncertainty. As this is not the case
343   for our selected real-world datasets, we augment them with synthetic noise that we aim to detect,
344
      effectively creating a semi-synthetic setting. For the three real-world datasets, we consider two sce-
      narios. We add five noise features to the datasets and heteroscedastic Gaussian noise to the targets
345
      with a standard deviation correlating with the features. Since the real-world datasets are small, we
346
      first use a simple noise model where the absolute sum of the noise features is the standard deviation
347   of the noise distribution, a setting referred to as 1-S. In a second scenario, 50-C, we use the more
348   complex polynomial noise model described in Section 2.4.1. To provide more data to the model in
349   the complex noise scenario, we replicate each data point in the train sets 50 times before sampling
350   additional uncertainty features and target noise. For the synthetic datasets, we similarly perform
351   experiments with a simple (S) and complex (C) noise model but without adjusting the dataset size.
352
      We evaluate the robustness of the uncertainty explanation methods for each dataset by estimating the
353   local Lipschitz continuity for 200 randomly selected data points from the test set. For each selected
354
      point xi , we compute a local Lipschitz estimate L̂(xi ) by introducing 100 perturbations. For each
355   feature, we sample a perturbation from a uniform distribution centered at the feature value with a
356   range of 2% of the range of the feature in the train set1 . This is not applicable to LSAT’s categorical
357   features. Instead, we resort to the discrete definition of local Lipschitz continuity. Specifically, we
358   compute L̂(xi ) for 200 data points sampled from the test set such that their neighborhood Nϵ (xi )
359   with ϵ = 0.2 contains more than five instances.
360
      To evaluate faithfulness, we apply standard Gaussian noise to perturb the three globally most impor-
361
      tant uncertainty drivers of the test data. We use real-world datasets without added noise features or
362
      other augmentations for this evaluation. We omit the LSAT dataset as it mainly contains categorical
363   features for which continuous perturbations lack meaning.
364
365   We note that we only add synthetic noise to estimate accuracy metrics, i.e. when calculating RRA
366
      and RMA. For all other experiments, we use the real-world datasets as is.

519   4     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
520
521
      We presented a straightforward strategy for explaining predictive aleatoric uncertainties, which re-
522
      quires minimal modifications to existing neural network regressors. We use neural networks with a
523   Gaussian output distribution to estimate uncertainty and apply explanation methods to the variance
524   output. Thereby, we can provide explanations of uncertainty estimates. In synthetic experiments, the
525   resulting explanations outperform alternative methods. As seen in the experiments with low uncer-
526   tainty instances, we can also explain how features contribute to a model’s certainty, which is relevant
527   in high-risk applications. Since conventional evaluation metrics are not always directly applicable,
528   we have introduced an evaluation protocol to assess uncertainty explainers. Parts of our evaluation
529   depend on the knowledge of ground truth noise sources. This necessitated the incorporation of syn-
530   thetic noise, which may deviate from the arbitrarily complex real-world noise patterns. We extend
531
      unsupervised explanation quality metrics for accuracy, faithfulness, and robustness to uncertainty
      attributions. In our benchmark, VFA compares favorably to CLUE and InfoSHAP. We establish that
532
      the selection of the explanation method and the dataset-dependent choice of the uncertainty esti-
533
      mation method are significant variables in generating high-fidelity uncertainty explanations. This
534   is highlighted by the performance of VFA-LRP compared to VFA-SHAP. LRP was initially de-
535   signed for image data, and a naive application to tabular data might not be ideal. Generally, as
536   we combine deep heteroscedastic regression with existing XAI methods, we inherit all the benefits
537   and limitations of these methods, including computational complexity. Future work might involve
538   the application to non-deep learning models and studying synergies in explaining point and uncer-
539   tainty predictions. For example, in the context of explainable active learning (Ghai et al., 2021), a
      visualization of both explainability modes could be beneficial.

749
750   We initially separated noise and mean-influencing features in the synthetic data to enable unambigu-
751   ous evaluations. However, in reality, we expect noise features to overlap with features influencing
752   the mean. Specifically, we add five mixed features to the existing setup. These mixed features are
753   generated using the same polynomial model as described in Section 2.4.1, but they contribute to both
754   the mean and the heteroscedastic noise. Thereby, the dataset comprises 80 features: 70 that exclu-
755   sively influence the target’s mean, five that exclusively affect the noise variance of the conditional
      target distribution, and five mixed features that impact both aspects.
