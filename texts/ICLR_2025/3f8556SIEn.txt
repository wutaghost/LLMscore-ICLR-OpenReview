009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Text-guided diffusion models make a paradigm shift in audio generation, facilitating
012               the adaptability of source audio to conform to specific textual prompts. Recent
013               works introduce inversion techniques, like DDIM inversion, to zero-shot editing,
014               exploiting pretrained diffusion models for audio modification. Nonetheless, our
015               investigation exposes that DDIM inversion suffers from an accumulation of errors
016               across each diffusion step, undermining its efficacy. Moreover, existing editing
017               methods fail to achieve effective complex non-rigid music editing while maintaining
018               essential content preservation and high editing fidelity. To counteract these issues,
019
                  we introduce the Disentangled Inversion technique to disentangle the diffusion
                  process into triple branches, rectifying the deviated path of the source branch
020
                  caused by DDIM inversion. In addition, we propose the Harmonized Attention
021
                  Control framework, which unifies the mutual self-attention control and cross-
022               attention control with an intermediate Harmonic Branch to progressively achieve
023               the desired harmonic and melodic information in the target music. Collectively,
024               these innovations comprise the Disentangled Inversion Control (DIC) framework,
025               enabling accurate music editing while safeguarding content integrity. To benchmark
026               audio editing efficacy, we introduce ZoME-Bench, a comprehensive music editing
027               benchmark hosting 1,100 samples spread across ten distinct editing categories.
028               This facilitates both zero-shot and instruction-based music editing tasks. Our
029               method achieves unparalleled performance in edit fidelity and essential content
030
                  preservation, outperforming contemporary state-of-the-art inversion techniques.1
                  Both code and benchmark will be released.

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Text-guided diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2020; Peebles & Xie, 2023) have
037   made great progress in audio generation (Evans et al., 2024a;b), leveraging their impressive capability
038
      for realistic and varied outputs. In particular, these models (Liu et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023; Liu
      et al., 2024) provide the foundation for prompt-based audio editing, offering new opportunities to
039
      modify audio landscapes for specific textual prompts. Early audio editing strategies rely on training
040
      models from scratch (Copet et al., 2023; Agostinelli et al., 2023) or test-time optimization (Paissan
041   et al., 2023; Plitsis et al., 2024), hampered by intensive computational demands. Recent works (Manor
042   & Michaeli, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) in zero-shot audio editing have been made through Denoising
043   Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020) and Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models
044   (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) inversion techniques, but challenges remain.
045
      Key among these challenges are maintaining fidelity of editing - ensuring the editing aligns with
046
      the provided instructions - and essential content preservation, ensuring that the unaltered musical
047   attributes in the target prompts remain unchanged. However, balancing these objectives involves a
048   careful exchange of information between the source and target branch in diffusion processes, with
049   existing inversion methods like DDIM proving sub-optimal for conditional diffusion models (Mokady
050   et al., 2023). Enhanced versions of edit-friendly DDPM inversion (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al.,
051   2024) make strides in preservation by imprinting the source onto the noise space. However, this
052   comes at the expense of reduced modification capabilities due to noise reduction.

      In this work, we rigorously examine the shortcomings of the DDIM inversion approach. Our
055   comprehensive analysis indicates that while techniques like DDIM inversion provide an editable
056   foundation for audio synthesis, they lack precision and may compromise the integrity of the original
057   audio. The primary issue stems from the assumption of perfect reversibility in the ordinary differential
058   equation (ODE) process, which is frequently not met during text-conditional editing. Consequently,
059   this leads to distortions during the inversion. And the implementation of Classifier-free Guidance
060   (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2021) aims to improve text adherence. However, it inadvertently amplifies the
061   accumulated errors from the inversion process. Moreover, attention control (Cao et al., 2023; Hertz
062   et al., 2022) has shown promise in achieving high fidelity and essential content preservation. For
063
      instance, MusicMagus (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces Cross-Attention Control for fine-grained music
      manipulation of rigid tasks. Nevertheless, these methods fail to resolve the issues of accumulated
064
      errors and struggle to achieve accurate editing for both rigid and non-rigid tasks, as illustrated in
065
      Figure 1. We introduce an innovative Disentangled Inversion Control technique to bridge this gap.
066   This technique has two main components: Harmonic Attention Control and Disentangled Inversion.
067   Cross-attention control (Hertz et al., 2022) and mutual self-attention control (Cao et al., 2023) have
068   demonstrated robust editing capabilities for rigid and non-rigid image editing tasks, respectively.
069   However, blindly combining these two approaches sequentially for music editing can result in sub-
070   optimal outcomes, particularly in the original dual-branch setup, where it struggles with global
071   attention refinement. To address this challenge, we introduce an intermediate branch called the
072   Harmonic Branch, designed to modify both rigid and non-rigid attributes in music progressively.
073   Furthermore, we disentangle the diffusion process into triple branches, correcting the deviation path
074
      caused by CFG in the source branch, which affects the essential content preservation. The other
      branches remain unchanged to ensure the highest possible edit fidelity.
075
076   Due to a lack of standardized benchmarks in audio editing, we introduce ZoME-Bench. The
077   first music editing benchmark consists of 1,100 audio clips, distributing them into 10 rigorously
078   curated editing categories across rigid and non-rigid tasks. Each entry is carefully assembled,
079   comprising a source prompt, a target prompt, human instruction, and blended words intended
080
      for editing. Experimental results on ZoME-Bench indicate that MEDIC outperforms baselines,
      achieving significant improvements in essential content preservation and editing fidelity. Additionally,
081
      MEDIC demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in the variable-length music editing settings of the
082
      MusicDelta dataset.
083
084   Our contributions can be summarized as follows. 1) We introduce a novel, training-free methodology
085   called Disentangled Inversion Control (DIC), designed to facilitate consistent manipulations of musi-
086
      cal elements and intricate non-rigid editing tasks. 2) An Harmonized Attention Control framework is
      introduced to unify cross-attention and mutual self-attention control, which enables both rigid and
087
      non-rigid editing. 3) We present Disentangled Inversion Technique to achieve superior results with
088
      negligible inversion error by branch disentanglement and correction, aiding in accurately editing the
089   music while preserving the content information. 4) We build a new benchmark for music editing,
090   named ZoME-Bench, which supports both zero-shot and instruction-based music editing.

100

106   Torre, 2023)/DDIM (Song et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024) inversion strategies to extract diffusion
107   noise vectors that match the source signal. Given its rapid and intuitive zero-shot editing capabilities,
      we have chosen inversion techniques as our primary research framework. In this work, we propose

      Inversion Techniques. The field of image inversion techniques has experienced significant progress
131   in recent years (Brooks et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022; Parmar et al., 2023; Dhariwal & Nichol,
132   2021). While the DDIM inversion proves effective for unconditional diffusion models (Song et al.,
133   2020; Wallace et al., 2023), its limitations become apparent when applied to text-guided diffusion
134   models, particularly when classifier-free guidance is necessary for meaningful editing. A range of
135   solutions (Mokady et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2022) have been proposed to address these challenges.
136   For example, Negative-Prompt Inversion strategically assigns conditioned text embeddings to Null-
137   Text embeddings, effectively reducing potential deviation during editing. Conversely, Edit Friendly
138   DDPM provides an alternative latent noise space via modified DDPM sample distributions, promoting
139
      the successful reconstruction of the desired image (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024). Optimization-
      based inversion methods using specific latent variables have recently gained popularity (Ju et al.,
140
      2023; Kawar et al., 2023). These are designed to minimize accumulated errors stemming from
141
      the DDIM inversion. Techniques such as Null-Text Inversion (Mokady et al., 2023) are promising,
142   but they introduce complexity and instability into the optimization process, making it relatively
143   time-consuming. Differently, we introduce a plug-in-plus method called Disentangled Inversion
144   Control to separate branches, achieving superior performance with considerably fewer computational
145   resources.

174   In the landscape of prompt-based editing (Dong et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023), the

      We diverge from the conventional use of cross-attention mechanisms and instead draw inspiration
      from the MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023) technique to refine music structure through self-attention
289
      queries. These queries adeptly navigate through non-rigid musical transformations, aligning with
290
      the designated musical theme or instrument (target prompt). The process begins by sketching the
291   foundational musical theme using the target’s self-attention components—Qtgt , K tgt , and V tgt .
292   This is followed by enriching this theme with elements resembling the thematic content from the
293   source (K src , V src ), steered by Qtgt . However, applying this attentive modulation uniformly over
294   all processing layers and through every denoising step might result in a composition excessively
295   mirroring the source. Consequently, echoing the ethos of MasaCtrl, our proposed solution selectively
296   employs mutual self-attention in the decoder portion of our music editing U-Net, initiated after a set
297   number of denoising iterations.

      3.4   D ISENTANGLED I NVERSION T ECHNIQUE
340
      Recognizing the limitations of using DDIM inversion without classifier-free guidance, we observe
341
      that it yields an easily modifiable but imprecise approximation of the original audio signal. Increasing
342   the guidance scale enhances editability, but sacrifices reconstruction accuracy due to latent code
343   deviation during editing.
344
345
      In order to address this issue, our methodology, which we have termed the Disentangled Inversion
      Technique disentangles into three branches: the source, the harmonic, and the target branch. This
346
      decoupling is designed to unleash the capabilities of each branch separately. For the source branch,
347
      we implement a targeted correction mechanism. By reintegrating the distance zt∗ − ztsrc into ztsrc , we
348   directly mitigate the deviation of the pathway. This straightforward adjustment effectively rectifies
349   the path and minimizes the accumulated errors introduced by both DDIM inversion and classifier-free
350   guidance, thereby enhancing consistency in the reconstructed audio. On the other hand, the target
351   branch and harmonic branch are left unmodified to fully leverage the innate capabilities of diffusion
352   models in generating the desired target audio. The branches’ untouched state ensures that the model’s
353   potential is utilized to its fullest extent, thereby ensuring the fidelity and integrity of the generated
354   audio. We will further discuss this in the Section 4.2.2. The algorithm of Disentangled Inversion
355   Technique has been outlined in Algorithm 2.
356   Typical diffusion-based editing (Han et al., 2023b; Miyake et al., 2023) involves two parts: an
357   inversion process to get the diffusion space of the audio, and a forward process to perform editing
358   on the diffusion space. Disentangled Inversion can be plug-and-played into the forward process
359   and rectifies the deviation path step by step. Specifically, Disentangled Inversion first computes the
                             ∗         src                                      src
360   difference between zt−1    and zt−1  , then adds the difference back to zt−1  in DDIM forward. We only
                                                                              src
361   add the difference of the source prompt in latent space and update zt−1 , which is the key to retaining
362
      the editability of the target prompt’s latent space.

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we explored the burgeoning realm of text-guided diffusion models for audio generation,
530   recognizing their potential to reshape source audio in alignment with specific textual prompts. We
531
      proposed the Disentangled Inversion Control to support both rigid and non-rigid editing tasks. Instead
      of a two-branch setting, we add an intermediate branch named the harmonic branch to progressively
532
      integrate harmonic and melodic information in music by cross-attention control and mutual self-
533
      attention control. To counteract the accumulated errors caused by DDIM inversion and CFG, we
534   introduced a simple but effective method named disentangled inversion to separate the diffusion
535   process into triple branches and eliminate the latent discrepancy distance in the source branch. Our
536   comprehensive evaluations, conducted on the ZoME-Bench—a robust benchmark for music editing
537   comprising 1,100 samples across 10 varied editing categories—attested to the superiority of our
538   methods. And the experiment results on variable length test sets and ablation studies further validated
539   the effectiveness and robustness of our method. We envisage that our work could serve as a basis for
      future zero-shot music editing studies.

874   For our evaluation, we utilize the pre-trained AudioLDM 2-Music model (Liu et al., 2023b). Our
875
      assessment employs a comprehensive set of metrics, namely CLAP, LPAPS, Structure Distance, and
      FAD. These metrics are calculated using the CLAP models available in the AudioLDM eval package,
876
      which is accessible at https://github.com/haoheliu/audioldm_eval. In line with the
877
      methodology described by Manor & Michaeli (2024), we apply a forward guidance of 3 and a reverse
878   guidance scale of 12 for DDPM inversion. For the DDIM inversion, the guidance scale is set to 5,
879   while for SDEdit, we employ a guidance scale of 12. The forward guidance of MEDIC is 1 while
880   the reverse scale is 5. We chose these values by exploring different guidance scales, as discussed in
881   Appendix D. We conduct all experiments in NVIDIA 4090. For our evaluation, we have selected
882   the public pre-trained AudioLDM 2-Music model (Liu et al., 2023b). To ensure a thorough and
883   multidimensional assessment, we measure performance using a suite of metrics that includes CLAP,
884   LPAPS, Structure Distance, and FAD and conduct on an NVIDIA 4090. The computation of these
885   metrics is facilitated by the CLAP models provided within the AudioLDM eval package, which is
886
      publicly available at https://github.com/haoheliu/audioldm_eval.
887   Our methodology is aligned with the protocol established by Manor & Michaeli (2024), where we
888   have adopted a forward guidance scale of 3 and a reverse guidance scale of 12 for DDPM inversion.
889   In contrast, the DDIM inversion employs a guidance scale of 5, and SDEdit utilizes a guidance scale
890   of 12. For Disentangled Inversion Control, we have determined the forward guidance to be 1 and the
891   reverse scale to be 5. These specific guidance scale values are selected after extensive experimental
892
      exploration, the details of which are discussed in Appendix D.

      C.1    M ETRICS
895
896
      Objective Metrics There are details about four metrics to evaluate the performance of our novel
897
      Disentangled Inversion Control framework: (1) CLAP Score (Elizalde et al., 2023): This criterion
898
      evaluates the degree to which the output conforms to the specified target prompt. (2) Struture
899   Distance (Ju et al., 2023): Leveraging self-similarity of audio features to measure the structure
900   distance between the source and edited audio. (3) LPAPS (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021; Paissan et al.,
901   2023): An audio adaptation of the Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al.,
902   2018), this measure evaluates the consistency of the edited audio with the source audio. (4) FAD
903   (Fréchet Audio Distance) (Kilgour et al., 2018): Analogous to the FID used in image analysis, this
904   metric calculates the distance between two distributions of audio signals.
905   Subjective Metrics To directly reflect the quality of the audio generated, we carry out MOS (Mean
906   Opinion Score) tests. These tests involve scoring two aspects: MOS-Q, which assesses the edited
907   quality of the audio, and MOS-P, which measures the content preservation of edited audio.
908
      For assessing editing fidelity, the evaluators were specifically directed to “Does the natural language
909
      description align with the audio?” They were provided with both the audio and its corresponding
910
      caption. They were then asked to give their subjective rating (MOS-Q) on a 20-100 Likert scale.
911
912   To assess essential content preservation, human evaluators were presented with source audio, target
913   audio, source prompt, and target prompt. They were then asked to answer the question, “To what
914
      extent does the target audio retain the essential features of the source audio, such as melody, instru-
      mentation, and overall style?” The raters had to select one of the options: “completely,” “mostly,” or
915
      “somewhat,” using a 20-100 Likert scale for their response.
916
917   Our crowd-sourced subjective evaluation tests were conducted via Amazon Mechanical Turk where
      participants were paid $8 hourly.
