                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advances in speech synthesis have enabled highly natural and speaker-
012
               adaptive speech generation by leveraging large-scale transcribed datasets. How-
013            ever, requiring tens of thousands of hours of annotated speech is impractical in
014            low-resource settings. Existing pre-trained speech models often utilize masked
015            speech inpainting for pre-training and show strong performance on various speech
016            generation tasks using limited task-specific data. Nonetheless, these models still
017            require external alignment mechanisms or extensive additional training to learn
018            alignment for alignment-aware tasks, such as text-to-speech (TTS). In this paper,
019            we propose A2 -Flow, an alignment-aware pre-training method for flow match-
020            ing models in speech synthesis. A2 -Flow integrates alignment learning directly
021
               into the pre-training process using discrete speech units, enabling the model to
               efficiently adapt to alignment-aware tasks without the need for separate align-
022
               ment mechanisms. By embedding alignment learning into pre-training, A2 -Flow
023
               facilitates alignment-free voice conversion (VC) and allows for faster convergence
024            during TTS fine-tuning, even with limited transcribed data, making it highly suit-
025            able for low-resource scenarios. Experimental results show that A2 -Flow superior
026            zero-shot VC performance compared to existing models and matches state-of-the-
027            art TTS performance using only a small amount of transcribed data. Moreover,
028            we demonstrate that A2 -Flow can be more efficiently applied to alignment-aware
029            speech synthesis tasks than existing pre-training methods, providing a practical
030            and scalable solution for high-quality speech synthesis across diverse settings.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large-scale speech synthesis models have shown exceptional performance in generating highly nat-
036   ural and expressive speech across a wide range of emotions and voice styles, achieving impres-
037   sive zero-shot text-to-speech (TTS) capabilities even with minimal reference audio (Wang et al.,
038   2023; Kim et al., 2024; Le et al., 2023; Kharitonov et al., 2023; Eskimez et al., 2024). Many non-
039
      autoregressive zero-shot TTS models (Shen et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023b; Le et al., 2023) follow
      the approach of early non-autoregressive models (Ren et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020) by using a
040
      phoneme duration predictor to align phonemes with speech. This approach simplifies the training
041
      of generative decoders by allowing them to focus solely on speech generation rather than align-
042   ment modeling. However, the naturalness of the generated speech becomes highly dependent on the
043   performance of the duration predictor, and this approach limits the generative decoder’s ability to
044   capture richer information beyond alignment.
045
      Recent non-autoregressive TTS approaches have aimed to eliminate the need for external alignment
046
      models by learning alignment jointly with speech generation (Lee et al., 2024; Lovelace et al., 2024;
047
      Gao et al., 2023; Eskimez et al., 2024). Among these, E2TTS (Eskimez et al., 2024) uses a flow
048   matching decoder to jointly model text-speech alignment and speech generation, resulting in highly
049   natural prosody and improved generalization to new speakers. However, similar to other zero-shot
050   TTS models, E2TTS relies on a large amount of transcribed data, which is often unavailable in low-
051   resource settings. SpeechFlow (Liu et al., 2024) mitigates the need for large transcribed datasets by
052   using untranscribed speech data during pre-training and generalizes well across tasks such as TTS
053   with minimal fine-tuning. However, it does not learn alignment during pre-training, making it less
      effective for alignment-aware TTS models like E2TTS, as shown in our experiments.

054
      In this work, we introduce A2 -Flow, an alignment-aware pre-training method that integrates discrete
055   HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) units into E2TTS’s training framework to learn alignment between unit
056   sequences and speech frames. By using de-duplicated units that retain only phonetic content, A2 -
057   Flow effectively learns alignment without relying on external duration models. This allows for direct
058   application to zero-shot voice conversion, where phonetic content can be transferred to the target
059   speaker’s voice without additional fine-tuning. For TTS tasks, we fine-tune A2 -Flow with transcribed
060   data to jointly learn text-speech alignment, eliminating the need for separate alignment modules. To
061   further enhance pronunciation accuracy, we introduce a simple timestep shifting strategy, improving
062   text alignment early in the sampling process and enhancing overall pronunciation accuracy.
063   Our experimental results demonstrate that our pre-training model successfully captures the align-
064   ment between unit sequences and speech frames. By leveraging this pre-training approach, A2 -Flow
065   efficiently learns text-speech alignment with 500 hours of data and a few fine-tuning iterations,
066   achieving high pronunciation accuracy. Even with a limited amount of transcribed data, A2 -Flow
067   outperforms many existing zero-shot TTS models and achieves comparable results to E2TTS. More-
068   over, we show that A2 -Flow can effectively learn text-speech alignment also for other languages,
069
      demonstrating its capability as a multilingual pre-training method. This highlights the flexibility and
      scalability of A2 -Flow, making it a robust foundation for multilingual TTS systems in low-resource
070
      settings. Additionally, without any fine-tuning, the pre-trained model itself outperforms existing
071
      zero-shot voice conversion models by a large margin, further validating the effectiveness of our
072   pre-training approach.

083   The proposed framework leverages flow matching to model the distribution of mel-spectrograms
084   for various speech synthesis tasks. The core idea is to transform a sample x0 from a simple prior
085   distribution p0 (x) into a data distribution q(x) through a time-dependent vector field vt .

      2.1.2   M ASKED S PEECH M ODELING F RAMEWORK
116   To apply flow matching to masked speech modeling, we first randomly mask regions of the mel-
117   spectrogram x, using a binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}D×T , and define the masked input as xmask =
118   (1 − m) ⊙ x. Our masking strategy follows Voicebox (Le et al., 2023), where between 70% to 100%
119   of the mel-spectrogram is randomly masked, with a 10% probability of fully masking the entire
120   input.
121
      During training, the masked mel-spectrogram xmask is concatenated with a noisy mel-spectrogram
122   ϕt,x1 (x0 ) along the channel dimension for each timestep t. The model is then trained to inpaint the
123   masked regions using the surrounding context based on the following modified CFM loss:
124
                                                                                                          2
125      Lmasked-CFM (θ) = Et∼U [0,1],q(x1 ),p0 (x0 ) ∥m ⊙ (ut (ϕt,x1 (x0 )|x1 ) − vt (ϕt,x1 (x0 ); θ, c))∥ ,   (6)
126
      where c represents all conditioning inputs, including xmask .
127
128   During inference, given a reference speech xref , we concatenate xref with a masked region along
129   the temporal axis to form xref
                                   mask , which serves as the conditioning input. The model then fills in
130   the masked region of the mel-spectrogram using the estimated vector field, taking into account the
131
      speaker information from the reference speech. This enables the model to perform zero-shot speaker
      adaptation based on the reference speech.

      2.1.3   C ONDITIONING I NPUT FOR D IFFERENT M ODELS
135   The conditioning input c plays a crucial role in guiding the generative model during training and
136   inference. Below, we outline the different conditioning inputs used in Voicebox (Le et al., 2023),
137   SpeechFlow (Liu et al., 2024), and E2TTS (Eskimez et al., 2024).
138
139   Voicebox Voicebox is a zero-shot TTS model that uses the masked mel-spectrogram xmask along
140   with a sequence of aligned phoneme transcripts as the conditioning input c. During training, ground
141   truth alignments are used, while during inference, estimated phoneme durations from a separate
142   phoneme duration predictor are used to perform alignment and enable zero-shot TTS.
143
144   SpeechFlow SpeechFlow is a pre-training method that uses only the masked mel-spectrogram
145   xmask as the conditioning input c during pre-training and does not employ any additional condi-
146   tioning. During fine-tuning, task-specific conditioning inputs can be added, such as using aligned
147   phoneme transcripts for zero-shot TTS, similar to Voicebox.
148
149   E2TTS E2TTS is a zero-shot TTS model that uses xmask and an unaligned text input y as condi-
150   tioning inputs c. To match the length of y to the speech input, filler tokens are padded at the end
151   of y. E2TTS learns the alignment between the unaligned text input and speech without requiring a
152
      separate duration modeling module, distinguishing itself from Voicebox’s approach.
153   By using unaligned text input as a conditioning input, E2TTS jointly models text-speech alignment
154   without explicitly learning phoneme alignment, allowing it to generate more natural speech with a
155   simpler architecture compared to Voicebox. However, E2TTS still requires a large amount of paired
156   text-speech data for effective training. Although E2TTS uses 200,000 hours of untranscribed data
157   for pre-training (Wang et al., 2024), it still requires tens of thousands of hours of paired text-speech
158
      data and additional training iterations to learn alignment.
159   Similarly, while SpeechFlow is pre-trained using only masked speech modeling without any con-
160   ditioning, it can serve as a good initialization point for tasks that do not require explicit alignment
161   learning, such as Voicebox. However, it remains unclear whether SpeechFlow can effectively per-
      form joint alignment and masked speech modeling with limited data under the E2TTS framework.

187   We introduce A2 -Flow, an alignment-aware pre-training method that utilizes discrete HuBERT
188   units (Hsu et al., 2021). This approach integrates masked speech modeling and alignment learn-
189   ing, thereby making untranscribed speech data useful for downstream tasks like TTS. By jointly
190   learning masked speech and alignment, A2 -Flow facilitates the alignment process during fine-tuning
191   and enables more efficient text-speech alignment learning, even with a small amount of transcribed
192   data. This results in TTS systems that can synthesize highly natural and intelligible speech without
193
      the need for external alignment mechanisms.
194   To achieve alignment learning during the pre-training stage, we incorporate discrete HuBERT
195   units—self-supervised speech representations obtained from untranscribed data—into the training
196   process. These units primarily capture phonetic content, enabling the model to learn alignment be-
197   tween the units and the corresponding speech frames. For joint learning of alignment and masked
198   speech modeling, we directly adopt the E2TTS training approach, where text inputs are replaced
199
      with discrete unit representations during pre-training, as depicted in Fig. 1.

      Alignment-Aware Pre-Training Our strategy for alignment-aware pre-training involves remov-
      ing alignment information from discrete speech units, allowing the model to relearn unit alignment
202
      directly during pre-training. To begin, we extract continuous speech representations from 16kHz
203
      speech waveforms using the HuBERT model and generate a 50Hz discrete unit sequence u50Hz
204   using HuBERT’s k-means quantizer. This 50Hz unit sequence represents speech as a sequence of
205   indices ranging from 0 to K − 1, where K is the number of clusters in the k-means quantizer. As
206   shown in Fig. 1, these 50Hz unit sequences often contain repeated indices for continuous speech
207   segments. Since u50Hz is already aligned with the speech, we remove these repetitions to eliminate
208   duration information, resulting in a de-duplicated unit sequence u = [u1 , ..., uL ].
209
      In A2 -Flow, we adopt a similar strategy to E2TTS by padding the de-duplicated unit sequence u
210   with filler tokens F to match the length of the corresponding mel-spectrogram x, creating a padded
211   sequence uPAD = [u1 , ..., uL , F, ..., F ]. This padded sequence is concatenated with xmask and used
212   as part of the conditioning input c to predict the masked regions by optimizing the training objective
213   defined in Eq. 6. During pre-training, A2 -Flow uses the de-duplicated units as additional context to
214   guide the inpainting of the masked regions. This approach helps the model learn to extract speaker-
215   specific and acoustic characteristics from the surrounding regions while aligning the de-duplicated
      units with the corresponding speech.

      For multilingual pre-training, we include language IDs as an additional input to the decoder to

220   Voice Conversion Unlike pre-training methods (Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), A2 -Flow
221   can be directly used for voice conversion tasks without requiring any additional fine-tuning. By
222   leveraging discrete HuBERT units that capture detailed phonetic content, A2 -Flow can effectively
223   convert the source speaker’s speech into the target speaker’s voice, achieving high-quality outputs
224   with minimal adjustment.
225   For voice conversion, we extract de-duplicated unit sequences usrc and uref from the source and
226   target speech, respectively, and concatenate the mel-spectrogram of the target reference speech xref
227   with a mask of the source length. Next, we concatenate the extracted de-duplicated sequences u =
228   [uref ; usrc ] and pad them with filler tokens to match the combined length of xref and the masked
229   source region. Feeding this sequence into the pre-trained flow matching model, the model generates
230   speech for the masked region by combining the content of usrc (source speech) with the speaker
231   characteristics of xref (target reference speech). This results in voice-converted speech that aligns
232
      the source content with the target speaker’s voice.
233   Through alignment-aware pre-training, A2 -Flow can generate speech samples from the de-
234   duplicated unit sequence, where duration information has been removed, rather than relying on
235   the original 50Hz unit representation. This flexibility allows A2 -Flow to perform voice conversion
236   with varied alignments, providing more diverse and natural outputs compared to conventional voice
237   conversion methods that depend solely on the original unit sequence.

      metrics and methods for both voice conversion and text-to-speech (TTS) tasks.

      Model Architecture We employ a modified Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023)
271   architecture by removing the 2D patchify layers. Our model configuration is identical to the DiT-L
272   variant, using a decoder with a hidden size of 1024, 16 attention heads, and a total of 24 trans-
273   former layers, resulting in 450M parameters. The model integrates Adaptive LayerNorm (AdaLN)
274   to incorporate embeddings of both the flow matching timestep t and the language ID used during
275   multi-lingual pre-training, allowing for effective conditioning on these factors.
276
      During the pre-training phase, we extract discrete unit sequences using the HuBERT-Large
277   model (Hsu et al., 2021), trained on 220K hours of multilingual data. The unit sequences are obtained
278   using a k-means quantizer trained on the Expresso dataset (Nguyen et al., 2023) with K = 2000
279   clusters. We utilize pre-trained checkpoints made available through the textlesslib library1 .
280
281   Data For pre-training, we use a total of 40K hours of speech data, which includes 37K hours
282   of English data from LibriTTS (Zen et al., 2019), LibriVox (Kearns, 2014) and Multilingual Lib-
283   riSpeech (MLS) (Pratap et al., 2020) datasets, combined with 3K hours of multi-lingual data from
284   the CML dataset, covering seven different languages. To fine-tune the pre-trained model for TTS,
285   we use the transcribed data from the LibriTTS dataset for English, and transcribed data from three
286   other languages (German, French, and Spanish) in the CML dataset to build separate TTS models
287
      for each language. The CML transcribed data includes 1500 hours of German, 440 hours of Spanish,
      and 280 hours of French speech. All speech data is resampled to 22kHz. We convert each 22kHz
288
      waveform into an 80-bin log-scale mel-spectrogram using a window length of 1024, hop length of
289
      256, and frequency range of fmin = 0 to fmax = 11025. These mel-spectrograms serve as the data x
290   used for flow matching in our experiments.
291
292   Training and Fine-tuning During pre-training, we use AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of
293   1e−4 . We train the model for a total of 700K iterations on 32 A100 GPUs, with a batch size of 4
294   per GPU. For fine-tuning on the TTS task, we initialize the model with the pre-trained weights, and
295   the optimizer is re-initialized. We fine-tune the pre-trained model separately on the transcribed data
296   from LibriTTS and CML-German, CML-French, and CML-Spanish datasets. For all fine-tuning
297   processes, we lower the learning rate to a peak value of 2e−5 , which is reached over 5000 iterations
298   using a warm-up schedule. We fine-tune the pre-trained model for 150K iterations on 8 A100 GPUs,
299   with a batch size of 4 per GPU. After reaching the peak learning rate, it is linearly decayed to zero
300
      over the remaining iterations.
301
302
      Inference During inference, we solve the ordinary differential equation using the Euler method,
      with 32 sampling steps and a default classifier-free guidance scale of γ = 2. For voice conversion, we
303
      set the default timestep shifting parameter to α = 1 to perform uniform timestep sampling, while for
304
      TTS, we use α = 3 to improve pronunciation accuracy. Once the mel-spectrograms are generated
305   by the flow matching decoder, we convert them into 22kHz waveforms using a BigVGAN-based
306   vocoder (Lee et al.)

308   Baselines for Voice Conversion We compare the performance of A2 -Flow against three voice
309   conversion baselines: Any-to-Any VC (Kovela et al., 2023), UnitSpeech (Kim et al., 2023a), and
310   SelfVC (Neekhara et al., 2024) on the LibriSpeech test-clean dataset. The Any-to-Any VC model is
311   trained on speakers from the test-clean dataset, making it a suitable many-to-many voice conversion
312   baseline that has already learned from reference speakers. UnitSpeech performs speaker adaptation
313   by fine-tuning for 500 iterations on a given reference audio, which makes it a fine-tuning-based
314   voice conversion baseline. Lastly, SelfVC, a recently proposed zero-shot voice conversion model,
315
      has been shown to outperform several voice conversion models in zero-shot settings, making it a
      strong benchmark for comparison.

332   Evaluation and Metrics We evaluate zero-shot TTS performance using two evaluation tasks fol-
333   lowing the approach described in (Wang et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023). The first task is the continuation
334   task, where for all test samples between 4 to 10 seconds long, we provide the first 3 seconds of audio
335   along with the entire transcript and generate speech beyond the initial 3 seconds. The second task
336   is the cross-reference synthesis task, where for each test sample between 4 to 10 seconds long, we
337
      use the transcript of the sample and a randomly selected 3-second segment from another sample of
      the same speaker as the reference audio for zero-shot TTS. For each task, the generated samples
338
      are evaluated by measuring speaker similarity to the 3-second reference audio using a speaker sim-
339
      ilarity metric, and pronunciation accuracy is measured using the word error rate (WER) between
340   the ground truth transcript and the transcript obtained from an ASR model applied to the generated
341   audio.
342
343
      To evaluate voice conversion performance, we use all samples between 4 to 10 seconds long in the
      test set. Each sample is used as the source audio, and a 3-second segment is randomly extracted
344
      from a different speaker’s sample to serve as the reference audio. The model then generates speech
345
      that matches the content of the source audio while adopting the voice characteristics of the reference
346   speaker. We evaluate the pronunciation accuracy and speaker similarity of the generated speech
347   compared to the reference audio. Performance is reported as the average WER and SECS values
348   across 1130 pairs from the LibriSpeech test-clean set.

      For the speaker similarity metric, we follow (Wang et al., 2023) and use a WavLM-based speaker
350
      verification model (Chen et al., 2022) to map both samples to speaker embeddings and measure
351
      the cosine similarity between them. We measure speaker similarity between the reference ground
352   truth audio and the generated audio, as defined as SECS-O in the Voicebox (Le et al., 2023). For
353   pronunciation accuracy, we also follow (Wang et al., 2023) and use the same HuBERT-L-based ASR
354   model (Hsu et al., 2021) to measure the WER of English-generated speech, and for other languages,
355   we use the Whisper-large v2 (Radford et al., 2022) model to measure WER.
356
      For subjective evaluation, we generate zero-shot TTS samples using A2 -Flow and compare them
357
      against samples downloaded from the demo pages of SpeechFlow and E2TTS. We conduct A/B
358   tests with 100 human evaluators, asking them to choose the sample that more closely matches the
359   reference audio in terms of prosody, emotion, and timbre. Each evaluator performs 9 A/B tests when
360   comparing with SpeechFlow and 19 A/B tests when comparing with E2TTS.

      4.3    A BLATION S TUDY
501
502   We perform an ablation study on the pre-trained A2 -Flow model for TTS tasks, exploring the impact
503   of different sampling steps, classifier-free guidance scales (γ), and timestep shifting scales (α) on
504   cross-reference synthesis performance. Table 5 shows that setting classifier-free guidance scale γ =
505   1 results in significantly worse WER and SECS-O, confirming γ = 2 as the optimal value. A key
506   finding of this work is that, for a flow matching model trained to jointly model alignment in TTS,
507   using timestep shifting with α = 3, which samples more frequently from the noisier t regions, results
508
      in improved pronunciation accuracy and better speaker similarity compared to uniform timestep
      sampling (α = 1) at the same number of sampling iterations. Therefore, we use α = 3 as the default
509
      value. For Euler sampling steps, performance does not improve beyond 32 steps, while reducing
510
      steps to 16 leads to a drop in performance. Thus, we use 32 steps as the default setting.

      5     C ONCLUSION
526
      In this work, we proposed A2 -Flow, an alignment-aware pre-training approach tailored for speech
527
      synthesis tasks that involve generating natural phonetic content, such as voice conversion and text-to-
528
      speech (TTS). By incorporating de-duplicated unit sequences instead of text into the E2TTS frame-
529   work, our method enables the model to learn the alignment between input units and speech during
530   the pre-training phase. This alignment-aware pre-training can be directly applied to zero-shot voice
531   conversion tasks or used to build a TTS model that jointly models text-speech alignment with min-
532   imal fine-tuning. As a result, A2 -Flow achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art models
533   like E2TTS using only 1% of the transcribed data. Moreover, our method consistently outperforms
534   existing zero-shot TTS and zero-shot VC models by a significant margin. We further demonstrated
535   that A2 -Flow can model text-speech alignment for multiple languages, making it adaptable to mul-
536   tilingual TTS scenarios. Our findings highlight that A2 -Flow is better suited for alignment-aware
537
      tasks compared to pre-training methods like SpeechFlow, which do not incorporate any condition-
      ing. Since our approach can learn effectively with only speech data and language IDs, A2 -Flow
538
      offers a viable solution for scenarios where large-scale transcribed datasets are not available, and
539
      provides significant advantages for alignment-aware speech synthesis tasks.

      A.2.1    T OTAL L ENGTH P REDICTOR
727
728   A2 -Flow utilizes a total length predictor to estimate the duration of the speech corresponding to the
729   input text sequence. The total length predictor is composed of a convolution-based pre-network for
730   projecting the reference audio and a transformer network for predicting the total length from the
731   reference audio and the text input. Specifically, the total length predictor takes the reference audio
732   xref and the text input sequence y as inputs. The reference audio xref is first processed through the
733
      convolutional pre-network, which consists of three convolutional layers, each with a kernel size of 5,
      to project xref into an intermediate representation href . Then, a special token [SP C] is prepended to
734
      the text input sequence y to provide a placeholder for predicting the total length. The representations
735
      href , the special token [SP C], and the text input sequence y are concatenated along the time axis
736   and input into the transformer network.
737
738   The transformer network employs Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) to capture positional infor-
739
      mation, with separate rotary embeddings applied to the reference audio and text input sequence. The
      network is configured with a hidden size of 512, 8 layers, and 8 heads in the multi-head attention
740
      mechanism, resulting in a total of 30 million parameters. The output from the transformer network is
741
      taken from the position of the special token [SP C], which is then projected into a scalar value. This
742
      scalar represents the logarithmic value of the speech length d, scaled by a scaling factor s, i.e., log ds .
743   This design enables the total length predictor to effectively estimate the duration of the generated
744   speech while leveraging both reference audio and text input.

876   A.4      L IMITATION
877
878   A limitation of A2 -Flow is that it relies on self-supervised speech units for pre-training, making it
879   less generalizable to non-speech audio domains compared to methods like SpeechFlow, which do
880   not use any specific conditioning. Another limitation arises from the E2TTS framework itself, which
881   necessitates a separate total length predictor to estimate the overall speech duration, preventing
882   joint modeling of the total duration of generated speech. Investigating pre-training methods that
883
      can jointly model all aspects of speech generation including total length of the audio could be a
      promising direction for future research.

      A.5      H UMAN E VALUATION M ETHOD
887
      To compare the performance of A2 -Flow with other models, we used the Defined AI 3 platform to
888   conduct human evaluations. Each evaluation judgment was compensated at a rate of $0.15, and the
889   evaluations were performed by 100 human evaluators, all located in the United States. Evaluators
890   were selected based on the platform’s internal agreement score filtering criteria to ensure reliability.
891
      Each evaluator assessed all provided samples, with payments calculated based on the number of
892
      judgments completed. Specifically, evaluators were compensated for 19 judgments for comparisons
893
      with E2TTS and 9 judgments for comparisons with SpeechFlow. During the evaluation, each evalu-
894   ator was presented with a 3-second reference audio clip alongside two audio samples generated by
895   the models in a randomized order. The following instruction was provided:
896
897
      “Between the two samples, which one sounds closer to the reference in terms of prosody, emotion,
      and timbre? If the two samples sound equally similar to the reference, choose ‘Neither.’”
898
899   This setup encouraged evaluators to consider the overall quality of the generated audio in relation
900   to the reference audio, encompassing aspects such as prosody, emotion, and timbre, to determine
901   which sample was better.
