011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Automated segmentation of metallographic images containing multiple phases
014            such as martensite, ferrite, and pearlite is essential for quantifying different phases
015            and thereby helping in the understanding properties of materials. Segmentation of
016            these phases is challenging as they often exhibit overlapping boundaries, simi-
017            lar textures, and other more complexities that require a holistic understanding of
018
               the microstructures and correct phase representation within the image. To this
               end, we propose a novel approach for learning phase representations that cap-
019
               tures the subtle differences between phases. Our proposed Phase Learning Module
020
               strategically integrates phase ratio information with image encodings to produce
021            ratio-aware features that preserve critical spatial details. Materials scientists can
022            roughly estimate phase ratios by examining an image, and our proposed model
023            leverages this expertise. While we use expert-estimated phase ratios during in-
024            ference, we train a model using accurate phase ratios obtained from target mask
025            images. To our knowledge, this is the first use of class ratios as input in a deep
026            learning segmentation model that serves as constraints to guide consistent phase
027            proportions in predictions. Experimental results demonstrate segmentation perfor-
028            mance improvements on both private and public datasets, with a 5.65% increase
029
               in Dice scores on the private dataset and a 6.48% improvement on the MetalDAM
               dataset with only 1.07% increase in model parameters. Furthermore, visualiza-
030
               tions show that our approach leads to learning of more distinct and better phase
031
               representations across models. The code and private dataset will be made publicly
032            available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Microstructure analysis is a fundamental aspect of materials engineering, without which no scientific
039
      understanding of engineering materials can be achieved (Biswas et al., 2023b). Microstructures in
      material science refers to the arrangement of phases, grains, and defects in a material as observed
040
      under a microscope (Yuan et al., 2021). The properties of materials vary widely depending upon
041
      the microstructure specifications and underlying phase constituencies (Matthews, 1998). A phase is
042   a part of microstructure that has a distinct crystalline structure and chemical composition (Sanyal
043   et al., 2021). Accurate identification and segmentation of different phases in a microstructure can
044   lead to understanding the characteristics of a material (Martin, 2006).
045
      Metallographic images are obtained using optical microscopy (OM), scanning electron microscopes
046
      (SEM), Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD), among others, and are used to identify and charac-
047
      terize different phases in a microstructure (Nogara & Zarrouk, 2018; Gintalas & del Castillo, 2022).
048   While OM provides low-magnification images suitable for approximate assessments, it lacks the
049   resolution needed for fine microstructural features (Zhan et al., 2007). EBSD offers high-resolution
050   imaging and detailed phase information but involves high acquisition costs and requires expert inter-
051   pretations (Kim et al., 2021; Swain et al., 2023). SEM strikes a balance by providing detailed high
052   resolution images with lower cost and complexity compared to EBSD. However SEM images have
053   difficulty in segmentation due to the high visual similarity between different phases, overlapping
      boundaries, and complex textures as shown in Fig. 1. These challenges often lead to ambiguities

      in phase separation and makes traditional data-driven segmentation models prone to errors (Mollens
055   et al., 2022).

      The deep learning based methods used for material segmentation are mostly based on convolutional
      neural networks (CNNs) which learns kernel mappings from input to output data without explicitly
071
      designing kernels using domain knowledge (Na et al., 2021). Semantic segmentation was popularly
072
      used since it could perform phase segmentation by assigning each pixel in the image to one of the
073   pre-defined categories (Santos et al., 2019). DeCost et al. (2019) used PixelNet (Bansal et al., 2017)
074   to find microstructures but was limited only to a certain phase and not multi-phase segmentation. Lai
075   et al. (2009) explored segmentation on contrast and etched steel datasets acquired via SEM and OM
076   imaging. However, it was only optimized to handle images with high contrast variations. Durmaz
077   et al. (2021) proposed the use of U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015a) architecture for distinguish-
078   ing bainite phase regions from irregular ferrite phase and addressed the complex phase problem by
079   framing into binary segmentation task. The work of Luengo et al. (2022) not only included in-depth
080   analysis on complex microstructures by comparing supervised, semi-supervised and unsupervised
081
      methods but also proposed a new benchmark MetalDAM dataset for public evaluations. The com-
      parison found out the effectiveness of semantic segmentation in achieving high accuracy compared
082
      to other segmentation methods. Recently, Biswas et al. (2023a) performed phase segmentation using
083
      a union of attention guided U-Net models by using HSV, RGB and YUV color spaces of input image
084   to capture different characteristics of the phases.
085
086   All previous methods have performed segmentation of phases in metallographic images based solely
087
      on the visual semantics of the input images. However, it remains unclear whether the models ac-
      tually learn meaningful phase representations. Do the models develop a holistic understanding of
088
      the phases within a metallographic image, or do they merely perform pixel-based classification to
089
      accomplish the segmentation task? Fig. 2(a) shows the visualization of phase representations across
090   previously used models. The visualizations are performed from the output of the image encoder and
091   then Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is used to select three most important channels which
092   are then plotted out. It can be seen that the original image embeddings of the models lack in distin-
093   guishing different phases in the microstructures, indicating that the models do not fully understand
094   the phase characteristics, despite achieving reasonable segmentation performance.
095
      The use of foundation models like Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) demon-
096   strate the potential of incorporating additional inputs such as visual prompts to guide segmentation.
097   These models have shown that conditions or rough hints can significantly enhance segmentation
098   performance. Motivated by this approach, we observed that material scientists can roughly estimate
099   the phase ratio by examining a metallographic image. In this paper, we propose a novel approach
100   of learning the phase representations using the phase ratio as domain knowledge input into the
101   model. We perform adaptations on SAM using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) which is detailed in the
102   Appendix A.1.
103   Our method integrates phase ratio information into the neural network architecture through a ded-
104   icated ratio encoder. The ratio encoding is then combined with image encodings to produce ratio-
105   enhanced features. These enhanced features are refined further through spatial-aware encodings that
106   preserves the spatial relationships and boundaries between different phases. We also introduce reg-
107   ulators in our model that modulate the influence of domain knowledge and allow the network to
      perform well even when no ratio input is provided. The phase ratio is calculated from the ground

238   2.2   I MPLEMENTING P HASE L EARNING
239
240   Directly adding the phase ratio information to the image encoding can be ineffective due to the
241   fundamental difference in the dimensionality and representational spaces of these two inputs. While
242   the image encoding captures spatial information in a high-dimensional feature space, the phase ratio
243   is a low-dimensional feature representing global phase proportions. To bridge this gap, we introduce
244   a Ratio Encoder that transforms the phase ratio into a feature representation compatible with the
245   image encoding.
246   The ratio encoder consists of a 2-layer MLP that encodes the given phase ratio into a ratio encoding
247   having n channels. n denotes the number of phases in the image. The diagram of the ratio encoder is
248   presented in 4(b). After the final segmentation mask is obtained, the phase ratio is calculated again
249   and compared with the phase ratio of the ground truth image. This loss is calculated using Mean
250   Squared Error (MSE) loss Kato & Hotta (2021) and is used to train the ratio encoder to learn the
251   correct phase proportions.
252   If we were to directly add the phase ratio encoding to the image encoding without any further
253   processing, it might result in a poor integration. We verified this with our experiments, where
254   we observed a decrease of around 2% in segmentation accuracy. This degradation likely occurred
255   because the model lacks context regarding the phase ratio information. The model cannot correlate
256   the phase ratio information with the precise location of the phases in the image encoding which
257   results in a disjoint representation that fails to guide the segmentation process effectively.
258   In our proposed method, the image encoding is passed through the Feature Extractor (FE) module,
259   which is responsible for segregating relevant feature maps for each corresponding phase. The FE
260   module consists of a convolutional layer that concentrates the image features into n channels, with
261   each channel corresponding to a specific phase ratio. Each of these phase-specific feature maps is
262   then processed using coordinate convolutions Liu et al. (2018) to embed explicit spatial information
263   into the feature maps. This ensures that the model retains spatial context for each phase and allows
264   it to correspond the phase ratio information to the correct phase regions. Finally, the phase-wise
265   feature maps are concatenated and fused with the encoded ratio information from the Ratio Encoder
266
      using element-wise multiplication as can be seen in 4(a).
267   In the final step, the encodings are processed through the Feature Aggregator (FA) Module, which
268   fuses the spatially aware features from the previous stages with the original features generated by
269   the image encoder. This integration is crucial as it not only ensures that the segmentation output is
      not solely dependent on the phase ratio information but also preserves the original image features.


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      As a result, the model is able to perform well even in cases where the ratio input is inaccurate or
271   absent.
272
273   In FA-module, the spatially aware features and ratio-encoded features are first concatenated channel-
274
      wise. These concatenated features are then passed through a convolutional layer with a sigmoid
      activation function, which helps normalize the feature values and allows for non-linearity in the in-
275
      teraction between the image and ratio features. The output of the convolutional layer is then split
276
      into two branches, each multiplied element-wise with the original spatially aware features and ratio-
277   encoded features, respectively. The element-wise multiplication allows the model to modulate the
278   influence of each feature type dynamically. Finally, the two multiplied feature maps are combined
279   through an element-wise addition operation. This fused feature map is then passed through a fi-
280   nal convolutional layer to generate the output feature map, which serves as the final segmentation
281   prediction. This is illustrated in Figure 4(c).
282
      This fusion process is regulated by two key parameters: γ and δ. These regulators control the
283   influence of Phase Ratio on the final segmentation. A higher value of γ reduces the impact of phase
284   ratios. On the other hand, a higher value of δ increases the influence of the ratio encoder, allowing
285   the model to more heavily rely on the phase ratio guidance.

290   3.1   DATASET
291
292
      The only publicly available dataset with SEM-based multi-phase micrographs (containing more than
      two phases) is MetalDAM Luengo et al. (2022). It consists of 42 labeled images across five distinct
293
      classes: matrix, austenite, martensite/austenite, precipitates, and defects. In MetalDAM, binary
294
      masks were initially used as pre-annotations and were subsequently refined by industry experts.
295   Although this method provided labeled data, the manual refinement introduced some subjectivity
296   and potential inaccuracies into the annotations.
297
298
      For our experiments, we used MetalDAM dataset along with a private alloy steel microstructure
      dataset. The private dataset comprises of images captured using SEM and labeled with the assistance
299
      of EBSD data through a superlabeler, which provided more objective and detailed annotations. This
300
      approach reduces the subjectivity often present in other datasets and ensures more accurate phase
301   labeling. It contains a total of 24 alloy steel microstructure images captured with a SEM at varied
302   magnification levels (2700x magnification - 6 images, 3000x magnification - 10 images, and 5000x
303   magnification - 8 images). The samples have a tensile strength of 780 MPa. Each image includes
304   three types of microstructures or phases: Bainite, Ferrite, and Martensite.
305
      Both datasets (private and MetalDAM) were split into training, validation, and test sets with a 70-
306
      20-10 ratio, respectively. To address the limited number of images, we applied sliding window
307   techniques and various geometric and photometric augmentations to increase the effective size of
308   the dataset. These augmentations included flipping, rotation, scaling (magnification), intensity ad-
309   justments, gamma correction, and contrast-based transformations.

      3.2   E XPERIMENTS
312
313   Our models were implemented using the PyTorch framework and was run on a single NVIDIA Titan
314   RTX GPU, with an Intel Core i7 6700 CPU, running on the Ubuntu 22.10 operating system. The
315   models were trained with the Adam optimizer Kingma & Ba (2017), with an initial learning rate of
316   1 × 10−5 and a batch size of 8 for 40 epochs. We used Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss to calculate
317   the phase ratio deviation between the ground truth and predicted mask. A weighted sum of Dice
318   Coefficient and Cross-Entropy (Dice CE) loss was considered an appropriate metric to evaluate the
319   performance of the models Naser & Alavi (2023). The γ and δ parameters in the model that are used
320
      as regulators for feature aggregator were learned by the model during training. The phase ratio input
      during inference mimicks the expertise of the user having 90% accurateness and was calculated
321
      using Appendix A.2.

526   In this paper, we proposed a novel method of learning phase representations for microstructural
527   segmentation in metallographic images where we leveraged expert’s knowledge on phase ratios to
528
      improve segmentation performances. By integrating phase ratio information into the segmentation
      process, our method provided valuable domain constraints that guided the model to produce more
529
      accurate and spatially coherent segmentations. The experimental results on both the private dataset
530
      and the MetalDAM dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, with average improve-
531   ments of 5.64% and 6.48% in Dice scores, respectively. The primary advantage of our approach lies
532   in its ability to maintain robust performance even when phase ratio information is unavailable during
533   inference. By allowing experts to provide estimates of the phase ratios and the ability of our model to
534   adhere to the same, shows its understanding of the phase constituency in the microstructures. More-
535   over, our approach improves segmentation accuracy especially when input phase accuracy during
536   inference exceeds 66.2%. While our proposed method of phase ratio guidance demonstrates signif-
537   icant improvements, the need for user-provided phase ratios during inference introduces a potential
538   limitation which will be addressed in future research. Moreover, future research could explore ex-
539
      panding the our proposed method to other domains beyond metallographic images and potentially
      yielding interesting and broader applications of representation learning.

      A.1    A DAPTING SAM FOR M ICROSTRUCTURE S EGMENTATION
676
677   SAM is built on transformer architecture and has demonstrated remarkable effectiveness in various
678   domains such as natural language processing and image recognition tasks. SAM includes a vision
679   transformer-based image encoder for extracting image features, a prompt encoder for integrating
680   user interactions like bounding boxes, and a mask decoder that generates segmentation results and
681   confidence scores using the image embedding, prompt embedding, and output token. For our ap-
682   proach, we employed the base Vision Transformer (ViT) model as the image encoder. Extensive
683   evaluation indicated that larger ViT models, such as ViT Large and ViT Huge, offered only marginal
684
      improvements in accuracy while significantly increasing computational demands. The base ViT
      model consists of 12 transformer layers, with each block comprising a multi-head self-attention
685
      block and a Multi Layer Perceptron (MLP) block incorporating layer normalization.
686
687   To adapt SAM for steel microstructure segmentation, we modified the attention layers of the SAM
688   encoder using LoRA. LoRA modifies the attention mechanism in the transformer by introducing
689   low-rank matrices into the query and value computations. The key idea is to decompose the weight
690
      updates into two low-rank matrices, which reduces the number of trainable parameters and compu-
      tational complexity.
691
692   For a given weight matrix W ∈ Rd×d in the attention mechanism, LoRA decomposes it into A ∈
693   Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×d , where r << d. The modified weight matrix is given by W ′ = W + BA
694   where A and B are trainable parameters, while W is kept frozen. This decomposition allows the
695   model to efficiently learn task-specific adaptations without extensive retraining. In SAM encoder,
696
      each transformer layer’s multi-head self-attention mechanism is adapted using LoRA. Specifically,
      we modify the query (Q) and value (V ) computations as follows:
697
698
699                                           Q′ = Q + BQ AQ                                         (2)
700
701
                                             V ′ = V + B V AV                                        (3)

      process to optimize the contribution of both feature types.
