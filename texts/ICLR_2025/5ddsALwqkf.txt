009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper describes a semi-automatic pipeline to generate challenging question-
012            answer-decoy sets for understanding long videos. Many existing video datasets
013            and models are focused on short clips (10s-30s). While some long video datasets
014            do exist, they can often be solved by powerful image models applied per frame
015            (and often to very few frames) in a video, and are usually manually annotated at
016            high cost. In order to mitigate both these problems, we propose a scalable dataset
017            creation pipeline which leverages large models (VLMs and LLMs), to automatically
018
               generate dense, time-aligned video captions, as well as tough question answer
               decoy sets for video segments (up to 15 minutes in length). Our dataset Neptune
019
               covers a broad range of long video reasoning abilities and consists of a subset that
020
               emphasizes multimodal reasoning. Since existing metrics for open-ended question
021            answering are either rule-based or may rely on proprietary models, we provide a
022            new open source model-based metric (GEM) to score open-ended responses on
023            Neptune. Benchmark evaluations reveal that current open-source long video models
024            perform poorly on Neptune, particularly on questions testing temporal ordering,
025            counting and state changes. Through Neptune, we aim to spur the development of
026            more advanced models capable of understanding long videos.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Videos are experiencing an explosion moment online, with new research constantly pushing the
032   frontier for video and language tasks such as video question answering (VideoQA) (Xu et al., 2017;
033
      Zhong et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Mangalam et al., 2023). Early video and
      language models, while adept at VideoQA, have largely focused on short, trimmed clips (less than
034
      1 minute long (Yu et al., 2019a; Xiao et al., 2021)). The recent release of powerful, longer context
035
      multimodal models (eg. Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024) and GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023)), however,
036   has ushered in the promise of models being able to reason over millions of tokens, covering longer
037   stretches of videos (many minutes long).
038
039
      While promising, these claims are often evidenced by qualitative examples, or results on small-size
      datasets – for example the 1H-VideoQA (Reid et al., 2024) benchmark, which while valuable, only
040
      consists of 125 questions. Popular video benchmarks for question answering still tend to focus on
041
      short, trimmed clips (e.g., Next-QA (Xiao et al., 2021)). Other datasets that do contain longer videos
042   are often ‘short-term’ benchmarks disguised as long-term ones, evidenced by models that are able to
043   solve them with a single (or a few) frames (e.g. some tasks on the LVU dataset (Wu & Krahenbuhl,
044   2021) such as scene prediction of movies). Other long video datasets may contain strong linguistic
045   biases in multiple choice evaluation, as shown by MoreVQA (Min et al., 2024), which gets strong
046   performance on EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2023) without access to the video at all, or can be
047   solved with external internet knowledge, such as those made from popular movies (Li et al., 2023d).
048   A key challenge in creating a truly long form video understanding dataset is the significant manual
049   cost required to select, watch, understand and annotate long videos with free-form natural language.
050   Answering challenging questions about longer videos is often a multimodal (as it may involve
051   listening to the audio track in addition to watching the video), and non-sequential endeavour (as
052   sometimes it is necessary to rewind and rewatch key parts to answer a question). Proposing suitable
053   high-level questions that are not trivially solved by a few frames is also tricky for humans to do
      consistently and with adequate diversity. The key aim of this paper is to solve this challenge by

077

079   Results show that our semi-automatic pipeline almost halves rater effort. Our dataset is called
080   Neptune1 , and covers a diverse range of videos, is multimodal (requires audio and visual information),
081   and poses challenging questions for videos that test a variety of reasoning abilities over long time
082   horizons. Neptune allows for two modes of evaluation: multiple-choice and open-ended question
083   answering. Since existing metrics for open-ended question answering are either rule-based and
084   derived from captioning (WUPS (Wu & Palmer, 1994), CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015), etc) or are

086   model on a generic answer equivalence dataset (Bulian et al., 2022) to score question answering
087

      new metric Gemma Equivalence Metric (GEM).
088
089   To summarise, we make the following contributions: (i) We propose a scalable pipeline to generate
090   complex QAD annotations for any video that halves rater time compared to manual annotation. (ii)
091   We use this pipeline to generate the Neptune evaluation-only dataset, which consists of 3,268 QAD
092   annotations for 2,405 videos. We also release a challenging subset, N EPTUNE -MMH for which vision
093
      plays an important role. (iii) We provide both multiple choice and open-ended evaluation metrics.
      For the latter, we propose a new open-ended metric called Gemma Equivalence Metric (GEM) which
094
      outperforms rule-based metrics on a manually annotated answer equivalence dataset; and finally (iv)
095
      We provide benchmarking and ablations of state-of-the-art VideoQA models on the Neptune sets.
096   Benchmarking shows a significant gap between open-source video models and properietary models
097   such as Gemini-1.5 and GPT-4. All data will be released publicly to the research community.

      ing multimodal video understanding systems’ ability to reason about videos (Xu et al., 2017; Zhong
103
      et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Mangalam et al., 2023). Vision and language models
104
      for this task can be broadly classified into three categories: (i) early end-to-end VLMs for this task
105   which typically consists of strong vision and language encoders/decoders, such as Flamingo (Alayrac

      Video QA Benchmarks: Key datasets have pushed towards assessing reasoning for temporal ques-
      tions (Grunde-McLaughlin et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021), longer videos (Yu et al.,
118
      2019a; Mangalam et al., 2023), as well as focusing on diverse domains like instructional (Yang et al.,
119
      2021) and egocentric videos (Gao et al., 2021; Mangalam et al., 2023). We summarise existing
120   VideoQA benchmarks in a table provided in the appendix. Most datasets either focus on shorter
121   videos (less than 100s), or are short video datasets ‘in disguise’, and can actually be solved with
122   a few frames (e.g. ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al., 2019b) or MovieQA (Tapaswi et al., 2016)). 1H-
123   VideoQA (Reid et al., 2024) consists of videos longer than 1 hour, but is limited to 125 questions and
124   is closed-source. Like Neptune, ActivityNet-RTL (Huang et al., 2024), CinePile (Rawal et al., 2024)
125   and EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2023) are generated by prompting LLMs, but cover only limited
126   domains and rely on existing annotations while Neptune covers a much broader spectrum of video
127   types and its pipeline is applicable to arbitrary videos. Most importantly, EgoSchema also has strong
128
      linguistic biases, while Neptune mitigates these through filtering (Sec. 5). Unlike other benchmarks
      which come with their own training sets (eg. MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016), ActivityNet (Yu et al.,
129
      2019a)), we propose a generalisation-focused zero-shot evaluation regime. The goal for Neptune is
130
      to benchmark any model, pre-trained with any external dataset or task, in order to assess real-world
131   domain transfer. Hence we release test sets only. More discussion on related datasets and dataset
132   pipelines is provided in the appendix.
133
134
      Metrics for open-ended VideoQA: Earlier QA datasets consisted of short answers (Xiao et al.,
      2021) (sometimes a single word), typically from a closed set, and therefore metrics such as accuracy
135
      or accuracy with exact match (EM) can be applied. As datasets have evolved with more real-
136
      world annotation (longer, open-set answers), designing a metric becomes challenging. Existing
137   rule-based metrics for captioning, such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004) and
138   CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) can be applied, however they all primarily measure n-gram overlap,
139   and do not capture the inherent subjectivity of the task, where different phrasing is often equally
140   valid. Other metrics for captioning include SPICE (Anderson et al., 2016) (adds action and object
141   relationships), while model-based metrics using earlier language models or image-language models
142   include BERT-Score (Zhang et al., 2020), BERT-Score++ (Yi et al., 2020) (fine-tunes BERT for
143   image captioning), LEIC (Cui et al., 2018), NUBIA (Kane et al., 2020), TIGEr (Jiang et al., 2019),
144   CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), and EMScore (Shi et al., 2022). For answer equivalence specifically,
145
      token F1 and exact match (EM) have been used, but suffer many of the same shortcomings that
      rule-based metrics do, and EM is often too strict for open-ended eval. BEM (Bulian et al., 2022)
146
      finetunes BERT on an answer-equivalence dataset, and shows that this provides a better score for
147
      QA. Recently, LLMs trained with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) that already
148   exhibit strong human alignment (Bubeck et al., 2023) are used in works such as VideoChatGPT (Maaz
149   et al., 2023) and MovieChat (Song et al., 2023) (LLM-as-a-judge). A challenge here is that the models
150   used (ChatGPT) are called via proprietary APIs, where the underlying model may be non-static,
151   thereby leading to non-reproducability in the metric. Instead, we take a state-of-the-art open-sourced
152   lightweight language model (Team et al., 2024a) and finetune it on a public answer equivalence
153   dataset (Bulian et al., 2022), to create an open-source, static, model-based evaluation metric.

238   4    DATASET C REATION P IPELINE
239
240   An overview of our pipeline can be found in Fig. 1. In order to reduce human effort, we leverage
241   automatic tools to (i) find suitable videos (ii) extract useful signals and then (iii) automatically
242   generate video level captions and QADs. We then send the data to human raters for the final manual
243   verification stages. Our pipeline can be applied to any generic YouTube video. This is unlike
244   existing data pipelines such as those used to create EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2023), which relies
245
      on human generated captions, SFD (Ghermi et al., 2024) and other movie related datasets, which
      requires movie titles, loglines and synopses (human-written), or MLVU (Zhou et al., 2024), which
246
      re-uses annotations from existing datasets for many of their tasks. This makes the dataset scalable, as
247
      YouTube has a constantly growing set of videos. Each stage is described in detail below.
248
249   4.1 V IDEO S ELECTION AND S IGNAL E XTRACTION
250   Video Selection: We begin with the YT-Temporal-1Bn (Zellers et al., 2022a) dataset. Because this
251   dataset has strong speech and visual alignment, it consists of a lot of videos where ‘talking heads’
252   dominate the screen (eg. VLOGs, product placements, etc). We attempt to reduce the number of such
253
      videos in order to capture more interesting scenes, objects and actions. This is done by extracting
      face detections with frontal gaze where face bounding-box height is greater than 20%, and removing
254
      videos where more than 30% of frames have such frontal gaze. We then apply safety filters to remove
255
      racy, local controversy content etc, as well as applying filters to maximise semantic and person
256   diversity. Details about these processes are provided in the appendix.
257   Signal Extraction: For each video we extract the following signals: (i) Frame captions: A visual
258   description of each frame (extracted at 1fps) is obtained from PaLI-3 (Chen et al., 2023b). (ii) ASR:
259   the speech is transcribed using the YouTube API; (iii) Metadata: We obtain the YouTube title and the
260   description for each video; and (iv) Shot boundaries for each video.
261
      4.2 AUTOMATIC V IDEO C APTIONING
262
      The signals described above (frame captions, ASR, title and description, shot boundaries) are
263
      automatically combined to create video-level captions in a multi-stage process. Examples of caption
264   quality are provided in the appendix, showcasing details such as visual elements, multiple events,
265   mood and atmosphere, details from the ASR, and even high level feelings and emotions. Video
266   captions are obtained using the following steps:
267   Shot Visual Captions: Using the shot boundaries, the frame captions are summarized into shot-level
268   descriptions (shot captions) by prompting the same LLM. We then create a script for each video
269   containing the shot timestamps, the shot visual captions and the ASR transcript.
      Topic and Description Pairs: If ASR exists, an initial list of structured topics for the video (along


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      with a short topic description) is formed by prompting an LLM with the ASR (see appendix). Note
271   that this already yields decent topics as the initial list of videos have been selected (by the YT-
272   Temporal-1Bn authors) to have a strong correlation between ASR and visual content.
273   Shot Clustering: Shots are then clustered per-video using an LLM prompted with the semantic
274   topics obtained above. In each cluster, there may be one or many shots that correspond to that topic.
275   A diagram on this stage and the exact prompt used is provided in the appendix.
276   Segment Captions: Consecutive shots of the same topic are then merged as one segment. Shots of
277   the same topic that are not contiguous are treated as separate segments (see appendix for an example).
278   We then generate dense captions for each segment using a custom prompt (see appendix).
279
      Adding Visual Support: To extract a better visual description of the segment that will be used for
      QA generation in the next phase, an extra step is performed to get visual support for each segment.
280
      That visual support is stored separately in conjunction with the dense caption for the segment. For
281
      this purpose, the dense caption from the previous step is used alongside the shot level visual captions.
282   The LLM prompt used is provided in the appendix, and the the LLM used for all the above steps is
283   Gemini-1.0-Pro (Gemini Team Google, 2023).
284
285   4.3 QAD (Q UESTION -A NSWER -D ECOY ) G ENERATION
286
      We automatically generate questions, answers and decoys (QADs) by feeding the video captions
287   from above to custom prompted LLMs. Our prompts are inspired by the EgoSchema dataset
288   pipeline (Mangalam et al., 2023), with key modifications to generate more visually focused questions,
289   as well as to generate questions belonging to a set of different question types. The exact prompts
290   used are provided in the appendix. We generate QADs in two stages: (i) Given the video captions
291   from the previous step, we first generate questions and answers; (ii) in the second stage we generate
292   six decoys given the questions and answers from the previous stage. We found this 2-stage method to
293   work better empirically than generating the QADs all in one go.
294
295   4.4 LLM- BASED B LIND F ILTER
296   QAD filter: LLM-based generation can sometimes yield QAD triplets that can be answered from
297   common sense or external world knowledge without the video as context. In particular, we observed
298   that LLMs are often capable of inferring the correct answer from subtle cues in the answer candidates,
299
      for example if the correct answer is a positive sentiment while the decoys are negative. To remove
      such questions, we apply an LLM-based blind filter. We prompt an LLM (Gemini-1.0-pro) to rank
300
      the answer candidates to a question. To avoid false rejections due to random correct guesses, we
301
      repeat this process three times and only filter out questions where the model predicted the correct
302   answer at least two times out of three (this number was selected to maximise number of videos left
303   given the accuracy trade-off and is discussed in the appendix). Chain-of-thought reasoning improves
304   accuracy so we ask the model to provide a rationale alongside its ranking.
305
306   4.5 M ANUAL R ATER V ERIFICATION
307   The final stage involves manual human verification. Raters are first asked to rate the quality of the
308   question based on 4 criteria (details in the appendix). If the question is not suitable, the entire QAD
309   set is discarded. If the question is accepted, raters annotate which modalities are required to answer
310   the question. Choices are: “audio+video”, “video-only”, or “audio-only”. Next, raters are asked
311   to either accept the answer as-is or modify it. Decoys are annotated in a final stage. Given the six
312   LLM-generated decoy candidates, raters verify that they are actually incorrect answers to the question
313   and select the four most challenging ones. If less than four decoys are suitable, we provide a text field
314   for raters to write their own decoys. Screenshots of the rater UI are provided in the appendix. We
315
      noticed that rater corrections reintroduce a small amount of questions that can be answered without
      context, so as a final step we repeat the QAD filter described above. We applied two rounds of manual
316
      rater verification to improve dataset quality. More details about rater training, replication (multiple
317
      raters per question) and pipelining are provided in the appendix.
318   Human Proposed Questions. To test the effectiveness and efficiency of the automatic portion of our
319   dataset pipeline, we asked raters to propose questions and answers entirely manually for a subset of
320   the dataset. We call this set HPQ (Human Proposed Questions). The raters are provided with a few
321   examples of each question type before they begin annotating. In total, we collect 270 QAs for 193
322   videos in this set. We use this set in two ways - (i) to quantitatively measure rater-time saved by our
323   automatic pipeline, and (ii) to estimate the amount of Gemini bias in our semi-automatic pipeline.
      The results for both are provided in Sec. 5.3.

337   5     E XPERIMENTS
338
339   We first introduce the two sets in Neptune and our evaluation metrics and then present evaluations
340   using both baseline and state-of-the-art models.
341   5.1 N EPTUNE S ETS AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
342   Neptune Sets: Because we seeded our dataset from the YT-Temporal-1Bn (Zellers et al., 2022b)
343   videos, we note that it contains some videos where ASR can play a big role in contributing to the video
344   content. In order to create a more challenging visual benchmark, we also provide Neptune-MMH
345   (multimodal human annotated), where we identify videos where vision should play an important
346   role. This is created by using the rater annotations for what modalities are required to answer the
347   question (described in Sec. 4.5), and discarding questions which the raters marked can be solved by
348   audio-only, and consists of 1,171 QADs for 1,000 videos. We encourage the community to evaluate
349
      on this harder subset as well.
      Evaluation: We explore two different protocols for evaluation of question answering - multiple
350
      choice evaluation (which involves selecting the correct answer amidst 4 decoys), and open-ended
351
      evaluation, which involves producing an answer directly without any decoys and assessing answer
352   quality directly. While the former has the advantage of easier metrics (simple accuracy), the latter
353   removes any potential confounding biases in the decoys. In the next section, we outline our process
354   for creating a new open-ended metric called GEM.
355   Gemma Equivalence Metric (GEM): As discussed in Sec. 2, existing metrics for open-ended QA
356   either lack robustness or rely on proprietary LLM APIs that can change over time. We therefore aim
357   to produce a static open-ended metric. Towards this, we first manually construct a labelled dev-set
358   with 292 (question, reference answer, candidate answer) triplets, with equivalence scores between 0
359   and 1. See appendix for details on the construction of the dev set. We then benchmark a number of
360
      rule-based and model-based metrics on this set in Table 1. To demonstrate the two ends of the scale,
      we first note that rule-based metrics such as CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004)
361
      obtain F1-Scores of 56.4 and 62.2, while an LLM-based metric using Gemini-1.5-pro (Reid et al.,
362
      2024) gets an F1-Score of 72.8 (but is closed-source). Next, we apply static open-source lightweight
363   language models, namely the Gemma family of models i.e. Gemma-2B (Team et al., 2024a), Gemma-
364   7B (Team et al., 2024a) and Gemma-9B (Team et al., 2024b) to judge the answers in a zero-shot
365   setting and find that performance improves with model size, with Gemma-9B bridging the gap well
366   between traditional metrics and the Gemini-1.5-pro based metric. Finally, we fine-tune Gemma-9B
367   on the open-source BEM answer equivalence dataset (Bulian et al., 2022), and find that we obtain
368   a very slight improvement, and hence that it performs the best on our dev-set among the Gemma
369   models. We call the metric obtained with this model Gemma Equivalence Metric (GEM). Note that
370   this metric takes into account the question when comparing whether two answers are equivalent,
371
      which is unlike captioning metrics such as CIDEr which omit the question entirely. In Table 4, we
      report open-ended evaluations using our proposed GEM metric in addition to closed-ended MCQ
372
      accuracy. We will release GEM publicly to enable reproducible open-ended evaluations.

532   6   C ONCLUSION
533
534   We present Neptune, a new benchmark for VideoQA with a focus on multimodal, high-level un-
535   derstanding of long videos. Neptune is created using a scalable pipeline for arbitrary videos that
536   minimizes (though not omits) human verification. Benchmarks are evaluated using MCQ and open-
537   ended evals – for which we provide a new, open-source metric. Limitations: The dataset may inherit
538   biases of the Gemini model used to generate QADs. While VideoQA is a good proxy for video
539   understanding, our dataset could be further improved by additional annotations – such as manually
      annotated temporal grounding, dense captions or entity labels.
