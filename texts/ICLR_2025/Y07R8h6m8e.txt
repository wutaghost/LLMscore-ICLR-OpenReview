011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-modal Large Language models (MLLMs) are always trained on data from
014            diverse and unreliable sources, which may contain misaligned or mislabeled text-
015
               image pairs. This frequently causes robustness issues and hallucinations, leading
               to performance degradation. Data valuation is an efficient way to detect and trace
016
               these misalignments. Nevertheless, existing methods are computationally expen-
017
               sive for MLLMs. While computationally efficient, the classical influence func-
018            tions are inadequate for contrastive learning models because they were originally
019            designed for pointwise loss. Additionally, contrastive learning involves minimiz-
020            ing the distance between the modalities of positive samples and maximizing the
021            distance between the modalities of negative samples. This requires us to evalu-
022            ate the influence of samples from both perspectives. To tackle these challenges,
023            we introduce the Extended Influence Function for Contrastive Loss (ECIF), an
024            influence function crafted for contrastive loss. ECIF considers both positive and
025            negative samples and provides a closed-form approximation of contrastive learn-
026
               ing models, eliminating the need for retraining. Building upon ECIF, we develop
               a series of algorithms for data evaluation in MLLM, misalignment detection, and
027
               misprediction trace-back tasks. Experimental results demonstrate our ECIF ad-
028
               vances the transparency and interpretability of MLLMs by offering a more ac-
029            curate assessment of data impact and model alignment compared to traditional
030            baseline methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Multi-modal Large Language models (MLLMs) (Yin et al., 2023; Koh et al., 2024) have garnered
036   significant attention for their ability to integrate and understand various data types, such as image,
037   text, and audio. Despite their growing application, existing MLLMs often suffer from robustness
038   issues (Carlini & Terzis, 2021) and hallucinations, primarily stemming from misaligned text-image
039
      pairs in the training data (Kim et al., 2023). These misalignments, manifesting as semantic mis-
      matches, contextual inconsistencies, or discrepancies between abstract and concrete elements, can
040
      severely degrade model performance. MLLMs assume consistent alignment between image-text
041
      pairs, but when this assumption fails, it leads to incorrect interpretations, ultimately degrading model
042   performance. Consequently, improving dataset transparency is crucial, as model developers need the
043   ability to trace and identify problematic data samples. However, diagnosing issues caused by mis-
044   aligned data, such as mislabeled or biased samples, is difficult when working with large text-image
045   datasets.
046
      Although the critical role of training data in shaping MLLM capabilities is well recognized, there
047
      remains a lack of robust evaluation mechanisms for data quality (Nguyen et al., 2022). To address
048   this, various data valuation methods (Jia et al., 2019; Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Yoon et al., 2020;
049   Han et al., 2020) have been introduced to enhance dataset transparency by quantifying the contri-
050   bution of individual data points to model performance. These approaches typically assign higher
051   contribution scores to training instances whose inclusion significantly boosts model performance
052   compared to their exclusion. Some methods, such as Shapley Value (Kwon & Zou, 2022), require
053   multiple retraining processes with different subsets of data, which is computationally expensive and
      impractical for large models. To overcome this limitation, influence function-based methods have


                                                         1

054
      gained popularity, as they estimate data contributions using gradient information, thereby avoiding
055   retraining (Choe et al., 2024).
056
057   However, applying influence functions to MLLMs poses significant challenges. (i) First, the influ-
058
      ence function was initially designed for M-estimators (Huber, 1981), which operate with pointwise
      loss. However, MLLMs rely on noise-contrastive estimation (Radford et al., 2021; Gutmann &
059
      HyvaŐąrinen, 2010; He et al., 2020) as their training objective. This objective encourages the model to
060
      draw positive pairs closer in feature space while pushing negative pairs apart, making the influence
061   function unsuitable for direct application to contrastive loss. (ii) Second, the influence of negative
062   pairs in contrastive learning has gained increasing attention recently (van den Oord et al., 2019;
063   Yuksekgonul et al., 2023). Robinson et al. (2021) emphasized the importance of negative samples,
064   especially ‚Äúhard‚ÄĚ negatives - samples that are mapped close in feature space but should ideally be
065   far apart. However, the original definition of the influence function does not consider the roles of
066   positive and negative samples. This oversimplified analysis is particularly prone to underestimat-
067   ing the impact of certain hard negative samples on the learning process (Chen et al., 2020a). (iii)
068   Lastly, calculating the necessary gradients and Hessian matrices for influence functions demands
069
      significant computational and memory resources, which becomes infeasible in the large-scale, high-
      dimensional context of MLLMs (Li et al., 2023a;b).
070
071   To address these challenges, we propose the Extended Influence Function for Contrastive Loss
072   (ECIF), a novel method designed to quantify data importance specifically for contrastive learning.
073   ECIF enjoys a closed-form approximation of the original contrastive loss, thus eliminating the need
074   for re-training - a process that is impractical in the era of large models. It also accounts for the dual
075
      role of data points as both positive and negative samples, providing a more comprehensive under-
      standing of their impact on model training. This approach provides a more accurate measurement
076
      of misalignment. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
077
078          ‚ÄĘ We propose ECIF, the first dual-perspective data valuation method for MLLMs, which
079            quantifies the impact of data points as both positive and negative samples. This compre-
080            hensive approach enables a more accurate measurement of data contribution, particularly
081            addressing the influence of negative samples in contrastive learning.
082          ‚ÄĘ Based on ECIF, we develop corresponding algorithms for different tasks, including iden-
083            tifying the most valuable data (related to specific tasks), misalignment detection, and mis-
084            prediction trace-back.
085          ‚ÄĘ Comprehensive experimental results demonstrate that ECIF can effectively and efficiently
086            remove the influence of samples compared to retraining and identify influential data in
087            the training set. Moreover, our methods based on ECIF are also effective in identifying
088            influential data (harmful data and valuable data) for fine-tuning, mispredictions trace back,
089            and detecting misaligned data.

091   2    R ELATED W ORK
092
093   Contrastive Learning. Recently, self-supervised contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020b) has
094   emerged as a highly effective approach for acquiring representations without the need for labeled
095   data (Donahue & Simonyan, 2019). This model utilizes a contrastive loss, which pushes dissimilar
096   data pairs apart while pulling similar pairs closer together. Contrastive learning plays a pivotal role in
097   advancing MLLMs by integrating and understanding information across diverse modalities, such as
098   text and images (Radford et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2024). In multi-modal contrastive learning tasks,
099
      proper alignment of the training data ensures accurate cross-modal associations, enabling models to
      learn and extract consistent feature representations (Wang & Isola, 2020). One of the key challenges
100
      in training with noisy, large-scale image-text pairs sourced from the internet is achieving effective
101
      alignment between these modalities. To address this, researchers have developed various methods,
102   such as those proposed by Gao et al. (2022) and Yao et al. (2021), which introduce finer-grained
103   and more extensive interactions between text and images to improve cross-modal alignment. De-
104   spite extensive research on contrastive learning, we are the first to explore the interactive influence
105   between pairs using influence functions. Our work bridges this gap by applying influence functions
106   in contrastive learning, allowing for a deeper understanding of both positive and negative samples.
107   This comprehensive approach enhances the accuracy of misalignment measurements in data pairs,
      providing a more thorough assessment of data valuation.

      Influence Function. The influence function, initially a staple in robust statistics (Cook, 2000; Cook
109   & Weisberg, 1980), has seen extensive adoption within deep learning since (Koh & Liang, 2017).
110   Its versatility spans various applications, including detecting mislabeled data, interpreting models,
111   addressing model bias, and facilitating machine unlearning tasks. For data removal, recent work
112   using influence function including unlearning features and labels (Warnecke et al., 2023), forgetting
113   a subset of image data for training deep neural networks (Golatkar et al., 2020; 2021), removing the
114   influence of nodes and edges in graph neural networks Wu et al. (2023), and model debiasing (Chen
115   et al., 2024). Besides, various studies have applied influence functions to interpret models across
116   different domains, including natural language processing (Han et al., 2020) and image classification
117
      (Basu et al., 2021), while also addressing biases in classification models (Wang et al., 2019), word
      embeddings (Brunet et al., 2019), and finetuned models (Chen et al., 2020a). Recent advancements,
118
      such as the LiSSA method (Agarwal et al., 2017; Kwon et al., 2023; Grosse et al., 2023) and kNN-
119
      based techniques (Guo et al., 2021), have been proposed to enhance the computational efficiency
120   of computing the influence function. Despite numerous studies on influence functions, we are the
121   first to extend them to contrastive learning. Moreover, compared to traditional models, contrastive
122   learning introduces additional complexity in influence function analysis, as it requires considering
123   data points in both positive and negative roles. Our dual-perspective approach of ECIF offers a more
124   comprehensive view of data impact, leading to more accurate measurements of misalignment in text-
125   image pairs. Bridging the theoretical gap between positive and negative pairs has posed significant
126   challenges in our work, which has been addressed in our proof.

186   trastive loss (3) using the influence function method. Generally, in the original influence function
187   method, a term in the loss function which only contain the fully information from the target sample
188   is up-weighted by ŌĶ. Then, a response function in (4) related to ŌĶ is derived. Within this analyti-
189   cal framework, when ŌĶ is set to ‚ąí1, the resultant loss and model parameters are the same as those
190   obtained by removing the sample via retraining. However, in the context of contrastive learning,
191   because the information of the sample point appears in every term of the loss function for its batch,
192
      it is not feasible to isolate the relevant information of this sample within a batch into an independent

      5      A PPLICATIONS OF ECIF
312
313   We have proposed ECIF to evaluate the contribution of training data in contrastive learning. The
314
      ECIF method enables us to estimate the change in the learned parameters őłŐā if a training example

532   7     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduced the Extended Influence Function for Contrastive Loss (ECIF), a novel
535   method to quantify data valuation in MLLMs. ECIF provides a dual-perspective analysis of data
536   points by considering both positive and negative samples, offering a more comprehensive under-
537   standing of their impact on model performance. By utilizing a closed-form approximation, ECIF
538   eliminates the need for re-training, making it highly practical for large models. Our approach is
539   applicable to enhancing fine-tuning, tracing mispredicted data, and detecting misaligned data, with
      results demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world tasks.
