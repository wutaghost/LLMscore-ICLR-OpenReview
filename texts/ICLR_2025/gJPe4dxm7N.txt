011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate estimation of time-varying treatment effects is crucial for optimizing in-
014            terventions in personalized medicine. However, observational data often contains
015
               complex confounding bias and temporal complexities, making counterfactual es-
               timation challenging. We propose Temporal Adaptive Convolutional Intervention
016
               Network (TACIN), a novel model that introduces an Intervention-aware Func-
017
               tional Convolution kernel to emphasize the role of treatments and capture com-
018            plex temporal treatment interactions. TACIN addresses confounding bias from
019            a domain generalization perspective, approximating the unknown target domain
020            using adversarial examples and incorporating Sharpness-Aware Minimization to
021            derive a generalization bound. This approach is more suitable for longitudinal
022            settings compared to existing methods inspired by domain adaptation techniques
023            due to inherent differences between static and longitudinal contexts. Experiments
024            on simulated datasets demonstrate TACIN’s superior performance compared to
025            state-of-the-art models for counterfactual estimation over time. The code for
026
               reproducing the experimental results is available in an anonymous repository at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TACIN-2D20.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Precise estimation of time-varying treatment effects is crucial in domains like personalized medicine,
032   where optimizing individual interventions relies on accurate estimating counterfactual outcomes
033   (Huang & Ning, 2012). Randomized Controlled Trials, despite being the gold standard for causal
034   inference (Hariton & Locascio, 2018), are limited by high costs and ethical concerns. Consequently,
035   research has shifted focus to methods tackling confounding bias and temporal complexities in ob-
036   servational data for accurate counterfactual estimation.
037   Time-varying confounders often introduce complex confounding bias in observational data, leading
038   to inaccurate estimates. Recent studies (Bica et al., 2020b; Melnychuk et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
039   2024) have addressed this issue by learning representations to break the association between histor-
040   ical information and treatment assignment, drawing inspiration from domain adaptation techniques
041   used in static causal inference settings (Shalit et al., 2017; Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019; Johans-
042   son et al., 2022). However, the suitability of domain adaptation in longitudinal settings remains
043   questionable due to inherent differences between the two contexts.
044   Due to the unobservability of counterfactuals, we aim to train a model on the factual (source) do-
045   main that generalizes well to the counterfactual (target) domain. In static settings (Figure 1(a)),
046   the target domain during testing is consistent with training and can be sampled, aligning with do-
047   main adaptation (Ganin et al., 2016). However, in longitudinal settings, the historical information of
048   length t generated under the training intervention policy (Figure 1(b)) differs from the τ -step inter-
049   vened historical information under a different testing policy (Figure 1(c)). The unknown true data
050
      generation process renders the target distribution unknown during testing, potentially explaining the
      ineffectiveness of current balancing strategies in longitudinal settings (Huang et al., 2024).
051
052   Another challenge in longitudinal settings arises from temporal complexities, particularly those
053   caused by interactions between treatments over time. For instance, Roemhild et al. (2022) em-
      phasize the existence of complex temporal interactions between antibiotics, which is crucial for

080

081   role that treatments play, we draw inspiration from VCNet, which allows the weights of the predic-

083   Convolution (IFC) kernel, ensuring that the convolutional kernel at each time step depends on the
084   related treatment. Specifically, we employ Radial Basis Functions (RBFs) as basis functions to fit
085   nonlinear functions of treatments, enhancing the model’s ability to handle complex temporal treat-
086   ment interactions. Furthermore, since the target domain is unobservable in longitudinal settings, we
087   revisit confounding bias from the perspective of domain generalization. TACIN approximates the
088   unknown target domain by generating adversarial examples using the Fast Gradient Sign Method
089

090

091
      this theoretical analysis can more effectively mitigate time-varying confounding bias. A compre-
092   hensive review of related work, which motivates and contextualizes our contributions, is provided
093   in Appendix A, which motivates and contextualizes our contributions. Extensive experiments on
094   simulated datasets validate the effectiveness of TACIN, demonstrating superior performance over
095   state-of-the-art models for counterfactual estimation over time.

124   plex temporal interactions, we propose an Intervention-aware Functional Convolution (IFC) kernel.
125   Explicitly encoding treatment information within the functional convolution operation enables IFC
126   to better understand treatment impact on patient outcomes over time. This intervention-aware ap-
127   proach facilitates the learning of intricate relationships between treatments and covariates, ultimately
128   improving the model’s capacity for predicting counterfactual outcomes accurately.
129   In our proposed IFC, we employ a one-dimensional dilated convolution kernel that differs from
130   traditional approaches (Oord et al., 2016) by incorporating the corresponding treatment as a function
131   at each time step. Specifically, the output at time step t can be computed as:

      Existing methods for causal inference with time-varying treatments often borrow ideas from
182
      the static setting and aim to address confounding bias through the lens of domain adaptation.
183
      Specifically, these approaches seek to learn representations that align the conditional distributions
184   P (Φ(H̄t )|At = aj ) across different treatment assignments aj , thereby removing the association
185   between time-dependent confounders present in the patient history H̄t and time-varying treatments
186   At (Bica et al., 2020b; Melnychuk et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). However, for this alignment to
187   successfully remove confounding bias, it is assumed that the distribution of the observed histories
188   H̄t remains consistent between training and testing (see Remark 1 for a detailed explanation). In
189   the longitudinal setting, this assumption may not hold, limiting the effectiveness of these methods
190   in real-world scenarios. To illustrate this, we can derive the distribution of H̄t in the training phase
191   as:

      A.1    C AUSAL INFERENCE WITH LONGITUDINAL DATA
651
652
      Recently, significant progress has been made in the field of longitudinal data analysis by incorpo-
653
      rating neural network techniques for counterfactual estimation. For instance, RMSN (Lim et al.,
654   2018) combines two propensity networks and employs an inverse probability weighting (IPTW)
655   based training method in its prediction model. G-Net (Li et al., 2021) enhances the traditional G-
656   computation technique through a deep learning framework. Similarly, CRN (Bica et al., 2020b),
657   which is also based on RNN networks, focuses on learning balanced representations to mitigate
658   confounding bias. In contrast to these works, CT (Melnychuk et al., 2022) proposes utilizing a more
659   powerful transformer model to better handle long-term dependencies, while ACTIN (Wang et al.,
660   2024) introduces a dual-module framework that effectively enhances the ability of simple models to
661   handle complex temporal treatment interactions. It is worth noting that CRN, CT, and ACTIN all
662
      adopt the idea of domain adaptation to learn balanced representations by eliminating the association
      between historical information and current treatment assignments to alleviate confounding bias.
663
664   There have been significant advances in causal inference based on longitudinal data, albeit with dif-
665   ferent settings from ours (Bica et al., 2020a; Hatt & Feuerriegel, 2024; Frauen et al., 2023; Seedat
666   et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2023; Hess et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). These works primarily focus on
667   addressing time-varying confounding factors in longitudinal observational data to achieve accurate
668
      causal effect estimation. For instance, the Time Series Deconfounder (Bica et al., 2020a) and Deep-
      ACE (Frauen et al., 2023) utilize recurrent neural networks and end-to-end deep learning models,
669
      respectively, combining latent variable inference and iterative G-computation formula to adjust for
670
      the impact of time-varying confounders. For irregularly sampled longitudinal data, Treatment Ef-
671   fect Neural Controlled Differential Equation (TE-CDE) (Seedat et al., 2022) and Bayesian Neural
672   Controlled Differential Equation (BNCDE) (Hess et al., 2024) propose differential equation-based
673   modeling approaches to estimate counterfactual outcomes, with BNCDE additionally providing un-
674   certainty estimates of treatment effects using Bayesian uncertainty quantification. Chen et al. (2023)
675   introduces a multi-task Gaussian process model that captures relationships between treatments, sub-
676   jects, and temporal variations by defining multi-task priors, thereby estimating dynamic treatment
677   effects. The aforementioned works mainly focus on causal inference under standard conditions,
678   while COSTAR (Meng et al., 2023) considers the problem of counterfactual estimation under dis-
679
      tributional shifts and improves model performance by introducing self-supervised learning, which
      sets it apart from other studies. Overall, these research efforts enrich the methods for causal infer-
680
      ence on longitudinal data, providing new perspectives for addressing issues such as time-varying
681
      confounders, irregularly sampled data, and distributional shifts.
682
683
684   A.2    C AUSAL INFERENCE WITH STAIC DATA
685
686   In static settings, a key challenge is that treatments are often assigned based on unit-specific covari-
687
      ates, leading to imbalanced covariate distributions across treatment groups. Addressing this imbal-
      ance is crucial for ensuring the reliability of causal inference (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al.,
688
      2017; Yoon et al., 2018). Inspired by domain adaptation, a line of research tackles this challenge
689
      by learning balanced representations between treatment and control groups. For instance, Shalit
690   et al. (Shalit et al., 2017) learn balanced representations by minimizing the Integral Probability
691   Metric (IPM) distance between the distributions of treated and control groups. These representa-
692   tion learning-based methods aim to eliminate distributional differences between treatment groups,
693   thereby mitigating the impact of confounding bias on causal effect estimation.
694
      Another challenge is how to differentiate treatment variables from other covariates within neural
695
      network architectures, which has been noted in previous works (Shalit et al., 2017; Schwab et al.,
696   2020; Nie et al., 2020). For binary treatments, Shalit et al. (Shalit et al., 2017) introduce the Counter-
697   factual Regression (CFR) framework, which learns a shared representation followed by two separate
698   ”heads” to predict post-treatment and control outcomes, effectively mitigating the potential loss of
699   treatment information in high-dimensional latent representations. This approach has been widely
700   adopted in subsequent studies (Louizos et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019).
701   For continuous treatments, Nie et al. (Nie et al., 2020) propose the Varying Coefficient Network
      (VCNet), allowing the prediction head weights to be continuous functions of the treatment, simi-


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      larly emphasizing the impact of treatment information. However, in longitudinal settings, how to
703   design models specifically for treatment information remains an open question.
704
705
      A.3   D OMAIN GENERALIZATION
706
707   Domain generalization (DG) aims to learn a model that can perform well on unseen target do-
708   mains, which is crucial for addressing distribution shift problems in real-world scenarios (Blanchard
709   et al., 2011; Muandet et al., 2013). Many methods have been proposed to tackle this issue, in-
710   cluding learning domain-invariant representations (Motiian et al., 2017; Matsuura & Harada, 2020),
711   data augmentation-based approaches (Xu et al., 2020; Volpi et al., 2018), and robustness training-
712   based methods (Shi et al., 2021; Foret et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024). Despite the significant
713   progress made by these methods in tasks such as image classification, their application to time se-
714
      ries data remains challenging. Compared to static data, the dynamic generative process of time
      series data makes their distribution more complex, which may be the reason why the strategy of
715
      learning domain-invariant representations is less effective in mitigating time-varying confounding
716
      bias. The flatness-related methods, such as Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al.,
717   2021), adopted in this paper aim to address the effects of domain shifts by identifying flat minima,
718   seeking regions in the loss landscape where small perturbations in the input have minimal impact
719   on the model’s predictions. By leveraging flat minima, these methods enhance the model’s robust-
720   ness to domain variations. It is worth noting that this approach is just one possible solution path,
721   and from the perspective of domain generalization, it can bring us more possibilities for mitigating
722   time-varying confounding bias.

868   In the study by (Geng et al., 2017), the authors model the volume of tumor growth for a period of
