009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The main focus of Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) is studying how
012            large Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) can be more efficiently solved when
013            addressed in a modular way, by combining partial solutions computed for smaller
014            subtasks. Despite their very intuitive role for learning, most notions of MDP ab-
015            stractions proposed in the HRL literature have limited expressive power or do not
016            possess formal efficiency guarantees. This work addresses these fundamental is-
017            sues by defining Realizable Abstractions, a new relation between generic low-level
018            MDPs and their associated high-level decision processes. The notion we propose
019
               avoids non-Markovianity issues and has desirable near-optimality guarantees. In-
               deed, we show that any abstract policy for Realizable Abstractions can be translated
020
               into near-optimal policies for the low-level MDP, through a suitable composition of
021
               options. As demonstrated in the paper, these options can be expressed as solutions
022            of specific constrained MDPs. Based on these findings, we propose RARL, a new
023            HRL algorithm that returns compositional and near-optimal low-level policies,
024            taking advantage of the Realizable Abstraction given in the input. We show that
025            RARL is Probably Approximately Correct, it converges in a polynomial number of
026            samples, and it is robust to inaccuracies in the abstraction.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) is the study of abstractions of decision processes and
032   how they can be used to improve the efficiency and compositionality of RL algorithms (Barto &
033   Mahadevan, 2003; Abel et al., 2018). To pursue these objectives, most HRL methods augment the
034   low-level Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman, 1994) with some form of abstraction, often a
035   simplified state representation or a high-level policy. As was immediately identified (Dayan & Hinton,
036   1992), compositionality is arguably one of the most important features for HRL algorithms, as it is
037   commonly associated with increased efficiency (Wen et al., 2020) and policy reuse for downstream
038   tasks (Brunskill & Li, 2014; Abel et al., 2018; Tasse et al., 2020; 2022). There is a common intuition
039
      that drives many authors in HRL. That is, abstract states correspond to sets of ground states, and
      abstract actions correspond to sequences of ground actions. This was evident since the early work in
040
      HRL (Dayan & Hinton, 1992), and was largely derived from hierarchical planning. However, the
041
      main question that still remains unanswered is which sequence of ground actions should each abstract
042   action correspond to? The answer to this question also requires the identification of a suitable state
043   abstraction. The resulting notion of MDP abstraction has a strong impact on the applicability and the
044   guarantees of the associated HRL methods.
045
      There is no shared consensus on what “MDP abstractions” should refer to. In the literature, the term
046
      is loosely used to refer to a variety of concepts, including state partitions (Abel et al., 2020; Wen et al.,
047
      2020), bottleneck states (Jothimurugan et al., 2021b), subtasks (Nachum et al., 2018; Jothimurugan
048   et al., 2021a), options (Precup & Sutton, 1997; Khetarpal et al., 2020), entire MDPs (Ravindran &
049   Barto, 2002; Cipollone et al., 2023), or even the natural language (Jiang et al., 2019). In addition,
050   most HRL methods have been validated only experimentally (Nachum et al., 2018; Jinnai et al., 2020;
051   Jothimurugan et al., 2021b; Lee et al., 2021; 2022b), leading to a limited theoretical understanding
052   of general abstractions and their use in RL. Some notable exceptions (Brunskill & Li, 2014; Fruit
053   et al., 2017; Fruit & Lazaric, 2017; Wen et al., 2020) give formal definitions of state and temporal
      abstractions, and provide formal near-optimality and efficiency guarantees. However, they do not


                                                           1
054
      define a high-level decision process, enforcing requirements that are often impractical on the ground
055   MDP directly.
056
057   In this work, we propose a new formal definition of MDP abstractions, based on a high-level decision
058
      process. This definition enables algorithms, such as the one proposed in this paper, that do not
      require specific knowledge about the ground MDP. In particular, we identify a new relation that
059
      links generic low-level MDPs to their high-level representations, and we use a second-order MDP
060
      as the high-level decision process in order to overcome non-Markovian dependencies. As we show,
061   the abstractions we propose are widely applicable, do not incur the non-Markovian effects that are
062   often found in HRL (Bai et al., 2016; Nachum et al., 2018; Jothimurugan et al., 2021b), and provide
063   near-optimality guarantees for their associated low-level policies. Such near-optimal policies only
064   result from the compositions of smaller options, without any global constraint. Due to this feature, we
065   call them Realizable Abstractions. An important feature of our work is also that we do not restrict the
066   cardinality of the state and action spaces for the ground MDP that does not need to be finite. Instead,
067   we require that the abstract decision process has finite state and action sets, so that we can compute
068   an exact tabular representation of the abstract value function.
069   We also address the associated algorithmic question of how to learn the ground options that realize
070   each high-level behavior. As we show, the realization problem, that is, the problem of learning
071   suitable options from experience, can be cast as a Constrained MDP (CMDP) (Altman, 1999) and
072   solved with off-the-shelf online RL algorithms for CMDPs (Zhang et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2022).
073   Based on these principles, we develop a new algorithm, called RARL (for “Realizable Abstractions
074   RL”), which learns compositional policies for the ground MDP and it is Probably Approximately
075
      Correct (PAC) (Fiechter, 1994). An additional novelty of this work is that the proposed algorithm
      iteratively refines the high-level decision process given in input by sampling in the ground MDP and
076
      it exploits the solution obtained from the current abstraction to drive exploration in the ground MDP.
077
078
079   Summary of contributions The contributions of this work are theoretical and algorithmic. We
080   propose Realizable Abstractions (Definition 2), we show a formal relation between the abstract and
081
      the ground values (Theorem 1 and Corollary 3), and we provide original insights on the conditions
      that must be met to reduce the effective horizon in the abstraction (Proposition 7). Regarding the
082
      algorithmic contributions, RARL is sample efficient, PAC, and robust with respect to approximately
083
      realizable abstractions and overly optimistic abstract rewards (Theorem 8).

086   R ELATED WORK
087
088
      State-action abstractions Similarly to Abel (2020), we organize most of the work in HRL in two
      groups: state abstractions, which primarily focus on simplified state representations, and action
089
      abstractions, which focus on high-level actions and temporal abstractions. From the first group, we
090
      mention the MDP homomorphisms (Ravindran & Barto, 2002; 2004), stochastic bisimulation (Givan
091   et al., 2003; Ferns et al.) and the irrelevance criteria listed in Li et al. (2006). These are all related
092   to the language of MDP abstractions used here. However, the limited expressive power of these
093   early works mainly captures specific projections of state features or symmetries in MDP dynamics.
094   As we discuss in Appendix B, our framework extends both MDP homomorphisms and stochastic
095   bisimulations of Givan et al. (2003). In the second group, the options framework (Precup & Sutton,
096   1997; Sutton et al., 1998; 1999) is one of the first to successfully achieve temporal abstraction in
097   MDPs. Options are partial policies, which can be interpreted as abstract actions, and can be fully
098   learned from experience (Bacon et al., 2017; Machado et al., 2017; Khetarpal et al., 2020). Thanks to
099
      these properties, many works implemented HRL principles within the theory of options, such as the
      automatic discovery of landmarks and sub-goals (Simsek & Barto, 2004; Castro & Precup, 2011;
100
      Kulkarni et al., 2016; Nachum et al., 2018; Jinnai et al., 2019; Ramesh et al., 2019; Jinnai et al.,
101
      2020; Jiang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022b). Nonetheless, since the state space is usually not affected
102   by the use of options, the lack of a simplified state representation limits the reuse of previously
103   acquired skills. The study of abstractions that involve both states and actions is the most natural
104   progression for HRL research. Nonetheless, many works in this direction (Ravindran & Barto, 2003;
105   Abel et al., 2020; Abel, 2020; Jothimurugan et al., 2021b; Wen et al., 2020; Infante et al., 2022)
106   only consider a ground MDP and a partition of the state space, without any explicit dynamics at the
107   abstract level. The main difficulty in defining abstract dynamics comes from the non-Markovian
      and non-stationary effects that often arise in HRL (Jothimurugan et al., 2021b; Gürtler et al., 2021).


                                                          2
108
      This works overcomes these issues and defines MDP abstractions as distinct decision process with
109   independent reward and transition dynamics.
110
111   HRL theory The theoretical work on HRL is still less developed compared to the empirical studies.
112
      The first PAC analysis for RL in the presence of options is by Brunskill & Li (2014). Later, Fruit &
      Lazaric (2017) and Fruit et al. (2017) strongly contributed to the characterization of the conditions
113
      that cause options to be beneficial (or harmful) for learning, such as the near-optimality of options
114
      and the reduction in the MDP diameter. Both of these findings are consistent with our results. Lastly,
115   Wen et al. (2020) developed PEP, an HRL algorithm, and derived its regret guarantee. Similarly to
116   our algorithm, PEP learns low-level policies in a compositional way. However, our algorithm does
117   not receive the “exit profiles” in input, which are unlikely to be known in practice.
118
      Compositional HRL and logic composition Logical descriptions and planning domains can also
119
      be used to subdivide complex dynamics into smaller subtasks. This approach can be seen as a state-
120
      action abstraction with associated semantic labels and has been explored in various forms (Dietterich,
121   2000; Konidaris et al., 2018; Illanes et al., 2020; Jothimurugan et al., 2021a; Lee et al., 2022a),
122   even in purely logical settings (Banihashemi et al., 2017). The main strength of logical abstractions
123   is their suitability for compositionality and skill reuse (Andreas et al., 2017; Jothimurugan et al.,
124   2021a; Neary et al., 2022). However, because of the difficulty of aligning logical representations
125   with stochastic environments and discounted values, most methods do not come with significant
126   near-optimality guarantees for the low-level domain.
127   Other algorithms Regarding the algorithmic contribution, RARL is capable of correcting and
128   adapting to very inaccurate abstract rewards. This feature has been heavily inspired by RL algorithms
129   for multi-fidelity simulators (Cutler et al., 2014; Kandasamy et al., 2016). However, the algorithm
130   cannot update the input mapping function. Unlike other works (Jonsson & Barto, 2006; Allen et al.,
131   2021; Steccanella & Jonsson, 2022), learning such a state partition remains outside the scope of this
132   work.

144   Decision Processes A k-order Markov Decision Process (k-MDP) is a tuple M = ⟨S, A, T, R, γ⟩,
145   where S is a set of states, A is a set of actions, 0 < γ < 1 is the discount factor, T : S k × A → ∆(S)
146   is the transition function, and R : S k × A → [0, 1] is the reward function. To generate the next state
147
      or reward, each function receives the last k states in the trajectory. In particular, at each time step h,
      R(sh−k:h−1 , ah ) returns the immediate expected reward rh . We denote the initial distribution of s0
148

149

150   rh ∼ R(sh−1 , ah ) and sh ∼ T (sh−1 , ah ) (Puterman, 1994). In any k-MDP, the value of a policy π
151   in some states s0:k−1 ∈ S k , written V π (s0:k−1 ), is the expected sum of future discounted rewards,
152   when starting from s0:k−1 , and selecting actions based on π. The function Qπ (s0:k−1 , ak ) is the
153   value of π when the first action after s0:k−1 is set to ak . Without referring to any states, the value of a

188   where Πs̄ := ⌊s̄⌋ϕ → A is the set of policies defined for the block. In essence, ϕ-relative options

190   ϕ-relative iff all of its options are. In the remainder of this paper, we only consider sets ϕ-relative

192   Ωs̄ := ∪s̄p Ωs̄p s̄ for all options in one block. Finally, we call policy of options any set Ω that contains
193
      a single ϕ-relative option for each pair s̄p s̄. We use this name because Ω can be fully treated as a
194
      policy for the ground MDP. V Ω is the value of the policy that always executes the only applicable
      option in Ω until each option terminates.

199   This section defines Realizable Abstractions and studies the properties they satisfy. To explain
200   the main intuitions behind our abstractions, we use the running example of Figure 1 (left), which
201   represents a ground MDP M, with a simple grid world dynamics. In this work, an MDP abstraction
202   is a pair ⟨M̄, ϕ⟩, where M̄ is a decision process over states S̄, and ϕ : S → S̄ is the state mapping
203   function. In the example, S̄ = {s̄1 , s̄2 , s̄3 }. The association of abstract states with ground blocks
204   in the partition is intuitive, as shown by the colors in the example. Actions and ground options, on
205   the other hand, are much less trivial to associate. In this work, with Realizable Abstractions, we
206   encode the following intuition: if an abstract transition (s̄1 , ā, s̄3 ) has a high probability of occurring
207
      from s̄1 in the abstract model M̄, then there must exist a ϕ-relative option that moves the agent
      from block ⌊s̄1⌋ to block ⌊s̄3⌋ in the ground MDP, with high probability and in a few steps. In other
208
      words, we choose to interpret abstract transitions as representations of what is possible to replicate,
209
      we say “realize”, in the ground MDP. Both conditions above, in terms of probability and time, are
210   equally important and consistent with the meaning of discounted values in MDPs. Here, we choose
211   ϕ-relative options because they only terminate after leaving each block. In the example, no direct
212   transition is possible between ⌊s̄2⌋ and ⌊s̄3⌋. So, we should have P(s̄3 | s̄2 , ā, M̄) = 0, for any ā.
213   Instead, selecting an appropriate value for P(s̄3 | s̄1 , ā, M̄) is much more complex, as it strongly
214   depends on the initial state of the option in the gray block. This is at the heart of non-Markovianity
215   in HRL. In this work, we address this issue through a careful treatment of entry states and allowing
      abstractions to model second-order dependencies. This allows us to condition the probability of the

365   discount factors satisfy Definition 2. However, to make the relation between γ and γ̄ more explicit,

      we prove the following proposition.
367   Proposition 7. If ⟨M̄, ϕ⟩ is an admissible abstraction for an MDP M, then, for any tuple (s̄p s̄, ā)

369
      This statement is composed of two results, hos̄ (s̄ | s) ≥ 1 − γ̄, which only constrains the occupancy,
370
      and Vs̄o ≤ hos̄ (s̄ | s)/(1 − γ̄) which also involves value. These inequalities encode the necessary
371
      conditions for reducing the effective horizon in the abstraction. The first inequality says that γ̄ can
372   only be low if the occupancy in every block is high. In particular, if there exists an option o that
373   leaves some ⌊s̄⌋ in one step, then hos̄ (s̄ | s) = 1 − γ and γ̄ = γ is the only feasible choice. The second
374   says that γ̄ can only be low if Vs̄o is also low with respect to hos̄ . In particular, if o collects in ⌊s̄⌋ a
375   reward of 1 at each step, then Vs̄o = hos̄ (s̄ | s)/(1 − γ) and γ̄ = γ is the only feasible choice. This
376   allows us to conclude that a time compression in the abstraction is possible only if: (i) the changes
377   between blocks occur at some lower timescale; (ii) rewards are temporally sparse. This confirms some
      common intuitions among the HRL literature, while it shows that sparse rewards are also important.

516   5    C ONCLUSION
517
518   This work answers one important open question for HRL regarding how to relate the abstract actions
519
      with the ground options. The answer is to relate the probability of abstract transitions with the
      probability of the temporally-extended transitions that can be obtained with options in the ground
520
      MDP. More specifically, this is given by the Realizable Abstractions, described in this paper. This
521
      notion also implies suitable state abstractions that formally guarantee near-optimal and sample
522   efficient solutions of the ground MDP. In future work, the sample complexity of Theorem 8 could be
523   expressed as an instance-dependent bound. This would highlight when HRL can be more efficient
524   than standard RL, in presence of accurate abstractions, even without relying on equivalence classes.

      Proposition 2. Let ⟨M̄, ϕ⟩ be an admissible abstraction of an MDP M. Then, for any abstract

       B    C ONNECTION WITH MDP H OMOMORPHISMS AND BISIMULATION
1033
1034
       In this section, we relate our new concept of realizable abstractions with two existing formal definitions
       of MDP abstractions, namely, MDP homomorphisms (Ravindran & Barto, 2002) and stochastic
1035
       bisimulation (Givan et al., 2003). As we demonstrate in this section, both MDP homomorphisms
1036
       and stochastic bisimulation are strictly less expressive (meaning, their assumptions are strictly more
1037   stringent) than realizable abstractions. As we show below, any MDP abstraction obtained via an
1038   homomorphisms or a stochastic bisimulation is also an admissible and perfectly realizable abstraction.
1039   On the other hand, there exists MDP-abstraction pairs, M and ⟨M̄, ϕ⟩, that satisfy the realizability
1040   assumptions but no associated MDP homomorphisms or bisimulation exists for the two. In an effort
1041   to make both MDP homomorphisms and stochastic bisimulation more widely applicable, they have
1042   been generalized to approximate the strict relations, respectively in Ravindran & Barto (2004) and
1043   Ferns et al.. Nonetheless, they only allow small variations around the strict equality, similarly to what
1044   we have done in this paper for realizable abstractions, but they cannot capture very different relations
1045
       from their original definition. Therefore, we will relate the strict relations of the various formalisms:
       admissible and perfectly realizable abstractions, MDP homomorphisms, and stochastic bisimulation.
