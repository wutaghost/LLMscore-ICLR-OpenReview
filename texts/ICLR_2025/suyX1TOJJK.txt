030                                              A BSTRACT
031           Event cameras offer unparalleled advantages for real-time perception in dynamic
032           environments, thanks to their microsecond-level temporal resolution and asyn-
033
              chronous operation. Existing event-based object detection methods, however, are
              limited by fixed-frequency paradigms and fail to fully exploit the high-temporal
034
              resolution and adaptability of event cameras. To address these limitations, we
035
              propose FlexEvent, a novel event camera object detection framework that enables
036           detection at arbitrary frequencies. FlexEvent consists of two key components:
037           FlexFuser, an adaptive event-frame fusion module that integrates high-frequency
038           event data with rich semantic information from RGB frames, and FAL, a frequency-
039           adaptive learning mechanism that generates frequency-adjusted labels to enhance
040           model generalization across varying operational frequencies. This combination
041           allows FlexEvent to detect objects with high accuracy in both fast-moving and
042           static scenarios, while adapting to dynamic environments. Extensive experiments
043           on large-scale event camera datasets demonstrate that our approach surpasses
044
              state-of-the-art methods, achieving significant improvements in both standard and
              high-frequency settings. Notably, FlexEvent maintains robust performance when
045
              scaling from 20 Hz to 90 Hz and delivers accurate detection up to 180 Hz, proving
046
              its effectiveness in extreme conditions. Our framework sets a new benchmark for
047           event-based object detection and paves the way for more adaptable, real-time vision
048           systems. The code will be made publicly available to facilitate future research.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Event cameras have garnered significant attention for their ability to capture dynamic scenes with
      microsecond-level temporal resolution (Gallego et al., 2022). Unlike conventional RGB cameras that


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      capture entire frames at fixed intervals, event cameras operate asynchronously, responding to changes
055   in pixel intensity at each location (Zou et al., 2022). This low-latency operation reduces motion blur
056   and enables highly energy-efficient sensing, making event cameras ideal for real-time applications
057   such as autonomous driving, robotics, and surveillance (Steffen et al., 2019).
058
      Despite their potential, existing event camera object detection methods often fail to fully leverage
059
      the high-frequency temporal information captured by these cameras (Cordone et al., 2022; Gehrig &
060
      Scaramuzza, 2022; Jeziorek et al., 2023). Most approaches align event data with the lower frequency
061   of RGB cameras by adopting a fixed time interval between event streams and frame-based annotations
062   (Perot et al., 2020; Gehrig et al., 2021b). While this strategy simplifies data processing, it inevitably
063   overlooks the rich temporal details embedded in high-frequency event streams, limiting its adaptability
064   to dynamic environments where temporal changes occur at varying rates (Perot et al., 2020). Given
065   that human annotations are often synchronized with slower frame rates, current detection models
066   miss valuable information from high-frequency event data, resulting in suboptimal performance when
067   rapid object detection is required in dynamic environments (Messikommer et al., 2020; Schaefer
068   et al., 2022).
069   To address these limitations, we introduce FlexEvent, a novel event camera object detection framework
070   designed to tackle the challenging problem of object detection at varying operational frequencies.
071   Our approach addresses the need for high-frequency detection in fast-changing environments, while
072   adapting to different operational frequencies. We propose two key innovations: (1) FlexFuser, an
073   adaptive event-frame fusion module, and (2) FAL, a frequency-adaptive learning mechanism.
074   Flexible Event-Frame Fusion. The first component, FlexFuser addresses the limitations of event
075   data, which often lacks semantic and texture-rich information, especially at higher frequencies (Zhou
076   et al., 2023), by synchronizing event data with frames and integrating the rich spatial and semantic
077   information from frames with the high-temporal resolution of event streams. It enables high detection
078   accuracy even in fast-moving environments. Furthermore, training on high-frequency event data is
079   computationally expensive and impractical due to the significant human effort required to label such
080   data. FlexFuser mitigates this by sampling event data at varying frequencies, aligning them with the
081
      normal frame rate during training, thus maintaining efficiency while preserving the high-frequency
      benefits at inference time.
082
083   Frequency-Adaptive Learning. The second component, FAL, enhances the generalization capability
084   of event camera detectors across varying operational frequencies, by generating frequency-adaptive
085   labels for the unlabeled high-frequency data. These labels allow the model to learn from high-
086   frequency event streams without manual annotations, and iterative refinement through self-training
087
      ensures that the model remains robust across different motion dynamics and frequency settings.
      Together, these two components allow for accurate real-time detection in rapid scene changes and
088
      adapt to a wide range of operational frequencies, by leveraging the temporal richness of event data
089
      and the semantic detail of RGB frames.
090
091   Our extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of FlexEvent on multiple large-scale event
092   camera datasets. Our approach consistently outperforms recent detectors across both standard and
093
      high-frequency settings. In particular, we achieve mAP gains of 15.5%, 9.4%, and 10.3% over
      previous best-performing detectors on the DSEC-Det (Gehrig & Scaramuzza, 2024), DSEC-Detection
094
      (Tomy et al., 2022), and DSEC-MOD (Zhou et al., 2023) datasets, respectively. Our model also
095
      maintains 96.2% of its performance when the operational frequency shifts from 20 Hz to 90
096   Hz, and delivers accurate detection at frequencies as high as 180 Hz, proving its robustness under
097   extreme conditions.
098
099
      In summary, our contributions are listed as follows:
100   ▶ The FlexEvent framework is designed to tackle the challenging problem of event camera object
101   detection at arbitrary frequencies, being one of the early attempts on this line of study.

      ▶ We propose FlexFuser, an adaptive event-frame fusion that leverages the strengths of both event
103   and frame data, enabling efficient and accurate detection in dynamic environments.
104
105   ▶ We introduce FAL, a frequency-adaptive learning mechanism that generates frequency-adjusted

      labels and improves generalization across a wide range of motion frequencies.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
114
115   Event Camera Object Detection. Event-based detection methods can be broadly split into two
116
      approaches: GNNs/SNNs and dense feed-forward models. GNNs build dynamic spatio-temporal
      graphs by subsampling events (Gehrig & Scaramuzza, 2022; Sun & Ji, 2023; Messikommer et al.,
117
      2020; Schaefer et al., 2022), but they face challenges in propagating information over large spatio-
118
      temporal regions, especially for slow-moving objects. SNNs offer efficient sparse information
119   transmission but are often hindered by their non-differentiable nature, complicating optimization
120   processes (Cuadrado et al., 2023; Cordone et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Dense, feed-forward
121   models represent the second approach. Initial methods using fixed temporal windows (Chen, 2018;
122   Iacono et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2019) struggled with slow-moving or stationary objects due to their
123   limited capability to capture long-term temporal data. Subsequent advancements incorporated RNNs
124   and transformers to enhance temporal modeling capabilities (Perot et al., 2020; Zubić et al., 2023; Li
125   et al., 2022; Gehrig & Scaramuzza, 2023; Peng et al., 2024), but these models often still lack semantic
126   richness and face difficulties in adapting to variable frequencies. EventDrop (Gu et al., 2021) and
127
      Shadow Mosaic (Peng et al., 2023) improve generalization using data augmentation techniques that
      introduce spatial and temporal manipulations to increase data diversity. However, they do not focus
128
      on high-frequency event data or fully leverage the rich temporal information of event streams.
129
130   Event-Frame Multimodal Learning. To overcome the limited texture in event streams, multimodal
131   fusion techniques combining event-based and frame-based data have gained traction across tasks,
132   such as deblurring (Sun et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2020), depth estimation (Gehrig et al., 2021a;
133
      Uddin et al., 2022), and tracking (Zhao et al., 2022; Gehrig et al., 2020). Earlier object detection
      approaches fused event and image data during post-processing (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019), but
134
      they lacked meaningful feature-level interaction. Recent works focus on deeper feature fusion (Tomy
135
      et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; 2021), with advanced methods introducing pixel-level spatial attention
136   or temporal transformers for asynchronous processing (Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Gehrig
137   & Scaramuzza, 2024; Cao et al., 2024). Some early attempts (Li et al., 2023; Gehrig et al., 2021a)
138   explore combining events and frames through asynchronous multi-modal fusion, enabling inference
139   at varying frequencies. However, they do not focus on high-frequency event data and fully leverage
140   the temporally rich nature of event streams. Furthermore, these methods still face challenges in
141   fully exploiting complementary strengths and addressing feature imbalance in event-frame detection.
142   Unlike previous methods, FlexEvent employs a more comprehensive fusion framework that effectively
143   combines high-temporal resolution event data with rich semantics from RGB frames, enabling robust
144
      object detection across varying frequencies while addressing feature imbalance.
145   Label-Efficient Learning in Event Data. Due to limited annotated datasets, label-efficient learning
146   has become an important area for event-based vision. Several studies attempt to reconstruct images
147   from event data (Rebecq et al., 2019; 2021; Stoffregen et al., 2020) or leverage knowledge distillation
148   from pre-trained frame-based models (Wang et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022b; Yang et al., 2023; Kong
149   et al., 2024). Other approaches utilize pre-trained models or self-supervised losses (Klenk et al., 2022;
150
      Wu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2019). LEOD (Wu et al., 2024) pioneered object detection with limited
      labels but did not address high-frequency generalization. A recent state-space model (Zubić et al.,
151
      2024) adapts to varying frequencies without retraining but struggles with detecting static objects at
152
      high frequencies due to reliance solely on event data. In contrast, FlexEvent is specifically designed
153   to adapt to varying event frequencies, ensuring consistent performance even in scenarios with limited
154   labels, and effectively detecting both stationary and fast-moving objects.

      Event Processing. Event cameras are bio-inspired vision sensors that capture changes in log intensity
189
      per pixel asynchronously, rather than capturing entire frames at fixed intervals. Formally, let I(x, y, t)
190   denote the log intensity at pixel coordinates (x, y) and time t. An event e is generated at (x, y, t)
191   whenever the change in log intensity ∆I exceeds a certain threshold C. Such a process can be
192   modeled as:
193                             ∆I(x, y, t) = I(x, y, t) − I(x, y, t − ∆t) ≥ C .                            (1)
194   Each event e is a tuple (x, y, t, p), where (x, y) are the pixel coordinates, t is the timestamp, and
195   p = {−1, 1} denotes the polarity of the event which indicates the direction of the intensity change.
196
      To leverage event data with convolutional neural network layers, we preprocess events into a 4D
197
      tensor E with dimensions representing the polarity, temporal discretization T , and spatial dimensions
198   (H, W ). This representation involves mapping a setting of events E within time interval [ta , tb ) into:

      Problem Formulation. Given two consecutive frames Fa and Fb captured at timestamps Ta and
      Tb , our objective is to leverage the event stream over the interval [Ta , Tb ] to detect objects at the
206
      end timestamp Tb . Existing event-based object detection methods often use fixed time intervals ∆T ,
207
      limiting adaptability to dynamic environments (Perot et al., 2020). Additionally, integrating spatial
208   information from RGB frames remains challenging, affecting performance in complex scenarios
209   (De Tournemire et al., 2020). To address this, we synchronize event data with frames and explore
210   varying training frequencies, leveraging the temporal richness of event cameras to improve detection
211   accuracy.

288   3.3   FAL: F REQUENCY-A DAPTIVE L EARNING M ECHANISM

      FlexFuser aggregates information from different frequencies using labeled low-frequency data. To
      adaptively tune the model to handle diverse frequencies by leveraging both labeled low-frequency
291
      data and unlabeled high-frequency data, we design a flexible frequency-adaptive learning (FAL)
292
      mechanism. FAL incorporates multi-frequency information into the training process through iterative
293   self-training. This approach enhances the model’s ability to generalize across varying frequencies,
294   making it more robust in different scenarios. The key steps of the FAL mechanism are as follows:
295
296
      Pre-Training with Low-Frequency Labels. Rather than training solely at the same frequency as the
      data collection frequency a, we enhance the model’s capability by training it at a higher frequency b.
297

298                   b/a
      event set {Ebi }i=0 , which corresponds to the labeled timestamp. This approach allows the model
299
      to capture valuable high-frequency temporal information while still utilizing low-frequency labels,
300
      improving its temporal understanding and robustness. The training objective is to minimize the
301   detection loss over the labeled data:

306   model generates high-frequency labels ŷ by performing inference on the entire high-frequency
307                   b/a

308   frequencies, improving the model’s ability to generalize across different temporal conditions.

311

312

351   Implementation Details. We trained our model using the YOLOX framework (Zheng et al., 2021),
352   optimizing with a combination of IoU loss, classification loss, and regression loss, averaged across
353   both batch and sequence length for stable training. We also introduce the extra MoE loss for balancing
354   the utilization among the experts. The model was trained for 100, 000 iterations with a batch size of
355
      8 and a sequence length of 11, using a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 . All experiments were conducted on
      two NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPUs with 24GB memory, with the entire training process completed in
356
      approximately one day. Due to space limits, more details are placed in the Appendix.

533
534   This paper introduces FlexEvent, an event camera object detection framework designed to operate
535   across arbitrary frequencies. By combining FlexFuser for adaptive event-frame fusion and FAL for
536   frequency-adaptive learning, we combine event data’s rich temporal information with the semantic
537   detail of RGB frames to overcome the limitations of existing methods and offer a flexible solution for
538   dynamic environments. Extensive experiments on large-scale datasets show that our approach signifi-
539   cantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-frequency scenarios, demonstrating
      its robustness and adaptability for real-world applications like autonomous driving and robotics.

892   A.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
893
      In this work, we adopt the mean Average Precision (mAP) as the primary metric to evaluate the
894
      performance of our object detection models, consistent with standard practices in the field. The
895   mAP metric provides a comprehensive measure of detection accuracy across multiple categories and
896   intersection-over-union (IoU) thresholds.
897
898
      Mathematically, the Average Precision (AP) for a single class is calculated as:
899                                                   Z 1
900                                            AP =         p(r) dr                                   (10)
901                                                    0

902
      where p(r) represents the precision at a given recall level r. The mean Average Precision (mAP) is
903   then computed as the mean of the AP values across all object categories and a range of IoU thresholds
904   (typically from 0.5 to 0.95 with a step size of 0.05). This provides an overall measure of model
905   performance across different levels of localization precision.
906
      In addition to mAP, we also report the following metrics from the COCO evaluation protocol (Lin
907
      et al., 2014):
908
909          • AP50 : The average precision when evaluated at a fixed IoU threshold of 0.50, indicating
910            how well the model performs with relatively lenient localization criteria.
911
912          • AP75 : The average precision at a fixed IoU threshold of 0.75, representing performance
913
               under stricter localization requirements.
914          • APS , APM , and APL : These metrics represent the average precision for small (S), medium
915            (M ), and large (L) objects, respectively. Object sizes are defined based on their pixel
916            area, with APS typically representing objects with areas less than 32 × 32 pixels, APM
917            representing areas between 32 × 32 and 96 × 96 pixels, and APL for objects larger than
               96 × 96 pixels.

919   capabilities across varying object sizes and localization precision levels, ensuring a comprehensive
920   evaluation of detection performance.

      A.4   T RAINING & I NFERENCE D ETAILS
923
924   We train our models using mixed precision to optimize both memory efficiency and training speed.
925   The training process spans 100,000 iterations, utilizing the ADAM optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with
926   a OneCycle learning rate schedule (Smith & Topin, 2019), which gradually decays from a peak
927   learning rate to enhance convergence.
928   Consistent with (Gehrig & Scaramuzza, 2023), we employed a mixed batching strategy to balance
929   computational efficiency and memory usage. Specifically:
930
931          • Standard Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT): Applied to half of the training samples,
932            allowing for full sequence training.
933          • Truncated BPTT (TBPTT): Used for the other half, reducing memory usage by splitting
934            sequences into smaller segments.
935
936   For data augmentation, we applied random horizontal flipping and zoom transformations (both
937   zoom-in and zoom-out) to enhance the diversity of training samples.
938   Our training process utilized the YOLOX framework (Zheng et al., 2021), a versatile object detection
939   framework known for its efficient and high-performing architecture. We employed a multi-component
940   loss function to optimize our model effectively:
941
942          • Intersection over Union (IoU) Loss: This loss component measures the overlap between the
943            predicted bounding boxes and the ground-truth boxes, ensuring that the predicted regions
944
               closely match the actual object locations.
945          • Classification Loss: This component evaluates the accuracy of class predictions for each
946            detected object, ensuring that the model correctly identifies the category of each detected
947            instance.
948          • Regression Loss: This loss assesses the precision of the predicted bounding box coordinates,
949            helping the model refine the location and size of bounding boxes to align closely with the
950            ground-truth annotations.
951
952   To ensure stable training, these loss components were averaged across both the batch and sequence
953
      length at each optimization step. This averaging process helped to reduce variance during training
      and facilitated smoother convergence of the model parameters.
954
955   Training Configuration. The training was conducted with a batch size of 8, which provided an
956   optimal balance between efficient GPU utilization and memory requirements. Each training sample
957   contained a sequence length of 11 frames, allowing the model to learn temporal dependencies
958   effectively. The learning rate was set to 1 × 10−4 , following a OneCycle learning rate schedule that
959
      allowed for efficient exploration of the learning space and helped in achieving faster convergence.
960   Hardware & Training Time. All training experiments were carried out on two NVIDIA RTX
961   A5000 GPUs, each with 24GB of memory, providing the computational resources necessary for
962   handling the high-resolution event data and RGB frames. The complete training process, including
963   all iterations and model optimization, took approximately one day, demonstrating the efficiency of
964   our implementation in terms of both training speed and resource utilization.

       Additionally, the computational efficiency provided by the adaptive event-frame fusion (FlexFuser)

1141   D.2   B ROADER I MPACT
1142
1143   The broader implications of FlexEvent include its potential to advance the field of event-based vision
1144   and enable new applications where high temporal resolution is crucial. By overcoming the limitations
1145
       of conventional fixed-frequency object detection methods, our approach paves the way for more
       flexible, adaptable AI systems. This could lead to improvements in areas such as drone navigation,
1146
       real-time video analysis for security purposes, and human-robot collaboration, where detecting
1147
       fast-moving objects and adapting to changing environments are critical.
1148
1149   Moreover, the development of efficient and scalable detection systems like our approach can drive
1150   further innovation in resource-constrained environments, such as low-power edge devices. These
1151
       advancements could make high-performance detection systems more widely available, particularly in
       developing regions or areas with limited access to computational resources.
1152
1153   However, as with any powerful technology, there is a risk of misuse. Enhanced object detection
1154   capabilities could potentially be exploited for surveillance purposes, raising privacy concerns. As
1155   event camera technology becomes more widespread, it is important to establish ethical guidelines
1156   and regulatory frameworks to ensure that these systems are used responsibly, particularly when
1157
       monitoring public spaces or collecting sensitive data.
1158
1159   D.3   K NOWN L IMITATIONS
1160
       While FlexEvent demonstrates significant performance improvements, there are several known
1161
       limitations to our approach.
1162
1163   Dependence on Event Camera Quality. The effectiveness of our approach relies on the quality of
1164   the event camera sensor. Inconsistent or noisy event data, especially under poor lighting or extreme
1165
       weather conditions, could affect detection performance. Future work could explore robustness to
       sensor noise and adaptation to diverse environmental conditions.
1166
1167   Limited Generalization to Unseen Scenarios. Although our approach shows strong performance
1168   across varying frequencies, it may still face challenges in completely unseen environments, where
1169   the motion dynamics and scene conditions differ significantly from the training data. Investigating
1170   methods for domain adaptation or online learning could help improve generalization to new contexts.
1171   Resource Requirements for High-Frequency Data. While FlexFuser mitigates the computational
1172   cost of training on high-frequency event data, processing extremely high-frequency event streams
1173   still requires substantial computational resources during inference. This could limit the scalability on
1174   resource-constrained devices or in real-time applications with stringent latency requirements.
