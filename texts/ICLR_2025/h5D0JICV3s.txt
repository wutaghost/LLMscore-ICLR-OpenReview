                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have recently enabled considerable advances
012            in the realm of image and video quality assessment, but this progress has yet to
013            be translated to the domain of 3D assets. We are interested in using these models
014            to conduct No-Reference Point Cloud Quality Assessment (NR-PCQA), where
015            the aim is to automatically evaluate the perceptual quality of a point cloud in
016            absence of a reference. We begin with the observation that different modalities
017            of data – text descriptions, 2D projections, and 3D point cloud views – provide
018            uniquely useful insights into point cloud quality. We leverage this to devise a mul-
019            timodal dataset construction strategy providing a holistic combination of multiple
020
               types and levels of information. We then construct PIT-QMM, a novel LMM for
               NR-PCQA that is capable of consuming text, images and point clouds to predict
021
               quality scores. Extensive experimentation shows that our proposed method out-
022
               performs the state-of-the-art by significant margins on popular benchmarks with
023            fewer training iterations, and thorough ablations validate our dataset construction
024            strategy. Code and datasets are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/pit-
025            qmm-BD1F/.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Point clouds are collections of 3D points with color, opacity, and other features representing ob-
031   jects or environments. Recent advances have cemented point clouds as pivotal data structures for
032   3D representation, particularly in applications such as autonomous driving, immersive gaming and
033
      digital twin systems (Qi et al., 2017; Afham et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a). These simple yet
      information-rich representations allow detailed spatial analysis with minimal assumptions about un-
034
      derlying geometry. However, this also means that they are highly susceptible to corruption at all
035
      stages of their operation cycle, from acquisition due to sensor inaccuracies, to compression losses
036   and transmission errors. Not only do these impairments affect perceptual quality for humans, but
037   they also directly impact downstream applications.
038
039
      As a result of this corruption, assessing the quality of point clouds automatically and at scale has
      emerged has a key research question. Traditional metrics like PSNR or SSIM (Wang, 2004), widely
040
      used for image and video quality assessment, have been adapted for point clouds, but these often fail
041
      to capture the intricacies of 3D data. Learning-based methods have improved upon these metrics,
042   but suffer from a lack of generalizability due to a scarcity of labeled training data. Most PCQA
043   (point cloud quality assessment) datasets are only of the order of hundreds of distorted samples
044   with labels (i.e. mean opinion scores, or MOS) due to the highly laborious data collection process.
045   This is particularly disadvantageous in the No-Reference (NR) setup, where there are no pristine
046   point clouds available to serve as a reference for quality assessment, leading to further scarcity of
047   information.
048   The foremost solution that has emerged to attain generalizability in the face of label scarcity has
049   been unsupervised pretraining on large datasets. In particular, approaches based on contrastive
050   learning have risen to the top of many benchmarks, such as CONTRIQUE (Madhusudana et al.,
051   2022), ReIQA (Saha et al., 2023) and recently in the point cloud domain, CoPA (Shan et al., 2024).
052   However, these require large numbers of positive and negative paired samples and extensive aug-
053   mentation schemes, which make them computationally intense to train even though the base datasets
      may be relatively small. Moreover, the design of such samples requires great thought and care, es-

054
      pecially in the quality domain, where the fine visual low-level details of media are paramount. This
055   leads to it being relatively difficult to apply the benefits of scaling, as observed in CONTRIQUE and
056   ReIQA, which are trained on million-scale base datasets.
057
058
      Elsewhere, foundation models trained on billion-size datasets with billions of parameters have de-
      livered impressive performance on several benchmarks. In particular, large image-text multimodal
059
      models (LMMs) have recently been applied to image and video quality assessment with great suc-
060
      cess, as in Q-Align (Wu et al., 2023b). While these models have delivered state-of-the-art perfor-
061   mance and generalizability in the 2D space, they do not straightforwardly extend to the 3D space.
062   One approach is to simply take 2D projections of the 3D content and apply the model, but as we
063   demonstrate in Section 4.5, this tends to yield sub-optimal results. This is likely because of the
064   loss of information in the projection process due to factors such as occlusion, depth ambiguity and
065   viewpoint dependency. There have been efforts to recover the lost information by supplementing
066   predictions from 2D quality foundation models with traditional point cloud quality statistics, as in
067   LMM-PCQA (Zhang et al., 2024). However, since they rely on handcrafted features to extract infor-
068   mation from the point cloud, they cannot leverage the benefits of training to learn more sophisticated
069
      quality features, and crucially, cannot learn the interactions between different modalities of data.
070   Efforts have been made to develop point-text LMMs, particularly for semantic tasks such as object
071   classification and question-answering. However, the fixed context length and quadratically-scaling
072   computational costs of transformers have limited their application to point clouds of relatively small
073   size and complexity i.e. on the order of tens of thousands of points. PCQA datasets usually contain
074   point clouds with hundreds of thousands to millions of points, as fine-grained details about quality
075
      are often not perceptible at lower resolutions. As a result, when these models are fine-tuned on
      PCQA datasets, there is either a tremendous loss of information due to downsampling, or a large
076
      domain shift in terms of the nature of the point clouds they were pretrained to handle. This leads to
077
      sub-optimal performance on PCQA, as we demonstrate in Section 4.5.
078
079   In essence, image-text quality foundation models are able to understand quality, but not 3D content,
080   whereas point-text multimodal models are able to understand 3D content but not at the granularity
081
      required for quality. This paper seeks to bridge this gap. To do so, we propose the Point-Image-
      Text Quality Multimodal Model, or PIT-QMM, which is a first-of-its-kind point-image-text LMM
082
      for PCQA. The key insight behind the construction of the model is that different modalities can
083
      provide complementary information about quality. Point cloud patches can provide information
084   about local variations which are typically lost in the projection process. Image projections can
085   supply a global picture about the point cloud which cannot currently be delivered by off-the-shelf
086   point cloud encoders for large clouds. Finally, text inputs can used to describe the psychometric
087   setup for rating collection, the details of which often change how the point cloud is perceived, and
088   thus can affect the final quality scores. PIT-QMM is trained end-to-end and is fully differentiable,
089   which means it may also be used as a training loss in applications where quality is a key factor for
090   performance, such as content generation, editing or enhancement.
091   Our main contributions may be summarized as follows:
092
093          • We bridge the algorithmic gap between 2D and 3D quality assessment with our proposed
094            PIT-QMM, which is the first end-to-end point-image-text multimodal model optimized for
095            PCQA.
096          • We propose a carefully-designed dataset construction recipe for NR-PCQA, where each
097            piece aims to leverage a complementary source of information relevant to PCQA.
098          • We perform thorough performance benchmarking, and show that our model beats state-
099            of-the-art methods by a large margin despite requiring fewer training iterations. We also
100            perform a thorough ablation study to validate the importance of each step in our dataset
101            construction recipe.

138   However, these metrics do not adequately account for the fact that the human visual system (HVS)

140   discrepancies. Thus, several metrics that consider both geometric structure and color have been
141
      considered, such as PCQM (Meynet et al., 2020), GraphSIM (Zhang et al., 2021) and MPED (Yang

142
      and pools them with an optimally-weighted linear combination. On the other hand, GraphSIM also
143
      considers projections, where it renders the point cloud onto six perpendicular image planes of a
144   cube and computes image-based quality features along with point cloud-based features. Finally,
145   these features are aggregated via linear combinations to produce a final score.
146
147
      The preceding metrics are all full-reference (FR), which means they assume that a pristine source
      point cloud is available. However, this may not be the case in many practical scenarios. For the no-
148
      reference (NR) case, traditional methods typically compute various handcrafted features and then
149
      train a regressor to obtain a predicted MOS score. For example, Zhang et al. (2022b) propose an
150   NR-PCQA metric that projects point clouds into feature domains based on geometry and color and
151   regresses the predicted MOS using a support vector machine (SVM). While hand-crafted features
152   have the advantage of explicit semantic meaning, our understanding of point cloud distortions itself
153   is still quite limited, which handicaps the development of features that can handle more complex
154   distortion settings.
155
      With the advent of deep learning, learning-based methods have instead become the leaders in
156   NR-PCQA. PQA-Net (Liu et al., 2021) takes cubic projections and extracts multi-view features,
157   which it then uses to jointly predict quality and distortion type. ResSCNN (Liu et al., 2023b) em-
158   ploys a voxel-based sparse 3D-CNN to process point clouds and regress on quality scores. MM-
159   PCQA (Zhang et al., 2022c) utilizes a multi-modal learning approach on point cloud patches and
160   image projections, where cross-modal attention is used to fuse image and point cloud features, upon
161   which a quality score is regressed. Nevertheless, all these approaches are highly dependent on the
      availability of labeled data, and thus have poor generalization once tuned.

162
      Several approaches have been tried to reduce the dependence on labeled data. IT-PCQA (Yang
163   et al., 2022a) uses unsupervised domain adaptation to transfer the power of deep quality evaluators
164   designed for the 2D case to the 3D setting, but the significant domain gap leads to unsatisfactory per-
165   formance. Recently, the release of LS-PCQA (Liu et al., 2023b), a relatively large-scale dataset for
166   point cloud quality assessment, has opened up avenues for pretraining, similar to the 2D case. As dis-
167   cussed earlier, contrastive learning has been employed with great success in image and video quality
168   assessment. CONTRIQUE (Madhusudana et al., 2022) trains similarity under quality-preserving
169   augmentations to obtain robust features useful for quality assessment. In the 3D space, CoPA (Shan
170   et al., 2024) takes image projections of point clouds and mixes patches from them to create positive
171
      pairs. As mentioned earlier, while contrastive approaches produce robust and useful features, they
      require a complex and expensive positive and negative pair generation process, which limits their
172
      application on even larger datasets.
173
174   Outside the quality space, multimodal large language models have shown impressive performance
175   in multimodal understanding, be it text-image (Liu et al., 2023a), text-audio (Huang et al., 2023),
176   text-motion (Jiang et al., 2023) etc. A key idea behind progress has been that of visual instruction
177
      fine-tuning as proposed in LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), which shows that LLMs can be made to under-
      stand visual content with a relatively simple two-stage fine-tuning scheme. Point-LLM (Xu et al.,
178
      2023) proposes LLaVA-style instruction fine-tuning for point clouds, where it leverages a pretrained
179
      point cloud encoder and projector to inject point cloud features and perform multimodal instruction
180   fine-tuning. PointBind (Guo et al., 2023) uses a contrastive learning approach to align point cloud
181   features with other modality features, allowing their downstream use in other applications. However,
182   all of these methods are designed with high-level semantic tasks like object classification in mind,
183   so cannot handle the large-scale point clouds in PCQA well.
184
      In the 2D space, Q-Bench (Wu et al., 2023a) demonstrated that LMMs not only understand high-
185
      level semantics, but also low-level details relevant for quality assessment. Wu et al. (2024) perform
186   an evaluation of LMMs for quality assessment, conducting ablations to figure out which setting
187   performs the best. Building on this, Q-Align finetunes an off-the-shelf vision-language LMM on
188   discretized quality scores and converts predicted logits to MOSs using softmax pooling. However,
189   we show that naively applying Q-Align to the 3D case results in a significant domain gap, likely due
190   to the information loss involved in moving from the 3D to the 2D case. Our proposed method there-
191   fore introduces the point cloud modality to the mix, and demonstrates that the advantages obtained
192   by LMM-based quality models also hold for the 3D case when the relevant information is available.
193   LMM-PCQA (Zhang et al., 2024) builds on Q-Align by first fine-tuning it on cubic image projec-
194   tions, and then training a regressor on predicted logits from the fine-tuned image-text model along
195   with classical point cloud structural features such as linearity and planarity. As discussed earlier,
196   this approach is limited in the way it extracts information from the point cloud, and cannot leverage
197   the unique power of multimodal training to learn interactions between the different data modali-
198   ties. On the other hand, our approach seamlessly integrates deep point cloud encoders with existing
199   image-text foundation models, thus enabling true end-to-end multimodal training.

237
      view represents the broad shape of the cloud to provide content-level information, which can guide
238
      the final quality score, as in ReIQA. Next, we randomly sample a small set of local patches from the
239   point cloud to provide information about local variations, such as high-frequency distortions. These
240   two views together form our point cloud inputs to the model. We also explore a variant where we
241   take patches at two different scales, analogous to the two-scale inputs typically observed in 2D qual-
242   ity assessment. Here, we have patches from the original point cloud and a uniformly downsampled
243   version of the point cloud, where we downsample by a factor of 2. We report the performance of
244   this variant in the ablation study. Note that these views comprise a small fraction of the total number
245   of points in the cloud (typically 3-5%). While processing such a small number of points makes our
246   method much more efficient as compared to processing the entire cloud, it also means that we need
247
      complementary global information to capture a more holistic picture of the point cloud.

249   3.1.2   I MAGE P ROJECTIONS
250
251   As discussed in Section 3.1.1, since we can only provide a limited set of local views with point
252   clouds due to model constraints, we also add image projections of the point cloud to our input.

254
      render N (P ) into multi-view images {xi ∈ RH×W ×C |6i=1 } from six perpendicular viewpoints

255
      projections not only provide a global view of the point cloud, but also allow us to leverage the power
256
      of pretrained large image quality models. We set the projection parameters as used by the original
257   dataset authors wherever available, else apply best effort settings as appropriate.

287   3.2     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
288
289   As shown in Figure 1, our PIT-QMM is a generative model that aims to complete multi-modal
290   sentences containing point clouds, images and text. The model consists of four main components -
291   an image encoder fim , a point cloud encoder fpoint , a point cloud embedding projector fpoint proj ,
292   and a large language model (LLM) backbone fllm .
293   The point cloud encoder fpoint takes in a point cloud P ∈ Rs×n×d , where s is the number of
294   sampled views, n is the number of points and d is the feature dimension. The output is a sequence
295   of patched point features X ∈ Rs×m×c , where m is the number of patch features and c is the feature
296   dimension. The projector fproj is a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that maps the point features X to
                                 ′
297   point tokens Y ∈ Rs×m×c , where c′ is the dimension of the point tokens, which is the same as the
                                                                              ′
298   text and image tokens. Finally we flatten this into a sequence Z ∈ Rsm×c , which we feed into fllm .
299                                                       ′   ′

300   The LLM fllm takes in a sequence of the form Rn ×c , where n′ is the length of the mixed image, text
301
      and point cloud input token sequence. As a decoder-only LLM, it produces a probability distribution
      of predictions for the next token of size RV for a given input sequence, where V is the vocabulary
302
      size of the LLM, and autoregressively produces the output sequence.
303
304   The image encoder fim and LLM backbone fllm in our implementation are architecturally similar
305   to the open source LMM mPLUG-Owl-2 (Ye et al., 2024). In addition to its base image encoder,
306   mPLUG-Owl-2 also has a visual abstractor that greatly reduces the number of tokens needed to
307
      represent an image, thus allowing us to process all six cubic views efficiently. Moreover, it features
      modality-aware modules that allow learning the interactions between different modalities effectively.
308
      However, note that the overall architecture is agnostic to the kind of image encoder, point cloud
309
      encoder and LLM used, and these may be easily replaced with any off-the-shelf variant as desired.

      3.3     T RAINING AND I NFERENCE
313
      The model is trained end-to-end by minimizing the negative log-likelihood of the token at each
314
      position. We ignore the image tokens, point cloud tokens, user prompt, and instruction tokens for
315   computing the loss, so that the model can focus on producing relevant and coherent responses.
316
317
      3.3.1    L ABEL S MOOTHING AND D ISCRETIZATION
318
319   As observed in Q-Align, LMMs optimized for quality perform better when they are asked to produce
320   discrete text labels, largely due to their bias to produce text as opposed to numeric values. As a result,
321   we follow a similar discretization strategy during training, where we convert continuous quality
322   scores to five-point Likert scale levels in our input prompts. The labels are equally spaced based on
323   the score ranges in the respective quality datasets. During inference, we convert the discrete labels
      into continuous quality scores by first assigning them discrete numeric levels (e.g. 1 to 5) and then


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      taking a weighted average of the numeric levels based on the corresponding token probabilities in
325   the output of the LLM.
326
327   3.3.2    T WO - STAGE T RAINING
328
329   As in LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), we also employ a two stage training strategy. In the first feature
330   alignment stage, we freeze all parameters except for the point cloud projector, and train on brief-
331   description instructions from the Cap3D (Luo et al., 2023) dataset, which is for 3D captioning. As
332   all the point clouds in this dataset are relatively small, we do not apply our point cloud sampling
333
      strategy at this step. This stage involves aligning the point cloud features with the image and text
      features.
334
335   In the second instruction-tuning stage, we unfreeze the image abstractor and add LoRA adapters to
336   the LLM and the point cloud encoder. We then finetune the weights end-to-end using the constructed
337   quality dataset. In this stage, all the modules are tuned specifically for point cloud quality assess-
338   ment. For the image abstractor, this involves adapting to the distribution shift of synthetic point
339
      cloud projections as opposed to the general space of images, whereas the point cloud encoder has to
      adapt to the distribution of local patches with high-frequency variations instead of object-level point
340
      clouds.

345   4.1     DATASETS
346
347
      Our experiments are based on three popular point cloud quality assessment datasets, namely LS-
      PCQA (Liu et al., 2023b), SJTU-PCQA (Yang et al., 2021), and WPC (Liu et al., 2022). LS-PCQA
348
      is a large-scale point cloud quality assessment dataset with 104 pristine and 24,024 distorted point
349
      clouds. Each pristine point cloud is impaired with 33 types of distortions under 7 levels. The la-
350   bels in LS-PCQA are mostly synthetically generated pseudo-MOSs, with only 930 samples having
351   psychometrically-collected true MOSs. We term this subset LSPCQA-small and report results of
352   ablations on it, along with WPC. SJTU-PCQA contains 9 reference and 378 distorted samples im-
353   paired with 7 types of distortions under 6 levels, while WPC contains 20 reference point clouds and
354   740 distorted sampled disturbed by 5 types of distortions.
355
356   4.2     E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
357
358   We tested our PIT-QMM model against other state-of-the-art models on all of the datasets described
359   in Section 4.1. We first constructed instruction-tuning data from the raw datasets as described in
360
      Section 3.1. Each sample is then a set of point cloud samples, cubic image projections and user-
      agent instruction text. We split each dataset into content-separated train-test sets in the ratio of
361
      4:1. We then minimized loss on the training set and obtained metrics on the test set. Due to the
362
      stochasticity involved in sampling from the point cloud, we computed metrics on the test set with 10
363   different seeds and took the mean. Finally, the test metrics were averaged over 5 different train-test
364   splits to obtain the final reported metrics. Two widely adopted evaluation metrics were employed to
365   quantify the level of agreement between predicted quality scores and MOSs: Spearman rank order
366   correlation coefficient (SROCC) and Pearson linear correlation coefficient (PLCC).
367
368   4.3     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
369
370   The point cloud projections were rendered with PyTorch3D (Ravi et al., 2020) at a resolution of
371   512 × 512. All point cloud samples are n = 8192 dimensional with 3 spatial coordinates and 3
372   RGB color coordinates, which makes d = 6. The furthest point sampling was done with the Python
373   package fpsample with the bucket-based FPS algorithm (Han et al., 2023). To sample local patches,
374
      we constructed a search tree using the Python package FAISS, sampled a single point randomly and
      then looked up the closest points near it to construct the final sample. We sampled three patches in
375
      total including the furthest point sample of the point cloud.
376
377   Our experiments were performed with Huggingface and PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) using 3 × 40
      GB NVIDIA A100 GPUs. For the point cloud encoder, we used Point-BERT pretrained with ULIP-

      2 (Xue et al., 2024) on the Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023) dataset. The point encoder outputs
379   m = 513 point features, each with c = 384 dimensions. The point cloud projector is taken as
380   a randomly initialized MLP. The projector contains three linear layers with the GeLU (Hendrycks
381   & Gimpel, 2016) activation, which maps point features to tokens with c′ = 5120 dimensions. The

383   which is a modified LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) model. Since we added two additional

385   and LLM were initialized from Q-Align.
386   For the alignment stage, we pretrained on the instruction-following variant of Cap3D as released in
387   Point-LLM (Xu et al., 2023) for 3 epochs with a batch size of 12. We used a learning rate of 2×10−3
388   with cosine annealing and a warmup rate of 0.3. All other hyperparameters were the same as those
389   used in Point-LLM. In the finetuning stage, we trained on LS-PCQA for 5 epochs, SJTU-PCQA
390   for 90 epochs and WPC for 30 epochs. We used a learning rate of 2 × 10−4 with cosine annealing
391   and a warmup rate of 0.3. For the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) modules, we used r = 128, α = 256,
392   and dropout of 0.05 on the multiway Vproj and Qproj layers in mPLUG-Owl2, and the V and Q
393
      matrices in Point-BERT.

      5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we presented a novel end-to-end no-reference point cloud quality assessment algorithm
535   based on LMMs. By leveraging complementary sources of information from different modalities
536   and the power of large pretrained modality encoders, the proposed PIT-QMM is able to predict
537   quality scores across a wide variety of distortion and content types. Extensive experiments show
538   that PIT-QMM is able to achieve competitive performance across a wide variety of benchmarks with
539   overall fewer training iterations than state-of-the-art methods. Thorough ablations also validated
      each step of our dataset construction strategy.
