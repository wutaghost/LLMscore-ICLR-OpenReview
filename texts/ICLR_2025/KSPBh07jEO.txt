008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010
               Large language models are increasingly relied upon as sources of information, but
011            their propensity for generating false or misleading statements with high confidence
012            poses risks for users and society. In this paper, we confront the critical problem of
013            epistemic miscalibration—where a model’s linguistic assertiveness fails to reflect
014            its true internal certainty. We introduce a new human-labeled dataset and a novel
015            method for measuring the linguistic assertiveness of Large Language Models which
016            cuts error rates by over 50% relative to previous benchmarks. Validated across
017            multiple datasets, our method reveals a stark misalignment between how confidently
018            models linguistically present information and their actual accuracy. Further human
019
               evaluations confirm the severity of this miscalibration. This evidence underscores
               the urgent risk of the overstated certainty Large Language Models hold which may
020
               mislead users on a massive scale. Our framework provides a crucial step forward
021
               in diagnosing and correcting this miscalibration, offering a path to safer and more
022            trustworthy AI across domains.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have markedly transformed how humans seek and consume in-
028
      formation, becoming integral across diverse fields such as public health (Ali et al., 2023), coding
029   (Zambrano et al., 2023), and education (Whalen & et al., 2023). Despite their growing influence,
030   LLMs are not without shortcomings. One notable issue is the potential for generating responses that,
031   while convincing, may be inaccurate or nonsensical—a long-standing phenomenon often referred to
032   as “hallucinations” (Jo, 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024b). This raises concerns about the
033   reliability and trustworthiness of these models.
034
      A critical aspect of trustworthiness in LLMs is epistemic calibration—the alignment between a
035   model’s internal confidence in its outputs and the way it expresses that confidence through natural
036   language. Misalignment between internal certainty and external expression can lead to users being
037   misled by overconfident or underconfident statements, posing significant risks in high-stakes domains
038   such as legal advice, medical diagnosis, and misinformation detection. While of great normative
039   concern, how LLMs express linguistic uncertainty has received relatively little attention to date (Sileo
040   & Moens, 2023; Belem et al., 2024).
041   Figures 1 and 5 illustrate the issue of epistemic calibration providing insights into the operation of
042   certainty in the context of human interactions with LLMs. We highlight the following key points in
043   these figures:
044
      • Distinct Roles of Certainty: Internal certainty and linguistic assertiveness have distinct functions
045     within LLM interactions, underlining their unique contributions to how they shape individual
046     beliefs.
047   • Human access to LLM certainty: Linguistic assertiveness holds a critical role as the primary
048     form of certainty available to users. Unlike internal certainty, which remains hidden within the
049     model’s computational processes, linguistic assertiveness is directly perceivable and influences
050     how users interpret the model’s outputs.
051   • Beyond Content: Users retrieve more than just the content from an LLM’s output. The style
052     and assertiveness of the language used also play a significant role, shaping perceptions through
053
        the communication of certainty. This interaction between the model’s output and its linguistic
        assertiveness is crucial for understanding the full impact on individual perceptions.

074   Several studies have explored the calibration of internal confidence in LLMs. For instance, Zhang
075   et al. (2024) examine confidence calibration, proposing techniques to reduce hallucinations and
076   enhance the model’s ability to answer known questions while avoiding unknown ones. However, they
077   overlook the role of linguistic assertiveness and how external certainty can still lead to epistemic
078   miscalibration even if internal confidence is addressed. Similarly, Ren et al. (2023) focus on factual
079
      knowledge and LLM behavior before and after retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). While they
      investigate internal confidence, they fail to frame miscalibration as an end-to-end issue involving both
080
      internal certainty and linguistic assertiveness, ignoring the interplay between model predictions and
081
      how confidence is expressed linguistically.
082
083   More recent studies aim to bridge the gap between internal confidence and linguistic assertiveness but
084   still face considerable limitations. Mielke et al. (2022) explore epistemic calibration but use limited
085
      scoring scales to measure both assertiveness and confidence, restricting continuous assessments.
      Their models also rely on a narrow range of datasets, which limits their applicability across diverse
086
      domains. Zhou et al. (2024a) address miscalibration using epistemic markers, but their method
087
      lacks real domain grounding and fails to consider the complexities of language. In contrast, our
088   study overcomes these limitations by fine-tuning models for a broader scope of topics and domains,
089   achieving state-of-the-art performance in assertiveness estimation.
090
091
      This review of existing work on LLM calibration and confidence reveals several gaps that our research
      aims to address:
092
093   • Lack of Integrated Approaches: Previous studies address either internal certainty or linguistic
094     assertiveness but rarely both simultaneously (Jiang et al., 2021). There is a need for comprehensive
095     frameworks that integrate these aspects to ensure LLMs communicate accurately and responsibly.
096   • Inadequate Assertiveness Measurement: Existing methods for measuring linguistic assertiveness
097     rely heavily on lexicon-based approaches (Pei & Jurgens, 2021) or subjective perceptions without
098     adequate validation (Steyvers et al., 2024). These methods often lack contextual depth and fail to
099     generalize across diverse domains.
100   • Limited High-Stakes Evaluation: Although some studies explore epistemic calibration, they
101
        cover a narrow range of topics and employ low-resolution measures of assertiveness, limiting their
        applicability in critical domains such as misinformation detection (Mielke et al., 2022).
102
103   To address these gaps, our paper provides:
104
105   • A New Assertiveness Detection Model: We train a new model using a diverse dataset to detect
106     linguistic assertiveness, improving the accuracy relative to previous approaches by incorporating
107     contextual nuance and aligning more closely with human perceptions. This approach also addresses
        limitations in lexicon-based methods, enhancing generalizability across domains.

      • Empirical Evidence of Epistemic Miscalibration: Our work provides a comprehensive compari-
109     son between internal certainty and linguistic assertiveness, documenting instances of miscalibration
110     across a broad range of domains. Our experiments reveal that LLMs frequently generate highly
111     assertive explanations despite low internal certainty, which can mislead users.
112   • Validation with Human Perception: We conduct comprehensive surveys assessing human
113     perceptions of LLMs’ linguistic assertiveness, confirming that misalignment poses a real risk.
114     This addresses the gap regarding alignment of computational measures with subjective human
115     perceptions of language.
116
      This study also presents a novel human-centered approach for developing a robust assertiveness
117
      scoring method, introducing a new dataset from multiple domains. To ensure reliability, we train
118   and compare several models, identifying the best estimator for assertiveness based on accuracy
119   and transferability across different contexts. After selecting the top-performing model, we validate
120   the results using human-surveyed data from the LIAR dataset (Wang, 2017), which was coded by
121   participants different from those who annotated the primary dataset. This comprehensive methodology
122   enables us to thoroughly assess both the objective and subjective aspects of assertiveness in language
123   model explanations.
124   Our findings reveal that when the model has low internal certainty, it generates explanations that
125   are significantly over-assertive, meaning the language used implies a higher degree of certainty
126   than warranted by the model’s actual confidence or knowledge base. This miscalibration could lead
127   users to misconstrue the model’s judgments as more reliable than they actually are. Our results
128   confirm a strong correlation between the GPT 4o’s assertiveness scores and human perceptions of
129   assertiveness, but a weak correlation between human perceptions and internal certainty, and an even
130   weaker relationship between GPT 4o model assertiveness and internal certainty. Together, the results
131   presented here provide the most extensive evidence of epistemic miscalibration in LLMs to date.1

133   2     C ONCEPTUALLY UNDERSTANDING CERTAINTY AND ASSERTIVENESS IN
134         NATURAL LANGUAGE
135
136   To elucidate the challenges of epistemic calibration, it is essential to understand the concepts of
137   certainty and assertiveness in natural language communication. These foundational notions underpin
138   how information is conveyed by LLMs and interpreted by users.
139
140   2.1     C ERTAINTY
141
142   Effective communication hinges on the accurate conveyance of certainty, enabling individuals and
143   systems to assess the reliability of information. In human communication, speakers use linguistic
144   cues to express their confidence levels, which listeners interpret to form judgments about the truthful-
145
      ness and credibility of statements (Budescu & Wallsten, 1985; Clarke et al., 1992). Similarly, for
      LLMs, effectively conveying certainty is crucial to ensure users can trust and interpret the provided
146
      information accurately. In this section, we decompose certainty into two key concepts: Internal
147
      Certainty and External Certainty. Misalignment between these two dimensions can potentially
148   lead to the need for Epistemic Calibration, as discussed in Section 2.2.
149
150   2.1.1    I NTERNAL C ERTAINTY
151
152   Internal certainty, also referred to as model confidence, represents the probability an LLM assigns to
153   a particular output based on its internal computations and parametric knowledge from its training
154   data. In tasks such as question-answering, internal certainty is often represented by the probability the
155
      model assigns to its selected response compared to alternative answers (Jiang et al., 2021; Hendrycks
      et al., 2021). For instance, when generating an answer, the model evaluates the likelihood of various
156
      possible responses, expressing its level of confidence in the chosen output.
157
158
      Internal Confidence Estimation Hendrycks et al. (2021) introduce baseline methods to detect
159
      misclassifications by examining model confidence, emphasizing that well-calibrated models should

      align their confidence estimates with the actual probability of correctness. One common approach
163   is token-level analysis, which examines the probability assigned to individual tokens to generate
164   fine-grained estimates of uncertainty (Jiang et al., 2021; Kuhn et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024). Another
165   method involves assessing the levels of variability across multiple outputs generated for the same input,
166   where greater variability suggests higher uncertainty (Xiong et al., 2024). An additional technique
167   is to employ external classifiers trained on both input data and the model’s internal representations
168   to predict uncertainty, providing a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s internal state
169   (Shrivastava et al., 2023).
170   Recent research has also focused on making internal confidence more interpretable for users. Some
171   approaches express internal certainty numerically (e.g., “80% confidence”) (Lin et al., 2022; Xiong
172   et al., 2024), while others adopt qualitative expressions (e.g., “I am not confident in the answer”)
173   (Mielke et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). These strategies aim to make internal confidence scores more
174   accessible by incorporating them directly into the model’s natural language outputs, bridging the gap
175   between the model’s internal certainty and its communication with users.
176
177   Challenges in Confidence Alignment As research on estimating the internal confidence of LLMs
178   has increased, scholars have started to focus on model calibration—i.e., the alignment between pre-
179   dicted probabilities and actual correctness. Desai & Durrett (2020) find that pre-trained transformers
180   such as BERT often exhibit poor calibration out-of-the-box, where their confidence estimates fail
181   to correspond to actual correctness. Jiang et al. (2021) also explore methods to improve model
182
      calibration, such as temperature scaling, which adjusts predicted probabilities to better match actual
      outcomes. However, most calibration methods focus on probabilistic outputs (internal confidence)
183
      without addressing how certainty is expressed through language generation. Si et al. (2022) expand
184
      on this by demonstrating that models like GPT-3 frequently produce overconfident responses even
185   when incorrect.
186
187   Despite these advancements, internal confidence scores are still largely inaccessible to users. Without
188
      these scores, it becomes essential for models to effectively communicate uncertainty through external
      means—such as linguistic cues—to ensure users correctly interpret the model’s output. This gap
189
      highlights the need for better alignment between internal certainty and external expressions of
190
      confidence, as misalignment can lead to epistemic miscalibration.
191
192
      2.1.2    E XTERNAL C ERTAINTY
193
194   External certainty refers to the level of confidence conveyed through the textual generation of an
195   LLM, as interpreted by an external observer. This type of certainty reflects how assertive, definitive,
196   or unambiguous the model’s output appears, regardless of the underlying internal confidence scores
197   generated during the prediction process (Mielke et al., 2022).
198
      A critical component of external certainty is Linguistic Assertiveness, which involves the use of
199   linguistic markers—such as modal verbs, adverbs, and other cues—that signal varying degrees of
200   confidence or uncertainty. For example, statements like “it is certain that” and “there is a possibility
201   that” differ significantly in their level of assertiveness, influencing how the user perceives the reliability
202   of the information presented.
203
      Human communication tends to favor the expression of uncertainty through linguistic means rather
204
      than numerical values (Erev & Cohen, 1990; Wallsten et al., 1993). Research on how individuals
205   interpret verbal cues of uncertainty reveals that people map linguistic expressions of confidence onto
206   numerical values in different ways, depending on the context, expertise, and domain in question
207   (Windschitl & Wells, 1996; Karelitz & Budescu, 2004). Although there is some variability in
208   individual interpretations, population-level studies show consistent patterns in how verbal expressions
209   of uncertainty correspond to probabilistic estimates (Budescu & Wallsten, 1985; Clarke et al., 1992).
210
211   Linguistic Assertiveness in LLM Outputs Mielke et al. (2022) examine how both humans and
212   LLMs interpret and generate expressions of uncertainty. They find that LLMs such as GPT-4 can map
213   uncertainty expressions to numerical equivalents similarly to humans, though they are more prone to
214   biases rooted in their training data. However, most prior research focuses on how LLMs interpret
215   uncertainty expressions rather than how they generate assertive or uncertain language aligned with
      their internal confidence scores.

219   internal certainty, thereby contributing to the broader goal of epistemic calibration.

      2.2       E PISTEMIC C ALIBRATION
222
223
      Epistemic Calibration is the process of aligning a model’s expressed confidence, conveyed through
224   linguistic assertiveness, with its actual reliability or correctness. Achieving this alignment requires
225   that the model’s linguistic expressions match the probabilistic confidence it has in its predictions.
226   However, if this balance is disrupted, significant communication issues can arise. For instance, if a
227   model uses assertive language but is internally uncertain, users may place undue trust in potentially
228   incorrect information. Conversely, when a model is internally confident but hedges its language, users
229   might doubt accurate information.
230   Figure 1 presents two contrasting examples that highlight varying levels of epistemic calibration in
231   LLM outputs. In both cases, the LLM responds to a user query, and we compute two certainty scores:
232   Internal Certainty and External Certainty (through linguistic assertiveness). In the first example, the
233   internal and external certainty scores are closely aligned, demonstrating high epistemic calibration.
234   Here, the model’s linguistic assertiveness accurately reflects its internal confidence, resulting in a
235   trustworthy output. In contrast, the second example reveals low epistemic calibration. Although the
236   internal certainty score is relatively low, the model’s linguistic assertiveness remains high, indicating
237   overconfidence in its response. In the following analysis, we compute internal certainty using the
238
      method outlined by Rivera et al. (2024) and use linguistic assertiveness, derived from our custom
      model detailed in Section 3—which is validated against human surveyed human ratings—as a proxy
239
      for external certainty quantification.

      3.1       DATASETS AND M ODELS
245
246   The dataset we use for the certainty calibration scoring task is the LIAR dataset, a misinformation
247   dataset consisting of 12,800 political statements fact-checked by PolitiFact (Wang, 2017). Each
248   statement is labeled as true or false based on PolitiFact’s classification.2 To augment this dataset, we
249   employ OpenAI’s GPT-4 to reassess the veracity of each statement, providing a foundation for the
250   certainty calibration analysis that follows.
251   Recognizing the limitations of previous methods for assertiveness calibratation, we compile a new
252   dataset and train our own models to achieve more accurate results. Existing approaches, such as Pei
253   & Jurgens (2021), rely on a BERT-based model that is constrained by input length and limited to
254   news and scientific articles, reducing their applicability to other domains. Other methods, such as
255   Byalyk & Nizhnik (2022), base assertiveness scores on lexicon-derived buckets, which limits their
256   adaptability to diverse contexts. To overcome these challenges, we curated a diverse dataset across
257   multiple domains to improve scalability and transferability, consisting of 800 data points equally
258   distributed across the following five sources:
259
      • Anthropic’s Persuasiveness dataset (Durmus et al., 2024): Text data that compares the persua-
260
        siveness of arguments generated by humans and LLMs.
261   • Globe and Mail (GM) Comments dataset (Kolhatkar et al., 2020): User-generated comments
262     from the Globe and Mail newspaper.
263   • Reddit Change My View (CMV) dataset (Wiegmann et al., 2022): User texts where persuasive
264     arguments successfully change another user’s viewpoint.
265   • Arguments generated by LLaMA 3-8B on LIAR dataset (Dubey et al., 2024): Text responses
266     generated by LLaMA 3-8B assessing the factuality of statements in the LIAR dataset.
267   • Pei’s assertiveness dataset (Pei & Jurgens, 2021): The dataset used to train Pei’s assertiveness
268     model, focused on assertiveness in scientific communication.

294   3.2   C OMPUTING INTERNAL CERTAINTY AND LINGUISTIC ASSERTIVENESS
295
296   To estimate the internal certainty of the LLM, we use the method outlined in Rivera et al. (2024).
297   The model provides an explanation for each misinformation classification and assigns an uncertainty
298   score. The scores are calibrated on a validation set using Platt’s method (Platt et al., 1999), ensuring
299   consistency between the explanation and certainty value. This process eliminates variability due
300   to different prompting techniques, allowing for a unified approach to certainty calibration. The
301
      robustness of this method is further validated in Appendix B.
302   To measure the linguistic assertiveness of LLM-generated explanations in the misinformation task,
303   we use the best-performing model from Section 3.1. We then compare this assertiveness measure to
304   the underlying certainty estimates obtained using the uncertainty quantification techniques described
305   above. By analyzing the gap between the model’s certainty and assertiveness, we quantify the degree
306   of calibration in its linguistic expressions.

      In the preceding sections, we provided empirical evidence highlighting the epistemic calibration
370
      problem. Our findings revealed a significant mismatch between the internal certainty and linguistic

452   7       D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
453
454   In this work, we introduced the novel problem of epistemic calibration for LLMs: ensuring that
455   the confidence expressed in a model’s communication aligns with its underlying reliability. We
456   argued that this normative ideal is critical for LLMs to serve as trusted and responsible information
457
      sources. Through a decomposition of the problem into external and internal certainty, we developed a
      framework for understanding and evaluating epistemic calibration in language models. Using our
458
      new measurement approach that greatly improves fidelity of assertiveness measurements compared to
459
      prior models, our empirical investigation of a state-of-the-art model reveals significant gaps between
460   the model’s internal confidence estimates and the assertiveness of its generated language. This
461   miscalibration poses risks to users, who may be misled by overconfident model outputs.
462
463
      Our work also highlights the need for further research to fully understand and address the challenges
      of epistemic calibration. One key direction is developing new training and inference techniques
464
      to improve the alignment between LLMs’ probability estimates and their linguistic expression of
465
      confidence. Another is studying the downstream impacts of epistemic miscalibration on user trust,
466   decision making, and information ecosystems, through a combination of user studies and large-scale
467   simulations. We believe that the epistemic calibration framework introduced in this paper provides
468   a valuable foundation for these future efforts. We discuss further applications, to RLHF, silicon
469   sampling, and debate, in Appendix H. Ultimately, achieving epistemic calibration in language models
470   is not just a technical challenge, but a societal imperative. As these models become ever more
471   integrated into our information-seeking and decision-making practices, ensuring that they express
472   confidence in a calibrated and responsible way is essential for mitigating the risks of misinformation,
473   confusion, and unwarranted trust.
474
475   8       L IMITATIONS
476
477   Despite the promising findings and advancements discussed in this paper, several limitations should
478   be acknowledged to provide a balanced perspective on the epistemic calibration of language models.
479   First, our primary evaluation focuses on the directionality of variation in assertiveness and certainty.
480   A model could be well-calibrated in terms of directionality but still on average excessively bombastic
481
      or timid. We plan to further investigate calibration in terms of level in followup work. Second, we do
      not experiment with the implications of epistemic miscalibration on the formation of human beliefs.
482
      Finally, while our study highlights the problem of epistemic calibration, it does not explore potential
483
      intervention strategies to mitigate this problem beyond the scope of our current methods. We provide
484   more details on the limitations of this study in section J of the Appendix.

1061   H     F UTURE A PPLICATIONS
1062
1063   The findings presented in this paper highlight the importance of epistemic calibration for the responsi-
1064   ble development and deployment of LLMs. By quantifying the gap between current models’ certainty
1065   and assertiveness, we demonstrate the need for new techniques and evaluations to align these proper-
1066   ties and ensure that models are communicating in a calibrated and trustworthy manner. However,
1067   our work also raises a number of important questions and challenges that must be addressed as the
1068
       field moves forward. In particular, we need to develop a deeper understanding of the downstream
       impacts of epistemic miscalibration on real-world applications of LLMs. In this section, we outline
1069
       several key directions for future research that we believe will be critical for advancing the epistemic
1070
       calibration agenda.
1071
1072
       H.1    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
1073
1074   A key determinant of model assertiveness is RLHF. In particular, Hosking et al. (2024) demonstrated
1075   that preference scores from human feedback overvalue the assertiveness of a model output relative
1076   to the factuality of a statement. This motivates our concern that a model’s expressed assertiveness
1077   overstates its level of internal certainty. Moreover, it means that better calibration here could be
1078   used to improve RLHF. For example, when human labelers are labeling which of two potential
1079   generations is better, we could make sure they have matching assertiveness, which would remove that
       as a confounder and lead to labels that better reflect characteristics we actually want (e.g., factuality).


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Similarly, LLMs have also been used to generate preferences scores to guide “RLAIF” Lee et al.
1081   (2023). Removing assertiveness confounders could provide even more value here, since a potentially
1082   over-assertive LLM is used in even more steps of the process.
1083
1084
       H.2    S ILICON M ODELING
1085
1086   Recent work by Argyle et al. (2023) demonstrates that LLMs can effectively replicate human-like
1087   behavior in the context of political discussions and belief formation. This finding opens up a
1088   promising avenue for studying the impact of epistemic miscalibration on the spread of misinformation
1089   using simulation-based approaches. By modeling social media discourse with LLMs that exhibit
1090   varying degrees of certainty and assertiveness, we can examine how these properties influence the
1091   propagation of beliefs across a network. One hypothesis is that models prone to over-certainty or
1092   over-assertiveness may be more likely to have their beliefs adopted and shared by other agents in the
1093   network, even when those beliefs are not well-supported by evidence. This could lead to the rapid
1094
       spread of misinformation in cases where a model generates highly confident but false or misleading
       statements. Conversely, a model that accurately calibrates its certainty and assertiveness to the
1095
       underlying reliability of its beliefs may be less likely to trigger runaway misinformation cascades.
1096
1097
1098   H.3    D EBATE
1099
       LLMs are increasingly being used in multi-agent settings such as debates and dialogues, where they
1100
       interact with each other or with humans to discuss complex topics, reason about arguments, and reach
1101   conclusions (Chan et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). These settings provide a rich test environment
1102   for studying the impact of epistemic calibration on the quality and outcomes of conversational
1103   interactions. One key challenge in debate and dialogue settings is ensuring that the participating
1104   agents engage in a productive and truthful exchange of ideas, rather than simply amplifying each
1105   other’s misconceptions or talking past each other. When agents are not well-calibrated in their
1106   expressed certainty, this can lead to a number of problems. An overconfident agent may dominate the
1107   conversation and steamroll over valid counterarguments, while an underconfident agent may fail to
1108   adequately defend its position even when it has strong evidence on its side.
1109   Epistemic calibration is thus crucial for LLMs to be effective debaters and dialogue partners. By
1110   accurately conveying their level of certainty in their statements, calibrated agents can facilitate a
1111   more balanced and substantive exchange of ideas. They are less likely to derail the conversation with
1112   unwarranted assertions or to let important points go unchallenged due to undue hesitance.

1203   J     L IMITATIONS
1204
1205   Despite the promising findings and advancements discussed in this paper, several limitations should
1206   be acknowledged to provide a balanced perspective on the epistemic calibration of language models.
1207
1208   Calibration metrics Our primary evaluation has focused on the directionality of variation in
1209   assertiveness and certainty. A model could be well-calibrated in terms of directionality but still on
1210   average excessively bombastic or timid. We plan to further investigate calibration in terms of level
1211   in followup work. Assertiveness can also be related to the content. For example, the LLM may be
1212   assertively saying that there is insufficient evidence (see Appendix E.3). Adding a third “unverified”
1213   class to the current “true” and “false” can help with this.
1214
1215
       Implications on the formation of human beliefs We focus on assertiveness calibration and do
       not experiment with the implications of epistemic miscalibration on the formation of human beliefs,
1216
       since it is highly varying and differs based on context and content. Breum et al. (2023) found that
1217
       assertiveness is closely linked to perception of LLM explanations, and we argue that calibrating
1218   is a necessary condition for trustworthy LLMs. In future work, we aim to also directly consider
1219   persuasiveness through controlled experiments with human participants, and analyze other factors
1220   involved such as length or number of explanations. Relatedly, the long-term impacts of miscalibrated
1221   assertiveness on user trust and belief formation also needs to be studied.
1222
1223   Intervention Strategies While our study highlights the problem of epistemic calibration, it does
1224   not explore potential intervention strategies to mitigate this problem beyond the scope of our current
1225   methods. Developing effective techniques for aligning internal certainty with external assertiveness
1226   requires further exploration. Future work should focus on creating and testing practical interventions
1227   that can be integrated into the training and deployment of language models.
