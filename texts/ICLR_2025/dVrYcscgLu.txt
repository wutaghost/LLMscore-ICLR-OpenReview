009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Empowerment has the potential to help agents learn large skillsets, but is not yet a
012            scalable solution for training general-purpose agents. Recent empowerment meth-
013            ods learn large skillsets by maximizing the mutual information between skills and
014            states, but these approaches require a model of the transition dynamics, which can
015            be challenging to learn in realistic settings with high-dimensional and stochastic
016            observations. We present an algorithm, Latent-Predictive Empowerment (LPE),
017            that can compute empowerment in a more scalable manner. LPE learns large
018
               skillsets by maximizing an objective that under certain conditions has the same
               optimal skillset as the mutual information between skills and states, but our objec-
019
               tive is more tractable to optimize because it only requires learning a simpler latent-
020
               predictive model rather than a full simulator of the environment. We show empir-
021            ically in a variety of settings, includes ones with high-dimensional observations
022            and highly stochastic transition dynamics, that our empowerment objective learns
023            similar-sized skillsets as the leading empowerment algorithm, which assumes ac-
024            cess to a model of the transition dynamics, and outperforms other model-based
025            approaches to empowerment.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Empowerment offers an intuitive approach for training agents to have large skillsets. In an
031   empowerment-based approach, the empowerment for a variety of states is first computed, in which
032   the empowerment of a state measures the size of the largest skillset in that state (Klyubin et al.,
033
      2005; Salge et al., 2013). The state empowerment values are then used as a reward in a Reinforce-
      ment Learning (Sutton & Barto, 1998) setting, encouraging agents to take actions that grow the size
034
      of their skillsets (Klyubin et al., 2008; Jung et al., 2012; Mohamed & Rezende, 2015).
035
036   The main roadblock to implementing the empowerment-based approach to training generalist agents
037   is that there is not yet a scalable way to compute the empowerment of a state. Recent empowerment
038   approaches seek to learn the most diverse skillset in a state by searching for the skillset (e.g., a skill-
039
      conditioned policy) with the largest lower bound to the mutual information between skills and states
      (Gregor et al., 2016; Eysenbach et al., 2018; Achiam et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2021;
040
      Strouse et al., 2021; Levy et al., 2024), which measures skillset diversity by capturing how distinct
041
      the skills are from one another in terms of the states they target. The problem with this approach
042   is that it requires an infeasible amount of interaction with the environment prior to each update to
043   the skillset. To estimate the mutual information lower bound for a single skillset in a single state,
044   many skills need to be executed in the environment from the state under consideration to obtain
045   the resulting skill-terminating states. But because empowerment seeks to find the most diverse
046   skillset across a distribution of states, these tuples of skills and states need to be collected for many
047   skillsets (e.g., skill-conditioned policies with small differences from the policy) starting from many
048   states. Because this amount of interaction prior to each update to the skillset is intractable, recent
049   empowerment approaches assume the agent has access to a model of the transition dynamics (i.e., a
050
      simulator of the environment) (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Gu et al., 2021; Levy et al., 2023; 2024). But
      this is not a scalable assumption because a model of the transition dynamics is typically not available
051
      and can be intractable to learn in settings with high-dimensional and stochastic observations.
052

      (LPE). LPE measures the diversity of a skillset using the difference of two terms: (i) the mutual in-

114   2.1   S KILLSET M ODEL AND E MPOWERMENT
115
      We model an agent’s skillset in a state using a probabilistic graphical model defined by the tu-
116   ple (S, A, Z, T , ϕ, π). S is the space of states; A is the space of actions; Z is the space of
117   skills; T is the transition dynamics distribution T (st+1 |st , at ) that provides the probability of a
118   state given the prior state and action. The transition dynamics are assumed to be conditionally in-
119   dependent of the history of states and actions (i.e., T (st+1 |st , at ) = T (st+1 |s0 , a0 , . . . , st , at )).
120   The remaining distributions ϕ and π are the learnable distributions in a skillset. ϕ repre-
121   sents the distribution over skills ϕ(z|s0 ) given a skill start state s0 . π represents the skill-
122   conditioned policy π(at |st , z) that provides the distribution over primitive actions given a state
123   st and skill z. Assuming each skill consists of n primitive actions, the full joint distribution
124
      of a skill and a trajectory of actions and states (z, a0 , s1 , . . . , an−1 , sn ) conditioned on a partic-
      ular start state s0 and skillset defined ϕ and π is given by p(z, a0 , s1 , . . . , an1 , sn |s0 , ϕ, π) =

      ϕ(z|s0 )π(a0 |s0 , z)p(s1 |s0 , a0 ) . . . π(an−1 |sn−1 , z)p(sn |sn−1 , an−1 ). Note that this definition is
126
      for closed loop skills. Skillsets can also use open loop skills, in which the skill-conditioned pol-
127   icy would be defined defined by the distribution π(a|s0 , z). The output of the open loop skill-
128   conditioned policy a is a concatenation of n primitive actions (i.e., a = [a0 , . . . , an−1 ]). The joint
129   distribution for a skillset containing open loop skills is the same as for closed loop skills except there
130   is only one sample taken from the skill-conditioned policy, which includes all n primitive actions.

193   3     L ATENT-P REDICTIVE E MPOWERMENT
194
195
      We introduce a new algorithm, Latent-Predictive Empowerment (LPE), that can measure the em-
196   powerment of a state in a more scalable manner. The key component of our algorithm is our objec-
197   tive for learning diverse skill-conditioned policies. Instead of maximizing the mutual information
198   between skills and states with respect to the skill-conditioned policy, we maximize an alternative
199   objective that has the same optimal skillset under certain conditions, but is also more tractable to
200   maximize because it only requires learning a latent-predictive model rather than a full simulator of
201   an environment. We maximize skillset diversity using the same actor-critic structures as used by
202   Skillset Empowerment, which is reviewed in Appendix section A.

345   3.3   L IMITATIONS
346
      The main limitation of Latent-Predictive Empowerment is that it can be limited to measuring only
347
      short term empowerment because of the use of open loop skills. The inability to adjust a policy
348   makes it difficult to target specific states over longer time horizons, particularly in domains with
349   realistic levels of randomness. As a result, LPE can be a poor way to measure longer term empow-
350   erment. Future work can investigate how a longer term empowerment can be computed from the
351   short term empowerment measured by LPE.

412   4.2   BASELINES
413
414   We compare LPE to three versions of Skillset Empowerment. The first version is regular Skillset
415   Empowerment, in which the agent is given access to the model of transition dynamics. Levy et al.
416   (2024) showed that Skillset Empowerment is able to learn large skillsets in all domains while both
417
      Variational Intrinsic Control (Gregor et al., 2016), an empowerment-based skill learning algorithm
      similar to Diversity Is All You Need (Eysenbach et al., 2018), and Goal-Conditioned RL were unable
418
      to learn meaningful skillsets. In the second version, the Skillset Empowerment agent learns a model
419
      of the transition dynamics p(st+ 1 |st , at ) using a VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2022) generative model.
420   We expect this agent to struggle in the stochastic settings because it is challenging to learn simulators
421   in these domains, which in turn means the agent may struggle to accurately measure the diversity of
422   a skillset. Learning a simulator in stochastic four rooms is difficult because the agent’s next location
423   occurs in the same offset location in any of the four rooms and it is difficult for VAE’s to learn
424   disjoint distributions. Further, learning a perfect simulator in the RGB QR code domains in which
425   the agent needs to predict the next QR code is not feasible due to the number of RGB-colored QR
426   code combinations.
427   In the third version of Skillset Empowerment, the agent learns a latent-predictive model using a
428   BYOL-Explore objective (Guo et al., 2022), which is a leading method for learning latent-predictive
429   models. Similar to other bootstrapping methods (Grill et al., 2020; Assran et al., 2023; Bardes
430   et al., 2024), BYOL-Explore trains a latent-predictive model pξ (zn |s0 , a) to match a state encoding
431   distribution pη (zn |sn ), in which the parameters of the state encoding distribution are updated as an
      exponential moving average of the latent-predictive model parameters: η ← αη + (1 − α)ξ. We also

503   5    R ELATED W ORK
504
505   There have been many prior works that have used empowerment to try to learn large skillsets. Early
506   empowerment methods showed how mutual information between actions and states could be opti-
507   mized in small settings with discrete state and/or action spaces (Klyubin et al., 2008; Salge et al.,
508   2013; Jung et al., 2012). Several later works integrated variational inference techniques that en-
509   abled empowerment-based skill learning to be applied to larger continuous domains (Mohamed &
510   Rezende, 2015; Karl et al., 2017; Gregor et al., 2016; Eysenbach et al., 2018; Sharma et al., 2019;
511
      Li et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2020). However, these methods were limited in the size of skillsets
      they were able to learn as they only maximize a loose lower bound on mutual information, making
512
      it difficult to accurately measure the diversity of a skillset (Levy et al., 2024).
513
514   Related to empowerment-based skill learning is unsupervised goal-conditioned reinforcement learn-
515   ing (GCRL) that learn goal-conditioned skills using some automated curriculum that expands the
516   distribution over goal states over time (Ecoffet et al., 2019; Mendonca et al., 2021; Nair et al., 2018;
517
      Pong et al., 2019; Campos et al., 2020; Pitis et al., 2020; Held et al., 2017; McClinton et al., 2021).
      The problem with GCRL is that in significantly stochastic settings where specific states cannot be
518
      consistently achieved, the GCRL objective also becomes a loose lower bound on the mutual infor-
519
      mation between skills and states, providing an agent with a weak signal for learning large skillsets.
520   In contrast, Skillset Empowerment and our approach learn tighter bounds on mutual information,
521   providing a dense signal for how to learn increasingly diverse skillsets.
522
523
      Also, related to our work is the large body of research for building world models in order to learn
      new representations (Hafner et al., 2019; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Gregor et al., 2019; Grill et al.,
524
      2020; Guo et al., 2022; Ghugare et al., 2023; Assran et al., 2023; Bardes et al., 2024; Pathak et al.,
525
      2017). Learning a world model is challenging because the full state space needs to be encoded into a
526   single compressed latent space in order to learn a new state representation. In contrast, LPE does not
527   learn models to learn a new state representation but rather to determine how many distinct actions
528   are available in a state. To do this, LPE groups together redundant actions that achieves the same
529   terminating states, which only requires encoding the more limited set of states sn that are reachable
530   in a small number n actions.
531
532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   Empowerment has the potential to help agents become general purpose agents with large skillsets,
535   but this potential may never be realized as long as measuring the empowerment of a state requires a
536   simulator of the environment. In this work, we takes a step toward a more scalable way to compute
537   empowerment by presenting a method that can measure empowerment using only a latent-predictive
538   model. We show empirically in a variety of settings that our approach can learn equally-sized
539   skillsets as the leading empowerment algorithm that requires access to a simulator of the environ-
      ment.

      Even though LPE agents only interact with the environment by greedily following its nearly deter-
789
      ministic skill-conditioned policy, equation 6 has built-in mechanisms that encourage agents to try
790   different skillsets that execute new actions. When LPE measures the skillset diversity of a large
791   number of candidate skillsets that contain small changes to one of the parameters of π, there will be
792   skillset candidates (ϕ, π) that incorporate new actions into the skillset that are not in the replay buffer
793   as they have not been executed by previous skillsets. For these skillsets that execute new actions in
794   addition to the previously discovered unique actions, the I(Z;   ˜ Zn |s0 , ϕ, π) term, which measures
795   the number of actions in a skillset, may increase which then pushes up the overall diversity score for
796   these candidate skillsets. The higher diversity score will in turn encourage the agent to “explore”
797   by changing its skillset to include skills that execute these new actions. If the agent does update its
798   skillset, once the new actions are executed in the environment and the states sn have been observed,
799
      the agent can keep this action in its skillset (i.e., continue to have some skill z execute this action) if
      it targets some new state sn or remove the action from the skillset if sn can already be achieved by
800
      some other skill in the skillset.

945   π(a|s0 , z)pξ (zn |s0 , ϕ, a) if π = fλ (s0 , ϕ) and 0 otherwise. pξ (zn |s0 , ϕ, a) is the latent-predictive
946   model learned by the VAE generative model. Note that set of variational posterior parameters ψ
947   used in the actor-critic update for ϕ is different than the set of variational parameters used in the
948   actor-critic update for the skill-conditioned policy.
949   In the second step, the critic Qρ (s0 , ϕ) is trained to approximate the diversity score of the (ϕ, π =
950   fλ (s0 , ϕ)) skillset using the updated variational posterior ψ parameters. This is done by minimizing
951   the following supervised learning objective with respect to ρ:
952
953
                            J(ρ) = Es0 ∼β,ϕ∼fˆµ [(Qρ (s0 , ϕ) − Target(s0 , ϕ))2 ],                                    (17)
954                         Target(s0 , ϕ) = Ez∼ϕ(z|s0 ),zn ∼pξ (zn |s0 ,ϕ,z) [log qψ (z|s0 , ϕ, zn )].
955
956   The actor is then updated my maximizing the following objective with respect to µ:
957
                                               J(µ) = Es0 ∼β [Qρ (s0 , fµ (s0 ))]                                      (18)

1023   K    I MPLEMENTING H IERARCHICAL AGENTS WITH LPE
1024
1025   Coding hierarchical agents that use the (ϕ, π) LPE skillsets as a temporally extended action space is
       simple. For the higher level policy π : S → Z that outputs a skill z from the LPE skillset given some


                                                        19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       state, attach a tanh activation function to this policy, which bounds the output to [−1, 1], and then
1027   multiply that output by ϕ, which will bound the skill action space to [−ϕ, ϕ] in every dimension,
1028   which has the same shape as d-dimensional cubic distribution that ϕ represents. (Note that in our
1029   implementation, ϕ is technically the log of the half length of each side of the d-dimensional cubic
1030   uniform distribution so the output of the tanh activation function should be multiplied by eϕ . We
1031   have ϕ represent the log of the half length of side so the ϕ actor fµ can output negative numbers.)
1032   Then once a skill z has been sampled, the skill can be passed to the LPE skill-conditioned policy
1033   π(a|s0 , z) which will then output an action sequence that can then be executed in the environment.
