009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011                We present a novel simulation-free framework for training continuous-time diffu-
012                sion processes over very general objective functions. Existing methods typically
013                involve either prescribing the optimal diffusion process—which only works for
014                heavily restricted problem formulations—or require expensive simulation to numer-
015                ically obtain the time-dependent densities and sample from the diffusion process.
016                In contrast, we propose a coupled parameterization which jointly models a time-
017                dependent density function, or probability path, and the dynamics of a diffusion
018                process that generates this probability path. To accomplish this, our approach
019
                   directly bakes in the Fokker-Planck equation and density function requirements as
                   hard constraints, by extending and greatly simplifying the construction of Neural
020
                   Conservation Laws. This enables simulation-free training for a large variety of
021
                   problem formulations, from data-driven objectives as in generative modeling and
022                dynamical optimal transport, to optimality-based objectives as in stochastic optimal
023                control, with straightforward extensions to mean-field objectives due to the ease
024                of accessing exact density functions. We validate our method in a diverse range
025                of application domains from modeling spatio-temporal events to learning optimal
026                dynamics from population data.

      such as solving optimal transport or generalized Schrödinger bridge problems, but these methods

089   2    R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Markov processes described by ordinary and stochastic differential equations have been used across
092   many application domains (Rubanova et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Cuomo et al., 2022;
093   Wang et al., 2023), with the most general problem settings involving simulation-based methods. This
094   refers to training neural differential equations of various kinds by simulating their trajectories and
095   differentiating through the objective function. While some works have solved the memory issue with
096   dfferentating through simulations (Chen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021), it remains
097   problematic to apply these at scale due to the computational cost of simulation. Furthermore, many
098
      probabilistic modeling applications (Grathwohl et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2019a; Koshizuka & Sato,
      2023) require the computation of the likelihood for maximum likelihood training, which can be more
099
      expensive than simulating trajectories.
100
101   This is where Neural Conservation Laws (NCL; Richter-Powell et al. (2022)) come in, which is
102   a modeling paradigm where the law of conservation such as eq. (2) is directly enforced as a hard
103   constraint. This allows optimization of the kind in eq. (1) to be mapped an unconstrained problem
104
      in the parameter space of an NCL model. However, while the original NCL model (Richter-Powell
      et al., 2022) was able to bake in the constraint in eq. (2), they did not provide a scalable way to bake
105
      in the density constraints in eq. (3) which is key for enabling maximum likelihood training.
106
107   A highly-scalable approach is the framework of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021),
      Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023), and stochastic interpolants (Albergo et al., 2023). However,

109   and ρ1 are provided for training. They cannot handle the general problem setup of eq. (1) but instead
110   directly prescribe the optimal solution which is then learned by a regression problem.

      3     M ETHOD
114
      We describe a novel framework which directly parameterizes both a velocity field ut and a density ρt
115
      that always satisfies the Fokker-Planck constraint in eq. (2) and density constraints in eq. (3). Our
116
      method is built on top of ideas introduced in Neural Conservation Laws (NCL; Richter-Powell et al.
117   (2022)) through the use of differential forms, but we take an alternative construction while providing
118   step-by-step derivations. We then discuss how likelihood-based generative models can fit within our
119   framework. The naïve construction, however, leads to a problem we call the spurious flux phenomenon
120   (Section 3.4) which we resolve by introducing a divergence-free component (Section 3.5).
121
122   3.1   N EURAL C ONSERVATION L AWS
123
124   In order to satisfy the Fokker-Planck constraint in eq. (2), we make use of a coupled parameterization
125   of both a probability path ρt , i.e. a time-dependent density function, and a flux jt (x) : RD+1 → RD
126
      that is designed, by construction, to always satisfy the continuity equation,
127                                              ∂t ρt + ∇ · jt = 0.                                           (5)
128   This equation imposes the condition that the total probability in a system must be conserved, and that
129   instantaneous changes in the probability can only be attributed to the local movement of particles
130   following a continuous flow characterized together by jt and ρt .
131
      We directly impose the continuity equation into the model as a hard constraint. This idea was
132
      previously explored in Neural Conservation Laws (NCL; Richter-Powell et al. (2022)); however, its
133   reliance on differential forms makes it difficult to extend, and they were not able to satisfy the density
134   constraints in eq. (3). Instead, we propose a simplified alternative construction and will derive the
135   core building blocks of NCL that are necessary for our approach following only basic principles.
136
      To model eq. (5), we introduce two vector fields aθt (x) : R1+D → RD and bθt (x) : R1+D → RD
137
      with parameters θ, and set
138
139                                          ρt = ∇ · aθt ,                                      (6)
140                                                jt = −∂t aθt + bθt .                                        (7)
141   With this choice we have:
142
      Lemma 1. Let ρt and jt be given by eq. (6) and eq. (7), respectively. Then the continuity eq. (5)
143
      holds iff bt is divergence-free, i.e. ∇ · bθt = 0.
144
145   Proof. Plugging eq. (6) and eq. (7) into the left hand side of eq. (5),
146
147                          ∂t ρt + ∇ · jt = ∂t ∇ · aθt − ∇ · (∂t aθt + bθt ) = −∇ · bθt .                    (8)
148   Therefore eq. (5) holds iff ∇ · bθt = 0, which verifies the claim.
149
150   Notice that ρt depends only on aθt , while jt is affected by both aθt and bθt . The extra degrees of freedom
151   coming from bθt will be important in order to resolve what we call the spurious flux phenomenon in
152   Section 3.4, and furthermore, it provides the needed flexibility in order to learn optimal solutions
153   of ut while leaving ρt invariant.

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533   We propose a simulation-free framework for training continuous-time stochastic processes over a
534   large range of objectives, by combining Neural Conservation Laws with likelihood-based models.
535   We demonstrated the flexibility and capacities of our method on various applications, including
536   spatio-temporal generative modeling, learning optimal transport between arbritrary densities, and
537   mean-field stochastic optimal control. Especially at low dimensional settings, our method easily
538   outperforms existing methods. However, the reliance on likelihood-based models make it difficult to
539
      be scaled up to high dimensions. We acknowledge these limitations and leave them for future works.
