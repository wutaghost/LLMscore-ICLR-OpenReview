009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Data augmentation is a highly effective method for addressing the issue of data
012            scarcity in machine learning and computer vision tasks. It involves diversifying
013            the original data through a series of transformations to improve the robustness and
014            generalization ability of the model. However, due to the disorder and irregularity
015            of point clouds, existing methods struggle to enrich geometric diversity and main-
016
               tain topological consistency, leading to imprecise point cloud understanding. In
               this paper, we propose SinPoint, a novel method designed to preserve the topolog-
017
               ical structure of the original point cloud through a homeomorphism. Additionally,
018
               it utilizes the Sine function to generate smooth displacements. This simulates ob-
019            ject deformations, thereby producing a rich diversity of samples. Our extensive
020            experiments demonstrate that SinPoint consistently outperforms existing Mixup
021            and Deformation methods on various benchmark point cloud datasets, improving
022            performance for shape classification and part segmentation tasks. Specifically,
023            when used with PointNet++ and DGCNN, SinPoint achieves a state-of-the-art ac-
024            curacy of 90.2 on shape classification with the real-world ScanObjectNN dataset.
025            Furthermore, our method is highly versatile and scalable, and it can adapt to dif-
026            ferent scenarios and requirements for point cloud tasks.

      With the widespread application of deep learning in computer vision, the point cloud, as a cru-
031   cial representation of three-dimensional shapes, is gradually attracting attention from the industry
032   Qi et al. (2017a;b); Li et al. (2018); Thomas et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2020a); Qian et al. (2022).
033   Compared to traditional two-dimensional images, point clouds offer richer three-dimensional spatial
034   information, providing broader application prospects for areas such as autonomous driving Navarro-
035   Serment et al. (2010); Kidono et al. (2011); Chen et al. (2017), scene understanding Verdoja et al.
036   (2017); Chen et al. (2019a); Sugimura et al. (2020), and virtual reality Rusu et al. (2008); Qi et al.
037   (2018). However, due to the dual constraints of technology and cost, there are relatively small-scale
038   3D datasets with limited labels and diversity. This limitation can cause the problem of overfitting
039
      and further affect the generalization ability of the network. Facing such challenges, effectively us-
      ing limited point cloud data for better training deep learning models has been an urgent problem to
040
      address. Recently, Data Augmentation (DA) has become a popular strategy in avoiding overfitting
041
      and improving the generalizability of models by increasing the quantity and diversity of samples.
042
043   DA has shown significant success in handling image data. Various methods, such as Cutout De-
044   Vries & Taylor (2017); Zhong et al. (2020), Mixup Zhang et al. (2017), Cutmix Yun et al. (2019),
045
      and other methods Verma et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2022), have been utilized to augment im-
      ages and enhance the robustness and generalizability of models. However, unlike regular images,
046
      point clouds are disordered and irregular, making it challenging to apply these DA methods di-
047
      rectly to point cloud data. Some existing point cloud DA methods only focus on a single type
048   of operation, such as simple geometric transformations (rotation, scaling, and translation), data
049   perturbations (adding noise and deleting points), or hybrid operations (simulated mixtures of im-
050   ages Chen et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2022; 2021); Zhang et al. (2022); Ren et al. (2022); Wang
051   et al. (2024)). While these methods may improve data diversity to some extent, they often over-
052   look the point cloud’s intrinsic structure and semantic details, resulting in a loss of topological
053   consistency in the augmented point cloud. For instance, PointMixup Chen et al. (2020), Point-
      CutMix Zhang et al. (2022), and SageMix Lee et al. (2022) all use different strategies to mix

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
149
150   Homeomorphism Derrick (1973) is an important mathematical tool used to describe the equivalence
151   relations between topological spaces. It keeps the proximity of points in space unchanged and makes
152   topological Spaces have the same topological properties. These properties make a homeomorphism
153   widely used in topology, geometry, physics, and other fields.
154   Definition 1. (Homeomorphism) Given two topological spaces X, Y , and given a mapping f :
155   X→Y . f is a homeomorphism of two spaces when it is satisfied that f is a bijection and f and f −1
156   are continuous, denoted as X ∼
                                   =Y.
157
      Definition 2. (Local homeomorphism) Let f : X→Y is a mapping between two topological spaces
158
      X and Y . If for every point x in X, exists a neighborhood U of x such that f (U ) is an open set in
159
      X and fU : U →f (U ) is a homeomorphism, then f is a local homeomorphism.
160
161   They have similar mathematical properties, both of which require the mapping and its inverse map-
      ping to be continuous and maintain the topological structure of the spaceArmstrong (2013).

162
      Proposition 1. (Topological consistency) If f is a homeomorphism from X to Y , then X and Y
163   have the same topological properties.
164
165   Proposition 2. (Reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity) The homeomorphic relation is an equiva-
166
      lence relation, and therefore it has reflexivity (any topological space is a homeomorphism to itself),
      symmetry (if X ∼ =Y , then Y ∼
                                   =X), and transitivity (if Y ∼=Z, then X ∼=Z).
167
168   Homeomorphism plays a vital role in ensuring topological consistency. First, a homeomorphism is
169   not only one-to-one (bijective) and continuous, but its inverse is also continuous. This guarantees the
170   preservation of the space topology. Second, when processing point clouds, using a homeomorphism
171   can ensure that the basic shape and structure of the point cloud remain unaltered after enhancement
172
      or transformation, thus making the augmented data more consistent with the actual scene or object.
173

175
      Inspired by the deep residual network He et al. (2016), we focus on obtaining continuous residual
176
      coordinates and generating augmented coordinates by adding offsets to the original coordinates.
177
      Specifically, for a given point cloud P = {p1 , p2 , ..., pn }, we only need to compute the residual
178                                 ′
      coordinates, represented by P − P , thus the augmentation process becomes:
179
                                                   ′
180                                              P = P + g(P ).                                           (1)
181
182   A homeomorphism can ensure that the original space and the deformed space have topological
183   consistency. Therefore, it is very important to select the appropriate residual function, which not
184
      only gains diversity but also needs to guarantee that the whole mapping is homeomorphic.

186   3.3   S IN P OINT
187
      To simulate the distortion and deformation of an object, we have chosen to use the Sine function
188
      as our residual function. The inherent periodic nature of the Sine function allows us to adjust the
189   number of regions that are deformed with precision. Additionally, by manipulating the amplitude
190   of the Sine function, we can precisely control the intensity of the deformation. This displacement
191   field, generated by the Sine function, effectively distorts and deforms specific local regions of the
192   point cloud data without altering the overall topology. As a result, the augmented point cloud data
193   contains more intricate and detailed local features. The standard Sine function is shown below:
194
                                             g(x) = Asin(ωx + φ),                                         (2)
195
196   where A, ω, and φ represent the amplitude, the angular velocity (control period), and the initial
197   phase, respectively. The displacement field generated by Sine function is introduced into a homeo-
198   morphism, and a homeomorphism based on Sine function can be obtained.
199
      Theorem 1. (Homeomorphism Based on Sine Function) Given two topological spaces X, Y , and
200   given a mapping f : X→Y = X + Asin(ωX + φ), if −1≤Aω≤1, then f is a homeomorphism,
201   else f is a local homeomorphism. (The proof is in the Appendix.)
202
203
      Since f is a homeomorphism, we can use it to augment the point cloud and ensure its topological
      consistency. Given a set of points P = {p1 , p2 , ..., pn }, where N represents the number of points
204
      in the Euclidean space (x, y, z). SinPoint applies a homeomorphism and the resulting augmented
205                 ′
      point cloud P is given as follows:
206
                                             ′
207                                        P = P + Asin(ωP + φ),                                          (3)
208
209   where Asin(ωP + φ) is displacement field of P . We need to adjust A and ω to produce more
210   diverse point clouds. In this paper, we set A ∼ U (−a, a) and ω ∼ U (−w, w) to obey the uniform
211   distribution. In this way, more samples with smooth deformation can be generated, which makes the
212
      distribution of samples more uniform.

      5     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We propose SinPoint, a novel data augmentation based a homeomorphism for Point Clouds that
480   deforms point clouds by Sine functions while maintaining topological consistency. We only need to
481   adjust the amplitude and frequency of the Sine function to generate a diverse and realistic sample
482   with smoothly varying deformations, which brings significant improvements to point cloud tasks
483   across several datasets. In the end, we conducted extensive experiments and demonstrated how Sin-
484   Point improves the performance of three representative networks on multiple datasets. Our findings
485   show that the augmentations we produce are visually realistic and beneficial to the models, further
      validating the importance of our approach to understanding the local structure of point clouds.

836
      1) One-to-one mapping between point clouds and labels.
837
838   Given point cloud P = (p1 , p2 , ..., pn ). Each point pi has a corresponding label li . So you can get a
839   one-to-one correspondence:
840                                        p1 → l1 , p2 → l2 , ..., pn → ln .                             (17)
841   This means that for each point pi , it has a corresponding label li , which is associated with the
842   geometric position of the point.
843
844
      2) Definition of homeomorphic mapping.
845   A homeomorphic map f (x) is a map that preserves topology and changes the positions of points but
846   not the relative relations between them.
847
      If we deform the point cloud by the homeomorphic mapping f (P ), then the new point cloud P ′ is:
848
849                                                P ′ = P + f (P ).                                           (18)
850
851   Therefore, each point position of the new point cloud P ′ is p′i = pi + f (pi ), that is, each point pi
852   moves to the new position p′i by mapping f (x).
853
      3) Label consistency.
854
855   Since the homeomorphic mapping one-to-one correspondence of points in the point cloud, the index
856   and label of each point are kept consistent after the point cloud is deformed. In other words, the
857
      label li corresponding to the deformed position p′i does not change. We can obtain the following
      relationship:
858
                               p′1 → p1 → l1 , p′2 → p2 → l2 , ..., p′n → pn → ln .                 (19)
859
860   Thus:
861                                        p′1 → l1 , p′2 → l2 , ..., p′n → ln .                               (20)
862
863   This means that in the deformed point cloud P ′ , the label of each point p′i remains the same as the
      label of pi in the original point cloud P .

1043   A Conventional Data Augmentation (CDA) Qi et al. (2017a;b); Wang et al. (2019b) for point clouds
1044   applies a global similarity transformation (e.g., scaling, rotation, and translation) and point-wise
1045   jittering. Given a set of points P = {pi |i = 1, 2, ..., N }, where N represents the number of points
                                                                         ′
1046   in the Euclidean space (x, y, z). The augmented point cloud P is given as follows:
1047                                                                           ′

1048                                                                     P = SRP + B.                                                                                   (21)
1049   where S > 0 is a scaling factor, R is a 3D rotation matrix, and B ∈ RN ×3 is a translation matrix
1050   with global translation and point-wise jittering. Typically, R is an extrinsic rotation parameterized
1051   by a uniformly drawn Euler angle for the up-axis orientation. Scaling and translation factors are
1052   uniformly drawn from an interval, and point-wise jittering vectors are sampled from a truncated
1053   Gaussian distribution.
1054
       Obviously, when B does not exist, CDA is a rigid transformation, and when B exists, CDA is simply
1055   a similarity transformation with jitter. Thus, CDA cannot simulate diverse shapes and deformable
1056   objects, and the enhanced sample has poor diversity.
