011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine learning models trained on imaging data have empirically shown an abil-
014            ity to detect complex and ill-defined artifacts, such as ones resulting from dif-
015
               ferences in which machines medical images were taken by, to a high degree of
               accuracy. Such artifacts are invisible to even the expert eye, yet readily lead ma-
016
               chine learning systems to focus on spurious correlations rather than relevant fea-
017
               tures. Several forms of these spurious correlations have been reported to interfere
018            with cross-institutional medical imaging research and model generalizability, rep-
019            resenting one of the most significant open problems in medical machine learning
020            for over a decade. Here, we present Generative Editing via Convolutional Ob-
021            scuring (GECO), a Generative Adverserial Network for MRI deartifacting. With
022            the application of GECO, classifiers’ ability to detect these spurious correlations
023            went from 97% down to a detection accuracy nearly equal to random chance. We
024            also observe over 98% structural similarity between the original and deartifacted
025            images, indicating the preservation of the vast majority of non-spurious informa-
026
               tion contained in the original images. In addition to solving the known problem
               of removing artifacts from MRI images, this method opens the door to potentially
027
               removing many other types of spurious correlations from images and other data
028
               modalities across many fields.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In the field of medical imaging there is a strong desire to train AI models for disease and pathology
034   detection. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a powerful, high-resolution modality which re-
035   quires no radiation exposure yet still provides valuable clinical information to medical professionals,
036   particularly with brain pathologies given the difficulty of imaging within the skull using X-rays. The
037   complexity of medical scans and their information-richness have made them a key target of machine
038   learning research. However, when machine learning models are trained on medical images (MRI or
039
      otherwise) they often encounter issues from spurious correlations. For example, it has been known
      for years that AI models easily memorize features of scans such as the particular model of machine
040
      used to take the scan. Because of this, models often fail to generalize beyond the training dataset
041
      (Badgeley et al., 2018). The specific artifacts used by models to predict machine type and man-
042   ufacturer are generally poorly understood and often invisible to the naked eye, so removing them
043   from images is a complex task. Here, we propose, GECO, a method for automatically removing
044   said artifacts, by leveraging generative deep learning techniques to ’harmonize’ an input image. We
045   develop a generator network that is capable of modifying an input MRI image to prevent a classifier
046   from being able to identify the machine manufacturer corresponding to medical images, while also
047   not affecting the quality of the image.
048   GECO takes in a MRI image and outputs via a generative network a ’deartifacted’ version of the
049   original image. This deartifacted product retains virtually all of the structure of the initial scan, but
050   eliminates or significantly reduces any identifying marks that would allow a deep learning model
051   to determine the spurious correlation from the original image. Our approach for doing so is to use
052   a GAN, which is trained to take raw MRI scans and remove as much as possible these spurious
053   correlations. Note that this is not the same process as removing scanning artifacts such as zipper
      or herringbone artifacts that affect image quality, but rather the intent is to remove features that are

      likely invisble to the human eye and impact a classifier’s ability to categorize the image. Our results
055   focus on image artefacts corresponding to different MRI manufacturers and field strengths. In each
056   case, across the training of these GANs we reduce the classification accuracy from nearly perfect
057   down to a level essentially equivalent to random guessing on the part of the classifier. We further
058   demonstrate that GECO is similarly capable of ”fooling” expert neuro-radiologists.

061   2    R ELATED WORK
062
063
064
      Examining related studies in this area, the work of Badgeley et al. (2018) serves as one of the primary
      inspirations for this project, and we set out to resolve some of the relevant issues via a state-of-the-art
065
      deep learning solution. Zech et al provided an overview of the difficulties in training classifiers on
066
      radiological data, and discussed potential reasons for the observed generalization gaps. Bagul et al.
067   (2017) and Coram et al. (2016), achieved performance similar to radiologists on specified tasks.
068   The latter case saw at least some successful generalization between institutions’ patient populations
069   - though it is not unsurprising that some cases would successfully generalize despite artifacts. Work
070   in this field indicates that CNNs have the capacity to attain a near-expert level of performance in
071   the absence of spurious correlations. Klein et al. (2021) developed an MRI image pre-processing
072   pipeline capable of detecting metadata in a scan using purely the image data alone, and we use their
073   architecture as a baseline for our classification task.
074   In the realm of MRI, different types of artifacts arise due to various reasons. Researchers have
075   been developing sophisticated methods to improve the quality of MRI scans. For instance, Lim
076   et al. (2023) devised an innovative approach using a GAN to mitigate motion artifacts. Motion
077   artifacts are prevalent and can severely degrade the quality of the scans. In another study, Berring-
078   ton et al. (2021) focused on the removal of spurious echo artifacts by employing a sensitivity en-
079   coding method. Their method efficiently estimated these artifacts and successfully removed them.
080   Schramm & Ladefoged (2019) conducted a comprehensive review of multiple strategies that have
081
      been successful in correcting metal artifacts. Their survey highlighted techniques, which have been
      instrumental in improving image quality in the presence of metal.
082
083   Interestingly, despite the multitude of studies focusing on various artifact types, to the best of our
084   knowledge, there is limited research on the removal of artifacts whose correlation properties and
085   origins are too complex to cleanly define. Yet such artifacts are well-established to significantly
086   impair the efficacy and generalizability of models trained on medical images. In medical imaging
087
      specifically, scanner-related artifacts can stem from calibration errors, hardware inconsistencies,
      or other scanner-specific issues, but they can also be produced by nuances of the software used
088
      to construct the images. There is not yet any known way to cleanly define general artifacts of
089
      this complexity, and in our view it is most likely the case that multifactorial processes produce the
090   complex spurious correlations which we attack in this paper. It is pivotal to identify and address these
091   artifacts to ensure the consistency and reliability of MRI scans across different imaging platforms,
092   and by extension between institutions. By producing a method which can eliminate such subtle and
093   complex yet impactful artifacts, we anticipate that GECO and future methods based on it will have
094   broad applicability to a variety of other sources of spurious correlations.
095
      In contrast with the work done by Yan et al. (2014) on photo editing via neural networks which
096   performed generic edits, our architecture for image editing is a one-shot, fully convolutional process
097   that has no reliance on segmentation maps or other learning tasks in order to generate the edited
098   image. Zhu et al. (2016) also published a generative approach to photo editing, but this was again
099   in the context of generic image editing and their method produces divergence between the input and
100   generated images which are not desirable for de-artifacting. Pathak et al. (2016) saw some success
101   at generating blocked out data from general images, though once again encountered the traditional
102   difficulties observed with generative networks such as blurriness in high-detail areas.
103   In summary, related approaches to image editing required some level of manual defining of desired
104   changes, which is highly problematic in the case of artifacts with unknown and complex distribu-
105   tions. Our work overcomes this difficulty by directly detecting the artifacts’ distributions and editing
106   them out all within a single, unsupervised architecture which we call Generative Editing via Convo-
107   lutional Obscuring, or ”GECO”. In this paper, we demonstrate that GECO is a powerful approach
      for removing unwanted information from images with complex distributions.

      3.1   T ECHNICAL A PPROACH
111
112   Our process begins with a large collection of MRI images, whose metadata is known to us but not
113   visible to humans in the images themselves. Two pieces of metadata were selected on the basis of
114   their complex impact on the images themselves: scanner manufacturer and scanner field strength.
115   MRI machines have designs which vary between manufacturers and play into the physics of the scan-
116   ners, and additionally the strength of the magnetic field generated by a scanner significantly impacts
117
      the images produced. For classifying by manufacturer, on the basis of data availability we selected
      four companies: GE Medical Systems, Siemens, Philips Medical Systems, or Imaging Biometrics.
118
      These companies are either machine manufacturers or providers of processing proprietary software,
119
      or both. In this paper we use ”manufacturer” to mean the label which is found in the ”manufacturer”
120   entry for an image’s DICOM file (a standard format in radiology). For field strengths, most com-
121   mercial models today operate at either 1.5 Tesla (T) or 3T, and so these were the natural classes for
122   the network to operate with in the field strength experiments. It is important to observe that this is a
123   classification problem rather than a real/fake detection problem as is more common in GAN training
124   systems. We assessed only one of the two classification problems - manufacturer and field strength
125   - in a given experiment, so separate results are provided for each.
126   The GECO generator that we found to be the most efficient is based upon the U-net architecture.
127   Developed by Ronneberger et al. (2015b), U-nets have proven extremely effective for image seg-
128   mentation and a variety of other problems. The overall structure of our GAN is inspired by Cycle-
129   GAN (Efros et al., 2017). Our pixelwise loss has similarities to the identity loss term included in
130   CycleGAN.
131
      The generator network is given only the original MRI scan itself, and outputs a ’deartifacted’ scan
132   which retains as much as feasible the initial features of the image. With the generator, we use a
133   Tanh function as the final activation to squeeze the output between -1 and 1. The classifier net-
134   work follows the structure used by DeepDicomSort (Klein et al., 2021), employing a similar set of
135   six convolutional layers, along with two dense layers on top for generating the final machine-type
136   classification scores. Our core architecture is shown in Figure 4.
137

138   another such that improving the loss of one worsens the loss of the other, with the goal that this
139   teaches the generator network a strong representation of what ’deartifacted’ images look like without
140   having to provide examples ourselves. We define our learning target for this problem by developing
141   a generator loss with two distinct components: the first is a sum of squared pixelwise errors between
142   the input image and the output image, and the second is a cross-entropy style loss applied to the
143   output of our classifier network. This ’inverse’ cross-entropy loss is low when the classifier outputs

145   (note the similarities to the TripleGAN structure (Li et al., 2022)). Note, as this portion of the

      conceptualization. The generator loss can be expressed as follows, where m is the total number of

      3.1.1   VARIANT C LASSIFICATION A RCHITECTURES
176
177   In terms of the classifying part of our architecture, we explore two other variants. The first one
178   includes a max-pooling layer after all convolutional layers, and for the other one we introduced a
179   discriminator network to explore three-player architectures. For the max-pool classifier, the pooling
180   layer guarantees that the dense head of the classifier will always receive N inputs where N is the
181   channel size of the final convolutional layer. This allows the classifier to be scale invariant, so long
182   as the features of the image are not vastly larger or smaller than the images on which the classifier
183   is trained.
184   In the case of the three-player architecture, we explore using a learned discriminator network in
185   place of the pixelwise MSE loss, resulting in a GAN structure with three distinct networks. In
186   this structure the generator modifies the input images, and the generator output is fed to both a
187   discriminator network that attempts to guess whether or not the image is unmodified or output from
188   the generator and a classifier network that attempts to guess which type of machine the image was
189   taken from. The discriminator is also fed real images from the dataset as a separate phase of training
190
      in each iteration. As a result, the discriminator and classifier are evaluated using cross entropy, and
      the generator is evaluated using a combination of our own loss function on the classifier’s output,
191
      and the reverse of the binary cross-entropy loss on the discriminator’s output. That is our generator
192
      loss becomes (where BCE stands for binary cross-entropy)
193

                                Ltrip.gen. =L̃gen. + BCE(Clas.(Gen.(X)), ⃗0)                                (3)

198   (where L̃gen. is used to tackle a variant on our initial problem). Thus, the generator attempts to both
199   fool the classifier into being unable to predict the class of the output image, while also producing
200
      ’realistic’ images as evaluated by the discriminator. A diagram of our variant three-player structure
      is shown in Figure 6.

203
204   In combination with our classification architectures, we also explore variants on the architecture of
205   our generator. The primary technique we explore here is the concept of a residual generator, that
206   is learning the edits to make to an image instead of the raw image itself. To do this, we construct
207   the generator as normal with all convolutional layers, but after generating the final output we scale
208   it down by a learnable parameter α and add it to the initial input. Mathematically, we represent the
209   output X ′ of the generator on an image X as:
210
211                                          X ′ = X + α ∗ Gen.(X)                                          (4)
212
213   With this structure, we allow the generator to decide the magnitude of the changes required to fool
214   the classifier, while not requiring it to learn the identity mapping that optimizes the MSE component
215   of the loss. We also explore a multi-residual block structure, with three residual blocks of two
      convolutional layers each. We believe that it is worth exploring these options due to the naturally

      3.2   BASELINE M ETHOD
224
225   There are no examples we were able to find in literature of relevant generative ’editing’ networks
226   that we are able to use a baseline comparison. Instead, we use a classification network as our
227   baseline for performance on this task and compare our post-learning classification results to show the
228   effectiveness of our generative network. To that end, we use the DeepDicomSort (Klein et al., 2021)
229   architecture and apply transfer learning to replacing the dense head with a set of weights trained
230   to classify the manufacturer of the machine used to take the scan instead of the type of scan being
231   taken. We use the weights hosted on the project’s github page, slightly adjusting the architecture
232   to fit our data. We then train the model for three epochs on the padded 512x512 unmodified MRI
233
      scans, freezing all weights in the convolutional backbone. As an output, we return the predicted
      class label of the input MRI scan and train with a cross-entropy loss. We obtain a loss of 0.0325,
234
      and a test accuracy of 0.9884, showing that even with a short duration of training, it is possible to
235
      achieve near-perfect classification on this task and to place greater context on the final results of our
236   generator.

      3.3   DATASET
239
240   We obtained images in the DICOM format as well as manufacturer information from the Cancer
241   Imaging Archive (TCIA). We use the Brain Tumor Progression and TCGA-LGG datasets. Our
242   preprocessing steps are inspired by DeepDicomSort’s (Klein et al., 2021) handling of the same
243   dataset. The brain images might have different orientation so we adjust output direction and origin.
244   Particularly, we focus on an Axial orientation. After constructing the whole 3D brain from a set of
245   DICOM files, we slice the brain horizontally into NIfTI slices (Figure 1e). We only keep meaningful
246
      images that have fewer than 90% of values set to pure black or pure white, as a heuristic to remove
      slices of the very top or very bottom of the brain. Before training, we zero pad all images to have
247
      the size as the largest image of the set (which in our case is 512x512). The final dataset contains
248
      21987 slices and consists of four classes: GE Medical Systems, Siemens, Philips Medical Systems,
249   or Imaging Biometrics. The class distributions are relatively balanced (ranging from 22% to 28%),
250   and we follow a 70/20/10 rule to split the training/validation/testing sets.
251
252   3.4   H YPERPARAMETERS
253
254   For our hyperparameter tuning process, we selected three variables to explore: generator learning
255   rate, classifier learning rate, and beta (our tuning parameter for the two-component generator loss).
256   We started our sweep for the classifier and generator around 5 × 10−3 as this was near the value used
257   in the DeepDicomSort classifier and it had empirically shown strong results. We tested log space
258   sweeps of approximately seven values per parameter, and achieved the best performance with the
259
      combined parameter set of 5 × 10−4 for the learning rate of the generator and the classifier, and a
      beta value of 0.02.

      3.5   M ETRICS OF GECO P ERFORMANCE
263
      We evaluate GECO using three performance metrics: Classification Accuracy, Structural Similar-
264   ity Index Measure (SSIM), and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR). The first metric quantifies the
265   ability of the generator to deartifact an image in terms of how often it is capable of fooling a classi-
266   fier trained on the output distribution, while the second and third metrics quantify the ability of the
267   generator to maintain the integrity of the input image throughout the de-artifacting process. Classi-
268   fication accuracy is calculated via running the generator on our test set, and feeding the generated
269   images into the corresponding classifier to achieve class predictions. These predictions are then
      compared against the true labels of the images fed into the generator, and we return the simple ratio


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      of correct guesses to total images. Since we have four classes, the ’optimal’ accuracy we expect is
271   0.25, representing effectively random guessing.
272
273   SSIM is calculated by computing similarity between an image before and after it is processed by
274
      the generator, with 1 being the maximum. Finally, PSNR is calculated by comparing the square
      of the maximum value of the input image to the mean-squared error between the input image and
275
      the generated image. This metric is generally reported in terms of ’decibels’ on a log scale, with a
276
      potentially infinite PSNR if the two images are identical.

283   We explored several structures for the GECO network based on a review of the techniques used for
284   generative modelling and methods for improving accuracy and robustness. Our results in Table 1 on
285
      the core architecture show high SSIM and PSNR reflecting visual clarity in the generated images as
      seen in Figure 1, and an accuracy of 26% from the classifier showing a large dip in performance com-
286
      pared to the pretrained model. We note that in failed experiments such as hyperparameter choices
287
      which led to mode collapse, poor visual fidelity, or restrained editing, the final classifiers retained
288   approximately 96% accuracy. This indicates that the quantiative results showing GECO’s efficacy
289   do not reflect a lack of opportunity for the model to learn to classify these images. If the generator
290   fails to train successfully for one of the defined and detectable issues listed above, the classification
291   accuracy remains at the baseline of approximately 96%, but when the generator trains successfully
292   the classifier’s accuracy is reduced significantly.
293
      Next, we tested two residual networks, both the multi-residual and full residual, and we observe sim-
294   ilar results to the core generator but with moderately degraded performance in terms of image quality
295   and deartifacting efficacy Table (1). In addition to the residual generators, we tested a three-player
296   architecture. This architecture frequently suffered from a mode collapse and instability during train-
297   ing. With the three-player architecture, we observed a slight improvement moving to two iterations
298   of the classifier instead of one per generator iteration, however in the process we observed that the
299   accuracy of the classifier during training did not increase as much compared to a one for one training
300   regime. For comparison, the two iteration classifier achieved a maximum accuracy of 42% during
301   training, while the one iteration classifier achieved a maximum accuracy of 47% during training. We
302
      also tested using three iterations for the classifier, but the SSIM decayed beneath the benchmark of
      0.95 where image quality began to degrade.
303
304   From these results and the data in Table 1, we select the core architecture as our optimal structure.

      During the training process, we observed that when SSIM dips below approximately 0.95 we begin
397
      to see irregularities in the generated image that are clear to the naked eye. Conversely for SSIM
398
      values at or above that estimated threshold the two images appear virtually indistinct. While this

400   the final say in the success of our model’s ability to maintain the semantically meaningful portions of
401   a scan must be provided by trained radiologists. Thus, we collected data via survey asking experts to
402   determine which scan they preferred between the unmodified and GECO-processed images as well
403   as asking five neuro-radiologists to compare and score the visual quality of both types of images.
404   We show our results in Figure 3.
405   From Figure 3a we observe that the large majority of cases show no significant preference between
406   the provided images, and that in the cases where a preference is exhibited it is largely random which
407   of the two images is preferred. Note that we refer to the original image as ’right’ and the generated
408   image as ’wrong’ as we assume that our generator has not actually improved the image quality in
409   any meaningful way, since it was not trained on any such task during this process.
410
      This data is further backed up by Figure 3b, from which we observe that when asked to rate the
411   quality of MRI scans on a scale of 1-10 our generated images achieved very nearly the same scores
412   on average as unprocessed images. There is an overall very minor trend of generated images being
413   perceived as slightly lower quality, but aside from radiologist 3 the scale of the effect is negligible.
414   Thus, we conclude that our model has succeeded at its task of removing identifying artifacts without
415   significantly impacting the image’s value as a diagnostic tool.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
418
419
      In this work, we show that GECO is able to de-artifact MRI brain images by achieving a drop
420   in classifier accuracy from ∼97% to ∼26% on the task of MRI manufacturer identification while
421   maintaining the visual quality of images as measured by SSIM and PSNR. Similarly, for MRI field
422   strength identification, GECO took the accuracy of a classifier from 97% to approximately 50% -
423   and for that problem this was achieved in only a few epochs of training. This faster learning likely
424   due to the fact that solving a two-class problem requires less data than a four-class problem in the
425   case of removing manufacturer-specific artifacts. The magnitude of these artifact detection accuracy
426   drops, indicates the generator network’s strong efficacy in removing artifacts from scans, and in
427   fact the final result is nearly equivalent to a classifier that is randomly guessing. Our architecture
428
      is particularly exciting given this level of efficacy was achieved with a relatively small number of
      convolutional layers in the model.
