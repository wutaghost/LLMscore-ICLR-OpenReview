009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Automated chip placement is an important problem in enhancing the design and
012            effectiveness of computer chips. Previous approaches have employed transfer
013            learning to adapt knowledge obtained via machine learning from one chip place-
014            ment task to another. However, these approaches have not notably reduced the
015            necessary chip design time, which is crucial for minimizing the total resource
016            utilization. This paper introduces a novel transfer learning approach called Pa-
017            rameter Monte Carlo Tree Search (PMCTS) that utilizes MCTS to transfer the
018
               learned knowledge from deep reinforcement learning (RL) models trained on one
               chip design task to another chip design by searching directly over the model pa-
019
               rameters to generate models for efficient chip placement. We employ MCTS to
020
               escape the local optima reached by training from scratch and fine-tuning meth-
021            ods. We evaluate our methodology on four chip design tasks from the literature:
022            Ariane, Ariane133, IBM01, and IBM02. Through extensive experiments, we find
023            that our approach can generate models for optimized chip placement in less time
024            than training from scratch and fine-tuning methods when transferring knowledge
025            from complex chip designs to simpler ones.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Chip placement is a fundamental and time-consuming stage in the chip design process (Cheng et al.,
031   2022). The process involves placing the various components, such as macros and standard cells, of
032   a netlist file, which contains a logical description of a circuit’s interconnections, in specific positions
033
      on the chip layout (Ellis-Monaghan & Gutwin, 2003). Standard cells refer to the fundamental logic
      gates such as NAND, NOR, and XOR, while macros represent functional blocks like static random-
034
      access memory (SRAM). The goal of placement is to minimize power, performance, and area (PPA)
035
      metrics while adhering to restrictions like placement density and routing congestion (Cheng & Yan,
036   2021). This is a longstanding problem, but the exponential growth of artificial intelligence (AI)
037   technology has generated an increased demand for advanced computational hardware. However,
038   due to the end of Moore’s Law and Dennard scaling, it is essential to shift towards specialized
039   architecture to cope with the rapidly increasing computational requirements of AI (Mirhoseini et al.,
040   2020). As such, AI-based techniques are expected to accelerate the chip design cycle, establishing a
041   mutually beneficial connection between hardware and AI.
042   Transfer learning methods can prove beneficial in reducing the cost and resource requirements of
043   developing machine learning (ML) models from scratch for novel domains (You et al., 2023). These
044   methods utilise pre-existing model weights rather than random initialization to reduce the computing
045   demand and enable faster adaptation, which in turn can support more design iterations (Cao et al.,
046   2010). Many transfer learning approaches exist, including zero-shot learning (Xian et al., 2017) and
047   domain adaptation (Farahani et al., 2021) to transfer knowledge from a source domain to a target
048   domain. However, zero-shot learning often requires the class sets or features to be identical, and in
049   the case of domain adaptation approaches, a degree of parallelism between the domains is necessary.
050
      As such, for chip design tasks, fine-tuning has been the most common approach Mirhoseini et al.
      (2020), though they have not demonstrated the expected increases in speed.
051
052   Search algorithms have played a crucial role in domains such as Neural Architecture Search (NAS)
053   (Li & Peng, 2020) and Hyper Parameter Optimization (HPO) (Alibrahim & Ludwig, 2021). These
      approaches are less commonly applied directly to the parameters of a model (Singamsetti et al.,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021; Mahajan & Guzdial, 2022; Doosti & Guzdial, 2023). Building on prior search-based trans-
055   fer learning approaches, we propose an approach for chip placement that directly updates network
056   parameters via search.
057
058
      Our study introduces a novel transfer learning method based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
      and MCTS using Upper Confidence Bounds for Trees (UCB) to balance exploration with exploita-
059
      tion (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006; Auer et al., 2002). MCTS is probabilistic and heuristic-driven,
060
      allowing it to adapt to the most promising search regions (Deng & Wu, 2023) and guiding our
061   proposed approach to effectively explore inside promising search regions. Our approach aims at
062   directly optimizing model parameters within a complex search space. It iteratively updates a search
063   tree by simulating possible outcomes for an RL agent in the chip design environment. Every node
064   in the tree corresponds to two networks: a policy network and a value network, and each node is
065   associated with a cost value: the performance of these networks in a specific chip design environ-
066   ment. The policy network, denoted as πθ (a|s), determines the probability of selecting an action a
067   in a given state s, with the parameter θ influencing this behaviour, and the value network provides
068   an estimation of the expected reward for the chip placement. The cost value is the weighted total of
069
      wirelength, congestion, and density costs associated with the chip design task; a node is considered
      more efficient if it creates chip placements with less cost value.
070
071   The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
072
073          • We propose a novel transfer learning-based optimization approach named Parameter Monte
074
               Carlo Tree Search (PMCTS) utilizing the MCTS algorithm to dynamically update the pa-
               rameters of a neural network to adapt models with a relatively small amount of computation
075
               and which can generate efficient chip placements.
076
077          • We validate the proposed methodology across four distinct chip design tasks—Ariane, Ar-
078
               iane133, IBM01, and IBM02—highlighting its effectiveness and stability in diverse chip
               design tasks.
079
080          • We show that transferring knowledge from a complex chip design task, such as IBM02, to
081            a simpler chip design, like Ariane, outperforms both training from scratch and fine-tuning
082
               approaches in terms of creating chip placements with reduced cost value in lesser time-
               frames. However, transferring knowledge from a simpler chip design to a more complex
083
               one does not yield the same benefits.

086   2     R ELATED W ORK
087
088   This section covers related work in transfer learning, parameter search, and chip placement prob-
089   lems.
090
091   2.1   T RANSFER L EARNING
092
      Transfer learning using deep neural networks (DNNs) refers to the process of transferring knowledge
093
      and parameters from a DNN trained on a source dataset to another DNN aiming to address a similar
094   target problem (Shafahi et al., 2019). Several methods, including zero-shot (Xian et al., 2017),
095   one-shot (Fei-Fei et al.), and few-shot (Ravi & Larochelle, 2017) approaches, exist to transfer
096   knowledge from a source domain to a target domain. However, these approaches often rely on
097   manually authored features or significant additional data to effectively guide the knowledge transfer
098   process. Our proposed approach does not require the incorporation of additional manually authored
099   features or significant additional training to adapt to a target domain.
100
101   2.2   PARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
102
103   Hyperparameter Paramater Optimization (HPO) is the most common search problem associated with
104
      DNNs (Feurer & Hutter, 2019). However, our primary focus is on optimizing parameters rather than
      hyperparameters.
105
106   The majority of current neural network (NN) research has been on deep learning, where the pri-
107   mary approach for training NNs is backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986), an algorithm used to
      compute the gradient of the loss function. Backpropagation-based approaches have been useful in


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      different domains for determining optimal parameters. However, backpropagation can often become
109   stuck in local minima (Siddique & Tokhi, 2001). Further, the optimal parameters for backpropaga-
110   tion appear to differ depending on the specific scenario. Sparse regularization methods in NNs focus
111   on a subset of the network to enhance computational and space efficiency without compromising
112   performance by addressing the issue of redundant and correlated structures. The backpropagation
113   technique proposed by Sun et al. (2017) involves computing a very small but vital part of the gra-
114   dient and modifying only the corresponding small portion of the parameters in each update. This
115   approach results in highly sparsified gradients that only modify highly relevant parameters for the
116   given training sample. Additionally, pruning strategies may compress a model without significantly
117
      reducing its capacity for prediction (Yeom et al., 2021). Our approach can be understood as a type
      of sparse optimization, where we rely on search to identify parameters for optimization.
118
119   Neuroevolution stands as an alternate optimization approach that involves the training of NNs using
120   evolutionary algorithms. Making use of evolutionary search facilitates significant functionalities
121   that are not commonly available to gradient-based methodologies, such as the ability to learn neural
122   network’s activation functions and architectures (Stanley et al., 2019). Aly et al. (2019) introduce
123
      an evolutionary metaheuristic aimed at optimizing DNNs. The objective is to search multiple regions
      of the search space while preserving a specific distance between those regions to guarantee diversity.
124
      Zhou et al. (2024) introduce a neuroevolutionary diversity policy search method for multi-objective
125
      reinforcement learning (RL) challenges, utilizing gradient-based genetic operations to equip each
126   policy with a buffer for gathering past experiences. A deep genetic algorithm proposed by Such
127   et al. (2017) competitively trains DNNs for difficult RL tasks. Their genetic algorithm exhibits
128   remarkable competitiveness with widely used algorithms in the domain of deep RL challenges. Our
129   proposed transfer learning method utilizes MCTS, a distinct search approach not previously applied
130   to these cases.
131
      There has been prior work on applying search algorithms to transfer learning tasks (Singamsetti
132   et al., 2021; Mahajan & Guzdial, 2022; Doosti & Guzdial, 2023). These approaches have shown suc-
133   cess at few-shot transfer learning and have generally found that tree-based optimization approaches
134   like MCTS outperform other search algorithms, such as hill-climbing and beam search (Mahajan
135   & Guzdial, 2022; Doosti & Guzdial, 2023). However, these approaches have not previously been
136   applied in an RL setting.

      2.3   AUTOMATED C HIP P LACEMENT
140
141
      The chip placement process involves determining the optimal positioning of various macrocom-
      ponents, a task that requires sophisticated multi-objective optimization to balance factors such as
142
      wirelength, congestion, and density. Various approaches have been developed to address this prob-
143
      lem, including conventional divide-and-conquer (Fiduccia & Mattheyses, 1988) and hierarchical
144   placement strategies (Tsay et al., 1988), as well as advanced deep RL techniques (Mirhoseini et al.,
145   2020). Myung et al. (2023) propose that pre-training networks to extract features from different
146   netlists and utilize them as encoders in the policy network can greatly decrease the duration of train-
147   ing. Moreover, Lai et al. (2022) present an approach for placement using RL. This method utilizes
148   convolutional NNs to learn position, wirelength, and view information for circuit modules, resulting
149   in a rich visual representation. Lee et al. (2024) discuss using diffusion models for chip placement.
150   They developed a NN with interleaved graph convolutions and multi-headed attention layers and
151   also a synthetic data generation algorithm. However, there are certain limitations associated with
152
      this approach. In particular, synthetic datasets with shorter wirelengths may result in models ex-
      hibiting poorer performance to out-of-distribution circuits. We note that we specifically focus on
153
      transfer learning as a way to speed up chip design tasks and, as such, do not compare with these
154
      approaches that train from scratch.
155
156   Historically, simulated annealing (SA) has been a long-standing method with proven success in
157   chip placement (Sarrafzadeh et al., 2003). SA is a search-based approach but optimizes the chip
158
      design directly rather than optimizing a chip design model. But SA shows a significant lack of
      speed, presents challenges in parallelization, and encounters difficulties adapting to current circuits’
159
      growing complexity and size.
160
161   The RL-based methodology of Mirhoseini et al. (2020) explores an RL agent trained to optimize the
      arrangement of chips. In our paper, we build upon the policy and value networks from their study.

163   from a complex chip design task to a simpler chip design can quickly create models that can generate

      3     S YSTEM OVERVIEW
168
169
      This section outlines our method, which is divided into two main components. First, we train a
170   model on a source chip design task. After that, our proposed transfer learning method based on
171   MCTS directly modifies the parameters of the trained source model through interactions with the
172   target chip design environment. This method creates a tree of child nodes, each of which represents
173   a distinct model. Lastly, we return the best model, which has the lowest cost value, from the tree.
174
175   3.0.1    S TEP 1: S OURCE T RAINING
176
177
      The first step involves training a model on the source chip design task T askS as described in step 1 of
      Algorithm 1. We apply a specific training configuration for each chip design task due to differences
178
      in the numbers of horizontal and vertical routes per micron, as well as the allocation values for
179
      macro horizontal and macro vertical routing. We employ an existing architecture of policy and
180   value networks and all other domain-specific hyperparameters from Mirhoseini et al. (2020). The
181   parameters of the policy model are updated using PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) based on the source
182   chip design task T askS . We use an existing proxy cost function from Cheng et al. (2023) as shown
183   in Equation 3.
184
185   3.0.2    S TEP 2: PARAMETER S EARCH
186
      During the second step, we utilize parameter search as a form of transfer learning to adapt the source
187
      model to the target chip design task. Initially, the root node NR corresponds to the source model Sm .
188
      From this root node NR , we generate a child node by applying a randomly selected search operator to
189   modify its parameters. Child nodes are created until the tree T has reached its maximum number of
190   nodes, NM which can be specified. To balance exploration with exploitation, we employ an ϵ value
191   of 0.1, so the best-performing child nodes are selected ninety percent of the time based on the Cost
192   value calculated on the target chip design task T askT by using the proxy cost function as shown
193   in Equation 3, but less-explored nodes are chosen periodically from all the nodes Ni . The selected
194   child node Nc will function as the new root node NR . The node NR will later go through further
195   parameter modification using a randomly selected search operator to generate a child node Nj ,
196   calculate the Cost value for the target chip design T askT , and backpropagate this information up
197
      the tree. These operations will terminate when the node reaches its maximum length of child nodes
      L. The search operator directly modifies the parameters of the model, allowing a potentially more
198
      efficient optimization process, as these can be understood as sparse updates. Lastly, the algorithm
199
      outputs the most efficient node NLC based on the Cost value from the search tree T for the target
200   chip design task T askT .

285
286   In this paper, we focus on the application of our proposed methodology across different chip design
287   tasks. We evaluate our approach on well-understood tasks that have been studied in prior transfer
288   learning work of Mirhoseini et al. (2020). This setting allows us to demonstrate that our method
289
      can produce optimized models in less time than corresponding baselines, which typically rely on
      traditional backpropagation.
290
291
292
      4.1   E NVIRONMENTS
293   We examine our approach on four distinct chip designs. The first design refers to the Ariane RISC-V
294   CPU found in the Google Circuit Training repository (Mirhoseini et al., 2020). It consists of 133
295   hard macro units and serves as a standard reference for the chip placement problem. Hard macros
296   refer to pre-built building blocks (Lavin et al., 2013). The second design, Ariane133, is a slight
297   variant of the first design while maintaining the 133-macro unit configuration (Cheng et al., 2023).
298   In addition, we assess our approach using two designs from the ICCAD04 benchmark, namely
299   IBM01 and IBM02, which consist of 246 and 271 hard macro units, respectively (Cheng et al.,
300   2023; Adya et al., 2004; Adya & Markov, 2002). The complexity of the IBM variations exceeds that
301
      of the Ariane versions.
302
303   4.2   BASELINES
304
      In this research, we utilize two primary baselines: the scratch model and the fine-tuning approach
305   from the prior work of Mirhoseini et al. (2020). Both baselines utilize conventional gradient descent
306   techniques for optimization, providing a comparison to evaluate the efficacy of our proposed method.
307   The training from scratch model baseline is used as a reference for how well an RL agent can perform
308   when trained on the target domain from random initialization. The fine-tuning baseline instead takes
309   a model trained on a source domain and fine-tunes it on a target domain. Through the comparison
310   with these baselines, we show the effectiveness of our approach in creating optimized models for
311   chip placement without primarily depending on gradient descent methods.

336
      We evaluate the performance of our approach utilizing four separate chip design tasks. We use all

      permutations of source and target domains. For example, when assessing our approach’s transfer

      6   D ISCUSSION
476
477
      Our proposed transfer learning approach, PMCTS, offers several key advantages that make it highly
      effective for solving the chip placement problem. One of the primary benefits is its simplicity,
478
      as it directly modifies the network’s parameters without requiring complex architectural changes
479
      or additional training on the target chip domains. Our experiments show that traditional methods
480   such as training from scratch or fine-tuning are time-consuming to reach high-performing models.
481   In contrast, our approach demonstrates greater speed and efficiency, resulting in reduced time and
482   computational costs. This makes it particularly appealing for chip placements where rapid devel-
483   opment and cost savings are critical. The shorter period required to generate effective models of
484   our approach, particularly when transferring knowledge from complex to simple chip designs, of-
485   fers significant advantages, thereby presenting a more economical and scalable solution to the chip
      industry.


                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      However, it is crucial to acknowledge that this benefit does not apply when the knowledge transfer is
487   reversed, going from simpler to more complex designs. This suggests that the complexity inherent
488   in certain chip designs contains vital information that greatly helps the transfer learning process.
489   However, simpler designs lack sufficient depth to transfer knowledge to complex designs. In the
490   majority of cases, our PMCTS approach shows higher efficiency compared to both training from
491   scratch and fine-tuning approaches. It would be worthwhile to explore the possibility of using either
492   the scratch model or the fine-tuned model as input to PMCTS to further enhance its performance.
493   However, considering that one key advantage of PMCTS is the ability to obtain a more efficient
494   model with significantly less computation time, we believe that this would contradict the purpose of
495
      this research. Therefore, we prefer to conduct an additional study of this in the future.
496   We believe that part of our results, which demonstrate superior performance in transferring knowl-
497   edge from complex to simple chip designs compared to the reverse, may be due to our selection
498   of search operators. Particularly, our second search operator essentially has the effect of reducing
499   parameter magnitudes in the network. This may align with pruning or other sparse optimization ap-
500   proaches in terms of simplifying a given model, making it more general, and encompassing a simpler
501
      task. In Table 9 in the Appendix A, we analyzed the sensitivity of PMCTS to our mutation func-
      tions, demonstrating the role of the multiplication operation. When transferring knowledge from
502
      complex to simple chip designs, removing the multiplication operator led to quicker convergence to
503
      better nodes. For instance, in the IBM01 to Ariane transfer, the minimum cost value of 1.1930 was
504   achieved after creating 30 nodes with all functions, whereas removing the multiplication operator
505   reduced this to just 17 nodes. In contrast, removing the multiplication operator significantly reduced
506   performance when transferring knowledge from simple to complex chip designs. These insights will
507   inform future refinements for our search operators to optimize performance in diverse transfer learn-
508   ing settings. In future work, we hope to explore alternative search operators, which may improve
509   the robustness of our approach.
510   We plan to extend our approach to more complex chip design tasks, like BlackParrot (Quad-
511   Core) and MemPool Group, to validate the effective trend of complex-to-simple knowledge transfer
512   (Petrisko et al., 2020; Riedel et al., 2023). In addition, alternative search algorithms like Quality-
513   Diversity can be explored given how they offer a wide range of optimized solutions that vary based
514   on a number of user-specified parameters of interest (Chatzilygeroudis et al., 2021).

518   7   C ONCLUSIONS
519
520
      This paper presents a novel transfer learning approach named Parameter Monte Carlo Tree Search
521
      (PMCTS) that utilizes MCTS to optimize model parameters for chip placement to generate efficient
522   models in a short timeframe while transferring knowledge from complex chip design tasks to less
523   complex ones and without requiring additional authored transfer features. The speed, along with
524   its effectiveness, makes our approach potentially useful for chip placement scenarios where rapid
525   optimization and implementation are crucial. By conducting experiments on four different chip
526   design tasks—Ariane, Ariane133, IBM01, and IBM02—we demonstrate that knowledge transfer
527   from complex chip designs to less complex ones results in improved performance, surpassing the
528   training from scratch and fine-tuning in terms of both time and performance. In contrast, the transfer
529   from simpler to more complex designs does not produce the same advantage in all scenarios.
