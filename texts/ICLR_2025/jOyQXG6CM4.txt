011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013             Large language models (LLMs) have had a transformative impact on a variety
014             of scientific tasks across disciplines such as biology, chemistry, medicine, and
015
                physics. However, ensuring the safety alignment of these models in scientific re-
                search remains an underexplored area, with existing benchmarks primarily focus
016
                on textual content and overlooking key scientific representations such as molecu-
017
                lar, protein, and genomic languages. Moreover, the safety mechanisms of LLMs
018             in scientific tasks are insufficiently studied. To address these limitations, we in-
019             troduce S CI S AFE E VAL, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the
020             safety alignment of LLMs across a range of scientific tasks. S CI S AFE E VAL
021             spans multiple scientific languages—including textual, molecular, protein, and
022             genomic—and covers a wide range of scientific domains. We evaluate LLMs in
023             zero-shot, few-shot and chain-of-thought settings, and introduce a ’jailbreak’ en-
024             hancement feature that challenges LLMs equipped with safety guardrails, rigor-
025             ously testing their defenses against malicious intention. Our benchmark surpasses
026
                existing safety datasets in both scale and scope, providing a robust platform for
                assessing the safety and performance of LLMs in scientific contexts. This work
027
                aims to facilitate the responsible development and deployment of LLMs, promot-
028
                ing alignment with safety and ethical standards in scientific research.

      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved significant breakthroughs in recent years. LLMs
035
      such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024b) and Llama-3.1 (meta llama, 2024) have garnered widespread
036   attention, and their application to scientific tasks has proven highly valuable across various domains
037   (Xie et al., 2023; Boyko et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Liao et al., 2024). While these models hold
038   substantial potential to accelerate scientific research, concerns regarding their safety, robustness,
039   and potential misuse in sensitive applications have become increasingly prominent (He et al., 2023;
040   Resnik & Hosseini, 2024).
041

042   and physics. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1 bottom right, malicious actors could potentially
043   exploit LLMs to design harmful genomic sequences, including mutations that enhance the infectivity

045   in technologies like benchtop DNA synthesis, which enable the rapid and flexible creation of DNA
046   sequences in laboratory settings (Hoose et al., 2023). Consequently, the barriers for bad actors to
047   engineer dangerous biological agents are significantly lowered. Numerous studies have highlighted
048   the risks posed by such dual-use technologies—where tools developed for beneficial purposes can
049   be repurposed for malicious intent (Urbina et al., 2022; Sandbrink, 2023; Quach, 2023; Anthropic,
050
      2023; Gretton et al., 2024; Baum et al., 2024).

053   the SciMT-Safety benchmark (He et al., 2023) employs a red-teaming strategy (Derczynski et al.,
      2024; Inie et al., 2023) to simulate risk-prone scenarios, assessing the models’ ability to mitigate

      Large Language Models for Scientific Tasks. Large Language Models have become powerful
121   tools in advancing scientific research, specifically tailored to handle complex, domain-specific tasks
122   such as molecular generation (Kim et al., 2021; Mazuz et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024), protein
123   structure prediction (Elnaggar et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024), and genomic analysis
124   (Ji et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). These models are obtained via pre-trained
125   directly on specialized scientific data (Gururangan et al., 2020; He et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2023b),
126   or fine-tuned from general-purpose base models (Xu et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023a). Different from
127   the natural language, key scientific languages (e.g., molecular, protein, and genomic languages)
128   demand customized tokenization and representation strategies. For instance, molecular language
129
      processing often utilizes formats like SMILES (Weininger, 1988) and SELFIES (Krenn et al., 2022)
      to encode chemical structures.
130
131
      Risks of Misusing the LLMs for Scientific Tasks. While LLMs hold great promise in advancing
132
      scientific research, their misuse poses significant risks. Urbina et al. (2022) demonstrated the dual-
133
      use dilemma by showing how AI tools originally developed for drug discovery could be repurposed
134   to generate toxic compounds, including chemical warfare agents. This exemplifies the dangerous
135   potential of these technologies. Similarly, Sandbrink (2023) cautions that the integration of LLMs
136   with biological design tools may lower the barriers to manipulating biological agents, making it
137   easier for non-experts to engage in harmful activities and increasing the scale of potential harm. In
138   nucleic acid synthesis, Wheeler et al. (2024) highlights the need for global standards to ensure that
139   LLMs used in this field do not aid malicious actors in circumventing existing safety measures. Fur-
140   thermore, Hoffmann et al. (2023) stresses the importance of embedding robust biosecurity protocols
141   in AI-driven synthetic biology to mitigate risks.
142
143
      Safety Assessment of LLMs for Scientific Tasks. Existing safety assessments have predomi-
      nantly focused on general-purpose LLMs (Gehman et al., 2020; Zhuo et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023;
144
      Zhang et al., 2024c), but specialized benchmarks for scientific models are relatively scarce. SciMT-
145
      Safety (He et al., 2023) is one of the first benchmarks to assess the safety of AI models in scientific
146   domains specifically. It emphasizes mitigating risks such as the misuse of AI in chemical synthesis
147   and biological design through red-teaming approaches. Similarly, SciKnowEval (Feng et al., 2024)
148   evaluates scientific knowledge across five levels in LLMs, with level 4 assessing a model’s capacity
149   to make sound, secure, and ethical decisions. This includes evaluating the harmfulness and toxicity
150   of the information generated, as well as understanding the ethical implications and safety concerns
151   inherent in scientific research. However, these benchmarks focus on a narrow range of scientific
152   domains and are relatively small in scope. More importantly, these benchmarks do not adequately
153   challenge models with built-in guardrail mechanisms.

191   3.1    R EGULATORY AND E THICAL F OUNDATIONS FOR S CIENTIFIC S AFETY
192
193   The S CI S AFE E VAL benchmark is grounded in key contemporary AI safety regulations, including
194
      standards developed by the National Technical Committee 260 (NTC 260) on Cybersecurity (Na-
      tional Technical Committee 260 on Cybersecurity of SAC, 2024), SB 1047 (Wiener et al., 2024),
195
      and international conventions such as the Biological Weapons Convention (BWC) (United Nations
196
      Office for Disarmament Affairs, 2020). Each of these frameworks plays a pivotal role in shaping
197   the design of S CI S AFE E VAL. NTC 260 and SB 1047 emphasize the need for cross-disciplinary
198   evaluation of AI safety, while the BWC highlights the importance of AI models in biological con-
199   texts to prevent misuse. By integrating these regulatory principles, S CI S AFE E VAL provides a robust
200   and comprehensive framework for evaluating the safety considerations of LLMs across a range of
201   scientific domains. More details about the regulations and guidelines are provided in Appendix A.
202
203   3.2    B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
204
205   To ensure our benchmark reflects the range of harmful content AI systems might encounter, we
206   generate the examples via combining instructions sourced from existing widely-used non-safety-
207
      focused datasets and harmful substances curated from well-established hazard databases (Marvin
      et al., 2023), with details in Appendix B. As a result, we construct a multi-faceted benchmark
208
      that evaluates LLM safety across multiple scenarios. S CI S AFE E VAL assess the safety of LLMs
209
      across multiple scientific disciplines and levels of complexity, using a large-scale, high-quality
210   dataset. The benchmark has four key attributes: (i) Multi-Disciplinary: It spans four critical scien-
211   tific fields—chemistry, biology, medicine, and physics with domain-specific tasks, covering various
212   types of harmfulness; (ii) Large Scale: It covers the natural and scientific language (e.g., protein se-
213   quences and molecule SMILES), offering 10 times more examples; (iii) High Quality: The dataset
214   is sourced from authoritative databases relevant to each scientific field, ensuring accuracy and rel-
215   evance; (iv) Multi-faceted Benchmarking: It supports a range of evaluation scenarios, including
      zero- and few-shot learning, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, and jailbreak testing.

      3.2.1   I NSTRUCTION G ENERATION FOR S CIENTIFIC TASKS
217
218   To collect the instructions for the four scientific domains, we emphasize the comprehensive coverage
219   of key tasks and safety considerations for each one. For chemistry-related instructions, we utilized
220   the dataset provided by Fang et al. (2023a), offering task instructions that focus on Molecule Gener-
221   ation, Property Prediction and Reaction Prediction. Biology-related instructions were sourced from
222   Guo et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2023), which includes Protein Function Prediction, Protein Se-
223
      quence Generation, Protein Structure Prediction Gene Classification and Gene Generation. In the
      medical domain, we incorporated task instructions from Tran et al. (2024), which are tailored for
224
      biomedical NLP tasks like Inference Reasoning and Knowledge Retrieval. Lastly, physics-related
225
      instructions are drawn from Nakamura et al. (2024) and Biden (2023), focusing on Knowledge Re-
226   trieval. This diverse selection of domain-specific instructions ensures that our benchmark captures a
227   wide array of instruction types across multiple scientific disciplines and their various tasks.

229   3.2.2   S UBSTANCES F ROM H AZARD DATABASES
230
231   Chemistry. We curated 4,983 toxic chemical compounds from PubChem (Kim et al., 2023), fol-
232   lowing a rigorous selection process based on key physicochemical properties, toxicological profiles,
233   and environmental impacts. The collected data includes substances characterized with 6 tags (Corro-
234
      sives, Environment, Acute Toxicity, Health Hazards and Explosiveness) in alignment with the GHS
      Hazard Class Pictograms (CHEMICALS, 2002) . We filtered out incomplete or non-compliant en-
235
      tries and the remaining data retained critical attributes such as compound names and their SMILES/-
236
      SELFIES (Weininger, 1988) representations. As a result, the S CI S AFE E VAL -C HEMISTRY encom-
237   passes a wide range of hazard information, including acute toxicity, chronic toxicity, and ecotoxicity,
238   providing a comprehensive resource for safety and hazard analysis in chemical research.
239
240   Biology. Our dataset encompasses both protein-based and gene-associated biohazards, systemat-
241   ically curated to ensure high coverage and relevance in biological safety research. (1) For protein
242   toxins, we selected 2,763 toxic proteins from UniProt, specifically utilizing the Toxin label. These
243   proteins are naturally occurring and are known to disrupt or kill other cells. Leveraging the Gene
244   Ontology (GO) classification system (Consortium, 2004), we further categorized these proteins into
245   11 subtypes under the “Toxin activity” class. During preprocessing, we removed entries with in-
246   sufficient annotations and retained complete protein names and FASTA (Pearson, 1994) sequences
247
      to maintain data integrity. We also included toxic proteins from a wide range of organisms and
      environments, spanning animals, plants, fungi, and pathogenic bacteria, to enhance diversity. (2)
248
      For gene-associated hazards, we utilized data from the Bacterial and Viral Bioinformatics Resource
249
      Center (BV-BRC) (Olson et al., 2023). The collected data includes 2,100 DNA virus entries, se-
250   lected based on six criteria: structural characteristics, family classification, host species, geographic
251   origin, year of collection, and sequence length. We adopted the Baltimore classification (Koonin
252   et al., 2021) to group DNA viruses into 8 major categories. We merged BV-BRC raw genomic
253   attributes into a unified dataset. This multi-dimensional approach to data collection ensures com-
254   prehensive coverage across different viral families, hosts, and geographic origins, maximizing the
255   S CI S AFE E VAL -B IOLOGY’s utility for further biological safety evaluations.
256
257   Medicine. The dataset in the medical domain was constructed from three key sources: PubChem
258   (Kim et al., 2023), Haz-Map (Brown, 2008), and the Catalog of Narcotic and Psychotropic Sub-
259   stances (CQLP) (IndiaGovernment, 2022), each providing comprehensive coverage of addictive,
260   toxic, and psychotropic chemical substances. (1) For addictive drugs, the selection criteria were
261
      primarily based on the Controlled Substance Act (CSA) (Lampe & Attorney, 2021) classifications,
      with a focus on medical utility, abuse potential, and addiction risk. (2) For toxic substances, data
262
      were extracted based on their occupational health impacts, particularly the extent to which chemi-
263
      cal exposure affects human health. During data processing, incomplete and non-compliant entries
264   were removed, ensuring the retention of key attributes like chemical names. (3) For psychotropic
265   substances, the selection criteria centered on dependence potential and legal regulation, with data
266   aligned with national and international guidelines, including the Narcotic and Psychotropic Sub-
267   stances Regulation (cql, 2013). Substances were categorized by their effects into 2 main tags.
268   S CI S AFE E VAL -M EDICINE provides a diverse representation of chemicals that pose risks to both
269   physical and mental health, underscoring its relevance for medical safety evaluations. We summa-
      rize the tag information across above scientific fields in Appendix C.

      Our evaluation incorporates zero- and few-shot settings, to assess the risk without and with the prior
296
      experience. Additionally, given the widespread adoption of CoT prompting (Wei et al., 2022) as
297   a standard technique in many applications, our benchmark includes it in the evaluation framework
298   to assess whether this structured reasoning approach contributes to enhancing model safety. Fur-
299   thermore, recognizing that state-of-the-art LLMs, such as GPT-4o, are equipped with built-in safety
300   mechanisms, we integrate jailbreak into our benchmark to enable a more thorough evaluation of
301   potential risks. Specifically, we use WildTeaming (Jiang et al., 2024) to generate jailbreak prompts
302   for every single seed prompts (i.e. origin prompts). Jailbreak cases are present in Appendix G.3.
303   This holistic evaluation ensures a multi-faceted risk assessment under various conditions.

342   Evaluation Mode. We consider zero-shot, few-shot and CoT settings. For few-shot examples,
343   we manually collected a pool of successful defense (i.e. refuse to answer) cases from the zero-shot
344   experiments for each subset. We then selected 3 representative examples demonstrating effective
345   strategies for handling malicious prompts across different scientific domains. These examples, with
346   2 more benign prompt-response pairs, as shown in Appendix G.1, were used to construct five-shot
347   prompts that provide the model with additional context and guidance during the evaluation. The
348   selection process focused on ensuring diversity in the types of attacks and defenses, allowing for a
349   comprehensive assessment of the model’s ability to generalize from a limited set of examples. For
350
      CoT prompting, we design reasoning paths that help LLMs identify why a specific instruction with
      hazardous substance content may be harmful or toxic. We concatenate “And think step by step. (1)
351
      Is the content harmful or toxic? (2) If the content is harmful or toxic, I should immediately refuse
352
      to answer and not output any more information.” at the end of the original prompt, see example
353   case in Appendix G.2. We expect the model to gradually realize that the query is harmful during the
354   reasoning process. For general-purpose LLMs like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024b), we evaluate the full
355   S CI S AFE E VAL dataset, assuming that these models possess knowledge across multiple domains,
356   such as Chemistry, Biology, Medicine, and Physics. For domain-specific scientific models, such as
357   DNAGPT (Zhang et al., 2023), we limit the evaluation to the S CI S AFE E VAL subsets relevant to their
358   specific scientific domain.
359
360
      Evaluation Criteria. Since our primary objective is to assess the safety performance of the mod-
361   els, we define failure as any response in which the model does not refuse to engage with a harmful
362   query. In other words, if the model provides any information in response to any malicious prompts
363   in S CI S AFE E VAL, it is considered a failure. Based on this binary criterion, each model’s response
364   is classified as either a pass or a fail. To ensure consistency and accuracy in evaluating whether a
365   language model’s response constitutes a refusal to answer, we designed the prompt in Appendix E,
366   which was utilized by the LLaMa 3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) model in our experiments.

481   4.3     D ISCUSSION
482
483   Safety Alignment of LLMs in Science Domains Remains Challenging. Our findings reveal that
484   existing LLMs struggle to effectively defend against harmful queries within professional scientific
485   domains. These models often fail when confronted with complex scientific language with malicious
      intent, leaving them susceptible to potential misuse. This highlights a critical gap in the safety


                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      alignment of LLMs, particularly in high-stakes scientific applications where precision and safety
487   are paramount. More importantly, this challenge is compounded by the inherent complexity in
488   scientific discourse, where nuanced terms can have vastly different implications depending on the
489   context. The inability of LLMs to fully grasp such subtleties raises concerns about their reliability in
490   environments where small errors can have outsized consequences, such as drug discovery, biohazard
491   research, or even climate modeling. Future advancements must focus not only on improving safety
492   mechanisms but also on developing a deeper understanding of domain-specific risks, ensuring that
493   AI systems are not just safe in a general sense but are meticulously aligned with the ethical and
494   practical demands of their respective fields.
495
496   Few-shot and CoT Approaches Enhance Overall Safety Performance. The experimental re-
497
      sults suggest that safety-specific few-shot prompting and Chain-of-Thought reasoning significantly
      improve the safety performance of LLMs in many scenarios. These techniques help models nav-
498
      igate harmful instructions more effectively by providing structured reasoning paths and additional
499
      context. However, this leads to important considerations: Are these improvements sufficient for
500   high-risk domains, or are they merely mitigating symptoms of deeper vulnerabilities? Few-shot and
501   CoT approaches offer structured reasoning, yet they may inadvertently introduce new biases or over-
502   fit to particular contexts, potentially limiting their generalizability. Furthermore, as the complexity
503   of scientific tasks grows, the capacity for even few-shot and CoT-enhanced models to maintain con-
504   sistent safety across diverse and evolving scientific challenges remains an open question. Moving
505   forward, the development of adaptive safety mechanisms—capable of evolving alongside scientific
506   knowledge—will be crucial to avoid rigid systems that may fall short in novel, unforeseen scenarios.
507
508   Sci-LLMs Are More Vulnerable than General-purpose LLMs. A large portion of domain-
509   specific scientific LLMs, pretrained on scientific literature, appear to be more vulnerable to harmful
510
      inputs than their general-purpose counterparts. These models often lack the defensive capabilities
      that few-shot or CoT techniques can provide, making them more susceptible to misuse. The un-
511
      derlying issue is that domain-specific models, by focusing heavily on scientific terminology and
512
      knowledge, may lose the broader, more diverse understanding that general-purpose models gain
513   from larger, more varied datasets. This raises an important question about the trade-off between
514   specialization and robustness: how can we retain the expertise of domain-specific models without
515   compromising their resilience to harmful inputs? Additionally, traditional safety mechanisms, de-
516   signed for more generalized models, may not seamlessly transfer to specialized domains, where
517   unique ethical concerns and contextual factors need to be considered. Future research must explore
518   hybrid approaches that combine domain-specific expertise with generalizable safety features, ensur-
519   ing that specialized models are not just effective but also safeguarded against malicious use across
520   scientific fields.

522   5   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we present S CI S AFE E VAL, a benchmark designed to assess the safety alignment of
525   large language models (LLMs) in scientific tasks across 31,840 harmful queries from four key sci-
526   entific domains: chemistry, biology, medicine, and physics. Our evaluation of both general-purpose
527   and domain-specific LLMs highlights that state-of-the-art models, regardless of being open or closed
528   source, still pose safety risks in scientific tasks. However, techniques such as few-shot examples and
529
      chain-of-thought prompting significantly improve safety across most models, with Claude 3.5 per-
      forming well in zero-shot and five-shot settings, and GPT-4o and o1-preview excelling in chain-of-
530
      thought scenarios. We hope S CI S AFE E VAL marks a crucial step toward advancing LLM safety and
531
      alignment in scientific domains, fostering the development of safer, more responsible AI systems.

      A       S AFETY R EGULATIONS AND G UIDELINES

      This section presents key regulations and guidelines that are essential for ensuring AI safety, with
813   a particular focus on Large Language Models (LLMs) applied within sensitive scientific domains.
814   These frameworks address ethical standards, security protocols, and risk mitigation measures that
815   guide the responsible development and deployment of AI technologies.
816
      National Technical Committee 260 on Information Security Standardization (China National
817
      Standardization Committee, 2024) 1 : The National Technical Committee 260 on Information Se-
818
      curity Standardization, under the Standardization Administration of China (SAC), is responsible for
819   establishing national standards on cybersecurity and AI safety. The committee’s work focuses on
820   developing guidelines to ensure AI systems are secure, robust, and aligned with ethical norms. The
821   2024 publication by NTC 260 outlines standards to guide developers in mitigating cybersecurity
822   risks, such as adversarial attacks, data breaches, and system misuse, particularly when AI is used
823   in sensitive scientific applications. These standards are highly relevant to the deployment of LLMs,
824   which process complex, domain-specific data that could lead to significant security and ethical chal-
825   lenges if improperly managed.
826   SB 1047 and AI Safety Regulations (California Legislature, 2024) 2 : California’s SB 1047 is a
827   legislative bill introduced by Senator Scott Wiener and others, focusing on the responsible devel-
828   opment and deployment of AI technologies. The bill emphasizes AI safety and ethics, proposing
829   guidelines for transparency, risk assessment, and accountability in AI systems. While still under
830   legislative review, SB 1047 underscores the importance of embedding safety as a core principle
831   throughout the AI lifecycle, covering both research and commercial applications. The legislation’s
832   relevance to LLMs lies in its call for mitigating risks such as misuse, unintended consequences, and
833
      the potential harm these models could introduce within scientific research domains.
834   UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (United Nations Educa-
835   tional, Scientific and Cultural Organization, 2021) 3 : In 2021, UNESCO adopted its Recommen-
836   dation on the Ethics of AI, calling for ethical frameworks that align AI technologies with human
837   rights and freedoms. These guidelines advocate for fairness, transparency, accountability, and en-
838   vironmental sustainability in AI systems across various domains, including scientific research. For
839
      LLMs, these recommendations are significant as they outline the necessity for safety mechanisms,
      ethical oversight, and inclusive practices, ensuring that AI technologies are developed and deployed
840
      in ways that support societal well-being and global ethical standards.
841
842   FDA’s Regulatory Framework for AI/ML-Based Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) (U.S.
843   Food & Drug Administration, 2021) 4 : The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has devel-
844   oped a framework for regulating AI and machine learning-based software that qualifies as a medical
845
      device. This framework stresses transparency, performance monitoring, and the need for continual
      improvement of AI systems that have an impact on patient health and safety. For LLMs that assist
846
      in biomedical research, drug discovery, diagnostics, or clinical support, these regulations ensure that
847
      AI models are held to stringent standards of safety, reliability, and clinical efficacy, thereby reducing
848   risks of unintended consequences or patient harm.
849
850   The EU AI Act (European Union, 2021) 5 : The European Union’s proposed AI Act, introduced in
851
      2021, is one of the most ambitious frameworks aimed at regulating AI technologies within member
      states. It categorizes AI applications based on their risk levels—unacceptable, high-risk, limited-
852
      risk, and minimal-risk—and places particular emphasis on high-risk AI systems, including those
853
      used in critical sectors like healthcare, infrastructure, and law enforcement. The Act mandates
854   transparency, data governance, and conformity assessments to ensure adherence to safety and ethical
855   standards. For LLMs, which may influence areas like medicine and biology, compliance with these
856   regulations is essential to uphold data quality, minimize risks, and maintain transparency.

      The Biological Weapons Convention (United Nations Office for Disarmament Affairs, 2020) 6 :
865   The Biological Weapons Convention (BWC) is an international treaty that prohibits the develop-
866   ment, production, and acquisition of biological and toxin weapons. The reaffirmation of the BWC
867   in 2020 highlights the global commitment to preventing the misuse of biological technologies. While
868   initially focused on biological warfare, the treaty has broader implications for modern biotechnolo-
869   gies, including AI-driven synthetic biology and genomics. As LLMs have the potential to support
870   the design of biological agents, their development must align with the BWC framework to prevent
871   accidental or malicious use in the creation of biological weapons.
872   OECD Principles on AI (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, 2019) 7 :
873   The OECD’s “Principles on Artificial Intelligence” set internationally agreed-upon standards to fos-
874   ter trustworthy and responsible AI. These principles stress the importance of democratic values,
875   inclusivity, and sustainability. Among these, the principle of “Robustness, Security, and Safety”
876   mandates that AI systems be resilient, secure, and reliable throughout their lifecycle. For LLMs,
877   adherence to these principles is crucial to ensure that they are robust against misuse, particularly
878   when handling sensitive data in scientific domains like genomics or chemical synthesis.
879   Asilomar AI Principles (Future of Life Institute, 2017) 8 : The Asilomar AI Principles represent
880   an early, widely-cited set of ethical guidelines for AI research and development, shaping discussions
881   around AI ethics globally. Though not legally binding, these principles advocate for research trans-
882   parency, accountability, and the alignment of AI systems with human values. Given the potential
883   for misuse of scientific LLMs, these principles are particularly relevant for encouraging shared re-
884   sponsibility, prioritizing long-term safety, and preventing arms races in AI development, fostering
885
      responsible innovation in scientific AI applications.
