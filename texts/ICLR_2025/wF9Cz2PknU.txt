011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the success of 2D and 3D visual generative models, there is growing interest in
014            generating 4D content. Existing methods primarily rely on text prompts to produce
015            4D content, but they often fall short of accurately defining complex or rare motions.
016
               To address this limitation, we propose MagicPose4D, a novel framework for refined
               control over both appearance and motion in 4D generation. Unlike current 4D
017
               generation methods, MagicPose4D accepts monocular videos or mesh sequences as
018
               motion prompts, enabling precise and customizable motion control. MagicPose4D
019            comprises two key modules: (i) Dual-Phase 4D Reconstruction Module which
020            operates in two phases. The first phase focuses on capturing the model’s shape using
021            accurate 2D supervision and less accurate but geometrically informative 3D pseudo-
022            supervision without imposing skeleton constraints. The second phase extracts the
023            3D motion (skeleton poses) using more accurate pseudo-3D supervision, obtained
024            in the first phase and introduces kinematic chain-based skeleton constraints to
025            ensure physical plausibility. Additionally, we propose a Global-Local Chamfer loss
026            that aligns the overall distribution of predicted mesh vertices with the supervision
027
               while maintaining part-level alignment without extra annotations. (ii) Cross-
               category Motion Transfer Module leverages the extracted motion from the 4D
028
               reconstruction module and uses a kinematic-chain-based skeleton to achieve cross-
029
               category motion transfer. It ensures smooth transitions between frames through
030            dynamic rigidity, facilitating robust generalization without additional training.
031            Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that MagicPose4D significantly
032            improves the accuracy and consistency of 4D content generation, outperforming
033            existing methods in various benchmarks.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The 4D generation task involves creating a temporal sequence of 3D models of moving objects.
039
      Given the difficulty of the general problem, recent approaches have made use of pre-trained models,
      using prompts to convey the user’s intentions. Recently, there has been a significant focus on the
040
      use of text/image-prompts to describe appearance and motion Ling et al. (2023); Ren et al. (2023);
041
      Zhao et al. (2023). The general pipeline of such existing consists of two stpdf: (i) acquiring static
042   geometry through a 3D generation (e.g., a text/image-to-3D) model Liu et al. (2023), which generates
043   3D representation such as meshes/implicit fields according to text/image prompts, and (ii) obtaining
044   motion information via a video generation model Blattmann et al. (2023a), which generates video
045   according to text/image prompts. This approach has achieved impressive 4D content results.
046
      However, challenges lie in facilitating users to freely and precisely specify the articulated motion
047
      of a non-rigid 3D object and the generation faithfully reflecting the prompts in terms of accurately
048   capturing desired object appearance, geometry, and motion. The main issues with current methods
049   are the following: (i) Temporal inconsistency in 3D geometry: Most current video generation
050   models fail to ensure temporal consistency of 3D geometry. This involves additional complexity when
051   objects are articulated because the 3D shape and movement must change mutually consistently during
052   motion. For instance, while using the existing methods for 4D animal generation, we have observed
053   unnatural and implausible configurations, such as the number of limbs of the object changing across
      different frames. (ii) Limited to common motions: Current video generation models perform well


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      in generating simple, subtle, and common actions, such as walking or small motions like shaking, but
055   they struggle to satisfactorily generate more complex motions, e.g., involving large or uncommon
056   movements such as "King Kong dancing Hip-Hop" and "pig running like a rabbit" as shown in Fig.5.
057   (iii) Text is inadequate for accurately describing the details of motion: As shown in Tab.4, most
058   existing methods use text as prompts to describe the desired motion, with some efforts Bahmani
059   et al. (2024a) allowing trajectory (root body transformation) control. However, they are unable to
060   specify detailed motions, e.g., by showing a real-world animal’s movement (say, via a monocular
061   short video).
062   To address these issues, we propose MagicPose4D, which enables detailed control over both appear-
063   ance and motion. Unlike existing methods, which rely mostly on text descriptions as motion prompts
064   and for which it is difficult to convey complex or rare motions, our approach accepts monocular
065   videos or dynamic mesh sequences as motion prompts. This allows for more precise supervision and
066   faithful 4D generation. MagicPose4D introduces two key modules: Dual-Phase 4D Reconstruction
067   Module and Cross-category Motion Transfer Module. The first module estimates a sequence of
068   3D models of the object while also simultaneously estimating motion parameters from the motion
069
      prompts, and the second module transfers this motion to the target object, which is generated by a 3D
      generation model controlled by appearance prompts.
070
071   Dual-Phase 4D Reconstruction Module: Given the complexity, especially with articulated objects,
072   we divide this module into two phases. First, we use image descriptors (e.g., segments, flow) as
073   2D supervision. Additionally, we employ relatively less accurate but geometrically informative
074   pseudo-3D models as geometric priors (using image-to-3D models) to provide 3D supervision for 3D
075
      reconstruction. This phase does not impose constraints emanating from the articulated nature of the
      object, e.g., captured in the underlying skeletal structure and plausible changes in it during motion,
076
      allowing each part to learn arbitrary rotations (R) and translations (t) while focusing on learning the
077
      model’s shape. In the second phase, we ensure the physical plausibility of the motion by enforcing
078   kinematic chain-based skeletal movement constraints. Additionally, we propose a Global-Local
079   Chamfer loss, which ensures that the overall distribution of predicted mesh vertices aligns with
080   the supervision while maintaining part-level alignment, without requiring additional annotations.
081   Cross-category Motion Transfer Module: This module achieves cross-species motion transfer
082   by mapping the skeleton of one species to another by establishing joint and limb correspondences
083   through a kinematic-chain-based representation of the skeleton. Our motion transfer module is
084   non-training-based. It helps improve generalization and prevents the poor performance seen in many
085   existing methods when tested on data with significant gaps from the training set. Also, we leverage
086
      dynamic rigidity Zhang et al. (2024c) to guarantee smoothness between frames, unlike the existing
      approaches Song et al. (2021; 2023), which perform frame-independent pose transfer.
087
088   The following are the main contributions of this paper: (i) A Novel 4D Generation Framework:
089   Our new framework leverages monocular videos as motion prompts, providing more accurate and
090   more precisely specifiable 4D action generation. (ii) Skeleton Based 4D Representation: By using
091   skeleton-mediated geometric and 3D prior, we achieve more accurate motion estimation and 3D
092
      reconstruction, improving the physical plausibility of the generation. (iii) Global-local Chamfer
      Loss: We introduce a novel loss function to better align estimated mesh vertices with the supervisory
093
      3D model overall while maintaining part-level alignment, without additional annotations. (iv)
094
      Cross-category Motion Transfer: Our cross-category mapping in terms of skeleton based dynamic
095   rigidity representation enables smooth transitions between frames and robust generalization without
096   additional training. (v) Outperforms SOTA: Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that
097   MagicPose4D provides highly accurate 4D content and significantly outperforms existing methods
098   for 4D reconstruction and pose transfer across all three benchmarks that we experiment with.

      3     M AGIC P OSE 4D
147
148   MagicPose4D accepts two distinct types of input prompts: (i) appearance prompts and (ii) motion
149   prompts. Consistent with recent methods Blattmann et al. (2023c); Tang et al. (2023), both images
150   and textual descriptions can function as appearance prompts, delineating the desired object and its
151   visual characteristics. In a departure from existing approaches, MagicPose4D enables users to specify
152   precise motions and trajectories by providing a video/mesh sequence representing the anticipated
153   movement.
154   As illustrated in Fig 1, MagicPose4D comprises three critical components: (i) the 4D Reconstruction
155   module (Sec.3.2), (ii) the Cross-Category Motion Transfer module (Sec.3.3), and (iii) the Image-to-
156   3D Generation module. Each module is tailored to facilitate distinct aspects of dynamic modeling,
157   enabling adaptive 4D reconstructions that align with user-defined specifications.

161   To represent an animated 3D model, our method learns static representations, such as the visible
      canonical shape S ∈ RN ×3 , the underlying skeleton Sk = {J ∈ RJ×3 , Bs ∈ RB , Pi ∈ RB }, and

      the skinning weights W ∈ RN ×B . Additionally, it captures time-varying parameters, including
163   the global-local (root body-bone) transformations τ = {τ0t , τ1t , . . . , τBt } and the camera parameters
164   Pc . Here, B, N , and J represent the number of bones, vertices on the mesh surface, and joints,
165   respectively. The transformations τit ∈ SE(3) include τ0t for the root body, with the remaining τit

189   3.2     D UAL -P HASE 4D R ECONSTRUCTION FROM VIDEO
190
191   Extracting the 3D motion of an articulated object directly from a monocular short video is extremely
192   challenging. MagicPose4D addresses this issue by reconstructing the object and extracting its motion
193   by learning its physically plausible skeleton.
194   Reconstructing 4D models from short monocular videos is also a challenging task as it requires jointly
195   learning numerous parameters, resulting in an extensive optimization space. Erroneous predictions of
196   any parameter can trigger cascading failures. To address these challenges, we propose a Dual-Phase
197   4D Reconstruction module, which employs differentiated supervision across two distinct phases and
198   focuses on learning diverse representations.
199   In the first phase, the primary focus is on accurately capturing the external appearance (shape) of the
200   model. The underlying skeleton serves merely as an intermediary variable, utilizing non-kinematic
201   chains skeleton and learnable skinning weights to afford the skeleton greater deformation freedom.
202   This approach accelerates the learning of correct shapes. In contrast, the second phase aims to derive
203   a more physically plausible motion reference for effective motion transfer. Therefore, we adopt
204   kinematic chain skeletons and heat diffusion-based skinning weights, which narrow the deformation
205   space of the skeleton, thereby ensuring the plausibility of the internal structure. (Sec.3.2.1)
206   From a supervision perspective, the first phase blends 2D and pseudo-3D supervision, updating the
207   pseudo-3D supervision at the end of this phase. In the second phase, the 2D loss is removed, relying
208   solely on the updated 3D pseudo-supervision to guide the learning process. (Sec.3.2.2)

251
      As shown in Fig. 2, given the input from a monocular video, we utilize segmentation models Kirillov
252   et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024a) and optical flow prediction models Teed & Deng (2020) to obtain
253   2D supervision. Furthermore, we employ an image-to-3D model to independently predict meshes
254   for each frame, serving as pseudo-3D supervision. A question arises: "Why not directly use an
255   image-to-3D model to independently predict 3D meshes for each frame?" As illustrated in Fig.6 (c),
256   objects always exhibit self-occlusion in the video, under which circumstances image-to-3D models
257   typically fail to produce accurate results. For instance, some frames might only depict a camel
258   with two visible legs, resulting in a 3D mesh sequence that does not effectively capture the action
259   information portrayed in the video. Moreover, as demonstrated in Video 3 of the supplementary
260
      materials, meshes generated directly from the image-to-3D module are independent of one another,
      thus lacking temporal continuity and smoothness. Although the initial pseudo-3D supervision may
261
      not be very accurate, it still provides valuable geometric priors that help address the information loss
262
      caused by insufficient perspectives of the object in the video.
263

265   a reconstruction loss, which consists of silhouette loss, optical flow loss, texture loss, perceptual
266
      loss, smooth, motion, and symmetric regularizations, and Global-Local Chamfer (GLC) Loss. In the
      second phase, we only leverage GLC Loss and regularization terms without 2D losses. The details of
267
      the 2D loss functions and regularizations will be described in the Appendix.6.6.

307   3.3   C ROSS -C ATEGORY M OTION T RANSFER
308
309
      In the second phase of 4D Reconstruction, as shown in Fig. 3(a), we extract the underlying skeletal
      motion of the reference meshes. Given a sequence of meshes for the reference object, the skeletoniza-
310
      tion module extracts the skeleton of the canonical shape, which is defined as the shape corresponding
311
      to time = 0. We fix the canonical skeleton and the skinning weights, thus controlling the skeleton’s
312   pose solely by learning the pre-frame angles and bone scales. Subsequently, by employing blend
313   skinning, we obtain the deformed mesh.
314
315
      The input to the skeletonization module is a mesh, along with an optional skeleton template. When
      the reference object is a commonly recognized form, such as a quadruped or a human, we utilize the
316
      corresponding skeleton template and embed it into the mesh. Following the methodology described
317
      in Baran & Popović (2007), we construct a coarse discrete representation to locate the approximate
318   position of the skeleton within the internal space of the mesh. This process involves embedding
319   the skeleton into a graph derived from the character’s internal volume and determining an optimal
320   solution using the A* algorithm across all possible matches. When a template of the object is not
321   available, we employ skeleton extraction methods Bærentzen & Rotenberg (2021); Zhang et al.
322   (2024c) to extract the canonical skeleton of the reference object. After learning the reference motion
323   represented by the pre-frame angles and the bone scale of the skeleton, we can readily compute the
      global-local transformation of the target object using forward kinematics, as illustrated in Fig. 3(b).

      4.4    D IAGNOSTIC
497
498   Effectiveness of Global-Local Chamfer Loss. Relying solely on the geometric information con-
499   tained in a short monocular video often makes it challenging to achieve satisfactory 3D reconstruction
500   results. This difficulty arises because most videos typically offer sparse viewpoints and the presence
501   of non-rigid object deformations increases the optimization space. Consequently, many methods
502   that rely only on 2D supervision Yang et al. (2021b; 2022b); Wu et al. (2023) struggle to ensure
503   effectiveness on in-the-wild videos. To enhance the generalization capability of the reconstruction
504   module, we utilize existing image-to-3D model methods to obtain geometric priors. By using these
505
      models, we generate pseudo-3D meshes for each frame as 3D supervision. We employ the Chamfer
      distance as the 3D loss objective to align the predicted 3D mesh closely with the pseudo-3D mesh
506
      in terms of point distribution. However, global Chamfer distance alone can only ensure similar
507
      point distributions between the two meshes and does not guarantee the correspondence of points as
508   expected. For instance, as shown in Fig.6(a), without using the local Chamfer loss, the overall point
509   distribution might be similar, but the mesh shape could be entirely incorrect, such as the left and right
510   legs being swapped or vertices originally on the right front leg being moved near the left hind leg.
511   By incorporating the local Chamfer loss, we enforce consistency between each part of the predicted
512   mesh and the pseudo mesh, thus ensuring pose consistency and significantly mitigating the previously
513   mentioned issues.
514   Temporal Consistency is crucial for 4D generation. As shown in Fig.6 (b), performing pose transfer
515   independently for each frame results in noticeable discontinuities when viewing the entire video.
516   To address this, we first define the canonical space using the initial frame, where all frames share
517   the same canonical mesh, skeleton, and skinning weights. Each subsequent frame is derived from
518   the deformation of the first frame using global-local transformations and blending skinning. This
519   approach ensures the consistency of the static model and rigging. Additionally, we introduce a
520   dynamic rigidity regularization term between consecutive frames, which minimizes the deformation
521   of the object between successive frames. This ensures temporal smoothness of the 4D content.
522
523   5     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
524
525   We introduce MagicPose4D, a novel framework for 4D generation providing more accurate and
526   customizable 4D motion transfer. We propose a dual-phase reconstruction process that initially uses
527   accurate 2D and pseudo 3D supervision without skeleton constraints and subsequently refines the
528   model with skeleton constraints to ensure physical plausibility. We incorporate a novel loss function
529   that aligns the overall distribution of mesh vertices with the supervision and maintains part-level
530   alignment without additional annotations. MagicPose4D enables cross-category motion transfer using
531
      a kinematic-chain-based skeleton, ensuring smooth transitions between frames through dynamic
      rigidity and achieving robust generalization without the need for additional training.
532
533   The main limitations of our method and existing works are: (i) Deformation based on blend skinning
534   relies on accurate and robust skeletons and skinning weights predictions, facing a trade-off between
535   generalization and accuracy. Learning-based methods have limited generalization due to restricted
536   training datasets, while non-learning methods suffer from inductive bias, leading to suboptimal results.
537
      (ii) MagicPose4D can infer poses quickly for pose transfer without training, but 4D reconstruction
      requires significant training (5 hours on an L40S). (iii) Our method struggles with detailed motion
538
      control, such as fingers and facial features, due to the challenge of capturing fine-grain details during
539
      4D reconstruction. These issues represent future research directions.

744   6.1    K EY D IFFERENCES B ETWEEN THE T WO S TAGES IN 4D R ECONSTRUCTION
745
746   In the first stage of reconstruction, similar to many existing methods [38, 31, 34], each bone can
747
      freely perform SE(3) transformations, making it challenging to ensure the physical plausibility of
      the internal skeleton. In experiments, we observed that while the output mesh was correct, the skin
748
      weights and bone transformations were not physically plausible for each variable. Additionally, the
749
      obtained bone transformations could not be directly used for motion transfer because the shapes,
750   sizes, and part proportions between the reference object and the target object could differ significantly.
751   Directly applying the same SE(3) transformation to corresponding parts would be unreasonable and
752   would not ensure pose similarity. Therefore, in the second stage of reconstruction, we use kinematic
753   chain skeletons and apply heat diffusion to obtain skinning weights, which narrow the deformation
754   space of the skeleton, thereby ensuring the plausibility of the internal structure. This method uses
755   forward kinematics to transfer pre-frame bone angles and bone scales, ensuring accurate motion
      (pose) transfer.

866   6.3.1    A NIMAL
867
868   Davis-Camel provides a real animal video in BADJA Biggs et al. (2018) with 2D keypoints and mask
869   annotations, derived from the DAVIS video segmentation dataset Perazzi et al. (2016) and online
870
      stock footage. We extract reference motion from the reconstructed mesh sequence and transfer it to
      other identities.
871
872   PlanetZoo includes RGB synthetic videos of different animals with around 100 frames each. Plan-
873   etZoo covers a 180-degree visual field captured by a moving camera to allow better evaluation of
874   3D reconstruction when imaging parameters must also be dynamically estimated due to the moving
875   camera, and over a large visual field. In addition, following BADJA Biggs et al. (2018), we also
876
      provide 2D key point annotations.
877   DeformingThings4D is a synthetic dataset containing 1,972 animation sequences containing 31
878   categories of both humanoids and animals. Each sequence consists of 40 to 120 frames of motion
879   animation. In this dataset, the first frame is the canonical frame, and its triangle mesh is given. From
880   the 2nd to the last frame, the 3D offsets of the mesh vertices are provided, and we export the triangle
881   meshes for all these frames. We use the motions of these animal mesh sequences as pose references
882
      and transfer the pose to different identities.

      use the motions of these sequences as pose references and transfer the pose to different identities.

      6.4     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
896
897   For Davis-Camel, PlanetZoo, EverybodyDanceNow, and those self-collected data without ground
898   truth mesh, we first train the Dual-Phase 4D Reconstruction Module on 2 NVIDIA L40S GPUs with
899   batch size 16 for 10 epochs with a learning rate of 0.0001. MagicPose4D uses Zero123 Liu et al.
900   (2023) and SF3D Boss et al. (2024) as the image-to-3D model for 4D reconstruction. We then transfer
901   the per-frame motion from Phase 2 of 4D Reconstruction to the target object with the Cross-Category
902   Motion Transfer Module. The motion transfer is not learning-based and does not require any trainable
903   parameters, which makes MagicPose4D generalize well to unseen identities and reference motions.
904   For all other data with ground truth mesh available, we directly train the second phase of the 4D
905
      Reconstruction Module and then transfer the motions.

983    7    B ROADER S OCIAL I MPACTS
984
985    The proposed MagicPose4D for motion transfer offers extensive applications, enhancing communi-
986    cation in digital environments by enabling more effective self-expression through avatars or digital
987    characters. Additionally, MagicPose4D has the potential to revolutionize the entertainment and media
988
       production industries by facilitating the creation of more lifelike and expressive characters in movies,
       video games, and animations.
989
990    However, this technology also presents potential negative social impacts. Privacy concerns arise from
991    the unauthorized creation of realistic animations of individuals, and the technology could facilitate
992    the spread of misinformation through deepfakes. Risks include job displacement in fields like acting
993    and modeling, psychological effects from the blurring of reality and virtual experiences, and the
994
       exploitation of the technology for unethical purposes. Furthermore, cultural insensitivity, security
       threats, and the misuse of realistic animal animations could have broader societal implications.
995
       Addressing these issues requires robust ethical guidelines, legal frameworks, and technological
996
       safeguards to ensure responsible use and mitigate harm.
