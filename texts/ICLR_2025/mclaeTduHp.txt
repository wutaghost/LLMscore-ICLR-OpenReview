009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing prevalence of large-scale models, both in vision and language
012            domains, presents significant challenges in terms of memory and resource con-
013            sumption. While model pruning is an effective method for compressing models
014            to alleviate these constraints, existing techniques either require extensive fine-
015            tuning, which is resource-intensive, or perform well only at low sparsity levels
016            (10%−50%), failing at high sparsity levels (50%−90%). To address these issues,
017            this paper introduces LAMP to mitigate the drawbacks associated with traditional
018
               pruning methods, namely high resource consumption in methods that require ex-
               tensive fine-tuning, and poor performance at high sparsity levels in methods that
019
               do not. It reduces memory overhead and alleviates performance degradation at
020
               high sparsity. Experimental results demonstrate that LAMP achieves slightly bet-
021            ter performance than SparseGPT at low sparsity levels and significantly better at
022            high sparsity levels in both language and vision models, without significantly in-
023            creasing memory consumption when compared to SparseGPT.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Since the seminal works by (Vaswani et al., 2017) on the Transformer architecture and (Dosovit-
029   skiy et al., 2020) on the Vision Transformer, there has been substantial progress in both vision and
030   language models leveraging the foundational Transformer structure. In language modeling, model
031   series such as OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b) have harnessed the
032   Transformer architecture to construct large language models that deliver outstanding performance.
033
      Similarly, significant vision models like Segment anything model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) have
      employed the Vision Transformer to develop robust image representations, demonstrating excellent
034
      performances in tasks such as segmentation, classification, and various downstream applications (Ke
035
      et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023).
036
037   These models are remarkably large in scale. For instance, the largest OPT model contains 175
038   billion parameters, while the latest Llama v3 (Llama Team, 2024) model boasts up to 405 billion
039
      parameters. The largest SAM model has 636 million parameters, which, although significantly
      smaller, still poses deployment challenges in certain scenarios. To facilitate the deployment of these
040
      large-scale models, model compression techniques are often employed to reduce their size, enabling
041
      them to meet hardware constraints.
042
043   Pruning is a critical model compression technique, often categorized into multi-shot and one-shot
044   methods based on pruning frequency. Traditional iterative pruning methods, based on the Lottery
045
      Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2018), involve an iterative prune-and-finetune process to grad-
      ually identify a sparse subnetwork that performs comparably to the original model, thereby reducing
046
      the number of model parameters. Classic methods like magnitude pruning (Han et al., 2015b) and
047
      more recent approaches such as LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023), which utilizes block pruning with
048   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) fine-tuning, adhere to this hypothesis. However, for large models, global
049   fine-tuning is resource-intensive, demanding significant GPU memory. Although LLM-Pruner re-
050   duces memory requirements using LoRA, it still requires storage for intermediate results and gra-
051   dients, while methods like LLM Surgeon (van der Ouderaa et al., 2023), despite avoiding global
052   fine-tuning, also demand substantial memory and time due to the use of second-order derivatives.
053   In contrast, one-shot pruning methods have gained traction by minimizing memory usage during
      fine-tuning. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) avoids global fine-tuning by compensating for

      2.1   T RANSFORMER A RCHITECTURE
101
102   Since its introduction (Vaswani et al., 2017), Transformer architecture has become the cornerstone
103   of language models due to its superior performance in language tasks. Following the development
104   of GPT (Radford et al., 2018), researchers have built increasingly large models using Transformer
105   as primary building blocks. The Transformer has also proven effective in vision tasks. For exam-
106   ple, SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), which is based on the Vision Transformer, particularly excels in
107   segmentation and serves as a foundational model for various downstream tasks (Wu et al., 2023;
      Mazurowski et al., 2023; Quan et al., 2024).

      Both language and vision models utilize a backbone composed of multiple Transformer blocks. The
109   majority of parameters in these models are concentrated within their respective backbones. These
110   Transformer blocks consist of multi-head self-attention and feed-forward neural networks with two
111   linear layers. Multi-head self-attention is generally considered more critical than the feed-forward
112   network.

115   2.2   L ARGE M ODEL P RUNING
116
117
      Prior to the advent of large models, the development of pruning methods primarily aimed to enhance
      model performance at target sparsity levels, leading to many highly effective techniques. Pruning
118
      methods can be categorized by granularity into structured and unstructured pruning. Specifically,
119
      unstructured pruning (Han et al., 2015b) modifies individual weights, generally achieving better per-
120   formance but requiring a sparse engine for inference acceleration on GPUs. In contrast, structured
121   pruning (Liu et al., 2017) operates on regular blocks of weights, allowing direct inference accel-
122   eration on GPUs, although it does not match the performance of unstructured pruning. Currently,
123   popular pruning methods are typically iterative (Frankle & Carbin, 2018). These methods iteratively
124   prune a portion of the weights, followed by global fine-tuning to restore performance, repeating
125   this cycle until the target sparsity is achieved. While iterative pruning improves the performance of
126   pruned models, it requires multiple rounds of fine-tuning, resulting in longer pruning times. Some
127   research has also focused on one-shot pruning methods (Han et al., 2015a; Lee et al., 2018). These
128
      methods usually offer faster pruning and some do not require fine-tuning the entire model, thus sav-
      ing significant memory costs. However, one-shot pruning methods typically do not achieve the same
129
      performance as iterative pruning methods.
130
131   With the emergence of large models, the computational and memory costs of global fine-tuning made
132   iterative pruning methods prohibitively expensive. However, some approaches have combined itera-
133   tive pruning with LoRA to apply this technique to large models (Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
134
      Additionally, the speed and lower computational resource requirements of one-shot pruning meth-
      ods have led to the development of several such techniques for large models, notably SparseGPT
135
      and Wanda. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) is a one-shot rapid pruning method belonging to
136
      Optimal Brain Surgeon (Hassibi & Stork, 1992) family. It independently analyzes each basic layer
137   of the model, formulates the pruning problem as a constrained optimization problem, and solves the
138   pruned weight values using the Lagrange multiplier method. SparseGPT builds on the OBS foun-
139   dation by leveraging the property of Cholesky decomposition to efficiently obtain different orders
140   of Hessian matrices, significantly accelerating the pruning process and reducing memory usage. Es-
141   sentially, SparseGPT consist of two parts: pruning and compensation, which are integrated during
142   the Lagrange multiplier method’s solution process. However, SparseGPT compensates for prun-
143   ing errors by adjusting unpruned weights within each basic layer, without considering inter-block
144   weight interactions. Wanda (Sun et al., 2024), another pruning method, is faster than SparseGPT. It
145
      introduces a strong prior for language models, assuming that weight importance is related to both
      the absolute value of the weight and the corresponding output. Wanda designs an importance metric
146
      that achieves good results without compensating for pruning losses. However, subsequent studies
147
      Williams & Aletras (2023) suggest that while Wanda performs well in many cases, it can occasion-
148   ally fall short compared to SparseGPT, and its performance in vision models is particularly lacking,
149   where its strong priors may lead to suboptimal results.

      Our primary objective is to develop an efficient pruning method for large models, including both
155
      vision and language models. This method should be executable within a reasonable time frame,
156   even under limited computational and memory resources. Additionally, it should sustain robust
157   performance at high sparsity levels. To achieve this, we have carefully balanced the need for high
158   performance with the imperative to minimize memory and time overhead. In this paper, we introduce
159   LAMP, a pruning strategy that effectively mitigates performance degradation at high sparsity levels
160   and facilitates rapid pruning in memory-constrained environments. The following sections detail
161   our method through four key aspects: Theoretical Foundations, Progressive Block Pruning, Sparsity
      Rearrangement, and Chunked Intermediate Storage.

183   3.1        P ROGRESSIVE B LOCK P RUNING
184
185   As discussed in the introduction, traditional pruning methods typically require global fine-tuning to
186   compensate for pruning-induced errors, necessitating the entire model to be loaded into memory.
187   If full-precision fine-tuning is used, additional memory is needed to store full-precision gradients,
188
      resulting in a memory demand of at least twice the model’s size, often more. While methods like
      SparseGPT avoid full-model fine-tuning, significantly reducing memory usage, they only compen-
189
      sate for errors within individual blocks and overlook inter-block interactions. Given that blocks
190
      function as nested entities, where earlier blocks influence subsequent ones, ignoring these inter-
191   actions can lead to error accumulation. This issue may be negligible at low sparsity but becomes
192   critical at high sparsity, leading to rapid performance degradation, as confirmed by our experiments.
193
194
      To address this, we propose Progressive Block Pruning, which enhances inter-block error compen-
      sation by building upon SparseGPT. As depicted in Fig. 2(a), Progressive Block Pruning leverages
195
      the modular structure of large models, processing each block sequentially using a sliding window
196
      approach. Initially, the sliding window contains two blocks. Since the operations are consistent after
197   each slide, we explain the method using the first two blocks.
198
199   In Fig. 2(a), the sliding window moves from left to right with a window length encompassing two
200
      blocks. Initially, the window contains B 1 and B 2 . Pruning is first applied to B 1 , followed by
      compensating for the pruning-induced error in B 1 using B 2 . The detailed calculation procedure for
201
      this compensation is outlined in Algorithm 1, as follows: The input data from the calibration set
202

      3.2.2    I NTRA -B LOCK S PARSITY R EARRANGEMENT
257
258   As shown in Fig. 2(b), the majority of the parameters within a Transformer block are contributed by
259   the matrices depicted, which are clearly hierarchical. Therefore, a block can essentially be viewed
260   as a nested function: the QKV matrices represent the innermost function, while the second fully
261   connected layer (fc2) represents the outermost function. According to the conclusions drawn in the
262   Theoretical Foundations section, if the same sparsity level is applied uniformly across all layers,
263   the errors introduced by the QKV matrices, which are further from the output of B i , will be more
264
      challenging to compensate for in B i+1 . On the other hand, fc2 is closer to the output of B i , making
      its errors easier to compensate for in B i+1 . Additionally, inspired by previous works, earlier layers
265
      tend to converge first (Chen et al., 2023), and faster convergence often leads to better generalization
266
      performance (Hardt et al., 2016). Therefore, the sparsity distribution should be rearranged by reduc-
267   ing the sparsity of the QKV matrices and increasing the sparsity of fc2. To ensure that the global
268   target sparsity remains unchanged, we rearrange the sparsity according to the following rule:

275   3.3   C HUNKED I NTERMEDIATE S TORAGE
276
277
      Proposed method requires the intermediate results of each sample after passing through each block.
      Since the storage space required for these intermediate results is significantly larger than the space
278
      occupied by the samples themselves, simply storing them in GPU memory, as done in typical train-
279
      ing processes, would consume a considerable amount of memory. Given that GPU memory is a more
280   valuable resource than system memory, this approach would lead to significant memory wastage. To
281   address this issue, we employ Chunked Intermediate Storage to reduce memory usage during the
282   pruning process.
283
      As in Fig. 2(c), all intermediate results are stored in system memory, and they are only loaded into
284
      GPU memory when needed for processing through a block. For instance, While the GPU processes
285
      B i and zji , the CPU simultaneously loads zj+1 i
                                                          into GPU memory and releasing it from system
286                      i                          i+1
      memory. After zj is processed and get zj , it will be transferred from GPU memory back to
287
      system memory. This way, only two intermediate results and one block are kept in GPU memory at
288
      any given time, minimizing memory usage without affecting the speed of forward pass.

293   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
294
      To evaluate the effectiveness of our method, we focus on models with 7 billion parameters or fewer,
295
      as these are the most commonly used in research and resource-constrained scenarios. Pruning these
296
      models is also the most feasible on a single consumer-grade GPU. Specifically, we validate our
297   approach using the OPT-125M/1.3B/2.7B/6.7B and Llama 2-7B language models from Hugging
298   Face, as well as the SAM-B/L/H vision models provided by Meta. All experiments were conducted
299   on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with 24GB of memory.
300
      For the hyperparameter settings of LAMP, learning rate is adjusted according to the sparsity level, as
301
      different sparsity levels introduce varying amounts of error. α and β are tuned based on the training
302
      dynamics.
303
304   For calibration data, the language model calibration dataset follows the approach of SparseGPT,
305   using 128 segments of 2048 tokens, randomly sampled from C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2020). Simi-
306
      larly, the vision model calibration dataset consists of 128 images, randomly sampled from the second
      compressed file of the SA-1B dataset (Kirillov et al., 2023) provided by Meta.
307
308   To comprehensively evaluate the performance of pruned models, we validate OPT using the
309   Wikitext-v2 (Merity et al., 2016) and PTB datasets (Marcus et al., 1994) from Hugging Face, with
310   perplexity (PPL) as the evaluation metric. We also evaluate the pruned Llama 2-7B model using ac-
311   curacy on seven Zero-Shot tasks: BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), RTE (Wang, 2018), HellaSwag (Zellers
312
      et al., 2019), WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021), ARC Easy and Challenge (Clark et al., 2018),
      and OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), following the experimental setup introduced by Sun et al.
313
      (2024) in their work on Wanda. This allows for a more task-specific evaluation of the pruned model’s
314
      generalization capabilities. For SAM, we validate the performance using the fourth compressed file
315   of the SA-1B dataset as well as the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014), employing Intersection over
316   Union (IoU) as the primary metric for visual models.

530   5           C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose an efficient pruning method, LAMP, which is effective for both vision and
533
      language models while consuming limited GPU memory. The LAMP method fine-tunes each block
534   individually by compensating for the inter-block errors introduced by pruning, then rearranges the
535   sparsity distribution based on the prior knowledge of the Transformer structure and pruning method.
536   Additionally, system memory is utilized to offload the GPU memory burden of storing intermedi-
537   ate results. The proposed method significantly improves the performance of models pruned at high
538   sparsity levels without increasing GPU memory consumption. We validate the LAMP’s effective-
539   ness across vision and language models of varying scales, achieving state-of-the-art performance in
      both domains.
