                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               The composition of training data mixtures is critical for effectively training large
010
               language models (LLMs), as it directly impacts their performance on downstream
011            tasks. Our goal is to identify an optimal data mixture to specialize an LLM for a
012            specific task with access to only a few examples. Traditional approaches to this
013            problem include ad-hoc reweighting methods, importance sampling, and gradient
014            alignment techniques. This paper focuses on gradient alignment and introduces
015            Dynamic Gradient Alignment (DGA), a scalable online gradient alignment algo-
016            rithm. DGA dynamically estimates the pre-training data mixture on which the
017            models’ gradients align as well as possible with those of the model on the specific
018            task. DGA is the first gradient alignment approach that incurs minimal overhead
019
               compared to standard pre-training and outputs a competitive model, eliminating the
               need for retraining the model. Experimentally, we demonstrate significant improve-
020
               ments over importance sampling in two key scenarios: (i) when the pre-training set
021
               is small and importance sampling overfits due to limited data; and (ii) when there
022            is insufficient specialized data, trapping importance sampling on narrow pockets of
023            data. Our findings underscore the effectiveness of gradient alignment methods in
024            optimizing training data mixtures, particularly in data-constrained environments,
025            and offer a practical solution for enhancing LLM performance on specific tasks
026            with limited data availability.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) are typically pre-trained on extensive, generic corpora sourced from
031   a variety of data domains (Brown et al., 2020b; Touvron et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022), with the
032   composition of these corpora often depending on domain availability or heuristics (Gao et al., 2020;
033   Together AI Team, 2023). While the diversity of natural texts allows the model to learn from various
034   knowledge sources, not all data domains are equally beneficial according to the targeted tasks. The
035   uncurated nature of web-crawled contents could lead to sub-optimal outcomes due to the variations
036   in data quality (Longpre et al., 2023). Plus, some domains may contain misinformation and biases, as
037   one potential source of hallucinations in language generation (Lin et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023).
038   To better generalize to the downstream target tasks, it is critical to identify the most beneficial
039   subset from large, generic pretraining corpora. While sample-level selection can be costly, domain
040   reweighting offers an efficient group-level approach. Domain reweighting methods assume that
041   samples from the same domain share similar features and search for optimal sampling weights across
042   domains (Xie et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024; Grangier et al., 2024).
043   The domains that most positively impact the target tasks should be assigned higher weights.
044   In this work, on top of a large, generic pretraining corpus, we assume we have access to a few
045   examples representative of the downstream task on which we want the model to generalize, a so-
046   called specialized set. For this setup, Grangier et al. (2024) recently proposed a simple and scalable
047   importance sampling based method to domain reweighting, where the weight of a domain is given by
048   the frequency of samples in the specialized set closest to the domain, where distance is measured
049   with SentenceBert (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) embeddings. This method determines the domain
050
      weights before any training and is model-agnostic.
051   Likewise, prior gradient-alignment methods determine a static domain weights for large-scale LM
052   training, often relying on a small-scale proxy model (Xie et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2024) or fitting
053   a scaling law (Liu et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024). While these methods show improvements over
      training on the natural distribution of a generic corpus, they do not dynamically update domain

054
      weights during training to adapt to the current model state. In practical training scenarios, a large
055   model may quickly overfit on certain domains with high weights. In such cases, an online weighting
056   method can respond by shifting emphasis to other domains.
057
058
      We propose Dynamic Gradient-Alignment (DGA), an online domain reweighting method that
      estimates step-wise optimal domain weights during model training. Inspired by D O GE (Fan et al.,
059
      2024), at each reweighting step, DGA upweights the data domain whose gradient aligns more with
060
      the model’s gradient on the specific set. From the optimization perspective, training the model on
061   the most-aligned data domain yields the greatest reduction in the targeted loss. By incorporating
062   an exponential-moving-average (EMA) term in online domain weights updates, DGA effectively
063   mitigates overfitting and prioritizes the domains that currently benefit the target task the most. Since
064   the domain weights and model parameters are updated concurrently, inaccurate domain weights can
065   potentially drive the model into suboptimal states, which further leads to snow-balled errors. In
066   such cases, the EMA term serves as a correction factor, guiding the model back to a more stable
067   state. As an additional contribution, we scale the domain reweighting methods into extremely fine-
068   grained domains (e.g. 262k domains) by introducing a novel distribution reweighting mechanism.
069
      Rather than directly reweighting 262k data domains, distribution reweighting reparameterizes the
      high-dimensional domain weights as a convex combination of weight vectors derived from a set of
070
      distributions estimated from embedding-based importance sampling (Grangier et al., 2024). With
071
      the number of distributions less than the number of training data domains, it allows DGA to scale to
072   thousands of domains and make the most of the fine-grained group-level features.
073
074   Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of DGA compared to standard pre-training and
075
      importance sampling baselines in two challenging cases: (1) the resource of training tokens in each
      domain is limited instead of infinite (§ 3.1), and (2) the domain granularity is extremely large, which
076
      introduces intractable computation overheads on the domain reweighting problem (§ 3.2).

112
113   Since the lack of data forbids optimizing directly Lspe , we look for the mixture α such that optimizing
114   the generic loss Lgen (θ, α) yields the smallest specific loss (Grangier et al., 2023). This is formalized
115   by the following bilevel optimization problem (Bracken & McGill, 1973; Dagréou et al., 2022):
116
                      α⋆ ∈ arg min Lspe (θ ⋆ (α)), such that θ ⋆ (α) ∈ arg min Lgen (θ, α)                   (3)
117                           α∈∆k                                            θ

      This bilevel formulation is intuitive: for a given weight α, the parameters obtained by minimizing the
119
      generic loss Lgen are θ ∗ (α), and we want those weights to yield a small specific loss. Notably, if
120
      the specific loss is a mixture of generic data with an unknown weight α̃, the bilevel formulation is
121   guaranteed to recover it. In other words:
122
      Theorem 1. Assume that there exists α̃ such that Dspe = mix(α̃) . Then, α̃ is a solution to the
123
      bilevel problem in Equation 3.

125   Proof. We let θ̃ the minimizer of Lspe . Then, for all α, we have by definition that Lspe (θ ∗ (α)) ≥
126   Lspe (θ̃). Furthermore, since Dspe = mix(α̃), we have that Lgen (θ, α̃) = Lspe (θ) for all θ, hence
127   minimizing this yields θ ∗ (α) = θ̃. Putting these results together, we have proven that for all α, it
128   holds Lspe (θ ∗ (α)) ≥ Lspe (θ ∗ (α̃)), so that α̃ is a solution to Equation 3.
129
130   We consider two types of methods to solve Equation 3. Static methods construct a single mixture
131   weight vector α and then minimize Lgen (θ, α); we describe in the next section how to obtain this
132   vector α. Online methods modify the weights dynamically during model training. They produce a
133   sequence of weights α(t) where t is the optimization iterate. In that case, at each training step, the
134   parameters θ (t) are updated by doing an optimization step — with gradient descent or Adam — on
135   the function Lgen (θ, α(t) ). We now discuss methods to obtain a weight vector α or a sequence α(t) .

198   Computational cost and memory overhead. The computation cost of DGA is compared to the cost
199   of a regular pre-training run. For a base run iteration, the main cost is tg , the cost of computing a
200   gradient with a mini-batch B. For DGA, we need to add the cost of updating the domain weights α,
201   which only happens every Tr iterations. This update requires computing the k + 1 gradients (one
202   per domain, one for Lspe ). Hence the average cost of one iteration of DGA is (1 + (k + 1)Tr−1 )tg .
203   Therefore, DGA’s compute overhead is small when Tr is large compared to the number of domains k.
204   During training, the memory is used by the optimizer state, the model gradients and its activations.
205   We assume the same precision for storing all vectors. The optimizer state (the model parameters and
206   the two EMA terms for Adam) and the gradients have a storage cost of 4mg , where mg denotes the
207   cost of storing the model parameters. The cost of storing the activations during backpropagation is
208   mb . Regular pretraining with Adam therefore costs 4mg + mb . DGA computes the required gradients
209   sequentially and does not require more memory to store activations. It simultaneously stores two
210
      gradients instead of one (one domain gradient and one specific gradient). DGA, therefore, costs
      5mg + mb : DGA memory overhead ranges from 0 (when mb ≫ mg ) to 25% (when mg ≫ mb ).

259   Our experiments focus on two challenging cases. First, given limited token resources within each
260   training domain, the model would risk overfitting with weights concentrated on a few domains.
261   Second, given large number of training domains, applying DGA on domain reweighting could
262   introduce intractable computation overheads linearly increasing according to the domain granularity.

297      Previous works on domain reweighting implicitly assume infinite token resources from all training
298      domains (Xie et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) while it is not always applicable in
299      real-world cases. The scenario with limited training resources is challenging for online domain
300      reweighting. Indeed, if the weights are concentrated on a few domains, e.g. on a single domain Di , a
301      large model will quickly overfit when the number of tokens in Di is small.
302     We expect DGA to mitigate overfitting by dynamically adjusting the domain weights. Specifically,
303     once a model starts overfitting on Di , the magnitude of the gradients ∇ℓ(θ, Di ) decreases as its
304     training loss ℓ(θ, Di ) is low, i.e. the domain knowledge from Di is well-learned. Consequently, the
305     corresponding gradient alignment score ai = ⟨∇ℓ(θ, Di ), ∇ℓ(θ, Dspe )⟩ decreases as well and DGA
306     explores other domains with higher alignment scores. In other words, DGA down-weights domains
307     once they are well-learned, thereby achieving a balance between exploration – by learning from
308
        diverse data domains – and exploitation, by intensively training on the most relevant domains.
309      However, with limited data per domain, we remark that DGA without EMA demonstrates drastic
310      changes at each domain weight update, focusing heavily on one domain at a time. Quickly changing
311      domain weights is problematic since we want to use the same domain weights for Tr steps in the
312      future. This motivates the introduction of the EMA update in Algorithm 1, which regularizes the
313      model and domain weights with the previous state when it starts to overfit.

327   suggesting a lack of robustness. Under a 0.1B token constraint, both DGA and DGA with EMA are
328   able to dynamically adjust domain weights to mitigate overfitting. In contrast, fixed domain weights
329   from importance sampling consistently lead to overfitting in token-limited scenarios, demonstrating
330   the limitations of static weighting strategies in comparison to dynamic approaches like DGA. It is
331   worth noting that adding the EMA has no negative effect on the learning efficacy when there is no
332   token limit, which can be used as a robust regularization in the online domain reweighting context.
333   Domain Weights Evolution. In the experiments with a limited generic token budget (subsection 3.1),
334   DGA without EMA often assigns excessive weight to one generic domain, leading to overfitting
335   due to the restricted number of training tokens. This iterative over-weighting pattern on generic
336   domain weights aligns with the observed loss spikes on the specific set (Figure 3a). In contrast, the
337   EMA helps to regularize the weight dynamics, effectively preventing the model from overfitting by

      The computational overhead from DGA scales linearly with the number of domain k. This is
343
      intractable for datasets segmented in many fine-grained domains and, consequently, prior domain
344   reweighting methods (Xie et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024) have
345   not been applied in that setting. The fine-grained setting motivates distribution reweighting as an
346   alternative to direct domain reweighting.
347
      Distribution reweighting leverages the strength from both embedding-based (importance sampling)
348
      and gradient-based (DGA) strategies. We consider a generic training set partitioned into k domains
349
      with a large k (e.g. 4096, 262k). We also have a set of N auxiliary datasets {S1 , . . . , SN }, called
350   basis sets, each from a specific domain of interest. We compute the importance sampling histograms
351   for each basis set as P = {p1 , . . . , pN }, pi ∈ ∆k , P ∈ Rk×N . We then use DGA to search over
352   a reparameterized space leveraging this basis. We define the domain weights αdomain ∈ ∆k as a
353   convex combination of N k−dimensional distributions derived from importance sampling,
354
                     αdomain ≈ P αdist = αdist,1 · p1 + αdist,2 · p2 + . . . + αdist,N · pN              (8)
355
356   where the low-dimensional weights αdist ∈ ∆N are learned by DGA. This allows the use of fine-
357   grained domain features while eliminating intensive gradient computation on each generic domain.
358   Compared to the (k + 1)/Tr overheads from domain reweighting, applying distribution reweighting
359   only incurs (N + 1)/Tr extra budget, where N is typically much smaller than k. Importantly, this is
360   equivalent to applying DGA with the N generic domains D̃1 , . . . , D̃N where D̃i = mix(pi ). Hence,
361   it does not require any modification to the base DGA algorithm; it suffices to be able to sample
362
      according to each mix(pi ). We provide the pseudo-code for the distribution reweighting with DGA
      in Appendix D.

      6    C ONCLUSION
529   To tackle two key challenges of online domain reweighting, we introduce Dynamic Gradient Align-
530   ment (DGA) as a stable and scalable data mixing method for LLM pretraining. Given a target
531   task, DGA is an online algorithm that adjusts the training data distribution according to the current
532   model status. This adaptation relies on an estimate of the progress on the target task from gradient
533   alignments. We show that under limited tokens within generic domains, DGA with EMA can notably
534
      mitigate overfitting and yields superior performance on the end-task by balancing exploitation and
      exploration. We also propose a novel distribution reweighting strategy, which enables DGA to scale
535
      up to extremely fine-grained data domains without incurring intensive computations. Our experiments
536
      on MMLU show that applying distribution reweighting with DGA effectively leverages fine-grained
537   domain knowledge to balance specialty and diversity during training. Our work demonstrates the
538   scalability of gradient-alignment-based data reweighting methods, as well as their efficiency in
539   data-constrained settings.

978    Despite the superior performance on language modeling, DGA can hardly outperform importance
979
       sampling in terms of accuracy scores on MMLU benchmark (Table 1), which indicates the improvement
       on language modeling ability may not be able to fully translated to reasoning capacities. For the
980
       limited improvement on accuracy, we provide two potential explanations: (1) since the target objective
981
       in the outerloop (Lspe in Equ. 3) is the next token prediction loss for language modeling, the language
982    modeling performance is expected to be improved. However, it does not necessarily translate to the
983    improved reasoning accuracy (2) MMLU is considered to be a challenging reasoning task, where the
984    accuracies can hardly be improved when the model capacity is below some specific threshold. This
985    phenomenon is well-illustrated by Figure 11. (G) in (Wei et al., 2022). How to derive an optimization
986    objective which directly benefits the reasoning accuracy would be a compelling future direction.

         F              C OMPARISON BETWEEN DGA AND D O GE
1243
1244
        We compare DGA with DoGE (Fan et al., 2024) in the context of task-adaptive training. Specifically,
1245    we use the RedPajama-v2 dataset clustered into k=64 domains as the generic dataset, and Arxiv
1246    from The Pile as the specialized domain.
1247
1248
        Following the methodology outlined by Fan et al. (2024), we implement a two-stage pipeline for
        domain-reweighted pretraining. First, we optimize the domain weights α on a proxy model. Next, we
1249
        compute the average domain weights over all time steps and use them as fixed sampling weights to
1250
        train the base model. For this setup, we train proxy models at two scales (31M and 125M parameters)
1251    and a base model at 125M parameters. For DGA, we follow Algorithm 1 to train the 125M base
1252    model. Unlike DoGE, DGA updates the domain weights dynamically in an online manner, eliminating
1253    the need for a separate proxy model or precomputed fixed sampling weights.
1254
        We report both the specialized loss on Arxiv over time (measured in GPT hours) and the validation
1255
        loss on RedPajama-v2, which reflects the retention of general knowledge from the generic dataset.
1256
        To evaluate the performance of DoGE as both an online and offline domain reweighting method, we
1257    present results from both the proxy model and the base model. All the models are trained with 1 ×
1258    Nvidia A100 GPU.
1259
1260
        According to Figure 15 (a), DGA greatly outperforms online DoGE (125M proxy model) and achieves
        comparable specialized loss as the offline DoGE (125M model trained w. 31M/125M Doge weights).
1261
        However, as an online algorithm, DGA does not require any proxies, which significantly outperform
1262
        DoGE in terms of efficiency. In addition, as shown in Figure 15 (b), DGA reaches a lower loss
1263    on RedPajama than both online and offline DoGE. It indicates that DoGE is more vulnerable from
1264    catastrophic forgetting compared to DGA.

       G.3   A BLATION ON H YPER - PARAMETERS
1405
1406   Ablations on step size η and update frequency Tr . We conducted the ablation experiments on
1407   both step size η and frequency Tr used for updating domain weights α, as described in Algorithm 1.
1408   According to Figure 18, using a step size that is too small will result in slow updates to the domain
1409   weights. On the other hand, applying a large step size (η) can accelerate the learning of domain
1410   weights but may also lead to training instability due to overly up-weighted domains. Since EMA can
1411   effectively stabilize learning, we recommend that practitioners choose η values between 0.1 and 0.5.
1412   Regarding the reweight frequency, using a smaller Tr generally improves the final specialized loss but
1413   increases computation costs. To balance cost and performance, we recommend setting Tr between
1414   30 and 100. However, these values should be tailored to specific use cases and levels of domain
1415   granularity.
