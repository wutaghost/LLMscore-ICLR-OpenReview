009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we introduce DiffusionTrend, a pioneering approach for virtual
012            fashion try-on that forgoes the need for training diffusion models, thereby offer-
013            ing simple, conventional pose virtual try-on services with significantly reduced
014            computational overhead. By leveraging advanced diffusion models, Diffusion-
015            Trend harnesses latents rich with prior information to capture the nuances of
016
               garment details. Throughout the diffusion denoising process, these details are
               seamlessly integrated into the model image generation, expertly directed by a
017
               precise garment mask crafted by a lightweight and compact CNN. Although our
018
               DiffusionTrend model initially demonstrates suboptimal metric performance, our
019            exploratory approach offers several significant advantages: (1) It circumvents the
020            need for resource-intensive training of diffusion models on large datasets. (2) It
021            eliminates the necessity for various complex and user-unfriendly model inputs.
022            (3) It delivers a visually compelling virtual try-on experience, underscoring the
023            potential of training-free diffusion models for future research within the commu-
024            nity. Overall, this initial foray into the application of untrained diffusion models
025            in virtual try-on technology potentially paves the way for further exploration and
026            refinement in this industrially and academically valuable field.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Virtual try-on technology (Song et al., 2023; Islam et al., 2024), which digitally superimposes
031   images of models wearing various outfits, represents a pivotal innovation in the fashion sector. This
032   advancement offers consumers an immersive and interactive experience with garments, allowing
033   them to preview how clothing might look on them without physically visiting stores. This not only
034   bolsters consumer confidence in their purchasing decisions but also conserves considerable time and
035   effort. The shopping experience might be elevated due to convenience and efficiency. For retailers,
036   virtual try-on technology streamlines their operations by obviating the need to employ live models
037   for merchandise display. It also circumvents the financial burdens associated with traditional product
038   photography, leading to a marked improvement in operational efficiency. From the perspective of
039
      commercial platforms, virtual try-on technology serves as a magnet for attracting a larger user base
      and fostering user loyalty. Moreover, it facilitates accurate market forecasting and trend analysis
040
      through data-driven insights, which in turn, stimulates the commercial growth of the entire platform.
041
      Collectively, virtual try-on is reshaping the landscape of the apparel retail industry, propelling the
042   fashion sector towards a future characterized by heightened efficiency and customization.
043
044   Traditional virtual try-on solutions (Choi et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2023)
045
      are predicated on a two-stage pipeline utilizing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
      et al., 2014). The initial stage in this framework involves the application of an explicit warp module
046
      to deform the clothing to the desired area on the body. The subsequent stage integrates the deformed
047
      clothing using a GAN-based try-on generator. To attain accurate clothing deformation, earlier
048   methodologies (Bai et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2021; Han et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023)
049   have employed a trainable network designed to estimate a dense flow map (Zhou et al., 2016), thereby
050   facilitating the mapping of the clothing onto the human form. In parallel, various approaches (Choi
051   et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2020; Issenhuth et al.,
052   2020), have been proposed to address the misalignment issues between the warped clothing and the
053   human body. Techniques such as normalization (Choi et al., 2021) and distillation (Ge et al., 2021;
      Issenhuth et al., 2020) have been implemented to mitigate these discrepancies. Recent advancements

070   in the field of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) have led to a notable enhancement in the quality of

072   context, contemporary research endeavors have leveraged pre-trained text-to-image diffusion models
073   to produce high-fidelity results for virtual try-on applications. The TryOnDiffusion model (Zhu et al.,
074   2023), for instance, employs a dual U-Net architecture to adeptly perform the try-on task. LADI-
075   VTON (Morelli et al., 2023) and DCI-VTON (Gou et al., 2023) either conceptualize clothing items
076
      as pseudo-words or integrate garments through the use of warping networks into existing pre-trained
      diffusion models. These innovative techniques have collectively contributed to the evolution of virtual
077
      try-on technology, offering more sophisticated and realistic outcomes.
078
079   Despite these advancements, a review of existing studies reveals that the high precision and realism
080   achieved by current methods require training on extensive try-on datasets (Morelli et al., 2022), partic-
081   ularly in diffusion-based approaches (Zhu et al., 2023; Morelli et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023). Indeed,
082
      diffusion models contain up to 800 million parameters and simulate the data generation process
      by iteratively modeling the diffusion and reverse diffusion steps. Each iteration involves complex
083
      probability distribution calculations and requires substantial computational resources, leading to
084
      considerable overall training costs. In the words, current methods rely on resource-intensive network
085   training. While intensive training is well-suited for generating complex poses and capturing fine
086   image details, the substantial computational costs can make it an unattractive option. This is particu-
087   larly true since, in most cases, consumers make purchase decisions after a simple try-on with regular
088   poses. Besides, as shown in Table 1, these methods often demand multiple types of model inputs,
089   such as densepose (Güler et al., 2018), segmentation maps (Gong et al., 2017), clothes-agnostic
090   images/masks/representations (Han et al., 2018) and keypoints (Cao et al., 2017), which can be
091   daunting for non-professional users. It becomes clear that there is a pressing need for the community
092   to explore more accessible and less resource-intensive methods, with the effort of less compromise
093
      on the quality of image generation—a domain that, to our knowledge, remains uncharted to date.
094   In marked contrast, our proposed DiffusionTrend offers a streamlined, lightweight training approach
095   that circumvents the need to train a diffusion model, thus liberating it from the reliance on expen-
096   sive and resource-intensive computational infrastructure. This technique simplifies the workflow
097   by dispensing with the requirement for intricate segmentation, pose extraction, and other prelimi-
098   nary processing steps for the input images, leading to a more accessible and economical solution.
099
      Specifically, a lightweight, compact CNN is utilized to outline the clothing in both the model and
      garment images. It harmonizes image and textual features and conducts clustering at the feature
100
      level to produce effective masks. Subsequently, we make full use of the latents derived from DDIM
101
      inversion (Song et al., 2020), which are replete with prior information and act as superior conduits
102   for the detailed features of the garment. During the early stages of the diffusion denoising process,
103   the seamless integration of the target garment into the model’s image reconstruction is achieved by
104   blending the latent representations of both the model and the garment. In the subsequent stages of
105   denoising, leveraging the self-repairing capabilities of the pre-trained latent diffusion model, we
106   maintain the model’s identity and background coherence by selectively replacing the latents in the
107   background areas. This approach ensures that the garment is harmoniously merged into the overall
      image, preserving the integrity of the original scene. Figure 1 shows several examples.

135   2.1   I MAGE E DITING THROUGH D IFFUSION P ROCESSES
136
137   The practice of integrating specific content into a base image to produce realistic composites is preva-
138   lent in image editing leveraging diffusion processes. Initially, the field was dominated by text-based
139
      models for image editing (Brooks et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023). InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al.,
      2023) employed paired data to train diffusion models capable of generating an edited image from an
140
      input image and a textual instruction. Conversely, Imagic (Kawar et al., 2023) harnessed a pre-trained
141
      text-to-image diffusion model to generate text embeddings that align with both the input image and
142   the target text. The abstract nature of text poses a limitation in accurately delineating the subtleties of
143   objects, therefore, image conditioning was introduced to offer more concrete and precise descriptions.
144   DCFF (Xue et al., 2022) pioneered the use of pyramid filters for image composition, which was
145   subsequently advanced by Paint by Example (Yang et al., 2023), employing CLIP embeddings of
146   the reference image to condition the diffusion model. The majority of contemporary methodologies,
147   such as Dreambooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) (all model parameters), Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022)
148   (a word vector for novel concepts), and Custom-Diffusion (Kumari et al., 2023) (cross-attention
149   parameters), relied heavily on fine-tuning techniques. In contrast, a handful of approaches (Hertz
150
      et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023) adopted a training-free paradigm. Prompt-to-prompt (Hertz et al.,
      2022) modified the input text prompt to steer the cross-attention mechanism for nuanced image
151
      editing, while MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023) employed a mask-guided mutual attention strategy for
152
      non-rigid image synthesis and editing. These training-free methods offer a cost-effective alternative,
153   eliminating extensive training while still delivering commendable generative outcomes.

      2.2   V IRTUAL T RY- ON WITH D IFFUSION M ODELS
156
157   Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in the domain of image editing, with
158   image-based virtual try-on representing a specialized subset of these tasks, contingent upon a specific
159   garment image. Adapting text-to-image diffusion models to accommodate images as conditions, is
160   straightforward, but the spatial discrepancies between the garment and the subject’s pose challenge
161   the fidelity of texture details in the virtual try-on outcomes (Li et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Morelli
      et al., 2023). Methodologies such as WarpDiffusion (Li et al., 2023), DCI-VTON (Gou et al., 2023),


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      and LADI-VTON (Morelli et al., 2023) conceptualize clothing as pseudo-words, employing warping
163   techniques through CNN networks to adjust clothing to various poses, yielding satisfactory results.
164   TryOnDiffusion (Zhu et al., 2023) employs a dual U-Net architecture for the virtual try-on task,
165   implicitly conducting garment warping through the interplay between cross-attention layers. This
166   approach effectively resolves the issue of texture misalignment without the need for a dedicated
167   warp module. Similarly, the StableVITON (Kim et al., 2023) incorporates zero cross-attention
168   blocks to condition the intermediate feature maps of a spatial encoder, thereby circumventing the
169   requirement for a warp module. The Wear-Any-Way (Chen et al., 2024) enhances the process of
170   virtual garment alteration, providing more adaptable control over the manner in which clothing is
171
      depicted. The IDM-VTON (Choi et al., 2024) enhances the virtual try-on process by integrating
      attention mechanisms and high-level semantic encoding into the diffusion model framework, ensuring
172
      an authentic clothing representation in virtual environments.

301   3.3      V IRTUAL T RY-O N I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
302
303   Initially, we adhered to MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023), using a reference U-Net to extract garment
304   features and facilitating key-value exchange with the main U-Net during the attention stage, all
305   without the need for additional training. Despite employing a dual-branch U-Net and attempting
306
      to guide the attention interaction with masks derived from our apparel localization network, the
      outcomes were less than satisfactory. This was especially true when it came to the accurate rendering
307
      of fine-grained garment details, as depicted in Figure 2(b). We surmise that this direct feature injection,
308
      while adept at generating content at the semantic level, falls short in capturing the fine-grained clothing
309   details essential for virtual try-on applications.
310
311   Moreover, recent studies (Choi et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) indicate that employing U-Net for feature
312
      extraction necessitates treating it as a high-parameter module, which entails considerable training
      expenses. This is counterintuitive to our objective of achieving a solution that does not require
313
      training of the diffusion model. Consequently, we have been refining our methodology iteratively. As
314
      illustrated in Figure 3(c), our virtual try-on image reconstruction process encompasses two critical
315   stages: the infusion of garment latents and the restoration of the background.
316
317   Garment Latent Infusion. Given a model image I m and a garment image I g , we utilize the
318
      apparel localization network from Sec. 3.2 to acquire the masks Mm and Mg , respectively. Next, we
      compute the bounding boxes B m and B g for Mm and Mg , respectively. By reshaping the contents
319
      of B g to the size of B m and applying appropriate perspective transformations to simulate image
320
      rotation, we align the target garment with the position and size of the clothing in the model image.
321
322   The model image I m and the aligned garment image I g are then transformed into the latent repre-
323   sentations z0m = E(I m ) and z0g = E(I g ). These latents are then subjected to the DDIM Inversion
      process as outlined in Eq. (3), yielding noisy latent sets {zt∗m }Tt=0 and {zt∗g }Tt=0 .

      generated try-on images remain realistic and natural under various complex poses, as confirmed by
      evaluation metrics. In contrast, DiffusionTrend aims to provide a resource-efficient, user-friendly

      tool for quickly confirming purchase intentions under simple try-on poses. Without training the
412
      diffusion model on large datasets, our basic operations fall short in handling complex poses, leading

510   5    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
511
512   Our DiffusionTrend model encounters several challenges: (1) The refinement in clothing generation
513   is not always optimal, occasionally leading to minor color variations and the presence of unnatural
514   textures and patterns; (2) It encounters difficulties in generating complex poses, especially in rendering
515   body parts that are not visible in the original model image, due to limitations inherent in the pre-
516   trained model. For instance, if the original model is depicted wearing a long-sleeved shirt and the
517
      target garment is a short-sleeved one, the model is unable to convincingly render the exposed arms.
518   These limitations suggest that future research should prioritize enhancing the recovery of fine-
519   grained details in clothing and strengthening the model’s ability to generate previously unseen body
520   parts. Moreover, further exploration into managing more complex poses and refining the quality of
521   background restoration are areas that merit deeper investigation.
522   Despite these shortcomings, DiffusionTrend offers a low-cost, lightweight paradigm for the virtual
523   try-on field that circumvents the need for extensive diffusion model training. Optimism remains high
524   that this approach will continue to evolve as the field advances. We respectfully ask the academic
525   community to recognize the value of this exploratory work and extend the necessary patience and
526   support for its further development.

      6    C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we have introduced DiffusionTrend, a novel try-on methodology that forgoes the need
      for training diffusion models, thereby offering straightforward, conventional pose virtual try-on
532
      services with minimal computational demands. Capitalizing on sophisticated diffusion models,
533
      DiffusionTrend harnesses latents brimming with prior information to encapsulate the nuances of
534   garment details. Throughout the diffusion denoising process, these details are effortlessly merged into
535   the model image generation, expertly directed by a precise garment mask generated by a lightweight
536   and compact CNN. Differing from other approaches, DiffusionTrend sidesteps the necessity for
537   labor-intensive training of diffusion models on extensive datasets. It also dispenses the need for
538   various types of user-unfriendly model inputs. Our experiments demonstrate that, despite lower
539   metric performance, DiffusionTrend delivers a visually convincing virtual try-on experience, all while
      maintaining the quality and richness of fashion presentation.
