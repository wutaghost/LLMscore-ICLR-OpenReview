009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Extreme multi-label classification (XMC) seeks to find relevant labels from an
012            extremely large label collection for a given text input. To tackle such a vast
013            label space, current state-of-the-art methods fall into two categories. The one-
014            versus-all (OVA) method uses learnable label embeddings for each label, excelling
015            at memorization (i.e., capturing detailed training signals for accurate head label
016
               prediction). In contrast, the dual-encoder (DE) model maps input and label text into
               a shared embedding space for better generalization (i.e., the capability of predicting
017
               tail labels with limited training data), but may fall short at memorization. To achieve
018
               generalization and memorization, existing XMC methods often combine DE and
019            OVA models, which involves complex training pipelines. Inspired by the success
020            of retrieval-augmented language models, we propose the Retrieval-augmented
021            Encoders for XMC (RAE-XMC), a novel framework that equips a DE model
022            with retrieval-augmented capability for efficient memorization without additional
023            trainable parameter. During training, RAE-XMC is optimized by the contrastive
024            loss over a knowledge memory that consists of both input instances and labels.
025            During inference, given a test input, RAE-XMC retrieves the top-K keys from
026            the knowledge memory, and aggregates the corresponding values as the prediction
027
               scores. We showcase the effectiveness and efficiency of RAE-XMC on four public
               LF-XMC benchmarks. RAE-XMC not only advances the state-of-the-art (SOTA)
028
               method DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024), but also achieves more than 10x speedup on
029
               the largest LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M dataset under the same 8 A100 GPUs training
030            environments. Our experiment code is available in the Supplementary Material.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Many real-world applications, such as e-commerce dynamic search advertising (Prabhu et al., 2018)
035
      and semantic matching in product search (Chang et al., 2021), can be formulated as eXtreme Multi-
036   label Classification (XMC) problems. These tasks involve retrieving relevant labels from a label
037   collection of extremely large size L, typically under a tight latency budget. In such applications,
038   L can be millions or more, and the label space is often long-tailed, posing a significant challenge
039   in designing XMC methods that are good at labels with varying frequencies (Dahiya et al., 2023a;
040   Gupta et al., 2024). For head labels, it necessitates methods capable of memorization, which involves
041   capturing all the detailed knowledge needed for accurate prediction. For tail labels, it requires
042   generalization, as the model needs to capture general label representations that can be used to predict
043   labels with few training instances.
044   Traditional one-versus-all (OVA) methods prioritize memorization via trainable label classifiers, yet
045   struggle to generalize effectively across tail labels (Gupta et al., 2024). Recently, the emergence of
046   pre-trained language models (LMs) has demonstrated strong generalization power, prompting the
047   adoption of dual-encoder (DE) architectures to effectively leverage label text for zero-shot XMC
048   tasks (Gupta et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2022; Aggarwal et al., 2023). However, without extensive
049   training, these models may not excel at memorization, suggesting a pure DE model is not sufficient
050
      to achieve good performance at head labels of XMC tasks.
051   To achieve both memorization and generalization power, competitive XMC approaches, such as
052   NGAMEDE+OVA (Dahiya et al., 2023a) and DEXADE+OVA (Dahiya et al., 2023b), usually follow a
053   complex two-stage solution. They first train a DE model that learns semantic embeddings between
      input and label text, which can generalize to unseen input queries and tail labels with text features.

054
      Then the DE model is further augmented by trainable one-versus-all (OVA) label classifiers, which
055   enhance the memorization capability of head labels with diverse intent of queries. Recently, the
056   pioneering work, DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024), achieved SOTA results on several XMC benchmark
057   datasets via a pure DE model, which significantly reduces the amount of trainable model parameters.
058   While being a strong predictive model, DEXML requires extreme long training time to memorize the
059   training signals of head labels, making it challenging to be applied to industrial applications.
060
      In this paper, we introduce Retrieval-augmented Encoders for XMC (RAE-XMC), a novel framework
061   that equips a dual-encoder (DE) model with the retrieval-augmented capability. RAE-XMC com-
062   pensates for the poor memorization capabilities of DE and eliminates the need for precise matching
063   between input instance and label embedding spaces, thereby significantly reducing the training
064   difficulty of XMC. Specifically, RAE-XMC trains the DE model to contrast over a joint knowledge
065   memory space that consists of label descriptions and input instances. During the inference, given a
066   test instance, RAE-XMC first retrieves the top b keys from the knowledge memory. RAE-XMC then
067   generates predictions by aggregating the values (i.e., labels) of these keys based on their scores. The
068   contributions of this paper are threefold as follows:
069          • We introduce RAE-XMC, a novel retrieval-augmented framework for XMC problems,
070            which enhances the underlying DE model with better memorization capability. Specifically,
071            RAE-XMC offers controllable trade-off between memorization and generalization, namely
072            the performance of head and tail label segments, respectively (cf., Section 5.3).
073          • RAE-XMC significantly reduces the training difficulty for XMC problems in two ways.
074            Firstly, it eliminates the need for training OVA label classifiers to memorize intricate complex
075            patterns in head labels. Secondly, it alleviates meticulously training of the DE model to
076            match label and input instance spaces (cf., Section 5.6).
077          • We conducted extensive experiments to showcase the effectiveness and efficiency of
078            RAE-XMC. Our proposed approach not only advances the current SOTA results from
079            DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024), but also comes with significantly less training time, achieving
080            more than 10x speedup on the largest LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M dataset (cf., Section 5.2).

      2    BACKGROUND M ATERIAL
082
083   We study the eXtreme Multi-label Text Classification (XMC) problem with label text. Consider
084   a training set of N examples D = {(xi , yi )}N   i=1 , where xi ∈ X is the i-th input instance and
085   yi ∈ {0, 1}L is the one-hot label vector with yi,ℓ = 1 indicating that the label ℓ is relevant to the
086   input instance xi . Depending on the availability of label text, ℓ denotes either a label index or label
087   text, respectively. The label space L consists of |L| = L labels. The goal of XMC is to learn a
088   scoring function sθ : X × L → R, parameterized by model parameters θ, such that sθ (x, ℓ) indicates
089
      the relevance between input instance x and label ℓ.
090   Model Parametrization. Based on the availability of label text features, there are two model
091   families for the XMC task: the One-versus-all (OVA) classifier and the Dual-Encoder (DE) model. The
092   OVA classifier, which ignores the label text features, consists of an input text encoder fϕ : X → Rd
093   and a classification matrix W ∈ RL×d . The scoring function of the OVA classifier then becomes
094   sθ (x, ℓ) = ⟨x, wℓ ⟩ where x = fϕ (x) is the input query embedding and wℓ (the ℓth row of W) is the
095   classification vector for label ℓ. For the OVA classifier, the trainable parameter θ = [ϕ; W] scales
096   linearly to the size of label space L, which can be large and difficult to train.
097   In contrast, our paper studies the DE model, where the scoring function is defined as sθ (x, ℓ) =
098   ⟨x, zℓ ⟩ = ⟨fϕ (x), hψ (ℓ)⟩, with fϕ : X → Rd as the input text encoder and hψ : L → Rd as the label
099   text encoder. Compared to the OVA classifier, the trainable parameters of the DE model, θ = [ϕ; ψ],
100   are often much smaller since they do not scale with the size of the label space.
101
      Learning. Given a training set D, the scorer sθ can be learned by minimizing some surrogate loss
102   functions for some metrics of interest (e.g., Precision@k and Recall@k). One popular choice is
103   the one-versus-all (OVA) reduction (Dembczynski et al., 2010) to L binary classification tasks and
104   employs binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss function. On the other hand, one can employ a multi-label
105   to multi-class reduction (Menon et al., 2019) and invoke the Softmax cross-entropy loss:

      Inference. Given a test query embedding q = fϕ (q) ∈ Rd and the offline pre-computed label
114
      embedding matrix Z ∈ RL×d , we aim to retrieve the k most relevant labels from Z in real-time (low

115
      Exact inference of MIPS requires O(L) time complexity, which is prohibited for XMC tasks where L
116
      can be millions or more. Thus, practitioners leverage Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search (ANN)
117   methods to approximately solve it in time sub-linear to the size of the label space L.
118
119   Conventional XMC methods with OVA classifiers learn a tree-based (Prabhu et al., 2018; Zhang
120
      et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022) ANN index or graph-based ANN index (Liu et al., 2021; Gupta et al.,
      2022) at the training stage, and deploy it for fast retrieving top k labels in O(log L). In contrast, DE
121
      models (Dahiya et al., 2023b; Gupta et al., 2024) encode the label embedding matrix Z, and apply
122
      existing ANN solvers (e.g., Faiss (Johnson et al., 2019), ScaNN (Guo et al., 2020), HNSWLib (Malkov
123   & Yashunin, 2018)) to build the ANN index and achieve fast retrieval in O(log L) time.

      3.1   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
129
130   The performance of Dual-Encoder (DE) methods for XMC is often hindered by the inherent challenge
131   of memorization, requiring the encoding of every detail to effectively predict head labels. Inspired
132   by the recent success of retrieval-augmented language models (Khandelwal et al., 2020; Guu et al.,
133   2020; Izacard et al., 2023; Borgeaud et al., 2022),which have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in
134   leveraging external knowledge to reduce the necessity of encoding all the factual knowledge, we
135   propose the RAE-XMC framework. Rather than relying solely on the encoding capabilities of DE
136   models, which may struggle to encapsulate all relevant information, RAE-XMC integrates retrieval
137   mechanisms to augment the inference process.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
290
      Extreme Multi-label Classification. Conventional XMC methods finetune Transformer encoders
291
      and learn one-versus-all (OVA) label classifiers for the XMC tasks (Chang et al., 2020b; Zhang
292   et al., 2021; Kharbanda et al., 2022). To tackle the large output space challenge, various label space
293   partitioning techniques or surrogate loss functions have been extensively studied (Prabhu et al., 2018;
294   Jain et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022). However, these approaches treat labels as featureless identifiers and
295   learn classification vector for each label, which may not generalize to unseen labels. On the other
296   hand, there are increasing number of work leverage the label text feature by employing Dual-Encoder
297   (DE) models that learn embeddings from both input and label text (Saini et al., 2021; Mittal et al.,
298   2021; Dahiya et al., 2021; 2023a;b). However, the two representative methods in this line of work,
299   namely NGAME (Dahiya et al., 2023a) and DEXA (Dahiya et al., 2023b), do not solely rely on
300
      a DE model. Specifically, NGAME is a two-stage approach that involves first training input and
      label embeddings via a DE model and then utilizing a classification network in the second stage.
301
      DEXA builds on NGAME to improve the encoder embeddings by augmenting them with auxiliary
302
      parameters. Very recently, DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024) is a pure DE model (without learning any
303   label classifier) that achieves SOTA results by using a decoupled InfoNCE loss function. Nevertheless,
304   DEXML suffers from extremely long training time issue as it relies on gradient cache (Gao et al.,
305   2021) to have a precise estimation of the label Softmax distribution that costs O(L) per training step.
306
307   Retrieval Augmented Language Models. Using external knowledge to improve deep neural net-
308   works has been widely explored in the context of retrieval-augmented language models (Khandelwal
309   et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2023; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Shi et al.,
310   2023). For example, kNN-LM (Khandelwal et al., 2020) interpolates the next-token probability by the
311   neural language model and the kNN model at inference stage. REALM (Guu et al., 2020) and many
312   of its follow-up work (Izacard et al., 2023; Borgeaud et al., 2022) consider learning the knowledge
313
      retriever jointly with the underlying language models. While sharing similar intuition to the proposed
      RAE-XMC framework, both kNN-LM and REALM are designed for language modeling tasks, which
314
      did not consider a multi-label formulation of the knowledge source.
315
316
      Retrieval Augmented Multi-label Text Classification. Retrieval-augment techniques have also
317
      been applied to small-scale multi-label text classifications (Chalkidis & Kementchedjhieva, 2023;
318   Wang et al., 2022), where the number of labels is only a few hundreds. Specifically, Chalkidis &
319   Kementchedjhieva (2023) considers a multi-stage ranking setup: the first stage learns a text encoder
320   and OVA label classifiers to build the knowledge memory; the second stage trains an expensive
321   cross-attention encoder to produce scores given the test input and the retrieved top-K labels from the
322   first stage. Wang et al. (2022) considers training a text encoder and OVA label classifiers with the
323   contrastive loss. At inference time, they interpolates the OVA label classifier scores with the top-K
      instances’ label one-hot vector retrieved from the instance space.

      We discussed some major difference between Wang et al. (2022) and our RAE-XMC framework:
325   Regarding problem setups, they focus on small-scale multi-label problems with hundreds of labels
326   and treat labels as featureless IDs. In contrast, RAE-XMC tackles extreme-scale multi-label (XMC)
327   problems with millions of labels and leverage the label text information. Speaking of model ar-
328   chitectures, they consider OVA label classifiers where the number of trainable parameters scales
329   linearly to L, which is not scalable for XMC tasks. On the other hand, RAE-XMC is parametrized by
330   Dual-Encoders where the number of trainable parameters does not scale with the number of labels L.
331   Finally, for prediction functions, they conduct two ANN searches: one from the kNN classifier on the
332   input space and the other from the label OvA classifier on the label space, to form final prediction
333
      scores. On the contrary, RAE-XMC conducts a single ANN search on the union of input and label
      space. See empirical comparison in Table 1 and Table 4 for more details.

338
339
      Datasets and Evaluation. We conduct experiments on four LF-XMC datasets, including LF-
      AmazonTitles-131K, LF-WikiSeeAlso-320K, LF-Wikipedia-500K, and LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M,
340
      where the prefix ‘LF’ denotes the datasets contain label descriptions. Details of these datasets and its
341
      statistics are presented in Table 6 of Appendix A. We use the same raw text input and training/test
342   data splits as in (Gupta et al., 2024) to have a fair and reproducible comparison. Following the
343   evaluation setup from XMC Repository (Bhatia et al., 2016) and XMC literature (Yu et al., 2022;
344   Gupta et al., 2024; Schultheis et al., 2024b), we consider Precision (P@1 and P@5), Recall (R@100),
345   and Macro average F1 to measure the standard XMC and retrieval metrics.
346
347
348   Baselines. We compare RAE-XMC to competitive XMC baselines, which fall into two categories.
349
      The first category is dual-encoder (DE) models without learning OVA label classifiers, including
      DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020), ANCE (Xiong et al., 2021), NGAMEDE (Dahiya et al., 2023a),
350
      DEXADE (Dahiya et al., 2023b) and DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024); The second category is deep
351
      learning models with learning OVA label classifiers, such as XR-Transformer (Zhang et al., 2021)
352   NGAMEDE+OVA (Dahiya et al., 2023a), DEXADE+OVA (Dahiya et al., 2023b), and OvA+kNN (Wang
353   et al., 2022). For OvA Classifier, we apply One-versus-All Binary Cross-Entropy (OvA-BCE) as the
354   training loss, and OvA+kNN Wang et al. (2022) applies kNN prediction on top of the OvA Classifier
355   model, as described in Equation 11.
356
      RAE-XMC falls into the first category, which has much smaller model parameters to learn. Thus,
357
      we consider the state-of-the-art (SOTA) DE method, DEXML as our main baseline. To have a fair
358   comparison, we use the same 66M parameter distilbert-base transformer encoder (Sanh et al., 2020)
359   for RAE-XMC, as used in NGAMEDE , DEXADE , DEXML, OvA Classifier and OvA+kNN (Wang
360   et al., 2022). All the experiments are conducted on an AWS p4d.24xlarge instance, with 8 Nvidia
361   A100 GPUs and 96 CPUs. See Appendix A for more details on the experiment setup and hyper-
362   parameters of RAE-XMC. Our experiment code is available in the Supplementary Material.

527   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529
      We introduce the novel RAE-XMC framework for XMC problems, which enhances the DE model
530   with retrieval-augmented capability and achieves new SOTA results for large-scale LF-XMC datasets.
531   While being effective and highly efficient to train, RAE-XMC also comes with two limitations. First,
532   the knowledge memory will bring additional inference overhead, both in space and time complexity.
533   How to select representative key-value pairs to reduce the size of knowledge memory is an interesting
534   and challenging avenue for future work. In addition, the performance of RAE-XMC may be hindered
535   if there is huge discrepancy between the knowledge memory (training corpus and label space) versus
536   the test instances. How to mitigate this issue by learning data-driven λ in the value matrix is another
537   interesting direction, which we save for future work.

852   B.1   A NALYSIS OF S AMPLED K EY N UMBER
853
854   In Section 3.3, we retrieve top b keys to approximate the softmax distribution. With greater b, the
855   performance increases accordingly, but with higher inference latency as a tradeoff. In Figure 3, we
856   examine the impact of different b regarding the ratio of retrieved query number / retrieved label
857   number and the performance.
858   Regarding the ratio of retrieved query number to retrieved label number, distinct trends emerge
859   between LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M and LF-Wikipedia-500K datasets. In LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M,
860   the ratio is lower, suggesting a higher reliance on label retrieval for predictions. Moreover, in
861   LF-AmazonTitles-1.3M, the ratio tends to increase with larger values of b, whereas in LF-Wikipedia-
862   500K, it initially rises and then declines. As b increases, performance across all metrics consistently
863   improves, but with varying impacts on individual metrics. Achieving optimal performance in @k
      metrics require higher values of b as k increases.

884
      In Table 8, we examine the performance variation of using different value of λ to compare the impact
885   of using various knowledge source for inference. By gradually increasing the value of λ, the weight
886   of kNN classifier increases accordingly. Comparing λ = 0.0 and λ = 0.01/0.1, we can find that
887   even though the prediction weight of the kNN classifier is very small, it can still complement the
888   dual-encoder prediction very well, thus improving the performance.
889
      Across all datasets excluding LF-Wikipedia-500K, RAE-XMC exhibits insensitivity to λ variations.
890
      Notably, setting λ to 0.5 suffices to attain optimal performance across most datasets, suggesting an
891   equitable importance of both knowledge sources in prediction. However, when there is a distinct
892   disparity between training and testing corpora, meticulously tuning λ may be necessary (Grave et al.,
893   2017; Khandelwal et al., 2020; Chang et al., 2024).
