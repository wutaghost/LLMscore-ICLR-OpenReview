011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Providing explainable molecular property predictions is critical for many scientific
014                domains, such as drug discovery and material science. Though transformer-based
015                language models have shown great potential in accurate molecular property predic-
016
                   tion, they neither provide chemically meaningful explanations nor faithfully reveal
                   the molecular structure-property relationships. In this work, we develop a frame-
017
                   work for explainable molecular property prediction based on language models,
018
                   dubbed as Lamole, which can provide chemical concepts-aligned explanations 1 .
019                We take a string-based molecular representation — Group SELFIES — as input
020                tokens to pre-train and fine-tune our Lamole, as it provides chemically meaningful
021                semantics. By disentangling the information flows of Lamole, we propose con-
022                sidering both self-attention weights and gradients for better quantification of each
023                chemically meaningful substructure’s impact on the model’s output. To make the
024                explanations more faithfully respect the structure-property relationship, we then
025                carefully craft a marginal loss to explicitly optimize the explanations to be able
026                to align with the chemists’ annotations. We bridge the manifold hypothesis with
027
                   the elaborated marginal loss to prove that the loss can align the explanations with
                   the tangent space of the data manifold, leading to concept-aligned explanations.
028
                   Experimental results over six mutagenicity datasets and one hepatotoxicity dataset
029
                   demonstrate Lamole can achieve comparable classification accuracy and boost
030                the explanation accuracy by up to 14.3%, being the state-of-the-art in explain-
031                able molecular property prediction. The code is available at the provided link:
032                https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Lamole-7482

035   1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Molecular property prediction aims to reveal the molecular structures-property relationships, assisting
038   scientists in screening molecules for various applications such as drug discovery and material
039   design (Fang et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2023; Tripp et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Ekström Kelvinius
040   et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024). Several learning-based models are devised based on the underlying
041
      molecular representations, such as graph-based and string-based molecular representations. Among
      them, string-based molecular representations, e.g., simplified molecular input line entry systems
042
      (SMILES (Weininger, 1988)), stand out for their simplicity and adaptability (Deng et al., 2023; Wigh
043
      et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023). By viewing the string-based molecular representation as a form of
044   "chemical" language, the Transformer-based language models (LMs) like Bert (Kenton & Toutanova,
045   2019) offer higher throughput and accuracy for molecular property prediction (Deng et al., 2023;
046   Chithrananda et al., 2020).
047

048
      the model’s predictions remain largely unexplored, impeding further advancements in the scientific
049
      domains. Typically, it is crucial to obtain explanations of predictions while achieving accurate
050   predictions. These obtained explanations could be used for scientific hypotheses validation or/and
051   providing actionable insights for refining investigations, such as optimization for molecular structural
052   design (Wu et al., 2023; Wellawatte et al., 2023; Das et al., 2022). With different types of molecular

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Several explainable GNNs are proposed to explain the relationship between the input graph and the
135   prediction (Sun et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2022; Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020;
136
      Lin et al., 2021). Among these works, structure similarity or attention weights are proposed to capture
      structural interaction (Sun et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2019). However, two similar substructures do
137
      not necessarily lead to interaction between them, and attention weights are often inconsistent with
138
      the feature importance (Jain & Wallace, 2019; Serrano & Smith, 2019; Abnar & Zuidema, 2020).
139   In addition, as shown in Fig. 1 (b)∼(d), some trivial structures received relatively high importance
140   scores, indicating the explanations might not align well with the chemical concepts.
141
142
      On the other hand, with string-based molecular representations, LMs show great potential in molecu-
      lar property prediction (Chithrananda et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Ahmad et al., 2022; Ross et al.,
143
      2022). However, the "black-box" characteristics of LMs hamper trust use of these potent computa-
144
      tional tools in scientific domains. Some explainability techniques could be applied to LMs. One way
145   is to use the attention weights over the input tokens. However, recent studies suggest that "attention
146   is not explanation" because attention weights could not reflect the true feature importance (Jain
147   & Wallace, 2019; Serrano & Smith, 2019; Abnar & Zuidema, 2020). Perturbation-based methods
148   perturb the inputs and evaluate the output changes to reveal the input importance. However, the
149   generated explanations may change drastically with very small perturbations (Agarwal et al., 2021).
150   Gradient-based methods determine the feature importance by the partial derivatives of the output to
151   each feature (Selvaraju et al., 2017). However, several works show that the gradient-based methods
152   may not be reliable, as they disregard the influence of model architectures on the output and fail to
153
      incorporate the information of the model architectures into the explanations (Adebayo et al., 2018;
      Agarwal et al., 2021; Rudin, 2019). Therefore, the explanation generation process should reflect
154
      the model reasoning process behind the model architectures. To this end, we disentangle the model
155
      architectures’ information flows to generate explanations that faithfully reveal the structure-property
156   relationship.

309   3.2   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
310
311   We bridge the manifold hypothesis with the marginal loss to theoretically show that the explanations
312   can respect the structure-property relationships. Before giving the proof, the notation and definition
313   regarding the manifold hypothesis are presented.
314   Manifold Hypothesis. It is widely believed that natural data, including molecules, distribute around
315   a manifold (Bordt et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Godwin et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2020). According
316   to the manifold hypothesis for gradient-based explanations (Bordt et al., 2023), if a feature lies in
317   the tangent space of a manifold, then the feature respects the manifold and contributes to the class,
318   and such a feature is desirable to be explained. We call these features "causal features" in our work.
319   Conversely, if a feature is orthogonal to the manifold, then the feature does not contribute to the class.
320
      We call these features "spurious features".
321   With the annotation masks, the causal features s∗ and spurious features s∗ can be distinguished by
322   s∗ = s ⊙ m(g) and s∗ = s ⊙ (1 − m(g)), respectively, where s∗ ∪ s∗ = s and s∗ ∩ s = ∅. By
323   projecting the causal features and spurious features into the data manifold M, the corresponding
      manifold regarding the causal features and spurious features can be defined as follows,

532   5      C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This work proposed Lamole for explainable molecular property prediction based on language models.
535   Lamole uses Group SELFIES as input for chemically meaningful semantics. By disentangling the
536   information flows of Transformer-based LMs, Lamole integrates attention weights into gradients to
537   generate explanations to quantify each chemically meaningful substructure’s impact on the model’s
538   output. Furthermore, one marginal loss is designed to calibrate the explanations to be more faithful by
539   aligning them with the chemists’ annotation. Lamole ’s effectiveness has been demonstrated through
      theoretical analysis and extensive experimental validation.

      A.3    E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
865
866
      Following ChemBERTa (Chithrananda et al., 2020), our BERT family models were pre-trained on a
867   set of 100,000 molecules from the ZINC dataset (Irwin et al., 2012). During pre-training, 15% of
868   tokens in each input string were randomly masked for masked language learning. For each dataset,
869   the ratio of samples with annotation masks over the data size is 10%. The pre-training process was
870   conducted for 10 epochs.
871
      We finetuned the models on the Mutag, Mutagen, PTC-FM, PTC-FR, PTC-MM, PTC-MR, and Liver
872
      datasets for the downstream explainable molecular property prediction tasks. During the fine-tuning
873   stage, for Mutag, Mutagen, and the four PTC datasets, we used an Adam optimizer with a learning
874   rate of 5e-5 and weight decay of 1e-5. The number of epochs is set to 60. For the liver dataset, we
875   used an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 5e-7, and the other parameter settings were the same
876   as the above. The margin term △1 in Eq. (5) is set to 1.
877
      ChemBERTa is a SMILES string-based Bert model, and the input for ChemBERTa is SMILES strings.
878
      For SmoothGrad+Bert, GradInput+Bert, and GradCAM+Bert, the inputs are Group SELFIES strings
879   for a fair comparison. For explainable GNNs and GCNs with feature-based explainability techniques,
880   we select edges with the top-K importance scores as the explanations, where K is the number of
881   edges in the corresponding ground truth substructures. For Bert with feature-based explainability
882   techniques, we select tokens with the top-K importance scores as the explanations, where K is 2 for
883   the Mutag, Mutagen, PTC-FM, PTC-FR, PTC-MM, PTC-MR, and Liver datasets, as 2 fragments
884   (benzene with chemical groups such as N = N, NO2 , and NH2 on the benzene) determine the
885   mutagenic class. For the Liver dataset, the K is the number of tokens of ground truth substructures.
886   We conducted experiments on the computer with an NVIDIA A100 GPU.

1043   The pre-training stage took 11.8 hours. After pre-training, we fine-tuned the model on the used
1044   dataset. On the Mutag dataset, this process took 158s, and the evaluation time was 15s. For the
1045   baseline methods, SmoothGrad+GCN and OrphicX, the total training and evaluation time is 87s and

1050   A.9     L IMITATION
1051
       The developed explainable molecular property prediction models have several limitations and require
1052
       further research.
1053
1054          1. Label annotations: Currently, the proposed Lamole still requires human label annotations as
1055             additional supervisory signals. For a fair comparison, we also use these annotations to align
1056             the generated explanation of compared baselines. Table 2 shows that Lamole outperforms
1057             baseline explainability techniques when using human ground truth annotations. To eliminate
1058             the human labor in labeling annotations, we also explored the possibility of using LLMs to
1059             annotate ground truth, as shown in Appendix A.4. The results indicate that a few human
1060             label annotations are still required to improve the explanation accuracy. Despite that, from
1061
                 the ablation studies, we show that only a few annotations can significantly improve the
                 explanation accuracy. Overall, there is a trade-off between the explanation accuracy and
1062
                 additional annotation costs.
1063
1064          2. Generalizability: Ensuring the generalizability of the explainable models to handle large
1065
                 and diverse molecular datasets across different chemical domains while maintaining inter-
                 pretability and faithfulness to structure-property relationships is an ongoing challenge.
1066
1067          3. Fidelity: The classification prediction performance of the algorithm needs further improve-
1068             ment. Future work may include incorporating larger Group SELFIES corpora and larger
1069
                 models to further unleash its ability in explainable molecular property prediction.
