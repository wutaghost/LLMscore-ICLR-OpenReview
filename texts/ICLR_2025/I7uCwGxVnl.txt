                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Model-based evaluation is at the heart of successful model development – as a re-
011            ward model for training, and as a replacement for human evaluation. To train such
012            evaluators, the standard approach is to collect a large amount of human preference
013            judgments over model responses, which is costly and the data becomes stale as
014            models improve. In this work, we present an approach that aims to improve evalu-
015
               ators without human annotations, using synthetic training data only. Starting from
               unlabeled instructions, our iterative self-improvement scheme generates contrast-
016
               ing model outputs and trains an LLM-as-a-Judge to produce reasoning traces and
017
               final judgments, repeating this training at each new iteration using the improved
018            predictions. Without any labeled preference data, our Self-Taught Evaluator can
019            improve a strong LLM (Llama3-70B-Instruct) from 75.4 to 88.3 (88.7 with major-
020            ity vote) on RewardBench. This outperforms commonly used LLM judges such as
021            GPT-4 and matches the performance of the top-performing reward models trained
022            with labeled examples.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large language models (LLMs) rely on strong evaluators at every stage of the development lifecycle.
029
      They are used at training time as reward models to align with human preferences (Bai et al., 2022;
030   Ouyang et al., 2022) or for iterative self-improvement (Yuan et al., 2024), and at inference time as
031   an alternative to human evaluation (Li et al., 2023; Chiang & Lee, 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Liu
032   et al., 2023). Improvements in evaluation capabilities will thus clearly benefit this entire workflow
033   – including empowering the scientific research process itself as we aim to develop better overall
034   techniques.
035
      Building such strong evaluator models usually relies on large amounts of high-quality preference
036   data from human annotation over model responses, which can be costly and time-consuming to
037   collect, as it requires expert annotation for challenging tasks (e.g., coding and mathematics). This
038   dependency on human-generated data poses significant challenges for scaling to new tasks or eval-
039   uation criteria. Furthermore, as new models inevitably improve over older ones, these existing
040   annotations will typically become outdated, as the judgments are based on annotations of older, less
041   performant, model responses.
042   In this work, we instead explore an iterative self-training approach (Figure 1) which uses no human
043   annotated preferences in the training loop, relying purely on synthetically generated data. Given a
044   seed model, our method first uses prompting to generate contrasting synthetic preference pairs for
045   a given input, such that one response is designed to be inferior to the other. Next, using the model
046   as an LLM-as-a-Judge, we generate reasoning traces and judgments for these pairs, which we can
047   label as correct or not given our synthetic preference pair design. After training on this labeled data
048   we obtain a superior LLM-as-a-Judge, from which we can then iterate the whole process in order
049   for it to self-improve.
050   In our experiments, starting from Llama-3-70B-Instruct, the proposed method improves the accuracy
051   on RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) from 75.4 to 88.7 (with majority vote, or 88.3 without).
052   This matches or outperforms the performance of reward models derived from the same Llama-3-
053   70B-Instruct model that uses human annotations, for example using the HelpSteer2 dataset (Wang
      et al., 2024c) of 10k annotations achieves 85.6 using the same LLM-as-a-Judge setup.

066   2     R ELATED W ORK
067
068   LLM-based Evaluators While traditional evaluation benchmarks employ automated metrics that
069   require a reference answer (Wang et al., 2019; Rajpurkar et al., 2016), these types of benchmarks can
070   pose severe limitations when evaluating open-ended or complex instructions where multiple valid
071   answers are possible (e.g., creative writing and coding). Because human evaluation per response can
072   be costly, many recent works have proposed LLMs as effective evaluators. These come in several
073   flavors: as classifiers that output scores directly (Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a) or via LLM-
074   as-a-Judge prompting that can first generate a chain-of-thought in natural language, which helps
075
      provide explanations for judgments (Zheng et al., 2023). Responses can also be scored alone (Kim
      et al., 2023) or pairwise relative to each other (Dubois et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023;
076
      Saha et al., 2024). LLM evaluation shows great promise as a scalable proxy for human raters, and
077
      in the case of LLM-as-a-Judge as an explainable proxy as well (Ye et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023).
078   However, many of these “off-the-shelf” evaluators demonstrate high variance across many tasks
079   (Bavaresco et al., 2024), indicating the need for improved methods.
080
081   Synthetic Data Synthetic data has emerged as a promising solution for efficiently acquiring train-
082   ing examples and can be particularly valuable in settings where real-world data can be hard to access
083   (e.g., weather data covering all conditions (Lam et al., 2023)) or where correct annotations can be
084   challenging to acquire (e.g., coding tasks (Liu et al., 2024)). Additionally, synthetic data has the
085   benefit of being easily customizable to specific requirements, such as different evaluation criteria or
086   safety constraints (Kim et al., 2023; El Emam et al., 2020; Howe et al., 2017). The use of synthetic
087
      data has been beneficial in model alignment (Lee et al., 2023), improving the original model’s capa-
      bilities (Yuan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b), and teaching the model
088
      new skills (Schick et al., 2023; Lanchantin et al., 2023). In the context of evaluation, synthetic data
089
      has been used to measure tasks such as factuality (Wei et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023), safety (Perez
090   et al., 2023; Hubinger et al., 2024), coding (Gu et al., 2024), and general instruction following (Zeng
091   et al., 2024), showing strong correlation with real human judgments. The West-of-n approach (Pace
092   et al., 2024) has been used to improve reward models by constructing preference pairs using the best
093   and worst scoring pairs from an initial model. For LLM-as-a-Judge models specifically, synthetic
094   responses have been generated by prompting the LLM to produce a given quality response (Kim
095   et al., 2023).
096
097   3     M ETHOD
098
099   We consider the setting of pairwise evaluation using the LLM-as-a-Judge approach (Zheng et al.,
100   2023) that takes:
101
102            • an input (user instruction) x; and
103            • two possible assistant responses y (A) and y (B) to the user instruction x; and
104            • the evaluation prompt containing the rubric and asking to evaluate and choose the winning
105              answer, see e.g., Figure 8.
106
107   The goal of the LLM-as-a-Judge model is to output a preference of which response y is better: A
      or B. In order to do this it is common to output, prior to the final judgment, a chain-of-thought (or

135   Such models can be used as pairwise reward models to build training data for preference optimiza-
136   tion, e.g., for training methods like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), Iterative DPO (Xu et al., 2023)
137   and Self-Rewarding methods (Yuan et al., 2024). They can also be used for evaluation; e.g., many
138   popular benchmark leaderboards are built by using a fixed LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation model (Li
139   et al., 2023) such as GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023).
140   We propose a novel recipe for training such an evaluator. Our overall method is an iterative train-
141   ing scheme that bootstraps improvements by annotating the current model’s judgments using con-
142   structed synthetic data – so that the Self-Taught Evaluator is more performant on the next iteration.
143
      Our overall pipeline is thus as follows:
144
145          • Initialization: We assume access to a large set of human-written user instructions, e.g., of
146            the type that is commonly collected in production systems, and an initial seed LLM.
147
             • Instruction Selection: We next select a challenging, balanced distribution of user instruc-
148
               tions from the uncurated set by categorizing them via LLM.
149
150          • Response Pair Construction: For each user instruction (example) we create a preference
151
               pair of two model responses (chosen & rejected), generating them via prompting such that
               the rejected response is likely of lower quality than the chosen response.
152
153          • Iterative Training: We then iterate the following two steps:
154              (i) Judgment Annotation: For each example, we sample from the current model up to N
155                  times LLM-as-a-Judge generated reasoning traces and judgments. If we find a correct
156                  judgment we add that example to our training set, otherwise we discard it.
157             (ii) Model Fine-tuning: We fine-tune the model on the newly constructed training set
158                  which yields an updated model for the next iteration.
159
160   Note that in each iteration of training the size of the training set depends on the quality of the current
161   model. We expect that as the model improves, the size of the training set will increase as well, as the
      model will be able to find more correct judgments, giving the model a kind of automatic curriculum.

      3.1   I NITIALIZATION
163
164   We assume we have access to a pool of user instructions {xi }. Each sample xi can either be one
165   single text instruction or a multi-turn dialog history of turns between the user and the assistant, with
166   the last turn being an instruction or question from the user. Instructions typically involve different
167   skills such as general knowledge and reasoning, coding, safety, and mathematical reasoning.
168
169   3.2   I NSTRUCTION S ELECTION
170
171   Given a pool of human-written user instructions, there may be a large degree of noise, as well as an
172   imbalance in terms of topic, variety, difficulty, and ability of the model to answer. We therefore aim
173   to select a subset of instructions to generate high-quality synthetic responses and judgments that can
174
      be further used for training.
175   We classify each input using an LLM into a given category, for example coding, reasoning, brain-
176   storming, etc. The precise prompt we use is given in Figure 7. We are then free to select data from
177   within those categories, and to discard certain categories not deemed to be useful for training.
178
179   3.3   R ESPONSE PAIR C ONSTRUCTION
180
181   For each input xi in our curated training pool, we next generate preference data involving two
                  (w)       (l)
182   responses yi and yi where w is expected to be preferable (winning) over l (losing). We achieve
183   this by generating the data in a synthetic manner without using human annotation.
184
      Given the instruction xi , we first prompt an instruction-following LLM to generate a baseline re-
185   sponse yiw as usual. We then prompt the LLM to generate a “noisy” version of the original instruction
186   x′i = ϕ(xi ). We do this using the prompt template given in Figure 2, where we ask to “generate
187   a modified instruction that is highly relevant but not semantically identical to the instruction above
188   from the user.” We then prompt the LLM for a high-quality response yil to x′i , which would not be a
189   good response for xi . This yields a synthetic preference yiw ≻ yil for the original input xi .
190
      This paired data is then used to construct training examples:
191
192                                                       (A)      (B)
                                                 (xi , yi       , yi     )
193
194   where we randomize the order of whether the winner is w = A or w = B, which is important to
195   deal with position bias for LLM-as-a-Judge inference.
196
197   3.4   J UDGMENT A NNOTATION
198
199   Our LLM-as-a-Judge model is used to generate evaluation judgments (reasoning chains and verdicts)
                                                   (A) (B)
200   {ji } for each training example ei := (xi , yi , yi ) in the following manner: for a given input ei ,
201   we collect N diverse evaluations J := {ji1 , . . . , jiN } by sampling from the model. We then apply
202   rejection sampling to filter J by removing jin when the final verdict disagrees with the ground truth
203   labeling, derived from Subsection 3.3. We then select a single correct reasoning chain and verdict at
204   random from the pool of correct solutions. If no such judgment exists (J is empty) then we discard
205
      the example.
206   This now allows us to construct our final training examples of synthetic preferences for fine-tuning:

208                                           ((xi , yi     , yi       ), ji ).

                  WildChat used in the main experiment, we also experimented with prompts annotated with

263
                  in the “harmless base” training split. Then we take human preferences provided by the
264
                  dataset as ground truth to perform rejection sampling to construct judgments.

281
282   Results on RewardBench are given in Table 1. We find that our Self-Taught Evaluator which is
283   trained iteratively on synthetic data without any annotated preference labels significantly improves
284
      over the seed Llama3-70B-Instruct model, matching top-performing reward models trained with
      labeled data. Our approach improves its results across training iterations, and achieves an overall
285
      score of 88.3 on iteration 5, while the seed model it starts from obtains 75.4. Training an LLM-as-
286
      a-Judge in a similar manner starting from the same seed using the labeled HelpSteer2 data we only
287   obtain 85.6, hence we obtain superior performance without using human labeled data. Compared to
288   the seed model, we observe improvements using our approach in evaluating instructions in the Chat
289   Hard, Safety and Reasoning categories, while being worse on the easier Chat category – perhaps
290   because our unlabeled training data focused the model on harder examples.

293   jority voting, our third iteration of Self-Taught Evaluator model reaches an overall performance of

415   6.2   S YNTHETIC BAD R ESPONSE G ENERATION
416
417   In our experiments we generate synthetic data by first generating a modified instruction, and then
418   a good response for the modified instruction – with the aim that this will be a bad response for
419   the original instruction. An alternative is to just prompt an LLM to generate a bad response to

492   We analyze the length distribution of the curated training set of selected instructions in Figure 3. The

494   In contrast, the full dataset (i.e., the full data before the instruction selection step of Subsection 3.2)

496   transcripts.

498   of each input instruction, as well as the length of the expected output, following the procedure in

500   instructions involving logical reasoning/science whereas the full dataset has a greater proportion
501   focused on relationships and entertainment. Finally, in Figure 5 we see that the anticipated length
502   of the response is higher for the full dataset than the curated one, perhaps because of the greater
503   frequency of lengthy, and sometimes repetitive instructions.

505   7     C ONCLUSION
506
507
      We present a scalable approach to build a strong generalist evaluator to perform model-based evalu-
508   ation of LLM outputs. Our method constructs synthetic preferences over pairs of responses without
509   using any human annotation. Our Self-Taught evaluator with iterative training over these synthetic
510   preferences greatly boosts the accuracy of a strong seed LLM (Llama3-70B-Instruct) as an evaluator,
511   from 75.4 to 88.7 on RewardBench, a new state-of-the-art for generative LLM-as-a-Judge methods.

      8     L IMITATIONS
515
      Generative LLM-as-a-Judge models usually have longer outputs and thus higher inference cost than
516
      reward models that simply output a score, as LLM-as-a-Judge typically first generates a reasoning
517
      chain. On the other hand, models that generate long reasoning chains are more susceptible to pro-
518   ducing hallucinated content. This highlights a trade-off between encouraging deeper reasoning and
519   mitigating the risk of generating inaccurate or fabricated information. Further, we have used rela-
520   tively large LLMs in this work (70B parameters) and made no study of whether our approach works
521   on smaller models. Since we use a seed model to generate first synthetic preferences during our iter-
522   ative training scheme, one of the assumptions is that the model is capable of generating reasonable
523   evaluations. Thus, our approach is limited by having a capable instruction fine-tuned model which
524   is already reasonably aligned to human (or legal/policy) preferences. Furthermore, we only investi-
525   gated and reported metrics involving evaluation accuracy improvements, rather than computational
526
      requirement concerns. While LLM-as-a-judge models can also be utilized to provide reward signals
      for optimizing LLM performance, our evaluation did not explore this application. Future work could
527
      investigate the potential benefits of using our model in this context. We also only investigated pair-
528
      wise evaluation, i.e., comparing two responses, whereas it is also possible to use LLM-as-a-Judge
529   models (or any other model) to evaluate the quality of single responses, e.g., giving them a score out
530   of 5 or 10, rather than a pairwise A vs B judgment. We leave evaluating single responses to future
531   work.
