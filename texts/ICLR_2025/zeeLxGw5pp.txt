009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Adversarial examples and Out-of-Distribution (OoD) inputs constitute major
012            problematic instances for the image classifiers based on Deep Neural Networks
013            (DNNs). In particular, DNNs tend to be overconfident with their predictions, as-
014            signing a different category with a high probability. In this work, we suggest a
015            combined solution to tackle both input types based on the Variational Autoen-
016            coder (VAE). First, we scrutinize the recent successful results in detecting OoDs
017            utilizing Bayesian epistemic uncertainty estimation over weights of VAEs. Sur-
018
               prisingly, contrary to the previous claims in the literature, we discover that we
               can obtain comparable detection performance utilizing a standard procedure of
019
               importance sampling with the classical formulation of VAE. Second, we dissect
020
               the marginal likelihood approximation, analyzing the primary source of variation
021            responsible for distinguishing inliers versus outliers, and establish a link with the
022            recent promising results in detecting outliers using latent holes. Finally, we iden-
023            tify that adversarial examples and OoD inputs have similar latent representations.
024            This insight allows us to develop separate methods to automatically distinguish
025            between them by considering their non-similarities in the input space. The sug-
026            gested approach enables pre-training a VAE model on specific input data, allowing
027            it to act as a gatekeeper. This achieves two major goals: defending the DNN clas-
028            sifier against potential attacks and flagging OoDs. Once pre-trained, VAE can be
029
               plugged as a filter into any DNN image classifier of arbitrary architecture trained
               on the same data inputs without the need for its retraining or accessing the layers
030
               and weights of the DNN.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are applied to a rather diverse set of safety-critical tasks ranging
036   from autonomous car driving to automatically-assisted medical diagnosis. However, the thorough
037   theoretical foundation of deep learning is still lacking. It results in a limited understanding of
038   how deep neural networks generalize. Such a situation led to the discovery of the following facts:
039   (i) there is a possibility to mislead the DNN classification with specifically forged inputs that, while
040   preserving the semantics from the point of view of human observers, result in a wrong classifica-
041   tion category by a DNN, i.e., the adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2013; Biggio et al., 2013;
042
      Goodfellow et al., 2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2016), and (ii) the inability of the DNN to infer the fact
      that the provided input does not adhere to the data distribution they have been previously trained on,
043
      i.e., the overconfidence of DNN predictions with OoD inputs 1 (Nguyen et al., 2015; Hendrycks &
044
      Gimpel, 2016; Nalisnick et al., 2018).
045
046   The discriminative nature of the supervised image DNN classifiers implies learning a mapping from
047   the input pixel space to the target labels. This mapping is usually considered to represent a cat-
048
      egorical distribution over labels y given the particular input x: p(y|x). However, in practice, the
      categorical distribution is based on the softmax activation function. As it has been recently formally
049
      proved, the softmax does not provide the desirable properties of categorical distribution and oper-
050
      ates in a way similar to the k-means clustering, i.e., it partitions the transformed input space into
051   several cones where every cone represents a different category (Hess et al., 2020). It may explain
052
         1
053        These inputs are commonly called outliers or Out-of-Distribution (OoD) inputs in the literature. Please
      note that we will use these terms interchangeably and consider them as synonyms.

054
      why DNNs struggle with both adversarial examples and OoD: on one hand, it is feasible to find an
055   adversarial direction from one category to another while attempting to preserve as little modification
056   to the input as possible, especially for the examples that lie far from the cluster centroids, and on an-
057   other hand when a new unseen data input arrives, the k-means clustering would necessarily cluster it
058   into one of the categories resulting in overconfident predictions of OoD as in-distribution examples.
059
      Recently, many different solutions have been proposed to address either the issue with adversarial
060
      examples (Zhang & Wang, 2019; Hu et al., 2019; Samangouei et al., 2018; Meng & Chen, 2017;
061   Hwang et al., 2019) or the issue with OoD inputs (Daxberger & Hernández-Lobato, 2019; Ren
062   et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2017). The works, though, that solve both problems
063   simultaneously within the same framework are few (Lee et al., 2018; Ahuja et al., 2019). These
064   works are based on learning the DNN class-conditional weight uncertainties, which imply access to
065   the model architecture, its weights, and output categories. Such an approach is closely interlinked
066   with the DNN model under the protection, and it also introduces the unnecessary inductive bias by
067   class conditioning. It makes the suggested methods non-modularizable and non-transferrable in a
068   plug-and-play manner to other DNN architectures that require the same functionality of protection
069
      against adversarial attacks or OoD detection and that have been trained on the same input data.
070   Conversely, we apply an unsupervised Deep Generative Modeling (DGM) to tackle both of the
071   problems, i.e., instead of learning discriminative mapping p(y|x) and subsequently attempting to
072   estimate the uncertainty of the weights under different inputs, DGM allows learning the approxima-
073   tion of a true distribution over the training data: p(x) which in theory should assign a low density to
074   the OoD and adversarial inputs. However, recent research revealed that such estimations are prone
075
      to errors, often providing higher likelihood values to both OoD and adversarial examples than to
      in-distribution data (Nalisnick et al., 2018).
076
077   To overcome this problem, we apply two recently suggested methods based on model parameter
078   sensitivity analysis. (1) We use a Bayesian DGM, namely, VAE, that learns the weights uncertainty
079   during training yielding the following posterior distribution: p(θ|D) for the training data D over the
080   model weights θ. It allows us to get an ensemble of the approximations of a true data distribution
081
      where each sample from the posterior θ ∼ p(θ|D) gives a separate instance of the model in the
      ensemble. Based on sampling from the posterior distribution, we estimate the likelihood of the
082
      input instance, however, instead of the usual calculation of the expected likelihood, we calculate the
083
      recently suggested scores of variance of the likelihoods between the different instance models in the
084   ensemble (Glazunov & Zarras, 2022). The high degree of model/epistemic uncertainty is captured
085   by the high values of the variance score. (2) We use recently suggested scores based on detecting
086   if the corresponding latent code is in the hole or not (Glazunov & Zarras, 2023). We apply a single
087   instance of classic VAE. Moreover, we enforce both compactness and continuity constraints on the
088   latent representation and the corresponding encoder map. Overall, we suggest a single DGM based
089   on VAE to detect both the OoD and adversarial inputs simultaneously, and we empirically evaluate
090   the suggested approach based on the several datasets achieving promising results.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
095
096   There are two different perspectives on adversarial examples that give rise to two different defini-
097   tions: one from the perspective of the generalization properties of the DNN and the other from the
098   attacker’s perspective. From the generalization perspective, an adversarial example (Szegedy et al.,
099   2013) is a technique in which the input for the DNN image classier is intentionally modified to look
100   almost the same as the original image to the human eye. Yet, it is perceived as something completely
101   different by DNN. DNNs incorrectly classify such adversarial examples from the human perspec-
102   tive. On the other hand, the attacker perspective does not necessarily demand the part that relates to
103   the imperceptibility of the difference (Biggio et al., 2013). On the contrary, if the miscreants want
104
      their attack’s outcome to succeed, they should not constrain themselves to the superfluous imper-
      ceptibility demands. In this paper, we concentrate on the imperceptible examples. In addition, we
105
      analyze both alternatives from the perspective of their internal representation.
106
107   Furthermore, we conduct the experiments with both the adversarial examples generated for the dis-
      criminative model under attack and the adversarial examples generated to attack our defending VAE

      filter. Specifically, for the former case, we consider three types of such attacks: Fast Gradient Sign
109   Method (FGSM (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), Carlini-Wagner (CW) attack (Carlini & Wagner,
110   2016), and Jacobian-based Saliency Map Attack (JSMA) (Papernot et al., 2016). For the latter case,
111   we evaluate attacks on the encoder in the same vein as in Kuzina et al. (2024).

113   2.1.1   FAST GRADIENT SIGN METHOD
114
115   The FGSM attacks DNNs by leveraging their learning process based on gradients (Goodfellow et al.,
116
      2014). FGSM can be described by the following formula:
117
118                              x′ = x + λ · sgn(∇x ℓ(hθ (x), ys )), x′ ∈ [0, 1]n
119
      Here ▽x ℓ is the gradient of the loss function w.r.t. the original input pixel vector x, ys is the true or
120
      source label for x, and θ stands for the model parameters that are constant.
121
122   Gradient w.r.t. x is easier to calculate with backpropagation than for θ which allows the fast genera-
123   tion of adversarial examples. FGSM exploits gradient ascent to increase the loss. Subsequently, the
124
      sign applies a max-norm constraint on the gradient value, and λ represents a small magnitude of the
      step in the direction of increasing the loss. It represents the untargeted type of adversarial attacks.
125
126   FGSM can be converted into a targeted attack by substituting the source label with a target one yt
127   and doing gradient descent instead of ascent, namely:
128
129                              x′ = x − λ · sgn(∇x ℓ(hθ (x), yt )), x′ ∈ [0, 1]n
130
131   However, due to the fact that FGSM is designed to be fast rather than optimal, it is not necessarily
132   guaranteed to produce the targeted adversarial examples of minimal perturbations.
133
134
      2.1.2   C ARLINI -WAGNER
135   Carlini-Wagner (CW) attack (Carlini & Wagner, 2016) aims at optimality in contrast with FGSM,
136   i.e., it attempts to generate as little pixel noise as possible to succeed in the attack. It poses the
137   following optimization objective:
138
139
                           minimize ||ε||p subj. to hθ (x + ε) = yt , x + ε ∈ [0, 1]n
140
141   where x ∈ [0, 1]n represents an image, ε ∈ [0, 1]n is added noise to the image, and yt is a target
142   class label of the image under attack. The noise level is calculated in terms of Lp norms. Authors
143   consider several norms; in this work, we concentrate on L2 -norm. This attack is one of the strongest
144   known adversarial attacks.
145
146   2.1.3   JACOBIAN - BASED S ALIENCY M AP ATTACK
147   JSMA (Papernot et al., 2016) leverages the saliency maps to devise an adversarial input. Namely,
148   it computes the forward derivative of the whole DNN (Jacobian) w.r.t. the input, and based on
149   this derivative, it constructs the saliency map. Large absolute values of the saliency map reveal the
150   features that have a significant impact on the final output. The JSMA takes the maximum absolute
151   value and perturbs it by a hyperparameter θ and repeats the process. The stopping criteria are either
152   a successful attack with misclassification or reaching the total perturbation threshold of Υ.
153

      2.2   T RANSFERABILITY
163
164   It has been discovered that different architectures of DNNs trained to tackle the same classification
165   problem on similar datasets tend to have similar fairly piece-wise linear decision boundaries that
166   separate categories in the input data domain (Goodfellow et al., 2014). This property is called
167   transferability. Transferability is especially dangerous since it allows to devise an adversarial attack
168   that universally targets all DNNs with a similar final objective in a black-box manner (Papernot
169   et al., 2016). Moreover, since we utilize a generative approach, we explore if there is transferability
170
      from the adversarial examples generated for a discriminative model to a generative one.
171
172
      2.3   O UT- OF - DISTRIBUTION
173
      Deploying a successful classifier requires from the system the ability to detect input data that are
174   statistically anomalous or significantly different from those used in training. This is especially im-
175   portant for DNN classifiers since DNNs with the softmax classifier tend to produce overconfident
176   predictions even for such Out-of-Distribution (OoD) inputs (Lee et al., 2018). The lack of reliability
177   of DNN classifiers when faced with OoDs was recently addressed by various methods (Hendrycks
178   & Gimpel, 2016; Hendrycks et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2017). According to recent research, the
179   softmax activation function does not model a categorical distribution but represents a k-means clus-
180   tering (Hess et al., 2020). That is why it seems logical to seek another approach. We decided to
181   consider using unsupervised DGMs for that purpose. In our case, we apply the same VAE model to
182
      detect the OoDs based on the sensitivity analysis.
183
184   3     M ETHODOLOGY
185
186   We employ an approach based on the sensitivity analysis of the model parameter w.r.t. the different
187   inputs. Namely, we test the level of stability of our model when dealing with OoDs versus IDs.
188   There are two possible ways to achieve this goal. The first one is to utilize epistemic uncertainty
189
      estimation that would allow us to sample model parameters to be subsequently used for sensitivity
      analysis. The second one is to employ the learned posterior distribution over the latent codes in VAE
190
      and sample posterior for different latent codes. This approach does not change the parameters of
191
      DNNs used in the model, however, it allows conducting sensitivity analysis w.r.t. different sampled
192   hypotheses from the latent posterior.

      3.1   E PISTEMIC UNCERTAINTY IN O O D AND IN ADVERSARIAL ATTACKS
195
196   It has been shown that DGMs do not produce valid estimations of p(x) when it comes to distin-
197   guishing between OoD and in-distribution (Nalisnick et al., 2018). Most of the results reveal DGMs
198   being overconfident when dealing with OoD data. Another work dedicated to adversarial defense
199   (Song et al., 2017) showed that it is possible to statistically differentiate between adversarial vs non-
200   adversarial input data using DGMs. In this work, we first estimate the weight uncertainty to address
201
      this issue utilizing Bayesian and, in particular, variational inference.

310   3.3     D ISENTANGLING THE VARIATION AND THE BAYESIAN INFERENCE
311
312   We identify the source of the variation observed with Bayesian VAEs. The general procedure of the
313   marginal likelihood estimation follows these steps:
314           1. Sampling the weights from the estimated posterior: θ ∼ p(θ|D).
315           2. Estimating the marginal likelihood for separate sampled models (Equation 2).
316           3. Computing a single value based on the separate estimated marginal likelihoods.
317
      As it can be seen, there are two possible sources of variation, namely, variation from Step 1 and
318   variation from Step 2. It was hypothesized by Daxberger & Hernández-Lobato (2019) that the
319   Bayesian inference over the DNN parameters is responsible for the observed variation of the results.
320   In our work, we test if it is indeed the case by eliminating the first step and estimating the variation in
321   the case of a simple classical VAE; namely, instead of sampling from p(θ|D), we use a single VAE
322   model that is used for marginal likelihood estimation several times. In such a case, all the variation
323   comes only from the importance sampling. We apply the same scores for the Bayesian VAEs to
      identify if the variation persists for the classical VAEs.

337   3.4     A NALYZING L ATENT R EPRESENTATION
338
339   Our experiments confirm that adversarial examples can be identified using the same scores success-
340   fully applied to outliers. It implies that adversarial examples occupy latent holes similar to the OoDs.
341
      The difference is that it is possible to control the strength of the adversarial attack. Hence, we can
      visualize the dynamics of the attack strength w.r.t. the learned data representation in the latent space.
342
      To that end, we employ the learning procedure suggested by Jiang et al. (2017) to mold the latent
343
      data manifold into a mixture of Gaussians, the so-called Variational Deep Embeddings (VADEs).

345   be visually inspected. In addition, no Lipschitz constraints are used for these experiments, so no
346   restraints are applied for the adversarial locations.

349   4     E XPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
350
351   Our experiments have been conducted on several datasets widely used for validation of OoD and
352   adversarial attacks, namely: MNIST(LeCun & Cortes, 2010), FashionMNIST(Xiao et al., 2017),
353
      SVHN(Netzer et al., 2011) and CIFAR10(Krizhevsky et al., 2010).
354   First, we estimated the impact of the number of dimensions of the latent space on the loss function.
355   The dimensionality is closely connected with the dataset on which the model is trained. MNIST
356   and FashionMNIST results reveal no particular need to exceed 10 latent dimensions since the loss
357   function didn’t significantly decrease after that value. For SVHN, we experimented with the number
358   of latent dimensions up to 50, and the most optimal results were achieved with dimensionality equal
359
      to 20.
360   For our tests, we used two different architectures: for grayscale images, we applied a multilayer
361   perceptron for both the encoder and decoder with two fully connected hidden layers. For RGBs
362   images, we applied a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with two convolutional layers of 32
363   and 64 filters. For epistemic uncertainty estimation, all layers that contain parameters have been
364   enhanced with the BBB, namely, convolutional 2D, fully connected, and convolutional 2D transpose.
365

        5         D ISCUSSION
509
510
511     The hole indicator confirms that transferability extends from discriminative to generative models,
512
        indicating a similar learned representation between those two approaches. Even though adversarial
        examples from the discriminative model end up in the latent holes of the VAE, the active defense
513
        through HMC cannot return to the regions with high probability. This suggests that despite some
514
        commonalities, differences still exist between discriminative and generative settings. Adversarial
515     attacks on the VAE’s latent space can be effectively distinguished from OoD inputs using active
516     defense strategies. Furthermore, the internal latent representations of near- and far-OoD instances
517     are similar to those of weak and strong adversarial attacks, respectively. Finally, contrary to common
518     belief, Bayesian inference over DNN parameters is not essential for sensitivity analysis. We observe
519     different levels of model stability w.r.t. inliers versus outliers, related to the differences in log-
520     likelihood variances, revealing a connection with the recently introduced score of the hole indicator.
521
522
523     6         C ONCLUSION
524
525     We explore two common types of problematic inputs in DNN classifiers: OoDs and adversarial
526     attacks. Our proposed solution uses a variational autoencoder (VAE) to address both problems si-
527     multaneously. We initially evaluate the effectiveness of using Bayesian estimation of epistemic
528     uncertainty from VAE weights to detect OoD inputs and discover that comparable results can be
529     achieved by importance sampling with classical VAE formulations without resorting to Bayesian
530     inference over weights. This result indicates that latent codes possess all the necessary information
531
        for measuring a model’s sensitivity. Furthermore, we introduce a simple algorithm that distinguishes
        generative adversarial examples from both outliers and discriminative adversarial attacks using ac-
532
        tive defense. It enables identifying if the VAE model is currently being under attack. In addition, this
533
        algorithm allows for detecting both types of adversarial attacks: one is based on the imperceptible
534     perturbations of the input image to the classifier, and it is based on the transferability of the adversar-
535     ial examples from discriminative to generative models, while another is based on the attacks aimed
536     at the encoder of the VAE. Finally, our approach allows a VAE model to be pretrained on specific
537     datasets so that it functions as a filter, serving the purpose of protecting the DNN classifier from
538     potential attacks and OoD inputs. This pre-trained VAE can be easily integrated as a filter with any
539     DNN classifier, regardless of its architecture, trained on the same dataset, eliminating the need for
        further training or modification of DNN configurations.
