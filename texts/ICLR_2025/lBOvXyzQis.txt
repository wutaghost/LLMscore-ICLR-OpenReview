                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            The concept of diversity is widely used in various applications: from image or
011
               molecule generation to recommender systems. Thus, being able to properly mea-
               sure diversity is important. This paper addresses the problem of quantifying diver-
012
               sity for a set of objects. First, we make a systematic review of existing diversity
013
               measures and explore their undesirable behavior in some cases. Based on this
014            review, we formulate three desirable properties (axioms) of a reliable diversity
015            measure: monotonicity, uniqueness, and continuity. We show that none of the
016            existing measures has all three properties and thus these measures are not suit-
017            able for quantifying diversity. Then, we construct two examples of measures that
018            have all the desirable properties, thus proving that the list of axioms is not self-
019            contradicting. Unfortunately, the constructed examples are too computationally
020            complex for practical use, thus we pose an open problem of constructing a diver-
021            sity measure that has all the listed properties and can be computed in practice.
022
023
024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Diversity of a collection of objects is a concept that is widely used in practice: image generation
027   models are required to generate a diverse sample of images for a given prompt, recommender sys-
028   tems are required to output a diverse set of suggestions for a query, molecule generation models often
029   aim at generating a collection of structurally diverse molecules with a given property. Diversity can
030   also play an important role in assessing how representative is a given dataset, e.g., in molecule gen-
031   eration (Xie et al., 2023) or neural algorithmic reasoning (Veličković & Blundell, 2021; Mahdavi
032   et al., 2023). Thus, being able to quantify diversity is important.
033   Traditional methods of assessing diversity may differ across domains and tasks. In the image gener-
034   ation domain, diversity ensures that at least some of the generated images can fit a user’s preference.
035   The average of pairwise distances between the output images is commonly used as a measure of di-
036   versity. For instance, Ruiz et al. (2023) compute diversity as the average LPIPS similarity between
037   the output objects, while Saharia et al. (2022) compute the average pairwise SSIM between the first
038   output sample and the remaining samples. Similarly, in recommender systems, diversity ensures that
039
      at least some of the model outputs can fit a user’s preference. The average pairwise distance between
      the outputs is a popular diversity measure in this domain (Alhijawi et al., 2022). Another way of
040
      assessing diversity is via the determinantal point process (DPP) approach that defines diversity as
041
      the determinant of the similarity matrix (Wilhelm et al., 2018). In the molecule generation domain,
042   the typical task is to generate a diverse collection of molecules with some predefined properties. The
043   underlying goal is to explore the whole space of such possible molecules and pick the best candi-
044   dates, so diversity of the output collection ensures that generated molecules are not clustered in one
045   area, while other areas are unexplored. A common diversity measure here is also the average pair-
046   wise distance between the outputs (Du et al., 2022), although sometimes the percentage of unique
047   generated molecules is reported (Hoogeboom et al., 2022). Finally, in a recent paper on generating
048   structurally diverse graphs (Velikonivtsev et al., 2024), a new measure called energy is proposed as
049   a better and more reliable alternative to the average pairwise distance.
050   Note that in all the examples above, diversity can also be thought of as coverage: the goal is to cover
051   different areas of the space of potentially valid outputs. Thus, in this paper, we use the terms diversity
052   and coverage interchangeably. In the literature, there have been a few attempts to analyze, compare,
053   or suggest better measures of diversity (Xie et al., 2023; Friedman & Dieng, 2023; Velikonivtsev
      et al., 2024). However, as we show in this paper, the problem is still underexplored.

054
      We limit the scope of our research to the following setup: we are given a collection of abstract
055   objects and their pairwise distances (or pairwise similarities). We define diversity measure as a
056   function that takes this collection as an input and returns some value as an output.
057
058
      First, we examine the existing diversity measures by providing examples of their undesirable behav-
      ior. Namely, we show that existing measures may either lead to unexpected results when comparing
059
      diversity of two datasets (i.e., assigning a higher score to a clearly less diverse dataset) or lead to de-
060
      generate solutions when being optimized. Motivated by these observations and previous studies on
061   diversity, we formulate three properties (axioms) that a good diversity measure should have. Mono-
062   tonicity requires that increasing pairwise distances between the objects increases diversity value.
063   Uniqueness requires that having a duplicate in the collection is worse for diversity than having any
064   non-duplicate object instead. The last property is continuity which requires diversity to be a contin-
065   uous function of pairwise distances. We support the necessity of these properties with examples of
066   abnormal behavior of diversity measures that do not have some of them. After that, we check which
067   of the existing measures have what properties, and find out that none has all three. Then, we prove
068   that the list of axioms is not self-contradicting by constructing two examples of measures that satisfy
069
      all of them. Unfortunately, the proposed measures are too computationally expensive (NP-hard) to
      be used in practice. Finally, we discuss why finding a diversity measure that has all three desirable
070
      properties and is computationally manageable is a non-trivial task. We leave the question of whether
071
      there exists a computationally feasible measure satisfying all the required axioms for future studies.

118   3    D RAWBACKS OF POPULAR DIVERSITY MEASURES
119
120
      In this section, we discuss why none of the measures defined above can be reliably used to quantify
      diversity. For this, we show intuitive examples of an undesirable behavior for each measure. These
121
      examples serve as the main motivation for our research and for the axioms we choose.
122
123   We start with discussing two usage scenarios of diversity measures. First, a diversity measure can be
124   applied to a given dataset to quantify its diversity. Thus, it should be able to identify which dataset
125   is more diverse. For instance, when choosing between two recommendation algorithms, one can be
126
      interested in comparing diversity of the retrieved sets of items. Second, diversity can be used as a
      goal of an optimization process. For instance, Velikonivtsev et al. (2024) generate sets of graphs
127
      that are maximally diverse. During the generation process, the authors iteratively modify the set of
128
      graphs by accepting modifications that improve a given diversity measure. Thus, a good diversity
129   measure should lead to diverse configurations of elements when being optimized.
130
131   Below we examine the diversity measures listed in Table 1 from these two perspectives: comparison
132
      and optimization. We say that a measure exhibits undesirable behavior w.r.t. comparison if there
      exists a pair of datasets, such that the first one is more diverse according to our intuitive perception of
133
      diversity, yet the diversity measure assigns the higher value to the second one. We say that a measure
134
      exhibits undesirable behavior w.r.t. optimization if the dataset with maximal diversity according to
135   this measure is not maximally diverse according to our intuitive perception of diversity. Note that
136   if a measure exhibits undesirable behavior w.r.t. optimization, it also exhibits undesirable behavior
137   w.r.t. comparison. Indeed, if a measure assigns the highest value to some not intuitively diverse set,
138   this means that it assigns a lower value to some dataset that is intuitively diverse, thus exhibiting
139   undesirable behavior w.r.t. comparison. The opposite is not necessarily true: some measures can be
140   suitable for optimization while being unable to reliably compare two non-optimal configurations.
141   Note that we limit our research to the simple case when the number of elements n is fixed, thus in
142   the examples below all the configurations are of the same size.
143

      4     A XIOMATIC APPROACH TO DIVERSITY MEASURES
238
239
      Motivated by our analysis in Section 3, we formulate a list of properties (axioms) that a reliable
240
      diversity measure is expected to satisfy. First, we formally define diversity measures, then formulate
241
      the desirable properties and discuss which existing measures satisfy which properties, and finally
242   review desirable properties suggested in previous studies and discuss how they relate to our setup.
243
244
      4.1   F ORMAL DEFINITION OF DIVERSITY MEASURE
245
246
      Assume that we are given a collection of n (possibly duplicating) objects X = (x1 , . . . , xn ) and
247   pairwise distances between them dij , which satisfy the following conditions:
248
249         1. ∀i, j : dij ≥ 0 and ∀i : dii = 0;
250
            2. if dij = 0, then ∀k : dik = djk ;
251
252         3. ∀i, j : dij = dji .
253
254   In terms of objects, the first property requires that the distance between any two objects is nonneg-
255   ative, and distance from an object to itself is 0. The second property requires that if two objects
256
      coincide, then they must have equal distances to any other object. The third property is symmetry of
      distance. Note that for generality, we do not require the triangle inequality to be satisfied by dij .
257
258   A diversity measure is a function that takes as input any such set of n objects and their pairwise
259   distances and outputs a real number. We assume that diversity depends only on distances dij and
260   does not depend on the nature of the objects xi itself. So, the input of our function can be fully
261   described as n × n matrix D with entries dij . Denote by Dn a subset of all n × n matrices satisfying
262
      the three properties described above. Then, the diversity function is a function from Dn to R. Since
      diversity is usually measured for a multiset of objects, we also require permutation invariance: if
263
      we permute (or rename) the objects in X (with correspondingly permuting the rows and columns of
264
      D), the value of diversity should not change. Thus, we get the following definition.
265
266   Definition 4.1. A diversity function is a permutation invariant function from Dn to R.
267
268   Note that we assume the number of elements n to be fixed. Thus, we do not aim to determine how
269   diversity should behave when the size of the dataset changes. Our paper shows that even for this
      (simpler) case is non-trivial to construct a suitable diversity measure.

      4.2   A XIOMS FOR DIVERSITY
271
272   In this section, we formulate three axioms that we require for a reliable diversity measure.
273
274
      Axiom 1 (Monotonicity). A diversity function must be strictly monotonously increasing with respect
      to all its arguments.
275
276   In other words, if we increase one or several pairwise distances while keeping all other distances
277   fixed, the value of diversity must increase. This axiom is natural to require since it represents
278   the meaning of diversity: the more objects x1 , . . . , xn differ from each other, the higher diversity
279   we expect. This property is analogous to monotonicity in Velikonivtsev et al. (2024), but has one
280   important difference: we do not require the objects of X to be pairwise distinct for monotonicity to
281   hold. This difference is critical for being able to compare datasets: we want to be able to tell which
282   configuration is more diverse even if they have duplicates. Otherwise, we may get a measure with
283   undesirable behavior, as shown by the example for Bottleneck and Energy in Section 3.
284   Axiom 2 (Uniqueness). Suppose we are given two collections of objects (and their pairwise dis-
285   tances) which differ only by one element: x1 , . . . , xn−1 , xn and x1 , . . . , xn−1 , x′n . Suppose x′n
286   coincide with at least one of x1 , . . . , xn−1 , while xn does not coincide with any of x1 , . . . , xn−1 .
287   Then, the diversity of the first collection must be higher than the diversity of the second collection.2
288
289
      This property reflects our intuition that having a duplicate (x′n ) in the multiset is worse for diversity
      than having a unique element (xn ) instead. Informally, we can say that having x′n does not help the
290
      multiset to cover any new part of the space since a copy of x′n is already present, while having xn
291
      covers some new area. Uniqueness allows one to avoid an undesirable behavior when the collection
292   with duplicates has higher diversity than an intuitively more diverse collection without duplicates
293   or even when the maximal diversity is achieved by a degenerate configuration (which happens to
294   Average and Diameter, as shown in Section 3). Let us note that the difference between our variant
295   of Uniqueness and the analogous property in Velikonivtsev et al. (2024) is that we do not require
296   all objects in X to be distinct. As for monotonicity, this modification is important for being able to
297   compare datasets even when they have duplicated elements.
298
      Axiom 3 (Continuity). A diversity function must be continuous.
299
300   This property was not present in previous works, but it is natural to require and we find it critical for a
301   reliable diversity measure. Indeed, in Appendix A we show that there are examples of discontinuous
302   functions that satisfy monotonicity and uniqueness while still exhibiting undesirable behavior. Thus,
303   having only monotonicity and uniqueness is not sufficient.

      6    D ISCUSSION
433
434
      In the previous section, we prove that the three axioms listed in Section 4.2 are not self-contradicting.
435   However, we have not been able to construct a measure that satisfies these axioms and is computa-
436   tionally feasible to be applied in practice. We pose this as an important open problem to be addressed
437   in future studies.
438
      Let us provide some intuition on why it is hard to combine monotonicity, uniqueness, and continuity
439
      in one function. We first formulate the following proposition that shows an additional restriction
440
      that these three axioms imply.
441
442
      Proposition 6.1. Suppose a diversity function has uniqueness and continuity. Let x1 , . . . , xk be a
      set of k pairwise different objects. Let C be a multiset of n − k objects, each of which coincides with
443
      one of x1 , . . . , xk . Then, diversity of the multiset {x1 , . . . , xk } ∪ C is the same for all such C.
444
445   We prove this proposition in Appendix E. Informally, Proposition 6.1 states that the diversity of a set
446   does not depend on which elements are duplicated. This agrees well with our intuition: duplicates
447   do not give any additional elements and thus are not supposed to affect diversity. On the other hand,
448   constructing a measure that is continuous while ‘ignoring’ duplicates is tricky since the object’s
449   property of being a duplicate is discontinuous. Indeed, we can move a duplicate by any small ϵ > 0
450   and it stops being a duplicate, so our measure should no longer ‘ignore’ it. In MultiDimVolume,
451   we address this problem by incorporating products of pairwise distances within subgraphs: any
452
      duplicate zeros the corresponding products and thus the placement of a duplicate does not affect the
      result. In IntegralMaxClique, we use a threshold t to filter out small edges, and thus duplicates do
453
      not affect the value for all t > 0.
454
455   The next proposition states that a diversity function satisfying all the axioms cannot be expressed
456   in a certain form. This particular form is motivated by the approach in Velikonivtsev et al. (2024):
457   the authors iteratively improve diversity of a set by updating one element at a time. Thus, they
458
      decompose a considered diversity function into the fitness of one element and diversity of the rest
      of the elements. Such decomposition would allow one to make quick updates of diversity (in linear
459
      time) when only one element is updated. In the proposition below, we show that for a proper diversity
460
      measure such decomposition cannot exist if we assume additive aggregation.
461
462   Proposition 6.2. Assume that a diversity function can be decomposed in the following way:
463                         Diversity(X) = F (d12 , d13 , . . . , d1n ) + G(x2 , . . . , xn ),
464
465   that is, the first term depends only on distances from one object x1 to all other objects, and the
466
      second term depends only on pairwise distances between the objects x2 , . . . , xn . Then, such a
      diversity function cannot simultaneously satisfy monotonicity, uniqueness, and continuity axioms.
467
468   We prove this proposition in Appendix F. This is a negative result showing why it can be difficult
469   to construct a proper diversity measure that is convenient for optimization. Note that, however, this
470   proposition is only proven for the additive aggregations, thus other options are potentially possible.
471
472   NP-hard measures in practice Finally, let us note that NP-hard diversity measures can still be
473   used in practice if a set of items that need to be evaluated is sufficiently small. For instance, if
474   a recommender service returns a set of k = 100 items and we want to measure diversity of this
475   set, then an NP-hard measure having all the desirable properties can potentially be used. Examples
476   of diversity measures constructed in Section 5 demonstrate that there are several different options
477   that can be used (since there are two measures that may also have variations still satisfying all the
478
      properties). We cannot rule out any of these measures based on their theoretical properties. Thus, a
      decision on which measure should be used may depend on a particular application.
479

481   7    C ONCLUSION
482
483   In this paper, we reviewed existing diversity measures and demonstrated via intuitive examples
484   that these measures cannot be reliably used for evaluating diversity. Based on these examples and
485   previous research on diversity measures, we formulated three simple axioms (desirable properties)
      for a reliable diversity measure: monotonicity, uniqueness, and continuity. It turns out that none


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of the previously known measures has all these properties. We constructed two diversity measures
487   that have all the desirable properties, thus proving that the axioms do not contradict each other.
488   Unfortunately, the constructed examples are too computationally complex for practical use.
489
490
      We leave for future research an important open problem of constructing a diversity measure that has
      all three desirable properties and is computationally feasible or proving that such a measure cannot
491
      exist. While our study does not answer this question, we believe that it gives some important insights
492
      into measures of diversity that are frequently used in practice. Being aware of what shortcomings a
493   particular measure has, one can use it more wisely. For instance, we cannot advise using Energy for
494   comparing diversities of arbitrary datasets, while it can be safely used as a target for optimization.

833

836   Lemma E.1. Suppose a diversity function has uniqueness and continuity. Let x1 , . . . , xn−1 be any
837   collection of n − 1 objects. We denote by A1 the collection of n objects x1 , . . . , xn−1 , x1 and by
838   A2 the collection of n objects x1 , . . . , xn−1 , x2 (note that A1 and A2 differ only by the last object).
839   Then, Diversity(A1 ) = Diversity(A2 ).
840
      Informally, this lemma says that we can remove the duplicate of x1 and add the duplicate of x2
841
      without changing the value of the diversity function. The proposition trivially follows from this
842
      lemma, so it is sufficient to prove it.
843
844   W.l.o.g., assume that Diversity(A1 ) − Diversity(A2 ) = ϵ > 0. Denote by A′2 the following collec-
845   tion: take A2 and increase the distance from the last object to all objects by small δ > 0 in such a
846
      way that diversity changes by less than 2ϵ (note that the last object is no longer a duplicate). By con-
      tinuity it is possible. Then, Diversity(A′2 ) is less than Diversity(A2 ) + 2ϵ . Thus, Diversity(A′2 ) <
847
      Diversity(A1 ). However, by uniqueness, we have Diversity(A′2 ) > Diversity(A1 ) since the last
848
      object of A′2 is not a duplicate, and the last object of A1 is a duplicate. So, we get a contradiction
849   which concludes the proof of the lemma.
