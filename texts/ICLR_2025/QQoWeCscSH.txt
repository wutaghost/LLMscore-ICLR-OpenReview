                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               End-to-end Large Speech Language Models (LSLMs) demonstrate strong poten-
012
               tial in response latency and speech comprehension capabilities, showcasing gen-
013
               eral intelligence across speech understanding tasks. However, the ability to follow
014            speech instructions has not been fully realized due to the lack of datasets and heav-
015            ily biased training tasks. Leveraging the rich ASR datasets, previous approaches
016            have used Large Language Models (LLMs) to continue the linguistic information
017            of speech to construct speech instruction datasets. Yet, due to the gap between
018            LLM-generated results and real human responses, the continuation methods fur-
019            ther amplify these shortcomings. Given the high costs of collecting and annotating
020            speech instruction datasets by humans, using speech synthesis to construct large-
021            scale speech instruction datasets has become a balanced and robust alternative.
022
               Although modern Text-To-Speech (TTS) models have achieved near-human-level
               synthesis quality, it is challenging to appropriately convert out-of-distribution text
023
               instruction to speech due to the limitations of the training data distribution in TTS
024
               models. To address this issue, we propose a query rewriting framework with multi-
025            LLM knowledge fusion, employing multiple agents to annotate and validate the
026            synthesized speech, making it possible to construct high-quality speech instruc-
027            tion datasets without relying on human annotation. Experiments show that this
028            method can transform text instructions into distributions more suitable for TTS
029            models for speech synthesis through zero-shot rewriting, increasing data usability
030            from 72% to 93%. It also demonstrates unique advantages in rewriting tasks that
031            require complex knowledge and context-related abilities.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   LLMs have demonstrated powerful performance in general intelligence, profoundly changing the
037   way humans interact with AI systems (OpenAI et al., 2024; Abdin et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024).
038   However, constrained by the text modality, existing LLMs are unable to meet the needs of rich
039
      real-world interactive scenarios, making it a natural idea to extend this general capability to more
      modalities (Fang et al., 2024). Recently, end-to-end LSLMs have shown great potential in terms of
040
      response latency and speech understanding, making it possible to extend this general performance to
041
      scenarios better suited for verbal interaction (Chu et al., 2023; 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Fathullah
042   et al., 2024). However, due to the lack of high-quality speech instruction datasets and heavily biased
043   training tasks, the ability of LSLMs to follow speech instructions is not fully realized, resulting in
044   a lack of intent perception in verbal interaction scenarios. Thus, building a high-quality large-scale
045   speech instruction dataset becomes a crucial foundation for advancing the ability to follow speech
046   instructions.
047
      Benefiting from abundant Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) datasets (Ardila et al., 2020; Pratap
048   et al., 2020; Guoguo Chen, 2021; Wang et al., 2024), early work aligned speech and text modali-
049   ties by having models repeat or recognize linguistic information in speech through textual instruc-
050   tions (Gong et al., 2023). To enhance the model’s understanding of paralinguistic information in
051   speech, traditional tasks in the speech domain (such as speaker classification, speech entity recogni-
052   tion, etc.) were integrated into training (Chu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024). These
053   training methods, which treat the speech modality as files rather than instructions, cause the model
      to lack the ability to follow speech instructions and lead to hallucinations due to the severe bias in


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      task distribution. To unlock the model’s potential to follow speech instructions, previous approaches
055   constructed speech instruction datasets by continuing the linguistic information of speech through
056   LLMs to restore the model’s instruction-following capabilities (Fathullah et al., 2024). However,
057   due to the gap between the generated results of LLMs and human-annotated ground truth answers,
058   using such continuation methods may further amplify these shortcomings.
059
      Unlike the image domain, which has abundant human-annotated data (Laurençon et al., 2024; Lin
060
      et al., 2015), constructing large-scale, manually narrated, and annotated speech instruction datasets
061   from scratch is challenging due to the high costs of collection and annotation. As a result, using
062   speech synthesis to construct datasets becomes a robust choice after weighing the pros and cons.
063   However, due to differences between TTS models and human speech narration, as well as halluci-
064   nation issues during the speech synthesis process, it is necessary to verify whether the synthesized
065   speech is linguistically equivalent to the text. On the other hand, since TTS models have a lim-
066   ited vocabulary, they cannot accurately convert out-of-distribution text, such as compound words,
067   abbreviations, or mathematical formulas, into speech, leading to the loss of linguistic information.
068   Previous work has shown that the semantic similarity between two texts can be calculated using em-
069   bedding models, demonstrating better robustness compared to methods like WER and more closely
070   aligned with how humans assess textual similarity (Muennighoff et al., 2023). Some high-quality
071   human-annotated datasets achieve superior annotation results by integrating the opinions of multi-
072   ple annotators, making the sample distribution more representative of real-world scenarios (Deitke
073   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2021). Meanwhile, query rewriting optimizes the text distribution without
074   significantly altering the semantics, making it better aligned with a specific distribution (Ye et al.,
075
      2024).
076   Inspired by this, we propose a query rewriting method with multi-LLM knowledge fusion, along
077   with multi-agent annotation and data quality validation. Specifically, we leverage LLMs’ general-
078   ization ability in zero-shot tasks to guide the rewriting of text instructions to fit the training distribu-
079   tion of TTS models. Additionally, we use multiple distinct LLMs to rewrite the text from different
080   perspectives. Next, we automatically extract linguistic information from the speech using multiple
081
      models and calculate its average similarity to the original text in the embedding space to avoid an-
      notation errors and achieve semantically optimal results. Finally, we fuse the knowledge of different
082
      LLMs in this zero-shot rewriting task to tackle challenging rewrites that require complex knowledge.
083
      Experimental results show that our method increased data usability from 71% to 93% and improved
084   the semantic similarity between the linguistic information in speech and the original text by 5%.
085
086   The main contribution of this paper can be summarised as follows:
087
             • We propose a query rewriting framework with multi-LLM knowledge fusion, along with
088
               a multi-agent annotation and validation method based on embedding space similarity, en-
089
               abling the low-cost, automated construction of high-quality speech instruction datasets.
090
091          • Experiments show that our proposed method demonstrates unique advantages in rule-based
092
               query rewriting, context-aware understanding, and complex knowledge integration, in-
               creasing the average data usability from 72% to 93%, while maintaining consistent per-
093
               formance across different validation methods.
094
095          • By comparing the training results using voice data of varying quality and alignment ob-
096            jectives, we validated the significant advantages of high-quality synthesized speech data in
097            alignment effectiveness and cross-modal consistency. It also demonstrates the importance
098
               of learning from real human responses to enhance the model’s ability to follow speech
               instructions.

103   Text-To-Speech TTS models have recently demonstrated impressive performance in terms of syn-
104   thesis quality and fluency. However, constrained by the reliance on reference speech, they lack
105   diversity in style (Wang et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023). Inspired by the image modality, using natu-
106   ral language descriptions of speaker styles to address this issue has become a promising approach.
107   However, due to the lack of large-scale speech datasets containing natural language descriptions, it
      is difficult to freely use natural language to control the style of speech synthesis. Lyth & King (2024)


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      constructed a large-scale speech dataset using multiple attributes for automated labeling, which sig-
109   nificantly improved the synthesis diversity of TTS models. In this work, we utilize GPT-4 (OpenAI
110   et al., 2024) to synthesize style descriptions and use Parler-TTS (Lacombe et al., 2024) to synthesize
111   speech.
112
113
      Speech-text alignment training in LSLMs Aligning speech and text modalities is crucial for
114   building the speech understanding capability of end-to-end LSLMs. Benefiting from abundant ASR
115   datasets (Ardila et al., 2020; Pratap et al., 2020; Guoguo Chen, 2021; Wang et al., 2024), early work
116   aligned the two modalities by instructing the model to repeat or recognize the linguistic information
117   in the speech (Shu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Gong et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023). However,
118   due to the severely biased task distribution, the model ignored following the instructions in the
119   speech and defaulted to performing language recognition (Fathullah et al., 2024). Recent work has
120   employed a continuation approach to construct speech instruction data, aiming to restore the model’s
121   ability to follow spoken instructions (Fathullah et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024). Due to the gap
122
      between the language model’s generated outputs and real human responses, using the continuation
      method to train the model can further amplify this deficiency (Seddik et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024).
123
      In this work, we propose a high-quality speech instruction synthesis method to address this issue.

      For the training data to train LSLMs, we calculate the data quality using Equation (5) and set a
288
      threshold t. The quality of all data used for training the model must exceed t. For data derived from
289

530
531
      In this paper, we propose a query rewriting method based on multi-LLM knowledge fusion, with
532   multi-agent annotation and validation for data quality. Experiments demonstrate that this method
533   consistently performs well across multiple datasets, improving the average data usability from 72%
534   to 93%. Through ablation studies, we analyzed the effectiveness of each component, and the results
535   show that different LLMs exhibit a certain degree of orthogonality in this zero-shot task. Moreover,
536   using multiple annotation agents helps to better reduce data quality evaluation errors and improper
537   data filtering caused by recognition and annotation errors of a single model. Our work enables the
538   automated construction of high-quality language instruction datasets.

1030   In this paper, we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) for training. For the training in Knowledge Fusion,
1031   we setting r = 8 and a = 16. The peak learning rate was set to 3e-4, and the cosine scheduler
1032   was used for learning rate adjustment. For the training in LSLMs Finetune, we set r = 8 and
1033   a = 32. The peak learning rate was set to 3e-5, and the cosine scheduler was used for learning rate
1034   adjustment. During training, we utilized 4 NVIDIA A40 GPUs, enabled gradient checkpointing to
1035   save memory, and applied gradient accumulation with backward occurring every 64 samples. The
1036   batch size per device was set to 1. For the generative evaluation phase, we evaluated our results on a
1037   single NVIDIA A40 GPU, setting top-p to 0.5, top-k to 20, repetition penalty to 1.1, and temperature
1038
       to 0.7. For speech synthesis, we use a single NVIDIA A40 GPU with the batch size set to 1.
