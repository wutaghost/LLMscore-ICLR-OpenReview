011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deploying reinforcement learning (RL) policies in real-world scenarios, partic-
014            ularly through offline learning approaches, faces challenges due to distribution
015            shifts from training environments. Past approaches have shown limitations such as
016            poor generalization to out-of-distribution (OOD) variations or requiring extensive
017            retraining on target domains. We propose Uncertainty-aware Adaptive RL, UARL,
018            a novel offline RL pipeline that enhances OOD detection and policy generalization
019
               without directly training in OOD environments. UARL frames distribution shifts
               as OOD problems and incorporates a new OOD detection method to quantify
020
               uncertainty. This approach enables iterative policy fine-tuning, starting with offline
021
               training on a limited state space and progressively expanding to more diverse
022            variations of the training environment through online interactions. We demonstrate
023            the effectiveness and robustness of UARL through extensive experiments on con-
024            tinuous control tasks, showing reliability in OOD detection compared to existing
025            method as well as improved performance and sample efficiency.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Robust Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers optimal solutions under relatively idealized theoretical
032   conditions (Iyengar, 2005; Nilim & El Ghaoui, 2005; Tamar et al., 2014). However, deploying these
033
      RL policies in real-world applications poses substantial safety concerns, as real-world environments
      often deviate from theoretical assumptions (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021; Garcıa & Fernández, 2015).
034
      The adoption of RL in real-world applications, such as robotics (Kober et al., 2013) and industrial
035
      control (Spielberg et al., 2019), has highlighted the importance of addressing these challenges.
036
037   The online nature of RL, requiring continuous interaction with the environment, often proves im-
038   practical in real-world settings due to associated risks and costs (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Levine et al.,
039
      2020). This challenge is compounded by Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) events, such as sensor noise
      or unmodeled environmental changes, which can substantially degrade the performance of trained
040
      policies (Zhao et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Danesh & Fern, 2021). Existing research on robustness
041
      and safety provides a foundation for tackling these issues, including robust control strategies that
042   optimize policies to handle worst-case scenarios (Iyengar, 2005; Nilim & El Ghaoui, 2005). However,
043   it introduces new challenges, particularly the distributional shift between the data collection policy
044   and the learned policy, which can be exacerbated during online fine-tuning (Lee et al., 2022; Zheng
045   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a).
046
      Domain randomization (DR) techniques enhance robustness by training in simulation and deploying
047
      the policy to real-world environments (Tobin et al., 2017). This approach has emerged as an
048   alternative, allowing training on pre-collected static datasets (Levine et al., 2020) from perturbed
049   environments in simulation. However, determining how to design the simulated environment to
050   reflect real-world variability accurately is often challenging. In addition, domain randomization
051   introduces significant safety concerns when testing potentially unsafe policies on physical hardware
052   (Mehta et al., 2020). Despite these advances, developing policies that ensure safety under unexpected
053   real-world conditions remains relatively underexplored in RL. Accelerating research on methods
      addressing these challenges is crucial for enabling safe real-world deployment of RL systems.

068   In this paper, we introduce Uncertainty-aware Adaptive RL (UARL), a novel approach that encourages
069   safe policy deployment under distribution shift without direct access to the target environment. As
070   shown in Fig. 1, UARL introduces a diversity term that consists of an ensemble of critics to quantify
071
      policy uncertainty and iteratively refines high-uncertainty regions (of the state space). UARL
      integrates nominal and repulsive datasets in the replay buffer and employs a verification module for
072
      safe adaptation to OOD scenarios. We validate UARL on MuJoCo benchmark tasks (Todorov et al.,
073
      2012), assessing performance, safety, and sample efficiency.
074
075   Contributions. Our key contributions are (1) a method for quantifying uncertainty and adapting
076   policy without direct interactions in the OOD environments, and (2) an efficient offline-to-
077
      (semi)online (O2O) RL strategy to balance the replay buffer. Unlike traditional O2O methods that
      transition directly from offline learning to online fine-tuning in the target environment, our approach
078
      leverages simulated environments to gradually expose the policy to increasing levels of environmental
079
      variability, eliminating the need to fine-tune policies with immediate real-world interaction. Compared
080   to domain randomization, our method avoids the risk of validating potentially unsuitable policies in
081   OOD environments, thereby improving the safety and reliability of RL in real-world applications.
082   These contributions collectively advance the field of safe and robust RL, offering a novel framework
083   that enhances policy generalization and safety across diverse simulated environments, while providing
084   a foundation for addressing the challenges of distribution shifts and OOD events in RL1 .

      2       R ELATED W ORK
087
088
      In this section, we provide a summary of related work in offline RL, offline-to-online RL, and
089
      ensemble methods. A comprehensive discussion of the Related Work is presented in App. C.
090
091   Offline RL. Traditional RL allows policies to interact freely with environments to discover optimal
092   strategies (Sutton & Barto, 2018). In contrast, offline RL addresses scenarios where online interaction
093
      is impractical or risky, learning solely from pre-collected datasets gathered by a behavior policy
      (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2020; Ernst et al., 2005; Kumar et al., 2019; Levine et al., 2020;
094
      Lange et al., 2012; Kostrikov et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020; Tarasov et al., 2024). This approach
095
      enables applications in domains where real-time learning might be unsafe. However, offline RL faces
096   a significant challenge in distributional shift, where the training data may not accurately represent the
097   deployment environment, potentially leading to suboptimal or unsafe behavior. Several approaches
098   address this issue by encouraging the learned policy to resemble the behavior policy (Jaques et al.,
099   2019; Wu et al., 2019; Siegel et al., 2020; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), promoting caution through action
100   alignment. Other methods explore training conservative critics for more cautious reward estimates
101   (Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021) or diversifying critics within the actor-critic framework to
102   improve robustness (An et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021).
103   Offline-to-Online RL. Building upon offline RL, O2O RL leverages previously collected offline
104   datasets to accelerate online RL training. This approach first pre-trains a policy with offline RL
105   and then continues to fine-tune it with additional online interactions (Nair et al., 2020; LEI et al.,
106   2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2022). However, naive O2O RL is often unstable due to the
107

      distributional shift between the offline dataset and online interactions. To address this, researchers
109   have proposed techniques such as balanced sampling (Lee et al., 2022), actor-critic alignment (Yu &
110   Zhang, 2023), adaptive conservatism (Wang et al., 2024), return lower-bounding (Nakamoto et al.,
111   2023), adaptive update strategies (Zheng et al., 2023), introducing online policies alongside offline
112   ones (Zhang et al., 2023a), and using weighted replay buffers with density ratio estimation (Lee

114   distributional shifts, our paper explicitly considers policy safety, particularly for OOD samples. Our
115   work builds upon weighted samples but offers a more efficient solution by leveraging learned critics
116   to assign weights to offline and online samples during fine-tuning.
117   Ensemble Methods combine multiple models to enhance performance and are widely used in ML
118   applications (Goodfellow et al., 2016). In OOD detection, ensembles offer robustness by leveraging
119   model diversity (Lee & Chung, 2020; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). While random initialization
120   contributes to diversity, controlled diversity is often achieved by manipulating the loss function
121   (Wabartha et al., 2020; Mehrtens et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2020; Pang et al., 2019). This allows
122   for fine-tuning ensemble behavior, particularly through regularization techniques or bias-variance
123
      decomposition (Wood et al., 2023; Arpit et al., 2022), enhancing both ID accuracy and OOD
      detection by ensuring disagreement in uncertain regions, known as “repulsive locations” (Hafner
124
      et al., 2020). In RL, ensembles play a key role in optimizing exploration strategies. Osband et al.
125
      (2016) introduced ensemble critics for more efficient exploration, and Lee et al. (2021) proposed
126   leveraging Q-ensembles to augment Q-value estimates using the mean and standard deviation of the
127   ensemble. These methods also support uncertainty estimation, crucial for handling OOD scenarios in
128   RL, by analyzing discordance between ensemble members’ predictions (Wabartha et al., 2020; Liu
129   et al., 2019; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Jain et al., 2020). Building on these foundations, we
130   employ critic disparity in actor-critic algorithms to effectively detect OOD instances, combining the
131   strengths of ensemble methods in both OOD handling and RL optimization.

173   3.2   E NSEMBLE D IVERSIFICATION
174
      As detailed in Sec. 2, ensembles show improved efficacy when supplemented with an extra loss term,
175
      beyond initial weight randomization, to regulate diversity. The DENN method (Wabartha et al., 2020)
176
      implements this approach by introducing a new diversity term, Ldiv , into the loss function. This
177   method utilizes the concept of repulsive locations, strategically selected data points designed to
178   induce disagreement among ensemble models, particularly at the boundary of the training distribution,
179   where the model’s predictions are less certain and may exhibit higher variance.
180
      Let X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xn } denote the inputs and Y be the corresponding output space of the nominal
181
      dataset. One approach to defining repulsive locations is to add noise to the input data: X ′ = {x + ϵ :
182
      x ∈ X }, where ϵ represents noise. Alternatively, an entirely different dataset can serve as repulsive
183   locations (Hendrycks et al., 2019). By introducing these repulsive locations, uncertainty is enforced at
184   the boundary of the training distribution, effectively propagating into OOD regions, thereby enhancing
185   OOD detection crucial for robust model performance. To leverage these repulsive locations, DENN
186   employs an ensemble of multiple models, each denoted as fi : X → Y . DENN promotes diversity
187   by constraining each fi to differ from a reference function g : X → Y , which was trained once on
188   the nominal dataset. This reference function g serves as a consistent baseline for diversity promotion.
189   DENN augments the conventional supervised learning loss function by:

355   Comparing other work on uncertainty-aware RL, our work explicitly identifies scenarios when the
356   policy encounters OOD situations without direct interaction in the target environment. This is a
357   crucial safety feature for real-world deployment. Additionally, the progressive nature also allows the
358   agent to adapt to increasingly complex environments while maintaining performance stability.
359   Our iterative training pipeline works with any off-policy RL algorithm using an ensemble of critics,
360   offering a flexible framework for improving robustness and safety in real-world RL. Our formulation
361   presented in Eq. 5 solely introduces a “diversity term” into the policy evaluation step; therefore,
362   UARL seamlessly integrates into any offline RL algorithm. In Sec. 5, we incorporated UARL into
363   CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), AWAC (Nair et al., 2020), and TD3BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021).

      Evaluation Criteria. Our comprehensive evaluation framework for UARL focuses on performance,
398
      robustness, and sample efficiency. We introduce systematic randomization to three key hyperparame-
399   ters in each environment: initial noise scale, friction coefficient, and the agent’s mass. By isolating
400   the effect of each hyperparameter, we assess the method’s adaptability to dynamic conditions. Specif-
401   ically, the noise scale is multiplied by 102 , while both the friction coefficient and the agent’s mass are
402   increased proportionately per iteration (App. A.2 provides further details). To ensure robust results,
403   we aggregate the outcomes of 5 random seeds, reporting the mean and a 95% confidence interval. We
404   evaluate the baselines and UARL based on the following criteria: (1) Cumulative return on evaluation
405   environments during training, which measures overall performance; (2) OOD accuracy, defined as
406   the critic variance’s ability to differentiate ID and OOD environments; (3) Sample efficiency, defined
407
      as the number of samples required to reach a pre-defined return threshold.
408   Dataset. Although the D4RL dataset (Fu et al., 2020) is widely used in offline RL, it lacks behavior
409   policy checkpoints needed for generating the repulsive dataset required by UARL. Thus, we created
410   a new dataset using arbitrary behavior policies. Key differences from D4RL include: (1) We use
411   MuJoCo v4 with Gymnasium, fixing bugs in the v2 version used by D4RL (Towers et al., 2023); (2)
412   We extend beyond D4RL’s three locomotion environments to include Ant and Swimmer; (3) For the
413
      behavior policy, we train SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) until convergence, generating 999 rollouts per
      environment. Despite these changes, our dataset’s metrics closely match those of D4RL. Therefore,
414
      we expect our findings to generalize to D4RL once the necessary behavior policies become available.
415
      The real-world dataset Dw is obtained by running the behavior policy in the environment with a wide
416   range of randomized parameter values, ensuring reflecting real-world variability.
417
418   Diversity Loss Hyperparameters. Selecting the hyperparameters λ and δ in the diversity loss term,
419
      LRL
        div (Eq. 5), is key to balancing the RL objective with promoting diversity among critics. We set
      λ adaptively such that the LRLdiv contributes approximately 10% of the total loss, striking a balance
420
      between the primary learning objective and the diversification goal. This choice ensures that the
421
      diversity term has a meaningful impact without overshadowing the original objective. As suggested
422
      by Wabartha et al. (2020), an effective range for δ is [10−3 , 10−1/2 ], from which we selected 10−2 .
423   While these choices are somewhat arbitrary, they are guided by the intuition that LRL    div should be
424   significant enough to influence learning without dominating it. The relative scale of λ allows the
425   agent to maintain its focus on task performance while still benefiting from the improved uncertainty
426   estimation provided by diverse critics. Our experiments show that these initial λ and δ values offer a
427   strong baseline, with further fine-tuning yielding additional improvements (see App. B.3 for details).

509   5.3               E FFECT OF BALANCING R EPLAY B UFFER
510
511   Finally, we evaluate the impact of the replay buffer balancing mechanism employed in UARL (Alg. 1).
512   Fig. 6 shows the effect of removing the replay buffer balancing feature while fine-tuning the policy in
513   the O2O RL setting. In both Ant-v4 and HalfCheetah-v4, the balancing mechanism consistently
514
      outperforms its counterparts across all three randomized hyperparameters. UARL with balancing
      shows faster learning, higher average returns, and less “unlearning” (overriding of previously learned
515
      behaviors), both in Ant-v4 (especially at the beginning of learning) and HalfCheetah-v4. Overall,
516
      UARL with balancing demonstrates increasing gains throughout the entire training process.
517
518   The results suggest that balancing the replay buffer leads to more stable and efficient learning,
519   particularly crucial in O2O RL where transitioning from offline pretraining to online fine-tuning
520
      can be challenging. The consistent improvement across various environmental hyperparameters
      indicates that the balancing mechanism’s benefits are robust to task dynamics variations. In essence,
521
      our experiments show that the replay buffer balancing mechanism is a key component in enhancing
522
      UARL’s performance, accelerating early-stage fine-tuning, and improving overall performance across
523   different environments and task hyperparameters.

526   6        C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , & F UTURE W ORK
527
528   Our proposed pipeline, UARL, addresses real-world RL deployment challenges through targeted,
529   iterative adaptation in simulation. It prevents trial and error via a representative dataset (Dw ) from
530
      the target environment, deploying only when confident. UARL improves efficiency and robustness
      with precise OOD detection, balanced O2O RL sampling, and gradual environment variation, without
531
      extensive randomization. Its uncertainty estimation is key for detecting OOD scenarios, particularly
532
      in robotics, where agents must navigate unexpected variations.
533
534   Despite these advantages, UARL has a few drawbacks. As a pilot study, we only explored randomizing
535   a single parameter at the time, while a more elaborate scheme could prove useful for real-world
536
      deployment. Moreover, UARL requires a real-world dataset Dw to determine when to exit Alg. 2. An
      incomplete or unrepresentative Dw could lead to overconfident policies. We summarize the limitation
537
      here and discuss in detail in App. D.
538
539   Future work will focus on automatizing the randomization sequence and validating the robustness of
      UARL beyond simulated environments, to real robotic systems.

1156   A.2     R ANDOMIZED H YPERPARAMETER S CALES
1157
       Due to the variety of dynamics and physics of the agent in each environment, the range of randomized
1158
       hyperparameters should be different. For instance, when initializing the Ant-v4 environment, the
1159   initial noise scale is 1 × 10−1 , while it is 5 × 10−3 for Hopper-v4. Because of this, we consider
1160   various ranges to scale the randomized hyperparameters. The values provided in Table 5 show the
1161   scales of the hyperparameters during each iteration of our algorithm.
1162
       The selection of these hyperparameter ranges is based on careful consideration of each environment’s
1163
       characteristics and the MuJoCo physics engine’s properties (Todorov et al., 2012):
1164
1165           • Initial Noise Scale: This hyperparameter affects the initial state variability. For more stable
1166             agents like Ant-v4 and HalfCheetah-v4, we start with a smaller scale (1 × 10−5 ) and
1167             gradually increase it to the default value (1 × 10−1 ). For less stable agents like Hopper-v4
1168             and Walker2d-v4, we begin with an even smaller scale (5 × 10−7 ) to ensure initial stability.
1169           • Friction Coefficient: In our experiments, we specifically modify the friction coefficient
1170             between the agent and the ground. MuJoCo utilizes a pyramidal friction cone approximation,
1171             where this coefficient directly affects contact dynamics and determines how the agent
1172             interacts with its environment. We maintain the default friction values initially, then gradually
1173             increase them to challenge the agent’s locomotion and stability. For Ant-v4, we make
1174             moderate increases due to its quadrupedal locomotion’s reliance on ground contact. For
1175             HalfCheetah-v4, where smooth forward motion is key, smaller increments are used. Higher
1176
                 initial friction coefficients are assigned to Hopper-v4 and Walker2d-v4 to stabilize their
                 balance, and these are increased substantially to test the agents under more challenging
1177
                 conditions.
1178
1179           • Agent’s Mass: In MuJoCo, an agent’s mass is determined by its constituent geoms. We
1180
                 scale the mass of all geoms uniformly to maintain the agent’s mass distribution. For
                 Ant-v4 and Swimmer-v4, we use larger mass increments (up to 15x) as these agents are
1181
                 inherently more stable due to their multi-limbed or water-based nature. For bipedal agents
1182
                 like HalfCheetah-v4, Hopper-v4, and Walker2d-v4, we use smaller increments to avoid

       B.3   UARL H YPERPARAMETER S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS
1891
1892   This subsection examines the sensitivity of UARL to its key hyperparameters: the diversity co-
1893   efficient λ and the diversity scale δ. These hyperparameters balance the standard RL objec-
1894   tive with the goal of promoting diversity among critics. We evaluate various combinations of
1895   λ ∈ {1%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%} and δ ∈ {10−3 , 10−2 , 10−1 } across two settings: Ant-v4, focusing
1896   on the agent’s mass hyperparameter, and HalfCheetah-v4, focusing on the initial noise scale. We
1897   tested these combinations with three baseline algorithms (AWAC, CQL, and TD3BC), using 5 random
1898
       seeds for each configuration, resulting in a total of 450 experimental runs.
1899   The hyperparameter selection process is grounded in a systematic exploration of the trade-off between
1900   diversity regularization and policy optimization. By varying the diversity coefficient λ and scale δ,
1901   we aim to understand how these parameters influence the learning dynamics of UARL. The chosen
1902   ranges reflect a careful consideration of the potential impact of diversity-promoting mechanisms on
1903   the RL objective.
1904   Fig. 18 and Fig. 19 demonstrate the influence of varying λ and δ values on agent performance
1905   during training, on Ant-v4’s mass and HalfCheetah-v4’s initial noise scale, respectively. While our
1906   chosen default configuration performs well, the results indicate the potential for further performance
1907   enhancement through careful hyperparameter tuning. The configurations with the most extreme
1908   values, specifically λ ∈ {15%, 20%} and δ = 10−1 , tend to have the most detrimental effect on
1909   performance. At higher diversity coefficients, the introduced regularization becomes increasingly
1910
       aggressive, potentially introducing noise that disrupts the learning process. This suggests an inherent
       trade-off where excessive diversity constraints can impede the algorithm’s ability to converge to
1911
       optimal behavior. This suggests that moderate values for these hyperparameters generally yield better
1912

       B.5   C OMPARISON WITH SOTA OOD BASELINES
2109
2110   The following experiment evaluates the effectiveness of our method compared to state-of-the-art
2111   baselines that focus on robustness and uncertainty estimation. Specifically, we compare our method
2112   against PBRL (Bai et al., 2022) and RORL (Yang et al., 2022), which which are designed to enhance
2113   robustness, as well as EDAC (An et al., 2021) and DARL (Zhang et al., 2023b), which are focused on
2114   improving uncertainty estimation.
2115   PBRL penalizes Q-values in high-uncertainty regions by leveraging the standard deviation of ensemble
2116   predictions, promoting conservative learning. To further enhance stability and mitigate extrapolation
2117   errors, PBRL incorporates OOD samples into the training buffer. These OOD samples, including
2118   states from the offline dataset and actions outside the dataset’s support, regularize the Q-function
2119   in uncertain regions. By applying stronger penalties for OOD actions in high-uncertainty areas,
2120   PBRL achieves robust and reliable policy learning. In contrast, RORL emphasizes robustness
2121   against adversarial observation perturbations and OOD states and actions. It employs a conservative
2122   smoothing technique to regularize value functions and policies near the dataset’s support, penalizing
2123
       overestimation in unfamiliar regions. RORL further introduces adversarial state perturbations within
       a predefined radius during training to test and improve robustness. Unlike PBRL, which focuses
2124
       on OOD actions for ID states, RORL penalizes both OOD states and actions, providing a more
2125
       comprehensive form of conservatism. Additionally, RORL minimizes policy distribution differences
2126   under perturbed states, ensuring stability in adversarial scenarios, making it particularly effective in
2127   environments with challenging observation perturbations and limited data coverage.
2128
2129
       EDAC aims to prevent over-estimation of Q-values during training with OOD samples, ensuring
       a conservative learning process. This is achieved by diversifying the gradients of Q-functions in
2130
       an ensemble, thereby reducing the alignment of Q-function gradients. By minimizing their cosine
2131
       similarity, EDAC increases the variance in Q-value predictions for OOD actions, which enhances
2132   the ability to penalize uncertain or risky actions. This uncertainty-aware approach helps avoid
2133   over-confident predictions, leading to more robust offline RL. On the other hand, DARL employs a
2134   non-parametric particle-based cross-entropy estimator that uses k-nearest neighbor search to measure
2135   uncertainty, projecting data into a distance-preserving, low-dimensional space to make the process
2136   efficient. This estimator allows DARL to accurately quantify uncertainty by capturing the relationship
2137   between samples in the dataset. Building on this, DARL incorporates adaptive truncated quantile
2138   critics, which adjust the extent of underestimation for Q-values based on sample uncertainty, ensuring
2139   conservative value estimation for high-uncertainty samples.
2140   Fig. 20 extends the results from the main paper’s Fig. 4 by including the performance of PBRL,
2141   RORL, EDAC, and DARL in OOD detection. As shown, PBRL and RORL struggle to effectively
2142   differentiate between ID and OOD dynamics, leading to overly optimistic policies in high-uncertainty
2143   regions and reduced robustness in OOD scenarios. EDAC and DARL demonstrate some success
2144   in distinguishing ID from OOD cases, but their performance is inconsistent across settings. For
2145   instance, EDAC accurately separates ID and OOD samples during the 1st iteration of fine-tuning
2146
       (2nd column), but achieves only 30% accuracy in distinguishing ID from OOD samples during the
       2nd iteration. Similarly, DARL performs well during the offline training phase (1st column) but fails
2147
       to maintain consistent performance thereafter. In contrast, UARL consistently outperforms these
2148
       baselines, reliably distinguishing ID and OOD dynamics across all settings, resulting in more robust
2149   and dependable policy behavior.

2217   C    R ELATED W ORK
2218
2219   Safe and Robust RL addresses critical challenges in high-stakes applications like robotics (Garcıa &
2220   Fernández, 2015). While safe RL focuses on avoiding harmful actions by imposing policy constraints
2221
       during training and deployment (Heger, 1994), robust RL aims to maintain performance stability under
       uncertainties or adversarial perturbations (Iyengar, 2005; Nilim & El Ghaoui, 2005). To enhance
2222
       robustness, domain randomization techniques train agents in varied, randomized environments
2223
       (Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Tobin et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2020; Mozian et al., 2020). However, this
2224   approach can be computationally expensive and challenging to tune for complex scenarios (Mehta
2225   et al., 2020). Additionally, those methods assume that policy can be trained in OOD scenarios, which
2226   poses significant risks in real-world applications.
2227
       Our proposed method, UARL, addresses these challenges by explicitly detecting OOD scenarios and
2228
       adapting policies based on uncertainty estimation without direct interactions in OOD environments.
2229
       This approach improves both safety and robustness without incurring the computational overhead
2230   associated with traditional domain randomization techniques.
2231
2232   Uncertainty-aware RL While there are several uncertainty-aware methods in offline RL, such
2233
       as MOBILE (Sun et al., 2023), PBRL (Bai et al., 2022), and RORL (Yang et al., 2022), which
       penalize OOD actions using uncertainty quantifiers, we respectfully distinguish our approach. Unlike
2234
       MOBILE, PBRL, and RORL, which primarily focus on robustness by penalizing uncertain actions to
2235
       avoid OOD scenarios during training, our work focuses on explicit OOD detection during deployment.
2236   This difference is crucial: while these methods aim to build robust policies that can operate in OOD
2237   conditions, our method is designed to identify when a system is operating in OOD situations prior to
2238   policy deployment, which is critical in safety-critical systems like robotics.
2239
       Our progressive environmental randomization method builds an agent’s ability to distinguish between
2240
       ID and OOD states, actively detecting when novel or uncertain scenarios arise. This capability
2241
       allows the agent to make informed decisions, such as requesting human intervention or guiding data
2242   collection for further policy refinement. While robustness is a beneficial side effect of our iterative
2243   fine-tuning, it is secondary to our primary goal of reliable OOD detection.
2244
2245
       Curriculum learning in RL aims to improve agent learning by progressively exposing the agent
       to increasingly complex tasks or environments (Narvekar et al., 2020; Li et al., 2024). Traditional
2246
       approaches involve manually designing a sequence of tasks with increasing difficulty, where agents
2247
       learn foundational skills before tackling more challenging scenarios (Graves et al., 2017). Recent
2248   advances have explored more nuanced transfer methods, such as REvolveR (Liu et al., 2022), which
2249   introduces a continuous evolutionary approach to policy transfer by interpolating between source
2250   and target robots through a sequence of intermediate configurations. While REvolveR focuses on
2251   morphological and kinematic transitions, it still shares the fundamental limitation of most curriculum
2252   learning approaches: relying on predefined task progressions that may not capture the unpredictability
2253   of real-world environments.
2254   In contrast, our approach focuses on uncertainty-driven adaptation, dynamically expanding the
2255   exploration space based on real-time uncertainty estimation rather than a predetermined task hierarchy.
2256   Unlike curriculum RL’s structured task progression, UARL enables continuous, adaptive learning that
2257   more closely mimics real-world environmental variability, particularly in scenarios with unpredictable
2258   and out-of-distribution events. Critically, our method avoids the safety risks associated with direct
2259   policy refinement in target domains by using an ensemble of critics to evaluate policy suitability
2260   without dangerous direct interactions.

2280   D    L IMITATIONS
2281
2282   While UARL demonstrates promising results in enhancing safety in RL, several key limitations
2283   warrant discussion.
2284   Table 7 highlights the computational overhead associated with UARL. The approach introduces
2285   moderate resource demands, with a 10% increase in training time and 50% higher memory usage
2286   compared to baseline methods which depends on the size of the repulsive dataset. However, these
2287   costs are offset by the potential for improved policy safety and robustness, particularly in detecting
2288   and adapting to OOD scenarios.
2289   A primary limitation of UARL is the sequential randomization of a single hyperparameter during
2290   iterative adaptation. While this controlled exploration aids stability, it may fail to capture the complex
2291   interplay between multiple environmental parameters. Future work could explore simultaneous
2292   multi-parameter randomization to better simulate real-world uncertainties.
2293
       The method also relies on manually defined parameter ranges for randomization, determined using
2294   domain expertise. This reliance may limit generalizability across diverse tasks and environments.
2295   Developing an automated mechanism to adaptively determine parameter ranges could enhance
2296   scalability and reduce human intervention.
2297
       Another challenge is the dependence on a proxy dataset Dw from the target environment, which
2298
       critically influences uncertainty estimation and policy refinement. If this dataset is incomplete or
2299
       unrepresentative, it can lead to suboptimal adaptations or overconfident policies. Techniques to ensure
2300   dataset quality and representativeness will be crucial for robust performance.
2301
2302   Finally, while UARL has demonstrated effectiveness on MuJoCo benchmark tasks, its applicability
2303
       to more complex, high-dimensional, real-world scenarios remains an open question. Extensive
       validation across diverse robotic and control domains will be necessary to establish its broader
2304
       relevance and effectiveness.
