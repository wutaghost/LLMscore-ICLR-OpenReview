                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               We explore leveraging large multi-modal models (LMMs) and Text2image models
010
               to build a more general embodied agent. LMMs excel in planning long-horizon
011            tasks over symbolic abstractions but struggle with grounding in the physical world,
012            often failing to accurately identify object positions in images. A bridge is needed
013            to connect LMMs to the physical world. The paper proposes a novel approach,
014            egocentric vision language planning (EgoPlan), to handle long-horizon tasks from
015            an egocentric perspective in varying household scenarios. This pipeline leverages a
016            diffusion model to simulate the fundamental dynamics between states and actions,
017            discusses how to integrate computer vision related techniques like style transfer and
018            optical flow to enhance ability of modeling spatial states and generalization across
019
               different environmental dynamics. The LMM serves as a planner, breaking down
               instructions into sub-goals and selecting actions based on their alignment with
020
               these sub-goals, thus enabling more generalized and effective decision-making.
021
               By using LMM, we can output text actions, using a series of mechanisms such as
022            reflection to perform high-level task decomposition and low-level action output
023            end-to-end. Experiments show that EgoPlan improves long-horizon task success
024            rates from the egocentric view compared to baselines across household scenarios.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The advent of large language models (LLMs) (et al., 2024b; Touvron et al., 2023) and large multi-
030   modal models (LMMs) (202, 2023; Girdhar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023) has
031   revolutionized the field of artificial intelligence. Their strong reasoning (Wang et al., 2023b; Wei et al.,
032   2023) and powerful generalization capabilities allow them to be directly applied in various scenarios.
033
      In the next step toward artificial general intelligence (AGI), researchers are considering enabling
      large models (LMs), especially LMMs, to break through the world expressed by text and images
034
      to interact with the physical world. They aim to build a general embodied agent that intelligently
035
      interacts with the physical world.
036
037   LMMs have demonstrated an impressive capability of planning for long-horizon tasks over symbolic
038   abstraction in the physical world (Wake et al., 2024). However, there’s still a piece of the puzzle
039
      missing. They have struggled to ground the text world with the physical world. For example, GPT-4V
      often fails to accurately identify objects’ positions in images. LMMs seem to know what to do next
040
      but do not understand how the world works. A world model (dynamics model) is hence needed to
041
      connect the LMMs to the physical world. There are two potential solutions. One is to implicitly
042   integrate environmental dynamics into the LMMs, that is, fine-tuning the LMMs based on a vast
043   amount of state-action sequences, such as PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023) and RT-2 (et al., 2023).
044   However, directly training large models requires extensive data and computational resources. The
045   other is to explicitly introduce a pre-trained world model, e.g., Text2image models (Radford et al.,
046   2021; Saharia et al., 2022), which can be used by LMMs as an auxiliary tool. Our work explores the
047   second path. We try to answer the question: can we leverage the LMMs and Text2image model to
048   build a more general embodied agent?
049   Some works already train Text2image/video models as world models for decision-making. However,
050   there still exist several limitations. First, their task scenarios often involve object manipulation, a fully
051   observable setting. This is uncommon in real-world scenarios, and their methods seem to struggle to
052   adapt to other practical scenes. For example, SuSIE (Black et al., 2023) and VLP (Du et al., 2023b)
053   require generating images several steps ahead, yet the error introduced by long-range predictions is
      substantial for most partially observed scenarios, e.g., autonomous driving. In contrast, we focus

054
      on a more challenging, partially observable setting. The embodied agent, like humans, tends to
055   complete more complex and comprehensive tasks, e.g., household tasks including manipulation and
056   navigation, from the egocentric view. Second, their framework has limited capability, mainly reflected
057   in three aspects: (i) their low-level policies are tailored to specific tasks, and generalize the polices to
058   new dynamics can lead to policy collapse; (ii) a key challenge in world models is how to represent
059   the mapping between state transitions and action information. In the aforementioned work, action
060   information is typically represented in text form. However, this representation is too coarse, making it
061   difficult to establish a mapping between coarse-grained text actions and fine-grained state transitions,
062   especially in comprehensive tasks and partially observable environments. (iii) The dynamics can
063
      vary for different entities even given the same action described by the text, e.g., turn left, due to the
      inherent different in these entities. The Text2image/Text2video world model lacks individual motion
064
      pattern information and cannot be generalized accurately to dynamics of other environments that are
065
      out of the training dataset. We hope the agent can generalize to different dynamics within the fixed
066   household scenario.
067
068   In this work, we propose egocentric vision language planning (EgoPlan), a general embodied agent to
069
      perform long-horizon tasks from the egocentric view and slove these three questions mentioned above.
      In our approach, we draw on perspectives and methods from the field of computer vision to enhance
070
      the world model. In a range of studies, optical flow is frequently utilized for human/robot action
071
      prediction (Ko et al., 2023) and scene understanding (Yang & Ramanan, 2020). This underscores the
072   rich information regarding actions and state transitions contained within optical flow data. Compared
073   to traditional text-based actions in world models, integrating optical flow into these models for
074   task planning could enhance spatial orientation understanding in navigation tasks and facilitate the
075   modeling of object motion prediction in manipulation tasks. Style transfer in computer vision enables
076   the integration of diverse content semantics and fine-grained image styles using a limited number of
077   samples. This capability can significantly enhance the world model’s ability to perform fine-grained
078   texture modeling and generation across different scenes.
079   We conduct a comprehensive evaluation and analysis of each module of the embodied agent. Em-
080   pirically, we demonstrate the high quality of image generation by the world model and the high
081   accuracy of optical flow prediction. Subsequently, we verify the world model’s effectiveness in aiding
082   decision-making in more complex tasks. Lastly, we confirm the method’s generalization capabilities
083   in a different environment. Our major contributions are summarized as follows:
084
085   • We have collected a dataset on Virtualhome, which views an action of the agent as a trajectory and
086     provides egocentric observations each time-step and fine-grained action information, visualising
087     optical flow, depth maps and semantic segmentation maps at each time step in the trajectory, which
088     will provide data support for navigation and manipulation tasks in the embodied environment.
089   • We propose EgoPlan, a framework for complex task planning that combines LMM and a world
090     model that predicts an egocentric view of the scene at the next time step after an action is executed
091     and the scene of the subgoal is completed. Optical flow information is computationally invariant to
092     different scenes and styles motion. Introduce optical flow into the world model leads the world
093
        model more sensitive to action position changes and adapt to scene changes during navigation.
        Then we borrow the idea of style transfer in computer vision and adopt the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
094
        model to fine-tune our diffusion world model by a small number of sample images, so as to enhance
095
        the ability of our framework to achieve few-shot generalization in different embodied scenarios.
096
097
      • For the action selection and decision-making module, we employ the LMM as the execution
        module in both the high-level task decomposition and low-level action selection components. The
098
        LMM’s strong multimodal understanding, reasoning capabilities, and text output abilities enable
099
        us to utilize a series of reflection and summarization mechanisms to accomplish tasks, while also
100     ensuring the agent inherits this ability of generalizing the downstream polices to new dynamics. We
101     demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework through LMM+world model planning experiments
102     on comprehensive tasks.

      2.1   D IFFUSION M ODEL
109
110   The diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2022) has been extensively studied in the field
111   of image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022) and image
112   editing (Gal et al., 2022; Hertz et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022). Diffusion models can achieve a high
113   degree of control during the image generation. In more detail, InstructPix2Pix (InstructP2P) (Brooks
114   et al., 2023) trains a conditional diffusion model that, given an input image and text instruction for
115   how to edit it, generates the edited image. ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b) is widely used to control
116
      the style of the generated image by using various forms of prior information, e.g., edge information
      and segmentation. By adding LoRA or adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019) modules to the network, the
117
      model trained on one data distribution can also be transferred to other data distributions (different
118
      visual styles) through a few picture examples. The images produced by current diffusion models are
119   of very high quality, highly realistic, and easily controllable. It prompts various fields to consider
120   using these generated images to assist in accomplishing other tasks. Our paper adopts the diffusion
121   model to generate task subgoals and predict the image of the next state for decision-making.
122
123   2.2   W ORLD M ODEL FOR D ECISION - MAKING
124
125   The world model is used to model the dynamics of the environment. It is crucial for building au-
126   tonomous agents and enabling intelligent interactions in various scenarios. However, developing
127   a precise world model remains a significant challenge in model-based decision-making. The ad-
128
      vancements in diffusion-based world models are reshaping how we model physical motion laws in
      real-world settings, particularly in robotics. UniPi (Du et al., 2023a) frames the decision-making
129
      problem in robotics as a Text2video task. The generated video is fed into an inverse dynamics model
130
      (IDM) that extracts underlying low-level control actions, which are executed in simulation or by a
131   real robot agent. Video Language Planning (VLP) (Du et al., 2023b) introduces a novel method for
132   task planning that integrates video generation with tree search algorithms. This methodology lets
133   robots plan over longer horizons by visualizing future actions and outcomes. Unlike previous works,
134   SuSIE (Black et al., 2023) leverages pre-trained image-editing models to predict the hypothetical fu-
135   ture frame. A low-level goal-reaching policy is trained on robot data to reach this hypothetical future
136   frame. Since one goal frame prediction does not require the model to understand the intricacies of the
137   robot’s low-level precisely dynamics, it should facilitate transfer from other data sources, e.g., human
138   videos. RoboDreamer (Zhou et al., 2024) advances the field by utilizing video diffusion to formulate
139
      plans combining actions and objects, solving novel tasks in unexplored robotic environments. We
      find it unrealistic to apply the Text2video model to partially observed scenarios. Moreover, it is still
140
      hard to predict the goal frame several steps ahead, as the shift in perspective could be significant.
141
      Therefore, we adopt the Text2image model to accurately predict the short-range outcome for one-step
142   planning.

      3     VH-1.5M DATASET
146
      Most datasets related to embodied agents, e.g., RT-X (et al., 2024a) and RH20T (Fang et al., 2023),
147
      employ the third-person view to avoid the visual occlusion issue, thus lacking data regarding the
148
      egocentric view (first-person view). There are some datasets, e.g., Alfred (Shridhar et al., 2020)
149   and Procthor (Deitke et al., 2022), that adopt a first-person perspective, however, they simplify
150   the state transition by assuming instantaneous completion of actions, which fails to mimic the
151   dynamics changes in real-world environments. We propose the VH-1.5M dataset based on the
152   VirtualHome (Puig et al., 2018; 2020) environment to address these limitations.
153
      We construct our dataset VH-1.5M in the VirtualHome environment, which comprises 50 distinct
154
      houses. Each house contains approximately 300 interactive objects, and the embodied agent can
155   perform more than 10 actions. Note that the VirtualHome environment is a simulator tailored for
156   embodied agents, offering a detailed simulation of a residential living scenario. It enables a range of
157   household tasks, e.g., navigation and object manipulation.
158
      The VH-1.5M dataset is organized in a structured manner, encapsulating the relationship between
159
      actions, houses, agents, and trajectories. Each task sequence entry follows a hierarchical structure,
160
      e.g., "/open/house_0/Female4/2_fridge" (female4 open the fridge2 in house0).
161

179   4     M ETHOD
180
181   Our embodied agent, EgoPlan, takes visual observation xt of the scene at the current timestep t and a
182   natural language goal g as inputs and outputs an action at to interact with the environment. Note that
183   the xt only partially represents the current environment state. In addition, the agent uses encapsulated
184
      skills as actions, such as moving forward, turning, and grabbing objects.
185   EgoPlan consists of two parts, as illustrated in Figure 2. The first is a dynamics model that gives the
186   agent the concept of the current environment, and the other is the planner that endows the agent with
187   decision-making capabilities. Intuitively, we humans first envision the outcomes of each action in our
188   minds, and then, by comparing the results, we make the best decision. In the same way, we use a
189   dynamic model to create an egocentric scenario where different actions can be taken, which is then
190
      fed into LMM to determine which action is more reasonable.

      4.1.1    L EARNING DYNAMICS
194
195   From a first-person perspective, the view after two or more steps may be completely different, making
196   it difficult to model. Therefore, we aim to model the fundamental dynamics model, pθ (xt+1 |xt , at ),
197   for one-step planning usage. In more detail, we want to generate a new image xt+1 , representing
198   the next state given the current visual observation xt and the text of the action at . Then, we cast our
199   eyes on the Text2image model and resort to the diffusion model for modeling specifically. It has an
200   irreplaceable advantage in easily incorporating other modalities as a condition.
201   Although the open-sourced diffusion model (Ho et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), pθ (xtar |xsrc , l),
202   trained on a wealth of online videos, has demonstrated the ability to predict the future, their generated
203   results are hard to control, and most are only semantically reasonable. Moreover, most of the text in
204   the pre-trained dataset consists of image descriptions l rather than action instructions a. Therefore,
205   supervised fine-tuning is adopted based on our VH-1.5M dataset to better model the dynamics,
206   pθsft (xt+1 |xt , at ). Formally, the training objective is given by:

233   4.1.2    G ENERALIZATION
234   We want to improve the model’s generalization ability from a different perspective. In other words,
235   instead of enhancing generalization through big data and large models, we aim to explicitly address the
236   differences between environments such as the visual style of indoor environments and the definition
237   of action amplitudes at the methodological level.
238
      Motion Regularization. Firstly, we must combine the motion information into the diffusion model
239
      to distinguish the different motion patterns. Optical flow has thus caught our attention. It refers to
240   the pattern of apparent motion of image objects between two consecutive frames caused by objects
241   or camera movement. In optical flow maps, colors represent the direction of motion, and the depth
242   or intensity of the colors indicates the magnitude of the motion, which is a general feature across
243   different environments.
244
      However, in practice, in the absence of the next observation, we cannot obtain the current optical
245
      flow, ft,t+1 . Inspired by other motion estimation works (Chen & Koltun, 2016; Zach et al., 2007),
246   we assume motion consistency holds over short intervals, meaning abrupt changes do not occur.
247   Consequently, the consecutive optical flow maps are highly correlated, allowing us to predict the
248   current optical flow map using the previous map. The previous map is calculated from the previous
249   two frames and reflects the actual motion pattern in the current environment.
250
      We notice that optical flow generation does not require complex texture generation, and it is expected
251
      not to cause a significant delay in the pipeline. Therefore, we adopt a less powerful but lightweight
252   generative model, VQ-GAN (Esser et al., 2021), and train it on our dataset to predict the optical flow
253   map. Empirically, the generalization ability to predict optical flow is much better than predicting
254   actual images. Formally, the training objective is given by:

275   4.2     P LANNING WITH DYNAMICS M ODEL
276
277   To avoid further training in new environments, we prompt the LMM, i.e., GPT-4V, as the planner. The
278   LMM needs to be responsible for high-level goal decomposition as well as low-level action selection.
279   Meanwhile, the pre-trained dynamics model can help the LMM better understand the world.
280
281
      4.2.1    G OAL D ECOMPOSITION
282   For long-term complex tasks, goal decomposition is an indispensable step. Subgoals can be repre-
283   sented in both text and image forms. For the text-based subgoal gtar , we prompt the LMM to generate
284   a reasonable one. In addition, we train another diffusion model, pθsft (xtar |xt , gtar ), to generate the
285   image-based subgoal xtar only based on the text-based subgoal and current observation. Note that in
286   order to complete long-horizon planning, the diffusion model is used in series of works to predict the
287   scene image of the state when the subgoal task is completed (Black et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), but
288   these works mainly focus on manpulation task. For composite tasks that integrate manpulation and
289
      navigation, especially for navigation tasks, it is often quite difficult to generate subgoal scene images,
      because the subgoal scene images often involve the change of the entire image scene information, and
290
      the joint position of most objects changes, which requires the model’s ability to understand spatial
291
      attributes. Not just editing the part of the image that involves an item. So predicting the image of the
292   subgoal can be more challenging than predicting the next observation, which means the results are
293   not very precise. We plan to delve into the impact of different types of subgoals on tasks. See Section
294   5.4.

297
298   Since we can only ensure that the prediction for the next step is relatively accurate, we adopt a
299

300
      we send the subgoal and all the visual outcomes to the LMM. Then, we prompt it to compare all
301
      the potential outcomes with the subgoal and determine which action can bring the agent closer to
302   the goal. So the process of goal decomposition and one-step planner is equivalent to the following

      Results. To demonstrate that our world model can well assist the LMM in task planning, we evaluate
414   various methods on 12 tasks, each task described by an instruction, in the VirtualHome environment.
415   Each task is tested 100 times, and the maximum step in one episode is 80. For each of the 12 tasks,
416   we abbreviated the task names for convenience. For example, the instruction of task 1, "take the
417   bread from the toaster and place it on the plate on the table," consists of four subtasks: a) walk to the
418   toaster, b) grab the bread, c) walk to the plate, and d) place the bread on the plate. We use "take and
419   place" to refer to task 1. Each task and instruction can be found in Appendix B.
420   These 12 instructional tasks are comprised of multiple sequential sub-tasks. For baselines, we use
421   GPT4 combined with React (Yao et al., 2023) as the task planner and policy, denoted as GPT4+React,
422   and it takes input as the JSON format text environment description. We also directly use GPT-4V to
423   make decisions, denoted as GPT4V, and we also combined GPT4V with React (Yao et al., 2023)
424   and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) as the task planner and policy. When employing the Reflexion
425   algorithm, its actor component is based on the React algorithm. These two baselines are denoted as
426   GPT4V+React and GPT4V+Reflexion. For ablation baselines, we use the fine-tuned InstrctP2P as
427   the world model, denoted as GPT4V+P2P. The world model that conditions on the previous optical
428
      flow map is denoted as GPT4V+PrevOF.
429   As shown in Figure 4, the world model significantly improves the GPT-4V ability on various
430   long-horizon tasks. Moreover, the inclusion of optical flow information enhances the accuracy of
431   image generation and further improves task planning performance. The results also demonstrate the
      effectiveness of the predicted optical flow map.

508   5.4   G ENERALIZATION
509
510   To assess the generalization of our method, we also evaluate its performance in a new household
511   environment. In more detail, we choose Habitat 2.0 due to its high-fidelity scenes compared with other
512   simulators, such as AI2-THOR. However, Habitat 2.0 does not provide any inter-frame regarding
513
      manipulation skills, which is unrealistic. Therefore, we only carry out experiments on navigation
      tasks.
514
515   To enhance usability, we use the pre-trained optical flow model, RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020), to
516   calculate the optical flow for the previous step since the optical flow cannot be directly obtained. The
517   RAFT results are shown in the last 2 columns of Figure 6. Since VQ-GAN has demonstrated some
518   degree of generalization ability to Habitat 2.0 in Section 5.3, we can predict the motion pattern of
519
      the new environment. The remaining task is to transfer the visual style to a new environment, and
      we adopt LoRA to fine-tune the world model. As shown in Figure 7, we successfully perform style
520
      transfer with a small amount of data (tens of samples), and the results with LoRA are closer to real
521
      scene images compared to those without LoRA visually.
522
523   Figure 8 shows the success rate of all methods on navigation tasks in Habitat 2.0, and we compare
524   our method with SuSIE. We can draw the same conclusion as in the VirtualHome environment:
525
      incorporating predicted optical flow into the world model enhances the agent’s decision-making
      capabilities. Additionally, our method achieved a high success rate, which further demonstrates its
526
      strong generalization ability. Due to the lack of generalization capability of the subgoals generated
527
      by the diffusion model in SuSIE for scenes with styles differing from the training set, the resulting
528   subgoals lacking sufficient information, often exhibit poor quality in downstream behavior cloning
529   methods.

531
532   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
533
534   This paper introduces EgoPlan, an embodied agent, using the LMM as the one-step planner and
535   the Text2image model as the world model for long-horizon tasks. We demonstrate its high-quality
536   image generation, precise optical flow prediction, and promising decision-making ability. More
537   importantly, we have demonstrated its generalization capabilities across different environments. It is
538   also important to acknowledge the limitations of EgoPlan. Currently, the agent uses encapsulated
539   skills as actions. It cannot perform low-level control, e.g., joint position. How to directly control
      low-level actions is left as future work.

815   A.1    W ORLD MODEL FOR DECISION - MAKING
816
      The Dreamer series (Hafner et al., 2020; 2022; 2024) models environmental dynamics in latent space
817   to predict future states within gaming contexts, enabling agents to learn tasks through imagination and
818   reducing the number of interactions needed for effective learning. However, as these world models
819   are developed in latent space rather than pixel space, they often struggle to generalize to unseen tasks
820   and environments. A world model constructed in pixel space may offer improved generalization
821   capabilities. Recent studies have sought to address how to learn world models from large-scale
822   video datasets (Liu et al., 2024). In Genie (Bruce et al., 2024), researchers utilize a latent action
823   representation, though their focus primarily revolves around 2D platform video games or simple
824   robotic actions. By meticulously orchestrating rich data across various dimensions, UniSim (Yang
825
      et al., 2023) simulates realistic visual experiences in response to actions performed by humans, robots,
      and other interactive agents. Overall, the applications of world models extend beyond gaming and
826
      robotics. For instance, in Escontrela et al. (2024), frame-by-frame video prediction is employed as a
827
      mechanism for providing rewards in reinforcement learning. DynaLang (Lin et al., 2023) explores
828   the integration of language prediction as an element of the world model, enabling the training of
829   multimodal world models using datasets that lack explicit actions or rewards. In DynaLang, the
830   representation is shared between vision and language within the world model.
831
832   A.2    E MBODIED AGENT WITH LMM S
833
834   Recent methods use LMMs to assist planning and reasoning in simulation environments (Fan et al.,
835   2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Yao et al., 2023)and robot learning (Ahn et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023;
836   Zeng et al., 2022). LMMs are also applied to help robot navigation (Parisi et al., 2022; Majumdar
837   et al., 2020) and manipulation (Jiang et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2023; Khandelwal et al., 2022). Among
838
      them, ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) uses chain-of-thought prompting by generating both reasoning traces
      and action plans with LMMs. SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022) leverages the ability of LLMs to understand
839
      human instructions to make plans for completing tasks without finetuning LLMs. Voyager (Wang
840
      et al., 2023a) leverages GPT-4 to learn and continually discover skills during learning. While these
841   studies demonstrate encouraging outcomes, they depend significantly on the inherent capabilities
842   of powerful large language models (LLMs), which poses challenges for their application to smaller
843   language and multimodal models (LMMs) with limited reasoning abilities.
844
      The successful integration of language as a semantically rich input for interactive decision-making un-
845
      derscores the pivotal role of LMMs in facilitating interaction and decision-making processes (Abram-
846
      son et al., 2020; Karamcheti et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). LMMs have also been employed across
847   various environments to support robot navigation (Parisi et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2021; Majumdar
848   et al., 2020) and manipulation tasks (Jiang et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2023; Karamcheti et al., 2022).
849   Recently, numerous approaches have emerged that leverage LMMs to enhance the planning and
850   reasoning capabilities of embodied agents. For instance, SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022) evaluates the affor-
851   dance of potential actions by combining their probabilities derived from LMMs with a value function.
852   Zeng et al. (2022) integrate a language and multimodal model (LMM) with a visual-language model
853   and a pre-trained language-conditioned policy (Shridhar et al., 2022) to facilitate open vocabulary
854   robotic tasks. Similarly, Huang et al. (2022a) illustrate that LMMs can be effectively utilized for plan-
855
      ning and executing simple household tasks, grounding LMM-generated actions by comparing their
      embeddings with a predefined list of acceptable actions. To incorporate environmental feedback, Inner
856
      Monologue (Huang et al., 2022b) enhances SayCan through a closed-loop principle. This principle is
857
      further employed in related works such as (Yao et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022b; Kim et al., 2024;
858   Singh et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c) to continuously monitor
859   agent behaviors and refine plans accordingly for tasks in domains like computer automation and
860   Minecraft. Furthermore, there are methods that prompt language and multimodal models (LMMs) to
861   generate temporally abstracted actions (Zheng et al., 2023). Dasgupta et al. (2023) utilize the LMM
862   as both a planner and a success detector for an agent, with their actor module requiring pre-training
863   using reinforcement learning to enable the agent to adhere to natural language instructions. While
      these studies yield impressive results, they are heavily dependent on the inherent capabilities of


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      powerful LMMs, such as GPT-4 and PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2023), which presents challenges
865
      when attempting to apply these approaches to smaller LMMs with limited reasoning abilities, such as
866   LLaMA-7B. GLAM (Carta et al., 2023) employs RL fine-tuning to achieve functional grounding
867   of LLMs and LMMs. However, their focus is primarily on simple primitive actions (e.g., turn left,
868   turn right, go forward) evaluated within toy environments, such as BabyAI (Chevalier-Boisvert
869   et al., 2018), using a significantly smaller encoder-decoder LMM, Flan-T5-780M. These primitive
870   actions possess a similar token count and lack substantial semantic meaning, which leads to an
871   underutilization of LMM capabilities. Consequently, they fail to adequately explore the effects of
872   prompt design and address the imbalance within the action space, resulting in additional instability
873   and reduced robustness.

      C    D ETAILS OF VH-1.5M’ S TEXT ACTIONS
957
958   The dataset includes a wide range of action sequences, each meticulously annotated with corre-
959   sponding text actions. These text actions are crucial for providing contextual information that aligns
960   visual actions with natural language descriptions. Below, we detail the process and structure used to
961
      generate the text actions for each action sequence in the dataset.
962   The generation of text actions for VH-1.5M involves a systematic and automated process. This
963   process ensures consistency and variety in the text actions, which are essential for robust training and
964   evaluation in vision-and-language tasks. The key steps in this process are as follows:
965   Verb Selection: A list of verbs related to various actions (e.g., "walk through," "close," "drink")
966   is predefined. For each identified action sequence directory, a verb is randomly selected from the
967   relevant list. This selection ensures a diverse representation of actions.
968
      Object Name Extraction: Each directory represents the object acted upon, which signifies the object
969
      affected by the action. However, if the action does not involve an object, such as "walk through" or
970
      "turn left," no extraction is necessary.
971
      Phrase Construction: Two types of phrases are constructed for each action sequence:


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Next Timestep Phrase: Describes the immediate next action in the sequence. For example, "next
973
       timestep: redeposit the plate".
974
975    Goal State Phrase: Describes the intended final action or goal of the sequence. For example, "the
976    goal state: redeposit plate".
977    Prompt File Creation: The constructed phrases are saved in a prompt json file within the respective
978    action sequence directory. This JSON file contains two keys: "next" and "goal," corresponding to the
979    next timestep phrase and goal state phrase, respectively.
