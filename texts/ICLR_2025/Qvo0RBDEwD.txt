011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013           Transformer architectures have shown promising results in time series process-
014           ing. However, despite recent advances in subquadratic attention mechanisms
015           or state-space models, processing very long sequences still imposes significant
016           computational requirements. Token merging, which involves replacing multiple
017           tokens with a single one calculated as their linear combination, has shown to
018           considerably improve the throughput of vision transformer architectures while
019
              maintaining accuracy. In this work, we go beyond computer vision and perform
              the first investigations of token merging in time series analysis on both time se-
020
              ries transformers and state-space models. We further introduce local merging, a
021
              domain-specific token merging algorithm that selectively combines tokens within a
022           local neighborhood, achieving two major benefits: a) Local merging can adjust its
023           the computational complexity from quadratic to linear based on the neighborhood
024           size to effectively scale token merging to long sequences; b) Local merging is
025           the first causal merging scheme enabling token merging in transformer decoders.
026           Our comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrates that token merging offers
027           substantial computational benefits with minimal impact on accuracy across various
028           models and datasets. On the recently proposed Chronos foundation model, we
029           achieve accelerations up to 5400 % with only minor accuracy degradations.

057   In this empirical study, we for the first time explore token merging within the time series domain.
058
      We introduce a novel local token merging algorithm whose computational complexity varies from
      quadratic to linear, based on the neighborhood considered for each token merge. This allows
059
      token merging to scale to long sequences. Further, local merging preserves causality and therefore
060
      enables token merging in transformer decoders. The algorithm is illustrated in figure 1. Through
061   comprehensive empirical evaluations, we analyze the impact of token merging on various time series
062   transformer models and state-space models. Our key contributions are as follows:
063
064   - Token merging in time series We present first studies on token merging in time series analysis,
065
        exploring its application beyond transformer architectures to include state-space models. For this
        purpose, we propose a domain-specific token merging algorithm that combines tokens within a local
066
        neighborhood around each token, preserving causality. Adjusting the size of the neighborhood
067
        allows the complexity of the algorithm to range from quadratic to linear. We show that this
068     adaptability improves upon classical token merging for long-sequence modeling tasks. Further, we
069     present first token merging in transformer decoders using our causal merging algorithm.
070   - Model acceleration Our findings from both pretrained models and those trained with token
071     merging reveal substantial computational savings with only slight reductions in accuracy across
072     five time series transformer architectures and datasets. We also assess five different model sizes
073     per architecture, noting greater relative accelerations in larger models.
074   - Token merging in foundation models Foundation models are becoming increasingly relevant
075     in time series processing, showing superior zero-shot capabilities compared to existing meth-
076     ods (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Das et al., 2023; Rasul et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2024;
077     Ansari et al., 2024). In this context, we enhance the throughput of the time series foundation
078
        model Chronos by up to 54.76×, with a marginal quality drop of 3 % in relative MSE using token
        merging. Across four out of five datasets, we identify Pareto optimal points where token merging
079
        simultaneously boosts throughput and increases accuracy. For example, on the ETTh1 dataset,
080
        token merging achieves a 14.17× acceleration while the MSE improves by 6 %.
081
      - Token merging patterns Our analysis of token merging’s effects identifies three distinct patterns:
082     1) a consistent decline in performance with an increasing number of merged tokens, 2) initial
083     improvements in accuracy with few merged tokens followed by a drop as merging increases, and 3)
084     scenarios where accuracy remains unchanged regardless of the token merging rate. We investigate
085     all patterns and find different explanations for all behaviors.

      2   R ELATED WORK
088
089
      Time series transformers In recent years, many transformer architectures with inductive biases for
090
      time series have been proposed, successfully outperforming classical and other deep-learning-based
091   methods in time series forecasting quality like recurrent neural networks (Li et al., 2019). Most of
092   them focus on reducing complexity by modifying the attention mechanism. LogTrans uses LogSparse
093   attention (Li et al., 2019), while Informer focuses only on the most relevant queries using ProbSparse
094   attention (Zhou et al., 2021). Additionally, many architectures adopt decomposition techniques
095   to model trend and seasonal patterns (Woo et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Liu
096   et al., 2022b). Autoformer leverages autocorrelation as a sequence-based similarity measure in the
097   attention mechanism (Wu et al., 2021). FEDformer uses the frequency domain to model time series
098   effectively (Zhou et al., 2022). Non-stationary Transformers further mitigate the effect of the time
099
      series distribution changing over time (Liu et al., 2022b). PatchTST embeds subsequences as tokens
      to capture local semantic information and reduce complexity (Nie et al., 2023). Other works apply
100
      hierarchical attention (Liu et al., 2022a; Cirstea et al., 2022) or leverage attention between the time
101
      series variates to better model multivariate patterns (Zhang & Yan, 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
102   Due to their success in the vision and NLP domain, transformer-based foundation models have
103   recently emerged for time series, often used in zero-shot settings. Many works focus on training
104   transformers directly on large and diverse time series datasets, usually with billions of tokens (Garza
105   & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Das et al., 2023; Rasul et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2024). Inspired by
106   the success of foundation models in NLP, the recently proposed Chronos model converts continuous
107   time series data into a fixed vocabulary and is trained on both real-world and synthetic data (Ansari
      et al., 2024). Besides, other research branches focus on fine-tuning vision or NLP models for time

108
      series (Zhou et al., 2023) and on applying large language models directly on time series data (Gruver
109   et al., 2023).
110
111   State-space models Due to the quadratic scaling of the attention mechanism, transformer archi-
112
      tectures suffer from significant computational cost when processing very long input sequences.
      Recently, state-space models have shown promising results in overcoming the quadratic complexity
113
      of transformers with respect to input length. Linear state-space layers solve the sequential processing
114
      requirement of RNNs through linear state-space representations (Gu et al., 2021). The S4 model
115   reduces memory requirements by conditioning the state-space matrix with a low-rank correction (Gu
116   et al., 2022). By using implicit convolutions and a data-aware gating mechanism, Hyena (Poli et al.,
117   2023) became one of the first state-space model architectures to match transformers on NLP tasks.
118   Later work uses hardware-aware algorithms to improve the performance of state-space models on
119   modern accelerators (Gu & Dao, 2023).
120
      Reducing tokens Many works reduce the number of processed tokens to increase the efficiency of
121   transformer architectures in computer vision and NLP, often by pruning (Meng et al., 2022; Goyal
122   et al., 2020). Marin et al. (2021) merge tokens in ViT architectures to reduce the loss of information
123   associated with pruning. Bolya et al. (2023) enhance the token merging algorithm, which they
124   successfully apply to already trained encoder-only models. Besides initial work on classification
125   tasks (Bolya et al., 2023), subsequent work applies token merging to diffusion models (Bolya &
126   Hoffman, 2023). Kim et al. (2024) combine merging and pruning, while other work investigates
127   optimal merging and pruning rates (Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023; Chen et al., 2023). Concurrent
128   work adapts token merging to preserve the spectral properties of the token space (Tran et al., 2024).
129
      However, their merging algorithm still has quadratic complexity, making it unsuitable for long
      sequence processing.
130
131   Sparse attention and token skipping Besides reducing the number of tokens, sparse atten-
132   tion (Child et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021) and token skipping (Raposo
133   et al., 2024) also decrease the computational requirements of transformer models. Sparse attention
134   computes a subset of the attention matrix. Therefore, it can only accelerate the attention mechanism
135
      itself and not the subsequent MLP, in contrast to reducing the number of tokens during token merging.
      According to Marin et al. (2021), this MLP can take over 60 % of the total computation in a ViT layer.
136
      Further, altering the network architecture from full attention to sparse attention requires a retraining
137
      of the model. Concurrent work, such as token skipping (Raposo et al., 2024), involves the selection
138   of a subset of tokens to be processed in a transformer layer. However, it has only been shown in
139   NLP when training from scratch. In contrast to sparse attention and token skipping, token merging
140   can accelerate already trained models and does not require any training data or fine-tuning. This is
141   especially important for recent foundation models, which are expensive to train. In our experiments
142   in sections 5.1 and 5.2, token merging successfully accelerates Informer and Autoformer, which
143   already employ sparse attention. We therefore consider token merging as an orthogonal approach.
144   Here, we propose a token merging algorithm for the time series domain, which extends beyond
145   previous investigations of token merging in ViTs (Bolya et al., 2023; Bolya & Hoffman, 2023). We
146   systematically evaluate the potential to reduce computational effort in time-series-specific transformer
147   architectures and state-space models.

      3    T OKEN MERGING
151
      Despite recent advances in efficient transformers, processing long input sequences still induces
152
      considerable memory requirements and computational effort. To address this, we propose local
153   merging, an efficient token merging algorithm for state-space models and long sequence processing.
154   Finally, we introduce causal merging as a special case of local merging to allow for token merging in
155   decoder architectures.
156
157   (Global) Token merging in computer vision Let a neural network f (x) = ΦL ◦ΦL−1 ◦· · ·◦Φ1 (x)
158   consist of L layers denoted as Φl , where each layer takes the output of the previous layer as input. We
159   assume that the input xl ∈ Rtl ×d consists of tl tokens with dimension d. Thereby, the input tokens
160   are generated by a tokenizer g : Rz → Rt×d out of z-dimensional input data u. In the computer
161   vision domain, u usually takes the form u ∈ Rw×h×c , where w, h, c are the width, height, and
      channels of the input image, respectively, and w · h · c = z.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To improve the computational efficiency of a given model, Bolya et al. (2023) combine the r most
163   similar tokens in each layer, reducing the tokens to be processed in layer l + 1 to tl+1 = tl − r.
164   Therefore, they split the set of all tokens into two disjoint subsets A, B in alternation to avoid merging
165   conflicts and allow for a parallelized computation of merging correspondences. Here A and B contain
166   tl /2 elements each, denoted as ai and bj respectively. The authors compute the cosine similarity
167   between all tokens in both subsets S = (sij ) and merge the top r most similar correspondences by
168   averaging the tokens accordingly. This results in a global token merging algorithm with quadratic
169   complexity. Lastly, the authors use a fixed r to enable batch processing without needing to pad
170   individual batch elements to the same shape after token reduction.
171
172   (Local) Token merging for time series In this work, we design token merging mechanisms for
173   time series architectures and demonstrate run-time and even performance improvements over various
174   datasets and models. We assume that the input u consists of m time stamps with n variates.
175   Previous work on token merging in image processing explored global merging schemes, where
176   every token of each subset A and B could be merged with each other (Bolya et al., 2023; Bolya &
177   Hoffman, 2023). However, computing the similarity S ∈ Rtl /2 × tl /2 between both sets of tokens
178   has a complexity of O(t2l /4), which is suboptimal for sequential data often consisting of long
179   token sequences (Godahewa et al., 2021; Grešová et al., 2023), and state-space models featuring
180   subquadratic complexity (Poli et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023).
181   Therefore, we propose local merging - a superset of token merging - by introducing k ∈ N, 1 ⩽
182   k ⩽ tl /2 as a locality constraint where we compute the similarity only on a local subset of tokens
183   Sloc = {sij | 1 ⩽ i, j ⩽ tl /2, |i − j| < k}. Figure 1 illustrates the proposed merging algorithm.
184   The locality constraint reduces the complexity to O(tl /2 + (k − 1)(tl − k)). Varying the locality,
185
      we achieve linear complexity by considering only neighboring tokens for merging up to quadratic
      complexity by considering a global merging pool, possibly exploiting more redundancy. For efficient
186
      computation, we refactor Sloc into a rectangular tensor. An upper bound for the resulting speed up
187
      can be given by speed up ⩽ 3 L 4L−1 · (4L − 1)−1 . The acceleration of deeper models is expected to
188   increase as more subsequent layers can profit from already merged tokens. Local merging additionally
189   preserves order and locality as an inductive bias for sequence processing.
190   Some time series transformers use processing mechanisms that require a minimum number of tokens
191   in the forward pass. To universally enable token merging in these architectures, we further introduce
192   q as the minimum number of remaining tokens. When encountering odd numbers of tokens tl , we
193   exclude the most recent token with the latest positional embedding from merging as we expect it to
194   contain the most relevant information.
195   We derive the complexity of the token merging procedures in appendix A.1.
196   Existing merging schemes are not suitable for causal operations, as global token merging over
197   arbitrary ranges breaks causality. To remedy this limitation and enable token merging in transformer
198   decoders, such as for recent decoder-only foundation models (Das et al., 2023) and encoder-decoder
199   architectures (Ansari et al., 2024), we propose a special case of local merging: By restricting the
200   merging neighborhood to only adjacent tokens with k = 1, local merging preserves causality.
201   However, many architectures require a fixed number of decoder output tokens or fixed dimensions for
202
      linear projection output layers. To maintain a constant output dimensionality while merging tokens to
      speed up the decoder, we unmerge all tokens in a final step. Coherent to our causal merging operation,
203
      we clone a previously merged token into two neighboring identical ones, to unmerge it. Bolya &
204
      Hoffman (2023) propose an unmerging algorithm for computer vision. However, they only leverage
205   non-causal global token merging. Moreover, they immediately unmerge after every merge, which
206   makes it unsuitable for long sequence processing, as it is unable to utilize the cumulative effect of
207   reducing tokens.

210   4    E XPERIMENTS
211
212   We systematically explore token merging in diverse settings on 5 time series datasets and 5 model
213   architectures in 5 different sizes each. Additionally, we investigate token merging in large foundation
214   models using Chronos in a zero-shot setting (Ansari et al., 2024). Finally, we demonstrate that token
215   merging can be applied to state-space models for long sequence processing by using a novel local
      merging algorithm featuring subquadratic complexity.

      Datasets We use time series forecasting datasets including ETTh1, ETTm1, Weather, Electricity
217   and Traffic for our transformer experiments. For state-space models, we use the long-range Dummy
218   Mouse Enhancers Ensembl dataset. See appendix A.2 for more details.
219
220
      Model architectures For our main experiments, we use 5 architectures, including Autoformer,
      FEDformer, Informer, Non-stationary Transformer, and the vanilla Transformer (Vaswani et al.,
221
      2017) as reference. For each model, we evaluate token merging for different model sizes with L ∈
222
      {2, 4, 6, 8, 10} encoder layers, which we train doing hyperparameter optimization (see appendix A.2).
223   We use an input length of m = 192, following the results of Nie et al. (2023), and a prediction
224   horizon p = 96 samples. Longer sequences would generally benefit token merging.
225   For experiments on the foundation model Chronos, we use the default input length of m = 512 and
226   prediction horizon p = 64 (Ansari et al., 2024). We compute the median from Chronos probabilistic
227   forecasts and report the MSE.
228   For our experiments on state-space models, we use HyenaDNA medium, a genomic foundation
229   model (Nguyen et al., 2023) based on the Hyena architecture (Poli et al., 2023). We use a large
230   input length of m = 16 000 nucleotides utilizing Hyenas subquadratic complexity. We chose Hyena
231
      over Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) to avoid specialized CUDA kernels and hope to make more general
      statements about the capabilities of token merging.
232
233   Applying token merging In our experiments, we generally find it beneficial to allow self-attention
234   to transfer information between tokens before merging them. Therefore, we apply token merging
235   between self-attention and the MLP in all transformer encoders as Bolya et al. (2023). For our main
236   experiments, we also apply our casual local merging with k = 1 in the transformer decoders between
237
      self-attention and cross-attention and finally unmerge all decoder tokens. In architectures utilizing
      additional tensors like attention masks or positional biases, we merge them using the same correspon-
238
      dences. Many transformers exhibit quadratic attention, imposing considerable computational cost. As
239
      a result, we do not find the token merging algorithm to introduce a substantially additional overhead.
240   Thus, we choose k = tl /2 to profit from a global merging pool for transformer encoders. Therefore,
241   we utilize different merging strategies in transformer encoders and decoders. In state-space models,
242   we merge tokens after the Hyena operator and choose k = 1 to not introduce an operation with
243   quadratic complexity into the architecture.
244
      Reproducibility of measurements We report all results on the same Nvidia A6000 GPU. For
245
      training, we utilize Nvidia V100 and A100 GPUs (see appendix A.2). We measure the end-to-end
246   inference time of the models using 2 warm-ups and 2 measurement runs per batch. The standard
247   deviation of the execution time is generally < 2 % in our experiments. Besides the inference time as
248   practically most relevant quantity, we report FLOPs as a more hardware independent measure using
249   the thop library (Zhu, 2022). We choose the maximum possible batch size and standardize the results.

254   We first present our main results for token merging on pretrained models and models trained with
255   token merging. We then explore token merging in transformer foundation models. Subsequently,
256
      we ablate different merging patterns, investigate why token merging improves prediction quality,

260   5.1   T OKEN MERGING IN PRETRAINED MODELS
261
      We investigate token merging in both the encoder and decoder on diverse time series transformer
262
      models with different inductive biases. All models are trained on the target dataset and token merging
263
      is applied only during inference time, as accelerating already trained models is of high practical
264   relevance. We choose token merging hyperparameters as described in appendix A.2, selecting the
265   fastest token merging trial on the validation set that is within an 0.01 increase in MSE compared to
266   the reference without token merging. If we do not find a trial with token merging satisfying these tight
267   criteria, we report results without token merging, mimicking how token merging might be applied in
268   practice. We perform all selections on the validation set and report all results on the test set.
269   The vanilla and Non-stationary Transformers have quadratic attention mechanisms, while the remain-
      ing architectures feature subquadratic attention complexities of O(tl · log(tl )) for Autoformer and

      Informer and O(tl ) for FEDformer. Regardless, our local token merging in the encoder together with

494
495                A fixed merging objective allows for batch processing without needing to pad individual time series to
496                the same length. However, it enforces a fixed r among independent batch elements, which might not
497                always be optimal. Determining the number of tokens to be merged dynamically using a similarity
498                threshold might increase quality as no dissimilar tokens are combined. Here, we leverage the single-
499
                   sample case to explore dynamic merging in optimal conditions. From a practical perspective, this
                   case might be relevant for on-device applications like smartphones or automated driving.
500
                   In figure 7b, we compare token merging utilizing a fixed r to dynamic merging varying the cosine
501
                   similarity threshold. Dynamic merging improves quality slightly in most settings. Therefore, we
502                suggest using a fixed merging schedule for batch applications and dynamic merging just for the
503                single-sample case. There is no equivalent r to dynamic merging schedules as they are similarity-
504                based and strongly layer-dependent. We report FLOPs as we observe substantial execution overhead
505                in time measurements.

      6    C ONCLUSION
545
      In this work, we explore token merging in the time series domain for the first time. We conduct an extensive
546   empirical study on transformer architectures and state-space models in diverse settings using various models and
547   datasets. We demonstrate that token merging can successfully accelerate pretrained models and sometimes even
548   improve their prediction quality. We further introduce a domain-specific local merging algorithm with variable
549   complexity and illustrate its effectiveness on the Hyena model. On the long-range Dummy Mouse Enhancers
      Ensembl dataset, this method outperforms traditional token merging approaches in throughput and accuracy.
550
      Additionally, local merging is the first causal token merging scheme, which we successfully demonstrate in
551   transformer decoders. Finally, we conduct several ablation studies to investigate when token merging is most
552   effective, including sequence length, positional embedding, and single-sample inference settings.
553   We hope that token merging will have a positive effect on reducing the resource consumption and environmental
554   impact of time series models.
555
556
      Limitations In our work, we divide all tokens into two sets and restrict merging to occur only between tokens
      from different sets. Future work can explore more flexible merging schemes for time series-specific architectures.
557   Moreover, we do not conduct ablations on all possible hyperparameters due to the large number of architectures
558   and datasets evaluated in this work. Additionally, future work might prioritize past merges or extend locality of
559   merging to periods of the time series. However, the latter leads to non-causal merging. Besides for the time
560   series domain, locality and the linear merging complexity might especially be relevant for high resolution images
      or videos, which future work can investigate.

      A.2     E XPERIMENTS
811
812
813   Here we list additional information concerning our experimental settings and resources.
814
      Datasets We base our experiments on 5 commonly used multivariate time series datasets covering different
815
      forecasting applications: ETTh1 and ETTm1 consist of 7 variates measuring the power load and temperature
816   of electric transformers in hourly and quarter-hourly granularity (Zhou et al., 2021). Weather consists of 21
817   meteorological quantities such as air temperature and is recorded every 10 minutes in 2020.1 Electricity measures
818   the energy demand of 321 consumers every hour (Godahewa et al., 2021). Traffic consists of 862 sensors in the
819   San Francisco Bay Area measuring the road occupancy hourly (Godahewa et al., 2021). We use the same data
      splits for training, validation and test as Wu et al. (2021) for consistency.
820
      Since the Chronos foundation model operates univariately and requires considerable computational resources,
821   we randomly sample the same 7000 time series from the test set for all Chronos evaluations. For the ETTh1
822   dataset, we do not observe relevant differences when comparing the results to the full test set.
823   To explore token merging in an additional sequence-based domain and on a second task, we use the Dummy
824   Mouse Enhancers Ensembl dataset (Grešová et al., 2023) for classifying genomic data. It contains very long
      sequences of nucleotides from a mouse.
825
826   Hyperparameter optimization For each transformer architecture, model size, and dataset we train 32 models
827   without token merging doing hyperparameter tuning of learning rate and dropout using HEBO (Cowen-Rivers
      et al., 2022). Here, we apply token merging during inference-time only. We choose the best model based on
828
      its validation MSE. We train 17 models with the found hyperparameters, the minimum possible qtrain , and
829   different uniformly spaced rtrain until all tokens are merged. We again choose the best model based on the
830   MSE for further evaluation. We do 185 hyperparameter optimization trials of both chosen models, trained with
831   and without token merging, using HEBO to find token merging inference hyperparameters rtest and qtest on
832   the validation set. Please note that r and q might be different for local merging in the encoder and causal local
      merging in the decoder. Finally, we evaluate once on the test set to report our results.

       A.7    DATASET PROPERTIES
1189
1190   We find properties of the target dataset that are particularly amenable to token merging. This way we can predict
1191   how well token merging will work on a new dataset and gain more insights in the behavior of token merging
1192   itself. We find that improvement in forecasting quality due to token merging in table 2 correlate with the spectral
       entropy of the dataset. Specifically, local merging achieves higher quality gains on high entropy datasets, such
1193
       as ETTh1, ETTm1 and Traffic (see table 6). We argue that local merging removes unnecessary information
1194   from complex signals with high entropy using its selective smoothing ability (see section 5.5). This allows
1195   the model to focus on only the relevant patterns of a signal and to achieve better prediction quality. Besides
1196   the spectral entropy, the same correlation is evident in the total harmonic distortion. Local merging adaptively
1197   low-pass-filters noisy distorted signals to condense the most relevant patterns and effectively improves the
       signal-to-noise-ratio. The greater noise in ETTh1, ETTm1 and Traffic compared to Weather and Electricity
1198
       can also be visually inspected in the respective frequency spectrum figures 16 to 20. Therefore, we expect
1199   larger improvement of prediction quality when applying local merging on high entropy signals with a low
1200   signal-to-noise ratio.
