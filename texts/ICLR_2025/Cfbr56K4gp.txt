                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) is the mainstream
010
               paradigm to align large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. Yet
011            existing RLHF heavily relies on accurate and informative reward models, which
012            are vulnerable and sensitive to noise from various sources, e.g. human labeling
013            errors, making the pipeline fragile. In this work, we formulate the problem of
014            performing robust RLHF with noisy reward models. Our goal is to design ro-
015            bust RLHF algorithms that explicitly acknowledge the potential noise in a reward
016            model. Our first contribution is an analysis that revealed a certain transformation
017            of the preference function improves its robustness to noise in the reward function.
018            This observation leads to a new reward function design that involves two steps:
019
               (1) an offline sampling step to obtain responses to prompts that serve as baseline
               calculation and (2) a contrastive reward calculated using the baseline responses in
020
               Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). We show that our suggested rewards enable
021
               the LLM to penalize reward uncertainty, improve robustness, encourage improve-
022            ment over baselines, calibrate according to task difficulty, and reduce variance
023            in PPO. We also empirically demonstrate contrastive reward can improve RLHF
024            substantially, evaluated by both GPTs and humans, and it consistently outperforms
025            strong baselines.

057   Our method explicitly acknowledges the imperfections of the reward model and calibrates the RLHF
058

064   reward information enables the RL policy to self-improve based on the observed differences. Em-
065   pirically, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed approach using extensive experiments
066   with both evaluations automated by GPT models, and by carefully solicited human evaluations.

068

070            tions in the reward model.

167   Advantages of Including Contrastive Penalty We further investigate the properties of r̂(x, y).
168   Following our binary reward level setting, we introduce the following two instance-dependent vari-
169   ables that capture the (in)consistency of the reward function on (x, y):
170            cx,0 := Pr(rψ (x, y) = 1|r∗ (x, y) = 0), cx,1 := Pr(rψ (x, y) = 0|r∗ (x, y) = 1).
171
      High cx,0 , cx,1 indicate high inconsistency/variance of the reward function on sample x, capturing
172
      the reward model’s uncertainty. We prove the following theorem:
173
      Theorem 2. Suppose rψ (x, y) and rψ (x, y base ) are conditionally independent given r∗ (x, y), then:
174
        Ey,rψ (x,ybase )|x [r̂ψ (x, y)] = (1 − cx,0 − cx,1 ) · Pr(rψ (x, y) ̸= rψ (x, y base )) · 2 Pr(r∗ (x, y) = 1) − 1 .

175
176
177   The above theorem reveals the following advantages in the proposed contrastive penalty reward:
178
179   Penalizing uncertainty The scale of rψ (x, y) − rψ (x, y base ) in expectation is linearly decreasing
180   w.r.t. (1 − cx,0 − cx,1 ) where high uncertainty (large cx,0 , cx,1 ) is penalized heavily by the constant.
181   In other words, when the reward function is highly inaccurate on certain x, the influence of x during
182   PPO drops linearly w.r.t. the uncertainty terms.
183
184   Improving robustness If we simplify the reward noise by assuming cx,0 ≡ c0 , cx,1 ≡ c1 , i.e. the
185
      reward function suffers a similar amount of mistakes for different (x, y) pairs, then the first constant
      linear term, i.e. (1 − c0 − c1 ), becomes irrelevant to the reward maximization problem and therefore
186
      improves the training’s resistance to this noise.
187
188
      Encouraging improvement It also reveals that contrastive reward encourages a new answer y that
189   substantially differs from the baseline answer y base through the term Pr(rψ (x, y) ̸= rψ (x, y base )).
190
191   Calibrating w.r.t the task difficulty The last term, i.e. 2 Pr(r∗ (x, y) = 1) − 1, downweighs the
192   tasks with greater difficulty, i.e. with a lower chance of observing high true reward r∗ (x, y) = 1.
193   This helps the PPO step focus less on the instances that might be inherently ambiguous in obtaining
194   a high-quality answer, caused either by bad prompting, or the nature of the question.
195
196   Variance reduction Baseline rewards are similar to (Weaver & Tao, 2013; Sutton & Barto, 2018),
197   which can be contributed to variance reduction. This is also evident from Theorem 2 that linear
198   terms, e.g. (1 − cx,0 − cx,1 ), properly scale the reward down and therefore reduces its variance.

200   3.2   P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
201
      The Intuiton of our method The design choice came from a principled derivation from our ques-
202
      tion originated from Equation 5: which Ψ transformation will improve the robustness of opti-
203
      mizing with only noisy rewards? The contrastive form arose as a result we proved in Theorem 1.
204   In retrospect, explaining this simple yet powerful term, the high-level intuition is that both the re-
205   wards and the contrastive rewards originate from the same reward model, making them susceptible
206   to similar inaccuracies if the reward model is not precise. By subtracting one from the other, the
207   influence of noise is reduced, and happens to be summarized into a constant in front of an affine
208   transformation, and this constant does not affect the optimization objective in expectation (though
209   it does affect second order convergence since it reduces the reward margin between the optimal and
210   suboptimal models), thereby enhancing the training’s resilience to this noise. This is supported by
211   the theoretical insight “Improving Robustness & Penalizing Uncertainty” through Theorem 2.
212   Furthermore, since we compute contrastive rewards for each prompt, the subtraction reveals the
213   relative performance of the current policy compared to the initial policy on those prompts. This
214   allows the optimization process to focus more on prompts with greater potential for improvement,
215   as suggested by the theoretical insight of “Encouraging Improvement” and demonstrated in Figure
      4.

      LLM Alignment LLM Alignment is typically categorized by whether a reward model is used. A
      popular method is Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022; Schulman
472
      et al., 2017) (RLHF), which has gained traction for its effectiveness in integrating human feedback.
473
      In addition to these, there are preference learning methods that do not use reinforcement learning,
474   such as RSO (Liu et al., 2024), RRHF (Yuan et al., 2023), and RAFT (Dong et al., 2023). All
475   of these methods employ reward models for optimization. However, human preferences are often
476   noisy and may exhibit ambiguous or conflicting intentions (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b).
477   Limited preference data can also result in reward models inaccurately generalizing human intent
478   (Lambert et al., 2023; Pitis, 2023). These imperfect reward models can cause LLMs to be prone
479   to training instability (Zheng et al., 2023b), overoptimization (Gao et al., 2022), or reward hacking
480   issues (Skalse et al., 2022). In contrast, methods like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), SLiC-HF (Zhao
481   et al., 2023), IPO (Azar et al., 2023) and KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) avoid using reward models,
482
      but they are still vulnerable to out-of-distribution data (Li et al., 2023). Our approach improves the
      reward modeling in RLHF and can also incorporate with other RLHF methods.
483

      our approach and the specific issues we aim to address. Our work focuses on studying robust Re-
499
      inforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) in the presence of noisy rewards, and our
500   principled derivations reveal that the reward penalty form contributes to robustness. Previous meth-
501   ods like RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024), ReMax (Li et al., 2024), GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), and
502   RL baselines (Kool et al., 2019) share a similar intuition: variance reduction in value estimation.
503   In practical terms, RLOO requires k online generations for each prompt and relies on an estimated
504   value function. ReMax, on the other hand, necessitates one additional greedy search sample and
505   utilizes a similar baseline method. Our approach diverges from RLOO and ReMax by omitting
506   the redundant online baseline samples, consequently, our method does not require extra generation
507   time during the RL stage, allowing for more optimization steps within the same budget. The GRPO
508
      method aims to reduce training resources by discarding the critic model and using estimated group
      scores to represent the value function. Similarly, the motivation differs significantly from our PPO-
509
      based method. Pairwise PPO (Wu et al., 2023) generates pairs of responses for each prompt and
510
      updates the policy using only relative feedback (from reward differences), which enhances the sta-
511   bility and efficiency of policy optimization. ChatGLM-RLHF (Hou et al., 2024) also akin to ours
512   primarily relies on overcoming challenges such as value instability and task bias. However, our ap-
513   proach not only harnesses the strengths of both entities but also incorporates a penalty term derived
514   from contrasting rewards to empirically establish a robust RLHF framework for LLM alignment.
515   This framework drives significant performance enhancements by facilitating self-assessment and
516   autonomous refinement within the RL agent.

      6      C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
519
520
      We aim to address issues related to the quality and instability of reward models in RLHF by intro-
521
      ducing a simple yet effective method. By integrating offline sampling and contrastive rewards, our
522   method improves the robustness of the RLHF process. Empirical results demonstrate the effective-
523   ness of our method, highlighting its ability to mitigate flaws and uncertainties in reward models. We
524   conduct extensive experiments, including evaluations by GPT models and human annotators.
525
      Discussion Our work takes inspiration from the noisy label literature (Natarajan et al., 2013; Liu &
526
      Tao, 2015; Zhu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021), where the goal is to analyze and learn accurately from
527
      the imperfect supervision signals. The ongoing discussion on the quality of reward models builds
528   a connection to the noisy label problem since effectively the RL stage is dealing with potentially
529   noisy feedback from the reward model. We believe further connecting with the ideas developed
530   in the noisy label literature can help fully unlock the power of RLHF. In addition, our approach
531   holds significant potential for implementing contrastive rewards in iterative settings. In essence,
532   after obtaining the policy from the initial round of policy optimization, we can use this policy as the
533   base model for contrastive rewards and initiate a second round of RL optimization. This iterative
534   process has the potential to further enhance the performance.
535   Limitation The offline sampling phase consumes a significant portion of computational resources,
536   particularly as sampling times increase. Given the ever-expanding size of LLMs, optimizing infer-
537   ence becomes paramount when deploying our robust RLHF framework. Currently, we have only
538   implemented a rudimentary and empirical version of robust RLHF, leaving ample space for im-
539   provement and extension. In the RLHF part, the sensitivity of hyperparameters and the stability of
      training remain challenging issues that are beyond the scope of this paper.
