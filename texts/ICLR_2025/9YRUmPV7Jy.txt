009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Random subspace method has wide security applications such as providing certified
012            defenses against adversarial and backdoor attacks, and building robustly aligned
013            LLM against jailbreaking attacks. However, the explanation of random subspace
014
               methods lacks sufficient exploration. Existing state-of-the-art feature attribution
               methods such as Shapley value and LIME are computationally impractical and lack
015
               security guarantees when applied to random subspace methods. In this work, we
016
               propose EnsembleSHAP, an intrinsically faithful and secure feature attribution for
017            random subspace methods that reuses its computational byproducts. Specifically,
018            our feature attribution method is 1) computationally efficient, 2) maintains essential
019            properties of effective feature attribution (such as local accuracy), and 3) offers
020            guaranteed protection against attacks on feature attribution methods. We perform
021            comprehensive evaluations for our explanation’s effectiveness when faced with dif-
022            ferent empirical attacks. Our experimental results demonstrate that our explanation
023            not only faithfully reports the most important features, but also certifiably detects
024            the harmful features embedded in the input sample.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Random subspace method (Ho, 1998) is widely employed for security purposes (Jia et al., 2021;
031   Levine & Feizi, 2020; Robey et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023;
032   Cao et al., 2023), such as providing certified defenses (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang
033   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021) against adversarial attacks, and enhancing the robustness of large
034   language models against jailbreaking attacks (Cao et al., 2023; Robey et al., 2023). This method
035   begins by generating predictions for multiple sub-sampled versions of a given input sample using a
036   base model. It then creates an ensemble model that aggregates these predictions using a majority
037   vote to determine the final prediction. As this approach only requires black-box access to the base
038   model, it can be applied across different base model architectures (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Zeng et al.,
039
      2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Robey et al., 2023). Understanding the output of the
      random subspace method is crucial. For instance, in defending against jailbreaking attacks, it’s
040
      essential for users to pinpoint the specific elements of the input prompt that lead to its classification
041
      as ‘harmful’ (or ‘non-harmful’). Additionally, when a certified defense is compromised by strong
042   empirical attacks, it becomes important for users to determine the specific adversarial words that
043   caused the misclassification.
044
045
      However, existing state-of-the-art feature attribution methods for black-box models (Lundberg &
      Lee, 2017; Chen et al., 2023b; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Enouen et al., 2023; Paes et al., 2024; Amara
046
      et al., 2024; Mosca et al., 2022; Lopardo et al., 2023) such as Shapley values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017;
047
      Chen et al., 2023b) and LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) have following disadvantages when applied to
048   random subspace method. Firstly, they incur prohibitively high computational costs. Specifically,
049   these methods involve randomly perturbing the input sample numerous times, denoted by M . The
050   explained model then generates a prediction for each perturbed version of the input. In the case of the
051   random subspace method, the objective is to explain the behavior of the ensemble model. Therefore,
052   for each perturbed input version, N sub-sampled variants of it are created and each is evaluated to
053   generate the ensemble model’s prediction, resulting in M × N total queries to the base model for
      just one input sample. In practical applications, both N and M can exceed 1, 000 (Enouen et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Levine & Feizi, 2020), which significantly increases the computational cost.
055   Secondly, current feature attribution methods do not provide security guarantees against the recently
056   proposed explanation-preserving attack (Nadeem et al., 2023; Noppel & Wressnegger, 2023). In this
057   type of attack, an adversary can perturb certain features of the input sample to cause misclassifications,
058   and at the same time conceal these changes by preserving the original explanation.
059
      Our contribution. In this work, we propose a computationally efficient feature attribution method
060
      for random subspace methods that is inherently faithful and secure. The key intuition is from the
061   fact that the output of the ensemble model aggregates prediction outcomes of all sub-sampled inputs,
062   and the impact of any specific sub-sampled input on the ensemble model’s output can be further
063   distributed to the individual features within that sub-sampled input. Consequently, we can infer the
064   contribution of each feature from the predictions of all sub-sampled inputs that contain it, which are
065   already calculated for producing the ensemble model’s prediction.
066
      We conduct a theoretical analysis to show that our method maintains key properties of Shapley
067   value and is provably robust against explanation-perserving attacks on feature attribution methods.
068   Additionally, we carry out empirical evaluations to assess the effectiveness of our explanations across
069   various security applications of the feature attribution method.

076   2.1   R ANDOM S UBSPACE M ETHOD

078   certified defense mechanisms (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang
079   et al., 2021) and protections against jailbreaking attacks (Robey et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023). In
080   particular, this method is agnostic to model architecture and scalable to large neural networks. Next,
081   we summarize a general framework for the random subspace method and introduce its security
082   applications.

118   Security applications of existing random subspace method. Random subspace method is used to
119   build state-of-the-art certified defenses (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Zeng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021;
120   Zhang et al., 2023). Many previous studies (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Zhang et al., 2023) showed that the
121   ensemble model built by a random subspace method is certifiably robust against adversarial attacks,
122   i.e., its prediction for a testing input remains unchanged once the ℓ0 -norm perturbation to the testing
123
      input is bounded.
124   Another strand of research (Robey et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) uses the random subspace method
125   to build robust LLM against jailbreaking attacks, leveraging the fragility of adversarially-generated
126   jailbreaking prompts to perturbations. These methods first use the LLM to generate responses for
127   each of the perturbed input prompts, and these responses are then labeled as either ‘harmful’ or
128   ‘non-harmful’ by checking keywords. Lastly, these labels are aggregated to determine whether the
129
      input prompt should be approved or rejected.
130   Existing studies mainly focus on robustness, leaving the explanation of the random subspace method
131   unexplored. Next, we introduce feature attribution to explain model outputs.
132
133   2.2   F EATURE ATTRIBUTION
134   Feature attribution aims to explain why a machine learning model makes a certain prediction for
135   an input by attributing the prediction to the most important features in the input. Existing feature
136   attribution techniques (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Chen et al., 2023b; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Paes et al.,
137   2024; Mosca et al., 2022; Petsiuk et al., 2018; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017;
138   Smilkov et al., 2017) fall into two main categories: 1) white-box methods, exemplified by integrated
139   gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) and DeepLIFT (Shrikumar et al., 2017), and 2) black-box
140   methods, including LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and Shapley values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Mosca
141
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020). White-box methods require knowledge
      of the explained model’s architecture, parameters, and gradients, whereas black-box methods do not
142
      rely on such detailed knowledge. This study concentrates on black-box feature attribution methods
143
      due to their general applicability across various model architectures.
144
145   Attacks to feature attribute methods. Recent studies (Noppel & Wressnegger, 2023; Nadeem
146   et al., 2023) have proposed the explanation-preserving attack to feature attribution methods. This
147
      attack involves adversarially perturbing the input sample in a manner that induces misclassifications
      while retaining the original explanation. This attack could be employed to conceal ongoing input
148
      manipulation (Zhang et al., 2020). For instance, an attacker could replace certain words in a clean
149
      sentence with adversarial alternatives, leading to misclassification, while those words still maintain
150   low relevance in the resulting explanation.
151
152   Limitations of existing feature attribute methods. Existing state-of-the-art black-box feature
153
      attribution methods (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Chen et al., 2023b; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Enouen et al.,
      2023; Paes et al., 2024; Amara et al., 2024; Mosca et al., 2022; Lopardo et al., 2023; Petsiuk
154
      et al., 2018) have following limitations. Firstly, they are computationally inefficient when applied
155
      to the random subspace method. Techniques such as LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and Shapley
156   values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Enouen et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b) necessitate a large number of
157   queries (e.g., 1,000) to the black-box model using perturbed versions of the test input. As detailed in
158   Section 2.1, each query to the ensemble classifier requires aggregating the prediction outcomes from
159   all sub-sampled versions of the perturbed test input, leading to prohibitively high computation costs.
160   Secondly, these methods lack theoretical guarantees regarding their performance when subjected
161   to explanation-preserving attacks. In the next section, we design a feature attribution method that
      overcomes these limitations.

164   3.1   F EATURE ATTRIBUTION FOR R ANDOM S UBSPACE M ETHOD
165
166   Consider an ensemble model H with base model h and sub-sampling size k. For a given test input x,
167   consisting of d features (denoted by x = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xd }), let ŷ represent the predicted label for
168   x, such that H(x, h, k) = ŷ. The objective of feature attribution (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Paes
169   et al., 2024; Amara et al., 2024; Lopardo et al., 2023; Chuang et al., 2024) is to assign an importance
170   score αiŷ to each element xi ∈ x, indicating its contribution to the ensemble model’s prediction of
171   ŷ. For instance, if x is a text consisting of d words, each word would receive an importance score.
172   By ranking these scores, users can easily identify the most influential words leading to the ensemble
173
      model’s prediction.
174
175
      3.2   D ESIGN G OAL
176
      Our approach is guided by three primary design goals. First, the feature attribution method should be
177   computationally efficient, as predictions from an ensemble model are already resource-intensive, so
178   the method must avoid repeatedly using the ensemble model for predictions. Second, it should adhere
179   to key properties of effective feature attribution (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), such as local accuracy.
180   Third, the method must be certifiably robust against explanation-preserving attacks. Specifically, if
181   an adversary modifies a small number of features in the input to change the model’s prediction, the
182   most important features reported by the attribution method should include these adversarially altered
183   features.

      5.3   P ROPERTIES OF E NSEMBLE SHAP
271
272   EnsembleSHAP possesses local accuracy and symmetry as derived from Shapley values (?Chen
273   et al., 2023a), whilst substituting the remaining two properties inherent to Shapley values, specifically
274   dummy and linearity, with order consistency (with Shapley value). The linearity property is omitted
275   because its application is not straightforward in the context of subspace methods. Furthermore,
276   the relaxation of the dummy property is from the observation that in many cases, people are more
277   interested in the comparative importance of features over their absolute importance scores (Lopardo
278   et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024). We introduce these properties below.
279   The first property is local accuracy. This property ensures that the explanation accurately reflects the
280   behavior of the ensemble model for the testing input x. It can be formally stated as follows.
281
282
      Property 1. (Local accuracy). For any x, h, and k, the importance score of all features sum up to
      pŷ (x, h, k), i.e., i∈x αiŷ (x, h, k) = pŷ (x, h, k).
                          ∑︁
283
284
285   The second property is symmetry. The symmetry property states that if two features contribute equally
286   to all possible feature subsets S ⊆ x, then feature i and j should receive the same importance score.
287   Property 2. (Symmetry). Given a pair of features (i, j), if for any S ⊆ x \ {i, j}, pŷ (S ∪ {i}, h, k) =
288
      pŷ (S ∪ {j}, h, k), then αiŷ (x, h, k) = αjŷ (x, h, k).
289
290
      The third property is order consistency (with Shapley value). This property ensures that if Shapley
291
      value ranks a feature as more significant, our attribution approach will also give it a higher importance.
292
      The Shapley value for random subspace method is defined in Section 5.2.
293
294   Property 3. (Order consistency with Shapley value). Given a pair of features (i, j), αiŷ (x, h, k) ≥
295   αjŷ (x, h, k) if and only if ϕi (pŷ ) ≥ ϕj (pŷ ), where ϕi (pŷ ) and ϕj (pŷ ) respectively represent Shapley
296   values of i and j.
297
298   We provide the proof details in Appendix A. Our method essentially relaxes the dummy property of
299   Shapley value to simplify its complex computation. Despite this alteration, the utility of the Shapley
300   value is preserved in most scenarios due to the property of order consistency. This observation is
301   supported by these commonly used metrics for feature attribution, such as the fidelity score (Miró-
302   Nicolau et al., 2024; Chuang et al., 2024), perturbation curves (Paes et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2020)
303   and faithfulness (Lopardo et al., 2023). These metrics rely on the relative order of importance scores
304   rather than their absolute values.

355
356   We evaluate the effectiveness of our feature attribution method in the context of certified defense
357   and defense against jailbreaking attacks, which are two critical security applications of random
358   subspace method. For certified defense, we first test our approach’s efficacy in scenarios without
359   any attacks. Subsequently, we explore its performance under conditions where the certified defense
360   can be compromised by powerful empirical attacks. Specifically, we employ a backdoor attack
361
      (BadNets (Gu et al., 2017)) and an adversarial attack (TextFooler (Jin et al., 2020)) to challenge the
      random subspace method (more details are provided in Appendix D.3). Our findings reveal that,
362
      even in instances where these attacks lead the ensemble model to incorrect predictions, our method
363
      successfully identifies the exact words responsible for the failure. For defense against jailbreaking
364   attacks, we evaluate three types of such attacks: GCG (Zou et al., 2023), AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2023),
365   and DAN (Liu et al., 2023). Our results demonstrate that our method is capable of identifying the
366   harmful query embedded within the jailbreaking prompt.

536   In this work, we propose an efficient feature attribution method for the random subspace method
537   that is provably secure against explanation-preserving attacks. Potential future directions include: 1)
538   investigating the explanation of random subspace method for privacy applications, such as machine
539   unlearning (Bourtoule et al., 2021) and differential privacy (Liu et al., 2020); and 2) developing
      provably secure feature attribution methods for general machine learning models.

541
542
      Our proposed method, EnsembleSHAP, provides a secure and efficient feature attribution for the
543   random subspace method, and helps build ethical and explainable ML models. Our method can
544   be applied to security-sensitive applications such as defending against adversarial and backdoor
545   attacks and building robust language models (LLMs) resistant to jailbreaking attacks. By providing
546   explanations for model decisions, we aim to enhance users’ trust to AI systems. Nonetheless, this
547   also means that practitioners must take responsibility for how these explanations are communicated
548   to end users, ensuring that they are not misleading or overly simplified.
