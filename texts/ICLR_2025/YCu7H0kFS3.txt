009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The rise of large language models (LLMs) has prompted increasing interest in their
012
               use as in-context learning agents. At the core of agentic behavior is the capacity for
013            exploration, or the ability to actively gather information about the environment. But
014            how do LLM agents explore, and how can we control their exploratory behaviors?
015            To answer these questions, we take a representation-level perspective, and introduce
016            Entropic Activation Steering (EAST), an activation steering method for in-context
017            LLM agents. Firstly, we demonstrate that EAST can effectively manipulate an
018            LLM agent’s exploration by directly affecting the high-level actions parsed from
019            the outputs of the LLM, in contrast to token-level temperature sampling. Secondly,
020            we reveal how applying this control modulates the uncertainty exhibited in the
021
               LLM’s thoughts, guiding the agent towards more exploratory actions. Finally, we
               demonstrate that the steering vectors obtained by EAST generalize across task
022
               variants. In total, these results show that LLM agents explicitly encode uncertainty
023
               over their actions in their representation space. Our work paves the way for a new
024            understanding of the functioning of LLM agents and to effective control of their
025            decision-making behaviors.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Successful agentic behavior requires a decision-maker to consider its beliefs about the world while
032   determining which action to take: Should I exploit what I know about the task? Should I search for
033   more information? Can I be sure that my decisions are correct? To build agents that are both effective
034   and reliable, it is paramount to assess whether they are able to autonomously ask these questions, to
035   find answers to them, and to incorporate these answers into their decision-making process.
036   These considerations are especially important when developing agents built on top of large language
037   models (LLMs). Due to their natural language interface and wide range of capabilities, LLMs hold
038   the promise of powering a new generation of agentic systems. In particular, they have been noted for
039   their ability to perform in-context learning, or the adaptation of their predictions based on examples
040   provided in the prompt. This capability sets the stage for deploying LLMs as in-context learning
041   agents, capable of perceiving the world, executing actions, and achieving diverse human-specified
042   goals by dynamically adapting their behavior in response to feedback from the environment.
043   However, in contrast to well-studied decision-making algorithms based on reinforcement learn-
044   ing (Sutton & Barto, 2018), relatively little is known about how LLM agents come to their decisions
045   through interaction. While the LLM operates at the token level, playing the role of the reasoning
046   engine behind the agent, decisions happen at a higher level of abstraction, after the output text
047   produced by the LLM is parsed into an action. Overall, the interaction between these two levels is
048   not well understood, and it plays a vital role in determining how the agent’s beliefs shape its action
049   distribution.
050   Indeed, recent work has shown that this process frequently goes awry, causing in-context LLM
051   agents to fail to produce sensible exploratory behavior (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024). They tend to be
052   overconfident, rapidly reducing the uncertainty about their decisions and committing to a particular
053   solution, even when it should be clear that more information is needed. How can we effectively
      intervene on this behavior?

078   In this paper, we introduce Entropic Activation Steering (EAST), a method to alter an LLM agent’s
079   subjective uncertainty over decisions and entropy over actions. EAST uses a dataset of logged
080   interactions between the LLM agent and an environment to obtain a steering vector. This vector is
081   computed as an entropy-weighted average of the (run-centered) representations that an LLM produces
082
      right before making a decision. Similarly to previous work in activation addition (Rimsky et al., 2023),
      the steering vector is applied at decision time by adding it, at a specific layer, to the representation
083
      corresponding to the tokens that are being generated by the LLM.
084
085   EAST directly controls the entropy of the agent’s distribution over actions, well beyond what is
086   achievable by simply modifying an LLM’s token sampling temperature. Moreover, EAST modifies
087   the subjective uncertainty expressed by an LLM agent in its ReAct-style thoughts (Yao et al., 2022),
088
      towards a less exploitative and more information-seeking attitude. With controlled experiments in
      bandit tasks expressed in language, we show that EAST is able to steer the agent towards more
089
      explorative behavior, effectively addressing the overconfidence exhibited by LLM agents.
090
091   We demonstrate that EAST generalizes to variations in prompts and LLMs. Surprisingly, we show that
092   the steering vectors we construct can transfer between tasks which are presented as different natural
093   language scenarios, but are equivalent from the sequential decision-making standpoint. Overall, the
094
      effectiveness of EAST and our in-depth analyses suggest that LLMs possess an abstract representation
      of the uncertainty over their decisions, and that it is possible to exercise direct control on it, paving
095
      the way to more interpretable and controllable LLM agents.

098   2     BACKGROUND
099
100   Modern language models interact with text input through a process of tokenization, in which a body
101   of text is broken down into small units known as tokens (Mielke et al., 2021). To begin, let Ω be
102
      a finite set of natural language tokens. We consider the set of token sequences of finite length, Ω∗ ,
      consisting of elements ω = (ω1 , . . . , ωn ) where n is the length of that sequence.
103
104   An LLM is a deep neural network, fθ , which maps a given sequence of tokens to a categorical distri-
105   bution over the next token that would follow, which we denote by pθ (· | ω). LLMs implement their
106   computations as a sequence of stacked layers, with the network producing intermediate activations
107   corresponding to each input token, z = fθℓ (ω) ∈ Rn×d for some layer ℓ and hidden dimension d. We
      write zi ∈ Rd for the activation corresponding to the i-th token.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      We are most commonly interested in producing completions C from the model given some prompt
109   P ∈ Ω∗ . This process proceeds by autoregressive sampling. We first sample a token c1 ∼ pθ (· | P ),
110   and then continue by the recurrence relation ck+1 ∼ pθ (· | P, c1 , . . . , ck ), repeating this process until
111   the model generates a special [EOS] token, yielding the completion C = (c1 , c2 , . . .). We denote
112   the distribution over completions implied by this process as LLM(· | P ).
113
      An in-context LLM agent interacts with an environment to perform a task described in an initial
114
      prompt P0 . At each timestep t ∈ {1, . . . , T }, the model generates a completion Ct ∼ LLM(·|Pt ). An
115   action at is then extracted by a parsing function, mapping the set C of possible completions to the set
116   A of possible actions in that environment. We consider this process of completion generation and
117   parsing to represent the agent’s stochastic policy over actions given some prompt, which we denote
118   π(·|Pt ). Note that the model’s completions may not always correspond to a valid action. In such cases,
119   the interaction immediately terminates. Once the action at is executed in the environment, it returns
120   some text feedback Ft to the agent. The interaction is iterated by concatenating the information into a
121   new prompt Pt+1 = (Pt , Ct , Ft ) up to the horizon T .
122   Our experiments focus on a Gaussian multi-armed bandit setting (Lattimore & Szepesvari, 2017), in
123   which the action space A is a set of possible arm choices and the feedback Ft is a string describing
124   a numerical reward drawn from a Gaussian distribution N (µa , σa ) associated to a particular arm
125   a ∈ A. At each round, the agent has to choose which arm to pick. The task description P0 tasks
126   the agent with maximizing the sum of the rewards it receives over time. This setting captures the
127   essential elements of self-evaluation and in-context learning across turns of interaction (Shinn et al.,
128   2023), making them easier to analyze.

131   3    R ELATED W ORK
132
133   By studying how LLM agents represent uncertainty and presenting a steering technique specific
134   for agents, our paper connects recent work in LLM agents and in representation engineering (Zou
135   et al., 2023). We will now provide an overview of the most relevant work from these two research
136   communities.
137
      LLM-based agents. LLMs have been recently employed for creating agents, leveraging their
138
      capabilities such as proposing actions (Wu et al., 2023), generating code (Wang et al., 2024; Ma
139   et al., 2024), or evaluating outcomes (Klissarov et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023). In this paper,
140   we focus on in-context LLM agents, which use the ability of LLMs to learn from data in their
141   prompt (Brown et al., 2020) to process a history of interactions with an environment. We employ an
142   LLM agent multi-armed bandit setup (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Schubert et al.,
143   2024; Binz & Schulz, 2023). The advantage of this setup resides in its ability to capture, in a more
144   controlled setting, essential aspects of good decision-making. These systems are typically based
145   on repeated interactions with a task, and heavily rely on the in-context learning abilities of existing
146   LLMs (Shinn et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Mirchandani et al., 2023). An important component in our
147
      discussion is the relationship between the token generation process and the action extraction process,
      which is encountered in recent work using reinforcement learning to train LLMs in decision-making
148
      tasks (Zhou et al., 2024).
149
150   Representations of LLMs and activation steering. Our analyses of the representation space of
151   LLM agents and our EAST method are closely related to recently proposed techniques for activation
152   steering (Subramani et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
153
      2024) and, more broadly, to the recent interest in interpreting the activations of LLMs (Zou et al.,
      2023; Heimersheim & Nanda, 2024). In particular, similarly to (Rimsky et al., 2023), we apply a
154
      steering vector during autoregressive unrolling by adding it to the activations at each position of
155
      generated tokens. Differently from these methods, the method we will present focuses on a sequential
156   decision-making setting. Furthermore, we intervene on the action entropy of an LLM agent by
157   leveraging a continuous-valued signal instead of the discrete contrastive approach applied in other
158   recent work (Rimsky et al., 2023; Turner et al., 2023). Our work is related to recent efforts on the
159   mechanistic interpretability of agents using reinforcement learning to navigate gridworlds (Mini et al.,
160   2023), or imitating humans to play chess (Karvonen, 2024). We instead focus on in-context LLM
161   agents based on pretrained models, connecting recent analyses of the representation space of LLMs
      in a supervised in-context learning setting (Hendel et al., 2023) to agentic use cases.

286   5   E NTROPIC ACTIVATION S TEERING
287
288   In the previous section, we have shown that changing the token sampling temperature does not have a
289   significant effect on the action distribution of the agent. Now, our motivation is to 1) identify at a
290   mechanistic level what drives exploratory behavior for an LLM agent and 2) develop new controls on
291   this behavior.
292   A natural candidate for doing this is the class of recent methods based on activation steering, which
293   derive steering vectors from datasets of LLM representations which are iteratively added to the
294   model’s activations during language model generation. These steering vectors are able to modulate
295   complex concepts such as refusal, sycophancy, or hallucination in an LLM’s outputs; the success of
296   this intervention also suggests that the steering vector directions represent semantically meaningful
297   concepts in LLM activation space (Rimsky et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024; Turner et al., 2023).
298   However, existing activation steering techniques are insufficient for intervening on the entropy of the
299   action distribution of an LLM agent, for two main reasons.
300
301         1. They typically assume access to a dataset with discrete labels for each prompt e.g. “harmful”
302
               or “safe”. In our setting, we are instead interested in controlling a continuous variable.
303         2. They are designed for non-agentic settings, in which each prompt is an i.i.d. sample from a
304            distribution and there is no feedback loop of interaction with an external system.
305   To overcome these shortcomings, we now introduce Entropic Activation Steering (EAST), an activa-
306   tion steering method that directly controls the LLM’s action entropy and subjective uncertainty by
307   intervening on its forward pass. EAST consists of two phases: first, computing a steering vector from
308   a dataset of interactions, and second, using the steering vector to modify the behavior of the agent.
309
      In the first phase, given a dataset of prompts Ptk obtained by letting the agent interact for K runs
310
      of T timesteps each, we compute the activations ztk = f ℓ (Ptk ) by giving a prompt Ptk as input,

312   the prompt. Then, for each prompt, we estimate the entropy hkt = − a∈A π(a|Ptk ) log(π(a|Ptk ))

314   corresponding action, and computing the entropy on the sampled actions. In practice, we use M = 25

344   The use of an entropy weight hkt generalizes the process for steering vector computation proposed in
345   previous work to a continuous setting in the following way. Existing methods typically work in a
346   contrastive fashion, obtaining the steering vector by subtracting the average representation of positive
347   examples from the average representation of negative examples. We can formally see this process as
348   one of defining a weight vector w and then taking an average of the representations in the dataset
349   weighted according to w. Through this lens, we can view existing contrastive methods as assigning
350   components wi such that wi ∈ {−1, 1} according to the label in the dataset. In this perspective, one
351   can interpret the entropy weight hkt as a continuous target, representing an extension of the implicit
352
      weighting implied by existing methods.
353   To handle the interactive nature of the decision task, EAST aggregates over independent runs P k
354   and timesteps within those runs Ptk . In contrast to the naïve approach of simply summing the
355   corresponding activations, EAST normalizes each activation ztk within a run by the average activation
356   over that run. We found in preliminary experiments that this approach is essential to produce
357   functioning steering vectors. This suggests that as the interaction history encoded in the prompt
358
      grows, LLM representations specialize to the particular events of that history; our normalization
      method works to target the specific component of representation space responsible for explorative
359
      behavior that is common across runs.
360
361   Overall, the first phase extracts a representation whose direction is aligned with the direction that
362   leads, on average, to high entropy. In the second phase, we apply the steering vector to influence the
363   LLM agent’s behavior. While generating a completion, we add the steering vector u, at each step,
364
      to the representation produced by the model at layer ℓ at the position of the last token. This yields
      a steered representation zbi = zi + βu, where β is a multiplier determining the amount of steering.
365
      Note that, when generating subsequent tokens after having applied the intervention on a previous
366
      activation, we keep that previous activation in the modified state until the action is executed.

517   7   C ONCLUSIONS
518
519
      In this paper, we studied how in-context LLM agents behave in sequential decision-making tasks
520   and how they represent uncertainty over actions. After having established that they tend to be
521   overconfident about their decisions, we introduced Entropic Activation Steering (EAST), a method for
522   influencing their exploration. We illustrated how token-level sampling and action generation interact,
523   and demonstrated that EAST can increase the entropy of an LLM agent’s action distribution and
524   alleviate its overconfidence, well beyond what is achievable by increasing the sampling temperature
525   at the token level. In addition, we have shown that EAST can modify the subjective uncertainty of an
526   LLM agent, influencing its thoughts towards more uncertain and explorative attitudes.
527   We believe that EAST can be used as a building block to steer an agent’s exploration in future
528   LLM-based systems and that EAST’s demonstration that LLMs explicitly represent uncertainty about
529   actions can inform the design of such systems. As designers of agentic LLM-based systems, it is
530   paramount for us to be able to interpret how they make decisions and to steer them towards more
531   desirable behaviors. EAST advances our understanding of the representation that in-context LLM
532   agents have about their uncertainty over decisions, and our ability to control it. Considering that
533   uncertainty over one’s actions is a fundamental aspect of successful decision-making, we believe our
534
      work to be a promising step in the development of interpretable and steerable in-context LLM agents.

724   A.1.3    C OMPUTATIONAL R ESOURCES
725
726
      All experiments were run on an internal compute cluster. All experiments require 8 CPUs and 32GB
      of memory. Because reproducing the experiments requires a large amount of LLM inference, we
727
      will focus the discussion here primarily on the GPU hardware and time used, as this is the main
728
      bottleneck.
729
730   The computational work required to reproduce the paper breaks down into a few types of experiments.
731   First, running interactions between the LLM and the bandit task: With T = 50 steps and M = 25
732
      completions per step, each single run requires about 10 minutes on 4x Nvidia A100 80GB GPUs,
      or 40 minutes in single GPU-minutes. This means that the results in Figure 2 took 3 ∗ 65 ∗ 40
733
      GPU-minutes = 130 GPU-hours. Extrapolating similarly to the experiments pictured in Figures 4 and
734
      the controlled interactions in Figure 6 produces a total estimate of 150 GPU-hours.
735
736   The EAST method itself is computationally inexpensive. Given the dataset of prompts {Ptk } we used
737   in Section 6.1 of size 3250, it requires computing the last-token activation for each prompt, a process
738
      which takes 1 GPU-hour on the same hardware mentioned above. Then, constructing the steering
      vector is a near-instant process of computing a weighted average, given the action entropies which
739
      were already recorded during the interaction stage.
740
741   Finally, computational resources were also dedicated to understanding the effects of EAST on a
742   validation set of 100 prompts, described in Section 6.2. These experiments require sweeping over a
743   large range of layers and multipliers at the cost of 20 GPU-minutes per layer and multiplier. As such,
744
      for all experiments in Section 6.2 the cost is approximately 100 GPU hours.
745   As such, the entirety of experiments in the paper required approximately 260 GPU-hours. The entirety
746   of work for the paper, including preliminary experiments, required an estimated 5x of this figure.
