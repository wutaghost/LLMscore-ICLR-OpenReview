011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multiple-choice question (MCQ) is a common task for evaluating large language
014            models (LLMs). LLMs’ performance on MCQ is often affected by various biases.
015            Previous research has extensively examined the impact of inherent option bias on
016            MCQ predictions, where this bias refers to a preference for a specific option ID
017            token introduced during the model’s training. However, in an in-context learning
018            scenario, few-shot prompting can also introduce a form of bias, known as context
019            option bias. This occurs, for instance, in extreme cases where all demonstration
020
               answers are consistently option A, in which case LLMs may predict A for the given
               question whatever the question is. Context option bias can significantly degrade
021
               LLMs’ performance. To better observe the LLMs’ behavior when affected by
022
               the context option bias, we deliberately use demonstrations with obvious context
023            option bias for MCQ to amplify the effect. The results indicate that certain attention
024            heads in LLMs are particularly sensitive to context option bias. Motivated by this
025            observation, we propose our approach, CoLo, to address this issue. First, using
026            samples with ordinary and biased demonstrations as input, CoLo compares the
027            outputs of two types of inputs and localizes attention heads sensitive to context
028            option bias through sequential interventions. Then, we propose an attention scaling-
029            based method to intervene in the identified attention heads during the inference
030            stage, thereby mitigating the impact of context option bias on the LLMs’ predictions.
031
               Experimental results demonstrate that CoLo effectively alleviates the impact of
               context option bias and improves the LLM’s robustness on MCQ tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Multiple-choice question (MCQ) is a common type of question-and-answer format in daily life and
039
      is also a common method used in the field of natural language processing to test the generalization
      ability of large language models (LLMs). There exist benchmarks and datasets specifically designed,
040
      spanning a variety of fields(Hendrycks et al., 2021; Talmor et al., 2018; Clark et al., 2018). We hope
041
      LLMs can well understand the question and choose the most appropriate answer from all options.
042   For this purpose, many efforts have been made and few-shot in-context learning has been shown to
043   be one of the effective methods. It provides some demonstrations before the question as prompts and
044   can largely improve an LLM’s performance on MCQs.
045
      Despite the above efforts, large language models are still often affected by certain biases and yield
046
      unexpected answers. Previous works(Wang et al., 2023; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Zheng et al.,
047
      2023) have investigated several inherent option bias of LLMs. For example, experiments indicate
048   that gpt-3.5-turbo tends to prefer option A (Zheng et al., 2023), which may result from the
049   uneven distribution of options in the training corpus. However, bias introduced by context, referred
050   to as context option bias, has not been carefully studied. As shown in Figure 1, take questions with
051   only two options as an example, if all demonstrations’ answers happen to be A, then LLMs will also
052   prefer to predict A no matter what question is given. In contrast, if answers for demonstrations do
053   not show too much preference to a certain option, the model will be more inclined to choose the
      correct answer, i.e., B in this example. The results indicate that context option bias has an impact on

075   However, demonstrations with evenly distributed options still introduce context option bias towards
076   certain answer choices. We observed that demonstrations with evenly distributed answer options
077

078

079

081   because the model tends to avoid selecting the same option consecutively, believing that the probability
082   of repeated occurrences of the same option is low, thus introducing a bias in answering questions
083   with similar option distributions.
084
      To better excavate the reasons behind this problem and resolve it, we deliberately construct some
085
      demonstrations with obvious context option bias and compare the model’s behavior under these
086   conditions with that under ordinary demonstrations. The experiments find that even with simple
087   option swaps in demonstrations, the attention map’s distribution in an LLM varies significantly.
088   This observation inspires us to test the attention distribution under conditions with obvious biased
089   demonstrations, identify the attention heads that are more sensitive to context option bias, and
090   intervene accordingly to mitigate the impact of context option bias on the model behavior.
091
      Following this motivation, we propose our method CoLo to localize and mitigate context option bias
092   in LLMs. Particularly, (1) CoLo first takes samples with biased demonstrations as input to obtain the
093   LLM’s predictions, which we refer to as biased answers. (2) We then intervene in different layers and
094   different attention heads within each layer. If the LLM’s predictions after the intervention is more
095   inclined towards the correct or ordinary answers, it indicates that the corresponding attention head is
096   sensitive to context option bias and likely to lead the model to biased answers. (3) CoLo selects a set
097   of attention heads that are most sensitive to context option bias and applies interventions to these
098   heads simultaneously during the subsequent inference stage.
099   Extensive experiments show that for MCQ in an in-context learning scenario, when there is context
100   option bias in demonstrations, our method can effectively mitigate the impact of the bias, improving
101   an LLMs’ accuracy on MCQ to a level similar to that with ordinary demonstrations without affecting
102   other LLMs’ abilities. Besides, the identified attention heads demonstrate powerful generalization
103   across datasets and tasks. That is, for a certain model, the attention heads identified with one dataset
104   also applies to other datasets. More importantly, even when the demonstrations look unbiased,
105   applying our proposed intervention can also further enhance the model’s accuracy. We assume it

      2     R ELATED WORK
121
      Inference intervention techniques. Inference intervention comprises various methods designed
122
      to modulate the behavior of large-scale models post-training. Commonly employed inference
123
      intervention strategies include activation editing(Li et al., 2024b), weight editing(Dai et al., 2022;
124   Meng et al., 2022), guidance vectors(Zou et al., 2023), and alter the output distribution through
125   comparison(Li et al., 2022; Chuang et al., 2023). Our method focuses on modifying the attention
126   distribution by attention scaling, which is a technique that originates from GPT-2(Radford et al.,
127   2019). Contrary to the broader method of global attention scaling of GPT-2, our approach is distinctly
128   less invasive, selectively targeting attention heads that are associated with context option bias. By
129   identifying and scaling the attention heads linked to context option bias, our method effectively
130   mitigates this bias using a significantly smaller dataset, requiring far fewer examples than those
131   needed for reinforcement learning (RL) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and fine-tuning-based methods (Hu
132
      et al., 2021). Our approach to structuring the comparison of specific contexts is analogous to the ICD
      method(Zhang et al., 2024).
133
134   Bias of LLMs. Our study uses bias to refer to the production of systematic errors in a model.
135   Although In-context learning can bring considerable task learning capabilities to the model(Pan,
136   2023), but Turpin et al. (2024) assess the LLMs’ unfaithfulness to CoT interpretation by constructing
137   context option bias features. That mean the abilities brought by in-context learning are not necessarily
138
      useful. Pezeshkpour & Hruschka (2023) point out the sensitivity of option position to model multiple-
      choice questions and Zheng et al. (2023) suggest that the option bias of the model comes more from
139
      the option token bias, and proposed Pride, a method to alleviate the option prior bias by estimating
140
      the prior and applying prior debiasing. Distinct from the Pride, CoLo intervenes internally within the
141   model to mitigate context option bias without impacting text generation processes, such as Chain of
142   Thought (CoT)(Kojima et al., 2022). This technique requires only a minimal number of samples to
143   identify a general head that responds to biases across various contexts. In contrast, Pride functions
144   primarily as a pre-processing technology, necessitating recalibration of bias each time the context
145   changes, and it often performs suboptimally in scenarios with limited data samples.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
150
151   During the model’s inference, different attention heads perform distinct functions(Zhang & Nanda,
152   2024). While some attention heads may correspond to option bias, it is not feasible to directly identify
153   which ones specifically linked to option bias during inference.
154
      To identify attention heads strongly associated with option bias, it is necessary to amplify the presence
155
      of the bias. Due to the difficulty of directly manipulating the model’s inherent option bias, we choose
156   to amplify context option bias by constructing biased demonstrations. We can get the state output of
157   the model in these two different situations stateb and stateo corresponding to biased and ordinary
158   demonstrations respectively. The gap between the two states arises from the amplification of context
159   option bias. We employ an intervention function f that acts on specific attention heads to reduce the
160   state difference between stateb and stateo . The extent of this reduction is compared to evaluate the
161   correlation strength between these attention heads and context option bias, allowing us to select the
      most relevant set of attention heads. The algorithm flow is described in detail in Section 3.2

      After identifying attention heads strongly associated with context option bias, different intervention
163   methods are employed to modify the attention distribution of these heads and mitigate context option
164   bias. We describe the intervention methods utilized in Section 3.3.

285   3.4   D ISCUSSION
286
287   How many additional labeled samples are required for CoLo? Small amount of additional
288   sample are required in Algorithm 1. Moreover, we can expand the dataset by modifying the context
289
      option bias in the demonstration. For instance, in a four-option MCQ, we can alter the options to
      consist entirely of A/B/C/D, thereby increasing the sample size fourfold. Clearly, a greater number of
290
      additional samples enhances the stability of the final attention head. We ultimately decided to use
291
      only 10 additional samples and expanded the dataset to N =40 by experiment.
292
293   How much additional computational overhead does CoLo introduce? The additional compu-
294   tational overhead introduced by the localization process is proportional to the number of samples
295
      N used for positioning. In our experiment, the model requires approximately 150N additional
      inferences, which depends on the number of layers and attention heads in the model. The calculation
296
      method is detailed in Appendix E. However, once the relevant attention heads are identified, it can be
297
      applied to other datasets without incurring any further computational overhead during inference.

367   4.2   ROBUSTNESS OF DIFFERENT EXPERIMENT SETTINGS
368
      To validate whether the attention heads obtained through CoLo genuinely correspond to context
369
      option bias, we perform mode transformations on the original MCQ input format, varying factors
370
      such as the number of options, the length of demonstrations, and the format of option identifiers.
371   Through these experiments, we aim to verify the following two points:
372
373         1. Whether the attention heads selected in the original MMLU remain useful to mitigate context
374            option bias after the mode transformations.
375         2. Whether the attention heads selected in different modes is consistent.

526   5              C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   We propose CoLo, a general localization method designed to mitigate context option bias and improve
529   the accuracy of MCQ. Specifically, by amplifying context option bias through rearranging the order
530   of options in demonstrations, we compare the LLMs’ outputs of biased and ordinary demonstration to
531   localizing attention heads strongly associated with context option bias. Attention scaling interventions
532   are then applied to reduce this bias. CoLo requires only small labeled samples to localize attention
533   heads which can be applied across different datasets. Once the attention heads requiring intervention
534   are identified, CoLo introduces no additional inference delay. Compared to previous debiasing
535   methods, CoLo has a natural advantage in extending to generative tasks.
536   While we have provided a plausible explanation for why the identified attention heads can mitigate
537   option bias, there remains a lack of theoretical proof supporting the efficacy of this method in reducing
538   such bias. Additionally, although the selection of attention heads shows generalizability, achieving
539   optimal results necessitates that the small labeled sample set used for positioning be identical to the
      final test set, which will incur additional offline computational overhead.

      I    S UPPLEMENTARY EXPERIMENTS
834
835
      We explored Kl and Kh using Gemma-2B model in Table 9. When the number of heads is between
836   6 and 8, the performance remains consistent; however, as the number of heads increases further, the
837   effectiveness of the method diminishes. Similarly, the number of intervention layers should not be
838   excessive. Based on our findings, we recommend using 2–3 intervention layers and 6–8 heads as
839   the most suitable configuration. Furthermore, with regard to the intervention layer, our experiments
840   on various models have identified a pattern: the optimal intervention layer is typically located in the
841   middle layers of the model.
842   In our study, we initially selected 10 samples but did not investigate or elaborate on how sample size
843   influences the method’s performance. To address this limitation, we conducted additional experiments
844   using Gemma-2b on the MMLU benchmark, varying the number of samples. Each experiment was
845   repeated five times, with the mean accuracy and Rstd reported. The results demonstrate that when
846   the sample size reaches 10, the performance improvement is approaching a plateau. Based on these
847   findings, we estimate that the minimum number of samples required is approximately 10–12 as shown
848   in Table 10. For improved stability, we recommend using 12 samples.
