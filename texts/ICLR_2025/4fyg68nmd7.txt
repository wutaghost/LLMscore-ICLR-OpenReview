009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                When trained on large-scale object classification datasets, certain artificial neural
012                network models begin to approximate core object recognition (COR) behaviors
013                and neural response patterns in the primate visual ventral stream (VVS). While
014                recent machine learning advances suggest that scaling model size, dataset size,
015                and compute resources improve task performance, the impact of scaling on brain
016                alignment remains unclear. In this study, we explore scaling laws for modeling
017                the primate VVS by systematically evaluating over 600 models trained under con-
018
                   trolled conditions on benchmarks spanning V1, V2, V4, IT and COR behaviors.
                   We observe that while behavioral alignment continues to scale with larger models,
019
                   neural alignment saturates. This observation remains true across model architec-
020
                   tures and training datasets, even though models with stronger inductive bias and
021                datasets with higher-quality images are more compute-efficient. Increased scaling
022                is especially beneficial for higher-level visual areas, where small models trained
023                on few samples exhibit only poor alignment. Finally, we develop a scaling recipe,
024                indicating that a greater proportion of compute should be allocated to data sam-
025                ples over model size. Our results suggest that while scaling alone might suffice for
026                alignment with human core object recognition behavior, it will not yield improved
027                models of the brain’s visual ventral stream with current architectures and datasets,
028                highlighting the need for novel strategies in building brain-like models. 1

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The advent of neural networks has revolutionized our understanding and modeling of complex neural
034
      processes. A particularly active area of study is the ventral visual stream in primates, a key pathway
      in the brain responsible for processing visual information Goodale & Milner (1992); Grill-Spector
035
      et al. (2001); Malach et al. (2002); Kriegeskorte et al. (2008). Neural networks, when trained on
036
      extensive datasets, have emerged as the most accurate quantitative tools for simulating the response
037   patterns of neurons within this stream Yamins et al. (2014); Schrimpf et al. (2018). These advanced
038   models offer a precise computational account of how neural mechanisms in the brain give rise to
039   visual perception.
040
      Recent developments in machine learning have emphasized the significance of both the volume of
041
      training data and the complexity of model architectures Kaplan et al. (2020); Hoffmann et al. (2022);
042
      Zhai et al. (2022); Bahri et al. (2022); Antonello et al. (2023); Muennighoff et al. (2023); Aghajanyan
043   et al. (2023); Isik et al. (2024). These findings raise the question: Can we build better models of
044   the brain by scaling up model architectures and dataset sizes? Recent studies have found that in
045   pre-trained models, the number of parameters and dataset samples respectively seem to improve
046   predictions of fMRI and behavioral measurements (Antonello et al., 2023; Muttenthaler et al., 2023).
047   With the numerous differences between pre-trained models however, the relative contributions of
048   model parameters and dataset size to brain and behavioral alignment are not clear.
049   In this paper, we examine how scaling – of model parameters and training dataset size – impacts
050   the alignment of artificial neural networks with the primate ventral visual stream. We systematically
051   train models from a variety of architectural families on image classification datasets which allows us
052   to independently control and observe the effects of model complexity and data volume. To capture

      2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Primate Visual Ventral Stream. The ventral visual stream, a critical pathway in the primate
089   brain, including humans, plays a key role in visual perception, extending from the occipital to the
090   temporal lobes and serving as the ”what pathway” for object recognition and form representation
091   Goodale & Milner (1992); Grill-Spector et al. (2001); Malach et al. (2002); Kriegeskorte et al.
092   (2008). Beginning in the primary visual cortex (V1), where basic visual information from retinal
093   ganglion cells is processed, the ventral stream proceeds through areas such as V2, V3, V4, and the
094   inferotemporal cortex (IT), each responsible for increasingly complex features of visual perception
095   Kandel et al. (2000). Despite decades of research and a wealth of brain data, the precise neural
096
      mechanisms underlying visual perception are not well understood.
097
      Modeling the Primate Visual Ventral Stream. Particular artificial neural networks (ANNs) are
098
      the most accurate models of brain responses in the visual ventral stream and associated core ob-
099
      ject recognition behaviors Schrimpf et al. (2018; 2020). Models optimized for ecologically viable
100   tasks (Yamins & DiCarlo, 2016) in particular have demonstrated strong brain and behavioral align-
101   ment (Yamins et al., 2014; Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014; Cadena et al., 2019; Schrimpf
102   et al., 2018; Nayebi et al., 2018; Kietzmann et al., 2019; Rajalingham et al., 2018; Zhuang et al.,
103   2021; Geiger et al., 2022) – notably these models are trained purely on image classification datasets,
104   without fitting to brain data.
105
106   Scaling Laws. Recent advancements in artificial intelligence are driven by scaling the model size
107   and training data. Empirical evidence suggests a power-law relationship between model perfor-
      mance and both model parameters and dataset size, indicating that continued scaling will further

108
      improve performance Kaplan et al. (2020); Cherti et al. (2023); Zhai et al. (2022); Hoffmann et al.
109   (2022); Dehghani et al. (2023); Henighan et al. (2020); Brown et al. (2020); Bahri et al. (2022);
110   Hestness et al. (2017). The power-law exponents enable the optimal allocation of compute between
111   model parameters and dataset samples, such that performance is maximized Kaplan et al. (2020);
112   Hoffmann et al. (2022).
113
      While scaling laws for machine learning performance has been extensively studied, the scaling laws
114
      for brain alignment remain unclear. Recent studies indeed suggest an involvement of both model
115   size and data volume in the functional alignment with brain data Azabou et al. (2023); Benchetrit
116   et al. (2023); Caro et al. (2024); Antonello et al. (2023). Conversely, Muttenthaler et al. Muttenthaler
117   et al. (2023) indicate that sample size is critical for behavioral alignment. We here unify these results,
118   in the realm of the primate visual ventral stream, into quantitative scaling laws for how model and
119   dataset sizes relate to alignment with the brain and behavior.

      Neural & Behavioral Alignment. To evaluate the alignment of our model with brain function, we
124   utilize a range of benchmarks from Brain-Score Schrimpf et al. (2018; 2020). These benchmarks
125   assess model performance by comparing model activations or behavior with primate neural data
126   using the same images. Specifically, the V1 and V2 benchmarks compare model outputs to primate
127   single-unit recordings from Freeman et al. (2013), using 315 texture images and data from 102
128   V1 and 103 V2 neurons. For the V4 and IT benchmarks, 2,560 images are used to match model
129   activations to primate Utah array recordings from Majaj et al. (2015), based on data from 88 V4
130   and 168 IT electrodes. A linear regression is trained on 90% of the images to correlate model and
131   neural data, with prediction accuracy for the remaining 10% evaluated using Pearson correlation,
132
      repeated ten times for cross-validation. The behavioral benchmark assesses model predictions for
      240 images against primate behavioral data from Rajalingham et al. (2018) using a logistic classifier
133
      trained on 2,160 labeled images. Pearson correlation is used to measure the similarity in confusion
134
      patterns between model predictions and primate responses. All benchmark scores are normalized to
135   their respective maximum possible values.
136
137   We define the model’s alignment score S (and an inverse Misalignment Score L = 1 − S) as the
138
      average across the V1, V2, V4, IT, and behavioral benchmark scores. Layers are committed to brain
      regions based on models trained on a full dataset, and applied to all variants trained with subsampled
139
      datasets. As we reused the same neural and behavioral data both to select the optimal model layer
140
      for readout and to assess the model’s alignment, we validated the benchmark results on a private
141   split of each dataset on Brain-Score. We observed an almost perfect correlation between the results
142   on the private and public splits (Appendix C).
143
144   Scaling Models and Data. We trained an array of standard models from several architecture
145   families. Specifically, we used ResNet18, 34, 50, 101, 152 from He et al. (2016); EfficientNet-
146   B0, 1, 2 from Tan & Le (2019); Vision Transformer ViTT, S, B, L from Dosovitskiy et al. (2021);
147   ConvNeXtT, S, B, L from Liu et al. (2022b); CORnet-S from Kubilius et al. (2019); and AlexNet
148   from Krizhevsky et al. (2012). We also trained 33 modified versions of ResNet18: 22 models ob-
149   tained by scaling the network width from 1/16 to 4 times the original size, and 11 models derived
150   by adjusting the depth. Similarly, we trained four additional ConvNeXt and ViT models by scaling
151   the width of the ConvNeXt-T and ViT-S architectures.
152   For our experiments, we selected two image classification datasets: ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) and
153   EcoSet Mehrer et al. (2021). ImageNet, with millions of labeled images across 1,000 categories, has
154   long been a benchmark in computer vision, designed to challenge and evaluate automated visual
155   object recognition systems. On the other hand, EcoSet is a more recent dataset, designed to provide
156   an ecologically valid representation of human-relevant objects. It contains over 1.5 million images
157   spanning 565 basic-level categories, curated to better reflect the natural distribution of objects in the
158
      real world, aligning with human perceptual and cognitive experiences.
159   To create subsets of ImageNet and EcoSet, we sampled d ∈ 1, 3, 10, 30, 100, 300 images per cate-
160   gory. For d ∈ 1, 10, 100, we repeated the runs with three random seeds to ensure robustness. For
161   ConvNeXts (Liu et al., 2022b) and ViTs (Touvron et al., 2022), we used the training recipes de-
      veloped by the original model authors. The remaining models were trained for 100 epochs using a

289
290   Experimental results indicate that modern architectures, such as ConvNeXt and Vision Transformers
291
      (ViTs), exhibit poorer neural alignment compared to models like ResNets and EfficientNets in low
      data regime. ResNets and EfficientNets, which have stronger inductive biases due to their fully con-

      volutional structures, demonstrate high neural alignment even at initialization. In Fig. 2, alignment
293

294   samples, however ConvNeXt and ViT requires more compute in order to start rising.
295
296   This difference in initial alignment also affects how the scaling laws evolve for each architecture.
297
      Models with weaker inductive biases require more extensive scaling—specifcally in terms of train-

298
      Consequently, the scaling curves for ConvNeXt and ViT models develop differently, highlighting
299
      that architectural choices not only impact baseline alignment but also influence the efficiency of
300   scaling strategies.

      4.3    M ORE DATA I S B ETTER T HAN M ORE PARAMETERS
304
      Our analysis reveals that increasing the size of the training dataset has a more significant impact
305
      on improving brain alignment than simply enlarging the number of model parameters. While both
306   strategies lead to performance enhancements, the benefits from data scaling exhibit less severe di-
307   minishing returns compared to model scaling. Specifically, models trained on larger datasets consis-
308   tently demonstrate superior neural and behavioral alignment with the primate ventral visual stream,
309   following a predictable power-law relationship.
310
      In contrast, expanding the model size without proportionally increasing the training data results in
311
      steeper diminishing returns in alignment performance. Larger models rapidly reach a point where
312   additional parameters do not translate into meaningful improvements. Fig. 2c estimates a saturation
313   level of 0.44 by scaling model sizes with all samples of training data whereas Fig. 3a predicts
314   maximum alignment of 0.48 and 0.50 for ImageNet and Ecoset respectively. This indicates that
315   scaling training datasets overall improves brain alignment better than models scaling. Furthermore,
316   Fig. 4b demonstrates that larger models of the same architecture family require much more samples
317   to achieve the same level of alignment.
318   To quantitatively capture the joint interaction between data and model scaling, we fitted a parametric
319   curve based on Eq.5, as shown in Fig.4a. This curve effectively models how compute (C), dataset
320   size (D), and model size (N ) collectively influence brain alignment. Utilizing the parametric rela-
321   tionships described in Eq. 9, we estimate that additional compute should be allocated following the
322   scaling laws D ≈ C 0.7 and N ≈ C 0.3 . These exponents indicate that, for optimal brain alignment,
323   computational resources should be predominantly invested in increasing the dataset size rather than
      the model size.

      4.4    O RDERED EFFECT OF SCALE ON ALIGNMENT
342
      Our study reveals a graded effect of scaling on alignment across the cortical hierarchy of the primate
343   visual system. Specifically, we observe that the benefits of increased training compute—achieved
344   through larger datasets and more complex models—vary systematically among different brain re-
345   gions, reflecting their position in the visual processing pathway. Fig. 5.a illustrates the alignment
346   as a function of training compute across various brain regions. We categorized the models into two
347   groups based on their architectural inductive biases. Group 1 includes most models with strong in-
348   ductive biases, such as ResNets and EfficientNets. These models start with higher neural alignment
349   scores even at initialization due to their fully convolutional architectures. Group 2 consists of mod-
350   els with weaker inductive biases, specifically ConvNeXt and Vision Transformers (ViTs). These
351
      models exhibit lower neural alignment in the low-data regime and require more compute to achieve
      similar alignment levels.
352
353   To quantify the impact of scaling on each brain region, we define the alignment gain per region as
354   A10α where A and α are parameters of Eq. 2. Our findings indicate that higher regions in the cortical
355   hierarchy show greater benefits from increased compute. Fig. 5b illustrates the alignment gain per
356   region, highlighting how higher cortical areas benefit more from scaling efforts. This ordered effect
357
      suggests that regions higher up in the visual hierarchy, such as the Inferior Temporal (IT) cortex and
      behavioral outputs, gain more substantially from additional data and increased model complexity. In
358
      contrast, early visual areas like V1 and V2 exhibit smaller alignment gains with increased compute,
359
      indicating a potential saturation effect.

362   5     D ISCUSSION
363
364   We establish scaling laws governing the effect of model and dataset scale on behavioral and brain
365   alignment with the primate visual ventral stream. While scale is a necessary component for all brain-
366
      like models, model architectures with priors such as convolutions, and datasets with high-quality
      images are more sample efficient, leading to alignment with smaller compute requirements. Scale
367
      especially improves alignment with higher-level visual regions, but brain alignment saturates across
368
      all conditions tested here whereas behavioral alignment continuously improves with increased scale.
369
370

371
      neural network models are converging. This observation has been likened to Plato’s concept of
372   an ideal reality (Huh et al., 2024). Our findings support this view in that neural network models
373   at scale tend to converge toward similar brain and behavioral alignment, regardless of their initial
374   architectural differences. However, we find that the representational convergence in current models
375   is distinct from representations in the brain’s visual ventral stream. Intriguingly, behavioral choices
376   on the other hand seem to converge to human-like core object recognition, suggesting that there
377   is no unique solution to human-like behavior. Even models with differing inductive biases, such
      as ConvNeXt and ViT (which start with poorer neural alignment), eventually converge to the same

420   Generalization Beyond Supervised Training. We assessed whether alternative training
421   paradigms can overcome the limitations observed in neural alignment under supervised learning.
422   Figure 7a illustrates the scaling of alignment as a function of compute spent during self-supervised
423   training of ResNet models using SimCLR Chen et al. (2020) on ImageNet. The results confirm the
424   trends observed in supervised training: behavioral alignment continues to improve with increased
425   compute, following a strong power-law relationship, while neural alignment approaches a saturation
426   point. This consistency suggests that the saturation in neural alignment is not exclusive to supervised
427   learning but may be inherent to the models or datasets employed.
428   The region-specific breakdown (as illustrated in Figure 13 of the appendix) further reinforces this
429   observation. Even in a self-supervised learning context, higher-level visual areas like IT and be-
430   havioral outputs demonstrate more pronounced improvements with increased compute, while early
431   visual areas like V1 and V2 show minimal gains. This suggests that the hierarchical nature of neural

      Taken together, our results demonstrate that while scaling both model parameters and training data
519
      size enhances behavioral alignment with human visual perception, it leads to saturation in neural
520   alignment with the primate ventral visual stream. Data scaling proves more effective than model
521   scaling in improving alignment, emphasizing the critical role of extensive and diverse training
522   datasets. We also find that architectural choices significantly influence alignment efficiency, with
523   models possessing strong inductive biases—such as fully convolutional networks—achieving higher
524   neural alignment even at initialization. Additionally, the impact of scaling varies across different
525   brain regions, benefiting higher cortical areas more than early visual areas. These findings suggest
526   that merely increasing scale is insufficient for modeling the intricate neural representations of the
527   brain’s visual system. Future work should investigate new approaches, including alternative archi-
528
      tectures and training strategies, to develop models that more accurately reflect the complexities of
      neural processing in the primate visual cortex.

533   compute-efficient models achieving higher neural alignment without extensive scaling. Addition-
534

871

873   heads. This library streamlines the process of setting up models for SimCLR and DINO training

877

881   B     A DDITIONAL I MAGE DATASETS
882
883
      To further validate our findings across diverse image distributions and to estimate scaling curves
      across different sample scales, we trained ResNet18 models on subsets of several large-scale image
884
      datasets: ImageNet-21k-P, WebVision-P, iNaturalist, and Places365. Below, we provide detailed
885
      descriptions of each dataset.
886
887
      B.1   I MAGE N ET 21 K -P
888
889   ImageNet-21k-P is a processed subset of the full ImageNet-21k dataset (Ridnik et al., 2021), which
890   originally contains over 14 million images organized into more than 21,000 categories following
891   the WordNet hierarchy. The ”P” denotes a pruned version where classes with insufficient images or
892   noisy labels are filtered out to enhance dataset quality. This results in a refined dataset that maintains
893   the richness of the original ImageNet-21k while improving label accuracy and image relevance. The
894   resulting dataset contains approximately 11 million training images across 10,450 classes.
895
896   B.2   W EB V ISION -P
897
      The WebVision dataset (Li et al., 2017) is a large-scale web image dataset designed to provide a
898
      real-world, noisy alternative to ImageNet. It originally contains over 16 million images categorized
899   into 5,000 classes. The images are collected from the internet using queries from search engines
900   like Google and Flickr, leading to a dataset that includes label noise, varying image resolutions,
901   and diverse visual contexts. Due to classes with very few available samples, we processed the
902   WebVision dataset similarly to ImageNet-21k-P to remove classes with insufficient images. The
903   resulting dataset, which we denote as WebVision-P, contains approximately 13.5 million training
904   images across 4,189 categories.
905
906   B.3   I NATURALIST
907
908
      iNaturalist Van Horn et al. (2018) contains 2.7 million photographs of organisms in their natural
      environments, representing 10000 species. The dataset features highly specialized fine-grained cat-
909
      egories and natural backgrounds, offering insight into how domain-specific visual features influence
910
      alignment scaling.
911
912
      B.4   P LACES 365
913
914   Places365 Zhou et al. (2017) is a large-scale scene-centric dataset containing approximately 1.8 mil-
915   lion training images across 365 scene categories. Unlike object-centric datasets such as ImageNet,
916   Places365 focuses on the recognition of environmental scenes, including natural landscapes, urban
917   settings, and indoor environments. Each category includes a wide variety of images to capture the
      diversity within scene types.

       D    P RETRAINED M ODELS
973
974
       As part of our comprehensive evaluation, we benchmarked a diverse set of pretrained models sourced
975    from both torchvision (maintainers & contributors, 2016) and the timm (Wightman, 2019)
976    libraries. We tested a total of 94, including ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2021), DaViT Ding et al. (2022),
977    LeViT Graham et al. (2021), ConvNeXt Liu et al. (2022a), MobileViT Mehta & Rastegari (2022),
978    MaxVit Tu et al. (2022), FastViT Vasu et al. (2023). Each model varies in parameter count, training
979    sample size, dataset source, and training objective, providing a broad spectrum for analysis.
980
       To verify the generalizability of our findings, we conducted evaluations with these pretrained mod-
981    els, including larger networks like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023),
982    which are pretrained on richer and more diverse datasets such as LAION Schuhmann et al. (2021;
983    2022). We also compared variations of these models by examining base pretrained models alongside
984    their fine-tuned counterparts on ImageNet, aiming to investigate the impact of fine-tuning on scaling
985    behavior.
986
       Our results indicate that models with extensive pretraining achieve enhanced behavioral alignment,
987    likely due to their exposure to richer and more varied data. However, similar to models trained
988    solely on ImageNet or EcoSet, these pretrained models still exhibit a saturation effect in neural
989    alignment with the primate visual ventral stream (VVS). This suggests that while larger and more
990    diverse datasets improve behavioral predictability, they do not substantially extend the scaling of
991    neural alignment beyond the observed plateau.
992
       The curves in Figure 9 closely follow the scaling patterns estimated for our trained models shown
993    in Figure 2.c, further validating that the observed saturation is consistent across different pretrain-
994    ing regimes and dataset scales. This reinforces our conclusion that scaling alone is insufficient to
995    overcome the limitations in neural alignment and highlights the need for alternative approaches to
996    improve alignment with neural representations.
