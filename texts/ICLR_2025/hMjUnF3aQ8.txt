      RL is the problem of finding an optimal policy, π : S →   − A, mapping states to actions, by an agent
021   which makes a decision in an environment and learn by trial and error. We model the problem by an
022   MDP Puterman (1994). Let at ∈ A be an action chosen at timestep t at state st ∈ S, leads to the
023   state st+1 ∈ S in the probability of P (st+1 |st , a) and resulting in the immediate reward of rt+1 .
024
      Q-learning Watkins & Dayan (1992), introduced in Watkins’ thesis Watkins (1989), is a widely-
025
      used tabular model-free algorithm that suffers from overestimation bias. This bias occurs when the
026
      Q-values for a policy π are overestimated relative to the true Q-values. The source of this issue is
027   the optimization of Q-values with respect to the argmax policy, which selects the maximum Q-value
028   during updates. These overestimated values are then propagated throughout the entire table via the
029   update process, ultimately degrading the algorithms performance.
030
      Double Q-learning Hasselt (2010) solves this problem by introducing the double estimator, updat-
031
      ing QA by QB Q-values with the action of a∗ = arg maxa∈A QA , ensures that a∗ is unbiased w.r.t.
032
      QB , turning the problem into an underestimation bias–where the Q-estimations are lower than the
033
      real MC returns–which cause to a poor exploration and finally to poor performance.
034
035   Weighted Double Q-learning Zhang et al. (2017) seeks to strike a balance between traditional Q-
036   learning and Double Q-learning by introducing a weighting factor w ∈ (0, 1). This factor combines
037
      the single-estimator and double-estimator in a unified update rule, with the Q-target estimation given
      by:

      SQT leverages ensemble methods as a mechanism to address overestimation bias, combining math-
057   ematical rigor with psychological principles. Mathematically, SQT minimizes the TD error through
058   critic updates using an ensemble of Q-networks. The loss function is formulated as:
059

066
067   In this formulation, the ensemble operator Qi=1...N combines predictions from N distinct Q-
068   networks to compute the Q-target, while the term SQT[B], defined as the standard deviation of
069   Q-network outputs, serves as a regularization mechanism. This regularization penalizes excessive
070   disagreement between networks, which can result from overestimations. The use of a batch-mean
071   standard deviation ensures that this penalization adapts dynamically based on the uncertainty in the
072
      predictions.
073   From a psychological perspective, this approach mirrors how humans make decisions under uncer-
074   tainty. In situations where biases or noise may distort judgment, individuals often consult multiple
075   perspectives or experts to reduce the impact of a single, potentially biased viewpoint. This concept,
076   known as the wisdom of the crowd, is reflected in SQT’s ensemble method, where multiple Q-
077   networks provide diverse estimates, resulting in a more balanced and accurate policy. The diversity
078
      of network outputs serves to offset optimistic biases that can arise from relying on a single estimator.
079   This mathematical strategy of aggregating ensemble predictions to compute target values, combined
080   with a penalty on disagreement, captures the cognitive process of weighing diverse opinions to re-
081   duce bias and make more reliable decisions. In human psychology, consulting multiple perspectives
082   leads to a reduction in overconfidence and improved decision accuracy. Similarly, in SQT, the en-
083   semble method mitigates overestimation bias by curbing the excessive optimism that can emerge
084
      from noise or errors in individual Q-networks.
085   By uniting these mathematical and psychological insights, ensemble methods offer a powerful so-
086   lution to overestimation bias in reinforcement learning. They provide both a formal mechanism
087   for smoothing out optimistic value predictions and a cognitive framework for incorporating diverse
088   estimates, promoting more cautious and reliable decision-making Tversky & Kahneman (1974);
089   Surowiecki (2005).

091   3   B IAS
092
093   Overestimation bias, a cognitive phenomenon rooted in human psychology, manifests as the ten-
094   dency to overestimate abilities, outcomes, or success probabilities in uncertain contexts. In behav-
095   ioral economics and psychology, this bias is exemplified by the Dunning-Kruger effect, where indi-
096   viduals with lower competence tend to overestimate their abilities, and optimism bias, which leads
097   to inflated expectations of positive outcomes and a disregard for potential risks Kruger & Dunning
098   (1999); Sharot (2011).
099   In the context of reinforcement learning, particularly in Q-learning, a similar phenomenon arises.
100   The overestimation of Q-values occurs because the algorithm maximizes over estimated rewards,
101   which, when subject to noise or approximation errors, results in upwardly biased value predictions.
102   This behavior parallels human decision-making, where overly optimistic assessments often lead to
103   suboptimal choices in situations of uncertainty Kahneman (2011).
104   Mathematically, in Q-learning with discrete actions Watkins & Dayan (1992), the value update is
105   performed using a greedy target, defined as:
106
107
                                             y = r + γ max Q(s, a)                                        (5)
                                                             a

109   Q(s, a), the expected value of this maximum is typically greater than the true value, as shown by
110   Jensens inequality Jensen (1906):

                                                   a                               a

116   Even when errors are initially zero-mean, this mechanism results in a persistent overestimation bias,
117   which is propagated through the Bellman updates. As errors are inherent in function approximation,
118
      this bias poses a significant challenge in practice An et al. (2021); Shi et al. (2022); Kakade &
      Langford (2002); Heger (1994); Kumar et al. (2020); Lyu et al. (2022).
119
120   On the other hand, TD3 Fujimoto et al. (2018) introduces a lower bound approximation by using
121   two Q-networks to form a conservative Q-target. However, this approach has its limitations, as max-
122   imizing the lower bound can result in a policy that is overly concentrated near a specific maximum.
123   When the critic’s estimates are erroneous and the maximum is spurious, this can lead to a systematic
124
      underestimation bias, resulting in suboptimal performance Ciosek et al. (2019). Mathematically, the
      expected value under such conditions follows:

129
130   This behavior underscores the inefficiency of relying solely on lower bound approximations for
131   exploration.
132
      While methods like Double Q-learning and ensemble techniques have been introduced to address
133
      overestimation bias in reinforcement learning, they often introduce a new issue: underestimation
134   bias. This occurs as a result of overly conservative Q-value estimates, which can skew action se-
135   lection towards suboptimal choices. In Double Q-learning, the decoupling of action selection from
136   evaluation reduces the risk of overestimation, but it can also lead to underexploration, as the policy
137   becomes too cautious in selecting actions. Similarly, ensemble methods that aggregate multiple Q-
138   networks to mitigate overestimation may suffer from excessive pessimism, where the policy under-
139   values potential rewards. This underestimation bias stifles exploration, ultimately degrading perfor-
140   mance in more complex tasks. Thus, while these methods provide a safeguard against overoptimism,
141   their tendency to undervalue future rewards requires careful balancing to avoid underexploration and
142
      the associated performance decline.

422   of SQT’s global penalty mechanism, which leverages the wisdom of the crowd principle by applying
423   a single penalty based on the batch standard deviation across all state-action pairs. In more complex
424   environments like humanoid, where large state and action spaces introduce greater uncertainty, this
425   global penalty helps smooth out inconsistencies by considering the broader context of the entire
426   batch. This approach makes SQT more robust in high-dimensional settings, effectively reducing the
427   overestimation bias and leading to more reliable policy improvements.
428   While SQTtuple excels in simpler environments due to its precise, localized penalties, the global batch
429   penalty in SQT proves essential for solving complex tasks, as evidenced by its superior performance
430   in the humanoid environment. Overall, this demonstrates the strength of SQT in handling greater
431   uncertainty in complex environments by utilizing the ensemble of predictions across the batch to
      balance exploration and exploitation.

      9   R ELATED WORK
433
434
435
      Overestimation bias is a common challenge in reinforcement learning, particularly in value-based
436
      methods that rely on function approximators like deep neural networks. Several approaches have
437
      been proposed to mitigate this issue, each with its own strengths and limitations.
438
439   Double Q-learning Hasselt (2010) was one of the earliest approaches designed to counter overesti-
440   mation bias. It achieves this by employing two distinct Q-tables, decoupling the processes of action
441
      selection and action evaluation. Specifically, the action chosen by one Q-table, QA , is used to up-
      date the other Q-table, QB , and vice versa. This separation reduces the likelihood of both Q-tables
442
      overestimating the same action value. Although Double Q-learning effectively addresses overes-
443
      timation, its applicability is limited to tabular environments and does not scale well to continuous
444   action spaces or high-dimensional problems. A variant, Cini et al. (2020), extends this framework.
445
446   TD3 Fujimoto et al. (2018) adapts the Double Q-learning principle to actor-critic architectures by
447
      utilizing two Q-networks to compute a conservative Q-target. The approach mitigates overesti-
      mation bias by selecting the smaller value between the two critics when determining the Q-target,
448
      thereby minimizing the risk of optimistic estimates. Despite its improvements over standard actor-
449
      critic techniques, TD3 can become excessively conservative in cases where overestimation bias is
450   less significant, potentially hindering learning speed and exploration efficiency. Variants such as
451   Fujimoto & Gu (2021); Fujimoto et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2021) build upon the TD3 framework.
452
453
      SAC Haarnoja et al. (2018) tackles overestimation bias by employing a maximum entropy frame-
      work, which enhances exploration by incorporating an entropy term into the Q-target. Specifi-
454
      cally, this method subtracts the log probability of the policy from the Q-target, encouraging more
455
      exploratory actions and preventing the policy from becoming overly deterministic. While SAC
456   effectively reduces overestimation bias and promotes exploration, it may result in suboptimal per-
457   formance in environments where extensive exploration is unnecessary. Additionally, the algorithm
458   can become computationally expensive due to the inherent stochasticity in policy updates. Several
459   variants, such as D’Oro et al. (2022); Ciosek et al. (2019), build on the SAC framework.
460
      Bootstrapped Q-networks Osband et al. (2016) use an ensemble of Q-networks to generate diverse
461
      estimates of the Q-values, encouraging a wider exploration of possible action values. Each member
462   of the ensemble is trained with different initial conditions or data subsets. By taking the minimum
463   Q-value across the ensemble, this method can mitigate overestimation bias while maintaining suf-
464   ficient exploration. However, Bootstrapped Q-networks are computationally expensive due to the
465   overhead of maintaining and training multiple critics, and the methods effectiveness can diminish if
466   the ensemble members fail to remain sufficiently diverse.
467
      MaxMin Q-learning Lan et al. (2020) extends TD3 by employing an ensemble of N critics rather
468   than two, balancing between optimism and pessimism based on the size of the ensemble. The Q-
469   target is the minimum of a subset of K critics from the ensemble, rather than the full ensemble, to
470   avoid excessive conservatism. While MaxMin Q-learning mitigates overestimation more effectively
471   than TD3, it introduces a trade-off between computational efficiency and conservatism. Larger
472   ensembles offer better bias control but at the cost of greater computation, while smaller ensembles
473   may not fully suppress overestimation bias.
474   TD7 Fujimoto et al. (2023) is a recent method that enhances TD3 by incorporating State Action
475   Learned Embedding (SALE) into the critic networks. SALE models the transition dynamics and
476   augments the input space of the critics and the policy, improving the accuracy of value estimates.
477   TD7 addresses overestimation bias similarly to TD3 by using multiple critics but also incorporates
478   learned representations of the environment’s dynamics to refine the Q-value predictions. However,
479   the additional complexity introduced by SALE can increase the difficulty of training and may require
480   more extensive tuning for different environments.
481   SQT presents a notable advantage over traditional methods by integrating an ensemble-based dis-
482   agreement penalty, which is rooted in the well-established mathematical concept of standard devi-
483   ation (std) Pearson (1920) for quantifying uncertainty. This strategy helps maintain conservative-
484   ness in Q-values, effectively minimizing overestimation bias while also fostering exploration. The
485   penalty mechanism utilized in SQT takes advantage of the diversity among Q-networks, motivating
      them to learn distinct features by differentiating their predictions from each other. Consequently,


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      this promotes exploration, allowing the Q-networks to capture various aspects of the environment’s
487   knowledge instead of rapidly converging to similar solutions.
488
489   In contrast, other methods, such as Double Q-learning or ensemble-based conservative formulas,
490
      prioritize conservativeness without considering exploration. These approaches, which rely on mini-
      mal Q-network ensembles or the double estimator, tend to suffer from underexploration due to their
491
      overly pessimistic nature. The excessive focus on conservativeness, although useful for bias reduc-
492
      tion, ultimately leads to poor performance. This is particularly evident in tasks requiring greater
493   exploration, where the lack of sufficient exploratory mechanisms restricts the algorithm’s ability to
494   discover better strategies, despite considerable computational effort.
495
496
      SQT overcomes this by balancing conservativeness and exploration, maintaining safe Q-values
      while encouraging sufficient exploration, thus offering a more robust solution that avoids the pit-
497
      falls of underexploration found in other conservative methods. This balance allows for improved
498
      performance across a wider range of environments with reduced computational overhead.

501   10    C ONCLUSION
502
503   SQT represents a novel Q-learning model-free online on-policy actor critic algorithm, specifically
504
      designed to address the overestimation bias present in RL algorithms. The central innovation of
      SQT is the inclusion of an uncertainty penalty within the Q-target formula, which is determined by
505
      the disagreement among Q-networks.
506
507   We integrated SQT with TD3, SAC, and TD7, resulting in an ensemble-based conservative actor-
508   critic approach. The performance of SQT was evaluated against SOTA actor critic model-free online
509   algorithms–across widely-used MuJoCo locomotion tasks: humanoid, walker, ant, and cheetah. The
510
      results consistently showed significant performance improvements with SQT, surpassing TD3, SAC,
      and TD7 on the majority of tested tasks.
