011

               The sharpest known high probability excess risk bounds are up to O (1/n) for em-
               pirical risk minimization and projected gradient descent via algorithmic stability
013
               (Klochkov & Zhivotovskiy, 2021). In this  paper, we show that high probability
014
               excess risk bounds of order up to O 1/n2 are    possible. We discuss how high
015
               probability excess risk bounds reach O 1/n2 under strong convexity, smooth-
016
               ness and Lipschitz continuity assumptions for empirical risk minimization, pro-
017            jected gradient descent and stochastic gradient descent. Besides, to the best of
018            our knowledge, our high probability results on the generalization gap measured
019            by gradients for nonconvex problems are also the sharpest.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Algorithmic stability is a fundamental concept in learning theory (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002),
025   which can be traced back to the foundational works of Vapnik & Chervonenkis (1974) and has a
026   deep connection with learnability (Rakhlin et al., 2005; Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2010; Shalev-Shwartz
027   & Ben-David, 2014). Simply speaking, we say that an algorithm is stable if a change in a single
028   example in the training dataset leads to only a minor change in the output model. Stability often
029   provides theoretical upper bounds on generalization error. The study of the relationship between
030
      stability and generalization enables us to theoretically understand and better control the behavior of
      the algorithm.
031
032   While providing in-expectation generalization error bounds through stability arguments is relatively
033   straightforward, deriving high probability bounds presents a more significant challenge. However,
034   these high probability bounds are crucial for understanding the robustness of optimization algo-
035   rithms, as highlighted by recent works (Feldman & Vondrak, 2019; Bousquet et al., 2020; Klochkov
036
      & Zhivotovskiy, 2021). In practical scenarios, where we often train models a limited number of
      times, high probability bounds offer more informative insights than their in-expectation counterparts.
037
      Therefore, this paper focuses on improving high probability risk bounds through an exploration of
038
      algorithmic stability.
039

042

      where the model is determined by parameter w from parameter space W ⊂ Rd . The performance of
043
      a model w on an example z can be quantified by a loss function f (w; z), where f : W × Z 7→ R+ .
044
      Then the population risk and the empirical risk of w ∈ W, respectively as

057   A frequently used alternative to generalization bounds, that can avoid the sampling error, are the
058
      excess risk bounds. The excess risk of algorithm A with respect to the dataset S is defined as
      F (A(S)) − F (w∗ ). This is crucial, as it can be decomposed into an optimization term and a gen-
059
      eralization term (Klochkov & Zhivotovskiy, 2021). To establish a bound on the excess risk, it is
060
      necessary to take into account both the generalization error and the optimization error. Recently,
061   Klochkov & Zhivotovskiy (2021) developed the results in Bousquet et al. (2020) and provided the
062   best high probability excess risk bounds of order up to O (log n/n) for empirical risk minimization
063   (ERM) and projected gradient descent (PGD) algorithms via algorithmic stability. Since then, nu-
064   merous of papers, for example (Yuan & Li, 2023; 2024), extended their results to various settings
065   within their method. However, all the excess risk upper bounds derived from their method can not
066   be smaller than O (log n/n). Considering this, we would like to have a question:
067   Can algorithmic stability provide high probability excess risk bounds with the rate beyond O(1/n)?
068
069   The main results of this paper answer this question positively. To address this question, this paper
070
      technically provides a tighter generalization error bound of the gradients, based on which, we build
      the relationship between algorithmic stability and excess risk bounds. We also establish    the first
071
      high probability excess risk bounds that are dimension-free with the rate O 1/n2 for ERM, PGD
072
      and stochastic gradient descent (SGD).
073
074   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
075
076          • We provide sharper high probability upper bounds for the generalization gap between the
077
               population risk and the empirical risk of gradients in general nonconvex settings. Currently,
               there is only one work (Fan & Lei, 2024) that investigates the high-probability generaliza-
078
               tion error bounds of gradients in nonconvex
                                                       √       problems using algorithmic stability, which
079
               establishes an upper bound of O (M/ n), where M denotes the maximum gradient of
080            loss functions value. In contrast, this paper provides tighter results. Our coefficient be-
081
                       √
               fore 1/ n depends on the variance of the gradients of the loss functions under the model
082            optimized by algorithm A. As is well known, optimization algorithms typically yield pa-
083            rameters that approach the optimal solution, which significantly reduces the term compared
084            to the maximum gradient.
085          • We build the relationship between algorithmic stability and excess risk bounds. Our results
086            can provide a more granular analysis dependent on optimal parameters. Compared to ex-
087            isting work (Klochkov  & Zhivotovskiy, 2021), we can achieve the excess risk bounds with
088            the rate of O 1/n2 under specific conditions. Under the same algorithmic stability, our
089            results also perform tighter. To the best of our knowledge, these are the first dimension-free
090            results with the order O 1/n2 in high probability risk bounds via algorithmic stability.
091          • Using our method,  we derive the first dimension-free high probability excess risk bounds

092            of O 1/n2 for ERM, PGD, and SGD,  addressing an open problem posed in Xu & Zeevi
093            (2024). While they achieved O 1/n2 excess risk bounds on ERM and GD algorithms
094            through uniform convergence, their approach requires  that the sample size satisfies n =
095            Ω(d). In contrast, we successfully obtain O 1/n2 bounds that do not depend on the
096            dimensionality using algorithmic stability.

101
      the foundational works of (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1974). It gave the generalization bound by ana-

103   Modern method of stability analysis was established by Bousquet & Elisseeff (2002), where they
104   presented an important concept called uniform stability. Since then, a lot of works based on uniform
105   stability have emerged. On one hand, generalization bounds with algorithmic stability have been
106   significantly improved by Feldman & Vondrak (2018; 2019); Bousquet et al. (2020); Klochkov &
107   Zhivotovskiy (2021). On the other hand, different algorithmic stability measures such as uniform
      argument stability (Liu et al., 2017; Bassily et al., 2020), uniform stability in gradients (Lei, 2023;

      hand, when the algorithm’s stability β = O(1/ n) the√ upper bound, according to Klochkov & Zhiv-
332   otovskiy (2021), can at most reach the order of 1/ n due to the algorithm’s stability constraints.
333   In contrast, our result shows that even under the assumption that F (w∗ ) = O(1), treating F (w∗ )
334   as a constant, we can achieve an order of O(1/n). On the other hand, their result is insensitive to
335   the stability parameter being smaller than O(1/n) and their best rates can only up to O(1/n). Our
336   results can be up to O(1/n2 ) under some specific assumptions. We will discuss in Section 4.
337
      Although we involve extra smoothness and PL condition assumptions, these assumptions are also
338   common in optimization community and analyzing the stability of algorithms. For example,
339   Klochkov & Zhivotovskiy (2021) introduced assumptions of strong convexity and smoothness in
340   their study of the stability and optimization results of the PGD algorithm. However, their method
341   did not fully leverage these assumptions when establishing the relationship between algorithmic sta-
342   bility and risk bounds. This is the fundamental reason why our results outperform theirs without
343   introducing additional assumptions. Our work can fully utilize these assumptions.

534   In this paper, we improve a p-moment concentration inequality for sums of vector-valued functions.
535   By carefully constructing functions, we apply this moment concentration to derive sharper gener-
536   alization bounds in gradients in nonconvex problems, which can further be used to obtain sharper
537   high probability excess risk bounds for stable optimization algorithms. In application, we study three
538   common algorithms: ERM, PGD, SGD. To the best of our knowledge, we provide the sharpest high
