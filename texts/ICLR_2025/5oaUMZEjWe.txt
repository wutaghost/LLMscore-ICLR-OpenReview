009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid progress of single-task architectures has dominated recent developments
012            in multi-talker speech processing, prompting the need for unified approaches. This
013            paper introduces a unified multi-speaker encoder (UME), a novel model archi-
014            tecture that jointly learns representations for diarization, separation, and multi-
015            speaker automatic speech recognition (ASR) tasks using a shared pre-trained
016            foundational speech encoder. We leverage the hidden representations from mul-
017            tiple layers of UME to effectively use information from different semantic levels,
018
               contributing to bottom-up alignment between tasks. This joint training approach
               captures the inherent interdependencies among the tasks, enhancing overall per-
019
               formance on overlapping speech data. Our evaluations demonstrate that UME
020
               achieves substantial improvements over the single-task state-of-the-art (SOTA)
021            baselines dedicated to speaker diarization, speech separation, and multi-speaker
022            ASR. Notably, for speaker diarization, UME achieved SOTA performance by low-
023            ering the diarization error rate (DER) from 3.24 to 2.19 on the Libri2Mix dataset.
024            Furthermore, our results in multi-speaker ASR outperform the previous results,
025            reducing the concatenated minimum-permutation word error rate (cpWER) from
026            11.9 to 9.2 on the LibriSpeech2Mix evaluation set.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Speaker diarization (SD), speech separation (SS), and multi-speaker automatic speech recognition
032   (ASR) are tasks of great importance that aim to comprehend and answer the question “who spoke
033
      what and when,” with applications to transcribing meetings and interviews, among others. Pre-
      vious studies in SD (Fujita et al., 2019a;b; Horiguchi et al., 2022), SS (Luo & Mesgarani, 2019;
034
      Wang et al., 2023), and multi-speaker ASR (Qian et al., 2018; Seki et al., 2018; Chang et al., 2020)
035
      have focused primarily on improving the quality of single-task models that operate independently
036   on acoustic information to separate or label speaker segments and transcribe the text in a speech-
037   processing system (Watanabe et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Raj et al., 2021a). A key limitation of
038   training tasks independently is that inter-dependencies cannot be leveraged.
039
      Most existing frameworks address this limitation by unifying speech-processing architectures
040
      (Boeddeker et al., 2024; Kalda et al., 2024). These architectures consist of either a joint ASR/SD
041
      (Mao et al., 2020), SS/ASR (Kanda et al., 2022), or a SD/SS (Maiti et al., 2023) task following a
042   fixed optimal order that can vary depending on the target scene scenario (Watanabe et al., 2020;
043   Chen et al., 2020; Raj et al., 2021a). These different target scenes suggest that we solve these tasks
044   jointly, independent of the order, so all these tasks can benefit from each other.
045
      Lately, there has been a shift towards employing pre-trained speech foundation models (SFMs)
046
      (Chen et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024a) in end-to-end (E2E) systems, which ef-
047
      fectively learn useful representations for various speech processing tasks (Yang et al., 2021). How-
048   ever they do not work well on multi-speaker conversation recognition. Additionally, it has been
049   demonstrated that different layers encode different types of information in SD and ASR tasks (Chen
050   et al., 2022). Preliminary observation from these studies shows that intermediate layers of the en-
051   coder extract a rich hierarchy of information, e.g., in WavLM large, initial layers and last layers are
052   more critical for SD and ASR tasks. Therefore, it makes sense to utilize multiple layers to jointly op-
053   timize all SD, SS, and ASR tasks effectively. The question, therefore, naturally arises: can we build
      a unified model that leverages all encoder layers to optimize performance across multiple tasks?

054
      Motivated by the potential of SFMs and E2E speech processing, we propose a unified multi-speaker
055   encoder (UME), a novel E2E speech-processing framework. The proposed framework is generaliz-
056   able to use any SFM, E2E SD, SS and multi-speaker ASR task. We selected OWSMv3.1 (Peng et al.,
057   2024b) as the shared encoder for this framework due to its widespread recognition, reproducibility,
058   open-source availability, and fast, efficient encoding capabilities. EEND (Horiguchi et al., 2022)
059   as a SD model due to its efficient overlapped E2E speech processing and Conv-TasNet (Luo &
060   Mesgarani, 2019) as a SS method as it is a very well-known separation model for time-domain over-
061   lapped speech handling and multi-speaker ASR (Chang et al., 2020) for its superior speech recogni-
062   tion performance in the E2E overlapped speech recognition. UME jointly optimizes all these tasks
063
      into a single network with multitask learning to minimize the error accumulation for a speech pro-
      cessing framework. Additionally, by extracting features from all the layers of the OWSMv3.1 shared
064
      encoder, we can effectively learn better-hidden representations from the encoder layers, bringing
065
      better information exchange and bottom-up alignment to all the tasks from different semantic levels.
066   We argue that such an E2E framework should provide a shared representation space for SD, SS and
067   multi-speaker ASR tasks and preferably have strong generalizability and learnability.
068
069
      We conduct extensive experiments on different design choices of UME using typically complete
      overlapped speech from the Libri2Mix dataset. The contributions are summarized as follows:
070
071
             • We propose a unified speech-processing framework to jointly optimize the performance of
072            SD, SS, and multi-speaker ASR tasks with hidden representations of the speech foundation
073            encoder.
074
075
             • We propose using a weighted sum of the pre-trained speech foundation encoder layers to
               simplify the connection between each task.
076
077          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework on two-speaker and three-speaker over-
078            lapped speech and obtain substantial performance improvement in each diarization, sepa-
079            ration, and multi-speaker ASR task.

      2     R ELATED WORK
082
083
      2.1   M ULTI -L AYER F EATURE L EARNING
084
085
      Multi-layer feature learning has been explored as an efficient approach for fully leveraging the infor-
086   mation present in various layers of neural networks to enhance the representation and generalization
087   abilities in single task for speech processing (Yang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022), natural language
088   processing (Peters et al., 2018; Dou et al., 2018), and computer vision (Zheng et al., 2021; Naseer
089   et al., 2021). In the field of computer vision, researchers (Zheng et al., 2021; Naseer et al., 2021) im-
090   proved semantic segmentation performance by aggregating features from different layers of visual
091   transformers, whereas in natural language processing, a weighted sum (Peters et al., 2018) of repre-
092   sentations from intermediate RNN layers or aggregation (Dou et al., 2018) of attention layers was
093   explored as an input for different task heads. Similar ideas have also been explored in single-task
094
      speech processing models (Yang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) to analyze the effect and contribu-
      tion of intermediate layers on single downstream task performance. However, the existing weighted
095
      sum of hidden representations from different layers of SFM is not explicitly explored for multiple
096
      task heads in a unified speech model, and there has been no exploration into their suitability for the
097   end-to-end speech processing framework.
098
099
      2.2   J OINT T RAINING
100
101
      Joint training (Watanabe et al., 2017) approaches have achieved significant success in speech pro-
102   cessing. With the rapid advancements in multitask learning-based joint training methods, researchers
103   combined tasks like SS and SD within a single neural network (Neumann et al., 2019; Kinoshita
104   et al., 2020), using various task combinations. Previous works have focused on joint training of
105   pairs such as ASR/SD (Shafey et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2020), SS/ASR (Kanda et al., 2022), or
106   SS/SD (Maiti et al., 2023; Boeddeker et al., 2024). Our work represents the first effort to jointly
107   train the SD, SS, and multi-speaker ASR within one unified model so that all tasks can benefit from
      each other.

273   4.1   DATASET
274
275   In UME, we aim to optimize all three tasks: diarization, separation, and multi-speaker ASR, using
276   multi-task learning in a unified framework. We require three ground truths to objectively evalu-
277   ate performance, i.e., diarization labels, separated sources, and text for each speaker. While real-
278   world multiparty datasets (Carletta et al., 2006; Horiguchi et al., 2021; Kamo et al., 2024) exist for
279
      diarization-only tasks, they often need separated sources and text. Therefore, we employ simulated
      conversation-like open-source datasets for training and evaluation. For training, we utilized the Lib-
280
      riMix (Cosentino et al., 2020) dataset. For evaluation, we employed both LibriMix and LibriSpeech-
281
      Mix (Kanda et al., 2020) datasets. LibriMix is a simulated dataset that generates speech mix-
282   tures using samples from LibriSpeech (train-clean100/train-clean360/dev-clean/test-clean) (Panay-
283   otov et al., 2015) and noise samples from WHAM! (Wichern et al., 2019). This dataset includes
284   training, validation, and testing sets for two-speaker (Libri2Mix) and three-speaker (Libri3Mix) mix-
285   tures. This study reports results solely on Libri2Mix (two-speaker) and Libri3Mix (three-speaker) to
286   effectively manage computational resources and reduce carbon footprint. This work used a 16kHz
287   sampling rate, the “mixboth (i.e., includes speaker mixtures and WHAM noise)” method, and the
288   “max” mode with 100% overlap. We choose the “max” mode as the ASR task is unfeasible com-
289   pared to the “min” mode due to the truncation of speech signals on minimum-length sequences. We
290
      evaluated our system using the Libri2Mix and Libri3Mix datasets with a complete 100% overlap, as
      well as the LibriSpeech2Mix and LibriSpeech3Mix datasets, which include a partial random overlap
291
      of at least 0.5 seconds. The minimum, maximum, and average durations of the utterances for the

310   4.2   E VALUATION METRICS
311
312   We evaluate diarization performance using the diarization error rate (DER%) (Fiscus et al., 2006)
313   with a collar tolerance of 0.0 seconds and median filtering applied over 11 frames. For the sep-
314   aration task, we evaluate using five objective metrics and report the results in source-to-distortion
315
      ratio improvement (SDRi (dB)) (Vincent et al., 2006), scale-invariant source-to-distortion ratio im-
      provement (SI-SDRi (dB)) (Roux et al., 2019), scale-invariant signal-to-noise ratio improvement
316
      (SI-SNR), short-time objective intelligibility (STOI) (Taal et al., 2010), and signal to artifacts ra-
317
      tio (SAR) Vincent et al. (2006) along with signal-to-interference ratio (SIR) (Vincent et al., 2006).
318   We report the multi-speaker ASR performance using the concatenated minimum-permutation word
319   error rate (cpWER) metric following prior work on permutation invariant training-based (PIT) multi-
320   speaker ASR methods (Chang et al., 2020; Kanda et al., 2020). It involves selecting the lowest word
321   error rate (WER) from the concatenated utterances of permuted speaker references and hypothesis
322   files. Unlike the method in (Watanabe et al., 2020), our cpWER computation is independent of the
323   speaker diarization branch, similar to (Chang et al., 2020), ensuring minimum error accumulation in
      the multi-speaker ASR process.

      4.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
325
326   UME employs a pre-trained supervised SFM encoder, OWSMv3.1 (Peng et al., 2024b) medium, the
327   feature extractor for all three tasks. We simplify the integration of the tasks following the evaluation
328   in SUPERB (Yang et al., 2021) and WavLM (Chen et al., 2022) and provide learnable weights (Sec-
329   tion 3.2) as input so that all the layers of the OWSMv3.1 contribute to the optimization of the tasks.
330   Firstly, for the EEND (Section 3.3) task, we directly input the weighted sum of the extracted features
331   with a frame length of 400 and a frameshift of 640 samples to the 1-layer RNN-based attractor with
332   a hidden size of 1024. The EEND task thereby has an input-output dimension of 1024. Secondly,
333
      for the separation task (Section 3.4), we concatenate the 1024-dimensional hidden representations
      of the OWSMv3.1 with the 256-dimensional encoded features of the 1-D convolutional encoder in
334
      Conv-TasNet (Luo & Mesgarani, 2019). Since the OWSMv3.1 has a downsampling rate of 40ms,
335
      introducing a mismatch in the time dimension, we upsample the pre-trained representations for each
336   time step to increase the resolution and ease the concatenation process. Following the concatena-
337   tion, we input the 1280-dimensional concatenated features into the stack of three TCN blocks with
338   eight convolutional layers with a hidden states dimension 512 for mask estimation. Finally, using a
339   linear projection layer, we project the 1024-dimensional OWSMv3.1 features for the multi-speaker
340   ASR task (Section 3.5) to 128 dimensions. We then introduce a Transformer-based post-encoder
341   and decoder (Chang et al., 2020) with four speaker-differentiating encoder blocks (SpeakerEncSD )
342   and six decoder blocks, each having 2048 linear units with an input dimension of 256. Before
343   the post-encoder, we encode the OWSMv3.1 features by a convolutional layer with a subsampling
344
      factor of four. During training in UME, we initialize the encoder parameters with the pre-trained
      OWSMv3.1 medium encoder and fine-tune the encoder layers for 70 epochs, while all task-specific
345
      parameters have a flat start (i.e., no parameter initialization for task-specific layers) and are trained
346
      for 70 epochs. For the ASR-initialized UME version, the multi-speaker ASR model is pre-trained
347   separately for 30 epochs, and then the ASR-specific head in the UME model is initialized from this
348   pre-trained model. This results in a total of 70 epochs of fine-tuning for the OWSMv3.1 encoder
349   layers, 70 epochs of training for the diarization and separation tasks, and 70 epochs of fine-tuning
350   for the ASR task. We use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with an initial learn-
351   ing rate of 4e − 4 (optimized empirically) and weight decay 1e − 06. The learning rate is warmed
352   up for 10,000 steps and then decayed linearly to zero for the rest of the training steps. Four A100
353   80GB GPUs are used during training, and the batch size is dynamically adjusted based on the input
354   length using the numel batch type in the ESPnet toolkit (Watanabe et al., 2018). In our experiments,
355
      the average batch size was 44, and it took six days to train the model for up to 70 epochs. For task-
      specific weights, we adopted a weighted-sum scalarization (Ehrgott, 2000) approach to simplify the
356
      multi-objective optimization problem into a single-objective (Bazgan et al., 2022) one by assigning
357
      equal weights to all task-specific losses (Section 3.6) (i.e., λasr = 0.33, λdiar = 0.33 , λsep = 0.34).
358   This approach assumes that the tasks are cooperative rather than conflicting, particularly in our
359   two-speaker and three-speaker scenarios, and reflects their equal importance in our framework. Fur-
360   thermore, we explored a two-stage strategy to optimize task-specific weights, inspired by the 4D
361   ASR work by (Sudo et al., 2023). However, as discussed in Section 5.2, this strategy resulted in
362   performance degradation for one or more tasks. Since the primary goal of this study is to develop a
363   unified framework capable of integrating multiple tasks rather than optimizing individual task per-
364   formance, we propose an equal-weighting strategy that assigns equal importance to all tasks. This
365   approach is validated by experimental results in Section 5.2, which demonstrate that simple equally
366
      weighted scalarization achieves state-of-the-art performance.

      6   C ONCLUSION
535
      In this paper, we propose UME, a unified framework for end-to-end speech processing, which in-
536
      tegrates speaker diarization, speech separation, and multi-speaker ASR with a weighted sum of
537
      hidden states of the intermediate layers. UME substantially outperforms strong baselines and previ-
538   ous works and achieves state-of-the-art performance on speaker diarization task. In the future, we
539   are interested in evaluating the performance on real datasets and extending it to a multilingual UME.

      7   L IMITATIONS
541
542
      While the proposed UME leveraging the weighted sum of hidden state representations of the in-
543   termediate layers as the bridge between speaker diarization, speech separation, and multi-speaker
544   ASR tasks simultaneously achieving substantial improvements over previous works, it still has some
545   limitations: (1) the absence of ground truth for all three tasks in real-world data makes it challeng-
546   ing to objectively evaluate the UME framework’s performance. Therefore, we focus on simulated
547   datasets, where ground truth is available, to ensure accurate comparisons. We acknowledge this lim-
548   itation and plan to explore real-world datasets in future work as they become more accessible and
549   standardized. (2) the current method employs a supervised pre-trained encoder, trained on clean and
550   non-overlapped speech with a low time resolution of 40ms and shows suboptimal performance on
551
      the separation task; (3) the proposed UME only supports two-speaker and three-speaker tasks, and
      it would be nice to able to support unlimited number of speaker tasks; (4) we have to pre-train an
552
      independent model for speaker-differentiating heads to get optimal multi-speaker ASR performance
553
      due to small amount of simulated dataset in the current method, which is time and resource inef-
554   ficient; (5) the effectiveness of applying UME to other speech domains (e.g., child speech, dialect
555   speech) needs further investigation.
