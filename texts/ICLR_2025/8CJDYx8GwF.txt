009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning-based classifiers are known to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks.
012            Existing methods for defending against such attacks require adding a defense
013            mechanism or modifying the learning procedure (e.g., by adding adversarial exam-
014            ples). This paper shows that for certain data distribution one can learn a provably
015            robust classifier using standard learning methods and without adding a defense
016
               mechanism. More specifically, this paper addresses the problem of finding a robust
               classifier for a binary classification problem in which the data comes from a mixture
017
               of Gaussian clusters with orthonormal cluster centers. First, we characterize the
018
               largest ℓ2 -attack any classifier can defend against while maintaining high accuracy,
019            and show the existence of optimal robust classifiers achieving this maximum ℓ2 -
020            robustness. Next, we show that given data sampled from the orthonormal cluster
021            model, gradient flow on a two-layer network with a polynomial ReLU activation
022            and without adversarial examples provably finds an optimal robust classifier.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The vulnerability of deep neural networks to adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2014), which are
      typically human-imperceptible perturbations to the input data, has led to numerous efforts in building
028
      defenses against these attacks (Shafahi et al., 2019; Papernot et al., 2016; Wong et al., 2019; Guo
029
      et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2019; Levine & Feizi, 2020; Yang et al., 2020; Sulam et al., 2020; Kinfu &
030   Vidal, 2022). These defenses have been counteracted by new adaptive attacks (Athalye et al., 2018;
031   Carlini et al., 2019; Croce & Hein, 2020), leading to new defenses and so on. Even in the era of Large
032   Language Models, adversarial attacks exist (Chao et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023), leading to undesired
033   or harmful model outputs, and the competition between adversaries and defenders continues (Robey
034   et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024). While such a competition allows us to design more robust networks, it
035   will not end unless many fundamental questions about adversarial robustness are answered.
036   One question is what is the maximum adversarial perturbation a neural network can tolerate? Many
037   works on certified robustness (Cohen et al., 2019; Fazlyab et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2018) aim to
038   find a certified radius such that a neural network can provably maintain a high prediction accuracy
039   for adversarial attacks within that radius. However, their reported certified radii are often too small
040   compared to what can be achieved by practical defenses (Tramèr et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2018; Gowal
041   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020). Yet, practical defenses come at the cost of computing adversarial
042   examples, or sophisticated model designs, mostly without theoretical guarantees, except for the case
043   of linear classifiers (Zou et al., 2021). This also motivates an intriguing question: Is it possible to
044
      (provably) find a robust network by standard training methods, without adversarial examples?
045   We argue that these questions can be answered by exploiting properties of the data distribution, which
046   most aforementioned works fail to do. Indeed, recent works show that the existence of a robust
047   classifier is closely related to data geometry. For instance, Pal et al. (2023; 2024) show that if the data
048   is localized, i.e., if the distribution of the data given the class concentrates in a set of small volume,
049   then a robust classifier is guaranteed to exist. Moreover, they show that a 2r separation (w.r.t. to some
050
      distance metric) between the sets that contain each class-conditioned probability mass is sufficient
      for the existence of a robust classifier against attacks of radius r in the same distance metric.
051
052   This paper shows that such a relationship between data geometry and adversarial robustness has
053   deeper implications: For certain data distributions, one can characterize the maximum robustness any
      classifier can achieve, based on how class-conditional probability masses are separated. Moreover,

      Learning optimal robust networks So far everything seems to be intuitive and straightforward
097   given the fairly simple distributional assumption. However, issues arise when one does not know
098   the data distribution a priori and seeks a classifier by training a neural network on sampled data via
099   gradient descent. As Figure 2 suggests, a trained multi-layer ReLU network fails to find a classifier
100   with the same
                 √
                      level of robustness as the Bayes classifier (which indeed can defend against attack of
101   radius ∼ 22 , as our results suggest). This matter is first discussed by Frei et al. (2023), where they
102   show that any two-layer ReLU network trained by gradient        descent under data samples from (1) is
103   non-robust against adversarial attacks of ℓ2 -radius Θ √1K , where K is the total number of clusters.
104   Later, Min & Vidal (2024) show that this issue is caused by the fact that a ReLU network fails to
105   learn, internally with its weight parameters, the multi-cluster structure of the data distribution, despite
106   that the sampled data points are revealing such a structure. Therefore, while the structural property of
107   the data distribution allows one to construct an optimal robust classifier, gradient descent algorithms
      on neural networks may struggle to learn these key properties, leading to non-robust classifiers.

      To address this issue, Min & Vidal (2024) propose to change the activation. More specifically,
109   replacing the ReLU activation with a polynomial ReLU activation (pReLU) with polynomial degree
110   p as a hyperparameter. They empirically show that when p = 3, the pReLU network can internally
111   learn the data structure, leading to a more robust classifier, and they conjecture that this improvement
112   in robustness happens when p ≥ 3. However, a rigorous analysis of convergence is not provided. Our
113   second set of results is to develop a full convergence analysis for gradient flow, a continuous time
114   limit of gradient descent by taking the stepsize to zero, on a two-layer pReLU network and show that:
115   Theorem (Theorem 3 & Corollary 1, informal). When p > 2 and the intra-cluster variance α2 is
116   sufficiently small, gradient flow on pReLU networks converges to a nearly optimal robust classifier.
117
118   Our analysis is based on prior works on gradient descent/flow with small initialization on two-layer
119   ReLU networks Maennel et al. (2018); Phuong & Lampert (2021); Boursier et al. (2022); Kumar &
120
      Haupt (2024); Chistikov et al. (2023); Wang & Ma (2023); Min et al. (2024) and extends to pReLU
      networks. We show how the implicit bias of the gradient flow dynamics critically depends on a
121
      careful choice of activation function, allowing the network to learn accurately the underlying data
122
      structure, which, as we have discussed, is essential for finding a robust classifier.

502   3.2.4   T ECHNICAL LIMITATIONS OF CURRENT RESULTS
503
504
      We conclude by discussing several technical limitations of our current results and potential avenues to
      address them. These limitations are listed in an order that the most challenging ones are stated first.
505
506
      Requirement on the initialization The initialization requires a non-degeneracy gap ∆ = Θ(1),
507
      which generally cannot
                          be achieved by random initialization: the cosines between neurons and cluster
508                   1                                               −2
      centers are O D with high probability. Given that D = Ω̃(α ), the actually non-degeneracy
                     √
509
510   gap of a random initialization is Õ(α). We have discussed this issue when we define non-degenerate
511   initialization in Section 3.2.1: When neurons are initialized close to the boundary between a Voronoi
512
      region Rk and another region Rl (or the void region R◦ ), whether they align with µk or with µl (or
      get further into void region) depends on the actually sampled points in the dataset. In this regard,
513
      when weights are randomly initialized, there is a “burn-in" phase during which neurons “choose"
514
      their target clusters depending on the samples, then once they get away from the boundary of these
515   Voronoi regions with ∆ = Θ(1) gap, we can characterize the GF dynamics afterward by Theorem 3.
516
517
      Upper bound on N Regarding our requirement Ω̃(α0−2 ) ≤ N ≤ õ(exp(α0−2 )), we have discussed
518
      the lower bound N  ≥ Ω̃(α0−2 ) in Section 3.2.3. In fact, one can remove this lower bound and get a
519
520
      final bound Õ √αN 0
                            in Theorem 3. The upper bound N ≤ õ(exp(α0−2 )) may seem puzzling. This
521   issue originates from ReLU nonlinearity: a data point must activate a neuron by having a positive inner
522
      product. Our analysis requires that a neuron wj is activated by every data point from
                                                                                         √ its target cluster,
523
      which√is translated into two conditions: 1) Θ(1) non-degeneracy gap; and 2) log N α0 = õ(1).
      Here log N α0 is essentially the radius of a ℓ2 -ball centered at a cluster center that can contain all
524
      the sampled points from that cluster with high probability. Without these conditions, there will be
525
      outliers in sampled points, which must be handled with extra analysis. We believe this is possible
526   because those outliers will be rare and thus may have a negligible effect on the dynamics.
527
528   Extension to classification losses Our results for the alignment phase directly apply to classification
529   losses: The choice of the loss ℓ(y, ŷ) only affects the alignment dynamics through ∇ŷ ℓ(y, ŷ)|ŷ=0 , and
530   this quantity is same (may up to a constant scaling) regardless of whether ℓ is exponential, logistic,
531   or ℓ2 . However, the analysis of convergence phase critically depends on ℓ: Recall    P that in Section
532   3.2.3 we show that the nominal weight norm dynamics are ż = (1 − z)z, z = j∈Nk ∥wj ∥2 for
533   ℓ2 loss. For exponential loss, the nominal dynamics become ż = exp(−z)z, whose closed-form
534   solution is not available. A better characterization of the solution to the nominal dynamics of the type
535   ż = exp(−z)z in future research naturally leads to an extension of Theorem 3 to classification losses.
536
537   Analysis until finite time T ∗ Our focus is on the distance between f (p) (·; θ(t)) and F (p) , thus we
538   restrict to the time interval [0, T ∗ ] when we have explicit control of all relevant quantities (alignment,
539   weight norms, etc.). To show convergence towards a minimizer of the loss after T ∗ , we believe

758   In this section, we solve the tasks of classifying cats and dogs (Cukierski, 2013) via transfer learning
759   using extracted features from a ResNet152 (He et al., 2016) trained on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
760   2009). We conjecture that the extracted features of the dog (or cat) class may naturally have many
761   clusters: when the feature extractor is trained on ImageNet, dogs are further labeled by their breeds.
762   Thus the extracted features of dogs of the same breed should be sufficiently close, and features of
763   dogs of different breeds should be sufficiently far apart, based on the well-known neural collapse

      phenomenon (Papyan et al., 2020; Galanti et al., 2021). If such a multi-cluster structure exists in the
      extracted feature, then we expect training pReLU as a classification head can achieve better robust
765
      accuracy compared to its ReLU counterpart.
