011                                          Abstract
012
013           In recent years, numerous studies have demonstrated the close connec-
014           tion between neural networks’ generalization performance and their ability
015           to learn low-dimensional representations of data. However, the theoreti-
016           cal foundation linking low-dimensional representations to generalization re-
017           mains underexplored. In this work, we propose a theoretical framework to
018           analyze this relationship from the perspective of interpolation and convex
019
              combinations. We argue that lower-dimensional representations increase
              the likelihood of new samples being expressed as convex combinations of
020
              the training set, thereby enhancing interpolation probability. We derive
021
              a generalization error upper bound under the interpolation regime, which
022           becomes tighter as the dimensionality of the representation decreases. Fur-
023           thermore, we investigate how the structure of the manifold aﬀects interpo-
024           lation probability by examining the volume of the convex hull formed by
025           the manifold. Our theoretical and experimental results show that larger
026           convex hull volumes are associated with higher interpolation probabilities.
027           Additionally, we explore the impact of training data volume on interpola-
028           tion, ﬁnding a signiﬁcant power-law relationship between increased data
029           volume, convex hull volume and interpolation probability. Overall, this
030
              study highlights the critical role of low-dimensional representations in im-
              proving the generalization performance of neural networks, supported by
031
              both theoretical insights and experimental evidence.

035   1   Introduction
036
037   Neural networks have achieved remarkable success across various tasks, often outperforming
038   humans in domains like natural language processing and computer vision (Devlin et al., 2018;
039   Brown et al., 2020; Raﬀel et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; He et al., 2022).
040   However, understanding the generalization capabilities of these models remains an open
041   problem. Current theories fail to explain how large-scale data and models contribute to this
042   generalization, leading to an overemphasis on increasing data and model size without clear
043   theoretical backing.
044   Recent studies have revealed several phenomena relevant to generalization in neural net-
045   works. For instance, models with ﬂatter minima often generalize better (Hochreiter &
046   Schmidhuber, 1997; Mulayoﬀ & Michaeli, 2020; Baldassi et al., 2021), and networks tend
047   to perform better in interpolation tasks, where test samples lie within the convex hull of
048   the training data (Barnard & Wessels, 1992; Haley & Soloway, 1992). Additionally, there
049   is growing evidence that neural networks compress input data into low-dimensional repre-
050
      sentations, which appears to improve generalization (Ansuini et al., 2019; Recanatesi et al.,
      2019).
051
052   Despite these insights, there is still no uniﬁed framework to explain why low-dimensional
053   representations correlate with better generalization. Furthermore, the connection between
      interpolation probability and low-dimensional representation has not been thoroughly ex-

      plored. This study aims to bridge this gap by proposing a theoretical framework that links
055   low-dimensional representations, interpolation probability and generalization error.
056
057   We hypothesize that lower-dimensional representations increase the likelihood of new sam-
058
      ples being represented as convex combinations of the training set, resulting in a higher
      interpolation probability. This, in turn, tightens the generalization error bound. Through
059

061
062   The structure of this paper is as follows: Section 2 reviews related work on neural network
063   generalization. Section 3 introduces the relevant background knowledge for this research.
064
      Section 4 experimentally analyzes how the embedding dimension, manifold structure, and
      interpolation probability change during the training process of neural networks. Section 5
065
      proves that in the interpolation regime, there exists an upper bound on the generalization
066
      error of neural networks, which decreases as the data dimension decreases. Section 6 theo-
067   retically discusses the relationship between dimension and interpolation probability. Section

069   of convex hull formed by the manifold) and interpolation probability. Finally, Section 8
070   explores the impact of data volume on interpolation probability from both theoretical and
071   experimental perspectives.
072
      Our key contributions are as follows:
073
074        • We empirically show that during training, neural networks reduce the dimension-
075          ality of their representations while increasing interpolation probability, leading to
076          improved generalization performance.
077        • We provide a theoretical framework that demonstrates how low-dimensional repre-
078          sentations increase interpolation probability, which in turn leads to tighter general-
079          ization error bounds in the interpolation regime.
080        • We explore how the structure of the representation manifold, particularly the volume
081          of the convex hull, inﬂuences interpolation probability, and we show that increased
082          training data expands this volume, enhancing generalization.

104   2   Related Works
105
106   Interpolation and Extrapolation in Neural Networks: For neural networks, inter-
107   polation refers to test samples falling within the convex hull of training samples, while ex-
      trapolation refers to test samples lying outside this convex hull. Generally, neural networks


                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tend to exhibit better generalization performance when performing interpolation (Barnard
109   & Wessels, 1992; Haley & Soloway, 1992). Recent work highlights that interpolation proba-
110   bility is related to the amount of data constituting the convex hull and the dimensionality of
111   the data (Bárány & Füredi, 1988; Balestriero et al., 2021). Given that the data processed by
112   models are typically high-dimensional and often insuﬃcient in quantity to meet theoretical
113   requirements, interpolation probabilities are theoretically quite low (Balestriero et al., 2021).
114   Thus, it is believed that neural networks are likely always performing extrapolation.
115   Low-dimensional Representation of Neural Network: Numerous studies indicate
116   that neural networks compress data, and this compression capability is closely linked to
117   their generalization performance (Yu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Chan
118   et al., 2022). The intrinsic dimension can quantify the dimensionality of the manifold where
119   a discrete set of points resides, serving as a measure of manifold complexity. By comparing
120   the diﬀerences in intrinsic dimension between raw data and its representations, we can char-
121   acterize the extent of compression achieved by the neural network. Research shows that as
122   data progresses through each layer from raw input to the embedding manifold, the intrinsic
123
      dimension of the embedding manifold consistently decreases, reﬂecting ongoing compression
      of the data (Ansuini et al., 2019; Recanatesi et al., 2019). Furthermore, lower-dimensional
124
      representations often correlate with better generalization performance (Ansuini et al., 2019).
125
      While there is widespread recognition of the importance of low-dimensional representations
126   for enhancing generalization, a theoretical explanation for why low-dimensional representa-
127   tions tend to perform better has yet to be established.

      3.2   Intrinsic dimension and Ambient Dimension
178
179   Let P ⊂ RN represent a set of sample points. We assume that these points lie on a low-
180   dimensional manifold M ⊂ RN , where N is the ambient dimension of the space. The
181   ambient dimension dim(RN ) = N refers to the dimension of the surrounding space, while
182   the intrinsic dimension dim(M) = d ≪ N refers to the dimension of the manifold on which
183   the data lies. In essence, the intrinsic dimension quantiﬁes the complexity of the underlying
184   structure of the data.

285

286

287   more compact data distribution. Compactness can be understood as the degree of concen-
288   tration in the data distribution, allowing the model to capture data features more eﬀectively.
289   However, as the diameter of the data distribution decreases, the model’s sensitivity to in-
290   put perturbations may increase, leading to a larger Lipschitz constant for the loss function.
291   This is because a compact embedding distribution can amplify the eﬀects of small changes,
292   causing the model to respond more sharply to these perturbations, which in turn aﬀects the
293   smoothness of the loss function and the model’s generalization performance.
294   Additionally, this theory highlights the impact of data dimensionality on generalization
295   performance. However, since the dimensionality of the original data is usually ﬁxed, the
296   theorem applies more to the model’s representation of the original data. This theorem can
297   explain why models with lower-dimensional embeddings tend to generalize better.

403   8   Impact of Data Volume on Interpolation Probability
404
405   In the previous analysis, we primarily focused on the impact of the manifold’s dimensionality
406   and structure on interpolation probability under a ﬁxed data regime. However, it is well
407   understood that, with the same model architecture, increasing the size of the training data
408   often leads to better performance. Therefore, we aim to theoretically explore how larger
409   datasets aﬀect interpolation probability.
410   Proposition 2 ((Hayakawa et al., 2023)). For an arbitrary d-dimensional random vector
411   X with E[x] = 0 and P(x ̸= 0) > 0, we have
412
413                          0 < Pd+1,X < Pd+2,X < ... < Pn,X < ... → 1.                       (14)
414   The conclusion still holds if we only assume pn,X > 0 for some n instead of E[X] = 0
415
416   This proposition highlights that as the number of data points n increases, the interpolation
417   probability Pn,X rises monotonically and approaches 1. This means that with a suﬃciently
418   large dataset, new samples are almost always interpolated within the convex hull of the
419   existing data. Importantly, the result holds even if we only assume a positive interpolation
420   probability for some ﬁnite n, making it applicable to a wide range of distributions.
421   We further validated this proposition through experiments. Speciﬁcally, we generated data
422   of the same dimensionality and constructed their convex hulls, calculating the corresponding
423   convex hull volume and interpolation probability for diﬀerent data sizes. Our experimental
424   results in Figure 5 show that as the data size increases, both the interpolation probability
425   and convex hull volume grow steadily, and the rate of increase follows an approximate power
426   law with respect to data size.
427   This behavior is consistent with various scaling laws observed in deep learning, such as the
428   relationship between model performance and the increase in data size and model parameters
429   (Kaplan et al., 2020; Gordon et al., 2021). In these scaling laws, although larger datasets
430   can improve model generalization, the beneﬁts exhibit diminishing returns. This aligns
431   with our observation of the convex hull volume expansion: as data size grows, the expansion

454   9   Discussion and Conclusion
455
456   This paper introduces a novel framework that connects low-dimensional representations
457   and interpolation probabilities to the generalization capabilities of neural networks. Our
458   approach also integrates manifold structure (convex hull volume) into a uniﬁed theoreti-
459   cal explanation for generalization, supported by both empirical validation and theoretical
460   proofs.
461
      The key contributions are: (1) A theoretical framework that links interpolation, manifold
462   geometry and generalization, oﬀering new insights into why low-dimensional, compact rep-
463   resentations lead to improved generalization. (2) Empirical results demonstrating the re-
464   lationship between Intrinsic dimension, convex hull volume, interpolation probability and
465   generalization. (3) Theoretical exploration of scaling laws showing how increasing data
466   volume aﬀects interpolation probability.
467
      However, the framework has certain limitations. Simpliﬁed assumptions about uniform
468   data distribution and stable manifold structures may not fully capture the complexity of
469   real-world datasets (Cooper & Green, 2017; Majeed, 2019), and the evolution of neural
470   network embedding manifolds is also far more dynamic and intricate than such assumptions
471   suggest (Kunin et al., 2020). Moreover, the focus on interpolation might overlook scenarios
472   where extrapolation is required for robust generalization, particularly in tasks involving out-
473   of-distribution data (Liu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024). The experiments, primarily on MNIST
474   and Cifar10, should also be extended to more complex datasets for broader validation.
475   Moreover, while convex hull volume oﬀers valuable insights into how manifold structure
476   inﬂuences interpolation probabilities, it assumes a relatively stable manifold structure. In
477   practice, however, manifold structures evolve continuously throughout the learning process
478   (Birdal et al., 2021; Magai & Ayzenberg, 2022), rendering convex hull volume an incomplete
479   metric. To fully capture the dynamic nature of representation manifolds during neural
480   network training, a more comprehensive structural quantiﬁcation method is needed. Such
481   a method should also be interpretable, providing insights into both how and why these
482
      structural changes contribute to improved generalization performance.
483   Future work should address these limitations by considering more realistic data distributions
484   and validating the results on larger, more diverse datasets. Additionally, exploring how dif-
485   ferent neural network architectures inﬂuence manifold learning and interpolation probability
      could further enhance the model’s generalization capabilities.

      A    Supplementary Analysis of Learning Dynamics
595
596   To verify the generalizability of the results in Section 4, we also analyzed multiple model
597   architectures (e.g., AlexNet, VGG16) across diﬀerent datasets (e.g., CIFAR10, MNIST), as
598   shown in Figure 6. The ﬁndings generally align with the theoretical trends discussed in the
599   main text: as training progresses, classiﬁcation performance improves, the intrinsic dimen-
600   sion of embeddings decreases, the convex hull volume ﬁrst drops and then gradually rises,
601   and interpolation probability increases. However, diﬀerent datasets and model architectures
602   introduce some subtle variations. For instance, when training CIFAR10 with AlexNet, we
603   observed that after an initial decrease, the intrinsic dimension begins to rise again, which

605

      limitations in high-dimensional spaces. Therefore, developing more accurate and eﬃcient
607   algorithms for estimating convex hull volume in high-dimensional spaces is a key area for
608   future work.
