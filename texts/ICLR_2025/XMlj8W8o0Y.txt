009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional model adaptation framework assumes the same vocabulary across pre-
012            training and downstream datasets, which often struggles with limited transfer flex-
013            ibility and efficiency while handling downstream datasets with different vocab-
014            ularies. Inspired by recent vision-language models (VLMs) that enable visual
015            recognition defined by free-form texts via reasoning on both images and texts,
016            we study vision-language model adaptation (VLMA), a new unsupervised model
017            adaptation framework that positions a pre-trained VLM as the source model and
018
               transfers it towards various unlabelled downstream datasets. To this end, we pro-
               pose a Hough voting-based Self-Training (HoughST) technique that introduces a
019
               multimodal Hough voting mechanism to exploit the synergy between vision and
020
               language to mitigate the distribution shift in image and text modalities simultane-
021            ously. Specifically, HoughST makes use of the complementary property of differ-
022            ent types of features within and across vision and language modalities, which en-
023            ables joint exploitation of vision and language information and effective learning
024            of image-text correspondences in the unlabelled downstream datasets. Addition-
025            ally, HoughST captures temporal information via temporal Hough voting which
026            helps memorize and leverage previously learnt downstream dataset information.
027            Extensive experiments show that HoughST outperforms the state-of-the-art con-
028            sistently across 11 image recognition tasks. Codes will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep learning-based vision models He et al. (2016); Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) have achieved great
      success in myriad image recognition tasks but at the price of laborious annotation of large-scale
034
      training images Deng et al. (2009). To circumvent the annotation constraint, model adaptation
035
      (MA) Liang et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2021) has been explored to transfer a vision model pre-
036   trained in certain labelled pre-training datasets towards unlabelled downstream datasets by mitigat-
037   ing the distribution shift in image modality. However, traditional MA Liang et al. (2020); Huang
038   et al. (2021); Liang et al. (2021) assumes that pre-training and downstream datasets have the same
039   vocabulary. It struggles while handling downstream datasets with different vocabularies, limiting its
040   flexibility and efficiency greatly in unsupervised transfer.
041
      Inspired by recent vision-language models (VLMs) Radford et al. (2021) that enable visual recog-
042   nition defined by free-form texts via reasoning on both images and texts, we study vision-language
043   model adaptation (VLMA), a new unsupervised model adaptation (UMA) framework that positions
044   a pre-trained VLM as the source model and transfers it towards various unlabelled downstream
045   datasets. VLMA requires a single pre-trained VLM only while transferring towards various down-
046   stream datasets of different vocabularies, instead of preparing multiple vocabulary-specific vision
047   models with respective source datasets, as illustrated in Fig. 1. In addition, VLMA allows un-
048   supervised transfer towards new downstream datasets with customized vocabulary, which greatly
049   mitigates the image annotation constraint and facilitates deep network training while handling var-
050
      ious new visual recognition tasks. On the other hand, the shift from traditional model adaptation
      toward VLMA comes with a new challenge, namely, the distribution shifts in both image modality
051
      and text modality.

086   The proposed HoughST can be viewed as a new type of self-training with Hough voting for the task
087   of VLMA. It has three desirable advantages: 1) it introduces visual Hough voting and textual Hough
088   voting and enables simultaneous mitigation of distribution shift in both image and text modalities
089   effectively; 2) it introduces temporal Hough voting along the adaptation process which allows har-
090   vesting previously learnt downstream dataset information effectively; 3) it works within an aligned
091
      image-text feature space which allows Hough voting not only within but also across vision, language
      and temporal dimensions, capturing their complementary advantages effectively.
092
093   In summary, the contributions of this work are threefold. First, we propose a novel vision-language
094   model adaptation framework that explores Hough voting upon self-training to learn effective image-
095   text correspondences over unlabelled downstream images. To the best of our knowledge, this is
096   the first work that explores Hough voting for VLMA. Second, we design Hough voting-based self-
097
      training that introduces a multimodal Hough voting mechanism over vision, language and temporal
      dimensions for simultaneous mitigation of image and text distribution shift in VLMA. Third, exten-
098
      sive experiments show that the proposed Hough voting-based self-training outperforms the state-of-
099
      the-art consistently across multiple image recognition tasks.

104   Model Adaptation (MA), a type of unsupervised transfer learning, aims to adapt a model pre-
105   trained on certain labelled pre-training datasets towards unlabelled downstream datasets. Most ex-
106   isting MA methods can be broadly grouped into two categories. The first category employs gener-
107   ative models to compensate for the unavailable pre-training datasets by reconstructing pre-training
      features Li et al. (2020); Tian et al. (2021); Qiu et al. (2021) or images Du et al. (2021); Yeh et al.


                                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2021); Kurmi et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021). The second approach explores self-training that learns
109   from unlabelled downstream images with predicted pseudo labels Liang et al. (2020); Huang et al.
110   (2021); Liang et al. (2021); Xia et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2022b; 2023). De-
111   spite their great success, most existing methods assume the same vocabulary across the pre-training
112   and downstream datasets and cannot handle downstream datasets with different vocabulary or new
113   downstream dataset with customized vocabulary. This limits the flexibility and efficiency of MA
114   greatly. We study vision-language model adaptation in this work, a new framework that reasons
115   both images and texts and allows unsupervised transfer learning towards various unlabelled down-
116   stream datasets. We design Hough voting-based self-Training that introduces a multimodal Hough
117
      voting mechanism to explore the synergy of vision and language to mitigate image and text distri-
      bution shifts simultaneously in VLMA.
118
119   Vision Language Model (VLM) Radford et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2021); Yuan et al. (2021a); Yu
120   et al. (2022); Tschannen et al. (2022); Yao et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Mu et al. (2022); Cui et al.
121   (2022); Li et al. (2021); Singh et al. (2022); Gao et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022a);
122   Shen et al. (2022); Alayrac et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2022); Lee et al.; Chen et al. (2022b;c); Geng
123
      et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2022); Zhong et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022b); Zhao et al. (2022); Dou
      et al.; Yao et al. aims to learn effective vision-language correlation from image-text pairs that are
124
      almost infinitely available on the Web. It has demonstrated great potential in open-vocabulary visual
125
      recognition by recognizing images with free-form texts. On the other hand, VLMs often suffer from
126   degraded performance due to distribution shifts with respect to various downstream datasets. Unlike
127   recent attempts Zhou et al. (2022c;b); Yao et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Khattak et al. (2022);
128   Xing et al. (2022); Bulat & Tzimiropoulos (2022); Lu et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022a); Ding et al.
129   (2022a); Pratt et al. (2022); Rao et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2023) that adapt VLMs by prompt tuning
130   with few-shot downstream dataset images, we focus on adapting VLMs towards various downstream
131   datasets by ingeniously exploiting the unlabelled images which are often off-the-shelf available in
132   abundance.
133   Hough Voting detects complex objects by aggregating votes from their subregions and surrounding
134   areas, leveraging spatially complementary information to enhance vision tasks. Existing methods
135   can be broadly classified into two categories. The first category is classical Hough voting, which
136   relies on traditional visual patterns. For example, Ballard (1981) detects the presence of complex ob-
137   jects by voting from image patches, Leibe et al. (2008) proposes the implicit shape model, Sun et al.
138   (2010) integrates depth information into Hough voting, Maji & Malik (2009) designs importance-
139   aware voting, and Gall et al. (2011); Gall & Lempitsky (2013) develop Hough forests. The second
140
      category is deep Hough voting, which incorporates voting mechanisms into deep neural networks.
      For instance, Kehl et al. (2016) uses deep features for 6D pose estimation, Milletari et al. (2017)
141
      learns deep features to build codebooks, and Qi et al. (2019); Lee et al. (2021) apply Hough voting
142
      within deep networks for 3D learning. In contrast to previous approaches, we propose HoughST that
143   works within an aligned image-text feature space which enables Hough voting not only within but
144   also across visual, language and temporal dimensions, effectively capturing their complementary
145   strengths for vision-language model adaptation.

229   We tackle the challenge of VLMA from a perspective of Hough Voting Ballard (1981); Qi et al.
230
      (2019); Lee et al. (2021). As illustrated in Fig. 2, we design Hough voting-based Self-Training
      (HoughST) that introduces visual Hough voting and textual Hough voting over self-training to mit-
231
      igate the distribution shifts in image and text modalities simultaneously. In addition, HoughST cap-
232
      tures temporal information via temporal Hough voting, which rectifies self-training via voting from
233   the features encoded by the intermediate models evolved along the adaptation process, ultimately
234   helping memorize and utilize previously learnt downstream dataset information.

236
      Textual Hough voting gathers the text features encoded from different types of text descriptions
      for Hough voting, aiming to leverage the complementary information of various text descriptions
237
      (i.e., different types of text descriptions for a class Lüddecke & Ecker (2022); Zang et al. (2022)) to
238
      mitigate the distribution shift in text modality. It employs a Large Language Model (LLM) Brown
239   et al. (2020); Wang & Komatsuzaki (2021) to generate different types of text descriptions for a
240   given class name and then encodes them by the VLM text encoder. The encoded text features are
241   then fused in a two-step manner: 1) uniformly average the multiple text features to acquire an initial
242   voting centroid; 2) calculate the final voting centroid by weighted average where the weight of each
243   feature is the distance between it and the initial voting centroid. This two-step voting operation
244   allows smooth feature fusion by weighting down the effect of corner cases, which is important for
245   textual Hough voting as the LLM-generated text descriptions are not always reliable (e.g., when
246   experiencing generation failures, LLM may generate only a full stop character “.” or a random
247
      word).

517   5            C ONCLUSION
518
519   This paper presents HoughST, a novel vision-language model adaptation framework that explores
520   Hough voting to learn effective image-text correspondences over unlabelled downstream dataset im-
521   ages. HoughST introduces a multimodal Hough voting mechanism over vision, language and tempo-
522   ral dimensions for simultaneous mitigation of image and text distribution shifts in VLMA. It requires
523   merely a single pre-trained VLM but achieves effective and efficient unsupervised model adaptation
524   towards various unlabelled downstream datasets, demonstrating its superiority in facilitating deep
525   network training while handling various new visual recognition tasks and styles. Extensive exper-
526   iments show that HoughST achieves superb recognition performance consistently across different
527
      backbones and image recognition tasks and styles. Moving forward, we will explore HoughST for
      other vision tasks such as segmentation and detection.

      Office Saenko et al. (2010) includes 31-class images collected from Amazon (A), Webcam (W) and

899   three styles in Office dataset, we further include an office Synthetic (S) style for benchmarking our
900   HoughST comprehensively. The Synthetic (S) style is provided by Ringwald & Stiefelhagen (2021)
901   and consists of 3100 images.

      Office-home Venkateswara et al. (2017) consists of 65-class images collected from Art (A), Cli-

      uct (P), Real-World (R) and Synthetic (S), where each style has 12300 images.
908   VisDA Peng et al. (2017) has over 280K images of 12 classes from Synthetic (S) style and Real-
909   World (R) style, which contain 152397 and 127760 images, respectively.

      DomainNet Peng et al. (2019) includes 345-class images from Clipart, Infograph, Painting, Quick-

1126   G    M ORE DISCUSSION OF TEXTUAL H OUGH VOTING
1127
1128   As described in the main manuscript, the proposed textual Hough voting fuses text features in a two-
1129   step manner: 1) uniformly average the multiple text features to acquire an initial voting centroid;
1130   2) calculate the final voting centroid by weighted average where the weight of each feature is the
1131   distance between it and the initial voting centroid. This two-step voting operation allows smooth
1132   feature fusion by weighting down the effect of corner cases, which is important for textual Hough
1133   voting as the LLM-generated text descriptions are not always reliable (e.g., when experiencing gen-
       eration failures, LLM may generate only a full stop character “.” or a random word).

1136   feature fusion strategy brings about 0.4% performance improvement on ImageNet, largely because
1137   it allows smooth feature fusion by down-weighting the effect of corner cases.

            A DAPTATION
1276
1277   Different from traditional domain adaptation that assumes the same vocabulary across source and
1278   downstream datasets, this work studies vision-language model adaptation (VLMA), a new unsuper-
1279   vised model adaptation (UMA) framework that positions a pre-trained VLM as the source model
1280
       and transfers it towards various unlabelled downstream datasets.
1281   We note that there are several other adaptation frameworks which also aim to handle the situation
1282   where the pre-training and downstream datasets have different vocabularies. In this section, we
1283   briefly introduce their frameworks and clarify the difference between them and the studied VLMA.
1284   Specifically, open-set domain adaptation Panareda Busto & Gall (2017); Saito et al. (2018); Liu et al.
1285   (2019), class-incremental domain adaptation Kundu et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2021) and partial domain
1286   adaptation Cao et al. (2018; 2019); Zhang et al. (2018), are proposed to handle the situation where
1287   the source and downstream datasets have different vocabularies. However, all these frameworks
1288   have certain limitations as compared the studied VLMA.
1289
       For example, open-set domain adaptation Panareda Busto & Gall (2017); Saito et al. (2018); Liu
1290   et al. (2019) adds an extra class called “unknown” to both source and downstream datasets such
1291   that it allows open-set adaptation by treating all the classes that are not shared between source and
1292   downstream datasets as the “unknown” class. However, open-set domain adaptation can merely
1293   classify all new target classes/concepts as a single “unknown” class even in an ideal case, which
1294   fails to respectively recognize new classes/concepts, limiting its flexibility and efficiency greatly in
1295   unsupervised transfer. Differently, VLMA allows to respectively recognize various new downstream
       categories/concepts, which is much more flexible.


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       Class-incremental domain adaptation Kundu et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2021) integrates domain
1297   adaptation and class-incremental learning (using one-shot or few-shot labelled downstream images)
1298   such that it allows to recognize new target classes/concepts during adaptation. However, it generally
1299   requires one-shot or few-shot labelled downstream images for each new class as a prerequisite, while
1300   VLMA is unsupervised and can work for new classes without requiring labelled target images.
1301
       Partial domain adaptation Cao et al. (2018; 2019); Zhang et al. (2018) assumes that the label set
1302
       of downstream dataset is a subset of the label set of source dataset. Differently, the studied VLMA
1303   does not have this constraint as it can work with various downstream classes Radford et al. (2015).

       tation framework that mitigates the image annotation constraint and facilitate deep network training
       while handling new visual recognition tasks. Furthermore, as our work is built upon open-source
1310
