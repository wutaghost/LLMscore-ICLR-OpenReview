010
011            G LOBALIZATION and migration have highlighted the critical need for effective
012            cross-language communication, particularly in healthcare. In Uganda, a multi-
013            lingual nation where Luganda is widely spoken, language barriers in predomi-
014            nantly English-speaking medical settings often lead to misunderstandings, mis-
015            diagnoses, and compromised patient care. This study aims to mitigate these is-
016            sues by developing a machine translation model built for medical communication,
017            specifically targeting translations from English to Luganda within the context of
018
               malaria diagnosis and community engagement. Utilizing recent advancements in
               machine learning, this research involves curating a parallel medical corpus, train-
019
               ing a transformer-based model with domain-specific adapters, and rigorously eval-
020
               uating the model’s accuracy and cultural sensitivity. The results demonstrate that
021            the MarianMT-Adapter LoRa model, when combined with active learning,
022            achieved a significant improvement in translation quality, evidenced by a BLEU
023            score increase to 56%. This model effectively reduced translation errors and
024            preserved the contextual integrity of medical texts. The findings are anticipated
025            to enhance healthcare communication, reduce disparities, and improve access to
026            medical knowledge for Luganda-speaking communities, providing a blueprint for
027            similar efforts in other multilingual environments.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In today’s rapidly digitalising world, the need for effective communication across different lan-
033
      guages is more critical than ever, particularly in essential sectors such as healthcare. Language
      barriers in medical settings can lead to serious consequences, including misunderstandings, misdi-
034
      agnoses, and inadequate treatment, all of which compromise patient care and safety Mustafa et al.
035
      (2023). This issue is especially relevant in multilingual societies like Uganda, where linguistic diver-
036   sity often clashes with the predominantly English-speaking medical workforce Hull (2016). Among
037   Uganda’s many languages, Luganda is widely spoken in the central region, including the capital,
038   Kampala Ssempuuma (2019). Despite its prevalence, there exists a substantial language gap in med-
039   ical settings where English remains the primary mode of communication. This gap hinders effective
040   patient-provider interactions and limits access to essential healthcare services for Luganda-speaking
041   communities.
042   This problem is aggravated by a lack of skilled medical translators, especially in rural and under-
043   resourced areas, and the inefficiencies of current human-mediated translation services, which often
044   fail to account for cultural nuances and the complexity of medical terminology. Artificial Intel-
045   ligence (AI), particularly in the form of Machine Translation (MT), offers a promising solution.
046   Advances in AI and Natural Language Processing (NLP) have led to significant improvements in
047   translation accuracy, but these developments have primarily focused on widely spoken languages
048   Khasawneh & Al-Amrat (2023). Lesser-known languages like Luganda remain underrepresented,
049   especially in specialized fields such as healthcare.
050   The scarcity of parallel text corpora in low-resource languages like Luganda presents a challenge for
051   traditional, supervised MT models. However, recent developments in unsupervised MT techniques,
052   which do not rely on parallel texts, offer a potential path forward. In the context of healthcare,
053   particularly in the fight against malaria—a disease with high prevalence in Uganda—there is an
      urgent need to communicate accurate information about symptoms, prevention, and treatment in

      local languages Chemonges Wanyama et al. (2021). This study aims to leverage AI technologies,
055   specifically unsupervised MT models, to develop a tool that can accurately translate medical texts
056   related to malaria from English to Luganda. By addressing the language barrier in medical com-
057   munication, this research seeks to improve healthcare outcomes in Luganda-speaking communities,
058   making healthcare more inclusive and accessible. The development of this MT model not only of-
059   fers a technological solution but also serves as a public health intervention, potentially transforming
060   healthcare delivery in linguistically diverse regions.

      2     L ITERATURE R EVIEW
063
064
      This section reviews existing literature related to supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised
065   learning techniques, focusing on their applications to machine translation and healthcare. Machine
066   learning (ML) has been applied to numerous healthcare tasks, including diagnosis, treatment, drug
067   discovery, and healthcare administration, while also advancing machine translation (MT) for low-
068   resource languages.
069
      Supervised learning, where labeled data is provided to train models, has shown significant success in
070
      domains with abundant labeled datasets. In the context of machine translation and healthcare, super-
071   vised models, such as Neural Machine Translation (NMT), rely on parallel corpora to achieve high
072   performance Abrishami et al. (2020). In healthcare, supervised learning techniques have improved
073   diagnostic accuracy and personalized treatment by processing patient data Ahmad et al. (2021);
074   Ballester & Carmona (2021).
075
      Unsupervised learning, which operates without labeled data, has become essential for low-resource
076
      languages where labeled corpora are scarce. Methods like Unsupervised NMT (UNMT) utilize
077   monolingual data and techniques such as back-translation to bridge language barriers, especially in
078   low-resource settings Artetxe et al. (2017); Lample (2019). This is particularly relevant to health-
079   care, where language diversity complicates communication and access to medical resources Haddow
080   et al. (2021).
081
      Despite these advancements, several research gaps persist. Supervised learning models struggle with
082
      scalability in low-resource environments due to their reliance on extensive labeled data. Moreover,
083   unsupervised approaches face technical challenges in maintaining translation accuracy, particularly
084   in domain-specific contexts like healthcare, where precise terminology is critical Conneau & Lample
085   (2019). Additionally, the lack of robust parallel corpora for low-resource languages in healthcare
086   poses a significant barrier to developing accurate MT systems for medical content Artetxe et al.
087   (2017).
088
      To address these gaps, this research focuses on improving unsupervised models for medical trans-
089   lation in low-resource settings. By leveraging monolingual data and reinforcement learning tech-
090   niques, we aim to enhance translation quality and overcome existing limitations. Applying con-
091   textual annotation frameworks, as used in MQM Utiyama & Wang (2023), can further refine error
092   detection in medical translations, contributing to more reliable models.

199
      The newly generated Luganda-English parallel corpus was subjected to a series of preprocessing
200   steps to ensure quality and usability. The steps included:
201
202           • Cleaning: Removal of noise, errors, and inconsistencies that may have been introduced

              • Normalization: Standardization of text to account for variations in spelling, grammar, and

                formatting.

                by machine learning models.
209           • Segmentation: Dividing the dataset into manageable subsets for training, validation, and

      3.5   M ODEL T RAINING
217
218
219
      For this study, we employed MarianMT as our baseline machine translation (MT) model Junczys-
      Dowmunt et al. (2018). MarianMT is a highly efficient and flexible neural MT framework built on
220
      the transformer architecture Vaswani (2017). The transformer has become the de facto standard for
221
      state-of-the-art MT systems due to its ability to model long-range dependencies and its paralleliza-
222   tion capabilities.
223
224   The MarianMT model architecture consists of an encoder-decoder structure, both of which are com-
225
      posed of multiple layers of self-attention mechanisms and feed-forward neural networks. The en-
      coder processes the input sentence from the source language, while the decoder generates the cor-
226
      responding translation in the target language. The self-attention mechanism enables the model to
227
      capture contextual dependencies effectively by weighing the importance of various words in the
228   input.

      3.5.1   A DAPTER O PTIMIZATION
271
272   To improve the performance of the baseline MarianMT model, we incorporated adapter modules us-
273   ing Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021). Adapters are lightweight modules added to each
274   layer of the pre-trained model, allowing it to adapt to new tasks or domains with minimal changes to
275   the original parameters. This approach is particularly beneficial when dealing with limited training
276   data or aiming to preserve the generalization capabilities of the pre-trained model.
277   The adapter modules, while being trained alongside the main model, consist of a much smaller
278   number of parameters, allowing efficient fine-tuning without requiring significant computational
279   resources. In this case, we fine-tuned the MarianMT model with adapters on the NLLB and UFAL
280   datasets, both with and without active learning.

321   3.5.2   ACTIVE L EARNING FOR M ODEL I MPROVEMENT
322
323   To further enhance our machine translation model, we employed an active learning approach, which
      iteratively selects the most informative samples from a pool of unlabeled data. This allows the model


                                                                6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      to improve its performance more efficiently by focusing on the most challenging data points Zhao
325   et al. (2020). For this study, we utilized uncertainty sampling.
326
327   We began by training the model on an initial dataset of 1,000 samples. After the training phase, the
328
      model’s performance was evaluated using a validation set. Based on this, uncertainty scores were
      computed from the prediction probabilities using entropy. Samples with the highest uncertainty
329
      were then selected for additional training. This process was repeated for five iterations, each time
330
      adding 500 new samples, followed by retraining and re-evaluation.
331
332   For the implementation, entropy-based uncertainty sampling was employed, selecting samples with
333   higher entropy for retraining. The model was trained using a batch size of 32, leveraging gradient
334
      accumulation and mixed precision to optimize memory usage. Evaluation was conducted using
      BLEU scores on the validation set to assess translation quality.
335
336   The MarianMT-Adapter LoRA model, combined with active learning, was subsequently trained for
337   two additional epochs on the malaria corpus, focusing on the most informative samples to improve
338   translation accuracy.
339   Overall, the training schedule involved the following configurations: MarianMT baseline for 30
340   epochs, MarianMT-Adapter LoRA for 5 epochs, and MarianMT-Adapter LoRA with Active
341   Learning for 2 epochs. All models were evaluated on the malaria corpus to ensure their relevance
342   to the target medical domain.

409
410   The results demonstrate both the strengths and limitations of the different models. The MarianMT
411   Baseline model, trained on the Makerere Corpus, provided a strong foundation but showed some
412   limitations in handling specific datasets, as evidenced by the BLEU score drop on the NLLB Dataset.
413   Adapter optimization with LoRA, though effective for adding task-specific functionality, introduced
414   additional challenges. The decline in performance across datasets suggests that this technique needs
415   further refinement and larger amounts of domain-specific data to achieve the desired results. How-
416   ever, the real breakthrough came when active learning was introduced.
417   Active learning greatly improved the MarianMT-Adapter LoRa model’s performance, as demon-
418   strated by the significant 6% increase in BLEU score. This approach allowed the model to itera-
419   tively focus on the most informative and challenging samples, reducing errors and producing more
420   accurate translations. Figure 4 showcases the improvements in translation quality, where the active
421   learning model corrected many of the errors present in the baseline.

439   5.3   L IMITATIONS
440
441   Despite these advancements, the study is not without limitations. The reliance on BLEU scores
442   as the primary evaluation metric may overlook nuanced translation errors, especially in a special-
443   ized domain like medical translation. BLEU, while useful for assessing overall accuracy, does not
444   capture the contextual or cultural aspects of translation quality, which are critical for healthcare
445
      communication.
446   Another limitation is the availability of parallel corpora in Luganda. The lack of extensive, high-
447   quality training data constrained the scope of improvements and hindered the ability to generalize
448   the model beyond the malaria domain. While active learning mitigated some of these challenges by
449   focusing on key samples, a larger dataset would likely yield even greater improvements.
450
451   5.4   F UTURE W ORK
452
453   Future research should focus on expanding the training datasets, particularly by engaging the local
454
      community in data collection and annotation. Crowdsourcing efforts involving native speakers and
      medical professionals could help build a more extensive and diverse corpus for model training.
455
456   In addition, future work will incorporate more advanced active learning techniques, including dif-
457   ferent sampling strategies that go beyond uncertainty sampling. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning ap-
458   proaches such as prefix-tuning or prompt-tuning could also be explored to enhance model adaptabil-
459   ity without requiring extensive retraining.
460   Human evaluations, conducted alongside BLEU scores, will provide a more comprehensive assess-
461   ment of translation quality, particularly in capturing the nuances of medical terminology and cultural
462   appropriateness. These human assessments will be crucial for refining models intended for health-
463   care applications.
