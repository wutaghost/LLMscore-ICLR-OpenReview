009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training deep neural networks typically relies on backpropagating high-
012            dimensional error signals—a computationally intensive process with little evi-
013            dence supporting its implementation in the brain. However, since most tasks
014            involve low-dimensional outputs, we propose that low-dimensional error signals
015            may suffice for effective learning. To test this hypothesis, we introduce a novel
016            local learning rule based on Feedback Alignment that leverages indirect, low-
017            dimensional error feedback to train large networks. Our method decouples the
018
               backward pass from the forward pass, enabling precise control over error signal di-
               mensionality while maintaining high-dimensional representations. We begin with
019
               a detailed theoretical derivation for linear networks, which forms the foundation of
020
               our learning framework, and extend our approach to nonlinear and convolutional
021            architectures. Remarkably, we demonstrate that even minimal error dimensional-
022            ity—on the order of the task dimensionality—can achieve performance matching
023            that of traditional backpropagation. Furthermore, our rule enables efficient train-
024            ing of convolutional networks, which have previously been resistant to Feedback
025            Alignment methods, with minimal error. This breakthrough not only paves the
026            way towards more biologically accurate models of learning but also challenges
027            the conventional reliance on high-dimensional gradient signals in neural network
028            training. Our findings suggest that low-dimensional error signals can be as effec-
029
               tive as high-dimensional ones, prompting a reevaluation of gradient-based learn-
               ing in high-dimensional systems. Ultimately, our work offers a fresh perspective
030
               on neural network optimization and contributes to understanding learning mecha-
031
               nisms in both artificial and biological systems.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Neural networks, like the mind of a child, learn through whispers of correction—a subtle feed-
037   back that shapes their understanding of the world. Yet, while the world often communicates in
038   the simplest of terms, our neural networks, with all their magnificent complexity, respond with an
039   overabundance of noise.
040
      Consider a typical real-world problem addressed by deep networks. Although the inputs to the
041
      networks are high-dimensional and the networks are overparameterized, the underlying structure
042   of many real-world tasks is often far simpler—a low-dimensional core. Consequently, the error
043   feedback received from the world, represented by the loss gradient, is also low-dimensional. Yet, as
044   this error signal propagates backward through the network, it gains dimensionality, raising important
045   questions about the efficiency and necessity of this approach.
046
      The increase in error dimensionality occurs as the error signals propagate through the over-
047
      parameterized layers, a consequence of the inherent coupling between the feedforward and feedback
048   processes. This coupling ensures that detailed error information is made available at each layer of the
049   network, solving the credit-assignment problem. However, such high-dimensional error propagation
050   is not strictly required for effective learning. In biological systems, particularly in the brain, error
051   signals often travel through indirect and constrained pathways, hinting that lower-dimensional error
052   feedback could suffice for learning. Despite this intriguing possibility, our understanding of how
053   error signal dimensionality impacts learning—and whether similar principles operate in biological
      systems—remains limited.

054
      Beyond the enigma of whether low-dimensional error signals suffice for effective training lies a
055   deeper question: how do these signals shape the neural representations that emerge during learning?
056   Specifically, we do not yet understand how the properties of error feedback influence neural tuning
057   curves and receptive fields or how to connect these emergent features to their underlying learning
058   mechanisms. Unraveling such connections could not only illuminate the processes by which the
059   brain learns but also pave the way for more efficient learning strategies in artificial networks.
060
      In this study, we investigate whether low-dimensional error pathways can serve as an effective al-
061   ternative to training deep networks. We explore the hypothesis that low-dimensional error signals
062   can drive efficient learning, thereby aligning artificial network training with the constraints seen in
063   real-world tasks and biological systems. By extending current Feedback Alignment methods that
064   decouple error feedback from the forward pass, we systematically manipulate the dimensionality
065   of error signals, probing whether these constrained pathways can still support the rich and complex
066   representations characteristic of deep learning.

068   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
069
070   We consider a multilayerd perceptron with L layers, each layer l computes its output as hl =
071   f (Wl hl−1 ), where Wl is the weight matrix, and f is an element-wise activation function. The input
072   to the network is h0 = x, and the final network output is ŷ = fL (WL hL−1 ), which approximates
073   the target y. The task dimensionality, denoted d, is at most the number of components in y and ŷ.
074
      Training the network involves minimizing a loss function L(y, ŷ) by adjusting the weights {Wl }.
075
      The error signal at the output layer, δL = ∂L
                                                 ∂ ŷ , is a d-dimensional vector, typically much smaller than
076
      the number of neurons in the hidden layers.
077
078   Backpropagation (BP) (Rumelhart et al., 1986) is the standard approach for training neural net-

079   works. It propagates the error backward through the network using δl = Wl+1        δl+1 ⊙ f ′ (Wl hl−1 ),

080   and updates the weights using ∆Wl = −ηδl hl−1 , where η is the learning rate. However, this method

081   requires the exact transpose of the forward weights, Wl+1   , which is biologically implausible (Gross-
082   berg, 1987; Crick, 1989). Moreover, BP tightly couples the error propagation with the forward pass,
083   limiting the ability to explore how different properties of the error signal affect learning dynamics.
084

090   networks (CNNs), where it often fails to achieve competitive performance (Bartunov et al., 2018).
091

099
      is constrained.

102   of error propagation (Crafton et al., 2019). However, these approaches result in significantly lower
103   performance and do not provide a systematic framework for studying how error constraints affect
104
      learning and representation formation.

190   Constraining error dimensionality with low-rank feedback To control the dimensionality of the
191   error feedback, we impose a low-rank constraint on the feedback matrix B. Rather than allowing
192   full-dimensional feedback, we decompose B as B = QP , where Q ∈ Rk×r and P ∈ Rr×m . When
193   r < min(k, m), B is low rank, which means that it can project the error signal onto at most r
194
      independent directions.
195   This low-rank structure introduces an ”r-bottleneck,” which limits the flow of error information
      1
196     . By controlling the value of r, we can systematically study how reducing the dimensionality of
197   the error signal impacts learning. Initially, we follow the original Feedback Alignment framework,
198   keeping Q and P as random matrices. However, as we will demonstrate, allowing Q and P to learn
199   is crucial to high performance.

411   4.1        C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
412
413   Our previous results demonstrate that fully connected networks can learn effectively from minimal
414   error signals, even on complex datasets, matching backpropagation (BP) performance. Here, we
415   extend this investigation to convolutional architectures.
416   Training convolutional networks with Feedback Alignment (FA) is notoriously difficult (Bartunov
417   et al., 2018; Launay et al., 2019). Recent work has made progress by learning feedback weights
418   within the FA framework (Bacho & Chu, 2024), but our approach differs by restricting the error
419   signal’s dimensionality using Restricted Adaptive Feedback (RAF). We aim to determine whether
420   convolutional networks can also benefit from low-dimensional error feedback.
421   We trained a VGG-like convolutional network with four blocks and batch normalization on the
422   CIFAR-10 dataset (see Appendix for details). Using RAF, we decoupled the error propagation from
423   the feedforward pass in all layers. Initially, we constrained the feedback error only in the blocks
424   containing 512 (Fig. 4a). Consistent with our findings in fully connected networks, the convolutional
425   networks learn well with a feedback matrix with a rank similar to the number of classes, d = 10.
426   To test whether convolutional networks can train when constraining all feedback paths, we further
427   constrained each block to have feedback matrices with ranks equal to 1/2, 1/4, or 1/8 of the block
428   width (Fig. 4b). Our results indicate that reducing the error dimensionality has minimal impact on
429   performance, except in the most extreme case. Specifically, constraining the feedback rank to 1/8 of
430   the block width resulted in a noticeable drop in performance. This finding aligns with our previous
431   results, as the layer with 64 channels received feedback with a rank of r = 8. Overall, our results
      demonstrate that convolutional networks can be efficiently trained using a minimal error signal.

449   5              E RROR D IMENSIONALITY S HAPES N EURAL R ECEPTIVE F IELDS
450
451   We have shown that neural networks can be efficiently trained using minimal error signals compa-
452   rable to task dimensionality. Here, we investigate how error dimensionality affects neural represen-
453   tations, providing insights into receptive fields observed in the brain.
454
      Lindsey et al. (2019) found that in convolutional models of the visual system, narrow feedforward
455   bottlenecks between the retina and the brain led to center-surround receptive fields in the retinal
456   layer, similar to mammals. Wider bottlenecks resulted in orientation-selective receptive fields, as
457   seen in salamanders. We hypothesize that these effects are due to constraints on the error signal
458   reaching the retina, rather than the feedforward bottlenecks themselves.
459
      To test this hypothesis, we trained a model similar to that of Lindsey et al. (2019) but with full-width
460
      layers throughout. Instead of constraining the feedforward pathway, we constrained only the error
461   signal using a low-rank feedback matrix trained with RAF (Fig. 4c), thereby isolating the impact of
462   error dimensionality on neural representations. We extracted the receptive fields of neurons in the
463   retinal layer using visualization techniques (Erhan et al., 2009) (Fig. 4d).
464
      Consistent with our hypothesis, constraining the feedback rank led to the emergence of center-
465
      surround receptive fields in the retinal layer. To further validate our hypothesis, we trained a model
466
      that included the feedforward bottleneck, similar to Lindsey et al. (2019) but used dRAF to train
467   the retinal layer without restricting the backward pathway. In line with our expectations, the retinal
468   receptive fields exhibited orientation selectivity (see Appendix).
469
470
      This experiment demonstrates that error dimensionality influences neuronal tuning and neural rep-
      resentations. Specifically, lower-dimensional error signals promote higher symmetries in emergent
471
      receptive fields. Our findings underscore the importance of considering the dimensionality and path-
472
      ways of error signals when studying neural computations in the brain.

475   6              D ISCUSSION
476
477
      Our work demonstrates that neural networks can be trained effectively using minimal error signals
478
      constrained to the task’s intrinsic dimensionality, rather than the higher dimensionality of the net-
479
      work’s representations. By adopting a factorized version of Feedback Alignment with low-rank
480   matrices and training both left and right spaces of the feedback matrix we showed that deep net-
481   works—linear, nonlinear, and convolutional—can achieve performance comparable to full back-
482   propagation even under stringent error-dimensionality constraints. This finding highlights that the
483   essential information required for learning is tied to the complexity of the task, as measured by the
484   number of output classes. Additionally, we revealed that constraining error dimensionality influ-
485   ences neural representations, providing a potential explanation for biological phenomena, such as
      center-surround receptive fields in the retina.

486
      The main goal of this work is to explore potential mechanisms for implementing gradient descent
487   in the brain. Recognizing that high-dimensional feedback is not necessary for effective learning
488   is a significant step toward developing more flexible and biologically realistic models of learning.
489   This insight suggests that the brain might utilize low-dimensional error signals to drive learning
490   processes, aligning with the anatomical and physiological constraints.
491
      While our novel learning rule bears superficial similarity to previous Feedback Alignment (FA)
492
      schemes, it is conceptually different. In traditional FA, learning the feedback weights aims to align
493   them with the feedforward weights Lillicrap et al. (2016); Akrout et al. (2019), mirroring full back-
494   propagation. In contrast, by factorizing the feedback matrix as B = QP we also align the feedback’s
495   row space with the source of the error, thereby improving the quality of the error signal itself. This
496   approach not only enhances learning efficiency but may also act as a form of regularization, a pos-
497   sibility that warrants further investigation.
498
      Our findings invite a rethinking of gradient descent dynamics in overparameterized networks. Typi-
499   cally, the weight dynamics during training are high-dimensional. However, when the error signal is
500   low-dimensional, the weight updates in each layer are confined to a much lower-dimensional sub-
501   space. This constraint could have implications for understanding the generalization capabilities of
502   neural networks, as it suggests that effective learning does not require exploring the full parame-
503   ter space. Exploring this connection could open new avenues for understanding the dynamics of
504   gradient descent in high-dimensional loss spaces.
505   In summary, our work highlights the critical role of error signal dimensionality in learning and repre-
506   sentation formation within neural networks. By demonstrating that low-dimensional error feedback
507   is sufficient for effective training, we bridge a gap between artificial neural network training and
508   biological limitations. This alignment advances our understanding of how the brain implements
509   supervised learning and provides a foundation for extending current learning frameworks.

874   E     C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
875
876   To extend our RAF algorithm to convolutional layers, we apply the rank constraint to the number of
877   channels in the error signal. This effectively constrains the dimensionality of the error signal across
878   the spatial dimensions. Similar to the implementation for fully connected layers in Algorithm 1, we
879   modify the backward pass in the same way for convolutional layers.
880   The key difference in the convolutional context is how the projection matrix P operates on the error
881   signals. In convolutional layers, P functions as a 1 × 1 convolutional filter, projecting the error
882   signal at each spatial location from m channels down to r channels. This reduces the error signal’s
883   dimensionality to r per pixel, adhering to the rank constraint.
884   As in the fully connected case, the matrix P is learned using Oja’s rule. However, the covariance
885   matrix C is computed over both the batch and spatial dimensions—that is, across all pixels in all
886   images within the batch. This approach captures the covariance structure of pixel representations
887   more effectively, enabling P to project the error signals appropriately in the convolutional setting.
888
