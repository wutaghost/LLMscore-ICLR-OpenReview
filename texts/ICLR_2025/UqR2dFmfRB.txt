009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            When the complete source sentence is provided, Large Language Models (LLMs)
012            perform excellently in offline machine translation even with a simple prompt
013            “Translate the following sentence from [src lang] into [tgt lang]:”. However, in
014            many real scenarios, the source tokens arrive in a streaming manner and simultane-
015            ous machine translation (SiMT) is required, then the efficiency and performance
016            of decoder-only LLMs are significantly limited by their auto-regressive nature. To
017            enable LLMs to achieve high-quality SiMT as efficiently as offline translation,
018
               we propose a novel paradigm that includes constructing supervised fine-tuning
               (SFT) data for SiMT, along with new training and inference strategies. To repli-
019
               cate the token input/output (I/O) stream in SiMT, the source and target tokens are
020
               rearranged into an interleaved sequence, separated by special tokens according to
021            varying latency requirements. This enables powerful LLMs to learn read and write
022            operations adaptively, based on varying latency prompts, while still maintaining
023            efficient auto-regressive decoding. Experimental results demonstrate that, even
024            with limited SFT data, our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance across
025            various simultaneous translation benchmarks and different evaluation metrics, and
026            preserves the original capabilities of offline translation. Moreover, EAST gener-
027            alizes well to document-level SiMT setting without requiring specific fine-tuning,
028            even beyond the offline translation model.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Simultaneous machine translation (SiMT) (Gu et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2019) is a critical technique
      for enabling seamless cross-linguistic communication in real-time scenarios, such as international
034
      conferences. Unlike offline neural machine translation (NMT), where the entire source sentence
035
      is available before translation begins, SiMT systems start translating before receiving the complete
036   input, achieving a balance between translation quality and latency.
037
038   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved significant advances in NMT, demonstrating impres-
039
      sive capacity when translating full sentences in offline settings (Xu et al., 2024a;b; Guo et al., 2024a;
      Feng et al., 2024). However, their application to SiMT remains underexplored and faces several sig-
040
      nificant challenges. First, most existing SiMT models (Zhao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024b; Raffel
041
      et al., 2024) are typically trained on offline NMT data due to the scarcity of SiMT-specific datasets.
042   This training setup does not align well with the demands of SiMT, which hinders the model’s ability
043   to learn how to translate effectively with incomplete input (Wang et al., 2023b; Sakai et al., 2024).
044   Second, many SiMT approaches focus on optimizing prompt structures to simulate SiMT for LLMs
045   (Wang et al., 2023a; Koshkin et al., 2024a;b; Guo et al., 2024b; Agostinelli et al., 2024; Cheng et al.,
046   2024), which typically requires recomputing the key-value (KV) cache since the prompt changes
047   continuously with the update of the source and target. This recomputation significantly increases
048   the computational cost and inference latency, limiting the efficiency of SiMT systems (Raffel et al.,
049   2024). Lastly, LLMs-based methods typically employs fixed read-write policies (Wang et al., 2023a;
050
      Agostinelli et al., 2024; Sakai et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Raffel et al., 2024), such as the wait-k,
      to achieve low-latency translations for LLMs. However, these methods fail to adaptively adjust its
051
      read/write actions based on sentence structure and context, leading to suboptimal translation quality.
052
053   In this paper, we introduce EAST, an Efficient and Adaptive Simultaneous machine Translation
      method with LLMs, which aims to achieve high-quality SiMT as efficiently as offline NMT. Specif-

103   2      R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Traditional SiMT SiMT is a challenging task in machine translation, aiming to balance translation
106   quality and latency. Unlike offline NMT, SiMT requires starting the translation before the full source
107   sentence is available. The read-write policy governs this decision-making process by determining
      whether the system should wait to read more source text or proceed with writing the translation based


                                                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on the partial input. Traditional SiMT models are usually built on an encoder-decoder architecture
109   or its variants, with fixed or trainable policies. A widely adopted fixed policy is the wait-k (Ma
110   et al., 2019), which has been extensively explored due to its simplicity (Elbayad et al., 2020; Zhang
111   & Feng, 2021; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). However,
112   it typically leads to suboptimal translations for complex contexts or non-monotonic language pairs
113   (Zhang et al., 2022). Instead of relying on pre-defined rules, adaptive policies dynamically learn
114   when to read and write, further improving the translation quality. To enable models to learn effective
115   read-write decisions, a variety of techniques have been applied, including reinforcement learning
116   (Gu et al., 2017; Arthur et al., 2021; Miao et al., 2021), dynamic programming (Miao et al., 2021;
117
      Liu et al., 2021), data augmentation Zhang et al. (2020); Deng et al. (2023), information transport
      theory (Zhang & Feng, 2022), and Hidden Markov Models (Zhang & Feng, 2023).
118
119   LLM-based SiMT The success of LLMs in offline NMT tasks has spurred interest in their ap-
120   plication to SiMT. Recent research has explored various approaches to leverage LLMs for SiMT.
121   However, the adaptive policies traditionally used in encoder-decoder architectures do not effectively
122   apply to LLMs. Some methods (Wang et al., 2023a; Koshkin et al., 2024a;b; Agostinelli et al., 2024)
123
      have focused on optimizing prompts in conjunction with a fixed wait-k policy. Moreover, Guo et al.
      (2024b) propose the Agent-SiMT framework, which incorporates a traditional adaptive SiMT model
124
      to assist in making read/write decisions. However, such methods face significant challenges, par-
125
      ticularly as the need to update prompts during inference prevents the reuse of historical KV caches,
126   resulting in inefficient recomputation and increased inference latency. The Agent-SiMT also com-
127   plicates the process by requiring the training of an additional traditional SiMT model. Recently,
128   some efforts (Sakai et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024) have utilized LLMs to generate SiMT data for
129   learning adaptive policy, yet challenges persist with inefficiencies in LLM-based SiMT systems.
130
      To improve translation efficiency, Wang et al. (2024) introduce the Conversational SimulMT frame-
131
      work, which employs a multi-turn dialogue decoding approach with generating SFT data by seg-
132   menting parallel sentences with an alignment tool. However, the tool can only output a fixed align-
133   ment configuration that is not easy for generalization. In addition, the method employs a fixed policy
134   during inference that reads a fixed number of words at each step, leading to a mismatch with the fine-
135   tuning process. Raffel et al. (2024) propose the SimulMask method to optimize the efficiency of the
136   prompt-based approaches, by introducing a policy-specific attention mask during fine-tuning. This
137   approach mimics the attention behavior during inference, restricting target token queries to only
138   attend to the corresponding part of the source sequence in the prompt. However, SimulMask may
139   not be suitable for adaptive policies because its complicated masking construction requires prior
140
      knowledge of the policy’s decision process.
141   In addition, these SiMT methods primarily focus on leveraging the translation capabilities of LLMs,
142   without exploring the adaptive read-write policies and generalization abilities. Unlike previous
143   methods, EAST enables the LLMs to learn adaptive read-write policies in various latency require-
144   ments, and utilizes an interleaved text structure to significantly improve the efficiency of the LLMs
145   during inference while maintaining consistency between fine-tuning and inference.

147   3     M ETHODS
148
149
      There are three main challenges in applying LLMs to SiMT. (i) The updated prefix source and target
150
      sentences increase computational costs and latency due to KV cache recomputation. (ii) Adaptive
151   read-write policies to support different latency requirements for LLM SiMT are still lacking, unless
152   a fixed wait-k policy is implemented. (iii) The generalization to multilingual language pairs may
153   require an additional SFT data. To address these challenges, we propose EAST, an Efficient and
154   Adaptive Simultaneous machine Translation method with LLMs. The EAST approach involves
155   three key components: the construction of SiMT data, the training of the LLMs on the SFT data,
156   and the inference with adaptive read-write policy.
157

196   3.2    T RAINING LLM S WITH S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING
197
198   To tackle the challenge proposed at the beginning of this section, we propose a two-stage SFT
199   training process on the two curated SiMT datasets.
200
201   Stage I: Activate SiMT of LLMs The objective of this stage is to teach the LLMs how to per-
202   form adaptive simultaneous translation by learning when to read and write in our designed for-
203   mat. To enable the model to learn these adaptive behaviors, we reorganized the aligned chunks in
204
      the SiMT data by interleaving between source and target chunks and introduce two special tokens
      (<|end-of-read|> and <|end-of-write|>), i.e.,
205
206                       [cx1 , <|eor|>, cy1 , <|eow|>, · · · , cxT , <|eor|>, cyT , <|eow|>]                              (2)
207
208   The special tokens act as explicit signals for the model to transition between reading and writing.
209
      The SiMT annotation process shows that each chunk contains enough semantic meaning for LLMs
      to carry out translations, ensuring that sequence reorganization does not lead to any loss of infor-
210
      mation for the model’s reading or writing decisions. Since the annotation process also encodes the
211
      degree of fragmentation into latency indicator tokens—“low”, “medium” or “high”, the SFT can
212   effectively guide the model to adapt to varying latency requirements. Figure 2 provides a compre-
213   hensive example of the SFT data.

      We train for one epoch during this stage on the larger SiMT-De-En-660K, employing full parameter
217   tuning. Since SiMT defined in our proposed format is generally a novel task for LLMs, full param-
218   eter tuning ensures that the LLM can effectively and successfully learn the auto-regressive SiMT.
219   Training for just one epoch helps mitigate the risk of overfitting during the full parameter tuning.
220
221
      Stage II: Generalize to Multilingual SiMT As the LLM acquires its auto-regressive SiMT capa-
222   bility in Stage I, its inherent multilingual proficiency enables it to generalize to multilingual SiMT,
223   even with limited SFT data. Consequently, we apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuning to a smaller
224   multilingual dataset of 90K instances including eight language directions. Additionally, during this
225   stage, we incorporate an offline NMT task to bolster the model’s ability to translate full sentences
226   and enhance overall translation performance. In fact, we can view offline translation as a specific
227   instance of SiMT by treating the entire sentence as a complete semantic chunk, e.g., x1:T = cx1 .
228
229   Loss In previous works on the SiMT or LLM-based SiMT models, loss calculation w.r.t. source
230   text is typically masked out, as it generally does not contribute to the training. However, in our case,
231   the cross-entropy loss is calculated on both the target text and the source text, as well as on special
232   tokens. The primary goal is to align with the auto-regressive design of interleaved sequences and
233   establish the appropriate reading and writing timing.

235   3.3   E FFICIENT I NFERENCE FOR A DAPTIVE S I MT
236
237   During inference, EAST performs auto-regressive token-by-token prediction aligned with the train-
238   ing process as shown in Figure 1(b). The process unfolds in two main phases:
239   Read-Predict-Discard During the read phase, the model sequentially receives source tokens and
240   predicts the next token. If the predicted token is not <|end-of-read|>, it is discarded, and the
241   next source token is appended to the current source chunk. Once <|end-of-read|> is predicted,
242   the model transitions to the translating phase. Note that the discarding operation in the read phase
243   does not violate the incremental appending of contexts, enabling EAST to efficiently utilize KV-
244   cache for faster generation.
245   Predict-Append Once the model enters the translation phase, it directly begins predicting the next
246   token. If the predicted token is not <|end-of-write|>, it is appended to the current target
247   chunk. When <|end-of-write|> is predicted, the model completes the current translation and
248   returns to the reading phase.
249
      Similar to the training phase, inference controls latency through indicator tokens—“low”,
250   “medium”, or “high”. Interestingly, an Interpolation Effect is observed, allowing for general-
251   ization to other latency levels using indicator tokens such as “low-medium” or “medium-high”.
252   Consequently, we can gather 3 to 5 observations to draw the BLEU-AL curve.

259   Datasets As introduced in the previous section, the primary SiMT SFT dataset to initiate novel task
260   learning is SiMT-De-En-660K, derived from 660K parallel pairs from the WMT15 De-En training
261
      dataset. For each pair, we utilize LLMs to generate SiMT chunk sequences at three different latency
      levels, as outlined in Eq. (1), while filtering out invalid examples. In addition, we construct a smaller
262
      multilingual SiMT SFT dataset, SiMT-Mult-90K, which includes 8 language directions: De↔En,
263
      Zh↔En, Ru↔En, and Cs↔En. For offline NMT training, we collect datasets from WMT17 to
264   WMT21, covering the same 8 translation directions, and refer to this collection as Off-Multi-120K.
265   As shown in Table 4 of Appendix, the sentence-level test data is extracted from WMT22 across the
266   same 8 translation directions as the offline NMT data. The majority of existing research primarily
267   focuses on sentence-level evaluation. However, in many real-world applications, such as speech
268   delivery, the input for SiMT often comes at the document level rather than isolated sentences. More-
269   over, LLMs have demonstrated impressive capabilities in long-form generation. Thus, we directly
      evaluate EAST on WMT22 document-level test data without additional fine-tuning.

      Evaluation Metrics For quality evaluation, we use automatic metric–SacreBLEU2 to compute the
271   corpus-level BLEU, along with neural evaluation metrics BLEURT3 (Sellam et al., 2020; Pu et al.,
272   2021) and COMET4 (Rei et al., 2020; 2022). For latency evaluation, we adopt Average Latency (AL)
273   (Ma et al., 2019), computational-aware Average Latency (AL-CA)5 , and Length-Adaptive Average
274   Lagging (LAAL) (Papi et al., 2022). In addition, we use Word Wall Time (WWT) Wang et al. (2024)
275   to evaluate the model’s decoding speed by calculating the actual inference time per word.

      Training Details We train our models using Llama-3-8B-Instruct Dubey et al. (2024) as the back-
277   bone, with full parameter tuning on Stage I and LoRA tuning on Stage II. All models are trained on
278   8 Nvidia A100 GPUs with a total batch size of 256, a learning rate of 1e-5, a cosine learning rate
279   scheduler, a warm-up ratio of 0.1 and a maximum sequence length of 1024. The number of epochs
280   is set to 1 for full parameter tuning and 2 for LoRA tuning, respectively. When using LoRA, the
281   LoRA rank, alpha, and dropout rate are set to 64, 128, and 0.05, respectively.

471
472   How many examples are needed to teach LLMs the novel SiMT task? In this section, we
473
      investigate the data size required for efficiently training LLMs on the novel SiMT task based on the
      SiMT-De-En-660K dataset. Figure 5 illustrates the changes in BLEU scores and AL values with
474
      data sizes for different latency settings–“low”, “medium”, and “high”. First, there is a significant
475
      improvement in BLEU score as the data size increases to approximately 10K. Beyond this point, the
476   rate of increase in BLEU score diminishes. Similarly, AL metrics also decrease notably as the data
477   size reaches around 10k, before stabilizing or showing minor fluctuations. This pattern is consistent
478   across all latency settings, indicating a general learning behavior of the model: rapid enhancements
479   in translation quality are achieved with the first 10K examples, followed by a phase where the model
480   focuses on refining its read/write policy across different latency levels, up to 100k examples.
481
      The results suggest that a relatively small dataset of just 10K SiMT examples may be sufficient to
482   achieve commendable translation quality. This finding aligns well with our multilingual dataset,
483   which contains approximately 10K examples per language, facilitating good performance across
484   different languages. Moreover, expanding the data size (up to 100K examples) can further optimize
485   the model’s read/write policy.

      5   C ONCLUSION
527
      In this paper, we introduce an Efficient and Adaptive Simultaneous Translation method using LLMs,
528
      EAST, designed to achieve high-quality SiMT with the efficiency of offline systems. By constructing
529
      SFT data, leveraging an interleaved token structure with explicit read-write signals and incorporating
530   latency-aware prompts, EAST enables LLMs to perform adaptive reading and translation based on
531   varying latency requirements. Our experimental results demonstrate that EAST not only achieves
532   state-of-the-art performance on SiMT benchmarks but also maintains high-quality translations in
533   offline settings. Additionally, EAST shows excellent generalization to document-level SiMT, high-
534   lighting its suitability for streaming translation in real-world scenarios. The model’s ability to reuse
535   the KV cache during inference further ensures computational efficiency, allowing it to match the
536   decoding speed of offline systems.

848   B.1               W HAT IS HALLUCINATION RATE OF THE LLM- BASED S I MT?
849
850   Hallucination is a significant challenge in traditional SiMT, as the models begin translating while
851   receiving input. This can prompt incorrect assumptions about the content yet to be received, result-
852   ing in hallucinated outputs. Additionally, hallucination is a common issue in the outputs of LLMs
853   across various generation tasks. Therefore, it is more essential to evaluate the hallucination phe-
854   nomenon in LLM-based SiMT. To effectively measure the hallucinations in our case, we utilize the
855
      hallucination rate (HR) metric (Chen et al., 2021), which quantifies the proportion of target words
      in the hypothesis that do not align with any source words. For this, we employ the fast-align6 tool
856
      to identify word-level alignments between the source text and the target translation.
857
858   Figure 7 illustrates the HR comparison on En↔De and En↔Zh test sets. EAST consistently demon-
859   strates a lower hallucination rate across all latency levels and test sets compared to the Llama3-
860   MNMT w/ wait-k. Unlike the wait-k policy, EAST can adaptively determine reading and writing
861
      actions based on the semantic context. This prevents the model from prematurely generating trans-
      lations, thereby reducing the production of hallucinated content and ensuring translations that are
862
      more accurate and faithful to the source text.
