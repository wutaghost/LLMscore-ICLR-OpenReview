011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            How can we ensure that Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) maintain strong
014            attention to visual input throughout the inference process? Recent advancements in
015            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated significant progress
016            across multiple domains. However, these models still face the inherent challenge
017
               of integrating vision and language for collaborative inference, which often leads
               to "hallucinations," outputs that are not grounded in the corresponding images.
018
               Many efforts have been made to address these challenges, but each approach comes
019
               with its own limitations, such as high computational costs or expensive dataset
020            annotation. Worse still, many of them fail to recognize the crucial role of visual
021            attention in guiding the model’s response generation. In our research, we identify
022            a key limitation in current LVLMs: the model’s diminishing attention to visual
023            input as the number of generated tokens increases, which results in performance
024            degradation. To address this challenge, we propose Image attention-guided Key-
025            value merging cOllaborative Decoding (IKOD), a collaborative decoding strategy
026            that generates image-focused sequences using key-value merging. This method
027            derives logits from shorter sequences with higher image attention through key-
028
               value merging and combines them with those from the original decoding process,
               effectively mitigating attention decay. Importantly, IKOD requires no additional
029
               training or external tools, making it highly scalable and applicable to various
030
               models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT, LLaMA, and Vicuna (Brown
036
      et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023) have profoundly impacted the development
037   of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), enabling significant progress accross various domains
038   like literature (Yang et al., 2024), agriculture (Zhu et al., 2024a), visual content generation (Zhu
039   et al., 2024b) and robotics (Ding et al.). However, LVLMs face inherent limitations in precisely
040   aligning vision and language modalities for collaborative inference. These shortcomings can lead
041   to LVLMs’ trustworthy problems like "hallucinations," where the model generates information not
042   grounded in the images. These problems have led to significant challenges in critical fields such as
043   finance (Kang & Liu, 2023) and medical diagnosis (Chen et al., 2024a), adversely impacting the
044   accuracy and safety of decision-making processes within these systems. Therefore, addressing this
045
      issue is crucial for enhancing the performance and reliability of LVLMs. Motivated by the concerns
      of misalignment between vision and language, various approaches have been proposed to address
046
      the issue of misalignment, including instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023; Lin
047
      et al., 2023), post-hoc techniques (Zhou et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023) and contrastive decoding (Leng
048   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). While these methods have demonstrated some
049   success, they often rely heavily on additional datasets, external tools, or computational resources. For
050   instance, post-hoc methods depend on external tools such as pre-trained vision-language models (Liu
051   et al., 2023b) and closed-source large models (Brown et al., 2020), which limits their potential for
052   widespread application and incurs high inference costs. Moreover, many of them are inspired by
053   methods designed specifically for single-modal language models, failing to recognize the crucial role
      of visual attention in guiding the model’s response generation.

054
      To address these challenges, we analyze the relationship between LVLM’s performance and its visual
055   attention. Our observations show key limitations in current LVLMs: as the number of generated
056   tokens increases, the model’s attention to the image gradually diminishes. Further experiments reveal
057   that this reduction in attention negatively impacts the model’s performance. Based on these findings,
058   we propose an Image attention-guided Key-value merging cOllaborative Decoding strategy (IKOD),
059   a collaborative decoding strategy that generates image-focused sequences while retaining most of
060   the essential information in the response. This approach involves obtaining logits with high image
061   attention from short sequences through compressing KV Cache and merging them with the logits
062   derived from the original decoding process, which can alleviate the decline in attention. Another
063
      advantage of our method is that it requires no additional training and does not rely on external tools.
064   Our primary contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We investigate the relationship between
065   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) performance and their visual attention, revealing that as the
066   sequence length increases, the model’s attention to the image diminishes. This diminishing attention
067   leads to performance degradation and errors in the generated responses. (2) We introduce IKOD,
068   an image attention-guided key-value merging collaborative decoding strategy. This method endows
069
      text sequence with high attention on image using key-value merging and integrates the augmented
      decoding process with the original decoding process to obtain a more accurate output distribution.
070
      (3) IKOD does not require additional training or external tools, which is more easily applicable to
071
      various models.

235   4     M ETHOD
236
237   While Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have made great progress in integrating visual and
238   textual modalities, they often face challenges in maintaining strong visual attention as sequence length
239   increases. To address these limitations, we propose an Image Attention-Guided key-value merging
240   strategy that selectively integrates key and value vectors based on their importance derived from
241
      image attention scores. We first propose the Image Attention-Guided Key-Value Merging Approach
      in Section 4.1. In Section 4.2, we introduce a collaborative decoding strategy to further enhance the
242
      cabilities of LVLMs. Finally, in Section 4.3, we present adaptive plausibility constraints to improve
243
      the model’s capacity for managing long-sequence image processing. The overall framework of our
244   method is illustrated in Figure 5.

      4.1   IMAGE ATTENTION - GUIDED KEY- VALUE MERGING
247
248   In this section, we propose a key-value merging strategy that prioritizes the integration of visual
249   features by selectively merging key and value vectors based on their importance determined by image
250   attention scores. The core idea is to identify anchor points in the key-value vectors that aggregate
251   surrounding contextual information. By recognizing the significance of visual attention in Large
252   Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), we can develop policies to predict which vectors in the key-value
253   storage will be most relevant for upcoming inference tasks. This approach helps reduce sequence
254   length and mitigates the problem of diminishing image attention.
255   Our method ensures that LVLM maintains a strong focus on crucial visual elements, thereby im-
256   proving the quality of generated tokens. During the key-value merging stage, this approach involves
257   two primary steps: 1) selecting important key-value anchors based on the layer-wise sum of image
258   attention scores, and 2) merging vectors based on the selected anchors.

336
337   Though collaborative decoding based on image attention enhance the LVLMs’ alignment, there
338   still exists a challenge. The logits of some implausible tokens may be unexpectedly enhanced.

469   Effect of Anchor Ratio λ. The anchor ratio λ is an important hyper-parameter reflecting the degree

      of KV Cache compression. A higher λ indicates more tokens are reserved and a lower degree of KV
      Cache compression, and λ = 1 implies the original generation procedure with full cache. We conduct

      an analysis on POPE-MSCOCO dataset to explore its effect. The results are depicted in Figure 6.
472

473   restricted, and λ = 0.4 is the best anchor ratio for both LLaVA-1.5 and InstructBLIP. The explanation
474   for this phenomenon could be summarized into two points: (1) Lower λ means less tokens are selected
475   as anchors, along with an excessive compressed KV Cache, resulting in a significant information loss
476   which is adverse to the next token generation. (2) Higher λ means the tokens with low attention on
477   image are not augmented enough by KV Cache compression. Based on the analysis, we set λ to 0.4
478   unless sepecially stated to get a better performance. More ablation studies and case studies can be
479   found in Appendix A.8 and Appendix A.9 respectively.

      OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) have fueled the development of Large Vision-Language Models

501   (LVLMs). These models effectively integrate large-scale pre-trained vision models into the LLMs’
502   representation space. LVLMs are generally classified into two main types: MLP-based models and
503   Q-former-based models. These models have demonstrated strong performance by combining LLMs
504   with image inputs, achieving notable success in image comprehension tasks. However, despite these
505   successes, LVLMs are not without flaws. They often encounter issues like "hallucinations," where

507   To address these challenges, recent studies have focused on methods such as instruction tuning (Lin
508   et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c), post-processing (Zhou et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023),
509   preference tuning (Yu et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024), and decoding strategies (Huang et al., 2023;
510   Chen et al., 2024b) to enhance the alignment between visual and textual information. However, these
511   approaches often come with significant drawbacks. Instruction tuning and preference tuning methods
512   require costly dataset annotation, introduce unintended biases, and demand extensive computational
513
      resources. Post-processing solutions correct hallucinated tokens in real-time, often relying on external
      tools like pre-trained vision-language models and stronger foundational models.

516   6.2   D ECODING S TRATEGIES FOR LVLM S
517
518   Decoding strategies are crucial for large models, as they determine how the model generates cor-
519   responding responses based on images and instructions. Additionally, they can enhance model
520   performance without the need for training. They play a pivotal role in shaping the output’s quality,
521   relevance, and coherence. Traditional strategies such as greedy decoding, nucleus sampling, beam
522   search, provide a variety of options for large models in terms of output diversity, reliability, and cer-
523
      tainty balance between randomness and relevance. Recently, decoding strategies for large foundation
      models have primarily concentrated on contrasting logits across different layers (Chuang et al., 2023),
524
      applying logit penalties (Huang et al., 2023), and employing contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2023;
525
      Chen et al., 2024b).

528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we investigate the impact of sequence length on image attention in Large Vision-
      Language Models (LVLMs), specifically focusing on how attention weakens as the sequence pro-
532
      gresses. Our analysis revealed a significant reduction in image attention towards the end of sequences,
533
      which correlates with a higher occurrence of hallucinated tokens and performance degradation in the
534   model. To address this issue, we introduce an image attention-guided Key-Value Merging strategy,
535   designed to enhance the model’s focus on visual elements by selectively merging key and value
536   vectors based on their attention scores. Furthermore, we propose a collaborative decoding method
537   named IKOD that combines the logits derived from the compressed KV Cache and original logits to
538   obtain a output distribution more grounded in image. Our experiments demonstrate that IKOD can not
539   only mitigate hallucinations in LVLMs but also enhance their comprehensive capacities, dismissing
      the need for additional training or external tools and making it applicable to various models.

775
776   POPE. The Polling-based Object Probing Evaluation (POPE) (Li et al., 2023b) is a widely-used
777   benchmark to assess object halucination in LVLMs, which contains 27,000 Yes/No questions in
778   three datasets: MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014), A-OKVQA (Schwenk et al., 2022), GQA (Hudson &
779   Manning, 2019). Each dataset has three nagative sample settings: random, popular, adversarial. It
780   adpots Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1 score as the evaluation metrics.
781   CHAIR. Caption Hallucination Assessment with Image Relevance (CHAIR) (Rohrbach et al., 2018)
782   is a popular method to evaluate object hallucination in image caption tasks. It compares generated
783   objects with grounde-truth objects to calculate the degree of hallucination. CHAIR evaluate object
784   hallucination from two dimensions: instance-level and sentence-level, denoted as CHAIRI and
785   CHAIRS respectively, which are computed as:
786                     |{hallucinated objects}|                  |{captions with hallucinated objects}|
787
          CHAIRI =                                  CHAIRS =
                       |{all mentioned objects}|                             |{all captions}|
788
789   VQAv2. VQAv2 (Goyal et al., 2017) balances the popular VQAdataset (Antol et al., 2015) by
790   collecting complementary images such that every question in the balanced dataset is associated with
791   a pair of similar images that result in two different answers to the question. It has approximately
792   twice the number of image-question pairs.
793   SQA. ScienceQA (SQA) (Lu et al., 2022) is a benchmark that consists of 21k multimodal mul-
794   tiple choice questions within the domain of science, along with annotations of their answers and
795   corresponding lectures and explanations.
796
      MME. Multimodal Large Language Model Evaluation (MME) (Fu et al., 2024) is a comprehensive
797   benchmark to assess the capabilities of LVLMs in multimodal tasks. It evaluates models with the
798   total score of Accuracy and Accuracy+ across two primary dimensions: perception and cognition,
799   containing 10 and 4 meticulously designed subtasks respectively.
800
      MMBench. MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c) is a meticulously curated dataset expanding the scope
801
      of evaluation questions and abilities. It introduces a rigorous CircularEval strategy which leverages
802
      large language models to convert free-form predictions into pre-defined choices, resulting in more
803   accurate evaluation results.
804
805   MM-Vet. MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023b) is an evaluation benchmark to assess the performance of LVLMs
806
      on complicated multimodal tasks, which focus on six core vision-language capabilities: recognition,
      knowledge, optical character recognition (OCR), spatial awareness, language generation, and math.
807
808   COCO Caption. The Microsoft COCO Caption dataset (Chen et al., 2015) contains over one and a
809   half million captions corresponding to more than 330,000 images. It used an evaluation server to
      score candidate captions using popular metrics, including BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE and CIDEr.

      A.4    OVERVIEW OF THE BASELINES
865
866
      LLaVA-1.5. LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024b) is an improvement based on LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024c). It
867
      modifies with a CLIP-ViT-L-336px visual backbone and MLP projection and incorporates academic-
868
      task-oriented VQA data with response formatting prompts, achieving state-of-the-art across 11
869   benchmarks at that time.
870
871   InstructBLIP. InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) utilizes an instruction-aware Query Transformer to
872
      extracts informative features tailored to the given instruction, demonstrating significant instruction
      following ability. It achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot performance across 13 datasets and also excels
873
      in some finetuned downstream tasks, like ScienceQA.
874
875   OPERA. OPERA (Huang et al., 2023) is a novel MLLM decoding method based on an Over-trust
876   Penalty and a Retrospection-Allocation strategy. It adds a penalty to the model logits to mitigate the
877   over-trust issue on summary token, along with a rollback strategy to correct the token selection.
878   VCD. Visual Contrastive Decoding (VCD) (Leng et al., 2024) calibrates model’s outputs through
879   contrasting output distributions derived from original and distorted visual inputs, thus reducing the the
880   over-reliance on statistical bias and unimodal priors, significantly mitigating the object hallucination
881   issue across different LVLMs.
882   HALC. HALC (Chen et al., 2024b) is a plug-and-play decoding algorithm to mitigate object hallucina-
883   tion in LVLMs. It operates on both local and global contexts, integrating a robust auto-focal grounding
884   mechanism to correct hallucinated tokens and a specialized beam search algorithm promoting further
885   visually matched generations.
886
      AGLA. AGLA (An et al., 2024) leverages an image-prompt matching scheme to get an augmented
887
      view of the input image where prompt-relevant content is reserved while others are masked. With
888   the augmented view, models can calibrate the output distribution by integrating generative global
889   features and discriminative local features.
890
891
      Silkie. Silkie (Li et al., 2023a) utilizes AI annotation to build a vision-language feedback (VLFeed-
      back) dataset. With preference distillation througth direct preference optimization (DPO) on it, Silkie
892
      achieves more comprehensive improvements compared to human-annotated preference datasets.
893
894   LLaVA-RLHF. LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023) introduces Reinforcement Learning from Human
895   Feedback (RLHF) from the text domain to the task of vision-language alignment. With the propsed
896   Factually Augmented RLHF, it augments the reward model with additional factual information and
897
      alleviates the reward hacking phenomenon in RLHF, resulting in a performance improvement.
898   RLHF-V. RLHF-V (Yu et al., 2024b) collects human preference on segment-level and performance
899   dense direct preference optimization on it, achieveing state-of-the-art performance in trustworthiness
900   among open-source LVLMs at that time.
