009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The computational complexity of the self-attention mechanism in popular trans-
012            former architectures poses significant challenges for training and inference, and
013            becomes the bottleneck for long inputs. Is it possible to significantly reduce the
014            quadratic time complexity of computing the gradients in multi-layer transformer
015            models? This paper proves that a novel fast approximation method can calculate
016            the gradients in almost linear time n1+o(1) where n is the input sequence length,
017            while it maintains a polynomially small approximation error 1/poly(n) across the
018            entire model. Our theory holds for general loss functions and when the multi-layer
019            transformer model contains many practical sub-modules, such as residual connec-
020            tion, casual mask, and multi-head attention. By improving the efficiency of gradi-
021
               ent computation, we hope that this work will facilitate more effective training and
               deployment of long-context language models based on our theoretical results.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT (Schulman et al., 2022), GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
028   2023), Claude 3.5 (Anthropic, 2024), Llama 3.1 (Llama Team, 2024), and others, have demon-
029   strated immense potential to enhance various aspects of our daily lives, e.g., conversation AI (Liu
030   et al., 2024), AI agent (Xi et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c), search AI (OpenAI, 2024), AI assis-
031   tant (Mahmood et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) and many so on. One of the most emergent abilities
032   of LLMs is dealing with long-context information, a format that is crucial for recording material
033
      like academic papers, official reports, legal documents, and so on. LLMs have proven adept at tack-
      ling long-context tasks, including Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao
034
      et al., 2023d), zero-shot summarization (Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024c), and maintaining very
035
      long-term conversations (Xu et al., 2021b; 2022), and so on. This proficiency has necessitated the
036   development of long-context modeling capabilities within LLMs.
037
038   The self-attention mechanism is crucial for the success of LLMs, since LLMs are mainly based on
039
      Transformer architecture whose key module is attention. In attention computation, we will compute
      the attention score between each pair of tokens, which is the complexity bottleneck during long
040
      context training and inference. In detail, we need to spend O(n2 d) running time for each self-
041
      attention block, which is quadratic in n, where n is the length of the context input and d is the
042   hidden feature dimension of the model. For example, LLaMA 3.1 405B (Llama Team, 2024), one
043   of the cutting-edge LLMs, supports n =128k and d = 4096, while taking 30.84M GPU training
044   hours, which underscores the need for more efficient training processes for such extensive context
045   models. Given the extensive context lengths of LLMs, this quadratic time complexity results in
046   critical challenges: (i) a marked decrease in training efficiency (He et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2023); and
047   (ii) significant energy usage, which in turn contributes to higher carbon dioxide emissions (Samsi
048   et al., 2023; Stojkovic et al., 2024).
049   One seminal work (Alman & Song, 2023) showed that the self-attention inference can be approxi-
050   mated in almost linear time. However, this result is for the inference time (forward pass), but does
051   not address the main challenge, which is the expensive computation in the training time (backward
052   pass). In this work, we address this main challenge, by proving that the gradient computation in the
053   back-propagation of self-attention can be approximated in almost linear time. This suggests we may
      be able to save the substantial resources required for training LLMs.

055
056   We first introduce some basic background, starting with defining the softmax function and the self-
057   attention module.

073   In contemporary LLMs, the architecture typically incorporates multiple layers of attention. Conse-
074   quently, in order to design a fast training algorithm for the entire model, it is imperative to examine
075   self-attention within the multi-layer transformer structure formally defined as follows.
076   Definition 1.3 (Multi-layer transformer). Let m denote the number of transformer layers in the
077   model. Let X be the input sequence. Let gi denote components other than self-attention in the i-th
078   transformer layer, and assume its forward and backward computations can be run in time linear in
079   its input sequence length. Let Attni denote the self-attention module in the i-th transformer layer
080   with weights WQi , WKi , WVi (see also Definition 1.2). We define an m-layer transformer as
081                 Fm (X) := gm ◦ Attnm ◦ gm−1 ◦ Attnm−1 ◦ · · · ◦ g1 ◦ Attn1 ◦ g0 (X),
082
083
      where ◦ denotes function composition.
084   In Definition 1.3, the gi includes the layer norm, MLP, residual connection, dropout, positional
085   encoding, multi-head concatenation, and other operations. All forward and backward computations
086   of these practical modules can be run in linear time with respect to n. Thus, in this work, we
087   mainly focus on the acceleration of self-attention module. Specifically, as shown in Definition 1.2,
088   the n × n attention matrix f (X) dominates the computational complexity, introducing a quadratic
089   bottleneck. In the exact computation case, if the attention matrix is full rank, no acceleration is
090   possible. However, by compromising negligible accuracy, designing a fast sub-quadratic algorithm
091   becomes feasible. Fortunately, by employing the polynomial kernel approximation method from
092
      Aggarwal & Alman (2022), we can approximate the attention matrix and achieve an almost linear
093
      time n1+o(1) algorithm, effectively breaking the quadratic bottleneck.

138   Long-context modeling in LLMs. As LLMs grow in size and capability, in-context learning
139
      (ICL) (Min et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a) has become a pre-
      ferred method for directing these models to perform a variety of tasks, as opposed to the resource-
140
      intensive process of fine-tuning. Nonetheless, research has indicated that longer prompts can im-
141
      pair LLMs performance due to the limitation on maximum sequence length during pre-training (Li
142   et al., 2024b). Consequently, extending the maximum sequence length during pre-training and fine-
143   tuning stages is imperative. Enhancing training efficiency is crucial given the prevalent use of the
144   Transformer architecture in LLMs, which incurs a quadratic computational cost relative to sequence
145   length. Addressing this challenge, some studies have explored continued fine-tuning of LLMs with
146   extended context lengths (Tworkowski et al., 2024), while others have experimented with the in-
147   terpolation and extrapolation capabilities of positional embedding (Chen et al., 2023). Shi et al.
148   (2024a) handles long context by compressing the input tokens. However, these approaches have not
149   fundamentally addressed the core issue: the quadratic computational cost associated with sequence
150
      length in the attention mechanism (Keles et al., 2023; Fournier et al., 2023). In this study, we delve
      into accelerating the attention mechanism, thereby addressing the long-context modeling issue at its
151
      essence.
152
153
      Attention acceleration. Attention mechanism has faced criticism due to its quadratic time com-
154
      plexity with respect to context length, a concern exacerbated by the increasing length in modern
155
      large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude 3.5 (Anthropic, 2024),
156   Llama 3.1 (Touvron et al., 2023; Llama Team, 2024), etc. Nevertheless, this limitation can be cir-
157   cumvented by employing polynomial kernel approximation techniques (Aggarwal & Alman, 2022),
158   which enable the derivation of a low-rank representation of the attention matrix. This innovation
159   significantly accelerates both the training and inference processes of a single attention layer, achiev-
160   ing almost linear time complexity (Alman & Song, 2023; 2024a), while our work supports both
161   training and inference for any multi-layer transformer. The foundational concept underpinning the
      work of Alman & Song (2023; 2024a) is the extension of the notion that polynomials can effectively


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      approximate exponential functions to the domain of matrices. Given that each entry of the attention
163   matrix is activated by a softmax function, the author of Alman & Song (2023) proposed the use
164   of a polynomial matrix to approximate the softmax-activated attention matrix. Additionally, they
165   demonstrated that this polynomial matrix can be factorized into the product of two low-rank ma-
166   trices. By strategically reordering the sequence of matrix multiplications, these low-rank matrices
167   are employed to diminish the computational complexity of the attention mechanism’s forward pass
168   to almost linear time. For more details, please refer to Section 3 in Alman & Song (2023). Fur-
169   thermore, this approach can be extended to higher-order attention mechanisms, i.e., tensor attention
170   (Alman & Song, 2024b; Liang et al., 2024h). Moreover, there are other theoretical approaches. For
171
      instance, Liang et al. (2024a) introduces the conv-basis method to accelerate attention computation.
      Han et al. (2024) proposes a near-linear time algorithm under the assumptions of uniform softmax
172
      column norms and sparsity.
173

175   definitions across the whole paper. Section 4 presents our primary findings, where we articulate our
176   novel algorithm that is capable of calculating gradients across the entire model in almost linear time.
177
      In Section 5, we explain the techniques we employ, including low-rank approximation, techniques
      for accelerating the computation of gradients, and an analysis of the approximation error. Section 6
178
      provides various extensions of our algorithm. Lastly, we conclude this paper in Section 7.

      4.2   FAST COMPUTING FOR MULTI - LAYER TRANSFORMERS
325
326   Based on the results demonstrated in previous sections, we are ready to introduce our main result:
327   the gradients of the whole transformer model can be approximated in almost linear time.
328
      Theorem 4.2 (Main result, formal version of Theorem 1.4). Let m denote the number of transformer
329   layers. Assume the number of layers m is constant. We assume d = O(log n) and each number in
330   matrices can be written using O(log n) bits. We can show that, for any i ∈ [m], all the gradient
331   components (see also Lemma 3.4) of the i-th layer can be computed by Algorithm 1 in almost linear
332   time n1+o(1) , and the approximation error of the entire m layer transformer model can be bounded
333   by 1/ poly(n).
334
335   Proof. We prove the theorem by directly combining Theorem 4.1 and Lemma 5.5.
336
337
      Theorem 4.2 demonstrates that, during the training of a multi-layer transformer model, at each
338
      training iteration, the gradient computation for the weight matrices of each layer can be performed
339
      in almost linear time n1+o(1) . This result supports the feasibility of fast training for any transformer-
340   based large language models (LLMs). Algorithm 1 highlights the significance of the gradient with
341   respect to the intermediate variables Ti (X). Due to the application of the chain rule in gradient
342   computation, the gradient of Ti (X) is indispensable for determining the gradients of the weight
343   matrices WQi , WKi and WVi at the i-th layer. Consequently, by iteratively computing the gradient
344   for Ti (X), we systematically propagate the gradient through to the initial transformer layer. The
345   rate of error accumulation in a transformer with m layers grows exponentially as nm . Namely, the
346   error increases from 1/ poly(n) to nm / poly(n). Nevertheless, because m is a constant and the
347   polynomial poly(n) has a high degree, the total error remains insignificant in practical scenarios.

      (2023) and Alman & Song (2024a). In Alman & Song (2023), the authors proposed an almost linear
352
      time algorithm for computing the forward process of the attention mechanism. In contrast, Alman &
353
      Song (2024a) introduced an almost linear time algorithm for the backward of attention mechanism.
354   However, Alman & Song (2024a) has the following limitations: (i) only computing gradients for
355   a single layer of the attention mechanism, which cannot extend to multiple layers; (ii) calculating
356   gradients with respect to a specific loss, namely the ℓ2 loss; (ii) computing gradients only for the
357   weight matrix WQi , WKi (as defined in Definition 1.2), but ignore other crucial components such as
358   the MLP layer following attention computation and the activation function.
359
      In our work, we have the following improvements beyond previous work: (i) we enable almost linear
360   time gradient computation across an entire transformer layer, incorporating both the MLP layer and
361   the activation function; (ii) our algorithm supports gradient calculation for general loss function
362   L(X) (see Definition 3.1); (ii) we extend the gradient calculation to include not only WQi , WKi but
363   also Ti (X) and WVi . These advancements collectively demonstrate a substantial leap forward from
364   the methodologies in Alman & Song (2023) and Alman & Song (2024a).

      5.1   L OW- RANK APPROXIMATION FOR ATTENTION MATRIX
369
370   In this section, we delve into the crucial techniques behind our work: the low-rank approxima-
371   tion of the attention matrix, which is achieved through the polynomial method (Alman et al., 2020;
372   Aggarwal & Alman, 2022). Drawing inspiration from Alman & Song (2023), the intuition of this
373   approximation lies in the fact that the attention matrix f (X) ∈ Rn×n (as defined in Definition 1.2),
374   also referred to as the similarity matrix in attention mechanism, can be effectively approximated by
375   low-rank matrices U1 , V1 ∈ Rn×k1 , where k1 = no(1) . The naive method for calculating the atten-
376   tion matrix f (X) has a time complexity of O(n2 ), whereas the input data X ∈ Rn×d contains only
377   d · n = n1+o(1) entries. This discrepancy suggests the potential of using low-rank representations
      of f (X) to design a fast algorithm.

      6   E XTENSIONS
504
505
      Multi-head attention and residual connections. Multi-head attention and residual connections
      are important components in attention mechanisms. These components were not involved in our ini-
506
      tial analysis for simplicity. Incorporating them into our algorithm is straightforward. This suggests
507
      that our algorithm can be readily adapted to more practical transformer models. The detailed analy-
508   sis for incorporating residual connection can be found in Section J and Lemma J.3. For the synergy
509   with multi-head attention, we provide comprehensive analysis in Section K and Lemma K.2.
510
511
      Causal attention mask. The causal attention mask is critical to prevent transformers from “cheat-
512   ing” during training by ensuring future information is not used. The full-rank characteristic of the
513   causal attention mask poses challenges for low-rank approximations. Nevertheless, we have iden-
514   tified a method to accelerate the computation of causal masked attention by exploiting its inherent
515   properties, showing almost linear time complexity. A comprehensive explanation is provided in
516   Section B.3. More detailed analysis can be found in Section I and Lemma I.7 and I.8.
517
518   Prompt tuning. Prompt tuning is a prevalent approach in parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT),
519   which requires the calculation of gradients on input data X. Given our algorithm can compute
520   gradients for intermediate variables Ti in almost linear time, we can adapt this acceleration to the
521   gradient for the input data X, thus enhancing the efficiency of the prompt tuning process. Additional
522   details are provided in Section B.5.
523
524   Synergy with system-level attention acceleration. Many contemporary works focus on system-
525   level acceleration of attention mechanisms, often by leveraging caching and mitigating I/O bottle-
526   necks. Our algorithm has the potential to integrate with such advancements. By combining our
527   theoretical improvements in computation time (from O(n2 ) to n1+o(1) ) with system-level optimiza-
528   tions, the overall efficiency of attention mechanism computation may improve further. We leave the
529
      implementation of our method on GPU as future work. More details can be found in Section B.4.

531   7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      The attention mechanism in transformer models has quadratic time complexity with respect to the
534   input token length. In this work, we proposed a novel Algorithm 1, which can approximately train a
535   multi-layer transformer model in almost linear time, introducing only a small error. Importantly, our
536   algorithm is designed to be compatible with general loss functions, practical sub-modules (residual
537   connection, casual mask, multi-head attention), and general gradient-based algorithms. It may be
538   seamlessly integrated with other system-level acceleration techniques. While we lack enterprise-
539   scale computational resources for training large language models to provide empirical support, our
      theoretical findings suggest that we can accelerate the training of LLMs in practice.

1153   B.1   M ULTI - HEAD ATTENTION
1154
1155   The multi-head attention mechanism was first introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017). This innovation
1156   allows a token to simultaneously attend to multiple positions within the same layer, thereby enriching
1157   the model’s capacity for capturing various dependencies. However, this enhanced capability comes
1158   with an increase in the size of the attention matrix f (X) from 1 × n × n to h × n × n, where
1159   h is the number of attention heads. To mitigate the computational burden, each head’s vector is
1160
       derived by splitting the original vector, reducing the dimensionality of each head to dh := d/h. To
       summarize, the key distinctions between multi-head and single-head attention are (1) an enlarged
1161
       attention matrix f (X) and (2) a reduced dimensionality dh within each attention head.
1162
1163
       Enlarged attention matrix. As previously discussed, the attention matrix’s dimensionality in-
1164
       creases with the number of heads, h. Despite this expansion, the application of the low-rank approx-
1165
       imation technique, as outlined in Section 5.1, ensures that the computation time for the attention
1166   matrix remains almost linear. Specifically, for a constant number of heads h in the multi-head mech-
1167   anism, the time complexity for computing f (X) ∈ Rh×n×n is h · n1+o(1) = n1+o(1) .
1168
1169
       Reduced dimensionality. Another differentiating factor of multi-head attention is the lower di-
1170
       mensionality processed by each head, i.e. dh := d/h, compared the full d in single-head attention.
1171   This reduction ensures that the gradient computation time does not increase with the introduction of
1172   multiple attention heads.
1173
1174
       We provide comprehensive analysis of the synergy of our algorithm with multi-head attention in
       Section K. We first prove in Lemma K.2, with the addition of multi-head attention, the gradient over
1175
       the attention mechanism can be computed in almost linear time. Then, we further prove that for any
1176
       multi-layer transformer, with multi-head attention, the gradient can be computed in almost linear
1177   time as well.

       B.3   C AUSAL ATTENTION MASK
1196
1197   In transformer training, attention mask is a crucial component, designed to prevent a given token
1198   from attending to future tokens in the sequence. Causal attention mask is a widely used attention
1199   mask, which is configured as a lower triangular matrix, where elements on or below the main diag-
1200   onal are ones, with all other entries being zeros.
1201
       Now we describe how to incorporate this into our algorithm. Let M ∈ {0, 1}n×n represent the
1202
       causal attention mask (see Definition I.2). Let fb(X) := D−1 (M ⊙ A) where A = exp(XW X ⊤ /d)
1203
       and D := diag((M ⊙ A) · 1n ). Lemma I.1 reveals that A has a low-rank representation given by
1204
       U0 V0⊤ . Using Lemma I.3, we know (M ⊙ (U0 V0⊤ )) · v for any vector v ∈ Rn can be computed in
1205   almost linear time.
1206
1207   To integrate the causal mask into the gradient computation within each transformer layer, we first
1208
       find all instances that have the structure of f (X) · H or (f (X) ⊙ (U V ⊤ )) · H, where H, U, V are
1209   low rank matrices. Then, we replace f (X) with fb(X) in these instances. More detailed analysis of
1210
       causal attention can be found in Section I. To be more specific, we group the gradient components for
       Ti , Wi , WVi into two categories, one for dot product (Lemma I.7), another for Hadamard product
1211
       (Lemma I.8). After showing each component can be calculated in almost linear time, the overall
1212
       gradient computation remains n1+o(1) time. Thus, our framework can seamlessly accommodate
1213
       causal attention masks.

       B.4   S YSTEM - LEVEL ATTENTION ACCELERATION
1216
1217   The attention computing acceleration involves a two-pronged strategy that leverages both system-
1218   level improvements (e.g. Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024)) and the
1219   theoretical time complexity improvements (e.g. our work and Han et al. (2024)).
1220
       Numerous efforts have been made in the literature to accelerate attention calculations at the sys-
1221
       tem level. For instance, Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024) targets
1222   the I/O bottleneck inherent in attention mechanisms. Studies such as block-wise parallel decod-
1223   ing (Stern et al., 2018) focus on implementing parallel decoding within transformer models to en-
1224   hance inference speed. Additionally, recent advancements in the field of speculative decoding, such
1225   as Medusa (Cai et al., 2024), leverage a smaller, more efficient model to generate predictions, with
1226   the larger model only responsible for validating, the smaller model’s outputs (Leviathan et al., 2023).
1227
       Despite these innovations, the aforementioned methods do not address the fundamental quadratic
1228   time complexity O(n2 ) of the attention mechanisms. This presents an opportunity to complement
1229   our low-rank approximation technique, with these system-level optimizations, thereby achieving
1230   an even greater acceleration in attention computation. For instance, we could design an I/O-aware
1231   algorithm for Algorithm 1, similar to the approach taken by Flash Attention, to effectively leverage
1232   GPU acceleration.
1233
       To implement our algorithm practically on GPU, we have some coding challenges to fix: (1) we
1234   need to define some new tensor operations in PyTorch, e.g. Eq. (5), Eq. (8); (2) we need to sys-
1235   tematically re-implement some back-propagation function of the current PyTorch function; (3) we
1236   need to implement some CUDA function to run our algorithm in parallel for the casual mask, see
1237   discussion in Section B.3. We may leave this as our future work.
