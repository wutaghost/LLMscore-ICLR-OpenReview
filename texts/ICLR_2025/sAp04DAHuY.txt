               During prediction tasks, models can use any signal they receive to come up with
012            the final answer - including signals that are causally irrelevant. When predicting
013            objects from images, for example, the lighting conditions could be correlated to
014            different targets through selection bias, and an oblivious model might use these
015            signals as shortcuts to discern between various objects. A predictor that uses
016            lighting conditions instead of real object-specific details is obviously undesirable.
017            To address this challenge, we introduce a standard anti-causal prediction model
018            (SAM) that creates a causal framework for analyzing the information pathways
019            influencing our predictor in anti-causal settings. We demonstrate that a classifier
020
               satisfying a specific conditional independence criterion will focus solely on the
               direct causal path from label to image, being counterfactually invariant to the
021
               remaining variables. Finally, we propose DISCO, a novel regularization strategy
022
               that uses conditional distance correlation to optimize for conditional independence
023            in regression tasks. We can show that DISCO achieves competitive results in
024            different bias mitigation experiments, deeming it a valid alternative to classical
025            kernel-based methods.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep neural networks can achieve compelling results in complex prediction tasks by learning
031   representations from raw input data LeCun et al. (2015). However, parts of the input data might be
032   unstable, biased, and unreliable. Consider the classification of images into cats and dogs. Suppose
033
      most cat images are captured outdoors, whereas dog images are mainly taken indoors. A naive model
      might capture the background as a salient feature, as it presents a strong signal across the training
034
      dataset. Thus, rather than learning to discriminate between dogs and cats based on their intrinsic
035
      characteristics, the model might erroneously prioritize background cues, rendering these models
036   unreliable. Preventing models from picking up such unreliable or undesirable cues has been discussed
037   in the literature from different perspectives:
038
039
      Domain Generalization (DG) asks if we can make a prediction model reliable in changing domains
      and data distributions. In our cats and dogs example, one might ask how much our trained model
040
      will deteriorate if employed in a new domain where cats and dogs are randomly placed outside and
041
      inside. Arjovsky et al. (2019) and Sagawa et al. (2019) deal with samples from different domains
042   with different data distributions and propose methods to make a model reliable in all of them. Instead
043   of only having data from different domains, some approaches explicitly model distribution changes in
044   certain attributes and assume one has direct access to these variables while making a model robust to
045   such changes (Makar et al., 2022; Makar & D’Amour, 2022; Zheng & Makar, 2022; Jiang & Veitch,
046   2022; Wang & Veitch, 2022; Pogodin et al., 2022; Mouli & Ribeiro, 2022; Mahajan et al., 2021;
047   Eastwood et al., 2022; Heinze-Deml & Meinshausen, 2021). Lastly, as a an attempt to unify DG
048   methods, Kaur et al. (2022) model the domains/environments and additional bias attributes, providing
049   causal graphs for relevant settings in many DG tasks.
050   Fairness attempts to avoid the use of unfair information in arbitrary machine-learning tasks (Mehrabi
051   et al., 2021; Barocas et al., 2023). In our example, we may consider it unfair that indoor pictures are
052   classified as dogs, independent of the photographed animal. A more common fairness scenario is to
053   analyze the potential discrimination of hiring practices with respect to protected attributes (sex and
      race) by computing observational criteria (e.g., demographic parity, equal opportunity) (Barocas et al.,

078   Causal Inference is the study of cause-effect relationships between variables and events (Pearl, 2009).
079   It provides us a language to describe statistics beyond observations and correlations and helps us
080   in estimating causal effects. Furthermore, with varying complexity and depth (e.g. allowing for
081   temporal changes, heterogeneous effects, ...), causal inference usually assumes the existence of stable
082   causal mechanisms. Returning to our cat and dog example, we can categorize it as an anti-causal
083
      prediction task (Schölkopf et al., 2012), since the labels cause the input image as illustrated in Fig. 1.
      In general, we assume that a dog always looks like a dog and there is a stable mechanism that
084
      makes it discernible from other animals, independent of varying lighting conditions, the animal’s
085
      surroundings, or the camera lens used to capture the image. We can use this kind of causal language
086   and analysis to make a model rely solely on task-relevant features instead of being fooled by spurious
087   or environment-specific attributes. For example, Veitch et al. (2021); Quinzan et al. (2022), model
088   the prediction task as a causal graph and define counterfactual invariance as a predictor’s stability
089   to counterfactual changes of distracting attributes. Given a single instance of a dog (Veitch et al.,
090   2021) (or a set of dogs (Quinzan et al., 2022)), they ask whether the model’s prediction changed if
091   the background was replaced with another one.
092   We see increased adaptation of causal techniques in fairness and DG and propose to unify different
093   views from before in the general anti-causal prediction setting. While (Veitch et al., 2021; Quinzan
094   et al., 2022) did solid pivotal work by coining terms such as counterfactual invariance and arriving
095   at useful observational criteria to achieve invariance, they used different causal settings binding
096   their statements to concrete graphical setups. Especially their final statements in invariance criteria
097   had significant discrepancies and their counterfactual statements where on different levels. We
098   want to provide a unifying approach by employing a more general and flexible graph, and treat our
099   counterfactual statements very explicitly.

      First, we introduce the required terminology and theory. We further extend the standard fairness
121   model (Zhang & Bareinboim, 2018; Plecko & Bareinboim, 2022) and allow any spurious correlation
122   connecting Y and X in the anti-causal prediction setting, thus creating the Standard Anti-Causal
123   Model (SAM). Then, we show that a predictor, which satisfies the conditional independence Ŷ ⊥
124   W, Z | Y , will only have a direct effect from Y to Ŷ , while the counterfactual indirect and spurious
125   effects will be zero, and the direct effect will be counterfactually constant on all observed Z and W.

      Structural Causal Model (SCM). SCMs are quadruples (V, F, U, PU ) where U are the exoge-
128
      nous variables. Their distribution PU is defined outside of the model. V are the random variables
129   in the model. They are composed of other variables from V and get their uncertainty “injected”

131   pa(vi ) ⊆ V are the parents of vi , and ui ⊆ U are the exogenous variables influencing vi .

      We assume a one-to-one correspondence between nodes in a causal graph and random variables. We,
133
      therefore, use capital letters, such as X for random variables, and lowercase x to indicate values that
134
      X can attain in its respective range. If not specified otherwise, bold letters X will denote random

146   Standard Anti-Causal Model (SAM) Fig. 1a shows the graph for the Standard Anti-Causal Model,
147   which is a generic causal graph for many anti-causal prediction tasks. X is the input random variable
148   for a prediction task, Y is the outcome, and Ŷ is the prediction made by a fixed model. Z is a group
149   of nodes that lie on alternative open paths between Y and X and are non-causal, and Z is a valid
150   adjustment set for (Y, X) A.1. We denote this by using Y –Z–X and point to appendix A for a more
151   detailed view. We especially allow any connections between elements in Z as long as it stays a valid
152   adjustment set. W are (intermediate or indirect) variables that lie on causal-paths between Y and
153   X. For the sake of simplicity, we assume there are no arrows between elements of W. Furthermore,
154   we assume that our task’s only stable relevant information flows directly from Y to X, rendering
155   the nodes in W, Z distracting/undesired. Relaxation and extension of these assumptions will be
156
      discussed in Sec.B.4.
157   If we trained a model oblivious to the SAM using classical empirical risk minimization (ERM), the
158   predictor would capture all information to make the best possible prediction. This would especially
159   mean that our model would rely on all paths leading from Y to X, thereby learning biased effects
160   irrelevant to the task. Since we do not want to generatively model the entire prediction task, we focus
161   on the effect of Y on the predictions Ŷ of a model and define the path Y → X → Ŷ as the direct
      path.

      3    DISCO: C ONDITIONAL I NDEPENDENCE W ITH D ISTANCE C ORRELATION

219   In the previous section, we showed that a predictor gθ utilizes only the direct path of information
220   from Y to X if we can ensure the conditional independence: Ŷ ⊥ W, Z | Y . Directly solving Eq.(6)
221   is generally impractical, as finding closed-form solutions is not trivial and generally not possible for
222
      black-box methods such as neural networks. Thus we relax the problem into a regularized problem
223   and optimize the model with minθ E(X,Y ) [L (Y, gθ (X))] + λReg(Ŷ , Z, W, Y ). We refer to the
224
      variables with undesired effects Z, W as bias variables in the following for simplification.
225
226   Related Work on Conditional Independence Regularization Recent research increasingly fo-
227   cuses on achieving conditional independence by a regularization penalty for neural networks. Max-
228   imum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and its conditional variants (c-MMD) are used by Makar et al.
229   (2022); Veitch et al. (2021); Makar & D’Amour (2022); Kaur et al. (2022) and are shown to be effec-
230   tive in handling binary and categorical attributes in classification settings. However, these methods
231   exhibit inefficiencies as they necessitate dividing batches into sub-samples (or strata) according to
232   all combinations of label values (e.g., y0 , y1 ) and bias attributes (e.g., a1 , a2 , a3 ). This approach is
233
      particularly cumbersome for scenarios with multi-bias, multi-class, or biases of many categories. In
      contrast, methods built on the Hilbert-Schmidt-Criterion (HSIC) Gretton et al. (2005; 2007), and its
234
      conditional variant Fukumizu et al. (2007); Zhang et al. (2012); Park & Muandet (2020) (HSCIC)
235
      were proposed to achieve the conditional independence criterion in regression and classification
236   tasks. Zheng & Makar (2022) extended HSIC to classification tasks with multiple, mixed-valued
237   biases, and Quinzan et al. (2022) adapted HSCIC with conditional independence regularization for
238   regression tasks, relying on multiple ridge regressions. Lastly, Pogodin et al. (2022) proposed CIRCE,
239   a modified variant estimating a notion similar to HSCIC. The method reduces the number of in-batch
240   regressions via the estimation of hyperparameters on a split of the training set.
241
242
      Current Challenges We conclude that most causally inspired deep learning methods were proposed
243
      for classification. To this end, we focus on regression and observe that existing methods like HSCIC
244
      and CIRCE introduce a significant number of additional hyperparameters: A regularization strength λ,
245   three bandwidth parameters for the (Gaussian) kernels, and a ridge regression parameter.
246
247
248   Our Contribution To overcome the limitations of current conditional independence regularization
249   techniques, we propose to regularize the training of deep learning models with Conditional Distance
250   Correlation (cdCor) Wang et al. (2015); Póczos & Schneider (2012), which is a measure of condi-
251
      tional dependence in arbitrary dimensions. The general idea is to bring the cdCor as close to zero as
      possible since cdCor(X, Y ; Z) = 0 ⇐⇒ X ⊥ Y | Z. cdCor is a relatively uncommon measure,
252
      primarily employed in biomedical settings for feature screening and related applications (Wang et al.,
253
      2015; Song et al., 2020). To our knowledge, this is the first instance of its use in a deep learning
254   context. In the following subsections, we derive a method for estimating it batch-wise to suit our
255   purposes.

      5    D ISCUSSION
487
488
      We proposed a unified description of the anti-causal prediction setting called SAM. Utilizing this
489
      causal analysis model, we derived that the conditional independence criterion Ŷ ⊥ W, Z | Y is
490
      sufficient to provide counterfactual invariance in anti-causal scenarios. In our case, we concluded
491   that if one wanted to remove all effects from W and Z, there is no need to distinguish between these
492   variables conceptually.
493
494
      At a first glance, distinguishing between variables W and Z might seem unnecessary as they are
      treated equally in the conditional independence term. However, if we look at the causal literature,
495
      we clearly see that this kind of pathway analysis is a useful and well-studied tool (Pearl, 2022;
496
      Shpitser & VanderWeele, 2011; Plecko & Bareinboim, 2022). It is especially natural to separate the
497   directionalities of causal flows: the flow of information can be direct, mediated, or entirely spurious.
498   The nodes in W have a completely different meaning than the ones in Z. We, therefore, see the result
499   of the simple conditional independence as something particularly handy, as variables in both sets
500   can be treated equally under certain assumptions. Lastly, although we kept our experimental setups
501   straightforward, one can create situations where mediated effects in W can be desirable. How to
502   proceed from there is discussed in the appendix B.4 where we assume we want to retain some of the
503   mediated effects.
504   We believe this causal framework is useful for understanding different setups in bias mitigation,
505   domain generalization, and similar. This obviously requires making causal assumptions, but we see
506   it as a chance to be precise and more scientific rather than perceiving it as a burden. Future efforts
507   can be put into how one can actually quantify and estimate the causal effects on the decisions using
508   pathway analysis. One can, for example, theoretically estimate the causal effects of different variables
509   on the final decision, leading to useful measures of invariance or dependence on undesired paths.
510   Moreover, we proposed optimizing the conditional independence criterion by utilizing the conditional
511   distance correlation metric. Given our experimental results, we conclude that DISCO is competitive
512   and slightly better than the competing models on certain datasets. DISCO, however, also has its
513   downsides that need to be addressed. While HSCIC and CIRCE perform kernel (ridge) regressions
514   that cost O(b2 ) given a batch size b, DISCO suffers from overhead as it has a compute and memory
515   requirement of O(b3 ). The difference is noticeable depending on the batch sizes one operates on, and
516   we accept this as a drawback of our method.
517   While DISCO incurs a higher computational cost of O(b3 ) compared to the O(b2 ) of HSCIC and
518   CIRCE for a batch size b, it offers a significant advantage in terms of hyperparameter optimization.
519   DISCO requires tuning only a single parameter for its Nadaraya-Watson estimation, whereas HSCIC
520   and CIRCE involve three such hyperparameters. This reduction in the hyperparameter search space
521   partially mitigates the computational overhead of DISCO. Furthermore, as demonstrated in Fig. D.7,
522   competing models can be highly sensitive to these parameters. Consequently, DISCO’s simpler
523
      hyperparameter landscape potentially leads to more robust model selection procedures and other
      training protocols. While the computational complexity trade-off (O(b3 ) vs. O(b2 )) can be substantial
524
      for large batch sizes, the reduction in hyperparameter search iterations (from p3 to p for p parameter
525
      values) offers a notable efficiency compensation in the overall experimental pipeline.
526
527   Overall, we conclude that SAM can readily describe different anti-causal prediction scenarios for
528   bias mitigation and enable the derivation of the simple conditional independence criterion. At the
529
      same time, DISCO is a competitive alternative to the established kernel-based methods for invariance
      tasks.

1029
1030   We acknowledge the big challenge of model selection in domain generalization, bias mitigation,
1031   fairness, and similar contexts. Regular model selection strategies, such as simply choosing the
1032   best validation target loss (MSE), are not applicable, as we would choose models that would have
1033   overfitted on the in-domain data and, thus, rely heavily on the bias.
1034   Since all the methods we evaluate include a regularization term, we use the total loss on the in-domain
1035   validation set instead. This measure provides a trade-off between invariance and performance by
1036   including the mean squared error (MSE) alongside the invariance penalty.

       Our next decision was inspired by Pogodin et al. (2022) to plot the OOD-test MSEs for different

       regularization parameters (λ). The reason for doing so is to better grasp how different regularization
