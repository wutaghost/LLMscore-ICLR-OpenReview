009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We provide the first proof of convergence for normalized error feedback algo-
012            rithms across a wide range of machine learning problems. Despite their popu-
013            larity and efficiency in training deep neural networks, traditional analyses of er-
014            ror feedback algorithms rely on the smoothness assumption that does not capture
015            the properties of objective functions in these problems. Rather, these problems
016            have recently been shown to satisfy generalized smoothness assumptions, and the
017            theoretical understanding of error feedback algorithms under these assumptions
018            remains largely unexplored. Moreover, to the best of our knowledge, all existing
019
               analyses under generalized smoothness either i) focus on single-node settings or
               ii) make unrealistically strong assumptions for distributed settings, such as requir-
020
               ing data heterogeneity, and almost surely bounded stochastic gradient noise vari-
021
               ance. In this paper, we propose distributed
                                                   √        error feedback algorithms that utilize
022
               normalization to achieve the O(1/ K) convergence rate for nonconvex problems
023            under generalized smoothness. Our analyses apply for distributed settings with-
024            out data heterogeneity conditions, and enable stepsize tuning that is independent
025            of problem parameters. Additionally, we provide strong convergence guarantees
026            of normalized error feedback algorithms for stochastic settings. Finally, we show
027            that normalized EF21, due to its larger allowable stepsizes, outperforms EF21 on
028            various tasks, including the minimization of polynomial functions, logistic regres-
029            sion, and ResNet-20 training.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Machine learning models achieve impressive prediction and classification power by employing so-
035   phisticated architectures, comprising vast numbers of model parameters, and requiring training on
036   massive datasets. Distributed training has emerged as an important approach, where multiple ma-
037   chines with their own local training data collaborate to train a model efficiently within a reasonable
038   time. Many optimization algorithms can be easily adapted for distributed training frameworks. For
039
      example, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) can be modified into distributed stochastic gradient de-
      scent within a data parallelism framework, and into federated averaging algorithms (McMahan et al.,
040
      2017) in a federated learning framework. However, the communication overhead of running these
041
      distributed algorithms poses a significant barrier to scaling up to large models. For example, train-
042   ing the VGG-16 model (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) using distributed stochastic gradient descent
043   involves communicating 138.34 million parameters, thus consuming over 500MB of storage and
044   posing an unmanageable burden on the communication network between machines.
045
      One approach to mitigate the communication burden is to apply compression. In this approach, the
046
      information, such as gradients or model parameters, is compressed using sparsifiers or quantizers to
047
      be transmitted with much lower communicated bits between machines. However, while this reduces
048   communication overhead, too coarse compression often brings substantial challenges in maintaining
049   high training performance due to information loss, and in extreme cases, it may potentially lead to
050   divergence. Therefore, error feedback mechanisms have been developed to improve the convergence
051   performance of compression algorithms, while ensuring high communication efficiency. Examples
052   of error feedback mechanisms include EF14 (Seide et al., 2014; Stich et al., 2018; Alistarh et al.,
053   2018; Wu et al., 2018; Gorbunov et al., 2020), EF21 (Richtárik et al., 2021; Fatkhullin et al., 2021),
      EF21-SGDM (Fatkhullin et al., 2024), EF21-P (Gruntkowska et al., 2023), and EControl (Gao et al.,


                                                        1
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
       2023). Several studies developing error feedback algorithms often assume the smoothness of an
055    objective function, i.e., its gradient is Lipschitz continuous.
056
057    However, many modern learning problems, such as distributionally robust optimization (Jin et al.,
058
       2021) and deep neural network training, are often non-smooth. For instance, the gradient of the
       loss computed for deep neural networks, such as LSTM (Zhang et al., 2020b), ResNet20 (Zhang
059
       et al., 2020b), and transformer models (Crawshaw et al., 2022), is not Lipschitz continuous. These
060
       empirical findings highlight the need for a new smoothness assumption. One such assumption is
061    (L0 , L1 )-smoothness, originally introduced by Zhang et al. (2020b), for twice differentiable func-
062    tions, and later extended to differentiable functions by Chen et al. (2023).
063
064
       To solve generalized smooth problems, clipping and normalization have been widely utilized in
       first-order algorithms. Gradient descent with gradient clipping was initially shown by Zhang et al.
065
       (2020b) to achieve lower iteration complexity, i.e., fewer iterations needed to attain a target so-
066
       lution accuracy, than classical gradient descent. Subsequent works have further refined the con-
067    vergence theory of clipped gradient descent (Koloskova et al., 2023), and improved its conver-
068    gence performance by employing momentum updates (Zhang et al., 2020a), variance reduction
069    techniques (Reisizadeh et al., 2023), and adaptive step sizes (Wang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;
070    Takezawa et al., 2024). Similar convergence results have been obtained for gradient descent using
071    normalization (Zhao et al., 2021), and its momentum variants (Hübler et al., 2024), including gen-
072    eralized SignSGD (Crawshaw et al., 2022). However, these first-order algorithms have mostly been
073    explored in training on a single machine. To the best of our knowledge, distributed algorithms under
074    generalized smoothness have been investigated in only a few works, e.g., by Crawshaw et al. (2024);
075
       Liu et al. (2022). Nonetheless, these works rely on assumptions limiting families of optimization
       problems, including data heterogeneity, almost sure variance bounds, and symmetric noise distri-
076
       butions around the mean assumptions. Furthermore, these first-order algorithms under generalized
077
       smoothness do not incorporate compression techniques to improve communication efficiency. These
078    aspects motivate us to develop distributed communication-efficient algorithms for solving nonconvex
079    generalized smooth problems.

146   2    R ELATED W ORKS
147
148   Error feedback. Error feedback mechanisms have been utilized in various algorithms with com-
149   munication compression, leading to significant improvements in solution accuracy, while reducing
150   communication. As the first version of these mechanisms, EF14 was introduced by Seide et al.
151   (2014), and later analyzed for first-order algorithms in both single-node (Stich et al., 2018; Karim-
152   ireddy et al., 2019) and distributed settings (Alistarh et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2019;
153
      Basu et al., 2019; Gorbunov et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Qian et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021). Next,
      EF21 is another error feedback variant proposed by Richtárik et al. (2021), which offers strong con-
154
      vergence guarantees for distributed gradient algorithms with any contractive compressors, without
155
      requiring bounded gradient norm or bounded data heterogeneity assumptions. EF21 can also be
156   adapted for stochastic optimization through sufficiently large mini-batches (Fatkhullin et al., 2021)
157   or momentum updates (Fatkhullin et al., 2024). More recently, EControl was developed by Gao
158   et al. (2023) to guarantee provably superior complexity results for distributed stochastic optimization
159   compared to prior error feedback mechanisms. To the best of our knowledge, these existing works
160   on error feedback have focused solely on optimization under traditional L-smoothness. In this paper,
161   we introduce a normalized variant of the EF21 methods (Richtárik et al., 2021) for solving noncon-
      vex generalized smooth problems. In particular, we prove that normalized EF21 under generalized


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162                                       √
      smoothness achieves the same O(1/ K) rate as original EF21 under traditional smoothness, and
163   demonstrate in experiments that normalized EF21 permits larger step sizes, and thus attains faster
164   convergence than the original EF21.
165
166   Non-smoothness assumptions. Empirical findings suggest that the traditional smoothness used
167   for analyzing optimization algorithms does not capture the properties of objective functions in many
168   machine learning problems, especially deep neural network training problems. This motivates re-
169   searchers to consider different assumptions to replace this traditional smoothness condition. First
170   introduced by Zhang et al. (2020b), the (L0 , L1 )-smoothness condition on a twice differentiable
171   function f (x) is defined by ∇2 f (x) ≤ L0 + L1 ∥∇f (x)∥ for x ∈ Rd . This (L0 , L1 )-smoothness
172   has been extended to differentiable functions without assuming the existence of the Hessian. For
173   instance, the smoothness with a differentiable function ℓ(x) (Li et al., 2024a), and symmetric gener-
174
      alized smoothness (Chen et al., 2023) cover the (L0 , L1 )-smoothness when the Hessian exists, and
      includes many important machine learning problems, such as phase retrieval problems (Chen et al.,
175
      2023), and distributionally robust optimization (Levy et al., 2020). Other classes of non-smoothness
176
      assumptions, which are not related to the generalized smoothness but capture other optimization
177   problems, include Hölder’s continuity of the gradient (Devolder et al., 2014), the relative smooth-
178   ness (Bauschke et al., 2017), and the polynomial growth of the gradient norm (Mai & Johansson,
179   2021). In this paper, we impose the generalized smoothness condition to establish the convergence
180   of normalized EF21 for solving deterministic and stochastic optimization.
181
182   Gradient clipping and normalization. Clipping and normalization are commonly employed in
183   gradient-based methods for solving generalized smooth problems. Clipped (stochastic) gradient
184   descent has been studied for both nonconvex and convex problems under (L0 , L1 )-smoothness con-
185   ditions by Zhang et al. (2020b); Koloskova et al. (2023). Extensions to clipped gradient algorithms
186
      have been proposed, including momentum updates (Zhang et al., 2020a), variance reduction meth-
      ods (Reisizadeh et al., 2023), and adaptive step sizes (Wang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Takezawa
187
      et al., 2024). Comparable complexities have been achieved for normalized gradient descent (Zhao
188
      et al., 2021), and its momentum-based variants (Hübler et al., 2024), including generalized SignSGD
189   (Crawshaw et al., 2022). Convergence properties of gradient-based algorithms have also been ex-
190   plored under more generalized forms of non-uniform smoothness, extending beyond the (L0 , L1 )-
191   smoothness by Zhang et al. (2020b) to cover a wider range of optimization problems. For example,
192   variants of (stochastic) gradient descent have been analyzed under α-symmetric generalized smooth-
193   ness by Chen et al. (2023), and under ℓ-smoothness involving certain differentiable functions ℓ(·)
194   by Li et al. (2024a;b). However, the majority of these analyses focus on the single-node setting.
195   To the best of our knowledge, only a limited number of works, such as those by Crawshaw et al.
196   (2024); Liu et al. (2022), have examined federated averaging algorithms for nonconvex problems un-
197
      der generalized smoothness. These works, however, often rely on restrictive assumptions, including
      data heterogeneity, almost sure variance bounds, and symmetric noise distributions centered around
198
      their means. In this paper, we develop distributed error feedback algorithms, which eliminate the
199
      need for the restrictive assumptions mentioned above, and rely on standard assumptions on objective
200   functions and compressors.

500     7                    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
501
502     In this paper, we have demonstrated that normalization can be effectively combined with EF21 to
503     develop distributed error feedback algorithms for solving nonconvex optimization problems under
504     generalized smoothness conditions. Specifically, normalized EF21 and normalized EF21-SGDM
505     achieve convergence rates of O(1/K 1/2 ) in deterministic settings and O(1/K 1/4 ) in stochastic
506     settings, respectively. These convergence rates match those of the vanilla EF21 and EF21-SGDM
507     algorithms. Unlike previous works on distributed algorithms under generalized smoothness, our
508
        analysis does not assume data heterogeneity or impose smoothness-dependent restrictions on the
        stepsize. Finally, our experiments confirm that normalized EF21 exhibits stronger convergence per-
509
        formance compared to the original EF21, due to its larger allowable stepsizes.
510
511     Our work implies many promising research directions. One interesting direction is to extend our
512     convergence results for normalized EF21 and normalized EF21-SGDM to accommodate decreasing
513     or adaptive stepsize schedules, as the constant stepsizes required by our current analysis can become
514
        impractically small when the total number of iterations is large. In particular, applying appropriate
        decreasing stepsizes to EF21-SGDM could overcome its current theoretical requirement of a suffi-
515
        ciently large mini-batch size for the stochastic gradient at initialization. Another important direction
516
        is the development of distributed and federated algorithms that leverage clipping or normalization
517     for minimizing nonconvex generalized smooth functions.
