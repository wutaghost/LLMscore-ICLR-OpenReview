009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, spatiotemporal graph convolutional networks have attained significant
012            success in spatiotemporal prediction tasks. However, they encounter out-of-
013            distribution (OOD) challenges due to the sensitivity of node-to-node messaging
014            mechanism to spatiotemporal shifts, leading to suboptimal generalization in un-
015            known environments. To tackle these issues, we introduce the SpatioTemporal
016            OOD Processor (STOP), which leverages spatiotemporal MLP channel mixing
017            as its backbone, separately incorporating temporal and spatial elements for pre-
018
               diction. To bolster resilience against spatiotemporal shifts, STOP integrates ro-
               bust interaction including a centralized messaging mechanism and a graph per-
019
               turbation mechanism. Specifically, centralized messaging mechanism config-
020
               ures Context Aware Units (ConAU) to capture generalizable context features,
021            constraining nodes to interact solely with ConAU for spatiotemporal feature
022            interaction. The graph perturbation mechanism uses Generalized Perturbation
023            Units (GenPU) to disrupt this interaction process, generating diverse training
024            environments that compel the model to extract invariant context features from
025            these settings. Finally, we customized a spatiotemporal distributionally robust
026            optimization (DRO) to enhance generalization by exposing the model to chal-
027            lenging environments. Through evaluations on six datasets, STOP showcases
028            competitive generalization and inductive learning. The code is available at
029
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ICLR2025-STOP.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Spatiotemporal prediction, as a critical task in urban computing, has become a prominent research
034
      area, providing valuable insights into future road conditions and enhancing transportation manage-
035
      ment systems (Xia et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Miao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). Within
036   the array of models, spatiotemporal graph convolutional networks (STGNNs) have distinguished
037   themselves as a top choice due to their power representation capabilities for graph data.
038
039
      However, the success of STGNNs hinges on the assumption of independent and identically dis-
      tributed (IID) training and testing environments. The environment typically comprises two crucial
040
      components: spatiotemporal data and the graph structure. This assumption is naturally vulnerable
041
      as data temporal distributions and graph structures naturally evolves, such as the introduction of new
042   entities (e.g., sensors or air quality monitoring stations). The temporal shift and structural shift pose
043   the spatiotemporal out-of-distribution (ST-OOD) problem.
044
045
      We conduct a performance comparison of several advanced STGNNs in both IID and OOD scenarios
      using LargeST-SD (Liu et al., 2023b) dataset as an example, as shown in Figure 1 (a). The results
046
      show that the performance of STGNNs can degrade rapidly when faced with ST-OOD challenges,
047
      especially in the case of structural shifts (S-OOD). One potential reason could be their reliance on
048   global node-to-node messaging for spatiotemporal interaction, such as using GCN or Transformer as
049   spatial learners. This implies that the node representations generated depend on message paths (i.e.,
050   graph structure) and features of neighboring nodes. As depicted in Figure 1 (b), when these elements
051   change in the testing environment, GCNs trained on a specific distribution may encounter challenges
052   in accurately capturing the updated node representations. This can lead to errors that propagate
053   across the entire graph, ultimately diminishing the accuracy of the overall graph representation.
      Furthermore, STGNNs commonly employ a stacked architecture with multiple modules to handle

071   this paper, we further undertake two efforts: First, we introduce a more robust messaging mechanism
072   for spatiotemporal feature interactions, which can replace the node-to-node messaging. Secondly,
073   we simplify the model structure, and the final prediction is jointly determined by both the temporal
074   and spatial components, no longer relying on a single representation, to reduce error accumulation.
075   In this paper, we propose a SpatioTemporal OOD Processor (STOP), which utilizes a lightweight
076   MLP as its backbone. Specifically, we incorporate a spatiotemporal channel mixing module with
077   prompt embedding and temporal decomposition techniques to enhance the temporal prediction com-
078   ponents. For spatial learning, we introduce a robust Centralized messaging mechanism that config-
079   ures Context Aware Units (ConAU) to learn invariant contextual features. The Centralized mech-
080   anism facilitates interaction between ConAU and nodes, replacing node-to-node messaging. Fur-
081   thermore, we design Generalized Perturbation Units (GenPU) to perturb this interaction process,
082   simulating spatiotemporal shifts and creating variable training environments. Additionally, we cus-
083
      tomize a spatiotemporal distributionally robust optimization (DRO) for GenPU to assist models in
      learning robust causal knowledge from challenging environments. We synthesize the prediction
084
      components across temporal and spatial dimensions to generate the final prediction. Our model is
085
      evaluated on six datasets, demonstrating robust generalization and inductive learning across various
086   OOD scenarios. Notably, its efficiency is also impressive.
087
088   Our contributions can be four-folds: (1) We introduce a Spatio-Temporal OOD Processor (STOP),
089
      incorporating a resilient centralized messaging mechanism and a graph perturbation mechanism.
      (2) The centralized messaging constrains nodes to interact exclusively with Context Aware Units
090
      (ConAU) for feature interaction, thereby enhancing the resilience of model to spatiotemporal shifts.
091
      (3) The graph perturbation mechanism, equipped with Generalized Perturbation Units (GenPU),
092   disrupts node interactions with ConAU and includes a specialized spatiotemporal distributionally
093   robust optimization (DRO) for GenPU, facilitating the model’s acquisition of causal knowledge
094   across diverse environments. (4) STOP demonstrates competitive performance on six datasets.

099
      Spatiotemporal prediction. As a crucial task in intelligent transportation systems (Li et al., 2024;
100
      Jin et al., 2022a;b), current popular spatiotemporal prediction models are predominantly based
101
      on spatiotemporal graph neural networks (STGNNs) (Zhang et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2024b;c).
102   These models focus on developing advanced variants to accurately characterize spatiotemporal
103   data, typically combining GCNs with sequential models to learn complex dynamics. For instance,
104   D2 STGNN (Shao et al., 2022b) and DCRNN integrate diffusion graph convolutional networks with
105   RNN to effectively capture temporal patterns. Meanwhile, STAEformer (Liu et al., 2023a) and
106   STNN (Yang et al., 2021) utilize Transformer to model long-term temporal dependencies. Some
107   continual learning approaches (Chen et al., 2021) sequentially fine-tune models using data subsets
      with new distributions to adapt to spatiotemporal changes, which are introduced in Appendix A.

      Unfortunately, the effectiveness of these models can only be demonstrated in environments similar
109   to the training set, leading to challenges when encountering OOD scenarios.
110
111   Spatiotemporal OOD learning. Inspired by advances in time series shift learning (Liu et al., 2022)
112
      discussed in Appendix A, researchers have specifically designed spatiotemporal OOD learning mod-
      els. For example, CauSTG (Zhou et al., 2023) introduces a causal framework that transfers global
113

114

116   STEVE (Hu et al., 2023) encodes traffic data into two disentangled representations and utilizes spa-
117   tiotemporal environments as self-supervised signals. In this paper, we reformulate their message-
118   passing mechanism, addressing the OOD challenge from a novel perspective.

208   4.2.1               CENTRALIZED MESSAGING MECHANISM
209
210   STGNN conventionally leverages a global node-to-node messaging mechanism for spatiotemporal
211   feature interactions, which, unfortunately, is vulnerable to structural variations (Finkelshtein et al.,
212
      2023; Han et al., 2024b), hindering its generalization capability to unknown graph structures.
213   To address these limitations, we propose the adoption of a resilient centralized messaging approach
214   that diverges from the traditional node-to-node communication paradigm. Our novel method incor-
215   porates context aware units, enabling each graph node to interact solely with these units to gather
      contextual features, mimicking a centralized messaging manner.

453   In this section, we analyze the sensitivity of the numer of ConAU and GenPU on the SD and

455   is set to 8 in SD dataset and 4 in KnowAir dataset. When K exceeds this value, the model cre-

457
      introducing noise. When K is less than this value, too few perception units fail to learn sufficient

458
      of GenPU M is set to 3 in SD dataset and 4 in KnowAir dataset. A smaller M may not provide
459
      sufficient training environment diversity, resulting in performance degradation. On the other hand,
460   an excessive number of GenPU does not necessarily improve performance. Too large M means that
461   the generated environment is too complex, which increases the learning difficulty of the model to
462   extract causal knowledge.

534   In this paper, we present a Spatio-Temporal Out-of-Distribution Processor, namely STOP, which
535   incorporates a spatial interaction mechanism and a graph perturbation mechanism to enhance re-
536   silience against spatiotemporal shifts. The spatial interaction mechanism employs a centralized
537   messaging pattern for nodes to engage with ConAU, facilitating spatial feature interactions. Through
538   the graph perturbation mechanism, random disruptions are introduced to diversify training environ-
539   ments, bolstering the model’s robustness. Assessment across numerous datasets in various OOD
      scenarios showcases the model’s robust generalization, inductive learning, and efficiency.

      A     R ELATED WORK
775
776   A.1   C ONTINUAL LEARNING WITH SPATIOTEMPORAL SHIFTS
777
778   Several studies (Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023a; Lee & Park, 2024; Chen & Liang, 2024) have
779   proposed continual learning strategies to tackle spatiotemporal graph prediction in scenarios with
780   spatiotemporal shifts. When the spatiotemporal data distribution undergoes changes, these models
781   engage in fine-tuning using a subset of new data to adjust to the updated data distribution. For in-
782   stance, TrafficStream (Chen et al., 2021) recommends utilizing subsets of newly added nodes and
783   significant temporal pattern data changes for fine-tuning the model. PECPM (Wang et al., 2023b)
784
      identifies conflicting nodes to enhance the fine-tuning process, focusing on nodes that have expe-
      rienced substantial changes. DLF (Wang et al., 2024b) introduces a streaming training strategy
785
      to continuously fine-tune the model to adapt to the dynamic nature of spatiotemporal data. TF-
786
      MoE (Lee & Park, 2024) partitions traffic flow into multiple homogeneous groups and assigns an
787   expert model responsible for each group, enabling each expert model to specialize in learning and
788   adapting to specific patterns. However, these models often compromise performance to improve
789   learning efficiency, resulting in lower performance compared to traditional spatiotemporal models.
790   Primarily, these models train and fine-tune on a sufficient amount of new distribution data (approx-
791   imately 21 days in one month) and test on the new data distribution, thereby adhering to the IID
792   assumption and encountering difficulties in OOD learning.
793
794
795   A.2   T EMPORAL SHIFT IN TIME SERIES
796
797   Various models have been developed in the time series domain to address temporal shifts in time
798   series data, particularly focusing on OOD learning challenges. For instance, RevIN (Kim et al.,
799   2021) employs a symmetric structure to eliminate and reconstruct distribution information based
800   on the input window’s statistics. AdaRNN (Du et al., 2021) categorizes historical time sequences
801
      into different classes and dynamically matches input data to these classes for contextual information
      identification. Additionally, a reversible instance normalization technique, proposed by (Kim et al.,
802
      2021), aims to mitigate temporal distribution shift issues. Non-stationary Transformers (Liu et al.,
803
      2022) introduce a normalization-denormalization technique to stabilize time series data, mainly for
804   transformer-based models. SAF (Arik et al., 2022) suggests test-time adaptation through a self-
805   supervised objective to enhance adaptation against distribution shifts. DIVERSIFY (Lu et al., 2024)
806   aims to leverage subdomains within a dataset to mitigate challenges arising from non-stationary
807   generalized representation learning. However, these models often overlook the modeling of spatial
808   dependencies. Spatial modeling is crucial in the field of spatiotemporal prediction, as it can examine
809   the states of neighboring nodes to enhance prediction performance, given the strong correlations that
      often exist among neighboring nodes (Jin et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023).

      C.1      BASELINE D ETAIL
867
868   In experiments, we compare a lot of spatiotemporal prediction models with spatiotemporal OOD
869   models. However, the original versions of many of these models are not compatible with the OOD
870   setting. Consequently, we had to remove certain non-essential code related to graph structures,
871   particularly node embedding techniques and adaptive graph structure learning techniques.
872   Node embedding technology. The researchers set a node embedding vector E ∈ RN ×ds to capture
873   node patterns adaptively, which are coupled with the size N of the graph structure. Therefore,
874   when the model is trained, it cannot be run directly into the test environment with ST-OOD. STID,
875   STAEformer, and BigST use this technology.
876
      Adaptive graph learning. This method generally use two noode embedding vectors E1 ∈ RN ×ds
877
      and E2 ∈ RN ×ds , and they multiply these two node embedding matrices, As = E1 E2⊤ ∈ RN ×N ,
878   to generate an adaptive adjacency matrix As for learning the adjacency matrix, which is then used
879   for GCN. GWNet, D2 STGNN, and CaST adopt this method.
880
881   C.2      E XPERIMENTAL DATASET DETAILS
882
883   In this paper, we utilized six datasets to evaluate the effectiveness of the models in OOD scenarios,
884   primarily from the domains of transportation and atmosphere. These datasets often span multiple
885   years. Among them, LargeST (Liu et al., 2024) collected five years of data from 8600 records,
886   sampled at a frequency of five minutes. PEMS3-Stream (Chen et al., 2021) is a naturally streaming
887   traffic dataset, recording data from July each year from 2011 to 2017, where the traffic structure
888
      expands year by year, naturally representing spatiotemporal shifts. Knowair (Wang et al., 2020)
      collected 18 atmospheric features sampled at an hourly frequency. We followed the following rules
889
      to create spatiotemporal OOD datasets.
890
891   Temporal shift: We used the first 60% of data from the first year as the training set, followed by 20%
892   of data for the validation set. We used the last 20% of data from subsequent years for the test set.
893   This longer time interval ensures changes in temporal distribution characteristics.
894   Structural shift: Apart from the PeMS3-Stream dataset, we selected a subset of nodes for training
895   and validation, approximately 75% of the total number, in the test set, we randomly masked 10% of
896   nodes to simulate node disappearance and added 30% of nodes as new nodes. This is because for
897   spatiotemporal systems, cities or detection systems generally tend to expand. Since PeMS3-Stream
898   is a natural streaming data set, we use it directly.

       C.9    A BLATION E XPERIMENT
1297
1298   We conduct thorough ablation experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of each component. The
1299   variants we created are shown in Table 17 and the experiments are shown in Table 17.
1300
       For the time module, we found that time decomposition and prompting provided the model with
1301
       better capabilities to capture the temporal patterns from the sequence perspective, while the intro-
1302   duction of Yt to make predictions from multiple components enhanced the model’s robustness.
1303
1304   Regarding the C&S messaging mechanism, the “w/o ConAU” variant, which removes the spatial
1305
       interaction module, resulted in a significant increase in error, indicating that the spatial interaction
       is still necessary in OOD scenarios. The “w/o LA” variant, which removes the low-rank attention
1306
       mechanism in the C&S spatial interaction module, performed poorly in prediction, as the traditional
1307
       node-to-node messaging mechanism is less robust to spatiotemporal shifts. The “w/o LA + DRO”
1308   variant performed better than the “w/o LA + RandomDrop” variant, demonstrating that the proposed
1309   graph perturbation mechanism is more effective than directly perturbing the dataset to generate
1310   diverse training environments in helping the model extract robust representations.
1311
       The “w/o DRO” variant exhibited a larger prediction error, suggesting that the inability to effectively
1312
       optimize the deployed GenPU mask matrix increased the complexity of the model’s learning process.
1313
       The “w/o (GenPU&DRO)” variant also showed a considerable increase in error, further highlighting
1314   the crucial importance of the proposed graph perturbation mechanism in enhancing the model’s
1315   robustness, as it allows the model to learn resilient representations from the perturbed environments.
1316
1317
       These ablation studies can demonstrate the positive impact of each designed component on enhanc-
       ing the overall performance of the model in out-of-distribution scenarios.
