009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper investigates the use of transformer architectures to approximate the
012            mean-field dynamics of interacting particle systems exhibiting collective behavior.
013            Such systems are fundamental in modeling phenomena across physics, biology,
014            and engineering, including gas dynamics, opinion formation, biological networks,
015            and swarm robotics. The key characteristic of these systems is that the particles are
016
               indistinguishable, leading to permutation-equivariant dynamics. We demonstrate
               that transformers, which inherently possess permutation equivariance, are well-
017
               suited for approximating these dynamics. Specifically, we prove that if a finite-
018
               dimensional transformer can effectively approximate the finite-dimensional vector
019            field governing the particle system, then the expected output of this transformer
020            provides a good approximation for the infinite-dimensional mean-field vector field.
021            Leveraging this result, we establish theoretical bounds on the distance between the
022            true mean-field dynamics and those obtained using the transformer. We validate
023            our theoretical findings through numerical simulations on the Cucker-Smale model
024            for flocking, and the mean-field system for training two-layer neural networks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The identification of dynamical system models for physical processes is a fundamental application
029
      of machine learning (ML). Of particular interest are systems of particles exhibiting collective be-
030   haviors—such as swarming, flocking, opinion dynamics, and consensus—are of significant interest.
031   These systems involve a large number of particles or agents that follow identical dynamics, which are
032   independent of the particles’ identities and are permutation-equivariant. Examples include biological
033   entities (Lopez et al., 2012), robots (Elamvazhuthi & Berman, 2019), traffic flow (Piccoli et al.,
034   2009; Siri et al., 2021), and parameters in two-layer neural networks (Mei et al., 2019). A common
035   approach to simplifying the analysis of such systems is to consider the continuum limit as the number
036   of particles n → ∞, resulting in mean-field models rooted in statistical physics. Instead of specifying
037   the dynamics of each agent, the particles are modeled using probability measures. This paper learns
038
      the mean-field dynamics of particles via particle trajectories using transformers.
039   There have been several works on learning mean-field dynamics using machine learning. For example,
040   recent works have utilized classical approaches. Pham & Warin (2023) constructed two different
041   types of neural network based function approximators for mean-field mappings and proved their
042   approximation capabilities. The works Feng et al. (2021); Lu et al. (2019) present a kernel-based
043   method for identifying dynamics of interacting particle systems Miller et al. (2023) employed kernel-
044
      based methods. The work Messenger & Bortz (2022) presents a weak form of the SINDy algorithm
      Brunton et al. (2016) for identifying mean-field dynamics of interacting particle systems.
045
046   This paper explores the use of transformers to approximate the dynamical systems governing the
047   collective behavior of interacting particles with permutation-equivariant dynamics. We define a new
048   transformer architecture for mean-field or measure-dependent vector fields by taking the expectation
049   of a finite-dimensional transformer with respect to a product measure, which we refer to as the
050
      expected transformer. This approach differs from recent works Vuckovic et al. (2020); Geshkovski
      et al. (2023); Furuya et al. (2024a); Adu & Gharesifard (2024) that express transformers as maps on
051
      the space of probability measures by defining a continuous version of attention.
052
053   Encoding permutation equivariance into the function class or model used for identifying such
      systems is potentially advantageous due to the benefits of the inductive bias in learning. This raises


                                                        1

054
      the question of which classes of functions guarantee approximation and learning benefits while
055   possessing permutation equivariance. Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017), which have achieved
056   state-of-the-art performance in many learning applications involving sequence-to-sequence mappings,
057   are one such model class.
058
      In this work, we analyze the approximation capabilities of the expected transformer and establish
059
      rates of approximation of the expected transformer as a function of the approximation error achieved
060
      by the finite-dimensional transformer and the sequence length used. Using this approximation result,
061   we demonstrate that the solution of the continuity equation—which describes the mean-field behavior
062   of interacting particle systems—can be approximated by approximating the vector fields using the
063   expected transformer model. We validate our theoretical findings through numerical simulations and
064   comparisons with established benchmarks, specifically on the Cucker-Smale model for swarming,
065   and the mean-field system for training two-layer neural networks.
066
      To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
067
068   1. We define a continuum version of the transformer as an expectation of finite-dimensional trans-
069      formers (see Equation 7). This is distinct from prior work such as Geshkovski et al. (2023) that
070
         define neural networks on infinite dimensional spaces directly. Instead, we lift a finite-dimensional
         model into infinite-dimensional space.
071
      2. We establish approximation rates of measure-valued maps by this expected transformer (see
072
         Theorem 3).
073   3. We show that the solution of the continuity equation can be approximated by approximating the
074      vector fields using the expected transformer (see Theorem 4).
075
076   Other related work Universal approximation of functions by neural networks has a long history.
077   One of the prominent earlier results Hornik et al. (1989) proved that continuous functions can be
078   well approximated by using neural networks of bounded depth but arbitrary width. For the standard
079   activation functions, the complementary result for bounded width but arbitrary depth has also been
080   shown in Telgarsky (2016); Yarotsky (2018). For bounded width and depth, Maiorov & Pinkus (1999)
081   provided such a result for special activation functions. Recent results such as Kidger & Lyons (2020);
082   Shen et al. (2022) have investigated bounded width and depth for more standard network architectures.
083   The capabilities of bounded width but arbitrary depth residual networks for approximating solutions
084
      of the continuity equation, such as those arising in normalizing flows, has been studied in Ruiz-Balet
      & Zuazua (2023); Elamvazhuthi et al. (2022).
085
086   Significant work has been done using ML to approximate solutions to differential equations, par-
087   ticularly solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs). Specifically, Physics Informed Neural
088   Networks (PINN) were introduced by Raissi et al. (2019) as a method for solving PDEs using neural
089   networks. PINNs have been successfully used in Cai et al. (2021); Weinan & Yu (2017); Bhatnagar
090
      et al. (2019). The idea here is to use the differential equation as the loss function for the neural
      network. Other works have also investigated the problem of approximating the solution operator
091
      to PDEs on a mesh (Guo et al., 2016; Zhu & Zabaras, 2018; Adler & Öktem, 2017; Bhatnagar
092
      et al., 2019). Building on this, (Kovachki et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020a;b) developed neural operators
093   defined on infinite-dimensional spaces to solve PDES, and Furuya et al. (2024b) provide universal
094   approximation results. Additionally, Li et al. (2023) provide quantitative results for approximating
095   eigenfunctions of the Laplace equation on manifolds.

528
529    In conclusion, this paper demonstrated the efficacy of transformer architectures in approximating
530
       the mean-field dynamics of interacting particle systems. We showed that finite-dimensional trans-
       former models can be lifted to approximate the infinite-dimensional mean-field dynamics. Through
531
       theoretical results on approximations of the vector field and solution to the continuity equation, as
532
       well as numerical simulations, we established that transformers can be powerful tools for modeling
533    and learning the collective behavior of particle systems. In the future, we would like to investigate if
534    transformers can be used to for mean-field control.
