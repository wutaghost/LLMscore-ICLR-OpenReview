                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Catastrophic forgetting is a fundamental challenge in neural networks that pre-
011            vents continuous learning, which is one of the properties essential for achieving
012            true general artificial intelligence. When trained sequentially on multiple tasks,
013            conventional neural networks overwrite previously learned knowledge, hindering
014            their ability to retain and apply past experiences. However, humans and other ani-
015
               mals can learn new things continuously without forgetting them. To overcome this
               problem, we devised an architecture that preserves significant task-specific con-
016
               nections by combining selective neuron freezing with Hebbian learning principles.
017
               Hebbian learning enables the network to adaptively strengthen synaptic connec-
018            tions depending on parameter activation. It is inspired by the synaptic plasticity
019            seen in the brain. By preserving the most important neurons using selective neuron
020            freezing, new tasks can be trained without changing parameter weights. Experi-
021            ments conducted on standard datasets show that our model significantly reduces
022            the risk of catastrophic forgetting, allowing the network to learn continually.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are an important aspect of modern Deep Learning models. Even
029
      though they are biologically plausible in certain aspects, they are still incapable of doing many
030
      important tasks that biological neural networks can easily perform. Continual Learning is one area
031   where ANNs don’t work well with their vanilla architecture Wang et al. (2024). To learn tasks
032   from a new distribution, ANNs have to be trained on the entire dataset from the beginning due to
033   which they end up forgetting the previously learned task Zenke et al. (2017). On the other hand, the
034   human brain can learn from non-stationary data and store it easily Mao et al. (2021); Kudithipudi
035   et al. (2022). It could be because of poor mimicry of the biological capabilities of the neurons that
036   ANNs currently don’t have this property, or it could also be because of some missing piece in the
037   architecture Bashivan et al. (2019).
038   Catastrophic forgetting is a problem faced by the current designs of ANNs where the network for-
039   gets the previously learned data distribution when trained on different data distributions Bagus &
040   Gepperth (2021); De Lange et al. (2022); Toneva* et al. (2018); Harun et al. (2023). Continual
041   Learning is an important and interesting property for a neural network to attain. There are a few dif-
042   ferent methods using which other papers have tried to solve this problem Zenke et al. (2017); Yang
043   et al. (2023). We have tried to solve this issue through architectural changes in this work. In gen-
044   eral ANNs, the parameters are trained to store the tuned values corresponding to the tasks. Updating
045
      these parameters for a different distribution makes the model forget the previous task. Therefore, the
      most intuitive way to retain the memory of the previous task is to freeze those parameters Hou et al.
046
      (2025). Then comes the next intuitive question, i.e., which parameters to freeze? We developed a
047
      method that utilizes Hebb’s principle to compute a hebbian update matrix which upon normalization
048   acts as scaler values to scale the incoming gradients with the connection importance. This allows
049   the algorithm to strengthen or weaken or completely ignore the gradients based on the connection
050   importance. As new tasks keep coming, it is made sure that the previously trained neurons are not
051   updated. The key highlight of this algorithm is that it dynamically selects the important neurons rel-
052   evant for the task based on the incoming data distribution and trains accordinglyAmato et al. (2019).
053   The idea inspired from how the brain uses both local and global feedback to optimize and update
      the neurons connections.

      In this paper, we will show how a general ANN can be extended to attain Continual Learning by
055   dynamically training sub-networks for different tasks Kohonen (1982). We show this is possible
056   using a simple combination of local and global feedback systems.

059   2     R ELATED W ORK
060
061   Continual Learning has been addressed through multiple approaches in the past. Here are some of
062   the techniques using which this was done:
063
064   2.1   R EGULARIZATION - BASED M ETHODS
065
066   Regularization techniques use penalties to limit changes to important parameters during the learning
067   of new tasks. These approaches retain task-specific knowledge by minimizing parameter updates
068   that could damage previously learned knowledge. One notable method is Synaptic Intelligence
069
      (SI), which stores task-relevant information over time to prevent catastrophic forgetting Zenke et al.
      (2017). Other approaches use elastic weight consolidation (EWC) or L2 regularization to maintain
070
      network stability while learning new tasks Kann et al. (2023).
071
072
073   2.2   R EPLAY-BASED M ETHODS
074
      Replay-based approaches store samples from previous tasks in memory and periodically revisit them
075
      during training on new tasks to maintain learned knowledge van de Ven et al. (2020). The stored
076
      samples can be used for rehearsal, ensuring the model retains information from earlier tasks. Recent
077   works have shown that even simple memory replay methods can outperform more complex strategies
078   when memory is managed effectively Bagus & Gepperth (2021). Techniques like generative replay
079   have also been explored Bashivan et al. (2019), where synthetic samples from previous tasks are
080   generated and used for rehearsal. Certain techniques make use of external memory to store and
081   fetch required data Du et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2024).
082
083   2.3   PARAMETER I SOLATION T ECHNIQUES
084
085   Parameter isolation methods assign different sets of parameters to different tasks, preventing inter-
086   ference between them. A prominent example is the Progressive Neural Network, which grows the
087   network by adding new neurons for each task, avoiding overwriting the learned parameters of previ-
088   ous tasks. Freezing critical parameters, based on neuron importance scores, is another approach to
089
      prevent interference Hou et al. (2025). This technique can be effective but may lead to inefficiencies
      as networks expand.
090
091
092   2.4   DYNAMIC A RCHITECTURES
093
094
      Dynamic architectures allow the model to grow and adapt based on the complexity of the tasks. This
      approach modifies the network’s architecture by adding or adjusting components as new tasks are
095
      introduced, making it more flexible in dealing with non-stationary data. Recent work using Heb-
096
      bian Learning has proposed dynamic architectures that automatically freeze important neurons and
097   connections, allowing the model to learn incrementally without catastrophic forgetting Amato et al.
098   (2019). Another recent work using probabilistic modeling has proposed approaches like Contin-
099   ual Learning with Adaptive Weights (CLAW), which adaptively determine shared and task-specific
100   components to mitigate catastrophic forgetting while enabling task transfer Adel et al. (2020).
101
102   2.5   M ETA -L EARNING A PPROACHES
103
104   Meta-learning, or learning to learn, has been applied to create models that quickly adapt to new
105   tasks with minimal forgetting. These methods often involve training the model on task sequences,
106   enabling it to generalize well to unseen tasks while retaining knowledge from prior ones. Meta-
107   learning-based Continual Learning has shown promise in effectively balancing stability and plastic-
      ity Zenke et al. (2017); Javed & White (2019).

      3     M ETHODOLGY
109
110
      In order to solve the Continual Learning problem, we need to stop the relevant parameters of the
111   previous tasks from changing while training for a newer task. This can be achieved by calculating
112   an “importance score” for each parameter. One of the ways to calculate this importance score is to
113   use a locally learning algorithms that computes a score using the activations of all the parameters of
114   a neuron. One such algorithm is Hebbian learning. Since this algorithm is really good at learning
115   patterns and it dynamically learns patterns according to the coming tasks, it can be applied to any
116   dataset, making it adaptable for dynamically identifying important neurons across different tasks.
117   However, it is crucial to ensure that previously trained neurons are not selected again during the
118   training of new tasks. We have designed an architecture where the gradients of the SGD algorithm
119
      is scaled by the locally received feedback. This feedback is computed using the Hebbian learning
      algorithm.
120
121   This model starts by training a set of parameters θ1 for a given task and subsequently training another
122   set of parameters θ2 for the next task. Here, both θ1 and θ2 belong to the set of parameters θ. The
123   goal of the network is to minimize the loss for a task Tn using the parameters θn which belongs to
124   the set θ.
125
126
                                              L(θ) = Ln (θn ) ∈ Tn
127
      The model learns to select important parameters for a given task and train them for that particular
128
      task. The important neurons are selected dynamically using the computed importance score. These
129
      scores are calculated using Hebbian Learning Bahroun & Soltoggio (2018). The learning rule for
130   Hebb’s Principle is: “Neurons that fire together wire together”. This is considered the property of
131   the human brain and is responsible for the plasticity of the connections. Frequently activating the
132   relevant connections allows us to learn the action for that particular task.
133
134   3.1     H EBBIAN L EARNING
135
136   3.1.1    I NTRODUCTION
137
138   Hebbian Learning is an unsupervised algorithm that updates the parameters according to their ac-
139
      tivation. Since activations are different for different kinds of data, the important parameters to be
      updated change according to activated values. In order to calculate an importance score for every
140
      neuron, we calculate the average of all the weights relevant to one particular neuron at a time and
141
      repeat the same for all the neurons. At the end, the values are scaled to get a score value. The
142   Hebbian weights are updated using the following formula :
143
144                                            δw = lr ∗ (yi ∗ xj )
145
146   Where w is the Hebbian parameter, lr is the learning rate, and x and y are input and output values
147   from the synapses, respectively. Hebbian Learning works locally by depending only on the input
148   and output values of the synapses. This means that it doesn’t rely on any global error signal and
149   works for unseen data. It naturally strengthens connections between co-active neurons, allowing the
150   network to find relevant connections for a particular data distribution.
151
152   3.1.2    O JA’ S RULE
153
      The base form of Hebbian learning doesn’t really normalize the weights but rather continuously
154   keeps on increasing the parameter if the connection is strong every time. In other words, the param-
155   eters very easily explodes and reaches infinity. Oja’s Rule stabilizes hebbian learning with a very
156   simple way. To update rule is as follows:
157
158                                      δw = lr ∗ yi ∗ (xj − yi ∗ wij )
159
160   Oja’s rule normalizes the weights and can be used in practice to find the first principal component,
161   effectively performing PCA. The resulting weight vector coincides with the principal axis. A single
      linear neuron trained with Oja’s rule thus extracts the most information it can from the input samples.

      3.1.3    L ATERAL I NHIBITION
163
164   The aforementioned learning rules of Hebbian learning are only applicable to networks with a single
165   output neuron. Every single neurons learns the first principal component, but this is not what we
166   want. It is expected for each of the neurons to learn different patterns to give prediction on all inputs.
167   Therefore we introduce competition among the neurons. Each neuron can now inhibit every other
168   neuron (in the same layer) and competition is established. When a certain input pattern is applied, the
169
      neurons compete and only a few neurons respond strongly to the pattern, while the other neurons are
      inhibited. Such an inhibition scheme is actually biologically motivated. This encourages different
170
      neurons to learn various patterns.
171
172
      3.2     S ELECTIVE T RAINING AND I NFERENCE M ASKING
173
174   Selective training is used to train only the selected neurons by freezing the others. This prevents the
175   loss of previously trained tasks. In PyTorch, we implemented hooks that automatically multiply the
176   scaled hebbian updated values with the gradients. In such a method, gradients are scaled according
177   to the importance of the connections and even completely ignored if the scaling value is 0. During
178   the forward propagation, we use a masking technique where the activation from irrelevant neurons
179   for a particular task is multiplied by zero. This allows the data to flow only through the few selected
180   neurons and train them.

205   3.3     A RCHITECTURE
206
207   Our architecture comprises three important modules: the ANN, Importance Calculator and Selective
208   Trainer. Importance Calculator uses Hebbian Learning, an unsupervised algorithm to dynamically
209   and locally calculate scores. Based on these scores, the Selective Trainer trains only those neurons
210   with a score above a pre-defined threshold. The Trainer also trains the selected neurons with the
211   received local feedback from the hebbian update. It simply scales the gradients of SGD with the
212
      connection importance.
213   Importance Calculator uses Hebbian Learning, an unsupervised algorithm to dynamically and lo-
214   cally calculate scores. Hebbian Learning is used here since it can learn important representation
215   from non-stationary data, especially unsupervised. These scores are then passed to the Selective
      Trainer, a function that uses hooks to select relevant neurons based on their scores and scale the

237   4     E XPERIMENT
238
239   All our experiments were conducted on the MNIST and the CIFAR-10 Dataset. We tested our model
240   for both task-incremental learning and domain-incremental learning. All our experiments were run
241   on a network with hidden layers as 256, 128 and 64 with ReLU activation functions and Sigmoid
242   activation at the end.
243
244   4.1     DATASET
245
246   4.1.1    TASK -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
247
      For our experiments, we divided the MNIST and the CIFAR-10 Dataset into two sets with the
248
      classes 0-4 and 5-9 respectively. Experiments could be conducted with any such classification data
249
      by dividing them into multiple tasks. One of the constraints of our architecture is that we can’t use
250   a continuous stream of data belonging to a variable number of classes.
251
252   The division of data doesn’t need to be equal all the time. Task-Incremental learning measures the
253
      amount of forgetting the network goes through after training the model on different tasks.
254
255   4.1.2    D OMAIN -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
256
      For this experiment, we create two different version of the same dataset. The first version is the
257   normal training data as it is and second version is added with different random properties like Hori-
258   zontalFlip, RandomRotation, RandomAffine, ColorJitter, random noise.
259
260
      The model is then trained on both the datasets one by one. In this case masks are not used for
      forward propagation as the entire dataset is used when it is trained both the times. Our algorithm
261
      showed improvements from the vanilla model.

      4.2     T RAINING
265
      4.2.1    TASK -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
266
267   The model is first trained on classes 0-4. The model automatically identifies the most activated
268   neurons and trains the regular gradients scaled with the importance factor of the connection. The
269   model is trained for 10 epochs with a learning rate scheduler to attain stability. After the first training,
      the mask representing the sub-network for this particular task is stored as a binary mask. This mask

      is then used for both further training and inference. While training for the subsequent tasks, the
271   previous masks are used to ensure that those neurons are not affected during further training.
272
273   The model is then trained on classes 5-9, using the same hyper-parameters as in the previous training
274
      phase. Simultaneously, it ensures that no neurons selected for the current training were used for
      prior tasks. This training approach can be generalized with N number of tasks. The network will
275
      continue learning tasks until it reaches its capacity, meaning all neurons have been utilized. In such
276
      a situation, the concept of a growing network comes into the picture. Implementing this is part of
277   the project’s future work.

      4.2.2    D OMAIN -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
281
      The model is trained on the regular dataset first. The model uses our algorithm which uses the
282   combination of gradient descent and hebbian learning. In this experiment there are no masks since
283   the entire data is used to train. The training with the second dataset then starts with a similar fashion
284   the first one.
285
      After the training, the model is tested on the test data of both the datasets one by one to compute the
286
      average accuracy.

289   4.3     I NFERENCE
290
291   For the task-incremental experiment, After the training phase, there exists a binary mask for each of
292   the task. Currently, to analyze the performance of the model, we are manually selecting the mask and
293
      measuring the metrics but it can be done using a gating system that automatically selects the relevant
      gate according to the given task during inference. A gating system can be easily implemented by
294
      training a simple auto-encoder for each task. During the inference phase, data can be passed through
295
      every auto-encoder to calculate the reconstruction error and hence choose the auto-encoder with the
296   least error. The mask corresponding to that particular auto-encoder would be used for the inference.
297
298   Whereas for the domain-incremental experiment, we just simply test the model on the test sets of
299
      both the datasets one by one.

388   5.3   T IME TO STABILITY
389
      The time (in terms of epochs or iterations) it takes for the model’s performance on a new task to
390
      stabilize after learning it. Stability is reached when the model’s performance on the new task no
391   longer fluctuates significantly. A shorter time to stability implies that the model adapts quickly to
392   new information, which is crucial for reducing training time and computational resources.

      While it converged in 10 epochs for the initial task, the model needed more epochs for the second

      epoch requirement for later tasks, the model consistently achieved convergence. All of these exper-

      iments were conducted with a seed value of 720 to ensure reproducibility.

399   6     F UTURE S COPE
400
401   Future scope for our project can be divided into two different aspects, i.e., implementing a Gating
402
      system and allowing the network to grow after it reaches its limitation.
403   Gating Mechanism: This is an important mechanism in order to attain true Continual Learning,
404   as the model needs to automatically identify the kind of task and use the respective sub-network
405   for inference. This can easily be implemented by training an auto-encoder for every new task and
406   measuring the reconstruction error.
407   Growing Network: It is understood that a fixed number of neurons cannot keep on learning forever.
408   Therefore, we need a mechanism using which the network grows once all the existing neurons are
409   exhausted. This allows the network to grow forever and attain actual Continual Learning. One
410   possible approach is to use Reinforcement Learning for the network to learn the rules of growing.
