009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Uncertainty estimation plays a vital role in enhancing the reliability of deep learning
012            model predictions, especially in scenarios with high-dimensional output spaces.
013            This paper addresses the dual nature of uncertainty — aleatoric and epistemic
014            — focusing on their joint integration in high-dimensional regression tasks. We
015            introduce an approach to approximate joint uncertainty using a low-rank plus
016
               diagonal covariance matrix, which preserves essential output correlations while
               mitigating the computational complexity associated with full covariance matrices.
017
               Specifically, our method reduces memory usage and enhances sampling efficiency
018
               and log-likelihood calculations. Simultaneously, our representation matches the
019            true posterior better than factorized joint distributions, offering a clear advancement
020            in reliability and explainability for deep learning model predictions. Furthermore,
021            we empirically show that our method can efficiently enhance out of distribution
022            detection in specific applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In the realm of deep learning, uncertainty estimation plays a pivotal role in enhancing the reliability of
      model predictions. This paper delves into the domain of uncertainty estimation, specifically focusing
028
      on regression tasks in high-dimensional output spaces.
029
030   In these scenarios, model predictions exhibit two types of uncertainty: aleatoric and epistemic
031   Kendall & Gal (2017). Heteroscedastic aleatoric or data uncertainty can be modeled as an inherent
032   component of the model output. There, the model output consists of the parametrization of an assumed
033
      distribution, such as a Gaussian distribution in the case of regression or a categorical distribution in
      the case of classification. This distribution is learned through minimizing its negative log-likelihood.
034
      In contrast, due to its complexity in deep neural networks, epistemic or model uncertainty is typically
035
      approximated by sampling from an proxy distribution of models Hüllermeier & Waegeman (2021).
036
037   Combining both in a single model usually results in a so-called second-order distribution Bengs et al.
038   (2023). On the one hand, it consists of a distribution over model weights capturing epistemic un-
039
      certainty. On the other hand, it models a distribution over plausible predictions representing aleatoric
      uncertainty. Sampling from the model weights and performing a transformation (forward pass) of
040
      the input data results in another distribution representing the aleatoric uncertainty. The shape of this
041
      second-order distribution limits further analysis, as it is difficult to visualize, and it does not allow
042   for calculation of the marginal likelihood of a sample. Therefore, the second-order distribution is
043   typically marginalized and approximated by a single distribution, representing the joint uncertainty.
044
045
      Traditionally, these uncertainties have been jointly modeled without considering correlations between
      outputs (e.g. pixels), assuming independent factorized univariate Gaussian distributions Kendall
046
      & Gal (2017). However, neglecting correlations can limit the comprehensive understanding of
047
      uncertainties, especially in scenarios where dependencies between model outputs exist, such as in
048   pixel-wise semantic segmentation Monteiro et al. (2020), pixel-wise regression tasks, such as optical
049   flow estimation or image in-painting, or graph node regression. Figure 1 illustrates the increased
050   representational power of full covariance matrices (right) compared to merely diagonal ones (left).
051   In both cases, samples from the weight space lead to multiple predictions consisting of mean and
052   covariance each. The expected covariance is used for calculating the aleatoric component Σa , whereas
053   the covariance of the means is used to calculate the epistemic component Σe . The sum of both results
      in the joint covariance matrix Σa + Σe = Σ.

071   Yet, incorporating correlations in high-dimensional output spaces poses a significant challenge,
072   given that the number of correlations between output dimensions scales quadratically in terms of
073
      memory complexity O(S 2 ) with the total number of outputs S. This leads to large covariance
      matrices, requiring considerable storage space and making calculations computationally infeasible.
074
      This renders many downstream operations like sampling from a normal distribution parameterized
075
      by these covariance matrices, which involves Cholesky decomposition O(S 3 ), computing the log-
076   likelihood of samples with matrix inversion O(S 3 ) and determinant computation (e.g. O(S 3 ) with
077   lower-upper (LU) Decomposition) practically impossible.
078
079
      In summary, an efficient representation of the joint uncertainty containing aleatoric and epistemic
      uncertainty without neglecting covariances for high-dimensional output spaces has not yet been
080
      explored.
081
082
      Related Work To estimate epistemic uncertainty, various Bayesian frameworks have been devel-
083
      oped, including methods like stochastic variational inference Blundell et al. (2015), Monte Carlo
084
      dropout Gal & Ghahramani (2016), deep ensembles Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017), stochastic
085   weight averaging Maddox et al. (2019), or Laplace approximation Daxberger et al. (2021). The mod-
086   eling of heteroscedastic aleatoric uncertainty has been well-established for some time Nix & Weigend
087   (1994); Skafte et al. (2019); Stirn & Knowles (2020); Seitzer et al. (2022). Building upon these works,
088   others have unified epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty in a single model Kendall & Gal (2017);
089   Depeweg et al. (2018); Stirn et al. (2023); Immer et al. (2024). However, all aforementioned methods
090   either evaluate their method only for prediction tasks with a single output value or approximate the
091   marginalized likelihood as a factorized Gaussian, disregarding inter-pixel correlations.
092   Covariances for uncertainty estimation have been modeled in various applications, including localiza-
093   tion Russell & Reale (2021), human pose estimation Gundavarapu et al. (2019), pixel regression Dorta
094   et al. (2018a;b); Duff et al. (2023), multi class predictions Willette et al. (2021), and segmentation
095   Monteiro et al. (2020). Some approaches that predict full covariance matrices are limited to low
096   dimensional model output spaces Russell & Reale (2021); Gundavarapu et al. (2019). Approaches
097   for handling high-dimensional output spaces typically sparsify the covariance matrix. However, some
098   of these approaches can only model uncertainty in the local neighborhood using a band Cholesky
099
      parametrization Dorta et al. (2018a;b); Duff et al. (2023). Some works Salinas et al. (2019); Monteiro
      et al. (2020); Willette et al. (2021); Nussbaum et al. (2022) use a low-rank plus diagonal (LR+D)
100
      parametrization, which is capable of capturing global correlations. Nehme et al. (2024); Yair et al.
101
      (2024) learn the low-rank factors of aleatoric uncertainty directly without adding a diagonal and
102   create a rank-deficient semi-definite covariance matrix. This may be sufficient for both sampling and
103   analysis, but it does not provide the positive definiteness required for calculating the log-likelihood.
104   Importantly, all these sparse solutions merely focus on aleatoric uncertainty. Zepf et al. (2023)
105   combine aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty with a LR+D representation. However, by partially using
106   the Maximum a posteriori (MAP) solution as a further approximation, they do not account for the
107   influence of the model uncertainty on the estimation of the aleatoric uncertainty, leading overall to a
      worse uncertainty estimate. Furthermore, they do not resolve the second-order distribution to provide

      In conclusion, while significant advancements have been made in modeling covariances for uncertainty
      estimation, the existing approaches suffer from limitations such as local sparsification, inadequate
124
      joint representations, and neglect of weight space uncertainty, indicating a need for further research
125
      to develop more comprehensive and globally accurate uncertainty estimation methods.
126
127   Contribution This work is the first to efficiently combine both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties
128   within any high-dimensional sparse joint representation, leveraging the LR+D framework. Unlike
129   existing approaches that approximate the second-order distribution with a factorized normal distri-
130   bution, neglecting correlations between outputs, our method maintains crucial correlations between
131   outputs while avoiding the heavy space and time requirements of a full covariance matrix even for
132   high-dimensional output spaces. We showcase the superior representational power of our approach
133   on multiple high-dimensional regression tasks, e.g. inpainting on the MNIST dataset, colorization of
134   grey-scale versions of the CelebA celebrity faces dataset Liu et al. (2015), and optical flow estimation
135
      on the Flying Chairs dataset Dosovitskiy et al. (2015).

137   2     M ETHOD
138
139   In this paper, we consider supervised learning tasks with high dimensional output spaces y ∈ RS ,
140   with S denoting the number of output units, e.g. pixels times the number of output channels. We aim
141   to approximate the posterior distribution p(W |X, Y ) over model weights W given input-output data
142   pairs (X, Y ). As computing p(W |X, Y ) directly is generally infeasible for neural networks, we
143
      approximate it using Bayesian methods like Monte Carlo Dropout (MCD), Stochastic Variational
      Inference (SVI), Deep Ensemble (DE) to provide a proxy distribution qθ∗ (W ), parametrized by θ.
144
145   To represent the joint uncertainty, we decompose the posterior predictive distribution p(y|x, X, Y )
146   of an unseen input-output pair (x, y) into two terms: p(y|x, W ), representing the likelihood of the
147   output given the input x and the network weights W , and the posterior distribution of weights given
148   the data p(W |X, Y ). We model p(y|x, W ) as a multivariate Gaussian distribution p(y|x, W ) =
149
      N (µW (x), ΣW (x)), where we keep the spatial complexity of the covariance matrix ΣW (x) low
      by constructing it in LR+D form. That is, we formulate it as a sum of small matrices, ΣW (x) =
150
      DW (x) + PW (x)PW ⊺ (x), with DW denoting a diagonal matrix of shape S × S and PW a tall matrix
151
      of shape S × RW . We choose a rank RW much lower than the number of outputs RW ≪ S, such that
152   only the most important directions of the aleatoric covariance are covered. We further enforce DW
153                                                                  ⊺
      to contain strictly positive diagonal entries and since PW PW     is always symmetric, ΣW is always
154   symmetric positive definite by construction and thus a valid covariance matrix. The ultimate goal
155   of this work is to calculate an efficient yet representative representation of the posterior predictive
156   distribution p(y|x, X, Y ).

      Proposed Method We empirically evaluate our method of joint aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty
262
      modeling using our LR+D representation in several experiments. In all experiments, we use variants
263
      of the U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015) architecture. We adapt the U-Net for Bayesian inference
264   by adding dropout, which we use for MCD Gal & Ghahramani (2016) and DE Lakshminarayanan
265   et al. (2017) or by using variational convolutional layer for SVI Blundell et al. (2015) to estimate a
266   distribution over model weights which estimates epistemic uncertainty. However, we note that our
267   approach is compatible with any Bayesian method suitable for large model outputs. We use a single
268   model with multiple outputs for the mean and the covariance prediction parts. To train this model, we
269   gradually we train the mean separately and gradually change increase the weight on the log likelihood
      loss for the covariance parameters. For architectural details, please refer to our code in the repository.

      Datasets and Tasks We evaluate our method in different settings on the MNIST, CelebA, and
271   Flying Chairs datasets for the tasks of inpainting, colorization, and optical flow.
272
273   We train a reconstruction model to inpaint distorted handwritten digits from the MNIST dataset. For
274
      the inpainting task, we mask out 5/7 of the image area. We use the official test set and split the
      training set into 50,000 train and 10,000 validation images. Figure 3.2 (bottom) shows reconstruction
275
      results for a single digit.
276
277   To evaluate performance on optical flow estimation, we use the Flying Chairs Dosovitskiy et al. (2015)
278   dataset. This dataset is resized to 192 x 256 and split into 18,297/2,287/2,288 training/validation/test
279   images. We provide visualizations of the predictions as part of the supplement.
280   To evaluate our method on facial images, we use the CelebA CelebA-HQ dataset, keeping the original
281   splits from CelebA Liu et al. (2015). The original split contains 24,183 images for training, 2,993
282   for validation, and 2,824 for testing (image size 256 × 256). We study two tasks on this dataset:
283   colorization and inpainting.
284
285   Baselines We compare the performance of non-Bayesian models with different Bayesian ap-
286   proaches. Additionally, we incorporate a diagonal (D) covariance matrix following the method of
287
      Kendall & Gal (2017). We extend this by using a low-rank plus diagonal (LR+D) parameterized
      distribution.
288
289   As a further baseline, we also provide results by following the approach by Zepf et al. (2023). Here,
290   we further approximate the aleatoric uncertainty term of Equation 1 to prevent sampling aleatoric P
291   matrices. This reduces the number of resulting columns from T × (R + 1) to T + R. It is achieved
292   by approximating the expectation of the aleatoric uncertainty Σa term with the aleatoric covariance
293
      prediction of the model with the expected weights:
294                                  Σa = Eqθ∗ (W ) [ΣW (x)] ≈ ΣEq∗ [W ] (x)
295   To compute this term, we require the expected weights of the Bayesian models to be well-defined. For
296   MCD, this is done by turning dropout off and rescaling the activations accordingly. For SVI, where
297   the weights follow Gaussian distributions, the expected weights are simply the means of the Gaussian
298   distributions. For DE, we are unable to define expected weights, hence this approximation is not
299
      evaluated in this case. Note that Zepf et al. (2023) refer to this approach as MAP solution, which
      coincides with the expected weights solution if the weight uncertainty is modeled with symmetrical
300
      unimodal distributions like Gaussians as commonly used by SVI and Laplace Approximation (LA).
301
      Furthermore, Zepf et al. (2023) do not provide a joint representation, and log likelihood calculation is
302   only possible using a combination of our methods.
303

      all three Bayesian methods. For this case, we let the model predict a matrix PW ∈ RS×R of rank
306   RW = 8 and for epistemic models, we draw T = 64 samples. The predictions are multivariate
307   Normal distributions, represented by their LR+D parametrization. Those predictions are joined to a

      Quantitative Results To evaluate performance, we use the negative log-likelihood, which measures
317
      how well a model predicts the observed data. Lower values imply that the model’s predictions are
318   closer to the actual outcomes. Quantitatively, we find that modeling epistemic uncertainty improves
319   the likelihood of unseen test sample predictions, as shown in Table 1. This finding holds for both
320   modeling the diagonal and modeling the LR+D across all experiments. Additionally, including co-
321   variances through our LR+D further improves the likelihood of unseen test sample predictions across
322   all experiments. The usefulness of expected weights E[W ] approximation for the aleatoric uncertainty
323   Σa is inconsistent. Our approach, to combine both into a joint multivariate uncertainty representation
      and using SVD, is superior in all performed experiments with all tested Bayesian methods.

392   3.3   A DDITIONAL R ESULT - O UT OF D ISTRIBUTION D ETECTION
393
394   We evaluate our method on the task of out-of-distribution detection on the MNIST dataset, where
395
      we omit the digit "2" from the training data. Similar to Subsection 3.2, we train four types of
      models, with and without epistemic uncertainty, using both diagonal and LR+D representations. Our
396
      aim is to study whether predictive distributions are more spread out for unseen out-of-distribution
397
      (OOD) test samples compared to in-distribution (ID) test samples. A common metric to evaluate the
398   spread of continuous distributions is differential entropy Thomas & Joy (2006). Figure 4 presents
399   histograms of differential entropies of the predictive distributions of two Bayesian models. The
400   left one’s output is parametrized using a diagonal normal distribution, whereas the right one uses
401   the LR+D parametrization. We observe that the LR+D parametrized network better separates the
402   ID entropies from the OOD entropies than the diagonally parameterized covariance model. For
403   quantitative analysis, we compute the average entropy for ID and OOD samples in the respective
404   columns. Furthermore, we calculate the Area Under the Curve (AUC) as a metric for the separation
405   of OOD and ID samples using differential entropy. Finally, we employ a Kolmogorov–Smirnov
406
      (KS) test to measure a distance between the distribution of differential entropies of ID and OOD
      samples. Higher values are preferred for both metrics. Our results demonstrate that both 1) modeling
407
      epistemic uncertainty and 2) covariances help to improve the differentiation between ID and OOD
408
      test samples for the tested dataset. Finally, we present a combination of both, using the expected
409   weights approximation E[W ] and the SVD approximation. Our method, which combine both and
410   compresses using SVD, is superior to all baselines.

510   4   D ISCUSSION
511
512   Conclusion In this work, we have explored the dual nature of uncertainties — aleatoric and
513   epistemic — and their integration in high-dimensional regression tasks. We proposed a novel
514   method that employs a low-rank plus diagonal covariance matrix to approximate joint uncertainty,
515   effectively preserving vital output correlations and significantly reducing the computational demands
516   that are inherent to full covariance matrix representation. Our approach lowers memory usage
517   and improves the efficiency of both sampling and log-likelihood calculations. Empirically, our
518   approach outperforms the commonly used factorized Gaussian representation. It exhibits a lower
519   negative log-likelihood, indicating superior performance in uncertainty estimation, particularly in
520
      high-dimensional regression tasks. Furthermore, it excelled in out-of-distribution (OOD) detection
      on the tested dataset, leveraging the criterion of differential entropy. This success underscores the
521
      method’s effectiveness in capturing and quantifying uncertainty.
522
523
524   Limitations Our method conceptually extends to any Bayesian framework; however, for simplicity
525
      and computational reasons, we restrict our evaluation to using Monte Carlo Dropout, Stochastic
      Variational Inference and Deep Ensemble. Further investigations into other Bayesian inference
526
      techniques should determine their empirical applicability. We expect that more advanced concepts
527
      will lead to better overall uncertainty estimation. The method is flexible with regard to the choice in
528   number of columns utilized in the low-rank plus diagonal parameterization of the covariance matrix.
529   A higher number of columns provides better overall uncertainty estimation but can lead to numerical
530   instabilities and increased computational complexity. Essentially, our method exhibits a trade-off
531   between the quality of uncertainty estimation and these factors, see Table 3. We hypothesize that
532   the numerical instabilities are related to the linear dependencies of the columns in the low-rank and,
533   hence, propose additional investigations to understand and mitigate their impact. Finally, our method
534   builds upon the assumption that uncertainties in output can be modeled by a single multivariate
535   Gaussian, even though this approximation is often used in the literature Kendall & Gal (2017);
536
      Monteiro et al. (2020); Duff et al. (2023). However, multivariate Gaussians may not be a suitable
      approximation for every task, for example, for uncertainties in translation or rotation in images.
537
      Exploring epistemic uncertainty under different distributions is a highly promising research question.
538
539   By more accurately approximating the true posterior than traditional joint distributions, our method
      enhances both the reliability and explainability of predictions from deep learning models.
