009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Adaptations that seem deficient compared to instruction-tuning (finetuning on
012            instruction-response pairs) can still implicitly yield instruction-following language
013            models. We show that training solely on responses, without any corresponding
014            instructions, yields instruction following. This suggests that instruction-tuning just
015            needs to teach the desired distribution of responses. However, we then show that
016            one can finetune on instruction-response pairs from a distribution unlike the test
017            distribution of instructions, like just poetry, or just math, and still yield a model
018
               that broadly follows instructions. Instead of acting, e.g., just as math models,
               these single-task models sometimes behave more as general-purpose chatbots
019
               for, e.g., non-math instructions. To begin to explain this implicit instruction
020
               tuning, we hypothesize that simple changes to a language model’s distribution
021            yield instruction following. We support this by hand-writing a rule-based adapter
022            that yields instruction-following behavior in language models. The rules are to
023            slowly increase the probability of ending the sequence, penalize repetition, and
024            uniformly change 15 words’ probabilities. In summary, adaptations made without
025            being designed to yield instruction following can do so implicitly.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Instruction tuning, finetuning on a broad distribution of responses (e.g., Tiramisu is made by...)
031   conditioned on instructions (e.g., Give me a recipe for tiramisu), yields instruction following from
032   language models for a wide range of instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022). Prior work has shown that
033
      instruction tuning is sample-efficient, requiring as few as 1000 broad-domain instruction-response
      pairs (Zhou et al., 2023a) or a carefully crafted prompt and few-shot instruction-response examples
034
      (Lin et al., 2024). We take this a step further, exploring the idea that instruction following can be
035
      yielded from language models even implicitly, i.e., through methods not explictly designed to do
036   so. We discover two forms of adaptation that perform implicit instruction tuning, being seemingly
037   deficient compared to explicit instruction tuning: (1) response tuning, training on only responses, and
038   (2) single-task finetuning, training on data from a narrow domain of goals, like poetry generation.
039
      We first demonstrate that response tuning, training on responses alone without conditioning on
040
      their instructions—is sufficient to yield instruction following (Section 4). In particular, using the
041
      LIMA dataset (Zhou et al., 2023a) for tuning, and evaluating on AlpacaEval 2, response-tuned
042   models win roughly 43% of the time against a comparable instruction-tuned model, where equal
043   performance would correspond to a 50% win rate. Response tuning provides no explicit information
044   about the mapping from instructions to responses, only information about the distribution of desired
045   responses. This suggests that an instruction-response mapping may be learned during pretraining, but
046   all desirable responses are too low-probability to be generated. We support this, finding that pretrained
047   models rank an instruction’s real response higher than a random other instruction’s response at the
048   same rate as instruction-tuned models.
049   From the response tuning results, it seems that the crucial part of instruction tuning is to teach the
050   distribution of desirable responses. However, we find that this is not crucial either. Finetuning on
051   single-task, narrow-domain data, like mapping English requests to Python snippets (MBPP; Austin
052   et al., 2021), or generating poetry from poem titles, also yields broad instruction-following behavior
053   (Section 5). That is, despite training only to generate only Python code or only poetry, models
      generate biographies or recipes when instructed. For example, a poetry-tuned Llama-2-7B wins

084   three rules that, when taken in a product with a pretrained language model, causes instruction

087   Our rule-based product-of-experts with Llama-2-7B wins 24.4% against instruction-tuned Llama-2-

089   In summary, our results show that adaptation methods not intended to yield instruction following
090   may yet do so implicitly. When adapting language models for specific purposes, we might not expect
091   that models would behave as general-purpose instruction following models on instructions dissimilar
092   from those tuned on, but our results suggest that this will often be the case. Our rule-based adapter
093   experiments begin to explain this, showing that very simple changes to a model’s distribution—not
094   obviously tied to following instructions—also yield instruction following.1

112   2    R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Designing instruction tuning datasets. A few years before instruction tuning became common,
115   the idea of a single model performing a range of tasks was explored by DecaNLP (McCann et al.,
116   2018). In the LLM era, stronger pretrained models led to more adoption. In this era, early datasets
117   also transformed a wide range of existing NLP tasks into a single common format (Mishra et al.,
118
      2022; Wang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2022). The introduction of ChatGPT in October
      of 2022 shifted focus onto the construction of instruction tuning datasets that reflect goals users
119
      might be interested in, as opposed to NLP tasks. Some are relatively small, with the intuition to
120
      teach nothing fundamentally new during instruction tuning (Taori et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023a).
121   Others, which tend to be more performant, can be quite large and contain reasoning chains and other
122   behaviors thought to be rare in pretraining (Ivison et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b;a; Yu et al., 2023);
123   this has included considerable non-academic open efforts (Teknium, 2023).
124
125   Studies on instruction tuning. Our work contributes to a set of discoveries concerning how little
126   it takes to get language models to follow instructions. Taori et al. (2023) showed that finetuning a
127   Llama model on 52,000 instruction-response pairs caused it to follow instructions to an extent that
128   was surprising at the time. Zhou et al. (2023a) showed that—with careful selection—one could use
129   as little as 1,000 instruction-response pairs and achieve strong results. This strongly suggested that
130   one does not need to cover all types of instructions during instruction tuning. Lin et al. (2024) then
131   showed that, with careful prompting and in-context few-shot examples, a handful of examples could
132   also lead to instruction following. Kung & Peng (2023) take the NaturalInstructions (Mishra et al.,
133
      2022) style of instruction, which includes a task description, an in-context example, and the data for
      the current example. They then remove either the task description or the in-context example (leaving
134
      the other), finding that performance degrades less than expected in either case. In concurrent work,
135
      Xie et al. (2024) find that synthesizing instruction-tuning data by using webtext as instructions and
136   generating a response with GPT-4 also yields instruction-following models. Shi et al. (2024) find that
137   training to maximize the joint likelihood of instruction and response improves over optimizing for
138   the conditional likelihood of the response. Finally our work contributes to mechanistic discussions
139   of instruction-tuning; for example, while Wu et al. (2024) find that instruction-tuning is crucial for
140   teaching the model to look more at the instruction, the success of response-tuning suggests this may
141   not be necessary.
142
143   Out-of-distribution generalization. Discussing out-of-distribution generalization is difficult to
144   do precisely in the world of language pretraining, especially when we don’t know what data went
145   into the pretraining. Furthermore, it’s often difficult to reason about the coverage of datasets with
146   10+ trillion tokens (Section 1.1). In computer vision and vision-language models, various studies
147   have shown that pretraining is the singularly important component in robustness to distribution shift
148
      (Carmon et al., 2019; Miller et al., 2021; Awadalla et al., 2022). In language as well, pretraining
      on diverse data is known to lead to better out-of-distribution performance (relative to the finetuning
149
      dataset) (Hendrycks et al., 2020). When we finetune, for example, on only poetry and test on recipes,
150
      this is out-of-distribution to the finetuning, but not to the pretraining.

157   cross-entropy loss with a large corpus of text. These pretrained, or base, models serve as starting
158   points, but they are typically adapted to directly respond to user queries.

      In this section, we explore response tuning, finetuning models on just responses–without any corre-
203
      sponding instructions. One intuition for the effect of instruction-tuning compared to pretraining is
204   that it teaches a language model that (1) the {instruction} part of the input is an instruction to
205   be followed, not a pattern to be continued. For example, for a prefix Give me a recipe for tiramisu, the
206   model should not continue with Give me a recipe for cake. And (2), it should teach that strings like the
207   {response} part of the input are considered desirable responses for that instruction. Instruction
208   tuning is just conditional probability estimation, p(y | x). Response tuning, on the other hand,
209   can only directly teach the marginal distribution of desirable responses, not their relationships to
210   instructions: we optimize only for p(y). So, we examine whether response tuning implicitly tunes for
211   a good estimate of p(y | x) when we only intentionally trained for p(y).

      4.2   T HE R ESPONSE R ANKING C APABILITY
296
      The success of response tuning suggests that we don’t need to teach base models an explicit mapping
297   from instructions to responses. Under what conditions is this true, yet base models do not follow
298   instructions? One possibility is that base models can rank a desired response for an instruction higher
299   than a desired response for another instructions, but scores a string that is not a desired response at
300   all higher than both.
301
      To explore this, we propose the response ranking capability: assigning a higher likelihood to the right
302
      response for an instruction than to a desirable response for a random other instruction. For independent
303   instruction-response pairs (instruction, response) ∼ D and (instruction′ , response′ ) ∼ D, and a
304   model pθ , the response ranking capability holds if
305
                             pθ (response | instruction) > pθ (response′ | instruction).                   (3)
306
307
      Since both probabilities might be small, the response-ranking capability can hold even for models that
      don’t follow instructions. With response tuning—increasing the probability of desirable responses—
308
      models with the response-ranking capability for many instructions may generate desirable responses.
309
      For the Alpaca training set, we compute the likelihood with which the response-ranking capability
310   holds for pairs of instructions for a pretrained, LIMA instruction-tuned, and response-tuned model.
311   Our results show that the capability holds for pretrained models to a similar extent as for instruction-
312   tuned models (Table 2).

      The success of response tuning suggests that models need not learn the instruction-response mapping,
317
      so maybe they just need to learn the distribution of desirable responses. To test this, we can train
318   on a distribution of responses that is bad for most responses—like poems, or the English-and-
319   math derivations of the Grade School Math dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021)—and see if models yet
320   generate desirable responses for other kinds of questions. We call training on a narrow domain of
321   problem—like generating poems or recipes from titles, or completing math word problems—single-
322   task finetuning. Intuitively, single-task finetuning might lead models to either (1) exhibit the task
323   behavior for any input, like generating math, or (2) “break” and output unpredictable low-quality
      outputs.

446   6     A 3-RULE A DAPTER FOR I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING .
447
448   In this section, we provide steps towards understanding the surprising commonness of instruction
449   following behavior. One appealing intuition is that the difference between a pretrained model’s
450   distribution and a corresponding instruction-following distribution is simple. Simple conditions
451   should be relatively easy to meet, and might be met by a variety of disparate adaptation methods.
452
      We provide direct evidence for the simplicity of a mapping from pretrained to instruction-following
453
      distribution by hand-writing a rule-based mapping with three rules. Prior work has shown that
454   changes in distribution are somewhat sparse in token space: in a desirable response, Lin et al. (2024)
455   found that 77.7% of token decisions would also have been made by a base language model. Still,
456   22.3% of tokens is not negligible, and even if it were, sparsity in the token space does not imply that
457   it is simple to determine which tokens should change. For intuition, a weak chess engine may agree
458   with a strong engine on, say, 95% of moves, but determining which 5% should be changed and how
459   to change them could be very complicated.
460
461   6.1   T HE RULE -BASED R ESPONSE -A DAPTER
462
463   A product of distributions. To adapt a pretrained language model to follow instructions via a
464
      rule-based adapter, we choose our resulting model to have the form of a local product of distributions.
      For a word w ∈ V and prefix x ∈ V ∗ , a base model pbase , and our rule-based adapter language model
465
      prules , the final distribution pa is:
466
467                              pa (w | x) = pbase (w | x)prules (w | x)/Z(x),                           (4)
468                                           P
      where the normalization term is Z(x) = w∈V pbase (w | x)prules (w | x).
469
470   Intuitively, a product of distributions is useful because it computes a soft AND function of the tokens
471   that are likely under each distribution (as opposed to, e.g., a soft OR if one were to average the
472   distributions). Put another way, it allows our rules to change the probabilities of the base model by
473
      multiplicative factors.
474

476
      rules distribution prules (· | x) is defined by computing the softmax over the vector of scores for all
477
      vocabulary items:
478                                                     h                               i

531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      Instruction following is not only possible without explicit instruction tuning (or careful prompting), it
534   is in some sense easy to stumble upon because some adaptations implicitly instruction-tune. Models
535   already encode instruction-response mappings, since training on responses alone leads to instruction
536   following. Training on, e.g., code, or math, or poetry, does not make language models generate
537   those things when asked for, say, a recipe. This is great in some sense; language models continue to
538   show astounding out-of-distribution performance. Except, in another sense, this shows it’s perhaps
539   surprisingly hard to get language models to change their behavior in general, since they are so prone
      to just following instructions outside the distribution of finetuning.

669   use core libraries, e.g., NumPy (Harris et al., 2020), PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), and Huggingface

675   learning rate, and 10% of the training consists of a linear warmup. When we sweep over epochs, we

680   B    A DDITIONAL E VALUATIONS
681
682   While head-to-head AlpacaEval scores of various finetuned models against their corresponding base
683   models provides a clear, soft notion of instruction following quality, instruction following evaluation
684   is many-faceted. In this section, we provide additional evaluations to provide extra context for our
685   main experimental results. We use Instruction Following Eval (IF-Eval) , which tests the extent to
686
      which particular easy-to-evaluate constraints are satisfied in a response. We use MTBench scores to
      give a holistic, non-comparative notion of instruction following quality.
687
688   Our IFEval results are in Table 7. We find that instruction-tuned models perform best at IFEval,
689   improving over base models substantially. Response-tuned models perform meaningfully worse than
690   instruction-tuned models, but still better than base models. Single-task finetuned models except for
691   GSM all perform worse than base models, indicating that single-task finetuning doesn’t teach the
692
      kind of simple constraint satisfaction that IFEval tests for. We report these results averaged across
      two finetuning seeds.
693
694   Our MTBench results are in Table 6. We find that response tuning and instruction tuning perform
695   roughly on par. Many single-task finetuning methods outperform the base models, but none perform
696   close to response-tuning or instruction-tuning. We also note that the absolute scores are low relative
697   to scores using the default GPT-4 judge; we used GPT-4o instead because we hit our GPT-4 monthly
698
      token limit. As such, these scores should be interpreted relative to other models in this paper, not to
      external MTBench scores. We report these results averaged across two or three finetuning seeds.
699
700   Taken together with our main results, these results show that (1) single-task finetuning and response-
701   tuning do elicit instruction-following behavior, but (2) do not lead to models that follow fine-grained
      constraints as well as instruction tuning.
