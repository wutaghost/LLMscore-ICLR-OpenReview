036                                              A BSTRACT
037
038           Controllable generation, which enables fine-grained control over generated out-
039
              puts, has emerged as a critical focus in visual generative models. Currently,
              there are two primary technical approaches in visual generation: diffusion models
040
              and autoregressive models. Diffusion models, as exemplified by ControlNet and
041
              T2I-Adapter, offer advanced control mechanisms, whereas autoregressive models,
042           despite showcasing impressive generative quality and scalability, remain under-
043           explored in terms of controllability and flexibility. In this study, we introduce
044           Controllable AutoRegressive Modeling (CAR), a novel, plug-and-play frame-
045           work that integrates conditional control into multi-scale latent variable modeling,
046           enabling efficient control generation within a pre-trained visual autoregressive
047           model. CAR progressively refines and captures control representations, which
048           are injected into each autoregressive step of the pre-trained model to guide the
049           generation process. Our approach demonstrates excellent controllability across
050
              various types of conditions and delivers higher image quality compared to previ-
              ous methods. Additionally, CAR achieves robust generalization with significantly
051
              fewer training resources compared to those required for pre-training the model.
052
              To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose a control framework for
053           pre-trained autoregressive visual generation models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Controllable generation represents a pivotal aspect of visual generative models, enabling precise and
057
      fine-grained control over generated outputs. This capability is indispensable for tasks that demand
058
      a high degree of precision and adaptability, positioning it as a significant area of focus within the
059   domain. Currently, there are mainly two primary paradigms that have substantially advanced the
060   field of visual generation: diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) and autore-
061   gressive models (Tian et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2021). While the diffusion paradigm has already
062   given rise to numerous widely adopted methods for controllable generation, the autoregressive ap-
063   proach remains underexplored, particularly in how to empower the strengths of this paradigm for
064   controllable generation, which constitutes the central emphasis of this work.
065   Diffusion models utilize iterative denoising processes based on Markov chains to produce high-
066   quality outputs. These models have inspired the development of widely used controllable generation
067   techniques such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2024), which can
068   provide granular control over the generation images by incorporating additional signals such as
069   edge maps and human poses. However, challenges arise when integrating diffusion models into
070   multimodal frameworks, particularly when interfacing with large language models (LLMs) (Chiang
071   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). The representations in diffusion models are inconsistent with the
072   embeddings used by LLMs, complicating their seamless integration. This discrepancy might hinder
073
      visual generation tasks that require direct collaboration between vision and language models in the
      future. As a result, these shortcomings necessitate the exploration of more unified approaches to
074
      controllable generative modeling.
075
076   On the other hand, autoregressive models, drawing inspiration from autoregressive language mod-
077   els (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020), offer a compelling alternative for visual generation tasks.
078   These approaches model image generation as a sequence prediction problem, which align with the
079
      representation used in LLMs and offer lower computational costs compared to diffusion models. By
      employing intricate and scalable designs, recent autoregressive models (Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al.,
080
      2024) have demonstrated generative capabilities comparable to those of diffusion models. However,
081
      existing autoregressive models have not yet fully explored the potential of controllable visual gen-
082   eration. In an earlier attempt, IQ-VAE (Zhan et al., 2022) introduced condition patches as prefix
083   tokens to generate subsequent image patches. This approach results in overly long sequences, which
084   significantly reduces efficiency. More recently, ControlVAR (Li et al., 2024) models conditions and
085   images simultaneously to guide the generation process. However, it restricts the ability to effectively
086   utilize pre-trained models, thereby increasing the training resources needed and reducing flexibility
087   and adaptability. These inefficiencies underscore the necessity for more versatile and streamlined
088   methods for controllable autoregressive generation.
089   To address the challenges mentioned above, we propose Controllable AutoRegressive Modeling
090   (CAR), a novel, end-to-end, plug-and-play framework designed to facilitate controllable visual au-
091   toregressive generation by leveraging pre-trained models. The core design of our framework in-
092   volves integrating multi-scale latent variable modeling, where the control representation is progres-
093   sively refined and injected into each step of a pre-trained autoregressive model. Specifically, we
094   employ the “next-scale prediction” autoregressive model VAR (Tian et al., 2024) as our pre-trained
095   base, and we freeze its weights to maintain its strong generative capabilities. Inspired by Control-
096
      Net (Zhang et al., 2023), we have also designed a parallel control branch to autoregressively model
      multi-scale control representation, which utilizes both the input condition signal and the embed-
097
      ding from the pre-trained base model. The prediction of each scale’s image token map depends on
098
      the previous image tokens and the extracted control information. Through this approach, our CAR
099   framework successfully captures multi-scale control representations and injects them into the frozen
100   base model, ensuring that the generated image adheres to the specified visual conditions.
101

116   2   R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Diffusion Models Diffusion models have attracted significant attention for their ability to generate
119   high-fidelity images through iterative noise reduction processes. These models operate by progres-
120
      sively transforming Gaussian noise into a data distribution, with each step in the Markov chain re-
      fining the image (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020). The introduction of the Denoising
121
      Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) marked a breakthrough, achieving state-
122
      of-the-art results in image synthesis. Following this, several approaches have aimed to improve the
123   efficiency and quality of diffusion models (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Watson
124   & Johnson, 2023). In the past two years, diffusion models have nearly become the de facto approach
125   in the realm of text-to-image and text-to-video generation (Saharia et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022;
126   Peebles & Xie, 2023; Podell et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a;b; Esser et al.,
127   2023; 2024). More recently, some works have increasingly focused on integrating diffusion models
128   into multimodal tasks (Nichol et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022; 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
129
130   Autoregressive Models Autoregressive models have emerged as a scalable alternative to diffusion
131   models in generative tasks, offering a more efficient architecture for image synthesis. Inspired by
132   the success of autoregressive models in language tasks, such as GPT (Radford et al., 2019; Brown,
133
      2020), their visual counterparts like DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) model image generation as a
      sequence prediction problem. This paradigm shift allows autoregressive models to generate high-
134
      quality images while circumventing the iterative nature of diffusion models, thereby reducing com-
135
      putational overhead. A number of excellent works adhering to this paradigm have emerged (Ge
136   et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; 2022; Tian et al., 2024; Team, 2024; Chern et al., 2024;
137   Liu et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2024).
138
139
      One of the major developments in this field is the application of discrete latent spaces, introduced by
      VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) and VQ-GAN (Esser et al., 2021), enabling efficient encoding
140
      and decoding of image data. Subsequent works have further enhanced the representational capacity
141
      of discrete visual encoders (Yu et al., 2023a;b; Luo et al., 2024). More recently, VAR (Tian et al.,
142   2024) provides a scaling-up modeling approach for discrete latent spaces, significantly enhancing
143   generation. Nonetheless, while these models exhibit better efficiency and comparable generation
144   quality to diffusion models, they still lack sophisticated controllable generation mechanisms. This
145   limitation restricts their applicability in tasks requiring user-driven or signal-driven generation. Ap-
146   proaches such as ControlVAR (Li et al., 2024) have made some progress; however, they remain
147   inflexible and fail to fully exploit pre-trained models, often necessitating fine-tuning.
148
149   Controllable Generation Controllable generation, where the model is guided by various condi-
150   tions during the generative process, has been an active area of research. Early works focused on
151   conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), where control
152   was imposed through explicit conditioning variables such as class labels. However, the challenge
153
      of maintaining both high-quality generation and precise control persists across different generative
      frameworks. Diffusion-based methods like ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and T2I-Adapter (Mou
154
      et al., 2024) have incorporated external control signals, such as pose or sketch, to achieve detailed
155
      manipulation of generated content. In contrast, controllable generation methods for autoregressive
156   models, especially efficient ones similar to ControlNet or T2I-Adapter in the diffusion context, have
157   not been fully explored. Actually, prior to our work, it was unknown whether similar capabilities
158   could be achieved with purely autoregressive models. While methods such as IQ-VAE (Zhan et al.,
159   2022) and ControlVAR (Li et al., 2024) allow for fine-grained control over the visual autoregres-
160   sive generation process by integrating conditional tokens or patches, they cannot flexibly leverage
161   pre-trained models, and increase computational complexity. Therefore, this paper aims to develop a
      more efficient and flexible controllable framework for autoregressive visual generation.

186   3     M ETHODOLOGY
187
188   We propose Controllable AutoRegressive Modeling (CAR) to explore the potential of autoregres-
189   sive models in handling controllable image generation task. We define the task as follows: given a
190   conditional control image C ∈ R3×H×W , where H and W represent the height and width, our goal
191   is to generate a controllable image I ∈ R3×H×W that aligns with the specified visual conditions.
192   The overall objective can be formulated as modeling the conditional distribution p(I | C).
193   In Section 3.1, we introduce the preliminary foundational concepts of the “next-scale prediction”
194   paradigm in visual autoregressive modeling. Following this, in Section 3.2, we explain how our
195   proposed CAR framework controls visual generation through multi-scale latent variable modeling.
196   By applying Bayesian inference, we identify that the learning objective of CAR is to obtain a robust
197   control representation. Finally, in Section 3.3, we thoroughly discuss the control representation
198   expression and the network optimization strategy.
199
200   3.1   P RELIMINARY K NOWLEDGE OF AUTOREGRESSIVE M ODELING
201
202   Traditional autoregressive models Van Den Oord et al. (2017); Esser et al. (2021) use a “next-token
203   prediction” approach, tokenizing and flattening images into sequences (x1 , x2 , . . . , xT ). Here, T
204   is the product of the height and width of the image feature map. Each token xt is predicted based
205   on the preceding tokens (x1 , x2 , . . . , xt−1 ). The final token sequence is quantized and decoded to
206   produce images.
207   However, a recent study (Tian et al., 2024) notes that this paradigm can lead to mathematical incon-
208   sistencies and structural degradation, which is less optimal for generating highly-structured images.
209   To resolve this, it introduces a novel visual autoregressive modeling paradigm (VAR), shifting from
210   “next-token prediction” to “next-scale prediction”. In VAR, each unit predicts an entire token map
211   at a different scale. Starting with a 1 × 1 token map r1 , VAR predicts a sequence of multi-scale
212   token maps (r2 , . . . , rK ), increasing in resolution. The generation process is expressed as:

220   3.2   C ONTROLLABLE V ISUAL AUTOREGRESSIVE M ODELING
221
222   As illustrated in Figure 2, our proposed CAR framework addresses controllable image generation by
223   modeling the conditional distribution p(I | C). The objective is to maximize the likelihood p(I | C),
224   ensuring that the generated image I conforms to the visual conditions specified by C.
225   Following the “next-scale prediction” paradigm of VAR, the CAR model adopts a multi-scale latent
226   variable framework, where latent variables (token maps) at each scale capture image structures at
227   progressively higher resolutions. The control information provides additional observations to aid in
228   inferring the latent variables at each scale.

      Model Architecture Design We employ the pre-trained VAR (Tian et al., 2024) as the base model,
317
      freezing it during training to preserve its generative ability and reduce training costs. For the learn-
318   able modules {θF , θT , θG }, we experiment with various choices and empirically select the optimal
319   design choices. For the fusion function F(·), we use a convolutional encoder to extract semantic
320   features from the control input ck , and add them to the base model input bk . For T (·), we design a
321   series of GPT-2-style Transformer (Radford et al., 2019) blocks, with the depth being half of that of
322   the pre-trained base model. For G(·), we inject ŝk into the base model output rk through concatena-
323   tion, which is followed by a LayerNorm (Ba, 2016) to normalize the distribution of the two domain
      features, and a linear transformation to adjust the channel dimension.

364
        generation baselines, ControlNet and T2I-Adapter. For a fair comparison, we retrained both models

      4.4     A BLATION S TUDIES
467
468   We conduct ablation studies on the ImageNet validation set to explore the different function choices
469   for each component in the CAR framework, including F(·), T (·), and G(·).
470
471
      Different Function Choices for F (·) We explore the impact of different methods for introducing
472
      conditional control ck to form sk in F(·). Specifically, we compare two strategies: 1) using the
473
      pre-trained VQ-VAE encoder from the VAR model to directly map the condition image to token
474   maps at various scales; 2) our approach, which resizes the condition images to different scales at
475   the pixel level and employs a shared, learnable convolutional encoder for control feature extraction.
476   The results are shown in Table 3, where the learnable encoder shows significant improvements in IS
477   scores, indicating enhanced image quality. We hypothesize that the pre-trained VQ-VAE encoder,
478   being designed for image reconstruction, may not effectively capture image semantics, making it
479   less suitable for extracting control semantics. Similar visualization results are illustrated in Figure 6,
480   where using the VQ-VAE encoder results in image distortion and poor quality.
481

484   volutional network, and 2) a GPT-2-style Transformer. As shown in Table 3 and Figure 6, the
485   Transformer shows significantly higher image quality compared to the simple convolutional net-
      work baseline, due to its powerful representation ability. Meanwhile, the Transformer-based en-

      Different Function Choices for G(·) We compare different injection functions G(·), where the

      model to update the image representation r̂k . Specifically, we compare three techniques: 1) applying

509   control representation using the mean and variance of the image representation, then adds these two
510   features; 3) our method, which concatenates the two representations, applies a learnable LayerNorm
511   to normalize the distributions, followed by a linear transformation to adjust the channel dimension.
512
      As shown in Table 3, adding the image and control features leads to a decrease in the IS metric, re-
513   gardless of whether zero convolution and cross normalization are applied before the addition. This
514   indicates that these operations result in a reduction in image quality compared to our approach. The
515   generation results in Figure 6 also demonstrate that these two baselines perform worse than our ap-
516   proach in terms of image quality and naturalness. We attribute this to the incompatibility of two
517   different domain representations. Although cross normalization tries to align the distribution across
518   the domain gap, such an operation is insufficient. Therefore, concatenating the two representations,
519   followed by LayerNorm, more effectively harmonizes the conditional and backbone features, ad-
520   dressing discrepancies in data distribution.

      5   C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      In this paper, we propose Controllable AutoRegressive Modeling (CAR), which establishes a novel
      paradigm for controlling VAR generation. CAR captures robust multi-scale control representations,
526
      which can be seamlessly integrated into pre-trained autoregressive models. Our experimental re-
527
      sults demonstrate that CAR outperforms existing methods in both controllability and image quality,
528   while also reducing the required computational costs. CAR represents a significant step forward
529   in autoregressive visual generation, offering a flexible, efficient, and scalable solution for various
530   controllable generation tasks.
531
532
      Discussion and Future Works While the proposed CAR framework demonstrates advancements
533
      in controllable visual generation, it still faces certain limitations inherent in the underlying VAR
534   model. Specifically, the reliance on sequential token prediction can sometimes limit the model’s ef-
535   ficiency, especially when dealing with long image sequences or when requiring precise fine-grained
536   control at high resolutions. The multi-scale injection mechanism used in CAR could also be ex-
537   tended to explore alternative injection strategies, such as attention-based or adaptive injection, to
538   further enhance control precision. Additionally, although the current design excels at injecting con-
539   trol signals in a recursive manner, extending the framework to handle more complex tasks, such as
      video generation, remains an open challenge for future work.
