011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Masked time-series modeling has widely gained attention as a self-supervised
014            pre-training method for multivariate time series (MTS). Recent studies adopt a
015
               channel-independent (CI) strategy to enhance the temporal modeling capacity.
               Despite the effectiveness and performance of this strategy, the CI methods inher-
016
               ently overlook cross-channel dependence, which is inherent and crucial in MTS
017
               data in various domains. To fill this gap, we propose ShuffleMTM, a simple yet
018            effective masked time-series modeling framework to learn cross-channel depen-
019            dence from shuffled patches. Technically, ShuffleMTM proposes to shuffle the
020            unmasked patches from masked series across different channels, positioned at
021            the same index. Then, Siamese encoders learn two views of masked patch rep-
022            resentations from original and shuffled masked series, simultaneously capturing
023            the temporal dependence within a channel as well as spatial dependence across
024            different channels. ShuffleMTM pre-trains the Siamese encoders to reconstruct
025            the original series by incorporating cross-channel information with intra-channel
026
               cross-time information. Our proposed method consistently achieves superior per-
               formance in various experiments, compared to advanced CI pre-training methods
027
               and channel-dependent methods in both time series forecasting and classification
028
               tasks.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Time series analysis plays a significant role in various domains, including energy, traffic and
034   medicine. Significant amount of time series are collected from IoT sensors and wearable devices,
035   with the majority being multivariate time series (MTS) that contains multiple channels (a.k.a., vari-
036   ables). Due to the high cost of labeling for its indiscernible dependent structure, self-supervised pre-
037   training has gained increasing popularity for identifying useful time series representations through
038   pretext tasks on vast amounts of unlabeled data (Yue et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2024b). Notably, con-
039
      trastive learning and masked modeling have demonstrated the superior performance in time series
      data, as well as in other fields like computer vision and natural language processing (Devlin et al.,
040
      2018; He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020).
041
042   Masked time-series modeling (MTM) focuses on learning temporal dependency through the recon-
043   struction of masked segments based on the unmasked parts (Dong et al., 2024a). Meanwhile, recent
044   MTMs have adopted the channel-independent (CI) strategy to strengthen the capability of modeling
045
      temporal relationships within a channel (Nie et al., 2023). CI MTMs have significantly improved
      the performance of various downstream tasks by concentrating on temporal patterns within each
046
      channel through univariate encoding (Nie et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). However, while CI methods
047
      separately learn cross-time dependency in each channel, their mechanism inherently overlooks the
048   dependence among channels in MTS: Separate processing of univariate series cannot incorporate
049   various interactions across channels, although these relationships might be implicitly reflected when
050   multiple channels are simultaneously optimized in a single iteration. As patterns of each channel
051   intricately influence each other (Zivot & Wang, 2006), neglecting the correlation among channels
052   produces sub-optimal performance in downstream tasks (Zhang & Yan, 2023). These analyses raised
053   an important question: how can we design a pre-training framework that effectively captures cross-
      channel dependence while maintaining the effectiveness of the channel-independent strategy?

066
      To fill the gap, we present ShuffleMTM, a simple yet effective self-supervised pre-training frame-
      work for multivariate time series. Unlike previous methods that recover masked patches from un-
067
      masked patches in the same channel (see Figure 1), ShuffleMTM proposes to randomly shuffle
068
      unmasked patches along the channel from masked series, termed as “shuffled masked series”. Then,
069   ShuffleMTM utilizes patch-based Transformers as encoder and Siamese networks to take the original
070   and the shuffled masked series as inputs at each branch. With this simple shuffling mechanism and
071   Siamese networks, ShuffleMTM learns two views of masked patch representations, each leveraging
072   the temporal dependence between patches in the channel and spatial dependence between patches
073   from different channels. Then, a decoder that takes the original and shuffled views of representations
074   integrates these representations by utilizing cross-attention and self-attention mechanisms. Lastly,
075   ShuffleMTM recovers each channel of raw time series by leveraging cross-channel information from
076   shuffled patches.
077   Empowered by this design, ShuffleMTM extends the channel-independent reconstruction task to ef-
078   ficiently capture both temporal dependence within a channel and spatial dependence across channels.
079   ShuffleMTM demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across various downstream tasks, including
080   time series forecasting and classification. The main contributions of our work are summarized as
081   follows:
082
             • We identify and solve a problem in existing MTM methods: the channel-independent MTM
083
               overlooks cross-channel dependence inherent in MTS data. To address this issue, we pro-
084
               pose ShuffleMTM, a simple yet effective pre-training framework for MTS data to capture
085            complex spatial and temporal patterns.
086
             • Specifically, ShuffleMTM shuffles unmasked patches along the channel to allow the model
087
               to attend to patches in other channels. In addition, ShuffleMTM leverages Siamese net-
088
               works to encode both the original and shuffled masked series. This design extends the
089            channel-independent reconstruction task to capture both spatial and temporal dependen-
090            cies, representing the first technical contribution of MTM to learning cross-channel depen-
091            dencies within the channel-independent strategy.
092
             • Experimentally, ShuffleMTM consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance in time
093            series forecasting and classification tasks. Ablation studies and further analyses show that
094            both the shuffling mechanism and the Siamese network architecture are effective. The
095            proposed ShuffleMTM is able to capture patch-level and channel-level dependencies, en-
096            hancing forecasting capacity and robustness compared to channel-independent MTM.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
099
100
      Channel Independence. Channel-independent (CI) methods separately model each channel as a
101
      univariate time series, while channel-dependent methods jointly model multiple channels. Without
102   explicitly considering interactions between channels, CI models focus on learning cross-time de-
103   pendency within a channel. The concept of channel independence was first proposed in Nie et al.
104   (2023), and subsequent research adopting this strategy has reported significant improvements in time
105   series forecasting and classification tasks (Xu et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024). The CI strategy has sev-
106   eral advantages: it improves adaptability to various temporal patterns within the channel, increases
107   training efficiency, and reduces the likelihood of overfitting (Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b).
      Analysis from Han et al. (2024) reveal that CI forecasting models are more robust, whereas channel-


                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dependent models have higher forecasting capability, leading to consistently improved performance
109   on MTS data with noise and distribution shifts. In this work, we propose a novel pre-training frame-
110   work that utilizes cross-channel dependence in the CI strategy and demonstrate that our pre-training
111   framework combines the advantages of both CI and channel-dependent models to achieve greater
112   robustness and capacity in MTS forecasting.
113
      Cross-channel Dependence. As the channels mutually influence one another in MTS, capturing
114
      cross-channel dependence is crucial in MTS modeling, allowing for richer representations of the
115   underlying patterns (Zhang & Yan, 2023). In time series forecasting, some deep models explicitly
116   capture the cross-channel dependence using convolutional neural networks or graph neural networks
117   (Wu et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023). For Transformer-based models, Crossformer (Zhang & Yan,
118   2023) proposes a two-stage attention layer in time and channel dimensions to utilize both cross-time
119   and cross-channel dependencies. UniTST (Liu et al., 2024a) applies self-attention to the flattened
120   time series and iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024b) inverts the embedding dimension to the channel
121   perspective and perform self-attention on channels.
122   However, applying self-attention sequentially in horizontal and vertical manners, as well as in the
123   inverted manner, is inefficient for learning dependencies between patches from other channels at
124   lagged locations (Zhao & Shen, 2024). Similarly, applying self-attention to flattened patches from
125   all channels allows access to unmasked patch embeddings with identical temporal information. This
126   simplification, however, may lead the encoder to learn spurious information, hindering its training
127   (Na et al., 2024). The proposed shuffling method dynamically imposes patches at lagged locations,
128   capturing patch-wise dependencies across channels and integrates cross-channel information into
129
      the channel-independent reconstruction task without relying on identical temporal information.
130   Masked Time-series Modeling. While various MTS research addresses channel independence and
131   cross-channel dependence, this paper focuses specifically on masked time-series modeling (MTM).
132   As a principal paradigm in self-supervised pre-training, MTM optimizes deep models to capture
133   temporal dependency by reconstructing masked parts from unmasked ones. Recent MTMs have
134   adopted the CI strategy to enhance the capability of modeling temporal correlation. PatchTST (Nie
135
      et al., 2023) is the first CI MTM that divides each variable into multiple patches and reconstructs
      masked patches, thereby enhancing its temporal modeling capacity. SimMTM (Dong et al., 2024b)
136
      also utilizes the CI strategy to learn embedding manifold of variables from multiple masked vari-
137
      ables. PITS (Lee et al., 2024) further advances the CI strategy to focus on temporal correlation
138   within the patch through a patch-independent strategy. TimeSiam (Dong et al., 2024a) introduces a
139   past-to-current reconstruction task in Siamese networks to accurately capture temporal correlations.
140   Previous CI MTM methods focus exclusively on modeling temporal dependency. Despite their nat-
141   ural advantages in modeling temporal interactions, these methods inherently overlook dependencies
142   among channels in MTS. Given the importance of cross-channel interactions in MTS modeling, we
143   propose a novel MTM framework that captures these dependencies in the CI strategy.
144

146   3     S HUFFLE MTM
147
148   To capture dependence among channels in a channel-independent setting, we propose to generate
149   shuffled masked series to utilize information from other channels in the reconstruction process (Sec-
150   tion 3.1). ShuffleMTM performs cross-view representation learning using shuffled masked series
151   and original masked series within Siamese networks (Section 3.2). ShuffleMTM then reconstructs
152
      each channel by integrating temporal dependency within the channel and spatial dependence from
      different channels (Section 3.3). During the fine-tuning stage, the weights of the ShuffleMTM en-
153
      coder are transferred to downstream tasks without utilizing the shuffled view (Section 3.4).
154

      3.2    C ROSS - VIEW R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
206
      For processing patched masked series, ShuffleMTM utilizes patch-based Transformer encoder (Nie
207   et al., 2023), where, the patches are mapped into the latent space of dimension dm through a learn-
208   able linear projection Wpatch ∈ Rdm ×P and a learnable positional embedding Wpos ∈ Rdm ×N :
209    (i)                 (i)
      xemb = Wpatch · xp + Wpos . Next, we apply a vanilla Transformer encoder (Vaswani, 2017) to
210   sequence of patch embeddings.
211
212   Given a pair of original and shuffled masked series, ShuffleMTM leverages Siamese networks to
213
      learn two views of masked series representations. Siamese networks (Bromley et al., 1993) are two-
      branch neural network architectures sharing model parameters. After the Siamese encoders, we can
214
      obtain pairs of representations of original and shuffled masked series as:

221   dependencies by attending to shuffled patches, similar to channel-dependent models. By processing

      across channels.

225   Note that any inductive bias associated with channel information is provided to the shuffled masked
226   series. Some previous studies have constructed the channel embeddings to encode the relationships
227   across channels (Zhang & Yan, 2023). However, there is no predetermined position for different
228   channels in MTS (Xiao et al., 2024). It is challenging to train embeddings to build inductive bias to
229
      understand the channel-wise structure (Su et al., 2024). Thus, ShuffleMTM excludes any channel-
      related bias, ensuring it focuses on the dependencies hidden within cross-channel patterns.

234   To incorporate learned cross-time and cross-channel dependencies into pre-training of masked mod-
235   eling, the output representations of the Siamese encoders are fed into a decoder with cross-attention
236   and self-attention mechanisms (Gupta et al., 2023). A decoder block consists of a cross-attention
                                                                          (i)                       (i)
237   layer, a self-attention layer and a Feed-Forward Network (FFN). z̄p acts as the query, and z̃p serves
238   for the key and value in the cross-attention layer. Next, the representation attends to each other via
239   the self-attention layer and is passed to the FFN. As the cross-attention layer is functionally simi-
240
      lar to learning the similarity matrix between target and reference in self-supervised correspondence
      learning (Gupta et al., 2023; Vondrick et al., 2018), the decoder can integrate different views of patch
241
      representations in the original and shuffled masked series. For clarity, we formalize this process as

      3.4   F INE - TUNING TO D OWNSTREAM TASKS
252
      Through the random shuffling mechanism in pre-training, ShuffleMTM learns both cross-time and
253
      cross-channel dependencies in multivariate time series. During the fine-tuning process, the shuffled
254
      view is not utilized and one branch of Siamese encoders for the shuffled view is removed. The
255   weights of the encoder are transferred to project each channel (univariate time series) into a deep
256   representation, which is then fine-tuned with a linear decoder layer to predict for the downstream
257   tasks. The cross-channel dependence learned during the pre-training is effectively transferred to the
258   downstream tasks without the shuffled view.
259
260
      3.5   R ELATIONS WITH P REVIOUS W ORKS
261
262
      It is notable that ShuffleMTM can be reduced to PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) if the branch for the
263
      shuffled masked series is removed and its corresponding decoder is replaced with a linear head.
264   However, ShuffleMTM is fundamentally different from PatchTST, as ShuffleMTM captures both
265   cross-time and cross-channel dependencies through simple masked series shuffling and the use of
266   Siamese encoders, while PatchTST focuses solely on cross-time dependencies within each chan-
267   nel. TimeSiam aims to enhance time-dependent representation learning, while ShuffleMTM ex-
268   pands CI’s limited focus on temporal dependency—a limitation shared by TimeSiam—to learning
269   cross-channel dependence. Additionally, TimeSiam employs complex time-difference embeddings,
      whereas ShuffleMTM has a simpler architecture that does not require any additional embedding.

452   6.1                  C ROSS -C HANNEL D EPENDENCE A NALYSIS
453
454   To validate ShuffleMTM’s capacity to capture cross-channel dependence in a channel-independent
455
      encoding, we conduct two experiments, focusing on both patch-level and channel-level dependence.
456   Patch-level dependence. We perform a similarity analysis on the shuffled patched series. Specif-
457   ically, we compute the cosine similarity between the self-attention map and the correlation coeffi-
458   cients of patches, derived from the randomly shuffled patched series across channels. A Transformer
459   encoder that effectively learns the dependencies of input patches should yield attention scores con-
460   sistent with the correlation structure (Liu et al., 2024b). We compare ShuffleMTM with patch-based
461
      CI MTM methods, PatchTST and TimeSiam. Additionally, we evaluate PatchTST pre-trained to
      reconstruct the original time series from shuffled masked series, referred to as PatchTST-shuffled.
462
463   As shown in Figure 8, ShuffleMTM achieves a higher cosine similarity between the attention scores
464   of shuffled patched series and their correlation matrix compared to other models. This finding sug-
465   gests that pre-training with shuffled masked series effectively captures cross-channel dependence in
466   the channel-independent encoding. Moreover, PatchTST-shuffled demonstrate lower average simi-
467
      larities and higher variances in both datasets than ShuffleMTM. This result suggests that processing
      shuffled masked series in a single-branch, channel-independent encoding is insufficient for learning
468
      cross-channel dependencies. These analyses validate the use of Siamese networks to better capture
469
      such dependencies.
470
471   Channel-level dependence. While ShuffleMTM processes each univariate time series in MTS in-
472   dependently, its embedding space captures the the cross-channel correlations present in the raw time
473
      series. To illustrate this channel-wise dependence, we present a case visualization on the correlation
      coefficients of channels in the raw time series and the pairwise distances of channel embeddings on
474
      the Traffic dataset, as shown in Figure 8. Max pooling is applied to extract channel embeddings from
475
      a series of patch embeddings, a technique commonly used in time series self-supervised methods
476   (Nie et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024b; Lee et al., 2024). The results show that the pairwise distances
477   between the learned channel embeddings closely align with the correlations between channels in the
478   raw time series. These findings confirm that pre-training with shuffled series captures the dependent
479   structure of channels in the raw time series.

481   6.2                  C APACITY-ROBUSTNESS A NALYSIS .
482

528
529
530   This paper proposes ShuffleMTM, a simple pre-training framework for masked modeling of multi-
531
      variate time series. Unlike previous works that primarily focus on enhancing temporal modeling ca-
      pacity within each channel, ShuffleMTM simultaneously captures both cross-time and cross-channel
532
      dependencies through its proposed masked series shuffling and Siamese encoders. Experimentally,
533
      ShuffleMTM demonstrates superior performance in time series forecasting and classification tasks
534   compared to state-of-the-art masked modeling methods, across in-domain, cross-domain, and label-
535   scarce settings. This work highlights the effectiveness of incorporating cross-channel dependencies
536   during pre-training, paving the way for various future studies. For example, we aim to develop
537   fine-tuning methods applicable to channel-independent encoders, further enhancing adaptability to
538   diverse cross-channel patterns during fine-tuning and inference. Additionally, we plan to extend our
539   approach to time series foundation models, which are crucial for capturing cross-time and cross-
      channel dependencies necessary for time series forecasting tasks.

776   C.1      D EFINITION OF M EASURES
777
      Han et al. (2024) proposes four measures, with two evaluating capacity and two evaluating robust-
778
      ness of a linear model. We extend these measures to a neural network forecaster. We denote the
779   training and test sets for forecasting as (X (tr) , Y (tr) ) and (X (te) , Y (te) ), respectively, and the neu-
780   ral network forecaster as fθ , parametrized by θ. The training and test sets can be decomposed into
781   univariate time series if the forecaster adopts the channel-independent strategy. We compute the
782   following measures to evaluate the model’s performance:
783
784   Train Error (Incapacity): The train error is defined as:
785                                                               L(tr) = ||fθ(tr) (X (tr) ) − Y (tr) ||2F
786                     where
787
                                                             θ(tr) = argminθ ||fθ (X (tr) ) − Y (tr) ||2F
788
789           is the optimal parameter for the training data. Training error measures the capacity com-
790           puted on the training set.
791   Test Error (Incapacity): The test error is defined as:
792                                                               L(te) = ||fθ(te) (X (te) ) − Y (te) ||2F
793
                        where
794
795                                                           θ(te) = argminθ ||fθ (X (te) ) − Y (te) ||2F
796           is the optimal parameter for the test data. Test error indicates the best error a forecaster can
797           achieve on the test data, which measures the capacity computed on the test set.
798   Generalization Error (Non-robustness): The generalization error is defined as:
799
800
                                                                L(gen) = ||fθ(tr) (X (te) ) − Y (te) ||2F .
801           It is the performance measure on the benchmark evaluation, which represents the test set
802           errors of a forecaster that achieves the lowest training error.
803   W Difference (Non-robustness): The W difference is computed as:
804
805
                                                             Diffθ = ||fθ(tr) (X (te) ) − fθ(te) (X (te) )||2F
806                   It computes the mean squared error between best-train-error forecaster prediction and best-
807                   test-error forecaster prediction on the test dataset, where each forecaster is trained in cal-
808                   culating train and test erors.
809
      For all measures, the lower value indicates the greater capacity and robustness.

      E.2      C OMPARISON WITH C HANNEL -I NDEPENDENT MTM
895
896   We evaluate the forecasting performance of ShuffleMTM and PatchTST on two synthetic datasets
897   exhibiting different cross-channel dependencies. Table 9 presents the forecasting performance of
898   ShuffleMTM and PatchTST on two synthetic datasets. We used the same model configuration for
899   both models to ensure a fair comparison. Both models are evaluated in forecasting scenarios with
900   prediction lengths of {32, 64, 96, 192} and a fixed input length of 96, with the patch length set to
901
      16. The results indicate that ShuffleMTM consistently outperforms PatchTST across all prediction
      lengths on the first dataset, demonstrating its ability to capture patch-level dependencies between
902
      channels with lagged structures. This analysis confirms that ShuffleMTM can capture fine-grained
903
      cross-channel dependencies. However, ShuffleMTM exhibits in Table 10 that greater forecasting
904   errors than PatchTST on the second dataset. Since each channel in the second dataset contains long-
905   term trends and lacks local dependencies on other channels, ShuffleMTM is less effective for short-
906   term forecasting. However, as prediction lengths increase, the performance difference between the
907   two models decreases. As the channels share the same long-term context, ShuffleMTM effectively
908   captures the long-term dependence, resulting in enhanced performance in long-term forecasting.
909
      In summary, ShuffleMTM effectively captures fine-grained patch-level dependencies between chan-
910   nels such as lagged dependencies, as shown in the analysis on the first synthetic dataset. In time
911   series that shares global temporal patterns, ShuffleMTM is ineffective in short-term forecasting, if
912   each channel is not dependent on the others in a local context. However, ShuffleMTM becomes
913   effective in long-term forecasting on this data if the channels share the long-term context.
