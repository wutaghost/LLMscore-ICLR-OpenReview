009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the complex dynamics of neural network training remains a central
012            challenge in deep learning research. Work rooted in statistical physics has identified
013            phases and phase transitions in neural network (NN) models, where models within
014            the same phase exhibit similar characteristics but qualitatively differ across phases.
015
               A prominent example is the double-descent phenomenon. Recognizing these
               transitions is essential for building a deeper understanding of model behavior and
016
               the underlying mechanics. So far, these phases are typically studied in isolation
017
               or in specific applications. In this paper, we show that phase transitions are a
018            widespread phenomenon. However, identifying phase transitions across different
019            methods requires populations that cover different phases. For that reason, we
020            introduce Phase Transition Model Zoos, a structured collection of neural networks
021            trained on diverse datasets and architectures. These model zoos are carefully
022            designed to help researchers systematically identify and study phase transitions
023            in their methods. We demonstrate the relevance of phase transitions across
024            multiple applications, including fine-tuning, transfer learning, out-of-distribution
025            generalization, pruning, ensembling, and weight averaging. The diversity of
026
               applications underscores the universal nature of phase transitions and their impact
               on different tasks. By providing the first structured dataset specifically designed
027
               to capture phase transitions in NNs, we offer a valuable tool for the community to
028
               systematically evaluate machine learning methods and improve their understanding
029            of phase behavior across a wide range of applications and architectures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Neural network (NN) research has made considerable progress in recent years. To continue making
034
      sustainable progress in NN research, there is a need for thorough understanding of methods.
035
      Currently, proposed methods are usually evaluated on a few datasets, or on leaderboards with a few
036   models. While it has become standard practice to report performance averaged over several random
037   seeds, these single performance metrics lead to very sparse feedback in a very complex hypothesis
038   and methods space and ultimately a gap in understanding. Addressing this challenge requires
039   systematic evaluation that strategically covers the hyperparameter and model space, demonstrating
040   where a method succeeds or fails in different regimes.
041
042   Identifying Phase Transitions in Neural Networks To identify relevant regimes and necessary
043   hyperparameter variations for method evaluation, phase transitions in neural networks provide
044   a useful perspective. Phase transitions have been studied extensively in the machine learning
045
      literature (Schwarze et al., 1992; Seung et al., 1992; Martin & Mahoney, 2019a;b). Within phases,
      models are relatively homogeneous, with abrupt changes from one phase to the next. One example for
046
      such a phase transition is the double descent (Nakkiran et al., 2019), which describes the transition
047
      from high to low generalization error with increasing model capacity. Other work describes the
048   transitions between where different NN methods perform well or fail: in training (Zhou et al., 2024),
049   ensembling (Theisen et al., 2023), pruning (Zhou et al., 2023), etc. Notably, while phases exist
050   in performance metrics, loss landscape metrics, as well as downstream applications, they do not
051   necessarily overlap. For instance, ensembling reduces the sensitity to training parameters, while
052   pruning benefits from noisier pretraining. Therefore, identifying the phases and phase transitions
053   in methods and how different methods affect them provides a more robust evaluation signal than
      individual data points that may lie in any of the phases.

070   Testing Methods on Populations of Neural Networks Evaluating methods for phase transitions
071   does present its own challenges, however. In particular, many machine learning methods depend
072   on pre-trained models. The phase transitions of these methods depend on the phase transitions
073   of the pre-trained models. The exploration of the phase distribution in these methods requires
074   systematic evaluation over different pre-trained models - model populations. However, the quality
075
      of the feedback and scope of phase exploration depends on the diversity of the population. Most
      freely accessible models are part of large public repositories like Hugging Face (Wolf et al., 2020) or
076
      the PyTorch model hub (Pytorch). Within those collections, however, models are of varying quality
077
      and mostly unstructured, making systematic evaluation a challenge. Structured populations have
078   been published as model zoo datasets (Schürholt et al., 2022b; Croce et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
079   2022; Honegger et al., 2023). These studies, however, consider the diversity of the model in their
080   populations only in terms of their generating factors. Phase transitions are not explicit target for
081   these datasets and therefore only occur incidentally. To systematically evaluate methods for phase
082   transitions in pre-trained models, there is therefore a need for populations with systematic variations
083   that covers a broad range of phases.
084
085   Contributions As a step towards robust evaluation, we present our Phase Transition Model Zoos
086   dataset. The dataset contains populations of trained models, containing ResNet and ViT architectures
087
      of varying sizes, trained on SVHN, CIFAR10, CIFAR100 or TinyImagenet (Netzer et al., 2011;
      Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009; Le & Yang, 2015). For each dataset-architecture combination, we have
088
      carefully trained grids of models with variations s.t. they contain the known phase transitions. We
089
      validate the phase transition with known descriptive loss landscape metrics and annotate the models
090   with them. The model zoos contain a total of 1829 models between 11K and 360M parameters. To
091   the best of our knowledge, this is the largest structured dataset of models.
092
093
      Furthermore, we demonstrate the importance of phase transitions as well as the usefulness of our
      datasets to identify them. We evaluate several fundamental neural network methods on them and
094
      show that phase transitions appear in fine-tuning, transfer-learning, pruning, ensembling and weight
095
      averaging, some of which have to the best of our knowledge not been previously documented. We
096   provide an overview of the results in Figure 1. Ensembling, for instance, is known to improve
097   robustness and decrease noisiness, which manifests in a larger high performance phase compared
098   to pre-trained models. Other methods show phases that distinctly overlap loss landscape metrics.
099   Weight averaging can be expected to improve performance in one regime towards the top of the
100   grid, and not improve performance in the other. This example shows that since different phases
101   behave qualitatively differently, systematic method evaluation should cover these different regimes
102   and identify the phase transitions.
103   The Phase Transition Model Zoos repesents a large collection of models, with the potential of being
104   highly valuable to methods that leverage diverse and structured populations of NNs. In particular,
105   as it is systematically generated to cover the different phases, it can help researchers systematically
106   identify phase transitions in their methods, and comprehensively benchmark those. We hope this will
107   allow for better understanding of the layout of phases in methods, how different methods affect the
      phase distribution, what the underlying mechanics for it are, and how to improve over them.

      2    L OSS LANDSCAPE TAXONOMY

      Phases in Neural Networks loss landscapes The motivation for introducing phases and phase
111   transitions in NN loss landscapes is rooted in statistical mechanics, where such phenomena explain
112   qualitative changes in system behavior (Martin & Mahoney, 2019a). Phases represent distinct
113   regions in the parameter space where the system’s properties are homogeneous or change smoothly,
114   while phase transitions mark abrupt changes in these properties. In NNs, phases manifest in terms
115   of generalization performance. A prominent example for such a phase transition is the double
116   descent pheonmenon (Nakkiran et al., 2019), a phase transition along the load axis (Liao et al., 2020;
117   Derezinski et al., 2020). Similar empirical observations have been made recently on the emergent
118   behaviors of large language models (Wei et al., 2022), in which non-smooth transitions can occur
119
      when some training hyperparameters (such as the model size) are modified. However, it is not
      conclusive whether these emergent behaviors are indeed sharp phase transitions or merely due to
120
      specific ways of experimental measurements (Schaeffer et al., 2024). These phases and transitions
121
      are expected due to the complex, high-dimensional nature of NN optimization, where varying control
122   parameters like data noise and training iterations can lead to qualitatively different behaviors, akin to
123   physical systems undergoing phase changes. Motivated by statistical physics, Martin & Mahoney
124   (2019a) identify two main types of hyperparameters in NN training: the noisiness of the training
125   process, dubbed temperature, and the amount of data relative to the size of the model, dubbed
126   relative load. Using that notion, distinct phases with qualitatively different model properties on
127   the temperature-load landscape can be identified (Yang et al., 2021). Interestingly, the phases and
128   phase transitions can be linked to the structure of the loss landscape (Yang et al., 2021). Specifically,
129   metrics such as the training loss, the sharpness of local minima, and mode connectivity or prediction
130
      similarity computed on the training data can be used to identify the phase of a model. This, in turn,
      allows for inference of model quality and the design of training algorithms that adapt when the phases
131
      change (Zhou et al., 2023).
132
133
      Loss landscape metrics Yang et al. (2021) categorize phases in load-temperature variations
134
      using four metrics. The first metric is the training loss, which evaluates whether the training data
135   is interpolated. The other metrics describe the sharpness of the local minima, the similarity between
136   models trained using different random seeds, and the connectivity between different local minima of
137   the loss landscape. It should be noted that Yang et al. (2021) used a certain set of metrics to measure
138   these loss landscape properties, but there are alternative metrics available. For example, the sharpness
139   of local minima can be measured using adaptive sharpness metrics (Andriushchenko et al., 2023;
140   Kwon et al., 2021), while similarity can be measured using disagreement (Theisen et al., 2023).
141   We define the loss landscape metrics following Yang et al. (2021). Let θ ∈ Rm denote the learnable
142   weight parameter, L be the loss function. We compute metrics using the train set unless stated
143   otherwise.
144

190   3     P HASE TRANSITION MODEL ZOOS
191
192   To create a population of models that covers relevant phases and can be used to evaluate for phase
193   transitions, we train strucured populations of NNs with several architectures on different datasets
194   following the blueprint introduced by Unterthiner et al. (2020). Within each model zoo population,
195   we systematically vary load-like and temperature-like hyperparameters to realize all of the phases.
196   For every model in the zoo, our dataset includes multiple checkpoints (i.e. saved model weights),
197   at different training epochs. We annotate these samples with performance metrics (training and test
198
      loss and accuracy), as well as the loss landscape metrics outlined in Section 2. We further track loss
      and accuracy on train, test, and — if available — validation sets. In the following, we first detail our
199
      model zoo generation scheme. Subsequently, we analyze our models with conventional performance
200
      metrics, but also with loss landscape metrics to quantify the qualitative diversity of our zoos and
201   validate that all of the phases are realized.
202

      3.1   M ODEL ZOOS GENERATION
204
205   We create 10 zoos from combinations between two architectures {ResNet, ViT} of different sizes and
206   four standard computer vision datasets {SVHN, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, TinyImagenet}. Details on
207   the model zoos configurations can be found in Table 1 in the Appendix. We choose ResNet (He et al.,
208   2016) and ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) architectures for the zoos because of their proliferation
209   in computer vision to achieve representative populations. Importantly, ResNet and ViT architectures
210   allow smooth scaling of model width and thus model capacity for the same architecture without the
211   need to adjust the learning scheme. The selection of datasets follows the same logic. We emphasize
212
      that our model zoo blueprint is not limited to either specific architectures nor to computer vision tasks.

214   Previous work identifies the phases on the surface spanned by load-like and temperature-like hyperpa-
215   rameters (Martin & Mahoney, 2019a; Yang et al., 2021) The load-like parameters can be understood
      as the amount training data relative to the model capacity. Temperature represents the noisiness of

246   3.2   P HASES S YSTEMATICALLY E MERGE IN E VERY Z OO
247
248   The model zoos are designed to cover different phases. In the following, we validate phase coverage
249
      by testing for the phases introduced by Yang et al. (2021) summarized in Section 2. Full phase plots
      for all 10 zoos and further details can be found in Appendix A.3. Our experiments demonstrate
250
      that phase transitions are consistently present in the training of neural networks across all models
251
      and datasets evaluated, with the exact phase layout affected by the architecture, dataset, and data
252   augmentation strategies. The specific characteristics of these phases remain consistent with the
253   four-phase taxonomy outlined in previous studies, validating our experiment setup.
254
255
      As illustrated in Figure 3 and in Figures 9-18, the phases manifest clearly in the combination of
      loss landscape metrics such as Hessian trace, mode connectivity, and CKA similarity. In particular,
256
      Phase IV-B, associated with the best test accuracy, is marked by low loss and high generalization
257
      performance. On the ResNet zoos, our results reveal that learning rate decay plays a significant
258   role in shaping the phase distribution. Specifically, decaying the learning rate by 1e4 under cosine
259   annealing increases the area of Phase IV (well-trained regime) while reducing the presence of Phase
260   II (under-trained regime), as the effect of batch size variations diminishes. This may be an indication
261   for why learning rate decay is so successful. Our experiments show, that the phase transitions
262   generalize across different datasets, architectures and training regimes. ViTs trained without strong
263   data augmentation show an interesting additional sharp transition from phase II to phase IV, see
264   Figure 11. Adding strong data augmentation appears to smoothen that transition again, but affects
265   mode connectivity and sharpness, see Figure 14.
266   The presence of the different phases and transitions across all combinations of architectures and
267   datasets studied validates the use of load-like and temperature-like hyperparameters for a systematic
268   evaluation of the training dynamics. By incorporating awareness of phase transitions, training
269   strategies can be better optimized for generalization and robustness, offering a valuable tool for
      improving both research methodologies and practical machine learning applications.

284   Fine-tuning pre-trained models is a widely used technique for

414   Specifically, we average weights in two
415   ways: (i) across 5 epochs within the same
416   model (Wortsman et al., 2022b), (ii) across
417   models within the same temperature-
418
      load cell, which we align using Git
      Re-Basin (Ainsworth et al., 2022).
419
420   Our results reveal distinct phase transitions
421   that correlate with loss landscape metrics
422   (see Figure 8, 9-17). Averaging over
423   epochs improves performance in early
424
      phases (I and II), where the Hessian trace
      is large, while averaging across seeds
425
      using Git Re-Basin alignment negatively impacts performance in Phases I and III, where mode
426
      connectivity is poor.
427
428   These findings underscore the importance of considering the phase of models in weight averaging
429   to ensure reproducibility and robustness. The phase-dependent performance variations suggest that
430
      aligning models purely based on initialization may not be sufficient. Our dataset, with its detailed
      phase annotations, provides a valuable resource for further investigation into how phases influence the
431
      success of weight averaging, enabling more robust and generalizable model combination strategies.

      5     A PPLICATIONS FOR P OPULATION BASED M ETHODS
433
434
      Our dataset offers diverse opportunities for research beyond evaluation and the identification of
435
      phase transitions, particularly for methods that leverage populations of models and could benefit
436   from annotated phase and loss landscape metrics. Here, we outline some of these applications.
437
438   5.1   P OPULATION - BASED TRAINING
439
440   Population-based methods for hyperparameter tuning have shown great promise (Jaderberg et al.,
441   2017; Li et al., 2020), yet they typically rely solely on validation performance for guidance. Our
442   dataset provides loss landscape metrics, such as Hessian trace and mode connectivity, which could be
443   used to guide models toward optimal phases during training. Recent works have demonstrated that
444   leveraging such metrics can improve hyperparameter tuning efficiency and training outcomes (Zhou
445
      et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2018). Our model zoos, with multiple checkpoints and variations, offer a rich
      resource to explore these dynamics and further optimize population-based methods.
446
447
      5.2   M ODEL ANALYSIS
448
449
      Predicting model properties based on weight statistics, without relying on test data, is an emerging
450   field of research (Eilertsen et al., 2020; Unterthiner et al., 2020). Our dataset, which includes detailed
451   annotations of loss landscape metrics across different phases, is ideal for training robust predictors
452   that generalize across diverse model populations. This could lead to improved methods for predicting
453   generalization power (Jiang et al., 2020) or detecting adversarial backdoors (Langosco et al., 2023).
454   Additionally, approximating expensive metrics such as mode connectivity through weight-based
455   predictors could reduce the computational cost of model analysis.
456
457   5.3   W EIGHT GENERATION
458
459
      Learned weight generation is another promising application of our dataset. Recent works have
      explored generative models for neural network weights (Peebles et al., 2022; Schürholt et al., 2022a;
460
      Soro et al., 2024). By conditioning weight generation on phase and loss landscape metrics, as
461
      provided by our model zoos, future methods could produce models that are better aligned with
462   specific target tasks or robustness criteria. Our dataset offers the diversity required to train such
463   methods, moving beyond the relatively homogeneous datasets used in prior work.
464
465
466   6     D ISCUSSION
467
468   Limitations In this work, the Phase Transition Model Zoos are limited to classification models
469   in the computer vision domain. We focus on one domain to achieve better coverage for different
470   computer vision datasets and architectures. Our work presents itself as a first step to make model
471
      zoos comprehensively cover phase transitions for a variety of applications, and we leave its extension
      to other tasks and domains for future work.
472
473
474   Conclusion The Phase Transition Model Zoos represent the largest structured collection of models
475   annotated with detailed loss landscape metrics. With it, we provide the research community with
476
      a powerful tool to explore and benchmark neural network performance across different phases. By
      systematically covering phase transitions, it allows the study of robustness, generalization, and failure
477
      modes of deep learning methods in a much more nuanced, comprehensive and reliable way.
478
479   We demonstrate the relevance of phase transitions by identifying phases in experiments on fine-tuning,
480   transfer learning, pruning, ensembling, and weight averaging. We show that these phases significantly
481   affect performance and that their impact varies from one method to another, offering valuable insights
482
      beyond conventional performance metrics.
483   With this work, we encourage the ML community to leverage phase awareness in their evaluations,
484   moving beyond single-point performance metrics and toward a deeper understanding of model behav-
485   ior. Our dataset offers a foundation for advancing methods in population-based training, model anal-
      ysis, and weight generation, contributing to more robust and generalizable machine learning models.

656   A.1      M ODEL Z OO C ONTENTS
657
658   In the main paper, we described the generation of the model zoos as well as explored their performance
659   and phase information. Here, we detail the contents of the datasets. A model zoo contains a set of
660   trained Neural Network models. For each of the zoos, we fix architecture and task combinations and
661   introduce variations in temperature-like and load-like parameters. We realize temperature variations
662   by varying the batch-size, and load variations by varying the model width. We chose the training
663   parameters and variation range such that the phases and phase transitions described by Yang et.
664   al (Yang et al., 2021) can be observed. We repeat each temperature-load combination with seeds
665   {1, 2, 3} to compute loss landscape metrics and get robust results.
666   For every model sample, there are model state checkpoints at intervals throughout training. The
667   checkpoints are in PyTorch format, which uses pickle to save ordered dicts. We will provide code
668   to convert the checkpoints to framework-neutral file formats. We annotate these samples with
669   performance metrics (training and test loss and accuracy), as well as the loss landscape metrics
670   (hessian eigenvalues, Bézier mode connectivity, CKA similarity). We add additional results like
671   model averaging performance, where applicable to individual models. The model zoos are generated
672
      with ray.tune 1 and largely follow their experiment structure. Each model in a population is
      contained in one folder. Checkpoints are kept in subfolders for the corresponding epochs. Each model
673
      is annotated with a config.json file to re-create the model exactly. Performance metrics are
674
      tracked for every epoch and saved in a results.json file for every model. For a subset of epochs,
675   we add loss-landscape metrics. All model zoos contain full meta-data configs and self-contained
676   Pytorch code, s.t. they can be re-instantiated exactly, re-trained, or fine-tuned. All code to train
677   grids, evaluate, compute loss landscape metrics and model averaging is available alongside the data.
678   Further, we provide code to i) recreate the model zoo datasets, ii) compute loss-landscape metrics, iii)
679   load the models, iv) re-create the figures in the main paper. In order to allow easy use of the dataset,
680   we plan to make adequate PyTorch dataset classes available upon publication.
681   This section will be updated upon dataset publication. Indeed, several statements are intentionally left
682   vague as of now. Our dataset is large, and will require a careful choice on what to include in order to
683   balance the dataset utility with its size. This will influence, in particular, the number of checkpoints
684   we include per model.

690   bility to the community. We fixed the load-temperature grids by exploring the boundary cases first
691   and establishing the presence of phase transitions, then filling in more resolution. We chose batch
692
      size as the temperature-like hyperparameter, and model width as the load-like hyperparameter: they

      A.4   I NTENDED USES
919
920   The dataset is a repository of trained deep-learning models with phase transitions. It is mainly
921   intended to study phase transitions on populations of neural network models. For every model,
922   we include multiple checkpoints, representing different training epochs, to allow for the study of
923   the training procedure. We also provide loss landscape metrics, to allow researchers to relate their
924   findings with the structure of the loss landscape. The dataset is intended to allow researchers to (i)
925   identify phases in different model properties or applications like the weight averaging examples in
926
      the main paper; and (ii) evaluate existing methods that rely on pre-trained models systematically
      on models of different phases, to get a better understanding under which conditions methods can
927
      be expected to perform well. Further examples of applications of our dataset are presented in our
928
      publication: model training, model property prediction, model generation, model combination, etc.
929
930   Please note that this dataset is intended for research on populations of models, not to further improve
931   performance on specific computer vision tasks directly. The models in our zoo were selected for
932
      their diversity in phases, not optimized for performance on their specific datasets; there may exist
      generating factors combinations achieving better performance with similar architectures.
933
934   The dataset is entirely synthetic and does not contain personally identifiable information or offensive
935   content. Authors bear all responsibility in case of violation of rights.
