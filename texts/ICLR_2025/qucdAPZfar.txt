009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The concept of sharpness has been successfully applied to traditional architectures
012            like MLPs and CNNs to predict their generalization. For transformers, however,
013            recent work reported weak correlation between flatness and generalization. We
014            argue that existing sharpness measures fail for transformers, because they have
015            much richer symmetries in their attention mechanism that induce directions in
016            parameter space along which the network or its loss remain identical. We posit that
017            sharpness must account fully for these symmetries, and thus we redefine it on a
018            quotient manifold that results from quotienting out the transformer symmetries,
019
               thereby removing their ambiguities. Leveraging tools from Riemannian geometry,
               we propose a fully general notion of sharpness, in terms of a geodesic ball on the
020
               symmetry-corrected quotient manifold. In practise, we need to resort to approxi-
021
               mating the geodesics. Doing so up to first order yields existing adaptive sharpness
022            measures, and we demonstrate that including higher-order terms is crucial to re-
023            cover correlation with generalization. We present results on diagonal networks
024            with synthetic data, and show that our geodesic sharpness reveals strong correlation
025            for real-world transformers on ImageNet.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Predicting generalization performance of neural networks, i.e., the difference between performance on
031   training data and that on a held-out test set, is an open problem. Metrics predictive of generalization
032   performance are useful because, for example, one can explicitly regularize such a metric during
033   training to improve generalization (as in Foret et al. (2021)), or to study generalization more broadly.
034
      There is a long history of hypotheses relating sharpness and generalization, with conflicting theories
035
      and conflicting evidence (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1994; Andriushchenko et al., 2023). General-
036   ization has been speculated as correlating with sharpness, it has been speculated as correlating with
037   flatness, and recent evidence has indicated that it has little to no correlation whatsoever, especially
038   in the case of transformers. Measures of sharpness have ranged from the trace of the Hessian to
039   worst-case loss in a neighbourhood, and have included adaptive and relative variations (Kwon et al.,
040   2021; Petzka et al., 2021). We wonder whether some of the confusion has resulted from the specificity
041   of the problem these measures have sought to overcome: the issue of parameter rescalings.
042   In contrast, we believe rescaling (Dinh et al., 2017) is a special case of a more pervasive and general
043   obstacle to measuring sharpness accurately, which we address here at a fundamental level and from a
044   principled perspective: the issue of full and continuous parameter symmetries. While the relationship
045   between sharpness and generalization is likely still a complex one, a crucial step towards studying it
046   must be to ensure we are studying the right quantity, and to do this we must overcome the obstacle of
047   symmetry.
048   In addition to discrete permutation symmetries over the parameters, continuous symmetries within
049   the parameter space occur naturally in neural networks (NNs), and are an intrinsic, data-independent
050   property. They emerge from the standard components we stack into larger architectures: normalization
051   layers (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015; Ba et al., 2016; Wu & He, 2018) induce scale invariance on the
052   pre-normalization weights (Salimans & Kingma, 2016); homogeneous activation functions like
053   ReLU introduce re-scaling symmetries between pre- and post-activation weights (Dinh et al., 2017);
      some normalization layers and softmax impose translation symmetries in the preceding layer’s

077   Adaptive flatness accounts for some symmetries, both element-wise and filter-wise, but does not

083
084
      Can we provide a one-fits-many recipe to develop symmetry-invariant quantities for a wider range of
      symmetries?
085
086   We answer this question positively in this paper by proposing a principled approach to eliminate
087   ambiguities stemming from symmetry. Essentially, this boils down to using a geometry that correctly
088   captures the symmetry-imposed equivalence of parameters. We apply concepts from Riemannian
089   geometry to work on the Riemannian quotient manifold implied by a symmetry group (Boumal,
090
      2023, Chapter 9). We thus identify objects on the quotient manifold—like the Riemannian metric
      and gradient—and show how to translate them back to the Euclidean space.
091
092   Our contributions are the following :
093
              (a) We introduce the application of Riemannian geometry (Boumal, 2023) to the study of neural
094               network parameter space symmetry: we propose using geometry from the quotient manifold
095               induced by a symmetry as a general recipe to remove symmetry-induced ambiguities in
096               parameter space. We do so by translating concepts like gradients from the quotient manifold
097               back to the original space through horizontal lifts.
098           (b) We thus propose geodesic sharpness, a novel adaptive sharpness measure. Through Taylor
099               expansions in our refined geometry, we show that (a) symmetries do introduce curvature into
100               the parameter space, and (b) ignoring that curvature results in previous adaptive sharpness
101               measures.
102           (c) We solve geodesic sharpness analytically for diagonal networks, where we find a strong
103               correlation between sharpness and generalization. We apply our approach to the GL(h)
104               symmetry in the attention mechanism, which has been unstudied and is higher-dimensional
105               than previously considered symmetries. We empirically verify our approach on large vision
106               transformers and find a stronger correlation than any previously seen in the literature (that
107               we are aware of) between sharpness and generalization, both for in-distribution and for all
                  distributional shifts we tested.

110
      Symmetry versus reparameterization Recently, Kristiadi et al. (2023) pointed out how to fix
111
      ambiguities stemming from incorrect reparameterization, i.e. a change of variables to a new parameter
112   space. They show that invariance under reparameterization follows by correctly transforming the
113   (often implicitly treated) Riemannian metric, into the new coordinate system. Our work focuses
114   on invariance of the parameter space M under a symmetry group G with action ψ : G × M →
115   M, (g, θ) 7→ ψ(g, θ) that operates on a single parameter space.
116
117   Symmetry teleportation: Other ways to circumvent the ambiguity is to view it as a degree of free-
118   dom and develop adaptation heuristics to improve algorithms which are not symmetry-agnostic (Zhao
119   et al., 2022a).
120
121   Geometric constraints & NN dynamics Some works aim at understanding the footprint of
122   symmetries onto the parameter space, e.g. in terms of geometrical constraints on derivatives like
123   the gradient or Hessian, or conserved quantities preserved throughout training (Kunin et al., 2021).
124   We choose a different approach in this work and aim at eliminating the ambiguity stemming from a
125   symmetry in a principled manner. Our approach is to take the parameter space’s quotient w.r.t. the
126   underlying symmetry group. While it reduces to many of the proposed post-hoc ‘fixes’ for simpler,
127   well-studied, symmetries like GL(1), we illustrate this approach for larger symmetry group that is
128
      prevalent in the attention mechanism of modern neural network architectures. This symmetry is
      GL(h) with h denoting the attention head’s dimension, and removes more degrees of freedom than
129
      previously studied symmetries.
130
131   Kunin et al. (2021) study the impact of continuous differentiable parameter space symmetries on
132   the Euclidean gradient, Hessian, and gradient flow dynamics. They show that such symmetries
133   lead to geometrical constraints on the Euclidean derivatives, which themselves lead to conserved
134
      quantities throughout gradient flow training. They focus on one-dimensional symmetries, such as
      translation, scale, and re-scale symmetry, whose groups are isomorphic to R or R+ . Their approach
135
      is based on augmenting a symmetric function f : M → R to F = f ◦ ψ : M × G → R with
136
137
      F g = f ∀θ ∈ M. The geometric constraints follow from differentiation in the ‘augmented space’,
138   which leads to interactions between the parameter space M and the symmetry group G.
139   This is different to our approach which tackles the reverse direction. We consider the geometry on the
140   quotient space induced by a symmetry group. This is akin to restricting the function f M/G : M → R
141   to the quotient space rather than an augmented space. We then ‘lift’ objects that reside in the quotient
142   space and feature its more sophisticated, symmetry-aware geometry, back into the original space
143   where computations happen. This should be seen as a form of symmetry correction to the Euclidean
144   objects, e.g. the gradient or Hessian.
145
      Our approach can also be applied to the one-dimensional symmetries studied by Kunin et al. (2021).
146
      We go beyond those cases by considering a higher-dimensional variant of re-scale symmetry, rep-
147   resented by the group of invertible matrices GL. To the best of our knowledge, this symmetry has
148   not been studied in the context of neural networks, although it is present in the attention mechanism
149   of large language models. We believe that the principled approach provided by quotient manifolds
150   enables a general treatment of symmetries, and allows going beyond one-dimensional cases that lead
151   to a more aggressive dimensionality reduction.

192   3.1   S YMMETRIES IN N EURAL N ETWORKS
193
194   Here, we give a brief overview and make more concrete the notion of NN symmetries, focusing on
195   those previously studied in Kunin et al. (2021).
196   Those symmetries lead to rather small effective dimensionality reduction as they are often of GL(1)
197   or GL+ (1), but they can still impact the network behaviour considerably. Let θ denote the parameters
198   of a neural network, 1A a binary mask, and 1¬A its complement such that their sum is a vector of
199   ones, 1A + 1¬A = 1. Let θA :− θ ⊙ 1A with ⊙ the element-wise product. Further, let A1,2 be
200   two disjoint subsets, A1 ∩ A2 = ∅ with masks 1A1 , 1A2 . Then we have the following common
201   symmetries, characterized by their symmetry group G, such that for any g ∈ G the parameter ψ(g, θ)
202   represents the same function as θ:
203          • Translation: ψ(α, θ) = 1A ⊙ α + θ with α ∈ Rh
204          • Scaling: ψ(α, θ) = αθA + θ¬A with α ∈ R>0
205          • Re-scaling: ψ(α, θ) = αθA1 + 1/αθA2 + θ¬(A1 ∨A2 ) with α ∈ R>0
206
207   Their associated groups are G = Rh , GL+ (1), GL+ (1), respectively. In practise, there may be
208
      multiple symmetries acting onto disjoint parts of the parameter space. Note that the re-scaling
      symmetry is essentially the symmetry that adaptive sharpness corrects for.

      7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, motivated by the success of adaptive sharpness measures in the study of generalization,
      we propose a novel adaptive sharpness measure: geodesic sharpness. We frame it in the context of Rie-
534
      mannian geometry and provide a one-size-fits-all recipe for including various parameter symmetries
535
      in the calculation of sharpness. We find that the symmetries introduce curvature into the parameter
536   space and that by ignoring that curvature we recover traditional adaptive sharpness measures. We
537   analytically investigate our measure on widely studied diagonal networks and empirically verify our
538   approach on large scale transformers, finding a strong correlation between geodesic sharpness and
539   generalization.

      Tangent vectors on the total space M, embedded in a vector space E can be viewed as tangent vectors
734   to E, but the tangent space of the quotient manifold, M is not as straightforward. First, note that
735   any element ξ¯ ∈ Tx̄ M that satisfies Dπ(x̄)[ξ]
                                                   ¯ = ξ (where D is the differential) is a candidate for a
736   representation of ξ ∈ Tx M. These aren’t unique, and as we wish to work without any numerical
737   ambiguity we introduce the notions of the vertical and horizontal spaces:
738   Definition B.2. For a quotient manifold M = M/ ∼, the vertical space at x̄ ∈ M is the subspace
739   Vx̄ = Tx̄ F = ker Dπ(x) where F = {ȳ ∈ M : ȳ ∼ x̄} is the fiber of x̄. The complement of Vx̄ is
740   the horizontal space at x̄: Tx̄ M = Vx̄ ⊕ Hx̄ .
741
      Definition B.3. There is only one element ξ¯x̄ that belongs to Hx̄ and satisfies Dπ(x̄)[ξ¯x̄ ] = ξ. This
742
      unique vector is called the horizontal lift of of ξ at x̄. We denote the operator that affects the procedure
743
      by liftx̄ (·) When the ambient space, E is a subset of Rn×p , the horizontal space can also be seen as
744   such a subset, providing a convenient matrix representation of a priori abstract tangent vectors of M.

      B.4    R IEMANNIAN M ANIFOLD
747
748   We give our total space M a smooth inner product over its tangent vectors to give a Riemannian
749   manifold.
750   Definition B.4. A Riemannian manifold is a pair (M, g), where M is a smooth manifold and g is a
751   Riemannian metric, defined as the inner product on the tangent space Tx M for each point x ∈ M,
752   gx (·, ·) : Tx M × Tx M → R. We also use the notation ⟨·, ·⟩x to denote the inner product.
753
754   Note that this definition is not as arcane as it may appear since any smooth manifold admits a
755   Riemannian metric, and we can consider the space of parameters of most neural architectures as
      constituting a smooth manifold, admitting at least a simple, Euclidean, metric.

1226   In general, in non-linear networks there is a tendency towards low-rank representations, which might
1227   make Assumption 5.1 seem excessive and counter to realistic situations. However, while the learned
1228          T
       WQ WK    tend to be low-rank, WQ and WK (on which Assumption 5.1 ought to apply) themselves
1229   are usually high/full (column) rank Yu & Wu (2023).

1233   H.2                          R ELAXATION
1234
1235   Due to the definition of metric 15, we need to invert matrices of the type of WQT WQ . When these
1236   are not full-rank, numerical stability can suffer. Due to floating-point precision rounding errors, in
1237   practice WQT WQ is always invertible, but sometimes the inverted matrices have huge singular values.
1238   To combat this, we introduce a relaxation parameter, so that WQT WQ → WQT WQ + ϵIh , which
1239   dampens the resulting singular values. Although we cannot take it to be exactly zero, as long as it is
1240   small enough, numerical stability is improved and the results remain roughly the same. We study the
1241   effects of varying this parameter on our results empirically below (Figure 11), using the same setup
       as in Section 5.3.2. The results are not significantly affected by the variation of this parameter.
