009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph embedding is a critical pre-processing step that maps elements of a graph
012            network, such as its nodes or edges, to coordinates in a d-dimensional space. The
013            primary goal of the embedding process is to capture and preserve various features
014            of the graph network, including its topology and node attributes, in the generated
015            embedding. Maintaining these graph features in the embedding can significantly
016            enhance the performance of the downstream machine learning tasks. In this work,
017            we introduce a novel family of graph embedding methods that leverage kinematics
018
               principles within a spring model and n-body simulation framework to generate
               the graph embedding. The proposed method differs substantially from state-of-
019
               the-art (SOTA) methods, as it does not attempt to fit a model (such as neural
020
               networks) and eliminates the need for functions such as message passing or back-
021            propagation. Instead, it aims to position the nodes in the embedding space such
022            that the total net force of the system is reduced to a minimal threshold, resulting
023            in the system reaching an equilibrium state. The spring model is designed as a
024            linear summation of non-linear force functions, with the shortest-path distance
025            serving as the adjusting parameter for the force factor between each node pair,
026            and therefore, inducing the graph topology in the force functions. In this work,
027            we attempted to reduce the complexity of the original algorithm from log(n2 )
028            to n log(n), while maintaining the performance metrics at a competitive level.
029
               The proposed method is intuitive, parallelizable, and highly scalable. While the
               primary focus of this work is on the feasibility of the force-directed approach, the
030
               results in unsupervised graph embeddings are comparable to or better than SOTA
031
               methods, demonstrating its potential for practical applications.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graphs have become the go-to data structure for representing complex systems and relationships
037   between data entities Wu et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021). A graph, denoted as G(V, E) is comprised
038   of a set of n nodes denoted as V = {u1 , u2 , ..., un }, and the set of edges connecting some node
039   pairs and denoted as E = {(ui , uj )}, such that ui , uj ∈ V. Graph embedding is the task of mapping
040   graph elements down to a vector space with d dimensions, such that d ≪ n. It has gained significant
041   attention in recent years due to the emergence of big data and advancements in machine learning
042   and deep learning techniques for graph representation learning.
043   In this paper, we propose a new family of graph embedding methods, dubbed Force-Directed embed-
044   ding, based on the principles of motion physics and Newton’s second law and a n-body simulation
045   scheme. By treating graph nodes as objects with mass that exert forces on each other and using
046   shortest-path distance between each pair as a parameter for determining the magnitude of the force
047   factor, we aim to map the graph elements to a vector space while preserving the graph’s topological
048   features. The force-directed spring model employed in this approach converges to a state where the
049   vector representation of nodes in the embedding space reflects their relative distances in the graphs
050
      as well as various graph features such as nodes clusters.
051   Unlike the conventional methods, we don’t fit a function based on a loss metric. Instead, we deploy
052   an iterative process to calculate the gradient of embedding, and update the node embeddings. There-
053   fore, the proposed method does not need backward pass to fit parameters of a function and provides
      a performance advantage.

      The proposed paradigm has an intuitive nature and is highly parallelizeable. By leveraging well-
055   established principles from physics and the mathematics of the n-body problem, this approach ex-
056   plores a new avenue for graph embedding. A proof of convergence for this Force-Directed method
057   was proposed in Lotfalizadeh & Al Hasan (2024) which also indicated the constraints for conver-
058   gence. In this paper, we extend the work at Lotfalizadeh & Al Hasan (2023) and reduce the com-
059   plexity of the algorithm by limiting the number of calculations to the forces between node pairs in a
060   limited subset, while maintaining the quality of the embedding at a competitive level.
061   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II discusses related work, Section III
062   presents the proposed Force-Directed graph embedding paradigm, Section IV details the experimen-
063   tal setup and results, and Section V concludes the paper and outlines future research directions.

066   2   R ELATED W ORKS
067
068   Existing graph embedding techniques can be broadly categorized into several types based on their
069   approach to capturing the structure and features of the graph. Walk-based methods, such as Deep-
070   Walk Perozzi et al. (2014) and node2vec Grover & Leskovec (2016), generate embeddings by con-
071   ducting random walks across the graph. Deep learning-based methods leverage graph neural net-
072   works (GNNs) to learn representations of graph vertices or entire graphs. Notable GNN approaches
073   include Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) Kipf & Welling (2016a); Chen et al. (2020), Graph-
074
      SAGE Hamilton et al. (2017), Graph Attention Networks (GATs) Velickovic et al. (2017), and Vari-
      ational Graph Auto-Encoders (VGAEs) Kipf & Welling (2016b). These methods incorporate both
075
      local graph topology and node features to learn expressive embeddings. Spectral-based methods
076
      Zhang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2018) aim to capture global graph properties into the node embeddings
077   by utilizing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian to embed nodes in a way that
078   preserves global graph properties. Matrix factorization methods Qiu et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2008)
079   capture the graph structure through decomposing the adjacency matrix or other matrix representa-
080   tions of a graph into lower-dimensional matrices. These methods aim to preserve node connectivity,
081   community structure, and node centrality in the lower-dimensional representation.
082
      Force-Directed approaches have been widely employed for graph visualization purposes Eades
083   (1984); Fruchterman & Reingold (1991); Kamada et al. (1989). These algorithms model the graph
084   as a physical system, where nodes are treated as particles and edges as springs or forces between
085   the particles, aiming to find a layout that minimizes the energy of the system. Advancements in
086   Force-Directed graph drawing algorithms Barnes & Hut (1986); Walshaw (2001); Hu (2005) have
087   enabled the visualization of larger and more complex graphs while preserving aesthetic properties
088   such as symmetry, uniform edge lengths, and minimal edge crossings.

093   Force-Directed graph embedding is inspired by the principles of motion physics. In this approach,
094   nodes are taken as objects with mass that can relocate in the embedding space under the influence
095

097

098   is calculated by diving the net force on node u, Fu , by its mass. In this setting, the degree of a node

201   4     T HE P ROPOSED M ETHOD FOR R EDUCING THE C OMPLEXITY
202
203   In this section, we present a stochastic method to reduce the complexity of Force-Directed method
204   by limiting the number of force computations to a limited subset of node pairs. While grouping
205   the nodes into k-hop neighborhoods enhances performance metrics, the process is still computa-
206   tionally expensive at O(n2 ). The proposed method decreases the complexity of the Force-Directed
207   embedding method to O(n∆(G)k + n log n), such that k ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4}.
208
209   4.1   T HE I DEA
210
211   The proposed idea is to calculate the net force on node u from a limited number of nodes, denoted
212   here by V(u). This set is comprised of the k-ball centered at u and a maximum of m nodes beyond
213   the k-ball. In other words, we calculate the forces from all the nodes at a maximum of k-hops dis-
214   tance from u, and m random nodes at a further distance. In our experiments, we let m = O(log n).
215   In equation 10, Bk (u) is the k-ball centered at u, and Rm,k is a set of a maximum of m nodes,
      sampled randomly from V, without substitution, and not in the k-ball.

233   4.2      T HE R ATIONALE
234
      The logic behind definitions for V(u) and the updated force functions is that to reflect the topolofy
235   of the graph in local and global granularity. By enforcing attraction and repulsion on u from all
236   the nodes in a close proximity, we can reflect the local topology of the graph in a short Euclidean
237   proximity. On the other hand, enforcing repulsion from distant nodes helps with avoiding folding of
238   the distant clusters into close vicinity of u and reflecting global structure of the graph.
