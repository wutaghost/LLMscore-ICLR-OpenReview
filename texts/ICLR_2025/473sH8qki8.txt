009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           This paper explores a reward-based policy to achieve zero-shot transfer between
012           source and target environments with completely different observation spaces.
013           While humans can demonstrate impressive adaptation capabilities, deep neural
014           network policies often struggle to adapt to a new environment and require a con-
015           siderable amount of samples for successful transfer. Instead, we propose a novel
016           reward-based policy only conditioned on rewards and actions, enabling zero-shot
017           adaptation to new environments with completely different observations. We dis-
018
              cuss the challenges and feasibility of a reward-based policy and then propose a
              practical algorithm for training. We demonstrate that a reward policy can be
019
              trained within three different environments, Pointmass, Cartpole, and 2D Car
020
              Racing, and transferred to completely different observations, such as different
021           color palettes or 3D rendering, in a zero-shot manner. We also demonstrate that a
022           reward-based policy can further guide the training of an observation-based policy
023           in the target environment.

042   1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Humans are known for their remarkable ability to adapt to completely new environments. For exam-
045   ple, once gamers master a racing game, they can adapt rapidly to new games with entirely different
046   visuals and themes. People can also navigate their surroundings in completely new cities despite
047   unfamiliar environments. Therefore, researchers have investigated the development of intelligent
048   agents that are capable of rapid adaptation in unseen environments. However, neural network agents
049   trained with deep reinforcement learning (deep RL) typically struggle in unfamiliar environments,
050
      even when trained with massive data. Therefore, developing robust agents that perform well in un-
      seen environments remains an open challenge in various fields, such as machine learning, artificial
051
      intelligence, and robotics.
052
053   This paper discusses the problem of rapid adaptation from a source problem to a target problem,
      where significant changes occur only in observation spaces. This change in observation reflects


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      various adaptation scenarios, such as altering color palettes, changing screen resolutions, switching
055   rendering engines from OpenGL to Unreal Engine, or transitioning from 2D to 3D visualizations, all
056   without making assumptions. We also assume that ground-truth state information is not accessible
057   to the policy in the target environment, following the definition of a Partially Observable Markov
058   Decision Process (POMDP). This assumption reflects situations where the internal programming
059   variables of a game or the ground-truth hardware states of a robot are inaccessible to the control
060   policy.
061   Our key hypothesis is that a reward can serve as a strong signal for rapid adaptation if it is sufficiently
062   dense. This work explores the development of a reward-based policy that takes only rewards as input,
063   disregarding observations. In the context of computer games, this would be analogous to playing a
064   game solely by observing the score. If successful, our reward-based agent could be deployed across
065   different environments with any visual renderings in a zero-shot manner. We note that assuming
066   the availability of rewards to the agent is somewhat unconventional. However, this assumption is
067   not entirely unreasonable, as POMDPs provide per-step rewards to deep RL algorithms anyway.
068   Furthermore, we will later relax this assumption by demonstrating that rewards can be more easily
069
      estimated from observations than high-dimensional state vectors.
070   In this work, we aim to explore the novel concept of a reward-based policy for rapid transfer, which
071   is only conditioned on the history of rewards and actions without observations. First, we discuss
072   our problem assumptions, such as significant changes in observation spaces while other MDP com-
073   ponents remain the same. Then, we introduce a reward-based policy, followed by discussions on
074   its difficulties, feasibility, requirements, and sub-optimal behaviors. Finally, we propose a practi-
075
      cal framework for training a reward-based policy, which is enabled by two key components: (1) a
      temporal history of the reward/action pairs and (2) the guidance of an expert policy.
076
077   We demonstrate that the proposed framework can successfully train a reward-based policy in three
078   environments: Pointmass, Cartpole, and Car Racing. The learned reward-based policies exhibit rea-
079   sonable behavior in all three environments, achieving approximately 60% to 90% of the performance
080   of standard observation-based policies. However, due to their independence from observations, they
081
      can be transferred to novel environments with significant observation shifts in a zero-shot manner,
      including palette swaps or even 2D-to-3D transfers. If the reward function is unavailable, we can
082
      rapidly learn a reward estimator for rapid transfer, which performs significantly better than training
083
      a state estimator. Lastly, we will conduct an ablation study on the effect of expert guidance.

086   2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Transfer learning in RL leverages existing information for rapid adaptation in a new scenario, which
089   has been approached by a wide range of algorithms. One possible approach is learning from demon-
090   stration (Schaal, 1996), where expert demonstrations are available to guide the learning process.
091   Offline methods can use these demonstrations for offline RL (Ma et al., 2019), (Yang & Nachum,
092   2021), (Li et al., 2023) and for pretraining to learn value functions or policies (Silver et al., 2016),
093
      (Stachowicz et al., 2023). Online methods use expert demonstrations to improve policy exploration
      during learning, and include policy iterations (Piot et al., 2014), (Chemali & Lazaric, 2015), policy
094
      gradients (Nair et al., 2018), (Kang et al., 2018), and Q-learning approaches (Brys et al., 2015),
095
      (Hester et al., 2018).
096
097   Another way to transfer knowledge is representation learning, which establishes reusable represen-
098   tations in the action or state spaces that can be shared between source and target environments.
099
      Progressive networks (Rusu et al., 2016) and PathNet (Fernando et al., 2017) directly train common
      reusable representations and leverage them during policy training. There exists a line of works that
100
      split networks into general and reusable modules (Andreas et al., 2017), (Devin et al., 2017). Gupta
101
      et al. (2017) learns an invariant feature space in order to better transfer between tasks. Bou-Ammar
102   & Taylor (2011), Taylor et al. (2007) learn inter-task mappings to deal with state differences be-
103   tween tasks. Successor representations (Dayan, 1993) seek to separate the environment dynamics
104   from the reward function. These have seen widespread use (Barreto et al., 2018), (Zhang et al.,
105   2017), (Konidaris & Barto, 2006) for transferring knowledge for different tasks with the same state
106   and action spaces. Zhang et al. (2018) does a similar approach of separating dynamics and reward
107   for learning a better policy for transfer. We avoid the need for dealing with different observations
      between the source and target environments by using the shared reward function as the policy input.

108
      The most relevant method of transfer for our work is to transfer at the policy level. A popular method
109   to transfer involves distilling information from an expert policy. This method uses supervised learn-
110   ing to match action distributions between the student and teacher policies, and is widely used for
111   robotic applications (Kumar et al., 2021), (Liang et al., 2023), (Miki et al., 2022). Policies can also
112   be directly reused for updating policies in a transfer setting, as in (Barreto et al., 2017), (Fernández
113   & Veloso, 2006), (Rajendran et al., 2017). This is the closest domain to our method, which directly
114   transfers an expert policy via reward information.
115   Another very similar sounding work to ours is the reward-conditioned policy (Kumar et al., 2019).
116   However, this work trains standard observation-based policies with an additional reward condition-
117   ing that allows for using suboptimal trajectories as optimal supervision. This is in contrast to our
118   work which uses no observation feedback and uses the reward directly as the policy input.

      3     R EWARD - BASED P OLICY
122
      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
123
124   A Partially Observable Markov decision process (POMDP) is a popular tool for modeling a sequen-
125   tial decision problem. It is defined as a tuple (S, O, A, R, T ), where S is the state space, O is the
126   observation space, A is the action space, T is the transition function that defines how states change
127   given an action, and R is the reward function. The policy π : O 7→ A takes action based on the given
128   observation without knowing the underlying state s ∈ S, and we want to find an optimal policy that
                                                                            PT
129   maximizes the discounted cumulative sum of the reward over time: t=0 γ t R(st , at ).
130
      Our scenario defines the transfer problem as training a reusable policy from a source to a target en-
131
      vironment. We assume the source and target environments should have the same transition, reward,
132   action, and state but may have very different observation spaces. Therefore, when the source en-
133   vironment is described as a tuple (S, O, A, R, T ), the target environment becomes (S, Õ, A, R, T ),
134
      where there is no assumption between O and Õ. For all environments we consider, we assume the
135   availability of a dense reward, which makes the training of a reward-based policy feasible.
136
137
      3.2   R EWARD - BASED P OLICY: D EFINITION AND P ROPERTIES
138
139   Our paper investigates a novel reward-based policy π : R × A 7→ A, which takes only the previ-
140   ous rewards and actions as input. Our key intuition is that a typical observation-based policy for
141   transfer inevitably requires an additional training process to map the observations from the target
142   environment to match those of the source environment, which requires considerable training sam-
143   ples. However, a reward-based policy can immediately be deployed in a zero-shot fashion if the
144   underlying system dynamics remain the same.
145   Difficulties. Learning a policy from only reward information is more difficult than using a standard
146   observation-based approach. This is because the reward is a scalar projection of the, in general, n-
147   dimensional state space that governs the dynamics of the task. This reduction in information makes
148   the learning task much more challenging as the partial observability makes estimating the value and
149   advantage functions more difficult. Exploration is also negatively impacted by the lower information
150   feedback. If we have sparse rewards, the problem is even worse, as there can be large periods with
151   no reward.
152   Feasibility. Despite these difficulties, it is still possible to train a reward-based policy for at least
153   some simple environments. Let us consider a simple 1D Pointmass environment, where the reward
154   is defined as a negative distance to the origin. In this environment, a reward-based agent can easily
155   estimate its current position from the given reward if it correctly determines the sign by checking
156   the previous action and reward changes. Even in N D Pointmass environments, we can design an
157   analytical algorithm similar to a triangulation method that determines the location from a set of angle
158
      measurements. Therefore, it seems to be true that we can train a reward-based policy using off-the-
      shelf algorithms; it just takes a nearly exponential amount of time with respect to the difficulty of
159
      the task.
160

194   Requirement for Dense Rewards. Another significant consideration is the requirement for a dense
195
      reward function. Because the reward is being used as a signal for solving the MDP, it should be a
      dense function that gives a good value throughout the state space, while a sparse reward would be
196
      much worse in general and often impossible. Let us consider the Pointmass problem again. If the
197
      reward is sparse and only given at the goal, the agent will not receive any meaningful learning signal
198   and will be unable to learn any useful behaviors.
199
200   Differences in Optimal Behaviors. Also important to note is the inherent difference in behavior
201
      between the observation-based and reward-based policies. Let us consider a 2D Pointmass envi-
      ronment. Given this very simple environment, an observation-based agent with direct access to the
202
      states x and y can easily learn the perfect actions to take to reach the goal directly. However, the
203
      reward-based agent will not be able to make an optimal action at the beginning because the only
204   information that is available immediately from a single reward/action pair is the distance to the
205   goal. The agent must take at minimum two information-gathering actions to localize itself from
206   the history; then, it can take optimal actions based on the localization result. Given this suboptimal
207   behavior for this very simple environment, it is fair to assume that learning will become much more
208   challenging in more complicated environments.

210   3.3      P RACTICAL F RAMEWORK FOR L EARNING A R EWARD - BASED P OLICY
211
212   Despite the discussed difficulties, we found that two key features can effectively improve the training
213   process of a reward-based policy:
214
              1. Temporal history of the reward/action pairs.
215
              2. Guidance of an expert policy, such as an observation-based agent.

228   To handle the temporal history, we first give the previous reward/action pair as inputs to a standard
229   MLP for feature extraction. These extracted features are then passed to a recurrent network. We
230   adopt an LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) as a network architecture, but any recurrent net-
231   work should perform similarly. The output of the LSTM is given as an input for the policy and value
232   networks, which are trained with RL using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al.,
233   2017), using code modified from Huang et al. (2022). It is essential for control that some recurrent
234   network is used for the inputs. In our experience, a single reward/action pair was not sufficient for
235
      effective control in most environments. On the other hand, the temporal history from the LSTM
      allows agents to estimate complicated contexts, particularly in more complex environments.
236
237   While a temporal history is necessary for control, we find that it is not sufficient for effective learn-
238   ing in practice. This degraded learning can be because a new highest reward record implies an
239   unseen situation to the agent. To improve policy learning, we include an additional expert guidance
240   loss along with the PPO loss from RL, where we train observation-based experts using a standard
241
      reinforcement learning algorithm. This loss is a supervised loss to regress the current policy ac-
      tions to the actions of the expert policy. The full loss for training is LP P O + Lguidance , where
242
      Lguidance = (at − a∗t )2 .

      4.3        Z ERO - SHOT T RANSFER VIA D IRECT R EWARD ACCESS
312
313   Assuming we have access to the reward at test time, one intuitive application of our method is
314   zero-shot transfer of a policy under observation shifts. If we take any of our environments and do
315   a color palette swap, either randomly chosen or intentionally designed to be difficult, our method
316   can transfer seamlessly. On the other hand, a standard observation-based policy would likely need
317   to be retrained for each specific grouping of colors. Example color shifts for our environments are
318   illustrated in Figure 6 and the effectiveness of our policy for the original and modified environments
319   is demonstrated in Table 1.
320   Another application we tested is for transferring a Car Racing policy. However, instead of a rel-

322   2D visualization of the Gymnasium Car Racing environment to the photorealistic 3D render from
323   Microsoft AirSim (Shah et al., 2017). These two environments are semantically similar and have
      the same action spaces and rewards. However, the dynamics are very different, which breaks our

357   4.4   R APID T RANSFER U SING E STIMATED R EWARD
358
359   In some circumstances, we may not have access to the reward at inference time. We demonstrate that
360   our method can still provide benefits in these scenarios. For the DMC Cartpole task, we examine
361   the problem of transferring a policy when dealing with an image-based observation. Assuming
362   access to a trained state-based policy and reward-based policy, we seek to learn an encoder that can
363
      map the image to the states and the rewards so that the policy can be directly reused in the target
      image domain. With each policy, we collect 100000 samples from the target image domain and use
364
      supervised learning to train an encoder to map to the original policy domain. For the given amount
365
      of data, we show that the reward can be effectively estimated and our reward-based policy can be
366   deployed with a small performance decrease while the state encoder fails to accurately estimate the
367   state and performs as effectively as a random policy. These results are shown in Table 2. A reward-
368   based policy with the learned estimator still works well even when the reward is not available, which
369   is much better than a state-based policy with a learned state estimator. This result demonstrates the
370   effectiveness of using rewards for transfer, even if the performance of a reward-based policy in the
371   source environment is suboptimal.
372   We hypothesize that our method works better as it is easier to estimate the 1 dimensional reward than
373   it is the higher dimensional (five, in this case) state as it is less susceptible to noise and overfitting.
374   This matches other works’ observations on using low-dimensional latent spaces for control rather
375   than the full state space. While we could perhaps get a similar result with a trained 1D latent space,
376   we think our method is better as the reward is an inherent component of the MDP and requires no
377   training. In addition, a reward is interpretable as we know what the reward is and it also offers direct,
      zero-shot transfer for environments with the same reward.

409   4.5        A BLATION ON E XPERT G UIDANCE
410
411
      To validate the design choice of including expert state-based guidance during training, we compare
      reward-based policies trained with and without guidance in Figure 7. This shows that for the Point-
412
      mass and Cartpole, extra guidance gives a small performance boost, while for the Car Racing task,
413
      it achieves much higher rewards. This is possibly caused by the relative difficulty of the tasks, as
414   in both the Pointmass and Cartpole tasks failure to effectively act just reduces the maximum reward
415   but the agent can try again, whereas for the Car Racing task, failure to maintain position on the track
416   terminates the episode.

419   5         C ONCLUSION
420
421   This paper presents a novel approach to solving MDPs using policies with only rewards and actions
422   as inputs. We examine the feasibility and practical methods for learning such reward-based poli-
423   cies, requiring temporal context provided by a recurrent network architecture and expert guidance
424   provided by an observation-based expert policy. We show the benefits of such policies for zero-shot
425   transfer among environments with the same dynamics and different observations, assuming direct
426   access to the reward at inference time. We also demonstrate the potential usefulness of our method
427   even when we do not have access to the reward and must estimate it.
428   There are several directions for future work. In this work, we assume identical transition functions
429   between the source and target environments to focus on observation shifts. However, a reward-
430   based policy could potentially handle changes in dynamics by augmenting its training process with
431   common transfer techniques such as domain randomization or system identification. Additionally,
      we evaluate the concept of a reward-based policy in relatively simple environments. It would be

      interesting to explore their potential in more realistic domains, such as real-world robotic control or
433   autonomous navigation.
