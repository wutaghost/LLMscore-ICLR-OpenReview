011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models are often challenged by generating erroneous or ‘hal-
014            lucinated’ responses, especially in complex reasoning tasks. To mitigate this,
015            we propose a retrieval augmented reasoning method, FiDeLiS, which enhances
016            knowledge graph question answering by anchoring responses to structured, ver-
017            ifiable reasoning paths. FiDeLiS uses a keyword-enhanced retrieval mechanism
018
               that fetches relevant entities and relations from a vector-based index of KGs to
               ensure high-recall retrieval. Once these entities and relations are retrieved, our
019
               method constructs candidate reasoning paths which are then refined using a step-
020
               wise beam search. This ensures that all the paths we create can be confidently
021            linked back to KGs, ensuring they are accurate and reliable. A distinctive feature
022            of our approach is its blend of natural language planning with beam search to
023            optimize the selection of reasoning paths. Moreover, we redesign the way rea-
024            soning paths are scored by transforming this process into a deductive reasoning
025            task, allowing the LLM to assess the validity of the paths through deductive rea-
026            soning rather than traditional logit-based scoring. This helps avoid misleading
027            reasoning chains and reduces unnecessary computational demand. Extensive ex-
028            periments demonstrate that our method, even as a training-free method which
029
               has lower computational costs and superior generality, outperforms established
               strong baselines across three datasets. The code of this paper will be released at
030
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FiDELIS-E7FC.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The emergence and application of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI,
037   2023; LLaMa-v3, 2024) have attracted widespread attention from researchers and the general public.
038   They demonstrate remarkable reasoning capabilities, managing to solve complex reasoning problems
039
      through step-by-step thinking and planning (Wang et al., 2022b; Wei et al., 2022b). However, the
      reasoning of LLMs is not always reliable and may conflict with factual reality (Pan et al., 2024),
040
      limiting their application in areas requiring high reliability, such as healthcare (He et al., 2023) and
041
      science (Taylor et al., 2022).
042
043   Knowledge graphs (KGs) store high-quality knowledge in structured triplets, such as Wikidata (Vran-
044   dečić & Krötzsch, 2014), YAGO (Suchanek et al., 2007), and NELL (Carlson et al., 2010). KGs
045
      offer structured, explicit, and up-to-date factual knowledge, including domain-specific knowledge,
      providing a faithful knowledge source for reasoning. Moreover, each piece of information in KGs
046
      can be traced back to its source, providing context and provenance. This traceability not only aids in
047
      verifying the reliability of the information but also provides clear pathways of reasoning, making
048   the interpretation process transparent. Due to its reliability and interpretability, it is considered as
049   a promising method to improve the reliability of LLM reasoning. Therefore, several attempts have
050   been conducted to integrate KGs with language models (Pan et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Hu et al.,
051   2023; Sun et al., 2023). Among them, knowledge graph question answering (KGQA) is a critical
052   task to verify the effectiveness of incorporating the knowledge from KGs into reasoning models (He
053   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022). However, effectively integrating KGs into reasoning
      models for KGQA presents several challenges, including data sparsity, the complexity of query

054
      interpretation, and the need for advanced inference capabilities. More specifically, we focus on two
055   main questions concerning the integration of KGs with reasoning models as follows:
056
057   (I) How to retrieve specific knowledge from KGs to allow more precise reasoning? Existing
058
      solutions include direct retrieval (Sun et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2022) and semantic parsing (Sun
      et al., 2020; Gu & Su, 2022). Direct retrieval utilize the query to find relevant knowledge triplets
059
      within the KGs. However, these triplets sometimes lack comprehensive semantic information due to
060
      variations in KG schema, such as entities being represented as machine identifiers (MIDs), rather
061   than descriptive labels. This lack of semantic richness can obscure the relevance of the retrieved
062   information with the user query, and becomes especially tricky in multi-hop question answering,
063   where seemingly unrelated intermediate triplets may be pivotal in deriving the correct answer. On
064   the other hand, semantic parsing aim to transform user queries into executable structured queries
065   (e.g., SPARQL), which are then run on KGs. However, these methods often grapple with issues
066   related to the non-executability or incorrect execution of the generated queries (Yu et al., 2022),
067   which undermines the relaibility of these methods.
068   (II) How to make the reasoning model understand and utilize the retrieved structured knowledge
069   in KGs? Existing solutions include (1) finetuning LMs to generate relation paths grounded by KGs.
070   These reasoning paths are then used for LLMs to conduct faithful reasoning and generate interpretable
071   results (Luo et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2022); (2) leverage the reasoning model to iteratively retrieve
072   and reason over subgraphs from the KG, deciding at each step to either answer the question, or to
073   continue the searching step (Sun et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). However, for the
074   first approach, the reasoning steps generated by language models are not guaranteed to exist in the
075
      KG, especially when multiple consecutive steps are combined into a reasoning path. We conduct
      error analysis in Section 3.3 and show that only 67% of generated reasoning steps are valid, while
076
      the remaining 33% of reasoning steps either have a format error or do not exist in the KG. The latter
077
      solution faces the challenge of determining the optimal stopping point for the exploration process.
078   For example, ToG (Sun et al., 2023) prompts LLMs to assess the adequacy of current reasoning paths
079   for answer generation. However, assessing the adequacy of reasoning paths is itself a complex task
080   that demands a deep understanding of the domain. This lack of clarity can easily result in premature
081   stopping or excessive continuation, further complicating the decision-making process in LLMs.
082
      To address these two challenges, we propose a retrieval augmented reasoning method, FiDeLiS.
083
      It is designed to enhance KGQA by anchoring responses to structured, verifiable reasoning paths.
084   FiDeLiS is composed of two major components, Path-RAG, which retrieves chain of entities and
085   relations from KGs in a effective manner (discussed in Section 2.1), and Deductive-verification
086   Beam Search (DVBS), which conducts deductive-reasoning-based beam search to generate multiple
087   reasoning paths leading to final answers (discussed in Section 2.2).
088
      The Path-RAG module uses a keyword-enhanced retrieval mechanism that fetches relevant entities
089
      and relations from a vector-based index of KGs to ensure high-recall retrieval. Compared to a vanilla
090   retriever which easily returns outputs that are not useful for finding the correct answer based on
091   relevance to the query, we incorporate LLMs to generate an exhaustive list of keywords from the
092   query to maximize coverage and ensure that no potential reasoning paths will be overlooked. Once
093   these entities and relations are retrieved, the module constructs candidate reasoning steps which are
094   then refined using a stepwise beam search discussed below.
095
      Next, DVBS leverages the deductive reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Ling et al., 2024; Huang &
096   Chang, 2023) as a clear-defined criterion for automatically directing the beam search process step by
097   step to create the complete reasoning paths. A key characteristic of DVBS is its integration of natural
098   language planning (Song et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022) and beam search. This combination enhances
099   the process of choosing the best reasoning paths by providing additional hints for decision making
100   in LLMs. In addition, we redesign how reasoning paths are evaluated by converting the scoring
101   process into a deductive reasoning task. This allows the LLM to validate the reasoning paths through
102   deductive verification rather than traditional logit-based scoring. This proposed deductive verification
103   serves as presie indicators for when to cease further reasoning, thus avoiding misleading reasoning
104
      chains and reducing the computational resources required. Overall, our main contributions include:
105
106
107   • We propose a retrieval augmented reasoning method, which enhances knowledge graph question
        answering by anchoring responses to structured, verifiable reasoning paths grounded by KGs.

       • We propose a step-wise keyword retrieval method that enhances the recall of relevant intermediate
109      knowledge from KGs. This ensures that all the paths we create can be confidently linked back to
110      KGs, ensuring that they are faithful and reliable.
111
       • We propose deductive verification as precise indicators for when to cease further reasoning, thus
112
         avoiding misleading the chains of reasoning and reducing unnecessary computation required.
113

119   Notation. Definition 1. A reasoning step is a pair (r, e), where r is the relation and e is the
120   corresponding entity. Definition 2. A reasoning path P is a pair (s, T ), where s is the starting entity
121   for the reasoning path, and T is a sequence of reasoning steps T = {t1 , . . . , tn } and tk = (rk , ek )
122   denotes the k-th reasoning step in the path and n denotes the length of the path. Definition 3. The
123   next-hop candidates given path P, denoted N1 (en ), are defined as the 1-hop neighborhood of en ,
124
      the last node in the reasoning path P. Definition 4. A reasoning path P = (s, T ) is valid if it is
      connected and each reasoning step tk = (rk , ek ) is correct (in the sense that (ek−1 , rk , ek ) is a triplet
125
      in the KG, where e0 is defined as s). For example, a valid reasoning path could be: P = Justin_Bieber
126    people.person.son                 people.person.ex_wife
127   −−−−−−−−−−→ Jeremy_Bieber −−−−−−−−−−−−→ Erin_Wagner, which denotes that “Jeremy Bieber"
128
      is the father of “Justin Bieber" and “Erin Wagner" is the ex-wife of “Jeremy Bieber". Each entity and
      relation are linked in the corresponding KG.
129
130   Overview. The framework of FiDeLiS is as shown in Figure 1. Given a query, it first retrieves the
131   reasoning step candidates from KGs (as discussed in Section 2.1). To ensure high recall, our method
132   first generates a list of keywords from the query. And then based on the generated keywords, it
133   retrieves entities and relations from an index (where the entities and relations are also embedded by
134
      vector representation obtained from an LM). Next, following the retrieved entities, n-hop reasoning
      step candidates are created and scored based on semantics similarity and neighborhoods aggregation.
135
      Then, a deductive verification-based beam search (discussed in Section 2.2) is employed to generate
136
      the top-k reasoning paths leading to the answer. First, a natural language plan is generated from
137   the query to provide more hints for LLMs decision making and then a beam search procedure is
138   conducted to score each reasoning step. We redesign the scoring function by converting the process
139   into a deductive reasoning task. This allows the LLM to validate connections between paths through
140   simple verification rather than traditional logit-based scoring. This change helps prevent easily
141   misleading reasoning chains and reduces the computational resources required.

      2.2   DVBS: D EDUCTIVE -V ERIFICATION G UIDED B EAM S EARCH
217
218   DVBS is designed to prompt LLMs to iteratively execute beam search on the reasoning step candidates
219   (provided by Path-RAG) to find the top-k most promising reasoning paths that lead to the answer
220   to the user query. At each timestep, we leverage the LLMs to choose the top-k reasoning steps
221   and decide whether to continue extending the reasoning paths or cease the extension process. It
222   comprises three main steps: planning, beam-search, deductive-verification, as depicted in Figure 1.
223   The implementation details of each step are elaborated in the following paragraphs.
224   Plan-and-Solve. Inspired by the recent works regarding planning capabilities of LLMs (Song et al.,
225   2023; Zhou et al., 2022), we prompt LLM to generate the planning steps for answering the user query,
226   denoted as w. This step aims to provide more hints for subsequent LLM decision making process.
227   The detailed prompt can be found in Section F.
228   Beam Search. To enable multi-step     reasoning, a reasoning path of T steps is sequentially generated

229   through several time steps as s1 , s2 , . . . , st , sT = s1:T , where st represents the reasoning step at
230   timestamp t. We constrain each reasoning step should within a set of candidates S t to control the
231   computation efficiency. We construct the reasoning step candidates S t based on the scoring function
232   defined in Eq. 3 by selecting the top-m reasoning steps with the highest scores. At each timestamp t,
233   we leverage the LLMs to select the top-k reasoning steps from the reasoning step candidates S t . The
234   beam search process at timestamp t is modeled as follows:

311   and DVBS frameworks are essential for FiDeLiS’s robust performance, with their removal causing
312   marked decreases in accuracy and Hits@1 scores across all evaluated datasets.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      KG-enhanced LLM. KGs have advantages in dynamic, explicit, and structured knowledge repre-
489
      sentation and techniques combining LLMs with KGs have been studied (Pan et al., 2024). Early
490   studies (Luo et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2022) embed structured knowledge from KGs into the underlying
491   neural networks during the pretraining or fine-tuning process. However, the reasoning steps generated
492   by LMs are observed to be prone to errors, which could be non-existent in the knowledge graph, and
493   can subsequently lead to incorrect reasoning during inference. Also, KG embedded in LLM sacrifices
494   its own nature of explainability in knowledge reasoning and efficiency in knowledge updating (Hu
495   et al., 2023). Another solutions are to keep the reasoning model as an agent to explore the external
496   structure knowledge source (Sun et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023a)
497   ToG (Sun et al., 2023) directly employs LLMs to output scores for candidate selections from the KG.
498
      This model operates at the relation-entity level of the KG, aiming to identify relevant triples that aid
      the LLM in making accurate and responsible final answer predictions.
499
500   Knowledge Graph-based Question Answering. To integrate LLMs for KGQA, retrieval-augmented
501   methods aim to retrieve the relative facts from the KGs to improve the reasoning performance (Li
502   et al., 2023; Karpukhin et al., 2020). Recently, UniKGQA (Jiang et al., 2022) which unifies the
503   graph retrieval and reasoning process into a single model with LLMs, achieves STOA performance.
504
      Semantic parsing methods convert the question into a structural query (e.g., SPARQL) by LLMs,
      which can be executed by a query engine to reason the answers on KGs (Sun et al., 2020; Lan &
505
      Jiang, 2020). However, these methods heavily rely on the quality of generated queries. If the query is
506
      not executable, no answers will be generated. DECAF (Yu et al., 2022) combines semantic parsing
507   and LLMs reasoning to jointly generate answers, which also reach salient performance.

510   5    C ONCLUSION
511
512   This paper proposes a retrieval-exploration interactive method specifically designed to enhance
513   intermediate steps of LLM reasoning grounded by KGs. The Path-RAG module and the use of
514   deductive reasoning as a calibration tool effectively guide the reasoning process, leading to more
515
      accurate knowledge retrieval and prevention of misleading reasoning chains. Extensive experiments
      demonstrate that our method, being training-free, not only reduces computational costs but also offers
516
      superior generality, consistently outperforming established strong baselines across three distinct
517
      benchmarks. We believe this study will significantly benefit the integration of LLMs and KGs, or
518   serve as an auxiliary tool to enhance the interpretability and factual reliability of LLM outputs.

759   A.1    K NOWLEDGE G RAPH - BASED Q UESTION A NSWERING (KGQA)
760
761   In this work, we mainly focus on the question answering task based on the knowledge graph.
762
763   Knowledge Graphs (KGs) contain abundant factual knowledge in the form of a set of triples: G =
764
      {(e, r, e′ ) | e, e′ ∈ E, r ∈ R}, where E and R denote the set of entities and relations, respectively.
765   Knowledge Graph Question Answering (KGQA) is a typical reasoning task based on KGs. Given
766   a natural language question q and a KG G, the task aims to design a function f to predict answers
767   a ∈ Aq based on knowledge from G, i.e., a = f (q, G). Following previous works Luo et al. (2024;
768   2023), we assume the entities eq ∈ Tq mentioned in q and answers a ∈ Aq are labeled and linked to
769   the corresponding entities in G, i.e., Tq , Aq ⊆ E.
770
771
772   B     P OTENTIAL I MPACTS AND L IMITATIONS
773
774
775   The proposed method holds the potential to significantly enhance the performance of large language
776   models (LLMs) by tackling the issue of hallucinations, thereby fostering deep, responsible reasoning.
777   By integrating KGs with LLMs, the approach not only facilitates more accurate knowledge retrieval
778   but also leverages deductive reasoning capabilities to steer the reasoning process and circumvent
779
      logical fallacies. The method is characterized by its stepwise, generalizable approach, and the use of
      deductive verification as a stopping criterion, which together may reduce superfluous computation and
780
      curb misleading reasoning chains. Moreover, due to its training-free nature and lower computational
781
      demands, this method could seamlessly serve as a plug-in for other existing frameworks.
782
783   However, it’s important to recognize certain limitations. Since the method is still in its early stages of
784   development, it might encounter unanticipated challenges or drawbacks in real-world applications.
785
      Its dependency on external KGs means that the quality and comprehensiveness of these resources can
      affect its overall effectiveness. Computational constraints might also arise when dealing with very
786
      large or intricate graphs. Furthermore, although the method has demonstrated promise in benchmark
787
      tests, its performance across more diverse or specialized tasks remains untested.
788
789
790
791   C     B OTTLENECK OF B EAM S EARCH E FFICIENCY
792
793
794   The bottleneck of computation is the beam search process, which contributes to N ∗ D times LLM
795
      calling, where D is the depth (or equivalently length) of the reasoning path, and N is the width of the
      beam-search (how many paths are remained in the pool in each iteration). Specifically, we need to
796
      call N D + D + C times LLM for each sample question, where C is a constant (equals to 1 if there is
797
      no error occurs when calling the API). Sun et al. (2023) illustrates that the computational efficiency
798   can be alleviated by replacing LLMs with small models such as BM25 and Sentence-BERT for the
799   beam search decision since the small models are much faster than LLM calling. In this way, we can
800   reduce the number of LLM calling from N D + D + C to D + C. However, Sun et al. (2023) shows
801   that this optimization sacrifices the accuracy due to the weaker scoring model in decision making.
802
      We noted that N D + D + C is the maximal computational complexity. In most cases, FiDeLiS does
803
      not need N D + D + C LLM calls for a question because the whole reasoning process might be
804   early stopped before the maximum reasoning depth D is reached if LLM determines the query can be
805   deductive reasoning by the current retrieved reasoning paths. As an illustration, Table 6 shows the
806   average numbers of LLM calls per question needed by FiDeLiS on different datasets. It can be seen
807   that in three KGQA datasets, the average numbers of LLM calls (ranging from ) are smaller than 21,
808   which is the theoretical maximum number of LLM calls calculated from N D + D + C when N = 4
809   and D = 4. We can also see that this average number gets even smaller for dataset covering a lot of
      single-hop reasoning questions, such as WebQSP.

      D.1    BASELINES
813
814   Baselines. (1) RoG (Luo et al., 2024): embed structure knowledge graph from KGs into the
815   underlying neural networks during the pretraining and fine-tuning process to generate the reasoning
816   path and explanation. (2) ToG (Sun et al., 2023): ask LLM to iteratively explore multiple possible
817   reasoning paths on KGs until the LLM determines that the question can be answered based on the
818   current reasoning paths. Our method FiDeLiS follows the similar paradigm. (3) NSM (He et al.,
819   2021) utilizes the sequential model to mimic the multi-hop reasoning process. (4) KD-CoT (Wang
820
      et al., 2023a) retrieves relevant knowledge from KGs to generate faithful reasoning paths for LLMs.
      (5) DeCAF (Yu et al., 2022) combines semantic parsing and LLMs reasoning to jointly generate
821
      answers, which also reach salient performance on KGQA tasks.

1118   To further mimic the real situations where KGs may not be of high quality (i.e., attributes of
1119   nodes/edges may be mislabeled, relations may not exist, etc.), we propose another experiment setting
1120   in this section to assess the model performance under conditions where KGs’ semantics and structure
1121   are deliberately perturbed and contaminated. Considering that KGs are typically annotated by humans
1122   and are generally accurate and meaningful, we introduce perturbations to edges in the KG to degrade
1123   the quality of the KGs.
1124   For the perturbation methods, we consider four perturbation heuristics based on (Raman et al., 2020)
1125   as follows: Relation Swapping (RS) randomly chooses two edges from T and swaps their relations.
1126   Relation Replacement (RR) randomly chooses an edge v1 , e, v2 ) ∈ T , then replaces e1 with another
1127   relation e2 = argminr∈R SG (v1 , e, v2 ), where SG (v1 , e, v2 ) uses ATS to measure the semantics
1128   similarity between two edges. Edge Rewiring (ER) randomly chooses an edge (v1 , e, v2 ) ∈ T , then
1129   replaces v2 with another entity v3 ∈ E\N1 (v1 ), where N1 (v1 ) represents the 1-hop neighborhood of
1130
       v1 . Edge Deletion (ED) randomly chooses an edge (v1 , e, v2 ) ∈ T and deletes it. We control the
       perturbation level based on the percentage of KG edges being perturbed.
