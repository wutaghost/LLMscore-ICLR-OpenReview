009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern optimization algorithms that incorporate momentum and adaptive step-
012            size offer improved performance in numerous challenging deep learning tasks.
013            However, their effectiveness is often highly sensitive to the choice of hyper-
014            parameters, especially the learning rate. Tuning these parameters is often diffi-
015            cult, resource-intensive, and time-consuming. Therefore, recent efforts have been
016            directed toward enhancing the stability of optimizers across a wide range of hyper-
017            parameter choices (Schaipp et al., 2024). In this paper, we introduce an algorithm
018
               that matches the performance of state-of-the-art optimizers while improving sta-
               bility through a novel adaptation of the NGN step-size method (Orvieto & Xiao,
019
               2024). Specifically, we propose a momentum-based
                                                        √            version (NGN-M) that attains
020
               the standard convergence rate of O(1/ K) under common assumptions, without
021
               the need for interpolation condition or assumptions of bounded stochastic gradi-
022            ents or iterates, in contrast to previous approaches. Additionally, we empirically
023            demonstrate that the combination of the NGN step-size with momentum results in
024            high robustness while delivering performance that is comparable to or surpasses
025            other state-of-the-art optimizers.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Adaptive methods such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) and RMSprop (Hinton et al., 2012) are widely
031   used in machine learning due to their established advantages over (momentum) SGD, particularly
032   in tasks such as training Transformers (Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2021; 2023). These methods
033
      adaptively scale the step-size across different dimensions (parameters) based on their respective
      statistics, effectively acting as a diagonal precondition.
034
035   Although these methods perform well in practice, existing theoretical analyses typically require
036   stringent assumptions on the noise structure of the stochastic gradients, such as sub-Gaussian noise
037   (Li et al., 2024) or affine noise models (Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). Relaxing these
038   assumptions remains an open challenge. Another well-known issue with Adam is its sensitivity to
039
      the step-size hyper-parameter (Wilson et al., 2017; Choi et al., 2019), particularly when training
      Transformers, where loss spikes are commonly observed (Molybog et al., 2023; Wortsman et al.,
040
      2023). This often necessitates careful adjustments of the hyper-parameters throughout the training
041
      process (Zhang et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023), which can be costly in terms of computa-
042   tional resources (Or et al., 2020). Consequently, there has been growing interest in developing
043   optimization methods that are more robust to hyper-parameter selection (Schaipp et al., 2024). In
044   addition to adapting the step-size, Adam and other state-of-the-art optimizers also rely on momen-
045   tum (Polyak, 1964), a broadly used technique that has been shown to enhance performance both
046   theoretically (Cutkosky & Mehta, 2020; Fatkhullin et al., 2024; Islamov et al., 2024) and practically
047   (Choi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2023; Jelassi & Li, 2022). Besides speeding up convergence, momen-
048   tum is known as a technique to reduce the variance of stochastic algorithms (Ma & Yarats, 2018;
049   Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019), improving stability as well as generalization in some settings (Jelassi
050
      & Li, 2022).
051   In this work, we address the aforementioned drawbacks of Adam by developing a new algorithm
052   based on the recently proposed NGN step-size (Orvieto & Xiao, 2024), an improved variant of the
053   Stochastic Polyak Step-size (Loizou et al., 2021), that has demonstrated strong resilience to step-
      size hyper-parameter tuning: in particular, the algorithm was shown to never diverge for any choice

054
      of the step-size hyper-parameter in the convex setting, and to exhibit strong curvature adaptation
055   properties strengthened by theoretical guarantees. However, the step-size of Orvieto & Xiao (2024)
056   simply adapts the learning rate through a scalar multiplier, leaving to future work the incorporation
057   of momentum and coordinate-wise variants – needed in complex problems such as optimizing trans-
058   formers, as motivated above. Here, we develop a momentum and step-size adaptive version of NGN
059   designed to enhance robustness in terms of hyper-parameter selection. We also present a theoretical
060   analysis alongside a practical evaluation of this approach, showcasing its improvements over current
061   state-of-the-art methods.
062   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
063
064             1. First, we introduce a new algorithm named NGN-M that combines the NGN step-size with
                                                                                                   √
065                momentum. We theoretically show that NGN-M achieves a convergence rate O(1/ K ) in
066                the convex regime without the typical requirements of interpolation or bounded gradient
067                assumptions found in earlier works.
068             2. Next, we focus on the problem of adapting the step-size rule towards a coordinate-wise
069                diagonal preconditioning. By integrating this diagonal step-size strategy with momentum,
070                we develop a new variant of NGN, called NGN-MD.
071             3. The theoretical results are supported by extensive empirical validation in various deep
072                learning settings where we demonstrate that NGN-M and NGN-MD not only preserve the
073                robustness property of the NGN step-size, but improve it further in many cases. The step-
074                size hyper-parameter resilience comes together with better performance comparable to that
075                of state-of-the-art algorithms.

      Polyak Step-size. When training a deep network with standard optimizers, tuning the learning
080
      rate is crucial but time-consuming and resource-intensive (Goodfellow et al., 2016). This issue is at
081
      the root of recent research focusing on transferring hyper-parameters across architectures at different
082   scales, therefore avoiding expensive tuning pipelines (Yang et al., 2022; 2023; Bordelon et al., 2023).
083   Yet, already in the convex setting choosing the learning rate can be difficult – an issue that was
084   studied already in Polyak (1987) and gave rise to the first adaptive method: the Polyak Stepsize (PS).
085   Recently, there has been a renewed interest adapting PS to modern settings (Loizou et al., 2021;
086   Orvieto et al., 2022; Jiang & Stich, 2024), delivering a theoretically principled way to adaptively
087   scale the gradient magnitude during training. PS-inspired methods have gained increasing interest
088   for their simplicity and adaptability, as they utilize local curvature and smoothness information to
089   accelerate algorithms and facilitate faster convergence. Orvieto & Xiao (2024) recently introduced a
090
      variant of the Stochastic Polyak step-size, called NGN, which further enhances the robustness of the
      step-size hyper-parameter and solidifies the link to Gauss-Newton preconditioning. The theoretical
091
      analysis in Orvieto & Xiao (2024) demonstrated that NGN does not diverge regardless of the choice
092
      of the step-size hyper-parameter, and converges fast when the step-size is appropriately tuned. In
093   contrast, the current theory of the SPS step-size with fixed step-size hyper-parameters (Loizou et al.,
094   2021) proves convergence to the exact solution only if the interpolation condition holds1 .
095
      Polyak Step-size and Heavy-ball Momentum. Heavy-ball momentum methods, stemming from
096
      the work of Polyak (1964), have gained significant attention over the years due to their benefits,
097
      including acceleration on convex quadratics (Jain et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2022; Bollapragada et al.,
098   2022), convex-like (Wang et al., 2022), and non-convex problems (Cutkosky & Mehta, 2020), as
099   well as their variance reduction abilities (Ma & Yarats, 2018; Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019). This
100   has led to growing interest in the combination of Polyak step-size and heavy-ball momentum, which
101   is now an active area of research (Barré et al., 2020; Saab et al., 2022; Barré et al., 2020; Wang
102   et al., 2023; Oikonomou & Loizou, 2024). Recently, Schaipp et al. (2024) demonstrated that a
103   geometrically principled combination of SPS and momentum leads to lower sensitivity to the step-
104   size hyper-parameter, although they did not provide strong theoretical convergence guarantees.

              The first observation from the results in Figure 5 is that the effective step-size of NGN-M and NGN-

519           not adaptive for sufficiently large step-size hyper-parameter α0 during the initial part or all of the

525            6                         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527
              This work introduced several novel adaptations of the NGN step-size method, incorporating support
528           for momentum and/or diagonal step-size. We provided a theoretical analysis of the convergence
529           rates for these algorithms and conducted an extensive empirical evaluation of their performance. The
530           experimental results show that combining momentum with the NGN step-size yields high robustness
531           to step-size hyper-parameter choices and performs competitively with state-of-the-art algorithms
532           across various settings.
533
              Given the significant complexity of the task, we defer the theoretical explanation of the step-size
534           resilience properties of NGN-M and analysis in the non-convex setting to future work. Further-
535           more, while the two proposed methods for incorporating weight decay into NGN-MDv1 outperform
536           AdamW and Momo-AdamW in training language models, they still exhibit some sensitivity to the
537           step-size hyper-parameter. This may, in part, be due to the limited understanding of the expected
538           effects of the weight decay technique, a topic that requires further investigation. Finally, one might
539           question the reasons behind the improvements of NGN-MDv1 over Adam, which could stem from
              the sub-optimal use of momentum in Adam, a direction deserving further exploration.

1441   E.1   D ESIGN C OMPARISON OF NGN-MDv1 AND NGN-MDv2
1442
1443   The derivations in equation 3 are used to provide an intuition of how one can add a diagonal step-
1444   size into NGN by choosing the regularization matrix Σk . By choosing Σk = Dk we recover the
1445   update direction of NGN-MDv1. In this case, we have only one global NGN step-size in front of Dk .
1446   The design of NGN-MDv2 follows a more straightforward intuition. In particular, it can be seen as a
1447
       direct extension of NGN to diagonal case by replacing the squared gradient norm ∥∇fSk (xk )∥2 by
1448
       the squared partial derivative (∇j fSk (xk ))2 for each parameter j ∈ [d].
1449   The main difference in comparison with Adam is the order in which the preconditioning and mo-
1450   mentum is applied. In both NGN-MDv1 and NGN-MDv2 we average the preconditioned updates
1451   Σ−1           k
          k ∇fSk (x ), i.e. we first apply preconditioning and momentum later. In contrast, in Adam the
1452   stochastic gradients are averaged to construct new momentum term, and then the momentum is
1453   preconditioned. In other words, the momentum is applied first and then it is followed by precondi-
1454
       tioning. We believe this change might be one of the reasons behind the step-size hyper-parameter
       resilience as well.
1455
1456   In practice, we found out that the tuned performance of NGN-MDv1 is slightly better than that of
1457   NGN-MDv2. Moreover, NGN-MDv1 demonstrates higher resilience to the choice of the step-size
       hyper-parameter than NGN-MDv2.

1459
1460   Implementing any version of NGN-MD in practice might be slightly more computationally expen-
1461   sive. However, we highlight that computing a step of NGN-MD does not involve matrix-vector
1462   operations since the preconditioner is a diagonal matrix, and the matrix notation is used only for
1463   the convenience of presentation. The additional computation cost that we have in NGN-MDv1 is the
1464   computation of ∥∇fSk (xk )∥2D−1 . This can be done by one pass over the gradient and summing the
                                       k
1465   terms (D1k )j (∇j fSk (xk ))2 for j ∈ [d]. This operation does not require additional matrix multiplica-

1467   add any significant costly operations in comparison with Adam.

1471
       Regularization techniques serve a fundamental purpose in minimizing generalization error. Orthog-
1472
       onally to their role for generalization, modern deep learning tasks often benefit from the use of
1473
       weight decay (Xiao, 2024). Despite its widespread application, the role of weight decay is poorly
1474   understood. Andriushchenko et al. (2023) suggested that it might provide implicit regularization by
1475   stabilizing the loss in over-parameterized neural networks and helping to balance the bias-variance
1476   trade-off that leads to lower training loss in under-parameterized networks. However, even in the
1477   case of SGD, there is still uncertainty regarding how the weight decay mechanism should be incor-
1478   porated, as various implementations may exist (Zhang et al., 2018).

       F.2    E MPIRICAL VALIDATION OF THE P ROPOSED C OMBINATIONS
1594   Having two possible ways of adding weight decay to NGN-MDv1, we test them on pretraining a
1595   70M transformer on language modeling. The validation perplexity at the end of training is reported
1596   in Figure 6. We note that when weight decay is turned off, both NGN-MDv1W and Dec-NGN-MDv1
1597   reduce to NGN-MDv1.
1598
       First, we observe that when weight decay is properly tuned, all algorithms improve over the base-
1599
       line case with no weight decay, which is consistent with the observation of Xiao (2024) and An-
1600   driushchenko et al. (2023) on AdamW. We also note that Dec-NGN-MDv1 and NGN-MDv1W require
1601   a smaller weight decay value compared to the other algorithms. Finally, the stability and perfor-
1602   mance of NGNMDv1 are preserved by both variations, allowing training with larger learning rates,
1603   and significantly improving over AdamW and Momo-Adam.
1604
       We do not observe a substantial difference between the two proposed modifications of NGN-MDv1
1605
       for this task. We remark however that these two versions serve substantially different purposes,
1606   and pretraining language models might not be the most representative task to evaluate the effect of
1607   adding regularization.
