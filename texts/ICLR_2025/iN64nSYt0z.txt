                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            Despite the remarkable success of Large Language Models (LLMs), they still
010            exhibit a limited capability to align their outputs to the user instructions. In this
011            work, we introduce a simple and effective method, which we name GUIDE, that
012
               mechanistically increases attention scores in instruction tokens. To support this
               operation, we present Influence, a novel metric that highlights how the user’s
013
               instructions propagate through the transformer layers and impact the LLM output.
014
               Our results show that GUIDE improves the accuracy of following instructions
015            29.4% to 60.4%, outperforming natural prompting alternatives and Supervised
016            Fine-Tuning up to 1M tokens.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Large Language Models (LLMs) are currently the state-of-the-art of most NLP tasks. Despite this
023   success, pretrained LLMs sometimes struggle to accurately interpret diverse users’ instructions and
024   may generate outputs that do not align with human expectations. Additionally, LLMs may produce
025
      biased or hallucinated facts, which can limit their practical usefulness. Previous work (Kuratov et al.,
      2024; Lu et al., 2024b) indicate that transformers are less prone to align with instructions as the
026
      context length grows (Kuratov et al. (2024); Lu et al. (2024b)). In such cases, rather than fulfilling
027
      the user’s request, the model generates nonsensical text or repeat segments from the prompt.
028
029   A common solution to this problem is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning
030   (RL). However, these approaches are resource-intensive, time-consuming, and sensitive to the specific
031
      data and task. Ideally, a more efficient approach would be one that, once implemented, does not
      require additional training.
032
033   In that sense, due to its low cost and broad accessibility, prompt engineering is widely used to align
034   the outputs of LLMs with user preferences. However, this method does not always produce consistent
035   results and can be very unstable, as demonstrated in (Sclar et al., 2024).
036   In this work, we introduce GUIDE (Guided Understanding with Instruction-Driven Enhancements),
037   a novel and systematic approach that allows users to emphasize critical instructions in their prompts.
038   GUIDE enables users to influence the attention given to specific tokens by simply enclosing important
039   text within tags like <!-> <-!> (as shown on Figure 1(a)). These special tags directs the LLM’s
040   focus, which is done by adding a bias to the attention scores toward the tokens they enclose. Our
041   implementation is open-source and designed for seamless integration. Our experiments demonstrate
042   that GUIDE significantly increases the likelihood of the model following key instructions and
043
      retrieving crucial information designated by the user, outperforming natural prompting techniques.
044   While GUIDE does not require additional training, it does necessitate the careful selection of how
045   much to increase attention weights. In our study, we propose default values for certain tasks, but we
046   also recognize the need to quantify these adjustments. To address this, we introduce a novel metric
047   called Influence. This metric measures the importance of specific tokens in relation to instruction
048   tokens within the text, and we use it to determine reasonable values for the increase in attention
049
      weights. We demonstrate that this metric correlates with the model’s probability of following specific
      instructions.
050
051   To that end, the main contributions of this work are:
052
053         1. The introduction of GUIDE: a mechanistic approach for emphasizing instruction tokens,
               without need of any further computational resources.

096   2    R ELATED WORK
097
098   Alignment and instruction following Alignment techniques have the objective to align LLM
099
      outputs with human preferences. Model fine-tuning usually aligns the output of the LLMs with
      human intents using Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al.,
100
      2022), Reinforcement Learning with AI Feedback (RLAIF) (Lee et al., 2023) or Direct Preference
101
      Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). However, these methods have three significant constraints:
102   they require specialized datasets, often with human annotations, thus reducing efficiency; they involve
103   substantial computational complexity and cost due to the need for additional training; and they
104   demand specialized expertise, as successfully implementing the process can be challenging. Given
105   these constraints, this type of fine-tuning is typically reserved for general-purpose alignment, ensuring
106   that models are Helpful, Harmless, and Honest (Shen et al., 2023) and are not suited to address
107   end users’ specific needs. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with techniques such as low-rank adapters
      (LoRA (Hu et al., 2021b)) offers a more accessible way to customize a model for individual user


                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      requirements. However, these techniques still face the same three limitations, albeit to a lesser extent.
109   Consequently, SFT is typically utilized only for very targeted use cases if used at all.
110
111   Utilizing LLMs for automated prompt engineering has demonstrated notable performance. Black-Box
112
      Prompt Optimization (BPO) is a sophisticated framework that automatically refines human-written
      prompts, often unstructured or ambiguous (Cheng et al., 2024). Similarly, the PE2 framework
113
      (Ye et al., 2024) enhances prompt performance by refining human-written prompts through a
114
      comprehensive search process. Although PE2 avoids additional model training, it increases
115   complexity, latency, and cost, limiting its scalability. Both BPO and PE2 are generally designed
116   for broad enhancements in prompt writing. They are not tailored to meet individual users’ specific
117   intentions or needs.
118
      Due to its low cost and large accessibility, prompt engineering is extensively used to align the output
119
      of the LLMs with user preferences. This is clearly demonstrated by popular LLM frameworks like the
120
      system in (Yang et al., 2024), which empowers LM agents to tackle software engineering tasks. This
121   system emphasizes crucial instructions through uppercase text and exclamation marks, like "PLEASE
122   DO NOT DO THAT!" or "THE EDIT COMMAND REQUIRES PROPER INDENTATION."
123   Similarly, the AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024a), a leading system for automated scientific discovery,
124   uses strong directives such as "ABSOLUTELY DO NOT ADD IT AGAIN!!!" to steer the model’s
125   behavior. These examples, drawn from highly influential frameworks widely used, underscore the
126   pressing need for end-users to signal what matters most to them in order to guide LLMs toward better
127   alignment with their goals. Currently, users rely on prompt engineering and forceful language to
128   achieve this alignment. However, this approach does not consistently deliver positive results as shown
129
      in (Sclar et al., 2024).
130   In contrast, our proposed method, GUIDE, offers a reliable and systematic approach that enables
131   users to mechanistically highlight critical instructions within the prompt.
132
133
134   Explainability for Transformers This is a particularly active area of research with many promising
135   research directions. In the context of this work, we primarily focus on methods that attempt to quantify
136   the significance of one token (or set of tokens) of interest.
137   Gradient metrics such as Relevance and GradCAM (Chefer et al. (2021a); Chefer et al. (2021b);
138   Selvaraju et al. (2019)) have demonstrated promising results in Computer Vision, NLP and Text-To-
139   Image tasks. Nonetheless, calculating gradients in large models, particularly those exceeding 7B
140   parameters, demands substantial computational resources.
141
      Activation Patching approaches (Meng et al. (2023); Zhang and Nanda (2024)) focuses on perturbing
142
      the inputs and checking how impactful will this perturbation be, based on where and how is it applied.
143   They propose two types of perturbation: adding a gaussian noise in token embeddings, or perturbing
144   the phrase in a semantical approach (changing important words in the phrase), and they evaluate the
145   importance of each token by the difference of distributions of logits into these with perturbations.
146
      Abnar and Zuidema (2020) introduced Attention Rollout, a method that uses attention weights to
147
      measure the impact of one token on another. This technique propagates attention weights through
148
      the layers to determine the importance of each token from any layer to the first layer. However,
149   this method is primarily applicable to encoder-only architectures like BERT. In contrast, generative
150   models typically use decoder-only architectures, making attention rollout less effectives.
151
152
      In this work, we introduce Influence, a simple and computationally efficient metric specifically
      designed for decoder-only models.

160   In this section, we present GUIDE, a novel and systematic approach that enables users to highlight
161   critical instructions within the text input provided to an LLM. To understand how GUIDE operates, it
      is essential first to revisit the core mechanism of self-attention, which drives the functioning of LLMs.

187   The logits, and hence the attention scores, are automatically computed by the model. We argue
188   that the end user should be able to influence the level of attention each token receives by explicitly

196   3.2      C ALIBRATING GUIDE
197
198
      Using GUIDE, the addition of ∆ directly increases the attention the model pays to the tokens of
      interest, amplifying their influence on the generated output. However, because attention scores must
199
      sum to one, this adjustment reduces the attention given to other tokens. If ∆ is set too high, the model
200
      might overly focus on the highlighted tokens, which could disrupt the generation process. Therefore,
201   it is crucial to select an appropriate ∆ that balances these effects.
202
203   Our experiments suggest that for the Mistral and Gemma-2 models, a ∆ of 2 works well for
204
      emphasizing instructions, while a ∆ of 1 is effective for highlighting specific information within the
      text. Besides, using ∆ values greater than 5 often led to nonsensical outputs (see Appendices D and
205
      H). Although these default values improve performance compared to natural prompting alternatives,
206
      the optimal choice of ∆ depends on various factors, including the model, the nature of the task, etc.
207   The most precise way to determine an appropriate ∆ is through hyperparameter tuning on a validation
208   set.
209
210
      In this work, we also introduce a heuristic approach for calibrating ∆ with just a couple of forward
      passes. The idea is to match the influence increase from ∆ to a "natural" level that could be achieved
211
      through conventional prompting, such as using uppercase (see Figure 2). This calibration requires a
212
      metric that evaluates the influence of the selected tokens and tracks how this impact propagates both
213   vertically across the stacked layers and horizontally across successive tokens.

272   Using Influence, we can calibrate GUIDE by choosing a ∆ that mimics natural attention enhancement,
273   such as writing in uppercase (Figure 2). This can be easily achieved with two forward passes, one
274
      with uppercase text and one without. ∆ is then defined as the difference in log-influence between the

275
      GUIDE typically has a greater impact on text generation compared to one highlighted with natural
276
      prompting, such as using uppercase, even if their influence scores are similar.
277
278   Moreover, in the absence of GUIDE, our experiments demonstrate that Influence correlates positively
279   with the likelihood of a set of tokens impacting the model’s output, such as following an instruction
280

302   In this section, we evaluate the benefits of GUIDE and Influence using Mistral-7b Instruct (Jiang
303
      et al., 2023) for following key instructions and retrieving crucial information designated by the user.
      In Appendix F, we conduct the same experiments with Gemma2-2b Instruct Team et al. (2024) with

307   4.1          D ESCRIPTION
308
309   Summarization in French To evaluate the capability of GUIDE to support LLMs in producing
310
      outputs aligned with the user’s query, we perform experiments related to text translation and
      summarization. In these experiments, we have used text from OpenWebText (Gokaslan and Cohen,
311
      2019), chosen for its variety in context lengths. We have divided the dataset into groups based on
312
      context length, containing texts from a 500-token window, such as (0, 500], (500, 1000], and so on.
313   From each group, we randomly selected 20 texts and generated 10 summaries for each text using
314   multinomial sampling (Wiher et al., 2022).
315
316
      A needle in a haystack To evaluate the impact of our approach on the model’s ability to retain
317
      information, we have conducted the Needle in a Haystack. This test involves embedding specific
318   information at a particular location within a text and then asking a question related to that information
319   at the end of the text. Our hypothesis is that by adding extra attention to this text, the model’s outputs
320   would improve, as the final representation should be more closely aligned with the information tokens.
321   We have followed the methodology outlined by (Kamradt, 2023). Specifically, we have inserted
322   specific information, referred to as the "needle" at variable positions within a given text. After this
323   insertion, we have asked a question to the LLM related to the inserted information (see the complete
      prompt in Appendix G).


                                                                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      To conduct this experiment, we have sampled 200 texts from the OpenWebText (Gokaslan and Cohen,
325   2019) dataset, selecting 50 texts for each context window of size 500, ranging from 0 to 6000 tokens.
326   For each text, the needle was inserted at 10 different quantiles (10%, 20%, ..., 100%). We placed the
327   needle immediately after a period (‘.’) to maintain the semantic integrity of the text.
328
329   JSON Generation To assess the efficiency of GUIDE in generating outputs in a specified format,
330   we have conducted experiments focused on JSON generation. For our inputs, we have used texts
331   from books written between 1510 and 1699, sourced from the BL Books dataset (Labs, 2021). We
332   have prompted the model to extract and generate key information about each book in a predetermined
333   JSON format, as detailed in G. We have randomly selected 300 books from the BL Books dataset and
334   divided each text into context length windows of 500 tokens, ranging from 0 to 4000 tokens. These
335   text segments were then incorporated into our template, where the Mistral model was expected to
336
      generate a JSON output that precisely followed the specified format.
337   We have inputted special attention into the tokens of Your response should follow
338   exactly this template and we have then evaluated the Jaccard index between the keys
339   of the generated JSON and the schema.
340
341   Influence For each of the experiments mentioned above, we have evaluated the relationship between
342   the Influence metric and the probability of obtaining correct outputs and compare it with Attention
343   Rollout and raw attention scores. We observe that, attention rollout shows a negative correlation
344
      and an AUC below 0.5 in two out of three experiments, supporting our hypothesis that attention
      rollout may not accurately reflect the model focus. Also as expected, we see that raw attention has
345
      a random behavior in two of three setups, with ROC AUC scores around 0.5. Influence shows a
346
      strong positive correlation and ROC AUC with the likelihood of following instructions, supporting
347   our hypothesis that it better quantifies attention flow compared to other non-gradient metrics. Due to
348   space limitations, further details are left for the appendix (Appendix C).

449   5    C ONCLUSION
450
451   While Transformers represent the state-of-the-art in almost all NLP tasks, they often exhibit
452   unexpected behaviors, particularly hallucination, which becomes more pronounced as context length
453   increases. This work introduces GUIDE, a mechanical approach for instruction alignment that does
454   not require further optimization. We demonstrate that GUIDE effectively mitigates hallucination in
455   instruction-following scenarios without significantly compromising output quality. We also introduce
456
      Influence, a novel metric for Transformer explainability that quantifies the importance of subsequences
      of tokens within the context and can be used to calibrate GUIDE at low cost. Both GUIDE and
457
      Influence are made publicly available in a Hugging Face-based pipeline.

734    C        E VALUATION OF I NFLUENCE
735
736    For each of the experiments mentioned in the main text, we have evaluated the relationship between
737    the Influence metric and the probability of obtaining correct outputs. To achieve this, we have
738    calculated both the ROC AUC score and the correlation between the importance of instruction tokens
739    and the last token in the sequence. Then, we have compared these results through non-gradient
740    metrics, such as Attention Rollout and raw attention scores. Our hypothesis is that Influence has a
741
       strong positive correlation with the probability of correct outputs.
742    Given that the ROC AUC is a classification metric, it was necessary to binarize our scores. In the
743    French summarization experiment, we have done this by assigning a score of 1 to texts in French and
744    0 to those in other languages. In the "needle in a haystack" experiment, a score of 1 was given to
745    prompts that successfully identified the needle information, while those that did not were assigned
746    a 0. Similarly, for the JSON generation experiment, outputs that adhered to the JSON format were
747
       assigned a 1, and those that did not were assigned a 0.
748
749    Results Table 1 shows the correlation and ROC AUC of each metric to correct output. We note that,
750    attention rollout shows a negative correlation and an AUC below 0.5 in two out of three experiments.
751    This observation supports our initial hypothesis that attention rollout may not accurately reflect the
752
       model focus. Also as expected, we also see that raw attention has a random behavior in two of three
       setups, with ROC AUC scores around 0.5.
