009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the challenges of aligning large models with human preferences lies in
012            both the data requirements and the technical complexities of current approaches.
013            Predominant methods, such as RLHF, involve multiple steps, each demanding
014            distinct types of data, including demonstrations data and preference data. In
015            RLHF, human preferences are typically modeled through a reward model, which
016            serves as a proxy to guide policy learning during the reinforcement learning stage,
017
               ultimately producing a policy aligned with human preferences. However, in this
               paper, we propose a fresh perspective on learning alignment based on inverse
018
               reinforcement learning principles, where the optimal policy is still derived from
019
               reward maximization. However, instead of relying on preference data, we directly
020            learn the reward model from demonstration data. This new formulation offers the
021            flexibility to be applied even when only demonstration data is available, a capability
022            that current RLHF methods lack, and it also shows that demonstration data offers
023            more utility than what conventional wisdom suggests. Our extensive evaluation,
024            based on public reward benchmark and HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard,
025            demonstrates that our approach compares favorably to state-of-the-art methods that
026            rely solely on demonstration data.

      that such data is known to contain valuable human preference information? One potential answer
055   lies in the utilization of inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) to formulate this problem, which has
056   the capability to learn both reward and policy simultaneously (Ng et al., 2000; Ziebart et al., 2008;
057   Fu et al., 2017; Zeng et al., 2022b). This allows it to potentially learn preferences directly from

060   modeled separately but learned in an interleaved manner. The interleaving between policy learning
061   and reward learning helps to model preferences solely from demonstration data, although those
062   preferences are implicit in the data (see Fig. 1). As our results show, this formulation indeed helps to
063
      improve the performance of the final model, particularly when compared to SFT, which also relies
      solely on demonstration data.
064
065   Summary of contribution of this work:
066
      • We develop a new IRL-based method for alignment that relies solely on demonstration data, yet
067
        still improves the resulting model’s performance. This method adopts a bi-level formulation, where
068     the reward and policy are modeled separately. In this formulation, the policy is framed as the
069     optimal solution to a KL-Divergence regularized policy optimization problem, constrained by a
070     reward estimator. The reward model is optimized using the demonstration data to ensure that its
071     corresponding optimal policy is the maximum likelihood estimator derived from the same dataset.
072   • We also present a theoretical connection with a recently proposed method SPIN (Chen et al., 2024),
073     which can demonstrate that SPIN is a special variant derived from the IRL formulation from a
074     bi-level optimization perspective.
075
      • We demonstrate that the reward learned solely from demonstration data exhibits strong generative
076     capabilities in assessing data quality. This is evidenced by evaluating the reward’s accuracy on a
077     hold-out preference dataset and a public reward benchmark (Lambert et al., 2024).
078
      • Empirically, we extensively evaluate our proposed method by fine-tuning the 1B Pythia model
079
        on the TL;DR dataset (Stiennon et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2024) and the 7B Mistral model on
080     the UltraChat dataset (Ding et al., 2023). Our numerical results demonstrate that the proposed
081     method compares favorably to both SFT and SPIN, as indicated by higher win rates in evalua-
082     tions conducted by ChatGPT and improved performance on downstream tasks in the Open LLM
083     Leaderboard (Myrzakhan et al., 2024).

      Imitation learning Imitation learning assumes the availability of a demonstration dataset containing
088
      expert data and focuses on learning from these demonstrations to match the expert’s policy. Behavior
089   cloning is one classic imitation learning algorithm which directly fits the demonstration data through
090   supervised learning (Pomerleau, 1988). However, naively fitting sequential demonstrations can lead
091   to distribution shift between demonstration trajectories and policy rollout. Moreover, since imitation
092   learning where future transitions depend on previous actions violates the common i.i.d. assumptions
093   made in statistical learning, naively fitting demonstrations trajectories can incur unfavorable regret
094   bound which has quadratic dependence on the problem horizon (Ross & Bagnell, 2010; Ross
095   et al., 2011). To address these challenges in imitation learning, it has been proposed to model the
096   policy as the (optimal) solution to a MDP under a specific reward function (Osa et al., 2018). To
097
      learn a policy that effectively utilizes demonstration trajectories to match the expert policy, inverse
      reinforcement learning methods are proposed to search for one optimal reward estimator which can
098
      classify demonstration trajectories from other policy rollouts and guarantee that its corresponding
099
      optimal policy can imitate the observed expert behaviors in the demonstration dataset (Ziebart et al.,
100   2008; Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2017; Zeng et al., 2022b; Garg et al., 2021).
101
102   Imitation learning for language modeling The connection between imitation and generative
103   language modeling can be traced back to some adversarial training methods for text generation (Yu
104   et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2021), although some of them may not draw explicit connection with imitation
105   learning. There are also works of applying inverse RL methods to a specific text generation tasks,
106   such as table-to-text generation (Ghosh et al., 2021), program generation (Ghosh & Srivastava, 2021),
107   and summarization (Fu et al., 2022). To efficiently imitate the demonstration data, Chen et al. (2024)
      proposed an imitation learning algorithm, SPIN, which construct synthetic preference data through


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pairing demonstration and model-generated continuations and then leverage direct preference method
109   (Rafailov et al., 2024) for policy fine-tuning. Li et al. (2024) shows the connection between SPIN
110   and IRL from a theoretical perspective and reveals that SPIN utilizes the parameterization technique
111   developed in (Rafailov et al., 2024) to avoid explicit reward modelling and reward learning from
112   computational simplicity. Although the parameterization technique introduced by Rafailov et al.
113   (2024) allows skipping the reward modeling subroutine and reduces the complexities of RLHF, it
114   faces challenges due to the distribution shift between preference data and model outputs. This shift
115   can lead to instability during the training process (Xu et al., 2024; Ivison et al., 2024). It is wroth
116   noting that while Li et al. (2024) discussed that the demonstration data can potentially benefit reward
117
      learning, it lacks practical algorithm for IRL methods which can iteratively enhance both reward
      model and policy by learning directly from demonstration data. More recently, Cundy & Ermon and
118
      Wulfmeier et al. (2024) apply the imitation learning algorithm IQLearn for the post-training step of
119
      large language models, making their approaches closer to ours. In contrast, we adopt the framework
120   of maximum likelihood inverse RL (ML-IRL) and derive a different objective, with results on broader
121   LLM post-training benchmarks. Similar with us, Sun (2024) draws a connection between inverse
122   RL and the supervised fine-tuning problem of LLMs, but lacks both algorithmic implementation and
123   experimental studies.

      We are now ready to design algorithms for the proposed maximum likelihood formulation Eq. (5)
257   aimed at aligning large language models (LLMs) using demonstration data. To begin with, first note
258   that the maximum likelihood formulation 5 and its surrogate estimation problem Eq. (6) takes a
259   hierarchical form, and it belongs to the class of problem named bi-level optimization. Generally
260   speaking, bi-level problems are not easy to optimize since they have a nested structure for two
261   optimization problems. In this section, we will propose one computationally tractable algorithm for
262   both reward learning and policy fine-tuning to solve the LLM alignment problem 6.
263   Before presenting the details, we emphasize that throughout this section, we aim to explicitly
264   identify both an optimal policy πr∗θ and a corresponding reward estimate r(·, ·; θ) that align with the
265   demonstration data. Specifically, the policy πr∗θ is considered an optimal solution with respect to
266   the reward estimate r(·, ·; θ), as defined by the policy optimization problem in equation Eq. (5b).
267   Given this optimal policy constraint relative to a specific reward estimate, we propose an algorithm to
268   tackle this single-stage, bi-level problem. It is important to note that our approach is a departure from
269   popular methods like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), which directly optimize a fixed loss function (see
      Eq. (4)) without explicitly modeling the reward.

      Our algorithm operates by alternating between two key steps: 1) Policy Alignment Step, where we
284   perform a policy improvement step towards solving Eq. (5b) under a fixed reward function r(·, ·; θ),
285   effectively aligning the policy with the current reward estimate, 2) Reward Alignment Step, where
286   we update the reward parameters θ using a stochastic gradient estimator to align the reward model
287   with the demonstration dataset. This training loop allows the policy and reward model to be fine-tuned
288   iteratively, potentially leading to better alignment with the demonstration data compared to standard
289   SFT methods which treat the loss function Eq. (1) as fixed.

353   dataset (Stiennon et al., 2020), available on Hugging Face. We use all prompts from the TL;DR
354   dataset as our prompt dataset. To create a demonstration dataset, we generate 10,000 high-quality
355
      summaries using a 6.9B parameter Pythia checkpoint (Huang et al., 2024) that was trained via a
      RLHF pipeline with human-annotated preference data. This model is publicly available on Hugging
356
      Face1 .
357
358   In our IRL pipeline which iteratively updating from policy and reward model through utilizing
359   the demonstration data, we begin by performing supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on a pretrained 1B
360   parameter Pythia model using the generated demonstration dataset, resulting in our initial SFT model.
361
      At each iteration, we construct a preference dataset by labeling the summaries generated from the
      6.9B PPO-trained checkpoint as preferred and the outputs generated by our current 1B Pythia model
362
      as non-preferred. Using this preference dataset, we train a reward model initialized from our 1B SFT
363
      model. We then apply the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm, guided by the estimated
364   reward model, to further fine-tune our 1B Pythia model, enhancing its performance beyond the initial
365   SFT checkpoint. For the PPO algorithm setup, we follow the hyperparameters and experimental
366   pipeline detailed in (Huang et al., 2024). Our implementation is consistent with their codebase2 .
367

      RLHF pipeline from three perspectives: (1) reward model accuracy, (2) reward scores measured
