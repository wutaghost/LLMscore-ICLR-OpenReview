                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               The graphon function is fundamental to modeling exchangeable graphs, which
010
               form the basis for a wide variety of networks. In this paper, we use the addi-
011            tive separable model as a parsimonious representation of the graphon, capable
012            of generating a low-rank connection probability matrix for network data. This
013            model effectively addresses the well-known identification challenges associated
014            with graphon functions. We develop an efficient estimation approach that lever-
015            ages subgraph counts to estimate the low-rank connection matrix and uses interpo-
016            lation to recover the graphon functions, achieving the minimax optimal estimation
017            rate. We provide the convergence rate of our method, and validate its computa-
018            tional efficiency and estimation accuracy through comprehensive simulation stud-
019
               ies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      With advancements in data collection and analysis techniques, the modeling of network data
024
      has become increasingly prevalent. Examples of network data include brain networks (Maugis
025   et al., 2020), co-authorship networks (Isfandyari-Moghaddam et al., 2023), and biological networks
026   (Kamimoto et al., 2023), among others. A critical challenge in analyzing such data is understanding
027   the underlying generative mechanisms, which are essential for tasks like studying dynamic evo-
028   lution (Pensky, 2019), predicting links (Gao et al., 2016), and detecting communities (Jin et al.,
029   2021). One effective modeling framework is that of exchangeable graphs, which represent networks
030   as graphs where nodes correspond to objects and edges represent connections between them. The
031   concept of exchangeability implies that the joint distribution of edges remains invariant under any
032   permutation of the nodes. According to the Aldous-Hoover theorem, any exchangeable graph can
033
      be characterized by a graph function, commonly known as a graphon. The study of graphons has
      gained substantial attention in the literature due to their ability to tackle a wide array of challenges,
034
      including explaining the asymptotic normality of subgraphs (Bickel et al., 2011) and conducting hy-
035
      pothesis testing for the equivalence of two graphs (Maugis et al., 2020). Furthermore, the graphon
036   model encompasses several widely used models as special cases, such as the stochastic block model
037   (SBM) (Holland et al., 1983), the random dot product graph (RDPG) model (Young & Scheinerman,
038   2007), and the latent space model (Hoff et al., 2002).
039
      The graphon is a symmetric, measurable bivariate function, which, without additional assumptions,
040
      is not directly estimable from a single observed network. However, by leveraging its eigenvalue-
041
      eigenfunction decomposition, we can impose a highly effective assumption: truncating the decom-
042   position to retain terms with leading eigenvalues, similar to principal component analysis. A key
043   benefit of this approach is that the resulting connection probability matrix P –the matrix formed
044   when the graphon function is evaluated at the nodes–naturally inherits the same low rank as the
045   truncated graphon.
046
      Building on this insight, we propose the Additive Separable Graphon (ASG) models, which el-
047
      egantly align the low-rank properties of both the graphon and the connection probability matrix.
048   To estimate this new low-rank network model, we have developed a highly efficient method that
049   harnesses well-established, scalable techniques for subgraph counting, enabling rapid and practical
050   implementation. The core idea is intuitive: a matrix of rank r can be decomposed into a sum of r
051   rank-1 matrices. By counting the number of O(r) subgraphs, we extract the information correspond-
052   ing to these r rank-1 matrices. Solving the associated system of equations allows us to estimate each
053   matrix individually. By combining these estimates, we reconstruct the final rank-r matrix, which
      serves as our estimator for P . The graphon function f is then estimated by utilizing sorting and

054
      interpolation techniques based on these rank-1 matrix estimates. The graphon captures the limiting
055   behavior of a graphon model, and our method leverages the low-rank structure of the graphon for
056   estimation. Notably, our method is tuning parameter-free, as it does not require bandwidth or other
057   adjustments, and it performs consistently well across various settings. On the other hand, estimat-
058   ing the connection probability matrix is important in practice and has attracted increasing attention,
059   see for example, all of which focus on estimating the connection probability matrix rather than the
060   graphon itself. In contrast, our method can estimate the graphon function and connection probability
061   matrix simultaneously.
062   In the existing literature, graphon estimation methods can be broadly categorized into two groups:
063   those focused on estimating the graphon itself, and those targeting the connection probability matrix
064   P . For graphon estimation, Olhede & Wolfe (2014) approximate the graphon using blocks, treating
065   it as a two-dimensional step function. Their method, however, requires finding the argmax of a per-
066   mutation, and their implementation relies on a greedy search algorithm that can be computationally
067   slow (see Section 4 for more details). Chan & Airoldi (2014) refine this by reordering nodes based
068   on degree and applying total variation minimization, assuming the one-dimensional marginals of
069
      the graphon are strictly monotone. However, this assumption is restrictive, as it excludes SBM. The
      resulting P from either of these approaches is generally not low-rank.
070
071   On the other hand, methods focused on estimating P include Chatterjee (2015), who impose a low-
072   rank assumption on P and propose using Universal Singular-Value Thresholding (USVT), treat-
073   ing the adjacency matrix as a perturbed version of P . Zhang et al. (2017) apply neighborhood
074   smoothing to estimate P , achieving near-minimax optimality. Gao et al. (2016) propose a mini-
075
      max optimal combinatorial least-squares estimator, which is also adopted by Wu et al. (2024+) for
      non-exchangeable network. Despite these advances, due to identification issues, graphon functions
076
      cannot be directly recovered from the connecting probability matrix produced by these methods.
077
078   The structure of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces our parsimonious low-
079   rank graphon model, termed the Additive Separable Graphon Model (ASG). Section 3 provides a
080   comprehensive description of the estimation procedures and algorithms for both r = 1 and r ≥ 2. In
081
      Section 4, we present simulation studies that demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method in
      terms of implementation speed and estimation accuracy. Section 5 offers a discussion and outlines
082
      potential directions for future research. Finally, an analysis of time complexity, a real data example,
083
      additional simulation results, an approach for selecting the rank r when it is unknown, the proofs of
084   the theoretical results, and the corresponding technical lemmas are provided in the appendix.

      2     A DDITIVE SEPARABLE GRAPHON MODEL

      We consider a random graph G = (V, E) using the graphon formulation. Specifically, for
099
      i = 1, . . . , n, where n represents the size of the network, each node i is associated with an in-
100
      dependent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variable Ui ∼ Uniform(0, 1). The edges Eij
101   are then independently drawn as Eij ∼ Bernoulli(f (Ui , Uj )) for i < j, where f (·, ·) is a symmetric,
102   measurable function f : [0, 1]2 → [0, 1] named the graphon. Additionally, we have Eii = 0 and
103   Eij = Eji for i > j. Although we focus on undirected graphs without self-loops, our methods
104   and theory can be readily extended to graphs with self-loops or directed graphs. Notably, many
105   large-scale real-world networks exhibit low-rank characteristics, such as memberships or commu-
106   nities. The SBM (Holland et al., 1983) and the RDPG (Young & Scheinerman, 2007) are popular
107   approaches for capturing unobserved heterogeneity in networks. For further discussion and real data
      examples, we refer to Athreya et al. (2018); Thibeault et al. (2024); Fortunato (2010).

289   Remark 3 (Comparison with the spectral method for estimating the connection probability matrix).
290   Spectral methods, such as USVT (Chatterjee, 2015), also estimate the connection probability matrix
291   by computing eigenvalues and eigenvectors. However, our approach differs in several important
292   ways. First, our motivation is fundamentally different: our primary goal is to estimate the graphon
293   function itself, rather than simply the connection probability matrix. This distinction leads to a
294   different methodology. While spectral methods rely on matrix spectral decomposition, our approach
295
      is based on subgraph counts, employing a moment-based technique that is a traditional and still
      evolving tool in statistical network analysis. Second, our method achieves the minimax rate for the
296
      mean squared error up to a logarithmic factor, without imposing smoothness assumptions on the
297
      graphon. In contrast, spectral methods typically require assumptions such as piecewise constant
298   or Hölder-class smoothness of the graphon to derive convergence rates, as shown in Xu (2018).
299   Lastly, for sparse graphons, our method empirically outperforms USVT, as demonstrated in Table 3,
300   highlighting its advantage in handling networks with lower densities.

525   5   D ISCUSSION
526
527   In this paper, we present an effective and efficient estimation method for the additive separable
528   graphon (ASG) model based on subgraph counts. We provide theoretical justifications for the meth-
529   ods applied to ASG(r) with fixed r, and evaluate their performance through simulation studies.
530
      There are several promising directions for future research. In our simulations, we found that the per-
531
      formance of our method in sparse graphons is competitive. Therefore, investigating the convergence
532   rate as well as optimality of our method in the context of sparse graphons would be a valuable next
533   step. Additionally, exploring the selection of “optimal” subgraphs offers another important research
534   avenue. Finally, it remains an open question whether our method can be extended to cases where r
535   diverges with n.
