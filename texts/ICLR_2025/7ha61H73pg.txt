009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large language models (LLMs) through fine-tuning is essential for tai-
012            loring them to specific applications. Therefore, understanding what LLMs learn
013            during the alignment process is crucial. Recent studies suggest that alignment pri-
014            marily adjusts a model’s presentation style rather than its foundational knowledge,
015            indicating that only certain components of the model are significantly impacted.
016            To delve deeper into LLM alignment, we propose to identify which layers within
017            LLMs are most critical to the alignment process, thereby uncovering how align-
018
               ment influences model behavior at a granular level. We propose a novel approach
               to identify the important layers for LLM alignment (ILA). It involves learning a
019
               binary mask for each incremental weight matrix in the LoRA algorithm, indicating
020
               the significance of each layer. ILA consistently identifies important layers across
021            various alignment datasets, with nearly 90% overlap even with substantial dataset
022            differences, highlighting fundamental patterns in LLM alignment. Experimental
023            results indicate that freezing non-essential layers improves overall model perfor-
024            mance, while selectively tuning the most critical layers significantly enhances
025            fine-tuning efficiency with minimal performance loss.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Aligning large language models (LLMs) with specific requirements is essential for enhancing their
031   utility across diverse applications (Luo et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023b; Li et al.,
032   2023). Fine-tuning LLMs during the alignment process can significantly improve the models’
033
      capabilities to meet targeted needs (Bubeck et al., 2023). Typically, alignment involves fine-tuning
      the model on diverse datasets, which may include both human-curated (Rajani et al., 2023) and LLM-
034
      generated (Taori et al., 2023) data. Such fine-tuning approaches encompass instruction tuning (Wei
035
      et al., 2021) and preference learning (Bai et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024). Given the significant
036   cost associated with full parameter fine-tuning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Hu et al.,
037   2021; Chen et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2024) algorithms have emerged as a popular alternative, offering a
038   balance between performance and resource efficiency.
039
      Understanding what LLMs actually learn during the alignment process remains a critical question.
040
      LIMA (Zhou et al., 2023) posits that the majority of knowledge and capabilities are developed during
041
      the pretraining phase, with alignment primarily serving to refine the model’s conversational style
042   and formatting. Using a well-selected set of 1,000 training examples for supervised fine-tuning
043   (SFT), LIMA successfully produced a high-quality aligned model. Similarly, URIAL (Lin et al.,
044   2023) investigated the token distribution of LLMs before and after alignment and found that most
045   changes were related to “stylistic tokens”, such as discourse markers and transition words, while the
046   knowledge-intensive content largely remained untouched, coming from the base pre-trained model.
047   These findings imply that the alignment process mainly adjusts the model’s presentation style rather
048   than altering its foundational knowledge.
049   To gain a deeper understanding of LLM alignment, we adopt a distinct approach by examining it at the
050   model parameter level. In our pilot study, we investigate the impact of different model components
051   on alignment performance, we conducted a simple analysis by fine-tuning only specific layers and
052   evaluating the resulting performance, as presented in Table 1. The results clearly indicate that fine-
053   tuning different components of the LLM leads to considerable performance differences. For instance,
      fine-tuning the feed-forward network (FFN) layers achieves performance similar to fine-tuning all

      To this end, we propose to identify the layers that are most critical to alignment performance during
091
      the fine-tuning process. We develop a novel approach for identifying the important layers for LLM
092
      alignment, called ILA. Specifically, we learn a binary mask for each incremental weight matrix in the

094   mask indicates that the corresponding layer has negligible influence during the fine-tuning phase,
095   while a value of one denotes that the layer is crucial for the process. We employ gradient descent to

098

100     differences in dataset, we find similar rankings of important layers during alignment for the same
101
        pre-trained model (see Fig. 1). This suggests that the alignment process equips the model with

102
        evidence corroborates previous research findings and offers new insights into LLM alignment.
103
104   • Enhancing Performance by Freezing Unimportant Layers. We show that freezing approximately

      Models and Baselines. We use four different models as the base for our experiments: L LAMA 2-
240
      7B (Touvron et al., 2023), L LAMA 2-13B (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama 3.1-8B (Dubey et al.,
241
      2024), and Mistral-7B-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023). The baselines include (1) LoRA (Hu et al., 2021):
242   We add trainable pairs of rank decomposition matrices in parallel to existing weight matrices,
243   including query/key/value projection (Wq , Wk , Wv ), output projection (Wo ) in the self-attention,
244   feed-forward networks (Wup , Wdown , Wgate ), and the output layer on top of the transformer (Whead ).
245   (2) AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023a): It dynamically adjusts the rank of incremental matrices to
246   control the parameter budget. Similar to LoRA, we add AdaLoRA modules to all linear layers of
247   the base model. (3) QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023): It is a fine-tuning method that significantly
248   reduces memory usage by quantizing the weights of pre-trained language models while maintaining
249   competitive performance. (4) Full Finetune: The model is initialized to the pre-trained weights and
250
      biases, and all model parameters undergo gradient updates.
251
252   Evaluation and Training Setup. Our evaluation of language model alignment encompasses
253   two main dimensions: (1) Language Understanding Ability: We utilized three distinct datasets
254   (i.e., MMLU (Massively Multitask Language Understanding) (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and Hel-
255
      laswag (Zellers et al., 2019) to evaluate this aspect. MMLU evaluates models across diverse subjects
      requiring specialized knowledge, while Hellaswag tests commonsense reasoning by asking the model
256
      to predict the most plausible continuation of a given context. (2) Conversational Ability: We use
257
      two different datasets: MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), which involves multi-turn conversations, and
258   Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), which involves single-turn conversations. We use GPT-4 to score the
259   responses. We asks GPT-4 to grade and give a score to model’s answer directly without pairwise
260   comparison, using the implementation version of MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023). For a fair compari-
261   son, we conduct a small range of training hyperparameter searches for LoRA and full fine-tuning to
262   ensure that we get strong baselines. More details are provided in Appendix B.
263
      Targeted Performance. (1) Language Understanding Ability: Recent research (Du et al., 2020;
264   Sun et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2024) suggests that the learning of language understanding tasks
265   essentially occurs during the pre-training phase of the base model. Therefore, significant performance
266   improvements in language understanding tasks (i.e., MMLU, Hellaswag) after alignment are not
267   expected. However, it is crucial to ensure the model retains the learned knowledge during alignment.
268   (2) Conversational Ability: Without alignment, the pre-train model’s conversational ability is poor.
269   For example, L LAMA 2-7B often produces incorrect or irrelevant responses on the Vicuna dataset.
      However, its conversational ability can be significantly improved through the alignment process.

289   4.1     L AYER S IGNIFICANCE IN LLM A LIGNMENT
290
291   In this subsection, we applied our ILA algorithm to identify the ranking of important layers during
292   alignment across three different datasets: No Robots, LIMA, and Alpaca-GPT4, as shown in Fig. 1.
293   Additionally, we analyzed the importance ranking of layers identified at different training milestones,
294   as depicted in Fig. 2. To further validate the similarity of these important layers, we used the Jaccard
295
      similarity coefficient to quantify the relationship between two sets. Specifically, we defined the top
      75% highest-scoring layers as the important layers, denoted as set S. The similarity between two
296
297
      distinct sets, S1 and S2 , is calculated as: J(S1 , S2 ) = |S1 ∩S2 |
                                                                 |S1 ∪S2 | . A value of J = 1 indicates identical
298   sets, while J = 0 indicates no overlap. Below, we highlight our main observations.
299
300       Consistency in Layer Importance Ranking Across Various Alignment Datasets. Our findings
301       demonstrate a remarkable consistency in layer importance ranking, as evidenced by: (1) the
302       retrieval of highly similar important layers across different alignment datasets, as shown in Fig. 1
303
          and Table 2; (2) the consistent identification of important layers despite the optimization of γ
          with varying random seeds, as illustrated in Table 3; (3) the ability to identify similar important
304
          layers at different or early (25%) training stages, as depicted in Fig. 2 and Table 4.
305
306
307
      The experimental results corroborate the robustness of our algorithm, which consistently identifies
      stable and similar layers across different alignment datasets. This is particularly noteworthy in light of
308
      recent work that suggests alignment fundamentally involves shifts in stylistic tokens (Lin et al., 2023).
309
      Thus, the essence of alignment is the pursuit of similar capabilities, which aligns with our discovery
310   that the important layers corresponding to different datasets exhibit similarity. This convergence of
311   findings underscores the intrinsic alignment of our algorithm’s performance with the fundamental
312   objectives of dataset alignment.
313
      Given the established importance ranking of the model layers, which proves stable for the alignment
314
      task, we must consider how to leverage this ranking. We will address this from both performance
315
      and efficiency perspectives. First, to maximize the performance of the fine-tuned model, we should
316   avoid fine-tuning layers that could negatively impact the model, focusing instead on those deemed
317   less significant. Second, to enhance the efficiency of fine-tuning and minimize resource consumption,
318   we should concentrate our efforts on layers that are particularly vital to the model’s success. Detailed
319   experiments and analyses of these two cases will be presented in the following section.

369   matrices), our method performed better. This outcome highlights that simply adjusting LoRA’s
370   matrix rank does not necessarily yield superior results in alignment tasks, as confirmed by other

375   using full training iterations on the No Robots dataset, and then directly applied this ranking to other
376   datasets. Although dataset-specific importance rankings can yield further improvements (see Table. 9
377   in Section 5), the consistent cross-dataset performance achieved using a single ranking highlights the
      robustness and generalizability of our approach.

      5    R ELATED W ORKS
494
495   Large Language Models (LLMs) Alignment. Language models are initially pretrained to learn
496
      general-purpose representations, enabling their transfer to a wide range of language understanding
      and generation tasks (Qiu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Nijkamp et al., 2022). To align these models
497
      with specific user needs and improve their performance on targeted applications, techniques such as
498
      Instruction Tuning (Zhang et al., 2023c; Sun et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2023) and Preference
499   Learning (Hejna et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Li et al.,
500   2024) are commonly employed. Tuning-based alignment can introduce issues such as forgetting in
501   LLMs (Wang et al., 2022a;b) and underfitting (Zhang et al., 2023c; Sun et al., 2023).
502
      To explore the nature of model alignment through various studies. LIMA (Zhou et al., 2023) achieved
503
      a well-aligned model by fine-tuning nearly 1,000 samples using SFT, and hypothesized that the
504
      alignment process essentially teaches the model how to conduct conversations in specific formats or
505   meet certain requirements without acquiring new knowledge. Similar findings have been reported in
506   recent studies (Chen et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Gudibande et al., 2023). Duan et al. (2023) analyzed
507   the hidden states of LLMs, exploring the similarities between in-context learning (ICL) and instruction
508   tuning (IT) regarding their impact on downstream tasks. URIAL (Lin et al., 2023) investigated the
509   token distribution before and after alignment, suggesting that alignment primarily shifts “stylistic
510   tokens” like discourse markers and transition words, while the distribution of knowledge-intensive
511   terms remains largely unchanged. Based on prior research, we hypothesize that the abilities learned
512   during alignment are relatively narrow in scope. To better understand this process, we propose an
513
      approach to identify which layers are genuinely important during alignment.
514   Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). To tackle the high computational costs of full-model
515   fine-tuning, especially with Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) ranging from billions to trillions
516   of parameters (Brown et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022), PEFT methods have been developed to reduce
517   parameter usage while maintaining the effectiveness and stability of knowledge transfer (Tang et al.,
518   2024; Peng et al., 2024). These approaches include partial fine-tuning, which selectively targets
519
      specific model components (Zaken et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2020; Ansell et al., 2021; Guo et al.,
      2020), and soft prompt-based fine-tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Asai et al., 2022).
520
      Notable methods include BitFit (Zaken et al., 2021), Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019), LoRA (Hu
521
      et al., 2021) and its variants (Zhang et al., 2023b; Meng et al., 2024). Recent studies (Pan et al.,
522   2024; Xu & Zhang, 2024; Panda et al., 2024) have shown that fine-tuning only a small portion of
523   a model while masking most components can still achieve promising results in LLMs. However,
524   these masking strategies are often applied randomly, akin to dropout, which is suboptimal and lacks
525   consistency. While effective for efficient fine-tuning, these methods provide limited insight into
526   understanding the alignment task. To overcome these limitations, our approach leverages the concept
527   of skill localization (Panigrahi et al., 2023) by dynamically identifying and fine-tuning the critical
528   components for each task. By focusing solely on the most important regions, this method significantly
529   improves the efficiency of model fine-tuning while ensuring strong performance.
530
531   6    C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In conclusion, our proposed method, ILA, focuses on identifying critical layers in the alignment
534   process by learning binary masks for LoRA weight matrices. ILA demonstrates consistent iden-
535   tification of important layers across different datasets, regardless of significant content variations,
536   suggesting that the alignment process imparts similar capabilities to the model irrespective of the
537   training data. This finding provides valuable insights into the specific roles of layers during alignment.
538   By strategically tuning only the most vital layers, ILA effectively reduces computational overhead,
539   and by freezing less important layers, it further enhances model responsiveness and accuracy, leading
      to more efficient resource utilization.
