009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Face video editing (FVE) requires maintaining temporal consistency and iden-
012            tity preservation while manipulating specific attributes. However, existing FVE
013            methods often introduce unwanted artifacts and affect non-target attributes during
014            editing. To address these limitations, we propose GuideEdit to enhance the pre-
015
               cision of face video editing. Given the inherent linearity of the latent variables in
               the bottleneck layer of the diffusion U-Net model, there exists a linear mapping
016
               between the input and the latent representation. This allows us to extract a latent
017
               basis within the latent space that effectively encodes the key features related to
018            target facial attributes. By comparing the latent basis of the original video to that
019            of the manipulated video, we quantify the manipulation degree, which indicates
020            the extent of changes made. This manipulation degree serves as a guide for deter-
021            mining the specific components to be edited, then we achieve more precise control
022            at each denoising step. Integrating this fine-grained control into the editing pro-
023            cess allows GuideEdit to enhance temporal consistency and preserve identity of
024            FVE, while minimizing the introduction of artifacts. Extensive experiments on
025            diverse real-world videos demonstrate the effectiveness of GuideEdit, showcas-
026
               ing its ability to achieve precise, high-quality edits that maintain coherence across
               frames and ensure the preservation of essential visual elements.

      Face attribute editing has emerged as an essential task in computer vision, with applications ranging
042   from film production to virtual reality, social media content, and digital avatars (Zhan et al., 2023;
043   Kim et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2018a; Zhu et al., 2020). While significant progress
044   has been made in face image editing (Shen et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022), compar-
045   atively fewer efforts have focused on FVE. The core challenge in FVE is to modify specific facial
046   attributes (i.e., expression, age or hairstyle) while maintaining the temporal consistency, identity
047   preservation, and background integrity of the video (Wang et al., 2024). Traditional image-based
048   editing methods can’t be applied to video editing directly, because they struggle to maintain consis-
049   tency across video frames due to the complex temporal dependencies and the intricate relationship
050
      between facial attributes and identity (Ceylan et al., 2023).
051   Several GAN-based methods for FVE utilize pre-trained StyleGAN models (Tzaban et al., 2022;
052   Patashnik et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2020) to facilitate the editing process. These
053   approaches commonly employ GAN inversion(Karras et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2022), where the pre-
      trained GAN is used to map the input video frames into a latent space, enabling the application of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      desired edits. However, the quality of the edited video is heavily reliant on the effectiveness of the
055   GAN inversion. These GAN-based methods often struggle to accurately reconstruct the original
056   input, resulting in suboptimal editing quality(Preechakul et al., 2022). More recently, diffusion
057   models renowned for their strong generative capabilities, have demonstrated success in FVE (Kim
058   et al., 2023; Preechakul et al., 2022), outperforming GAN-based approaches in editing quality. The
059   editing process in diffusion-based FVE methods is typically framed as a conditional generation
060   task (Zhang et al., 2023; Croitoru et al., 2023), where the desired target attribute is progressively
061   introduced into the video at various stages of the denoising process (Kim et al., 2023). However,
062   simply introducing the target attribute at different denoising steps without additional constraints can
063
      inadvertently affect the other attributes of the video, such as identity, expression, or background.
      This occurs because the diffusion model lacks precise control over the editing process (Zhao et al.,
064
      2024; Yu et al., 2023), leading to undesired modifications in non-target regions or features.
065
066   To improve diffusion-based FVE and achieve precise control, we propose GuideEdit that edits real-
067   world face videos without affecting the identity and background features, while ensuring time con-
068   sistency (as presented in Figure 1). Given the local linearity of the latent variables in the bottleneck
069
      layer of the UNet architecture (Park et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2022), a linear mapping exists be-
      tween the inputs and the latent variables. However, since the latent variables encode both the input
070
      frame features and the assigned attributes features, directly using them to measure the impact of tar-
071
      get attributes could result in interference from unrelated components (Park et al., 2023). Therefore,
072   GuideEdit leverages the local linearity property to isolate and extract only the latent basis vectors that
073   are most relevant to the target attributes, avoiding unintended modifications to other elements. To
074   ensure precise control, GuideEdit corrects the directional deviation of the estimated noise between
075   the input with the introduced attribute and the original input according to the similarity between the
076   latent basis of the newly introduced target attributes and the original video. This correction refines
077   the denoising process to focus exclusively on the components associated with target attributes, en-
078   suring that only the target attribute is modified while preserving other attributes. As a result, the
079   effectiveness of the manipulation process is significantly enhanced, allowing for more precise and
080
      consistent editing without compromising the integrity of the original video.
081   We summary the contributions of our proposed method shortly as follows.
082
083          • We propose a new approach GuideEdit for FVE within the diffusion model framework,
084            where precise control is achieved by leveraging the local linearity of the latent variables in
085            the bottleneck layer of a UNet architecture.
086          • We introduce a latent basis extraction mechanism that identifies the most influential features
087            of the input video’s conditions. By calculating the similarity between the latent basis of
088            the original and edited video, we quantify the degree of modification, providing a precise
089            control signal for the editing process.
090          • We present a proximal guidance mechanism that uses the latent basis similarity to guide
091            the denoising process in the diffusion model. This ensures that changes are confined to the
092            specified target attribute, reducing unintended alterations and enhancing the quality of the
093            edited video.
094          • Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed
095            method, showing improvements in identity preservation, target attribute modification, and
096            temporal consistency.

      2     R ELATED WORK
099

      2.1   FACE V IDEO E DITING
101
102   Existing methods for FVE can be broadly categorized into two types: GAN-based and diffusion-
103   based methods. GAN-based methods typically leverage pre-trained GAN models like Style-
104   GAN (Tzaban et al., 2022; Karras et al., 2019) for face video manipulation. A common technique in
105   these methods involves GAN inversion, where the input video frames are mapped to the latent space
106   of a pre-trained GAN (Karras et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2022), and the desired edits are applied by
107   manipulating the latent codes (Patashnik et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2020). While GAN-based methods
      have achieved high-quality image synthesis, they suffer from several drawbacks in the context of


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      video editing. The effectiveness of GAN-based video editing is heavily dependent on the quality of
109   the GAN inversion, which often struggles to perfectly reconstruct the input video, leading to loss of
110   detail or failure to preserve identity features (Preechakul et al., 2022).
111
112
      In diffusion-based methods, the editing process is typically formulated as a conditional generation
      task, where the target attribute is introduced into the video during the denoising process (Kim et al.,
113
      2023). These models gradually modify the video by reversing a noising process, progressively
114
      refining the video’s attributes over several steps. Diffusion-based methods offer several advantages
115   over GAN-based approaches. Due to their iterative nature, diffusion models can more effectively
116   preserve temporal consistency, as the modifications are made gradually, and the generative process
117   considers the entire video context. However, these methods typically involve a trade-off in terms of
118   computational cost, as the iterative denoising steps are time-consuming, leading to slower inference
119   times compared to GAN-based methods.
120
121   2.2   L ATENT S PACE A NALYSIS
122
123   The study of latent spaces has gained significant attention in recent years. In the field of Generative
124   Adversarial Networks (GANs), researchers have proposed various methods to manipulate the latent
125
      space to achieve the desired effect in the generated images (Ramesh et al., 2018; Patashnik et al.,
      2021; Abdal et al., 2021; Shen & Zhou, 2021; Härkönen et al., 2020). More recently, several studies
126
      have examined the geometrical properties of latent space in GANs and utilized these findings for
127
      image manipulations (Choi et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021). Some studies have applied Riemannian
128   geometry to analyze the latent spaces of deep generative models (Arvanitidis et al., 2017; 2020;
129   Chen et al., 2018; Lee & Park, 2023; Lee et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2018). (Shao et al., 2018)
130   proposed a pullback metric on the latent space from image space Euclidean metric to analyze the
131   latent space’s geometry. This method has been widely used in VAEs and GANs because it only
132   requires a differentiable map from latent space to image space. And (Park et al., 2023) extend it into
133   diffuison models (DMs) to investigate the geometry of latent space of DMs to facilitate the image
134   editing. However, it is challenging for the pullback metric to accurately capture the geometry of
135   the latent space from the image space, as the image space contains excessive information, making it
136
      difficult to identify the correct directions for editing.
137
138   2.3   I NVERSION - BASED G UIDANCE
139
      DDIM inversion (Song et al., 2020) exhibits great potential in editing tasks by deterministically cal-
140
      culating and encoding the context information in a latent and reconstructing the original image with
141   it. Applying editing prompt upon the inverted latent code to guide the denoising process greatly
142   improved the test-time efficiency. Leveraging optimization on null-text embedding, Null-text Inver-
143   sion (Mokady et al., 2023) further improved the identity preservation of the edit. However, all these
144   methods rely on optimization at test-time for accurate reconstruction, which typically requires sev-
145   eral minutes. Negative-prompt inversion (NPI) (Miyake et al., 2023) further reduces the computation
146   cost for the inversion step while generates similarly competitive reconstruction results as Null-text
147   inversion. However, NPI may occasionally introduce artifacts due to its underlying assumptions.
148   And ProxEdit (Han et al., 2024) introduces an inversion guidance technique that applies a one-step
149
      gradient descent on the current latent representation, aligning it with the inversion latent to correct
      errors introduced during the reconstruction process. However, this ProxEdit method requires manu-
150
      ally setting correction thresholds for different editing tasks, which can introduce additional bias.

153   3     D IFFUSION - BASED FACE V IDEO E DITING
154
155   Let X = {x1 , ..., xn } represent a video consisting of n frames, where each xi is a single frame
156   from the original video. The goal of diffusion-based human video editing is to manipulate specific
157   attributes of the human subjects in the video (i.e., facial expressions, hairstyles) while preserving
158
      other attributes such as identity, background, and temporal consistency. The editing process in
      diffusion-based methods can be formulated as a conditional generation task, where target attributes
159
      are encoded as conditioning inputs and introduced during the video reconstruction process.
160
161   The video frames are firstly reversed into noisy representations by forward diffusion process. Then
      the forward diffusion process progressively applies noise to the input frames, resulting in noisy rep-


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      resentations Xt = {xt,1 , ..., xt,n } for each time step t ∈ T , where T is the total number of diffusion
163   steps. The reverse diffusion process reconstructs the video by gradually removing the noise, and dur-
164   ing this process, the target attribute encoded as a condition ∆c is introduced at denoising steps. The
165   reverse process is typically parameterized by a neural network Fθ with parameters θ that predicts
166   the noise in each frame, guiding the denoising process:
167                       pθ (Xt−1 |Xt , ∆c) = N (Xt−1 ; µθ (Xt , t, ∆c), Σθ (Xt , t, ∆c))                  (1)
168
169   Thus, the final output video X̂ = X0 = {x̂1 , ..., x̂n } retains the introduced attribute, while preserv-
170
      ing identity and background details.
171   However, while diffusion-based FVE introduces target attributes effectively, it struggles to preserve
172   identity and background details due to the lack of precise control in the editing process. To address
173   this limitation, we introduce GuideEdit in Section 4, which enhances the accuracy and quality of
174   diffusion-based FVE.

224
      The noisy representations XTr and XTc obtained in Section 4.1 are put into a pre-trained UNet F to
      predict the noise of each frame, we use Fe and Fd to denote the encoder and decoder of the UNet
225
      respectively. Since the extraction of the latent basis is identical for both XTr and XTc , we use XTc
226

227   latent variable, and C represent C c at time step t.
228
229   The latent variable H in the bottleneck layer of the U-Net has been shown to exhibit a locally linear
230
      structure (Kwon et al., 2022), which makes it suitable for using the Euclidean metric to measure
      changes in H (Kim et al., 2023). In the denoising process, the transformation from the input repre-
231
      sentations to the latent space can be expressed as Fe : X , C → H, where Fe maps the input X and
232
      the editing conditions C to the latent variable H.
233
234   However, since X contains a lot of information unrelated to the specific editing direction, the vari-
235   ability it introduces into H might not align with the desired editing directions. To overcome this
236
      issue, we focus primarily on how C (the editing condition) influences H, effectively isolating the
      impact of the target attribute from other unrelated aspects of X . This approach enables us to better
237
      control the editing process by only adjusting the components of H that are relevant to the intended
238
      changes, ensuring more precise and consistent video edits.

      5.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
307
308   Dataset. We evaluate the performance of our proposed GuideEdit on real-world videos sampled
309   from the HDTF dataset (Zhang et al., 2021) and the VoxCeleb dataset (Nagrani et al., 2017). Specif-
310   ically, we randomly select 20 videos from each dataset, ensuring diversity across gender, age, and
311   skin tones. Each video consists of hundreds of frames, from which we randomly sample 32 consecu-
312   tive frames for each evaluation. The selected frames are aligned and cropped following the approach
313   in (Tzaban et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023), and subsequently resized to a resolution of 256 × 256.
314   Baseline. We compare our method extensively with several previous state-of-the-art baselines.
315   We choose diffusion-based editing method DVA (Kim et al., 2023) and transformer-based method
316   Latent-trans (Yao et al., 2021). For GAN-based methods, we choose STIT (Tzaban et al., 2022),
317   PTI (Roich et al., 2022) and StyleCLIP (Patashnik et al., 2021). Some of the baseline methods are
318   designed for image editing, we adapt them into the video editing paradigm (the details can refer to
319   Appendix C.1). It is important to note that, for a fair comparison of the reconstruction abilities of
320
      different editing methods, the original videos are used solely as input. None of the editing methods
      have access to the original videos during the output stage, ensuring that the reconstruction quality is
321
      evaluated independently of the input data.
322
323   Metric. For comprehensive evaluation of our proposed GuideEdit and the baseline methods, we
      utilize a range of evaluation metrics. For the evaluation of reconstruction performance, we use


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018b), MSE and FID. For time consistency evalua-
325   tion of manipulated videos, we apply TL-ID and TG-ID (Tzaban et al., 2022). For evaluating video
326   editing performance, we use the Identity Preservation Rate (IPR), Target Attribute Change Rate
327   (TACR) (Yao et al., 2021), and CLIP score. The attribute preservation rate measures the proportion
328   of samples where non-target attributes remain unchanged during editing. The identity preservation
329   score represents the average cosine similarity between the embeddings of the original frames and
330   the manipulated results, reflecting how well the subject’s identity is maintained. The CLIP score is
331   computed based on the alignment between the target attribute and the edited frames.
332   Implementation. We implement the proposed GuideEdit using a diffusion autoencoder with a UNet
333   architecture as the noise estimator. To enhance the model’s ability to reconstruct the background in
334   face videos, we fine-tune the pre-trained diffusion autoencoder from (Kim et al., 2023) on the HDTF
335   dataset (the details of finetuning the diffusion autoencoder can refer to Appendix C.3). Note that
336   during the editing process, the pre-trained diffusion autoencoder model remains frozen. We use the
337   DDIM sampler, setting the inference time steps to 1000. The batch size for inference is set to 4, and
338   all inference is performed on 4 RTX4090 GPUs.

526   6                  C ONCLUSION
527
528   In conclusion, we present GuideEdit, a novel diffusion-based method for FVE that effectively ad-
529   dresses the critical challenges of maintaining temporal consistency and preserving identity while
530   manipulating specific attributes. Our approach leverages the inherent linearity of latent variables in
531
      the bottleneck layer of the diffusion U-Net model, enabling us to extract a latent basis that encodes
      key features related to target facial attributes. By comparing the latent basis of the original video
532
      with that of the manipulated video, we quantify the manipulation degree, which indicates the ex-
533
      tent of changes made. This manipulation degree serves as a guidance for determining the specific
534   components to be edited, allowing for fine-grained control at each denoising step. Integrating this
535   precise control into the editing process enhances temporal consistency and ensures the preserva-
536   tion of identity, all while minimizing the introduction of artifacts. Extensive experiments conducted
537   on diverse real-world videos demonstrate the effectiveness of GuideEdit, showcasing its ability to
538   achieve precise, high-quality edits that maintain coherence across frames and preserve essential vi-
539   sual elements. This work not only advances the state of the art in FVE but also highlights the
      potential of diffusion-based methods for future generative modeling applications.

888   We finetune the pre-trained diffusion autoencoder from Kim et al. (2023) on the HDTF dataset. The
889   loss function used for finetuning consists of two components. The first component is the standard
890   DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models) loss function, represented as:
891
                                         Lddim = Eϵt ∼N (0,I) ||ϵrt − ϵt ||1                              (11)
892
893   where ϵrt can refer to Equation 3 and ϵt is the true noise, t ∈ T . This loss is minimized to ensure
894   accurate denoising and reconstruction during the finetuning process.
895   To enhance the robustness of the model to noise, we sample images given the time step with two
896   different noise realizations, denoted as ϵ1 and ϵ2 , where ϵ1 , ϵ2 ∼ N (0, 1). The sampled images are
897   represented as X̂t1 and X̂t2 .
898

900                                    Lreg = Eϵ1 ,ϵ2 ∼N (0,1) ||X̂t1 − X̂t2 ||1                          (12)
901
902   This loss encourages the model to accurately predict the noise for both sampled images, thereby
903   improving its robustness against variations in noise during the denoising process.
