011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            Limited access to dedicated navigation data in visually impaired individuals is a signifi-
014            cant bottleneck in the development of AI-driven assistive devices. To address this, we have
015
               developped a virtual environment designed to extract various human-like navigation data
               from procedurally generated labyrinths. Using reinforcement learning and semantic seg-
016
               mentation, we trained a convolutional neural network to perform obstacle avoidance from
017
               synthetic data. Our model outperformed state-of-the-art backbones including DINOv2-B
018            in safe pathway identification in real world. In conclusion, despite being trained only on
019            synthetic data, our model successfully extracted features compatible with safe navigation
020            in real-world settings, opening new avenues for visually impaired.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Globally, millions of people live with blindness, which is associated to severe restrictions in mobility and as
026   well as a significant increased risk of fall-related injuries (Wood et al., 2011; Brunes & Heir, 2021; Singh &
027
      Maurya, 2022). The recent development of retinal and cortical prostheses has not achieved vision restoration
      in blinds yet. As an alternative, Sensory Substitution Devices (SSDs) hold significant promise for aiding
028
      visually impaired individuals by converting environmental sensor data into tactile or auditory stimuli (Bach-
029
      y Rita, 1972; Jicol et al., 2020). However, current SSDs face limitations in conveying complex visual scenes
030   through skin or ears (Elli et al., 2014), primarily due to the narrow bandwidth of these sensory channels,
031   which can lead to cognitive burden (De Jong, 2010). Indeed, artificial intelligence (AI), and in particular
032   deep learning, enables the extraction of relevant information to be conveyed to the blind.
033
      Contrary to autonomous driving (Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022; Toromanoff et al., 2019) and robotics (Shah &
034
      Levine, 2022; Kruse et al., 2013), SSDs have not benefited from the availability of navigation datasets. As
035
      a result, current applications of deep learning for effective navigation aids predominantly rely on general-
036   purpose datasets for object recognition and classification (Scalvini et al., 2023; Mukhiddinov & Cho, 2021;
037   Kim et al., 2023; Kerdegari et al., 2016), semantic segmentation (Tapu et al., 2017; Zheng & Weng, 2016), or
038   depth estimation (Bai et al., 2017; Sharma et al., 2016; Asiedu Asante & Imamura, 2023). While these appli-
039   cations can extract high-level features that improve environmental understanding for the visually impaired,
040   they do not convey information about navigation decisions. A few studies have used navigation-specific data
041   to provide guidance (Zheng & Weng, 2016; Kerdegari et al., 2016), but the amount of data was limited to a
042   few hundred samples due to the resource-intensive nature of the collection process.
043   The aim of this study is twofold: (i) To address the unavailability of human navigation data, we propose a
044   generic method for training AI systems specifically designed for the blind. Our approach relies on synthetic
045   semantic segmentation maps to optimize SSD outputs in virtual environments, enabling straightforward real-
046   world transferability without requiring advanced domain adaptation (Xu et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023). (ii)


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      We evaluate the efficacy of this method by first training an AI-based system for obstacle avoidance in virtual
048
      environments and then demonstrating its ability to enable safe navigation using low-dimensional navigation
049   cues in both virtual and real-world settings. To support this, we introduce NavIndoor, a new virtual environ-
050   ment designed for the automatic generation of synthetic human-like navigation data. NavIndoor leverages
051   procedural generation to create randomized, obstacle-filled mazes with structure similar to real-world indoor
052   environments, allowing for the simulation of various navigation scenarios in an efficient, safe, and scalable
053   manner.
054
      In Section 3, we describe our method for optimisation of AI-based SSDs within virtual environments and
055   deployment in real-world setting. In Section 4, we present the newly proposed virtual environment and
056   demonstrate that a compact CNN allows safe navigation in it. Finally, in Section 5, we illustrate the model’s
057   proficiency in real-world settings by estimating the forward navigation boundary in the Active Vision Dataset
058   and showing that our model outperforms standard backbones in safe pathway identification (AUC 0.92).
059   Furthermore, we show that incorporating random morphological operators around obstacles during training
060   in virtual environments improves generalization to real-world data. The scalable and flexible nature of our
061   method, combined with its potential for generalization to various navigation tasks, underscores its promise
062   in enhancing feature extraction for future sensory substitution devices.
063   We summarize our contributions as follows:
064
065          • We identify a significant gap in the literature regarding the use of navigation data to improve Sen-
066
               sory Substitution Systems.
             • We release NavIndoor, an open-source software for the computationally efficient generation of pro-
067
               cedurally generated, obstacle-filled environments, enabling seamless integration with AI systems.
068
               NavIndoor facilitates the efficient creation of various large-scale, human-like navigation datasets.
069          • We show that synthetic data enables the extraction of low-dimensional features for navigation by
070            individuals with visual impairments.
071          • We demonstrate that applying basic morphological operators to synthetic semantic segmentation
072            maps enhances performance in real-world conditions after training.

      S ENSORY S UBSTITUTION D EVICES AND AI FOR THE VISUALLY IMPAIRED
095
096   We reviewed the literature on sensory substitution devices and electronic travel aids released from 1970.
097   The literature review was performed in PubMed and completed with research using arXiv, Elicit, and con-
098   ventional search engines. Papers proposing new systems for sensory substitution were included, resulting in
099   61 studies. Detailed methodology and results are presented in Appendix A.
100
      Various SSDs have been proposed to improve navigation for the blind. Traditional visual SSDs often utilized
101
      image mapping through haptic feedback (Bach-y Rita et al., 1998; Kajimoto et al., 2004; 2006; DANILOV
102   & TYLER, 2005) or audio representations derived from images (Auvray et al., 2007; Meijer, 1992a). How-
103   ever, these approaches demand substantial attentional resources (Lee, 2019; Theurel et al., 2013), leading
104   to cognitive overload (De Jong, 2010), and limiting their application to controlled environments (Elli et al.,
105   2014).
106
      Consequently, image and signal processing have been used to improve extraction of salient features using
107
      infrared, LiDAR, ultrasonic and mostly (50/61, 82%) visual sensors.
108
109   The use of machine learning and neural networks were introduced in 2009 and changed drastically the design
110   of new devices by involving 58% (28/48) studies since then. Applications of neural networks for sensory
111
      substitution devices include image segmentation, object recognition, or classification (Busaeed et al., 2022;
      Scalvini et al., 2023; Asiedu Asante & Imamura, 2023; Afif et al., 2020; Sulaman et al., 2023; Bhatlawande
112
      et al., 2022; Chaudhary & Dr. PrabhatVerma, 2023; Mukhiddinov & Cho, 2021), object tracking (Tapu et al.,
113
      2017), speech understanding (Bai et al., 2017), image captioning (Ganesan et al., 2022; Kavitha et al., 2023),
114   optimization of auditory representation of images with GANs (Kim et al., 2021; 2023; Hu et al., 2019; Port
115   et al., 2021), and best action prediction (Zheng & Weng, 2016; Kerdegari et al., 2016).
116
117
118   AI- BASED DEVICES FOR OBSTACLE AVOIDANCE
119
      Out of 20 neural-network SSD studies, 9 were aimed at obstacle avoidance tasks, which is critical for safe
120
      navigation. Limitations with such approaches mainly rely on computational and energetic cost, because
121
      such systems often require substantial hardware resources to perform multiple scene understanding tasks
122   in parallel and in real-time (Mahendran et al., 2021). Also, complex operations such as depth estimation
123   may require expensive or heavier sensors, such as stereo camera (Caraiman et al., 2017; Asiedu Asante &
124   Imamura, 2023).
125
      On the other hand, 2 studies (Zheng & Weng, 2016; Kerdegari et al., 2016) proposed to estimate directly
126
      the best possible action for the blind, but such tasks require extensive and costly acquisition of human
127
      navigation data. In (Zheng & Weng, 2016), authors collected 4109 tuples of GPS/visual sensor information
128   labelled with the best possible action (forward/left/right/stop) predicted with a deep neural network. In
129   (Kerdegari et al., 2016), authors collected 4051 samples comprising an ultrasonic measurement coupled
130   with a performed action (forward/left/right) predicted with a multilayer perceptron.

      D OMAIN ADAPTATION FROM SIMULATION TO REAL ENVIRONMENTS
134
      Domain shift is a primary concern when deploying deep learning models trained in simulations into the real-
135
      world. Approaches to enable the transferability of AI systems from virtual to real-world environments have
136   primarily focused on achieving photorealism by scanning real 3D scenes, as highlighted in (Xia et al., 2018).
137   This strategy allows AI systems to utilize material textures for executing complex tasks and to leverage
138   detailed 3D scene representations stored in external memory. Such systems have primarily benefited robotics
139   (Hirose et al., 2019; Kang et al., 2019), where they enable fully autonomous agents to undertake complex
140   scene understanding tasks.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      Besides, SSDs are designed to prioritize the transmission of low-dimensional features that can be computed
142
      in real-time and easily interpreted by humans for navigation, and thus do not necessarily need to store a
143   3D rich representation of the surrounding environments for complex autonomous navigation tasks. Indeed,
144   on the other hand, semantic segmentation has been proposed as domain-agnostic features to allow better
145   generalization for robotics navigation in real-world (Hong et al., 2018; Chaplot et al., 2020). In a similar
146   manner, we propose to use a simple semantic view of the scene as an input for bridging domain gap. However
147   our approach is not designed to be deployed on fully autonomous agents, and focuses on extraction of low-
148   dimensional navigation cues that could be interpreted by humans with haptic or auditory feedback. This
149   approach enables the model to learn better obstacle avoidance as shown in Section 4, and considerably
150   reduces the domain gap with real-world data. Compared with photorealism approaches, the use of semantic
151
      views also reduces the input dimensionality providing faster training and avoiding texture biases.

187   ment. In the context of mobility assistance, SSDs are expected to function in a wider range of environments

      3   M ATERIALS AND M ETHOD
205
206
207
208   We propose a scalable approach that leverages virtual environments, reinforcement learning, and transferable
209   features to extract navigation-related features for SSDs, as illustrated in Figure 2. The navigation task is
210   initially learned within a virtual environment by a digital twin equipped with sensor(s) and implemented
211   through a reward function.
212   We used semantic segmentation masks as input for learning blind navigation within virtual environments,
213   because they have lower dimensionality compared with depth maps, but still capture enough information for
214   allowing navigation as shown in robotics (Hong et al., 2018; Chaplot et al., 2020).
215
      Collection of synthetic navigation data was performed within virtual environments in the form of (st , at , rt ),
216
      where st , at , and rt represent a semantic view, memory of actions taken by a blind digital twin, and reward
217
      values at time-step t, respectively. The navigation task is learned through policy learning from the semantic
218   segmentation maps. Leveraging reinforcement learning, we estimate a parametric model Qθ (st , at ) using
219   Q-learning. The Q-value function represents the expected sum of future rewards an agent would achieve in
220   state st , choosing action at , and navigating optimally thereafter. Following the training, we evalyated the
221   performance of Qθ with real-world states.
222
      To show the effectiveness of our approach, we propose to learn obstacle avoidance from a single head-
223
      mounted visual sensor, aligning with current start-of-the-art (see Figure 1) but without extensive real-world
224   data collection and annotation. Semantic segmentation maps were processed to regroup obstacles in a single
225   class and processed as 1 × 128 × 128 tensors to reduce input dimensionality. We also leveraged proce-
226   dural generation, allowing for randomization of virtual environments, semantic-segmentation specific data
227   augmentation and used a compact convolutional neural network for Q-value estimation (1.6M parameters).
228   These features aimed at improve model robustness and allow extraction of relevant feedback in real-time in
229   a sensor-lightweight and energy-efficient pipeline.
230   For synthetic data generation, we developed a virtual environment, NavIndoor, encompassing the necessary
231   features, and in which training was performed (Section 4). The following section presents the experimental
232   setup employed for learning obstacle avoidance, including design of reward function, virtual environment,
233   data randomization mechanisms, model architecture, hyperparameters optimisation as well as the model’s
234   results within virtual setting.

      3D E NVIRONMENT AND BLIND DIGITAL TWIN
253
254
255   NavIndoor is built upon Unity and MLAgents library (Juliani et al., 2018). It was developed as a platform
256
      for generation of synthetic data from navigation of a blind digital twin. NavIndoor is designed to create
      sequential, partially observable, static, procedurally generated environments filled with walls, obstacles, and
257
      collectibles. The environment generation includes maze generation and the sampling of a random starting
258
      point where the agent begins its exploration, in order to prevent memorization biases (Zhang et al., 2018).
259   The generation procedure for mazes is based on the Depth First Search algorithm (Tarjan, 1972) and de-
260   picted in Figure 3. Obstacles include both cuboids of various shapes and open-source low-polygons assets.
261   The environment was designed in Unity to allow for easy parametrization integration through Python for
262   generation of mazes, agents and sensors with different properties.
263
      A blind digital twin was designed to navigate these mazes. It has a discrete action space A =
264
      {forward, backward, rotate left, rotate right}. The agent is equipped with a frontal monocular camera hat
265   has a field of view (FOV) of 115◦ × 100◦ . The camera sensor is configured to return 128 × 128 semantic
266   segmentation maps along with RGB views of the current scene.
267

      R EWARD D ESIGN AND O BSERVATIONS
271
272   We propose using collectibles located at the center of the maze’s cells to design the reward function. The
273   agent receives a positive reward when collecting a coin, which encourages exploration of the maze. It
274
      receives a negative reward when colliding with a wall or an obstacle. At each timestep, the environment
      returns a semantic map of the current view segt as well as an RGB view ft . The semantic map has five labels:
275
      floor, obstacles, walls, coins, other. At timestep t, the state st = (segt−2 , segt−1 , segt , (ai )i∈[t−m,t−1] ) is
276
      extracted, where segi is the semantic segmentation map at timestep i, ai is a one hot encoding of action
277   taken at timestep i, and m is an action memory length parameter. Encoding semantic maps as 2D arrays
278   by associating a single value to walls (-0.5), floor (0.5), and obstacles (-1) provided better stability during
279   training compared to multi-channel semantic encoding. Additionally, our experiments showed that providing
280   time context through previously performed actions allowed the agent to escape situations where it would get
281   stuck during learning, leading to better obstacle avoidance strategies.

313   presented in Figure 4. It is composed of image and actions encoders followed with fully connected layers.

315

316
      difficulty) to determine the best training setting. Details on training and grid search are given in Appendix B.
317
      The best trained model (VC) setup was then used to trained another model (VCD) using data augmentation.
318   Data augmentation included erosion and/or dilatation morphological operators around obstacle shapes using
319   a fixed 3x3 square kernel with probability p = 0.2 for each image and operator. Morphological augmentation
320   was followed by random uniform changes in each pixel label with probability p = 0.05. These changes aim
321   to simulate potential errors during semantic segmentation of real-world images and to make the model robust
322   to various obstacle shapes. Training setups included a model was trained from standard RGB views of the
323   camera and a model trained with unvisible collectibles (NVC).
324   Two human individuals were also trained to collect the maximum amount of coins while avoiding collisions
325   in to assess human performance and compare it to our models. Human performance was evaluated under
326   settings with both visible and invisible collectibles, as well as RGB inputs, with 10 episodes for each indi-
327   vidual. Each episode lasted for 24 seconds (corresponding to 400 decision timesteps) and humans navigated

      6   C ONCLUSION
461
      In this study, we introduced a new framework aimed at improving mobility for visually impaired people
462
      through the use of synthetic data. We proposed a virtual environment specifically designed for generating
463   human-like navigation data, which can be used for training and evaluating deep learning models for SSDs.
464   Compared to previous approaches, our method offers scalability and real-time extraction of low-dimensional
465   features for safe navigation from a single visual sensor. Indeed, the proposed method ensures a high compat-
466   ibility with the lightweightness, cognitive and hardware constraints associated with SSDs. These advances

       Training was conducted alongside the agent’s exploration of the environment, utilizing an ϵ-greedy policy.
