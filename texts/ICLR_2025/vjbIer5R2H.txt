009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In the transductive learning setting, we are provided with a labeled training set
012            and an unlabeled test set, with the objective of predicting the labels of the test
013            points. This framework differs from the standard problem of fitting an unknown
014            distribution with a training set drawn independently from this distribution. In this
015            paper, we primarily improve the generalization bounds in transductive learning.
016            Specifically, we develop two novel concentration inequalities for the suprema of
017            empirical processes sampled without replacement for unbounded functions, mark-
018            ing the first discussion of the generalization performance of unbounded functions
019            in the context of sampling without replacement. We further provide two valuable
020
               applications of our new inequalities: on one hand, we firstly derive fast excess risk
               bounds for empirical risk minimization in transductive learning under unbounded
021
               losses. On the other hand, we establish high-probability bounds on the generaliza-
022
               tion error for graph neural networks when using stochastic gradient descent which
023            improve the current state-of-the-art results.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In the field of machine learning research, the analysis of stochastic behavior based on empirical
030   processes is an essential component of learning theory, particularly in understanding and enhanc-
031   ing algorithm performance. The supremum of empirical processes plays a crucial role in vari-
032   ous application scenarios, such as empirical process theory, Rademacher complexity theory, Vap-
033
      nik–Chervonenkis theory, etc. In recent years, concentration inequalities for traditional suprema
      of empirical processes are fully established fields and have been well studied in the literature such
034
      as [36, 4, 5, 1, 24, 39, 12, 29]. All these inequalities based on the assumption of independent and
035
      identically distributed random variables. However, in many practical contexts, the i.i.d. assumption
036   does not hold, such as when training and testing data are drawn from different distributions or when
037   there is temporal dependence among data points. Such scenarios are prevalent in fields like visual
038   recognition and computational biology, necessitating alternatives to Talagrand’s inequality.
039
      Another significant context in learning theory is transductive learning which was firstly introduced
040
      by [40]. In transductive learning, the training samples are independently and without replacement
041
      drawn from a finite population, as opposed to the classic model of independent and with replacement
042   sampling. In this setting, the learning algorithm not only acquires a labeled training set but also
043   receives a set of unlabeled testing instances, with the goal of accurately predicting the labels of
044   the test points. This configuration naturally arises in numerous applications such as text mining,
045   computational biology, recommendation systems, visual recognition, and malware detection. In
046   these cases, the number of unlabeled samples often far exceeds that of labeled samples, and the cost
047   of labeling the unlabeled samples is high. Consequently, the development of transductive algorithms
048   that leverage unlabeled data to enhance learning performance has increasingly attracted attention.
049   In theoretical analysis of transduction learning, we need to sample without replacement, which leads
050   to big challenge and has not been fully understood yet. [13] firstly extended the global Rademacher
051   complexities into transductive learning and established the inequalities without replacement. [38]
052   derived two concentration inequalities using Hoeffding’s reduction method and the entropy method.
053   Nevertheless, both [13] and [38] considered only bounded function. In real scenarios, where the
      maximum value of the function may be large and even unbounded, but the frequency of very large


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      values tends to be small. To the best of our knowledge, the analysis in unbounded functions random
055   variables in transductive learning has not been studied yet.
056
057   In this paper, we focus on sampling without replacement with unbounded functions. We introduce
058
      a novel concentration inequality for empirical process upper bounds under the scenario of sam-
      pling without replacement, particularly for the case of unbounded functions. This represents the
059
      first attempt to discuss generalization performance for unbounded functions under the condition of
060
      sampling without replacement.
061
062   In Section 2, we provide the definition of the transductive learning set-up, including the basic no-
063   tations and the discussion of two related transductive learning settings introduced by [40]. We also
064
      introduce the notations of the unbounded random variables used in the following sections.
065   Our new concentration inequalities for the case of unbounded functions are provided in Section 3,
066   which are, to the best of our knowledge, the first concentration inequalities for sampling without re-
067   placement for classes of unbounded functions. Furthermore, we discuss two significant applications
068   of the new inequalities: firstly, we derive high-probability fast excess risk bounds for unbounded
069   loss in transductive learning based on local uniform convergence in Subsection 4.1; secondly, in
070
      Subsection 4.2, we provide generalization error bounds for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with
      unbounded loss when utilizing Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) which is better than the state-of-
071
      the-art work [37] when m = o(N 2/5 ). All the proofs in this paper are given in Appendix.
072
073   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
074
075          • We derive two novel concentration inequalities for suprema of empirical processes when
076            sampling without replacement for classes of sub-Gaussian and sub-exponential functions,
077            which is the first in transductive learning.
078          • We provide fast excess risk bounds for transductive learning considering Bernstein condi-
079            tion with unbounded losses. To the best of our knowledge, existing results do not provide
080            fast rates in GNNs.
081
             • Applying our inequalities, we obtain the generalization gap of GNNs for node classifica-
082            tion task for stochastic optimization algorithm. In more detail, we establish high probabil-
083            ity bounds of generalization error and test error under sub-Gaussian and sub-exponential
084            losses. Thanks for considering the variance information, our results are better than [37] in
085            some scenarios.

090   In transductive learning, the learner is provided with m labeled training points and u unlabeled test
091
      points. The objective of the learner is to obtain accurate predictions for the test points. Two different
      settings of transductive learning were given by [41]. One assumes that both the training and test sets
092
      are sampled i.i.d. from a same unknown distribution and the learner is provided with the labeled
093
      training and unlabeled test sets. Another assumes that the set XN consisting of N arbitrary input
094   points without any other assumptions regrading its underlying source is given. Then we sample
095   m ≤ N objects Xm ⊆ XN uniformly without replacement from XN which makes the inputs in
096   Xm dependent. Finally, for each input x ∈ Xm , the corresponding output Y from some unknown
097   distribution P (Y |X). Thus we obtain all the labels for the set Xm , we denote the training set as
098   Sm = (Xm , Ym ). The remaining unlabeled set Xu = XN \Xm , u = N − m is the test set.
099

463   Our work in this section differs significantly from that of [37]. They used the concentration inequal-
464   ities based on [13] to derive generalization bounds, while proving that certain modern neural net-
465   work structures satisfy Lipschitz continuity under their assumptions. In contrast, we employ newly
466
      proposed concentration inequalities that relax the boundness condition and also consider variance
      information which obtain improved rates under the same settings.
467
468   While previous papers have utilized technologies based on concentration inequalities proposed by
469   [13] and then bound the transductive Rademacher complexity, deriving the generalization error us-
                                                                                               2

                                2                  2

472   for unbounded distributions. For the sub-exponential distribution, we even need to introduce the

      concentration inequalities under the sub-Weibull distribution to address the issue. Em is introduced
      due to the Hoeffding’s reduction method and is distinct from the traditional gap between the pop-

      ulation and the samples. This requires us to convert it into Rademacher complexity and then use

481
482   In this paper, we focus on transductive learning settings. Firstly, we introduce two newly concentra-
483   tion inequalities for the suprema of empirical processes sampled without replacement for unbounded
484   functions. Using our inequalities, we derive the first fast risk bounds for ERM in transductive learn-
485   ing under bounded losses. On the other hand, we provide improved risk bounds for GNNs with
      SGD, which is better than the state-of-the-art work [37] when m = o(N 2/5 ).
