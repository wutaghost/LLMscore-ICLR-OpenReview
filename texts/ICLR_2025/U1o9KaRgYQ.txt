009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The emergence of large-scale multimodal generative models has drastically ad-
012            vanced artificial intelligence, introducing unprecedented levels of performance
013            and functionality. However, optimizing these models remains challenging due to
014            historically isolated paths of model-centric and data-centric developments, lead-
015            ing to suboptimal outcomes and inefficient resource utilization. In response, we
016
               present a novel sandbox suite tailored for integrated data-model co-development.
               This sandbox provides a comprehensive experimental platform, enabling rapid
017
               iteration and insight-driven refinement of both data and models. Our proposed
018
               “Probe-Analyze-Refine” workflow, validated through applications on state-of-the-
019            art LLaVA-like and DiT-based models for image-to-text and text-to-video tasks,
020            yields significant performance boosts, such as topping the VBench leaderboard.
021            We also uncover fruitful insights gleaned from exhaustive benchmarks, shedding
022            light on the critical interplay between data quality, diversity, and model behavior.
023            All codes, datasets and models are open-sourced to foster future progress.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      The advent of multimodal generative models has revolutionized artificial intelligence, pushing the
028
      boundaries of functionality and creativity across various domains (OpenAI, 2024a;b; Wang et al.,
029
      2024b). Recognizing the pivotal role of training data in shaping model performance, there are
030   fast-growing efforts to curate datasets of larger scales and higher quality (Jakubik et al., 2024).
031
032   However, the development trajectories of these models and datasets have historically diverged, guided
033
      more by intuition than by systematic co-development methodologies. Recent advances in enhancing
      multimodal generative models tend to be either model-centric or data-centric, rarely bridging the two
034
      aspects cohesively. For example, model-centric methods focus on algorithmic enhancements and
035
      architectural innovations under fixed data priors, while data-centric strategies usually concentrate
036   on processing and cleaning datasets independently of specific model training contexts (Qin et al.,
037   2024). Both approaches usually suffer from a lack of systematic guidance and cooperative synergy,
038   relying heavily on heuristic exploration and single-perspective expertise. This fragmented landscape
039   presents a significant barrier to achieving optimal model performance, as the interplay between data
040   characteristics and model capabilities remains largely underexploited.
041
      Moreover, the practical implementation of multimodal generative models is further complicated by
042   infrastructure constraints, escalating computational costs, and the accelerating pace of development
043   cycles (Xu et al., 2024c). In the age of large-scale models with rapidly growing model parameters
044   and dataset sizes, the processes of data processing and model training become increasingly resource-
045   intensive, demanding substantial time and computations. Due to the absence of cost-effective
046   platforms that simplify and speed up data-model co-development, researchers and developers often
047   face the dilemma of prioritizing result-driven development at the expense of thorough, insight-led
048   exploration. This deficiency hinders the iterative refinement for both domains, leading to sub-optimal
049   outcomes as improvements in one domain are hard to inform, apply and enhance each other directly.
050   To fill this gap, we introduce the Data-Juicer Sandbox, a comprehensive suite for facilitating the
051   co-development of multimodal data and generative models. Building upon an open-source data
052   processing system tailored for multimodal generative models, Data-Juicer (Chen et al., 2024a), our
053   sandbox suite further integrates a wealth of off-the-shelf components optimized for usability and
      compatibility with existing model-centric infrastructures. Collectively, it offers flexibly customizable

097   2   R ELATED W ORKS
098   Model-Centric Progress in Multimodal Large Models. Multimodal large models have gained
099   prominence for their remarkable capabilities (OpenAI, 2024a;b). Advances in training algorithms
100   (Caffagni et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a) and model architectures (He et al., 2024a; Yin et al., 2024)
101   have fueled this interest. Transformer scaling remains a prevalent approach (Xu et al., 2023), though
102   high computational demands and optimization challenges often restrict insights to specific datasets
103   and create a gap in understanding how implicit data biases affect model performance.
104   Trends in Data-Centric Development. Recently, a shift towards data-centric development has
105   emerged (Jakubik et al., 2024), emphasizing data handling as key to efficacy of large models such
106   as CLIP (Gadre et al., 2023). Despite the increasing recognition of data processing, the heteroge-
107   neous nature of multimodal data leads to predominantly heuristic approaches (Long et al., 2024),
      underscoring the pressing need for more systematic methodologies for data-model co-development.

119   3.1   M OTIVATION AND D ESIGN
120   Why do we need data-model co-development? In the era of large models, the development of both
121   data and models necessitates collaboration involving numerous algorithm researchers and system
122   engineers. Training data for large models is often highly heterogeneous in terms of quality, context,
123   type, and timeliness. The processing and mixing of these datasets, known as data recipes, are complex
124   and varied (Ge et al., 2024). The scale of data amplifies the stakes for refinement attempts on both
125   data and models, imposing substantial computational and time burdens. Traditional data-centric or
126
      model-centric strategies fall short by optimizing in isolation, leading to diminished overall efficiency
      and resource misallocation (Qin et al., 2024). When either component requires adjustment, the dual
127
      optimization challenge inflates costs, as one part may have already reached near-optimal status.
128
129   Why do we need a sandbox laboratory? Given the high cost associated with iterative development,
130   existing methods often resort to heuristic approaches for improving data or models. For example,
131   scaling up “cleaned” datasets can be problematic because determining what constitutes a “clean”
132
      dataset and measuring its quality qualitatively remains a challenge. Further, the impact of iterations
      on data and models is difficult to attribute due to numerous influencing factors and considerable
133
      engineering effort required. More insightful solutions are thus needed for data-model co-development.
134
135   A unified sandbox environment offers controlled experimentation with low overhead, high trans-
136   ferability, and guided optimization. It permits users to swiftly iterate and optimize data recipes
137   using cost-controlled datasets and models, with insights readily scalable to full-scale production
138
      environments, thus addressing larger computational demands of data processing and model training.
139   The three-layer orchestration of Data-Juicer sandbox. To accommodate the multifaceted work-
140   flows intrinsic to data-model co-development, we design a versatile, user-friendly sandbox laboratory,
141   as depicted in Fig. 1. The laboratory incorporates a spectrum of components for activities such as data
142   analysis, filtering, recipe optimization, model training, inference, and evaluation, all orchestratable
143   via configuration files. The architecture is stratified into three tiers: bespoke end-to-end workflows,
144
      generic development behaviors, and foundational data-model development capabilities.
145   The top tier delineates co-development workflows executed sequentially across four phases: probing
146   data/models, refining data recipes, executing data/model operations, and evaluation. The sequence of
147   tasks within each phase is adjustable through an input configuration file, permitting users to leverage
148   pre-established and effect-proven workflows or customize their own with ease. Moreover, users
149   can flexibly introduce or innovate classes at the capabilities level (such as novel models, metrics, or
150
      data processing algorithms) and behavior hooks (like multidimensional data quality assessments and
      adaptive adjustments based on multiple probe outcomes) interchangeably.
151
152   This design allows for streamlined configuration and reuse of established infrastructure. Importantly,
153   it expedites the prototyping of data and model development solutions, integrating actionable and
154   measurable capabilities for swift feedback derivation and informed decision-making.

      3.3     A P ROBE -A NALYZE -R EFINE W ORKFLOW FOR DATA -M ODEL C O -D EVELOPMENT
182
183
      To demonstrate the usage of the sandbox laboratory, here we elucidate a built-in systematic workflow
184
      illustrated in Fig. 2. We commence with a series of cost-effective contrastive explorations answering
185   several key questions that can enhance large-scale data-model co-development:
186
187   (1) Considering the variability in data sources, models, and downstream tasks, we initially seek to
188
      ascertain which data processing operators (OPs) contribute most effectively to enhancing specific
      target model metrics. This involves creating equal-size data pools, each processed uniquely by a
189
      singular OP and subsequently ranked by data metrics. Models are trained on these data pools, enabling
190
      us to perform an in-depth analysis of OP effectiveness and its correlation with model performance
191   across various quantitative and qualitative indicators.
192
193   (2) Guided by insights derived from the most impactful OPs that ranked highest by model metrics,
194
      we proceed to study whether these OPs can be effectively combined into data recipes and scaled
      up. To facilitate this exploration, we establish a hierarchical data pyramid, wherein data pools are
195
      categorized across different tiers based on their ranked model metric scores. This stratification also
196
      elucidates the viability of OP combinations when scaled with increased data volumes.
197
198   (3) Finally, we delve into optimizing data utilization through a dual analysis focusing on duplication
199   and diversity. We will assess whether the training process would benefit more from the repeated
200
      use of high-quality data pools or from the inclusion of lower-quality data to expand the overall data
      pool. Through a systematic examination of cost-controlled experiments, we can formulate optimized
201
      data recipes and datasets, which are then leveraged in more resource-intensive training sessions to
202
      cultivate models with superior performance.
203

205   3.3.1    S INGLE -O PERATOR DATA P OOLS
206
207
      Starting with an initial dataset D, we define a single-OP data pool Pi as the dataset processed
      exclusively by the i-th OP (OP i ) available in Data-Juicer as Pi = DJ [OP i (ρi )](D), where DJ
208
      denotes the data-processing function implemented by Data-Juicer, and ρi is the hyper-parameters
209
      governing the operation of OP i . The OPs of Data-Juicer can compute specific statistics for every
210   data pool, and apply threshold criteria to selectively filter or modify the data based on these statistics.
211   Within this workflow (demonstrated in the upper left part of Fig.2), the initial dataset D is processed
212   by N studied filter OPs, and each Pi is sorted by statistical measures and segmented into three
213   equal-sized data pools Pi,low , Pi,middle and Pi,high , representing data with low, middle and high stats,
214   respectively. Besides, D is randomly sampled to serve as a control group Drandom such that all the
215   3N + 1 data pools have the same data size. This stratification fosters discriminative insights across
      varying degrees of data processing intensity.

244   3.3.2    M ULTI -O PERATORS DATA P OOLS
245   Having explored the individual impact of each OP, our next step logically involves understanding
246   the dynamics when multiple OPs are applied sequentially, as seen in a data “recipe” (illustrated
247   in the bottom left part of Fig.2). This sequence of OPs is referred to as S. Our aim is to discern
248   whether these OPs complement or counteract each other’s effects. To achieve this, we extend the data
249   pool construction methodology from previous individual OP scenarios
                                                                             to multi-OP recipe scenarios:
250   PS = DJ [OP i (ρi )] ◦ DJ [OP j (ρj )] ◦ · · · ◦ DJ [OP k (ρk )] (D), where i, j, ..., k ∈ S.
251   The number of possible multi-OP data pools grows exponentially with the addition of each new
252   OP, necessitating a strategic selection of combinations to explore. Drawing on insights from single-
253   OP experiments, we propose two pragmatic strategies: (1) combining “Top” OPs based on their
254   progressively diminishing impacts on model performance and (2) clustering OPs based on their
255   Pearson correlation coefficients and then combining “Top” OPs within each category. More details
256   and empirical results on these strategies can be found in Appendix B.5 and C.3 respectively.
257   In alignment with our methodology for single-OP data pools, we consistently train and evaluate
258   models for each selected multi-OP data pool, while ensuring the process remains cost-effective by
259   maintaining a small pool size still yielding reasonable and distinguishable training outcomes.
260
261   3.3.3    P YRAMID - SHAPED DATA P OOLS
262
263   Incorporating a greater number of OPs in a recipe may lead to enhanced data quality; however,
264   the resultant data pool volume decreases exponentially with each additional OP. This phenomenon
265   prompts a critical investigation: should we prioritize reusing high-quality data or incorporate lower-
266
      quality yet more abundant data to escalate training dataset sizes?
267   To encapsulate this inherent trade-off between data scale and quality, we devise a hierarchical pyramid
268   architecture for data pools. Given m “top” OPs, we can create 2m − 1 combinations of these OPs,
269   as depicted in the left-bottom area in Fig. 2. For example, the combination of three OPs, OP 1,2,3 ,
      resides at the highest level of the hierarchy but results in the smallest data pool after Data-Juicer


                                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      data processing. The combinations of two OPs, such as OP 1,2 , are placed at a lower level, and
271   the resulting data pool encompasses that of OP 1,2,3 is several times larger in volume. Leveraging
272   findings from Section 3.3.2, we can pinpoint preferred OP combinations within this pyramid structure.
273   Progressing downward through the pyramid, data pools exhibit a descending average OP ranking
274   (potentially indicative of reduced data quality) alongside an increase in volume.
275
      To reconcile the desire for larger datasets without compromising quality, we devise two experimental
276
      strategies built upon this data pyramid: (1) iterative model training with data repetition from the
277   top-layer (highest-quality) data pools, and (2) non-repetitive training incorporating progressively
278   lower-quality, larger-volume data pools from the lower-layer pools. Specifically, we extract a
279   predetermined quantity of data from the top-layer pool for iterative training with variable repetition
280   rates. In parallel, as a baseline, we assemble a non-repeating dataset by consolidating pools from the
281   upper to lower pyramid levels to match the size of the iterated dataset.
282
      These comparative studies allow us to qualitatively assess the efficacy of data reuse compared to
283   the inclusion of suboptimal data within a fixed dataset size. Notably, all training data is uniformly
284   sampled from the corresponding data pools derived from D, ensuring that the relative proportions
285   of the pyramid data pools remain consistent as D expands. Consequently, the insights gained from
286   these experiments can be extrapolated to larger-scale data contexts, informing efficient and effective
287   scaling of data-model co-development practices.

      to be more diverse. Similarly, texts with low language scores indicate that the language of the text
355
      is more difficult to identify, suggesting that such texts are less common and correspond to a more
356   diverse data pool. Please refer to Appendix C.2 for further quantitative evidence and analysis.
357
358
359                             Observation 3      (Spatiotemporal Dynamics)
360     Dynamic information in the data presents a heightened learning challenge for image-to-text
361     generation compared to text-to-video generation.
362
363
364   Moreover, given the static nature of images, image-to-text generation sometimes requires parsing
365   dynamic content, which compels models to engage in creative inference for accurate interpretation
366
      and response. This challenge is evident in the difficulties image-to-text models encounter when
      trained on data rich in dynamic information. As shown in Table 1, image-to-text models perform
367
      commendably with fewer text action numbers, while text-to-video models display contrasting trends
368
      regarding the values of text action numbers and video motion scores.
369
370
371                                 Observation 4     (Modality Alignment)
372     A high degree of alignment between different modalities within the data is crucial for model
373     performance in both image-to-text and text-to-video generation.
374
375
376   Finally, both image-to-text and text-to-video models show a common preference for a higher degree
377   of alignment between modalities. This is supported by their exemplary performance in scenarios
      where measures of image-text similarity, phrase grounding recall, and frames-text similarity are high.

400      The optimal data recipe does not necessarily arise from combining the best individual OPs, nor
401      does adding more high-performing OPs always lead to superior outcomes.
402
403   Given the exponential growth in possible OP combinations, we focus on experimenting with com-
404   binations of three OPs at a time. An intuitive strategy is to combine the top three OPs with the
405   best overall performance from Table 1. However, as shown in Figures 3(a) and 3(b), combining
406   higher-performing OPs does not always yield better results. For instance, in the image-to-text experi-
407
      ment depicted in Fig. 3(a), the data pool with a high image NSFW score, despite performing best in
      single-OP experiments, generally diminishes performance when combined with others. Similarly, in
408
      Fig. 3(b), while pairwise combinations of OPs show positive gains, integrating the top-performing OP
409
      into the combination of high frames-text similarity and low video NSFW score reduces the relative
410   improvement over the baseline from 2.48% to 1.88%.
411
412   Additionally, we categorize OPs based on correlations and select the optimal OP from each category
413
      to form recipes. However, detailed results in Appendix C.4 show that this approach also did not yield
      better outcomes, challenging the common assumption in existing SOTA works that stacking various
414
      intuitively useful data cleansing actions can synergistically enhance performance.

417
         The performance of a single OP is positively correlated with the performance of the recipe
418
         created from its combination. Starting with high-performing OPs is a good initial step in
419      exploring optimal higher-order data recipes.

      Although the Top-3 OP recipes exhibit suboptimal performance, we observe positive gains when

532   5    C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In this paper, we introduced the Data-Juicer Sandbox, a pioneering open-source suite designed to
535   facilitate the co-development of multimodal data and generative models. By integrating a flexible and
536   comprehensive array of customizable components at different levels, our sandbox enables systematic,
537   cost-effective exploration and optimization, effectively bridging often-disjointed domains of data
538   and model development. Through applying the proposed “Probe-Analyze-Refine” workflow, we
539   showcased how our sandbox can yield not only significant improvements in both dataset and models,
      but also valuable insights into the complex interplay between data processing and model performance.

      A    D ISCUSSION ON M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
831
832
      Model-Centric Progress in Multimodal Large Models. Multimodal large models have captivated
      researchers with their formidable capabilities (OpenAI, 2024a;b), leading to a surge in model-centric
833
      development efforts. These focuses mainly lie in refining training algorithms (Caffagni et al., 2024;
834
      Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024), advancing model architectures and components (He et al., 2024a;
835   Yin et al., 2024; Jiao et al., 2024), and harnessing the models’ potential for various applications (Wang
836   et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). There is a growing consensus that transformer-
837   based scaling is predominant (Xu et al., 2023). However, the high computational requirements
838   imposed by scaling laws (Xu et al., 2024c) and the optimization challenges inherent to large models
839   (Manduchi et al., 2024) often confine insights to specific datasets or vague data characteristics. This
840   situation leaves a significant gap in comprehending the extent to which models’ performance and
841   behavior hinge upon implicit assumptions and inductive biases embedded within the underlying data
842   distributions. In contrast, our work demonstrates a feasible and promising path to fill in this gap by
843
      explicitly linking data processing effects with the downstream performance of trained models through
      numerous contrastive sandbox experiments.
844
845   Trends in Data-Centric Development for Multimodal Large Models. An emerging trend shifts
846   the focal point from models to data (Jakubik et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024), underscored by the notion
847   that large models function akin to data compressors (Delétang et al., 2024). Echoing the principle
848   of “garbage in, garbage out”, meticulous data processing is recognized as pivotal. Efforts now
849
      isolate data manipulation as a primary experimental variable in multimodal generative modeling (He
      et al., 2024b). Nonetheless, multimodal data processing involves highly heterogeneous processing
850
      workflows, vast quantities, diverse types, and the high cost of training downstream models. This
851
      complexity results in predominantly heuristic approaches, such as data filtering and synthesis guided
852   by human intuition (Long et al., 2024).
853
854   For example, one well-studied model type is CLIP. DataComp (Gadre et al., 2023) introduces a
855
      benchmark to filter out high-quality data from 12.8 billion image-text pairs in Common Crawl to
      train better CLIP models, considering Filter Operators such as CLIP score, image size, and caption
856
      length. MetaClip (Xu et al., 2024a) aims to reproduce CLIP’s data by introducing a raw data pool
857
      and finding a balanced subset based on CLIP’s concepts. Unlike these data-centric approaches that
858   isolate the model and training settings, focusing solely on the quality and scale of training datasets,
859   our work emphasizes systematic methodologies for data-model co-development, considering both
860   data and models equally important. Specifically, we incorporate performance signals from sandbox
861   reference models on many downstream tasks, conducting importance and correlation analysis to link
862   data pools and these model metrics. Additionally, we explored more model types beyond CLIP, such
863   as LLaVA-like and DiT-based models for image-to-text and text-to-video tasks, and identified better
      training datasets for these models using our workflow.

      Open-Source Infrastructure for Multimodal Large Model Development. The landscape for
865   multimodal model development has advanced significantly, boasting a variety of strong infrastructures
866   and frameworks for training and evaluation. Prominent examples include Transformers (Wolf et al.,
867   2020), Diffusers (von Platen et al., 2022), NeMo (Harper et al.), MMagic (MMagic Contributors,
868   2023), ESPNet (Peng et al., 2023), and MMBench (Liu et al., 2023).
869
      However, when it comes to multimodal data infrastructure, the primary contributions have been
870
      datasets and dataset-specific preprocessing tools, such as DatasetsHub from HuggingFace (Lhoest
871   et al., 2021). The standardization and efficient utilization of practical expertise and foundational data
872   processing capabilities remain unaddressed. Existing frameworks for data processing predominantly
873   focus on single-modal data (TogetherAI, 2023; Bradski, 2000; Hwang et al., 2023), underlining the
874   early stages of development in systematic platforms for multimodal data (Chen et al., 2024a; Du
875   et al., 2023).
876
      Recognizing the critical interplay between datasets and models—where comprehensive, high-quality
877   datasets enhance model performance, and advanced models contribute to the generation of even more
878   refined datasets—our work stands out by introducing an innovative intermediary layer. We seamlessly
879   integrate cutting-edge model-centric multimodal infrastructure with the Data-Juicer system. This
880   integration fosters a streamlined and insightful co-development environment for both models and
881   data, bridging the current gap and setting a standard for future efforts in the multimodal domain.

946   B.3    D ISCUSSION ON THE D ETERMINATION OF DATA P OOLS
947
948   In the sandbox experiments in Section 4, we split the data pools into three buckets. The number of
949   buckets reflects the trade-off between data intervention intensity (via operator stats) and the reliability
950   of model feedback (the metric changes ∆ of post-training downstream tasks compared to the models
951   trained on random sampled data pools). More buckets lead to greater statistical differences between
952
      buckets with different ranks (especially the first and last ones), strengthening attribution to data
      processing effectiveness. However, more buckets also reduce per-bucket data, increasing the risk of
953
      inadequate data for models to exhibit reasonable ∆.
954
955   As a result, we do not aim for models to be “training done” or “converged” in this sandbox experiment
956   setting. Instead, we want to observe enlightening changes—positive or negative—in models after
957   targeted data intervention versus random data sampling. In our early experiments, we tested bucket
958
      counts of [2,3,4,5] to evaluate whether a model trained on randomly sampled data could reasonably
      decrease loss after one epoch and show statistically significant changes on downstream tasks. Our
959
      findings indicate that three buckets are empirically good for our scenarios. Once determined, all
960
      controlled experiments are aligned to one complete epoch and matched to the random pool data size.

975    B.5   S TRATEGIES TO C OMBINE OP S ACCORDING TO C ORRELATIONS
976
       In addition to assembling the OPs with the overall best performance, we also incorporate an analysis
977
       of inter-OP relationships into our recipe formulation process. Our workflow accommodates two
978    strategies, with specific applications detailed in Section C.3:
979
980    • The first method involves computing Pearson correlation coefficients between the statistics gener-
981      ated by these OPs. Using a hierarchical clustering algorithm (Ward Jr, 1963), we group the OPs
982      into k clusters. From each cluster, we select the OP whose data pool yields the strongest model
983      performance to form potential combinations.
984    • Alternatively, leveraging the outcomes of single-OP tests, we calculate Pearson correlation coeffi-
985      cients for each pair of dimensions within the evaluation metrics. Hierarchical clustering is again
986      employed to categorize the metrics into k classes. The top-performing OP from each class is chosen
987      to create the combinations. This approach allows us to investigate whether these combinations lead
988      to concurrent improvements or mutual inhibition across the evaluative metrics.

1275
       those for OCR and coding tasks, resulting in a greater number of categories upon classification. In
1276   contrast, VBench’s evaluation metrics can be broadly divided into several classes, including static
1277   video metrics (e.g., subject and background consistency), dynamic metrics (e.g., dynamic degree),
1278   and video quality indicators (e.g., aesthetic quality and imaging quality).
1279
       Notably, the dynamic degree negatively correlates with many other metrics, particularly those favoring
1280
       static videos, thus preventing videos without movement from being rated as optimal. Based on these
1281
       observations, for text-to-video generation, we apply a hierarchical clustering algorithm (Ward Jr,
1282   1963) to classify the VBench metrics into three categories based on their correlations: static video
1283   metrics, dynamic video metrics, and video quality along with video-text matching. We then select the
1284   best-performing OP for each of these three metric categories, where each excels in different aspects.
1285   These selected OPs are subsequently combined and experimentally evaluated together.
