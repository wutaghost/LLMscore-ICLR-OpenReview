011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Applying either Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) or full fine-tuning to
014           Large Language Models (LLMs) often results in its inherent limitations. To over-
015           come this issue, we propose a novel “hybrid fine-tuning” approach that jointly
016           updates both LLMs and PEFT modules using a combination of zeroth-order and
017
              first-order optimization methods. To analyze this approach, we develop a theo-
              retical framework centered on the concept of “hybrid generalized smoothness”,
018
              which accounts for the heterogeneous nature of the optimization landscape in
019
              joint LLM and PEFT training. We provide a rigorous convergence analysis for
020           the convergence of SGD algorithm under multiple learning rates and demonstrate
021           its effectiveness through extensive empirical studies across various downstream
022           tasks and model architectures. Our work not only offers a solution to the practical
023           challenge of LLM fine-tuning but also contributes a broader theoretical foundation
024           for analyzing hybrid optimization problems in machine learning.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), demon-
030   strating remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks. To adapt these models for specific
031   domains or to modify their core behaviors, researchers and practitioners commonly employ full
032   fine-tuning (Malladi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024).
033   Full fine-tuning, which involves updating all parameters of an LLM, has been a classical approach
034   for downstream tasks (VM et al., 2024; Minaee et al., 2024). However, this method is extremely
035   computationally expensive, requiring the calculation of gradients for the entire model. To address
036   this limitation, two common approaches have emerged: (1) Zeroth-order full fine-tuning (Malladi
037   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024): This method approximates gradients without directly computing
038   them, reducing computational overhead while still allowing updates to all model parameters. (2)
039   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (Lester et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021; Li & Liang,
040   2021): These techniques aim to adapt LLMs by tuning only a small portion of parameters while
041
      keeping the base model frozen. This approach significantly reduces computational requirements
      and memory usage.
042
043   However, simply applying either of these methods has been shown to be insufficient: The PEFT
044   method (e.g. LoRA) doesn’t learn new knowledge (Gudibande et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2024),
045   while the zeroth-order full-parameter fine-tuning suffers from slow convergence due to the lack
046   of gradient information (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017). This limitation highlights a critical gap in
047
      current approaches, leading to the following question:
048
                  Q: How can we achieve both benefits of full fine-tuning and PEFT methods
049               while maintaining the efficiency?
050
051   To address this challenge, we propose a novel approach, hybrid fine-tuning, which jointly updates
052   both the PEFT module and the LLM that adapts zeroth-order (ZO) optimization techniques to update
053   the base model. By leveraging ZO methods, we can perform fine-tuning without calculating the full
      gradient of the base LLM, thereby reducing computational costs. Simultaneously, this approach

      allows us to update PEFT modules using the first-order gradient information, boosting performance
055   beyond traditional zeroth-order full fine-tuning.

113   We summarize our contributions as follows:
114
115   (1) We introduce the hybrid fine-tuning paradigm, a novel approach that addresses the limitations
116       of both full fine-tuning and traditional PEFT methods. By combining zeroth-order optimization
117       for LLMs with first-order methods for PEFT modules, we achieve a balance between adaptation
118
          power and computational efficiency. This innovative strategy allows for more comprehensive
          model updates without incurring the full computational cost of traditional fine-tuning.
119
120   (2) We develop a comprehensive theoretical framework for analyzing hybrid optimization prob-
121       lems in the context of hybrid fine-tuning. Our concept of hybrid generalized smoothness (Def-

          joint LLM and PEFT training. We provide rigorous convergence analysis for our proposed

          dation for hybrid fine-tuning approaches. Notably, we analyze SGD with random reshuffling,

      1.2   R ELATED W ORK
143
144   Zeroth-order Optimization in Fine-tuning LLMs Recent work has explored zeroth-order op-
145   timization methods for fine-tuning LLMs, which aligns with our approach of using zeroth-order
146   methods for the LLM component in hybrid fine-tuning. Malladi et al. (2023) demonstrated the com-
147   patibility of zeroth-order methods with both full fine-tuning and PEFTs. This laid the groundwork
148   for our hybrid approach that combines zeroth-order LLM updates with first-order PEFT updates.
149   Zhang et al. (2024) provided a comprehensive benchmark for zeroth-order optimization in LLM
150
      fine-tuning, offering valuable insights that informed our experimental design. Ling et al. (2024)
      combines the zeroth-order fine-tuning of LLMs with the federated learning. Several studies have in-
151
      corporated variance reduction techniques (Gautam et al., 2024) into zeroth-order methods or second-
152
      order method (Zhao et al., 2024) to enhance stability and convergence in fine-tuning LLMs. While
153   we focus on a different aspect, these stability improvements could easily be integrated into our hy-
154   brid framework. Existing literature (Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) also
155   discusses the sparsity of pre-trained LLMs, which further enhances the performance of zeroth-order
156   optimization approach.
157
158   Generalized Smoothness of Large Machine Learning Models The concept of generalized
159   smoothness has emerged as a crucial theoretical framework for understanding the optimization land-
160   scape of large machine learning models, including LLMs. Recent studies have shown that traditional
161   smoothness assumptions often fail to capture the complex optimization landscape of deep neural net-
      works (Zhang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024). More explicitly, Zhang et al. (2019) demonstrated that the

      local smoothness constant in neural networks is often proportional to the gradient norm, challeng-
163   ing the conventional assumption of uniform smoothness. This insight aligns with our observations
164   in hybrid fine-tuning, where different components of the model (LLM and PEFT modules) exhibit

      2.1   T HE L-S MOOTHNESS C ONDITION
189
190   In the traditional optimization problem, which is usually used to characterize the full fine-tuning of
191   LLM or the PEFT methods, the concept of L-smoothness is fundamental in the optimization theory
192   and plays a crucial role in characterizing the behavior of SGD algorithms.
193
      Formally, a smooth function f : Rd → R is said to be L-smooth (also known as L-Lipschitz
194
      continuous gradient) if there exists a constant L > 0 such that its Hessian matrix is uniformly
195   bounded by the constant L; that is,
196                                            LId ⪰ ∇2 f (x),
197
      where Id is the d × d identity matrix and “⪰” represents that LId − ∇2 f (x) is positive semi-
198
      definite (PSD). This condition can be equivalently expressed in terms of the function’s gradient:
199
      ∥∇f (x) − ∇f (y)∥ ≤ L∥x − y∥. The constant L is closely related to the learning rate choice in
200   the convergence analysis of gradient-based algorithm. As demonstrated in Carmon et al. (2020), the
201   optimal learning rate is linearly scaled with respect to L1 .
202
203
      While L-smoothness has been demonstrated to hold for all smooth functions over a compact domain
      (Hewitt & Stromberg, 2012), it has limitations when applied to complicated landscapes encountered
204
      in our hybrid training approach described in Eq. (1). We recap these limitations we have introduced:
205
206   (1) L is dynamically changing during training. The constant L usually fails to maintain uni-
207       formity over the entire parameter space. In many practical scenarios, different regions of the
208       parameter space may exhibit vastly different smoothness properties. For instance, Zhang et al.
209       (2019) has demonstrated that the local smoothness constant L is linear in the gradient norm. We
210       also have illustrated this non-uniformity for transformer-based language models in Figure 1a.
211   (2) L can be different for different parameters. Distinct types of modules or variables within the
212       system usually present highly diverse smoothness conditions. For example, small randomly-
213       initialized modules often have smaller L compared to large pre-trained neural networks. This
214       consideration becomes particularly crucial in hybrid systems where we deal with fundamentally
215       different types of parameters. We have illustrated this point in Figure 1b: The LoRA module
          demonstrates a substantially lower L value compared to the base LLM.

527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In conclusion, this work introduces a novel hybrid fine-tuning approach for large language models
531
      that combines zeroth-order optimization for the base model with first-order optimization for PEFT
      modules. Motivated by the hybrid generalized smoothness of the hybrid system in Section 2.1, we
532
      develop a theoretical framework centered on this theoretical challenge introduced by the hybrid fine-
533
      tuning method. Our empirical examples and convergence analysis built in Theorem 3.4 demonstrate
534   the necessity of applying different learning rates for different PEFT modules. Our analysis achieves
535   the best-known sample complexity under much milder conditions in the existing literature. Exten-
536   sive empirical evaluations across multiple NLP tasks, model architectures, and PEFT techniques
537   validate the theoretical insights and show consistent performance gains over traditional fine-tuning
538   methods. By addressing fundamental challenges in joint LLM and PEFT training, our work opens
539   new avenues for efficient model adaptation and provides a solid foundation for future research on
      optimizing hybrid systems in machine learning.

1136
       In this paper, we evaluate our proposed hybrid-tuning method across a diverse spectrum of scenarios
1137
       including three distinct tasks, two transformer-based language models, and three PEFT methods.
1138
       This extensive exploration not only demonstrates the broad applicability of our approach but also
1139   provides robust evidence for its effectiveness and versatility in enhancing model performance across
1140   various domains and architectures. In this section, we will briefly review these components and
1141   delve into more details of our experiment settings.
1142
1143
       D.1   OVERVIEW OF TASKS
1144
1145   In this section, we briefly discuss the task we consider in our paper. All of tasks are ready to use
1146   in the ZO-Bench code base (Zhang et al., 2024) and we follow the default setting and the same
1147   train/test/validation split of their original implementations.
1148
1149
       Text Binary Classification In this paper, we consider the Stanford Sentiment Treebank v2 (SST2)
1150   dataset (Socher et al., 2013) and the Word-In-Context (WIC) dataset (Pilehvar & Camacho-Collados,
1151   2018), which presents the simplest binary text classification problem. The SST2 dataset is suffi-
1152   ciently simple and convenience to use to verify our motivating examples (as demonstrated in Fig-
1153   ure 1a and Figure 1b). The WIC dataset provides a more challenging task that requires understanding
1154   word meanings in different contexts. Both datasets serve as excellent benchmarks for evaluating the
1155   performance of our proposed methods in binary text classification tasks.
1156
1157   Question Answering The Choice Of Plausible Alternatives (COPA) dataset (Roemmele et al.,
1158   2011) is a common benchmark for evaluating the commonsense causal reasoning ability of a lan-
1159   guage model. It contains one thousand English-language questions answer pairs. We choose this
1160   task to evaluate our approaches in improving the question-answering capabilities of models, partic-
1161   ularly in scenarios requiring causal inference and commonsense reasoning.
1162
1163   Common Sense Reasoning Task We consider the WinoGrande dataset (Sakaguchi et al., 2021),
1164   which presents a challenging common sense reasoning task. The WinoGrande dataset is designed to
1165   be a more difficult and larger-scale version of the original Winograd Schema Challenge, requiring
1166   models to demonstrate human-like reasoning capabilities. By including WinoGrande in our exper-
1167   iments, we aim to assess how well our approaches can enhance a model’s ability to reason about
1168
       complex scenarios and make appropriate inferences based on contextual information.
1169
1170   D.2   OVERVIEW OF PEFT M ODULES
1171
1172   In this paper, we mainly consider three types of PEFT modules. In our proposed hybrid-tuning
1173   approach, we jointly train one of these PEFT modules with the base LLM to improve the con-
1174
       vergence and overall performance. The following paragraphs provide a detailed overview of the
       three main PEFT modules considered in this study: Prompt Tuning, Prefix Tuning, and Low-Rank
1175
       Adaptation (LoRA). In our experiments, we follow the default configuration of Zo-Bench code base
1176
       (Zhang et al., 2024) without making additional modifications. It is worth noting that our hybrid-
1177   tuning methods are also applicable to other recently developed PEFT techniques including (1) other
1178   LoRA variants such as X-LoRA (Buehler & Buehler, 2024), Llama-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2023b),
1179   AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023a), LoHa (Hyeon-Woo et al., 2021), and LoKr (Yeh et al., 2023); (2)
1180   other soft prompts techniques such as P-tuning (Liu et al., 2021; 2023); and (3) Infused Adapter by
1181   Inhibiting and Amplifying Inner Activation (IA3) methods (Liu et al., 2022).
1182
1183   Prompt Tuning Prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) is a lightweight fine-tuning method that
1184   prepends trainable continuous prompt tokens to the input. These prompt tokens are optimized during
1185   training while keeping the pre-trained language model parameters frozen. This approach allows for
1186   task-specific adaptation with a small number of parameters. Prompt tuning is particularly effective
1187   for large language models and can be seen as a form of soft prompting that learns optimal input
       representations for specific tasks.

1220   Prefix Tuning Prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) extends the concept of prompt tuning by adding
1221   trainable prefix tokens not only to the input but to each layer of the transformer model. This method
1222

1223

1224
       parameters.

       LoRA Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method
       that adds low-rank decomposition matrices to the weights of the pre-trained model. Instead of di-
1228
       rectly updating the model’s weight matrices, LoRA introduces pairs of rank decomposition matrices
1229
       for each weight matrix being tuned. These low-rank matrices are initialized randomly and trained
1230   to adapt the model to specific tasks. LoRA significantly reduces the number of trainable param-
1231   eters while maintaining competitive performance compared to full fine-tuning. It offers several
1232   advantages, including faster training, lower memory requirements, and the ability to switch between
1233   multiple fine-tuned tasks efficiently by changing only the LoRA parameters.
