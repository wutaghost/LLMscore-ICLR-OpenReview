009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Knowledge Editing (KE) algorithms alter models’ weights to perform targeted
               updates to incorrect, outdated, or otherwise unwanted factual associations. To
012
               better identify the possibilities and limitations of these approaches, recent work
013
               has shown that applying KE can adversely affect models’ factual recall accuracy
014            and diminish their general reasoning abilities. While these studies give broad in-
015            sights into the potential harms of KE algorithms, e.g., via performance evaluations
016            on benchmarks, we argue little is understood as to why such destructive failures
017            occur. Is it possible KE methods distort representations of concepts beyond the tar-
018            geted fact, hence hampering abilities at broad? If so, what is the extent of this dis-
019            tortion? Motivated by such questions, we define a novel synthetic task wherein a
020            Transformer is trained from scratch to internalize a “structured” knowledge graph.
021            The structure enforces relationships between entities of the graph, such that edit-
022
               ing a factual association has “trickling effects” on other entities in the graph (e.g.,
               altering X’s parent is Y to Z affects who X’s siblings’ parent is). Through eval-
023
               uations of edited models and analysis of extracted representations, we show that
024
               KE inadvertently affects representations of entities beyond the targeted one, dis-
025            torting relevant structures that allow a model to infer unseen knowledge about an
026            entity. We call this phenomenon representation shattering and demonstrate that it
027            results in degradation of factual recall and reasoning performance more broadly.
028            To corroborate our findings in a more naturalistic setup, we perform preliminary
029            experiments with pretrained GPT-2-XL and Mamba models, reproducing the rep-
030            resentation shattering effect therein as well. Overall, our work yields a precise
031            mechanistic hypothesis to explain why KE has adverse effects on model abilities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) have led to unprecedented advances in several domains (Gemini
036   Team, 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Thoppilan et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al.,
037   2022; Qin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Ahn et al., 2022; Driess et al., 2023). However, the static
038   nature of their training pipelines implies that as our world evolves, models’ internalized knowledge
039
      can become incorrect or outdated. To address this, recent work has proposed several protocols
      for knowledge editing (KE), wherein the goal is to minimally and precisely alter model weights
040
      such that only the targeted information (and its relevant associations) are updated, but all unrelated
041
      information remains (ideally) unaffected (Mitchell et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022a; 2023; Dai et al.,
042   2021; Cheng et al., 2023; De Cao et al., 2021; Sinitsin et al., 2020).
043
044   Despite significant work on the topic, it still remains unclear precisely what effects KE should have
045
      on a model. For example, assume you edit the fact that “Michael Jordan won the 1998 NBA MVP”
      to “Reggie Miller won the 1998 NBA MVP”, then what should the impact of such an edit be?
046
      Should the model now believe Michael Jordan and the Chicago Bulls never reached the NBA finals
047
      in 1998? Should it perhaps believe Reggie Miller was on the Chicago Bulls? Should the pop quote
048   “Be like Mike” (Wikipedia, 2024) now become “Be like Reggie”? As Hofweber et al. (2024);
049   Hase et al. (2024) argue, it is difficult to design clear, well-defined answers for such questions.
050   Motivated by this, recent work has started investigating precisely what effects KE actually has on
051   the model (Hoelscher-Obermaier et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Lynch et al., 2024). For example,
052   Cohen et al. (2023) demonstrate that knowledge beyond the edited fact can often be impacted in a
053   detrimental manner, such that the model begins to have an incoherent understanding of the world;
      Gupta et al. (2024) demonstrate unrelated facts are often forgotten by the model post-editing; and

      While these works clearly demonstrate the detrimental impacts of editing on a model, they still
071
      leave open the question precisely why such harms occur—at a mechanistic level, how are model

074
075
      This work. To address the questions above, we aim to develop a mechanistic understanding of the
      impact of KE on a model’s internals. For this purpose, we argue we must solve two problems: (i)
076
      identify how a model expresses knowledge about some predefined set of entities in its representa-
077
      tions, and (ii) investigate how this mechanism is affected as we apply KE to alter a fact correspond-
078   ing to a subset of the entities. Instead of attacking a complicated system that may be difficult to
079   interpret (e.g., an off-the-shelf LLM), we take inspiration from a multitude of recent papers that
080   establish synthetic abstractions of the target system and develop precise hypotheses as to why the
081   phenomenon-in-question occurs (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023c;a;b; Okawa et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2022;
082   Li et al., 2023a; Lubana et al., 2024). Specifically, we define a data-generating process that yields en-
083   tities arranged in a structured knowledge graph. This structure is defined via use of a predefined set
084   of relations that locally constrain how entities relate to each other (similar to parent-child relations).
085   Given enough entities and relations, such local constraints manifest a broader global structure in the
086
      knowledge graph. Performing traversal over the nodes of this knowledge graph, we get sequences
      that can be used as “strings” to train a Transformer on. As we show, this protocol leads to the model
087
      precisely encoding the structure of the graph in its latent representations. However, when KE is
088
      applied to edit either incorrectly learned facts or insert counterfactual knowledge (using the method
089   proposed by Meng et al. (2022a)), we find latent representations are heavily distorted and the graph
090   structure completely destroyed—we call this phenomenon representation shattering. Interestingly,
091   this phenomenon manifests in proportion to how far the proposed edit moves a given node from its
092   current location to a new location in the graph (defined via edge distance). We thus hypothesize
093   representation shattering underlies the detrimental effects of KE on a pretrained model’s factual and
094   reasoning capabilities at broad. Overall, we make the following contributions in this work.
095
      • Structured Knowledge Graphs as a Toy Setting for Investigating Impact of KE. We propose
096
        use of a structured knowledge graph wherein entities (nodes) are connected to each other via
097
        predefined local constraints (relations) that manifest into a broader, global structure in the graph
098     (see Sec. 3). Training Transformers on strings (path traversals) from the graph, we find model
099     representations precisely encode the global structure of the graph. This allows us to assess the
100     impact of KE at a more mechanistic level, since distorting a fact now has global effects that can
101     be precisely (and, in fact, visually) delineated by analyzing the model representations.
102   • Representation Shattering as a Mechanistic Hypothesis to Explain Detrimental Effects of
103     KE. We find KE distorts latent representations for entities in the graph such that the global ge-
104     ometry learned during pretraining is, at times, completely destroyed—we call this phenomenon
105     representation shattering and hypothesize it underlies the detrimental effects of KE on model
106     capabilities observed in prior work (see Sec. 4). As we show, the extent of harm on latent rep-
107     resentations turns out to be correlated to the amount an edit alters the graph from its original
        organization into the new, desired one.

      • Investigations with Off-the-Shelf LLMs. Using pre-trained GPT2-XL and Mamba models, we
109     provide evidence for our claims about representation shattering in more naturalistic settings. For
110     one, we find real-world analogues to our synthetic knowledge graph structures (i.e., days of the
111     week) and reproduce similar shattering phenomena in GPT2-XL and Mamba to what we observe
112     in our toy setup (see Sec. 4.5). Additionally, we further reinforce the generality of our findings
113     with preliminary replications of representation shattering under more complex knowledge graph
114     geometries, such as trees (i.e. countries and their cities).

116   2     R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Knowledge Editing. Several protocols for knowledge editing (KE) have been proposed in recent
119   work. Early work defined meta-learning based approaches (Sinitsin et al., 2020; De Cao et al.,
120   2021; Mitchell et al., 2022) and established the broader desiderata for what properties a KE pro-
121
      tocol should satisfy; e.g., ensuring facts unrelated to the target one are not hampered via the edit-
      ing protocol. Building on work aimed at understanding how Transformers encode knowledge in
122
      their internals (Geva et al., 2020), modern KE protocols focus on defining closed-form operations
123
      that involve (i) localizing knowledge to specific components in a model (e.g., MLP layers) and (ii)
124   defining operations to alter a factual association by assuming the fact is represented in a localized
125   manner (Meng et al., 2022a; 2023).
126
127
      Evaluations of Knowledge Editing Methods. As argued by Hase et al. (2024); Hofweber et al.
      (2024), the problem of KE is relatively ill-defined. Consequently, it is unclear that when we edit
128
      knowledge within a model, what effects said edits should have on other facts it may have inter-
129
      nalized during training. Prior work has hence taken an alternative approach, primarily focusing on
130   developing an empirical understanding of what the phenomenology of KE protocols is: e.g., if an
131   edit is performed, how are counterfactual statements or unrelated facts affected. These works gen-
132   erally show that KE in fact has extreme detrimental effects on a model, e.g., hampering both its
133   broader internalized knowledge and its reasoning abilities (Hase et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2023;
134   Hoelscher-Obermaier et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024). While the primary method-
135   ology used in such papers is to perform empirical benchmarking of a model that has undergone
136   editing, we instead focus on a mechanistic analysis of how editing alters a model’s representations
137   (albeit primarily in a toy synthetic task) to yield the undesirable effects on model abilities.
138   Explaining Models via Synthetic Tasks. To disentangle the failures of KE methods from the fail-
139   ures of the models themselves, we argue for use of a more controllable and interpretable setup. Such
140   a setup can help identify a concrete hypothesis for why KE has undesirable effects on the model,
141   which we can then analyze in naturalistic settings by designing more precisely defined experiments.
142   This methodology of designing toy, control tasks to investigate hypotheses for phenomenology of
143   a neural network has yielded promising results in recent years, providing, e.g., a concrete hypothe-
144
      sis for how chain-of-thought reasoning aids model capabilities (Prystawski et al., 2024; Feng et al.,
      2023), models for emergent capabilities (Okawa et al., 2023; Lubana et al., 2024), existence of
145
      nonlinear representations (Engels et al., 2024), and failure modes for compositional generaliza-
146
      tion (Zhou et al., 2023).

149   3     F ORMALZING KNOWLEDGE EDITING
150
151
      Epistemology has grappled with the nature of knowledge for centuries (Chappell, 2005). In this work
      we adopt a humble, yet precise definition of knowledge based on structured knowledge graphs. A
152
      knowledge graph is used to represent how facts, entities, and relations are interlinked, giving rise
153
      to notions of consistency, coherency, and reasoning across different pieces of information. Using
154   these definitions, we will define a synthetic data generation process on knowledge graphs, in order
155   to systematically study knowledge editing in Transformers.
156
157   3.1   K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS
158
159   A knowledge graph consists of a collection of entities X = {xi }ni=1 , and a collection facts F that
160   relate different entities. For example, a graph defined on entities X = {“Alice”, “Bob”, “Carol”}
161   can encode the fact “Alice is the advisor of Bob” using the relation “advisor”, represented as
      (“Alice”, “advisor”, “Bob”).


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Definition 3.1 (Knowledge graph). Formally, a knowledge graph G = (X, R, F ) consists of nodes
163   X, relations R, and facts F , where each fact f = (xi , r, xj ) ∈ R is defined by a relation r ∈ R
164   between two entities xi , xj ∈ X.
165
166   A relation sub-graph corresponds to a sub-graph constructed by only considering facts that use
167   relation r. For example Gadvisor is a sub-graph that specifies all facts for the relation “advisor”.
168   Every knowledge graph contains a collection of facts that can be inferred from the graph.
169   Related pieces of information such as “Alice’s advisor was Bob” and “Bob’s advisor was Carol”
170   can be composed to form cohesive statements such as “Alice’s advisor’s advisor was Carol. To
171   capture such statements, we define compositions of relations below. The composition of relations
172   are essential to capture ripple effects that occur in the knowledge graph after an edit (Cohen et al.,
173   2023) to a relation in R.
174   Definition 3.2 (Composition of relations). A composition of relations ⃗r = (r1 , r2 , · · · , rk ) ∈ Rk
175   with respect to knowledge graph G is defined such that for every fact f = (xi , ⃗r, xj ), there exists a
176   collection of facts {(xi , ri , xi+1 )}ki=1 for which x1 = xi and xk+1 = xj . In other words, any fact
177   defined on the composition of relations has a corresponding set of facts defined on relations from R.
178   Furthermore, the set of facts form a path in the knowledge graph such that the sequence of relations
179   in the path are r1 , r2 , · · · rk .

255   Evaluation (seen facts). We assess the model’s ability to remember facts seen during training, both
256   before and after an edit. Specifically, to analyze whether the model has learned the fact (xi , ⃗r, xj ),
257   we prompt it with an entity xi and a relation ⃗r, expecting it to produce xj as the next token. In
258   practice, the model outputs can vary across prompts: we account for this by averaging the softmax
259
      probabilities across 5 randomly sampled sequences of the form . . . xi⃗r and using the output token
      with the highest probability.
260
261
      Evaluation (unseen facts). We also evaluate the model on two other criteria. (1) Compositional
      inference. In addition to facts seen in the training data, we evaluate the model on compositions
262
      of relations. The model must preserve geometric structures of the data in order to compositionally
263
      generalize after a knowledge edit. (2) Logical inference. A key feature of reasoning in natural
264   language is logical inference. For example, if Alice is said to be the advisor of Bob, then Bob is an
265   advisee of Alice (even if it is not explicitly stated). Our data generation process has similar relations,
266   such as clockwise and anti-clockwise 1-hop neighbors. By “holding out” one direction for some such
267   pairs of relations from being observed verbatim in the training dataset, i.e., the relation may only
268   appear compositionally, we can assess the degree to which the model internalizes properties among
269   related relations. We can also evaluate if editing a fact for a relation changes the fact for other related
      relations, i.e., we check if the model’s knowledge is logically self-consistent after an edit.

294   3.4   R EPRESENTATION SHATTERING
295
296   In this work, we explore the hypothesis that knowledge editing methods distort the geometry of the
297   representations of entities in the knowledge graph. We believe this distortion can give us insight into
298   why knowledge editing degrades the general capabilities of the model. In the following sections, we
299   investigate the following hypothesis.
300   Hypothesis 3.3 (Representation shattering). Language models embed related entities on a mani-
301   fold in their internal representations. KE methods distort this manifold in order to insert new facts
302   or alter old ones, i.e., they shatter model representations. The extent of representation shattering
303
      increases with the distance between the old fact and the desired new fact on the manifold.
304   To quantify the extent of representation shattering, we define a precise metric to capture the amount
305   of distortion of the representations:

      5    C ONCLUSION
511
      In this work, we introduced a synthetic framework to analyze the side effects of knowledge editing
512
      in transformers, identifying “representation shattering” as a key factor behind performance degrada-
513
      tion. Specifically, we show preserving representational structures underlying a model’s knowledge
514   is crucial to avoiding negative consequences of knowledge editing: distortion of such structures
515   impacts a model’s broader capabilities. To arrive at this hypothesis, we design a controlled frame-
516   work that allows investigations into models modified by knowledge editing protocols, offering clear
517   representation-level explanations for why knowledge editing can harms models’ broader capabilities
518   that generalize to real-world models like GPT-2. While the use of simplified tasks and models can
519   limit the scope of our conclusions, since larger, more complex real-world models may exhibit addi-
520   tional dynamics that our framework does not capture, we do believe that testing knowledge editing
521   protocols on setups similar to our synthetic, knowledge graph one will significantly aid design of
522
      better editing protocols. We claim failing even such simple, albeit systematically defined settings,
      likely implies the editing protocol should not be readily trusted or applied at scale.

783
784   Rank-One Model Editing (ROME), proposed by Meng et al. (2022a), is a popular knowledge editing
785   algorithm used on LLMs. Their contributions are two-fold: first, through “causal tracing,” they find
786   that early-layer MLP modules of transformer models are implicated in encoding factual associations.
787   Second, interpreting feed-forward layers as linear associative memories encoding key-value pairs,
788   ROME applies a rank-one update to the MLP weights.
789   Notationally, for a factual association (xi , r, xj ), the key is the entity xi while the value is xj . In
790                                                  (l−1)

829   In our implementation of ROME tailored to our model, we apply the edit at layer 1 as it is the

       F.6    K NOWLEDGE E DITING WITH NATURALISTIC T REES
1297
1298   In our experiments, we primarily focus on synthetic knowledge graphs with cyclical structures.
1299   While the simplicity of cycles is desirable for our synthetic experiments, real human knowledge
1300   and language can exhibit more complex structures. For example, geographical ground-truths can be
1301   expressed in a tree structure, with entities like cities/countries/continents having relations with other
1302   cities/countries/continents, i.e. xi = Paris, r = located in country, xj = France.
1303   Here, we ask: does the representation shattering hypothesis hold for more realistic tree-shaped
1304   knowledge graphs in more complex models like GPT-2? To answer this question, we take inspi-
1305   ration from the classic “The Eiffel Tower is located in the city of Rome” example of counterfactual
1306   knowledge editing (Meng et al., 2022a). For our purposes, we edit the country associations of major
1307   cities. In particular, we consider the following five countries: France, Spain, Italy, Germany, and
1308   the United Kingdom. Then, we also consider the five most populous cities of each country, totaling
1309   25 cities: Paris, Marseille, Lyon, Toulouse, Nice, Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Sevilla, Zaragoza,
1310
       Rome, Milan, Naples, Turin, Palermo, Berlin, Hamburg, Munich, Köln, Frankfurt am Main, London,
       Birmingham, Liverpool, Glasgow, and Sheffield. The knowledge graph involving these city-country
1311
       pairs contains facts such as (xi = Paris, r = located in country, xj = France). The ground-truth
1312
       arrangements of the cities and countries form a tree (Fig. 17a).
1313
1314   From the latent space of LLMs, however, it is difficult to extract clean tree-like geometries. When
1315   we project the representations for tokens corresponding to the country and city names using Isomap,
1316
       the result does not yield a discernible tree shape (Fig. 17b). Despite the exact structure of the latent
       space not being clear, the notion of “distance” in the manifold can still be applied. For example,
1317
       in Fig. 17b, Spain is closer to France than is the United Kingdom; therefore, the edit “Paris is a
1318
       city in the country of Spain” has a smaller counterfactual edit distance than does the edit “Paris
1319   is a city in the country of the United Kingdom.” Fig. 18a and Fig. 18b show the representation
1320   manifold Isomaps after applying the edits “Paris is a city in the country of Spain” and “Paris is a
1321   city in the country of the United Kingdom,” respectively, using ROME on GPT-2. First, we find that
1322   both counterfactual edits cause the representations for all cities and countries to collapse inward.
1323   Moreover, the edit to “the United Kingdom” causes a greater distortion than the edit to “Spain,” as
1324   is evident both by visual inspection and by the numerical representation shattering quantity R(D∗ ).

1371   To take a step in verifying whether this finding is generalizable, we applied counterfactual edits to
1372   each of the 25 selected cities. For each city, we computed the country which constitutes the “closest”
1373   and “furthest” counterfactual edit distance on the model’s representation manifold. After applying
1374   the two counterfactual edits, we computed R(D∗farthest ) and R(D∗closest ). Across the 25 cities, the
1375
       average ratio R(D∗farthest )/R(D∗closest ) was 1.1483. In other words, when changing a city’s parent
       country, editing to a close country on the representation manifold yields less shattering than editing
1376
       to a country which sits far away on the manifold.

1378   These preliminary results align with our main hypothesis: KE methods distort language models’
1379   representations in order to insert new facts or alter old ones (i.e. representation shattering), and the
1380   extent of representation shattering increases with the distance between the old fact and the desired
1381
       new fact on the manifold.
