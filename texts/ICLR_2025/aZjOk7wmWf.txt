009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have demonstrated strong performance in graph
012            mining tasks due to their message-passing mechanism, which is aligned with the
013            homophily assumption that adjacent nodes exhibit similar behaviors. However,
014            in many real-world graphs, connected nodes may display contrasting behaviors,
015            termed as heterophilous patterns, which has attracted increased interest in het-
016            erophilous GNNs (HTGNNs). Although the message-passing mechanism seems
017            unsuitable for heterophilous graphs due to the propagation of class-irrelevant infor-
018            mation, it is still widely used in many existing HTGNNs and consistently achieves
019
               notable success. This raises the question: why does message passing remain effec-
               tive on heterophilous graphs? To answer this question, in this paper, we revisit the
020
               message-passing mechanisms in heterophilous graph neural networks and refor-
021
               mulate them into a unified heterophilious message-passing (HTMP) mechanism.
022            Based on HTMP and empirical analysis, we reveal that the success of message
023            passing in existing HTGNNs is attributed to implicitly enhancing the compatibility
024            matrix among classes. Moreover, we argue that the full potential of the compat-
025            ibility matrix is not completely achieved due to the existence of incomplete and
026            noisy semantic neighborhoods in real-world heterophilous graphs. To bridge this
027            gap, we introduce a new approach named CMGNN, which operates within the
028            HTMP mechanism to explicitly leverage and improve the compatibility matrix. A
029            thorough evaluation involving 10 benchmark datasets and comparative analysis
030
               against 17 well-established baselines highlights the superior performance of the
               HTMP mechanism and CMGNN method.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown remarkable performance in graph mining tasks, such as
037   social network analysis (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Zhang et al., 2022) and recommender systems (Wang
038   et al., 2019; He et al., 2020). The design principle of GNNs is typically based on the homophily
039
      assumption (McPherson et al., 2001), which assumes that nodes are inclined to exhibit behaviors
      similar to their neighboring nodes (Ma et al., 2022). However, this assumption does not always hold
040
      in real-world graphs, where the connected nodes demonstrate a contrasting tendency known as the
041
      heterophily (Zhu et al., 2021a). In response to the challenges of heterophily in graphs, heterophilous
042   GNNs (HTGNNs) have attracted considerable research interest (Ma et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022;
043   Zhu et al., 2023), with numerous innovative approaches being introduced recently, such as Abu-El-
044   Haija et al. (2019); Bo et al. (2021); Luan et al. (2022); Song et al. (2023). However, the majority of
045   these methods continue to employ a message-passing mechanism, which was not originally designed
046   for heterophilous graphs, as they tend to incorporate excessive information from disparate classes.
047   This naturally raises a question: Why does message passing remain effective on heterophilous graphs?
048   Recently, a few efforts (Ma et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023) have begun to investigate this question
049   and reveal that vanilla message passing can work on heterophilous graphs under certain conditions.
050   However, the absence of a unified and comprehensive understanding of message passing within
051   existing HTGNNs has hindered the creation of innovative approaches. In this paper, we first revisit the
052   message-passing mechanisms in existing HTGNNs and reformulate them into a unified heterophilous
053   message-passing (HTMP) mechanism, which extends the definition of neighborhood in various ways
      and simultaneously utilizes the messages of multiple neighborhoods. Specifically, HTMP consists of

054
      three major steps namely aggregating messages with explicit guidance, combining messages from
055   multiple neighborhoods, and fusing intermediate representations.
056
057   Equipped with HTMP, we further conduct empirical analysis on real-world graphs. The results reveal
058
      that the success of message passing in existing HTGNNs is attributed to implicitly enhancing the
      compatibility matrix, which exhibits the probabilities of observing edges among nodes from different
059
      classes. In particular, by increasing the distinctiveness between the rows of the compatibility matrix
060
      via different strategies, the node representations of different classes become more discriminative.
061
062   Drawing from previous observations, we contend that nodes within real-world graphs might exhibit a
063   semantic neighborhood that only reveals a fraction of the compatibility matrix, accompanied by noise.
064
      This could limit the effectiveness of enhancing the compatibility matrix and result in suboptimal
      representations. To fill this gap, we further propose a novel Compatibility Matrix-aware Graph Neural
065
      Network (CMGNN) under HTMP mechanism, which utilizes the compatibility matrix to construct
066
      desired neighborhood messages as supplementary for nodes and explicitly enhances the compatibility
067   matrix by a targeted constraint. We build a benchmark to fairly evaluate CMGNN and existing
068   methods, which encompasses 17 diverse baseline methods and 10 datasets that exhibit varying
069   levels of heterophily. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the superiority of CMGNN and
070   HTMP mechanism. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
071
072   • We revisit the message-passing mechanisms in existing HTGNNs and reformulate them into a
073
        unified heterophilous message-passing mechanism (HTMP), which not only provides a macroscopic
        view of message passing in HTGNNs but also enables people to develop new methods flexibly.
074
075   • We reveal that the effectiveness of message passing on heterophilous graphs is attributed to
076     implicitly enhancing the compatibility matrix among classes, which gives us a new perspective to
077
        understand the message passing in HTGNNs.
078   • Based on HTMP mechanism and empirical analysis, we propose CMGNN to unlock the potential
079     of the compatibility matrix in HTGNNs. We further build a unified benchmark that avoids the
080     issues of current datasets for fair evaluation1 . Experiments show the superiority of CMGNN.

143

144   methodological analysis of some representative HTGNNs and more details can be seen in Appendix

147
      (i) To characterize different neigborhoods, the AGGREGATE function in HTMP includes the neigh-
      borhood indicator Ar to indicate the neighbors within a specific neighborhood r. The adjacency
148
      matrix A in VMP is a special neighborhood indicator that marks the neighbors in the raw neigh-
149
      borhood. To further characterize the aggregation of different neighborhoods, HTMP introduces the
150   aggregation guidence Br for each neighborhood r. In VMP, the aggregation guidance is an implicit

152   used form of the AGGREGATE function is AGGREGATE(Ar , Br , Zl−1 ) = (Ar ⊙ Br )Zl−1 Wrl ,
153   where ⊙ is the Hadamard product and Wrl is a weight matrix for message transformation. We take

155   and the aggregation guidance in the following.

159   matrix I ∈ RN ×N as a special neighborhood indicator for acquiring the ego messages of central
160   nodes. The aggregation guidance Br ∈ RN ×N can be viewed as pairwise aggregation weights in

178   Based on HTMP mechanism, we further dive into the motivation behind the message passing of
179   existing HTGNNs. Our discussion begins by examining the difference between homophilous and
180
      heterophilous graphs. Initially, we consider homophily ratios he and hn , as outlined in Section 2.
      However, a single number can not indicate enough conditions for a graph. Ma et al. (2022) propose
181
      the existence of a special case of heterophily, named ”good” heterophily, where the homophily
182
      ratios stay low but the VMP mechanism can achieve strong performance. Thus, to better study the
183   heterophily property, we introduce the Compatibility Matrix (Zhu et al., 2021a) to describe graphs:
184
      Definition 1. Compatibility Matrix (CM): The potential connection preference among classes within
185
      a graph. It is formatted as a matrix M ∈ RK×K , where the i-th row Mi denotes the connection
186   probabilities between class i and all classes. It can be estimated empirically as follows:
187
188                                  M = Norm(CT Cnb ),         Cnb = ÂC,                                (3)
189   where Norm(·) denotes the L1 normalization for matrix row vectors and T is the matrix transpose
190   operation. Cnb ∈ RN ×K is the semantic neighborhoods of nodes, which indicates the proportion of
191   neighbors from each class in the neighborhoods.
192
193   We first visualize the CM of a homophilous graph Photo (Shchur et al., 2018)
194   in Figure 1. It displays an identity-like matrix, where the diagonal elements
195   can be viewed as the homophily level of each class. With this type of CM, the
196
      VMP mechanism learns representations comprised mostly of messages from
      same the class, while messages of other classes are diluted.
197
198   Then how does HTMP mechanism work on heterophilous graphs with po-
199   tentially chaotic CMs such as Amazon-Ratings (Platonov et al., 2023) in
200   Figure 2(a)? The ”good” heterophily inspires us, which we believe corre-
201   sponds to a CM with enough discriminability among classes. We conduct Figure 1: Observed
202
      experiments on synthetic graphs to confirm this idea, with details available CM of Photo.
      in Appendix C. Also, we find ”good” heterophily exists in real-world graphs though it is not as
203
      significant as imagined. As a result, we have the following observation:
204
205   Observation 1. (Connection between CM and VMP). When enough (depends on data) discriminabil-
206
      ity exists among classes in CM, vanilla message passing can work well in heterophilous graphs.
207   This observation is similar to some prior works (Ma et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023) which emphasize
208   data while our focus is more on the message passing. Further, we have the following theorem with
209   detailed proof in Appendix D:
210
      Theorem 1. The discriminability among the representations learned by the message-passing mecha-
211   nism is positively correlated with the discriminability among classes in the compatibility matrix.
212

      neighborhood indicators and neighborhood discrimination are designed to construct neighborhoods

407   6      B ENCHMARKS AND E XPERIMENTS
408
409   In this section, we conduct comprehensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the
410   proposed CMGNN with a newly organized benchmark for fair comparisons.
411
412   6.1        N EW B ENCHMARK
413
414   As reported in Platonov et al. (2023), some widely adopted datasets in existing works have critical
415   drawbacks, which lead to unreliable results. Therefore, with a comprehensive review of existing
416   benchmark evaluation, we construct a new benchmark to fairly perform experimental validation.
417   Specifically, we integrate 17 representative homophilous and heterophilous GNNs, construct a unified
418   codebase, and evaluate their node classification performances on 10 unified organized datasets with
419
      various heterophily levels.
420
421
      Drawbacks of Existing Datasets. Existing works mostly follow the settings and datasets used
      in Pei et al. (2020), including 6 heterophilous datasets (Cornell, Texas, Wisconsin, Actor, Chameleon,
422
      and Squirrel) and 3 homophilous datasets (Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed). Platonov et al. (2023)
423
      pointed out serious data leakages in Chameleon and Squirrel, while Cornell, Texas, and Wisconsin
424   are too small with very imbalanced classes. Further, we revisit other datasets and discover new
425   drawbacks: (i) In the ten splits of Citeseer, there are two inconsistent ones, which have smaller
426   training, validation, and test sets that could cause issues with statistical results; (ii) Cora’s data split
427   ratios are inconsistent with the expected ones. These drawbacks may lead to certain issues in the
428   conclusions of previous works. The details of dataset drawbacks are listed in Appendix G.1.
429
430   Newly Organized Datasets. To avoid the issues of method comparison caused by above drawbacks,
431   we have collected and filtered suitable graph datasets from heterophilous GNNs methods and other
      fields (e.g. Anomaly Detection). This collection spans various levels of homophily, providing a robust


                                                                                      8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      foundation for performance evaluation. The datasets used in the benchmark include Roman-Empire,
433   Amazon-Ratings, Chameleon-F, Squirrel-F, Actor, Flickr, BlogCatalog, Wikics, Pubmed, and Photo.
434   Their statistics are summarized in Table 2, with details in Appendix G.2. For consistency with
435   existing methods, we randomly construct 10 splits with predefined proportions (48%/32%/20% for
436   train/valid/test) for each dataset and report the mean performance and standard deviation of 10 splits.
437
438   Baseline Methods. As baseline methods, we choose 17 representative homophilous and het-
439   erophilous GNNs, including (i) shallow base model: MLP; (ii) homophilous GNNs: GCN (Kipf &
440   Welling, 2017), GAT (Veličković et al., 2018), APPNP (Gasteiger et al., 2019), GCNII (Chen et al.,
441   2020); (iii) heterophilous GNNs: H2GCN (Zhu et al., 2020), MixHop (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019),
442   GBK-GNN (Du et al., 2022), GGCN (Yan et al., 2022), GloGNN (Li et al., 2022), HOGGCN (Wang
443
      et al., 2022), GPR-GNN (Chien et al., 2021), ACM-GCN (Luan et al., 2022) and OrderedGNN (Song

445

447   settings can be found in Appendix G.4 and H.1.

      6.2   M AIN R ESULTS
450
451   Following the constructed benchmark, we evaluate methods and report the performance in Table 2.
452
      Performance of Baseline Methods. With the new benchmarks, some interesting observations and
453
      conclusions can be found when analyzing the performance of baseline methods. First, comparing the
454   performance of MLP and GCN, we can find ”good” heterophily in Amazon-Ratings, Chameleon-F,
455   and Squirrel-F, where GCN performs better than MLP under this kind of heterophily. Meanwhile,
456   ”bad” homophily may also exist as shown in BlogCatalog and Wikics, where the homophily level
457   is insufficient for vanilla message-passing methods (GCN, GAT) to outperform MLP. These results
458   once again support the observations about CMs. Therefore, homophilous GNNs can also work well
459   in heterophilous graphs as GCNII has an average rank of 4.7, which is better than most HTGNNs.
460   This is attributed to the initial residual connection in GCNII actually playing the role of ego/neighbor
461   separation, which is suitable in heterophilous graphs. As for heterophilous GNNs, they are usually
462
      designed for both homophilous and heterophilous graphs. Surprisingly, MixHop, as an early method,
      demonstrated quite good performance. In fact, from the perspective of HTMP, it can be considered
463
      a degenerate version of OrderedGNN with no learnable dimensions. As previous SOTA methods,
464
      OrderedGNN and ACM-GCN prove their strong capabilities again.
465
466   Performance of CMGNN. CMGNN achieves the best performance in 6 datasets and an average
467   rank of 2.1, which outperforms baseline methods. This demonstrates the superiority of utilizing
468
      and enhancing the CM to handle incomplete and noisy semantic neighborhoods, especially in
      heterophilous graphs. Regarding the suboptimal performance in Actor, we believe that this is due
469
      to the CM in this dataset are not discriminative enough to provide valuable information via the
470
      supplementary messages and hard to enhance. In homophilous graphs, due to the identity-like CMs,
471   the overlap between distributions is relatively less, leading to a minor contribution from supplement
472   messages. Yet CMGNN still achieves top-level performances.
473
474
      Comparision with CM-based methods. Some existing methods also utilize the compatibility
      matrix (CM) to redefine pair-wise relations (i.e. edge weights) for existing edges, such as label
475
      propagation in CLP, log-likelihood estimation in EPFGNN, and prior belief propagation in CPGNN.
476
      In contrast, CMGNN leverages CM and virtual neighbors to construct supplementary messages while
477   preserving the original neighborhood distribution. As a result, CMGNN achieves better performances
478   and benefits from the approach of utilizing CM in the following aspects: (i) Better robustness for
479   low-quality pseudo labels; (ii) Unlock the effectiveness of CM for low-degree nodes; (iii) More
480   accurate estimation of CM. More detailed analyses are available in Appendix H.2.1

527   7       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   In this paper, we revisit the message-passing mechanism in existing heterophilous GNNs and
530   reformulate them into a unified heterophilous message-passing (HTMP) mechanism. Based on the
531   HTMP mechanism and empirical analysis, we reveal that the reason for message passing remaining
532   effective is attributed to implicitly enhancing the compatibility matrix among classes. Further, we
533   propose a novel method CMGNN to unlock the potential of the compatibility matrix by handling the
534   incomplete and noisy semantic neighborhoods. The experimental results show the effectiveness of
535   CMGNN and the feasibility of designing a new method following HTMP mechanism. We hope the
536
      HTMP mechanism and benchmark can further provide convenience to the community.
537   This work mainly focuses on the message-passing mechanism in existing HTGNNs under the
538   semi-supervised setting. Thus, the other designs in HTGNNs such as objective functions are not
539   analyzed in this paper. The proposed HTMP mechanism is suitable for only a large part of existing
      HTGNNs which still follow the message passing mechanism.

697   A    R ELATED W ORKS
698
699   Homophilous Graph Neural Networks. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have showcased impres-
700   sive capabilities in handling graph-structured data (Wu et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024). Traditional
701   GNNs are predominantly founded on the assumption of homophily, broadly categorized into two
      classes: spectral-based GNNs and spatial-based GNNs. Firstly, spectral-based GNNs acquire node


                                                      13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      representations through graph convolution operations employing diverse graph filters (Kipf & Welling,
703   2017; Defferrard et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2018a). Secondly, spatial-based methods gather information
704   from neighbors and update the representation of central nodes through the message-passing mecha-
705   nism (Gasteiger et al., 2019; Veličković et al., 2018; Hamilton et al., 2017). Moreover, for a more
706   comprehensive understanding of existing homophilous GNNs, several unified frameworks (Ma
707   et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021b) have been proposed. Ma et al. (2021) propose that the aggregation
708   process in some representative homophilous GNNs can be regarded as solving a graph denoising
709   problem with a smoothness assumption. Zhu et al. (2021b) establishes a connection between various
710   message-passing mechanisms and a unified optimization problem. However, these methods have
711
      limitations, as the aggregated representations may lose discriminability when heterophilous neighbors
      dominate (Bo et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020).
712
713   Heterophilous Graph Neural Networks. Recently, some heterophilous GNNs have emerged to
714   tackle the heterophily problem (Bo et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021a;b; Pei et al., 2020;
715   Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022; Luan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Chien et al., 2021;
716   Song et al., 2023; Suresh et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2022). Firstly, a commonly adopted strategy
717
      involves expanding the neighborhood with higher homophily or richer messages, such as high order
      neighborhooods (Zhu et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021a), feature-similarity-based neighborhoods (Jin
718
      et al., 2021a;b), and custom-defined neighborhoods (Pei et al., 2020; Suresh et al., 2021). Secondly,
719
      some approaches (Bo et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Luan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Yan et al.,
720   2022) aim to leverage information from heterophilous neighbors, considering that not all heterophily
721   is detrimental et al.(Ma et al., 2022). Thirdly, some methods (Zhu et al., 2020; Abu-El-Haija et al.,
722   2019; Chien et al., 2021; Song et al., 2023) adapt to heterophily by extending the combine function
723   in message passing, creating variations for addition and concatenation.
724
      Reviewing Heterophilous Graph Neural Networks. Due to heterophilous GNNs have attracted
725
      more and more research attention. Some surveys have provided a macroscopic view for reviewing
726   heterophilous GNNs, categorizing heterophilous GNNs with shallow analysis. Specifically, Zheng
727   et al. (2022) categorizes the designs of heterophilous GNNs into non-local neighbor extensions
728   and GNN architecture refinement. Zhu et al. (2023) examines the impact of heterophilous graph
729   characteristics on GNNs. For categorizations, it simply lists some effective designs in heterophilous
730   GNNs. Gong et al. (2024) reviews heterophilous graph learning, where message passing is only a
731   minor aspect of its taxonomy with a broader view. However, these works offer guidance primarily
732   at the conceptual level and categorize existing heterophilous GNNs based on literature summaries,
733   lacking in-depth analysis of message-passing mechanisms. In this paper, we provide a uniform
734
      symbolic form and categorize existing methods based on the values of component modules. Further,
      our review guides the design of new SOTA heterophilous message-passing mechanisms.

743   B.1     A DDITIONAL A NALYSIS OF HTMP M ECHANISM
744
745   B.1.1    N EIGHBORHOOD I NDICATORS
746
747   The neighborhood indicator explicitly marks the neighbors of all nodes within a specific neighbor-
748   hood. In existing heterophilous GNNs, neighborhood indicators typically take one of the following
749   forms: (i) Raw Neighborhood (Raw); (ii) Neighborhood Redefining (ReDef); and (3) Neighborhood
750   Discrimination (Dis).
751   Raw Neighborhood. Raw neighborhood, including A and Ã, provides the basic neighborhood
752   information. The only difference between them is whether there is differential treatment of the node’s
753   ego messages. For example, APPNP (Gasteiger et al., 2019) applies additional weighting to the ego
754   messages of nodes compared to GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017). In heterophilous GNNs, ego/neighbor
755   separation is a common strategy that can mitigate the confusion of ego messages with neighbor
      messages.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Neighborhood Redefining. Neighborhood redefining is the most commonly used technique in
757   heterophilous GNNs, aiming to capture additional information from new neighborhoods. As a repre-
758   sentative example, high-order neighborhood Ah can provide long-distance connection information
759   but also result in additional computational costs. Feature-similarity-based neighborhood Af is often
760   defined by the k-NN relationships within the feature space. Fundamentally, it only utilizes node
761   features and thus needs to be used in conjunction with other neighborhood indicators. Otherwise,
762   the model will be limited by the amount of information in node features. GloGNN (Li et al., 2022)
763   introduces fully-connected neighborhood 1 ∈ RN ×N , which can capture global neighbor information
764   from all nodes. However, it can also cause significant time and space consumption. Additionally,
765
      there are some custom-defined neighborhood Ac . For example, Geom-GCN (Pei et al., 2020) rede-
      fines neighborhoods based on the geometric relationships between node pairs. These neighborhood
766
      indicators may have limited generality, and the effectiveness is reliant on the specific method.
767
768   Neighborhood Discrimination. Neighborhood discrimination aims to mark whether neighbors share
769   the same label with central nodes. The neighborhoods are partitioned into positive Ap and negative
770   ones An , which include homophilous and heterophilous neighbors respectively. GGCN (Yan et al.,
771
      2022) divides the raw neighborhood based on the similarity of node representations with a threshold
      of 0. Explicitly distinguishing neighbors allows for targeted processing, making the model more
772
      interpretable. However, its performance is influenced by the accuracy of the discrimination, which
773
      may lead to the accumulation of errors.
774
775
      B.1.2   AGGREGATION G UIDANCE
776
777   After identifying the neighborhood, the aggregation guidance controls what type of messages to
778   gather from the corresponding neighbors. The existing aggregation guidance mainly includes three
779   kinds of approaches: (1) Degree Averaging (DegAvg), (2) Adaptive Weights (AdaWeight), and (3)
780   Relationship Estimation (RelaEst).
                                                                      1      1
781   Degree Averaging. Degree averaging, formatted as Bd = D− 2 1D− 2 or Bd = D−1 1, is the most
782   common aggregation guidance, which plays the role of a low-pass filter to capture the smooth signals
783   and is fixed during model training. Further, combining negative degree averaging with an identity
784   aggregation guidance I ∈ RN ×N can capture the difference between central nodes and neighbors, as
785   used in ACM-GCN (Luan et al., 2022). Degree averaging is simple and efficient but depends on the
786   discriminability of corresponding neighborhoods.
787   Adaptive Weights. Another common strategy is allowing the model to learn the appropriate ag-
788   gregation guidances Baw . GAT (Veličković et al., 2018) proposes an attention mechanism to learn
789   aggregate weights, which guides many subsequent heterophilous methods. To better handle het-
790   erophilous graphs, FAGCN (Bo et al., 2021) introduces negative-available attention weights Bnaw
791   to capture the difference between central nodes and heterophilous neighbors. Adaptive weights can
792   personalize message aggregation for different neighbors, yet it’s difficult for models to attain the
793   desired effect.
794   Relationship Estimation. Recently, some methods have tried to estimate the pair-wise relationships
795   Bre between nodes and use them to guide message aggregation. HOG-GCN (Wang et al., 2022) esti-
796   mates the pair-wise homophily levels between nodes as aggregation guidances based on both attribute
797   and topology space. GloGNN (Li et al., 2022) treats all nodes as neighbors and estimates a coefficient
798   matrix as aggregation guidance based on the idea of linear subspace expression. GGCN (Yan et al.,
799   2022) estimates appropriate weights for message aggregation with the degrees of nodes and the
800   similarities between node representations. Relationship estimation usually has theoretical guidance,
801
      which brings strong interpretability. However, it may also result in significant temporal and spatial
      complexity when estimating pair-wise relations.
802
803
      B.1.3   COMBINE F UNCTION
804
805   After message aggregation, the COMBINE functions integrate messages from multiple neighborhoods
806   into layer representations. COMBINE functions in heterophilous GNNs are commonly based on
807   two operations: addition and concatenation, each of which has variants. To merge several messages
808   together, addition (Add) is a naive idea. Further, to control the weight of messages from different
809   neighborhoods, weighted addition (WeightedAdd) is applied. However, it is a global setting and
      cannot adapt to the differences between nodes. Thus, adaptive weighted addition (AdaAdd) is


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      proposed, which can learn personalized message combination weights for each node, but it will result
811   in additional time consumption. Although the addition is simple and efficient, some methods (Zhu
812   et al., 2020; Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019) believe that it may blur messages from different neighborhoods,
813   which can be harmful in heterophilous GNNs, so they employ a concatenation operation (Cat) to
814   separate the messages. Nevertheless, such an approach not only increases the space cost but may
815   also retain additional redundant messages. To address these issues, OrderedGNN (Song et al., 2023)
816   proposes an adaptive concatenation mechanism (AdaCat) that can combine multiple messages with
817   learnable dimensions. This is an innovative and worthy further exploration practice, but the difficulty
818   of model learning should also be considered.
819
820   B.1.4    FUSE F UNCTION
821   Further, the FUSE functions integrate messages from multiple layers into the final representation.
822   For the FUSE function, utilizing the representation of the last layer as the final representation is
823   widely accepted: Z = ZL . JKNet (Xu et al., 2018b) proposes that the combination of representations
824   from intermediate layers can capture both local and global information. H2GCN (Zhu et al., 2020)
825   applies it in heterophilous graphs, preserving messages from different localities with concatenation.
826   Similarly, GPRGNN (Chien et al., 2021) combines the representations of multiple layers into the
827   final representation through adaptive weighted addition.

       B.3    A NALYSIS AND A DVICE FOR D ESIGNING M ODELS
1112
1113   The HTMP mechanism splits the message-passing mechanism of HTGNNs into multiple modules,
1114   establishing connections among methods. For instance, most messages passing in HTGNNs have
1115   personalized processing for nodes. Some methods (Du et al., 2022; Bo et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021a;
1116   Suresh et al., 2021) utilize the learnable aggregation guidance and some others (Jin et al., 2021b;
1117   Luan et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2022) count on learnable COMBINE functions.
1118   Though neighborhood redefining is commonly used in HTGNNs, there are also many methods (Du
1119
       et al., 2022; Bo et al., 2021; Luan et al., 2022; Chien et al., 2021; Song et al., 2023) using only raw
       neighborhoods to handle heterophily and achieve good performance. Degree averaging, which plays
1120
       the role of a low-pass filter to capture the smooth signals, can still work well in many HTGNNs (Zhu
1121
       et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021b; Pei et al., 2020; Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019; Chien et al., 2021). High-
1122   order neighbor information may be helpful in heterophilous graphs. Existing HTGNNs utilize it
1123   in two ways: directly defining high-order (Zhu et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021a; Abu-El-Haija et al.,
1124   2019; Wang et al., 2022) or even full-connected (Li et al., 2022) neighborhood indicators and by the
1125   multi-layer architecture of message passing (Chien et al., 2021; Song et al., 2023).
1126
       With the aid of HTMP, we can revisit existing methods from a unified and comprehensible perspective.
1127
       An obvious observation is that the coordination among designs is important while good combinations
1128   with easy designs can also achieve wonderful results. For instance, in ACM-GCN (Luan et al.,
1129   2022), the separation and adaptive addition of ego, low-frequency, and high-frequency messages
1130   can accommodate the personalized conditions of each node. OrderedGNN’s design (Song et al.,
1131   2023), which includes an adaptive connection mechanism, ego/neighbor separation, and multi-layer
1132   architecture, allows discrete and adaptive combinations of messages from multi-hop neighborhoods.
1133   This advises us to take into account all components simultaneously when designing models. As
       an illustration, please be cautious about using multiple learnable components. Also, here are some

       To prove Theorem 1, we start with an assumption:
1205
1206   Assumption 1. The semantic neighborhood   C nb of each node follows a class-specific distribution
       guided by CM, where Cinb = d1i j∈N (i) Cj indicates the proportion of neighbors from each class

1210
       According to Assumption 1, the discriminability in CM is positively correlated with the discrim-
       inability in semantic neighborhoods. Thus, if the message-passing mechanism is able to preserve
1211
       the discriminability of the semantic neighborhood in the obtained representations, then Theorem 1
1212
       holds. It would be sufficient if each distinct semantic neighborhood corresponds to a different output
1213   representation, in other words, the message-passing mechanism is an injective function for modeling
1214   semantic neighborhoods. We further state two assumptions:
1215
       Assumption 2. The input node messages Zl−1 of the message-passing layer exhibit clustering
1216
       characteristics, where the average distance within a class is significantly smaller than the average
1217   distance between different classes.
1218
1219   This implies that the input messages of nodes from the same class are linearly correlated within a
1220   certain range of error.
1221
       Assumption 3. The clustering centers of each class’s input messages exist, formatted as K prototypes
1222   {ck |k ∈ 1, ..., K}.
1223
1224   Taking the most general mean aggregation as an example, we have the following theorem:
1225
       Theorem 2. Let MEAN({Zl−1         j |j ∈ N (i)}) be mean operator that aggregate neighbor messages
1226
1227   for node i, ck be the prototype of k-th class. Function MEAN({Zl−1     j |j ∈ N (i)}) is approximately
1228
       injective if it is satisfied that all class prototypes ck are orthogonal to each other.
1229
       The injectivity ensures that each element in the domain of the input (i.e. semantic neighborhoods
1230   and neighbor messages) has a distinct and unique output in the output domain. We find that as long
1231   as the conditions of Theorem 2 are satisfied, the mean aggregation can be regarded as an injective
1232   function within a certain range of error. Thus, the whole message-passing mechanism can be an
1233   approximately injective function for modeling the semantic neighborhoods when the COMBINE
1234   function is also injective, which can be easily satisfied. In practice, the orthogonality of prototypes is
1235   hard to be satisfied completely but the difference among prototypes is still significant. Thus, even
1236   if the message-passing mechanism is not completely injective, most of the discriminability can be
1237   preserved, making Theorem 1 hold.

       Proof of Lemma 1. We make the proof of Lemma 1 from the perspectives of both its sufficiency and

       equation implies that v = 0. Thus we can conclude that null(T ) = {0}, as desired.
1247
1248   Necessity: Suppose null(T ) = {0}, u, v ∈ V . If T (u) = T (v), then T (u) − T (v) = T (u − v) = 0.
1249   Thus u − v = 0, which implies that u = v. Hence T is injective, as desired.
1250
1251   Having Lemma 1 proved, now we express the mean aggregation in the form of P Z nb = b, where
1252   P ∈ R1×|N (i)| denotes the mean aggregation operator, Z nb ∈ R|N (i)|×dr is the matrix consist of
1253   neighbor messages and b is the resulting representation. Assuming that the messages of neighbors
1254   from the same class are linearly dependent, we can rewrite the equation as P ′ Z p ≈ b, where
1255   P ′ ∈ R1×K is a weighted mean operator, Z p ∈ RK×dr is a matrix consisting of the prototypes
1256
       {ck |k ∈ 1, ..., K} of K classes.
1257   The injectivity of mean aggregation operator P involves considering the solution for P ′ Z p = 0.
1258   Clearly, if it is satisfied that all ck are orthogonal to each other, the null space null(P ′ ) = {0},
1259   indicating that the mean aggregation operator is approximately injective, as desired.
1260
1261   The above analyses provide theoretical support for Observation 1 and Observation 2. Based on
1262   Theorem 1, VMP can work well with discriminative CM regardless of homophily levels and HTMP
1263   can achieve better performance by enhancing the discriminability of CM.

1533   H.2.1     D ETAILED A NALYSIS ABOUT THE COMPARISON BETWEEN CMGNN AND EXISTING
1534             CM- BASED METHODS
1535
1536   Specifically, CMGNN achieves better performances and benefits from the approach of utilizing CM in
1537   the following aspects: (i) Better robustness for low-quality pseudo labels: Existing CM-based methods
1538   utilize CM to guide the weights of propagation, which can lead to error accumulation with inaccurate
1539
       pseudo labels. This is a common limitation of CM-based methods. In CMGNN, the CM is used to
       construct supplementary messages while original neighborhoods are preserved, mitigating the impact
1540
       of inaccurate pseudo labels. (ii) Unlock the effectiveness of CM for low-degree nodes: Existing
1541
       CM-based methods redefine pair-wise relations only for existing edges, limiting the effectiveness
1542   of CMs for low-degree nodes. In CMGNN, virtual neighbors can provide prototype messages from
1543   every class, enhancing neighborhood messages for low-degree or even isolated nodes. (iii) More
1544   accurate estimation of CM: While existing CM-based methods take naive approaches to estimate
1545   or initialize CM, CMGNN considers the effects of node degrees and model prediction confidence,
1546   resulting in more accurate CM estimation, especially in real-world situations. Additionally, CM in
1547   CMGNN is continuously updated with more accurate pseudo labels, creating a positive cycle.

       Complexity Analysis. The number of learnable parameters in layer l of CMGNN is 3dr (dr + 1) + 9,
       compared to dr dr in GCN and 3dr (dr + 1) + 9 in ACM-GCN, where dr is the dimension of
1646
       representations. The time complexity of layer l is composed of three parts:
1647
1648   (i) AGGREGATE function: O(N dr 2 ), O(N dr 2 + M dr ) and O(N dr 2 + N Kdr ) for identity neigh-
1649   borhood, raw neighborhood and the supplementary neighborhood respectively, where N and M = |E|
1650   denotes the number of nodes and edges;
1651   (ii) COMBINE function: O(3N (3dr + 1) + 12N ) for calculating adaptive weights and O(3N ) for
1652   combination;
1653
       (iii) FUSE function: O(1) for concatenations.
1654
1655   Thus, the overall time complexity of L-layer CMGNN is O(L(N dr (3dr +K +9)+M dr +18N )+1),
1656   or O(LN dr 2 + LM dr ) for brevity.
