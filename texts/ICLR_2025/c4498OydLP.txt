009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, represented by LoRA, play an essential
012            role in adapting large-scale pre-trained models to downstream tasks. However,
013            fine-tuning LoRA-series models also faces the risk of overfitting on small training
014            datasets, and there’s still a lack of theoretical guidance and practical mechanisms
015            to control overfitting on LoRA-based PEFT methods. This paper introduces a
016            novel dropout-based sparsity regularizer for LoRA, dubbed LoRA Dropout, which
017            mitigates overfitting by applying refined dropout to LoRA’s low-rank matrices.
018            We establish a theoretical framework that models dropout in LoRA as a sparse
019            fine-tuning process and derive a generalization error bound under this sparsity
020
               regularization. Theoretical results show that appropriate sparsity can tighten the
               gap between empirical and generalization risks and thereby control overfitting. We
021
               further enhance the sparsity patterns in conventional dropout methods and propose
022
               an innovative LoRA Dropout method for more precise sparsity regularization
023            to achieve better overfitting reduction. Furthermore, we introduce a test-time
024            ensemble strategy and provide theoretical evidence demonstrating that the ensemble
025            method can further compress the error bound and lead to better performance.
026            Extensive experiments on various NLP tasks validate the effectiveness of our LoRA
027            Dropout framework in improving the model’s performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      In recent years, Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; He et al.,
      2020; Touvron et al., 2023) have demonstrated increasingly superior performances in various NLP
034
      tasks as the rapid growth of model parameter scale. However, with the increasing model capacity
035
      and complexity, the challenge arises when adapting the PLMs to specific downstream tasks, as
036   fully fine-tuning often requires substantial computational resources. Therefore, a new fine-tuning
037   paradigm emerges named Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), aiming to adapt PLMs to specific
038   downstream tasks with minimal adjustments to their parameters.
039
      Among the works in the field of PEFT (Houlsby et al., 2019; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Hu
040
      et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024), the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) method (Hu et al.,
041
      2021) and its variants (Dettmers et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Zi et al., 2023) have been the most
042   effective and widely adopted. The basic idea behind LoRA is that only some zero-initialized delta
043   weight matrices get optimized during fine-tuning, and the original pre-trained parameters remain
044   unmodified. To improve parameter efficiency, LoRA further decomposes the delta weight matrix into
045   the product of two low-rank matrices.
046
      However, one significant challenge when fine-tuning LoRA-series models on downstream tasks is
047
      overfitting. As shown in Figure.1(a), the gap between train and test losses on both LoRA becomes
048   larger during fine-tuning, indicating a strong overfitting tendency of LoRA training. Simply reducing
049   the rank of LoRA (i.e. reducing learnable parameters) could help alleviate overfitting, but fewer
050   learnable parameters indicate less expressive power, and might lead to suboptimal performances.
051   Therefore, it is hard to select a proper rank that could balance the expressiveness and overfitting risk.
052   AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023) proposes to automatically prune unimportant parameters with learned
053   importance scores during training to prevent overfitting. However, this parameter selection method
      heavily relies on gradients of parameters on the training data, which in turn makes the selected

      The dropout regularization (Hinton et al., 2012; Srivastava et al., 2014) is known as one of the most
068
      popular and effective techniques in deep learning to control overfitting. It works by randomly masking
069
      neurons and does not require to reduce parameter budget. Recently there have been works attempting
070   to combine dropout with LoRA to control the overfitting risk when fine-tuning (Wang et al., 2024).
071   However, these works fail to answer the following profound question:
072
073
       What is the theoretical mechanism behind the alleviation of overfitting on the training data
      through random dropout of LoRA parameters?
074
075   In this paper, we answer the above question and build our theoretical framework by modeling the
076   training process with dropout from the perspective of sparse fine-tuning, and show that fine-tuning
077   LoRA with dropout can be viewed as an optimization problem with sparsity regularization. We further
078   provide a generalization error bound under the sparsity regularization framework. Through this bound,
079
      we reveal that introducing appropriate sparsity on LoRA tunable parameters during fine-tuning helps
      to balance the empirical risk minimization and complexity of the adaptation function. Therefore,
080
      proper dropout would help tighten the gap between empirical and generalization risks and reduce
081
      overfitting on the training data.
082
083   Furthermore, with our theoretical framework, we identify the flaws in the original dropout method
084   and improve upon it. The theoretical analyses point out a crucial condition for effectively applying
085
      the dropout mechanism in LoRA training, namely the sparsity condition. While most prior studies
      have implemented dropout on hidden representations or at the token level (Wang et al., 2024),
086
      they fall short in generating a sufficiently diverse sparsity pattern across the parameter space. This
087
      limitation hampers the precision of sparsity regularization. To address this issue, we introduce an
088   innovative approach called LoRA Dropout, which enhances the dropout process by incorporating
089   more expressive sparsity patterns for the updated parameters—without significantly increasing
090   GPU memory overhead. We also propose a test-time ensemble method with an ensemble classifier
091   consisting of models with different parameter dropouts. This ensemble method can further improve
092   the model’s test-time performance, which is also supported by theoretical evidence.
093
      In summary, we conclude the main contributions of this paper as follows. We provide theoretical
094   analyses on the generalization error bound of dropout on LoRA, balancing the empirical risk mini-
095   mization and complexity of the adaptation function class through sparsity regularization. Based on
096   the theoretical evidences, we propose a novel dropout framework for LoRA-based models. With ex-
097   pressive sparsity patterns, our LoRA Dropout method can effectively promote model’s generalization
098   ability on downstream tasks. Moreover, we propose an ensemble strategy during the inference stage,
099   and show that this strategy will lead to an ensemble model with a tighter error bound and further
100   enhance the model’s test-time generalizability. Extensive experiments conducted on a wide range of
101   NLP tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in improving the model’s performances.

      3.2       L O RA D ROPOUT
236
237   The conventional dropout method only conducts dropout on the hidden representations, which is
238   equivalent to masking random columns for the delta weight matrix ∆W . Such dropping strategy fails
239   to generate sufficiently diverse sparsity patterns. However, it is also not realistic to generate a sparsity
240   mask that covers every single element in ∆W , since current LLMs usually consist a huge number of
241   parameters, and generating such sparsity masks will leads to great GPU memory overhead. Therefore,
242   we propose a dropout strategy in the reparameterized space of LoRA to improve the diversity of the
243   generated sparsity pattern without significantly increasing GPU memory overhead. Specifically, for a
244
      LoRA module described in Eq.8, we randomly drop rows and columns from both tunable low-rank
      parameter matrices:
245
246                                                                              ⊤
         Â = A · diag(mA ), mA ∼ Bern(1 − p); B̂ = B ⊤ · diag(mB ) , mB ∼ Bern(1 − p), (9)
247
248   where mA ∈ Rn2 and mB ∈ Rn1 are mask vectors drawn from the Bernoulli distribution, and
249   p denotes the probability that the parameters get dropped. Note that we conduct dropout on the
250   input/output dimension of both matrices as applying dropout on the rank dimension would decrease
251   the rank of LoRA, significantly impacting its expressive power. Additionally, performing dropout on
252
      the rank dimension will not increase the sparsity of the product of LoRA matrices, while theoretical
      evidence in Section 2 highlights the significance of sparsity in our framework. With the dropout, the
253
254
      forward pass would be ĥ = W0 x + B̂ Âx.

258   quasi-SVD method, ∆W = P ΛQ, where P ∈ Rn1 ×r and Q ∈ Rr×n2 are left and right singular

      3.3   T EST- TIME E NSEMBLE S TRATEGY
277
278   To further enhance the model’s performance during inference time, inspired by the MC dropout
279   mechanism (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), we propose a test-time ensemble method. Unlike the
280   conventional dropout that is deactivated when testing, our ensemble strategy aggregates the outputs
281   of models under different dropouts during inference time to get the final output, which can be viewed
282   as sampling and aggregating models    from a parameter distribution with a Monte Carlo method.
283   Specifically, let M θ 0 + ∆θ(mk ) denote the model with LoRA parameter under dropout mk ,
284
      then the output o of the ensemble model is

288   where N is the number of dropout instances. Here we justify the effectiveness of this test-time
289   ensemble strategy by providing a theoretical analysis of how it helps further tighten the error bound.
290   During the fine-tuning phase, we optimize Eq.(3) through accumulating gradient steps under dif-
291   ferent dropout instances. This fine-tuning procedure is essentially optimizing the generalization
292   risks given the distribution D of model parameters θ, which is Eθ∼D E(x,y) Lλ (M(x; θ), y), where
293   M(x; θ) denotes the output of model M given the input x parameterized by θ. During the inference
294   phase, with the test-time ensemble strategy, we are actually aggregating model outputs across the
295   distribution D of parameter θ to conduct final predictions, namely the ensemble classifier, which has
296   an error of E(x,y) Lλ (Eθ∼D M(x; θ), y). We present the following proposition that depicts a tighter
297   generalization error bound with the ensemble classifier:
298   Proposition 3.1 (Error Bound of Test-time Ensemble). If the loss function Lλ (e.g. cross-entropy) is
299   convex w.r.t. the final output o = M(x; θ) of model M, then we have:
300                         E(x,y) Lλ (Eθ∼D M(x; θ), y) ≤ Eθ∼D E(x,y) Lλ (M(x; θ), y).                  (13)
301
302   Proof Sketch. Taking expectation on parameter θ is equivalent to taking expectation on the final
303   output o from a certain distribution. Then simply apply Jensen inequality under the convex condition
304   of o and the inequality holds. See Appendix A.2 for detailed proofs.
305
306   Moreover, the convexity holds for most cases in LLM training or fine-tuning scenarios, as we often
307   take cross-entropy as the loss function and the softmax as the final output layer, and those functions
308   are convex in the entire space Rd . Hence, the inequality says that the generalization error of the
309   ensemble classifier (i.e., LHS of (13)) is no greater than the training generalization error (i.e., RHS
310   of (13)) for most LLM tuning scenarios, implying that the ensemble classifier with LoRA Dropout
311   can further compress the error bound given by the LoRA Dropout fine-tuning and demonstrate better
      test-time generalizability.
312
313   To summarize, Theorem 2.4 and 3.1 together depict the full theoretical sketch of our practical
314   framework. The fine-tuning phase applies LoRA Dropout to control the generalization error by
315   balancing the trade-off between the empirical risk minimization and the complexity of adaptation,
316   and the inference phase applies multiple dropout instances to accomplish an ensemble classifier with
317   a tighter error bound and further enhances the test-time generalizability.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
487
488
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) PEFT focuses on efficiently adapting pre-trained lan-
489   guage models to downstream tasks by fine-tuning a few additional parameters or a subset of pre-trained
490   parameters. Current mainstream PEFT approach can be roughly divided into three categories (Lialin
491   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Additive Fine-tuning methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al.,
492   2020; He et al., 2022; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021) focus on adding extra tunable parameters
493   by introducing additional layers or learnable prompts. Partial Fine-tuning methods (Zaken et al.,
494   2022; Xu et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2023) select a subset of pre-trained parameters for fine-tuning.
495   Reparameterization Fine-tuning methods (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Edalati et al., 2022)
496   adopt low-rank representations to minimize the number of trainable parameters. In this paper, we
497
      focus on the most effective and widely adopted method, LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and its variants,
      which decompose the learnable delta weight into the product of two low-rank matrices. The rank
498
      of the decomposition is essential for LoRA. A small rank may lead to insufficient expressive power,
499
      while a large rank could result in overfitting. One of LoRA’s variants, AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023)
500   proposes to decompose the delta weight through a quasi-SVD method, and select parameters through
501   importance scoring. Nevertheless, this selection method also relies on gradients on the training
502   set, leading to an additional risk of overfitting. In this work, we propose a theoretically grounded
503   dropout framework for LoRA-series methods, filling the gap that the LoRA-based PEFT methods
504   lack theoretical guidances and practical mechanisms to control overfitting.
505
506   Dropout Regularization The dropout mechanism (Hinton et al., 2012) is a well-known and widely-
507   adopted technique in deep neural networks to prevent overfitting. In standard dropout, each neuron
508   in the network is omitted from the network with a certain possibility during training. Subsequently,
509   various dropout techniques for specific model structures are introduced, like Spatial dropout (Tompson
510   et al., 2015) for convolutional layers and Recurrent dropout (Semeniuta et al., 2016) for recurrent
511
      neural networks. Meanwhile, works have been done to explore the theoretical factors behind dropout’s
      ability to suppress overfitting. Some works believe that the the model learns a geometric mean over
512
      the ensemble of possible sub-networks through dropout (Warde-Farley et al., 2013; Baldi & Sadowski,
513
      2013), and some works view dropout from a Bayesian perspective and argue that model with dropout
514   can be interpreted as a Bayesian model approximating a posterior over parameters (Gal & Ghahramani,
515   2016). Recently there has been work trying to combine dropout with LLM (Wang et al., 2024). But
516   this work mainly focuses on conduct dropout on the transformer structure instead of LoRA, and
517   also lacks theoretical analysis. To the best of our knowledge, currently there’s little theoretical work
518   on applying dropout on LoRA-based PEFT models, where fine-tuning happens on the delta weight
519   matrices with low-rank decompositions.
520
521
      6   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
522
523
      To control the overfitting risk when fine-tuning on downstream tasks, in this paper, we propose a theo-
524   retically grounded LoRA Dropout framework designed for LoRA-based PEFT methods. Theoretical
525   analyses from the perspective of sparse show that sparsity introduced by LoRA Dropout helps tighten
526   the between empirical and generalization risks and thereby control overfitting. A test-ensemble strat-
527   egy is proposed based on LoRA Dropout and theoretically shown to further compress the error bound.
528   We conduct experiments on various tasks and PLMs, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of
529   our method on improving model’performances.
530   Despite the promising results, we still want to point out the limitations. Though LoRA Dropout
531   introduces no additional tunable parameters compared to LoRA, sampling multiple dropout instances
532   during training and testing does introduce considerable time overhead. For future work, we aim to
533   design a parallel computing framework for LoRA Dropout, expecting to improve in both performance
534   and efficiency.
