009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Data augmentation is widely used in machine learning to enhance training datasets
012            by introducing minor variations to the original data, traditionally aiming to prevent
013            overfitting and improve model performance. This paper explores a novel applica-
014            tion of data augmentation during the inference stage to enhance out-of-distribution
015            (OOD) detection. The proposed method involves replicating the inference image
016            multiple times, applying various transformation techniques to each replica, and
017            then evaluating the detectors using these augmented images. The effectiveness of
018            this approach is assessed across different detectors, models, and datasets, demon-
019            strating its potential to improve OOD detection capabilities.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   In the current era of Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML) models play a crucial role
025   in numerous applications ranging from computer vision to natural language processing. However,
026   despite their success, these models often face inputs for which they have not been trained (i.e.,
027   uncertainty). Out-of-distribution (OOD) data, which are different from training data in terms of
028   distribution, can lead to erroneous and unreliable predictions, which in turn can have detrimental
029   consequences in critical applications (e.g., functional safety-related systems). Therefore, OOD data
030   detection has become an important area of research in the trustworthy ML field.
031   OOD detectors are tools that can identify if a given input falls outside the distribution of the training
032   data. They are crucial in ensuring that the model performs well (in the sense of reducing false-
033   positives) not just on the data that contains images of the training classes, but also on new, unseen
034   and unknown. If we perform a thorough review of the state of the art, we observe that different
035   detectors are proposed (see Section 4). The standard approach for OOD detection involves gener-
036   ating a scoring function from the trained network such that the In-Distribution (ID) samples show
037   significantly different scores than OOD.
038   Data augmentation is a technique commonly used in ML to increase the amount of training data
039   while diversifying the “view” of an input to which a model is exposed. It consists of creating new
040   data samples conceptually class agnostic, from existing ones by applying transformation techniques
041   (e.g., rotation, scaling, horizontal/vertical flipping). It is commonly used during model training with
042   the aim of preventing overfitting, increasing data diversity and quantity, improving model perfor-
043   mance and compensating for missing data.
044   In this paper, we propose employing augmentation during inference and combine it with OOD de-
045   tectors to increase the models’ degree of trustworthiness. Unlike the traditional use of data augmen-
046   tation, which is to use it only in training, our approach is based on replicating the inference image a
047   number of times and applying transformation techniques to each of the replicas. In this way, we can
048   evaluate each replica on the detector(s) and observe if the results are consistent with small perturba-
049   tions of each input. We evaluate this approach on different detectors, models and datasets, in order
050
      to know if this can improve the detection of OODs. For this purpose, we have taken as a base work
      the articles of Sun et al. (2022) and Park et al. (2023).
051
052   The remainder of this publication is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the proposed ap-
053   proach. Section 3 performs the evaluation of the contribution in two different scenarios. Section 4
      performs a review of the available literature. Finally, Section 5 draws the obtained conclusions.

      2   A PPROACH DESCRIPTION
055
056
      In this study, we propose a novel approach to improve OOD detection using multiple transformed
057   instances of a single image. Our approach relies on the principle of internal consistency. Similar to
058   how psychological tests use varied phrasing of similar questions to enhance accuracy, completeness,
059   and reliability, our proposed method evaluates the consistency of detector results when subjected to
060   sets of transformed inputs. In other words, it reduces response bias and ensures that responses are
061   not influenced by the interpretation of a single question (or input image, in our case).
062
      Thus, just as psychologists ask the same question, rephrased differently, we evaluate the model
063   with the same original image but replicated and transformed in a different way. Hence, allowing to
064   judge consistency of response. Input transformations cannot be either too small, as this would only
065   result in an increase of resource usage without any benefit, or too much, as this would only create a
066   significant reduction of the classification capabilities of the model. In this paper, we propose using
067   common augmentation methods for the input transformations. Note that what actually constitutes
068   an optimal augmentation level is still an open question.

      We calculate the median using the results obtained by the detectors. That is, we calculate the median

124   This approach leverages the power of ensemble methods and robust statistics (the median) to provide
125

127   From a computational point of view, our approach leads to an increase in processing time and a

130   nitude is not a showstopper, the overhead seems tolerable. Note though that in this research we do

134
135   In this section, we evaluate the performance of the OOD detectors when combined with our ap-
136   proach. Specifically, we assess the detectors by applying them to augmented replicas. For this
137   purpose, we calculate the median value of the detector results for the replicated images. For ex-
138   ample, if we have 8 replicas, we observe the score provided by the detector for these 8 cases and
139   compute the median result. By calculating the median of the replicates, we can find the midpoint of
140
      the same varied question, i.e., the same image is transformed differently.
141   Note that the scripts used to perform the evaluation are published in Unknown (2024) and are based
142   on code from Sun et al. (2022) and Park et al. (2023).
143
      OOD Detectors. The analysis is performed using various differential detectors identified during the
144   literature review: Energy by Liu et al. (2020), Nearest Neighbor Guidance (NNGuide) by Park et al.
145   (2023), Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP) by Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016), MaxLogit by Wei
146   et al. (2022), Self-Supervised Outlier Detection (SSD) by Sehwag et al. (2021), Mahalanobis by Lee
147   et al. (2018), and K-th Nearest Neighbor (KNN) by Sun et al. (2022). For further information on
148   these detectors, see Section 4.
149
      Metrics to Evaluate the Performance of the Approach. To validate our approach, we examine
150   two metrics: (i) the false positive rate for OOD instances when the true positive rate for ID instances
151   is set at 95% (i.e., False Positive Rate at 95% True Positive Rate (FPR95)), and (ii) the Area Under
152   the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC). These metrics are widely used in OOD de-
153   tection to evaluate the performance of models for distinguishing between ID and OOD (as in Sun
154   et al. (2022); Park et al. (2023); Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016); Wei et al. (2022)). In OOD detection,
155   lower values of FPR95 indicate superior performance, as they represent fewer negative samples be-
156   ing incorrectly classified as positive when the true positive rate is set to 95%. Conversely, higher
157   values of AUROC indicate higher performance, as they reflect a greater ability of the model to dis-
158
      tinguish between in-distribution and out-of-distribution samples. Consequently, for our approach to
      be considered valid, FPR95 must decrease and AUROC should increase as the number of augmented
159
      replicas increases.
160

      different scenarios. This involves using various training data (i.e., ID datasets with different sizes),

407   4      R ELATED WORK
408
409   In the realm of OOD detectors for Neural Networks (NNs), several noteworthy methods have been
410   proposed. MSP method by Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016), leverages probabilities from softmax distri-
411   butions, observing that correctly classified examples often have greater maximum softmax probabil-
412   ities than erroneously classified and OOD examples. The MaxLogit method by Wei et al. (2022), ad-
413
      dresses the overconfidence issue in neural networks by enforcing a constant vector norm on the log-
      its during training. The Mahalanobis method utilizes the Mahalanobis distance for OOD detection
414
      (Lee et al., 2018). The Self-Supervised Outlier Detection (SSD) method employs self-supervised
415
      representation learning (Sehwag et al., 2021) followed by a Mahalanobis distance-based detection
416   in the feature space. The GradNorm method uses information extracted from the gradient space for
417   OOD detection (Huang et al., 2021). The KNN method employs non-parametric nearest-neighbor
418   distance for OOD detection (Sun et al., 2022). The Energy-based method uses an energy score for
419   OOD detection, distinguishing in- and out-of-distribution samples more effectively than traditional
420   softmax scores (Liu et al., 2020). Lastly, the Nearest Neighbor Guidance (NNGuide) method guides
421   the classifier-based score to respect the boundary geometry of the data manifold, reducing the over-
422   confidence of OOD samples while preserving the fine-grained capability of the classifier-based score
423   (Park et al., 2023). These methods represent the current state-of-the-art in OOD detection for NN.
424
425   5      C ONCLUSIONS
426
427   This paper proposes to use augmented replicas during inference to improve OOD detectors. This
428   work offers a simple but effective contribution. By simply replicating images and transforming
429   them before inference, we are able to improve OOD detection in the vast majority of the cases. In an
430   abstract, one can say that we allow the OOD detectors to view the inputs from slightly different per-
431   spectives. This approach enhances the “knowledge” they may gain, including determining whether
      an input is OOD. Furthermore, the contribution is based on simple statistics such as the median,

      but it is also open to more elaborate statistical methods. Therefore, with the presented results, we
433   conclude that our proposal is a contribution to the field of trustworthy AI.
