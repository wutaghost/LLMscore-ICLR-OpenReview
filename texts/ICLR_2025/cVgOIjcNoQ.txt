011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            With the rapid development of large language models, researchers have created in-
014            creasingly advanced spoken dialogue systems that can naturally converse with hu-
015
               mans. However, these systems still struggle to handle the full complexity of real-
               world conversations, including audio events, musical contexts, and emotional ex-
016
               pressions, mainly because current dialogue datasets are constrained in both scale
017
               and scenario diversity. In this paper, we propose leveraging synthetic data to en-
018            hance the dialogue models across diverse scenarios. We introduce ShareChatX,
019            the first comprehensive, large-scale dataset for spoken dialogue that spans diverse
020            scenarios. Based on this dataset, we introduce OmniChat, a multi-turn dialogue
021            system with a heterogeneous feature fusion module, designed to optimize feature
022            selection in different dialogue contexts. In addition, we explored critical aspects
023            of training dialogue systems using synthetic data. Through comprehensive ex-
024            perimentation, we determined the ideal balance between synthetic and real data,
025            achieving state-of-the-art results on the real-world dialogue dataset DailyTalk. We
026
               also highlight the crucial importance of synthetic data in tackling diverse, complex
               dialogue scenarios, especially those involving audio and music. For more details,
027
               please visit our demo page at https://sharechatx.github.io/.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence, spoken dialogue systems (Jokinen & McTear,
033   2009) have emerged as a crucial branch of human-computer interaction. Many voice assistants,
034   such as Siri (Hoy, 2018) and Cortana (Hachman, 2019), leverage automatic speech recognition (Yu
035   & Deng, 2016) to transcribe speech into text and generate corresponding responses, enabling con-
036   versational capabilities. Driven by the progress in large language models (LLMs)(Touvron et al.,
037   2023), modern spoken dialogue systems(OpenAI, 2024b) now possess enhanced reasoning and
038   understanding abilities, allowing for more complex dialogue functions based on speech content.
039
      However, unlike traditional text-based dialogue systems Qin et al. (2023), spoken dialogue systems
      must also account for a wealth of multi-modal information beyond words. Significant efforts have
040
      been made to enhance multi-modal large language models for understanding various types of audio.
041
      Audio-Flamingo (Kong et al., 2024) has developed a text conversation dataset centered on audio
042   events and music, enabling text-based dialogues built around these elements. Qwen-Audio 1/2 (Chu
043   et al., 2023; 2024), trained on 520,000 hours of audio-related tasks, has equipped its models to
044   comprehend speech, audio, music, and other full-scene audio inputs. EMOVA (Chen et al., 2024)
045   introduces a framework that integrates spoken dialogue with multimodal tasks, enabling a spoken
046   dialogue model that can “see, hear, and speak”. Although these models have demonstrated some
047   ability in handling spoken dialogues, the limitations in the scale and diversity of current dialogue
048   datasets have led to the lack of a spoken dialogue system that can effectively understand speech
049   emotions, audio events, or interpret background music in complex spoken dialogue scenarios.
050   Compared to the vast amounts of text-based conversational data available online (Sordoni et al.,
051   2015), collecting spoken dialogue corpora presents significantly more challenges: (1) Limited Scale
052   of Spoken Dialogue Data. Acquiring spoken dialogue data is both more complex and costly than
053   gathering text data (Cieri et al., 2004), resulting in much smaller datasets. High-quality spoken
      data (especially data with multi-turn interactions and emotional complexity across different sce-

104

118   2.1    S POKEN D IALOGUE DATASETS
119
      Intelligent dialogue has long been a central focus of artificial intelligence research. To advance
120
      the development of dialogue systems, researchers have collected vast amounts of open-domain text
121   dialogue data from public platforms like Twitter (Sordoni et al., 2015) and Weibo (Shang et al.,
122   2015) for training purposes. However, the scale of spoken dialogue datasets has remained limited,
123   hindering the progress of spoken dialogue systems. Early efforts (Godfrey et al., 1992; Cieri et al.,
124   2004) involved constructing datasets by recruiting participants to record spoken dialogues, but this
125   approach was resource-intensive and costly. Later, researchers (Poria et al., 2018) turned to publicly
126   available resources such as TV shows to collect spoken dialogue data, resulting in the creation of
127   numerous real-world spoken dialogue datasets that were systematically annotated with emotional
128   information. With the maturation of large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2024a), researchers
129
      have begun synthesizing spoken dialogue data using AI-generated methods. For instance, Kong
      et al. (2024) adopted ChatGPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024b) to generate AF-Dialogue, a textual dialogue
130
      dataset set within specific audio event or music scenarios. Lin et al. (2024) employed large language
131
      model (OpenAI, 2024a) and the controllable TTS models to create StyleTalk, a dataset focused on
132   capturing different emotions to generate contextually appropriate responses.
133
134   However, obtaining large-scale, scenario-specific spoken conversation data remains challenging,
135
      limiting the scale and diversity of existing spoken dialogue datasets. To address this, we introduce
      ShareChatX, the first large-scale, omni-scenario synthetic spoken conversation dataset. ShareChatX
136
      covers a broad range of conversations, including those focused on speech emotion (-Emotion), audio
137
      events (-Audio), and music understanding (-Music).
138
139
      2.2    L ARGE AUDIO -L ANGUAGE M ODEL
140
141   With the development of large language models, increasingly powerful audio language models have
142   emerged, leveraging extensive training corpora to achieve comprehensive audio understanding ca-
143   pabilities. SpeechGPT (Zhang et al., 2023) integrates discrete speech units into the LLM, making
144   it the first speech-centric large language model. Qwen-Audio1/2 (Chu et al., 2023; 2024) build the
145   first comprehensive large-scale audio model on more than 30 audio-related tasks, including speech
146   recognition, speech translation, and audio event detection. Salmonn (Tang et al., 2023) addresses
147   the problem of task overfitting in audio models by introducing more complex story generation tasks.
148
      Building on the understanding of audio, a series of spoken dialogue models have been developed to
      promote more intelligent human-computer interaction. Audio-Flamingo (Kong et al., 2024) creates
149
      an audio event-centric text dialogue dataset, enabling multi-turn, audio-focused text conversations.
150
      StyleTalk (Lin et al., 2024) focuses on emotional conversation tasks and introduces the first spoken
151   dialogue model that responds with different emotional tones.
152
153   However, due to limitations in training data, most current spoken dialogue models are restricted to
154
      question answering purpose (Chu et al., 2024) or experiments on small-scale datasets (Lin et al.,
      2024). To address this gap, we propose using synthetic data to enhance the performance of spo-
155
      ken dialogue models across various scenarios and introduce OmniChat, the first multi-turn spoken
156
      dialogue model designed for diverse contexts.

188
      Textual Dialogue Scripts. Leveraging the powerful reasoning capabilities of large language mod-
189
      els (OpenAI, 2024a), we create textual dialogue scripts tailored to different topics and scenarios
190   using detailed prompt templates. In this process, we instruct the model to first generate N rounds
191   of historical dialogue, followed by responses and emotions that match the contextual flow. The di-
192   alogue topics for -emotion subset are generated with large language models, the audio descriptions

194   -music subset is sourced from MusicCaps (Agostinelli et al.). For further details, see Appendix D.2.
195
      Spoken Dialogue. In the textual script generation step, we generated the style parameters Tistyle
196
      (gender, pitch, speed, emotion) and the corresponding text content Ticontent for each sentence Ti .
197
      Using these style parameters and the text content, we employed the open-source controllable TTS
198   model, CosyVoice-Instruct (Du et al., 2024), to synthesize the corresponding speech Si .
199
200   Dialogue Verification. To ensure the quality of the voice conversation data, we implemented a
201
      dual verification method combining model-based and manual checks. Since each voice clip in the
      conversation is generated separately, we used a speaker diarization model (Plaquet & Bredin, 2023)
202
      to confirm that the same speaker’s voice maintained consistent timbre. Additionally, we applied
203
      an ASR model (Radford et al., 2023) to ensure that the word error rate (WER) across all samples
204   did not exceed 5%. For each conversation, we attempted synthesis up to 10 times until the entire
205   conversation met the required standards. Finally, manual inspection was conducted to verify that
206   each sample adhered to the logic of natural human conversation.
207
      Audio/Music Integration. For ShareChat-Audio and ShareChat-Music, we overlay the correspond-
208
      ing audio and music onto the spoken dialogue data. For -audio subset, a large language model (LLM)
209
      is used to determine whether the event is temporary or continuous. Temporary audio events, such as
210   a door slamming or a phone ringing, are short-lived sounds that occur briefly and are spliced before
211   the first voice segment. In contrast, continuous audio events, like background chatter or street noise,
212   are prolonged sounds that persist over time and are looped as background sound throughout the con-
213   versation. For the -music subset, we randomly apply two different methods to combine the music
214   with the dialogues. To ensure the authenticity of the final dialogue, all audio and music components
215   are overlaid according to Petermann et al. (2022) when combined with speech. For further details,
      see Appendix D.1.

246
247
      As illustrated in Figure 2, subfigure (a) depicts our proposed OmniChat, a multi-turn spoken di-
248   alogue model built upon a large language model. This model is capable of generating the most
249
      appropriate response acoustic style Tstyle                       content
                                              Assist,t and content TAssist,t for various voice inputs and

      dialogue scenarios. In subfigure (b), after extracting features using multiple expert models, the het-
251   erogeneous fusion module M IX -F ORMER is employed to produce the final voice feature input. The
252   detailed introduction is as follows:
253
254
      Multi-Expert Audio Feature Extraction In spoken dialogue, capturing acoustic features be-
255
      yond just the speech content is crucial. To model these features, we employ multiple ex-
256   pert models, each specializing in a different dimension of the speech. For the speech content,
257   we utilize Whisper model (Radford et al., 2023) to extract speech content features Fs , trained
258   with weak supervision on large-scale speech corpora, represented as Fsi = {Fsi,1 , · · · , Fsi,n } =
259   Whisper-Encoder(Dhuman,i ). For emotional information, we use Emotion2vec (Ma et al.,
260   2023), a speech emotion representation model trained with self-supervision on extensive emotional
261   speech datasets, which captures the emotional nuances of the speech Fei = {Fei,1 , · · · , Fei,n } =
262   emotion2vec(Dhuman,i ). To enable the model to understand broader non-speech elements, such
263   as audio events and music, we incorporate the Beat model (Chen et al., 2023) as a non-speech au-
264   dio feature extractor Fbi = {Fbi,1 , · · · , Fbi,n } = Beat-Encoder(Dhuman,i ). Since the feature
265   frame rates of these audio expert encoders are consistent, the three expert features can be temporally
266   aligned as {(Fsi,j , Fei,j , Fbi,j ) | j ∈ [1, N ]}, where N is the number of frames in each audio feature.
267
268   Mix-Former for Heterogeneous Fusion The importance of different features can vary signifi-
269   cantly across dialogue system scenarios. For example, beat features are essential in music-related
      environments but may interfere with emotion-centric dialogues. To address this, we propose a het-

271   diverse expert features while minimizing interference.

306   5.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
307
308   We adopt the Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model (Dubey et al., 2024) as the backbone LLM. All audio
309
      data are resampled to 16 kHz for consistency. In the windowed Q-Former, we set K = 1 , resulting
      in a single trainable query, and use L = 17 , which corresponds to approximately 0.33 seconds per
310
      window. The models are trained for 30,000 steps with a batch size of 48 on eight A800 GPUs. For
311
      more detailed training information, refer to Appendix C.
312
313   To evaluate model performance, we conducted experiments on two datasets: DailyTalk (Lee et al.,
314   2023) and our proposed ShareChatX. For testing, we randomly selected a test set from each subset of
315
      DailyTalk and ShareChatX, ensuring that the training and test sets were non-overlapping. Following
      previous studies (Lin et al., 2024), we employed both quantitative and qualitative metrics to evaluate
316
      model performance. The quantitative evaluation was divided into two aspects: content and style.
317
      For content evaluation, we utilized widely recognized text generation metrics, including vocabulary-
318   level scores such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), and METEOR (Banerjee
319   & Lavie, 2005), as well as semantic-level metrics like BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019). For style
320   evaluation, we computed weighted F1 scores for speaking emotion. In addition to the quantitative
321   metrics, we conducted qualitative analyses using GPT-based metric (Yang et al., 2024) and manual
322   evaluation. The detailed prompt template for GPT evaluation can be found in Appendix C.3. For a
323   dialogue with T turns, we use the previous T − 1 turns as context and predict only the response for
      the T -th turn.

      5.3   H OW D OES DATA S CALE I MPACT THE S POKEN D IALOGUE M ODELS ?
400
401
402   Spoken dialogue models based on large language models must learn the mapping between speech
403   and text from scratch, and the scale of training data plays a crucial role in their performance. How-
404   ever, what scale of data is sufficient to support the training of effective spoken dialogue models?
405
      To explore this question, we conducted a comparative analysis of the three most commonly used
      input modalities for dialogue models, as shown in Figure 3: (1) text-based dialogue models (using
406
      text as input, represented by the green line), (2) ASR-based spoken dialogue models (utilizing ASR
407
      transcriptions along with speech input, represented by the orange line), and (3) direct spoken dia-
408   logue models (relying solely on speech input, represented by the blue line). The following analysis
409   highlights the key findings as the dataset scale ranges from 5K to 80K samples.
410
411
      Speech Models Surpass Text Models (5K-10K) At the 5K-10K data scale, models incorporating
      speech input (either with or without ASR transcriptions) begin to outperform text-based models. For
412
      example, the BLEU score of the direct speech model improves from 4.65 at 5K to 4.94 at 10K, while
413
      the text-based model lags behind with a BLEU score of 4.86. Speech data, which contains not only
414   semantic content but also emotional cues, allows the model to capture richer information than text
415   alone, leading to better performance as the dataset size increases.
416
417
      Direct Speech Model Outperforms ASR-based Model (10K-20K) Between the 10K and 20K
      data scale, the direct speech model (without ASR text) begins to outperform the ASR-based model.
418
      For instance, the METEOR score of the direct speech model reaches 17.59 at 20K, while the ASR-
419
      based model trails slightly behind at 16.96. At this scale, the ASR transcriptions no longer provide
420   additional useful information; in other words, this amount of data is sufficient for the model to learn
421   the mapping from speech to semantics from scratch.
422
423
      Textual Input Becomes Redundant (20K-80K) As the dataset size increases further (20K-80K),
      the performance of the speech-only model continues to improve, while the performance of the ASR-
424
      based model plateaus. For example, in the BLEU metric, the direct speech model improves from
425
      6.06 at 20K to 6.17 at 80K, whereas the ASR-based model shows diminishing returns, rising only
426   from 5.79 to 5.85. This suggests that as the model is trained on larger datasets, speech alone is
427   sufficient to capture all necessary information, including emotional cues and context. In contrast,
428   the text input becomes redundant, as it lacks the multimodal information present in speech, such as
429   tone, intonation, and emotion. This redundancy not only fails to improve performance but can also
430   hinder the model by introducing unnecessary complexity. For instance, at the 80K data scale, the
431   ROUGE-L score of the speech-only model reaches 20.02, while the model using both speech and
      ASR text achieves only 19.01.

445   5.4   O PTIMAL S AMPLING R ATIOS OF S YNTHETIC AND R EAL DATA IN S POKEN D IALOGUE
446         M ODELS
447
448   Researchers have demonstrated that achieving optimal performance across various tasks requires
449   balancing synthetic and real data during training. Synthetic data enhances model robustness, while
450
      real data ensures alignment with the target domain’s distribution. Yet, the question arises: what is
      the ideal sampling ratio for spoken dialogue models? (Please note that the importance of data scale
451
      has been demonstrated in subsection 5.3. The experiments in this subsection focus solely on the
452
      sampling rate, with the synthetic data scale fixed at 80K.)
453
454   To explore this, we experimented with various sampling ratios, as shown in Table 4, to determine
455   the optimal balance between synthetic and real data: (1) Low Ratio (α = 0.1) Ensures Lexi-
456
      cal Consistency. At a sampling ratio of α = 0.1 (one synthetic sample for every ten training
      samples), the model achieved a BLEU of 5.07 and a ROUGE-L of 13.29, outperforming models
457
      trained exclusively on real data (α = 1.0) or synthetic data (α = 0.0). This indicates that in-
458
      corporating a small proportion of synthetic data helps the model achieve better consistency at the
459   word level, while real data ensures alignment with natural spoken dialogues. (2) Moderate Ratio
460   (α = 0.2) Achieves Sentence-Level Consistency. Further increasing the proportion of synthetic
461   data improved the model’s ability to generate semantically coherent responses. At a sampling ratio
462   of α = 0.2, the model’s F 1e score increased by 4.41 compared to α = 0.1, demonstrating that
463   this ratio allows the model to achieve optimal performance at the sentence level in terms of mean-
464   ing and emotion control. (3) Excessive Ratio (α > 0.2) Leads to Performance Decline. When
465   the ratio of synthetic data exceeded α = 0.2, performance in real conversation scenarios began to
466   decline. For instance, the ROUGE-L dropped by 0.91 when α increased from 0.2 to 0.3, indicating
467
      that an excessive reliance on synthetic data can hinder the model’s ability to generalize to real-world
      conversations. Based on these findings, a sampling ratio of α = 0.2 provides the ideal balance,
468
      achieving optimal performance in real-world dialogue scenarios.

      5.6      S POKEN D IALOGUE S YSTEM FOR C OMPLEX S CENARIOS .
504
505
      In spoken dialogue models, the presence of background sounds such as audio events or music re-
506   quires the model to understand not only the emotional and contextual content of speech but also
507   the various background sounds that influence the conversation. To explore how to effectively train
508   spoken dialogue models for complex scenarios, we conducted experiments on the ShareChat-Audio
509   dataset, as shown in Figure 4. Our results highlight several key factors for training effective spoken
510   dialogue models in these complex, multimodal environments:
511
      The Effect of Pre-Training on Large-Scale General Dialogue Data. Across all metrics, models
512   pre-trained on larger-scale general dialogue data (E-PT+A-FT) achieved significantly better results
513   than models trained solely on music data (A-FT). For instance, the BLEU score of the E-PT+A-FT
514   model (6.007) with 27K training data was higher than that of the A-FT model (5.310). This indicates
515   that large-scale pre-training equips the model with a stronger understanding of language structure
516   and general conversational dynamics, which is crucial for handling complex scenarios that involve
517   background sounds.
518   The Impact of Scenario-Specific Dialogue Data. The scale of the spoken dialogue data in complex
519   scenarios also plays a critical role in model performance. When fine-tuned with 27K ShareChat-
520   Audio samples, the ROUGE-L score of the pre-trained model (E-PT) increased from 17.350 to
521   18.649. However, when the model was fine-tuned with only 5K-10K music samples, its performance
522   did not surpass that of the model pre-trained on general dialogue data. For example, the METEOR
523   score of the model fine-tuned on 5K spoken dialogue data in audio scenario (14.006) was lower than
524   that of the pre-trained model (15.890). This shows that while fine-tuning on scenario-specific data
525   improves performance, a sufficient volume of such data is necessary to fully support the model in
526
      complex environments.

531
      Spoken dialogue systems have been hindered by the scarcity of large-scale, high-quality spoken
      dialogue data. To address this challenge, we introduced the use of synthetic datasets to enhance
532
      the performance of dialogue models. In this paper, we presented ShareChatX, the first large-scale
533
      dataset covering diverse, complex scenarios such as emotional dialogues, audio events, and music.
534   Through extensive experimentation, we determined the optimal balance between real and synthetic
535   data, as well as the required data size for training spoken dialogue models. These findings pro-
536   vide valuable insights for future dialogue model development. Furthermore, our proposed system,
537   OmniChat, demonstrated superior spoken dialogue synthesis across various scenarios, delivering
538   emotionally appropriate and high-quality language responses. These advancements underscore the
539   crucial role of synthetic data in advancing spoken dialogue systems and optimizing their perfor-
      mance in real-world applications.

      A     L IMITATIONS
703
704
      The spoken dialogue system we proposed, Omnichat, currently focuses on generating the most
705
      appropriate reply content and emotions but still relies on a controllable TTS model to synthesize
706   speech for replies. However, the research in this article emphasizes the understanding capabilities
707   of spoken dialogue systems, and the conclusions drawn can also serve as a reference for end-to-end
708   spoken dialogue models that directly generate speech. In the future, we will explore the application
709   of synthetic data in developing end-to-end spoken dialogue systems.
710

      B     E THICAL D ISCUSSION
713
714
      Spoken dialogue systems developed using public data may face risks such as inappropriate guidance
      or offensive language. Due to the complexity and diversity of conversations in public datasets, it can
715
      be challenging to determine whether the content poses risks, such as encouraging criminal behavior.
716
      In contrast, dialogue systems developed using synthetic data can better ensure ethical consistency in
717   conversation content. Additionally, this paper is intended solely for academic research and does not
718   result in commercial products, so the ethical risks are minimal at present. We plan to explore how
719   to further reduce the risk of accidental guidance in voice dialogue systems in the future.

724   C.1   D ETAILS FOR D IALOGUE ON DAILY TALK
725
      In mixed training with real and synthetic data, we sample from both datasets at a specific sampling
726
      rate α. For each training instance, a random number µ is drawn between 0 and 1. If µ < α, the
727
      model selects samples from the synthetic data for training. If µ ≥ α, the samples are selected from
728   the real data for training. We randomly selected 220 samples from DailyTalk as the test set. We will
729   open-source the test set partitions in this work to facilitate comparison in future studies.

734   ceed to evaluate it. For ShareChat-Audio and ShareChat-Music, we leverage a model pre-trained on
735   ShareChat-Emotion and fine-tune it on these two subsets to better adapt the model for specific com-
736   plex scenarios. Both the pre-training on ShareChat-Emotion and the fine-tuning on the two subsets
737   are conducted for 30,000 steps each. We have 3,731 dialogues for the -emotion test set, 1,555 for
