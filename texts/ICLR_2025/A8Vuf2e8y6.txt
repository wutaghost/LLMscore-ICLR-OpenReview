009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural fields (NeFs) have recently emerged as a state-of-the-art method for en-
012            coding spatio-temporal signals of various modalities. Despite the success of NeFs
013            in reconstructing individual signals, their use as representations in downstream
014            tasks, such as classification or segmentation, is hindered by the complexity of the
015            parameter space and its underlying symmetries, in addition to the lack of powerful
016            and scalable conditioning mechanisms. In this work, we draw inspiration from the
017            principles of connectionism to design a new architecture based on MLPs, which
018
               we term NeoMLP. We start from an MLP, viewed as a graph, and transform it
               from a multi-partite graph to a complete graph of input, hidden, and output nodes,
019
               equipped with high-dimensional features. We perform message passing on this
020
               graph and employ weight-sharing via self-attention among all the nodes. NeoMLP
021            has a built-in mechanism for conditioning through the hidden and output nodes,
022            which function as a set of latent codes, and as such, NeoMLP can be used straight-
023            forwardly as a conditional neural field. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our
024            method by fitting high-resolution signals, including multi-modal audio-visual data.
025            Furthermore, we fit datasets of neural representations, by learning instance-specific
026            sets of latent codes using a single backbone architecture, and then use them for
027            downstream tasks, outperforming recent state-of-the-art methods.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The omnipresence of neural networks in the last decade has recently given rise to neural fields (NeFs)
033
      (cf. Xie et al. (2022)) as a powerful and scalable method for encoding continuous signals of various
      modalities. These range from shapes (Park et al., 2019), scenes (Mildenhall et al., 2020), and images,
034
      (Sitzmann et al., 2020), to physical fields (Kofinas et al., 2023), CT scans (Papa et al., 2023; de Vries
035
      et al., 2024), and partial differential equations (Yin et al., 2022; Knigge et al., 2024). Consequently,
036   the popularity of neural fields has spurred interest in neural representations, i.e. using NeFs as
037   representations for a wide range of downstream tasks.
038
039
      Existing neural representations, however, suffer from notable drawbacks. Representations based on
      unconditional neural fields, i.e. independent multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) fitted per signal, are
040
      subject to parameter symmetries (Hecht-Nielsen, 1990), which lead to extremely poor performance
041
      in downstream tasks if left unattended (Navon et al., 2023). Many recent works (Navon et al.,
042   2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Kofinas et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024a; Papa et al., 2024) have proposed
043   architectures that respect the underlying symmetries; the performance, however, leaves much to be
044   desired. Another line of works (Park et al., 2019; Dupont et al., 2022) has proposed conditional
045   neural fields with a single latent code per signal that modulates the signal through concatenation,
046   FiLM (Perez et al., 2018), or hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016), while, recently, other works (Sajjadi
047   et al., 2022; Wessels et al., 2024) have proposed set-latent conditional neural fields—conditional
048   neural fields with a set of latent codes—that condition the signal through attention (Vaswani et al.,
049   2017). Whilst the study of Rebain et al. (2022) showed that set-latent neural fields outperform
050
      single latent code methods as conditioning mechanisms, existing set-latent neural fields are based on
      cross-attention, which limits their scalability and expressivity: coordinates are only used as queries in
051
      attention, and cross-attention is limited to a single layer.
052
053   We argue that many of these drawbacks stem from the lack of a unified native architecture that
      integrates the necessary properties of neural representations and eliminates the shortcomings of

054
      current approaches. To address these concerns, we draw inspiration from connectionism and the
055   long history of MLPs to design a new architecture that functions as a standard machine learning
056   model—akin to an MLP—as well as a conditional neural field. The paradigm of neural networks,
057   from the early days of Perceptron (McCulloch & Pitts, 1943), to MLPs with hidden neurons trained
058   with backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986), to modern transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), shares
059   the connectionist principle: cognitive processes can be described by interconnected networks of
060   simple and often uniform units.
061   This principle is lacking from current conditional neural field architectures, since conditioning is
062   added to the network as an ad-hoc mechanism. In contrast, motivated by this principle, we take a
063   closer look at MLPs; more specifically, we look at MLPs as a graph— similar to a few recent works
064   (Kofinas et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024a; Nikolentzos et al., 2024)— and design a novel architecture
065   that operates on this graph using message passing. First, we convert the graph from a multi-partite
066   graph to a fully-connected graph with self-edges. Instead of using edge-specific weights, we employ
067   weight-sharing via self-attention among all the nodes. We initialize the hidden and output nodes with
068   noise and optimize their values with backpropagation. Finally, we use high-dimensional features for
069
      all nodes to make self-attention and the network as a whole more scalable.
070   We make the following contributions. First, we propose a new architecture, which we term NeoMLP,
071   by viewing MLPs as a graph, and convert this graph to a complete graph of input, hidden, and
072   output nodes with high-dimensional features. We employ message passing on that graph through
073   self-attention among the input, hidden, and output nodes. The hidden and output nodes can be used
074   as a learnable set of latent codes, and thus, our method can function as a conditional neural field.
075
      We introduce new neural representations that use sets of latent codes for each signal, which we
      term ν-reps, as well as datasets of neural representations, which we term ν-sets. We fit datasets of
076
      signals using a single backbone architecture, and then use the latent codes for downstream tasks,
077
      outperforming recent state-of-the-art methods. We also demonstrate the effectiveness of our method
078   by fitting high-resolution audio and video signals, as well as multi-modal audio-visual data.

081   2     BACKGROUND
082
083   Neural fields Neural fields (NeFs), often referred to as Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), are
084   a class of neural networks that parameterize fields using neural networks (cf. Xie et al. (2022)). In
085   their simplest form, they are MLPs that take as input a single coordinate (e.g. an x − y coordinate)
086   and output the field value for that coordinate (e.g. an RGB value). By feeding batches of coordinates
087
      to the network, and training to reconstruct the target values with backpropagation, the neural field
      learns to encode the target signal, without being bound to a specific resolution.
088
089   Conditional neural fields introduce a conditioning mechanism to neural fields through latent variables,
090   often referred to as latent codes. This conditioning mechanism can be used to encode instance-specific
091   information (e.g. encode a single image) and disentangle it from the backbone architecture, which
092   now carries dataset-wide information.

096   3.1   F ROM MLP TO N EO MLP
097
098   We begin the exposition of our method with MLPs, since our architecture is influenced by MLPs
099   and builds on them. Without loss of generality, a multi-layer perceptron takes as input a set of scalar
100   variables {xi }Ii=1 , xi ∈ R, coalesced into a single high-dimensional array x ∈ RI . Through a series
101   of non-linear transformations, the input array is progressively transformed into intermediate (hidden)
102   representations, with the final transformation leading to the output array y ∈ RO .
103   Akin to other recent works (Kofinas et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024b; Nikolentzos et al., 2024), we look
104   at an MLP as a graph; an MLP is an L + 1-partite graph, where L is the number of layers. The nodes
105   represent the input, hidden, and output neurons, and have scalar features that correspond to individual
106   inputs, the hidden features at each layer, and the individual outputs, respectively. We perform message
107   passing on that graph, after making it more amenable for learning. First, we convert the connectivity
      graph from an L + 1-partite graph to a fully-connected graph with self-edges. Since the forward

      Our method operates in two distinct stages: fitting and finetuning. During fitting, our goal is to
      optimize the backbone architecture, i.e. the parameters of the model. We sample latent codes for
251
      all the signals of a fitting dataset and optimize them simultaneously with the backbone architecture.
252
      When the fitting stage is complete, after a predetermined set of epochs, we freeze the parameters of
253   the backbone architecture and discard the latent codes. Then, during finetuning, given a new signal,
254   we sample new latent codes for it and optimize them to minimize the reconstruction error for a number
255   of epochs. We finetune the training, validation, and test sets of the downstream task from scratch,
256   even if we used the training set to fit the model, in order to make the distance of representations
257   between splits as small as possible.
258
      In both the fitting and the finetuning stage, we sample completely random points from random signals.
259   This ensures i.i.d. samples, and speeds up the training of our method. During the fitting stage, we also
260   sample points with replacement, as we observed a spiky behaviour in the training loss otherwise. We
261   provide the detailed algorithms of the fitting and the finetuning stage in Algorithm 1, and Algorithm 2
262   in Appendix A, respectively. We provide further implementation details in Appendix D.

313   4.1    F ITTING HIGH - RESOLUTION SIGNALS
314
315   First, we evaluate our method at fitting high-resolution signals. We compare our method against Siren
316   (Sitzmann et al., 2020), an MLP with sinusoidal activations, RFFNet (Tancik et al., 2020), an MLP
317   with random Fourier features and ReLU activations, and SPDER (Shah & Sitawarin, 2024), an MLP
318   with sublinear damping activations combined with sinusoids. Our goal is to assess the effectiveness
319   of our method in signals of various modalities, and especially in multimodal signals, which have been
320
      underexplored in the context of neural fields. Hence, we choose signals that belong to two different
      modalities, namely an audio clip and a video clip, as well as a multi-modal signal, namely video with
321
      audio.
322
323   For audio, we follow Siren (Sitzmann et al., 2020) and use the first 7 seconds from Bach’s cello
      suite No. 1 in G Major: Prelude. The audio clip is sampled at 44.1 kHz, resulting in 308,700 points.

325   This video clip lasts for 10 seconds and is sampled at 25 fps, with a spatial resolution of 272 × 640,

332   Training details For audio, we follow Siren (Sitzmann et al., 2020) and scale the time domain
333   to t ∈ [−100, 100] instead of [−1, 1], to account for the high sampling rate of the signal. For the
334   audio-visual data, we model the signal as f : R3 → R9 , i.e. we have 3 input dimensions (x, y, t),
335   and 9 output dimensions: 3 from video (RGB) and 6 from audio (6 audio channels). Similar to the
336   audio clip, we also scale the time domain, which is now used as the time coordinate for both the
337   audio and the video points. For the points corresponding to audio, we fill their xy coordinates with
338   zeros. Furthermore, since all points come from either the video or the audio modality, we fill the
339   output dimensions that correspond to the other modality with zeros. Finally, during training, we mask
340
      these placeholder output dimensions, i.e. we compute the loss for the video coordinates using only
      the RGB outputs, and the loss for the audio coordinates using only the 6-channel audio outputs.

      4.2   F ITTING ν- SETS & D OWNSTREAM TASKS ON ν- SETS
379
380   Next, we evaluate our method on fitting ν-sets, i.e. fitting datasets of neural representations of signals
381   with NeoMLP, as well as performing downstream tasks on ν-sets. We compare our method against
382   Functa (Dupont et al., 2022), DWSNet (Navon et al., 2023), Neural Graphs (Kofinas et al., 2024),
383   and Fit-a-NeF (Papa et al., 2024). Functa is a conditional neural field that uses an MLP backbone and
384   conditioning by bias modulation. DWSNet, Neural Graphs, and Fit-a-NeF, on the other hand, are
385   equivariant downstream models for processing datasets of unconditional neural fields. For these three
386
      methods, the process of creating datasets of neural representations corresponds to fitting separate
      MLPs for each signal in a dataset, a process that is independent of the downstream models themselves.
387
      Since these methods have the step of generating the neural datasets in common, we use shared
388
      datasets for these methods, which are provided by Fit-a-NeF.
389
390   We consider three datasets, namely MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009),
391   and ShapeNet10 (Chang et al., 2015). We evaluate reconstruction quality for MNIST and CIFAR10
392
      with PSNR, and for ShapeNet with IoU. For CIFAR10, we follow the setup of Functa (Dupont
      et al., 2022), and use 50 augmentations for all training and validation images during finetuning. For
393

470   Importance of RFF As shown by Rahaman et al. (2019), neural networks suffer from spectral bias,
471   i.e. they prioritize learning low frequency components, and have difficulties learning high frequency
472   functions. We expect that these spectral biases would also be present in NeoMLP if left unattended.
473   To that end, we employed Random Fourier Features (RFF) (Tancik et al., 2020) to project our scalar
474   inputs to higher dimensions. Compared to alternatives like sinusoidal activations (Sitzmann et al.,
475
      2020), RFFs allow our architecture to use a standard transformer.
476   To examine the spectral bias hypothesis, we train NeoMLP without RFF, using a learnable linear
477   layer instead. We train this new model on the “bikes” video, and on MNIST. We present the results in
478   Table 5. The study shows that RFFs clearly help with reconstruction quality, both in reconstructing
479   a high-resolution video signal, and on a dataset of images. Interestingly, the reconstruction quality
480   drop from removing RFFs does not translate to downstream performance drop, where, in fact, the
481
      model without Fourier features is marginally better than the original.

      in MLPs, and while the performance of such methods is constantly increasing, it still leaves much
498

499
      Sajjadi et al., 2022; Chen & Wang, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; 2024; Wessels et al., 2024) that proposes
500   neural representations through conditional neural fields. Of those, Sajjadi et al. (2022); Zhang et al.
501   (2023); Wessels et al. (2024) have proposed set-latent conditional neural fields that condition the
502   signal through attention (Vaswani et al., 2017). Zhang et al. (2023) proposed 3DShape2VecSet, an
503   architecture that employs cross-attention and self-attention to encode shapes into sets of latent vectors
504   and decode them. Our method differs from this method, since it does not rely on cross-attention to
505   fully encode a coordinate in a set of latents. Instead, it employs self-attention, which allows for better
506   information propagation and enables the model to scale to multiple layers.
507
508   MLPs as graphs A few recent works (Kofinas et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024a;b; Nikolentzos et al.,
509
      2024; Kalogeropoulos et al., 2024) have explored the perspective of viewing neural networks as
      graphs and proposed methods that leverage the graph structure. Kofinas et al. (2024) focus on the task
510
      of processing the parameters of other neural networks and represent neural networks as computational
511
      graphs of parameters. Their method includes applications to downstream tasks on neural fields.
512   Lim et al. (2024b) investigate the impact of neural parameter symmetries, and introduce new neural
513   network architectures that have reduced parameter space symmetries. Nikolentzos et al. (2024) show
514   that MLPs can be formalized as GNNs with asynchronous message passing, and propose a model that
515   employs a synchronous message passing scheme on a nearly complete graph. Similar to this work,
516   we also use a complete graph and employ a synchronous message passing scheme. In contrast to this
517   work, we employ weight-sharing via self-attention and high-dimensional node features. Further, we
518   focus on neural field applications instead of tabular data, and explore conditioning via the hidden and
519   output embeddings.
520
521   6     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we presented NeoMLP, a novel architecture inspired by the principles of connectionism
524   and the graph perspective of MLPs. We perform message passing on the graph of MLPs, after
525   transforming it to a complete graph of input, hidden, and output nodes equipped with high-dimensional
526   features. We also employ weight-sharing through self-attention among all the nodes. NeoMLP is
527   a transformer architecture that uses individual input and output dimensions as tokens, along with
528   a number of hidden tokens. We also introduced new neural representations based on the hidden
529
      and output embeddings, as well as datasets of neural representations. Our method achieves state-of-
      the-art performance in fitting high-resolution signals, including multimodal audio-visual data, and
530
      outperforms state-of-the-art methods in downstream tasks on neural representations.
531
532
      Limitations Our ν-reps are subject to permutation symmetries, indicating that inductive biases can
533
      be leveraged to increase downstream performance. Namely, while the output embeddings are already
534   ordered, as they correspond to individual outputs, the hidden embeddings are subject to permutation
535   symmetries. Future work can explore more elaborate methods based on set neural networks, such as
536   Deep Sets (Zaheer et al., 2017), that exploit the inductive biases present in ν-reps. Further, the latent
537   codes used in ν-reps, namely the hidden and output embeddings, carry global information. Instilling
538   locality in latent codes can be useful for fine-grained downstream tasks, such as segmentation. Future
539   work can explore equivariant neural fields (Wessels et al., 2024), which would localize the latent
      codes by augmenting them with positions or orientations.

732   B      N EO MLP SYMMETRIES
733
734   Our ν-reps, and more specifically, the hidden embeddings, are subject to permutation symmetries.
735   Intuitively, when we permute two hidden embeddings from a randomly initialized or a trained
736   model, we expect the behaviour of the network to remain the same. In this section, we formalize
737   the permutation symmetries present in our method. NeoMLP is a function f : R(I+H+O)×D →
738   R(I+H+O)×D that comprises self-attention and feed-forward networks applied interchangeably
739   for a number of layers, following Equations (2) and (3). As a transformer architecture, it is a
740   permutation equivariant function. Thus, the following property holds: f (PX) = Pf (X), where P
741   is a permutation matrix, and X is a set of tokens fed as input to the transformer.
742
                                                     h                              i
                                                           I         H          O
      Now consider the input to NeoMLP: T(0) = {ii }i=1 , {hj }j=1 , {ok }k=1 , T(0) ∈ R(I+H+O)×D .
743
744   We look at two cases of permutations, namely permuting only the hidden neurons, and permuting only
745
      the output neurons. The permutation matrix for the first case, i.e. permuting only the hidden neurons,
      is P1 = II×I ⊕ PH×H ⊕ IO×O , where I is the identity matrix, PH×H is a permutation matrix, and
746
      ⊕ denotes the direct sum operator, i.e. stacking matrix blocks diagonally, with zero matrices in the
747
      off-diagonal blocks. Each P1 corresponds to a permutation π1 ∈ SH .
748
749   Applying this permutation to T(0) permutes only the hidden neurons:

752   Next, we apply NeoMLP on the permuted inputs. Making use of the equivariance property, the output
753   of the function applied to the permuted inputs is equivalent to the permutation of the output of the
754   function applied to the original inputs.
755
                                           f P1 T(0) = P1 f T(0)                                      (11)

      Since the network is only using the output tokens in the final step as an output of the network, the
757   overall behaviour of NeoMLP is invariant to the permutations of the hidden nodes.
758
759   We can follow the same principle to show that permuting the output nodes results in different outputs.
760
      The permutation matrix in this case is P2  = II×I ⊕ IH×H ⊕ PO×O . The equivariance property still
761
      holds, namely f P2 T(0) = P2 f T(0) . However, the output tokens are now used as the output
      of the network. This means that permuting the output tokens would result in permuting the output
762
      dimensions of a signal, which is clearly not equivalent to the original signal.
763
764   A corollary of the permutation symmetries is that if we start with a randomly initialized model,
765   apply a permutation on the hidden nodes to create another model, and then train the two models
766   independently, these two trained models would be identical up to the permutation of the hidden nodes.
767
      This observation is important for downstream tasks, as it shows the existence of equivalence classes
      that should be taken into account by the downstream models.

770   C       C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
771
772   While NeoMLP comfortably outperforms Siren in the task of fitting high-resolution signals, it is
773   also more computationally expensive. We quantitatively measure the computational complexity
774   of our method using the fvcore library3 . We evaluate on the “bikes” video signal, and use the
775   hyperparameters described in Appendix E. We report the FLOPs for 1 input (i.e. 1 coordinate) in
776   the forward pass. NeoMLP has 51.479 MFLOPs, out of which 17.83 MFLOPs correspond to the
777
      attention itself and 33.55 MFLOPs correspond to the FFNs. In the same setup, Siren (Sitzmann et al.,
      2020) has 3.15 MFLOPs.
778
779   Despite having a higher computational complexity compared to the baselines, NeoMLP can actually
780   fit high resolution signals faster, and does so while having a smaller memory footprint, since it can
781   make use of small batch sizes. Figure 5 shows the runtime of NeoMLP for fitting high-resolution
782   signals, compared to the baselines. The x-axis represents wall time in seconds and the y-axis
783
      represents the reconstruction quality (PSNR). Table 6 shows the corresponding GPU memory and
      batch size, along with the total runtime for fitting high resolution signals.
784
785   Finally, despite the large difference in FLOPs, the forward pass of NeoMLP is almost as fast as the
786   forward pass of Siren, considering the same batch size. Namely, we ran a full evaluation on the “bikes”
787   signal, on an Nvidia H100 GPU, using a batch size of 32,768. NeoMLP takes 139.74 seconds, while
788   Siren takes 131.01 seconds. NeoMLP, however, cannot fit larger batch sizes in memory, while Siren
789
      can fit as big as 1,048,576. With this batch size, Siren requires 79.18 seconds for a full evaluation.
