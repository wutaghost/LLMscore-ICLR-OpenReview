011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This work studies the matrix sensing (MS) problem through the lens of the Re-
014            stricted Isometry Property (RIP). It has been shown in several recent papers that
015            two different techniques of convex relaxations and local search methods for the
016
               MS problem both require the RIP constant to be less than 0.5 while most real-
               world problems have their RIPs close to 1. The existing literature guarantees a
017
               small RIP constant only for sensing operators having an i.i.d. Gaussian distribu-
018
               tion, and it is well-known that the MS problem could have a complicated land-
019            scape when the RIP is greater than 0.5. In this work, we address this issue and
020            improve the optimization landscape by developing two results. First, we show
021            that any sensing operator with a model not too distant from i.i.d. Gaussian has a
022            slightly higher RIP than i.i.d. Gaussian, and that its RIP constant can be reduced
023            to match the RIP constant of an i.i.d. Gaussian via slightly increasing the number
024            of measurements. Second, we show that if the sensing operator has an arbitrary
025            distribution, it can be modified in such a way that the resulting operator will act
026            as a perturbed Gaussian with a lower RIP constant. Our approach is a precon-
027
               ditioning technique that replaces each sensing matrix with a weighted sum of all
               sensing matrices. We numerically demonstrate that the RIP constants for different
028
               distributions can be reduced from almost 1 to less than 0.5 via the preconditioning
029
               of the sensing operator.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In this paper, we focus on an important class of problems in non-convex optimization and ma-
035
      chine learning, named matrix sensing. The goal of the matrix sensing problem is to recover
036   a low-rank matrix from a set of limited linear measurements. To be more specific, given m
037   sensing matrices A1 , . . . , Am ∈ Rn×n , we define the linear sensing operator A as A(M ) =
038   [⟨A1 , M ⟩, . . . , ⟨Am , M ⟩]T for all M . The matrix sensing problem is formulated as the following
039   non-convex optimization problem:
040
                                      1
041                            min      ∥A(M ) − b∥2     subject to rank(M ) = r.                        (1)
042
                             M ∈R n×n 2
043   where b = A(M ∗ ) is the observed vector, M ∗ is the unknown ground truth matrix, and r denotes
044   the rank of M ∗ . Since the matrix sensing problem for an arbitrary solution M ∗ (being a rectangular
045   matrix or a square sign indefinite matrix) can be converted to an expanded matrix sensing problem
046   whose solution is a symmetric and positive semidefinite matrix (Zhang et al., 2021), we assume that
047   M ∗ is positive semidefinite and symmetric without loss of generality.
048   The matrix sensing problem has a wide range of real-world applications in signal processing and
049   machine learning, such as the training of neural networks (Li et al., 2018), reconstruction of images
050   and videos (Fowler et al., 2012; Baraniuk et al., 2017), wireless sensor network (Razzaque et al.,
051   2013), and quantum computing (Shabani et al., 2011; Ayanzadeh et al., 2020). It has attracted
052   significant attention in recent years as it sheds light on a board range of non-convex optimization
053   problems, serving as a theoretical guarantee in deep learning theory (Li et al., 2018; Scarlett et al.,
      2022). The complexity of the matrix sensing problem lies in the low-rank structure that creates

057   To overcome the above-mentioned non-convexity, one line of research relaxes this problem into a
058

      rank constraint with a nuclear norm constraint. However, solving the SDP relaxation requires a
059
      large amount of calculations. Another popular way to deal with the low-rank constraint is the Burer-
060
      Monteiro (BM) factorization (Burer & Monteiro, 2003), which explicitly factorizes the low-rank
061   matrix M into the form M = XX ⊤ where X ∈ Rn×r (note that this factorization uses the fact that
062   M ∗ is positive definite and symmetric). Hence, the matrix sensing problem can be formulated as

066   With this natural reparametrization, the number of parameters reduces from O(n2 ) in M to O(nr)
067   in X, where r is usually close to 1. Problem (2) is unconstrained, and therefore simple first-order
068   methods, such as Gradient Descent (GD), can be applied to solve the problem. However, the factor-
069   ized problem (2) is highly non-convex and N P-hard to solve. There have been extensive studies on
070   the optimization landscape of the matrix sensing problem (Candes & Tao, 2010; Candès & Recht,
071   2012; Recht et al., 2010; Ge et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018), and it turns out that the success of both
072   SDP relaxation and local search methods relies on a condition named Restricted Isometry Property
073   (RIP), which is defined below.
074   Definition 1 (RIP (Candès & Recht, 2012)). Given a natural number s, the linear map A : Rn×n 7→
075   Rm is said to satisfy the Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) condition of rank s for a constant δ,
076   denoted as δs ∈ [0, 1), if the inequality
077
                                (1 − δs ) ∥M ∥2F ≤ ∥A(M )∥2 ≤ (1 + δs ) ∥M ∥2F                             (3)
078
079   holds for all matrices M ∈ Rn×n satisfying rank(M ) ≤ s.
080
081   Intuitively, the RIP is a condition guaranteeing that linear measurements approximately preserve
082
      the Euclidean geometry of low-rank matrices. Specifically, a sensing operator satisfies the RIP if
      it acts nearly as an isometry on the set of low-rank matrices, ensuring that the distances between
083
      these matrices are preserved after measurement. When δs = 0, solving the matrix sensing problem
084
      is trivial, while δs close to 1 implies a complicated landscape for the matrix sensing problem where
085   the number of local minima could be exponential (Yalçın et al., 2023). Note that the RIP constant is
086   not unique. If δs is an RIP constant, every number greater than δs is also an RIP constant.
087
088
      The RIP condition is crucial for the success of various recovery algorithms, as it underpins their
      ability to reconstruct the original matrix accurately from compressed measurements. Started by
089
      the convex relaxation approach, Recht et al. (2010) and Candès & Recht (2012) demonstrated that
090
      when the RIP constant satisfies the inequality δ5r ≤ 1/10, the SDP relaxation is exact, allowing
091   for the exact recovery of the ground truth M ∗ . Later, Bhojanapalli et al. (2016) examined the
092   factorized problem (2) and showed that δ2r ≤ 1/5 suffices to guarantee that all second-order critical
093   points for (2) correspond to the ground truth solution. Zhu et al. (2018) further established that
094   δ4r ≤ 1/5 is sufficient for the global recovery of the ground truth via a local search method. The
095   recent paper (Zhang et al., 2021) showed that δ2r < 1/2 is the tightest bound for guaranteeing such
096   global properties.
097   Through the lens of RIP, one can guarantee benign optimization landscape and convergence to global
098   optimality, solving the matrix sensing problem either using convex relaxations such as SDP or using
099   non-convex methods such as the BM factorization with a random initialization. Furthermore, when
100   the RIP constant is small, local search has a linear convergence rate for the factorized problem (2)
101   (Zheng & Lafferty, 2015; Lee & Stöger, 2023). Moreover, strict-saddle property holds if δ2r < 1/2 ,
102   and this result was developed for general low-rank optimization problems beyond matrix sensing (Bi
103   et al., 2022). While the bound δ2r < 1/2 is sharp , it is not satisfied for most real-world problems
104   except in special cases such as a class of isometric distributions.

      Given 0 < δ < 1 and 1 ≤ r ≤ m, it turns out that A is a nearly isometric random variable, with high
119   probability, i.e., δr (A) ≤ δ if m = Θ(rn/δ 2 ) (Recht et al., 2010; Candès & Plan, 2011). Indepen-
120   dent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) Gaussian entries with variance 1/m are nearly isometrically
121   distributed, and the literature of matrix sensing has heavily relied on the i.i.d. and Gaussian as-
122   sumptions to justify the use of RIP. However, in practice we often have no prior knowledge of the
123   distribution of the sensing matrices, and in addition the independence assumption is hardly satisfied.
124
      Motivated by this inconsistency and the importance of RIP, this paper offers theoretical insights
125   into deviations from nearly isometric distributions. We first study the case when the problem is not
126   Gaussian due to small perturbations and derive an upper bound on the change to the RIP constant in
127   terms of the distance of the distribution of the given operator from a Gaussian distribution. More-
128   over, we study whether an operator with an arbitrary distribution can be modified so that it acts as
129   a perturbed Gaussian for which the above result on its RIP constant can be applied. For the case
130   where the true distribution deviates significantly from normal distributions, we introduce a precon-
131   ditioning algorithm that replaces each sensing matrix with a weighted sum of all sensing matrices.
132   We discuss how this technique makes the resulting operator behave similarly to perturbed Gaussian
133
      distributions, leading to a reduction in the RIP constant and improving the optimization landscape.
134   The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we demonstrate the robustness of the RIP constant
135   to small perturbations to the sensing operator. We show that nearly-isometric measurements under a
136   modest perturbation continue to satisfy the RIP, thereby ensuring the reliable recovery of low-rank
137   matrices. This finding is significant as it broadens the applicability of matrix sensing techniques to
138   real-world scenarios by relaxing the restrictive Gaussian assumption.
139   Furthermore, we investigate the role of orthogonalization in enhancing the optimization landscape
140   of the matrix sensing problem. In Section 3, we show that the orthogonalization of the sensing
141   matrices can improve the RIP constant, making the landscape more favorable for efficient recov-
142   ery algorithms. To achieve this, we propose a novel preconditioning method that optimizes the
143   mixing of the measurements to reduce the RIP constant. We provide a theoretical analysis for the
144   proposed method, and empirically show that it is highly effective on various types of measurement
145
      distributions, including Poisson, uniform, and correlated Gaussian distributions. In particular, we
      demonstrate that the original RIP constants for these distributions could be close to 1 for which the
146
      SDP relaxation and local search methods would fail to work, while the preconditioning technique
147
      reduces the RIP to less than 0.5 so that both of these optimization methods can correctly solve the
148   modified problem.
149
150   By addressing the above two aspects, our work contributes to a deeper understanding of the matrix
151
      sensing problem with non-Gaussian models. We propose practical solutions to enhance recovery
      performance, paving the way for more robust and efficient applications in matrix sensing and be-
152
      yond.

261
262   In the previous section, we proved that small deviations from nearly isometrically distributed sens-
263   ing matrices will slightly increase the RIP constant. However, real-world sensing matrices often
264   have unknown probability distributions that cannot be approximated by Gaussian models, for which
265   several empirical results have shown that the RIP constant is often close to 1. To address this issue,
266
      we consider a sensing operator A coming from an arbitrary probability distribution and develop a

269   complexity of the matrix sensing problem is increased, e.g., via a tensor-based lifting technique.

525   4   C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   The results presented in this paper highlight several critical insights into the behavior of sensing
529   operators and their impact on the Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) constant. When dealing with a
530   nearly isometric operator perturbed by a sub-Gaussian term, the impact of deviation from the nearly
531
      isometric case can be effectively mitigated by increasing the number of measurements. Specifically,
      the RIP constant ensures that a benign optimization landscape can be preserved even in the pres-
532
      ence of perturbations to the sensing operator. Thus, even in the presence of perturbations, careful
533
      adjustment of the number of measurements provides a practical approach to deal with non-Gaussian
534   distributions. Our findings also demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that the proposed
535   preconditioning algorithm significantly improves the RIP constant for various distributions. A no-
536   table observation is that, after preconditioning, the RIP constant is nearly independent of the orig-
537   inal distribution. This finding simplifies practical implementations, as it eliminates the need for
538   distribution-specific assumptions about the sensing matrices. Practitioners can rely on the precondi-
539   tioned sensing matrices to provide consistent RIP performance, primarily governed by the values of
      r, m, and n.
