011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-to-3D generation by distilling pretrained large-scale text-to-image diffusion
014
               models has shown great promise but still suffers from inconsistent 3D geometric
               structures (Janus problems) and severe artifacts. The aforementioned problems
015
               mainly stem from 2D diffusion models lacking 3D awareness during the lifting.
016
               In this work, we present GeoDream, a novel method that incorporates explicit gen-
017            eralized 3D priors with 2D diffusion priors to enhance the capability of obtaining
018            unambiguous 3D consistent geometric structures without sacrificing diversity or
019            fidelity. Specifically, we first utilize a multi-view diffusion model to generate
020            posed images and then construct cost volume from the predicted image, which
021            serves as native 3D geometric priors, ensuring spatial consistency in 3D space.
022            Subsequently, we further propose to harness 3D geometric priors to unlock the
023            great potential of 3D awareness in 2D diffusion priors via a disentangled design.
024            Notably, disentangling 2D and 3D priors allows us to refine 3D geometric pri-
025
               ors further. We justify that the refined 3D geometric priors aid in the 3D-aware
               capability of 2D diffusion priors, which in turn provides superior guidance for
026
               the refinement of 3D geometric priors. Our numerical and visual comparisons
027
               demonstrate that GeoDream generates more 3D consistent textured meshes with
028            high-resolution realistic renderings (i.e., 1024 × 1024) and adheres more closely
029            to semantic coherence.
030
031   Diffusion models Saharia et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022); Ramesh et al. (2022) have signifi-
032   cantly advanced text-to-image synthesis. Inspired by their success, it is appealing to lift this success
033   from 2D to 3D because this achievement holds significant potential impacts on the modern game and
034   media industry. Template-based generators Chen et al. (2023a) and 3D native generative models Li
035   et al. (2023b); Wang et al. (2023c); Mo et al. (2023); Nichol et al. (2022); Jun & Nichol (2023)
036   provide a natural and direct approach to the lift. However, these methods usually show compelling
037   results for limited categories due to the lack of extensive 3D data. Recently, the Score Distillation
038   Sampling (SDS) Poole et al. (2022) and Variational Score Distillation (VSD) Wang et al. (2023d)
039
      have been introduced to optimize 3D representations such that images rendered from any view-
      points match the text-conditioned image distribution evaluated by a pretrained text-to-image (T2I)
040
      model. This is an exciting direction because it allows for generating 3D assets from any given text
041
      prompt, circumventing the need for any 3D data. Despite these methods yielding satisfactory results
042   on a wide range of geometrically symmetrical 3D shapes, empirical observations indicate that SDS
043   and VSD losses still suffer from inconsistent 3D geometric structures (Janus problems) Wikipedia
044   (2023) and severe artifacts Wang et al. (2023d); Shi et al. (2023b) with asymmetric geometry. This
045   is primarily due to the lack of 3D awareness in 2D diffusion models, which inherently makes the
046   lifting from 2D observations into 3D ambiguous.
047
      As a remedy, learning 3D priors from 3D datasets seems theoretically reasonable and correct. How-
048   ever, 3D data remains expensive and sparse compared to the plentifully available images. Therefore,
049   the most promising avenue Qian et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023b); Sun et al. (2023) presently is to
050   equip 2D diffusion priors with 3D priors learned from relatively limited 3D data, aiming to achieve
051   the best of both worlds. Recently, with the release of large-scale 3D datasets, Objaverse Deitke et al.
052   (2023b) and Objaverse-XL Deitke et al. (2023a), a few works Liu et al. (2023c); Li et al. (2023c);
053   Shi et al. (2023b); Ye et al. (2023) have attempted to finetune pre-trained 2D diffusion models using
      multi-view images rendered from 3D dataset. This involves obtaining multi-view images from the

      To resolve this issue, we introduce GeoDream, a novel method that incorporates explicit generalized
115   3D priors with 2D diffusion priors to enhance the capability of obtaining unambiguous 3D consis-
116   tent geometric structures, while maintaining diversity and high fidelity. Our contributions are listed
117   below. (i) In contrast to methods mentioned above that rely on consistency between multi-view pri-
118   ors, we propose to obtain 3D native priors in the 3D world space, which are well-suited to handle the
119   inherent lack of perfect consistency within the multi-view predicted priors, and naturally free from
120   inconsistencies caused by camera viewpoint transition. (ii) We justify that disentangling 3D and
121   2D priors is a potentially exciting direction for maintaining both the generalization of 2D diffusion
122   priors and the consistency of 3D priors. In other words, providing hints through 3D priors to unlock
123
      the great potential of 3D awareness in 2D diffusion priors, without the need for invasive finetune 2D
      diffusion models.

125   Specifically, we start by reconstructing cost volume as native 3D priors by aggregating the predicted
126   multi-view 2D images into 3D space. Such aggregation operations have been widely used in MVS-
127   based techniques Yao et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2022); Long et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023b),
128   which are known to be robust and generalized to provide valuable cues for geometric reasoning.
129
      We find that such operations are well-suited for handling imperfect and inconsistent multi-view
      predictions. The reason is that they involve multi-view information aggregation, which helps filter
130
      out inconsistent content to some extent, rather than dealing with each view individually. Foremost,
131
      we conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate that our proposed 3D priors adapt to multiple
132   views predicted by various off-the-shelf multi-view diffusion models, such as Zero123 Liu et al.
133   (2023c), MVDream Shi et al. (2023b) and Zero123++ Shi et al. (2023a). Moreover, we introduce a
134   critical viewpoint sampling strategy to promote the stability of the 3D priors. Despite recent attempts
135   to reconstruct cost volume as 3D priors, such as One2345 Liu et al. (2023b) and SyncDreamer Liu
136   et al. (2023d). However, they treat the cost volume as a fixed 3D prior. In contrast, we propose a
137   novel method that allows the integration of 2D diffusion priors and 3D priors in an optimizable way.
138   We conduct extensive experiments to investigate the respective roles of 2D diffusion priors and 3D
139   priors in 3D generation tasks. We hope that the incorporating design will bridge the gap between
140
      2D and 3D priors, contributing to a harmonious synthesis of both.
141   Specifically, we further propose incorporating 3D priors with 2D diffusion priors in a disentangled
142   solution. Existing multi-view diffusion priors are equipped with 2D diffusion priors in a coupled
143   way, including generating multiple views as supervision Liu et al. (2023c); Shi et al. (2023a) or
144   distilling the probability density as a loss Shi et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023c); Sun et al. (2023);
145   Qian et al. (2023) to compute gradients for optimizing 3D representations. Instead, we justify that
146
      leveraging the geometric clues provided by 3D priors can effectively unleash the great potential
      3D awareness capability inherent in 2D diffusion priors, referred to as ”disentangled design”. Very
147
      recent works have started to explore how to evoke 3D-aware ability in 2D diffusion by altering score
148
      functions Hong et al. (2023) or negative text prompts Armandpour et al. (2023). These efforts have
149   made surprising progress, yet the performance remains unstable regarding 3D consistency. Our
150   insight is that going through geometric priors to unlock the great potential of 3D awareness in 2D
151   diffusion is a promising direction that is both general and stable. Moreover, we rely solely on the
152   awakened 3D-aware capability of 2D priors to guide the optimization of Neural Implicit Surfaces
153   (NeuS) Wang et al. (2021) without the supervision of 3D priors, thereby avoiding compromising the
154   inherent advantages of 2D priors in terms of generalization and creativity. We show that 3D priors
155   can be further refined to boost rendering quality and geometric accuracy. The 2D diffusion priors
156   benefit from gradually evolved 3D priors, which in turn provide superior guidance for unleashing
157
      the 2D priors. Finally, we use DMTet Shen et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2023) to extract textured mesh
      from optimized NeuS for mesh fine-tuning. Unlike previous work Poole et al. (2022); Wang et al.

      3D Generation Guided by 2D Priors. Deep generative models have driven the field of 3D gen-
      eration. Some efforts utilize Variational Auto Encoders (VAEs) Kingma & Welling (2013) for tex-
180
      ture generation Henderson & Ferrari (2020); Henderson et al. (2020), while Generative Adversarial
181
      (GAN) Models Goodfellow et al. (2014) investigate 3D-aware GAN training Chan et al. (2022);
182   Deng et al. (2022). Thus far, diffusion models have exhibited relatively better generalizability and
183   training stability for diverse object generation compared to GANs and VAEs, and thus have gradu-
184   ally become recent focal points in 3D generation. Specifically, recent endeavors attempt to leverage
185   the potent 2D diffusion priors to aid 3D generation by coupling it with a 3D representation, such as
186   NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021), DMTNet Shen et al. (2021), or NeuS Wang et al. (2021), among oth-
187   ers, bypasses the necessity for extensive text-3D datasets for training 3D generative models. Such
188   methods involve various techniques, including score distillation sampling schedules like SDS Wang
189   et al. (2023a), SJC Poole et al. (2022), VSD Wang et al. (2023d) and ISM Liang et al. (2023)
190
      losses, which optimize the 3D representation by enhancing high likelihood evaluated by the 2D
      diffusion models. A coarse-to-fine training strategy Chen et al. (2023a) strengthens texture repre-
191
      sentation, decoupling geometric and texture aspects of 3D representation for finer optimization Lin
192
      et al. (2023), improving 3D representation Tang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023b). Although these
193   methods demonstrate the ability to generate photo-realistic and diverse 3D assets with user-provided
194   textual prompts, they are prone to the notorious 3D inconsistency issues (Janus problems) during
195   the lifting process due to their reliance on 2D diffusion models for training, which lack 3D knowl-
196   edge. Despite some current methods attempting to address 3D inconsistency by altering score func-
197   tions Hong et al. (2023) or negative text prompts Armandpour et al. (2023), performance remains
198   instability in terms of 3D consistency. In this work, we aim to explore the distinctive advantages
199   of incorporating explicit 3D priors with 2D priors, enabling the generation of highly detailed 3D
200   objects while remarkably mitigating 3D inconsistency issues.
201   3D Generation Guided by 3D Priors. Learning 3D priors from 3D datasets seems theoretically

205   using Tri-plane Shue et al. (2023) or feature grid Wang et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023d) encoding 3D
206   representations into the latent space. Additionally, OpenAI has explored models aiming to directly
207
      generate 3D formats using several million internal 3D shapes, such as point clouds Nichol et al.
      (2022) or neural radiance fields Jun & Nichol (2023). However, their generalizability to the scope
208
      of their 2D counterparts remains unverified, due to the relative sparsity of 3D data compared to the
209
      abundance of available 2D images. Consequently, the most promising avenue currently is to equip
210   2D diffusion priors with 3D priors learned from relatively limited 3D data, intending to achieve the
211   best of both worlds. Recently, with the release of a large-scale 3D dataset called Objaverse Deitke
212   et al. (2023b) and Objaverse-XL Deitke et al. (2023a), some work Liu et al. (2023c); Yang et al.
213   (2023); Liu et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023c); Shi et al. (2023b); Ye et al. (2023); Cao et al. (2023);

356   Baselines. We report our performance with the latest 3D generation methods, including Dream-
357   Fusion Poole et al. (2022), ProlificDreamer Wang et al. (2023d), MVDream Shi et al. (2023b),
358   GSGEN Chen et al. (2023b), Fantasia3D Chen et al. (2023a), Magic123 Qian et al. (2023) and
359   Wonder3D Long et al. (2023). Specifically, DreamFusion Poole et al. (2022), Fantasia3D Chen
360   et al. (2023a) and ProlificDreamer Wang et al. (2023d) adopt a similar approach to optimize 3D
361   representation through the score function of a 2D diffusion model, without intervening in 3D pri-
362   ors. We compare our results with these three methods, highlighting the distinct advantages of in-
363
      ferring 3D-consistent geometry and reducing artifacts by incorporating explicit 3D priors. Mean-
      while, MVDream Shi et al. (2023b) and Wonder3D Long et al. (2023) are very recent proposals to
364
      use multi-view consistency priors, which are derived from finetuned multi-view diffusion models
365
      trained on synthetic multi-view rendering image data. GSGEN Chen et al. (2023b), on the other
366   hand, addresses 3D inconsistency by initializing geometry with Point-E Nichol et al. (2022) gen-
367   erated shapes. By comparing these methods, we demonstrate that our introduced 3D priors offer
368   greater generality in challenging and uncommon cases and effectively prevent the generation of 3D
369   assets with lighting and texture styles similar to the synthetic rendered dataset. Magic123 Qian et al.
370   (2023) adopts a coupled approach, optimizing the 3D representation by using both 3D and 2D priors
371   as losses. The comparisons with Magic123 justify that the disentangling 3D and 2D priors allows
372   for the simultaneous harnessing of the generalization capabilities of 2D diffusion priors and the
373   3D consistency of 3D priors. In contrast, Magic123 requires careful design of the balance weights
374
      between 3D and 2D loss to avoid compromising between the two types of priors.
375   Implementations. For DreamFusion Poole et al. (2022), ProlificDreamer Wang et al. (2023d) and
376   Fantasia3D Chen et al. (2023a), we utilize their implementations in the ThreeStudio Guo et al.
377   (2023) library for comparison. For MVDream Shi et al. (2023b), GSGEN Chen et al. (2023b),
      Magic123 Qian et al. (2023) and Wonder3D Long et al. (2023), we use their official implementation.

398   Experiment Setup. We collected 35 prompts from various sources, including prompts from pre-
399
      vious work Shi et al. (2023b); Long et al. (2023) and real user inputs in the wild. To compre-
      hensively assess 3D consistency and semantic coherence, we intentionally selected more prompts
400
      indicating asymmetric geometric structures (80% of the collected prompts) and fewer prompts indi-
401
      cating symmetric geometric structures (20%). For a fair comparison, we render 3D assets generated
402   by our method and baselines by circling around the object at a default elevation and camera dis-
403   tance Guo et al. (2023), resulting in 120 images. We then evaluate the gap between the rendered
404   images and reference images generated by Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) based on the
405   collected prompts. We sample 10k points on the generated meshes to calculate 3D metric. To
406   demonstrate that our method is trivially adaptable to various multi-view diffusion models, we ran-
407   domly use either Zero123 Liu et al. (2023c) or MVDream Shi et al. (2023b) and Zero123++ Shi
408   et al. (2023a) for subsequent experiments. For the effect of different diffusions on the results, please
409   refer to the supplementary for details.
410   2D Metrics. Following DreamFusion Poole et al. (2022), we use CLIP R-score to measure seman-
411   tic coherence, defined as the probability of rendered images retrieving the correct caption among
412   collected prompts. Additionally, we choose FIDCLIP Kynkäänniemi et al. (2022) for image fidelity
413   measurement, which is calculated by the disparity in distribution between the rendered image and
414   reference image features, both encoded by CLIP ViT-B-32 Radford et al. (2021). We average the
415   metric over 120 rendered images for the quantitative comparison.
416   3D Metric. These metrics mentioned above are for measuring 2D images. Limited by rendering
417   angles and geometric self-occlusion, 2D metrics often struggle to assess 3D objects in 360 degrees
418   fully. To the best of our knowledge, no metrics have yet been introduced in text-to-3D tasks for
419   evaluating the semantic consistency of 3D assets. Therefore, to lift semantic coherence measure-
420   ment from 2D to 3D, we propose using Uni3D Zhou et al. (2024), the largest 3D presentation model
421   with one billion parameters under text-image-pointcloud alignment learning objective. We adopt a
422
      similar strategy to the CLIP R-score, except that we replace the image and text encoders in the CLIP
      with the point cloud and text encoders from the Uni3D, referred to as ”Uni3Dscore ”.
423
424   User study. 3D reconstruction tasks are typically evaluated of the error reconstructed shape com-
425   pared to the ground truth Ma et al. (2021). However, these metrics are difficult to apply to text-to-3D
426   tasks, as there is no ground truth. We additionally conduct a user study for geometry consistency
427   measurement. We collected responses from 20 participants. Each user is presented with a 360-
428
      degree perspective of objects and asked to select: whether the 3D object exhibits structural consis-
      tency. We then report the rate of consistency as an auxiliary metric, referred to as ”Cons. Rate”. We
429
      collected responses from 30 participants.

      ors is a potentially exciting direction, as it provides a flexible way to further refine 3D priors while
      maintaining the ability of 3D priors to unleash 2D diffusion priors.

520
      We significantly improve the rendering fidelity of images and the details of texture meshes, while
521
      greatly alleviating the notorious Janus problem. Specifically, our proposed disentangled solution
522   provides geometric cues to the distillation process and allows us to properly utilize the implicit 3D
523   prior present in the large-scale text-to-2D image diffusion models. Additionally, the disentangled
524   design offers a flexible way to optimize 3D priors gradually. The visual and numerical comparisons
525   with the state-of-the-art methods justify our effectiveness and show our superiority over the latest
526   methods in 3D generation.

      E    D EFINITION OF T HE JANUS P ROBLEM
847
848
      We explain in further detail the definition of the Janus problem (3D inconsistency), which refers to a
849   phenomenon that the learned 3D representation, instead of presenting the 3D desired output, shows
850   multiple canonical views of an object in different directions Wikipedia (2023); Armandpour et al.
851   (2023). For instance, when the given prompt indicates an asymmetric geometric structure, such as
852   a person or an animal, the generated 3D asset has multiple faces but lacks complete and correct
853   back views. In contrast, when the given prompt indicates a symmetric structure, such as a cake or a
854   hamburger, which does not have strictly defined back views, issues of 3D inconsistency typically do
855   not arise. Therefore, when calculating the subjective metric, geometrically symmetric 3D assets do
856   not suffer from 3D inconsistency by default.
857
