009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper explores the potential of leveraging large language models (LLMs) as
012            low-level action planners capable of executing long-horizon tasks based on nat-
013            ural language instructions. Although LLMs can act as the “brain” of robots by
014            excelling in high-level task planning, they are not yet capable of directly guid-
015            ing the “body” to execute low-level motion plans. This limitation stems from a
016
               communication gap between the “brain” and the “body”. Specifically, LLMs lack
               access to rich spatial semantic information from the robot’s real-time observations,
017
               hindering their ability to generate precise and actionable low-level plans. To ad-
018
               dress this, we propose a unified framework that bridges high-level and low-level
019            planning by establishing an efficient communication interface between LLMs and
020            robots. Our insight is to formulate the task as playing chess with LLMs. We
021            map the room into a semantic chessboard, which we call Room to Chessboard
022            (R2C). Each grid represents the position and size of objects inside the room. We
023            find that chessboard is succinct enough for LLMs to conduct semantic searches
024            with global view of the room. Also, the chessboard is informative enough to
025            convey detailed environmental state for LLMs to predict executable low-level ac-
026            tions. Additionally, we enhance decision-making through a Chain-of-Thought
027
               (CoT) paradigm, improving LLMs’ interpretability and action reasoning. We im-
               plement R2C using both fine-tuned open-source LLMs and closed-source models
028
               like GPT-4, and demonstrate its efficacy on the challenging ALFRED benchmark.
029
               Our results show that with communication based on chessboard, LLMs can serve
030            as effective low-level action planners, and can generalizes well to different set-
031            tings and open-vocabulary robotic planning tasks. View the demos on our project
032            page: https://anonymous4cv.github.io/Room2Chessboard/.

037   The pursuit of general embodied agents focuses on developing robust systems that capable of un-
038   derstanding natural language commands to meet a wide range of human requirements. Traditional
039
      robotic learning methods have shown success in executing complex embodied tasks. However, they
      often face difficulties in generalizing to unseen environments or novel tasks due to their dependence
040
      on task-specific training data and rigid planning mechanisms. Recently, Large Language Models
041
      (LLMs) have emerged as promising candidates for enhancing embodied agents. Leveraging vast
042   amounts of training data, LLMs exhibit strong generalization abilities across various domains. This
043   makes them particularly suitable for tasks requiring flexible reasoning and decision-making in com-
044   plex, free-form settings.
045
      Several pioneering works Huang et al. (2022a); Driess et al. (2023); Song et al. (2023); Huang et al.
046
      (2022b) have explored the potential of LLMs as the “brain” of the embodied systems. Their strong
047
      generalization enables them to apply knowledge across diverse scenarios, effectively managing a
048   wide range of embodied tasks. However, most existing LLM-based agents Huang et al. (2022a);
049   Ahn et al. (2022); Song et al. (2023) primarily focus on high-level task planning, where LLMs de-
050   compose long-horizon tasks, e.g., “bring me an egg” into subgoals such as “go to egg” → “take
051   it back”. Translating these subgoals into executable low-level actions still delegated to APIs such
052   as low-level policy networks Chaplot et al. (2020); Jang et al. (2022); Kalashnikov et al. (2021)
053   trained on robotic trajectory data or deterministic algorithms like Sethian (1999); Dijkstra (2022).
      Nevertheless, while LLMs possess extensive world knowledge, they lack the spatial awareness of

081   (2022); Song et al. (2023), which can reduce the overall efficiency of these frameworks in real-world

084   The core barrier Song et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023) to unleashing the potential of LLMs lies in the
085   ineffective communication between the LLM (brain) and the Robot (body). On one hand, the Robot
086   can hardly convey spatial information from the environment to the LLM, leaving the LLM devoid
087   of decision-making grounds. On the other hand, the LLM struggles to efficiently communicate
088   low-level decisions to the Robot, resulting in the Robot relying solely on external navigation APIs.
089   Therefore, there is a necessity to establish a “common language” between LLM and the Robot — A
090   platform to deliver sufficient environment information and low-level plans.
091   We propose a novel Room to Chessboard (R2C) framework, which transforms complex embodied
092   planning tasks into a chess-like game. As shown in Fig.1, R2C utilizes a grid-based chessboard as
093   an effective communication platform between the robot and the LLM. On one side, the robot can
094   continuously maps its observations onto the chessboard, which contains essential information such
095   as object semantics, scene layout, and object size, while avoiding information overload. On the
096   other side, the LLM is unlocked as a low-level action planner, guiding the robot through explainable
097   “moves” to complete the task.
098   Specifically, R2C realizes high-level and low-level planning in a unified framework. The LLM
099   first decomposes a long-horizon task into a sequence of subgoals. Each subgoal is then tackled
100   during the low-level planning stage. At this stage, we introduce an Environment Filter to maintain
101   the task-aware environment information onto a compact chessboard. Since the chessboard grid
102   size is calibrated to match the robot’s step length, the LLM can perform low-level planning on the
103   chessboard by predicting the robot’s next position. To further enhance LLM’s understanding of the
104
      game, we formalize a Chain of Thought Decision (CoT-D) task for LLM to enhance its overall
      spatial reasoning and decision-making capabilities.
105
106   To construct a chessboard representation that aligns with the complexity of the environment while
107   remaining manageable for LLMs, we design an Environment Filter to abstract the environment.
      Initially, new observations are transformed into a detailed 3D semantic map. However, such high-

      In R2C framework, LLM needs robust long-context understanding capabilities to comprehend the

121   We test our R2C framework on the comprehensive ALFRED Shridhar et al. (2020) benchmark,

123   and fine-tune open-source LLMs using our novel CoT-D tasks. R2C achieves state-of-the-art (SoTA)
124   performance among LLM-based methods. Additionally, LLMs trained with CoT-D show strong spa-
125   tial perception and task-planning capabilities. Moreover, our low-level action planner demonstrates
126   strong generalization across open-vocabulary tasks, showcasing the versatility and robustness of the
127   R2C framework in complex environments.
128   The main contributions of this paper include:

139   2.1   TASK P LANNING I N ROBOTICS
140
      Task planning in robotics Shridhar et al. (2020); Puig et al. (2018) involves generating a sequence
141
      of actions for robots to execute in the environment to achieve a specific goal. In real-world ap-
142   plications, the instructions are typically complex, resulting in long-horizon tasks that encompass
143   a variety of embodied activities, such as navigation Anderson et al. (2018a); Gu et al. (2022) and
144   object interaction Levine et al. (2018).
145
      Early approaches Pashevich et al. (2021); Suglia et al. (2021) simply integrate visual and textual
146
      inputs to generate contextually appropriate action sequences. Besides, one widely used approach is
147
      reinforcement learning (RL) Majumdar et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2019); Tan & Bansal (2019); Hong
148   et al. (2020). Although the above end-to-end methods have good performance, they rely entirely on
149   instruction-trajectory training data, resulting in poor generalization to unknown environment Min
150   et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2023); Song et al. (2023).
151
      Therefore, some recent approaches Min et al. (2021); Blukis et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2023); Inoue &
152
      Ohashi (2022) break down the complex task into the multiple modules to reduce the high data cost.
153
      FILM Min et al. (2021) propose a framework with four submodules including language processing,
154   semantic mapping, semantic search, deterministic policy. However, they rely on task prior like
155   template-based instruction analysis and try using deterministic or a pre-trained low-level controller,
156   which make them struggle in adapting to novel robotic tasks.

205   2.3    S CENE R EPRESENTATIONS
206
207   Scene representation is crucial for embodied tasks, particularly when using LLMs as planners.
208   Topology graphs Anderson et al. (2018b); Ku et al. (2020) are often used for navigation but limit
209   movement to predefined node connections, reducing their practicality in real-world applications.
210   Object-centric scene graphs Gu et al. (2023); Rana et al. (2023) offer object-relative positions but
211   lack the spatial details needed for low-level motion planning.
212   In contrast, our proposed chessboard representation incorporates both physical spatial and semantic
213   information, making it suitable for a wide range of navigation tasks. As an abstraction of the seman-
214   tic map Min et al. (2021); Lu et al. (2023), this grid-based chessboard balances conciseness with
215   semantic richness, preserving essential details like object semantics, scene layout, and size, while
      avoiding the complexity of high-dimensional data.

      3     M ETHODS
217
218   We aim to unlock LLMs as low-level action planner that can follow the instructions to solve long-
219   horizon tasks. We first introduce our newly designed Room to Chessboard framework in Sec.3.1,
220   and then illustrate the Chain-of-Though Decision (CoT-D) fine-tuning task formulation in Sec.3.2.
221
222   3.1     ROOM TO C HESSBOARD
223
224   Previous works, such as LLM-Planner Song et al. (2023) and SayCan Ahn et al. (2022), rely solely
225
      on LLMs to parse instructions and decompose tasks into high-level subgoals. The execution of
      each subgoal is then handled by a predefined controller that generates a sequence of low-level ac-
226
      tions. In contrast, we introduce the Room to Chessboard (R2C) framework (Fig. 2), which enables
227
      LLMs to perform both High-Level Planning (HLP) and Low-Level Planning (LLP) within a unified
228   framework.
229
230   First, the LLM conducts HLP, processing step-by-step instructions into a sequence of subgoals.
231
      Each subgoal is then handled at the LLP stage. At each timestep, an egocentric RGB-D image is
      processed by an Environment Filter to generate a chessboard abstraction. The updated chessboard
232
      is subsequently converted into object occupancy coordinates, which are fed to the LLM. Acting like
233
      a chess player, the LLM predicts the robot’s next move on the chessboard, guiding it to navigate or
234   interact with the environment.
235
236   3.1.1    H IGH -L EVEL P LANNING (HLP)
237
238   At the beginning of an episode, the LLM processes the natural language instruction L into a sequence
239   of high-level subgoals, denoted as G = [G1 , G2 , ..., GK ]. Each subgoal Gk is a tuple (Action,
240   Object), where Action ∈ AH is a primitive action chosen from the set of navigation action
241
      (GOTO) or interaction actions (e.g., PICKUP, PUT). The Object refers to the semantic class of the
      interacted object (e.g., SAFE, CD). These subgoals are executed sequentially during the LLP stage.
242
243
244
      3.1.2    E NVIRONMENT F ILTER
245   Since the environment is partially observed, the agent has never explored the entire environment
246   and must construct a map based on its own observations online. At each timestep, the Environment
247   Filter F (ot , Gk ) takes the new observations ot as input to filter out the goal-related environment
248   information conditioned on the current subgoal Gk . This filtered information is then updated on a
249   dynamically updated semantic chessboard B to represent the current state of the environment. The
250   computation of F involves two parts: semantic mapping and map to chessboard.
251   Semantic mapping. We first build an online semantic map of the room, inspired by prior work
252   Min et al. (2021). At each timestep t, the agent receives an egocentric RGB-D observation ot =
253   {Irgb , Idpt }. An off-the-shelf instance segmentation model processes Irgb , and combined with Idpt ,
254   the observation is converted into a point cloud, with each point labeled with predicted semantic
255   categories. This 3D point cloud is then voxelized and flattend along the Z-axis to form the 2D
256   semantic map M. The resulting semantic map is represented as an allocentric (C + 2) × M ×
257   M binary grid, where C is the number of object categories while two additional channels denote
258
      obstacles and explored areas in each cell. Here, M represents the map resolution.
259   Map to chessboard. We further abstract the map into a compact chessboard. Map to Chessboard
260   (M2C) function consists of two cascaded kernels. The first kernel is a dilation kernel Kdila , to
261   prevent collisions between the agent and obstacles. Similar to the dilation algorithm in image pro-
262   cessing Serra (1982), it expands all occupied pixels of obstacles (including objects) outward by δ
263   in the map. The second kernel is a max pooling kernel Kpool , which performs    max
                                                                                         pooling on the
264   map. Then the map is down-sampled from size M to size W , where W = D          ω and ω is the grid
265   size, which is calibrated to match the length of agent’s step.
266   Next, we aggregate this map with multiple object layer into a unified single-layer chessboard. To
267   address the many-to-one projection problem, where overlapping objects might appear in the same
268   grid, e.g., “apples on a table”, we introduce a Goal-aware Aggregate function A(.). This function
269   prioritizes both the relevance of the current subgoal, i.e., the target object Object in current subgoal
      Gk , and the size of the object. The most relevant and smallest objects are placed at the top layer,

      and then we merge object layers from bottom to top to produce the final chessboard. The overall

      3.1.3    L OW-L EVEL P LANNING (LLP)
277
278
      The chessboard filtered out from the Environment Filter provides compact yet sufficient environ-
279   mental state information for LLM. We then simulate a “chess game ” between the LLM and the
280   robot to perform low-level planning. The LLP task is formulated as a single-step generation task for
281   the LLM. As shown in Fig. 2, various state information is collected, including current subgoal Gk ,
282   chessboard state U, action history Q and game rules R. Our prompt system P organizes this data
283   and feeds it into the LLM to generate the next position prediction u. Note that LLP is invoked only
284   for navigation subgoals, as interaction subgoals in ALFRED can be handled by predefined low-level
285   actions once the agent reaches the visible range of the target object.
286   Chessboard state. To translate the chessboard state for the LLM, we convert it into textual object
287   occupancy coordinate sets. The chessboard coordinate system originates from the upper left corner,
288   with the X-axis pointing down and the Y-axis to the right. For each object on the chessboard, we
289   gather a set of occupancy coordinates Utc = {xi , yi }, where c ∈ [1, C + 2] and xi , yi ∈ [1, W ]. The
290   agent’s current position is the previous step’s target position wt−1 , and the initial agent position w0
291   is predefined in the benchmark.
292   Game Rules. To assist the LLM in planning, we define key game rules R: 1) Basic inputs, including
293   chessboard dimensions W , maximum steps T , and maximum failures F ; 2) Action space, simplified
294   to adjacent grids in four directions (up, down, left, right), i.e., the robot is only allowed to move 1
295   block at a time, due to the limited spatial reasoning ability of current LLMs; 3) Collision rules,
296   where grids occupied by objects or obstacles are considered illegal moves.
297   Action history. We maintain a queue Q with a fixed length τ to store recent successful movement
298   coordinates, providing the LLM with contextual information about previous actions.
299
300
      At each step t, the prompt system combines all this information and feeds it to the LLM, which
      simulates a chess player to decide the next move. The LLP models a policy π, defined as:
301
302
303                                        ut = π(P(Gk , Ut , R, Qt )),                                    (2)
304
305   where ut = (xt , yt ) is the predicted next position on the chessboard. According to the defined
306   action space, the available next positions are restricted to the adjacent grids in four directions xt+1 ∈
307   [xt − 1, xt + 1] and yt+1 ∈ [yt − 1, yt + 1], with xt+1 , yt+1 ∈ [1, W ].
308   Since the chessboard grid size is calibrated to match the robot’s step length, the predicted adja-
309   cency target position ut can be directly converted to a sequence of executable low-level actions
310   a = {ai }, ai ∈ Anav in the real-world environment. For example, in the ALFRED benchmark,
311   Anav includes (MoveForward, 0.25m), (TurnLeft, 90◦ ) and (TurnRight, 90◦ ). Based on
312   the predicted target position, the movement in any of the four directions can be converted into the
313   corresponding action sequence (e.g., TurnLeft and MoveForward to move to the left adjacent
314
      grid). Additionally, if the output wt is within the visible range of the target object OBJECT in the
      current subgoal, the system moves on to the next subgoal. See more details of the complete R2C

      planning pipeline in pseudo code Algo.1 in appendix.

319
320   To enhance the decision-making capabilities of LLMs in our chess game, we introduce an inter-
321   pretable fine-tuning paradigm with two key features: 1) simultaneous training of high-level Task
322   Decomposition (TD) and low-level Chain of Thought Decision (CoT-D) tasks to unify HLP and
323   LLP in within a single LLM, and 2) a Chain-of-thought Decision (CoT-D) process composed of
      four sub-parts are designed to enhance LLM’s rule comprehension and spatial reasoning abilities.

355   The specific content of the four sub-tasks will be introduced below. Examples can be found in Fig.2,
356   with details available in the appendix.
357
      Key Information Extraction: Due to the intricate information contained within the chessboard,
358
      when textualized, it becomes a lengthy document with substantial information. Considering
359
      that LLM often encounters issues such as context loss when comprehending long texts, this
360   task is designed to train LLM in processing task-related long texts and extracting key infor-
361   mation. This task requires LLM to extract relevant information about the current coordinates
362   and target objects based on the chessboard information inputs. The form used in data annota-
363   tion is as follows: The current position of the agent is [COORDS], and the
364   target is [OBJECT NAME], which is located at [COORDS SET].
365
      Direction Judgment: The conversion from a chessboard grid image to text is based on a two-
366   dimensional Cartesian coordinate system, and all spatial relationship understanding relies on a good
367   coordinate system understanding. Despite explaining the rationale for establishing this coordi-
368   nate system in the instruction using prompts like Establish a coordinate system with
369   the top left grid as (1,1), testing has shown that LLM still frequently misunderstands
370   our settings. This could be attributed to the length of the text and the scarcity of spatial reasoning
371   tasks incorporated during the pre-training of contemporary LLM. To ensure LLM’s accurate com-
372   prehension of the chessboard grid coordinate system, this task requires LLM to judge the direction
373   between the target and the current coordinates. The format employed in data annotation is as fol-
374
      lows: the target is at..., which is on the [DIRECTION] of the agent.
375   Target Prediction:The locations where different categories of objects appear often have priors. For
376   example, sofas are likely to appear opposite the television. Therefore, we aim for LLM to develop
377   the capability of predicting target locations. This can minimize the ineffective or inefficient explo-
      ration process for the robot to find target objects, thereby improving the efficiency of the system

378
      in completing embodied tasks. The expression utilized in data annotation is: Based on the
379   chessboard analysis, the target is likely located near [COORDS].
380
381   Selection Analysis:The tasks outlined above will enhance LLM’s comprehension of the spa-
382
      tial relationships within the chessboard and the goals of the overall embodied task. Based
      on this, we require LLM to analyze all possible next positions according to the rules of the
383
      chessboard. Throughout this analysis process, utilizing the aforementioned analysis as a foun-
384
      dation, LLM will provide analysis for each choice. The specific expression in the anno-
385   tation is: [COORDS]: This position is on the [DIRECTION] of the agent,
386   [Reason]. The [Reason] part is derived from annotations generated by GPT-4 based on
387   the agent’s current state and the correlation between the choice and the correct orientation.
388   For example: Objects in this direction have already been discovered;
389   we should head to areas that have not been explored.

392   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
393
394   Benchmark. We evaluate our method on the challenging ALFRED Shridhar et al. (2020) bench-
395   mark, which includes 7 types of long-horizon tasks across 207 unique environments and 115 dif-
396   ferent object types. In our experiments, we adhere to the benchmark’s settings, including low-level
397   actions, maximum agent steps, and failure limits.
398   Chessboard settings. The physical room size D is set to 16m, which is set to be the approximate
399   maximum room size, and the grid size ω is the length of the agent step, 0.25m. Thus, the size of the
400   semantic map and the chessboard is M = 320 and W = 64 separately. However, to represent more
401   complex environment, one can choose a more fine-grained chessboard. We show such demo in the
402   appendix6.3.
403   Model settings. For the zero-shot R2C, we use the public GPT-4-turbo API OpenAI (2023) without
404   any example. Considering the testing cost, we randomly sampled a subset of size 100 covering all 7
405   types of tasks strictly according to the task distribution of ALFRED. For fine-tuning, we collected a
406   total of 264,915 data samples for fine-tuning, including 70,000 samples for the Task Decomposition
407   tasks and 194,915 samples for the Chain-of-Thought Decision tasks. All data collection was based
408   solely on the training set of ALFRED. During training, we conduct full-parameter fine-tuning on
409   both the Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 model Jiang et al. (2023) and the Llama-7B-Chat model Touvron
410   et al. (2023) using all the data. Both models were trained for only one epoch. We conduct all fine-
411
      tuning experiments using 4 NVIDIA H100 GPUs, and all evaluations are performed on 4 NVIDIA
      A40 GPUs. The codes will be released after the paper is accepted.

463   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
464
465   We performed an ablation study to analyze the impact of different R2C modules, as shown in Tab.2.
466
      Our base model uses raw RGB-D frames with pre-trained Mask-RCNN for instance segmentation,
      ensuring a fair comparison with previous works Pashevich et al. (2021), Min et al. (2021). Ground
467
      truth segmentation (GT Seg.) significantly improves performance, highlighting segmentation as a
468
      bottleneck, especially in simulation environments where domain gaps between simulated and real-
469   world data are pronounced.
470
471   Using ground truth subgoals (GT Goal) also boosts SR, revealing the impact of ambiguity in natural
472
      language instructions. Annotator confusion between categories like desk lamps and floor lamps can
      hinder task decomposition, though LLMs’ strong generalization helps mitigate this.
473
474   Finally, removing the CoT-D framework, which forces the model to directly output position coor-
475   dinates without rationale, results in a significant performance drop, , especially in unseen scenes.
476   This highlights the importance of fine-tuning with CoT-D subtasks for better spatial reasoning and
477   action planning. Ablating individual subtasks is challenging due to their interdependence. We ablate
478
      removing the Selection Analysis part, where decisions are made without analyzing options and it
      causes a noticeable performance decline.

520   4.5   E XPLORATION ON O PEN - VOCABULARY TASK
521
522   While tasks in datasets like ALFRED are predefined and limited to 7 templates with fixed execution
523   orders, real-world demands are far more diverse. Standard instructions such as “walk around the
524   table” render high-level LLM planners relying on navigation APIs ineffective, as there is no cus-
525
      tomized API for such tasks. We refer to these as open-vocabulary embodied tasks. In contrast, the
      R2C framework equips LLMs to directly make low-level action plans, enabling them to adaptively
526
      handle a wide range of tasks. As shown in Fig.4, we test open-vocabulary tasks with GPT-version
527
      R2C, demonstrating superior performance and highlighting the importance of integrating low-level
528   planning for real-world applications.
529
530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
      We introduce the Room to Chessboard (R2C) framework, which map the complex room into a
532
      chessboard as a communication platform between the LLM and the robot, unlocking the LLM as
533
      low-level planner to directly guide the robot adaptively finish the embodied tasks. To address the
534   spatial reasoning tasks on chessboard, we design a CoT fine-tuning framework that enhances LLMs’
535   ability to make interpretable low-level decisions. Experiments show that R2C outperforms existing
536   LLM-based high-level planners, even in zero-shot settings, and allows 7B models to surpass GPT-
537   4. Furthermore, R2C enables LLMs to tackle open-vocabulary tasks where API-based frameworks
538   fall short. However, R2C still faces challenges, such as handling very large scenes. Future work
539   will focus on optimizing R2C for larger environments and exploring its potential for various open-
      vocabulary tasks.

      6.5   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF GPT-4 E XPERIMENTS
830
      In all our GPT-4 experiments on the ALFRED benchmark, we use the official API without any
831
      in-context examples. The specific model employed is gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09.
832
833   For subgoal decomposition, we approach this as a translation problem. The central concept involves
834   translating noisy high-level plan annotations into a subgoal format, structured as a subgoal verb with
835   corresponding subgoal objects. Each high-level plan annotation is directly mapped to a subgoal
836
      expression.
837   In the prompt, we initially inform GPT-4 of the format and limitations of the subgoal expres-
838   sion, specifically the subgoal verb and objects. The subgoal verb is restricted to a predefined
839   set: [GotoLocation, PickupObject, PutObject, CoolObject, HeatObject,
840   CleanObject, SliceObject, ToggleObject]. For specific verbs like HeatObject, we
841   clarify their usage. The subgoal objects are task-dependent; for each task, we maintain a list of rel-
842
      evant large and small objects, and GPT-4 is restricted to using only these listed items to predict the
      subgoal objects. We also provide GPT-4 with the task description and the high-level plan annotations
843
      in a list format, organized in chronological order of subgoals.
844
845   Finally, we define the output format for GPT-4 using a static example, employing a “—” to separate
846   subgoal sequences. Upon receiving GPT-4’s response, we process the reply to extract the subgoal
847   list. This involves using the “—” delimiter in the output format to segment the sequence into a list,
848
      followed by subgoal cleaning. This cleaned list then guides the low-level actions.
849   For the prompts, we first ensure that GPT-4 understands the rules of a chessboard game, explaining
850   the board size, coordinate system, and terms to describe spatial relations: left, right, up, down,
851   leftup, rightup, leftdown, and rightdown. We then convert the current state of the chessboard into
852   text, which includes object names and their occupied coordinates. We specify that GPT-4 cannot
853   move to coordinates occupied by an object and can only travel one block at a time.
854   After setting up the chessboard game, we provide GPT-4 with all available information including
855   the task description, current subgoal, current position, and available positions. The task description
856   aligns with the current subgoal format. The candidates for available positions are the blocks im-
857   mediately adjacent (up, down, left, right) to the current position, ensuring no objects occupy these
858   blocks.
859   Finally, we instruct GPT-4 on its tasks and how to solve problems using a chain-of-thought approach.
860   Specifically, we ask GPT-4 to verbalize its reasoning, analyzing the spatial relationship between
861   the target and current positions, and between the current position and available positions. After
862   reasoning, we prompt GPT-4 to indicate its next desired position and analyze whether the subgoal
863   is achieved, thereby enhancing its understanding of the subgoals and the overall task. The output is
      formatted in JSON with keys for “reason”, “next position”, and “subgoal done”. Importantly, we
