009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In autonomous driving, accurate motion prediction is essential for safe and effi-
012            cient motion planning. To ensure safety, planners must rely on reliable uncertainty
013            information about the predicted future behavior of surrounding agents, yet this
014            aspect has received limited attention. This paper addresses the so-far neglected
015            problem of uncertainty modeling in trajectory prediction. We adopt a holistic
016            approach that focuses on uncertainty quantification, decomposition, and the in-
017
               fluence of model composition. Our method is based on a theoretically grounded
               information-theoretic approach to measure uncertainty, allowing us to decompose
018
               total uncertainty into its aleatoric and epistemic components. We conduct ex-
019
               tensive experiments on the nuScenes dataset to assess how different model ar-
020            chitectures and configurations affect uncertainty quantification and model robust-
021            ness. Our analysis thoroughly explores the uncertainty quantification capabilities
022            of several state-of-the-art prediction models, examining the relationship between
023            uncertainty and prediction error in both in- and out-of-distribution scenarios, as
024            well as robustness in out-of-distribution.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In a machine learning driven Autonomous Driving (AD) stack, motion prediction connects the up-
      stream task of environment perception with the downstream task of ego-motion planning (Hu et al.,
030
      2023). The role of a motion predictor is to infer the future motion of traffic agents relevant to the ego,
031
      ensuring safe and efficient progress toward a goal (Hagedorn et al., 2024). To achieve this, a predic-
032   tor must tackle several challenges such as imperfect perception, complex interactions between traffic
033   agents, as well as the multitude of distinct potential actions each agent might undertake, motivated
034   by different goals. Such challenges drive the need to consider the problem in a probabilistic manner
035   and incorporate uncertainty into prediction outputs. Accurately quantifying prediction uncertainty
036   is essential for ensuring interpretability and building trust in the overall system.
037
      In the AD community, the future motion of surrounding traffic agents is often modeled in the form
038   of trajectories. Thus, probabilistic trajectory prediction involves capturing a distribution p(y|x, D)
039   of future trajectories y conditioned on contextual data x and a dataset D. Contextual data x usually
040   contains past trajectories of surrounding agents and map information. There are different strategies
041   for capturing this highly multi-modal distribution owing to the distinct actions or goals. Some meth-
042   ods attempt to directly predict the modes of the distribution along with their associated weights (Gao
043   et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021; Deo et al., 2022). Others use a parametric mixture distribution, such as
044   a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), where the modes correspond to the predicted trajectories (Tol-
045   staya et al., 2021; Varadarajan et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Look et al., 2023). Alternatively, gener-
046
      ative trajectory prediction models use well-known autoencoder or diffusion architectures to model
      latent variables and draw trajectory samples (Salzmann et al., 2020; Seff et al., 2023; Janjoš et al.,
047
      2023a; Jiang et al., 2023).
048
049   The majority of approaches for modeling the distribution of future trajectories, p(y|x, D), in AD
050   rely on neural networks. They are often underspecified by the available data, meaning that no single
051   parameter configuration is favored. When considering uncertainty in the model parameters, the
052
      predictive distribution (Kapoor et al., 2022; MacKay, 1992) over future trajectories is computed as
                                   Z                             Z

      where W represents the neural network weights, and p(W|D) represents the posterior distribution.
055   The predictive distribution represents a Bayesian model average, meaning that instead of relying
056   on a single hypothesis with a specific set of parameters, it considers all possible parameter config-
057   urations, weighted by their posterior p(W|D). This marginalization process removes the reliance
058   on a single weight configuration in the predictive distribution, resulting in better calibration and
059   accuracy compared to traditional training methods (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020). Since the exact pos-
060   terior is often intractable, various approximations q(W) have been developed, such as variational
061   inference (Graves, 2011), Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), Laplace approximation (Ritter et al.,
062   2018), deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017), or Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
063
      methods (Welling & Teh, 2011).
064   Despite many successful approaches of approximating the posterior distribution, the AD prediction
065   community has not yet tried to quantify or to decompose the uncertainty of trajectory prediction
066   models in a theoretically principled manner (Wilson, 2020). This gap is notable, especially consid-
067   ering the potential benefits of decomposing the uncertainty. The total uncertainty can be decom-
068   posed into two types: aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty (Wimmer et al., 2023; Hüllermeier, 2021).
069
      Aleatoric uncertainty represents the inherent variability within the data, such as the equal likelihood
      of a vehicle turning left or right at a T-junction. This type of uncertainty cannot be reduced, even
070
      with more data. In contrast, epistemic uncertainty arises from the lack of knowledge or information
071
      and can be reduced by collecting more data (Wimmer et al., 2023). Knowledge of epistemic uncer-
072   tainty is helpful in various contexts, e.g. risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (Depeweg et al., 2018)
073   and Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection (Amini et al., 2020). By understanding the sources of un-
074   certainty, one can confirm that an autonomous vehicle finds itself in an OOD scenario by observing
075   a higher epistemic uncertainty. This can be an important signal for a planner that uses predictions in
076   its decision-making. By incorporating this information, planners can make more informed decisions
077   and potentially take preventative actions in situations of high uncertainty.

      In this paper, we address the challenge of modeling the uncertainty of trajectory prediction models
109   within the autonomous driving (AD) domain from a holistic perspective. We focus on the quantifi-
110   cation and decomposition of uncertainty, as well as the influence of modeling choices related to the
111   approximate posterior q(W). Our method employs an information-theoretic approach (Hüllermeier,
112   2021), which quantifies aleatoric uncertainty through conditional entropy and epistemic uncertainty
113   using mutual information. Fig. 1 shows the predictive distribution p(y|x, D) and its accompany-
114   ing uncertainty values obtained by our proposed method for different scenarios of the nuScenes
115   dataset (Caesar et al., 2020). We summarize our contributions as below:
116
117
            1. We propose a novel method to quantify and decompose the uncertainty of trajectory predic-
               tion models, utilizing conditional entropy and mutual information to measure aleatoric and
118
               epistemic uncertainty. Both terms are approximated through Monte-Carlo (MC) sampling.
119
120         2. We analyze the relationship between uncertainty and prediction error in both in-distribution
121            and out-of-distribution scenarios. Additionally, we evaluate robustness in handling out-of-
122            distribution scenarios.
123         3. We study how modeling choices of the approximate posterior affect uncertainty calibration
124            and model robustness, considering different configurations of deep ensembles and Dropout.

127   2     M EASURING THE U NCERTAINTY FOR T RAJECTORY P REDICTION
128
129   This section details our method for decomposing uncertainty into aleatoric and epistemic compo-
130   nents. We start by defining the problem of uncertainty decomposition in trajectory prediction in
131
      Sec. 2.1. Then, in Sec. 2.2, we describe our approach for calculating these uncertainties using an
      MC approximation. We provide pseudo-code in App. A. Finally, we discuss the limitations of our
132
      approach with possible avenues to address these limitations in Sec. 2.3.
133
134
135   2.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
136
      Our method focuses on uncertainty quantification in trajectory prediction tasks. The problem is
137
      defined as predicting the future trajectory of a target agent in a driving scene based on current
138
      observations. Formally, let x ∈ RTin ×Fin represent the past features of an agent, where Tin is the
139   number of observed timesteps and Fin denotes the number of input features, such as coordinates,
140   velocities, accelerations, and other relevant data. In line with recent trajectory prediction literature
141   (Deo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2021), we also incorporate additional contexts, such
142   as static map information and the past trajectories of surrounding agents, into the model input. A
143   trajectory prediction model f (x) = y, parameterized by W, uses this input to estimate a future
144   trajectory y ∈ RTout ×Fout . Here, Tout represents the prediction horizon, and Fout is the number of
145   output features to predict, such as coordinates. Given the multi-modal nature of an agent’s future
146   behavior, an extended version of this model predicts multiple future trajectories. The distribution
147
      over potential future outcomes, p(y|x, W), can take various forms, such as a categorical distribution
      Deo et al. (2022), a mixture of Laplacians (Liu et al., 2024), a GMM (Nayakanti et al., 2023), or
148
      a non-parametric form (Jiang et al., 2023). Finally, we define an ensemble (Zhou, 2012) as a set
149
      of M trajectory prediction models. These models may have different parameterizations and could
150   belong to different model families. The ensemble can be constructed using various techniques, such
151   as Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) (Welling
152   & Teh, 2011), or deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). This ensemble introduces a
153   distribution q(W) over neural network parameters, which is an approximation to the true posterior
154   p(W|D) (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020).
155
      Our objective is to develop a method for uncertainty quantification to assess a model’s trustworthi-
156   ness. However, the type of uncertainty to address is not always clear. On one hand, high uncertainty
157   may stem from novel, previously unseen traffic scenarios. On the other hand, randomness arising
158   from unpredictable driver behavior can lead to multiple plausible predictions. While previous works,
159   such as Gilles et al. (2022) and Janjoš et al. (2023b), do not distinguish between uncertainty types,
160   we argue that decomposing uncertainty is crucial for understanding the sources of a model’s predic-
161   tions. Therefore, following concurrent literature (Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009; Hüllermeier,
      2021), we decompose uncertainty into epistemic and aleatoric components.

      2.3   D ISCUSSION
209
      The approach presented above effectively quantifies uncertainty in trajectory prediction. Yet, it is
210   important to acknowledge some current limitations and potential solutions. One notable concern is
211   the increased memory and computational burden, which may be prohibitive for a real-time appli-
212   cation such as trajectory prediction. A possible avenue to address this limitation is through the use
213   of ensemble distillation. Studies have shown that it is possible to distill an ensemble into a single
214   model, thereby significantly reducing computational overhead while maintaining comparable accu-
215   racy Malinin et al. (2019). These technique offers a promising direction for future work, ensuring
      that our approach remains both efficient and performant.

234   3   E XPERIMENTS
235
236   In this paper, we introduce a novel information-theoretic approach to measure and decompose the
237   uncertainty of the predictive distribution of trajectory prediction models in the AD domain. We
238   model the approximate posterior q(W) over neural network weights via sampling-based methods,
239   such as dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) and deep ensembles Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017).
240   For simplicity, we refer to any collection of neural networks as an ensemble. Our experimental
241   analysis is divided into four parts, where we explore both the uncertainty quantification capabilities
242   of our method and the impact of different ensemble compositions. First, in Sec. 3.1, we benchmark
243   our method against an alternative approach to quantify the uncertainty on the original nuScenes
244
      dataset (Caesar et al., 2020), which is a commonly used real-world trajectory prediction dataset for
      AD. We measure the correlation between the uncertainty and the prediction error and explore how
245
      epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties complement each other. In the subsequent parts, we create
246
      artificial OOD scenarios by manipulating the nuScenes dataset in various ways. Specifically, we
247   propose four different methods for manipulating the original nuScenes dataset, such as removing
248   lane information or omitting parts of the past trajectories of various agents. A detailed explanation
249   of our nuScenes manipulations is provided in App. D. In the second experimental part in Sec. 3.2,
250   we examine the robustness of various models and ensembles across different OOD scenarios. We
251   observe an overall increase in prediction error, indicating that our artificial OOD scenarios are more
252   challenging than the original dataset. In the third part in Sec. 3.3, we investigate how the correlation
253   between uncertainty and prediction error is affected in these OOD scenarios. Lastly, in Sec. 3.4, we
254   study whether we can detect OOD scenarios by analyzing the different types of uncertainty.
255   Throughout our experiments, we use our novel method to measure the total uncertainty and decom-
256   pose it into aleatoric and epistemic components to understand their relative importance. We generate
257   trajectory predictions from the ensemble using the approach described in Distelzweig et al. (2024),
258   which involves Model-Based Risk Minimization (MBRM) to draw trajectories from an ensemble
259   of prediction models. For single models, we generate trajectories via Topk sampling, which selects
260   the most likely trajectories (Liu et al., 2024). We rely on LAformer Liu et al. (2024), PGP Deo
261
      et al. (2022), and LaPred Kim et al. (2021) to construct different ensembles of trajectory prediction
      models. These three models are among the best-performing models with available open-source im-
262
      plementations. In our experiments, we evaluate different ensemble configurations, including deep
263
      ensembles, dropout ensembles, and single models. We use an ensemble size of three in all experi-
264   ments; for deep ensembles, we sample three different models, and for dropout ensembles, we sample
265   three different masks. Prediction performance is assessed in terms of minADE and Minimum Final
266   Displacement Error (minFDE) over k proposals. The minADEk measures the average point-wise L2
267   distances between the predicted trajectories and the ground truth, returning the minimum over the k
268   proposals (Caesar et al., 2020). In contrast, minFDEk considers only the final predicted point. De-
269   tailed prediction results for different models and ensemble configurations on the original nuScenes
      dataset are provided in App. C.

272   Identifying scenarios with high prediction error is critical for safety, as it helps determine when to
273   trust the system or when the driver needs to take control. In this experiment, we study the correla-
274   tion between different types of uncertainty and prediction error using the original nuScenes dataset.
275   More concretely, we compute the Pearson correlation coefficient ρ between each type of uncer-
276   tainty and the minADEk . We benchmark our proposed method against Filos et al. (2020), which
277   is an uncertainty quantification approach for planning. Prediction and planning are closely related
278
      tasks in AD (Hagedorn et al., 2024), and to the best of our knowledge, Filos et al. (2020) is the
      only other method with an architecture-agnostic approach that addresses uncertainty quantification
279
      in these domains. Filos et al. (2020) estimates the uncertainty by calculating the variance of the log-
280
      likelihood of future trajectories with respect to the parameters, i.e., Varq(W) [log p(y|x, W)]. Unlike
281   our method, this approach requires access to the future trajectory y. We report the correlation co-

283   minADE1 and minADE10 are provided in App. E.

355
356   In Sec. 3.1, we investigated whether the uncertainty estimates from our method offer indications
357
      of the reliability of our model’s predictions. However, it remains unclear if these findings are also
      applicable to OOD scenarios. In this experiment, we analyze the correlation between uncertainty
358
      and prediction error in OOD scenarios across different ensembles, and we compare these correlation

         4                    R ELATED W ORK
448
449      Anticipating the future motion of traffic participants is a critical component of autonomous driving
450
         systems (Hu et al., 2023). Due to the safety-critical nature of these systems, it’s essential to ac-
         count for and propagate uncertainties across the entire prediction stack (McAllister et al., 2017). For
451
         instance, planners need to factor in motion prediction uncertainty to accurately assess the risks asso-
452
         ciated with various driving maneuvers (Filos et al., 2020). In the following subsections, we review
453      related work on both motion prediction as well as quantification and decomposition of uncertainty.
454
455
         4.1                  M OTION P REDICTION FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
456
457      The future motion of other traffic participants is influenced by a multitude of observable and un-
458      observable factors, rendering it a challenging modeling task. These factors include, among others,
459      the latent goals and preferences of traffic participants, social norms and traffic rules, complex inter-
460      actions with surrounding traffic, as well as constraints induced by the static environment (Rudenko
461      et al., 2020). The shortcomings of the perception system, which provides noisy and partial obser-
462      vations, pose an additional challenge. These challenges necessitate a probabilistic formulation of
463      the prediction task to adequately model the uncertain and multi-modal nature of future motion. In
464      general, prediction models consist of two components: a behavior backbone, which encodes the
465
         traffic scene, and a decoder, which models the predictive distribution. We will highlight various
         implementations of the two components below.
466
467      Early prediction approaches (Casas et al., 2018; Chai et al., 2020; Phan-Minh et al., 2020) propose
468      encoding the past trajectory of observed traffic participants and the elements of the static environ-
469      ment (e.g., lane boundaries, crosswalks, traffic signs) by rendering the scene in a semantic bird’s
470      eye view image and applying well-established convolutional neural networks (He et al., 2016). Such
471
         image-based representations of the scene have largely been replaced by vectorized representations
         due to their inefficiency (Gao et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Deo et al., 2022;
472
         Nayakanti et al., 2023). In a vectorized representation, all entities of the static and dynamic envi-
473
         ronment are approximated by a sequence of vectors. Models for sequential data, such as temporal
474      convolutional networks (van den Oord et al., 2016) or recurrent neural networks (Hochreiter &
475      Schmidhuber, 1997; Chung et al., 2014) are used to encode the sequences and interactions between
476      entities are modeled using pooling operations (Alahi et al., 2016), graph neural networks (Hamilton,
477      2020), or Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017).
478
         The future motion of traffic participants is typically characterized by a sequence of states over multi-
479
         ple time steps, known as trajectories (Ngiam et al., 2022; Varadarajan et al., 2022). Several strategies
480      are employed to capture the highly multi-modal distribution over trajectories conditioned on the en-
481      coded scene. Many approaches represent the distribution by a set of trajectories with associated
482      mode probabilities. The trajectories are either regressed by the model (Cui et al., 2019; Liang et al.,
483      2020; Kim et al., 2021; Deo et al., 2022) or fixed a priori (Phan-Minh et al., 2020). Other approaches
484      use parametric mixture distributions, such as GMMs (Khandelwal et al., 2020; Tolstaya et al., 2021;
485      Varadarajan et al., 2022) or mixtures of Laplacians (Liu et al., 2024). Alternatively, generative
         models such as conditional variational autoencoders (Lee et al., 2017; Bhattacharyya et al., 2019;


                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Salzmann et al., 2020; Janjoš et al., 2023a), generative adversarial networks (Gupta et al., 2018;
487   Huang et al., 2020; Gómez-Huélamo et al., 2022), normalizing flows (Schöller & Knoll, 2021), or
488   diffusion models (Jiang et al., 2023) model the trajectory distribution via latent variables.
489
490   4.2   U NCERTAINTY M ODELING , D ECOMPOSITION AND Q UANTIFICATION
491
492   The majority of current trajectory prediction models solely account for aleatoric uncertainty by
493   modeling a probability distribution on the output space (Varadarajan et al., 2022). To incorporate
494   epistemic uncertainty in a theoretically sound manner, one can adopt a Bayesian framework (Kendall
495   & Gal, 2017; Depeweg et al., 2018; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Wilson, 2020). A Bayesian neural
496   network assumes a distribution over the network weights instead of a point estimate to account for
497
      the lack of knowledge about the data-generating process (Hüllermeier, 2021; Jospin et al., 2022).
      Since analytically evaluating the posterior distribution over the weights is intractable for modern
498
      neural networks, approximate inference techniques such as Variational Inference (VI) or forms of
499
      MCMC must be considered (Jospin et al., 2022). Due to its simplicity, MC Dropout, which can
500   be interpreted as an approximate VI method (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), is used by many percep-
501   tion approaches in AD (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Abdar et al., 2021) and is also employed by Janjoš
502   et al. (2023b) for modeling epistemic uncertainty of a trajectory predictor. Another well-established
503   approach to account for epistemic uncertainty is deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017;
504   Jospin et al., 2022; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020). Prior work (Filos et al., 2020) uses deep ensembles
505   to approximate the posterior distribution in their epistemic uncertainty-aware planning method. We
506   apply MC Dropout as well as deep ensembles to approximate the uncertainty over network weights
507   and systematically assess their performance in the context of trajectory prediction.
508   A common information-theoretical measure for the uncertainty is the entropy of the predictive distri-
509   bution as a measure of the total uncertainty, which can be additively decomposed into the conditional
510   entropy and mutual information, representing a measure of aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty (De-
511   peweg et al., 2018; Smith & Gal, 2018; Hüllermeier, 2021; Wimmer et al., 2023). Alternative
512   measures based on the variance were proposed by Depeweg et al. (2018). While variance-based
513   measures are suitable in cases where the predictive distribution is a uni-modal Gaussian, it is less
514
      suitable for multi-modal outputs, such as trajectories. Our approach thus relies on entropy-based
      measures to quantify the uncertainties of trajectory prediction models. However, variance can be
515
      useful in other contexts; e.g., Gilles et al. (2022) uses the variance of the predicted heat map over
516
      future positions as an uncertainty measure. Another variance-based uncertainty heuristic is pro-
517   posed by Filos et al. (2020) in the related field of motion planning for AD. This approach, however,
518   only quantifies the epistemic uncertainty and requires access to the future trajectory. Some methods
519   train separate models (Pustynnikov & Eremeev, 2021) or include additional heads with auxiliary
520   tasks (Wiederer et al., 2023; Janjoš et al., 2023b) to learn a proxy measure of the uncertainty of a
521   trajectory prediction model without a proper decomposition.
522
      To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to thoroughly investigate the theoretically sound
523   modeling, decomposition, and quantification of uncertainties for trajectory prediction models. All
524   other published trajectory prediction approaches either do not holistically consider all three aspects
525   or resort to heuristics.
526

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
      Understanding and addressing uncertainty in probabilistic motion prediction for AD remains a key
530
      challenge. This paper addresses this gap by proposing a general approach to quantify and decom-
531
      pose uncertainty using an information-theoretic framework. We demonstrate that our estimates of
532   aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty provide meaningful indicators of prediction error, making them
533   reliable for assessing prediction performance. Through an extensive evaluation, we examine both
534   in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios under various posterior assumptions. Overall, our
535   approach advances principled uncertainty modeling in motion prediction for AD.
536
      A promising future direction is to integrate our uncertainty quantification framework in the planning
537
      system of an autonomous vehicle (Teng et al., 2023; Hagedorn et al., 2024). This opens the possibil-
538   ity of combining autonomous driving planning research with risk-sensitive reinforcement learning
539   (Depeweg et al., 2018), enabling the system to make informed decisions in uncertain situations.

803   B    D ISTRIBUTION A SSUMPTIONS
804
805   In this section, we briefly cover the choice of a GMM for the distribution p(y|x, Wm ) assumed in
806   Eq. 5. Note that p(y|x, Wm ) can initially take either categorical or continuous forms, depending
807   on the model. However, we transform p(y|x, Wm ) into a GMM, a continuous distribution, for two
808   reasons. First, for a categorical distribution, the probability p(y|x, Wm ) is zero for any sample not
809   generated by the model with parameters Wm . This is because categorical distributions only as-
      sign non-zero probabilities to discrete outcomes they were trained on, making them unsuitable for


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      continuous trajectory prediction tasks. A GMM, on the other hand, smooths out the discrete distri-
811   bution, which aligns better with the continuous nature of the regression task. Second, handling a
812   mixture of categorical and continuous distributions introduces numerical issues. When p(y|x, Wm )
813   is continuous, we model the logarithm of the density. However, when it is discrete, we model the
814   probability directly. This discrepancy leads to numerical challenges during the log-sum-exp opera-
815   tion when calculating Ĥ(y|x, D). The resulting predictive distribution p(y|x, D) is then represented
816   as a mixture of M GMMs, where each GMM corresponds to one of the models in the ensemble.
817   For computational convenience, we perform these calculations only for the final predicted timestep,
818   i.e., the endpoint. Fig. 2 shows the GMM fitted to the final positions of the predictions from each
819   model, which represents the distribution of the final coordinates of the predicted trajectories over a
820   two-dimensional continuous grid.
