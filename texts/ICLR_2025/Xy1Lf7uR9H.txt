                                                   A BSTRACT
013
               Interpretability for machine learning models is becoming more and more impor-
014
               tant as machine learning models become more complex. The functional ANOVA
015
               model, which decomposes a high-dimensional function into a sum of lower di-
016            mensional functions so called components, is one of the most popular tools for
017            interpretable AI, and recently, various neural network models have been devel-
018            oped for estimating each component in the functional ANOVA model. However,
019            such neural networks are highly unstable when estimating components since the
020            components themselves are not uniquely defined. That is, there are multiple func-
021            tional ANOVA decompositions for a given function. In this paper, we propose a
022            novel interpretable model which guarantees a unique functional ANOVA decom-
023            position and thus is able to estimate each component stably. We call our proposed
024
               model ANOVA-NODE since it is a modification of Neural Oblivious Decision En-
               sembles (NODE) for the functional ANOVA model. Theoretically, we prove that
025
               ANOVA-NODE can approximate a smooth function well. Additionally, we ex-
026
               perimentally show that ANOVA-NODE provides much more stable estimation of
027            each component and thus much more stable interpretation when training data and
028            initial values of the model parameters vary than existing neural network models
029            do.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Interpretability has become more important as artificial intelligence (AI) models have become more
034
      sophisticated and complicated in recent years. Various methods for interpretable AI can be catego-
035
      rized into two groups. One is transparent box design, where interpretable machine learning models
036   such as linear models and decision trees are used to learn a prediction model. Typically, interpretable
037   models are inferior to black box models in terms of prediction powers. The other group consists of
038   post-hoc interpretation methods that try to interpret a given black box models (Lundberg, 2017;
039   Ribeiro et al., 2016). While post-hoc interpretation methods do not hamper the prediction power of
040   a given black box model at all, they often exhibit instability and lack faithfulness (Slack et al., 2020).
041
      In this paper, we focus on the transparent box design based on the functional ANOVA model (Ho-
042   effding, 1992). The functional ANOVA model approximates a given complex high-dimensional
043   function by the sum of low dimensional (e.g., one or two dimensional) functions referred to as
044   components. One of the most representative examples of the functional ANOVA model is the gen-
045   eralized additive model (GAM, Hastie & Tibshirani (1987)), which consists of the summation of
046   one-dimensional functions, each corresponding to an input feature. Low dimensional functions are
047   easier to understand, and thus the functional ANOVA model is popularly used for interpretable AI
048   (Lengerich et al., 2020; Märtens & Yau, 2020).
049   Recently, various learning algorithms for the functional ANOVA model based on neural networks
050   have been proposed (Agarwal et al., 2021; Radenovic et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2021). While they
051   provide accurate prediction models, existing neural networks struggle to estimate each component
052   in the functional ANOVA model due to unidentifiability (Lengerich et al., 2020). That is, completely
053   different components could result in the same prediction model. Figure 1 presents the plots of the
      main effects estimated by NA2 M, NB2 M, and our proposed ANOVA-N2 ODE on C ALIFORNIA

078   A simple remedy for resolving the issue of unidentifiability is to impose a constraint on the compo-
079   nents to make them identifiable. There are several such constraints, some of which can be found, for
080   example, in Gu & Wahba (1993); Chastaing et al. (2012); Hooker (2007). One of the most popular
081
      identifiability constraints is the so called ‘sum-to-zero’ condition, which ensures that each compo-
      nent is orthogonal in a certain Hilbert space.
082
083   Learning the functional ANOVA model by using neural networks under such an identifiability con-
084   straint, however, is not easy since a standard gradient descent algorithm could not be applicable.
085   The aim of this paper is to develop a specially designed neural network that automatically satis-
086   fies the sum-to-zero condition and thus can be learned by a standard gradient descent algorithm.
087
      The proposed neural network is a modification of Neural Oblivious Decision Ensembles (NODE,
      Popov et al. (2019)) for the functional ANOVA model. Chang et al. (2021) propose a modification of
088
      NODE for the GAM called NODE-GAM, but NODE-GAM is not effective in estimating each com-
089
      ponent due to the identifiability issue. We modify NODE such that each component in the functional
090   ANOVA model is estimated uniquely. We call our algorithm ANOVA-NODE.
091
092   There exist various learning algorithms for the functional ANOVA model under the sum-to-zero
093
      condition such as Gu & Wahba (1993); Lin & Zhang (2006); Kim et al. (2009) that do not use
      neural networks. An advantage of ANOVA-NODE compared to these non-neural algorithms is that a
094
      gradient descent based optimization algorithm can be used in learning and hence end-to-end learning
095
      is possible when it is combined with other neural networks. See Section 4.5 for an example.
096
097   Overall, our contributions are as follows.
098
099     • We propose a novel XAI model (ANOVA-NODE) which trains the functional ANOVA model
100       under the sum-to-zero condition using a gradient descent-based optimization algorithm.
101
102
        • We prove the universal approximation property in the sense that ANOVA-NODE can approxi-
          mate any smooth function up to an arbitrary precision.
103
104
        • By analyzing multiple benchmark datasets, we illustrate that ANOVA-NODE provides more
105
          stable estimation and interpretation of each component compared to the baseline models, in-
106
          cluding NAM (Agarwal et al., 2021), NBM (Radenovic et al., 2022), NODE-GAM (Chang
107       et al., 2021) and XGB (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) without losing prediction accuracy.

142

148

152   ponent of GAM. NBM achieves a significant reduction in training time compared to NAM by using
153   basis DNNs to train all components. NODE-GAM extends Neural Obilvious Decision Ensembles
154   (NODE, Popov et al. (2019)) for GAM.

      4.2   P ERFORMANCE IN COMPONENT SELECTION
399
      An important implication of stable estimation of the components is the ability of selecting signal
400
      components. That is, ANOVA-NODE can effectively identify signal components in the true function
401   by measuring the variations of the estimated components. For example, we can consider the l1 norm
402   of each estimated component (i.e, ∥fS (xS )∥1 ) as the important score, and select the components
403   whose important scores are large. This simple component selection procedure would not perform
404   well if component estimation is unstable.
405
      To investigate how well ANOVA-NODE selects the true signal components, we conduct an experi-
406
      ment similar to the one in Tsang et al. (2017). We generate synthetic datasets from Y = f (x) + ϵ,
407   where f is the true prediction model and ϵ is a noise generated from a Gaussian distribution with
408   mean 0 and variance σϵ2 . Then, we apply ANOVA-N2 ODE, NA2 M and NB2 M to calculate the im-
409   portance scores of the main effects and second order interactions and examine how well they predict
410   whether a given component is signal. For the performance measure of component selection, we use
411   AUROC obtained from the pairs of ∥fˆS ∥1 and rS for all S ⊂ [p] with |S| ≤ 2, where fˆS are the
412                                       (k)
      estimates of fS in f and rS = I(∥fS ∥1 > 0) are the indicators whether fS are signal or not.
413
414   For the true prediction model, we consider the three functions f (k) , k = 1, 2, 3 whose details are
415   given in Appendix C.1. We set the data size to 15,000 and set σϵ2 to make the signal-to-noise ratio
416   become 5. Table 2 compares the AUROCs of ANOVA-N2 ODE, NA2 M and NB2 M, which clearly in-
417
      dicates that ANOVA-N2 ODE outperforms the baseline models in component selection. More details
      of component selection with ANOVA-N2 ODE are given in Appendix H.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531   In this paper, we propose a novel XAI model called ANOVA-NODE for estimating the functional
532   ANOVA model stably based on Neural Oblivious Decision Tree (NODT). We theoretically demon-
533   strate that ANOVA-NODE can approximate a smooth function well. We also empirically show that
534   prediction performance of ANOVA-NODE is comparable to its competitors.
535   One way to make ANOVA-NODE computationally more efficient is to combine ANOVA-NODE
536   and NBM (Radenovic et al., 2022), which has significantly fewer weight parameters. We explored
537   this approach, and the algorithm and results of the experiment are given in Appendix G.2. Even
538   though this algorithm is computationally more efficient than ANOVA-NODE itself, analyzing high-
539   dimensional data is still computationally demanding due to too many components. In general,
      scaling-up interpretable AI algorithms is a promising future research topic.

1515
1516   We consider the two concepts of interpretation: Local and Global which are roughly defined as:
1517
1518   Local Interpretation: Information about how each feature of a given datum affects the prediction.
1519   SHAP is a notable example of local interpretation. For the functional ANOVA model, the predictive
1520   values of each component at a given datum would be considered as local interpretation.
1521
1522   Global Interpretation: Information about how each feature is related to the final prediction
1523   model. The importance scores of each feature (e.g. global SHAP (Molnar, 2020)) and the func-
1524
       tional relations between each feature and the prediction model (e.g. the dependency plot of SHAP
       (Molnar, 2020) are examples of global interpretation. For the functional ANOVA model, the im-
1525
       portance score, which can be defined as the l1 norm of the corresponding component as is done in
1526
       Section 4.2, and the functional relation identified by the functional form of each component are two
1527   tools for global interpretation.

2539
2540
       In this section, we describes the comparison between ANOVA-NODE and additive higher-order
2541   factorization machines (AHOFMs, Rügamer (2024)).
2542
2543   Higher-Order Factorization Machines (HOFMs). Higher-Order Factorization Machines
2544   (HOFMs, Blondel et al. (2016)) are a Factorization Machine(FM) which considers up to higher-
2545   order interaction. Blondel et al. (2016) proposed an algorithm that efficiently learns HOFMs using
2546   the properties of the ANOVA kernel, even as the input dimension p increases.
2547
2548   Additive higher-order factorization machines (AHOFMs). AHOFMs (Rügamer, 2024)) ap-
2549   proximate each component in the functional ANOVA model using a spline basis representation.
2550   That is, for j = 1, ..., p, AHOFMs estimate fj by
2551                                                     Mj
2552
                                                         X
                                            fj (xj ) =         Bm,j (xj )βm,j
2553                                                     m=1
2554
2555   where {B1,j , ..., BMj ,j } are the basis functions and {b1,j , ..., bMj ,j } are the coefficients. Furth-
2556
       more, for high-order interaction S, AHOFM estimates fS via extended spline basis representation
       which used the tensor product spline. Finally, Rügamer (2024) proposed an algorithm for efficiently
2557
       learning AHOFMs, which applies the method used in HOFMs to AHOFMs, taking higher-order
2558
       interactions into account.
2559
2560
       Comparison between ANOVA-NODE and AHOFMs. We first describe a comparison of the
2561   scalability between AHOFMs and ANOVA-NODE, followed by the applicability of AHOFM to
2562   ANOVA-NODE, and finally, the differences between ANOVA-NODE and AHOFM.
2563
2564
       If all higher-order interactions are considered, the method proposed by Rügamer (2024)) is more
       efficient in terms of scalability compared to ANOVA-NODE. However, the experiments in this pa-
2565
       per show that considering interactions up to the second order is sufficient for real data. Moreover,
2566
       when all higher-order interactions are considered in the functional ANOVA model, it is likely to be
2567   overfitted.
2568
2569   Since ANOVA-NODT is also a model represented by the spline basis representation of equation (2)
2570
       in Rügamer (2024)(entmax can be viewed as basis function) factorization method can be used to
       improve the scalability of ANOVA-NODE. Applying the method used by AHOFMs to ANOVA-
2571
       NODE seems like an interesting research topic, but in this paper, we proposed NBM-NODE in
2572
       Appendix G.2, which enhances the scalability of ANOVA-NODE by utilizing the method by which
2573   Radenovic et al. (2022) improved the scalability of NAM (Agarwal et al. (2021)).
2574
2575   There are two main differences between ANOVA-NODE and AHOFMs: one is whether the basis
2576
       function is learned, and the other is whether the sum-to-zero condition is satisfied. To be specific,
       AHOFMs do not learn the basis function, but ANOVA-NODE can be seen as learning the basis
2577
       function by learning the parameters γ and b in the entmax function. Also, since AHOFMs do not
2578
       satisfy the sum-to-zero condition, the estimated components are not identifiable. However, unlike
2579   AHOFMs, the estimated components by ANOVa-NODE are identifiable. That is, ANOVA-NODE
2580   provides more reliable interpretations than AHOFMs.
