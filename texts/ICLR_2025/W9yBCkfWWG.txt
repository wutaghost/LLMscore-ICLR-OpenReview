009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Coordination in multi-agent systems is critical for optimizing collective outcomes
012            and is applicable in diverse fields such as drone swarms, emergency response, and
013            more. Despite extensive research, the distributed coordination strategy alignment
014            problem—where all agents follow the same strategy and execute the prescribed ac-
015            tions without a global coordinator—remains largely unexplored, posing challenges
016            in scalability and privacy preservation. We introduce a new research problem
017            termed “federated coordination", which seeks to achieve decentralized strategy
018
               alignment across distributed agents while maintaining the privacy of strategy
               choices. To address this problem, we propose a framework that employs an energy-
019
               based model. It facilitates decentralized strategy alignment by associating agent
020
               states with coordination strategies through local minimum energy values. We
021            address privacy concerns through a simple yet effective communication protocol
022            that protects strategy selections from eavesdropping and information leakage. Our
023            extensive experimental results validate these contributions, demonstrating scal-
024            ability and reduced computational demands. This enhances the practicality of
025            coordination systems in multi-agent settings.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multi-agent coordination has been an active research area for years (Busoniu et al., 2008; Cao et al.,
031   2012; Yan et al., 2013; Torreno et al., 2017; Rizk et al., 2019; Gronauer & Diepold, 2022). By
032   coordinating, agents can interact in a structured manner, optimizing the collective outcomes of their
033
      efforts. This coordination is applied across various fields, including drone swarms, computer networks,
      and emergency response. Effective coordination results in more robust and adaptable systems,
034
      capable of handling complex scenarios more effectively. The current study of coordination presents a
035
      variety of challenging research problems including multi-agent reinforcement learning (Lowe et al.,
036   2017; Zhang et al., 2020; 2021), communication efficiency (Jiang & Lu, 2018; Zhang et al., 2019),
037   uncertainty handling (Oliehoek et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2017), task allocation (Matarić et al.,
038   2003; Skaltsis et al., 2021), the scalability of coordination (Arslan & Yüksel, 2016; Qu et al., 2020),
039   cooperative games (Rahwan et al., 2015; Chalkiadakis et al., 2022), etc. These challenges drive
040   innovations to push the boundaries of what multi-agent systems can achieve.
041
      While the existing research has produced fruitful results, the problem of coordination strategy align-
042   ment is almost unexplored. Specifically, in many real scenarios (e.g., security management for ad
043   hoc networks, military operations, etc.), there can be multiple strategies to be selected. Coordination
044   strategy alignment means all cooperative agents follow the same strategy and execute the prescribed
045   actions. Typically, the literature implicitly assumes the presence of a global coordinator who synchro-
046   nizes the strategy selection across all agents (Celli & Gatti, 2018; Farina et al., 2018; Cacciamani et al.,
047   2021). This centralized approach, while simplifying strategy alignment, poses a high requirement on
048   the communication infrastructure, i.e., the global coordinator needs to communicate with every agent
049   for every alignment. This centralized communication often impedes scalability and is infeasible in
050
      environments without a global communication infrastructure.
051   Another overlooked aspect is the preservation of privacy concerning the chosen coordination strategy.
052   The privacy of current strategy selection is important in competitive environments. Revealing the
053   current strategic choice can significantly degrade coordination performance as it allows opponents to
      tailor their responses more effectively. To ensure privacy, communication channels can be secured

054
      with encryption techniques, though this increases computational and communication resource usage.
055   Additionally, considering potential information leakage from agents within the cooperative team
056   introduces a complex requirement: each agent should know only its specific role as dictated by the
057   coordination strategy, without awareness of the overall strategy.
058
      In this paper, we propose federated coordination, a new research problem aiming to achieve de-
059
      centralized strategy alignment while maintaining the privacy of the current strategy selection. As a
060
      first attempt to address this problem, we introduce a novel framework that employs an energy-based
061   model to establish correlations among agents. Specifically, given a set of energy states (each held
062   by one agent), the energy-based model returns a specific energy value. Some sets of energy states
063   result in local minimum energy and are termed local minima. Our key idea is to associate each local
064   minimum with a specific coordination strategy. That is, we set each agent’s energy state of a local
065   minimum as a key in the agent’s dictionary, with the agent’s actions prescribed by the associated
066   strategy as the value. For each coordination alignment, every agent randomly initializes its energy
067   state and participates in an energy minimization process. This process keeps updating the energy
068   states until they converge to a certain local minimum. Then, each agent uses its energy state within
069
      the attained local minimum as a key, and executes the actions designated under that key. Collectively,
      the actions performed by all agents constitute the coordination strategy corresponding to the achieved
070
      local minimum, thereby ensuring the alignment of the coordination strategy of agents.
071
072   Crucially, our algorithm only requires local communication. During energy minimization, each
073   agent only needs to know the energy state of its immediate neighbors to compute the gradient of its
074   own energy state, facilitating a decentralized coordination process. This reduces the dependence on
075
      centralized communication infrastructure and facilitates the deployment in wild areas. Moreover,
      each agent knows only its own actions, yet the collective strategy is aligned across all agents.
076
077   Regarding privacy, we consider three levels of opponent abilities: 1) predict the coordination strategy
078   distribution based on historical selections, 2) eavesdrop on communicated information, and 3) access
079   to confidential information stored by some agents. We show that our distributed strategy alignment
080   process enables the strategy distribution to be non-stationary (§ 4.3), which prevents the opponent
081
      from making accurate predictions. Moreover, we develop an encryption-free yet privacy-preserving
      communication protocol, ensuring that the current strategy choice remains confidential even if an
082
      opponent intercepts all communication. Furthermore, our protocol guarantees that even if some
083
      agents disclose their own information, the confidentiality of the data pertaining to agents not directly
084   connected to any compromised agents remains protected.
085
086   To summarize, this paper makes three key contributions. 1) We introduce federated coordination, a
087
      new problem that focuses on achieving distributed strategy alignment while preserving the privacy of
      strategy selections. 2) We propose a novel framework to address the proposed federated coordination
088
      problem. It provably synchronizes agents’ strategies without centralized control, exhibits scalability
089
      and robustness, and protects the privacy of strategy selections against eavesdropping and information
090   leakage. 3) We conduct comprehensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our framework
091   and explore its various attributes, e.g., scalability, robustness, and reduced computational demands.

093   2    R ELATED WORK
094
095   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) aims to learn coordination strategies (Sunehag et al.,
096   2017; Rashid et al., 2018; Son et al., 2019) rather than strategy alignment. It often adopts the
097   centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) paradigm(Hernandez-Leal et al., 2019),
098   where agents are trained centrally but execute strategies independently. While MARL can achieve
099
      distributed coordination, the learned strategies are typically stationary, being vulnerable to adversarial
      training attacks (Gleave et al., 2020). Cacciamani et al. (2021) propose to switch strategies by a
100
      global signal. This reliance on a global signal poses challenges in scenarios without a centralized
101
      coordinator and can compromise the scalability and privacy of the system.
102   Consensus algorithms are widely used to achieve distributed agreement on a common value among
103   agents (Proskurnikov et al., 2016; Li & Tan, 2019; Amirkhani & Barshooi, 2022). However, all
104   agents agreeing on the same value means one compromised agent can leak critical information about
105   the whole system. In comparison, in our method, agents converge to different energy states, with
106   these states collectively corresponding to a joint action. Our method can be viewed as a more general
107   form of consensus, aiming to achieve a coordinated outcome instead of agreeing on a single value.
      Game theory provides a mathematical framework for analyzing strategic interactions among agents.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Concepts such as multiple equilibria and adversarial team games are particularly relevant (von Stengel
109   & Koller, 1997; Kalogiannis et al., 2022; Anagnostides et al., 2023). We build on these concepts
110   by addressing the strategy alignment problem in a distributed manner, ensuring that agents can
111   collectively reach a favorable equilibrium without revealing their strategy choices to opponents.
112   Differential privacy is a framework designed to ensure that the removal or addition of a single data
113   point does not significantly affect the overall outcome, thus protecting individual data entries from
114   being inferred (Dwork, 2008; Gong et al., 2020). Hence, it protects the algorithm inputs, whereas our
115   approach focuses on maintaining the privacy of strategy selections, i.e., the algorithm outputs.
116   In summary, while studies in MARL, consensus algorithms, and game theory are all related to
117   coordination, they often neglect how to align agents’ strategies in a distributed manner. Differential
118   privacy mainly focuses on protecting the privacy of data inputs rather than coordinated outcomes.
119   Our proposed framework for federated coordination addresses these gaps by enabling decentralized
120   strategy alignment and leveraging an energy-based model for flexible and secure coordination.

122   3       P RELIMINARY
123
      Motivating application: Consider a scenario where drone swarms are deployed in remote areas
124
      without access to centralized servers. These drones are equipped with various strategies, e.g., different
125   attack formations. To optimize their performance, the drones must randomly switch between these
126   strategies for each operation, ensuring unpredictability in their tactics Paruchuri et al. (2009); Yang
127   et al. (2024). However, before each attack, all drones in the swarm must agree on the same randomly
128   selected strategy. Given the lack of centralized coordination, the drones rely on local communication
129   channels, which are vulnerable to eavesdropping by adversaries. This presents a significant challenge
130   in ensuring secure and effective strategy coordination among the drones in the swarm.
131
132   3.1       F EDERATED COORDINATION PROBLEM
133   We use adversarial team games to model scenarios where cooperative agents face an opponent. A
134   static, normal-form adversarial team game (von Stengel & Koller, 1997; Anagnostides et al., 2023)
135   is defined by a tuple Γ(N , O, A, B, U ). Γ consists of a team of N cooperative agents N facing an
                                                                                               QN
136   opponent O1 . Each agent from N has a set of available actions Ai , so that A := i=1 Ai denotes
137   the joint action space of N . Also, the opponent O has a finite and nonempty set of actions B. We
138   denote by a = (a1 , . . . , aN ) ∈ A a joint action of N , and b ∈ B an action of the opponent O.
139   U : A × B → R is a utility function. The cooperative agents share the same utility represented by
140   U (a, b) and the team game is assumed to be zero-sum, i.e., the opponent’s utility is −U (a, b). To
141   maximize the utility, the team N normally has multiple coordination strategies2 , each maximizing
142
      utility against specific opponent actions. Let S := {am } with cardinality as S denote the set of
      strategies (the formal definition of S is in § A.4). Note that in normal-form games, a strategy is a
143
      joint action. In Markov games (Kalogiannis et al., 2022), a strategy is a joint policy (π1 , . . . , πN )
144
      where πi is the policy of agent i mapping a given state to a distribution over Ai . Since this paper
145   focuses on ensuring that agents follow the same strategy, whether it is a joint policy or a joint action
146   is irrelevant. Henceforth, we will use a joint action am = (am             m
                                                                    1 , . . . , aN ) to represent a strategy.
147
148
      Existing works assume that a global coordinator synchronizes strategies among agents so that their
      joint action locates within S. However, there are numerous scenarios where such a coordinator
149
      does not exist, e.g., ad hoc networks, autonomous drone swarms, etc. In these scenarios, strategy
150
      alignment must be conducted in a distributed manner. Furthermore, due to the existence of an
151   opponent, cooperative agents must preserve the privacy of their current strategy selection. Otherwise,
152   the opponent can choose the best response bm to the disclosed strategy am such that U (am , bm ) ≤
153   U (am , b) ∀b ∈ B. This introduces a new research problem, federated coordination, with the
154   following objectives: 1) the agents in N coordinate their actions in a distributed manner to ensure the
155   resulting joint action a belongs to S; 2) the opponent has no information about a.

      4.1   S TORAGE OF COORDINATION STRATEGIES
224   To associate local minima {vl } with coordination strategies {am }, a naive approach is to randomly
225   generate W and b, find all local minima of the resulting energy-based model, and link each local
226   minimum to a coordination strategy. However, with random W and b, determining all local minima
227
      of the corresponding energy-based model is very difficult, if not impossible. The best we can do
      is to repeat the following procedures: randomly initialize the energy states of all agents, conduct
228
      the energy minimization process, and identify one local minimum when the minimization process
229
      converges. Repeating these procedures is computationally expensive, and we can never be sure if
230   we have identified all local minima. If the energy states of agents converge to an unknown local
231   minimum during distributed strategy alignment, these states cannot be used to retrieve agents’ actions
232   since the unknown local minimum is not associated with any strategy. In addition, it is possible
233   that the number of local minima L determined by the random W and b is smaller than the number
234   of strategies. This means some strategies will never be selected through the distributed strategy
235   alignment, which could be undesired.

238   than the number of strategies. Specifically, the algorithm leverages the fact that the gradient of each

      4.3   A NALYSIS OF PRIVACY- PRESERVING CAPABILITY
325
326   Before the analysis, we present the Threat Model outlining various levels of opponent ability:
327   L1: Prediction of coordination strategy distribution. The opponent predicts the distribution of the
328
      coordination strategies by analyzing the historical selections of cooperative agents.
      L2: Eavesdropping on communicated information. The opponent eavesdrops on the communica-
329
      tion channels used by the cooperative agents.
330
      L3: Access to Confidential Information. The opponent can gain access to confidential information
331   stored by some of the cooperative agents.
332   Moreover, all opponents know the procedures of the distributed strategy alignment process and have
333   G and F. In the following, we analyze the privacy-preserving capability of our framework when
334   facing opponents with different abilities.
335
      For an opponent with L1 ability, our framework inherently can use a distributed method to make the
336
      strategy distribution non-stationary, preventing the opponent from learning an accurate distribution.
337   Specifically, given W and b, the local minimum to which the energy states converge is determined
338   by the initial energy states and the optimizer used to update energy states. Consequently, the strategy
339   distribution depends on the distribution of initial energy states and the chosen optimizer. Thus, each
340   agent can independently and periodically alter its optimizer and method for initializing energy states,
341   ensuring a non-stationary strategy distribution.
342
      When facing an opponent with L2 ability, our framework can guarantee the privacy of current strategy
343   selection through the proposed communication protocol. Specifically, the opponent can eavesdrop the
344   communicated messages {Wij ρ(uti ) ⊕ G(sedpij , t)|i, j = 1, . . . , N, and Aij = 1}. As it does not
345   know W, it has no way to compute G(sedpij , t) and cannot get Wij ρ(uti ). Therefore, the opponent
346   has no information to infer the current strategy selection. In comparison, if the agent i directly sends
347   ρ(uti ), the opponent can compute {uti } through {ρ−1 (ρ(uti ))}. Given a converged {uti }, the opponent
348   may infer the strategy selection if it has seen these converged energy states before. Alternatively, the
349   agent i may send Wij ρ(uti ). In this case, the opponent cannot recover {uti } due to the lack of Wij .
350   However, when the energy states converge, the opponent will obtain {Wij ρ(vil )} corresponding to a
351   certain vl . If this vl has been converged to before, the opponent will find {Wij ρ(vil )} was received
352   before and thus can infer the current strategy as the strategy at that time. Our method prevents the
353   above issue by adding noise G(sedpij , t). It ensures that {Wij ρ(vil ) ⊕ G(sedpij , t)} are different in
354   different alignment processes even for the same vl .
355
      Regarding an opponent with L3 ability, our framework can protect the information of the agents
356   not directly connecting with the compromised agents. Assuming the agent k in Figure 1(b) is
357   compromised, which means the opponent knows Wik . Based on the received Wik ρ(utk )⊕G(sedpik , t)
358   and Wik ρ(uti ) ⊕ G(sedpik , t), the opponent can recover utk and uti because it can compute G(sedpik , t)
359   through Wik . However, utj is still safe as it is included in the message Wij ρ(utj ) ⊕ G(sedpij , t) and
360   the opponent cannot recover it without the knowledge of Wij .

465   5.3   C OMPUTATIONAL EFFICIENCY
466
      The communication protocol in our proposed framework is encryption-free yet privacy-preserving.
467
      One may wonder whether we can use encryption techniques to protect the privacy of {ρ(ui )} instead
468   of using the noise information produced by random number generators. In this part, we compare the
469   computational time of our method with that of the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) which is
470   one of the most efficient symmetric block cipher (Mahajan & Sachdeva, 2013). We use a 128-bit key
471   size, which is widely adopted to balance security and efficiency.
472
      Note that when using AES to protect ρ(ui ), it first needs to encrypt ρ(ui ) on the sender side and
473
      then decrypt the message on the receiver side. In contrast, our method requires performing matrix
474   multiplication, i.e., Wij ρ(ui ), and a bit-wise XOR operation on the sender side, and only a bit-wise
475   XOR operation on the receiver side, as the receiver can directly use Wij ρ(ui ) to compute gradients.
476   Given that the computational time of the XOR operation is negligible, we only compare the times of
477   AES encryption (denoted as “AES Enc”), AES decryption (denoted as “AES Dec”), and the sending
478   operation of our method (denoted as EFC, i.e., energy-based federated coordination). Table 3 displays
479   the results for handling ρ(ui ) of different dimensions (M = 20, 60, 100, 140, 180). It demonstrates
480   that the computational time of our method is an order of magnitude lower than that of AES, indicating
481   the computational efficiency of our method.

513   6   D ISCUSSION ON LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
514
515   As the first work addressing the challenging problem of federated coordination, our primary goal is
516   to establish a baseline framework and demonstrate its effectiveness. However, we recognize certain
517   limitations in our current approach and identify several promising directions for future research.
518
519
      Pre-determined mapping between energy states and strategies To simplify the setup and provide
520
      a clear proof of concept, our current implementation uses a pre-determined mapping between energy
521   states and strategies. While effective for validating the framework, this approach may limit flexibility
522   and adaptability in complex scenarios. Future research could explore dynamic and learned mappings,
523   enabling agents to autonomously adapt energy-strategy relationships to evolving environments
524   and coordination challenges. energy-based model could incorporate temporal dependencies to
525   handle dynamic environments, potentially leveraging time-evolving graph structures for inter-agent
526   interactions.
527
528   Pre-defined strategies Our approach differs intentionally from MARL by focusing on decentralized
529   alignment for pre-defined strategies. Integrating strategy learning mechanisms into our framework
530   represents an exciting avenue for future work.

      7   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In this paper, we introduce federated coordination, a new problem for decentralized strategy alignment
535   in multi-agent systems that preserves privacy. Using an energy-based model, our novel framework
536   synchronizes agents’ strategies without a central coordinator, reducing dependence on global com-
537   munication and maintaining privacy through an encryption-free protocol against eavesdropping and
538   information leakage. Extensive experiments show our framework’s efficiency, scalability, lower
539   computational demands compared to AES cipher, and robustness against communication link loss.
      This makes it a promising solution for decentralized, privacy-preserving multi-agent coordination.

      A.2   M ORE DISCUSSION ON RELATED WORK
731
732   The proposed federated coordination problem encompasses several essential ingredients, including
733   coordination, decentralized agreement, the existence of multiple strategies, and privacy. In this
734   section, we discuss the existing research areas that study each of these aspects.
735
      Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) aims to learn strategies for multiple agents to facilitate
736
      their coordination(Sunehag et al., 2017; Rashid et al., 2018; Son et al., 2019). It often adopts the
737
      centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) paradigm(Oliehoek et al., 2008; Hernandez-
738   Leal et al., 2019), where agents are trained centrally but execute strategies independently. While
739   MARL can achieve distributed coordination, the primary focus is on learning coordination strategies
740   rather than strategy alignment. Moreover, the learned strategies are typically stationary, making
741   them vulnerable to adversarial training attacks (Gleave et al., 2020). To enable agents to switch their
742   strategies dynamically, existing work often relies on a global signal to coordinate policy changes
743   (Cacciamani et al., 2021). This reliance on a global signal poses challenges in scenarios without a
744   centralized coordinator and can compromise the scalability and privacy of the system.
745   Consensus algorithms are widely used to achieve distributed agreement on a common value among
746   agents in a network (Proskurnikov et al., 2016; Li & Tan, 2019; Amirkhani & Barshooi, 2022). These
747   algorithms ensure that all agents eventually converge to the same value, facilitating coordinated

805
806   A static, normal-form adversarial team game (von Stengel & Koller, 1997; Anagnostides et al., 2023)
807   is defined by a tuple Γ(N , O, A, B, U ). Γ consists of a team of N cooperative agents N facing an
                                                                                        QN
808   opponent O. Each agent from N has a set of available actions Ai , so that A := i=1 Ai denotes
809   the joint action space of N . Also, the opponent O has a finite and nonempty set of actions B. We
      denote by a = (ai , . . . , aN ) ∈ A a joint action of N , and b ∈ B an action of the opponent O.

810
      U : A × B → R is a utility function. The cooperative agents share the same utility represented by
811   U (a, b) and the team game is assumed to be zero-sum, i.e., the opponent’s utility is −U (a, b).
812
813   Below, we formally define the set of coordination strategies from which cooperative agents choose.
814   Coordination strategy set Given two joint actions am and an of N , we say am is strictly better
815   than an if U (am , b) > U (an , b), ∀b ∈ B. A joint action am ∈ A is optimal with respect to
816   an opponent action b ∈ B if U (am , b) ≥ U (an , b), ∀an ∈ A. Let ≫ denote the strictly better
                            ∗
817   relationship and a → b denote the joint action a being optimal with respect to the opponent action
                                                                                   ∗
818   b. The set of coordination strategies S := {am ∈ A | ∃b ∈ B, am → b and ∀an ∈ A \ S, ∃b ∈
                        ∗
819   B such that an → b =⇒ ∃am ∈ S such that am ≫ an }. This means that for every am ∈ S, it is
820   optimal with respect to a certain opponent action b ∈ B. Furthermore, for any joint action an ∈       /S
821   that is optimal for a certain opponent action, there exists a joint action am in S that is strictly better
822   than an . It implies that for any joint action an ∈

823   or there exists at least a joint action am ∈ S that is strictly better than it. We can construct S by first
824   grouping all joint actions that are optimal for certain opponent actions and then removing any joint
825   actions for which a strictly better alternative exists.

964   A.7     D ISCUSSION ON SOLVABILITY OF E QUATION 4
965
      The existence of solutions to Equation 4 is mathematically intricate due to implicit constraints
966
      imposed by the topology A and the predetermined set of local minima {vl }.
967
968   The block matrix W defined in Equation 5 is subject to implicit constraints determined by the
969   topology A. To elucidate these constraints, consider a scenario where the inter-agent weights are
970   homogeneous, i.e., Wij = Wi′ j ′ for all indices i, j, i′ , j ′ . In this case, the block matrix can be
971   represented as a Kronecker product, W = A ⊗ W, implying that det(W) = det(A) det(W). If
      the communication network is singular, indicated by det(A) = 0, then the block matrix is inherently


                                                         18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       singular, resulting in det(W) = 0. Consequently, Equation 4 becomes unsolvable. In more general
973
       cases as outlined in Equation 5, the relationship involving W becomes more complex. There is no
974
       straightforward method to address solvability in the most general form, thus an optimization-based
975    approach is employed, utilizing J(W, b) to assess the problem’s solvability.
976
977    In this approach, W is treated as a parameter within a linear system. The solution to Equation (4) can
978    fall into one of three categories: (1) infinitely many solutions, (2) a unique solution, or (3) no solution,
979
       contingent on the relationship between the degrees of freedom (d.o.f) and the number of constraints.
       In both cases (1) and (2), the optimal value of J ∗ (W, b) is 0, while in case (3), J ∗ (W, b) is greater
980
       than 0.
981
982    Case (1): To eliminate this scenario, Algorithm 2 is employed to ensure that the number of constraints
983    is at least equal to the d.o.f. Subsequently, a small Gaussian noise (standard deviation< 0.0001) is
984    added to each preset binary-coded local minimum. This guarantees the linear independence of the
985
       equations specified by Equation 3, thereby eliminating case (1).
986    Case (2): This is the desired outcome. Adding Gaussian noises to the preset local minima results in
987    W and b that cause the real local minima to deviate slightly from the preset values, e.g., a preset
988    minimum of 3 may result in real minima of 3.00005, 3.0001, etc. However, since the converged state
989    is mapped to binary encoding, this ensures alignment with the preset local minima.
990    Case (3): In this instance, an optimal solution, denoted as W∗ and b∗ , can still be identified by
991    minimizing the least-squares error J(W, b). This solution does not exactly satisfy Equation (4),
992    and J(W∗ , b∗ ) quantifies the deviation of {vl } from the true local minima determined by W∗ and
993    b∗ . Given the convexity of J, the loss is distributed among {vl }. A small J(W∗ , b∗ ) (average loss
994    per local minimum < 0.1) indicates proximity of true local minima to each preset vl . The binary
995    coding mapping ensures that the true minimum aligns with the preset minimum. Conversely, a large
996    J(W∗ , b∗ ) suggests that the current communication topology is inadequate for achieving alignment,
997
       prompting adjustments to the topology prior to system deployment.
