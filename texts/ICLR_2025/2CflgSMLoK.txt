                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Data selection is designed to accelerate learning with preserved performance.
010
               To achieve this, a fundamental thought is to identify informative data samples
011            with significant contributions to the training. In this work, we propose Evolved
012            Sampling (ES), a simple yet effective framework for dynamic sampling performed
013            along the training process. This method conducts batch level data selection based
014            on differences of historical and current losses, significantly reducing the back
015            propagation time with modest additional overheads while maintaining the model
016            performance. Due to its conciseness, ES is readily extensible to incorporate set level
017            data selection for further training accelerations. As a plug-and-play framework, ES
018            consistently achieves lossless training accelerations across various models (ResNet,
019
               ViT, ALBERT), datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, GLUE), and optimizers (SGD, Adam),
               saving up to 40% wall-clock time. Particularly, the improvement is more significant
020
               under the noisy supervision setting. When there are severe corruptions in labels, ES
021
               can obtain accuracy improvements of approximately 20% relative to the standard
022            batched sampling. Our results motivate further investigations on the data efficiency
023            aspect of modern large-scale machine learning.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep learning has showcased remarkable performance across a variety of real-world applications,
029   particularly leading to unparalleled successes of large вҖңfoundationвҖқ models (Touvron et al., 2023;
030   Rombach et al., 2022). On the other hand, since these large models are usually trained on web-scale
031   datasets, the overall computation and memory loads are considerably increasing, calling for more
032   efficient developments of modern machine learning. Efficient learning involves several aspects,
033
      centering around models, data, optimization, systems, and so on (Shen et al., 2023).
034   For data-efficient machine learning, the core is to properly evaluate the importance per data sample
035   in the original (large-scale) datasets. A broad array of methods is applied in a static manner, where
036   the samplesвҖҷ importance is determined before the training. By leveraging the feature representations
037   of data (Swayamdipta et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023b), this importance can be evaluated based on a
038   variety of metrics such as distances (Har-Peled & Mazumdar, 2004; Huang et al., 2023; Bachem
039
      et al., 2015; Xia et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2023; Sorscher et al., 2022), uncertainties (Coleman et al.,
      2020; Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018; Margatina et al., 2021; Dasgupta et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021),
040
      errors (Toneva et al., 2019; Paul et al., 2021; Langberg & Schulman, 2010; Munteanu et al., 2018),
041
      etc, and learned via procedures from the meta optimization (Killamsetty et al., 2021c;b; Jain et al.,
042   2024; Wang et al., 2022) and dataset distillation (Nguyen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Zhao &
043   Bilen, 2023). However, these approaches can be prohibitively expensive to apply in practice, since
044   their dependence on feature representations requires additional (pre-)training in advance.
045
      Another array of methods lies in a dynamic sense, where the samplesвҖҷ importance is simultaneously
046
      evaluated along the training process. Dynamic sampling methods can be further divided into two
047
      categories: set level selection, to prune the whole dataset at the beginning of each epoch (Qin et al.,
048   2024; Raju et al., 2021; Thao Nguyen et al., 2023; Attendu & Corbeil, 2023), and batch level selection,
049   to sample subsets from original batches for back propagation (Kawaguchi & Lu, 2020; Katharopoulos
050   & Fleuret, 2017; 2018; Mindermann et al., 2022). Nevertheless, these dynamic sampling methods
051   leverage similar strategies to evaluate the samplesвҖҷ importance. Based on the naive intuition that
052   samplesвҖҷ contributions to the learning are directly associated with gradient updates, it is natural
053   to re-weight data samples with scales of gradients or losses during training. Sampling methods
      based on the gradients (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021a; Hanchi et al., 2022;

076   To tackle these challeges, we propose a novel dynamic sampling framework, Evolved Sampling (ES),
077   which incorporates the loss evolution or differences along the training process to determine samplesвҖҷ
078   importance and conduct batch level selection, without the demand of pre-trained reference models.
079   Importantly, ES employs the technique of decoupled exponential moving averages (EMAs) to compute
080   sampling weights/probabilities, where one iterative scheme is designed to mix current losses with
081   tracked weights updated by the single EMA over historical losses. Due to its simplicity, this procedure
082   is effortless to implement and only introduces mild computational overheads with negligible memory
083
      costs, while significantly reducing the number of samples used for back propagations (BPs) and
      consequently saving the overall wall-clock time, without degrading the model performance. Moreover,
084
      ES facilitates convenient extensions to data pruning on the set level, i.e. Evolved Sampling with
085
      Pruning (ESWP), leading to further accelerations with lossless model performance. We demonstrate
086   the differences in details between our proposed methods (ES/ESWP) and previous dynamic sampling
087   methods in Table 1.
088
089
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
090          вҖў On the theoretical side, we provide justifications that decoupled EMAs applied in ES(WP)
091            introduce additional differences of losses across time, which is a certain type of first-order
092            information, and hence appropriately alleviates the effect of loss oscillations on sampling
093            with more sufficient exploitation of the loss dynamics. From another perspective, ES with
094            gradient decent can be viewed as the solution to a distributionally robust optimization (DRO)
095            problem, where the reference objective is approximated via historical losses.
096          вҖў We carry out extensive experiments to verify the effectiveness, efficiency, robustness and
097            flexibility of ES(WP). It is shown that ES(WP) consistently achieves lossless training
098            accelerations across various models (ResNet, ViT, ALBERT), datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet,
099            GLUE), and optimizers (SGD, Adam), saving up to 40% wall-clock time. In addition, certain
100            hyper-parameters of ES are flexible to be tuned to trade-off between model performance
101
               and training costs. Moreover, ES(WP) also exhibits boosted performance particularly under
               the noisy supervision setting: Given severe noises in labels, ES(WP) can obtain significant
102
               accuracy improvements of approximately 20% relative to the standard batched sampling.
103
104   The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we discuss the related work on static and

106   former sampling approaches and corresponding theoretical justifications. Numerical experiments and
107   ablation studies are provided in Section 4. The discussions and outlook are provided in Section 5. All
      the details of proofs and experiments are found in the appendices.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
117

      Static sampling. Methods to sampling statically can be based on geometry, uncertainty, error, meta
      optimization, dataset distillation, etc. With numerous studies on theoretical guarantees (Har-Peled
119
      & Mazumdar, 2004; Huang et al., 2023; Bachem et al., 2015), the coreset selection is designed to
120
      approximate original datasets with smaller (re-weighted) subsets, typically achieved by clustering in
121   representation spaces (Xia et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2023; Sorscher et al., 2022). Uncertainty-based
122   methods use probability metrics such as the confidence, entropy (Coleman et al., 2020) and distances
123   to decision boundaries (Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018; Margatina et al., 2021; Dasgupta et al., 2019; Liu
124   et al., 2021). Sampling methods based on errors assume that training samples with more contributions
125   to errors are more important. Errors are evaluated with merics such as forgetting events (Toneva et al.,
126   2019), G RA N D & EL2N score (Paul et al., 2021), and sensitivity (Langberg & Schulman, 2010;
127   Munteanu et al., 2018). As is discussed before, these static sampling methods require extra training,
128   leading to considerable costs in both computation and memory.
129   Dynamic sampling. Methods to sampling dynamically typically leverage metrics based on losses
130   and gradients along the training process. Loss-adaptive sampling re-weights data points during the
131   training according to current losses (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2017; Jiang et al., 2019; Loshchilov &
132   Hutter, 2016; Schaul et al., 2016) and historical losses(Oren et al., 2019; Sagawa et al., 2020). To
133   name a few, Ordered SGD (Kawaguchi & Lu, 2020) selects top-q samples in terms of the loss ranking
134   per training step. InfoBatch (Qin et al., 2024) randomly prunes a portion of less informative samples
135
      with losses below the average and then re-scales the gradients. KAKURENBO (Thao Nguyen et al.,
      2023) combines current losses with the prediction accuracy and confidence to design a sampling
136
      framework with moving-back. Kumar et al. (2023) and Balaban et al. (2023) assign weights as
137
      functions of current losses based on the robust optimization theory. Attendu & Corbeil (2023) and
138   Raju et al. (2021) use the exponential moving average over past losses for sampling. There are also
139   studies adopting reference models, including Mindermann et al. (2022); Deng et al. (2023); Xie et al.
140   (2023a) and so on. These methods either exploit the information of losses inadequately, or require
141   to train additional architectures. Gradient-based sampling methods involve (i) gradient matching,
142   such as CRAIG (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) and G RAD -M ATCH (Killamsetty et al., 2021a), which
143   approximate the вҖңfullвҖқ gradients computed on original datasets via the gradients computed on subsets;
144   (ii) gradient adaption, where the sampling probability is basically determined by current scales of
145   gradients (Hanchi et al., 2022; Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018). A recent work (Wang et al., 2024)
146
      uses a intricate layer-wise sampling scheme with complex variance control. Obviously, gradient-based
      sampling methods lead to much more computation and memory overheads than loss-based methods.
147
148   Set level versus batch level. Dynamic sampling methods can be divided into two categories based
149   on the level where data selection is performed: (i) set level selection, to prune the whole dataset at
150   the beginning of each epoch (Qin et al., 2024; Raju et al., 2021; Thao Nguyen et al., 2023; Attendu
151   & Corbeil, 2023); (ii) batch level selection, to sample subsets from the original batches for back
152
      propagations (Kawaguchi & Lu, 2020; Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2017; 2018; Mindermann et al.,
      2022). These two types of methods, facilitating training accelerations from different perspectives, are
153
      not mutually exclusive. However, to the best of our knowledge, we are not aware of any algorithms
154
      combining both of them.

      5          C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we propose a simple yet effective framework, Evolved Sampling, which can be applied
533
      to general machine learning tasks to improve the data efficiency in a dynamic manner. By further
534   adopting differences of historical losses to determine samplesвҖҷ importance for data selection, Evolved
535   Sampling can achieve lossless training with significant accelerations, particularly when there are
536   severe noises in labels. Studies in the future may include three aspects: (i) More rigorous mathematical
537   analysis on the effect of data selection (e.g. Kolossov et al. (2024)); (ii) More specific applications,
538   such as data selection/reduction on domain mixtures (e.g. Chen et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023a)); (iii)
539   More efficient and scalable implementation, such as data parallelism (You et al., 2017; 2020). These
      directions are certainly worthy of explorations in the future.
