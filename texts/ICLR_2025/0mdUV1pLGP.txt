012                                               A BSTRACT
013
014            The Hawkes process (HP) is commonly used to model event sequences with self-
015            reinforcing dynamics, including electronic health records, stock trades, and social
016            media interactions. Traditional HPs capture self-reinforcement via parametric im-
017            pact functions that can be inspected to understand how each event modulates the
018            intensity of others. Neural network-based HPs offer greater flexibility, resulting in
019
               improved fit and prediction performance, but at the cost of interpretability, which
               can be critical in medicine and other high-stakes settings. In this work, we aim
020
               to understand and improve upon this tradeoff. We propose a novel HP formula-
021
               tion in which impact functions are modeled by defining a flexible impact kernel,
022            instantiated as a neural network, in event embedding space, which allows us to
023            model large-scale event sequences with many event types. This approach is more
024            flexible than traditional HPs, because we do not assume a particular parametric
025            form for the impact functions, yet more interpretable than other neural network
026            approaches, because self-reinforcing dynamics are still entirely captured by the
027            impact kernel, which can be inspected. If needed, our approach allows us to trade
028            interpretability for flexibility by contextualizing the event embeddings with trans-
029            former encoder layers. Results show that our method accurately recovers im-
030
               pact functions in simulations and achieves competitive performance on real-world
               datasets even without transformer layers. This suggests that our flexible impact
031
               kernel is often sufficient to capture self-reinforcing dynamics effectively, imply-
032
               ing that interpretability can be maintained without loss of performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The Hawkes process (HP) is a powerful tool for modeling event sequences with self-reinforcing
037   dynamics, making it applicable to domains such as electronic health records (EHRs) (Wang et al.,
038   2018), stock trading (Bacry et al., 2015), and social media interactions (Yang & Leskovec, 2011).
039   Unlike the Poisson process, which assumes events occur independently over time with a constant
040   intensity, the HP captures the influence of past events on the likelihood of future occurrences by in-
041
      troducing self-excitation. This self-exciting property allows the HP to model the clustering behavior
      frequently observed in real-world event data (Hawkes, 1971; Ogata, 1988). For instance, in EHRs,
042
      a patient’s sequence of diagnoses, prescriptions, and lab tests can reflect how each medical event
043
      influences subsequent treatments and health outcomes. While much research focuses on predicting
044   future events (Shchur et al., 2019; Mei & Eisner, 2017), we aim to build interpretable models that
045   reveal how events influence one another, shedding light on Granger causality.
046
      However, capturing the self-reinforcement inherent in traditional Hawkes Processes (HPs) often
047
      relies on parametric impact functions, which may fall short in representing the intricate patterns
048   found in real-world data (Linderman & Adams, 2014). To overcome these limitations, researchers
049   have increasingly turned to neural networks integrated with point processes, fostering the devel-
050   opment of more adaptable models. For instance, the Neural Hawkes Process leverages recurrent
051   neural networks to model conditional intensity functions, effectively capturing non-linear and long-
052   range temporal dependencies (Mei & Eisner, 2017). Similarly, the Transformer Hawkes Process
053   utilizes self-attention mechanisms to adeptly manage event dependencies within sequences of vary-
      ing lengths (Zuo et al., 2020). Additionally, the Intensity-Free method for learning Temporal Point

      Processes (TPP) offers a novel approach by directly learning the conditional density of inter-event
055   times, thereby circumventing the computationally intensive integrals required by traditional inten-
056   sity functions (Shchur et al., 2019). Another noteworthy advancement is the Neural Spatio-Temporal
057   Point Processes (ODETPP), which models the evolution of hidden states through neural ordinary
058   differential equations, simplifying previous models by focusing exclusively on temporal dependen-
059   cies without incorporating spatial components (Chen et al., 2020). While these innovations provide
060   richer representations of intensity functions, they often do so at the expense of interpretability (Xu
061   et al., 2020).
062   Many of these methods, while powerful, relax so many of the Hawkes process’s original assumptions
063   that they lose key features and become black-box models (Linderman & Adams, 2014). This lack
064   of transparency makes it difficult to interpret event interactions or uncover Granger causality, which
065   can be critical in fields like medicine, marketing, and biology (Eichler et al., 2017). In these do-
066   mains, understanding the relationships between events is as important as predicting the events them-
067
      selves. For example, in healthcare, identifying how medical events relate can guide clinical research
      and improve patient care (Liu et al., 2019), and emerging regulations governing use of models for
068
      clinical decision-making emphasize interpretability(U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2023). In
069
      business, uncovering how marketing events drive sales informs strategy (Hawkes, 2018). In animal
070   behavior studies, finding associations between behaviors can reveal biological mechanisms (Ward
071   et al., 2022). To serve these needs, we require models that balance flexibility with transparency.
072
      In this work, we aim to understand and improve the tradeoff between interpretability and flexibil-
073
      ity in HP models. To do this, we propose a novel HP formulation in which impact functions are
074   modeled by defining a flexible, neural network-based impact kernel in event embedding space. Our
075   formulation maintains the core formulation of the Hawkes process, combining a baseline intensity
076   with an impact function summed over all previous events, and thereby retains the Hawkes process’s
077   key properties, such as positive intensity and additive influence. However, to improve flexibility,
078   we replace the traditional exponential decay assumption(Hawkes, 1971) with neural network-driven
079   impact functions, allowing for more nuanced modeling of event dependencies without sacrificing
080   interpretability.
081   By working in embedding space, we limit the dimensionality of our impact kernel, which allows us
082   to model large-scale event sequences with tens of thousands of event types. Each dimension in this
083   space represents a broader event topic, with the impact kernel capturing the relationships between
084   these topics. This makes large-scale event modeling computationally feasible while enhancing in-
085   terpretability, as the interactions are understood at the topic level.
086   In some cases, it may be desirable to trade interpretability for greater flexibility. Our approach allows
087   users to explore this tradeoff directly by adding optional transformer encoder layers to contextualize
088   the embeddings of each event in a given sequence based on the previous history of events. Adding
089   these layers reduces interpretability and sacrifices the property of additive influence, but in principle,
090
      it also allows the model to capture more complex dependencies between event types. Importantly,
      however, our results show that this sacrifice is rarely necessary, because our flexible impact kernel
091
      alone is sufficient to capture the dynamics of most real-world sequences.
092
093   In summary, our contributions are:
094
095
            1. Novel Hawkes Process Formulation: We introduce a generalized Hawkes process where
096            impact functions are defined via a flexible, neural network-based impact kernel within an
097            event embedding space.
098
099
100         2. Controlled Tradeoff Between Interpretability and Flexibility: We develop a method to
101
               explicitly manage the balance between interpretability and model complexity by incorpo-
               rating transformer encoder layers to contextualize event embeddings based on the historical
102
               sequence of events.
103
104
105         3. Maintaining Interpretability Without Sacrificing Performance: We demonstrate that
106            in real-world settings, transformer encoder layers are often unnecessary to achieve state-
107            of-the-art performance, thereby maintaining interpretability without compromising the
               model’s effectiveness.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110   Generalized Hawkes Process Traditional Hawkes processes are limited in their ability to model
111   complex event dynamics due to their reliance on simple exponential decay for event intensity. To
112   address these limitations, neural network-based extensions have been developed. One key advance-
113
      ment is the Neural Hawkes Process (NHP), which generates events sequentially using recurrent neu-
      ral networks. NHP replaces the traditional intensity function with one parameterized by a recurrent
114
      model, allowing it to better capture dependencies between events.
115
116   Another generalized Hawkes process is the attention-based Hawkes process. One of the classic ex-
117
      ample is the Transformer Hawkes Process(THP)(Zuo et al., 2020). THP leverages attention mech-
      anisms to model event intensity, using transformer-based encodings to incorporate both temporal
118
      and contextual information. It enhances the intensity function by introducing terms to account for
119
      event timings and baseline effects. By doing so, THP captures the influence of past events on future
120   ones in a more flexible and context-aware manner. Like other generalized Hawkes processes, both
121   NHP and THP are trained to maximize the likelihood of observed event sequences, enabling them
122   to model more complex event interactions.
123
124   Self-Attentive Hawkes Process (SAHP) The Self-Attentive Hawkes Process (SAHP) (Zhang et al.,
125   2020) extends the traditional Hawkes process by incorporating a self-attention mechanism. This en-
126   hancement allows for better modeling of complex event dependencies and improves interpretability.
127
      SAHP quantifies the influence of historical events on future ones using attention weights, providing
      an interpretable measure of how past events affect subsequent occurrences. By accumulating these
128
      attention weights, SAHP effectively quantifies the statistical influence between different event types,
129
      making it valuable for both predicting event sequences and explaining event relationships.
130
131
      However, SAHP represents a significant departure from the classical Hawkes process, which models
      the impact of events through explicit time-decaying influence functions. In contrast, SAHP relies
132
      on learned attention weights without explicitly capturing the temporal dynamics of influence decay
133
      inherent in the original impact function.

      3.3   F ORMULATING THE I MPACT K ERNEL IN E MBEDDING S PACE
217
218   Let W ∈ RD×M represent the embedding matrix, where M is the number of event types and D
219   is the embedding dimension. The embedding for event type i is given by W e(i) , where e(i) is the
220   standard basis vector selecting the i-th column of W . Similarly, the embedding for event type j is
221   W e(j) . The embedding matrix W may be shared between the input and output events or may differ,
222   i.e., W1 e(i) for input and W2 e(j) for output events.
223   For a given time interval between events, we define an impact kernel K(∆t) ∈ RD×D in the em-
224   bedding space. In this formulation, the impact kernel is instantiated as a neural network with D2
225   outputs. The impact of an event of type i at time ti on the intensity of an event of type j at time tj
226   is expressed as:
227
228                              ϕi,j (tj − ti ) = (W e(i) )⊤ K(tj − ti )(W e(j) )                       (8)
229
230   Intuitively, for each event pair (ki , kj ), we select coloumns from the embedding matrix W and
231   compute a linear combination of the impact kernels, weighted by each event’s contribution, to re-
232   construct the impact function for that pair. This method, referred to as the Embedded Neural Hawkes
233   Process(ENHP), reduces the dimensionality of the impact kernels, making it well-suited for high-
234
      dimensional event spaces, while also capturing the sparse structure of event interactions. Addition-
      ally, the embedding matrix W introduces interpretability by mapping events to latent topics, where
235
      each dimension corresponds to a distinct attribute. To ensure the non-negativity of the intensity, we
236
      apply a softplus function to all components, including W , K(t), and µk .

275   4.1   BASELINE
276
277   All the intensity-based generalized Hawkes process models mentioned in the related work section
278
      will be included as baseline methods, including NHP, THP, and SAHP. Additionally, we include the
      following two methods:
279
280   Attentive Neural Hawkes Process : AttNHP(Yang et al., 2022) introduces an attention mechanism
281   into the traditional Neural Hawkes Process to enhance its ability to model event dependencies. In
282   this approach, instead of relying solely on a recurrent neural network to capture the influence of past
283   events, attention weights are applied to determine the relative importance of each historical event in
284   predicting future occurrences. This makes the model more accurate and easier to understand because
285   it focuses on the key events in the history.
286
      Fully Neural Network based Model for General Temporal Point Processes FullyNN-TPP(Omi
287   et al., 2019) uses a purely neural network-driven approach to model temporal point processes, re-
288   placing traditional hand-crafted intensity functions. This model leverages deep neural networks to
289   parameterize the conditional intensity function of events, directly learning complex temporal depen-
290   dencies. By employing flexible neural architectures, such as recurrent layers or temporal convolu-
291   tion, this method generalizes well across different types of event sequences without making strong
292   assumptions about the underlying temporal dynamics.

      5    C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this work, we addressed the challenge of modeling event sequences with self-reinforcing dynam-
522   ics by proposing a flexible Hawkes process model that maintains interpretability. Our approach
523   leverages a neural impact kernel in event embedding space, allowing it to capture complex event de-
524   pendencies without assuming specific parametric forms, while still retaining the core interpretability
525   of traditional Hawkes processes.
526   We also introduced a mechanism to balance flexibility and interpretability by adding transformer
527   encoder layers to contextualize event embeddings. However, our results showed that the flexible
528   impact kernel alone is often sufficient to model the dynamics of real-world sequences, reducing the
529   need for additional contextualization in most cases.
530   Overall, our method demonstrates competitive performance with existing models while maintaining
531   interpretability, making it suitable for high-stakes applications where understanding event inter-
532   actions is crucial. This balance between flexibility and interpretability can help inform decision-
533   making and uncover temporal dependencies in various domains.

628   A.2   E XPERIMENT: D IFFERENT MODEL DIMENSION VS LIKELIHOOD
629
630   In this experiment, we evaluate the performance of models on the MIMIC-IV and Meme datasets by
631   varying the embedding dimensions. The original event dimension of MIMIC-IV is 159, while that
632   of Meme is significantly larger at 5,000. As expected, the reduction in log-likelihood for MIMIC-IV
633
      remains relatively small when the dimension is reduced below 10, whereas Meme experiences a
      more pronounced decline. This is reasonable, given that Meme’s higher original dimension suggests
634
      more potential active relationships between event types. Despite this, both datasets achieve strong
635
      log-likelihood performance in lower dimensions (10-50), indicating that many event pairs in the
636   original impact kernel dimension are likely unrelated. This further suggests that the original impact
637   kernel may have a sparse structure. Our method effectively recovers the impact function even in low
638   dimensions, highlighting its ability to capture the essential relationships while reducing dimensional
639   complexity.
640   However, as shown in the figure4, there is a noticeable drop in performance for MIMIC-IV when
641   the dimension is set to 40. A potential reason for this decline could be the relatively long maxi-
642   mum sequence length in the MIMIC-IV dataset, which increases the GPU memory requirement for
643   computation. As a result, the batch size had to be limited to 16, which might cause instability in
644   training. Since the sequences in MIMIC-IV vary greatly in length, with some batches containing
645   very short sequences, this variability could lead to an unstable training process, ultimately affecting
646   the model’s performance.
