                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               As Large Language Models continue to advance, Reinforcement Learning from
               Human Feedback is increasingly regarded as a promising approach for enhancing
011
               their capabilities and achieving more sophisticated forms of intelligence. How-
012
               ever, the reliance on reward-model-based alignment methods introduces signifi-
013            cant challenges due to the inherent instability and imperfections of Reward Mod-
014            els (RMs), which can lead to critical issues such as reward hacking and misalign-
015            ment with human intentions. In this paper, we introduce a reward-robust RLHF
016            framework aimed at addressing these fundamental challenges, paving the way for
017            more reliable and resilient learning in LLMs. Our approach introduces a novel
018            optimization objective that carefully balances performance and robustness by in-
019            corporating Bayesian Reward Model Ensembles to model the uncertainty set of re-
020            ward functions. This allows the framework to integrate both nominal performance
021
               and minimum reward signals, ensuring more stable learning even with imperfect
               RMs. Empirical results demonstrate that our framework consistently outperforms
022
               baselines across diverse benchmarks, showing improved accuracy and long-term
023
               stability. We also provide a theoretical analysis, demonstrating that reward-robust
024            RLHF approaches the stability of constant reward settings, which proves to be ac-
025            ceptable even in a stochastic-case analysis. Together, these contributions highlight
026            the framework’s potential to enhance both the performance and stability of LLM
027            alignment.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reinforcement Learning (RL), particularly in the form of Reinforcement Learning from Human
033   Feedback (RLHF), has become a pivotal methodology for aligning foundational models with hu-
034   man values and preferences. It has played a crucial role in enhancing the capabilities of Large
035   Language Models (LLMs) to generate responses that are more helpful, harmless, and honest, con-
036   tributing to significant breakthroughs such as OpenAI’s o1 model (OpenAI, 2024). The standard
037   RLHF framework consists of two key phases. First, a Reward Model (RM) is trained on preference
038   data annotated by Human or Artificial Intelligence (AI) feedback.Following this, Proximal Policy
039
      Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) is applied to refine the model’s performance based on
      the learned reward function. This structured approach ensures that LLMs operate in a manner that
040
      is consistent with ethical guidelines and user expectations, thereby enhancing their capability and
041
      trustworthiness in practical applications.
042
043   The quality of the RM is crucial to the success of PPO. A poor RM may provide incorrect signals
044   for certain data points during the PPO training phase, ultimately compromising the performance of
045
      the fine-tuned model. Several issues arise from an imperfect RM. One such issue is reward hack-
      ing, where the model exploits flaws in the reward function, optimizing for behaviors that maximize
046
      the reward signal without genuinely improving task performance. Another challenge is overfitting
047
      and underfitting: an overfitted RM captures noise or specific patterns in the training data that fail
048   to generalize to new data, while an underfitted model may miss important patterns altogether (Gao
049   et al., 2023). Additionally, misalignment with human preferences can occur, as biases among anno-
050   tators—whether human or AI (Bai et al., 2022a; Lee et al., 2023)—make it difficult to align the RM
051   with the diverse preferences of humanity, leading to discrepancies between the model’s behavior
052   and human expectations. All the issues mentioned brings us to a critical question:
053
                Given that the RM is imperfect, how can we perform RM-based RLHF better?

054
      In this paper, we propose a reward-robust RLHF framework to overcome this challenge. To enhance
055   the model’s robustness to the reward signal while avoiding an overly conservative optimization pro-
056   cess, we introduce a novel objective that strikes a balance between performance and robustness.
057   The performance component is guided by a nominal RM, which serves as an approximation of the
058   ideal reward function. Meanwhile, the robustness component accounts for worst-case scenarios by
059   considering the uncertainty in the reward functions. To capture both the nominal reward and the
060   uncertainty, we introduce Bayesian Reward Model Ensembles (BRME). BRME utilizes a multi-
061   head RM, where each head outputs the mean and standard deviation (std) of a Gaussian distribution,
062   from which the final reward is sampled. BRME has two main advantages over traditional RM that
063
      generates a single scalar as the reward: First, we demonstrate that std can reflect the confidence of
      each head in its output reward, allowing the output with the lowest std to be reasonably selected
064
      as the nominal reward. Additionally, we show that BRME outperforms traditional RMs in both the
065
      coverage of the reward distribution and accuracy on preference test sets. Ablation study on the RM
066   training supports the claims above. The proposed reward-robust RLHF framework consistently out-
067   performs standard RLHF across several widely-used benchmarks. In long run training processes,
068   the proposed method shows stronger stability and better performance compared with the baselines.
069
      Beyond presenting empirical performance results, we also aim to provide deeper theoretical insights.
070
      First, we delve deeper into the inherent imperfections of RMs, arguing that even with an ideal
071
      annotator, a perfect reward function is unlikely, and the resulting RM is inherently insufficient.
072   We design a synthetic toy model that directly illustrates these limitations. Second, we provide a
073   theoretical justification for the superiority of our method over standard RLHF. Given that the actual
074   reward is inherently biased—resulting in either over-scoring or under-scoring during training—we
075   show that, in the long run, under-scoring is preferable to over-scoring. Third, we conduct a stochastic
076   case analysis within our reward-robust RLHF framework and prove that the resulting policy remains
077   acceptable, as stochastic scenarios often arise when the RM handles out-of-distribution (OOD) data.
078   Our analysis shows that the robustness regularization term narrows the reward distribution, making
079   the training process closer to the constant reward setting thus more stable, which is preferable to the
080
      uncontrollable optimization driven by badly assigned rewards.
081   Contributions. 1) We propose a reward-robust RLHF framework, introducing BRME to model both
082   nominal rewards and uncertainty, outperforming traditional RMs in reward distribution coverage and
083   accuracy. 2) We provide theoretical insights into RM imperfections, showing through a synthetic
084   toy model that even with an ideal annotator, a perfect reward function is unattainable. 3) We show
085   that under-scoring is preferable to over-scoring in long-term training, given the inherent bias in
086
      accessible rewards. Additionally, we conduct a stochastic case analysis, demonstrating that the
      proposed framework remains effective in OOD scenarios, with the robustness regularization term
087
      stabilizing the training process by narrowing the reward distribution.

092   2.1   R EWARD -M ODEL - BASED A LIGNMENT IN LLM S
093
      The core idea behind RM-based alignment in is to use a RM, typically trained on human/AI-
094
      annotated data, to guide the optimization of the language model’s policy (Christiano et al., 2017;
095
      Ouyang et al., 2022). Although RM-free algorithms such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) and
096   IPO (Azar et al., 2024) have also been developed, it is generally observed that these methods tend to
097   be less effective compared to RM-based approaches in many scenarios (Xu et al., 2024; Yan et al.,
098   2024b). While RM-based approach has shown success in several domains, it is not without chal-
099   lenges. One major issue is the potential misalignment between the RM’s output and the true human
100   preferences, which can lead to unintended behaviors, commonly known as reward hacking (Amodei
101   et al., 2016). Additionally, the reward model’s generalization ability is often limited by the quality
102   and diversity of the annotated data, which can result in overfitting to specific data points and un-
103   derfitting to others (Lee et al., 2021). These shortcomings underscore the importance of developing
104
      alternative methods that enhance reward robustness.
105   Recent research has explored various approaches to address these challenges. Shen et al. (2024)
106   introduces a penalty term on the reward, named as contrastive rewards, to improve the effectiveness
107   of RMs. Eisenstein et al. (2023) finds that RM ensembles can help mitigate reward hacking in certain
      scenarios. Zhang et al. (2024b) proposes a lightweight uncertainty quantification method to assess


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the reliability of the reward model to avoid over-optimization. Zhang et al. (2024a); Zhai et al. (2023)
109   uses low-rank adaptation (LoRA) to increase the diversity of RMs to improve the performance of
110   RLHF. Yang et al. (2024b) retains the language model head and add regularization to improve the
111   generalization capability of RMs.
112
      Our work differs from the above research in several key aspects: 1) We propose a generally robust
113
      RLHF framework that outperforms standard RLHF across most benchmarks tested. Within this
114
      framework, we employ BRME to model an uncertainty set and introduce a trade-off objective that
115   balances nominal performance and robustness, rather than simply using the mean or median of all
116   the rewards as done in Eisenstein et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b). In BRME, we use a Bayesian
117   multi-head RM to characterize the uncertainty of each reward head, which distinguishes from Zhai
118   et al. (2023). The effectiveness of BRME is supported by ablation studies. 2) Using a synthetic
119   toy model, we demonstrate the inherent imperfections of the reward model, even when utilizing an
120   unbiased and ideal annotator. This finding builds upon and complements prior research on annotator
121   disagreement (Dubey et al., 2024). 3) We provide a theoretical explanation for the superiority of our
122   method through a stochastic-case analysis. Given that the actual reward is inherently biased, tending
123
      either to over-score or under-score, we first demonstrate that, over the long term, under-scoring is
      preferable to over-scoring.

      2.2   ROBUST R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
127   In the realm of robust RL, robustness is primarily focused on addressing uncertainties related to tran-
128   sition, observation, action, or disturbance (Moos et al., 2022). Transition-robust approaches deal
129   with uncertainties in system dynamics by deliberately adjusting state transition probabilities (Heger,
130   1994; Nilim & El Ghaoui, 2005; Satia & Lave Jr, 1973; Givan et al., 2000). Observation-robust
131   methods involve distorting the perceived system state to influence policy decisions (Zhang et al.,
132   2020; Gleave et al., 2019). Action-robust designs modify system transitions by introducing distur-
133   bances to the agent’s actions (Tessler et al., 2019). Disturbance-robust strategies account for external
134   forces that introduce uncertainty into system behavior (Pinto et al., 2017).
135   In contrast, there has been comparatively less focus on reward robustness. Xu & Mannor (2006)
136   consider an MDP with an uncertain reward function and then propose a weighted sum between a
137   nominal and a robust performance criterion, which directly inspires our work. The trade-off can be
138   directly made on the expected return (Xu & Mannor, 2006) or by defining a chance constraint opti-
139   mization problem (Delage & Mannor, 2010). Other research targets the rectangularity assumption,
140   which assuming that the uncertainty in each state is independent of all other states, identifying it as
141
      a primary source of the reward uncertainty (Mannor et al., 2012; 2016; Goyal & Grand-Clement,
      2023). Vadori et al. (2020) proposes a risk-sensitive RL approach to handle the reward uncertainty
142
      by applying Doob decomposition on the reward. Wang et al. (2020) develops a robust RL framework
143
      where the agents can only observed perturbed rewards.
144
145   In conclusion, due to the focus of past RL applications, particularly in domains like robotics, re-
146   search on reward robustness has not received sufficient attention. Notably, in the context of LLM
147
      training, the uncertainty inherent in the reward model significantly impacts final performance and
      hinds the progress of LLM development (Chen et al., 2024). Coste et al. (2023) introduce worst-case
148
      optimization in LLMs to mitigate overoptimization. However, their approach relies on a purely em-
149
      pirical method to characterize uncertainty through intra-ensemble variance, lacking both structure
150   and interpretability. Therefore, the development of specialized reward-robust algorithms is not only
151   necessary but also urgently required. Our work is one of the pioneering efforts to introduce reward
152   robustness into the LLM RLHF/RLAIF pipeline.

188   4   I NHERENT I MPERFECTION OF R EWARD M ODELS
189
190   In this section, we demonstrate that imperfection is an inherent characteristic of RMs in
191   RLHF/RLAIF pipelines. This imperfection arises from two key factors: 1) Disagreements between
192   annotators, which significantly affect the quality of the preference dataset. 2) The inherent difficulty
193   in achieving an optimal reward model, even with perfectly aligned annotators.
194   The issue of disagreement among human annotators in the RLHF pipeline has been noted in previous
195   research (Bai et al., 2022a). More recently, researchers have begun using AI as annotators in what is
196   known as RLAIF, claiming comparable or even superior performance to RLHF (Lee et al., 2023; Bai
197   et al., 2022b). However, our evaluation experiments revealed that in the RLHF process, the scoring
198   consistency between human annotators and domain experts was approximately 70%, whereas in the
199   RLAIF process, the consistency between multi-agent AI annotators and domain experts dropped to
200
      around 64%. The details can be found in Appendix A.
201   Even more surprising is the finding that, even with a perfectly aligned annotator, obtaining an opti-
202   mal RM is nearly impossible. To demonstrate this, we introduce a simple toy model similar to that
203   in Xu et al. (2024); Yan et al. (2024b). We construct discrete spaces consisting of 8 prompts and
204   8 responses. The LLM policy πθ is modeled as a three-layer MLP that processes a one-hot vector,
205   representing a specific response, to produce a categorical distribution over the responses. The best
206
      match for each input prompt is the response sharing the same index.
207   When constructing the preference dataset, assuming we have an ideal annotator, we can ideally select
208   the perfect match for each input prompt—essentially the diagonal elements of the matrix—and two
209   random other elements to create two preference data pairs.
210   The RM trained on this preference dataset and the actor model learned from the PPO process is
211   shown in Figure 1. It is evident that both the reward model and the actor deviate significantly
212   from the optimal. The underlying reasons can be attributed to 1) insufficient data coverage, and
213   2) disturbances in model training. In more complex real-world semantic spaces, these issues are
214   amplified, further degrading the quality of the trained RM. It’s important to note that in this synthetic
215   experiment, only the coverage of the responses was considered. In actual scenarios, insufficient
      coverage of the prompts themselves can severely affect RM training as well.

      and repeated PPO training under each setting for 800 steps. It is important to note that when λ = 1,
359
      the algorithm degrades to standard RLHF with a single nominal RM. The performance evaluation
360   results at 200 and 800 steps are shown in Figure 3.
361
362   In the short run (200 steps), although there are a few exceptions—such as ANLI-r1 and LAMBADA,
363
      where λ = 0 outperforms the other settings—in most cases, the trade-off versions with λ = 0.4 and
      λ = 0.6 show better performance. This suggests that, even early in training, incorporating a balance
364
      between performance and robustness offers notable advantages. The ability of the reward-robust
365
      RLHF framework to temper the optimization process appears to result in more stable performance
366   gains compared to standard RLHF. Compared with standard RLHF, the average accuracy of reward-
367   robust RLHF increases by 0.99% and 1.40% respectively when λ = 0.4 and 0.6. On certain datasets,
368   such as arithmetic and ANLI-r3, the improvement exceeds 3%.
369
      Over the long run (800 steps), the advantages of incorporating robustness measurement become
370
      even more pronounced. Nearly all experimental groups with λ ̸= 1 exhibit improved performance
371
      by the end of 800 steps, confirming the long-term benefits of reward-robust RLHF. In contrast,
372   standard RLHF with λ = 1 not only fails to capitalize on the full optimization process but, in
373   some cases, even results in negative performance growth. For example, in tasks like ANLI-r1 and
374   GSM8K, we observe a decrease in model capability during the additional 600-step optimization
375   process under standard RLHF. This highlights a key limitation of the purely performance-driven
376   RLHF approach: its vulnerability to the risk of overfitting or misguidance from imperfect reward
377   signals. Compared with standard RLHF, the accuracy of reward-robust RLHF increases by 2.42%
      and 2.03% respectively when λ = 0.4 and 0.6.

408   6         D ISCUSSION
409
410   In this section, we provide insights into why the proposed reward-robust RLHF framework is ef-
411   fective and how it improves upon the previous standard pipeline. Given that the reward signal is
412   inherently imperfect, we empirically demonstrate that over-scoring is more detrimental than under-
413   scoring, which supports our choice of minimum return as a robustness measure. Additionally, we
414   show that in the stochastic case where rewards are given randomly, selecting the minimum reward
415
      ensures that the trained model in PPO remains at least acceptable. In Section 6.3, we also do abla-
      tion study to compare our method with traditional RM with MLE loss as well as other integration
416
      strategies such as using mean reward.
417
418
      6.1       OVER -S CORING VS . U NDER -S CORING
419
420   We have demonstrated that RMs are inherently imperfect (Section 4), meaning the rewards used in
421   the PPO training are either over-scored or under-scored. Through PPO training on minimum reward
422   and maximum reward, we show that over-scoring is significantly more harmful than under-scoring
423   in PPO training. The results of this comparison are illustrated in Figure 4a. In 12 of the total 16
424   benchmarks, under-scoring setting outperforms the over-scoring setting (see Table 4, Appendix C.2).
425
      We also specifically analyzed the dynamic performance changes under two different settings. We
426   conducted PPO training for 1000 steps, measuring performance every 200 steps. Generally, training
427   with the minimum reward setting proved to be more stable: in the early stages, in some tasks such
428   as ARC and PIQA, the minimum reward setting showed slower progress compared to the maxi-
429   mum reward setting. However, as training continued, the minimum reward generally led to a more
430   consistent and stable improvement, whereas the maximum reward setting gradually resulted in per-
431   formance degradation. A comprehensive result on all datasets is provided in Figure 5a. Additional
      results and discussions are deferred to Appendix C.

432
      In our reward-robust RLHF framework, rewards tend to be under-scoring. To see this, we test
433   BRME on UltraFeedBack (Cui et al., 2024), diverse preference dataset with annotated rewards. We
434   approximately treat the annotation as the ground-truth reward, measuring the scoring gap of each
435   reward head as well as the minimum reward head and the ground-truth reward. Note that the rewards
436   are normalized with respect to its resource. The result is shown in Figure 5b. For a single head, the
437   number of under-scoring and over-scoring cases is roughly equal. However, in the reward-robust
438   RLHF setting, selecting the minimum rewards leads to under-scoring becoming more frequent.
439   We propose the following hypotheses to explain these observations: 1) In reward-robust RLHF, the
440   optimization process tends to be more conservative, which could benefit long-term exploration. In
441   essence, reward-robust RLHF might prioritize optimizing the lower bound of the return function. 2)
442   In language tasks, exploring an incorrect direction may be more detrimental than rejecting a correct
443   one. For a given prompt, there are often multiple valid responses. Even if the model misses one
444   correct optimization path, it may still be able to explore alternative directions. However, with a
445   more aggressive optimization strategy (such as over-scoring), the model might be more prone to
446   pursuing a wrong direction, potentially resulting in reward hacking.

448   6.2   S TOCHASTIC C ASE A NALYSIS
449
450   If the RM is poorly trained or handling OOD data, the extreme scenario is that rewards are given
451
      randomly. In this section, we demonstrate that even in such a stochastic case, the reward-robust
      RLHF framework still yields an acceptable model, and we provide an explanation for why this is the
452
      case. First, we have the following lemma,
453
454   Lemma 1. If the reward model provides a constant reward for all actions during PPO training, the
455   actor will not be optimized, as the gradient of the PPO objective function with respect to the policy
456
      parameters will be zero.
457
      The proof for Lemma 1 is straightforward since it is easy to find the advantage functions for all
458
      states will be all zero when the reward is a constant. We defer the complete proof to Appendix E.
459
460   If we use reward robust RLHF training (setting λ = 0 in Eq. 7), as we are choosing the minimum
461   reward, the range for reward will be narrowed (see Figure 5b and Figure 8b) and is closer to the con-
462
      stant reward situation. Since the starting point of our training is usually a well-trained SFT model,
      even if the optimization process degrades, the model can still maintain relatively good performance.
463
      In the contrast, if we use a random reward model, the optimization will be uncontrollable and the
464
      model will collapse in a fast speed.
465
466   We also conduct PPO experiments for 200 steps with random rewards on real LLMs. The exper-
467   imental setup is identical to that described in Section 5.2, except that the rewards from each head
468
      were sampled from a Gaussian distribution N (0, 1). A control group was also set up, in which all
      rewards were set to zero. The results are shown in Figure 4b. Although nearly all performances
469
      declined, the constant reward setting exhibits the smallest decrease, while the random reward setting
470
      shows the largest decline. The minimum random reward setting falls in between, indicating that it
471   can help the model remain stable even in a highly unpredictable random-reward environment.
472
473
      Remark 1. In the practice, the model optimization process is likely to oscillate, but it will eventually
      converge back to the inital-SFT model in a long run theoretically. The reason why it will oscillate
474
      is that the critic model is not ideal in the early stage, so the advantage A   bt is not 0 even all the
475                                                                           πθ (a|x)
476   rewards given are identical. However, as the KL divergence (-β log π0 (a|x) ) is also considered in
477   the optimization objective (4), the actor policy will be eventually pulled back close to the reference
478   policy.

521   7       C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE WORKS
522
523   In this paper, we proposed a reward-robust RLHF framework to address the problem of reward
524   hacking in LLM alignment. We demonstrated that imperfection is an inherent characteristic of cur-
525
      rent reward model training, leading to the model exploring incorrect optimization directions. To
      mitigate this issue, we trained BRME with multi-head outputs to model the uncertainty set of the
526
      reward function. We showed that the head with the minimum std effectively models the nominal
527
      reward function, representing the most confident scoring output. The newly proposed robustness-
528   performance trade-off objective was proven effective, consistently outperforming baselines across
529   most benchmarks. Furthermore, we demonstrated that under-scoring is preferable to over-scoring
530   when dealing with imperfect RMs. Even in the stochastic-case scenario, where rewards are as-
531   signed randomly, the reward-robust RLHF framework still yields an acceptable model. Finally, we
532   acknowledge the framework’s limitations, which are discussed in detail in Appendix G.
533
      Since our method can be easily integrated into existing pipelines, there is potential to further improve
534   performance by incorporating additional reward sources to better model the uncertainty set. Future
535   work will explore the adoption of heterologous reward sources, including RMs trained on diverse
536   datasets and direct scoring from closed-source LLM APIs such as GPT-4, as well as other markers
537   mentioned in (Yan et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023). The advantage of using heterologous models lies
538   in their diverse base training datasets, which result in more varied reward scores, thereby improving
539   the coverage of the uncertainty set. Preliminary exploration results on heterologous reward fusion
      are provided in Appendix F.

752   For human-annotated data, we conducted an agreement test on 209 data points, each containing one
753   prompt and two responses. The prompts were selected from an internal dataset by the PM team, and
754   the responses were generated by Baichuan2-13B (Yang et al., 2023). Data categories include general
755   knowledge, logical reasoning, tables, mathematics, etc. We used two distinct annotator groups: one
      consisted of highly educated internal annotators who had undergone multiple rounds of specialized


                                                     14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      annotation training, referred to as the Expert Group. The other group was composed of external
757   annotators hired from the general public, referred to as the External Group. For each data point,
758   annotators were tasked with a Good-Same-Bad evaluation: 1) G: response 1 is better than response
759   2. 2) S: both responses are of the same quality. 3) B: response 1 is worse than response 2. We
760   compared the G/S/B annotations for the same data between the two groups. Across all samples, the
761   consistency rate was 70%, with a 4.5% rate of opposite judgments (one group scored G while the
762   other scored B). When only considering G/B samples, the consistency rate increased to 77%, with
763   an opposite rate of 6.5%.

765   ferred to as the AI Group. The PM team biasedly sampled 85 examples, focusing on cases where
766   the Expert Group and External Group showed inconsistent labeling. For these data points, the con-

768   External Group and the AI Group was 44%. In 9% of the cases, the Expert Group differed from both
769   the External Group and the AI Group.

1060   signals are averaged, which can smooth out the variability in individual rewards but may also mask
1061   valuable distinctions between reward sources. Comparing it with other strategies allows us to assess
1062   how different approaches to integrating reward signals impact the stability and performance of the
1063   model during training. The results are presented in Figure 9.
1064   The performance of the mean strategy consistently falls between the min and max strategies, indicat-
1065   ing that while averaging rewards offers a more balanced approach, it does not capture the advantages
1066   of more targeted integration strategies. Specifically, in the later stages of training (after 800 steps),
1067   the mean strategy shows a tendency toward performance decline, suggesting that the averaging pro-
1068   cess may dilute the reward signal over time, leading to suboptimal policy learning. This is especially
1069   evident in tasks requiring precise optimization, where overly smoothing the reward signal prevents
1070
       the model from fully leveraging high-quality responses.
1071   On most datasets where PPO has a significant effect, the reward-robust RLHF setting with a trade-
1072   off parameter of λ = 0.6 outperforms the mean strategy. The trade-off strategy balances nominal
1073   performance with robustness, allowing the model to benefit from both high-performing and con-
1074   servative reward signals. This balance is crucial in long-term training, as it mitigates the risk of
1075   overfitting to specific rewards and helps maintain consistent performance gains.
1076   The mean strategy, while easier to implement, lacks the ability to differentiate between high-quality
1077   and low-quality reward signals effectively. By averaging all signals, it fails to account for the under-
1078   lying uncertainty or variance in the RM. In contrast, the reward-robust RLHF approach, particularly
1079   with λ = 0.6, preserves the benefits of more robust exploration and ensures that the model can
       continue improving in the later stages of training, as observed in the experiments.

       G     L IMITATIONS
1281
1282
       While the proposed reward-robust RLHF framework shows improved performance on automatic
1283
       evaluation benchmarks, several non-negligible limitations remain. First, for some prompts, if all
1284   reward heads in the BRME fail to provide correct signals and exhibit similar error patterns, the
1285   reward hacking behavior seen in standard RLHF cannot be entirely mitigated, even with the reward-
1286   robust approach. This suggests that the ultimate optimization still heavily depends on the model’s
1287   capacity and generalization ability, particularly in handling out-of-distribution (OOD) data. As a
1288   result, improving the quality of the reward model and the data used to train PPO is likely to be the
1289   most critical factor influencing long-term training success.
1290   Second, some of the theoretical hypotheses proposed to explain the experimental results in Sec-
1291   tion 6.1 still require further validation. Conducting fine-grained analysis, particularly regarding the
1292   specific impact of over-scoring and under-scoring signals on the PPO exploration process, will be
1293   highly valuable. Addressing these gaps will be one of our future research focuses.
