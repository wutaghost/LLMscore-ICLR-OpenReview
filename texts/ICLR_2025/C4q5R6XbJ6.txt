009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) excel at multimodal perception and
012            understanding, yet their tendency to generate hallucinated or inaccurate responses
013            undermines their trustworthiness. Existing methods have largely overlooked the
014            importance of refusal responses as a means of enhancing MLLMs reliability. To
015            bridge this gap, we present the Information Boundary-aware Learning Framework
016            (InBoL), a novel approach that empowers MLLMs to refuse to answer user queries
017            when encountering insufficient information. To the best of our knowledge, InBoL
018            is the first framework that systematically defines the conditions under which re-
019            fusal is appropriate for MLLMs using the concept of information boundaries pro-
020
               posed in our paper. This framework introduces a comprehensive data generation
               pipeline and tailored training strategies to improve the model’s ability to deliver
021
               appropriate refusal responses. To evaluate the trustworthiness of MLLMs, we
022
               further propose a user-centric alignment goal along with corresponding metrics.
023            Experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in refusal accuracy
024            without noticeably compromising the model’s helpfulness, establishing InBoL as
025            a pivotal advancement in building more trustworthy MLLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have marked a significant
032   breakthrough in AI research, especially in vision-language tasks (McKinzie et al., 2024; Bai et al.,
033
      2023; Tong et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). By integrating visual
      information with large language models (LLMs), these models have exhibited profound capabilities
034
      in multimodal understanding and reasoning, allowing them to perform complex tasks. Despite the
035
      impressive progress, MLLMs still face notable challenges. One prominent issue is their tendency to
036   generate factually incorrect or hallucinated content, where models confidently describe non-existent
037   visual elements or provide responses that include incorrect knowledge (Bai et al., 2024; Zhong
038   et al., 2024). Such hallucinations not only reduce the accuracy of the models but also undermine
039   their truthfulness in practical applications, hindering them from being trustworthy AI assistants.
040
      To improve the trustworthiness of MLLMs, previous works primarily focus on improving multi-
041
      modal alignment algorithms to enhance the models’ perceptual and reasoning capabilities, thereby
042   increasing the truthfulness of their outputs (Yu et al., 2024a;b; Amirloo et al., 2024). However, all
043   models have intrinsic limitations in their knowledge and perceptual capabilities, making them prone
044   to produce inaccurate or misleading responses when confronted with tasks beyond their capabilities.
045   Therefore, another effective approach to improving trustworthiness is to train these models to rec-
046   ognize their boundaries and refuse to answer questions when appropriate. While refusal responses
047   may not directly assist the user, they are truthful since no misinformation is provided.
048   Despite the critical role of refusal responses, few studies have focused on effectively training
049   MLLMs for this capability. Existing approaches (Liu et al., 2023b; Cha et al., 2024) primarily
050   target ambiguous or unanswerable queries, such as those involving non-existent visual elements, but
051   fall short of addressing the broader challenges related to intrinsic limitations and self-awareness in
052   MLLMs (Wang et al., 2024b). This gap underscores the need for strategies that enable MLLMs to
053   recognize their limitations, ensuring they either provide accurate responses or appropriately refuse
      to answer when necessary.

054
      While research on trustworthiness in MLLMs is still limited, efforts to improve reliability by training
055   models to refuse answering unknown questions have been extensively studied in LLMs (Amayue-
056   las et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Liang
057   et al., 2024). These studies typically generate instruction and preference data that include refusal
058   responses, guiding models to avoid answering questions beyond their knowledge boundaries. Trust-
059   worthiness is evaluated by examining how well a model can recognize its limitations—providing
060   helpful responses within its knowledge scope and abstaining from answering questions outside of it.
061   However, applying this framework to MLLMs introduces several unique challenges. In multimodal
062   scenarios, trustworthiness depends not only on the model’s knowledge but also on its interpretation
063
      of visual input and perceptual capabilities, adding complexity to training. Additionally, evaluating
      trustworthiness requires classifying test questions as ‘known’ or ‘unknown’ based on the model’s
064
      knowledge boundary—a task complicated by the often ambiguous nature of these boundaries. More-
065
      over, knowledge boundaries vary between models, making consistent comparison of trustworthiness
066   across models using a common, model-agnostic evaluation set particularly challenging.
067
068   To address these limitations, we propose novel approaches for both training and evaluating the trust-
069
      worthiness of MLLMs. For model training, we introduce the Information Boundary-aware Learning
      Framework (InBoL), the first to establish the concept of information boundaries and systematically
070
      define the conditions under which MLLMs should appropriately refuse to respond. This marks a
071
      significant advancement in trustworthiness training for MLLMs. Building on these boundaries, we
072   develop a data construction pipeline that generates ‘I Don’t Know’ (IDK) instruction and preference
073   data from any VQA dataset. Using this data, we implement two key training methods: IDK Instruc-
074   tion Tuning (IDK-IT) and Confidence-aware Direct Preference Optimization (CA-DPO), which en-
075   ables MLLMs to recognize their information boundaries and refuse to answer when necessary. To
076   evaluate model trustworthiness, we propose a novel alignment objective centered on human prefer-
077   ences rather than intrinsic model metrics. We argue that users consider an MLLM as trustworthy
078   when it provides as many helpful responses as possible while minimizing misinformation. This
079   user-centric approach simplifies evaluation and introduces a model-agnostic framework for assess-
080
      ing trustworthiness. Our experimental results demonstrate that InBoL significantly improves the
      trustworthiness of baseline models by enhancing their ability to appropriately refuse responses while
081
      maintaining helpfulness. This work introduces a new paradigm for developing trustworthy MLLMs
082
      and sets the foundation for future advancements in this critical area.
083
084   Overall, the key contributions of our work are as follows:
085
             • InBoL Framework: We propose the InBoL framework, which introduces the novel con-
086
               cept of information boundaries and integrates a comprehensive data construction pipeline
087            along with tailored training methods. InBoL enhances the trustworthiness of MLLMs by
088            empowering them to recognize these boundaries and refuse to answer when lacking suffi-
089            cient information, setting a new benchmark for trustworthiness training.
090
             • User-centric Trustworthiness Evaluation: We introduce a novel, user-centered alignment
091            objective that shifts the focus of evaluation from model-based metrics to human prefer-
092            ences. This approach simplifies the evaluation process and is model-agnostic. Addition-
093            ally, we present several metrics that offer a comprehensive and generalizable method for
094            evaluating the trustworthiness of different MLLMs.
095          • Experimental Validation: We conduct extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness
096            of our approach, demonstrating significant improvements in MLLMs’ ability to recognize
097            information boundaries while preserving helpfulness. Our detailed analyses offer valuable
098            insights into the broader impact of this method, paving the way for future developments in
099            trustworthy MLLMs.

195   3     I NFORMATION B OUNDARY-AWARE L EARNING FRAMEWORK
196
197   To enhance the trustworthiness of MLLMs, we propose the Information Boundary-Aware Learning
198   Framework (InBoL). This framework includes a data construction pipeline designed to generate
199   model-specific ‘IDK’ instruction and preference data by considering the intrinsic and extrinsic in-
200
      formation boundaries of MLLMs. Furthermore, we incorporate ‘IDK’ instruction tuning (IDK-IT)
      and confidence-aware direct preference optimization (CA-DPO) for model training. The goal of
201
      this framework is to improve the model’s ability to provide appropriate refusal responses, thereby
202
      reducing misinformation and increasing reliability of MLLMs.
203
204
      3.1   I NFORMATION B OUNDARY
205
206   The core of our framework is to train MLLMs to recognize when to refuse, thereby avoiding the gen-
207   eration of misinformation. While previous work on LLMs generally restricts refusals to questions
208   outside the model’s knowledge boundary, multimodal scenarios introduce additional complexity, as
209   both visual information and knowledge must be considered.
210
      To address this, we introduce ‘extrinsic’ and ‘intrinsic’ information boundaries for MLLMs, as
211   illustrated in Figure 1. In multimodal scenarios, a trustworthy MLLM should answer questions
212   only when it has sufficient information and refuse when it does not, and these boundaries serve as
213   guidelines for this decision-making process. We define the information boundaries as follows:
214
215          • Extrinsic Information Boundary: In multimodal scenarios, MLLMs depend on extrinsic
               visual inputs to respond to user queries. The extrinsic information boundary defines the

      3.2   DATA C ONSTRUCTION
256
      To train MLLMs to appropriately refuse questions, we need to collect the three types of VQA data
257   outlined in Figure 1. For any given VQA dataset, we propose a data construction pipeline that classi-
258   fies questions into the first two types based on confidence estimation, while generating unanswerable
259   questions as the third type from the available data. Additionally, we reorganize the generated data
260   into a standardized format, as shown in Figure 2, which is then used to create the ‘IDK’ instructions
261   and preference data.
262

263

264   boundary. To further determine if these questions fall within the intrinsic information boundary, we
265   estimate the model’s confidence. Following prior works (Cheng et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Yang
266   et al., 2023), we sample multiple responses from the original model and calculate the accuracy rate
267   to estimate its confidence. We found that simple string matching—checking if the correct answers
268   appear in the generated responses—was insufficient, as the model sometimes generates semantically
269   similar answers that differ in wording. To address this issue, we develop a hybrid evaluator by
      utilizing a LLM to evaluate responses marked as incorrect by the string-matching method. More

      3.3   M ODEL T RAINING FOR I NFORMATION B OUNDARY AWARENESS
320
321   To enhance the model’s ability to recognize and refuse questions beyond its information boundary,
322   we propose two training strategies: ‘IDK’ Instruction Tuning (IDK-IT) and Confidence-aware Di-
323   rect Preference Optimization (CA-DPO). These strategies can teach the model not only to provide
      accurate responses but also to give refusal responses when it lacks the necessary information.

      IDK Instruction Tuning Instruction tuning is an effective method for aligning the model’s re-
325   sponses with desired behaviors. In our framework, we train the model with ‘IDK’ instructions. This
326   training approach improves models trustworthiness by reducing the generating misinformation.
327
328   Confidence-aware Direct Preference Optimization Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is a
329
      technique that optimizes a model’s policy using preference data (Xu et al., 2023; Rafailov et al.,
      2024; Hong et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). While DPO can guide models to prefer correct answers
330
      and learn to refuse when needed, it does not leverage the model’s intrinsic confidence to dynamically
331
      adjust its behavior. To address this, we propose Confidence-aware DPO (CA-DPO), which integrates
332   the model’s confidence score into the optimization process.
333
334   As shown in Figure 3, we define two preference pairs for ‘Mixed’ samples: p1 (correct > incorrect)
335
      and p2 (refusal > incorrect). For consistency, we define ‘Known’ samples with p1 = p2 = (correct
      > refusal), and ‘Unknown’ samples with p1 = p2 = (refusal > incorrect). Our approach uses the
336
      confidence score to dynamically balance the emphasis between these preference pairs. The CA-DPO
337

732   alignment. Additionally, Wang et al. (2024a) introduces a multimodal DPO objective that optimizes
733   both image and language preferences, avoiding the over-prioritization of language-only preferences.
734
735   A.2   I MPROVING TRUSTWORTHINESS BY R EFUSAL
736
737   With the increasing capabilities of foundational models and the growing prevalence of AI agents,
738   the trustworthiness of (multimodal) large language models has garnered significant attention. For
739   LLMs, researchers primarily focus on the reliability of the model’s knowledge, aiming for mod-
740   els to acknowledge their limitations and refuse to answer when encountering unknown knowledge.
741
      Yang et al. (2023) construct an honesty alignment dataset based on models’ knowledge boundaries,
      replacing incorrect or uncertain LLM responses with “I don’t know,” and fine-tuning the model on
742
      this data. Cheng et al. (2024) proposed the concept of “Knowledge Quadrants,” constructed the IDK
743
      dataset, and applied supervised fine-tuning (SFT) as well as preference-aware optimization to help
744   models recognize their intrinsic knowledge boundaries. Zhang et al. (2024a) introduced R-tuning,
745   which involves constructing and fine-tuning on a refusal-aware dataset, enhancing model’s capabili-
746   ties to refuse answering appropriately. Chen et al. (2024) directly judged whether the knowledge lies
747   within the boundaries based on the model’s intrinsic state and constructed training data to help the
748   model express these boundaries. Liang et al. (2024) and Xu et al. (2024) employed Reinforcement
749   Learning from Knowledge Feedback to teach models to refuse questions outside their knowledge
750   boundaries, thus reducing hallucinations.
751   In multimodal scenario, only few works have considered the issue of refusal to answer. Unlike uni-
752   modal models, which focus on intrinsic boundaries, MLLMs mainly concentrate on the challenge
753   of unanswerable questions. Liu et al. (2023a) proposed three types of negative instructions involv-
754   ing misleading or false premises in images, which models must learn to refuse. Cha et al. (2024)
755   introduce the VQAv2-IDK dataset, which also annotates questions with “I don’t know” answers to
      train models to appropriately refuse to respond when faced with unanswerable or ambiguous ques-


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      tions. Additionally, Shi et al. (2024) and Wang et al. (2024b) included subsets with unanswerable
757   questions to evaluate the trustworthiness of MLLMs. Despite these advances, no prior work has
758   systematically considered both the intrinsic boundaries of models and the extrinsic information pro-
759   vided in the input. Therefore, we propose the I-BaLF framework, which holistically integrates both
760   aspects to guide MLLMs in refusing to answer when appropriate, thus significantly improving their
761   overall trustworthiness.

      B     L IMITATION
764
765
      In this work, we did not explore the generation of explanations for refusal responses, an important
766
      and underexamined area. From the model’s perspective, many questions require reasoning processes
767
      to determine whether sufficient information is available to provide an accurate answer. By incorpo-
768   rating explanations for refusal responses, the model could better learn when to refuse appropriately,
769   thereby enhancing its awareness of its own limitations and boundaries. From the user’s perspec-
770   tive, unexplained refusals may lead to confusion or dissatisfaction. Providing clear and interpretable
771   justifications for refusals could make the refusal mechanism more transparent and user-friendly,
772   significantly improving the overall user experience.
773
      For future work, we plan to focus on enabling the model to generate well-reasoned and contextually
774   appropriate refusal explanations. This will involve developing methodologies for constructing rele-
775   vant datasets and designing robust evaluation frameworks to assess the quality and relevance of the
776   generated explanations. By making refusal responses more informative and transparent, we aim to
777   further enhance the trustworthiness of the model while ensuring a more positive and engaging user
778   experience.

789   We found that an MLLM’s output answer can contain the ground truth answer or generate a phrase
790
      with identical semantic meaning. Thus, using string matching only to evaluate an LLM’s capacity is
      insufficient. In this paper, we propose to employ hybridized string matching and LLM-based evalu-
791
      ation methods to evaluate the accuracy of models. During our hybrid evaluation, we first use string
792
      matching to filter the model outputs that contain the exact ground truth answer and use Llama2-13B
793   to check whether the remainder contains phrases that express the same semantic meaning.
794
795   To verify the effectiveness of our hybrid evaluator, we randomly sample 200 MLLM outputs from
796
      the VQAV2 and OVEN datasets for human annotators to assess the consistency between our hybrid
      evaluator and human evaluation. In these 200 samples, the Cohen’s Kappa coefficient between our
797
      hybrid evaluator and human evaluator is 0.885, which is significantly higher than the coefficient of
798
      0.749 for string matching. This result demonstrates the strong alignment between our method and
799   human judgment.

1052   G.4    D ISTINGUISHING I NTRINSIC AND E XTRINSIC I NFORMATION D EFICITS
1053
1054   As mentioned in Section 3.1, we categorize “unknown” questions into two types based on whether
1055   the model fails to answer due to a lack of visual information or internal knowledge. A key question
1056   is whether the model can differentiate between these two cases—that is, whether it can identify the
1057   specific type of information it lacks. To investigate this question, we conducted a linear probing
1058
       experiment to explore whether the model’s internal representations encode features that differentiate
       between these two types of “unknown” cases.
1059
1060   We selected 2,000 ”unknown” questions from the training dataset, characterized by a confidence
1061   score below 2, and fed them into the MLLM. For each question, we extracted the hidden state
1062   from the final layer of the model. We then trained a three-layer linear classifier to categorize these
1063   ”unknown” questions into two classes. To evaluate the classifier, we used a set of 200 “unknown”
1064
       questions (confidence scores below 2) from the in-domain test set. The classification accuracy of
       the linear probing experiment is shown in Table 7.
1065
1066   The results demonstrate that a simple linear classifier achieves high accuracy in distinguishing be-
1067   tween intrinsic and extrinsic deficits based on the hidden states of the MLLM. This suggests that
1068   the model’s internal representations encode features that effectively differentiate between these two
1069   types of “unknown” questions. This finding indicates that the model has the potential to recognize
1070
       the source of missing information, even though its explicit refusal responses do not currently artic-
       ulate this distinction. Incorporating this capability into the model’s outputs could further enhance
1071
       trustworthiness by providing more transparent refusals. Future work could leverage this potential
1072
       by introducing mechanisms for explanation behind refusals, thereby aligning the model’s behavior
1073   more closely with user expectations for trustworthy AI systems.
