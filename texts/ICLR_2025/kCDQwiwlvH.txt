009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual active search (VAS) has been introduced as a modeling framework that
012            leverages visual cues to direct aerial (e.g., UAV-based) exploration and pinpoint
013            areas of interest within extensive geospatial regions. Potential applications of
014            VAS include detecting hotspots for rare wildlife poaching, aiding in search-and-
015            rescue missions, and uncovering illegal trafficking of weapons, among other uses.
016            Previous VAS approaches assume that the entire search space is known upfront,
017            which is often unrealistic due to constraints such as a restricted field of view
018
               and high acquisition costs, and they typically learn policies tailored to specific
               target objects, which limits their ability to search for multiple target categories
019
               simultaneously. In this work, we propose DiffVAS, a target-conditioned policy
020
               that searches for diverse objects simultaneously according to task requirements
021            in partially observable environments, which advances the deployment of visual
022            active search policies in real-world applications. DiffVAS uses a diffusion model to
023            reconstruct the entire geospatial area from sequentially observed partial glimpses,
024            which enables a target-conditioned reinforcement learning-based planning module
025            to effectively reason and guide subsequent search steps. Our extensive experiments
026            demonstrate that DiffVAS excels in searching diverse objects in partially observable
027            environments, significantly surpassing state-of-the-art methods across datasets.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Consider a scenario where a search-and-rescue mission is underway, and rescue personnel needs to
033
      scan across hundreds of potential regions from a helicopter to locate a missing person. A crucial
      strategy in such operations involves using UAVs to capture aerial imagery that can help identify a
034
      target of interest (e.g., the missing person). However, constraints like a limited field of view, high
035
      acquisition costs, time constraints, and restricted bandwidth between the sensor and the processing
036   unit can make the search extremely challenging, demanding strategic decisions on where to query
037   next based on the observations gathered so far. A similar challenge arises in other scenarios, such
038   as locating a specific vehicle in an abduction case – however, note that the target may differ, but the
039   underlying problem structure remains the same. In fact, many other scenarios share this general
040   structure, such as anti-poaching enforcement (Fang et al., 2015), pinpointing landmarks, identifying
041   drug or human trafficking sites, and more (Fang et al., 2016; Bondi et al., 2018).
042   In this work, we derive and formalize a general task setup that encompasses these types of scenarios,
043   and that allows for controllable and reproducible model development and experimentation. We
044   refer to our proposed task setup as Target-Conditioned Visual Active Search in Partially Observable
045   environments (TC-POVAS), the details of which are given in Sec. 2. The setup of TC-POVAS is as
046   follows: Given a target category (or multiple target categories, depending on the task requirement),
047   one should leverage a series of partially observed glimpses – which are sequentially queried during
048   active exploration – to locate as many target objects as possible. Note that the number of allowed
049   queries is limited in TC-POVAS, to reflect factors such as time or resource constraints.
050   TC-POVAS builds on the visual active search (VAS) framework in which one aims to find a target
051   object using visual cues through sequential exploration (Sarkar et al., 2023; 2024a). Past work on
052   visual active search (VAS) has assumed access to a complete description of the search space (typically
053   an image that spans the whole area) for making decisions. However, in many real-world situations,
      e.g. search-and-rescue operations, an entire image of the search space may not be available upfront.

054
      For example, an autonomous drone on a rescue mission might only be able to capture partial glimpses
055   through a series of narrow observations due to constraints like a confined viewing range and high data
056   collection costs. In these scenarios, the agent has to make decisions with incomplete information,
057   and thus models trained assuming access to complete images will struggle.
058
      The challenge is twofold: (i) the agent must query the most informative patch from a partially
059
      observed scene to maximize information gain about the search space, and (ii) it must simultaneously
060
      ensure that this patch helps achieve the goal of locating regions containing the target objects. One
061   might question why an agent cannot simply learn to choose patches that reveal target regions directly,
062   without the need for acquiring knowledge about the underlying scene. The challenge arises because
063   reasoning in an unknown partially observable environment is inherently difficult. Thus, an agent must
064   strike a balance between exploration – identifying patches that reveal the most information about
065   the search space – and exploitation – focusing on areas likely to contain the target object based on
066   updated knowledge about the environment. An optimal agent must master this delicate balance to be
067   effective. Additionally, previous VAS policies (Sarkar et al., 2023; 2024a) are designed to search
068   for specific target objects and cannot handle multiple categories simultaneously, which limits their
069
      adaptability to specific task preferences.
070   To address these challenges, and to effectively tackle the TC-POVAS task setting, we propose DiffVAS,
071   a novel framework that consists of two key modules: (1) a diffusion-based conditional generative
072   module (CGM) and (2) a target-conditioned planning module (TCPM). The goal of the CGM is to
073   learn how to reconstruct the entire scene (search space) contingent on the partially observed glimpses
074   gathered so far. To achieve this, we employ a neural network architecture that enables precise control
075
      over image generation by conditioning the diffusion-based generative model on the partially observed
      glimpses. Such a CGM attains fine control over image generation by integrating input conditions, like
076
      previously observed glimpses, directly into the model’s intermediate layers, influencing the output at
077
      various stages of the diffusion process. This layered integration allows the model to align closely
078   with the input conditions, ensuring that the generated image adheres to the desired structure while
079   benefiting from the diffusion model’s generative capabilities.
080
081
      The objective of the TCPM is to decide which patch to query next by analyzing the scene generated
      by the CGM and the partially observed glimpses, with the aim of revealing as many target regions as
082
      possible within the query budget. To accomplish this, the TCPM must learn to simultaneously explore
083
      the environment efficiently to maximize information gathering (exploration) and select patches
084   that reveal as many target regions as possible based on its acquired knowledge of the environment
085   (exploitation). To this end, we develop an RL-based policy that learns to balance exploration
086   and exploitation. To train the policy, we design a reward function that – besides encouraging
087   target discovery – takes into account two key factors: local uncertainty and global reconstruction
088   quality. Together, these factors measure how effectively the policy issues actions that contribute
089   to gaining information about the environment. Additionally, we designed the TCPM to be target-
090   conditioned, which enables it to search for different target categories according to task requirements
091   and handle multiple categories simultaneously. We accomplish this by introducing an inference
092
      strategy that leverages target-conditioned probability distributions over grid cells for each target
      category, computed via TCPM, and learning target-aware state representation by leveraging cross-
093
      attention. Finally, we conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of DiffVAS.
094
095   In summary, we make the following contributions:
096   • We introduce TC-POVAS, a novel task setup that addresses target-conditional (TC) visual active
097     search (VAS) in partially observable (PO) environments, and which extends traditional VAS to
098     become more closely aligned with practical scenarios.
099   • We propose DiffVAS, an agent that effectively tackles this task by reconstructing the whole search
100     area as it explores and searches for targets. Unlike previous approaches, DiffVAS can search a
101     diverse range of target objects and tackle multiple target categories simultaneously.
102   • We demonstrate the significance of each component within DiffVAS through a comprehensive
103     series of quantitative and qualitative ablation analyses.
104
      • Our extensive experimental evaluations using two publicly available satellite imagery datasets
        (xView and DOTA), across various unknown target settings, demonstrate that DiffVAS significantly
105
        outperforms all baseline approaches. The code and models will be made public.

177   In this section we introduce DiffVAS, a diffusion-guided, reinforcement learning (RL)-based agent
178
      designed to address VAS in partially observable environments. DiffVAS is composed of two main
      modules: (1) a conditional generative module (CGM) and (2) a target-conditioned planning module
179
      (TCPM). Next, we detail each component of the proposed DiffVAS framework, starting with the
180
      training strategy for both modules to learn an efficient policy, followed by the inference procedure.

      3.1   T RAINING
183
184   Our approach uses a two-phase training strategy: In the first phase, we train the CGM, and then we
185   freeze its parameters while training the TCPM in the second phase. The purpose of the CGM is to
186   synthesize the entire scene (i.e., the search space) from the partially observed glimpses collected so far,
187   thereby assisting the TCPM in deciding the next query location. To achieve this, the conditional gen-
188   erative model leverages a diffusion-based adapter-style approach (Mou et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023).
189   Diffusion models are powerful
190
      generative models that allow for
      precise control over the attributes
191
      of the generated samples. While
192
      these diffusion models trained on
193   large datasets have achieved suc-
194   cess, there is often a need to intro-
195   duce additional controls in down-
196   stream fine-tuning processes. In
197   our case, the CGM finetunes the
198   diffusion model by integrating in-

200   served glimpses xht while pre-

511   5    R ELATED W ORK
512
513   Visual active search (VAS). The VAS framework was first introduced by Sarkar et al. (2024b),
514   who framed it as a budget-constrained MDP and tackled it using deep RL. Sarkar et al. (2023;
515
      2024a) introduced a meta-learning approach that enables the policy to utilize supervised information
      gathered during the search. Key limitations of previous VAS approaches are the reliance on full
516
      observation of the search area and the focus on learning policies tailored to specific target objects,
517
      making them incapable of handling multiple target categories simultaneously. Similar to VAS, is
518   the task of active geo-localization (Pirinen et al., 2022; Sarkar et al., 2024c), in which an agent with
519   aerial view observations of a scene seeks to actively localize a goal. However, that task considers
520   only the single-target location and assumes access to an observation of the target location.
521   Autonomous UAV exploration. Methodologically, our work also falls within the broad scope of
522   literature within autonomous control and navigation of UAVs (Dang et al., 2018; Popović et al., 2020;
523   Stache et al., 2022; Meera et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021; Bartolomei et al., 2020; Sadat et al., 2015).
524   Many of these prior works (Wu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Thavamani et al.,
525   2021; Meng et al., 2022a;b) assume access to a global lower-resolution observation of the whole area
526
      of interest, while DiffVAS reconstructs the region of interest from partial observations.
      Active scene/object reconstruction. There is extensive prior work on active reconstruction of scenes
527
      and/or objects (Jayaraman & Grauman, 2016; 2018; Xiong & Grauman, 2018; Pirinen et al., 2019).
528
      However, these methods typically focus solely on optimizing for reconstruction, while our ultimate
529   goal is identifying target-rich regions. Success for our task hinges on balancing exploration (obtaining
530   useful information about the scene) and exploitation (finding objects of interest).
531
532   6    C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   We have presented DiffVAS, a novel multi-target visual active search approach that generalizes across
535   domains. At its core is a diffusion-based conditional generative module (CGM) that dynamically
536   reconstructs the search area, enabling the target-conditioned planning module to plan movements
537   effectively in a partially observable environment. Furthermore, our inference method enables DiffVAS
538   to handle tasks that involve searching multiple target categories simultaneously, with varying category
539   counts. Trained with a novel reward balancing exploration and exploitation, DiffVAS outperforms
      strong baselines and prior methods, while demonstrating excellent zero-shot generalization.
