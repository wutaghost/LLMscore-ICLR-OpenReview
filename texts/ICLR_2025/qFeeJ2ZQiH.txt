009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning requires models to train continuously across consecutive tasks
012            without forgetting. Most existing methods utilize linear classifiers, which strug-
013            gle to maintain a stable classification space while learning new tasks. Inspired
014            by the success of Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KAN) in preserving learning
015            stability during simple continual regression tasks, we set out to explore their po-
016
               tential in more complex continual learning scenarios. In this paper, we introduce
               the Kolmogorov-Arnold Classifier (KAC), a novel classifier developed for con-
017
               tinual learning based on the KAN structure. We delve into the impact of KAN’s
018
               spline functions and introduce Radial Basis Functions (RBF) for improved com-
019            patibility with continual learning. We replace linear classifiers with KAC in sev-
020            eral recent approaches and conduct experiments across various continual learning
021            benchmarks, all of which demonstrate performance improvements, highlighting
022            the effectiveness and robustness of KAC in continual learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep learning models are typically trained on a fixed dataset in a single session, achieving im-
      pressive performance on various static tasks. In contrast, real-world scenarios continuously evolve,
028
      necessitating models that can learn incrementally from a data stream. However, in such scenar-
029
      ios, these models often encounter a significant challenge known as catastrophic forgetting (French,
030   1999). Continual learning (De Lange et al., 2022; Belouadah et al., 2021; Parisi et al., 2019; Go-
031   lab & Özsu, 2003) investigates how to effectively train models in such dynamic environments with
032   sequential data exposure, aiming to adapt and avoid forgetting over time.
033
      Class incremental learning (CIL) (Rebuffi et al., 2017), as a key challenge in continual learning,
034
      has garnered extensive research interest. It involves the continuous introduction of new classes with
035
      ongoing tasks, requiring the model to conduct classification on all encountered classes after training
036   on new tasks. Most CIL methods retain exemplars and employ techniques, such as knowledge distil-
037   lation (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Douillard et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2024) or dynamic architectures (Chen
038   & Chang, 2023; Douillard et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024b), to mitigate forgetting.
039   With the rise of pre-trained models, numerous studies (McDonnell et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023)
040   have explored their applications in CIL, achieving impressive results. Among these, prompt-based
041   approaches (Wang et al., 2022d;c; Smith et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024b) have attracted considerable
042   attention.
043   Among existing methods, McDonnell et al. (2024); Goswami et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2020) focused
044   on feature space design through carefully crafted classifiers and training or inference strategies,
045   achieving excellent performance. These studies demonstrate that a well-structured feature space can
046   effectively mitigate forgetting because a stable distribution is crucial for continual classification tasks
047   while the design of classifiers is essential for constructing the feature space and reducing forgetting
048   in continuous tasks. However, most existing approaches (Gao et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024; Smith
049   et al., 2023) utilize linear classifiers or nearest class mean classifiers (NCM) (Rebuffi et al., 2017),
050
      with limited research focused on developing a specific classifier for CIL to effectively mitigate
      catastrophic forgetting. Therefore, designing an efficient classifier that replaces the existing simple
051
      classifiers and enhances current approaches will significantly advance the development of CIL.
052
053   Recently, a novel model architecture, Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KAN) (Liu et al., 2024) ,
      has been proposed, demonstrating natural effectiveness in continual learning. The authors compare

      In this paper, we present the Kolmogorov-Arnold Classifier (KAC), a plug-and-play classifier for
      Continual Learning based on the KAN architecture. Leveraging the Kolmogorov-Arnold represen-
081
      tation theorem (Kolmogorov, 1961), we incorporate learnable activation functions on the edges of
082
      the classifiers. We find that the conventional KAN with B-spline functions struggles with high-
083   dimensional data, leading to inadequate model plasticity, which may weaken the models’ plasticity
084   when directly introduced as a classifier. This limitation forces models to undergo excessive updates
085   when learning new tasks, resulting in significant forgetting.
086
      To address this, we explore spline functions and identify Radial Basis Functions (RBF) as an effec-
087
      tive alternative for continual learning. By utilizing RBF in our KAC, we enhance the model’s ability
088
      to adapt CIL while minimizing forgetting. Thanks to these learnable spline activations, the KAC
089   allows the model to select specific activation ranges of interest for each channel while preserving
090   the distribution of other parts, and RBF makes it more compatible with CIL. As shown in Fig. 1b,
091   these learnable activations help the model select interesting parts of each channel and activate them
092   for determination rather than activating all edges like a simple linear classifier in Fig. 1a. This brings
093   notable benefits to class incremental learning. When new tasks arrive, the learnable activation func-
094   tions assist the model in selecting relevant parts of each channel for updating. This prevents the
095   drift of irrelevant features during the training process for the new tasks. Meanwhile, the deactivated
096   portions of the old tasks remain unaffected by these updates, reducing the forgetting of old tasks.
097   To demonstrate the superiority of KAC, we conduct experiments on several prompt-based continual
098   learning approaches, which are built upon a pre-trained backbone where the classifiers play a key
099   role in these approaches. The models employing our method achieve significant improvement across
100   various CIL scenarios on multiple datasets by simply replacing the linear classifier with our KAC
101   without making any other modifications or hyperparameter adjustments. Additionally, experiments
102   conducted in the Domain Incremental Learning (DIL) (Wang et al., 2022b) setting reveal that our
103
      method can also improve performance, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
117
      Class Incremental Learning aims to learn a sequence of classification tasks sequentially, where
118
      the number of classes increases with each task. The primary challenge in it is catastrophic for-
119
      getting(McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). Several studies work on it and they can be broadly cate-
120   gorized into three main strategies: regularization-based, structure-based, and replay-based meth-
121   ods. Regularization-based methods reduce forgetting by employing knowledge distillation tech-
122   niques(Wen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022; Douillard et al., 2020) or imposing constraints on key
123   model parameters(Kang et al., 2022; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Structure-based methods(Chen &
124   Chang, 2023; Wang et al., 2022a; Douillard et al., 2022) mitigate forgetting through dynamic net-
125   work architectures. Replay-based methods retain a small portion of old data(Jeeveswaran et al.,
126   2023; Rebuffi et al., 2017) or use auxiliary models(Kim et al., 2024a; Gao & Liu, 2023; Shin et al.,
127   2017) to generate synthetic data, which are combined with new-class data to update the model.
128   CIL with Pre-trained Models have demonstrated their competitive performance in Class Incre-
129   mental Learning due to their strong transferability. Techniques such as LAE (Gao et al., 2023) and
130   SLCA (Zhang et al., 2023) enhance model adaptation through EMA-based updates and dynamic
131   classifier adjustments. RanPAC (McDonnell et al., 2024) employs random projection to improve
132   continual learning, while EASE (Zhou et al., 2024) focuses on optimizing task-specific, expandable
133   adapters to enhance knowledge retention.
134   Benefiting from parameter-efficient tuning in NLP, prompt-based methods have achieved promising
135   results in Class Incremental Learning. These approaches utilize adaptive prompts to guide frozen
136   transformer models, facilitating efficient task-specific learning without modifying encoder parame-
137   ters. Techniques like L2P (Wang et al., 2022d), DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022c), S-Prompts (Wang
138   et al., 2022b), CODA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023), HiDe-Prompt (Wang et al., 2024), and CPrompt
139   (Gao et al., 2024a) introduce diverse prompt selection strategies to improve task learning, knowledge
140
      retention, and model robustness.
141   Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KAN) (Li, 2024) is a novel network architecture based on the
142   Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem (Kolmogorov, 1961). It represents multivariate func-
143   tions as combinations of multiple univariate functions and uses nonlinear spline functions for ap-
144   proximation. Some explorations focus on how to apply KAN to solve scientific problems (Koenig
145   et al., 2024; Bozorgasl & Chen, 2024; Howard et al., 2024), while others seek various basis functions
146
      to enhance performance (Aghaei, 2024; Bozorgasl & Chen, 2024; Li, 2024). Many works (Bresson
      et al., 2024; De Carlo et al., 2024; Genet & Inzirillo, 2024; Malashin & Mikhalkova, 2024) ap-
147
      ply KAN across various fields and investigate how to effectively leverage its advantages in these
148
      domains. These studies encourage us to explore the application of KAN in continual learning.

211   3.2           C ONVENTIONAL KAN LAYER IS NOT A GOOD CONTINUAL CLASSIFIER
212
213   In Liu et al. (2024), the authors present experimental results from a toy 1D regression task, demon-
214   strating that the locality of splines can inherently avoid catastrophic forgetting. This insight inspires
215   us to introduce KAN to CIL. A straightforward way to leverage the locality of KAN is directly utiliz-
      ing a KAN layer to develop a continual classifier, replacing the linear classifier in CIL methods. To


                                                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      achieve this, we simply replace the linear classifier h(x) with a KAN layer that has an input dimen-
217   sion of d and an output dimension of C. We compared their performances across several baseline
218   methods. The experimental results are shown in Fig. 2, demonstrating that the simple substitution of
219   replacing the linear classifier with a KAN layer does not lead to any improvement, even achieving
220   worse performance.
221
      We decompose the KAN layer into two parts: the residual connection b(x) and the B-spline curve
222
      spline(x) and individually replace the linear classifier with these two components to investigate
223   why directly introducing the KAN layer increases forgetting. A surprising finding is that the B-
224   spline functions lead to a severe performance drop across all baselines.
225
226
      To understand why the B-spline curve replacing the conventional linear classifier leads to severe
      forgetting, we need to delve deeper into the differences between linear layers and splines. In high-
227
      dimensional complex data, spline functions encounter the curse of dimensionality (COD) (Hammer,
228
      1962); as the data dimensionality increases, the model struggles with data approximation (Köppen,
229   2002; Montanelli & Yang, 2020; He, 2023). This is because splines cannot effectively model the
230   compositional structure present in the data, while linear classifiers benefit from their fully con-
231   nected structure, allowing them to learn this structure effectively (He & Xu, 2023). Although KAN
232   networks mitigate COD through approximation theory (Liu et al., 2024) by stacking KAN layers,
233   approximating high-dimensional function remains a challenging problem for a single spline layer,
234   whereas it is relatively straightforward for conventional linear classifiers.
235   It is precisely the weak fitting ability of B-spline functions on high-dimensional data that leads to
236   severe forgetting when it is introduced into CIL. In CIL, a network typically consists of a backbone
237   F that encodes images to feature embeddings and a classification head h, which serves as a high-
238   dimensional projection mapping the embeddings to class probabilities. Most methods accommodate
239   new classes by adding classifiers while sharing the backbone across all tasks. The final logits l for
240   classification are always calculated as:
241
242                                    l = h(F (x)), h = [h1 , h2 , · · · , ht ].                         (4)
243
244
245   To prevent significant forgetting caused by changes in the backbone that affect the feature space, the
246
      model must maintain stable backbone parameters during training on new tasks. Consequently, many
      methods use regularization techniques to restrict changes in feature embeddings (Li & Hoiem, 2017;
247
      Kim et al., 2024b; Wen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022). However, due to the limited approximation
248
      capability of a single B-spline layer, the model requires more extensive updates to the backbone
249   parameters compared to conventional linear classifiers to achieve good performance on new tasks.
250   This extensive updating can severely disrupt the feature space, leading to pronounced forgetting.
251
252
      Based on the above analysis, we believe that the weak fitting ability of a single B-spline function
      prevents the model from leveraging the locality of the KAN layer. Therefore, we need to enhance the
253
      spline function’s fitting ability to adapt the KAN structure to CIL tasks. Lin et al. (2017); Lai & Shen
254
      (2021) indicates that, in specific senses, a shallow KAT-based layer can break the COD problem
255   when approximating high-dimensional functions through designed basis functions with particular
256   compositional structures, motivates us to explore the types of basis functions that are compatible
257   with CIL.

      4.3    A BLATION S TUDY
465
466   Ablation on the number of basis functions. The number of basis functions N is a key hyperpa-
467   rameter of KAC. An excessive number of basis functions may lead to additional computations and
468   result in a significantly high dimensionality of W . Conversely, a small N may weaken the approx-
469   imation ability of KAC. To explore an appropriate value for N , we conduct an ablation study on it.
470   Fig. 5 shows the average incremental accuracy for four approaches using KAC with different num-
471   bers of basis functions in the 20 steps experiment on ImageNet-R. It indicates that simply increasing
472   the number of basis functions does not benefit mitigating forgetting. Most approaches exhibit better
473
      performance when N = 4 or N = 8, encourages us to set N as 4 in our experiments.
474   The impact of structure over complexity. To demonstrate that the advantages of KAC lie in the
475   introduced KAN structure, not the additional computations, we replace the RBFs with an MLP layer,
476   setting its output dimension to N × n to align the number of parameters with KAC using RBFs,
477   allowing us to make a fair comparison between the two structure. Tab. 4 shows the performance
478   of replacing RBFs with the MLP structure implemented on CODAPrompt. Upon comparison, we
479
      discover that whether the additional MLP structure is updated alongside the model or not, it does not
      yield any positive effects. This indicates that the advantages of KAC stem from its KAN structure
480
      rather than a simple increase in the dimensionality of the classification space.
