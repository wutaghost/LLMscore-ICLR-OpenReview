                                                             A BSTRACT
010
011
                   The availability of high-quality data is one of the most important factors in im-
012                proving the reasoning capability of LLMs. Existing works have demonstrated the
013                effectiveness of creating more instruction data from seed questions or knowledge
014                bases. Recent research indicates that continually scaling up data synthesis from
015                strong models (e.g., GPT-4) can further elicit reasoning performance. Though
016                promising, the open-sourced community still lacks high-quality data at scale and
017                scalable data synthesis methods with affordable costs. To address this, we intro-
018                duce ScaleQuest, a scalable and novel data synthesis method that utilizes “small-
019                size” (e.g., 7B) open-source models to generate questions from scratch without
020
                   the need for seed data with complex augmentation constraints. With the efficient
                   ScaleQuest, we automatically constructed a mathematical reasoning dataset con-
021
                   sisting of 1 million problem-solution pairs, which are more effective than existing
022
                   open-sourced datasets. It can universally increase the performance of mainstream
023                open-source models (i.e., Mistral, Llama3, DeepSeekMath, and Qwen2-Math)
024                by achieving 29.2% to 46.4% gains on MATH. Notably, simply fine-tuning the
025                Qwen2-Math-7B-Base model with our dataset can even surpass Qwen2-Math-7B-
026                Instruct, a strong and well-aligned model on closed-source data, and proprietary
027                models such as GPT-4-Turbo and Claude-3.5 Sonnet.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
049
050
      How to improve the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) has attracted sig-
051   nificant attention. The success of recent advanced models, such as OpenAI o1 and Claude-3.5,
052   heavily depends on access to extensive, diverse, and high-quality reasoning datasets. However, the
053
          1
              Code, data, and models will be publicly available.


                                                                   1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      proprietary nature of the data presents a significant barrier to the open-source community. Recent
055   works have highlighted data synthesis as a promising approach (Ntoutsi et al., 2020) to address data
056   scarcity for instruction tuning (Inan et al., 2023). As recent works have disclosed that crafting the
057   right questions is crucial for eliciting the reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Yu et al., 2023a; Shah
058   et al., 2024), the core of reasoning data synthesis lies in creating large-scale and novel questions.
059
      Previous efforts in reasoning data synthesis have demonstrated the effectiveness of leveraging pow-
060
      erful language models to generate instructions. We categorize these approaches into two types:
061   question-driven approaches and knowledge-driven approaches. Question-driven methods include
062   question rephrasing (Yu et al., 2023a), evol-instruct (Xu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Zeng et al.,
063   2024), question back-translation (Lu et al., 2024), or providing few-shot examples (Mitra et al.,
064   2024). These methods are limited in data diversity, as the generated problems closely resemble
065   the seed questions, with only minor modifications such as added conditions or numerical changes.
066   This lack of diversity hampers their scalability potential. To improve question diversity, recent
067   knowledge-driven works (Huang et al., 2024b) scale question synthesis by constructing knowledge
068   bases (Li et al., 2024b) or concept graphs (Tang et al., 2024) and sampling key points (Huang et al.,
069
      2024a) from them to generate new questions. Nevertheless, the above two types of approaches com-
      monly rely on strong models, like GPT-4, to synthesize new questions, but the high API costs make
070
      it impractical to generate large-scale data. As a result, despite these advancements, the open-source
071
      community still faces a shortage of high-quality data at scale and cost-effective synthesis methods.
072
073   To meet this requirement, we explore a scalable, low-cost method for data synthesis. We observe
074   that using problem-solving models to directly synthesize reasoning questions, as explored in Yu
075
      et al. (2023b) and Xu et al. (2024), falls short in synthesizing reasoning data, as shown in Figure 1
      (see Llama3-8B-Magpie results). Accordingly, we propose a novel, scalable, and cost-effective data
076
      synthesis method, ScaleQuest, which first introduces a two-stage question-tuning process consist-
077
      ing of Question Fine-Tuning (QFT) and Question Preference Optimization (QPO) to unlock the
078   question generation capability of problem-solving models. Once fine-tuned, these models can then
079   generate diverse questions by sampling from a broad search space without the need for additional
080   seed questions or knowledge constraints. The generated questions can be further refined through a
081   filtering process, focusing on language clarity, solvability, and appropriate difficulty. Moreover, we
082   introduce an extra reward-based filtering strategy to select high-quality responses.
083
      We generated data based on two lightweight, open-source models: DeepSeekMath-7B-RL (Shao
084   et al., 2024) and Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024a), producing a final dataset of 1 mil-
085   lion question-answer pairs. As shown in Figure 1, our synthetic dataset boosts performance by
086   29.2% to 46.4% across four major open-source models: Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama3-
087   8B (Dubey et al., 2024), DeepSeekMath-7B (Shao et al., 2024), and Qwen2-Math-7B (Yang et al.,
088   2024a). Compared with other publicly available datasets such as MetaMath (Yu et al., 2023a),
089   DART-Math (Tong et al., 2024), and NuminaMath (Li et al., 2024c), our approach demonstrates
090   great scalability in both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation. In terms of in-domain evaluation,
091   our method outperforms existing high-quality open-source datasets, achieving better results with the
092
      same amount of data. For out-of-domain evaluation, compared with other datasets, the performance
      of our synthetic dataset continues to show promising trends as the volume of training data increases,
093
      indicating significant potential for further improvements through ongoing data scaling.

204   2.4   Q UESTION F ILTERING
205
      After the QFT and QPO phases, we obtained two question generators: DeepSeekMath-QGen and
206
      Qwen2-Math-QGen. There are still some minor issues in the generated questions, primarily re-
207   lated to language, solvability, and difficulty. To address these challenges, we applied the following
208   filtering steps:
209
210   Language Filtering The question generator models still produce a substantial number of math
211   questions in other languages, accounting for approximately 20%. Since our focus is on English
212   math questions, we removed non-English questions by identifying questions containing non-English
213   characters and filtering out those samples.
214
215   Solvability Filtering Although QPO effectively enhances the solvability of generated questions,
      some questions remain nonsensical. This is primarily due to (1) poorly constrained questions, where


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      missing conditions, redundant conditions, or logical inconsistencies occur, and (2) questions that do
217   not yield meaningful outcomes (e.g., answers involving the number of people should result in a
218   non-negative integer). To filter out such samples, we used Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct to evaluate
219   whether the question is meaningful and whether the conditions are sufficient. The prompts used for
220   the solvability check are provided in Figure 12.
221
222   Difficulty Sampling We measure the difficulty of a question using the fail rate (Tong et al., 2024)
223   — the proportion of incorrect responses when sampling n responses for a given question. This met-
224   ric aligns with the intuition that harder questions tend to result in fewer correct responses. Following
225   Tong et al. (2024), we used DeepseekMath-7B-RL as the sampling model to evaluate the difficulty
226   of each question in the training sets of GSM8K and MATH, obtaining the fail rate for each question
227   as its difficulty score. We then used this data to train a difficulty scorer. Specifically, we built upon
228
      DeepseekMath-7B-Base and added a classification head on top of the model’s hidden state. The
      difficulty score d is computed and optimized as:
229

      where W and b are the weights and biases of the classification head, hl represents the last hidden
234
      state of the sequence, and di is the predicted difficulty score for the i-th question. The loss func-
235   tion L is the mean squared error (MSE), where yi represents the true difficulty score for the i-th
236   question. We then used the scorer to predict the difficulty of each synthetic question and sample
237   based on the question’s difficulty. Specifically, we filtered out a portion of the questions generated
238   by DeepSeekMath-QGen that were overly simple. In contrast, the difficulty distribution of Qwen2-
239   Math-QGen was more balanced, so no sampling was necessary.

241   2.5   R ESPONSE G ENERATION WITH R EWARD F ILTERING
242
243   Prior efforts to guarantee the quality of solutions include two aspects: (1) rejection sampling (Yuan
244   et al., 2023): Large language models (LLMs) are tasked with generating multiple responses, specif-
245   ically reasoning paths, for each instruction. Only reasoning paths that lead to the correct answer are
246   preserved as solutions (Tong et al., 2024). (2) If the correct answer is unavailable, a majority voting
247
      method is used (Huang et al., 2024a), selecting the answer that appears most frequently across mul-
      tiple reasoning paths and retaining these as the solutions. We use the reward model score as a metric
248
      for evaluating the quality of responses, considering its broader applicability, as there is often no sin-
249
      gle correct answer in other reasoning tasks like code generation and tool planning. Specifically, for
250   each question, we generate 5 solutions and select the solution with the highest reward model scores
251   as the preferred solution. In our experiments, we use InternLM2-7B-Reward (Cai et al., 2024) as
252   our reward model. This choice was primarily guided by the model’s performance on the reasoning
253   subset of the Reward Bench (Lambert et al., 2024).

      Training Problem Designers Our question synthesis process relies on two problem designer mod-
260   els: Deepseek-QGen and Qwen2-Math-QGen, which were trained using QFT (section 2.2)
261   and QPO (section 2.3), based on DeepSeekMath-7B-RL (Shao et al., 2024) and Qwen2-Math-7B-
262   Instruct (Yang et al., 2024a), respectively. During the QFT stage, both models are trained on a mixed
263   training subset of GSM8K and MATH problems, containing a total of 15K problems. We trained
264   for only 1 epoch, considering that training for more epochs might cause the models to overfit the
265   training problems and negatively impact the diversity of generated questions. We also used sequence
266   packing (Krell et al., 2021) to accelerate training. In the QPO stage, we use 10K preference data for
267   training, with a learning rate of 5e-7 and a batch size of 128.

      Evaluation and Metrics We assessed the fine-tuned models’ performance across four datasets of
325   increasing difficulty. Along with the widely used GSM8K (elementary level) and MATH (competi-
326   tion level), we included two more challenging benchmarks: College Math (Yuan et al., 2023) (col-
327   lege level) and Olympiad Bench (He et al., 2024) (Olympiad level). For evaluation, we employed the
328   script from Tong et al. (2024) to extract final answers and determine correctness by comparing an-
329   swer equivalency. The generated outputs were all in the form of natural language Chain-of-Thought
330   (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2022) through greedy decoding, with no tool integration, and we report
331   zero-shot pass@1 accuracy.
332
333   Compared Baselines The main point of comparison is data synthesis methods, including: (1)
334   WizardMath (Luo et al., 2023) proposes a reinforced Evol Instruct method; (2) MetaMath (Yu et al.,

349   ScaleQuest significantly outperforms others Table 1 presents the results. ScaleQuest signifi-
350
      cantly outperforms previous synthetic methods, with average performance improvements ranging
      from 5.6% to 11.5% over the prior state-of-the-art (SoTA) on both general base models and math-
351
      specialized foundation models. Qwen2-Math-7B-ScaleQuest achieved a zero-shot pass@1 accuracy

353   tasks, Qwen2-Math-7B-ScaleQuest outperformed its teacher model, Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct, with

358   comparison with other baselines, we have only applied supervised fine-tuning (SFT) in this work,

360   publicly available, we could not strictly control for the same training data volume. Experiments
361
      with controlled training data volumes can be found in the scaling trend in Figure 1 and Appendix C.

      Reward are used. Detailed results and more insights into the selection of these models are provided

367   ScaleQuest scales well with increasing data We also explored the scalability of our dataset. We

      2023a), DART-Math (Tong et al., 2024), and Numina-Math (Li et al., 2024c). We trained the model
      using Llama3-8B and observed how its performance scaled with increasing data size. The results are
370
      presented in Figure 1. For the in-domain evaluation (MATH), our method demonstrates high data
371
      efficiency, achieving superior results with the same amount of data. In out-of-domain evaluations
372   (Olympiad Bench), it also shows strong scalability, continuing to improve even as other datasets
373   reach their limits. A limited question set leads to constrained improvements in model performance,
374   as demonstrated by the results of DART-Math, which relies on a small number of questions and
375   generates numerous correct answers through rejection sampling. Limited questions face a scalability

491
492   4.1   M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
493
494
      Solving math problems is regarded as a key measure of evaluating the reasoning ability of LLMs.
      Recent advancements in mathematical reasoning for LLMs, including models like OpenAI o1,
495
      Claude-3.5, Gemini (Reid et al., 2024), DeepSeekMath (Shao et al., 2024), InternLM2-Math (Cai
496
      et al., 2024), and Qwen2.5-Math (Yang et al., 2024b), have spurred the development of various
497   approaches to improve reasoning capabilities of LLMs on math-related tasks. To strengthen the
498   math reasoning capabilities of LLMs, researchers have focused on areas such as prompting tech-
499   niques (Chia et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), data construction for pretrain-
500   ing (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Azerbayev et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024) and
501   instruction tuning (Luo et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023), tool-integrated reasoning(Chen et al., 2022;
502   Gao et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
503   2024), and preference tuning (Ma et al., 2023; Luong et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024).
504   Our work primarily focuses on math data synthesis for instruction tuning.
505
506   4.2   DATA S YNTHESIS FOR M ATH I NSTRUCTION T UNING
507
508   High-quality reasoning data, particularly well-crafted questions, is in short supply. Prior efforts
509
      have mostly started with a small set of human-annotated seed instructions and expanded them
      through few-shot prompting. We categorize them into two types: question-driven augmentation and
510
      knowledge-driven augmentation. Previous works focus on enhancing seed questions by introducing
511
      additional constraints or numerical changes to increase the reasoning steps required. For instance,
512   WizardMath (Luo et al., 2023) uses a series of operations to increase the complexity of questions
513   and answers with GPT-3.5. MetaMath (Yu et al., 2023a) enhances the questions in GSM8K (Cobbe
514   et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) by rewriting them in various ways, such as through
515   semantic rephrasing, self-verification, and backward reasoning. Xwin-Math (Li et al., 2024a) and
516   MMIQC (Liu & Yao, 2024) further explore the scalability of the synthetic data. However, these
517   methods face a diversity challenge, as few-shot prompting often results in new instructions that are
518   too similar to the original seed questions (Li et al., 2024b). To increase diversity, recent works
519   have focused on knowledge-driven data synthesis, where they summarize world knowledge from
520
      the seed questions and use it to generate synthetic datasets (Didolkar et al., 2024; Shah et al., 2024).
      MathScale (Tang et al., 2024) extracts math concepts from seed questions and then generate math
521
      reasoning data. KPMath (Huang et al., 2024a) begins by extracting topics and key points from seed
522
      problems using a labeling model, and sample multiple topics and key points for instruction synthe-
523   sis. There are other methods for enhancing dataset quality as well. DART-Math (Tong et al., 2024)
524   focuses on enhancing the quality of responses by using rejection sampling to generate multiple cor-
525   rect answers for each query from GSM8K and MATH. In contrast, Numina-Math (Li et al., 2024c)
526   improves its dataset by collecting more real-world and synthetic data, then reformatting (Fan et al.,
527   2024) the responses using GPT-4o. This high-quality data can be integrated with our constructed
528   dataset, resulting in an improved data mix for more effective instruction tuning.
529
530
      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we propose ScaleQuest, a novel data synthesis framework that unlocks the ability of
533
      open-source smaller models to independently generate large-scale, high-quality reasoning data from
534   scratch, at a low cost. By training the problem-solving models on a small subset of questions, we
535   effectively activate their question-generation capabilities. We also introduce a response enhance-
536   ment method. With these techniques, we successfully developed a fully synthetic math reasoning
537   dataset consisting of 1 million question-answer pairs. Using this dataset, we fine-tuned the model
538   and achieved remarkable improvements, with gains ranging from 29.2% to 46.4% compared to the
539   base model. The fine-tuned 7B model, Qwen2-Math-7B-ScaleQuest, outperforms all competitors in
      the 7B-70B range and even surpasses proprietary models like GPT-4-Turbo and Claude-3.5-Sonnet.

1006   More ablation Results of each submethod In Figure 5, we discussed the effectiveness of each
1007   submethod, including Question Fine-Tuning (QFT), Question Preference Optimization (QPO), and
1008
       Reward Filtering (RF), in a stepwise manner. To further refine this ablation study, we examined
       various combinations of these submethods. We excluded the combination of w/o QFT and w/ QPO,
1009
       as QPO is meaningless without QFT, which is essential for question generation. The results are
1010
       illustrated in Table 10. From the results, we can more precisely observe the contributions of each
1011   submethod to overall performance improvements. We found that QFT and QPO contribute signif-
1012   icantly to the improvement of SFT performance, while the impact of QPO seems less pronounced.
1013   We would like to clarify that the limited improvements from QPO are due to two main reasons:
1014   (1) QPO primarily optimizes the solvability of questions, and its influence on response quality is
1015   indirect. (2) Though the impact of QPO in SFT may be minimal, it significantly enhances the data
1016   generation efficiency. Specifically, QPO improves the solvability of generated questions from 75.4%
1017   to 83.6%, a meaningful enhancement that boosts the efficiency of data utilization. While the effect
1018   may appear minimal due to subsequent solvability filtering, our detailed analysis shows that 28.8%
1019
       of unsolvable questions were filtered out in the baseline setting, whereas after QPO, only 19.4%
       were deemed unsolvable. This represents a 9.4% reduction in computational overhead.
