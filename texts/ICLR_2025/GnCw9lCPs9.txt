                                                            A BSTRACT
015
016            Learning relevant, transferable representations of actions to drive model-based reinforcement learn-
017
               ing processes stands as a major challenge in robotics on the path to general-purpose autonomous
               agents, equivalent in their reasoning power to Large Language Models. To this end, we introduce a
018
               novel framework which allows autonomous agents to learn how to represent their actions as high-
019            dimensional rotations over the system’s observations. We then show how such representations may be
020            considered optimal under the assumption that actions are distance-preserving, and present how these
021            representations of low-level actions can be composed to represent sequences of actions and allow
022            for multi-scale hierarchical learning and long-horizon planning. We finally discussed schemes to
023            compare such representations in order to allow for a better informed transfer of skills across tasks and
024            better understand the agent’s behaviour, before conducting experiments using a modified TD-MPC2
025            agent to better quantify in concreto the limitations of our framework.
026
027
028   1   Introduction
029
030   Following recent advances in the field on Natural Language Processing, which allowed several Large Language
031   Models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020 (2)) or LLAMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023 (28)) to achieve near-
032   human performance over a variety of language-based tasks, considerable effort was put into exploiting the implicit
033   representations and world models contained within such models to build or enrich reinforcement learning datasets
034   (Tiafas et al., 2024 (30), Zheng et al., 2024(35)) , to plan and direct low-level agents to help them tackle long-horizon or
035
      complex tasks (Zhang et al., 2023 (36), Sun et al., 2024 (25), Zhou et al., 2024 (37)), or to extract from low-dimensional
      observations adequate system state descriptors (Chen et al., 2023 (3)), for instance. However, such approaches face
036
      a dual challenge: while larger models are more precise and better able to adapt with minimal fine-tuning to a wide
037   variety of downstream tasks; on the other hand, they are also expensive to run and use as part of reinforcement learning
038   schemes, where hundreds of thousands or even millions of updates are often required to reach convergence on some
039   tasks and where an agent is often required to plan or react in real-time.
040
      In this paper, we propose an alternative to the general, humanly-understandable representations provided by LLMs
041
      in the context of predictive control and planning tasks. Instead of replicating the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017
042
      (31)) architecture by learning sequences of words and their correspondence in a latent action skill space, we leverage
043   experimental observations to first derive a set of useful primitive actions, and learn their representation as a group action
044   over the agent’s observation space. We then combine and refine these primitives into relevant longer sequences of
045   actions in downstream learning to further improve and enrich the agent’s skills and reasoning abilities with sequences
046   of increasing complexity while preserving the predictive power of our action model.
047
      In practice, we recast the problem of learning action representations and dynamics as an instance of the well-known
048   Wahba problem (Wahba, G, 1965 (32)). This problem, originally set by the astrophysicist Grace Wahba, seeks to
049   find an element of SO(n) to optimally shift one set of coordinates to another, where each shift is defined using a
050   noisy matching of basis vectors from each set. We in turn match the observed environment state before executing a
051   given action to the observed state after performing this action. This allows us to derive the corresponding rotation as a
052   representation of the action executed by the agent, to be used in downstream learning. Crucially, these representations
053   only depend on dΩ , the state observation space’s dimension, as long as the same training environment is reused across
      tasks. They also require as little as O(dΩ ) observations to become uniquely optimal representations.

054
      In our experiments, we implement our SILEO framework within a slightly modified TD-MPC2 model architecture to
055   account for a finite set of actions instead of the high-dimensional hypercubes used in the original implementation. After
056   a short pre-training designed to identify relevant actions through a clustering of the embeddings provided by the agent’s
057   policy, we calculate their relevant representations as rotations and reuse them as a set of primitive skills. These action
058   primitives equate to a fixed embedding in the agent’s initial action or command space. In downstream learning, we then
059   use these representations as the dynamics model of a Model Predictive Control planning algorithm, and refine these
060   representations with new observations while maximising the entropy of our action set. This is achieved by replacing
061   overused skills with the most useful sequences of skills starting with them. To summarise our main contributions:
062
063   Action Representations We present a novel scheme to represent actions in model predictive control as learned
064
      rotations over either an observation space or a learned state latent space, alongside the conditions required for these
      representations to be near-optimal
065
066
      Multi-scale Hierarchical Learning We propose a method to refine during downstream learning a given set of action
067   representations into longer sequences, to be used as higher-level skills in planning
068
069   Transfer Learning We show that learned action representations may be reused across tasks and agents while
070   mitigating training performance loss, while remaining useful to evaluate the relative complexity and similarity of one
071   task compared to another
072
073   Long Horizon planning We put forward several ways to extend in the future the low-level action-based SILEO
074   framework implemented in our experiments to subtasks. We then show how long-horizon planning may then be recast
075   as a (multiscale) factoring problem.

077   2   Preliminaries
078
079   Problem formulation This work is built under the theoretical framework of Partially Observable Markov Decision
080   Processes, whose resolution aims at optimising under a certain reward function the sequence of actions taken by an
081   agent from a given state, under the assumption that the decision-making agent only has access to noisy observations of
082   these states. Formally, a POMDP is a 7-uple (S, A, R, T, Ω, O, γ), where
083
      - S is a set of states                   - T : S × A → S is a random variable - O : S → S is a random variable
084
      - A is a set of actions                   representing state transitions      representing state observations
085
      - R : S × A → R is the reward            - Ω is a set of observations         - γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor
086
087
088
         As with all Markov Decision Processes, at each time step t the agent is expected to take an action a ∈ A to move
      from a lower-reward state st ∈ S to a hopefully higher-reward state st+1 . However, without knowledge of the ground
089
      truth state at this time step the agent only has access to a random observation ωt ∈ Ω, given by the value of O(st ).
090
      Moreover, the transition from an observed state and the action chosen to the ground truth state at the next time step is
091   also random, represented by the transition random variable T: Ω × A → S.
092
093   Assumptions In this work, we assume that both conditional random variables T and O follow a Gaussian probability
094   distribution. This assumption allows us to merge both Gaussian noises as one, that is, to consider a unified random
095   variable τ : S × A → S defined as τ = T ◦ (O ⊗ (IdA )), which as the composition of two Gaussian random variables
096   also follows a Gaussian probability distribution. This variable directly maps a ground truth state and action to another
097   ground truth state without having to consider individually the output of O and then T.
098   We further assume that the set of observations O ⊂ Ω can be embedded in a finite-dimensional real vector space, with
099   dimension d. While this assumption is standard in a control setting, we further assume that this embedding is entirely
100   located in the d-dimensional unit sphere S d−1 . While this may seem egregious, since any compact subset of Rd−1 is
101   homeomorphic to a subset of S d−1 (see Appendix 1.1 for a detailed proof), this condition can be met by any compact
102   Hausdorff embedding as long as it is projected to a higher dimension for our computation.
103   Finally, we assume that notwithstanding a Gaussian noise given by τ , the effect of each action may be represented
104   globally as an endomorphism of S d−1 , id est a matrix from the orthogonal group O(d) and even SO(d). Under our
105   previous assumptions, this is equivalent to assuming that the actions over the embedded observation space are both
106   quick to execute and mostly distance-preserving (see Appendix 2 for a rigorous proof), which while reasonable in a
107   control setting may not be applicable in a context where significant external forces (e.g gravity) or numerous collisions,
      in particular inelastic, are to be expected.

      Wahba’s problem In this work, action representations rely on a solution to Wahba’s problem provided by Ruiter and
109   Forbes in 2014 (22). This least-squares problem seeks to find an optimal rotation matrix between two matching sets
110   of (weighted) noisy coordinate vectors. Here, we recast the problem using system observations before an action was
111   performed as a coordinate set, and system observations after this action as the second, to deduce a representation of the
112   action as the best rotation matrix to match these observations. Under our assumptions, notably since τ is Gaussian, we
113   may then find optimal solutions to each instance of Wahba’s problem using SVD-derived methods.
114   In more precise terms, given an action a ∈ A, and N > 1 d-dimensional observations (ωk , ωk′ )1≤k≤n such that for
115   all k, there has been a time step when τ (ωk , a) = ωk′ , we seek to find Ra ∈ SO(d) that minimises the following

120   3     Approach
121
122   In this section, we introduce in detail our SILEO framework, and its applications to Reinforcement Learning tasks and
123   foremost control; we present both the theoretical advantages and potential drawbacks of the presented framework for
124   dynamics and model-based planning (section 3.1), multi-level hierarchical learning from the composition of primitive
125   and composite actions (section 3.2), transfer learning and cross-task comparison using geodesic distance (section 3.3)
126   and long horizon planning in controlled environments (section 3.4). Implementation details and results are further
127
      presented in section 4.

129
      3.1   Representing actions by their dynamics
130   In prior work, notably derived from SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018 (6)) or DRQ (Kostrikov et al., 2020 (13)) agents such as
131   PEARL (Rakelly et al., 2019 (20)) or TD-MPC (Hansen et al., 2022 (7)), actions are identified by their embeddings in a
132   latent action space of arbitrary dimension, usually within a hypercube centered in 0. While a bijective interpretation
133   function is used to translate these embeddings into concrete actions realised by an agent’s actuators, there is no global
134   equivalent at the reasoning level to be used by the agent. The latent action space is only used at a local level by neural
135   networks tasked with predicting the next latent state or an expected reward when given as input an observation and an
136   action for instance.
137   Unlike with words or pictures in the case of Transformer-derived agents however, there is intuitively a topological group
138   structure that can be derived from this set of low-level actions in a control setting. Indeed, though this group and its
139   structure may be hard to assess directly, most actions taken by an agent in control benchmarks such as RLBench (James
140   et al., 2019 (12)), ALFREd (Shridhar et al., 2020 (24)), Meta-World (Yu et al., 2019 (34)) or the DeepMind Control
141   Suite (Tassa et al., 2018 (27)) are invertible, often by simply reversing the command sequence at the actuator’s level.
142   Moreover, most actions can be continuously composed with one another without breaking the rules of the training
143
      environment, and the bijection between latent action space and actuator command is designed to be continuous and
      even an homeomorphism. However, this group structure is computationally intractable to determine, since even if we
144
      consider only a set of low-level actions as generators, simply chaining two actions one after the other requires us to
145   consider the result in a space larger than the initial actuator command space to account for the fact that the resulting
146   action is no longer a low-level actuator command, which leads recursively to an indefinitely large action space.
147
148
      It is therefore natural to try and devise a way to consider these actions taken from an inaccessible group as group actions,
      more precisely to consider instead of the group itself a representation of this topological group on the observation
149
      space. Let us recall that given a group G, a representation (ρ, V ) of this group is comprised of a vector space V and a
150
      morphism ρ : G → Aut(V ) GL(V ). Assuming that actions a ∈ A over the observation space Ω from our PO-MDP
151   have this topological group structure, a faithful (that is, injective) representation would allow us to identify these actions
152   with elements of Aut(Ω). Under our assumptions stated in the preliminaries, Aut(Ω) would be O(d), and our actions
153   may even be represented by SO(d) as shown in Appendix 1, where d = dim(Ω).
154
      In order for an agent to learn this faithful representation from its experience interacting with its environment, we can
155
      leverage our framework by recasting the problem as an instance of Wahba’s problem and treating each action as a shift
156   with Gaussian noise from one system of coordinates in the observation space Ω to another. Since under our assumptions
157   a given action from the latent action space acts on the observation space using an element of SO(d), an agent can
158   observe its effects on the system through a replay buffer or with online experiments and deduce, for instance using
159   Singular Value Decomposition-derived methods(22), a solution to this instance of Wahba’s problem with as little as
160   O(d) observations if they are sampled independently at random. Indeed, with high probability O(d) observations
161   form a basis of the latent d-dimensional observation space Ω, forming the d-dimensional basis required by the method
      presented in by de Ruiter et al., 2013 (22) to find the unique optimal rotation on S d that represents our given action.

196   3.2   Self-improving logic
197
      One of the main benefits of our SILEO framework lies in the ability to consider the global dynamics of actions instead
198
      of considering these dynamics on a per-state basis. This allows us to efficiently generalise but also refine and build
199   new skills using global operations instead of considering trajectories built from pointwise dynamics. In our framework,
200   composing one action with another is therefore as simple as multiplying the two rotation matrices that represent them.
201   This enables second-order logic operations for an agent, which not only learns to manipulate actions but also discovers
202   how they are related as either "parents" or "continuations" during its training. Finally, we draw inspiration from
203   Natural Language Processing and notably tokenisation processes used by Large Language Models, to define entropy
204   maximisation (following Wen et al., 2024 (33)) as a relevant criterion for refining the skillset learned by the agent.
205   Following Sutton et al., 1999 (26), a policy π : Ω → A may be trained to predict the best action to take following a
206   given observed state ω ∈ Ω. However, this policy is biased by design, since its intent is to continuously assign to latent
207   states an action to take, which may lead to imbalance or approximations if some actions are much more frequently found
208   than others in a given dataset regardless of their relevance to a given task. Instead, we propose to balance our actions
209   and maximise the entropy of our action set by splitting frequently found actions into composite ones of greater duration,
210   and to complement the policy with reward estimation in order to differentiate skills born from the same primitive action.

244   We call this algorithm a skills pruning algorithm, since it essentially cuts skills that overshadow others to allow for
245   smaller, more balanced branches to regrow in their stead. Its goal is to produce skills of varying complexity but roughly
246   equivalent in terms of frequency. It uses as roots the skills already learned by the agent, and considers for frequent skills
247   which other skills, composite or not, would be the most likely continuations to this skill. It then creates new skills from
248   the composition of our root skill and the most rewarding continuations, for them to be considered alongside others in
249
      downstream learning. A policy becomes mostly irrelevant in the setting of an expanding skills graph, although it does
      help restraining the search scope in the resulting graph; what matters however is consistent reward estimation across
250
      different depths of prediction resulting from the chaining of long or short skills in planning.
251
252   The resulting set of learned skills or actions may be represented as a graph, using as roots one of the primitive actions.
253   While initially the graph is a disjoint set of small trees, after further pruning connected components merge and form a
254   single connected graph. This graph may be analysed using different primitive skills as roots to explore the "transitions",
255
      that is the inheritance links from a parent skill/node to a more complex descendant, using methods such as CEM
      planning (Huang et al., 2021 (11)) or PETS (Chua et al., 2018 (4)), to ensure the most relevant skill is chosen by the
256
      agent at a given state and time step. This method allows for an agent to learn how to balance its different logic and
257
      planning scales, since under a given planning horizon of n actions, it can learn to use actions of varying length or
258   complexity, where the choice of more complex skills translates greater mastery of and confidence in an action sequence.
259
260
      This algorithm remains sensitive on the time of pruning however; indeed, agents which are more proficient than others
      at a given task usually have better defined clusters of actions and thus less action primitives and more composite skills.
261
      The repeated pruning of a graph during training however allows an agent to adapt to its most recent experience by
262
      integrating new and often more complex composite skills to its repertoire, reducing the total number of hyperparameters
263   in the algorithm to 3: pruning frequency, accepted deviation from mean or median and the size of the buffer to be
264   considered while pruning. These ensure the long-term viability of the hierarchical learning undertaken by the agent to
265   ensure relevant composite skills are learned even among changing environmental conditions.

317   3.4   Guided exploration and long-horizon planning
318
319   Using rotations as representations for an agent’s actions also allows it, on slightly modified observation spaces, to
320   plan a trajectory without needing external rewards or policies. Indeed, given a starting region O ∈ Ω and a target
321   region C ∈ Ω, and a matching of d = dim(Ω) points (oi , ci )0<i<d−1 (both independent and with unit norm) from
322   both regions, we may consider as for low-level actions a rotation matrix S ∈ SO(d) which seeks to verify for all i,
323   S(p(oi )) = p(ci ), where p is an homeomorphism between Rd and Rd+1 . We can therefore deduce a rotation matrix to
      represent a given task, and even any sub-tasks which we would deem relevant from the state transitions desired.

343   Within our framework, assuming that some actions have been learned, the process of finding a solution to a task or
344   a sub-task becomes equivalent to finding that of an approximate factoring problem, where a planner would attempt
345   to reconstruct the task’s rotation matrix using the subtasks or low-level actions at its disposal, represented by their
346   rotations. Such a problem is NP-hard in the general case, as if even if we consider simultaneously reducible rotations
347   as actions, solving this problem becomes equivalent to solving a multidimensional knapsack problem as presented in
348   Püchinger et al., 2010 (19) by using the eigenvalues’ rotation angles as weights. A simpler version of this problem
349   limiting itself to reconstructing a task from a set of sub-tasks may be solved however in a small enough time to be viable.
350
      Such an approach would be an alternative to LLM guidance as defined in Pallagani et al., 2024 (16) and used recently
      in Plan-Seq-Learn (Dalal et al., 2024 (5)) for instance, where in order to complete a task a Large Language Model sets
351
      the order of sub-tasks to complete while a low-level agent independently learns how to execute each sub-task.
352
353   In order to bias the factoring algorithm and help it find a solution more quickly, two complementary approaches might
354   be needed. Firstly, in order to evaluate whether or not a given factoring is viable, a distance metric over the elements
355   of SO(d) that we consider would transform the factoring algorithm into a (reversed) hill-climber. While SO(d) is
      finite-dimensional and as such all of its distance metrics are equivalent (that is, they define the same metric space
356
      topology), the geodesic distance described in section 3.3 would be a particularly meaningful candidate distance metric,
357
      as minimising it is exactly minimising the difference between current and target singular values.
358
359   Second, in order to handle the cases where some states might be unreachable from others (due to the presence of
360   walls for instance in a maze or due to the limitations of an actuator), a dilatation of the observation space that would
361
      significantly increase the distance between such states would allow a distance-based hill climber to avoid forbidden or
      impossible actions, while preserving the structure and task-agnostic nature of the observation space. An illustration of
362
      this process using a maze and a set of valid trajectories is shown above in Figure 3.
363
364   While in the case of the maze such a dilatation could be pre-computed, a solution analogous to a gravity field could also
365   be used in cases where the underlying observation space is not easily understood or segmented. In essence, during a
366
      pre-training phase, each observation from the buffer would add weight to its position, adding in practice a coordinate in
      a supplemental dimension. The problem would then be recast with this additional dimension and after smoothing the
367
      differences to make this modified observation space a smooth manifold, forming a new observation space to be treated
368
      as any other would be otherwise using our framework during training.
369
370   Concrete implementation of these methods are however highly dependent on the resolution of a specific task, and as
371
      such have not been explored in our experiments; it is therefore left to future work to determine the viability of using our
      SILEO framework for factoring-based long-horizon planning.

      4.1   walker-walk
398
399   Likely one of the "easiest" tasks of the dataset with a 25-dimensional state observation space and a 6-dimensional
400   action space, the original TD-MPC2 agent converges in this task in less than 20 000 iterations, reaching a reward of
401   900-950 depending on the random seed, to be compared to an average reward of around 45 using random actions.
402   However, this task primarily aims at teaching a simple two-legged "walker" how to fight against gravity to be able to
403   walk without falling down. It is therefore a great example of tasks that cannot be solved using solely our framework,
404   since the assumption that actions are (mostly) distance-preserving does not hold when fighting against a dominant
405
      external force with such a simple agent. Indeed, in our experiments the agent seemingly learns how to f ail the task,
      converging at a reward of around 10 which is significantly below the performance of random actions.
406
407   To validate the hypothesis that gravity was at fault, we used two different projections for the observations used in the
408   pruning algorithm and the trainer to ensure the states used to deduce representations would have nothing to do with
409   those used in dynamics prediction to update these representations. This allowed our agent to maintain performance
410
      slightly above the level of random actions for a short time after pre-training, before reverting to its previous behaviour
      after enough updates were made to erase the transitions used in the pruning algorithm from the agent’s memory.

414
      Significantly more complex than the previous task, with a 223-dimensional state observation space and a 38-dimensional
415   action space, the dog-run task represents a moderate challenge to the unmodified TD-MPC2 agent, which in 200 000
416   updates reaches an average reward just under 200, to compare to an average reward of 6 for random actions. Our
417   framework initially improves the performance of the agent from the end of pre-training onwards, with an average
418   performance at 50 000 steps moving from 6.0 to 14 and equivalent performance at 100 000 steps. However, due
419   to the small (ranging from 16 to 512) set of actions chosen for pruning, and thus extremely coarse topology of our
420   actions, downstream learning is noticeably slower. The limitations of our model also show here, since even though the
421   observation space is much larger gravity is once again the main competing force working against our agent.
422   In order to evaluate the relative contributions of each of these factors, we leveraged vectorised environments and mean
423   pooling to train the agent on 4 different environments, half of which planned their actions using our framework, the
424   rest with the original agent’s policy. All policy actions proposed by the latter 2 environments were recast as SILEO
425   actions for model updates. The idea behind this approach is to compensate for the handicap of a finite set of actions by
426   planning on both the original continuous setting and our modified discrete framework to add some observation noise,
427   before updating the agent by matching policy actions their nearest actions in our small set of skills.
428   In these experiments, we also evaluated the contribution of composite actions, which are initially more vulnerable to
429   unaccounted external forces. In both cases, the number of initial primitive actions was set to 16, with a limit on the total
430   number of actions set to 512. We found that although slower and with far higher variance as the number of actions was
431   reduced, this mixed approach both with and without composite actions initially kept up with the original agent at first
      and kept improving with more training steps, although at a slower rate ranging from a third to a tenth of the original.

493   5   Limitations
494
495   As discussed earlier in this paper and illustrated by our experiments, the use of orthogonal special group elements
496   to represent robotic commands has its limitations. Some of these are imputable to the limitations of the theoretical
497   framework itself and to certain assumptions that simply do not hold in a concrete setting for solving certain tasks,
498   while others probably have more to do with implementation choices that favoured speed of execution and ease of
499   interpretation over accurate modelling that would conform in every aspect to the requirements of our theory.
500   Firstly, the choice of the class of functions, or rather the group of linear operators chosen as the representation space is
501   relatively restrictive; indeed, as presented in Appendix 1, it is based on the dual assumption that actions are quick to
502   execute and preserve distances between states, in other words that the environment and the agent are essentially rigid and
503   devoid of obstacles. In addition, the computations required, which are based on the assignment of transitions associated
504   to these actions, did not easily lend themselves to the use of a continuous space in the image of the topological group
505   SO(n), which forced us to use only a finite sample of actions drawn from this group and probably seriously limited the
506
      learning speed in our experiments.
507   Future work could seek to overcome these problems by defining in advance relevant elements of the group as primitive
508   actions, before generating from these well-chosen elements a subgroup of SO(n) or any other group chosen to represent
509   the dynamics of these actions. This approach would be more rigid as it would restrict itself in advance to certain
510   primitives. Indeed, while infinitesimal generators could be used, these would be drawn from the Lie group of the
511   function class used for representation and therefore deal with a class of operators that is much more diverse and difficult
512
      to understand. However, it would provide a partial solution to the discretisation problem, and would be more compatible
      with an end-to-end deep learning approach instead of relying on algebraic operations that are disconnected from the
513
      other parameters of the agent’s systems.
514
515   Another solution to this problem would be to allow the agent to learn the representation itself, i.e. a continuous
516   morphism defined over a continuous space of actions and with values in SO(d) or any other relevant group of functions
517
      acting on the space of observations. Although this approach would be more complex to implement, we did not pursue it
      in this paper primarily because it would have been more difficult to construct composite actions insofar as the control
518
      space generally has no group structure. That being said, using a much higher-dimensional space which would extend
519
      the original action/command space together with an encoder/decoder that would map this larger space to the rotations
520   associated with action sequences of varying length could allow a viable continuous representation to be learned.
521
522
      Another difficult hypothesis to verify in our experiments was the reversibility of actions. Indeed, while this is a
      prerequisite for preserving distances, it is partially inadequate in the implementation chosen, which only retains
523
      transitions ‘forwards’ in time and does not attempt to establish a bidirectional model (as proposed for instance by Lai et
524
      al., 2020, (15), Hu et al., 2023 (10) or Höftmann et al, 2023 (9)), even though learned rotations lend themselves just as
525   much if not better to bidirectional planning (as proposed by G. Baldassarre in 2003 (1)) compared to unidirectional
526   models.
527
528
529
      6   Conclusion
530
531
      In this paper, we presented the theoretical justification and ran experiments to build and refine a framework enabling RL
      agents to develop Self-Improving Logic from Experimental Observations, thus proposing a novel method to build and
532
      refine representations of actions as group actions. We therefore allow autonomous agents engaged in a reinforcement
533
      learning procedure to derive, from the dynamics of the actions they perform, a dynamic representation that may be
534   intuitively understood by human beings while remaining useful to the optimisation of the agent’s decision-making
535   process. While the initial results are encouraging as they were obtained on tasks deliberately chosen to be difficult
536   to solve in order to test the limits of our framework, the proposed implementation still suffered from limitations that
537   impacted its overall performance. There also remains several open questions both in terms of evaluation and concrete
538   implementation of certain ideas or natural extensions of this paper, which would need to be answered by future works
539   in order to confirm that this new perspective is viable and could improve in the long run both the performance and our
      understanding of RL agents and their reasoning capabilities.

704   A.1     Homeomorphism between a compact K ⊂ Rd and K ′ ⊂ S d
705
706   While the following is an elementary geometric result derived from the homeorphism between a d+1-dimensional
707   Riemannian sphere without a pole and a d-dimensional plane, we use this appendix to present different homeomorphisms
708   that may be used for experiments, and their advantages and drawbacks in a control setting. Here the compact subset K
709   on the plane is implicitly either the observation space or a learned latent space, both of which are assumed compact
      either as a consequence of the scope of each training environment or as the output of a multi-layer perceptron with

      linear activation for instance.

      A.1.2    Remarks
733
734   While stereographic projections are conformal, that is, preserve angles, they do not preserve distances; while the
735   section of S d+1 cut by the plane xd+1 = 1/2 in our example remains unchanged, the section of the plane formed
736   by the openTdisc within this circle is the image of the part of the sphere that lies "below" the plane. To be precise,
737   π −1 (S d+1 xd+1 < 1/2) = B( √12 ), yielding significant distance distortions. It is even a theorem; due to a sphere
738   having positive Gaussian curvature compared to a plane’s null Gaussian curvature, no homeomorphism between the
739   two can be simultaneously isometric and conformal. While their preserving of angles make stereographic projections
740
      more useful for our framework, since SO(d) is parametrised by sets of d rotation angles, in a RL setting with sensitive
      gradients isometric projections might yield better results.
741
742   One such projection would be the "wrapping" of K, which is in essence the inverse of an isometric projection of the
743   sphere’s surface. On S d , such an isometric wrapping may be achieved using a normal geodesic coordinate system
744   (with the associated geodesic distance) around a reference point. Taking as reference point x0 = (0, ..., 0, 1), and d
745   normal geodesics intersecting at x0 for instance, B(0, π) in Rd is equivalent to S d \(−x0 ) by equating normal geodesic
746   coordinates centered on x0 and Cartesian coordinates centered on 0. If K ⊂ Rd is a set of possible observation and
747
      supx∈K ||x||∞ < M , a simple scaling operation would likewise allow to project isometrically this d-dimensional
      observation set K on the d-dimensional sphere. However, beyond being more expensive to compute due to the back-
748
      and-forth conversion between Cartesian and geodesic coordinates, rotations using such isometric projections would be
749
      less meaningful due to the distortion of angles. As an example, on the 2-dimensional sphere this process already yields
750   equilateral triangles whose sum of angles is equal to 270°.

796   A.2.2    Approximation in SO(n)
797
798   We have shown that for any "action" a ∈ A, its group action on the observation space Ω could be extended into a linear
799   isometric map of Rn . As is, we already have ra ∈ O(n), therefore either ra ∈ SO(n) or ra ∈ Sym(Rn ).
800   In the latter case, as the determinant is a continuous function one cannot expect to find an element of SO(n) in an
801   arbitrary close neighbourhood of ra . This continuous nature also implies, by the intermediate value theorem and since
802   A is a topological group, that the set of all a ∈ A such that det(ra ) = −1 is disconnected from its complementary. The
803   same theorem also implies that actions in the same connected component as the null action act through elements of
804   SO(n) on the observation space.
805   However, let us recall that we are considering agent actions, that is, actions executable at a time step by a given RL agent.
806   This implies notably that the topological group A is either connected or very close to being connected. Indeed, we
807   may intuitively consider that any command given to an actuator may even be pathwise connected to the null command.
808   The actions considered in our framework however also encompass to a certain extent environmental perturbations,
809   represented by our composite random variable τ in our problem formulation. As such, discrepancies may emerge and it
      cannot be ruled out that due to threshold effects for instance A might contain several connected components.

      A.3.3    Optimality of the resulting dynamics model
865
866   The entire purpose of our framework is to enable a RL agent to learn and refine action representations through their
867   experimental dynamics. In order to restrain action dynamics to elements of SO(n) however, we made the essential
868   hypothesis that actions would be distance-preserving. While already difficult to satisfy on the original observation
869   space, by projecting this space on the n-1-dimensional sphere we introduced another distortion, at least of either angles
870   or distances.
871   If a distance-preserving projection is used, modelling the dynamics on the sphere becomes equivalent to modelling
872   them on the original space; in that case, since under our hypothesis the group action associated with an action a ∈ A is
873   identifiable with an element of SO(n), and since as shown before the uniqueness of the solution depends only on the
874   rank of the set of observations used, this representation is optimal. Indeed, the purpose of any dynamics model is to
875   learn the group action associated with each of the possible actions in the set A, and under our assumptions solving a
876
      particular instance of Wahba’s problem accomplishes this task.
877   The most relevant and convenient projections however are stereographic projections. Indeed, since they preserve angles,
878   a rotation matrix learned on the n-1-dimensional sphere may be restricted to the original observation space and be
879   interpreted with minimal calculations. Furthermore, while they distort the distances between observation vectors, these
880   distortions are locally negligible since these projections are C ∞ . We may therefore reuse the small time increments
881
      argument used in Appendix 2 to restrict the actions considered to those that can be completed by the agent in a small
      enough time frame. Intuitively, these actions have smaller eigenvalues, which confines the image of any given point by
882
      this action to a small neighbourhood around it and prevents significant distance distortions. Thus, even in the case of
883
      stereographic projections, it remains possible under this condition to approximate the distance-preserving requirement
884   and to achieve a near-optimal dynamics model under our framework.

      A.4     Complexity considerations
887
888      A first criterion to take into account before considering the theoretical performance of our approach is the complexity
889   of the operations to be carried out. As it stands, each action in our method requires two components: a vector giving
890   its position in the command space and a rotation translating the dynamics observed by the agent, giving a minimum
891   memory complexity in O(a ∗ d2 ) floating point numbers if the control space is of dimension a and the observation space
892   is of dimension d. In our implementation, we also used a pool of 2.d state transitions used to periodically recalculate
893   the rotation, which was not necessary as this update could be done without keeping the transitions in memory, and did
894   not change the total memory complexity. With a finite number of actions a priori independent of a or d, this theoretical
895
      complexity remains unchanged, although in our experiments an agent was able to build up to 11,000 actions after
      pruning, including 800 primitives for a space of dimension 223, suggesting a real memory cost of O(d3 ) or even O(d4 )
896
      floats.
897
898
899
         From a computational point of view, evaluating the dynamic model comes down to a simple matrix product, resulting
      in a cost of O(d2 ) multiplications. This is the main computational advantage of our method, since it allows us to
900
      estimate the dynamics of an agent with its environment with a quadratic cost with respect to the complexity of the
901   environment in question and not in O(||(a + d) ∗ d||3 ) for example by using a perceptron with two hidden layers as
902   in TD-MPC2. The cost of updating a rotation matrix after observing a state transition is also relatively low. Indeed,
903   the cost of the singular value decomposition of a matrix compiling transitions as used in our implementation, which
904   corresponds to the creation of an action, is in O(d3 ) operations, although very fast in practice when optimised by the
905   Pytorch library. For an optimal theoretical complexity, assuming that each rotation has singular values of multiplicity at
906   most 1, for example due to rounding errors or experimental noise in the observations, the singular value decomposition
907   operation is differentiable (cf.Papadopoulo and Lourakis, 2000 (17)), reducing the cost of an update to O(d2 ) operations
908   described in the article to be added to the calculation of the same complexity of y t z which is the matrix to which to
909
      apply this method if y → z is the observed transition. Updating the action used after an iteration therefore costs O(d2 )
      in theory; we have chosen an implementation with a cost of O(d3 ) in practice, however, due to the many non-optimised
910
      operations in Python that are required by the method proposed by Papadopoulo and Lourakis.
911
912   As for the functions used at greater or lesser intervals during training, their complexity is more significant. The pruning
913   algorithm used stores a defined number of transitions during pre-training before any rotation is calculated, as a basis for
914
      the K-Medoids algorithm used to build a first set of transitions.
915   Insofar as a larger number of stored transitions increases the representativeness of our action set, an order of magnitude
916   of O(d2 ) transitions was used, for a total memory footprint of O(d3 ) floating point numbers. The other arrays used, for
917   example the one constructed from this one to store the vectors in the control space representing the observed actions,
      have a lower or similar memory cost. Assuming a maximum number of repetitions T , the K-Medoids algorithm used (a

      variant of Lloyd’s heuristic) thus has a complexity in O(d5 .T ), insofar as the method is applied to the O(d2 ) commands
919   given at each transition. The number of primitive actions retained, although small in general, can go up to O(d) in
920   practice. By retaining the dominant terms, the part of the pruning algorithm dedicated to the construction of new
921   primitives therefore has a memory footprint of O(d3 ) floating point numbers and costs O(d5 T ) elementary operations
922   in our implementation. The second part of the pruning algorithm, corresponding to the creation of composite actions,
923   incurs a much lower cost. In fact, the number of occurrences of a given action can be stored and updated with it,
924   allowing us to identify those that are over-represented in a time and memory space that is linear with the number of

936   from one to the other has a time complexity in O(d4 ) operations assuming a very large number of actions of the order
