                                                                                     A BSTRACT
033
034           World models integrate raw data from various modalities—such as images and
035           language to simulate comprehensive interactions in the world, thereby displaying
036
              crucial roles in fields like mixed reality and robotics. Yet, applying the world
              model for accurate video prediction is quite challenging due to the complex and
037
              dynamic intentions of the various scenes in practice. In this paper, inspired by
038
              the human rethinking process, we decompose the complex video prediction into
039           four meta-tasks that enable the world model to handle this issue in a more fine-
040           grained manner. Alongside these tasks, we introduce a new benchmark named
041           Embodied Video Anticipation Benchmark (EVA-Bench) to provide a well-rounded
042           evaluation. EVA-Bench focused on evaluating the video prediction ability of hu-
043           man and robot actions, presenting significant challenges for both the language
044           model and the generation model. Targeting embodied video prediction, we propose
045           the Embodied Video Anticipator (EVA), a unified framework aiming at video
046           understanding and generation. EVA integrates a video generation model with a
047
              visual language model, effectively combining reasoning capabilities with high-
              quality generation. Moreover, to enhance the generalization of our framework,
048
              we tailor-designed a multi-stage pretraining paradigm that adaptatively ensembles
049
              LoRA to produce high-fidelity results. Extensive experiments on EVA-Bench
050           highlight the potential of EVA to significantly improve performance in embod-
051           ied scenes, paving the way for large-scale pre-trained models in real-world pre-
052           diction tasks. The video demo and benchmark information will be available at
053           https://sites.google.com/view/iclr25-eva.

      To benchmark this task, we start by reformulating the problem in a coarse-to-fine manner. Given
      common sense that frame-level prediction of video generation is a minimum step of prediction, it is
094
      not enough for a task-level prediction. As shown in Figure 2, each task-level prediction step requires a
095
      comprehensive understanding of the current situation, followed by a next-step prediction of what will
096   happen. Such prediction, in the human mind, could further become pictures or so-called imagination.
097   Then we wonder whether this imagination satisfied this task or not. Such a rethinking process keeps
098   going until this prediction step is completed. Therefore, with the inspiration of recent work (Wei
099   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b; Kawaharazuka et al., 2024), we address these challenges by defining
100   the task within embodied scenes into four meta tasks, Action-Description, How-To, Finish-Thinking,
101   Next-Step, as shown in Figure 1. Furthermore, to evaluate such cases, we introduce an embodied
102   video anticipation benchmark (EVA-Bench) to evaluate the video prediction task.
103   We further propose a novel world model, the Embodied Video Anticipator (EVA), which is capable of
104   generating future prediction videos for embodied scenes, thereby enhancing the interaction experience
105   between humans and machines. To achieve this, we re-collect published datasets from multiple
106   domains to create a diverse dataset and introduce a multi-stage pretraining method. We build an
107   embodied video predictor through a video generation model(VDM) and visual language model(VLM).
      Additionally, we implement a cross-attention alignment model for end-to-end optimization. We

126   2   R ELATED W ORK
127
128   Video Generation With the advent of diffusion-based visual generation models, there has been
129   significant progress in extending the capabilities of video generation. For instance, models like
130   VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023; 2024) and VideoPoet (Kondratyuk et al., 2023) have demonstrated
131   impressive abilities in generating high-quality video. Moreover, video generation models with image
132   conditions like Dynamicrafter (Xing et al., 2023), Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023)
133   and Animatediff (Guo et al., 2023) meet impressive generation quality and have already been used
134
      in many areas. Such weakness also happens in some long video generation method (Wang et al.,
      2023a; Yin et al., 2023). These video-generation models lack reasoning abilities and still struggle
135
      with consistency and understanding.
136
137   World Model World models aim to provide future predictions based on current observations. This
138   concept has been explored in various domains, including Genie (Bruce et al., 2024), which shows
139   interesting ability in gaming simulation, Vista (Gao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b), etc., in
140
      autonomous driving. Video prediction is a special world-model-like task, Seer (Gu et al., 2024),
      AID (Xing et al., 2024) adapting image-to-video generation model to predict the motion of future
141
      frames. Additionally, world models such as RoboDreamer (Zhou et al., 2024) and AVDC (Ko et al.,
142
      2023) have been utilized as robot simulators. Unisim (Yang et al., 2023), for instance, combining
143   pre-trained web-scale data with embodied videos expands world models’ applications. VLP (Du
144   et al., 2023b) integrated language and video generation models for robot planning but stayed at the
145   concept level. A task-level video predictor is still needed.
146
      Embodied Dataset and BenchmarkOn the other hand, such multimodal benchmarks are still
147
      missing. Open-X (Padalkar et al., 2023) provides comprehensive robot data, Ego-exo4d (Grauman
148
      et al., 2024) provides multi-level language annotation to key steps, and Webvid-10M-Action (Xing
149   et al., 2023) updates the action annotation to the internet video dataset. The MMWorld (He et al.,
150   2024) benchmark includes embodied tasks for visual-question-answer tasks. However, there is still
151   a lack of a video-language-to-video-language dataset that can define and benchmark the prediction
152   ability of the world model.
153
      In summary, world models, whether as simulators or future predictors, require a well-defined task
154
      benchmark and model paradigm to advance the field.

179   3.1   TASK F ORMULATION
180
      To begin with, we noticed that there is an existing multi-level of language prediction (Cheng et al.,
181
      2024); in this paper, we limited the discussion to three levels, frame-level, task-level, high-level, etc.
182   Task-level prediction is direct language instruction, like ”pick up the book”, and the serial task-level
183   formulate a higher level instruction, e.g., ”Cleaning the table.” Modern video generation models can
184   make frame-level predictions by extending the previous frames.
185
      This paper aims to solve the video prediction problem at the task level. Given common sense that
186
      frame-level prediction of video generation is a minimum step of prediction, it is not enough for a
187
      task-level prediction. So, the formulation of a task-level prediction and a multi-round of frame-level
188   prediction are required.
189
190   The world model function M takes visual observations O and a question Q as inputs, producing a
191   predicted video V̂ and a text response Â as outputs:
192                                               (V̂ , Â) = M(O, Q)                                           (1)
193
194
      To ensure reliable task-level predictions, the model first comprehends the observed videoO. It
      then decides whether to provide frame-level or task-level predictions. This decision is transformed
195
      into predicted videos V̂ , which are then iteratively refined until they meet task-level requirements.
196
      This process involves four key tasks: Action-Description (demonstrating understanding), How-To
197
      (indicating simulation quality), Finish-Thinking (checking task-level generation satisfaction), and
198   Next-Step (showing task-level prediction capability). Only with a solid foundation in these four tasks
199   can the world model make further tasks or higher-level predictions.

      4.1   VLM BACKBONE
271
272   VLM Backbone In EVA, we utilize a 7B Visual Language Model (VLM) backbone called ChatU-
273   niVi (Jin et al., 2024). The visual foundation model is CLIP (ViT-L/14) (Radford et al., 2021),
274   while the LLM backbone is the Vicuna-v1.5 (Zheng et al., 2023) model. Moreover, an adaptive
275   parameter-free token clustering algorithm (Jin et al., 2024) is used to reduce the number of video
276   tokens. This backbone setting introduces additional computational overhead and allows training
277   to remain within the 2048-token limit. We first trained the visual project on the Video-Descrtption
278
      dataset. We then independently trained the full parameter fine-tuning of LLM on the QA Instruction
      Tuning dataset, as shown in Figure 4.
279
280   Interaction Token Inspired by (Peng et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024), we use special tokens to fit the
281   task better. For image input, the VLM uses an <image> token as a placeholder within text tokens.
282   Before being processed by LLM, the <image> token is replaced by visual feature tokens, obtained
283   through a visual encoder and visual projector layer. For video inputs, the number of <image> tokens
284
      used corresponds directly to the number of frames in the video. During generation, we concatenate
      prefix token <IMG P> with VLM language input as query embeddings to extract the feature. As
285
      shown in Figure 4, after obtaining the query embeddings, we use it as a condition for VDM to
286
      substitute for text prompt.
287
288
      4.2   VDM BACKBONE
289
290   We utilize a 1.5B pre-trained Latent video diffusion model (VDM) called Dynamicrafter (Xing et al.,
291   2023) for the generation model. The VDM conditions on image, fps, and language embedding
292   features to generate a 16-frame video in 2 seconds. It UNet incorporates temporal and spatial
293   transformers, as illustrated in Figure 4, and is pre-trained on a large-scale web video dataset, enabling
294   it to generate dynamic content for open-domain images.

355   4.5   M ULTI - STAGE T RAINING S TRATEGY
356
357
      To train EVA, we employ a three-stage process. In the first stage, we train the Video-Language
358   Model (VLM) and Video-Domain Model (VDM) separately, and using datasets like COCO (Chen
359   et al., 2015) and a curated subset of CC3M-595K (Sharma et al., 2018) in VLM. The second stage
360   involves aligning the VLM with an instruction tuning dataset, transforming it into an embodied QA
361   bot using multimodal instruction data from sources such as MIMIC-IT (Li et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu
362   et al., 2023), and VideoChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024), along with our EVA instruction tuning dataset
363   (introduced in Section 5). In the final stage, we adapt the entire pipeline and train the adapter and VDM
364   Ensemble-LoRA for each task, using the VLM’s output hidden embedding as a condition control
365   signal. This stage addresses the diverse visual distribution among EVA-Instruction datasets(EVA-
366
      Instruct) by tuning the Ensemble-LoRA and adapter to maximize generation quality. More detailed
      information on these stages is provided in the appendix and experiment.

370   4.6   S ELF -A SK INFERENCE
371
372   Last, we propose self-ask inference steps to achieve the embodied prediction task. Given the question
373   and visual observation, we first forward the inference of the VLM and obtain the hidden language
374   state. The hidden state was later forwarded to the generation adapter, which was used as a condition
375   to generate the prediction video. After each round of generation, the model follows the self-ask
376   strategy (Press et al., 2022), which rethinks by asking the model itself, as shown in Figure 3, to check
377   its prediction is a completed video. If the last frame does not satisfy the task completion requirement,
      the model will autonomously generate the extension of the video based on the previous prediction.

393
394   In this section, we give a comprehensive experiment to evaluate the multimodal understanding and
395   generation ability of EVA on four meta-tasks. First, we assess EVA’s VQA reasoning abilities on the
396   Action-Description task in Section 5.1, highlighting the knowledge gaps in existing Vision-Language
397   Models (VLMs) regarding embodied scenes. Next, we provide a detailed comparison of generation
398   performance in Section 5.2, including the video score metrics from EVA-Score on the Finish-Thinking
399   task. In Sections 5.3 and 5.4, we compare EVA against four baselines using EVA-Score to represent
400   the capabilities when facing How-To and Next-Step tasks. Both qualitative and quantitative results
401
      are presented throughout, with additional qualitative analyses available in Appendix B. More video
      demonstrations can be found on the anonymous page at https://sites.google.com/view/iclr25-eva.
402
403   EVA Instruction Tuning Dataset(EVA-Instruct) It encompasses four tasks featuring videos of
404   humans, real-world robots, and simulated robots. The complete EVA instruction-tuning dataset
405   consists of 500K QA pairs sourced from Open-X-Embodiment (Padalkar et al., 2023), Ego4d
406   (Grauman et al., 2022), Ego-Exo4d (Grauman et al., 2024), and CALVIN (Mees et al., 2022). The
407
      EVA-Instruct is presented in a conversational format, paired with single images and videos as visual
      input. Data sources are summarized and more details are included in Appendix A.5.
408
409   Datasets for EVA-Bench. EVA-Bench includes a curated collection of 125 high-quality samples
410   from our EVA-Instruct dataset. These samples encompass real-world robots, simulated robots, and
411   egocentric human daily activities. The benchmark is categorized based on meta-tasks. details are
412   included in Appendix D.
413   Implementation details. We set up two kinds of models, EVA-Generator and EVA. EVA-Generator
414   uses Dynamicrafter as the backbone and fully fine-tunes it on the EVA-Instruct. EVA constructs an
415   end-to-end pipeline with fine-tuned ChatUniVi as our VLM backbone and EVA-Generator as our
416   VDM backbone, in the middle, we use a generation adapter to align the feature embedding among
417   two backbones and only train the adapter with the EVA-Instruct.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduced the Embodied Video Anticipator (EVA), a unified framework for video
530   understanding and generation in embodied scenarios. By decomposing complex video prediction
531
      tasks into four meta-tasks and using the Embodied Video Anticipation Benchmark (EVA-Bench)
      for evaluation, we demonstrated significant improvements in predicting human and robot actions.
532
      EVA’s integration of a video generation model with a visual language model, along with a multi-stage
533
      pretraining paradigm, enhances its performance in real-world applications. Our extensive experiments
534   highlight EVA’s potential to advance large-scale pre-trained models in practical prediction tasks.
535
536   Limitation In this work, we benchmark the giving embodied video prediction task. However, such
537
      benchmarks only consider the interleaved video and language. In embodied scenes, we are expecting
      to introduce action, sensors, and more modalities into this dataset. Moreover, it will introduce more
538
      diverse scenarios, including autonomous driving, human-like robots, etc., in the future.

816   A.1   V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODEL
817
818   The Modern Video Language Model (VLM) is based on a Large Language Model (LLM). It leverages
819   the powerful language capabilities of a pre-trained language model to transform the input image
820   or video In into latent visual features ϕ and then generates a language description output τ . The
821   representation equation of VLM is:
822
823
824
                                                τ = VLM(ϕ + ψ)                                           (6)
825
826   where ϕ = Encoder(In ), and ψ represents the text embedding. Here, τ is typically in the language
827   embedding sequences, and the visual encoder projects the visual content In into the text embedding
828   domain. By this method, VLM trains the visual information in an autoregressive format, similar to
829   another language model.
830   Given our limited computational resources, it was essential to represent more video frames using
831   fewer video tokens. ChatUniVi’s adaptive parameter-free token clustering method significantly
832   reduces the number of video tokens without introducing additional computational overhead, allowing
833   training to remain within the 2048-token limit. We independently trained the VLM on the Embodied-
834   Video-Description dataset, as shown in Fig. 4.
835   We tested several VLM backbones, including ChatUniVi (Jin et al., 2024), LLaVA-OneVision (Li
836   et al., 2024a), LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024a), MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024), and other mdoels. We
837   found that while existing models were capable of generating detailed descriptions, they struggled with
838   tasks involving prediction and planning, primarily due to the lack of relevant data in their training
839   corpora. Additionally, the simplicity of the text prompts used in the diffusion model’s training data
840   necessitated concise responses from the VLM. These responses needed to be composed of short,
841   straightforward sentences that clearly include a subject, verb, object, location, and destination. As a
842
      result, we needed to fine-tune the VLMs fully on our dataset.
843
844   A.2   L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODEL
845
846   The Diffusion Model (Ho et al., 2020) is a type of generative model that iteratively refines a noisy
847   input to generate high-quality data samples. It leverages a series of denoising steps to transform
848   an initial noise distribution z0 ∼ N (0, I) into a desired data distribution x. The process involves
849   gradually removing noise from the input, guided by a learned model ϵθ to produce a clear and
850   coherent output. The latent diffusion model(LDM) further uses VAE to scale down the input features
851
      and reduce computation costs. Given the initial condition, the representation equation of the LDM
      (Rombach et al., 2022) is:
852
853
854
                                           xT = VAE(Unet(z0 ) + ϵ)                                       (7)
855
856
857   where z0 is the initial noise, xT is the final output, Unet is the denoising network, and ϵ represents
858   the text or other control condition embeddings. The Variational Autoencoder (VAE) further decode
859   the latent feature into video.
860   In our comparisons with Animatediff (Guo et al., 2023), VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2024), and
861   Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024), we found that Dynamicrafter (Xing et al., 2023), which employs
862   additional image condition injection methods, excels in retaining low-level features and maintaining
863   high consistency for training-free longer video extensions. The core components of this VDM include
      a VAE encoder and decoder, an image condition resampler, and a denoising UNet.

906
907   During the multimodal pretraining stage, we use image-caption pairs from datasets such as COCO
908
      (Chen et al., 2015) and a filtered subset of CC3M (CC3M-595K) (Sharma et al., 2018), curated by
      LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023). The Visual Language Model (VLM) is pre-trained for one epoch with a
909
      batch size of 128, using the AdamW optimizer (Kingma, 2014) and a cosine learning rate schedule.
910
      The learning rate is set to 2e-3, with a warmup rate of 0.03.
911
912   In the joint instruction tuning stage, we incorporate multimodal instruction data from several sources:
913   (i) the EVA-Instruct dataset, (ii) multimodal in-context datasets like MIMIC-IT (Li et al., 2023), (iii)
914
      visual instruction datasets such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), and (iv) video instruction data from
      VideoChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024). All input images or frames are resized to 336 × 336. Chat-UniVi
915
      is trained for two epochs with a batch size of 128 and a learning rate of 2e-5.
916
917   Stage 1: Training VLM and VDM Separately In the first stage, we train the Video-Language
      Model (VLM) and Video-Domain Model (VDM) separately to fit them into the embodied prediction

      domain. During VLM training, we aim to align the video encoder with a language model using
919   image-caption pairs from various datasets, including COCO (Chen et al., 2015) and a curated subset
920   of CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018) (CC3M-595K) screened by LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023).
921
922
      Stage 2: Aligning VLM with Instruction Tuning Dataset In the second stage, we further align the
      VLM with the instruction tuning dataset, transforming it into an embodied QA bot. We incorporate
923
      open-domain multimodal instruction data from multiple sources: multimodal in-context instruction
924
      datasets (MIMIC-IT (Li et al., 2023)), LLaVA visual instruction datasets (Liu et al., 2023), and video
925   instruction data from VideoChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024). We then add our EVA instruction tuning
926   dataset as described in Section 3. All input images and frames are resized to 336 × 336.

      Stage 3: Adapting the Pipeline and Training Ensamble-LoRA In the final stage, we adapt the
      entire pipeline and train the adapter and VDM Ensamble-LoRA specifically for each task. Using
929
      the output hidden embedding of the VLM as a condition control signal, we employ a Q-former
930
      generation adapter to project the feature into VDM’s condition input shape. This stage utilizes
931   the EVA instruction tuning dataset. Despite the diverse visual distribution among EVA-Instruction
932   datasets, the language annotations are similar. Under the limited data scale, these domains can
933   negatively affect each other. Therefore, we propose a special tuning method that tunes the Ensamble-
934   LoRA and adapter to maximize generation quality. In this setup, the generation adapter is trained
935   on the whole dataset, while the Ensamble-LoRA is trained on each domain separately. We compare
936   tuning VDM LoRA, CDM full-parameter tuning, and a two-stage inference method in Section 5.

1285   D.5    E VALUATION P ROMPTS
1286
       To evaluate general Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in a zero-shot setting, results using standard
1287
       text evaluation metrics are often poor. Through experimental analysis, we found that traditional
1288
       metrics like BLEU, METEOR, and ROUGE-L do not effectively capture similarities in actions,
1289   objects, locations, and other essential factors. Therefore, we follow the approach proposed by
1290   EgoThink (Cheng et al., 2024) and employ GPT-4O to assess the predictions of these VLMs.
1291
1292
       However, directly using EgoThink’s prompts often leads to extreme scores, with many reasonable
       predictions being rated as 0. We believe this discrepancy stems from the increased complexity of
1293
       tasks in embodied scenes, which demands a more nuanced and sophisticated evaluation framework.
1294
1295   For the step description generation task, we guide GPT-4o to assess predictions based on four key
       criteria: object, action type, location, and attribute. Each criterion is scored as follows: 1 if fully

1296
       correct, 0.5 if partially correct or somewhat aligned, and 0 if incorrect. The final score is the average
1297   of these four criteria.

1300

1301

1302

1303   involves movement (e.g., moving an object from one place to another), the evaluation considers
