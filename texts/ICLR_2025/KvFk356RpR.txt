009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            As machine learning becomes increasingly data-dependent, concerns over privacy
012            and content regulation among data owners have intensified. Machine Unlearning
013            has emerged as a promising solution, allowing for the removal of specific data
014            from pre-trained systems to protect user privacy and regulate information. Ex-
015            isting research on Machine Unlearning has shown considerable success in elim-
016            inating the influence of certain data while preserving model performance. How-
017
               ever, the resilience of Machine Unlearning to malicious attacks has not been thor-
               oughly examined. In this paper, we investigate the hidden vulnerabilities within
018
               current Machine Unlearning techniques. We propose a novel adversarial attack,
019
               the Unlearning Mapping Attack (UMA), capable of undermining the unlearning
020            process without altering its procedures. Through experiments on both generative
021            and discriminative tasks, we demonstrate the susceptibility of existing unlearning
022            techniques to UMA. These findings highlight the need to reassess unlearning ob-
023            jectives across various tasks, prompting the introduction of a Robust Unlearning
024            standard that prioritizes protection against adversarial threats. Our extensive stud-
025            ies show the successful adaptation of current unlearning methods to this robust
026            framework. The Python implementation will be made publicly available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      In recent years, the rapid growth of Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Neural Networks (DNNs)
031
      has led to significant advancements in various fields, including image classification (Krizhevsky
032
      et al., 2012; Sahoo et al., 2019), natural language processing (Xu et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2018),
033   and autonomous driving (Litman, 2020). As DNNs become increasingly data-dependent, personal
034   data may be involved in model training, raising critical privacy concerns. In response to privacy
035   regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Regulation, 2018) and the Cal-
036   ifornia Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) (Pardau, 2018), the concept of Machine Unlearning (MUL)
037   has emerged as a potential solution. MUL aims to remove specific data from trained models, allow-
038   ing for the "right to be forgotten." Beyond privacy concerns, content regulation has become another
039   key motivation for machine unlearning (Kurmanji et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024). To remove
040   impermissible knowledge such as unlicensed copyrighted material (Yao et al., 2023), malicious in-
041
      formation (Yao et al., 2023), or harmful capabilities (Shumailov et al., 2024) from models, machine
      unlearning ensures that the unlearned models align with ethical and legal standards.
042
043   While existing MUL techniques have demonstrated strong performance in eliminating the influence
044   of specific data on both privacy-sensitive and content-sensitive tasks (Warnecke et al., 2021; Li
045   et al., 2024; Graves et al., 2021; Tarun et al., 2023; Golatkar et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022), they often
046   overlook a critical vulnerability: the susceptibility of unlearned models to malicious attacks. This
047
      gap raises a crucial question regarding the robustness of current MUL methods:
048
      Are unlearned systems sufficiently resilient to withstand malicious attempts aimed at recovering or
049                                 exploiting the unlearned information?
050
051   Recent research has explored this question by focusing on specific domains such as diffusion models
052   (DMs) and large language models (LLMs). In the context of DMs, several approaches have been
053   proposed to exploit vulnerabilities in content erasure (Zhang et al., 2025; Tsai et al., 2024; Han
      et al., 2024; Pham et al., 2023), yet none address potential remedies for these threats. The most


                                                          1
054
      relevant to ours is the concurrent study (Yuan et al., 2024). It leverages adversarial Suffix prompting
055   to compromise unlearned LLMs, but does not extend its analysis beyond LLMs.
056
057   To address this critical question in a broader context, particularly including various discriminative
058
      tasks and generative tasks in the computer vision domain, we revisit the concept of machine un-
      learning and introduce an unified framework to investigate the hidden weaknesses of current MUL
059
      methods: successfully unlearned knowledge can resurface through appropriate probes. To address
060
      this limitation, we introduce and formulate the concept of robust unlearning and a novel post-MUL
061   adversarial attack, namely Unlearning Mapping Attack (UMA), for empirical verification of MUL.
062   Unlike traditional attempts to alter the MUL process or to tamper with the data to be unlearned, our
063   UMA attack targets failing unlearned models (i.e. reintroducing the unlearned knowledge) without
064   being involved in the unlearning process. As a post-MUL attack, UMA only requires access to the
065   model before and after the unlearning process, which practically makes the model provider or the
066   MUL service provider an ideal candidate for carrying out such an attack. Our experiments, con-
067   ducted on both discriminative and generative tasks using datasets such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
068   Tiny-ImageNet, and ImageNet-1k, demonstrate that even when a model appears to have forgotten
069
      specific data, UMA can successfully retrieve the forgotten information, revealing a critical vulnera-
      bility in existing unlearning techniques.Furthermore, to mitigate this threat, we performed prelimi-
070
      nary studies to incorporate the robust unlearning concept into current unlearning methods to prevent
071
      adversaries from recovering forgotten data, ensuring that unlearned models remain secure even in
072   the face of advanced attacks like UMA.
073
074   Our contribution in this study can be summarized as follows:
075
             • We revisit the definition of Machine Unlearning, and formulate the novel perspective: ro-
076
               bust unlearning.
077
078          • We introduce a post-MUL malicious attack, UMA, to undermine Machine Unlearning,
079
               exposing a critical threat in existing unlearning methods. It compromises the unlearned
               systems without requiring any modifications to the data or the unlearning process, thus
080
               positioning it as a superior evaluation metric for machine unlearning verification.
081
082          • We present extensive empirical efforts to mitigate the threat on the basis of current MUL,
083
               enhancing system robustness against such vulnerability.

085   2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087   Machine Unlearning. Machine unlearning (Cao & Yang, 2015) was initially introduced as a method
088   to remove the influence of specific data points from machine learning models, driven by privacy and
089   security concerns. The most straightforward unlearning method involves retraining the model from
090   scratch without the undesired data. However, as models like LLMs increase in size and complexity,
091   this retraining approach has become computationally prohibitive. To address this, researchers have
092   developed alternative methods, including exact unlearning and approximate unlearning. Exact un-
093
      learning (Golatkar et al., 2020) aims to produce a model that behaves indistinguishably from a model
      trained from scratch without the forgotten data. For instance, SISA (Bourtoule et al., 2021) proposes
094
      to train separate models using multiple disjoint data shards and achieves exact learning by retrain-
095
      ing the corresponding shard models associated with unlearning requests. Considering scalability
096   and cost-efficiency, approximate unlearning provides a more practical alternative, relaxing some
097   constraints to improve efficiency while maintaining acceptable performance. For example, the first-
098   order unlearning leverages the first-order Taylor series expansion of model parameters to compute
099   gradient updates for MUL, while the second-order method incorporates the inverse Hessian matrix
100   for more accurate parameter adjustments (Warnecke et al., 2021). To enhance stability and accuracy
101   across different domains while maintaining computational efficiency, recently, SalUn (Fan et al.,
102   2024) employed a weight saliency map, assigning different importance levels to model parameters,
103   allowing them to be updated at varying rates. Despite these advancements, existing methods remain
104
      vulnerable to sophisticated attacks aimed at extracting forgotten information, leading to significant
      privacy and security concerns.
105
106   Attacks to MUL. The unlearning target usually deals with privacy-related or security-sensitive data,
107   it is naturally assumed to be at risk of malicious attacks by adversaries. In fact, prior research has
      already explored various malicious attempts targeting MUL. Adversarial text prompts and Concept


                                                        2
108
      Inversion attacks are particularly investigated to undermine DMs for content erasure (Zhang et al.,
109   2025; Tsai et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Pham et al., 2023). Targeting more generic unlearning
110   scenarios, Qian et al. (2023) examines the possibility of injecting small, targeted noise into forget
111   samples within the forget set. This manipulation leads the unlearned model to fail in classification
112   tasks. Liu et al. (2024a) induces backdoor behavior in a model through the standard MUL process
113   with selected data. Thudi et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2024) examine how attackers can deceive others
114   into believing the MUL process has been completed when, in reality, the model has not forgotten
115   the specified samples, or the unlearning process was never carried out. They leverage techniques
116   from Data Ordering Attacks(Shumailov et al., 2021) to falsify Proof-of-Unlearning (PoUL), with
117
      the intent of either enhancing model performance or reducing computational costs. Different from
      these works either editing the unlearning data or modifying the unlearning algorithms, this study
118
      investigates post-MUL attacks to expose the hidden vulnerabilities in existing machine unlearning
119
      approaches.
120
121   Unlearning Verification. To evaluate the efficacy of an unlearning algorithm, various verification
122   methods are proposed. Attack-based verification simulates a malicious attacker attempting to resur-
123
      face or extract sensitive information from the unlearned model. For instance, membership inference
      attacks (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017) are explored to determine whether a specific sample was part of
124
      the training data. Similarly, model inversion attacks (Fredrikson et al., 2015) try to retrieve input
125
      samples given the model’s outputs, potentially leaking class information to the attacker. Backdoor
126   attacks, or backdoor verifications, are also widely used to assess unlearning efficacy (Sommer et al.,
127   2020; Gao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). Alternatively, following Proof of Learning (PoL) (Jia et al.,
128   2021), reproducing verification (Zhang et al., 2024) works by recording logs during the unlearning
129   operation, allowing users or third-party inspectors to reproduce the process and verify its authentic-
130   ity, ensuring that the unlearning was executed as claimed. Beyond the above two categorical veri-
131   fication approaches, accuracy verification (Golatkar et al., 2020; Mehta et al., 2022) quantifies the
132   unlearning performance on the retaining and forgetting data, and Relearning time verification (Tarun
133   et al., 2023) infers the unlearning efficacy by counting the elapsed time to relearning the forgotten
134
      information on the unlearned models. In this study, we introduce the UMA attack for evaluating
      unlearning efficacy.

      3.2   ROBUST U NLEARNING
217
218   The limitations and risks discussed in the previous section underscore significant safety concerns
219   with unlearned models, particularly their vulnerability to malicious exploitation for knowledge
220   resurfacing. While current methods generally satisfy the conditions in (1), they fail to guarantee
221   that all traces of the unlearned data are entirely removed from the model, as emphasized in Proposi-
222   tion 2 and 3. Furthermore, given that machine unlearning is inherently tied to security, it is crucial
223   that unlearning algorithms exhibit strong resistance to adversarial attacks. In response, we propose
224
      a new definition, Robust Unlearning, that is adaptable across various machine learning contexts and
      resilient to malicious attacks, offering a more comprehensive and secure approach to unlearning.
225
226   Definition 4 (Robust Unlearning). A unlearning process, U (f (·; ✓), Du ), is considered robust if
227   8x 2 Du , 8 x 2 Rd , we have conditions in (1), plus ||fu ( x , ✓u ) f (x, ✓)|| > "1 for generative
228   tasks and ||fu (x + x , ✓u ) f (x, ✓)|| > "1 for discrimnative models (where || x || < ✏).
229
230   Intuitively, Robust Unlearning ensures that the system is incapable of producing the specified infor-
231   mation, whether under normal conditions or in the presence of adversarial manipulation. We propose
232   this as a comprehensive and robust standard for defining and evaluating unlearning algorithms. It is
233   important to note, however, that the commonly used retraining method, a baseline in many existing
234
      unlearning techniques, does not inherently satisfy the criteria for Robust Unlearning. Due to the
      generalization capabilities of neural networks, even models trained without specific data may still
235
      generate corresponding information Shumailov et al. (2024), thereby compromising the effective-
236
      ness of the unlearning process. To identify MUL-specific vulnerabilities, the unlearned model can
237   be compared to a retrained model using the Robust Unlearning criteria in Definition 4. For instance,
238   our empirical experiments on discriminative models with class-wise unlearning in Table 1 show that
239   retraining achieves strong robustness, while those evaluated unlearning algorithms are less robust to
240   adversarial attacks, suggesting these vulnerabilities are intrinsic to the unlearning methods.

      4     U NLEARNING M APPING ATTACK
243
244
      Building on this foundation of Robust Unlearning in the previous section, this section formally intro-
245   duces our Unlearning Mapping Attack. It can serve two primary purposes in the context of machine
246   learning. First, UMA generates malicious perturbations tailored to unlearned models, resurfacing
247   forgotten knowledge for adversarial use. Second, UMA is a practical and actionable verification tool
248   for benchmarking unlearning methods. By applying UMA to various unlearning algorithms with the
249   same set of forget data, we can quantitatively assess their vulnerabilities against adversarial prob.
250
251   4.1   T HREAT M ODEL
252
253   Generally, in a machine unlearning workflow, the individuals involved can be divided into two
254   groups: servers and participants (Liu et al., 2024b). Servers include model and service providers,
255
      who are responsible for providing the model or inference platform, while participants consist of data
      contributors and general users. While much of the existing research has focused on threats from par-
256
      ticipants, such as information leakage (Chourasia & Shah, 2023; Chen et al., 2021), adversarial
257
      unlearning (Liu et al., 2024a; Qian et al., 2023), and sabotage the unlearning process (Marchant
258   et al., 2022), only a limited number of studies have examined unlearning threats from the server
259   side(Thudi et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024).
260
261
      In the unlearning threat we formulate in the previous section, the attacker is considered to be in
      the role of the service provider. We assume the attacker has full knowledge of the model before
262
      and after the unlearning process, as well as access to specific samples that need to be unlearned.
263
      Unlike previous studies where the service provider may attempt to fake unlearning to improve model
264   performance or lower computational cost Zhang et al. (2024), here, we assume the attacker’s goal
265   is to extract the forgotten information from the unlearned models. Given the increasing complexity
266   of deep neural network architectures such as LLMs, it becomes nearly impossible to trace the exact
267   amount of knowledge that remains within the network after unlearning, making these models highly
268   susceptible to such attacks. Note, while the server is the most likely adversary due to its privileged
269   access to the white-box attack setting, the threat is not strictly limited to the server. Anyone with
      access to the knowledge could theoretically execute UMA.

      4.2   ATTACK F ORMULATION
271
272   Recall that reproducing verification, such as Proof of Unlearning (PoUL), is a unique form of un-
273   learning verification designed to ensure transparency from the model training side by requiring the
274   model trainer to report model parameters, data used, and the unlearning algorithm applied. While
275   Thudi et al. (2022) points out that a forged map can be easily created to fake PoUL, the potential for
276   malicious unlearning attempts from the server side is still limited, if not completely prevented.
277   Here, we propose an unlearning mapping attack, which focuses on uncovering residual information
278   within the neural network rather than injecting new information. This attack method enables us
279   to map the original output to a new input, allowing the unlearned neural network to still produce
280   information that should have been removed. In this context, the UMA attack can be formulated as

363   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP

422
423
      As shown in Table 1, class-level unlearning for discriminative tasks generally shows strong robust-
      ness against unlearning mapping attacks, especially in the case of retraining methods. This not
424
      only provides empirical evidence that a model can satisfy the robust unlearning criteria but also
425
      validates that class-wise discriminative unlearning effectively aligns the unlearned model with a re-
426   trained model. Note that we also include Test Accuracy(TA) in our evaluation metrics. While TA
427   does not indicate any robustness of an unlearning method, it does reflect the strength of the unlearn-
428   ing. Since most unlearning methods have some hyperparameters that can adjust their strength, with
429   stronger unlearning resulting in lower test accuracy, one can perform very strong unlearning to have
430   good unlearning performance (low UA and MIA) and very low test accuracy which is impractical.
431   Therefore, TA serves as a balancing metric, where lower TA can imply stronger unlearning, but the
      trade-off needs to be considered for robustness.

525   ing inference, applying UMA-targeted purifiers to all queries before feeding them to the unlearned
526   model might remove this adversarial noise and prevent forgotten knowledge from resurfacing. Pre-
527   liminary studies and their quantitative results in Appendix A.2 show the potential of data purification
528

      alternative toward robust unlearning systems.

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we uncover a critical vulnerability present in most Machine Unlearning methods.
534   We introduce the Unlearning Mapping Attack, which is designed to extract unlearned information
535   from a system following the unlearning process. Our experiments demonstrate that the Unlearning
536   Mapping Attack can successfully retrieve information that was supposed to be forgotten, across
537   both generative and discriminative tasks. Additionally, we conducted a preliminary study on plug-in
538   robust unlearning, which enhances the system’s resilience to such attacks. We hope our work draws
539   attention to the importance of robustness in Machine Unlearning and encourages the integration of
      robustness considerations when designing and evaluating unlearning methods.
