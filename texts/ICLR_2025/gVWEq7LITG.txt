011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Robotic manipulation with dexterous hands is inherently challenging due to their
014            high-dimensional action spaces and the lack of large-scale, high-quality demon-
015            strations. While there are many videos involving interactions between human
016            hands and objects, the frequent, dynamic occlusions between human hands and
017            objects complicate the accurate and robust tracking of hand and object poses, mak-
018            ing it challenging to convert these interactions into high-quality dexterous robotic
019            demonstrations. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel Training-Free
020
               Hand-Object pose tracking pipeline (TF-HOT) that leverages differentiable ren-
               dering and rich priors from pre-trained 2D foundation perception models to per-
021
               form optimization of human hand and object pose trajectories from input videos.
022
               Our method is efficient, allowing us to convert an in-the-wild video to pose trajec-
023            tories in 1 minute, and we demonstrate state-of-the-art performance of our method
024            over in-the-wild videos. Finally, we illustrate an application of our method in im-
025            itation learning by training policies to follow the pose trajectories extracted from
026            TF-HOT, allowing us to learn dexterous manipulation policies that significantly
027            outperform reinforcement learning and imitation learning methods that do not uti-
028            lize hand-object pose trajectory following.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Dexterous hand manipulation tasks are crucial in advancing robotics and artificial intelligence, with
035   significant applications in areas such as virtual reality (VR), teleoperation, and human-robot inter-
036   action (Qin et al., 2022). These tasks are inherently challenging due to their high-dimensional state
037   and action spaces, which lead to poor sample efficiency and complex reward designs when using
038   reinforcement learning (RL) methods (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Recent advancements
039
      in imitation learning have provided alternative approaches to tackle these challenges by leveraging
      demonstrations to guide the learning process (Qin et al., 2023). However, obtaining high-quality
040
      demonstrations for dexterous manipulation is non-trivial. Traditional methods often rely on devices
041
      like VR equipment or exoskeletons for teleoperation (Cheng et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Fang
042   et al., 2024), which may not capture natural human hand movements and may be costly for scaling
043   up to diverse environments and object interactions. Additionally, the discrepancy between human
044   hand kinematics and robotic hand designs introduces gaps between simulation and reality.
045
      Hand-object pose estimation is a fundamental task in computer vision (Chen et al., 2023; Qi et al.,
046
      2024; Liu et al., 2021; 2022; Yang et al., 2021; 2024a; Hasson et al., 2019) that can bridge this
047
      gap by extracting key information from human demonstrations captured in the wild. Accurate es-
048   timation of hand and object poses enables robots to learn manipulation skills directly from human
049   behaviors observed in unstructured environments. However, there are several challenges: 1) Occlu-
050   sion: Severe occlusions during hand-object interactions degrade the performance of pose estimation
051   algorithms. 2) Object diversity: Existing datasets are limited in object diversity and are often cap-
052   tured in controlled environments, limiting the generalizability of trained models to unseen objects
053   and in-the-wild scenarios. 3) Annotation difficulties: Obtaining precise 3D annotations for hand
      and object poses is labor-intensive and impractical at scale, especially in real-world settings.

054
      Current approaches to hand-object pose estimation can be broadly categorized into learning-based
055   methods and optimization-based methods. Learning-based methods typically require datasets with
056   object and hand pose annotations, which are expensive and time-consuming to collect at large scale,
057   especially for real-world data. Thus, these models often struggle to generalize to unseen objects and
058   complex environments. On the other hand, optimization-based methods can potentially generalize
059   better but often rely on multi-camera setups, which limits their use in real-world scenarios.
060
      To address these limitations, we introduce a novel, training-free hand-object pose tracking approach
061   called TF-HOT (Training-Free Hand-Object Tracking). Our core idea is to perform inference-
062   time optimization of pose parameters by utilizing both 3D point cloud observations and rich priors
063   from pre-trained 2D foundation perception models. This method eliminates the need for large-
064   scale annotated data for training a hand-pose tracking model, thereby reducing training costs and
065   enhancing our method’s adaptability for real-world applications. Specifically, we parameterize the
066   optimization variables as a 3D parametric hand model (i.e., MANO (Romero et al., 2022)) and
067   a 6DoF object pose. We then leverage 2D hand joints, 2D hand and object masks, 3D point cloud
068   observations, and multiple regularization terms as constraints to guide the optimization process. This
069
      design effectively utilizes the rich knowledge and strong generalization capabilities of pre-trained
      2D perception models, resulting in a robust and generalizable hand-object pose tracking system.
070
071   Our approach offers several key features: Training-free deployment: TF-HOT requires no model
072   training. Each trajectory’s hand-object pose optimization process can be completed within 1 minute,
073   making TF-HOT adaptable to diverse scenarios and enabling scalable data generation for applica-
074   tions like imitation learning. High accuracy and robustness: By utilizing differentiable rendering-
075
      based joint optimization of hand and object poses and incorporating rich semi-supervisory signals
      from existing 2D foundation models, TF-HOT achieves high accuracy across diverse environments
076
      and remains robust to hand-object occlusions. We also demonstrate the superior performance of
077
      TF-HOT on the public DexYCB dataset compared to baseline methods. For imitation learning
078   applications, we further propose a method named Pose Trajectory Following (PTF) that trains
079   policies to control the robot to follow the pose demonstration trajectories extracted by TF-HOT. By
080   doing so, we can effectively learn dexterous manipulation tasks and significantly outperform imi-
081   tation learning and reinforcement learning methods that do not utilize hand-object pose trajectory
082   following.

      Hand and Object Pose Estimation Hand pose estimation has been tackled using various input
088
      modalities, including depth-based, RGB-based, and multimodal approaches. Depth-based methods
089
      have leveraged Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
090   in works such as (Oberweger et al., 2015; Oberweger & Lepetit, 2017). Additional CNN-based
091   approaches have been proposed, including (Tompson et al., 2014) and (Madadi et al., 2017), while
092   (Malik et al., 2018) introduced synthetic depth data for training. Ensemble learning strategies were
093   also employed (Guo et al., 2017b;a) and are used in some anchor-based methods (Xiong et al.,
094   2019). A recurrent approach using LSTM was presented in (Deng et al., 2022). For RGB-based
095   methods, (Ge et al., 2019) proposed a Graph CNN, while (Zimmermann & Brox, 2017) introduced
096   network-implicit 3D articulation priors. Works such as (Jiang et al., 2023) utilized 3D anchor
097   points. Furthermore, methods extending MANO (Romero et al., 2022), incorporating partial depth
098
      information, were explored by (Baek et al., 2019).
099   In terms of object pose estimation, direct inference of 3D poses has been addressed through various
100   approaches (Xiang et al., 2017). Two-step methods, which first lift 2D keypoints to 3D, have been
101   explored in works such as (Tekin et al., 2018) and (Kehl et al., 2017), while coarse-to-fine strategies
102   were employed in (Rad & Lepetit, 2017). Object pose estimation under severe occlusion has also
103   been investigated by (Peng et al., 2019). (Sun et al., 2022) introduced a non-CAD-based method
104
      for real-time pose tracking. CAD-based methods like (Wen et al., 2024) have shown limitations
      when dealing with heavily occluded objects.
105
106   Many hand-object interaction methods are based on the MANO model (Liu et al., 2021; Chen et al.,
107   2023), and several extended approaches have been proposed to improve performance. These ex-
      tensions include the use of contact potential fields (Yang et al., 2021; 2024a), biomechanical con-

131   Imitation Learning for Dexterous Manipulation Imitation learning (IL) is a promising paradigm
132   for learning robot manipulation policies from expert demonstrations. The simplest form, Behavior
133   Cloning (BC), learns policies to mimic expert actions from offline expert demonstrations and has
134   been shown effective on a wide range of problems (Bain & Sammut, 1995; Pomerleau, 1988; Flo-
135   rence et al., 2022). However, BC struggles with out-of-distribution samples (Ross et al., 2011). An-
136   other approach to imitation is Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) (Abbeel & Ng, 2004; Haldar
137   et al., 2023), which focuses on learning to estimate expert reward function through online interac-
138
      tions, but it suffers from low sample efficiency (Kostrikov et al., 2018; Shen et al., 2022). Incor-
      porating external data into Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithms provides another approach to
139
      solving dexterous manipulation tasks, such as augmenting the replay buffers of RL (Vecerik et al.,
140
      2017; Radosavovic et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2022), or further incorporating additional behavior cloning
141   loss terms (Rajeswaran et al., 2017). However, such methods typically rely on state-action demon-
142   strations, which may not always be feasible when learning directly from human demonstrations
143   (e.g., videos). Another method of incorporating expert demonstrations into RL is using state-only
144   demonstrations to shape the reward (Xu et al., 2023; Christen et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2023). In our
145   work, we utilize pose-only demonstrations from in-the-wild RGBD videos to optimize policies for
146   dexterous manipulation tasks.

      3   M ETHOD
150
151   Given an RGB-D video as input, the objective is to estimate the hand and object poses in each frame.
152
      As depicted in Fig. 1, we introduce a Training-Free 3D Hand and Object pose joint optimization
      pipeline (TF-HOT).
153
154   We use the MANO (Romero et al., 2022) model to represent the 3D hand shape. The MANO model
155   provides two core functions, J and M, which, given the input parameters of pose θ, shape β, rotation
156   r, and translation t, yield the hand mesh M and 3D hand joints j 3d . For brevity, we define the set of
157   hand-related parameters as γ = {θ, β, r, t}.
158   For object pose estimation, we assume that the object model Mobj is readily available. In practice,
159   these models can be obtained through learning-based 3D reconstruction methods from single-view
160   or multi-view images (Wei et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b; Xu et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2023).
161   During the optimization process, the object pose P for each frame is parameterized as a quaternion
      and a translation vector.

302   Initialization Proper initialization is critical in tackling this high-dimensional optimization prob-
303
      lem. In our implementation, we use different strategies for initializing the first frame and subsequent
      frames within a video.
304
305   For the first frame, we initialize the object pose using an off-the-shelf object pose estimation network
306   (Wen et al., 2024). For the hand pose, we uniformly sample N global hand rotations from the SO(3)
307   manifold and randomly sample N pose and shape parameters. The hand’s global translations are
308   initialized by aligning the center of the hand model Mhand to the center of the hand point cloud. We
309
      then optimize γ0i by minimizing Ltotal (γ0i , P0 ) while keeping P0 fixed, and select the optimal hand
310   parameters γ0j corresponding to the lowest 2D joint error: j = arg mini L2d (γ0i ).
311   For subsequent frames, the optimized hand and object poses from the previous frame (γt−1 , Pt−1 )
312   are used as the initialization for frame t.

322   initial pose and finger positions match the first frame of demonstration. To perform imitation learn-
323   ing using the pose-only demonstration, we design a specific trajectory-following reward (Tao et al.,
      2023) for dexterous hand manipulation (see supplementary materials for more details) that measures

345   4.1     DATASETS AND M ETRICS
346
347   We conduct hand-object pose estimation experiments on two datasets: the public dataset
348
      DexYCB(Chao et al., 2021), a benchmark for hand-object interaction, and an in-the-wild dataset
      that we collected by ourselves using a RealSense D435 RGB-D camera.
349
350   DexYCB is a widely used real-world RGB-D dataset designed for hand-object interaction tasks,
351   particularly object pick-up. It presents significant challenges due to its inclusion of fast hand motions
352   and a variety of objects. We evaluate our method across four object categories—box, bottle, can,
353   and bowl—using a total of 384 videos.
354   In our in-the-wild dataset, we captured 14 videos comprising 1,918 frames and featuring six distinct
355   objects. Our method is evaluated across all available videos and frames.
356
      For the DexYCB dataset, we use the following metrics for evaluation: 1) Hand pose evaluation: We
357
      report the mean per joint position error (MPJPE) and the pixel distance between projected 2D joints
358   and their ground-truth locations (J2E). 2) Object pose evaluation: We measure the rotation error rerr
359   and the translation error terr for object pose estimation.
360
361
      For the in-the-wild dataset, ground-truth labels are unavailable. Therefore, we evaluate hand and
      object pose estimation using the following proxy metrics: 1) Hand pose evaluation: We report the
362
      pixel distance between the projected 2D joints and the 2D joints predicted by MMPose, denoted as
363
      J2E∗ . 2) Object pose evaluation: We use the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between the rendered
364   object masks and the masks predicted by SAM2, denoted as IoUobj . Additionally, we report the
365   visible-aware 3D surface distance (defined in Eq. 5) normalized by the number of points in the point
366   cloud, which we denote as SDobj .

396   MPJPE and J2D, we argue that its superior results stem from dataset-specific priors embedded in its
397   training process, which leads to overfitting on the DexYCB dataset and limits its ability to generalize.
398   For object pose estimation, our method achieves the lowest translation error, which we attribute to
399   the use of our visible-aware surface loss that enhances object localization accuracy.
400   Evaluation on in-the-wild dataset When evaluated on the in-the-wild dataset, our method
401   demonstrates superiority in both quantitative metrics (see Table 1) and qualitative outcomes (see
402   Fig. 5(b)). HOTrack’s reliance on point cloud input makes it sensitive to the quality of the point
403   cloud, which, in this case, is derived from masks predicted by SAM2. The instability of the hand
404   mask predictions negatively impacts HOTrack’s performance. In contrast, HOISDF, which only uti-
405   lizes RGB input and lacks direct access to 3D information, suffers from overfitting due to its reliance
406   on learned dataset-specific priors. As a result, HOISDF struggles to generalize when confronted with
407   data that includes unseen camera poses and objects not present in its training data.

530   5                 C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we proposed a Training-Free Hand and Object pose tracking framework (TF-HOT).
533
      Our method leverages differentiable rendering and rich priors from pre-trained 2D perception mod-
534   els for efficient optimization of human hand and object pose trajectories from input videos. We
535   demonstrate that TF-HOT achieves superior performance over baseline methods on in-the-wild
536   videos. Additionally, we showcase the application of our method in learning dexterous robotic
537   tasks by introducing a Pose Trajectory Following (PTF) algorithm that trains a policy to follow the
538   pose demonstrations extracted by TF-HOT from videos. Experiments demonstrate that our approach
539   facilitates better and easier dexterous policy learning compared to reinforcement learning and imi-
      tation learning methods that do not utilize hand-object pose trajectory following.

837   B      D ETAIL OF P OSE T RAJECTORY-F OLLOWING R EWARD
838
839   In our method, two key trajectories need to be tracked: one is the pose of the hand’s fingertips
840   relative to the manipulated object, and the other is the absolute pose of the object in the world
841   frame. The difference between two poses is measured by a function d(p1 , p2 ), where p denotes
842   the pose, consisting of both position and quaternion. We consider two poses to be matched when
843   d(p1 , p2 ) is smaller than a constant ϵ. At each step of the environment state, the trajectory-following
844   reward is a metric that evaluates how much progress the state has made toward matching the given
845   demonstration. Algorithms for computation of the trajectory-following reward during one episode
846   rollout are described in Alg. 1. This reward function encourages the hand to achieve the correct
847
      posture relative to the object, while simultaneously guiding the object to the desired pose.

892
893   We design two types of environment rewards: sparse and dense. The sparse reward is a two-stage
894   reward, where the agent receives 0.4 when the object is lifted and 1 when the object reaches the goal.
895   The dense reward extends the sparse reward by adding terms for reaching. It can be written as:
896
897                r = max {Normalize (w1 rhand reaching + w2 robj reaching + w3 Ilifting ) , Isuccess } ,   (11)
898   where rhand reaching = (1 − tanh(c1 · d(hand, obj))), robj reaching = 1 − tanh(c2 · d(obj, goal)), and
899   w1 , w2 , w3 , c1 , c2 are constants. These terms are designed to encourage the hand to reach the object
900   and lift it to the goal position.

938   E    L IMITATIONS
939
940   Despite the strengths of our method, it encounters limitations in scenarios where the hand is com-
941   pletely occluded or absent in the point cloud, as it lacks sufficient 3D priors to estimate accurate po-
942   sitions. However, these challenges could be mitigated by extending our framework to a multi-camera
943   setup, which would improve accuracy through the aggregation of losses from multiple viewpoints.
944   Additionally, our method has the potential to be utilized for automatic data annotation, offering a
945
      valuable tool for future research and applications.
