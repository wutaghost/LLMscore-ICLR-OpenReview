009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Loss spikes often occur during pre-training of large language models. The spikes
012            degrade the performance of large language models and sometimes ruin the pre-
013            training. Since the pre-training needs a vast computational budget, we should
014            avoid such spikes. Based on the assumption that the loss spike is caused by the
015            sudden growth of the gradient norm, we explore factors to keep the gradient norm
016            small through an analysis of the spectral norms of the Jacobian matrices for the
017            sub-layers. Our ﬁndings suggest that stabilizing the pre-training process requires
018
               two conditions: small sub-layers and large shortcut. We conduct various experi-
               ments to empirically verify our theoretical analyses. Experimental results demon-
019
               strate that methods satisfying the conditions effectively prevent loss spikes during
020
               pre-training.

      In this research, we provide theoretical analyses focusing on the loss spike problem during LLM
047
      pre-training. We identify the upper bound of the gradient norms for the Transformer-based LLMs
048   through analyses on the spectral norms of the Jacobian matrices for the sub-layers. If the upper
049   bound is large, the gradients may spike suddenly, and we assume that this phenomenon causes the
050   loss spike. Then, we indicate that the upper bound is large in the typical setting, such as the widely
051   used implementation, Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2020), and thus, the loss spike is likely to occur.
052   In addition, to make the upper bound sufﬁciently small, we introduce two conditions: (1) initializing
053   the parameters of sub-layers with a small value and (2) making the standard deviation of each em-

484   various discussions on the architecture (Xiong et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Takase et al., 2023;

      Xiong et al. (2020) theoretically analyzed gradient scales of each part in Transformers, and indi-
      cated that the Pre-LN Transformer is more stable than the Post-LN Transformer, that is the original
491
      Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). Since the Pre-LN Transformer is more stable than
492
      the Post-LN Transformer theoretically and empirically, recent studies mainly have used the Pre-LN
493   Transformer to construct an LLM. We also assume using the Pre-LN Transformer in the analysis on
494   the training dynamics in this paper.
495
496
      To stabilize the LLM pre-training, Le Scao et al. (2022) applied the layer normalization to the em-
      bedding layer. Zeng et al. (2023) used shrink embedding gradient technique (Ding et al., 2021). In
497
      this study, we theoretically proved that the layer normalization to the embedding layer controls the
498
      upper bound of the gradient norms of sub-layers when we use the widely used initialization method
499   for LLMs (Nguyen & Salazar, 2019; Shoeybi et al., 2020), and thus, it stabilizes the pre-training.
500
501   For the initialization methods, Nguyen & Salazar (2019) proposed a strategy to initialize parameters
502
      of Transformers with small values to stabilize their training. Zhang et al. (2019) and Huang et al.
      (2020) indicated that we can remove layer normalizations in Transformers if we use their proposed
503
      initialization methods. Wang et al. (2022) adjusted initial parameter scales based on the number
504
      of layers to stabilize the Post-LN Transformer. In this study, we indicated that the widely used
505   initialization method (Shoeybi et al., 2020), which makes parameters small, is necessary to stabilize
506   the LLM pre-training. Moreover, we proved that we can prevent the loss spike problem by making
507   the standard deviation of embeddings close to 1.

509   Efﬁciency As shown in Table 3, our modiﬁcation enables the pre-training with a relatively larger
510   learning rate, and can achieve better performance. Thus, this study can be regarded as on the ef-
511   ﬁciency of LLM pre-training because our modiﬁcation can construct a better LLM with a given
512   budget. Strubell et al. (2019) and Schwartz et al. (2019) reported that recent neural methods require
513   substantial computational costs, and thus, they argued that we have to explore a cost-efﬁcient ap-
514   proach. Rajbhandari et al. (2020) proposed ZeRO that reduces memory redundancies during the
515   multi GPU training without increasing communication volume. Dao et al. (2022) focused on GPU
516
      memory reads/writes, and proposed FlashAttention that accelerates the speed of attention mecha-
      nisms in Transformers. To reduce the number of computations in the attention mechanism, Shazeer
517
      (2019) proposed the multi-query attention that shares one key and value across all of the attention
518
      heads in each layer. Takase & Kiyono (2023) explored several parameter sharing strategies, and
519   indicated that parameter sharing across some layers can achieve comparable performance to the
520   vanilla model with a small number of parameters. Moreover, several studies have explored a better
521   construction way with a limited budget (Izsak et al., 2021; Takase & Kiyono, 2021). We believe that
522   we can take advantage of their ﬁndings to make our LLMs more efﬁcient.

      8   C ONCLUSION
526
527
      This paper explored why large language models (LLMs) sometimes experience loss spikes during
      pre-training. To provide evidence, we speciﬁcally focused on the gradients of sub-layers. We in-
528
      troduced an upper bound for the gradient norms through an analysis of the spectral norms of the
529
      Jacobian matrices for the sub-layers. We then theoretically identiﬁed two conditions for avoiding
530   loss spikes: small sub-layers and large shortcut. To meet these conditions, we show that using
531   the widely adopted initialization method for LLMs can make the sub-layer parameters small, and
532   that embedding scaling or incorporating layer normalization into the embedding layer can make the
533   standard deviation of each embedding close to 1, resulting in large shortcut. Experimental results
534   indicated that methods satisfying these conditions avoid loss spikes. Furthermore, these methods al-
535   low for training with a relatively larger learning rate, leading to improved performance. We hope our
536   theoretical analyses and empirical ﬁndings will help avoid wasting valuable time and computational
537   budgets during LLM construction.

1227   As described in Section 2.1, recent studies use the Pre-LN Transformer architecture to construct their
1228   LLMs because the architecture is more stable. In contrast, some recent studies reported that the Post-
1229   LN Transformer, which is the original architecture, can achieve better performance than the Pre-LN
1230   if we address the instability issue in the Post-LN, i.e., the vanishing gradient problem (Liu et al.,
1231   2020; Takase et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022). We discuss whether the Pre-LN Transformer entirely
1232
       underperforms the Post-LN. We conducted experiments on machine translation experiments because
       previous studies mainly focused on them.
1233
1234   We followed the experimental settings in Takase et al. (2023). Table 7 shows the details of hyper-
1235   parameters. We used the WMT English-to-German training dataset (Vaswani et al., 2017; Ott et al.,
1236   2018), and evaluated each model in newstest2010-2016. We used the encoder-decoder architec-
1237   ture proposed by Peitz et al. (2019). To stabilize the Post-LN Transformer, we applied Deep-
1238
       Net (Wang et al., 2022) and B2T connection (Takase et al., 2023). We compared them to Scaled
       Embed, that is, the Pre-LN Transformer with the stabilizing techniques described in this paper.
