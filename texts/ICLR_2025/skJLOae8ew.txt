011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In contemporary architectural design, the generation of innovative and efficient
014            floor plans remains a critical challenge. This research introduces a novel appli-
015            cation of diffusion models, specifically adapted for the generation of architec-
016            tural floor plans. Unlike traditional generative models that broadly target image
017            generation, our approach harnesses the state-of-the-art in diffusion technology to
018
               produce detailed, functional, and visually appealing architectural designs. We
               demonstrate that diffusion models, when finely tuned and conditioned, not only
019
               embrace ’implicit, human-learned’ architectural semantics but also enhance de-
020
               sign efficiency and creativity. The paper details our methodology from adapt-
021            ing the U-Net architecture within diffusion frameworks to incorporating advanced
022            upscaling techniques, significantly reducing computational overhead while main-
023            taining high-resolution outputs. Our results show a promising direction for inte-
024            grating AI in architectural design, opening new avenues for automated, creative
025            design processes that could revolutionize the industry.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The fusion of artificial intelligence (AI) with architectural design has opened new pathways for
031   innovation in how spaces are conceived and visualized. Recent advancements in generative mod-
032   els, particularly diffusion models, have shown unprecedented success in image-generation tasks.
033   However, their application in specialized domains like architectural design, where detail, accuracy,
034   and adherence to design principles are paramount, remains largely unexplored. This study seeks
035   to bridge this gap by adapting and enhancing diffusion models for the specific task of generating
036   architectural floor plans.
037   The motivation behind this research is twofold: firstly, to explore the potential of state-of-the-art
038   AI models to understand and implement complex, implicit rules that govern architectural aesthetics
039   and functionality; and secondly, to provide a tool that significantly augments the architect’s ability
040   to generate diverse design alternatives quickly. By focusing on the specific use case of floor plan
041   generation, we aim to demonstrate how diffusion models can be meticulously tailored to not only
042   generate images but to do so in a way that adheres to professional architectural standards.
043   Our approach involves a customized adaptation of the U-Net architecture configured within a dif-
044   fusion modeling framework. This adaptation is geared towards capturing the nuanced requirements
045   of architectural designs, including the layout of spaces and their functional relationships. Further,
046   we employ upscaling techniques post-generation, allowing the model to operate efficiently at lower
047   resolutions without sacrificing output quality, thus addressing the dual challenges of detail fidelity
048   and computational efficiency.
049   This paper outlines our comprehensive methodology, from dataset preparation and model architec-
050   ture design to the detailed training regimen and the subsequent image enhancement techniques. We
051   present empirical results that illustrate the model’s capability to produce professional-grade floor
052   plans and discuss the potential applications of this technology in real-world architectural practices.
053   Lastly, we explore future enhancements that could enable more interactive and user-specific design
      capabilities, underscoring the transformative potential of AI in the architectural field.

      3     D IFFUSION M ODELS
101
      3.1   OVERVIEW OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
102
103   Diffusion models are a class of generative models that have gained prominence for their ability to
104   produce high-quality, high-fidelity images. Essentially, diffusion models work by gradually trans-
105   forming a distribution of random noise into a structured image over a series of iterative steps, each
106   guided by a learned reverse diffusion process. This process is often visualized as reversing the
107   diffusion of particles from a concentrated point outwards, hence the name ”diffusion models” Sohl-
      Dickstein et al. (2015).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      3.2   R ATIONALE FOR U SING D IFFUSION M ODELS
109
110   The decision to utilize diffusion models in the generation of architectural floor plans was driven by
111   several factors. First, the nature of diffusion models to generate images through a gradual refining
112   process allows for the capture of intricate details that are essential in architectural designs, such as
113   the layout of rooms, placement of doors and windows, and the delineation of living spaces. This
114   ability to manage and manipulate fine details aligns well with the requirements for generating usable
115   and precise architectural plans. Furthermore, diffusion models have demonstrated a lower tendency
116
      towards mode collapse compared to GANs, offering a richer diversity in generated outputs which is
      beneficial for exploring a wide range of architectural styles and configurations Ho et al. (2020).
117
118
119
      3.3   C URRENT S TATE OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
120
      Recent advancements in diffusion models have established them as a leading technology in image
121   generation. The introduction of conditional diffusion models has further expanded their applicabil-
122   ity, allowing for the generation of images based on specific conditions or attributes, thereby increas-
123   ing their utility in tasks requiring high degrees of specificity and customization Dhariwal & Nichol
124   (2021). In the field of architectural design, these advancements present a promising avenue for not
125   only generating floor plans from scratch but also modifying existing ones according to precise user
126   specifications. The state-of-the-art models now feature improvements in training efficiency and the
127   quality of the generated images, which make them particularly suitable for applications where detail
128   and accuracy are paramount Nichol & Dhariwal (2021).

130   4     A RCHITECTURE OF THE U-N ET M ODEL
131
132   4.1   H ISTORY AND BACKGROUND OF THE U-N ET
133
134   The U-Net model was originally developed for biomedical image segmentation. The model was
135   designed to work with a limited amount of data but still deliver strong performance, which was
136
      crucial in the medical imaging field where high-quality, annotated datasets are often small. U-
      Net’s architecture is particularly noted for its effectiveness in handling multi-class segmentation
137
      Ronneberger et al. (2015).
138
139
      4.2   D ESCRIPTION OF THE A RCHITECTURE
140
141   The architecture of U-Net is characterized by its symmetric shape, which gives it the name ”U-Net.”
142   It consists of a contracting path to capture context and a symmetrically expanding path that enables
143   precise localization. The model uses a series of convolutional layers and max pooling layers in the
144   contracting step to extract features and reduce the spatial dimensions of the input image. Each step
145   in the expansive path consists of an upsampling of the feature map followed by a convolutional layer
146   that halves the number of feature channels. A crucial feature of U-Net is the concatenation of feature
147   maps from the contracting path to the upsampled output, a method known as skip connections. These
148   connections help the network to propagate context information to higher resolution layers, allowing
149
      for more precise localization Ronneberger et al. (2015).
150
151   4.3   D IFFERENCES B ETWEEN U-N ET M ODELS AND OTHER M ODELS
152
      Unlike many deep learning models that primarily focus on down-sampling to learn increasingly
153
      abstract representations, U-Net maintains a large amount of high-resolution information through
154   its expansive path. This is in contrast to models like the standard convolutional networks, which
155   may lose important local information due to repeated pooling operations. The ability of U-Net to
156   maintain high-resolution details makes it exceptionally good at capturing the nuances of images,
157   which is essential for tasks requiring precise segmentation and detailed reconstructions.
158
159   4.4   S UITABILITY OF U-N ETS FOR A RCHITECTURAL F LOOR P LAN G ENERATION
160
161   U-Nets are particularly well-suited for the task of generating architectural floor plans due to their
      powerful segmentation capabilities and the ability to handle fine-grained details—a necessity in

162
      architectural designs where accuracy in the layout and clear differentiation of spaces are crucial.
163   For this research, the U-Net’s ability to effectively process and reconstruct complex spatial rela-
164   tionships within an image allows it to generate detailed and precise architectural layouts from a
165   high-dimensional latent space. Furthermore, the skip connections in U-Nets help in recovering the
166   exact spatial hierarchies and relationships of different architectural elements, such as walls, doors,
167   and room designations, ensuring that the generated floor plans are not only aesthetically pleasing
168   but also architecturally coherent.

      5.2   T RANSFORMATION OF THE E NTIRE DATASET
183
      The dataset transformation process involves several key steps designed to ensure that the input im-
184   ages are in a uniform format, which helps in reducing model complexity and improving training
185   efficiency and most importantly, the final generated images. Here is an outline of the transforma-
186   tions applied:
187
      a) Detection of the Floor Plan from the Image
188
189
             • Objective: Isolate the architectural floor plan from any extraneous elements within the
190            image. More specifically, separate the floor plan from the colored background.
191
             • Method: Implement a detection algorithm that utilizes the wall outline image attribute
192
               from the dataset to distinguish the walls of the floor plan from the colored background.
193
194
      b) Rotation to Align the Floor Plan
195
196          • Objective: Given that the dataset presents floorplans with different orientations, here we
197            want to ensure that all floor plans are oriented in the same direction, which is essential for
198            consistent model training.
199          • Method:
200
                 – Contour Detection: The process begins by converting the mask image, which high-
201
                   lights the floor plan’s footprint, to grayscale using the cv2.cvtColor function from
202
                   the OpenCV library Bradski (2000). This grayscale image is then binarized using the
203                cv2.threshold function to create a binary mask where the floor plan is repre-
204                sented by white pixels, and the background is black.
205              – Edge Detection: To refine the contours, the grayscale image is blurred using a Gaus-
206                sian filter cv2.GaussianBlur to reduce noise, and then edges are detected using
207                the Canny edge detection algorithm cv2.Canny Bradski (2000).
208              – Angle Calculation: The largest contour, corresponding to the floor plan, is identified
209                using the cv2.findContours function. From this contour, the minimum area
210                rectangle that can enclose the floor plan is computed using cv2.minAreaRect.
211                The angle of this rectangle is extracted, and a correction is applied if the angle is less
212                than -45 degrees. This correction is necessary to ensure the correct orientation, as
213                angles close to -90 degrees would otherwise flip the floor plan Bradski (2000).
214              – Rotation Application: Once the correct rotation angle is determined, the floor
215                plan is rotated using the cv2.getRotationMatrix2D function to create a
                   rotation matrix, followed by cv2.warpAffine to apply this rotation. The


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
                    cv2.warpAffine function is configured with cv2.INTER CUBIC interpola-
217                 tion for the image and cv2.INTER NEAREST for the mask to preserve edge
218                 sharpness, ensuring the rotation does not introduce padding or distortions. The
219                 cv2.BORDER REPLICATE mode is used for the image rotation to handle border
220                 pixels, while cv2.BORDER CONSTANT with a zero border value is used for the mask
221                 Bradski (2000).
222
223   c) Background Standardization
224
225          • Objective: Homogenize the background of all images to a uniform color, enhancing the
226            model’s ability to focus on the structural elements of the floor plans.
227          • Method: The background of each image is standardized by first checking if the image
228            contains an alpha channel (transparency) and converting it to a standard RGB format using
229            OpenCV’s cv2.cvtColor function Bradski (2000), if necessary. A binary mask is then
230            created by thresholding the grayscale version of the mask, and inverting it so that the back-
231            ground is highlighted. This binary mask is expanded to match the RGB channels of the
232            original image using cv2.cvtColor, ensuring compatibility for background replace-
233            ment. The replacement is performed by creating a new background array filled with the
234            target color (white) using NumPy Harris et al. (2020), and applying it to the non-floor plan
235
               areas indicated by the mask. This process is executed using a custom function, ensuring
               that all images have a consistent, uniform background color.

290   7     E NHANCING R ESULTS THROUGH U PSCALING T ECHNIQUES
291
292   7.1   I NTRODUCTION TO U PSCALING
293
294
      Upscaling, in the context of image processing, refers to the technique of increasing the resolution of
      an image to enhance its resolution and detail. This is achieved by interpolating additional pixels into
295
      the original image using various algorithms designed to predict and replicate the underlying patterns
296
      and textures.
297
298
      7.2   R ATIONALE FOR U SING U PSCALING
299
300   In the generation of architectural floor plans using AI, the resolution of output images directly im-
301   pacts their usability. Higher-resolution images can provide more detail, making them more practical
302   for real-world applications. However, generating high-resolution images directly from the model can
303   be computationally intensive and inefficient. By using upscaling, we can produce lower-resolution
304   images during the generation phase—which requires less computational power—and subsequently
305   enhance their resolution and detail post-generation, achieving great results at a lower cost.
306
307   7.3   A PPLICATION OF U PSCALING T ECHNIQUES
308
      Following the generation of the floor plan images by our model, each image is processed through
309
      the upscaling tool. This post-processing step refines the visual details of the plans, enhancing lines,
310   borders, and textures.
311
312
      7.4   T RADE - OFFS AND A DVANTAGES
313
314   Opting to upscale lower-resolution images rather than directly generating high-resolution outputs
315   presents several advantages. Primarily, it allows for faster model training and less intensive use of
316   computational resources during the generation phase. Training models on high-resolution images
317   not only requires significantly more memory and processing power but also increases the complexity
318   of the model, leading to longer training time.
319   The trade-off here involves balancing the initial quality of generated images with the effectiveness
320   of the upscaling process. By choosing to generate images at a lower resolution, we accept a compro-
321   mise on initial detail with the understanding that the subsequent upscaling will restore and enhance
322   these details efficiently. This strategy has proven effective, enabling the production of high-quality
323   architectural floor plans with a fraction of the computational cost associated with high-resolution
      image generation.
