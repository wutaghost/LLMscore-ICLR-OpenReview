009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a class of structured diffusion models, in which the prior distribution
012            is chosen as a mixture of Gaussians, rather than a standard Gaussian distribution.
013            The specific mixed Gaussian distribution, as prior, can be chosen to incorporate
014            certain structured information of the data. We develop a simple-to-implement
015            training procedure that smoothly accommodates the use of mixed Gaussian as
016
               prior. Theory is provided to quantify the benefits of our proposed models, com-
               pared to the classical diffusion models. Numerical experiments with synthetic,
017
               image and operational data are conducted to show comparative advantages of our
018
               model. Our method is shown to be robust to mis-specifications and in particular
019            suits situations where training resources are limited or faster training in real time
020            is desired.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Diffusion models, since Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020b), have soon emerged as a powerful
026   class of generative models to handle the training and generation for various forms of contents, such
027
      as image and audio. On top of the success, like many other models, training a diffusion model can
      require significant computational resources. Compared to more classical generative models such as
028
      generative adversarial networks (GAN), the inherent structure of diffusion models requires multiple
029
      steps to gradually corrupt structured data into noise and then reverse this process. This necessitates
030   a large number of training steps to successfully denoise, further adding to the computational cost
031   associated with the network and data size.
032
033
      That said, not all scenarios where diffusion models are used enjoy access to extensive training re-
      sources. For example, small-sized or non-profit enterprises with limited budget of compute may
034
      desire to train diffusion models with their private data. In those cases with limited resources, the
035
      training of standard diffusion models may encounter budget challenges and cannot afford the train-
036   ing of adequate number of steps. In addition, there are scenarios one desires to train in real time with
037   streaming data and aims at achieving certain training performance level as fast as possible given a
038   fixed amount of resources. In such cases, it is preferable to further improve classical diffusion mod-
039   els to achieve faster training.
040
      If training resources are limited, insufficient training can hinder the performance of the diffusion
041
      models and result in poorly generated samples. Below in Figure 1 is an illustrative example on gray-
042   scale digits images, showing the performance of denoising diffusion probabilistic models given
043   different training steps. When the model is trained for only 800 steps, it has limited exposure to the
044   data, and as a result, the generated samples are likely to be blurry, incomplete, or show a lack of
045   consistency in terms of digit shapes and structures. The model at this stage has not yet learned to
046   fully reverse the noise process effectively. Our work was motivated by the considerations to improve
047   training efficiency so that, if resources are limited, even with fewer training steps one can achieve
048   certain satisfying level of performance.
049   In this work, we aim to provide one approach based on adjusting the prior distribution, to improve the
050   performances of classical diffusion models when training resources are limited. Classical diffusion
051   models use Gaussian distribution as the prior distribution, which was designed due to the manifold
052   hypothesis and sampling in low-density areas Song & Ermon (2019). However, this approach does
053   not use the potential structured information within the data and considerably adds to the training
      complexity. Admittedly, when training resources are not a constraint, or when the data structure is

088   1.1   R ELATED L ITERATURE
089
090   Diffusion models and analysis. Diffusion models work by modeling the process of data degra-
091   dation and subsequently reversing this process to generate new samples from noise. The success
092   of diffusion models lies in their ability to generate high-quality, diverse outputs. Their application
093
      has expanded across fields such as image and audio synthesis tasks Kong et al. (2020); Dhariwal
      & Nichol (2021); Leng et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2024); Skorokhodov et al.
094
      (2024), image editing Meng et al. (2021); Avrahami et al. (2022); Kawar et al. (2023); Mokady et al.
095
      (2023), text-to-image generation Saharia et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); Kawar et al. (2023), and
096   other downstream tasks including in medical image generation Khader et al. (2023); Kazerouni et al.
097   (2023) and modeling molecular dynamics Wu & Li (2023); Arts et al. (2023), making them a pivotal
098   innovation in the landscape of generative AI. Tang & Zhao (2024) provide further understanding of
099   score-based diffusion models via stochastic differential equations.
100
      Other methods for efficiency improvement. Various literature have contributed to improve the
101
      performance of the diffusion models by proposing more efficient noise schedules Kingma et al.
102   (2021); Karras et al. (2022); Hang & Gu (2024), introducing latent structures Rombach et al. (2022);
103   Kim et al. (2023); Podell et al. (2024); Pernias et al. (2024), improving training efficiency Wang
104   et al. (2023); Haxholli & Lorenzi (2023) and applying faster samplers Song et al. (2020a); Lu et al.
105   (2022a;b); Watson et al. (2022); Zhang & Chen (2023); Zheng et al. (2023); Pandey et al. (2024b);
106   Xue et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024). In addition, Yang et al. (2024) employs a
107   spectrum of neural networks whose sizes are adapted according to the importance of each generative
      step.

      4     M IXED D IFFUSION M ODELS
229
      We propose in this section the mixed diffusion models. Instead of maintaining Gaussian prior distri-
230
      bution, our model chooses the Gaussian mixture model. While still keeping the benefits of Gaussian
231
      prior distributions, i.e., the manifold hypothesis and sampling in low density area, the additional
232   parameters enable the model to incorporate more information about the data distribution and further
233   reduce the overall loads of the reverse process. In what follows, we first introduce how to incorpo-
234   rate data information into the prior distribution and an additional dispatcher. We leave more details
235   of the mixDDPM and the mixSGM in Subsection 4.1 and Subsection 4.2, respectively.
236
      In general, the prior distributions in the mixed diffusion models belong to a class of Gaussian mixture
237
      distributions with centers c1 , · · · , cK . The number of centers K, as well as the specific values of the
238   centers c1 , · · · , cK , are predetermined before training and sampling in our model. These parameters
239   can be flexibly chosen by users of the model, either through domain knowledge, or through various
240   analysis methods. There is no need to concern whether the choices are optimal or not. Instead,
241   such choices only need to contain some partial structure information of the data known by users.
242   For instance, users may employ clustering techniques in some particular low-dimensional spaces of
243   the data or use labels on some dimensions of the data. Additional discussions on possible center-
244   selection methods are provided in the Appendix.
245   Now for each data sample x0 ∼ pdata , a dispatcher D : Rd → {1, 2, · · · , K} assigns x0 to one of
246   the center. In the context of this work, the dispatcher is defined in the following way:

533   6   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we propose and theoretically analyze a class of mixed diffusion models, where the
536   prior distribution is chosen as mixed Gaussian distribution. The goal is to allow users to flexibly
537   incorporate structured information or domain knowledge of the data into the prior distribution. The
538   proposed model is shown to have advantageous comparative performance particularly when the
539   training resources are limited. For future work, we plan to further the theoretical analysis and
      examine the performance of mixed diffusion models with data of different modalities.

880          1. Data-driven clustering method. The data-driven clustering method first applies a tradi-
881             tional data-clustering technique Rousseeuw (1987); Kaufman & Rousseeuw (2009) to the
882             samples from the data distribution. To be more specific, the method first calculates the av-
883             erage Silhouette’s coefficient for samples from the data distribution under different number
884             of clusters. By maximizing the average Silhouette’s coefficient over different values of K,
885             the method determines an optimal value of K. Subsequently, the method applies the k-
886             means algorithm to find the K cluster centers for the data distribution and we denote them
887             by c1 , · · · , cK ∈ Rd . We summarize the implementation of this method in Algorithm 3
