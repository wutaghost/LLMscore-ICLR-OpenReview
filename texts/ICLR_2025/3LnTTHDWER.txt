009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite significant progress, accurately assessing the reasoning capabilities of
012            Large Language Models (LLMs) remains both a challenging and divisive subject.
013            Many existing benchmarks either suffer leakage, or reflect patterns in the training
014            data, leading to ambiguous results. We present CLEAR (Conlang Logic Evaluation
015            And Reasoning), a novel benchmark designed to test the reasoning and problem
016
               solving capabilities of LLMs in new environments. CLEAR uses Conlangs (Con-
               structed Languages) for few-shot translation tasks, which require some linguistic
017
               knowledge to solve, but primarily the ability to make new patterns from tokens in
018
               unfamiliar contexts using logical operations. These conlangs represent a unique
019            challenge, as while translation examples are plentiful, these conlangs each have a
020            unique combination of rules, are self contained, and are absent in the training cor-
021            pus. We present an evaluation of current frontier models over multiple metrics as
022            a baseline for future research. We will be releasing CLEAR as a public benchmark
023            to drive progress towards AI systems more capable of general reasoning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs) is a challenging task. Evaluating the abilities of LLMs
028
      to reason, is both challenging and divisive. With the use of LLMs rapidly filtering into many areas
029
      of life, from code completion (Rozière et al.) to designing proteins (Li et al.), it has never been more
030   important to evaluate these models robustly.
031
032   We feel that insight into the true reasoning capabilities of LLMs is lacking, largely due to the com-
033
      plexity of measuring this performance. As this is such a nascent area of study there are many
      complimentary measures of reasoning, which can broadly be categorised into two domains. One
034
      is to take the contents of an advanced subject and correctly draw the right conclusions to complex
035
      problems(Rein et al.). This is crucial in areas such as law, scientific analysis, and mathematics to
036   name a few, and will be familiar to anyone who has used an LLM as an education tool.
037
038   However, while this level of accomplishment across a field is already arguably beyond that of many
039
      humans, it does not measure the ability to adapt to new information, which for people is second
      instinct. This is the second kind of intelligence to evaluate. How well can the model adapt to new
040
      information, leverage what is already known, and yield correct reasoning. This second intelligence
041
      is the key component required for the kinds of scientific discovery and path towards a more general
042   system, and we argue is under evaluated in current benchmarks. Unfortunately it is largely under
043   evaluated because it is a very challenging problem to solve, how do you find a task that challenges
044   the LLM that is not represented in the training data? One such attempt is the Abstract Reasoning
045   Corpus (Chollet) which provides LLMs with geometrical problems to solve, which are visually
046   intuitive to a human but challenging to the LLM, and crucially absent from the training data. We
047   build on this philosophy with the proposed CLEAR benchmark.
048   Here, in our attempt to evaluate the reasoning abilities of LLMs, we focus on one very specific task,
049   learning a new language. We provide the model with an example of an unseen constructed language
050   (Conlang) with translation sentences in English and some preliminary information describing how
051   the language works, then ask for translations to example sentences. A cartoon of this setup is shown
052   in Fig. 1. Such constructed languages have been predominantly been found in language learning
053   enthusiast circles as toy languages but can exhibit all the features of real natural languages, while
      also having the advantage of not being present in the training set. The simplicity of this task will

      repeated words start to become apparent, regardless or prior exposure to the language.

072   To‘learn’ the rules of such a language is relatively simple. The translation tasks require a basic un-
073   derstanding of the grammatical rules and patterns with which languages are generally manipulated,
074   and an understanding of simple logical rules. (We will revisit this concept in Sec.3.1.) Both are areas

078

079   advantage of this task is that translation tasks are prevalent in the training data, so the pattern should
080   be well understood, the complexity comes almost entirely from having to impose meaning onto a
081   set of poorly initialised tokens.
082
      We believe this poses a promising route to evaluate the in context learning capabilities of LLMs.
083
      These translation tasks show the extent to which the models can (1) use simple logical rules, to
084
      (2) update the meaning of tokens based on new information. This offers insight into the in-context
085   learning abilities of the model, and to the reasoning ability of models to update beliefs based on new
086   information.

098
      Few-Shot Prompting of Large Language Models Vaswani et al. showed the promise of LLMs
      for machine translation tasks, and Brown et al. (2020) introducing GPT-3 showed that LLMs con-
099
      ditioned with several previously unseen examples is able to adapt to novel tasks without updating
100
      model parameters, in a method known as few-shot prompting.
101
102   Posing all tasks as sequence to sequence problems as in Chowdhery et al. (2022) and Raffel et al.
103   (2023) makes the task of injecting few shot prompts simple to apply to a wide range of tasks, in-
104
      cluding translation. Multilingual models have been shown to exhibit the same behaviour (Lin et al.,
      2022) even in low resource cases, due to the ability to utilize high resource language pairs.
105
106   All current frontier models are trained on a diverse mix of natural languages (Touvron et al.Dubey
107   et al.OpenAI et al.), which has yielded improved translation ability. Although as was found in
      Workshop et al. the multilingual ability is strongly correlated with data quality, particularly in low


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      resource languages. However, despite strong progress, translation of completely unseen languages
109   requires either reasoning capability, or significant utilisation of other translation tasks in the training
110   corpus.
111
112
      Logical Reasoning of Large Language Models Recent work has shown signs that LLMs are ca-
      pable of performing tasks that require logical reasoning and problem solving, despite largely being
113
      trained on next-token-prediction, many benchmarks show exceptional exam performance in fields
114
      such as law (OpenAI et al.), PhD level scientific domains (OpenAI, 2024), and mathematics (Zhang
115   et al.). The source of this phenomena is an area of open debate, as either representative of an internal
116   world view, or repeating patterns from the training data. Many examples claim to prove or disprove
117   this using, for example, classic riddles and then twisting the setting subtly.
118
      More concretely, Wei et al. proposed the Chain of Thought method, where the model is asked to
119
      generated additional outputs during generation akin to a stream of though, and has generally been
120
      found to improve performance on tasks requiring multi step reasoning. Kojima et al. demonstrated
121   similar results using additional prompts like ”think step by step”. It is argued that these results
122   show the inherent reasoning in LLMs. Taking this approach, further work such as Zelikman et al.
123   (2022) and Zelikman et al. (2024) starts to train a iterative loop to improve model outputs, it has
124   been speculated that this kind of approach is behind the latest developments in OpenAI (2024).
125   The mechanism for this in context learning is still unknown with arguments proposing effective in
126   context gradient descent (Shen et al., 2024), however the true mechanism remains unknown.
127   Logical Reasoning There are many benchmarks quantifying aspects of the reasoning abilities of
128   these models. Notable attempts include Srivastava et al. where a collection of tasks that require
129   multiple steps of reasoning to complete has been organised. Other benchmarks are more specific,
130   for example consists of questions on a short text where indirect relationships must be extracted, Liu
131   et al. relies on a multiple choice approach, this is nice as it allows for subtle differences to be probed,
132   and Tafjord et al. goes further requiring the model to produce from a set of facts the logical steps
133   that justify the reasoning. Law (OpenAI et al.) and scientific (OpenAI, 2024) exam questions partly
134   address this reasoning, but are hard to isolate from genuine facts present in the training corpus. Even
135
      strong cutting edge benchmarks such as GPQA(Rein et al.) have limitations, and are more focused
      on the ability to solve complex domain specific questions, which relies on specific knowledge and is
136
      subject to leakage. The different attempt by Chollet formulates geometric problems that are outside
137
      any training corpus as a hard evaluation problem.
138
139   Similarly to the step-by-step approach, many examples exist where a scratchpad is provided to the
140   LLM to output a set of intermediate tokens in an attempt to allow the model to guide towards a local
141
      minima (Nye et al.). However, there is increasing evidence (Ye & DurrettTurpin et al.) that while
      the use of intermediate tokens can help reach a better output, the intermediate explanation or chain
142
      of thought is subject to just as many ”hallucinations” as other outputs. Recent evaluations find the
143
      latest generation of LLMs still fail to plan multiple steps sufficiently (Valmeekam et al.).
144
145   Translation Evaluation Metrics Machine Translation (MT) requires automated metrics to operate
146   at scale and to provide consistent scoring mechanisms. Traditional metrics such as BLEU (Papineni
147
      et al., 2002), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), ROUGE (Lin, 2004) have survived the test of
      time largely due to their simplicity. BLEU is arguably the most widespread as both the simplest,
148
      performing n-gram checks, and the most limited, not providing any flexibility in translation out-
149
      put through word stems or synonyms. As there are often many equally valid possible translations,
150   alternative methods attempt to accommodate this. BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020) uses a BERT
151   (Devlin et al., 2019) model to compute similarity scores between candidate and target sentences us-
152   ing contextual embedding, allowing for more sophisticated translations. COMET (Rei et al., 2020)
153   takes this approach further by using a dedicated neural network to judge the translation. However,
154   such methods introduce their own limitations, and may perform unpredictably on languages not
155   represented in their training corpus.
156   Other metrics such as chrF (Popović, 2015) and chrF++ (Popović, 2017) take the n-gram approach
157   to characters, offering more granularity for translations missing subtle features in the output. Addi-
158   tionally metrics such as YiSI (Lo, 2019) attem pt to align outputs closer to human preference.
159
      There are also edit based methods that evaluate the quality of the translation through the number
160
      of edits required to convert the candidate sentence into the reference. Metrics such as TER (Panja
161
      & Naskar, 2018) count the number of insertions, deletions, shifts, etc required to match the target


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      sentence. EED (Stanchev et al., 2019) extends this to better capture human preference, and CDER
163   (Leusch et al.) and CharacTER (Wang et al., 2016) operate at the character level making them
164   suitable to languages with rich morphology, and possibly robust to tokenization issues in LLMs.

170   3.1   T RANSLATION E XAM S CRIPTS
171
172
      The exam scripts were collated from online resources used primarily as entrance examinations for
173
      university level language degrees, however they are also popular within language learning commu-
174   nities online. Students applying to study Modern Foreign Languages at Universities such as Oxford
175   University must take an entrance exam. A part of that exam is the Language Aptitude Test, which
176   is designed to evaluate students on their ability to parse and understand a new unseen language that
177   doesn’t rely on real world knowledge.
178
      The motivation is to standardize the test such that prior knowledge of the language doesn’t provide
179
      some applicants an advantage over others, and the results should correlate with likely ability to study
180   new languages. We propose to take this motivation and apply it to LLM evaluation.
181
182   Each exam would consist of a preamble describing the translation task, proving some details to help
183
      understand how to approach the conlang (i.e. is the language independent of word order, are the
      nouns gendered, etc.) then split into three or four sections. The sections then each provide a set of
184
      translation sentences, with increasing complexity as the exam progresses. Each section then has a
185
      number of questions translating from English to the Conlang, and from the Conlang to English. All
186   translation sentences are possible to derive provided the information from the previous sections has
187   been understood. This format maps well onto a few-shot prompt for an LLM.
188
189
190
      3.2   M ETHODOLOGY
191
192
      The dataset was constructed by scraping the publicly available exam scripts and their answers, and
193
      parsing them into individual questions as prompts for the language models. Questions for the each
194
      section all share the same preamble and translation examples, but have a different target sentence to
195   translate. For example where section (a) might have 10 example sentences and 4 translation tasks,
196   we repeat the stem and examples as the prompt for each of the 4 translation tasks. While there is
197   some duplication of analysis here, this tests the ability of the model to extract the relevant patterns
198   in different views, and the preliminary sections are required to solve the more complex later parts of
199   the exam. As the exams are intended to be conducted as a complete exercise each question builds
200   on knowledge derived for previous answers. Therefore for each subsequent section we append the
201   new translation examples to the previous question stem and previous translations. This means the
202   model has access to the example translations for (a) + (b) when answering (b), and (a) + (b) + (c)
203
      when answering (c). etc.
204   A simplified example of a question is given in Fig. 2. Here we highlight the patterns we might expect
205   to extract as a human attempting to translate this task. We might start by trying to understand the
206   word order, then find some patterns that allow us to start matching candidate word stems. Then start
207   thinking about the endings / grammatical changes to the words based on their contexts.

      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
271
272   In our metrics we are looking for a number of properties, which are slightly different to usual trans-
273   lation cases. Typical translation metrics have moved away from exacting n-gram matches (Papineni
274   et al., 2002) to allow for more flexible and natural translation (Zhang et al., 2020). While this is a
275   significant improvement in natural language translation, we want to quantify the translation using
276   harsh scores in order to evaluate the logical reasoning behind the translation.
277   The Conlang translations do not require creative solving, the vocabulary should be inferred from
278   that used in the question stem, and the subtle variations to answers are often exactly the small
279   grammatical errors that we are trying to evaluate the models on. To that end we have chosen the
280   following metrics.
281
      BLEU: This is our main metric, BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) is a longstanding popular metric in
282   machine translation due to its simplicity and interpretability. Additionally it has no dependence on
283   the language, and the strong penalty for mistakes is preferable in the context of looking for exact
284   translations of very tightly controlled sentences.
285
      The BLEU metric computes pn (modified) n-gram precision scores for lengths up to N , weighted
286
      by wn , and is given by

290                                          c     i=0 wn log pn     if c ≤ r.
291
      This score is modified by the brevity term which penalises the output sentence of length c for being
292
      shorter than the target sentence of length r. This gives us BLEU ∈ [0, 1]. As BLEU penalises any
293   mismatching words harshly, we enforce a strict measure on difference between words like ‘horse’
294   vs ‘horses’ or ‘run’ vs ‘ran’ as while the meaning may be semantically similar the error implies the
295   underlying grammar has not been understood.
296
      ROUGE: (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) This is another n-gram matching
297
      metric, where in contrast to BLEU the main focus is recall. Recall is important in evaluating trans-
298
      lations, as it would be possible to get a very high precision score for the produced translation, but to
299   miss a significant fraction of the translation. BLEU takes this into account with the brevity penalty,
300   but is an imprecise measure of how much the translation covers the target (Lin, 2004). This makes
301   it less focused on the precision of the translation, but provides a different angle to to evaluate the
302   outputs.
303
      METEOR: The principle is to combine precision (P ) and recall (R) evaluation into a single met-
304
      ric, uni-grams are matched between the candidate and the reference sentences and the number of
305   matches computed (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005).
306
307

308                              METEOR =                     1 − 0.5                                     (2)
                                          9·P +R                      #matches
309
310   The METEOR score has a different trade off to the BLEU score, here semantic variations are allowed
311   through the use of word stems and paraphrases. There is an obvious drawback to metrics like
312   METEOR in this case, as the more sophisticated stages of the algorithm rely on finding matches to
313   the stems of words, or synonyms. This is obviously not possible for Conlangs, and we will have to
314   fall back to the basic METEOR implementation for ENG→CON translations.
315   chrF: (Character n-gram F score) follows a similar philosophy to BLEU, but computes the n-grams
316   on the character level instead. Typically n-grams of up to 6 are chosen and the harmonic mean of
317   precision and recall is computed. It’s generally found that for morphologically rich languages, a low
318   chrF score correlates with human preferences over metrics such as BLEU which focus on the word
319   level ?. The metric is computed as:
320
                                   chrF = (1 + β 2 ) · (P · R)/(β 2 ∗ P + R),                             (3)
321
322   where β controls the weighting between precision and recall. The drawbacks with this metric in
323   common use cases are advantageous in evaluating the translations in CLEAR , the metric is sensitive
      to very small changes which while they may not fundamentally harm understanding, they show

324
      where the models have not parsed the logic correctly, and the lack of semantic variation keeps the
325   metrics clean for the Conlangs.
326
327   EED: (Edit Evaluation Distance) A similar concept to chrF, where the score reflects the number
328
      of edits required to transform one sentence into another. Edits include insertions, substitutions,
      deletions, etc. and a lower score is better (Stanchev et al., 2019).
329
330   TER: (Translation Edit Rate) This metric takes the same concept as EED, but looks at the word
331   level for transformations, in a method that is designed to mirror more closely the process a human
332   translator might take (Snover et al.).
333   CharacTER: (Character-level Translation Edit Rate) A culimation of the above metrics, which
334   aplies the translation edit rate approach to the character level, but with awareness of the word bound-
335   aries through the addition of special characters between words (Wang et al., 2016). This results in
336   a more sensitivity when compared to word level metrics, and better provides partial credit for trans-
337   lations.
338   Exam Setting: We also introduce a weighted version of some of the metrics to account for the
339   question complexity and output sentence length. Later questions in the exams can be worth up to 6
340   points, compared to the 3 points per question for the early parts. Therefore we weight the output of
341   some of the metrics (those whose outputs naturally scale between 0 and 1) by the points associated
342   with that question, and compute a score over the entire dataset. As detailed in Sec.3.3 there is a
343   possible total of 576 points, but for simplicity we scale this as a percentage.

345   4.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
346
347   Each translation task was presented to the LLMs in a consistent way, containing the question stem,
348   the translation examples, and the system prompt. The format of the question naturally lends itself
349   to few-shot-prompting with the description of the task, then several lines (10 - 30) of example
350   translations, followed by the actual question asking for a translation either from the constructed
351
      language to English or visa-via. Each translation was formatted in the same way throughout the
      dataset for consistency.
352
353   The system prompt was a simple but firm guide to further reinforce the ‘exam’ setting, and to
354   encourage the model only to output the answer, not to re-frame the question in the response. This
355   was designed not to enhance performance, we prefer to keep the core ‘prompting’ in the original
356   question stem, and was mostly for controlling the output to encourage short answers of just the
357
      desired translation sentence.
358   This sort of output made it simpler to apply basic text cleaning to ensure we were evaluating the
359   output translation and not any additional text. We decided this provided the cleanest analysis of the
360   model’s ability to reason on the raw questions without additional guidance. Several system prompts
361   were used, and the variation in model performance as a result was deemed negligible, so the system
362   prompt was chosen based how clean the outputs were.

448   5.3   T RANSLATION D IRECTION
449
450
      One striking result we find is the discrepancy between metric scores and the translation direction.
      When translating from CON→ENG in all cases we see the model achieve a higher scores than in the
451
      ENG→CON cases. This reduction for ENG→CON can be seen clearly in the example metrics shown
452
      in Fig.3a. This is unsurprising, as we expect models trained on a largely English language corpus
453   to be better at generating English text, but the scale of the discrepancy hints at a deeper issue. In
454   Fig.3a showing the results for the BLEU exam score, the ENG→CON score of top performing model
455   (Claude Sonnet 3.5) would rank 14th when compared to the CON→ENG . We argue this is the most
456   challenging metric in the benchmark suite, and as seen in Table.1 is dominated by GPT-4 o1.
457
      The tasks should be identical in both directions, however the model’s ability to generate grammat-
458
      ically correct English sentences perhaps offers an advantage, as a sentence like ‘The judges hate
459   the rams’ while unusual follows an expected English pattern. If enough information can be aggre-
460   gated from the original ‘kertós misóntoi hógons’ that it’s something about judges (probably plural),
461   something about hate, something about rams (again probably plural). There aren’t very many ways
462   to construct this as a grammatically correct English sentence. Additionally this is why the slightly
463   eccentric nature of many of the translated phrases is such a desirable feature, if the model simply
464   guesses at a sensible phrase but misses a subtle variation it will be penalised. This same phenomena
465   is much harder to ape when doing translations of ENG→CON , and as such we find the ENG→CON
466   BLEU exam score the strictest evaluation of the model’s reasoning ability.
467
468   5.4   P ROMPT C OMPLEXITY
469
      Slightly counter to intuition, the model performance was not strongly inversely correlated with ques-
470
      tion complexity (as measured by points). We found some models dropped in performance with more
471
      complex questions, while others increased, and some seemed to find the medium level difficulty
472   questions the hardest before rebounding with the most complex ones. Further details of these results
473   can be found in Appendix. A.1, but we suggest the statistical uncertainty due to limited dataset size
474   leaves this as an area for further study.

      6     D ISCUSSION
478
      6.1   W INNERS AND L OSERS
479
480   As can be seen from Table.2 the top ranks are dominated by large and recent models - suggesting
481   the convergence of large labs on a similar capability when it comes to reasoning. However, we
482   see the Claude family of models represented in the 1st and 3rd ranked spots, with the new GPT-4
483   o1 (preview) model in 2nd. The multi-step reasoning has clearly had a big impact for this latest
484   OpenAI model compared to previous iterations, and this is seen most apparently in the ENG→CON
485   translations where this model placed 1st in 11/11 evaluations. Despite this posed as a step change
      in how LLMs will be deployed in the future, the two highest placed models from Anthropic are

486
      strongly competitive and are apparently single models. This results raises further questions on what
487   differentiates the Anthropic dataset, or if there are simlar chain-of-thought approaches being done
488   behind the scenes.
489
490   6.2   I MPLICATIONS FOR LLM C APABILITIES
491
492   The questions in CLEAR aim to evaluate the ability of LLMs to parse new information and synthesis
493   new meanings and connections. As the exam is based on a constructed, and therefore unseen,
494   languages this task does not rely on memorisation, but on a basic grasp of language components,
495   and the ability to apply fairly simply logical patterns to those components using a broad set of
496   grammatical rules. The results are promising, especially taken for the CON→ENG direction, however
497
      the disparity found in the ENG→CON translations suggest the current state of LLMs struggle to adapt
      to new information and to update beliefs accordingly when the content falls outside of the training
498
      corpus. This has implications for the more open ended reasoning tasks we would ultimately wish to
499
      utilise LLMs for, and suggests significnat further work is needed.
500
501
      6.3   L IMITATIONS OF THE S TUDY
502
503   A clear limitation is the dataset size, we have a dataset consisting of 140 unique questions, from 12
504   different exams, so there is a fair degree of overlap between questions. For example for the same
505   set of 10 example translations we will have 4 versions of the question stem, just with different final
506   translation.
507
      Another major limitation is the evaluation of the impact tokenization plays on this particular task.
508   Conlangs by their nature often are composed of unusual strings of characters, and we suspect this
509   plays a role in some of the difficulty the LLMs demonstrate with the task. A full evaluation of the
510   impact of tokenization is beyond the scope of this paper.
511
      A final major limitation of this study is linked to the power of this initial evaluation, we do not think
512
      as of submission that these questions are included in any training set we know of. Publishing this
513
      work and encouraging evaluation of the models on this dataset will no-doubt increase the availability
514   of these example questions / answers in web-crawled datasets, removing the power of this specific
515   study. Such dilution takes time however, and we hope this study will encourage further work in this
516   area.
517
518   6.4   F UTURE W ORK
519
520   Future work will require the development of a significantly larger, and more diverse set of Conlangs,
521   however this requires a substantial effort from skilled linguists to ensure the Conlangs are both valid,
522   and the question structure consisting of the minimally required information to derive the translation.
523
      There would also be value in exploring how this kind of in-context learning could be used to update
      the properties of certain tokens, and how this might lead to simple methods to circumvent safety
524
      features of current SOTA LLMs.
525
526
527   7     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We have presented a new dataset benchmark, CLEAR , to evaluate the in-context reasoning capabil-
530   ities of LLMs in performing translation tasks on unseen Conlangs. Our initial benchmarks across a
531   diverse set of LLMs show promising abilities in the largest models to adapt to new information and
532
      solve complex reasoning tasks. However, we observed a significant drop in performance for tasks
      requiring a higher degree of reasoning, particularly in translating ENG→CON . This task, which
533
      removes the possibility of relying on the model’s ability to construct grammatically correct En-
534
      glish sentences, exposes limitations in true reasoning capabilities. These findings suggest that while
535   progress has been made, there remains a substantial gap in general reasoning ability. We hope that
536   by presenting as a new public benchmark, we will encourage further research targeting this specific
537   problem. Ultimately, this work aims to pave the way towards models capable of robust, general
538   reasoning ability across diverse and novel contexts.
