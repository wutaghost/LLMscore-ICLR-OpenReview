009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Ensuring the alignment of artificial intelligence (AI) systems with human objectives
012            is a critical challenge in the development of safe and effective AI technologies.
013            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has been a predominant
014            method to tackle this challenge. However, this framework operates under the
015            unrealistic assumptions that human preferences are accurate reflections of their
016            desires and that they remain constant over time. This paper identifies and challenges
017            these assumptions by illustrating how they can lead to undesirable consequences,
018            particularly when human beliefs about the environment are incorrect or mutate over
019            time. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel framework termed practical
020
               alignment. This framework redefines the alignment objective to accommodate the
               variability and irrationality of human beliefs, emphasizing the need for AI systems
021
               not only to learn from but also to teach humans about the world. We discuss the
022
               theoretical underpinnings of practical alignment and introduce MindGrid, a toolkit
023            designed to simulate and evaluate alignment scenarios. Our experimental results
024            using large language models in teaching scenarios underscore the importance of
025            teaching skills as a requisite capability to achieve alignment.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Ensuring the alignment between the behaviors of AI systems and the expectations of their human
032   users is of paramount importance for the development of safe and effective AI technologies. A widely
033
      adopted approach to addressing this challenge is reinforcement learning from human (preferential)
      feedback (RLHF; (Knox & Stone, 2009; Nguyen et al., 2017; Christiano et al., 2017; Kreutzer et al.,
034
      2018; Ouyang et al., 2022)), in which an AI system infers a human’s reward function from rating
035
      feedback and optimizes its behavior according to that function. While this framework has led to
036   significant empirical improvements, it still suffers from numerous conceptual flaws, primarily due to
037   its simplistic model of human communication and cognition (Casper et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023;
038   Siththaranjan et al., 2023; Knox et al., 2022).
039
      This paper highlights and addresses the drawbacks of RLHF that arise from two unrealistic assump-
040
      tions it makes about humans: (1) that human preferences perfectly reflect their desires and (2) that
041
      human preferences remain unchanged over time. These assumptions are often violated in practice
042   because human preferences are shaped by the humans’ beliefs about the world, which are inherently
043   fallible and malleable. In scenarios where these two assumptions do not hold, RLHF becomes either
044   inapplicable or fails to produce the desired real-world outcomes.
045
      We illustrate this failure with a toy example in Figure 1. In this scenario, a human remotely instructs
046
      a robot to pick up a ball as quickly as possible. The human mistakenly believes that the door to the
047
      room where the ball is located is currently locked, while in reality, it is open. Following a typical
048   RLHF process, the robot asks the human to compare two plans: (A) get the key, open the door, pick
049   up the ball, and (B) go through the door, pick up the ball. Given their current belief, the human
050   expresses a preference for plan (A). This response leads the robot to infer that the human wants it to
051   pick up both the key and the ball, rather than just the ball. Here, assumption (1) is violated because
052   the human’s behavior fails to communicate their true desire to the robot. According to RLHF, the
053   robot should respect this inferred desire by executing plan (A). However, doing so would ultimately
      disappoint the human when they realize their actual desire has not been fulfilled.

074
075   To address the issues illustrated, we develop a novel alignment framework named practical alignment.
076   Our framework provides a precise mathematical language to characterize and amend the fundamental
077   limitations of RLHF. The key innovation of this framework is the explicit modeling of human beliefs
078
      about the world as a source of influence on their preferences. Within this framework, we identify a
      critical issue with the RLHF model: it defines the alignment objective based on human subjective
079
      beliefs, necessitating the assumption of the correctness and stability of these beliefs for the objective
080
      to be well-defined. In contrast, practical alignment defines the alignment objective as fulfilling human
081   desires in the real world, rather than in their imagination. This objective not only embodies the
082   intuitive goal of alignment but also offers technical advantages by removing the dependency on
083   human beliefs. As a result, it enables the modeling of scenarios where these beliefs may be false or
084   subject to change. Thus, practical alignment provides a solid foundation for developing alignment
085   algorithms that effectively address human irrationality and fickleness.
086
      The objective of practical alignment encourages an AI system to not only learn from humans but
087   also to (truthfully) teach them about the world. RLHF equips AI systems with no motivation for the
088   latter task. Using our theoretical framework, we analyze the catastrophic consequences resulting
089   from employing RLHF to tackle general practical alignment problems. We narrate a specific account
090   in which this approach gives rise to a manipulative AI system that deludes humans to prove its
091   effectiveness.
092
      Despite its importance as demonstrated by our framework, teaching problems are largely underex-
093   plored in the field of AI, where most current efforts focus on learning. To facilitate progress on
094   these problems and on practical alignment teaching problems in general, we have developed a toolkit
095   called MindGrid, which can be used to simulate human and AI collaborators with divergent world
096   models. Using this toolkit, we set up a teaching problem and evaluate the performance of various
097   large language models. Our results underscore the necessity of teaching in a practical alignment
098   process and reveal the limitations in reasoning and language grounding of large language models.

100   2    R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Alignment Frameworks. A well-known formulation of alignment is Cooperative Inverse Rein-
103   forcement Learning (CIRL; (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016)), which describes an AI system attempting
104   to maximize an unknown reward function, the parameters of which are fully observed by a human.
105   RLHF is a special instance of CIRL (Shah et al., 2020). Practical alignment can be viewed as an
106   extension of CIRL in which the human only partially observes the true reward parameters. Another
107   way to describe this difference is that CIRL outlines a communication process with a priori common
      ground: the agents share a world model that accurately emulates the real world. Our framework


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      encompasses more realistic scenarios in which such common ground does not initially exist and must
109   be cultivated through cooperative communication.
110
111   Modeling the Irrationality and Fickleness of Human Preference. Efforts to model irrationality in
112   preference learning incorporate elements of uncertainty (Laidlaw & Russell, 2021) and various types
113   of human cognitive biases (Chan et al., 2021). Lang et al. (2024) presents a framework explaining how
114   partial observability can lead to deceptive behavior, a topic also explored in this work. Reddy et al.
115   (2018) and Tian et al. (2023) propose algorithms for inferring human beliefs from demonstrations.
116   Recently, Carroll et al. (2024) introduced the DR-MDP framework to model changeable preferences.
117   Compared to this framework, ours explicitly models human beliefs and can also explain human
118
      irrationality. Siththaranjan et al. (2023) models inputs to the preference function that are unknown to
      the AI system, which can also account for various instances of human irrationality and fickleness.
119
      Our work, however, focuses on information that remains unknown to the human.
120
121
      Algorithmic Modifications of RLHF. Numerous improvements have been made to the components
122   of the RLHF pipeline, including advancements in optimization algorithms (Rafailov et al., 2024;
123   Ding et al., 2024), feedback mechanisms (Wu et al., 2024), and the human-AI interaction model Li
124   et al. (2023); Kwon et al. (2023). Our contributions lie at the conceptual rather than algorithmic level.
125   We show that RLHF is inherently constrained by its unrealistic conceptualization of alignment and
126   can only be radically improved through a more robust conceptual framework.

130   3.1   O STENSIBLE ALIGNMENT
131
132   To motivate practical alignment, we first formulate a more restricted framework to characterize
133   approaches like RLHF, which attribute an internal reward function to a human and train an AI system
134   to infer and maximize that function. The word “ostensible” suggests that the optimal behavior within
135   this framework is initially perceived as “aligned” by the human, even though it may not be.
136   Formally, ostensible alignment concerns communication between two agents: a human H and an AI
137   system A. The human is assumed to possess a reward function R(π; θH ) parameterized by θH ∈ Θ.
138   This function assigns a real-valued score to a solution plan π proposed by the AI system. For every
139   θH chosen by the human, the AI system seeks the plan that maximizes R(π; θH ).
140   An ostensible alignment process (OAP) describes a communication model between the agents.
141   Communication occurs in episodes, each of which consists of two phases: discussion and evaluation.
142   The discussion phase has T turns, during which the two agents exchange information. The evaluation
143   phase has a single turn, in which the plan is announced and evaluated. At the beginning of the
144   discussion phase, the human samples θH from a distribution PΘH . An initial conversation context
145   c0 ∈ C is drawn from a distribution PC . The two agents implement communication policies
146   pH (u | c, θH ) and pA (u | c) to decide what utterance u to output in each turn. The AI system’s
147   policy pA is conditional on a current context c, where that of the human, pH , is additionally dependent
148
      on their preference parameters θH . We use p(uH , uA | c, θH ) to denote the joint communication
      policy. In the t-th turn (0 ≤ t < T ), the agents speak ut = (uH        A             H

168   3.2   P RACTICAL ALIGNMENT
169
170   We introduce practical alignment which extends ostensible alignment. The goal of practical alignment
171   is to find plans that lead to outcomes in the real world that a human desires. The key novelty of this
172   framework is to model explicitly the relationship between a “world model” of an agent and its reward
173
      function, and defines the alignment objective as a function of the true world model rather than the
      imaginary, mental world model of a human. This results in (1) an alignment objective that better
174
      reflects the intuitive goal of alignment and (2) the ability to account for different properties of the
175
      reward function, such as its imperfect and changing nature.
176
177   Our framework is instantiated within the Markov decision process (MDP) setting. Let S be the set of
178   all possible world states and ∆(X) denote the space of probability distributions over a set X. We
179

      can be taken in each state, s0 is a dummy start state, γ is a discount factor, and Pω : S × A → ∆(S)
180
      is a transition function parameterized by the input variable ω. In the context of our framework, we
181
      refer to a policy π : S → ∆(A) as a plan.

185

235   irrationality and fickleness of human preferences, which is not possible in ostensible alignment.

328   5.2   O STENSIBLE ALIGNMENT CAN PERPETUATE HUMAN NORMATIVE MIS ALIGNMENT
329
330   Whereas the previous section portrays ostensible alignment and human normative alignment as
331   independent objectives, this section presents a hypothetical account in which these two objectives are
332   at odds with each other. This phenomenon arises from a mistake made in a well-intentioned attempt
333   to enhance RLHF for practical alignment. RLHF is an approach in which the AI system lacks not only
334

335

336
      the system still pursues an inadequate goal like ostensible alignment, this idea could lead to the emer-
337   gence of a rogue AI system that prevents human from learning truths. A radical solution must augment
338   an AI system with both the skills and the incentives to truthfully teach humans about the world.

395   descriptive preference function R(·; θH ) and choose the better one as the final plan. In the non-trivial
396   case where π H ̸= π A , if the chosen plan is suboptimal with respect to the normative reward function
397   R(·; θ⋆ ), one of the following cases must have happened:
398
      1. Under-appreciation occurs when the AI system proposes the actually better plan, R(π A ; θ⋆ ) >
399

401      R(π H ; θ⋆ ), but the human agrees with it, R(π A ; θH ) > R(π H ; θH );
402   3. In the previous cases, the human picks the actually worse plan. Under-achievement occurs
403      when the human picks the actually better plan π = arg maxπ′ ∈{πH ,πA } R(π ′ ; θ⋆ ), but it is still
404      suboptimal, R(π; θ⋆ ) < maxπ′ R(π ′ ; θ⋆ ).
405

406
      degradation of the relationship between the human and AI system, which can hinder future collabora-
407
      tion. Especially, when an AI system is under-appreciated, it may be unfairly seen as incompetent,
408   despite its ability to identify the best solution. Ensuring human normative alignment can completely
409   eliminate under- and over-appreciation. This approach also helps mitigate under-achievement by
410   fostering realistic expectations about the plan’s performance in the real world.

413   6    E XPERIMENTS
414
415   Building benchmarks for practical alignment is challenging due to the necessity of human interaction.
416   Conducting experiments with real humans is expensive, non-reproducible, and subject to strict safety
417   regulations, while creating realistic human simulators presents significant technical difficulties. To
418
      address this issue, we develop MindGrid, a toolkit based on MiniGrid (Chevalier-Boisvert et al.,
      2023) that can simulate simple practical alignment problems. MindGrid enables the easy creation of
419
      agents with divergent mental models in a grid world, mimicking real-life agents with varying beliefs.
420
      The toolkit can be used for early algorithm testing or conducting proof-of-concept experiments in
421   theoretical studies. More details about this toolkit are available in Appendix B.
422
423   Using MindGrid, we construct a teaching problem (Figure 3) where an agent must infer a human’s
424
      false beliefs from their solution to a problem and generate a response to correct those beliefs. This
      scenario underscores the critical role of teaching in solving practical alignment problems.
425
426   We emphasize that our goal is not to introduce a high-fidelity benchmark or propose a state-of-the-art
427   method for practical alignment—such objectives are beyond the scope of this paper. Instead, we aim
428   to (1) to demonstrate our theory while highlighting the importance of teaching, and (2) to present a
429   prototypical benchmark that can inspire future work.
430
431   Scenario. We simulate a practical alignment problem in which an AI system and a human col-
      laborate to devise a plan that successfully completes a task in an environment. Only the AI system

433   ω0H ̸= ω ⋆ , which is an outdated version of the real environment. Specifically, the real environment is
434   generated by making several edits to the imaginary environment.
435
436
      During the discussion phase, the human first presents to the AI system the plan π0 = πopt (θ0H )
      which is optimal with respect to the imaginary environment. This plan apparently would fail in the
437
      real environment. The task of the AI system is to generate a language utterance that describes the
438
      edits that could transform the imaginary environment into the real one. This language utterance
439   is essentially aimed at changing the human’s beliefs about the real environment. We construct a
440   simulated human that, upon hearing this utterance, will update its imaginary environment to ω1H .
441   After this change, the two agents engage in an ostensible alignment process, after which the AI
442   system learns π1 = πopt (θ1H ), the optimal plan with respect to the human’s new imagination of the
443   real environment. We do not perform an actual ostensible alignment process; instead, we simulate
444   only the outcome of a perfect ostensible alignment process, which is the plan πopt (θ1H ).
445   The evaluation metric in this problem is the practical alignment gap incurred by the final plan:

454   Task and environment. The specific task with which we experiment is to control an avatar to
455   pick up a colored ball on a 10 by 10 grid. The reward of taking an action is -1 and the reward of
456
      retriving the ball at the end is 100. MindGrid supports two layouts for this task: room-door-key
      and treasure-island. For each layout, we implement various edits that can be composed together to
457
      generate diverse environment variants. For example, treasure-island features 12 edits, resulting in at
458
      least 212 = 4096 environment variants. Editing an environment can change the optimal plan. For
459   example, making the lava safe obviates the need to go through a bridge to enter the island; flipping
460   the grid along the vertical axis alters the optimal plan in most cases.
461
462
      The action space also contains high-level actions in addition to the primitive actions provided by
      MiniGrid. Each action represents a skill—a policy function evoked with a set of parameters (e.g., go
463
      to [position], pick up [object]). A plan is a sequence of parameterized skills (e.g., open the door, get
464
      the ball). This emulates a natural-language plan spoken by a human. The abstractness of the plan also
465   increases the complexity of the problem. Because the actual implementation of the skills are hidden
466   from the AI system, it has to accurately interpret the language descriptions of the skills to be able to
467   infer the human’s imaginary environment. Notably, several skills under-specifies the actual execution.
468   For example, “go next to [object]” does not indicate the final position of the avatar after execution.
469   Hence, the problem requires considering different possible interpretations of the plan, or reasoning
470   abstractly rather than attempting to imagine the detailed execution. Due to the nature of the skill
471   actions, the action space in this problem is relatively large; for example, the skill “go to [position]”
472   entails 100 possible actions. Hence, computing optimal plans using reinforcement learning is not
473
      viable. We implement a hybrid planner that combines rules and shortest-path search to efficiently
      generate the optimal plan in any environment variant.

521   7    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we present a more rigorous theoretical framework for human-AI alignment. We illustrate
524   that alignment is fundamentally a three-party relationship among humans, AI systems, and the world.
525   We argue that overlooking the alignment between humans and the world risks dire consequences.
526   This realization calls for a shift in envisioning the role of AI systems. Instead of merely passive
527   learners, absorbing human intent, AI should play a more active role, guiding humans in their journey
528   to understand and navigate the world. Teaching AI to embrace this new role without abusing it,
529   however, is an extraordinarily complex challenge. Our paper merely scratches the surface of this
530   intricate problem, exposing the deep difficulties in inferring false beliefs and conveying world models
531
      through language. The real-world manifestations of these challenges will demand solutions far
      more sophisticated than those explored in this work. Those solutions must be able to answer these
532
      questions: how can AI systems select which aspects of a complex world to convey to a human? How
533
      do we cultivate systems that are committed to truth, while still being pragmatic communicators who
534   can persuade humans to trust their guidance? How can these systems distinguish between human
535   beliefs it should seek to influence and those it ought to respect? and finally, a profound question that
536   remains unresolved by our framework: what happens when a unique, unchanging “real” world is too
537   nebulous to define? Despite these unanswered questions, we believe that empowering AI systems to
538   teach humans truthfully and effectively stands as one of the most pressing challenges in AI—one
539   whose resolution could profoundly enhance both the benefits and the safety of AI technologies. We
      hope that this work will inspire further research and greater investment in this critical endeavor.
