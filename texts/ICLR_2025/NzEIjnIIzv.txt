009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Decoder-only large language models typically rely solely on masked causal at-
012            tention, which limits their expressiveness by restricting information flow to one
013            direction. We propose Bitune, a method that enhances pretrained decoder-only
014            LLMs by incorporating bidirectional attention into prompt processing. We evaluate
015            Bitune in instruction-tuning and question-answering settings, showing significant
016            improvements in performance on commonsense reasoning, arithmetic, and lan-
017            guage understanding tasks. Furthermore, extensive ablation studies validate the
018            role of each component of the method, and demonstrate that Bitune is compatible
019
               with various parameter-efficient finetuning techniques and full model finetuning.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Large Language Models (LLMs) are being deployed in numerous practical applications where
025   humans engage with them through various forms of natural language interaction. In use cases such as
026   general purpose assistants (OpenAI, 2024), medical diagnosticians (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023),
027   game-conversation generation (Cox & Ooi, 2023) or coding-assistants (Roziere et al., 2023), the
028   ability for an LLM to precisely interpret and respond to user inputs is of primary concern.
029   Correspondingly, Instruction-Tuning (IT) (Chung et al., 2024; Ouyang et al., 2022a) is the prevailing
030   paradigm for finetuning LLMs after their self-supervised pretraining phase to improve them for such
031   tasks. Here, the model is trained on a dataset comprised of pairs of instructions and corresponding re-
032   sponses. Given the instruction-with-response structure of IT data, the generation of an LLM response
033   can be divided into two phases: first, converting the instruction into key and value embeddings, which
034   we refer to as instruction features; second, using these features to autoregressively generate an answer.
035
      Due to this task’s inherently conditional nature, the instruction features’ effectiveness is crucial for
      obtaining high-quality model outputs.
036
037   In the past, bidirectional attention (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997) has been a key technique for obtaining
038   stronger features for words or tokens. This is because the meaning of a word depends greatly on
039   its context. In particular, for some words in a sentence, the information that comes later might be
040
      far more informative for generating a meaningful representation and resolving ambiguities. With
      only uni-directional causal attention, where the representation of each word is restricted to depend
041
      solely on the words that came before, this cannot be achieved. This is the reason why many previous
042
      transformers such as encoder-only BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and encoder-decoder T5 (Raffel et al.,
043   2020) employed bidirectional attention to improve the encoding of the input and why tasks like text
044   retrieval (Lewis et al., 2020; Li & Li, 2023) still rely on this.
045
      However, in the context of LLMs, architectures utilizing bidirectional attention have fallen out of
046
      favor, as decoder-only models such as GPT (OpenAI, 2024) and Llama (AI@Meta, 2024) have
047
      focused on and vastly improved the generative performance of language models. These architectures
048   are trained by large volumes of data with next-token prediction, eschewing any look-ahead mechanism
049   for the sake of better autoregressive modeling. As there is simply more unlabeled data available for
050   pretraining, training a decoder-only architecture on unlabeled data, and then finetuning it for tasks
051   with instruction-tuning, is the best modus operandi of today (Wang et al., 2022). However, with this
052   switch to decoder-only architectures, we lost bidirectional attention in the process. As we know this
053   can improve feature representations for instructions, we set out to re-introduce bidirectional attention,
      such that it can be integrated into pretrained decoder-only LLMs.

073   Our new method Bitune adds bidirectional attention to decoder-only architectures and combines it
074   with causal attention to generate two sets of instruction features, using two different sets of weights.
075   These features are then integrated, utilizing learnable mixing coefficients, and later used as the
076   KV-cache for response generation. Notably, the autoregressive response generation process remains
077   unaffected by the bidirectional attention and continues to be causal. By realizing these adaptations
078
      with parameter-efficient finetuning methods, we introduce only a minimal set of new parameters.
079   Overall, our contributions are as follows:
080
                • We propose a novel method, Bitune, that improves the performance of pretrained decoder-
081
                  only LLMs in instruction-following and question-answering settings.
082
083             • We evaluate the method on multiple downstream tasks, showing consistent improvements
084
                  over the baselines.
085             • We conduct an extensive ablation study investigating the necessity of each component of the
086               method, and showing the method’s PEFT-agnosticism, as well as its effectiveness in full
087               finetuning scenarios.

089   2      B IDIRECTIONAL I NSTRUCTION -T UNING
090
091   In the instruction-tuning setting (Ouyang et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2024), a dataset D consists of
092   instruction-answer pairs that are used to adapt the model in a supervised fashion. Formally, a dataset
093   of size N can be described as D = {q, a}N     i=1 , where q and a are instructions and answers. The
094   training objective is to model p(a|q) in an autoregressive manner: This means the answer is generated
095   one token at a time, such that token ai at position i has access to all earlier tokens:
096                                                                |a|
                                                     p(a|q) = Πi=1 p(ai |a1 , . . . ai−1 , q),
097
098   where |a| denotes the length of the answer. Note how compared to the regular language modeling
099   objective, the response is already conditional (on the instruction q) even for the first generated token.
100   This naturally leads response-generation to be divided into two phases: prefilling and decoding. Dur-
101   ing the prefilling phase, the entire instruction – also often called a prompt – is processed concurrently
102   to generate a series of features to be stored. For a Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017),
103   these features are those of the key and value vectors, which can be stored in a KV-cache to avoid
104   costly recomputations. During the subsequent decoding phase, the model generates output tokens
105
      sequentially, one token at a time, based on the KV-cache of the instruction and the already generated
      tokens.
106
107   In this work, we introduce Bitune, a method to leverage this two-phase process to improve instruction-
      tuning of language models. In our approach, the model processes the instruction with both causal

      Parameter Efficient Fine-tuning Note that the components of the model, other than the key and
196   value projections, can have their own separate sets of weights as well. In the case of full finetuning,
197   this approach would require an additional set of full weights, which is impractical for large models.
198
      Instead, we adapt our model using parameter-efficient finetuning methods. These introduce only a
199
      fraction of trainable parameters, making it viable to have two modified variants of the model within a
200
      single forward pass. In the default configuration of our method, we utilize the Low-Rank Adaptation
201   (LoRA) of Hu et al. (2022) to adapt the model. However, Bitune can utilize different methods for
202   updating the weights, including full model finetuning and other parameter-efficient techniques, as
203   demonstrated in our ablations section.

      3     E XPERIMENTS
206
207   3.1   I NSTRUCTION -T UNING
208
209   Our core experiments involve training pretrained language models on an instruction-tuning dataset
210   and zero-shot evaluating them on downstream tasks. We evaluate Bitune on multiple models,
211   comparing results to standard finetuning with LoRA, and zero-shot results of pretrained models
212   without finetuning.
213   Specifically, we use a subset of the cleaned UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) dataset, which contains
214   instructions and corresponding answers generated by various LLMs. From this dataset, we select
215   completions generated by GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024), ensuring high-quality responses for training. To fit
      every model on a single GPU, we filter out samples longer than 512 tokens, which leaves us with

      roughly 10,000 samples for training. For results on another instruction-tuning dataset, please see the
217   Appendix 6.5.
218
219
      We test the method on pretrained decoder-only language models of two different scales of ap-
      proximately 2 billion and 7 billion parameters. The specific models used in our experiments are:
220
      Gemma 2B and 7B (Gemma Team et al., 2024), Llama2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023) and Llama3 8B
221
      (AI@Meta, 2024), and Phi-2 (Li et al., 2023), which has 2.7 billion parameters. We use HuggingFace
222   Transformers (Wolf et al., 2020) implementation of these models.
223
224
      For updating the weights we use the HuggingFace PEFT (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) implementation
      of LoRA, with the default rank of 8, and apply it to all linear layers of MLP and self-attention
225
      components of the model. We compare Bitune with the following three baselines: Pretrained -
226
      initial model without any finetuning; LoRA - model finetuned with LoRA without Bitune-specific
227   modifications, using rank of 8 as used in our method; and LoRA16 - model finetuned with LoRA,
228   using a rank of 16 to provide a fair comparison in terms of the number of parameters, as our method
229   introduces two sets of weights.
230
      For each model, we tune the learning rate on the LoRA baseline using steps on the approximate
231
      logarithmic scale (1e−4, 3e−4, 1e−3, 3e−3), and then apply the same rate to the other approaches.
232   Note that this potentially puts our method at a disadvantage compared to the LoRA baseline. All
233   hyperparameters are reported in the Appendix 6.2.
234
      Models are evaluated zero-shot on multiple-choice tasks to assess their performance. For com-
235
      monsense reasoning, we use the PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019),
236
      ARC-Challenge (Clark et al., 2018), and SIQA (Sap et al., 2019) datasets, while for language un-
237   derstanding, we use the MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) benchmark. Each task consists of a series
238   of questions, each with multiple choices, where only one answer is correct. As the tasks follow the
239   question-answer pattern, they are compatible with the instruction-tuning setting.
240
      For evaluation, we use the Language Model Evaluation Harness framework (Gao et al., 2023). This
241
      framework formats each question using a predefined template, tokenizes the question-choice pairs,
242   runs them through the model, and compares the log-likelihoods of the choices to determine the
243   selected answer. For each model and approach configuration, we conduct experiments using three
244   different random seeds, and average the results.
245
      Models are loaded and trained using bfloat16 precision, except for the mixing part, which operates
246
      in the full 32-bit floating-point format. This high level of precision for the mixing of features is
247   important, as minor numerical inaccuracies in the learnable coefficients and intermediate results of
248   the mixing operation may lead to significant deviations in the model’s behavior.
249
      In the decoding phase of the inference with Bitune, to initiate generation, the model requires at least a
250
      single token to obtain a set of attention queries, in addition to the keys and values extracted from the
251
      instruction. To facilitate this, one can introduce a new learnable <sep> token that would be placed
252   at the beginning of modeled answer, or utilize an existing token. For our experiments, we opted to
253   move the last token of the instruction template to the beginning of the modeled answer. For details on
254   the instruction template used, please refer to the section 6.7 of the Appendix.
255
      Results. Table 1 shows consistent and significant gains after instruction-tuning with Bitune, with
256
      the highest gains seen on the Gemma-2B model, showing a 4 percentage point (pp) improvement over
257   the baseline LoRA and a 9.3 pp improvement over the pretrained model. For the other models, the
258   average gains over baseline finetuning are equal to 1.8, 1.4, and 0.9 pp, for Llama3-8B, Llama2-7B,
259   and Phi-2 respectively.
260
      It is worth noting that the Gemma-7B model shows the lowest average improvement across all tasks,
261
      with merely 0.1 pp gain over the baseline finetuning. It is also a single case where the baseline
262   pretrained model achieved the highest score on a task, MMLU, with degraded performance in all
263   fine-tuning approaches. However, this is not an issue with the model’s scale, as significant gains are
264   observed with the Llama2-7B and Llama3-8B models.

364          • Do we need separate weights, or can the same weights be used to generate both causal and
365            bidirectional features? - To answer this question, we do not introduce the second set of
366            weights and use the same LoRA for both passes on the prompt, calling it Shared Weights.
367
368   The results, averaged over three seeds and presented in Table 3, indicate that all variants of the method
369   lead to gains over the baseline LoRA finetuning. However, the highest gains are observed in the full
370
      variant of Bitune, demonstrating that each component contributes to the method’s effectiveness.
371   Different PEFT Methods To verify the impact of different PEFT methods on the performance of
372   our method, we compare Bitune in combination with the following techniques: LoRA (Hu et al.,
373   2022), that reparametrizes weight updates as a multiplication of two low-rank matrices; DoRA (Liu
374   et al., 2024), which decomposes these weight updates into direction and magnitude; and IA3 (Liu
375
      et al., 2022), that instead rescales activations with learnable vectors.
376   The results are shown in Table 4. We find consistent gains across all three PEFT methods we analyze,
377   with gains ranging from +1.6% to +4.0% for averaged accuracy. This demonstrates that Bitune is
      PEFT-agnostic and can be combined with existing and future innovations in PEFT methods.

426   Our approach shares similarities with the concept of "prefix language modeling", which enables a
427   decoder-only model to handle bidirectional context within a prefix (instruction) while maintaining
428   causal generation for the output sequence. The prefix-LM architecture was introduced by Liu et al.
429   (2018) and further explored and popularized by Raffel et al. (2020). In their work on T5, Raffel et al.
430   (2020) pretrained the prefix-LM architecture alongside other architectures, such as encoder-decoder
431   and decoder-only models, demonstrating that prefix-LM outperforms decoder-only models on both
      training objectives: denoising and language modeling.

445   The prefix-LM approach has been used in UniLM (Dong et al., 2019), which trains a single transformer
446   on three types of language modeling tasks: unidirectional, bidirectional, and sequence-to-sequence
447
      prediction. UniLM employs a shared Transformer network and utilizes specific self-attention masks
      to control the context that predictions are conditioned on, where the sequence-to-sequence task is
448
      equivalent to the prefix-LM approach.
449
450   Additionally, UL2 (Tay et al., 2023) introduces a pretraining objective called "Mixture of Denoisers",
451   which combines various denoising strategies, including the prefix-LM approach. Lastly, XLNet (Yang
452
      et al., 2019) also allows for non-causal word ordering by allowing random permutations to be used
      with a next-token prediction objective.
453
454   All these works focused on the model pretraining. As for the utilization of pretrained causal language
455   models, Springer et al. (2024) show in their work that simply repeating the input to these models
456   improves the quality of token embeddings for text-retrieval. This work addresses the limitation
457
      that token embeddings in autoregressive models cannot contain information from tokens appearing
      later in the input. By repeating the input twice, the early tokens are allowed to encode information
458
      about later tokens, thereby improving the quality of the embeddings. Another approach, LLM2Vec
459
      (BehnamGhader et al., 2024), demonstrates that pretrained causal LLMs can be effectively converted
460   to BERT-like encoders. It can be done by enabling bidirectional attention, training the model on the
461   objective of masked token prediction, and applying unsupervised contrastive learning.

      5      D ISCUSSION
465
      Limitations. During standard instruction-tuning training, the instruction and the answer are pro-
466   cessed in a single forward pass. In our method, this processing is explicitly split into phases, extracting
467   instruction features with two passes & answer modeling, increasing both training time and memory
468   usage. As for the inference, these two passes on the instruction, bidirectional and causal, can be
469   processed either in parallel, impacting memory usage, or sequentially, increasing latency.
470
      However, this is a minor limitation in the context of instruction-tuning, since typically smaller datasets
471   are used compared to pretraining, leading to relatively short training times. Furthermore, at inference
472   time the added latency for processing the instruction is negligible, as the bulk of compute is used for
473   the autoregressive answer generation: e.g. only a 0.2s increase from 11.5s to 11.7s for a 200 token
474   response to a 50 token instruction for Llama3-8B (see Appendix 6.4 for more results).
475
      Conclusion. This work proposes the first method to utilize the instruction-answer structure of IT
476   datasets that enables bidirectional processing in pretrained decoder-only models. Bitune demonstrates
477   general applicability across different models and scales, and it consistently improves performance
478   across commonsense reasoning, arithmetic, and language understanding tasks. We further demon-
479   strate that our method is compatible with different existing PEFT methods and will likely benefit
480   from further developments in this area.
