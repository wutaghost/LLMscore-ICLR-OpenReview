009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the mechanisms behind Large Language Models (LLMs) is crucial
012            for designing improved models and strategies. While recent studies have yielded
013            valuable insights into the mechanisms of textual LLMs, the mechanisms of Multi-
014            modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) remain underexplored. In this paper, we
015            apply mechanistic interpretability methods to analyze the visual question answer-
016
               ing (VQA) mechanisms in the first MLLM, Llava. We compare the mechanisms
               between VQA and textual QA (TQA) in color answering tasks and find that: a)
017
               VQA exhibits a mechanism similar to the in-context learning mechanism observed
018
               in TQA; b) the visual features exhibit significant interpretability when projecting
019            the visual embeddings into the embedding space; and c) Llava enhances the exist-
020            ing capabilities of the corresponding textual LLM Vicuna during visual instruction
021            tuning. Based on these findings, we develop an interpretability tool to help users
022            and researchers identify important visual locations for final predictions, aiding in
023            the understanding of visual hallucination. Our method demonstrates faster and
024            more effective results compared to existing interpretability approaches. Our code,
025            data and interpretability tool will be made available on GitHub.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown, 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Tou-
030
      vron et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable results in numerous downstream tasks, including read-
031   ing comprehension (Xiao et al., 2023), question answering (Tan et al., 2023), and sentiment analysis
032   (Deng et al., 2023). However, their interpretability remains limited, and the underlying mechanisms
033   are not yet well understood. This lack of clarity poses a significant challenge for researchers at-
034   tempting to address issues such as hallucination (Yao et al., 2023), toxicity (Gehman et al., 2020),
035   and bias (Kotek et al., 2023) in LLMs. Therefore, understanding the mechanisms of LLMs has
036   become an increasingly important area of research. Recently, efforts have been made to explore
037   the mechanisms behind different LLM capabilities, including factual knowledge (Meng et al., 2022;
038   Geva et al., 2023), in-context learning (Wang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023), arithmetic (Stolfo et al.,
039
      2023), and reasoning (Wang & Zhou, 2024).
040   Although numerous studies have explored the mechanisms of LLMs, they have predominantly fo-
041   cused on textual LLMs, often overlooking Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs). It has
042   been demonstrated that features from different modalities, such as images and audio, can signifi-
043   cantly enhance the core abilities of LLMs (Zhang et al., 2024). Therefore, investigating the mecha-
044   nisms of MLLMs is essential. In this paper, we examine the mechanism of visual question answer-
045
      ing (VQA) in Llava (Liu et al., 2024b), marking the first attempt to extend visual instruction tuning
      within an existing textual LLM, Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023). As illustrated in Figure 1(a), Llava
046
      takes an image Xv and a question Xq as input. The image patches (a series of sub-figures of the
047
      input image) are transformed into a sequence of image embeddings Hv by a projection matrix W
048   within a visual encoder, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Simultaneously, the question is transformed
049   into a series of word embeddings by the embedding layer. The transformed image embeddings and
050   question word embeddings are then processed by the model to generate the final answer Xa . Our
051   study seeks to address three key questions: a) What is the relationship between the mechanisms of
052   VQA and textual QA (TQA)? b) Are the visual features interpretable under textual LLM’s inter-
053   pretability analysis method? c) How does Llava acquire its VQA ability during visual instruction
      tuning?

079   We explore these questions on the color answering task, as color information is one of the most
080   prominent features in images, making it an ideal starting point for understanding the mechanism of
081   VQA. For VQA, we collect animal photos from the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) featuring an
082
      animal [A] and its correct color [C], and then ask ‘What is the color of [A]?’. For TQA, we generate
      a corresponding textual context for each photo, such as ‘[A] is [C].’ For instance, the complete input
083
      might be ‘Dog is brown. Q: What is the color of the dog? A:’, with the correct answer ‘brown’.
084
      We investigate the TQA mechanism in Vicuna, as shown in Figure 1(b). In shallow layers, the
085   color position (‘brown’) extracts the animal information (‘dog’). In deep layers’ attention heads, the
086   value-output matrices extract the color features (‘brown’), while the query-key matrices compute
087   the similarity between the last position (encoding the question about ‘dog’) and the color position’s
088   animal features, determining how much the probability of ‘brown’ is increased. When the question
089   involves the same animal as the textual context, the attention score at the color position is large,
090   and this position shows the largest log probability increase among all positions because it contains
091   substantial information relevant to the final prediction ‘brown’.
092   Next, we investigate the mechanism of VQA in Llava, starting by using log probability increase
093   scores to identify the most important image regions, which we find to be the image patches related
094   to the animals (as shown in Figure 2). We then apply the same methods used in textual question
095   answering (TQA) to analyze the value-output matrices and the query-key matrices for these key
096   output vectors. Our analysis reveals that the VQA mechanism in Llava is similar to that of TQA: the
097   value-output matrices extract color information, while the query-key matrices compute the similarity
098   between the question content and the animal features. Further, we employ interpretability methods to
099
      analyze the visual features in the embedding layer by projecting them into the embedding space (Dar
      et al., 2022). We discover that the visual embeddings exhibit significant interpretability regarding
100
      colors and animals, indicating that these embeddings already contain essential information about
101
      both. Based on these findings, we propose the hypothesis for the VQA mechanism shown in Figure
102   1(c). This hypothesis suggests that the visual embeddings store information about animals and
103   colors, which is then transferred to deeper layers via the positions’ residual streams. In the deep
104   layers’ attention heads, the value-output matrices extract color features, while the query-key matrices
105   calculate the similarity between the question and the animal features. Finally, we compare the
106   most important heads across vicuna TQA, Llava TQA, and Llava VQA, finding that the important
107   attention heads are similar in all scenarios. This result suggests that Llava enhances Vicuna’s existing
      abilities during visual instruction tuning.

109   stand the important image patches that influence final predictions in Llava’s VQA (Figure 6), which

138   2.1     U NDERSTANDING T EXTUAL LLM S
139
140
      Causal intervention (Vig et al., 2020) is a common method for identifying important modules in
      LLMs (Zhang & Nanda, 2023; Makelov et al., 2023), by computing the change of the final prediction
141
      when intervening the module. Using causal intervention, Meng et al. (2022) find the medium MLP
142
      layers in GPT2 store important parameters for knowledge. Stolfo et al. (2023) find similar stages
143   in arithmetic tasks. A serious of studies (Merullo et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023) focus on
144   constructing the internal circuit in transformers from input to output, taking the attention heads and
145   MLP layers as basic units. Elhage et al. (2021) and Olsson et al. (2022) find that the induction
146   heads are helpful for predictions like [A][B] ... [A] => [B]. Hanna et al. (2024) explore how GPT2
147   computes greater-than algorithm. Gould et al. (2023) find the successor heads help predict the next
148   number like Monday => Tuesday. Wang et al. (2022) study how GPT2 performs the indirect object
149   identification task. Prakash et al. (2024) investigate the circuit between and after fine-tuning and find
150   that fine-tuning enhances existing mechanisms. Conmy et al. (2023) propose a method to construct
151
      the circuits automatically. Another type of works aim to explore the neurons’ interpretability (Dai
      et al., 2021; Sajjad et al., 2022; Nanda et al.; Gurnee et al., 2023). Geva et al. (2022) find that
152
      the MLP neurons are interpretable when projecting into the unembedding space. Dar et al. (2022)
153
      observe that other vectors are also interpretable in the unembedding space. Yu & Ananiadou (2024)
154   calculate log probability increase to identify the important modules for the predictions.
155

      2.2     U NDERSTANDING M ULTIMODAL LLM S
157
158   Compared with textual LLMs, only a few studies have investigated the mechanisms of MLLMs.
159   Stan et al. (2024) design a interpretability tool for vision-language models using average attention,
160   relevancy map and causal interpretation. Basu et al. (2024) apply causal intervention methods to un-
161   derstand the information storage and transfer in MLLMs. Tong et al. (2024) study the shortcomings
      of the visual encoder CLIP. Gandelsman et al. (2023) explore the interpretability of CLIP.

      In this section, we explore the mechanism of TQA in Vicuna. We analyze 1,000 color-answering
219
      sentences of the form ‘[A] is [C]. Q: What is the color of [A]? A:’, where [A] represents the animal
220   and [C] represents the color. These sentences are derived from 1,000 images sampled from the
221   COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014). For VQA, the input consists of an image and the question ‘Q:
222   What is the color of [A]? A:’. The only difference between VQA and TQA is that, in the case of
223   TQA, the image is translated into a textual context.
224
      Inspired by previous studies (Olsson et al., 2022; Yu & Ananiadou, 2024), we propose the hypothe-
225
      sis shown in Figure 1(b) for TQA: In shallow layers, the color position extracts the animal informa-
226   tion, while the last position encodes the question information. In deep layers’ attention heads, the
227   value-output matrices extract color information from the color position, and the query-key matrices
228   compute the similarity between the last position’s question features and the color position’s animal
229   features. When the question and the textual context refer to the same animal, the similarity score is
230   high, leading to an increased probability of the color token in the final prediction’s distribution.
231

232   Does the color position play the largest role in predicting the color token? b) Do the value-

234   extract the animal features from the textual context? d) Does the last position encode the
235   animal features in the question? To explore these questions, we design two comparison sentences:
236   ‘[A1] is [C]. Q: What is the color of [A]? A:’ and ‘[A] is [C]. Q: What is the color of [A1]? A:’,
237   where [A1] represents a different animal. We refer to the original sentence (‘[A] is [C]. Q: What is

344   first. A crucial question arises: Can this method be applied to analyze information stored in visual
345
      embeddings? Specifically, will the color and animal tokens rank highly when the visual embeddings
      are projected into the embedding matrix E? To investigate this, we projected the top 20 positions’
346
      visual embeddings into embedding space E and computed the MRR scores for different tokens. The
347
      MRR for the correct color versus a random color is 0.455 versus 0.013, and for the correct animal
348   versus a random animal, it is 0.076 versus 0.0003. We also calculated the MRR for the correct color
349   and correct animal at a random position, which were 0.003 and 0.004, respectively. These results
350   suggest that the top 20 positions already contain substantial information about the correct animal
351   and color, whereas the random position does not.
352
      Conclusion. Based on the experimental results, we propose the hypothesis about the mechanism of
353
      VQA illustrated in Figure 1(c). The visual embeddings generated by the projection W and the CLIP
354   visual encoder already contain information about the animal and the color (evidence e). This infor-
355   mation is propagated through the positions’ residual streams into the deep layers. In the deep layers’
356   attention heads, the value-output matrices extract color information (evidence b), while the query-
357   key matrices compute the similarity between the animal information and the question information
358   at the last position (evidence c and d). When the similarity is high, the color information related to
359   the animal in the question is more effectively transferred to the last position, thereby increasing the
360   probability of correctly predicting the color token.

444

447   dog itself. This suggests that the average attention score may fail to pinpoint the true reason behind
448   the final prediction. In contrast, our method can accurately identify the important image patches
449   related to the dog. The interpretability of these patches, identified by the log probability increase
450   score, is grounded in the analysis from Section 3, offering a more reliable and robust understanding.

452   a significant issue that has been extensively studied (Li et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Bai et al.,

474   5    C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we utilize mechanistic interpretability methods to investigate the mechanism of VQA
477   in Llava. We find that the mechanism of VQA is similar to that of TQA. The visual embeddings
478   encode the information of the animals and the colors, and the last position encodes the information of
479   the question in shallow layers. In deep layers’ attention heads, the value-output matrices extract the
480   color information from the visual embeddings, and the query-key matrices compute the similarity
481   between the last position’s question features and the visual positions’ animal features, controlling
482   the probability of the final prediction. Moreover, we find that Llava enhances existing abilities of
483
      Vicuna during visual instruction tuning. Based on this analysis, we design an interpretability tool
      for locating the important image patches related to the final prediction, which has low computational
484
      cost, better interpretability and can be utilized for understanding visual hallucination. Overall, our
485
      method and analysis is helpful for understanding the mechanism of VQA.
