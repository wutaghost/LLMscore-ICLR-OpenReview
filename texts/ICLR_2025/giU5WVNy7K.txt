009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed natural language processing,
               yet their substantial model sizes often demand significant computational resources.
013
               To conserve computing resources and increase inference speed, it is crucial to prune
014
               redundant parameters, especially for general users who often need expert models
015            tailored to specific downstream scenarios. However, current pruning methods pri-
016            marily focus on maintaining models’ general capabilities, either requiring extensive
017            post-training or performing poorly due to coarse-grained pruning. In this work,
018            we design a Custom Pruning method (Cus-Prun) to prune a large general model
019            into a smaller expert model for specific scenarios. Cus-Prun positions an expert
020            model along the “language”, “domain” and “task” dimensions. By identifying and
021            pruning irrelevant neurons, it creates expert models without any post-training. Our
022            experiments demonstrate that Cus-Prun consistently outperforms other meth-
023
               ods, achieving minimal loss in both expert and general capabilities across various
               models from different model families and sizes.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Team
030
      et al., 2024) have revolutionized the field of natural language processing, emerging as powerful tools
031   with widespread applications across various languages (Cui et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a), domains
032   (Li et al., 2023a; Roziere et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), and tasks (Azerbayev et al., 2024; Alves et al.,
033   2024). However, the impressive performance of LLMs often comes at the cost of immense model sizes,
034   mostly containing billions of parameters and thus demand significant computing resources (Goldstein
035   et al., 2023; Musser, 2023). To address this issue, researchers have recently proposed various pruning
036   methods for LLMs. These methods aim to reduce model parameters while maintaining the model’s
037   overall performance through techniques such as removal of unimportant structures (Men et al., 2024;
038   Song et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023), matrix approximation (Zhao et al., 2024a;
039
      Sharma et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al., 2024), and extensive post-training after pruning (Wang et al.,
      2024; Xia et al., 2024).
040
041   These existing pruning methods have primarily focused on preserving the general capabilities of
042   the model, often evaluated using compound benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021)
043   consisting of a broad spectrum of tasks. While aiming for overall versatility, they may not align
044   well with real-world user needs, which are usually more specific and targeted. For instance, a user
045
      might require a question-answering model tailored specifically for the education domain in German.
      Such specialized request aligns closely with the fundamental motivation behind pruning: to create a
046
      smaller model by eliminating unnecessary parameters. In this context, “unnecessary” becomes much
047
      clearer—parameters that are irrelevant to the specific use case can be considered redundant. Pruning
048   could therefore be leveraged to remove parameters irrelevant to the target language, domain, or task,
049   thereby producing a more specialized expert model for the desired application. However, current
050   pruning techniques primarily focus on general capabilities, especially for traditional NLP tasks in
051   English, and often employ coarse-grained pruning approaches, and sometimes require extensive
052   post-training after pruning (Xia et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a; Men et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).
053   Therefore, a more fine-grained and expert model targeting approach is needed to effectively tailor
      models to particular user needs while maintaining the general performance.

074
075   In this work, we introduce a novel Custom Pruning (Cus-Prun) method, designed to prune a
076   large general model into a small specialized expert model tailored for specific scenarios. We
077   first define the expert model by positioning the target user’s needs along three key dimensions:
078   language (e.g., English, Chinese, Germain), domain (e.g., E-commerce, education), and task (e.g.,
079   QA, summarization). Then motivated by existing studies that certain neurons are responsible for
080   certain functions (Zhao et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024), Cus-Prun identifies
081   and preserves critical neurons that are more relevant to particular languages, domains, or tasks,
082   while pruning less relevant ones, ultimately leading to a smaller expert models. Specifically, as
083
      illustrated in Figure 1, Cus-Prun involves two main steps: First, it identifies irrelevant neurons for
      each dimension by assessing the impact of their removal on the generated output when processing
084
      corresponding corpus. A neuron is deemed irrelevant if zeroing its parameters affects the output by
085
      a specified margin. Such corpus for each dimension could be easily constructed from the relevant
086   plain text documents. Next, we construct the expert model by pruning common irrelevant neurons
087   across all dimensions. Therefore, it allows for the creation of expert models that excel in specific
088   scenarios, such as the German QA model in the education domain, without the need for extensive
089   post-training or fine-tuning. Furthermore, Cus-Prun’s flexibility allows it to focus on one, two,
090   or all three dimensions (language, domain, task) as needed, making it adaptable to a wide range of
091   real-world applications where specialized LLMs are required.
092   We conduct comprehensive experiments to evaluate the performance of Cus-Prun across various
093   scenarios. Experimental results demonstrate that it consistently outperforms other pruning methods in
094   all settings. For three-dimensional specific expert models, Cus-Prun prunes 25.0% of parameters
095   while incurring only a 14% drop in expert capability (averaging across multilingual, multidomain,
096   and multitask datasets) and 12% on general capability (averaging performance on three representative
097   compound NLP benchmarks) for Llama2-13B. In contrast, others suffer a 38% reduction in expert
098   capabilities and a 29% decline in general capabilities. This trend is consistent across multiple models
099   from different model families and sizes, such as Mistral-Nemo, Llama3-8B, and Llama3-70B. For
100
      more focused applications, such as two- or one-dimensional specific expert models (e.g., language-
      domain specific or language-specific models), Cus-Prun also surpasses other pruning methods,
101
      demonstrating its versatility and effectiveness across various specialized settings.

106   An expert model could be generally positioned from three dimensions: “language” (L ∈ L), “domain”
107   (D ∈ D), and “task” (T ∈ T), which can be represented as LLMExp := (L, D, T ) ∈ L × D × T.
      Specifically, the language dimension encompasses various languages such as English, Spanish, and

      Thai. The domain dimension covers different fields like finance, legal, and medical. The task
109   dimension includes various applications such as question-answering, data-to-text, and summarization.
110
111   As expert models focus solely on specific capabilities, some unused capabilities such as support for
112
      irrelevant languages or domains will inevitably become redundant. To optimize computing resources
      and increase inference speed, we could prune redundant parameters that do not align with our current
113
      objectives. In this section, we propose a custom pruning method named Cus-Prun to derive expert
114
      models with flexible customization granularity.

      2.1   F OUNDATIONAL C USTOM P RUNING
117
118   Drawing inspiration from recent neuron interpretation studies (Tang et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024;
119   Zhao et al., 2024b) that many parameters in the model are irrelevant to processing a specific “lan-
120   guage”, we hypothesize that this phenomenon can be extended to other dimensions such as “domain”
121   and “task”, meaning that certain parameters remain unused when handling a specific dimension.
122   In contrast to other studies that examine redundant layers (Song et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024) or
123   modules (Zhang et al., 2024), Cus-Prun involves a more fine-grained investigation focusing on
124   redundant neurons, which are defined as individual rows or columns within the parameter matrix of a
125
      language model. Concretely, when handling each dimension, we identify a specific set of irrelevant
126
      neurons in the original LLM, denoted as ÑL , ÑD , and ÑT for L, D, and T , respectively. An expert
      LLM can be obtained by removing neurons that are irrelevant to all three dimensions. Specifically, to
127
      identify irrelevant neurons corresponding to the selected dimension, we construct a corpus within that
128
      dimension while ablating others. For example, to determine irrelevant neurons for a specific language
129   LExp , we create a corpus set CLExp = {(LExp , D, T )|D ∈ D, T ∈ T}, comprising documents in
130   language LExp across various domains D and tasks T . We then identify neurons that are irrelevant
131   across all documents in CLExp , i.e.,

158   Besides three-dimensional expert models, requirements involving constraints in one or two dimensions
159   are also common in real-world applications (Roziere et al., 2023; Alves et al., 2024). For instance, a

161   specific model (such as a Chinese Medical LLM) constrains two dimensions. Therefore, in this
      section, we extend Cus-Prun to prune expert models in different granularities.

501   6    R ELATED W ORK
502
503   LLM Compression Given the high costs associated with training, inferencing, and tuning LLMs,
504   many studies explore methods to compress the model to conserve computing resources, including
505   model compression (Zhu et al., 2023), quantization (Xu et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
506
      2024; Li et al., 2024), and pruning (Wang et al., 2019). In the context of pruning, sparsity serves as a
      structural pruning (Li et al., 2022; 2023c; Kurz et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2024),
507
      which doesn’t save computing resources but leverages GPU calculation properties for acceleration. In
508
      addition, some works develop unstructuraled pruning methods aimed at reducing model parameters
509   while maintaining general performance. They either employ extensive post-training (Ma et al., 2023;
510   Xia et al., 2024; Muralidharan et al., 2024), nor adopt coarse-grained pruning method at structure
511   such as approximating all parameters (Zhao et al., 2024a), removing entire layers (Men et al., 2024),
512   or eliminating network structures (Zhang et al., 2024). However, they fail to capture the model’s
513   expert capability thus fail to be applied to more specific downstream scenarios.
514
515   Customizing Model The rapid evolution of LLMs has led to a growing need for customization to
516   meet specific requirements across various fields. Language-specific models are being developed to
517   address unique linguistic needs (Cui et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b), while domain-specific models
518
      cater to specialized areas like healthcare and software development (Li et al., 2023a; Roziere et al.,
      2023; Li et al., 2023b). Task-specific models further enhance performance for particular applications
519
      (Azerbayev et al., 2024; Alves et al., 2024). However, correctly customizing these models requires
520
      extensive fine-tuning with a tailored training corpus. This challenge highlights the need for efficient
521   methods to acquire and refine expert models, ensuring LLMs can be adapted effectively to meet
522   diverse industry demands.

      7    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      LLMs offer impressive capabilities but come with substantial computational costs. Efficient pruning
      of redundant parameters is crucial for conserving resources and improving speed, especially for
528
      users requiring specialized models for specific tasks. While current pruning methods often demand
529
      extensive post-training or lack precision, our proposed method, Cus-Prun, creates smaller expert
530   models without post-training. By mapping models along "language," "domain," and "task" dimensions
531   and pruning irrelevant neurons, Cus-Prun achieves efficient expert model creation in a finer-
532   grained manner. Experimental results demonstrate that Cus-Prun consistently outperforms existing
533   techniques on three-dimensional specific models. Furthermore, Cus-Prun can be tailored to more
534   realistic scenarios by targeting just one or two dimensions, such as language-domain or language-
535   specific models, experimentally outperforming other pruning methods in these contexts as well.

      A.1.2      M ULTIDOMAIN S ETTINGS
774   Settings For multidomain setting, we can obtain two corpora: CDExp = {(L, DExp , T )|L ∈ L, T ∈
775   T} and C(L,T )Exp = {(D, (L, T )Exp )|D ∈ D}. The first corpus contains samples in a specific
776   domain across various languages and tasks, while the second corpus contains samples from a spe-
777   cific language-task combination across different domains, i.e., the target dataset in other domains.
778   Specifically, for CDExp we employ specific domain corpus, including English split of medical cor-
779   pus (García-Ferrero et al., 2024) for medical domain, general finance corpus for finance domain6 ,
780   general Twitter corpus (Kharde & Sonawane, 2016), and English split of Amazon corpus (Keung
781   et al., 2020). For C(L,T )Exp , we employ the corresponding datasets in general domains, including
782   CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019) for MedMCQ, open table question-answering OTT-QA (Chen
783   et al., 2020) for FinTQA, general sentiment analysis (Attia et al., 2018) for TSA and AMSA.
784
785   Experiment Details Hyperparameters, including the sizes of CDExp and C(L,T )Exp , are determined
786   using the validation set of the Amazon sentiment analysis dataset and then applied to testsets in other
787
      multidomain datasets. Furthermore, accuracy is the metric used for all datasets.
788
      A.1.3      M ULTITASK S ETTINGS
789
790   Settings For multitask setting, we can obtain two corpora: CTExp = {(L, D, TExp )|L ∈ L, D ∈ D}
791   and C(L,D)Exp = {(T, (L, S)Exp )|T ∈ T}. The first corpus contains samples in a specific task
792   across various languages and domains, while the second corpus contains samples from a specific
793   language-domain combination across different tasks, i.e., the target dataset in other tasks. Specifically,
794   for CTExp we employ specific task corpus, including XSum corpus (Abacha & Demner-Fushman,
795   2019) for summarization task, general conterfact corpus7 for counterfactual task. For C(L,D)Exp , we
796   employ the corresponding datasets in other tasks, including MedQCQ (Pal et al., 2022) for MedSum,
797   AMSA (Zhang et al., 2015) for AMSum and AMContFact.
798
799   Experiment Details Hyperparameters, including the sizes of CTExp and C(L,D)Exp , are determined
800   using the validation set of the Amazon counterfactual dataset and then applied to testsets in other
801
      multitask setting datasets. Furthermore, accuracy is the metric used for ARC-c, GSM8K, MMLU,
      and AMContFact, while Rouge-L (Lin, 2004) is used for MedSum and AMSum.
