009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphs are essential tools for modeling complex relationships. While prior re-
012            search with earlier generations of large language models (LLMs) showed them
013            to struggle with basic graph primitives, we find that the situation has changed
014            with modern state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs, which excel at these tasks. Given
015            these advances, we propose a more challenging evaluation problem: graph mod-
016            ification, a foundational, interpretable, and non-trivial problem in which an LLM
017            must determine the outcome of adding or deleting a given sequence of nodes or
018
               edges, and potentially then compute on the resulting modified graph. We intro-
               duce GraphModQA, a novel benchmark dataset comprising graph modification
019
               question-answer pairs designed to rigorously test LLMs’ abilities in graph manip-
020
               ulation and dynamic reasoning. Our results show that while SOTA LLMs perform
021            well on static graph property tasks, their accuracy degrades on graph modifica-
022            tion tasks; their performance is particularly low as the number of modifications
023            increases, and when the adjacency matrix is used to represent the graph — an es-
024            sential encoding not explored in previous work. We provide new techniques for
025            improving performance on graph modification tasks, and we introduce Modify-
026            and-Print (MAP) prompting, which asks models to output the intermediate adja-
027            cency matrices at each step, and which markedly improves the models’ perfor-
028            mance. Our findings highlight a critical gap in current LLM capabilities regarding
029
               dynamic graph reasoning tasks and underscore the potential of techniques like
               MAP prompting to mitigate these challenges.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized various aspects of natural language process-
035
      ing, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating human-like text (Brown
036   et al. (2020)). Despite their success, the intersection of LLMs and graph-based tasks remains un-
037   derstudied. Graphs are fundamental for modeling complex relationships in domains such as social
038   networks and recommender systems (Schneider et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022)). Understanding how
039   LLMs can be leveraged to process and reason about graph structures is crucial for advancing their
040   applicability in these areas.
041
      Previous work by Wang et al. (2024a) explored the ability of pretrained LLMs to handle a variety of
042   graph tasks. This study included tasks such as calculating shortest paths and simulating graph neural
043   networks, highlighting the challenges LLMs face with graph-structured data. Building upon this,
044   Fatemi et al. (2023) introduced GraphQA, a synthetic dataset of graph property question-answer
045   pairs, which included additional and more fundamental graph property tasks, such as calculating
046   the number of nodes in the graph or the degree of a particular node, noting the simplicity and
047   interpretability of these tasks compared to those in Wang et al. (2024a). Through these experiments,
048   Fatemi et al. (2023) concluded that LLMs, in particular models from the PaLM family (Anil et al.
049   (2023)) performed poorly on fundamental graph property tasks when provided with various graph
050
      encoding functions such as incident lists or textual descriptions, indicating a limitation in their ability
      to process and reason about structured data represented in graphs.
051
052   While prior research with earlier generations of large language models (LLMs) showed them to
053   struggle with basic graph primitives, it is unknown how modern state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs per-
      form on these property tasks. Modern LLMs have shown huge performance increases across many


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      reasoning tasks compared to PaLM (Dubey et al. (2024)), suggesting the possibility that these per-
055   formance increases may translate into the domain of graph reasoning. Our empirical analysis reveals
056   that contemporary state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs now excel at basic graph property tasks. Models
057   such as o1-mini and Llama 3.1 405B demonstrate proficiency in identifying patterns and making
058   simple inferences from graph data across different encoding methods.
059
      Given these advances, we propose a more challenging evaluation problem: graph modification.
060
      Unlike static graph property tasks, graph modification requires models to perform a sequence of
061   operations—such as adding or removing nodes or edges—and then answer questions about the re-
062   sulting graph or output the modified graph itself. These tasks, which have yet to be studied in the
063   context of LLMs, are foundational and interpretable yet non-trivial, as they necessitate maintaining
064   and updating an internal representation of the graph through each modification step. The task of out-
065   putting the final modified graph is especially complex due to the intricacies of the output space. In
066   real-world applications, graphs are rarely static; they evolve over time with the addition or removal
067   of nodes and edges. This dynamic nature is evident in domains like social network analysis, where
068   relationships and interactions constantly change (Kazemi et al. (2020)), and in evolving knowledge
069
      bases that need to adapt to new information (Trivedi et al. (2017); Schneider et al. (2022)). To fully
      assess the graph reasoning capabilities of modern LLMs, it is essential to evaluate their ability to
070
      understand and manipulate graphs that undergo modifications. Reasoning on these graphs com-
071
      bines the inherent difficulty of dynamic state maintenance with high-level reasoning about the final
072   modified graph, making it significantly more rigorous in evaluating a model’s capability to handle
073   evolving graph structures.
074
075
      We introduce GraphModQA, a novel benchmark dataset containing graph modification question-
      answer pairs. GraphModQA is designed to rigorously test LLMs’ abilities in graph manipulation
076
      and dynamic reasoning. It includes a variety of graph encoding functions, with the addition of the
077
      adjacency matrix—a fundamental representation not explored in previous work. Compared to static
078   graph property tasks, we find that SOTA LLMs’ performance degrades notably on graph modifica-
079   tion tasks, especially as the number of modifications increases. This decline is most pronounced
080   when using the adjacency matrix encoding, highlighting the unique challenges posed by this encod-
081   ing due to its dense and numerical nature.
082
      To address this low performance, we explore and evaluate techniques aimed at improving LLM per-
083
      formance on graph modification tasks. We find that Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting can lead to
084   performance increases for Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Llama 3.1 405B, yet we observe little to no perfor-
085   mance gain in most cases where more CoT examples are included in the prompt. Across all baseline
086   models and on multiple modification tasks, we find prompting the LLM to print intermediate graphs
087   leads to notable performance gains. We call this technique Modify-and-Print (MAP) prompting, a
088   simple yet effective technique where models are instructed to print the intermediate graph resulting
089   from each modification step. MAP prompting significantly improves the models’ ability to reason
090   about the final graph over multiple modification steps. By explicitly generating the intermediate
091   states, the models can better track changes and maintain accurate internal representations, leading
092
      to enhanced performance on the final tasks.
093   While we identify prompting techniques that improve the performance of LLMs on graph modifica-
094   tion tasks, overall, LLMs are still not proficient in modifying graphs. The observed difficulties with
095   graph modifications and adjacency matrix encodings underscore the need for improved models or
096   training strategies that can handle dynamic, structured data more effectively. These results call for a
097   shift in benchmarking practices toward tasks that require manipulation of graph data, thereby better
098
      aligning evaluations with real-world applications in dynamic networks and systems.
099   In summary, this work makes the following contributions:
100
101
102         1. Empirical Evidence of LLM Capabilities: We demonstrate that modern SOTA LLMs ex-
103
               cel at basic graph property tasks across various encoding functions, challenging previous
               notions of their limitations.
104
105
106         2. Introduction of GraphModQA: We present a novel benchmark dataset designed to eval-
107            uate LLMs on graph modification tasks, providing a rigorous testbed for dynamic graph
               reasoning.

      LLMs for graph reasoning: Wang et al. (2024a) explores the capability of LLMs to tackle various
      graph-based tasks. This study evaluates tasks such as topological sort, maximum flow, and bipartite
148
      graph matching. Fatemi et al. (2023) delves into different methods for encoding graphs as text, with
149
      a particular focus on evaluating different encodings of graphs as text. This work builds upon Wang
150   et al. (2024a) by introducing more interpretable, straightforward, and fundamental tasks, focusing
151   on fundamental graph properties. The graph property tasks evaluated in Fatemi et al. (2023) in-
152   clude: Node Count (counting the total number of nodes in the graph), Edge Count (counting the
153   total number of edges in the graph), Node Degree (calculating the degree of a given node), Edge
154   Existence (determining if an edge exists between two nodes), Connected Nodes (identifying nodes
155   connected to a given node), and Cycle Check (determining whether or not a cycle exists in the
156   graph).
157   Both sets of tasks found in Wang et al. (2024a) and Fatemi et al. (2023) are limited to returning graph
158   properties. In addition, Fatemi et al. (2023) use LLMs in the PaLM family as their benchmarks and
159   concluded that LLMs perform poorly on these tasks. Our work evaluates SOTA LLMs on the graph
160   property tasks defined in Fatemi et al. (2023), finding that modern LLMs strongly outperform PaLM
161   62B on these tasks. We then shift the focus to graph modification tasks, which potentially better
      evaluate the graph reasoning and manipulation capabilities of state-of-the-art LLMs.

      abilities to perform reasoning over dynamic graph structures.

195
196   This section outlines our approach to measuring the graph reasoning abilities of LLMs, detailing the
197   notation used, our evaluation methodology, and the construction of our datasets.
198
199
200
      3.1   N OTATION
201
      Let f represent the interface function to a generative AI model, which processes high-dimensional
202
      discrete input tokens W and produces output in the same token space. For this study, f refers to a
203   pre-trained Large Language Model (LLM). We define graphs as G = (V, E), where V is the set of
204   nodes (vertices) and E ⊆ (V × V ) is the set of edges connecting them.
205
206
      3.2   G RAPH R EASONING WITH LLM S
207
208
      Following Fatemi et al. (2023), we evaluate an LLM’s graph reasoning abilities by presenting it
209
      with a graph G and a question Q. The LLM generates an answer A, which is compared to a ground-
210   truth solution S. Fatemi et al. (2023) conducted their evaluation over a dataset D of (g(G), q(Q), S)
211   triples . We define g(G) as the graph encoding function, which can represent G in various textual for-
212   mats, such as an adjacency matrix, an incident list, or a descriptive format. Similarly, we define q(Q)
213   as the question rephrasing function, which can employ different prompting methods, including zero-
214   shot prompting and chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al. (2022)). The performance of the LLMs
215   is evaluated by iterating over D, and calculating the proportion of answers A = f (g(G), q(Q)) that
      match with the corresponding ground-truth solution S.

      3.3     G RAPH QA
217
218   Fatemi et al. (2023) developed GraphQA, a significant and comprehensive synthetic dataset com-
219   prising (g(G), q(Q), S) triples. The questions in GraphQA target basic graph properties, such as
220   counting nodes and edges, with answers formatted as simple integer counts or yes/no responses.
221   The primary objective of Fatemi et al. (2023) was to explore the performance implications of vari-
222   ous graph encoding functions g(.), question rephrasing functions q(.), and LLM architectures f (.)
223   on the GraphQA dataset.

225   4     G RAPH M OD QA
226
227   In this section, we introduce GraphModQA, a novel dataset specifically designed to evaluate the
228   graph modification capabilities of LLMs. We provide a detailed description of the graph generation
229   process, the structure of the dataset, and the types of modifications included to rigorously test LLMs’
230   abilities to manipulate graph structures.
231
232   4.1     G RAPH G ENERATION
233
      The foundation of GraphModQA lies in the diverse and robust generation of graph structures. Con-
234
      sistent with the methodologies outlined by Wang et al. (2024a) and Fatemi et al. (2023), we generate
235
      250 undirected Erdős-Rényi (ER) graphs, where the total number of nodes in each graph, n, is sam-
236   pled from a uniform distribution on a finite interval, and for each pair of nodes (i, j), the probability
237   p that an edge exists between them is also sampled from a uniform distribution U (0, 1). This diver-
238   sity in graph structure is crucial for ensuring that the dataset comprehensively evaluates the LLMs’
239   graph reasoning abilities across different graph configurations.
240
241   4.2     DATASET S TRUCTURE
242
243   GraphModQA is constructed as a collection of (g(G), m(M, k), q(Q), S) 4-tuples. Here, G again
244   represents the generated graph. We introduce m(M, k) as the modification sequence function,
245
      which outputs a sequence of k modifications of type M to be performed on G. Q denotes a final
      question on the resulting graph, and S is the ground-truth solution to the question after all modi-
246
      fications have been applied. We illustrate this in Figure 1 and in Section A.9, which shows some
247
      example prompt inputs and model outputs. Additionally, we show the algorithms used to construct
248   GraphModQA in Section A.3.
249
250   4.2.1    G RAPH E NCODING F UNCTION g(G)
251
252   Similar to Fatemi et al. (2023), we define g(G) as the graph encoding function, which represents
253   the graph G in a format suitable for input to the LLM. In GraphModQA, we utilize two encoding
254   functions previously defined in Fatemi et al. (2023), namely the Incident List and Coauthorship en-
255
      codings. In addition to these two encodings, we introduce the Adjacency Matrix encoding in this
      work and focus on it in the main sections of the paper due to its challenging nature for LLMs.
256
      The adjacency matrix is a matrix representation where each entry Aij indicates the presence (1) or
257
      absence (0) of an edge between nodes i and j. Surprisingly, this encoding has yet to be explored
258   as a graph encoding function in previous studies. Effectively manipulating adjacency matrices is
259   important for LLMs because they are fundamental to many modern graph algorithms and applica-
260   tions. Adjacency matrices are widely used for storing and processing graph data in computational
261   systems due to their suitability for matrix operations and compatibility with linear algebra-based
262   techniques. Enabling LLMs to interpret and manipulate adjacency matrices extends their applicabil-
263   ity to a broader range of real-world tasks in network analysis, computational biology, and machine
264   learning for graphs, where adjacency matrices are a standard representation.
265   Additionally, the adjacency matrix provides a more challenging representation for the LLMs. This is
266   not only because the adjacency matrix presents a dense numerical format that lacks the natural lan-
267   guage cues of other encodings, or that the format forces models to reason on both the presence and
268   absence of edges, but also because it relies on an implicit numbering scheme for nodes, where node
269   identifiers correspond directly to the indices of the matrix. When modifications such as node re-
      moval occur, this implicit numbering becomes particularly challenging, as the nodes in the resulting


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      graph must be renumbered to maintain a contiguous matrix structure. For example, if an adjacency
271   matrix represents nodes 0 to 4 and node 2 is removed, the third row and column are eliminated, and
272   subsequent nodes are effectively renumbered—node 3 becomes node 2, node 4 becomes node 3.
273   This renumbering adds an extra layer of complexity for the LLM to manage during reasoning and
274   updates, increasing the difficulty of accurately interpreting and manipulating the graph.
275
276   4.2.2    M ODIFICATION S EQUENCE F UNCTION m(M, k)
277
278   The modification sequence function m(M, k) lists in text the sequence of k modifications
279
      m1 , m2 , ..., mk to be performed on G, resulting in a final graph Gk . The intermediate modifica-
      tions in each sequence must belong to the same modification type M , where these types include: 1)
280
      Add Edge (instructing the model to add an edge between two sampled and unconnected nodes), 2)
281
      Remove Edge (instructing the model to remove the existing edge between two sampled and con-
282   nected nodes), 3) Add Node (instructing the model to add a new node to the graph), 4) Remove
283   Node (instructing the model to remove an existing node from the graph, along with all its associated
284   edges), and 5) Mix (uniformly sampling one of the four previously defined modifications at each
285   step k in the sequence).
286
287   4.2.3    F INAL Q UESTION Q AND Q UESTION R EPHRASING q(Q)
288
289   We ask the LLM to answer a final question Q based on the final modified graph Gk . We include
290
      multiple graph property questions from Fatemi et al. (2023) and give additional details regarding
      these tasks in Section A.1. In addition to these property tasks, we introduce another final question,
291
      Print Graph, which requires the LLM to output the entire Gk in the same format as the graph
292
      encoding function g(G). This task is particularly challenging because it necessitates the model to
293   accurately reconstruct and output the full graph structure after multiple modifications, demanding
294   precise state tracking and a comprehensive internal representation.
295
296
      We define q(Q) as the question rephrasing function, which can involve different prompting methods.
      In GraphModQA, we explore various prompting techniques to assess their impact on the models’
297
      performance, including zero-shot prompting (providing the question without any additional con-
298
      text or examples), Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting with 1 to 3 examples (including a list of
299   examples that each demonstrate the reasoning process step-by-step), and Modify-and-Print (MAP)
300   prompting (a novel prompting technique for graph modification tasks introduced in Section 5.3).
301
302   4.3     DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
303

305   structing a single dataset entry:
306
307           • Graph encoding function g(): We use three encoding types: Adjacency Matrix, Inci-

                dent, and Coauthorship.
309           • Modification type M : There are five modification types: Add Edge, Remove Edge, Add

311           • Number of modifications k: This ranges from 1 to 5.

              • Final question Q: Five question types are used: Node Count, Edge Count, Node Degree,

      5.2   G RAPH M ODIFICATION TASKS
348
      Building upon the baseline established with static graph property tasks, we investigated the per-
349   formance of SOTA LLMs on the more challenging graph modification tasks introduced in Graph-
350   ModQA. To evaluate the ability of LLMs to handle dynamic graph modifications, we utilized a
351   dataset comprising 250 initial graphs where the size of each graph n is drawn from U (7, 20). For
352   each of these graphs, using the Adjacency Matrix encoding, we applied 1 to 5 modifications for
353   each of the five modification types—Add Edge, Remove Edge, Add Node, Remove Node, and
354   Mix—resulting in multiple sets of modified graphs. After applying the specified modifications to
355   each initial graph, we posed the Print Graph final question to the LLMs, instructing them to out-
356   put the resulting modified graph in the form of an adjacency matrix. This comprehensive approach
357
      allows us to systematically evaluate the models’ capabilities in maintaining and updating internal
      graph representations across varying levels of complexity. We illustrate the performance of each
358
      LLM in Figure 2, and we report the performance of each LLM on different graph encoders and final
359
      questions in the Appendix in Section A.6.
360
361   Our results indicate that across all modification types, models generally perform worse as the number
362   of modifications increases, which suggests challenges in maintaining and updating an internal graph
363
      representation over multiple steps. Notably, the models perform the worst on the Remove Node and
      Mix modifications. The difficulty with the Remove Node modification can likely be attributed to
364
      the challenges associated with managing the adjacency matrix representation, where when a node
365
      is removed, not only must the corresponding row and column be deleted, but the indices of all
366   subsequent nodes must be decremented to maintain the proper numbering scheme. In the Mix
367   modifications, the models face the compounded challenge of handling a variety of modification
368   types within a single sequence. The necessity to adapt to different operations—such as adding an
369   edge in one step and removing a node in the next—requires flexible reasoning and robust state
370   tracking, which current LLMs struggle to perform effectively with the adjacency matrix encoding.
371   Overall, while Claude 3.5 Sonnet outperforms other models across the five modification types, o1-
372   mini demonstrates superior performance on the two most challenging tasks, Remove Node and Mix,
373   after a few modification steps. This suggests that o1-mini’s internal reasoning capabilities become
374
      increasingly effective as the complexity of the modification sequence grows.
375   Overall, our findings indicate that while SOTA LLMs have made substantial progress in handling
376   static graph property tasks, significant challenges remain in the context of dynamic graph modifi-
377   cations, especially when dealing with complex encodings like the adjacency matrix. These results
      emphasize the need for improved models and prompting techniques to enhance the graph reason-

416   5.3.1    C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
417
      Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al. (2022)) is a technique that encourages the model to
418
      generate intermediate reasoning steps before producing the final answer. By providing examples of
419
      detailed reasoning in the prompt, the model is guided to follow a similar process when answering
420   new questions. In our experiments, we evaluated the impact of including 1, 2, and 3 CoT examples
421   in the prompt on the models’ performance. The models differed crucially in how much they were
422   helped by CoT prompting. For Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Llama 3.1 405B, we observed in Figures
423   4 and 5 respectively that CoT prompting generally helps boost performance across all five modifi-
424   cation types. In contrast, for GPT-4o mini, we did not observe significant changes in performance
425   with CoT prompting, as shown in Figure 6. For all models, CoT performance remained relatively
426   consistent regardless of the number of examples included, indicates that they may not be leveraging
427   the additional reasoning steps provided in the prompt to enhance its performance on these tasks.
428   Interestingly, with the o1-mini model, we observed a large drop in performance when using CoT
429   prompting (Figure 3). In general, the model performed worse with CoT examples compared to
430   zero-shot prompting across all modification types. This decline in performance is likely due to the
431   fact that o1-mini reasons internally, and external CoT prompting does not complement its internal
      reasoning processes.

458   5.3.2   M ODIFY- AND -P RINT (MAP) P ROMPTING
459
460
461
      We introduce the Modify-and-Print (MAP) prompting technique as a simple yet effective method
462   to improve model performance on graph modification tasks. In MAP prompting, we instruct the
463   model to output every intermediate graph after each modification. By requiring the model to ex-
464   plicitly generate the intermediate adjacency matrices, we aim to help it maintain a clear internal
465   representation of the graph as it undergoes changes. We illustrate MAP prompting in Section A.9.
466
      We find that MAP prompting performs especially well on edge-related tasks (Add Edge and Re-
467
      move Edge). This is evident for the o1-mini and Claude 3.5 Sonnet models in Figures 3 and 4, where
468   MAP prompting consistently achieves higher accuracy than zero-shot and CoT prompting. The im-
469   provement is especially pronounced with the o1-mini model, where there is a large gap between
470   MAP prompting and the other methods. This suggests that MAP prompting effectively supports
471   the model’s reasoning process by reinforcing state tracking through explicit output of intermediate
472   graphs. On the other modifications—Add Node, Remove Node, and Mix—MAP prompting tends
473   to remain competitive with CoT prompting.
474   An interesting observation emerges when examining the performance of MAP prompting on the first
475   modification step. Intuitively, MAP prompting should perform near-identically to zero-shot prompt-
476   ing when only one modification is applied, as there is only one intermediate modification, which is
477   the final answer. However, we observe that MAP prompting greatly outperforms zero-shot prompt-
478   ing even on the first modification step. This indicates that the presence of the instruction to output
479   intermediate graphs has a significant positive effect on the models’ performance. Furthermore, this
480   suggests that MAP prompting not only aids in state tracking but also likely enhances the models’
481   attention to the modification process, leading to more accurate outputs.
482   Overall, MAP prompting demonstrates its potential as a powerful technique to improve LLM per-
483   formance on dynamic graph reasoning tasks. By encouraging explicit generation of intermediate
484   states, it helps models navigate complex sequences of modifications, especially in tasks involving
485   edge additions and removals. This finding underscores the importance of prompting strategies that
      align closely with the reasoning demands of the task.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we have explored the graph reasoning capabilities of state-of-the-art large language
489   models (LLMs) by introducing GraphModQA, a novel benchmark designed to assess models on
490   dynamic graph modification tasks. Our findings reveal that while modern LLMs excel at basic graph
491   property tasks—a significant improvement over previous generations—they exhibit notable perfor-
492   mance degradation when tasked with modifying graphs, especially as the number of modifications
493   increases. This decline is most pronounced when using the newly-explored Adjacency Matrix en-
494   coding, highlighting the challenges LLMs face in interpreting and manipulating dense numerical
495   representations that require precise state tracking, node renumbering, and the recognition of both
496   the presence and absence of edges.
497   To address these challenges, we investigated the effectiveness of in-context learning strategies,
498   including Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting and our proposed Modify-and-Print (MAP)
499   prompting technique. We found that both CoT and MAP prompting can significantly improve
500   model performance, but their effectiveness varies depending on the task and the model. CoT prompt-
501   ing aids models by providing guided reasoning steps, which is particularly beneficial for models
502   like Claude 3.5 and Llama 3.1 in handling complex decision-making processes. MAP prompting
503
      enhances performance by requiring models to explicitly generate intermediate graph states, thereby
      aiding in state tracking and manipulation tasks—this was especially effective across all models in
504
      edge addition and removal modifications. These findings suggest that leveraging the appropriate
505
      prompting technique can help overcome specific challenges in dynamic graph reasoning. Our work
506   highlights the importance of tailored prompting strategies and calls for further research into meth-
507   ods that enhance LLMs’ abilities to process and reason about dynamic graph structures in various
508   contexts.

      A.1   F INAL Q UESTIONS
597
598   After applying the sequence of k modifications to the initial graph G , we pose a final question Q to
599   the LLM based on the resulting modified graph Gk . The final questions are designed to assess the
600   model’s understanding and reasoning about the graph’s properties and structure. The following are
601   the types of final questions included in our evaluation:
602
603
             • Node Count: Calculate the total number of nodes in the modified graph Gk . This question
               evaluates the model’s ability to accurately track the addition or removal of nodes throughout
604
               the modification sequence.
605
606          • Edge Count: Calculate the total number of edges present in the modified graph Gk This
607
               requires the model to account for all edge additions and deletions, testing its capability to
               maintain an updated edge set.
608
609          • Node Degree: Report the degree of a uniformly sampled node in the modified graph Gk .
610            The degree of a node is the number of edges incident to it. This question assesses the
611
               model’s understanding of local graph topology and its ability to compute node-specific
               properties after modifications.
612
613          • Connected Nodes: List all nodes that are directly connected to a uniformly sampled node
614            in the modified graph Gk . This task tests the model’s ability to identify and retrieve adja-
615
               cency information for a given node.
616          • Print Graph: Output the entire modified graph Gk in the same format as the graph en-
617            coding function g(G). This question is particularly challenging as it requires the model to
618            reconstruct the complete structure of the graph after all modifications, demanding precise
619
               state tracking and comprehensive internal representation.
620
      These final questions cover both global properties (e.g., node and edge counts) and local properties
621
      (e.g., node degree, connected nodes), as well as the ability to reproduce the full graph structure.
622   By including a variety of question types, we aim to thoroughly evaluate the LLMs’ proficiency in
623   understanding, manipulating, and reasoning about graphs after dynamic changes.

       A.7   E DGE D ENSITY AND G RAPH S IZE A BLATION
1405
1406   In this section, we investigate how varying edge density and graph size impact model perfor-
1407   mance. For this analysis, we evaluate graphs with sizes n ∈ {7, 10, 15, 20} and edge densities
1408   p ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 0.9}. For each combination of size and density, we generate 100 unique input graphs
1409   and follow the same procedure outlined in Algorithm 6 to create additional examples, focusing
1410   specifically on the Print Graph task. All evaluations are conducted using Claude 3.5 Sonnet with
1411   the Adjacency Matrix encoder.
1412   With the Add Edge modification (Figure 25), the model maintains strong performance, with slight
1413   drops in performance observed as the number of nodes in the graph increases. Interestingly, the
1414   model performs poorly when asked to add a single edge for low-density graphs, indicating that the
1415   sparsity of the matrix may be influencing the model’s ability to update the correct 0 entry, an issue
1416   that the model seems to correct as it makes more modifications.
1417   The Remove Edge modification (Figure 26) shows strong overall performance, but an inverse trend
1418   compared to Add Edge is observed. As the number of nodes increases, the model struggles at
1419   removing edges from high-density graphs, and this challenge becomes more pronounced with an
1420   increasing number of modifications. This suggests that the model struggles to accurately identify
1421   the correct 1 entry to update in the adjacency matrix for dense graphs.
1422   The Add Node modification (Figure 27) demonstrates very strong performance initially, but accu-
1423   racy declines as the number of nodes in the graph increases. At moderate edge densities on large
1424   graphs (n = 20), the model performs well, but its performance begins to falter at higher densities.
1425   For low-density graphs, the model struggles more significantly, with accuracy further decreasing as
1426   the number of modifications increases.
1427
       Finally, for the Remove Node modification (Figure 28), both graph density and size significantly
1428   impact performance. The model struggles increasingly as the number of nodes grows. Interestingly,
1429   performance is lowest for low-density graphs, while high-density graphs tend to yield the best results
1430   overall across all graph sizes.
