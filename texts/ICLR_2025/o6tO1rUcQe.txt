010
011
               Weather prediction is crucial for decision-making in various social and economic
012
               sectors. The classical numerical weather prediction methods cannot incorporate
013
               the historical observations to enhance the underlying physical models, whereas the
014            existing data-driven, deep learning-based weather prediction methods disregard
015            either the physics of the weather evolution or the topology of the Earth’s surface.
016            In light of these disadvantages, we develop PASSAT, a novel Physics-ASSisted
017            And Topology-informed deep learning model for weather prediction. PASSAT at-
018            tributes the weather evolution to two key factors: (i) the advection process that can
019            be characterized by the advection equation and the Navier-Stokes equation; (ii) the
020            Earth-atmosphere interaction that is difficult to both model and calculate. PASSAT
021            also takes the topology of the Earth’s surface into consideration, other than simply
022
               treating it as a plane. Therefore, PASSAT numerically solves the advection equa-
               tion and the Navier-Stokes equation on the spherical manifold, utilizes a spherical
023
               graph neural network to capture the Earth-atmosphere interaction, and generates
024
               the initial velocity fields that are critical to solving the advection equation, from
025            the same spherical graph neural network. These building blocks constitute a deep
026            learning-based, physics-assisted and topology-informed weather prediction mo-
027            del. In the 5.625◦ -resolution ERA5 data set, PASSAT outperforms both the state-
028            of-the-art deep learning-based weather prediction models and the operational nu-
029            merical weather prediction model IFS T42.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Weather prediction is of paramount importance to social security and economic development, and
036   has attracted extensive research efforts since the ancient time. Among the modern weather prediction
037   methods, numerical weather prediction (NWP) is built upon differential equations that govern the
038   weather evolution (Randall et al., 2007; Bauer et al., 2015). These differential equations attribute the
039
      weather evolution to the advection process and the Earth-atmosphere interaction (Rood, 1987;
      Smith et al., 1990), as shown in Figure 1. The advection process is the evolution of weather variables
040
      (described by the advection equation) driven by the evolution of their velocity fields (described by
041
      the Navier-Stokes equation). The Earth-atmosphere interaction encompasses other complex physical
042   processes in the atmosphere, such as radiation, clouds, and subgrid turbulent motions. One particular
043   challenge in NWP is that the Earth-atmosphere interaction is difficult to model and calculate, form-
044   ing a bottleneck of improving the accuracy of NWP (Hourdin et al., 2017; Kochkov et al., 2024). Be-
045   sides, the accuracy of NWP does not improve with the increasing amount of historical observations.
046
      On the other hand, data-driven methods that predict the weather based on the historical observations,
047
      especially deep learning models, have become very popular in recent years (Ren et al., 2021). With
048   the aid of high-quality and ever-accumulating data, state-of-the-art deep learning models have dem-
049   onstrated great potentials and been integrated into the modern weather prediction systems. Besides,
050   deep learning-based models are able to remarkably shorten the time consumption in the prediction
051   stage (Bi et al., 2023; Lam et al., 2023). However, these models disregard either the physics of the
052   weather evolution or the topology of the Earth’s surface. Thus, their predictions are often unreliable
053   due to the lack of the physical constraints or suffer from the distortions caused by the topological
      structure (Schultz et al., 2021).

074   1.1   E NHANCING D EEP L EARNING WITH D IFFERENTIAL E QUATIONS
075
      Combining with the differential equations that characterize the weather evolution can enhance the
076
      precisions, efficiency and robustness of deep learning models, as the differential equations provide
077
      valuable prior knowledge (Xiang et al., 2022). Some works incorporate differential equations into
078   losses during training deep learning models (Daw et al., 2021; de Bezenac et al., 2018). However,
079   tuning weights for the differential equations and computing stochastic gradients of the losses bring
080   new challenges. Some other works utilize deep learning models to correct NWP models (Kwa et al.,
081   2023; Arcomano et al., 2022; Kochkov et al., 2024). Though having high accuracies, these approa-
082   ches are computationally demanding since they need to both solve all differential equations and train
083   end-to-end neural networks. The closest to ours are (Verma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), in which
084   neural networks are trained with the aid of differential equations. However, they both overlook the
085   Navier-Stokes equation that drives the elocution of the velocity fields.
086   Despite that these physics-assisted deep learning models are harder to train and slower in inference
087   compared to the end-to-end deep learning methods, they significantly enhance the robustness of pre-
088   dictions and demonstrate remarkable potentials (Chen et al., 2018).
089
090   1.2   TAKING T OPOLOGY OF E ARTH ’ S S URFACE INTO C ONSIDERATION
091
092   The historical observations used during training most deep learning-based weather prediction mod-
093   els are on planar latitude-longitude grids, other than on the spherical surface of the Earth. Neverthe-
094   less, neglecting the Earth’s topology introduces remarkable distortions, as shown in Figure 2 (Mai
095
      et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2018). For example, the points that are close to the poles turn to be denser
      on the spherical manifold than on the planar latitude-longitude grid. A notable consequence is that
096
      one weather pattern appears differently on the sphere and the plane, such that capturing the weather
097
      pattern on the plan suffers from distortions. These distortions also affect the patches and convolution
098   kernels, negatively impacting the deep learning models based on convolutional neural networks or
099   vision transformers (Coors et al., 2018). In addition, the velocity fields defined on the planar are
100   significantly distorted when increasing the latitude towards the poles, which will bring biases to the
101   deep learning models that learn the velocity fields (Verma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023).

151   Numerical weather prediction (NWP). NWP is a fundamental physics-based method for weather
152   prediction (Scher, 2018), utilizing the underlying differential equations to predict how the weather
153   will evolve over the time. For example, the operational Integrated Forecast System (IFS) consists
154   of several NWP models with different spatial resolutions (Bouallègue et al., 2024). Despite of its
155   widespread applications, modeling and calculating the complex Earth-atmosphere interaction are
156   challenging. In addition, solving the differential equations is sensitive to the initial conditions, and
157   also computationally demanding (Kochkov et al., 2024).
158   Deep learning-based weather prediction. Deep learning models learn from the historical observa-
159   tions so as to predict the weather. Although time-consuming during training, deep learning models
160   are rapid during prediction since they do not involve solving the differential equations. State-of-the-
161   art deep learning-based weather prediction models include FourCastNet (Kurth et al., 2023), Pangu
      (Bi et al., 2023), GraphCast (Lam et al., 2023), ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023), Fengwu (Chen et al.,

200   3   M ETHODS
201
202   Considering the attributions of the weather evolution illustrated in Figure 1, we accordingly build
203   a physics-assisted and topology-informed deep learning model for weather prediction, abbreviated
204   as PASSAT. Given any initial time, PASSAT: (i) generates the initial velocity fields of the weather
205   variables with the velocity branch of a spherical graph neural network; and then autoregressively (ii)
206
      predicts the effects of the Earth-atmosphere interaction with the interaction branch of the spherical
      graph neural network; (ii) numerically solves the advection equation on the spherical manifold; (iv)
207
      numerically updates the velocity fields through solving the Navier-Stokes equation on the spherical
208
      manifold, aided by the initial velocity fields provided by (i). In the following, we will discuss how
209   PASSAT captures the evolution of the weather variables and their velocity fields, via integrating the
210   two differential equations and the spherical graph neural network (see also Figure 3).
211
212
      We disregard the impact of vertical actions and focus on analyzing the advection equation and the
      Navier-Stokes equation on the spherical manifold. All the analyses and approaches presented below
213
      can be readily extended to scenarios where the vertical actions are taken into account.

      3.1   S PHERICAL M ANIFOLD
220
221   The historical observations used during training most deep learning-based weather prediction mod-
222   els are on planar latitude-longitude grids, other than on the spherical surface of the Earth. Ignoring
223   this topology information leads to remarkable distortions in both the neural networks and the differ-
224   ential equations. In order to avoid such distortions, we project the weather variables from a planar
225   latitude-longitude grid onto the Earth’s surface. Without loss of generality, we assume the Earth’s
226   surface to be an ideal unit sphere, with a radius of 1 unit length (6371km).
227   We denote the unit sphere S = {s ∈ R3 | ||s||2 = 1} as the Earth’s surface. Any spatial coordinate
228   s on the unit sphere corresponds to a point (ϕ, θ) within the planar latitude-longitude grid, where θ
229   is the latitude and ϕ is the longitude. Thus, we use s and s(ϕ, θ) interchangeably. Given any spatial
230   coordinate s, eϕ (s) ∈ R3 and eθ (s) ∈ R3 are two orthogonal unit vectors originated from s and
231   along the parallel and meridian directions, respectively. We denote ∇s as the spatial gradient on the
232   unit sphere and · as the inner product.

      As shown in Table 1, PASSAT and GraphCast outperform the other models, demonstrating the supe-
478
      riority of incorporating the topology information. Below, we conduct ablation studies to assess the
479
      effectiveness of the physical information used inside PASSAT. First, we assess the effectiveness of
480   the Navier-Stokes equation. To do so, we retrain PASSAT (without the Navier-Stokes equation) by:
481   (i) keeping to numerically solve the advection equation; (ii) keeping to use a spherical graph neural
482   network to learn the earth-atmosphere interaction; (iii) using the velocity branch of the spherical
483   graph neural network, other than the Navier-Stokes equation, to generate the velocity fields for all
484   times. This approach is similar to NowcastNet. Second, we further assess the effectiveness of both
485   the Navier-Stokes equation and the advection equation. To do so, we retrain PASSAT (without the
      Navier-Stokes equation and the advection equation) by using a spherical graph neural network to

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      predict the weather in an end-to-end manner, without numerically solving either the Navier-Stokes
487   equation or the advection equation. This approach is similar to GraphCast.
488
489   The results are depicted in Table 2. For medium-term prediction, updating the velocity fields by the
490
      Naiver-Stokes equation incorporates the physical constraints, and is hence better than updating the
      velocity fields from the velocity branch of the spherical graph neural network. Besides, it is able to
491
      significantly reduce the memory consumption. Further removing the advection equation essentially
492
      affects the prediction ability of PASSAT, showing that treating the entire advection process (includ-
493   ing the advection equation and the Earth-atmosphere interaction) as a black box is inappropriate. In
494   comparison, PASSAT only treats the Earth-atmosphere interaction as a black box.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
498
      In this paper, we propose PASSAT, a novel physics-assisted and topology-informed deep learning
499
      model for weather prediction. PASSAT seamlessly integrates the advection equation and the Navier-
500
      Stokes equation that govern the evolution of the weather variables and their velocity fields, with a
501   graph neural network that estimates the complex Earth-atmosphere interaction and the initial veloc-
502   ity fields. PASSAT also takes the topology of the Earth’s surface into consideration, during solving
503   the equations and training the graph neural network. In the 5.625◦ -resolution ERA5 data set, PAS-
504   SAT outperforms both the state-of-the-art deep learning-based weather prediction models and the
505   operational numerical weather prediction model IFS T42.
506
      As future works, we will extend PASSAT in the following aspects: (i) We will enhance PASSAT by
507   incorporating more weather variables. (ii) We will refine PASSAT via training over a data set with a
508   finer resolution. (iii) We will incorporate new forward integration method that is more efficient than
509   the Euler’s method, during training and prediction. We expect that PASSAT is able to motivate more
510   research efforts in combining physics, topology and historical observations for weather prediction.

909   D.2     T RAINING S TRATEGIES
910
911   We train PASSAT and the baseline deep learning models from scratch in an autoregressive manner
912   (except for ClimaX); that is, we treat the current predictions as observations and feed them back to
913   the models to generate future predictions.
914   The training of all models follows a two-phase approach: pre-training and fine-tuning (Lam et al.,
915   2023). In the pre-training phase, we train each model to predict the weather variables for the future
916   6 hours and the future 12 hours, focusing on the short-term prediction capability. In the fine-tuning
917   phase, we gradually increase the lead time from 18 hours to 72 hours, for the sake of strengthening
      the medium-term prediction capability.


                                                             17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      We use the AdamW optimizer with parameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999, and set the weight decay
919   as 0.05. Gradient clipping is also employed, with a maximum norm value of 5. We use PyTorch for
920   training, validation and prediction, on four GeForece RTX 2080.
921
922
      In the pre-training phase, the batch size is 8 per GPU (32 in total). In the fine-tuning phase, the batch
      size is 2 per GPU (8 in total) to prevent memory overflow. In the pre-training phase, we adjust the
923
      learning rate with the Cosine-LR-Scheduler, and set the maximum and minimum learning rates to
924
      1e-3 and 3e-7, respectively. In the fine-tuning phase, the learning rate is 7.5e-8.
