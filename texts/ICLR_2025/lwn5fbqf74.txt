                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Dramatic advances in the quality of the latent diffusion models (LDMs) also led
012            to the malicious use of AI-generated images. While current AI-generated image
013            detection methods assume the availability of real/AI-generated images for training,
014            this is practically limited given the vast expressibility of LDMs. This motivates
015            the training-free detection setup where no related data are available in advance. In
016            this paper, we propose that the level of aliasing detected in reconstructed images
017            produced by the autoencoder of LDMs can serve as a criterion for distinguishing
018            between real and AI-generated images. Specifically, we propose a novel detection
019            score function, termed high-frequency influence (HFI), which quantifies the impact
020
               of the spatial filtering-based high-frequency components of the input image on the
               perceptual reconstruction distance. HFI is training-free, efficient, and consistently
021
               outperforms other training-free methods in detecting challenging images generated
022
               by various generative models. We also show that HFI can successfully detect
023            the images generated from the specified LDM. HFI outperforms the best baseline
024            method while achieving magnitudes of speedup.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   With the rapid advancement of generative AI, we are now able to generate photorealistic images in a
030   desired context within seconds. The crucial factor in this achievement is the emergence of foundational
031   Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (MidJourney, 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). LDMs represent the
032   integration of recent breakthroughs of vision models, including the powerful generation quality of
033
      diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021), the joint text-image representation learned
      by vision-language pre-trained models (Radford et al., 2021), and the improved inference speed
034
      through compression in the latent space (Kingma & Welling, 2014). However, with the progression
035
      of such models, we should also consider the potential negative impacts such generated images may
036   have on society. For example, LDMs can produce fake images that could cause societal confusion
037   (Yim, 2024) or infringe upon intellectual property rights (Brittain, 2024). Moreover, applying AI-
038   generated images on training generative models may deteriorate model performance (Alemohammad
039   et al., 2024). Therefore, AI-generated image detection or deepfake detection methods, which aim to
040   distinguish AI-generated images from real images, are gaining significant attention.
041
      However, most AI-generated image detection methods adhere to traditional settings that are discon-
042   nected from real-world challenges. As shown in Figure 1, these methods follow a training-based setup.
043   Namely, they are designed to train on data sampled from a given real image distribution with similar
044   AI-generated data, and they are tested on the data sampled from the same real image distribution
045   against other AI-generated data. While such a setup was effective when generative models were
046   typically designed to fit a single specific dataset, it faces practical limitations when we aim to detect
047   LDM-generated data in general, which are trained on billions of images (Schuhmann et al., 2022).
048   Moreover, such LDMs can generate hallucinated images that may not have been encountered in
049   reality (The Guardian, 2024), which induces extra effort to acquire matching real images.
050   We explore a training-free AI-generated image detection framework that assumes no access to training
051   images as an alternative to traditional setups. A few methods in this domain (Ricker et al., 2024;
052   He et al., 2024) aim to design a universal score function that distinguishes AI-generated images
053   from real ones by leveraging representations from large-scale pre-trained models. For example,
      Ricker et al. (2024) and He et al. (2024) leverage the representation of the autoencoder of LDM and

132   2.2   L ATENT DIFFUSION MODELS
133
134   LDMs (Rombach et al., 2022) efficiently generate high-dimensional images by modeling the diffusion
                                         ′    ′   ′
135   process on the latent space Z ⊂ RC ×H ×W instead of the data space X ⊂ RC×H×W with C = 3.
136   Hence, LDMs first pre-train the encoder E : X → Z and the decoder D : Z → X . This paper will
137
      refer to LDM as a comprehensive framework for autoencoder-based text-to-image generative models.
138   While the autoencoder of the LDM is defined as VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2014), weight on the
139   KL-regularization loss during training is negligible, resulting in negligible variance in the latent space
140   (Rombach et al., 2022). Hence, we view the encoder and the decoder as the deterministic mapping
141   between X and Z throughout the paper. We set AE(x) := D ◦ E(x) for the rest of the paper.
142
143   2.3   ATTRIBUTION OF LDM- GENERATED IMAGES .
144
145   A recent line of works (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024) aims to detect data generated from the given LDM
146
      model M as a means of alternative to explicit watermarking (Fernandez et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023).
      In specific, for a given LDM model M1 , existing methods design an uncertainty score function that
147
      distinguishes the belonging data generated from M1 and the non-belonging data generated from the
148
      other generative model M2 . We denote such task as ”M1 vs M2 ” model attribution task.
149
150
151   3     M ETHOD
152
153   Our motivation is grounded in the phenomenon of aliasing (Gonzalez & Woods, 2006), which occurs
154   when an original signal contains high-frequency components exceeding the subsampling rate, leading
155   to distortions in the downsampled signal and subsequently affecting its upsampled reconstruction.
156   Recent studies (Agnihotri et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b) have also explored aliasing within pixel-
157   wise prediction tasks, where images are typically downsampled and upsampled through convolutional
158
      layers, analogous to the functioning of the autoencoder in LDMs. In this work, we propose that the
      extent of aliasing present in reconstructed images can serve as a reliable metric for distinguishing
159
      between real and AI-generated images.
160
161   We show our motivation with the reconstruction of the real image. Figure 2a and 2b show the sample
      of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and its reconstruction through the SDv1.1 autoencoder, respectively.

177   The newly suggested score function is designed to capture infinitesimal distortions of reconstructed
178   images sampled from the test dataset through the lens of autoencoders (i.e., AE(x)) pre-trained
179   on the train dataset ν (e.g., LAION-aesthetics (Schuhmann et al., 2022)). By taking a directional
180   derivative in the direction of x − F(x), the score function amplifies the difference (i.e., distribution
181   shift) between the train and test dataset in high-frequency information. As conventional encoders E
182
      inevitably produce compression errors in the high-frequency domain, we argue that the geometric
      direction to x − F(x), that estimates the difference of high-frequency information, is an optimal
183
      choice to reveal the failure behavior of autoencoders.

185   On the other hand, on text-to-image generative models that are trained on large-scale data distribution
186   similar to ν, we expect the HFI score to be lower on their generated data. Furthermore, our design of
187   Eq 1 is also motivated by architectural similarities of generative models. Generative models employ
188
      upsampling layers composed of a series of convolution kernels (Rombach et al., 2022; Dhariwal &
      Nichol, 2021). We anticipate that our spatial term, x − F(x), will capture these local correlations,
189
      resulting in lower HFI scores.

238   In this section, we evaluate the efficacy of our proposed HFI in detecting images generated by text-to-

242   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUPS
243
244   Evaluation protocols. Our main experiments are divided into two scenarios. We first report the
245   performance between the autoencoder-based methods in the cross-autoencoder setup. To be specific,
246   we compare HFI against the autoencoder-based baseline, AEROBLADE (Ricker et al., 2024), under
247   various autoencoder choices. We use the autoencoder of the Stable diffusion (SD)v1.4/v2-base
248
      (Rombach et al., 2022), Kandinsky2.1 (Razzhigaev et al., 2023), and MiniSD-diffusers (Labs, 2022)
      throughout the experiment. Then, we compute the ensemble performance of HFI and AEROBLADE
249
      and report their performance against the other AI-generated image detection methods.
250
251   Metric. We mainly report the area of the region under the Precision-Recall curve (AUPR) and the
252   area of the region under the ROC curve (AUROC). We additionally report the average rank across the
253   dataset in the cross-autoencoder setup.
254   Datasets. We test the efficacy of HFI applied in various data domains. For the natural image domain,
255   we experiment on the GenImage (Zhu et al., 2023) benchmark where the real image is from the
256   ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). GenImage constitutes data generated by 8 generative models that
257   are not limited to LDMs: ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), BigGAN (Brock et al., 2018), GLIDE
258   (Nichol et al., 2022), Midjourney (MidJourney, 2022), SD v1.4/1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022), VQDM
259   (Gu et al., 2022b), and Wukong (Gu et al., 2022a).
260   We also evaluate HFI on the DiffusionFace (Chen et al., 2024c) benchmark where the real image
261   is from the Multi-Modal-CelebA-HQ (Xia et al., 2021) dataset. The benchmark contains 4 LDM-
262   generated categories: images generated from the noise via SDv1.5/2.1 models (SD1.5 T2I, SD2.1
263   T2I) and images generated from image-to-image translation starting from real images via SDv1.5/2.1
264   models (SDv1.5 I2I, SDv2.1 I2I). Notably, SDv1.5 I2I and SDv2.1 I2I correspond to SDEedit (Meng
265   et al., 2021) where styles of the real image are still present in the generated image.

      Leveraging frequency maps as a key representation has also been a major practice of training-based

      contain noticeable frequency artifacts. Li et al. (2021) fuse the spatial and frequency representation
459   and adopt a single-center loss for pulling out manipulated face representation. Wang et al. (2023a)
460   introduce a relation module that connects multiple domains. Tan et al. (2024b) incorporate the FFT
461   module inside the classifier. Although these methods operate in the frequency domain, our findings
462   indicate that spatial low-pass filtering may provide a more effective mapping.
463
      Training-free AI-generated image detection. Since no training data is available, training-free
464
      AI-generated image detection methods utilize a pre-trained model to design the uncertainty function.
465   Ma et al. (2023) utilize the error of deterministic forward-reverse diffusion process to detect diffusion-
466   generated images. Ricker et al. (2024) utilize the perceptual distance between the input and its
467   reconstruction through the autoencoder of LDMs to detect LDM-generated images. He et al. (2024)
468   utilizes the cosine similarity on the feature of Dinov2 (Oquab et al., 2024) between the input data and
469   its perturbation. The perturbation is constructed by injecting Gaussian Noise into the input.
470
      Aliasing in image domain. Zhang (2019) discusses aliasing in the classification task that aliasing
471   occurs while in subsampling and design a blurring-based pooling operation for the remedy. Vascon-
472   celos et al. (2021) propose the variant by positioning the blurring operation before the non-linearities.
473   Karras et al. (2021) propose a custom upsampling layer to deal with aliasing while training generative
474   adversarial networks. Agnihotri et al. (2024) also modify the operation in the upsampling layer in
475   the pixel-wise prediction task. However, to our knowledge, most of the existing works focus on the
476   training phase.

479   7      C ONCLUSION
480
481   This paper proposes HFI, a novel, training-free AI-generated image detection method based on the
482   autoencoder of LDM. HFI measures the influence of the high-frequency spatial component on the
483   distortion of the reconstructed image through the autoencoder. HFI can effectively distinguish 3 types
484   of distributions: images generated from the given autoencoder, other AI-generated images, and real
485   images. Hence, HFI achieves the state-of-the-art in both training-free AI-generated image detection
      and generated image detection of the specified model.
