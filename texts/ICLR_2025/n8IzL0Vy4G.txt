009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Language models are known to encode a great amount of factual knowledge
012            through pretraining. However, such knowledge might be insufficient to cater to
013            user requests, requiring the model to integrate external knowledge sources and
014            adhere to user-provided specifications. When answering questions about ongoing
015            events, the model should use recent news articles to update its response; when
016
               asked to provide recommendations, the model should prioritize user specifica-
               tions over retrieved product reviews; when some facts are edited in the model,
017
               the updated facts should override all prior knowledge learned by the model even
018
               if they are conflicting. In all of the cases above, the model faces a decision be-
019            tween its own parametric knowledge, (retrieved) contextual knowledge, and user
020            instruction knowledge. In this paper, we (1) unify such settings into the problem
021            of knowledge preference and define a three-level preference hierarchy over these
022            knowledge sources; (2) compile a collection of existing datasets IfQA, MQuAKE,
023            and MRQA covering a combination of settings (with/without user specifications,
024            with/without context documents) to systematically evaluate how well models obey
025            the intended knowledge preference; and (3) propose a dataset synthesis method
026            that composes diverse question-answer pairs with user assumptions and related
027
               context to directly fine-tune LMs for instilling the hierarchy of knowledge. We
               demonstrate that a 7B model, fine-tuned on only a few thousand examples auto-
028
               matically generated by our proposed method, effectively achieves superior perfor-
029
               mance (more than 18% improvement across all evaluation benchmarks) in adher-
030            ing to the desired knowledge preference hierarchy.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Language models memorize factual knowledge during pretraining, which allows them to perform
036   open-domain question answering with remarkable accuracy. However, the knowledge encoded
037   within the model (parametric knowledge) might be erroneous or incomplete, falling short of users’
038   expectations. Some applications require the language model to leverage the most recent knowledge,
039
      such as the latest election results, or stock prices. This is typically set up as closed-domain QA or
      retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) where the newer knowledge is presented as extra context to
040
      the language model. While much effort has been spent on improving retrieval and ranking results, it
041
      would be futile if the model simply disregards the input and sticks to its own “prior beliefs” (Long-
042   pre et al., 2021; Yu & Ji, 2023). Even if the model only occasionally appears obstinate, this will
043   largely undermine user trust as now users would need to fact-check every claim against the provided
044   context. In these applications, it is critical to ensure that contextual knowledge is preferred over
045   the models’ parametric knowledge. Another type of application including personalized search and
046   recommendation requires the integration of user preferences. User preferences should always be
047   respected over model parametric knowledge and contextual knowledge. Model editing (Meng et al.,
048   2022a;b; De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023) can be seen as a special case
049   of such preferences, where the new facts override learned facts even if they are counterfactual in na-
050
      ture. In all of these settings (RAG, closed-domain QA, integrating user beliefs and model editing),
      we observe that the key is to enforce a certain priority among knowledge from different sources.
051
052   The strife between parametric knowledge and contextual knowledge has been measured across many
053   models and forms of contexts (Longpre et al., 2021; Neeman et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Xie et al.,
      2024; Kortukov et al., 2024). While earlier models (T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), Roberta (Liu et al.,

055   2021), recent larger models (OPT (Zhang et al., 2022), GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020)) show potential
056   in successfully updating their answers through in-context edits (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhong et al.,

      2    F ORMULATION OF K NOWLEDGE P REFERENCE
117
118
119   When the parametric knowledge (intrinsic knowledge) (Petroni et al., 2019; Mallen et al., 2022)
120   of an LLM is insufficient to give the correct answer to user queries, we can introduce external
121   knowledge either in the instruction or as additional context.
122   Instruction Knowledge is the knowledge injected through user instructions. Instruction knowledge
123   can refer to rules or principles that govern how the model should utilize other types of knowledge, i.e.
124   problem-solving constraints from user instructions and assumptions from hypothetical questions.
125
      Context Knowledge is the potentially noisy context provided to the LLM during inference time.
126
      One typical case is the retrieved passages in retrieval-augmented generation. The retrieved passages
127   can provide newly-updated knowledge or domain-specific knowledge which is generally expected
128   to override or complement LLMs’ own knowledge in RAG.
129
130
      We take the RAG case in Fig. 1 as an example where the user queries the LLM with a question
      (ignore the question assumption first). Resolving the question requires solving a model preference
131
      problem where we want the LLM to prioritize relevant knowledge in the retrieved context over
132
      knowledge embedded in the LLM’s parameters. Sometimes, users will give their own constraints or
133   requirements for answering the query (e.g., the question assumption in Fig. 1). Correspondingly, to
134   fulfill the user requirements, the LLM should override the original way it utilizes the knowledge, by
135   following a new reasoning flow and utilizing different pieces of context knowledge and parametric
136   knowledge. Then, the RAG case in Fig. 1 is fundamentally a knowledge preference problem where
137   we further give the instruction knowledge the highest priority in the inference process. More gener-
138   ally, in this work, we define Hierarchical Knowledge Preference built on these types of knowledge.
139   Hierarchical Knowledge Preference. In applications of LLMs, conflicts between instruction
140   knowledge, context knowledge, and parametric knowledge are frequently inevitable. For instance,
141   a user may provide counterfactual hypothesis or unprecedented constraints which may conflict with
142   the retrieved documents or the LLMs’ own knowledge (Yu et al., 2023). Meanwhile, the retrieved
143   documents serving as the context knowledge may bring facts which disagree with LLMs’ outdated
144   or wrong memory (Vu et al., 2023). Ignorance or inappropriate handling of these knowledge con-
145   flicts can result in nondeterministic inference behaviors of LLMs, thus undermining downstream
146   LLM-based applications. We define our hierarchy of ideal knowledge preference as follows:
147   (i) Instruction Knowledge ≻ Context Knowledge. The knowledge from the instruction should be ac-
148   corded the highest priority so that LLMs can orient all of the reasoning power or acquired knowledge
149   toward fulfilling the system-level or user-level requirements.
150
      (ii) Context Knowledge ≻ Parametric Knowledge. As the parametric knowledge is mainly acquired
151   in the pre-training stage which restricts the parametric knowledge itself to be timely corrected, up-
152   dated, or expanded, we assume the retrieved or given context knowledge should be generally pre-
153   ferred at the time of inference.1 Note that our knowledge preference is defined for the scenarios
154   where direct knowledge conflicts arise. This means that the information irrelevant to solving the
155   target problem or answering the target query should be regarded as noise and it does not contribute
156   to any knowledge conflicts.
157
158
159      1
           In the knowledge conflict scenarios where the context or the retrieved contents are flawed (e.g., mislead-
160   ing or not completely accurate), models’ own parametric knowledge could be more reliable. In this work,
161   we assume the retrieved contents are generally helpful and should be prioritized over parametric knowledge.
      Otherwise there is no such need for RAG in such scenarios. We leave this for future work.

170   3.1   E VALUATING P REFERENCE FOR I NSTRUCTION K NOWLEDGE
171
172   To evaluate LLMs’ preference for instruction knowledge, we focus on the case where counterfactual
173   assumptions are introduced by the instruction, which is a typical scenario calling for the preference
174
      for instruction knowledge and it’s more likely to introduce explicit and direct knowledge conflicts
      between the instruction knowledge and other types of knowledge.
175
176   Among existing works, IfQA (Yu et al., 2023) is a human annotated counterfactual QA benchmark
177   where the question introduces hypothetical conditions. We adopt the test set of its full split which
178   has 700 instances in total for evaluating the priority of instruction knowledge in retrieval-augmented
179   setting. We utilize two setups for retrieval augmented setting: (i) w/ Gold Passages where the
180
      oracle context following the question is given, and (ii) w/ Mixed Passages where the top-3
      retrieved passages from Wikipedia dump along with the oracle contexts and the question is given to
181
      be more realistic. The F1 and Exact Matching (EM) scores are reported.
182
183   However, the knowledge conflicts introduced by IfQA may not be explicit and significant enough.
184   For example, in the question If sea levels had risen significantly over the past decade, which country
185   would have been the first to be submerged?, the instruction knowledge sea levels had risen signifi-
186
      cantly over the past decade does not directly conflict with the oracle context passage which is about
      the world’s lowest-lying country.
187
188   Therefore, we further extend a knowledge editing benchmark MQuAKE-CF-3k (Zhong et al., 2023)
189   to be InstructMH-3k to evaluate the preference between instruction knowledge and context knowl-
190   edge. MQuAKE-CF-3k contains multi-hop QA instances based on human-filtered relations, entities,
191   and crafted templates for verbalizing relation triples, but without context passages. Each relation
192
      triple is guaranteed to be recallable by GPT-J (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021). Each multi-hop QA
      instance is associated with a fact chain (sequentially linked relation triples), and knowledge edits.
193
      So we integrate the knowledge edits with the original question to obtain a counterfactual multi-hop
194
      question (see the question in Fig. 7 for an example). For each factual relation triple needed to get
195   to both the original answer before fact chain editing and the new answer after fact chain editing,
196   we adopt GPT-3.5 to synthesize one supporting context passage which will be given along with the
197   question to the testee LLMs. We evaluate the F1 and EM scores according to both the original answer
198   and the new answer. If testee LLMs well prioritize the instruction knowledge and generally prefer
199   context knowledge than parametric knowledge, they should follow the counterfactual instruction as-
200   sumptions, focus on the suitable passages in the context, and reach the new answer instead of the
201   original answer, leading to higher evaluation scores with new answers than with original answers.
202
203   3.2   E VALUATING P REFERENCE FOR C ONTEXT K NOWLEDGE
204
205   To evaluate LLMs’ preference for context knowledge, we adopt the test set of MRQA (Fisch et al.,
206   2019), covering BioASQ (Tsatsaronis et al., 2015), DROP (Dua et al., 2019), DuoRC (Saha et al.,
207   2018), RACE (Lai et al., 2017), RelationExtraction (Levy et al., 2017), and TextbookQA (Kembhavi
208   et al., 2017) across various domains. We divide the evaluation into two parts. The first part is the
209   evaluation on the open-book QA on the whole test set, denoted as MRQA. This quantifies the general
210   capability of testee LLM to comprehend and prioritize the context knowledge regardless of whether
211   the context knowledge conflicts with their parametric knowledge or not. F1 and EM are reported.
212   The second part of the evaluation (denoted as CounterMemoryMRQA) is conducted on the subset
213   of the test set where LLMs’ parametric knowledge is conflicted with the context knowledge. So we
214   first probe the parametric knowledge of each testee LLM with 3-shot exemplars (taken from MRQA
215   dev set) to obtain the target test subset. Then, we measure the proportion of test instances for which
      the model correctly updates its answer (denoted as P(Uc )) and the proportion of test instances for

      which model incorrectly updates its answer (denoted as P(Ui )).2 If a testee LLM well prioritizes
217   the context knowledge, P(Uc ) should be significantly higher than P(Ui ).

244   In terms of the instance contents, in contrast to synthesis-based approaches which rely on LLMs to
245   synthesize the entire input and output of each instance, our goal is to provide maximal control on
246   the synthesized contents while ensuring the expected quality. In terms of the data format, we mainly
247   focus on the single-hop and the multi-hop question answering data given reference passages which
248   is related to broad downstream applications of LLMs, especially in the retrieval-augmented setting.
249   First, we gather a corpus of Wikipedia passage chunks as oracle contexts for subsequent single-
250   hop QA data synthesis. To enhance the efficiency of the corpus to serve for fact-related QA data
251   synthesis, we trace back to the Wikipedia passages that contain evidence for verifiable instances

253   entities are fewer than 5. This results in a corpus of high-quality Wikipedia passages denoted as C.

      4.2   M ODELING P REFERENCE FOR I NSTRUCTION K NOWLEDGE
305
306   To synthesize instruction tuning data which grants the highest preference priority for instruction
307   knowledge, we resort to counterfactual question answering. The counterfactual assumptions or hy-
308   potheses set up the instruction knowledge which will directly conflict with the parts of the factual
309   “retrieved passages” and likely deviates from the LLMs’ parametric knowledge. Such synthesized
310   data can guide LLMs to prioritize the instruction knowledge, overriding conflicted parts of the con-
311   text knowledge and potentially the parametric knowledge, to reach the correct answer.
312   Specifically, for each randomly sampled passage di , we prompt GPT-4o based on di to synthesize
313   an single-hop QA instance containing: (1) The counterfactual question which introduces counter-
314   factual and hypothetical conditions or incidents. (2) The precise, concise, no-trivial, and uniquely-
315   derivable answer through counterfactual reasoning based on di , the hypothetical question, and com-
316   mon sense4 . (3) Extra information as an additional passage to make sure the answer is uniquely

      4.3   M ODELING P REFERENCE FOR C ONTEXT K NOWLEDGE
343
344   The goal of modeling the preference for context knowledge is to teach LLMs to prefer the “retrieved
345   contexts” over their own parametric knowledge. Sticking to the data format of single-hop and multi-
346   hop QA with reference passages, we achieve this goal by synthesizing factual QA instances with
347   answers supported by the passages but opposed by the LLMs’ parametric knowledge.
348   For single-hop QA instances, we prompt GPT-4o with passage di to synthesize the factual question,
349   the corresponding answer, the step-by-step answer derivation, and an additional passage to further
350   make sure the answer is uniquely derivable from the contexts. For multi-hop QA instances, we
351   leverage the unedited fact chain li and prompt GPT-4o to synthesize the multi-hop question, a list of
352   passages verbalized from relation triples of li to ensure the tail entity of li is uniquely derivable, and
353   the step-by-step answer derivation. One special design is that, we will first probe a list of base LLMs
354   with the synthesized question to filter questions that can be correctly answered by the base LLMs’
355   parametric knowledge. This step is done before further synthesizing the remaining components of
356
      the new instance for efficiency. Please refer to Appendix A.4 for prompt templates used here.

      7     R ELATED W ORK
504
      Knowledge Conflicts. Previous related studies have focused on the preference of language models
505
      between external context knowledge and the internal parametric knowledge (Longpre et al., 2021;
506
      Xie et al., 2024; Kortukov et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). Xie et al. finds that LLMs generally
507   prefer evidence consistent with their parametric knowledge over the conflicting evidence (2024).
508   Another finding is that LLMs demonstrate strong confirmation bias when external evidence con-
509   tains consistent information with parametric knowledge which is also supported by a more recent
510   study (Kortukov et al., 2024). On the other hand, external evidences that are coherent, convincing,
511   though conflicting with parametric knowledge can still make LLMs highly receptive to them (Xie
512   et al., 2024; Kortukov et al., 2024). Different from them, we further refine knowledge conflicts into
513   instruction knowledge, context knowledge, and parametric knowledge for study and we resort to
514   regularizing LLMs’ behaviors under different knowledge conflicts.
515   Improving LLMs Under Conflicts. Existing works have investigated how to regularize the behav-
516   iors of LLMs in conflicts. One typical scenario is to edit new knowledge into LLM artifacts to inject
517   external knowledge to override the parametric knowledge. Corresponding methods include revis-
518   ing the LLM weights, applying adaptor networks, and integrating explicit memories (Meng et al.,
519   2022a;b; De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023). Our work introduces the
520   instruction knowledge to integrate the goal of this research direction with a more complex scenario
521
      where external contexts cause extra knowledge conflicts. Furthermore our work resort to instruction
      tuning to enable such knowledge injections against knowledge conflicts inherently in inference time.
522
523   Recent works have explored improving the safety of LLMs against jailbreak attacks inside instruc-
524   tions. OpenAI has introduced instruction hierarchy (Wallace et al., 2024) to teach LLMs to ignore
525   jailbreak instructions. In contrast, our work focuses more on knowledge conflicts and building pref-
526   erence hierarchy between the instruction as a whole, the context passages, and LLMs’ parameters.
527
528   8     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we unify different settings where LLMs should integrate external knowledge (e.g., user
531   specifications, retrieved passages, and updated knowledge) with their internal knowledge by intro-
532   ducing instruction knowledge, context knowledge, and parametric knowledge. We further defined a
533   knowledge preference hierarchy over three types of knowledge as a blueprint to achieve this unified
534   target. For systematic evaluation on the LLMs’ knowledge preference, we compiled a collection of
535   existing benchmarks covering different preference settings. To teach LLMs to inherently follow this
536
      knowledge preference hierarchy, we synthesized various instruction tuning data (H IER P REF) with
      source data from Wikipedia and Wikidata. Comprehensive evaluation and analysis show the supe-
537
      rior performance of H IER P REF over vanilla instruction tuning in terms of following the knowledge
538
      preference hierarchy. As future work, the question of how many samples will be enough for LLMs
539   to achieve perfect knowledge preference can be further investigated.

       First of all, prioritizing the instruction knowledge or the knowledge provided by users leads to a
       fine-tuned LLMs well following the human instructions or human provided knowledge. Similar to
1342
       related instruction tuning works, this may raise safety concerns since user instruction can also con-

1344   focus of this work, we leave this for research works on LLM safety. Potential solutions include
1345   further refining the instruction knowledge into system level instruction knowledge (more prioritized
1346   constraints or knowledge handled by LLM providers and customers can not modify them in applica-
1347   tions) and user level instruction knowledge so that safety issues can be addressed. Another potential
1348   solution is to add a safety guard. Second, our prompting format of synthesized QA instances for in-
1349   struction tuning can be more diverse as we currently mainly use Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023)’s prompt
       template and surrounds different instance components with fixed tags. To achieve our goal of this
