009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces F5-TTS, a fully non-autoregressive text-to-speech system
012            based on flow matching with Diffusion Transformer (DiT). Without requiring
013            complex designs such as duration model, text encoder, and phoneme alignment,
014            the text input is simply padded with filler tokens to the same length as input
015            speech, and then the denoising is performed for speech generation, which was
016            originally proved feasible by E2 TTS. However, the original design of E2 TTS
017            makes it hard to follow due to its slow convergence and low robustness. To address
018
               these issues, we first model the input with ConvNeXt to refine the text representa-
               tion, making it easy to align with the speech. We further propose an inference-time
019
               Sway Sampling strategy, which significantly improves our model’s performance
020
               and efficiency. This sampling strategy for flow step can be easily applied to ex-
021            isting flow matching based models without retraining. Our design allows faster
022            training and achieves an inference RTF of 0.15, which is greatly improved com-
023            pared to state-of-the-art diffusion-based TTS models. Trained on a public 100K
024            hours multilingual dataset, our Fairytaler Fakes Fluent and Faithful speech with
025            Flow matching (F5-TTS) exhibits highly natural and expressive zero-shot ability,
026            seamless code-switching capability, and speed control efficiency. Demo samples
027            can be found at https://F5-TTS.github.io. We will release all code and
028            checkpoints to promote community development.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent research in Text-to-Speech (TTS) has experienced great advancement (Shen et al., 2018; Li
      et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2020; 2021; Popov et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Tan et al.,
034
      2024). With a few seconds of audio prompt, current TTS models are able to synthesize speech for
035
      any given text and mimic the speaker of audio prompt (Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b). The
036   synthesized speech can achieve high fidelity and naturalness that they are almost indistinguishable
037   from human speech (Shen et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Le et al., 2024).
038
039
      While autoregressive (AR) based TTS models exhibit an intuitive way of consecutively predict-
      ing the next token(s) and have achieved promising zero-shot TTS capability, the inherent limita-
040
      tions of AR modeling require extra efforts addressing issues such as inference latency and exposure
041
      bias (Song et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024a; Han et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024).
042   Moreover, the quality of speech tokenizer is essential for AR models to achieve high-fidelity syn-
043   thesis (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
044   2023a; Bai et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2024). Thus, there have been studies exploring direct modeling
045   in continuous space (Liu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2024) to enhance synthesized
046   speech quality recently.
047
      Although AR models demonstrate impressive zero-shot performance as they perform implicit du-
048   ration modeling and can leverage diverse sampling strategies, non-autoregressive (NAR) models
049   benefit from fast inference through parallel processing, and effectively balance synthesis quality and
050   latency. Notably, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) contribute most to the success
051   of current NAR speech models (Shen et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2024). In particular, Flow Matching with
052   Optimal Transport path (FM-OT) (Lipman et al., 2022) is widely used in recent research fields not
053   only text-to-speech (Le et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024b; Mehta et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Eskimez
      et al., 2024) but also image generation (Esser et al., 2024) and music generation (Fei et al., 2024).

083
084   Unlike AR-based models, the alignment modeling between input text and synthesized speech is
085   crucial and challenging for NAR-based models. While NaturalSpeech 3 (Ju et al., 2024) and Voice-
086   box (Le et al., 2024) use frame-wise phoneme alignment; Matcha-TTS (Mehta et al., 2024) adopts
087
      monotonic alignment search (Kim et al., 2020) and relies on a phoneme-level duration model; recent
      works find that introducing such rigid alignment between text and speech hinders the model from
088
      generating results with higher naturalness (Eskimez et al., 2024; Anastassiou et al., 2024).
089
090   E3 TTS (Gao et al., 2023a) abandons phoneme-level duration and applies cross-attention on the input
091   sequence but yields limited audio quality. DiTTo-TTS (Lee et al., 2024) uses Diffusion Transformer
092   (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) with cross-attention conditioned on encoded text from a pretrained
093
      language model. To further enhance alignment, it uses the pretrained language model to finetune the
      neural audio codec, infusing semantic information into the generated representations. In contrast,
094
      E2 TTS (Eskimez et al., 2024), based on Voicebox (Le et al., 2024), adopts a simpler way, which
095
      removes the phoneme and duration predictor and directly uses characters padded with filler tokens
096   to the length of mel spectrograms as input. This simple scheme also achieves very natural and
097   realistic synthesized results. However, we found that robustness issues exist in E2 TTS for the text
098   and speech alignment. Seed-TTS (Anastassiou et al., 2024) employs a similar strategy and achieves
099   excellent results, though not elaborated in model details. In these ways of not explicitly modeling
100   phoneme-level duration, models learn to assign the length of each word or phoneme according to
101   the given total sequence length, resulting in improved prosody and rhythm.
102   In this paper, we propose F5-TTS, a Fairytaler that Fakes Fluent and Faithful speech with Flow
103   matching. Maintaining the simplicity of pipeline without phoneme alignment, duration predictor,
104   text encoder, and semantically infused codec model, F5-TTS leverages the Diffusion Transformer
105   with ConvNeXt V2 (Woo et al., 2023) to better tackle text-speech alignment during in-context learn-
106   ing. We stress the deep entanglement of semantic and acoustic features in the E2 TTS model design,
107   which has inherent problems and will pose alignment failure issues that could not simply be solved
      with re-ranking. With in-depth ablation studies, our proposed F5-TTS demonstrates stronger robust-

      ness, in generating more faithful speech to the text prompt, while maintaining comparable speaker
109   similarity. Additionally, we introduce an inference-time sampling strategy for flow steps substan-

111   be seamlessly integrated into existing flow matching based models without retraining.

      2.1   F LOW M ATCHING
116
117   The Flow Matching (FM) objective is to match a probability path pt from a simple distribution p0 ,
118   e.g., the standard normal distribution p(x) = N (x|0, I), to p1 approximating the data distribution q.
119   In short, the FM loss regresses the vector field ut with a neural network vt as
120                                                                            2
121                                  LF M (θ) = Et,pt (x) ∥vt (x) − ut (x)∥ ,                            (1)
122
      where θ parameterizes the neural network, t ∼ U[0, 1] and x ∼ pt (x). The model vt is trained over
123   the entire flow step and data range, ensuring it learns to handle the entire transformation process
124   from the initial distribution to the target distribution.
125
126
      As we have no prior knowledge of how to approximate pt and ut , a conditional probability path
      pt (x|x1 ) = N (x | µt (x1 ), σt (x1 )2 I) is considered in actual training, and the Conditional Flow
127
      Matching (CFM) loss is proved to have identical gradients w.r.t. θ (Lipman et al., 2022). x1 is the
128
      random variable corresponding to training data. µ and σ is the time-dependent mean and scalar
129   standard deviation of Gaussian distribution.
130
131   Remember that the goal is to construct target distribution (data samples) from initial simple distri-
132
      bution, e.g., Gaussian noise. With the conditional form, the flow map ψt (x) = σt (x1 )x + µt (x1 )
      with µ0 (x1 ) = 0 and σ0 (x1 ) = 1, µ1 (x1 ) = x1 and σ1 (x1 ) = 0 is made to have all conditional
133
      probability paths converging to p0 and p1 at the start and end. The flow thus provides a vector field
134
      dψt (x0 )/dt = ut (ψt (x0 )|x1 ). Reparameterize pt (x|x1 ) with x0 , we have

      To view in a more general way (Kingma & Gao, 2024), if formulating the loss in terms of log
142   signal-to-noise ratio (log-SNR) λ instead of flow step t, and parameterizing to predict x0 (ϵ, com-
143   monly stated in diffusion model) instead of predict x1 − x0 , the CFM loss is equivalent to the
144   v-prediction (Salimans & Ho, 2022) loss with cosine schedule.
145
      For inference, given sampled noise x0 from initial distribution p0 , flow step t ∈ [0, 1] and condition
146
      with respect to generation task, the ordinary differential equation (ODE) solver (Chen, 2018) is
147
      used to evaluate ψ1 (x0 ) the integration of dψt (x0 )/dt with ψ0 (x0 ) = x0 . The number of function
148   evaluations (NFE) is the times going through the neural network as we may provide multiple flow
149   step values from 0 to 1 as input to approximate the integration. Higher NFE will produce more
150   accurate results and certainly take more calculation time.

152   2.2   C LASSIFIER -F REE G UIDANCE
153
154   Classifier Guidance (CG) is proposed by Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), functions by adding the gradient
155   of an additional classifier, while such an explicit way to condition the generation process may have
156   several problems. Extra training of the classifier is required and the generation result is directly
157   affected by the quality of the classifier. Adversarial attacks might also occur as the guidance is
158
      introduced through the way of updating the gradient. Thus deceptive images with imperceptible
      details to human eyes may be generated, which are not conditional.
159
160   Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) proposes to replace the explicit classifier
161   with an implicit classifier without directly computing the explicit classifier and its gradient. The
      gradient of a classifier can be expressed as a combination of conditional generation probability and

162
      unconditional generation probability. By dropping the condition with a certain rate during training,
163   and linear extrapolating the inference outputs with and without condition c, the final guided result is
164   obtained. We could balance between fidelity and diversity of the generated samples with

166                          vt,CF G = vt (ψt (x0 ), c) + α(vt (ψt (x0 ), c) − vt (ψt (x0 )))                    (4)
167   in CFM case, where α is the CFG strength.1

170   3     M ETHOD
171
172   This work aims to build a high-level text-to-speech synthesis system. We trained our model on the
173
      text-guided speech-infilling task (Bai et al., 2022; Le et al., 2024). Based on recent research (Lee
      et al., 2024; Eskimez et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), it is promising to train without phoneme-level
174
      duration predictor and can achieve higher naturalness in zero-shot generation deprecating explicit
175
      phoneme-level alignment. We adopt a similar pipeline as E2 TTS (Eskimez et al., 2024) and propose
176   our advanced architecture F5-TTS, addressing the slow convergence (timbre learned well at an early
177   stage but struggled to learn alignment) and robustness issues (failures on hard case generation) of
178   E2 TTS. We also propose a Sway Sampling strategy for flow steps at inference, which significantly
179   improves our model’s performance in faithfulness to reference text and speaker similarity.

183   Training The infilling task is to predict a segment of speech given its surrounding audio and full
184   text (for both surrounding transcription and the part to generate). For simplicity, we reuse the symbol
185   x to denote an audio sample and y the corresponding transcript for a data pair (x, y). As shown in
186

      from the audio sample x, where F is mel dimension and N is the sequence length. In the scope of
187
      CFM, we pass in the model the noisy speech (1 − t)x0 + tx1 and the masked speech (1 − m) ⊙ x1 ,
188
      where x0 denotes sampled Gaussian noise, t is sampled flow step, and m ∈ {0, 1}F ×N represents a
189   binary temporal mask.

222   3.2   F5-TTS
223
224   E2 TTS directly concatenates the padded character sequence with input speech, thus deeply entan-
225
      gling semantic and acoustic features with a large length gap of effective information, which is the
      underlying cause of hard training and poses several problems in a zero-shot scenario (Sec.5.1). To
226
      alleviate the problem of slow convergence and low robustness, we propose F5-TTS which acceler-
227
      ates training and inference and shows a strong robustness in generation. Also, an inference-time
228   Sway Sampling is introduced, which allows inference faster (using less NFE) while maintaining
229   performance. This sampling way of flow step can be directly applied to other CFM models.
230
231
      Model As shown in Fig.1, we use latent Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) as
      backbone. To be specific, we use DiT blocks with zero-initialized adaptive Layer Norm (adaLN-
232
      zero). To enhance the model’s alignment ability, we also leverage ConvNeXt V2 blocks (Woo et al.,
233
      2023). Its predecessor ConvNeXt V1 (Liu et al., 2022) is used in many works and shows a strong
234   temporal modeling capability in speech domain tasks (Siuzdak, 2023; Okamoto et al., 2024).
235
236   As described in Sec.3.1, the model input is character sequence, noisy speech, and masked speech.
237
      Before concatenation in the feature dimension, the character sequence first goes through ConvNeXt
      blocks. Experiments have shown that this way of providing individual modeling space allows text
238
      input to better prepare itself before later in-context learning. Unlike the phoneme-level force align-
239
      ment done in Voicebox, a rigid boundary for text is not explicitly introduced. The semantic and
240   acoustic features are jointly learned with the entire model. Not directly feeding the model with
241   inputs of significant length gap as E2 TTS does, the proposed text refinement mitigates the im-
242   pact of using inputs with mismatched effective information lengths, despite equal physical length in
243   magnitude as E2 TTS.
244
      The flow step t for CFM is provided as the condition of adaLN-zero rather than appended to the
245
      concatenated input sequence in Voicebox. We found that an additional mean pooled token of text
246   sequence for adaLN condition is not essential for the TTS task, maybe because the TTS task requires
247   more rigorously guided results and the mean pooled text token is more coarse.
248
249
      We adopt some position embedding settings in Voicebox. The flow step is embedded with a sinu-
      soidal position. The concatenated input sequence is added with a convolutional position embedding.
250
      We apply a rotary position embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) for self-attention rather than sym-
251
      metric bi-directional ALiBi bias (Press et al., 2021). And for extended character sequence ŷ, we
252   also add it with an absolute sinusoidal position embedding before feeding it into ConvNeXt blocks.
253
254   Compared with Voicebox and E2 TTS, we abandoned the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) style skip
255
      connection structure and switched to using DiT with adaLN-zero. Without a phoneme-level duration
      predictor and explicit alignment process, and nor with extra text encoder and semantically infused
256
      neural codec model in DiTTo-TTS, we give the text input a little freedom (individual modeling
257
      space) to let it prepare itself before concatenation and in-context learning with speech input.
258
259   Sampling As stated in Sec.2.1, the CFM could be viewed as v-prediction with a cosine schedule.
260   For image synthesis, Esser et al. (2024) propose to further schedule the flow step with a single-peak
261
      logit-normal (Atchison & Shen, 1980) sampling, in order to give more weight to intermediate flow
      steps by sampling them more frequently. We speculate that such sampling distributes the model’s
262
      learning difficulty more evenly over different flow step t ∈ [0, 1].
263
264   In contrast, we train our model with traditional uniformly sampled flow step t ∼ U[0, 1] but apply a
265   non-uniform sampling during inference. In specific, we define a Sway Sampling function as
266                                                             π

                                    fsway (u; s) = u + s · (cos( u) − 1 + u),                               (7)
                                                                2

      function to obtain sway sampled flow step t. With s < 0, the sampling is sway to left; with s > 0, the

      sampling is sway to right; and s = 0 case equals to uniform sampling. Fig.3 shows the probability
271   density function of Sway Sampling on flow step t.

273   Conceptually, CFM models focus more on sketching the contours of speech in the early stage (t →
274
      0) from pure noise and later focus more on the embellishment of fine-grained details. Therefore, the
      alignment between speech and text will be determined based on the first few generated results. With

279   4       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
280
281   Datasets We utilize the in-the-wild multilingual speech dataset Emilia (He et al., 2024) to train
282   our base models. After simply filtering out transcription failure and misclassified language speech,
283   we retain approximately 95K hours of English and Chinese data. We also trained small models for
284   ablation study and architecture search on WenetSpeech4TTS (Ma et al., 2024) Premium subset, con-
285   sisting of a 945 hours Mandarin corpus. Base model configurations are introduced below, and small
286   model configurations are in Appendix B.1. Three test sets are adopted for evaluation, which are
287   LibriSpeech-PC test-clean (Meister et al., 2023), Seed-TTS test-en (Anastassiou et al., 2024) with
288
      1088 samples from Common Voice (Ardila et al., 2019), and Seed-TTS test-zh with 2020 samples
      from DiDiSpeech (Guo et al., 2021)2 . Most of the previous English-only models are evaluated on
289
      different subsets of LibriSpeech test-clean while the used prompt list is not released, which makes
290
      fair comparison difficult. Thus we build and release a 4-to-10-second LibriSpeech-PC subset with
291   1127 samples to facilitate community comparisons.
292
293   Training Our base models are trained to 1.2M updates with a batch size of 307,200 audio
294
      frames (0.91 hours), for over one week on 8 NVIDIA A100 80G GPUs. The AdamW opti-
      mizer (Loshchilov, 2017) is used with a peak learning rate of 7.5e-5, linearly warmed up for 20K
295
      updates, and linearly decays over the rest of the training. We set 1 for the max gradient norm clip.
296
      The F5-TTS base model has 22 layers, 16 attention heads, 1024/2048 embedding/feed-forward net-
297   work (FFN) dimension for DiT; and 4 layers, 512/1024 embedding/FFN dimension for ConvNeXt
298   V2; in total 335.8M parameters. The reproduced E2 TTS, a 333.2M flat U-Net equipped Trans-
299   former, has 24 layers, 16 attention heads, and 1024/4096 embedding/FFN dimension. Both models
300   use RoPE as mentioned in Sec.3.2, a dropout rate of 0.1 for attention and FFN, the same convolu-
301   tional position embedding as in Voicebox(Le et al., 2024).
302
      We directly use alphabets and symbols for English, use jieba3 and pypinyin4 to process raw Chinese
303   characters to full pinyins. The character embedding vocabulary size is 2546, counting in the special
304   filler token and all other language characters exist in the Emilia dataset as there are many code-
305   switched sentences. For audio samples we use 100-dimensional log mel-filterbank features with 24
306   kHz sampling rate and hop length 256. A random 70% to 100% of mel frames is masked for infilling
307   task training. For CFG (Sec.2.2) training, first the masked speech input is dropped with a rate of 0.3,
308   then the masked speech again but with text input together is dropped with a rate of 0.2 (Le et al.,
309   2024). We assume that the two-stage control of CFG training may have the model learn more with
310   text alignment.
311   Inference The inference process is mainly elaborated in Sec.3.1. We use the Exponential Moving
312   Averaged (EMA) (Karras et al., 2024) weights for inference, and the Euler ODE solver for F5-
313   TTS (midpoint for E2 TTS as described in Eskimez et al. (2024)). We use the pretrained vocoder
314   Vocos (Siuzdak, 2023) to convert generated log mel spectrograms to audio signals.
315   Baselines We compare our models with leading TTS systems including, (mainly) autoregressive
316   models: VALL-E 2 (Chen et al., 2024), MELLE (Meng et al., 2024), FireRedTTS (Guo et al.,
317   2024a) and CosyVoice (Du et al., 2024b); non-autoregressive models: Voicebox (Le et al., 2024),
318   NaturalSpeech 3 (Ju et al., 2024), DiTTo-TTS (Lee et al., 2024), MaskGCT (Wang et al., 2024),
319   Seed-TTSDiT (Anastassiou et al., 2024) and our reproduced E2 TTS (Eskimez et al., 2024). Details
320   of compared models see Appendix A.

499   (left), and small models’ performance on Seed-TTS test-zh with Sway Sampling (right).

501
      As stated at the end of Sec.3.2, Sway Sampling with s < 0 scales more flow step toward early-stage
502
      inference (t → 0), thus having CFM models capture more startup information to sketch the contours
503   of target speech better. To be more concrete, we conduct a “leak and override” experiment. We
504   first replace the Gaussian noise input x0 at inference time with a ground-truth-information-leaked
505   input (1 − t′ )x0 + t′ x′ref , where t′ = 0.1 and x′ref is a duplicate of the audio prompt mel features.
506   Then, we provide a text prompt different from the duplicated audio transcript and let the model
507   continue the subsequent inference (skip the flow steps before t′ ). The model succeeds in overriding
508   leaked utterances and producing speech following the text prompt if Sway Sampling is used, and
509   fails without. Uniformly sampled flow steps will have the model producing speech dominated by
510   leaked information, speaking the duplicated audio prompt’s context. Similarly, a leaked timbre can
511   be overridden with another speaker’s utterance as an audio prompt, leveraging Sway Sampling.
512   The experiment result is a shred of strong evidence proving that the early flow steps are crucial for
513   sketching the silhouette of target speech based on given prompts faithfully, the later steps focus more
514   on formed intermediate noisy output, where our sway-to-left sampling (s < 0) finds the profitable
515   niche and takes advantage of it. We emphasize that our inference-time Sway Sampling can be
516   easily applied to existing CFM-based models without retraining. And we will work in the future to
517   combine it with training-time noise schedulers and distillation techniques to further boost efficiency.
518
519   6   C ONCLUSION
520
521   This work introduces F5-TTS, a fully non-autoregressive text-to-speech system based on flow
522   matching with diffusion transformer (DiT). With a tidy pipeline, literally text in and speech out,
523   F5-TTS achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot ability compared to existing works trained on industry-
524   scale data. We adopt ConvNeXt for text modeling and propose the test-time Sway Sampling strategy
525   to further improve the robustness of speech generation and inference efficiency. Our design allows
526   faster training and inference, by achieving a test-time RTF of 0.15, which is competitive with other
527
      heavily optimized TTS models of similar performance. We will open-source our code, and models,
      to enhance transparency and facilitate reproducible research in this area.

818

      Voicebox (Le et al., 2024) A non-autoregressive large-scale model based on flow matching trained

823

825   NaturalSpeech 3 (Ju et al., 2024) A non-autoregressive large-scale TTS system leverages a fac-

827   speech based on disentangled attributes. The 500M base model is trained on Librilight (Kahn et al.,
828
      2020) a 60K hours English dataset. We compared with scores reported in Ju et al. (2024).

830   Diffusion Transformer and leverages a pretrained language model to enhance the alignment. We
831   compare with DiTTo-en-XL, a 740M model trained on 55K hours English-only dataset, using scores
832   reported in Lee et al. (2024).
833   FireRedTTS (Guo et al., 2024a) A foundation TTS framework for industry-level generative
834   speech applications. The autoregressive text-to-semantic token model has 400M parameters and
835   the token-to-waveform generation model has about half the parameters. The system is trained with
836   248K hours of labeled speech data. We use the official code and pre-trained checkpoint to evaluate5 .

      MaskGCT (Wang et al., 2024) A large-scale non-autoregressive TTS model without precise align-

840   acoustic (S2A) model. The model is trained on Emilia (He et al., 2024) dataset with around 100K
841   Chinese and English in-the-wild speech data. We compare with results reported in Wang et al.

      Seed-TTS (Anastassiou et al., 2024) A family of high-quality versatile speech generation mod-

       C    S UBJECTIVE E VALUATION D ETAILS
1081
1082
       To evaluate speech quality, we conduct a CMOS subjective evaluation. 20 natives were invited for
1083   both English and Mandarin to evaluate 30 rounds with randomly selected utterances for all three
1084   test sets and all model variants. Evaluators were informed in detail about the guidelines and scoring
1085   criteria for the CMOS test, for example, the general instruction part:
1086
1087        • Most important: use high-quality studio headphones and a good sound card!
1088        • Listen through all test files and test sets before you do any ratings to get used to the material.
1089
            • Rate the quality of the test items only compared to the reference on top.
1090
1091        • Try to rate the overall impression of a test item and don’t concentrate on single aspects.
1092
       For the CMOS test, the overall quality of a generated speech is first rated from −3 (bad quality
1093
       compared to the reference) to +3 (much better than the reference) integer scale, then reported in
1094
       average differentials with received scores of ground truth speech. For SMOS, a 1 to 5 with 0.5 as an
1095   interval rating is employed (higher better). Judges are to score the similarity between the synthesized
1096   and prompt speech with clearly differentiated instructions mentioning:
1097
1098        • Try to rate concentrating on the speaker similarity aspects with reference speech.
1099
1100   We encourage more rigorous and transparent subjective evaluations, such as releasing used samples
1101   if not open-sourcing the model checkpoints. Meanwhile inviting more evaluators leads to more
1102   comprehensive and fair rating scores. Just for reference, DiTTo-TTS (Lee et al., 2024) received
1103
       and reported 6 and 12 ratings for SMOS and CMOS, respectively, NaturalSpeech 3 (Ju et al., 2024)
       invited 12 natives to judge 20 samples for CMOS and 10 samples for SMOS.
