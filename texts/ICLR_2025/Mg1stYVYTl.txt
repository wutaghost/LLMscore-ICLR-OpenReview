011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Spatial-temporal forecasting plays a pivotal role in urban planning and computing.
014            Although Spatial-Temporal Graph Neural Networks (STGNNs) excel in modeling
015            spatial-temporal dynamics, they often suffer from relatively poor computational
016            efficiency. Recently, Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) have gained popularity in
017            spatial-temporal forecasting for their simplified architecture and better efficiency.
018
               However, existing MLP-based models can be susceptible to noise interference,
               especially when the noise can affect both input and target sequences in spatial-
019
               temporal forecasting on noisy data. To alleviate this impact, we propose Robust
020
               Spatial-Temporal Information Bottleneck (RSTIB) principle. The RSTIB extends
021            previous Information Bottleneck (IB) approaches by lifting the specific Markov
022            assumption without impairing the IB nature. Then, by explicitly minimizing the
023            irrelevant noisy information, the representation learning guided by RSTIB can be
024            more robust against noise interference. Furthermore, the instantiation, RSTIB-MLP,
025            can be seamlessly implemented with MLPs, thereby achieving efficient and robust
026            spatial-temporal modeling. Moreover, a training regime is designed to handle the
027            dynamic nature of spatial-temporal relationships by incorporating a knowledge
028            distillation module to alleviate feature collapse and enhance model robustness
029
               under noisy conditions. Our extensive experimental results on six intrinsically noisy
               benchmark datasets from various domains show that the RSTIB-MLP runs much
030
               faster than state-of-the-art STGNNs and delivers superior forecasting accuracy
031
               across noisy environments, substantiating its robustness and efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Spatial-temporal forecasting holds great significance in modeling complex dynamic systems (Bai et al.,
038   2020; Guo et al., 2021a; Deng et al., 2021). It needs to capture both temporal and spatial dependencies
039
      to accurately predict important statistics, e.g., traffic flow states or electricity consumption.
040   Previous works in spatial-temporal forecasting have effectively adopted convolutional neural networks
041   (CNNs) (Lai et al., 2018), recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Meng et al., 2020), temporal convolution
042   networks (TCNs) (Wu et al., 2019) to model spatial-temporal relations. Lately, there has been a
043   growing interest in spatial-temporal graph neural networks (STGNNs) (Shao et al., 2022b;c; Wu
044   et al., 2019) due to their strong capacity. Though achieving exceptional performance, STGNN-based
045
      methods suffer from slow computational efficiency. To alleviate this, a few recent works (Shao et al.,
      2022a; Qin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Yi et al., 2024) adopt Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP)
046
      due to its advantageous efficiency. However, MLP-based models become less effective when facing
047
      spatial-temporal noise perturbation, which is common in real world (Jiang et al., 2023b; Tang et al.,
048   2023; Fang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2023). As shown in Fig. 1, two time series
049   may become indistinguishable in both the historical input end and the forecasting target end due to
050   the presence of noise perturbation. This is termed as “sample indistinguishability” in (Shao et al.,
051   2022a). There is no theoretically grounded principle in existing MLP-based models that can alleviate
052   this issue. Besides, we can also observe severe feature collapse, i.e., feature collapse is reflected
053   by much lower feature variance, which is used for quantitative analysis of the diversity among the
      learned features (Papyan et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2023a; Bardes et al., 2021) (See Section 3).

066   Methods based on adversarial training (Jiang et al., 2023b), graph information bottleneck (GIB) (Tang
067
      et al., 2023), mathematical tools (Choi et al., 2022), frequency domain MLPs (Yi et al., 2024),
      Biased TCN (Chen et al., 2024), Spatial-temporal Curriculum Dropout (Wang et al., 2023a) have
068
      been proposed to combat the noise for robust representation learning. However, spatial-temporal
069
      data often undergoes preprocessing through a sliding window mechanism, where a sequence can
070   serve as either the input or the target when residing in different windows. Noise potentially harms
071   both ends, termed as “dual noise effect”. Consequently, the enhancement of these methods is often
072   marginal since they only consider a single end effect. While Robust Graph Information Bottleneck
073   (RGIB) (Zhou et al., 2023) effectively combats bilateral edge noise for link prediction, we reveal that
074   generalizing RGIB directly to MLP networks for spatial-temporal forecasting is difficult: the GNN
075   model architecture and graph data assumption it relies upon is very different from the spatial-temporal
076   data that is continuous multivariate time series. It is significant to derive additional guiding principles
077   and specific instantiation to handle such a scenario.
078   In this paper, we first disclose that spatial-temporal data noise is detrimental to (i) both forecasting
079   input and target and (ii) both predictive performance and feature variance. To combat it, we introduce
080   a new theoretically sound principle, named Robust Spatial-Temporal Information Bottleneck (RSTIB),
081   generalizing the RGIB principle to mitigate the dual noise effect in spatial-temporal data. Particularly,
082   it lifts the Markov assumption typically assumed in IB while not impairing the IB nature. In doing
083   so, the derived additional noisy information and the original redundant information are explicitly
084
      reformulated and minimized. RSTIB-MLP, guided by the RSTIB principle, is further instantiated
      for robust spatial-temporal modeling. Subsequently, combined with the instantiation, we propose
085
      a training regime to handle the dynamic relations between different time series via an innovative
086
      knowledge distillation module. The key idea is to balance the informative terms within the objective
087   by accounting for the quantified noise impact, thereby being better balanced and less impacted by
088   noisy information. We quantify the noise impact to each time series by defining a new noise impact
089   indicator (Definition 4.2) and incorporate this knowledge for each time series.
090
      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
091
092
093   • To the best of our knowledge, it is the first work to derive and extend IB for handling the dual
094     noise effect in spatial-temporal forecasting. We reveal that dual noise effect can lead to significant
095
        degradation in both of the predictive accuracy and feature variance.
096
097   • We propose the RSTIB principle, a general theoretical framework to robustify MLP networks. A
098     corresponding computationally efficient instantiation, named RSTIB-MLP, is devised by utilizing
099     pure MLP networks for robust forecasting on noisy spatial temporal data, with theoretical support
100
        for its robustness due to the RSTIB principle.
101
102   • A new training regime is further designed to enhance the robust representation learning. This
103     regime incorporates a novel knowledge distillation module, strengthening robustness and boosting
104     the variance of its learned features.
105
106   • Benchmark datasets from various domains are adopted for noisy and clean evaluations. Extensive
107     comparisons based on our theoretical analysis and empirical studies demonstrate our method’s
        superiority.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Spatial-Temporal Forecasting (STF). Efforts in STF have led to the development of sophisticated
111   models such as AGCRN (Bai et al., 2020), GraphWaveNet (Wu et al., 2019), and STExplainer (Tang
112   et al., 2023), which leverage STGNN-based methodologies to model series-wise dependencies over
113   time. Recent explorations have integrated Neural-ODE-based (Jin et al., 2022) and self-supervised
114   learning paradigms (Li et al., 2022) to enhance spatial-temporal modeling. Despite their predictive
115   capabilities, these methods often suffer from computational efficiency issues when compared with
116   MLP-based approaches.
117   MLP-based Approaches for STF. In response to the efficiency challenge, MLP-based approaches
118   have gained attention. Notable works include STID (Shao et al., 2022a), which incorporates spatial-
119   temporal identity information to achieve superior performance over STGNN-based methods, and
120   STHMLP (Qin et al., 2023), which employs a hierarchical MLP structure to capture various aspects
121   of spatial-temporal data. FreTS (Yi et al., 2024) applies MLPs in the frequency domain. Specifically,
122   its advantage of the energy compaction can help MLPs to preserve clearer patterns while filtering out
123   influence of noises. However, these methods have not yet explored the dual noise effect in the face of
124
      comprehensive noise perturbations.
125   Robust Representation Learning with Information Bottleneck Principle. The Information
126   Bottleneck (IB) principle has emerged as a guiding framework for robust representation learning.
127   Initially applied in Deep Variational Information Bottleneck (DVIB) (Alemi et al., 2016), IB has since
128   found applications in diverse domains (Peng et al., 2018; Higgins et al., 2016). Notably, GIB (Wu
129   et al., 2020) extends IB to graph-structured data for supervised learning. Subsequent advancements,
130
      such as STExplainer (Tang et al., 2023), build upon the GIB principle for explainable representations.
      While these methods can enhance robustness to some extent, they overlook the presence of noise
131
      in the forecasting target. RGIB (Zhou et al., 2023) takes a step forward by decoupling mutual
132
      information to enhance such robustness, but generalizing it to MLP networks for spatial-temporal
133   forecasting remains unexplored.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
170
171   In this section, we introduce the Robust Spatial-Temporal Information Bottleneck (RSTIB) principle,
172   a theoretical framework designed to be more general for enhancing robust spatial-temporal modeling.
173   Following this, we detail a novel instantiation termed RSTIB-MLP, which leverages data reparam-
174   eterization techniques for continuous multivariate time series. We also design a training regime,
175   incorporating a knowledge distillation module, to further enhance the performance of spatial-temporal
176   forecasting. This approach capitalizes on the dynamic spatial-temporal relationships inherent in the
177   data, resulting in a better balance of informative terms within the objective.
178
179   4.1   D ERIVING THE RSTIB P RINCIPLE
180
      Let X represents the input to the IB model and its variants, obtained from X h and the attachment of
181
      spatial-temporal information from a specially designed module. Formally, given the input X, target
182
      Y , and encoding Z from X, the learning objective of the standard IB principle can be formulated as
183   follows:
184                                  min LIB = −I(Z, Y ) + β × I(X, Z),                                 (2)
185   where I(·, ·) denotes mutual information (MI), and β ≥ 0 is a Lagrange multiplier for controlling the
186   trade-off between the compression of X and the preservation of Y . The Markov chain Z − X − Y
187   is assumed in IB (Alemi et al., 2016). We can use the information diagram (Fig.2a) to depict the
188   IB, where we represent information of X and Y as circles. Then IB encourages to cover as much of
189   I(X; Y ) and as little of H(X|Y ) as possible.

202   However, the vanilla IB is sub-optimal in our scenario. By drawing inspirations from (Jiang et al.,
203   2023b; Choi et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), we firstly have the
204
      following assumptions about spatial-temporal data:
205   Assumption 4.1. Noisy Nature of spatial-temporal Data. We focus on spatial-temporal data that
206
      inherently exhibits noisy characteristics. Under the sliding window mechanism, a sequence can serve
      different purposes when residing in different windows, either as the input or the target. Consequently,
207
      the noise elements can potentially reside in the input and target areas. For simplicity, we presume
208
      the noise type in our analysis to adhere to Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN), a prevalent and
209   empirically approximated noise model in practical applications (Lim & Puthusserypady, 2007).
210
      Assumption 4.2. Invariant and Variant spatial-temporal Patterns. A dynamic spatial-temporal
211
      graph exhibits a dual nature, wherein each node, representing a time series, embodies both spatial-
212   temporal invariant patterns conducive to generalized predictions across all time windows and
213   spatial-temporal variant patterns reflecting underlying time-varying and node-specific dynamics.
214
215   Following these assumptions, it is naive to assume the Markov assumption Z − X − Y , which
      results in I(Z; Y | X) = 0. This implies that we directly overlook the noisy information behind

      H(Y |X) (i.e., the noisy information conveyed by the target data). Fortunately, Wieczorek & Roth
217   (2020) demonstrate that a lower bound of I(Z; Y ) can be derived without relying on the Z − X − Y
218   assumption, thus lifting the Z − X − Y Markov restriction. It is achieved in the DVIB model, which
219   assumes X − Z − Y assumption by its construction. We apply it to our specific scenario by assuming
220   only the Markov chain condition X − Z − Y (See Proposition 4.5). The introduced additional term,
221   I(Z; Y | X), as represented in Fig.2b, must be minimized as well, along with the original irrelevant
222   information, i.e., I(X; Z|Y ). Accordingly, we introduce the following reformulations:
223   Proposition 4.1. Reformulate I(Z; Y | X) and I(X; Z | Y ). The sum of I(Z; Y | X) and
224   I(Y ; Z|X) can be reformulated as: I(Z; Y | X) + I(Z; X | Y ) = I(Z; X, Y ) − I(X; Y ; Z). Proof.

      Robustness Study. We evaluate the robustness of RSTIB-MLP by injecting noise into both the input
433   and the target area, similar to the empirical study we conduct for evaluating the harmful aspect of

436   This finding underscores the robustness of RSTIB-MLP, which can be attributed to its consideration

        6                    C ONCLUSION
530
531
       In noisy spatial-temporal forecasting scenarios, noise perturbation can degrade forecasting accuracy
       and induce feature collapse. We propose the Robust Spatial-Temporal Information Bottleneck (RSTIB)
532
       principle for guiding robust representation learning to mitigate these effects. By leveraging RSTIB,
533
       we instantiate our method using a pure MLP network, resulting in a computationally efficient and
534    robust RSTIB-MLP model for the task. Additionally, we incorporate a knowledge distillation
535    module into our training regime. Knowledge distillation can enhance feature diversity and improve
536    predictive accuracy by better leveraging the knowledge from previously trained teacher models to
537    balance informative terms within the objective of RSTIB-MLP. Through comprehensive evaluation
538    encompassing feature variance and predictive performance metrics, our approach demonstrates
539    superior performance in handling of noise. It maintains robust forecasting accuracy under challenging
       conditions while computationally more efficient than stat-of-the-art STGNN-based methods.

      B.1   M ATHEMATICAL P RELIMINARIES AND D EFINITIONS
829
830   This section provides mathematical preliminaries concerning entropy and mutual information using
831   three discrete random variables X, Y , and Z for illustrative purposes. It is important to note that
832   these variables do not carry specific meanings within this context and the notations used here are
833   distinct from those in the main discussion. Additionally, this section offers an intuitive understanding
834   of each term.
835   Definition B.1. Entropy. We define the entropy H(X) of a discrete random variable X as a measure
836   of its uncertainty, using its marginal distribution p(x). Mathematically, entropy is expressed as:
837                                                     X
838                                       H(X) = −          p(x) log p(x),                                  (7)
839                                                    x∈X

840   where the summation extends over all possible outcomes x of the random variable X. The function
841   H(X) quantifies the expected information content or uncertainty inherent in X’s outcomes.
842   Definition B.2. Joint Entropy. The entropy of two random variables X and Y can be jointly
843   considered by viewing them as components of a single vector-valued random variable. This joint
844   entropy is defined as:
845                                               X
846                              H(X, Y ) = −            p(x, y) log p(x, y)
847                                                x∈X,y∈Y
                                                    XX
848                                           =−              p(x, y) log p(x, y),                         (8)
849                                                x∈X y∈Y
850
851
      where p(x, y) represents the joint probability distribution of X and Y . This definition encapsulates
      the total uncertainty present when considering the distribution of both variables simultaneously.
852
853   Definition B.3. Conditional Entropy. Given two discrete random variables X and Y , the conditional
854
      entropy of X given Y is defined as:
                                               X           X
855                              H(X|Y ) = −         p(y)      p(x|y) log p(x|y),                       (9)
856                                              y∈Y       x∈X
857
858   where p(x|y) is the conditional probability of X given Y , and p(y) is the marginal distribution of Y .
859   A value of H(X|Y ) = 0 implies that knowing Y completely determines X, signifying no remaining
860
      uncertainty about X once Y is observed.
861   This concept allows us to understand H(X) as a priori entropy of X, while H(X|Y ) represents
862   a posteriori entropy—reflecting the uncertainty in X after Y is known. The reduction in entropy,
863   H(X) − H(X|Y ), quantifies the amount of information Y provides about X, which is formally
      termed mutual information in Definition B.4.

1014   In Fig. 8a, the entropy of X, i.e., H(X), and the entropy of Y , i.e., H(Y ), are depicted as circles, with
1015   their mutual information I(X; Y ) represented in the overlapping area. The representation learning
1016   guided by the IB principle aims to optimize the information flow by retaining as much relevant
1017   information about Y in Z as possible while minimizing the redundant information from X. This
1018   principle targets reducing the irrelevant information H(X|Y ) Z captures, namely I(X; Z|Y ), aiming
1019   for what is termed the “minimal sufficient representation”, ideally encapsulating solely I(X; Y ).
1020   Achieving this optimal representation presents substantial challenges due to the intrinsic complexities
1021   of the models and the varied selection of parameters and hyperparameters, such as β in Eq. (2).
1022   Incorporating the IB model with deep learning, where mutual information terms are modeled using
1023   deep neural networks (DNNs), has proven successful. The DVIB method leverages deep learning
1024   to approximate the IB model, finding a sufficient statistic Z given X while retaining pertinent side
1025   information about Y . The approach involves parameterizing the conditional probabilities P (Z|X)
       and P (Y |Z) using DNNs, thus enabling direct recovery of the terms in the original IB objectives.

       I.2   A FURTHER COMPARISON OF RSTIB AND OTHER IB PRINCIPLES
1518
       This section elaborates on the comparative analysis between the Information Bottleneck(IB), its vari-
1519
       ants and our proposed Robust Spatial-Temporal Information Bottleneck (RSTIB) principle. Notably,
1520   RSTIB extends the capabilities of Deep Variational Information Bottleneck (DVIB) and Robust Graph
1521   Information Bottleneck (RGIB). Given that RGIB itself generalizes the Graph Information Bottleneck
1522   (GIB), our comparison primarily focuses on the IB model as introduced by (Tishby et al., 2000),
1523   alongside its significant extensions: DVIB (Alemi et al., 2016), GIB (Wu et al., 2020), RGIB (Zhou
1524   et al., 2023), and our RSTIB.
1525
       Briefly speaking, compared to IB, as well as DVIB and GIB derivatives, the RSTIB introduces
1526   significant advancements by accounting for spatial-temporal data noise present both in input and
1527   target regions, enhancing robustness in both theoretical constructs and instantiation. While the RSTIB
1528   extends the RGIB principle, it diverges by considering lifting specific Markov assumption typically
1529   held to explicitly minimize the irrelevant information terms. The subsequent reformulations can
1530   ensure the integrity of the IB principle. In other words, RSTIB ensures, both theoretically and
1531   practically, that encoding Z does not reduce to the trivial solution Z = Y and preserves information
1532   from both X and Y . This enhancement is meticulously analyzed in Section G, a thoroughness not
1533   typically found in RGIB’s analysis. Furthermore, the instantiation of the RSTIB principle does not
1534
       depend on specific data structural assumptions inherent to the instantiations of RGIB, which are based
       on graph data and assume that the number of edges in the pruned graph, denoted as |ZA |, does not
1535
       exceed those in the original graph, |A|. Therefore, the RSTIB framework demonstrates more general
1536
       potential applications and robustness, making it suitable for instantiating in Multi-Layer Perceptron
1537   (MLP) networks for spatial-temporal forecasting.
1538
1539   Analytically, traditional models such as IB, DVIB, and GIB predominantly focus on minimizing the
1540
       conditional entropy H(X|Y ) while maximally preserving H(Y |X). These models operate under the
       implicit assumption that I(Z; Y |X) = 0, adhering strictly to the Markov chain condition Z − X − Y .
1541
       This approach proves effective for specific applications, such as classification tasks. However, in
1542
       spatial-temporal forecasting, with the Assumption 4.1 and 4.2 proposed about spatial-temporal
1543   data, such Markov assumption is too restrictive. The noise-related irrelevant information could be
1544   obscured within this restriction, thereby questioning the direct adoption of the Markov assumption
1545   Z−X−Y . Besides, RGIB, by its definition, considers an explicit relationship between the information
1546   terms and attempts to balance them in a self-controlled way. Some of its derived terms, such as
                                                                                  −                +
1547   H(Z|X, Y ), is minimized by controlling H(Z) to be within the range γH        < H(Z) < γH     , given
1548   that H(Z) ≥ max{H(Z|X), H(Z|Y )} ≥ H(Z|X, Y ). This requires a delicate balance within the
1549   RGIB objective. In comparison to these, RSTIB adopts a distinct approach. It lifts the Markov
1550   condition of Z − X − Y by adhering to only the X − Z − Y assumption, which is less restrictive
1551
       while not impairing the bottleneck nature of the representation Z. This formulation introduces
       I(Z; Y |X) ̸= 0, with the existing I(Z; X|Y ) to be minimized, enhancing robustness against noise
1552
       perturbations in both input and target. Besides, RSTIB focuses on learning the “minimal sufficient
1553
       representation” while minimizing explicitly expressed and reformulated irrelevant information under
1554   the X − Z − Y Markov assumption. This strategic orientation provides a theoretical guarantee that
1555   the encoding of Z neither reduces to the trivial solution Z = Y nor compromises the information
1556   from X and Y which results in I(X; Z) = 0 and I(Z; Y ) = 0 respectively.
1557
       Regarding instantiations, the GIB is inherently intertwined with the Graph Attention Network (GAT)
1558
       architecture. While the RGIB mitigates this constraint by eliminating the need to modify the Graph
1559
       Neural Network (GNN) architecture, it still necessitates reliance on GNNs and their inherent graph
1560   structures. This dependence is under the assumption that the number of edges in the pruned graph,
1561   denoted as |ZA |, does not exceed those in the original graph, denoted as |A|. However, such an
1562   assumption can not hold when generalizing to Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) networks, where graph
1563   structures are inapplicable. Meanwhile, spatial-temporal data often comes with no pre-defined graph
1564   structure. GIB/RGIB-based method can not directly be applied to such scenario. Besides, the DVIB
1565   adheres strictly to both Markov chain assumptions, which imposes overly restrictive constraints on
       the optimization process for the potential set of joint distributions P (X, Y, Z). In response to these


                                                         29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       limitations, we propose the RSTIB-MLP instantiation, as outlined in Section 4.2. The information
1567   flow of RSTIB-MLP follows Fig.11, which is under less restrictive Markov assumption for a wider
1568   array of potential joint distributions while not impairing IB nature with theoretical guarantees. Besides,
1569   it integrates three independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) Gaussian distributions as the prior
1570   distributions for the input, representation, and target regions, which play the role of minimizing the
1571   irrelevant information during the optimization. These circumvent the dependency on graph structures,
1572   which can be instantiated in MLPs for spatial-temporal forecasting.
