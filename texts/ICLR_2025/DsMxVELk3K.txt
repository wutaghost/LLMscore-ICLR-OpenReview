011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Lossy text compression reduces data size while preserving core meaning, making
014            it ideal for summarization, automated analysis, and digital archives where exact fi-
015
               delity is less critical. While extensively used in image compression, text compres-
               sion techniques, such as integrating entropy coding with autoencoder latent repre-
016
               sentations in Seq2Seq text generation, have been underexplored. A key challenge
017
               is incorporating lossless entropy coding into denoising autoencoders to improve
018            storage efficiency while maintaining high-quality outputs, even with noisy text.
019            Prior studies have mainly focused on near-lossless token generation with little at-
020            tention to space efficiency. In this paper, we present a denoising autoencoder with
021            a rectified latent representation that compresses variable-sized inputs into a fixed-
022            size latent space without prior knowledge of dataset dimensions. By leveraging
023            entropy coding, our model achieves state-of-the-art compression ratios alongside
024            competitive text quality, as measured by diverse metrics. Its parameter count is ap-
025            proximately 196 times smaller than comparable models. Additionally, it achieves
026
               a compression ratio of 67× while maintaining high BLEU and ROUGE scores.
               This significantly outperforms existing transformer-based models in memory ef-
027
               ficiency, marking a breakthrough in balancing lossless compression with optimal
028
               space optimization.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Text compression is an essential part of data compression that involves diminishing the volume of
034   textual data while preserving its informational content. This process is divided into lossless compres-
035   sion, which allows full data recovery, and lossy compression, which strategically sacrifices details
036   to achieve higher compression ratios. The exponential growth of digital information has introduced
037   noteworthy challenges in storage and transmission efficiency (Office, 2023), specifically in contexts
038   where exact textual reproduction is not compulsory. Lossy text compression has numerous appli-
039
      cations; lossy text storage and compression offer practical solutions for non-critical documents and
      archiving, where exact preservation of every detail is not essential. This approach is useful for in-
040
      ternal reports, outdated or old document versions, corporate archives, and educational institutions,
041
      where key information needs to be retained, but minor errors or formatting loss won’t impact us-
042   ability. Libraries, digital archives, and universities can compress vast collections, such as research
043   papers or public domain books, to save storage space while maintaining accessibility. Additionally,
044   chatbots, email archiving, and web search engines benefit from lossy text processing, enabling fast
045   retrieval and facilitated communication. This makes lossy text compression a valuable, efficient tool
046   for handling large-scale text data while optimizing storage and retrieval performance.
047
      Image compression harnessing neural networks, especially through Variational Autoencoders, has
048   gained prominence (Geleta et al., 2023), meanwhile myriad approaches have been discovered to
049   reduce textual volume while preserving salient information. Existing research underscores the ef-
050   ficacy of transformer-based models, including BERT (Li et al., 2023), LLaMA (Valmeekam et al.,
051   2023), and ALBERT (Li et al., 2021), LSTM (Prato et al., 2019), in maintaining contextual integrity
052   during decompression across diverse linguistic landscapes. In particular, architectural modifications
053   such as the shared encoder have shown strong performance (Li et al., 2020). Furthermore, chasing
      the trend of fine-tuning domain-specific pre-trained models and incorporating the LoRA (Hu et al.,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021) technique has also resulted in praiseworthy results (Ge et al., 2023). Cross-lingual augmen-
055   tation strategies enhance the capabilities of transformer models for languages with diverse resource
056   availability, an aspect that has not been investigated in the work of (Mao et al., 2022). Notwith-
057   standing these advancements, recent studies have encountered limitations. The study conducted by
058   Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2023) leveraged the operational procedure of arithmetic coding and incor-
059   porated it with GPT for lossless text compression, lacking a quest for harnessing general-purpose
060   compressors for an extensive comparison of compression ratios. Additionally, it is important to
061   note that non-autoregressive decoding may not flawlessly recover the original text, and iterative
062   inspection of meaning preservation might be time-consuming (Ge et al., 2022). (Ge et al., 2023)
063
      employed a LoRA-configured Llama-2-7b for context compression, albeit with augmented param-
      eters, exacerbating the already prodigious parameter count characteristic of contemporary LLMs.
064
      Meanwhile, (Qin et al., 2023) executed an autoencoding task generating dynamic text segments
065
      with residual connections, achieving a mere tenfold compression—a ratio deemed insufficient. Fur-
066   thermore, (Wang et al., 2021) provided an autoencoding model trained to reconstruct input texts by
067   means of a combination of token embeddings as a bottleneck, a strategy susceptible to overfitting,
068   while the compression ratio r is lower. Strengthening the fixed-size bottleneck remains a pivotal
069   challenge for Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) due to their intrinsic self-attention
070   mechanism. While prior research ((Rae et al., 2019; Malireddy et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021))
071   has explored text compression in LLMs, they frequently grapple with the challenge of mitigating
072   memory intricacy. Despite their capacity for fixed-size latent spaces, LSTM-based autoencoders
073   frequently produce inadequate results in autoregressive decoding tasks. Using these orthodox ap-
074
      proaches, we tackle the issue of memory complexity from an alternative standpoint: employing
      lossy text compression.
075
076   In this study, we introduce TextEconomizer, a uniquely tailored autoencoder-based approach de-
077   signed for English autoencoding tasks, guided by a sophisticated noisy text process, consisting of
078   a single encoder-decoder layer built upon a bidirectional gated recurrent unit (GRU). TextEcon-
079   omizer optimizes the balance between model complexity and computational efficiency, while si-
080
      multaneously improving space efficacy that enhances qualitative text performance through attention
      mechanisms and boosts quantitative compression efficiency by continuously refining the fixed-size
081
      bottleneck. Besides, the integration of entropy coding further compresses the latent representation,
082
      solidifying TextEconomizer as a memory-efficient monolingual method. The contribution of our
083   study is summarized below:
084
085         • A pragmatic text noise process is tailored to encompass a wide range of text distortions,
086           allowing the neural network to uncover and learn to fix multifarious mistakes during train-
087           ing.
088         • A monolingual autoencoder-based method called TextEconomizer has paved the way for
089           enhanced performance in autoencoding tasks, extending the relevance of autoencoding be-
090           yond image compression to text-based applications.
091         • Benchmarking across diverse corpora demonstrated that TextEconomizer acquires state-of-
092           the-art memory efficiency with negligible degradation in text quality.
093
            • We rigorously evaluated the efficiency of the refined latent representation in capturing in-
094           tricate linguistic patterns, further compressing it by harnessing entropy coding.
095
            • An exploration into the impact of the size of the training corpus on the efficacy of TextE-
096
              conomizer in restoring identical text within English text is conducted, shedding light on its
097
              potential contributions in the realm of decompression.

103   interest and contributing to the development of innovative methodologies and datasets. Our exten-
104
      sive study aims to summarize key findings and showcase the evolving landscape of text compression
      methodologies across different language contexts, including lossy (Li et al., 2023; 2020; 2021; Ge
105
      et al., 2022) and lossless (Valmeekam et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2022) techniques.
106
107   The advent of text compression has seen the employment of transformer-based methods where GPT,
      Llama, BART, and a single-layer transformer have been used. Among lossy techniques, (Li et al.,

109   and then restoring them using a Transformer-based model. Among compressive-memory-based
110   methods, (Rae et al., 2019) introduced a refined extension of the Transformer architecture (Vaswani
111   et al., 2023), employing a compressive-memory-based approach that shrinks past activations into
112   more consolidated representations instead of discarding them.

      Encompassing Transformer-based lossy autoencoding and translation, (Ge et al., 2023) proposed
114

119
      Within LSTM-based autoencoding tasks, (Malireddy et al., 2020) introduced an indicator vector to

121   the aforementioned approach, (Tissier et al., 2019) introduced an autoencoder-based model that

123   (Acharya et al., 2019) used a similar approach, decomposing and transforming the embedding layer
124   through matrix factorization, and employing lower-rank matrices to enhance storage efficiency.
125
      Our study has also revealed that lossless text compression techniques have yielded exceptional re-
126
      sults by incorporating diverse techniques with transformers. (Valmeekam et al., 2023) proposes a
127   novel method that uses the LLM to predict the next token (based on probability ranking) in a text
128   sequence based on a window of past tokens. (Huang et al., 2023) introduced a novel method that uti-
129   lizes the GPT model to calculate probability distributions for each token and represent the entire text
130   with a single number. Additionally, (Delétang et al., 2023) compared predictive models and lossless
131   compressors and recommended using large self-supervised language models for compression.
132
      Transformer-based techniques, though superior in autoregressive decoding evaluation, encounter
133   memory constraints. Despite these successes, the application of latent representations for text com-
134   pression remains underexplored. It is noteworthy that transformers require prodigious datasets for
135   optimal performance.

150   From the WMT19 corpus, we extracted the English sentences from the Chinese-to-English pairs.

152   isolated the answer column. Since BookCorpus is monolingual, no extraction was needed. This

155

159   in the sentence. We then employ an iterative technique, examining each character Si ∈ S and sys-
160   tematically eradicating any character not present in our predefined character set C. This meticulous
161   preprocessing assures a standardized and sophisticated textual dataset for subsequent neural network
      processing.

251   Given an input sequence of tokens X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }, where n denotes the sequence length,

253

254
      by the trainable matrix Exi = Embedding(xi ). To mitigate overfitting, we applied dropout to the
255
      embeddings, yielding DExi = Dropout(Exi ). Through backpropagation during training, these
256   metrics are iteratively refined to minimize the loss function. The resultant DExi is then propagated
257   through K bi-directional Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) layers. These layers, predicated on four
258   primary components—update gate zt , reset gate rt , candidate hidden state h̃t , and final hidden state

260   from both directions, which are subsequently concatenated, yielding the output:

345
      We evaluated the efficacy of our method using the compression ratio (ratio of the original consumed
346   memory to the compressed consumed memory) and the memory reduction (difference between the
347   original memory usage and the compressed memory usage). Furthermore, we measured our model’s
348   performance in accurately restoring text by evaluating it with BERTScore, BLEU score 6, ROUGE-
349   N, ROUGE-L, and Perplexity (PPL) score.
350

351
      of text by calculating the cosine similarity of their embedding tokens. This metric outputs precision,
352   recall, and f1 score.
353
354   BLEU. (Papineni et al., 2002) The BLEU metric estimates the quality of candidate text by assigning
355
      precision scores to n-grams and comparing them with one or more reference texts. Scores range from

356
      follows:

      ROUGE (Lin, 2004) ROUGE-1 measures unigram overlapping between the generated and refer-

511   7     C ONCLUSION
512
513   This study presents a memory-efficient baseline for the task at hand, proposing the TextEconomizer,
514
      a monolingual autoencoder-based approach that leverages attention mechanisms and a novel text
      noising strategy. TextEconomizer refines the fixed-size latent representation and additionally lever-
515
      ages the compatibility of entropy coding algorithms to condense the latent space more efficiently,
516
      therefore adeptly maneuvering the intricate linguistic complexities inherent to the task. It surpassed
517   transformer-based methods in parameter and memory efficiency across various corpora, with only a
518   negligible quality trade-off. Notably, we demonstrated that pre-training traditional transformers with
519   minimal settings can acquire performance ≈2% below of best-performing models in autoencoding
520   tasks—by integrating our sophisticated noisy text processing, therefore questioning the notion that
521   autoencoder-based approaches are only adequate for image compression, extending their relevance
522   to text-based tasks. Our work opens further avenues for efficient natural language processing in
523   resource-constrained settings. Our future research directions include knowledge distillation from
524   multilingual to our monolingual model and large-scale experiments integrating contrastive learning
525
      techniques.
