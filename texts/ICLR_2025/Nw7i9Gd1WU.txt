009                                                 Abstract
010
011              We study online leader-follower games where the leader interacts with a myopic
012              follower using a quantal response policy. The leaderвҖҷs objective is to design an
013              algorithm without prior knowledge of her reward function or the state transition
014              dynamics. Crucially, the leader also lacks insight into the followerвҖҷs reward func-
015              tion and realized rewards, posing a significant challenge. To address this, the
016              leader must learn the followerвҖҷs quantal response mapping solely through strate-
017              gic interactions вҖ” announcing policies and observing responses. We introduce
018
                 a unified algorithm, Planning after Estimation, which updates the leaderвҖҷs poli-
                 cies in a two-step approach. In particular, we first jointly estimate the leaderвҖҷs
019
                 value function and the followerвҖҷs response mapping by maximizing the sum of the
020
                 Bellman error of the value function, the likelihood of the quantal response model,
021              and a regularization term that encourages exploration. The leaderвҖҷs policy is then
022              updated through a greedy planning step based on these estimates. Our algorithm
023              achieves a sublinear regret in the context of general function approximation. More-
024              over, this algorithm avoids the intractable optimistic planning and thus enhances
025              implementation simplicity.

028   1     Introduction
029
030   Stackelberg games are a class of games that feature strategic decision-making under a leader-follower
031   structure. These games find broad applications in various domains, such as economics, finance,
032   societal systems, and so on (He et al., 2007; Von Stackelberg, 2010; Keyhani, 2003; Sinha et al.,
033
      2013; Ghosh & De, 2021; Koh et al., 2020; Qiu et al., 2021). In the simplest two-player case, the two
      players are referred to as the leader and follower, respectively. These two players have misaligned
034
      objectives and different information structures, and their interactions can be sequential and dynamic.
035
036   In this game, the leader has more advantages in the sense that she can regularize the followerвҖҷs
037   behavior by announcing her policy before the two players take actions and promising to commit
038   to it. In that case, the leaderвҖҷs policy becomes common knowledge. The follower, knowing the
039
      leaderвҖҷs policy, determines his policy by solving his decision-making problem determined by both
      the leaderвҖҷs policy and the followerвҖҷs reward function. As a result, the followerвҖҷs policy is a strategic
040
      response to the leaderвҖҷs policy, and such a mapping (the response model) depends on the followerвҖҷs
041
      reward function. From the leaderвҖҷs point of view, the response model specifies how the followers
042   strategically interact with the leader, and the leader aims to maximize her cumulative rewards in
043   expectation. The leaderвҖҷs policy that maximizes her cumulative rewards in the presence of the
044   strategic follower, together with the followerвҖҷs response policy, constitutes a Stackelberg equilibrium
045   of the game. This notion characterizes the optimal behavior of such a leader-follower game.
046
      While there have been many existing works proposing sample-efficient multi-agent reinforcement
047
      learning (MARL) algorithms for solving dynamic games, the study of solving Stackelberg equilibria
048   from data via MARL is relatively scarce. Most of these works focus on Nash-(Perolat et al., 2017),
049   Correlated-(Cigler & Faltings, 2011), or coarse correlated equilibria(Sessa et al., 2022) of Markov
050   games. When it comes to Stackelberg equilibria, the hierarchical and strategic nature make it hard to
051   learn from data. The main challenge lies in the estimation of the response model of the follower. When
052   the response model is unknown to the leader, she needs to infer the response model, or equivalently,
053   estimate the followerвҖҷs reward function from data. This entails a challenging exploration problem вҖ“
      the leader has to find a sequence of policies such that the followerвҖҷs responses to them are sufficiently

054
      informative. Moreover, as shown in Bai et al. (2021), such a problem is ill-posed when the follower
055   is fully rational, i.e., returning a deterministic reward-maximizing action. In this case, even if the
056   followerвҖҷs reward function is accurately estimated, the resulting estimated response model still has a
057   considerable error.
058
      To address this challenge, Chen et al. (2023) propose to study Markov Stackelberg games (MSG) with
059
      the follower adopting a quantal response model. That is, the follower solves an entropy-regularized
060
      reward maximization problem and the response policy is stochastic. In this case, after announcing a
061   policy and observing the action taken by the follower, the leader can estimate the followerвҖҷs reward
062   function via maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). Based on this observation, in the online setting,
063   Chen et al. (2023) proposes a sample-efficient algorithm based on optimistic planning, in the context
064   of general function approximation. In particular, their algorithm constructs a confidence for the
065   followerвҖҷs reward function via MLE, and a confidence set for the leaderвҖҷs value function using the
066   Bellman error. However, due to the hierarchical structure, this Bellman error takes the followerвҖҷs
067   reward function as a parameter. As a result, optimistic planning over these coupled confidence sets
068   is highly intractable. Therefore, the following question remains elusive:
069
                   Can we design a sample-efficient and easy-to-implement MARL framework
070
                     for Markov Stackelberg games with general function approximation?
071
072   In this paper, we provide an affirmative answer to this question. Focusing on the online setting
073   of Markov Stackelberg games where the follower is myopic and boundedly rational, we propose an
074   easy-to-implement algorithm, dubbed Planning after Estimation (PES). In particular, in each episode,
075   the algorithm updates the leaderвҖҷs policy in two steps. First, in the estimation step, we estimate the
076   leaderвҖҷs value function and the followerвҖҷs quantal response model together using a combined loss
077
      function. This loss function combines (i) the likelihood loss for estimating the followerвҖҷs reward
      function, (ii) the Bellman loss for estimating the leaderвҖҷs value function, and (iii) an additional term
078
      that promotes exploration. Such an exploration-promoting term is defined as the expected rewards
079
      of the leader based on the given value function and response model. In the second step, based
080   on the estimated value function and response model, we update the leaderвҖҷs policy by solving the
081   greedy policy. Compared to the optimistic planning algorithm proposed in Chen et al. (2023), our
082   algorithm circumvents intractable optimistic planning, which involves joint planning of the leaderвҖҷs
083   policy, value function, and the вҲҡfollowerвҖҷs quantal response model. Furthermore, we prove that
084   PES achieves a sublinear OМғ (рқ‘‘ рқ‘җ рқ‘Ү)-regret with general function approximation, where рқ‘‘ рқ‘җ is the
085   decoupling coefficient (Xiong et al., 2022) that captures the complexity of the employed function
086   classes and рқ‘Ү is the number of episodes. As a result, our PES is provably sample efficient and
087   amenable to implementation at the same time. Furthermore, as a concrete example, we instantiate
088   the leader-follower game to the problem of reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF),
089   demonstrating the efficacy of our algorithm.

091   2   Related Work
092
093   Online Stackelberg Games. Most existing works on learning Stackelberg equilibria in (Markov)
094   games via online RL assume the follower is myopic and perfectly rational (Bai et al., 2021; Zhong
095   et al., 2023; Kao et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023). In specific, Bai et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023) focus
096   on the static setting. Bai et al. (2021) consider a centralized setting where the central controller can
097   determine the actions taken by both the leader and the follower, and Zhao et al. (2023) assume the
098   follower is omniscient in the sense that the follower always plays the best response policy, which is
099
      similar to our setting. They show that when the follower is perfectly rational, the regret of the leader
      exhibits different scenarios depending on the relationship between the leaderвҖҷs and the followerвҖҷs
100
      rewards. Besides, Kao et al. (2022) assume that the leader and follower are cooperative and design a
101
      decentralized algorithm for both the leader and follower, under the tabular setting. Zhong et al. (2023)
102   study online and offline RL for the leader, assuming the followerвҖҷs reward function is known, and thus
103   the best response of the follower is known to the leader. Our work is more related and comparable
104   to Chen et al. (2023). In particular, Chen et al. (2023) extensively studied Markov Stackelberg
105   games in the context of general function approximation. They proposed an algorithm framework,
106   which is provably sample efficient under assumptions that the follower is bounded rational and either
107   myopic or farsighted. However, they constructed confidence sets for the response model and leaderвҖҷs
      value function and introduced optimistic planning (Auer et al., 2008) to update the leaderвҖҷs policies.

108
      Such a method involves joint planning of the leaderвҖҷs policy and value function, and the followerвҖҷs
109   quantal response model, so the planning steps become computationally intractable, which means the
110   algorithm is very hard to be implemented in practice. In this paper, we propose our PES algorithm
111   to overcome this drawback. Instead of using tedious optimistic planning, we exploit the benign
112   property of the Shannon entropy function to recover the followerвҖҷs reward function via his policy.
113   After estimating the reward function, we execute the planning step by solving the вҖқgreedyвҖқ policy.
114   Compared with Chen et al. (2023), our algorithm is not only easy-to-implement but also easier to
115   show theoretical guarantee.
116   Online RL with General Function Approximation. Recently, various works propose RL algo-
117   rithms in the context of general function approximation (Jiang et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2019; Jin et al.,
118   2021; Xiong et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a). Among these works, Our work is most relevant to Jin
119   et al. (2021); Xiong et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024a). Specifically, Jin et al. (2021); Xiong et al. (2022)
120   introduce the Multi-agent decoupling coefficient that characterizes the exploration difficulty of the
121   Markov Decision Process (MDP) problems. In Section 5, we introduce similar notions of decoupling
122   coefficient for learning the leaderвҖҷs optimal policy. In particular, we introduce two versions of the
123
      decoupling coefficient that capture the complexity of the leaderвҖҷs Bellman error and the followerвҖҷs
      quantal response error. Besides, Liu et al. (2024a) proposed an easy-to-implement RL algorithm
124
      framework named Maximize to Explore (MEX) and instantiating MEX on the 2-player zero-sum
125
      game setting. However, their algorithm framework can not be easily instantiated in MSG, because the
126   followerвҖҷs Bellman error is not accessible in our setting, since either the followerвҖҷs reward function
127   or his realized rewards remains unknown.

533   In this paper, we propose an easy-to-implement RL algorithm, Planning after Estimation (PES) to
534   efficiently solve MSG in the context of general function approximation. Compared to the other
535   concurrent works , our algorithm circumvents intractable optimistic planning, which involves joint
536   planning of the leaderвҖҷs policy, value function, and the followerвҖҷs quantal response model. In the
537   theoretical analysis,
                    вҲҡ       we prove that with a set of simple hyper-parameter choices, PES achieves a sub-
538   linear OМғ (рқ‘‘ рқ‘җ рқ‘Ү)-regret with general function approximation, where рқ‘‘ рқ‘җ is the decoupling coefficient
539   and рқ‘Ү is the number of episodes. At last, we apply PES to the RLHF setting by formulating the
      RLHF as a turn-based Stackelberg game to demonstrate the efficacy of our algorithm..
