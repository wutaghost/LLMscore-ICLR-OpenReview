008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            In reinforcement learning, a generalizable world model to mimic the environment
011            is crucial for the assessment of various policy values in downstream tasks such
012            as offline policy optimization and off-policy evaluation. Recently, studies have
013            shown that learning a world model with sparse connections identified by causal
014            discovery techniques can improve generalizability. So far, these studies focus on
015            discovering a single and global causal structure. In this paper, we discuss a more
016            practical setting in which the agent is deployed in an environment mixed with dif-
017            ferent causal mechanisms, called superposed causal relationships in this article. In
018
               this case, global causal discovery techniques will derive a degraded dense causal
               relationship, which will fail to improve the generalizability of the learned model.
019
               To solve the problem, we propose Superposed cAusal Model (SAM) learning.
020
               SAM learning is an end-to-end framework that learns a transformer-based model
021            which can recognize the causal relationships that the agent is encountering on the
022            fly and then adapts its predictions. The experiments are conducted in two simu-
023            lated environments, where SAM shows powerful identify abilities in environments
024            with superposed causal relationships. Both the dynamics model and the policies
025            learned by the SAM generalize well to unseen states.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Learning an accurate environment model that approximates state transitions is crucial in fields such
032   as offline reinforcement learning (offline RL) (Levine et al., 2020b). By utilizing world models,
033
      costly real-world trial-and-error processes can be avoided. The primary role of these models is to
      unbiasedly answer counterfactual queries; that is, given certain states, they can correctly predict what
034
      might happen if we were to execute actions unseen in the training data. Previous studies have shown
035
      that learning a causal world model—which identifies causal relationships and employs sparse con-
036   nections for environment model learning—can improve generalization ability (Wang et al., 2022).
037
038   In these studies, sparse connection patterns are identified using causal structure discovery tech-
039
      niques (Ding et al., 2022). These patterns skip connections between the current state-action pair and
      the next state when no causal relationship exists, enhancing model efficiency. Unfortunately, most
040
      existing methods assume that a single causal model governs the entire dataset. However, in many
041
      real-world decision-making tasks, data exhibit multimodal characteristics and substantial hetero-
042   geneity. For instance, in dynamic resource allocation tasks, the causal relationships between actions
043   and outcomes can differ across various contexts or states within the same environment, reflecting an
044   underlying multimodal causal structure. Similarly, the causal relationships between driving strate-
045   gies and environmental feedback can vary under different traffic conditions (such as rush hour vs.
046   non-rush hour, city streets vs. highways), highlighting the multimodal nature of causal structures
047   within the same environment.
048   In this paper, we aim to advance causal world model learning by transitioning from single causal
049   world model learning to superposed causal world model learning, indicating that the environments
050   we encounter encompass multiple causal relationships. Currently, the challenge of identifying mix-
051   tures of causal graphs from data has not been extensively explored in the literature. Most recent
052   works, such as (Varambally et al., 2024), have addressed the challenge of inferring causal models
053   from mixture distributions. However, these approaches do not tackle sequential decision-making
      tasks.

      Specifically, we consider environments for decision-making that include an unknown number of
076
      causal relationships. Each time an episode is reset, a new causal relationship is generated, affecting
077   the transition process and the optimal action (see Figure 1 for detailed comparison). To this end,
078   we propose a causal world model learning algorithm, Superposed cAusal Model (SAM) learning.
079   SAM is a general end-to-end world model learning algorithm that identifies the causal relationships
080   of the current episode on the fly and predicts transitions based on the identified causal relationships.
081   SAM is built upon the Transformer architecture to infer causal relationships from past interaction
082   trajectories, with a unified training objective based on the evidence lower bound of the trajectory
083   generation likelihood in our setting. Thanks to the minimalist design of the implementation, SAM is
084   generally effective across different tasks and environments. We verify this through extensive exper-

      iments.

089   2.1    C AUSAL W ORLD M ODEL IN R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
090
091   Model-based RL improves policy learning by constructing environment models, including dynamics
092   and reward predictors, through maximizing the likelihood of collected trajectories (Moerland et al.,
093
      2023). In online RL, these models enhance sample efficiency by aiding planning (Buesing et al.,
      2018; Hafner et al., 2019; Argenson & Dulac-Arnold, 2021), generating synthetic data (Janner et al.,
094
      2019; Hafner et al., 2020; Kaiser et al., 2020), or refining Q-value estimates (Feinberg et al., 2018;
095
      Amos et al., 2021). For offline RL (Levine et al., 2020a), the learned model facilitates effective
096   offline policy evaluation (Argenson & Dulac-Arnold, 2021) and policy optimization by generating
097   counterfactual trajectories (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020).
098
099
      Integrating causality into reinforcement learning (RL) has attracted substantial interest due to its
      benefits in generalization and transferability, particularly in representation learning (Huang et al.,
100
      2022; Liu et al., 2023), policy learning (Buesing et al., 2019; Mozifian et al., 2020), and dynam-
101
      ics learning (Wang et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Hwang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2022). Within
102   the realm of causal dynamics learning, Wang et al. (2022) explicitly learn causal dependencies by
103   regulating the number of variables used to predict each state variable. Ding et al. (2022) enhance
104   Goal-Conditioned RL with a causal graph to create generalizable models and interpretable policies.
105   Hwang et al. (2024) propose a dynamics model that infers fine-grained causal structures, using a
106   discrete latent variable to segment the state-action space to different fine-grained graph for model
107   prediction. Zhu et al. (2022) theoretically demonstrate that causal world-models outperform non-
      causal models in offline RL by integrating causal structures into generalization error bounds. Unlike

      these approaches that discover a single, global causal structure, this paper addresses a more prac-
109   tical scenario where the agent operates in an environment with mixed causal mechanisms, termed
110   superposed causal relationships. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to address super-
111   posed causal relationships in model-based RL, demonstrating that this approach not only identifies
112   ground-truth causal structures but also outperforms baselines in generalization.

115   2.2   M ULTIPLE C AUSAL S TRUCTURED G RAPH D ISCOVERY
116
117   Recently, researchers have begun exploring causal discovery in scenarios where causal relationships
118   between variables vary across domains. Non-deep learning methods, such as SSCM (Huang et al.,
119   2019), model both variability and consistency in causal relationships, leveraging commonalities to
120   achieve statistically reliable estimates while preserving individual causal characteristics. Markham
121
      et al. (2022) proposed a kernel function based on distance covariance to measure causal structure
      similarity, identifying homogeneous subgroups through clustering. However, these methods often
122
      require manual design of scoring functions and can be computationally intensive, limiting scalabil-
123
      ity to large datasets. On the other hand, neural network-based approaches (Löwe et al., 2022; Lorch
124   et al., 2022; Varambally et al., 2024) treat causal discovery as a black-box problem, assuming a
125   distribution in the data and using variational inference to uncover domain-specific inductive biases.
126   Our method is inspired by them but differs in that we consider environments for decision-making
127   that include an unknown number of causal relationships. Each time an episode is reset, a new causal
128   relationship is generated, affecting the transition process and the optimal action. However, these
129   methods often require manual scoring function design and can be computationally intensive, lim-
130   iting scalability to large datasets. In contrast, neural network-based approaches(Löwe et al., 2022;
131   Lorch et al., 2022; Varambally et al., 2024) treat causal discovery as a black-box problem, assuming
132
      data distribution and using variational inference to uncover domain-specific biases. Our method is
      inspired by these but differs by focusing on decision-making environments with an unknown number
133
      of causal relationships. Each episode reset introduces a new causal relationship, affecting transitions
134
      and optimal actions, creating a novel RL setting distinct from previous multiple causal structured
135   graph discovery literature.

      search-based methods. For example, CDL (Wang et al., 2022) utilizes conditional mutual infor-
193

194

195   have high computational cost, cannot scale to larger environments, and require carefully designed
196   search processes tailored to the task, this paper aims to propose a method that is scalable in terms of
197   data volume. Therefore, unlike previous work, we employ a score-based method to discover causal
198   masks from offline datasets, which models the entire causal graph discovery as a differentiable opti-
199   mization objective, enabling end-to-end graph discovery and prediction. We derive the optimization

234

236            decision-making data with multiple causal relationships (see Section 5.2)?
237         2. RQ2: Does superposed causal graph learning simplify the learning transition and enhance
238            generalizability compared to single-graph models or dense models (see Sections 5.3)?
239         3. RQ3: Can SAM identify causal relationships encountered by the agent in real time to
240            facilitate better decision-making (see Section 5.4)?

242   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
243
244   Most reinforcement learning environments lack explicit causal graphs for generalization purposes.
245   To evaluate our methods, we constructed benchmarks based on the widely recognized Chemical
246   environment (Ke et al., 2021) and a custom-designed Minigrid environment Chevalier-Boisvert et al.
247
      (2023) as shown in Figure 2. For each environment, we evaluate the model under two settings: the
      target setting and the spurious setting. In the target setting, the policy aims to perform on one of the
248
      latent causal tasks with the model trained on the superposed causal dataset. In the spurious setting,
249
      the policy is tested in an environment with noise on spurious correlation, as shown in the following,
250   which is the key setting to demonstrate the generalization ability. We conducted experiments in the
251   offline setting, where each environment encompasses multiple settings of latent causal relationships.
252   Our dataset comprises 200,000 transition steps for each environment.
253
254         1. Mixed-Chemical: The Chemical environment (Ke et al., 2021) consists of 10 colored
255            nodes, where color changes are governed by an underlying causal graph. At each step, the
256            agent changes the color of one node, causing its descendant nodes in the causal graph to
257
               change color sequentially. The goal is to change all node colors to a desired target color.
               We modified the environment to include nine randomly generated, unknown causal graphs,
258
               transforming it into a multi-causal graph reinforcement learning environment. Data col-
259
               lection was performed using a random policy, generating datasets in the offline setting. In
260            the spurious setting, the agent receives noisy observations for certain nodes. By disentan-
261            gling the superposed causal graphs, the agent can ignore spurious correlations and improve
262            generalization.
263
            2. Confusing-Minigrid: Minigrid Chevalier-Boisvert et al. (2023) is a grid-based environ-
264            ment where the agent aims to navigate around obstacles to reach a target grid in as few
265            steps as possible. We introduced multiple latent causal graphs that determine whether ob-
266            stacles can be moved or covered with the agent and other obstacles, resulting in diverse
267            environmental transitions. We name the tasks with the format of M {?} − C{?} to de-
268            note the properties of the obstacles. This transforms the environment into a multi-causal
269            graph reinforcement learning setting. Given the complexity of the state space, we utilized
               an early-stopping Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm for data collection. In

520   6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522   In this paper, we introduced Superposed cAusal Model (SAM), a novel approach for learning su-
523   perposed causal world models that can handle multiple, dynamic causal relationships in sequential
524   decision-making environments. SAM addresses the limitations of existing causal world models,
525
      which assume a single causal structure governs the entire dataset. By allowing for the identification
      of distinct causal relationships across different episodes, SAM significantly improves generalization,
526
      especially in heterogeneous environments where causal dynamics vary across contexts. Through ex-
527
      tensive experiments in environments like Mixed-Chemical and Confusing-Maze, we demonstrated
528   that SAM effectively learns and adapts causal relationships in real-time. Our results show that SAM
529   outperforms existing baselines, particularly in challenging spurious settings where noise and multi-
530   modal causal structures obscure traditional models’ performance. Unlike dense models that strug-
531   gle with redundant relationships and causal models that cannot handle mixtures of causal graphs,
532   SAM robustly disentangles overlapping causal structures, leading to superior prediction accuracy
533   and decision-making performance across various tasks.
534   SAM’s ability to generalize across complex and evolving environments suggests its potential for
535   real-world applications in areas like dynamic resource allocation and autonomous systems. Despite
536   its promising results, SAM has some limitations, especially that SAM’s computational cost increases
537   with the complexity of the environment due to the use of a Transformer-based architecture. Future
538   work could explore scaling SAM to larger datasets, further improving its efficiency, and investigating
539   its applicability in broader decision-making contexts where causal structures are even more diverse
      and challenging.
