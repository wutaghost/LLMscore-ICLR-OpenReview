      1    Introduction
023
024
      Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) aims to solve a sequential decision making
025
      problem, where a number of agents sharing an environment collaborates. Accompanied by
026   advancements in algorithms (Sunehag et al., 2017; Rashid et al., 2020), MARL has shown
027   impressive success in various fields such as robotics (de Witt et al., 2020) and autonomous
028   driving (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2016). Beyond its empirical success, there has also been
029   notable interest in theoretical investigations (Zhang et al., 2018b; Dou et al., 2022).
030
      MARL has been studied under various scenarios including an access to central reward
031
      function (Tan, 1993; Claus and Boutilier, 1998; Littman, 2001). In particular, our interest
032   lies in the the distributed learning paradigm where agents collaborate to solve a shared
033   problem, constrained to communicate solely with their neighboring agents and does not
034   have access to central reward function. Such setting has came of interest due to its wide
035   applications (Blumenkamp et al., 2022; Prabuchandran et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2021).
036   Compared to scenarios where a centralized coordinate exists, the distributed paradigm has
037   advantage in terms of privacy-preservation and scalability. One notable example is the
038   distributed adaptation of temporal-difference (TD) learning, as demonstrated in studies
039   by Doan et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020); Lim and Lee (2023), to name a few.
040   Meanwhile, in the literature of single-agent RL, Q-learning (Watkins and Dayan, 1992) is
041   one of the most important algorithms in RL. The non-linear max-operator in Q-learning
042   algorithm imposes difficulty in the analysis, and its non-asymptotic analysis has been an
043   active research area recently (Even-Dar et al., 2003; Chen et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2023;
044   Li et al., 2024). However, distributed learning framework for Q-learning has not been
045   studied in detail. In particular, distributed Q-learning has been studied in an asymptotic
046
      sense (Kar et al., 2013), i.e., the algorithm converges over time as it approaches infinity,
      or in a non-asymptotic sense under additional assumptions on the problem (Heredia et al.,
047
      2020; Zeng et al., 2022b). Wang et al. (2022) studied a version of distributed Q-learning in
048
      tabular setting but differs from the one in Kar et al. (2013). This motivates our study to
049   understand its non-asymptotic behavior under tabular setup, i.e., all the state-action values
050   are stored in a table. Our contribution can be summarized as follows:

064   Related Works:
065   The non-asymptotic behavior of distributed TD-learning was studied in Doan et al. (2019);
066   Sun et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020); Lim and Lee (2023), which were motivated from the
067   distributed optimization and control literature (Nedic and Ozdaglar, 2009; Wang and Elia,
068   2010; Pu and Nedić, 2021). Distributed versions of various TD-learning algorithms were
069   investigated in Macua et al. (2014); Lee et al. (2018). As for actor-critic algorithm (Konda
070   and Tsitsiklis, 1999), its extension to distributed setting was studied in Zhang et al. (2018a;b);
071   Zhang and Zavlanos (2019); Zeng et al. (2022a). Meanwhile, Yang et al. (2023) considered a
072   distributed policy gradient approach. Moreover, Zhang et al. (2021) investigated distributed
073
      algorithm for fitted Q-iteration, which is similar to solving a least squares problem. Fur-
      thermore, a line of research has focused on dealing with exponential scaling in the action
074
      space Lin et al. (2021); Qu et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2024).
075
076   The distributed Q-learning algorithm under the setting when only the local reward is observ-
077   able, was first studied by Kar et al. (2013). They proposed the so-called QD-learning proving
078   asymptotic convergence using two-time scale stochastic approximation approaches. Zeng
079
      et al. (2022b); Heredia et al. (2020) proved finite-time bounds of distributed Q-learning with
      linear function approximation. However, the works require additional strong assumptions,
080
      which may not hold even in the tabular setup. In particular, Zeng et al. (2022b) considered a
081
      strongly monotone condition to hold, and Heredia et al. (2020) posed a particular assumption
082   on the state-action distribution. Wang et al. (2022) studied a distributed Q-learning model
083   motivated from the adapt-then-combine scheme (Chen and Sayed, 2012) in the distributed
084   optimization literature and provided a sample complexity bound in terms of high-probability.
085
      Considering a single-agent case, the non-asymptotic analysis of Q-learning has made great
086
      success. An incomplete list is provided in the following: An early result by Even-Dar et al.
087
      (2003) studied the sample complexity under i.i.d. observation model. Lee et al. (2023)
088   developed a switched system method to analyze the behavior of Q-learning. Qu and Wierman
089   (2020) considered a shifted Martingale approach to deal with the Markovian observation
090   model. Li et al. (2024) proved the sample complexity using refined analysis under the
091   Markovian observation model.
092
      Meanwhile, a separate line of research focusing on multi-agent problems is the federated
093
      reinforcement learning literature (Khodadadian et al., 2022; Woo et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
094   2023). This approach differs from the distributed learning scenario in two key aspects: it
095   employs a centralized controller, and all agents share a common reward function.
096
097
      The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 provides background for the MARL setting.
      Section 3 provides result under i.i.d. observation model and sketch of the proof. The result
098
      for Markovian observation model is provided in Section 4.

      In this section, we provide comparison with recent works analyzing non-asymptotic behavior
487   of distributed Q-learning algorithm. Our analysis relies on the minimal assumption in
488   sense that we do not require any assumption further than standard assumptions in the
489   literature, e.g., the state-action distribution induced by the behavior policy, is positive for
490   all state-action pairs in Assumption 3.1.
491
       Heredia et al. (2020) considered linear function approximation scheme to represent the
492
      Q-function with continuous state-space and finite-action space scenario. However, to prove
493   the convergence, it requires the following condition:
494
495                                 dmin > γ 2 d∗max := max d(s, π ∗ (s)),                              (15)
                                                           s
496
      which is difficult to be met even in the tabular case, and an example is given in Appendix H.
497
498   Furthermore, Zeng et al. (2022b) considered a Q-learning model under linear function
499   approximation with continuous-state space and finite action space. The work also covered
500   the case when the features for linear function approximation is differently selected for each
501   agents. However, it requires the following condition to hold for all Q ∈ R|S||A| :
                                      ∗                                                        2
502              (γDP (ΠQ Q − ΠQ Q∗ ) − D(Q − Q∗ ))⊤ (Q − Q∗ ) ≤ −κ ∥Q − Q∗ ∥2 ,                        (16)
503
504   for some κ > 0. We have provided examples where the above conditions in (15) and (16) are
505   not met even in the tabular case in Appendix Section H.
506   Overall, the assumptions used in Heredia et al. (2020); Zeng et al. (2022b) allows the analysis
507   to follow similar lines to that of convex optimization literature. To the best of our knowledge,
508   there is no existing literature that demonstrates how to extend convex optimization analysis,
509   or an analogous approach, to the analysis of Q-learning under the tabular setup. This gap in
510   the literature makes the analysis challenging and is the primary reason we rely on switched
511   system analysis. Due to different settings, their sample complexity is not directly comparable
512
      with ours.
513     Wang et al. (2022) proposed a distributed Q-learning algorithm in the tabular setting,
514   which is motivated from the adapt-then-combine algorithm, whereas our algorithm considers
515   combine-and-adapt scheme (Chen and Sayed, 2012) in the distributed optimization literature.
516   The work presents a sharper bound on the sample complexity (1−γ)51dmin ϵ2 compared to ours
                                                                            h
517         1                                                                     1
            6 4
       (1−γ) dmin ϵ2 but it only holds for restricted range of ϵ, i.e., ϵ ∈   0, 1−γ   while our results
518
      do not have such restriction. More importantly, the algorithm proposed by Wang et al.
519
      (2022) requires two steps for a single update, whereas in our paper, we focus on a one-step
520   algorithm that is algorithmically simpler and more efficient. Specifically, we analyze the
521   traditional and widely adopted QD-learning algorithm proposed in Kar et al. (2013), for
522   which a finite-time error analysis for the original form has been lacking in the literature.
523   Additionally, we enhance the efficiency of QD-learning by employing a constant step-size,
524   as opposed to the two-time-scale decaying step-size used in traditional QD-learning. This
525   modification can significantly improve the convergence speed empirically.
