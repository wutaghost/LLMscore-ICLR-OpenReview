011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision-language pre-trained models (VLP) exhibit remarkable capabilities in pro-
014            cessing images and textual information. However, they are vulnerable to multi-
015            modal adversarial examples. Notably, adversarial examples generated for a spe-
016            cific model can potentially deceive different models, known as adversarial trans-
017            ferability. The potential threats posed by adversarial transferability to models in
018
               practical applications have heightened interest in studying the transferability of
               adversarial examples. Recent works have indicated that leveraging data augmen-
019
               tation and image-text modal interactions can significantly enhance the transfer-
020
               ability of adversarial examples for VLP models. A crucial aspect of this im-
021            provement hinges on how information between different modalities is aligned.
022            Despite this, they have overlooked the critical issue of finding the optimal align-
023            ment between data-augmented image-text pairs. This oversight creates adversarial
024            examples overly customized to the source model, consequently restricting their
025            transferability potential. In our research, we first explore the interplay between
026            image sets produced through data augmentation and their corresponding text sets.
027            We find that augmented image samples can align more effectively with specific
028            texts while exhibiting less relevance to others. Motivated by this, we propose an
029
               Optimal Transport-based Adversarial Attack, dubbed OT-Attack. The proposed
               method formulates the features of image and text sets as two distinct distribu-
030
               tions and employs optimal transport theory to determine the most efficient map-
031
               ping between them. This optimal mapping informs our generation of adversarial
032            examples to enhance their transferability. Extensive experiments across various
033            network architectures and datasets in image-text matching tasks show that our
034            OT-Attack is more transferable to unseen target models than existing methods.

045

050   adversarial examples remain effective even in the absence of detailed knowledge about the model’s
051   inner workings, such as its model architecture, weights, and gradients, etc (Han et al., 2023; Gu
052   et al., 2023). Motivated by the practical significance of transfer-based adversarial attacks and adver-
053   sarial transferability (Gubri et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2022; Byun et al., 2022; Waseda et al., 2023),
      in this paper, we primarily study the transferability of adversarial examples across VLP models.

      2     BACKGROUND A ND R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Vision-Language Pre-trained Models. Vision-language pre-training (VLP) (Chen et al., 2023) is a
119
      pivotal technique in augmenting multimodal task performance, capitalizing on extensive pre-training
      with image-to-text pairs. Traditionally, much of the research in this area has relied on pre-trained ob-
120
      ject detectors, using region features to create vision-language representations. However, the advent
121
      of Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022) has instigated a method-
122   ological shift. Increasingly, studies advocate adopting ViT in image encoding, which involves an
123   end-to-end process of transforming inputs into patches. VLP models can be broadly classified into
124   fused and aligned VLP models. Fused VLP models, as exemplified by architectures like ALBEF (Li
125   et al., 2021) and TCL (Yang et al., 2022), utilize individual unimodal encoders for processing token
126   and visual feature embeddings. These models then employ a multimodal encoder to amalgamate im-
127   age and text embeddings, crafting comprehensive multimodal representations. Conversely, aligned
128   VLP models use unimodal encoders to process image and text modality embeddings independently.
129   Vision-Language Tasks. Image-text retrieval. Image-Text Retrieval (ITR) (Cao et al., 2022; Li
130   et al., 2023) is a task that retrieves relevant instances from a database using one modality (image
131   or text) to query the other. It splits into image-to-text retrieval (TR) and text-to-image retrieval
132   (IR). Models like ALBEF and TCL calculate semantic similarity scores between image-text pairs
133   for initial ranking, then employ a multimodal encoder for final ranking. Conversely, models like
134   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) directly rank based on similarity in an unimodal embedding space,
135   showcasing varied ITR methodologies. Image captioning. Image captioning (Hossain et al., 2019;
136
      Ghandi et al., 2023) generates textual captions for images and is crucial in VLP models. This
      task requires converting visual content into coherent, contextually relevant text, which differs from
137
      image-text retrieval. Visual grounding. Visual Grounding (Deng et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2023)
138
      entails identifying and locating objects or regions in an image per language descriptions, requiring
139   precise text mapping to visual elements.
140
141   Transferability of Adversarial Examples. Co-Attack by Zhang et al. (2022) integrates visual
142
      and textual attacks, exploiting VLP model multimodality. The Set-level Guidance Attack (SGA)
      advances this by aligning augmented images with multiple texts, enhancing adversarial example
143
      transferability across black-box models. The shift from individual to integrated attacks like Co-
144
      Attack and SGA illustrates the evolution of adversarial strategy against VLP models.
145
146   Optimal Transport. Optimal Transport (OT), a concept first introduced by Monge (Villani et al.,
147   2009), its unique ability to match distributions has led to its widespread application in various the-
148
      oretical and practical areas. This includes its use in generative models and structural alignments
      involving sequences (Arjovsky et al., 2017), graphs (Xu et al., 2019), and image matching (Zhang
149
      et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021).

156   We analyze two scenarios: white-box and black-box attacks. In a white-box model Mwhite , the
157   adversary has complete access to the model’s architecture, parameters, and gradients, which allows
158
      for direct optimization to generate adversarial examples. In contrast, a black-box model Mblack is
      opaque, restricting the adversary to indirect methods based on observed outputs or behavior.
159
160   This work focuses on generating adversarial examples on a white-box model and leveraging these
161   examples to attack a black-box model. This approach is aimed at evaluating the transferability of
      adversarial examples and the effectiveness of attack strategies. A well-designed loss function L

213
214   Defining Source (P) and Target (Y) Distributions. In the Optimal Transport framework, we
215   begin by defining two fundamental distributions: the source distribution P and the target distribution
      Y. These distributions represent the starting and ending points of the transportation process in the


                                                        4
216
      Optimal Transport problem. Specifically, the source distribution P = (p1 , p2 , . . . , pn ) and the
217   target distribution Y = (y1 , y2 , . . . , ym ) describe the quantities to be transported from and to each
218   respective location.
219
220
      The Transportation Matrix T. In the context of Optimal Transport, the matrix T = [Tij ] of size
      n × m is referred to as the transportation matrix. Each element Tij represents the amount of a
221
      commodity or resource transported from the i-th source in P to the j-th target in Y. This matrix
222
      effectively captures the transportation scheme between the sources and targets.
223
224   The matrix T must satisfy certain constraints to ensure an optimal transportation plan. The Marginal
225   Constraints are:

529
530
531
      We propose an Optimal Transport-based Adversarial Attack, dubbed OT-Attack. The proposed OT-
      Attack formulates the features of image and text sets as two distinct distributions, leveraging optimal
532
      transport theory to identify the most efficient mapping between them. It utilizes their mutual similar-
533
      ity as the cost matrix. The derived optimal mapping guides the generation of adversarial examples,
534   effectively improving adversarial transferability. Extensive experiments across diverse network ar-
535   chitectures and datasets in image-text matching tasks demonstrate the superior performance of the
536   proposed OT-Attack in terms of adversarial transferability. Significantly, our results also show that
537   OT-Attack is also effective in cross-task attacks, including image captioning and visual grounding,
538   and poses a considerable challenge to commercial models such as GPT-4 and Bing Chat, highlight-
539   ing the evolving landscape of adversarial threats in advanced AI applications. This underscores the
      need for robust defenses against sophisticated attacks.

      F.2   C ONVERGENCE T HRESHOLD AND S INKHORN I TERATION L IMIT
958
959   The convergence threshold (thresh) and the iteration limit of the Sinkhorn algorithm are strongly
960   interdependent. The convergence of the Sinkhorn algorithm ensures that the transport matrix P =
961   diag(r) · K · diag(c) satisfies the prescribed marginal distributions u and v. Convergence is typically
962   assessed by measuring the change in the scaling factors r or c between successive iterations, where
963   the error metric (e.g., ∥r(k) − r(k−1) ∥) must fall below a predefined threshold ϵ. Alternatively,
964   convergence can be determined by the deviation of the row and column sums of P from u and v. A
965   maximum iteration limit is often imposed to prevent infinite loops.
966   In this study, the default settings were thresh = 1.00 × 10−2 and an iteration limit of 100. First,
967   we analyzed the average number of iterations under thresh = 1.00 × 10−2 , finding that the mean
968   iteration count for generating 1,000 adversarial samples was only 2.3. Further, we examined the
969   error scalar after each iteration. The error scalar starts at the order of 1.00 × 103 after the first
970   iteration, reaches 1.00 × 10−2 within two iterations, and decreases to 1.00 × 10−3 or 1.00 × 10−4
971   after three iterations. This analysis indicates that thresh should range between 1.00 × 103 and
      1.00 × 10−6 . If thresh exceeds 1.00 × 103 , the Sinkhorn algorithm converges in just one iteration.
