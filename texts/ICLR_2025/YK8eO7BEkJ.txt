009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Normalization layers are crucial for improving the training efficiency and stability
012
               of deep neural network architectures. The recently proposed Mamba network has
               demonstrated significant potential in competing with Transformers. However, as
013            with many deep architectures, the training stability of Mamba remains a signifi-
014            cant challenge, and normalization techniques are key to addressing this issue. In
015            this paper, we systematically investigate the effects of normalization type, position
016            and combinations on the Mamba Block. On the one hand, we conducted extensive
               experiments to evaluate the impact of applying various normalization layers before
017
               or after the SSM module(the core module of Mamba Block). On the other hand,
018            we performed thorough experiments to assess the effects of combining diverse
019            normalization techniques before and after the SSM module. Our analysis encom-
020            passes both long sequence modeling and image classification tasks. The results
021            show that applying normalization layers after the SSM module (if used only once)
               and combining different normalization layers before and after the SSM module
022            can enhance training stability and improve Mamba performance. Furthermore, we
023            provide practical recommendations for selecting appropriate normalization tech-
024            niques in designing Mamba architectures and validated them on other datasets.
025            We hope that our insights will help mitigate training instabilities in deep learning
026
               and foster the development of more robust architectures. All codes and models
               used in this study will be open-sourced on GitHub.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The Mamba architecture is a state-of-the-art model designed to efficiently handle long sequences in
032   sequence modeling by leveraging selective structured state space models (Selective SSM) (Gu et al.,
033   2021). However, training Mamba presents significant challenges due to the inherent unpredictabil-
034   ity of SSM (Hamilton, 1994). Normalization plays a crucial role in effectively training deep neural
035
      networks (Huang et al., 2023), which provides a potential solution for training Mamba. There are
      various usages of normalization techniques in the variants of Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023;
036   Bai et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024; Ting et al., 2024), but without providing the
037   sufficient evidence for supporting why one should use like that. Are there any patterns in the use of
038   normalization in Mamba? And how can we better utilize normalization techniques in Mamba? To
039   answer these questions, it is necessary to conduct a comprehensive investigation into various nor-
      malization strategies. In this study, we systematically evaluate the effect of normalization types,
040
      positions, and combinations on Mamba’s performance and stability in sequence modeling and im-
041   age classification tasks. We also provide valuable recommendations on how to select normalization
042   techniques when designing new Mamba frameworks.
043
      For normalization types, different normalization methods have distinct characteristics, functions,
044   and applicable scenarios. Mamba and its variants have utilized diverse normalization methods (Gu
045   & Dao, 2023; Bai et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024; Ting et al., 2024). However,
046   no work has yet explored the impact of these normalization methods on Mamba’s performance or
047
      provided general recommendations on choosing a suitable normalization technique. To address this,
      we explore the impact of several widely used normalization techniques, including BN, LN, GN, IN,
048   and RMSN, in both sequence modeling and image classification tasks.
049
050
      For normalization positions, using normalization layers at different positions can adjust the data
      distribution at those locations, thereby affecting the model’s performance. Currently, many Mamba
051   variants utilize normalization layers at different positions relative to the SSM module, such as (Ma
052   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024d; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024e). This raises a
053   question: what is the optimal position to apply normalization? To address this question, we explored
      the impact of applying normalization layers before or after SSM on Mamba’s performance.

054
      For normalization combinations, Different normalization methods adjust data distributions from
055   various perspectives. Currently, several Mamba variants adopt different normalization methods be-
056   fore and after the SSM. On one hand, some variants use the same normalization method both before
057   and after SSM, such as (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen & Ge, 2024; Bai et al., 2024b; Gong et al., 2024).
058   On the other hand, some variants use different normalization methods before and after SSM, respec-
      tively, such as (Fan et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024a; Hatamizadeh & Kautz, 2024; Qu et al., 2024;
059
      Ju & Zhou, 2024;?). Can combining different normalization methods achieve better performance?
060   What is the optimal combination strategy? These questions remain unanswered. Therefore, it is
061   necessary to explore the impact of applying different combinations of normalizations on Mamba’s
062   performance. To this end, we investigated the effects of pairwise combinations among five common
063
      normalization methods.
064   We analyzed the results of the above three exploratory experiments. Our analysis, rooted in the
065   scale invariance of deep learning (Papyan, 2018), revealed that the weight matrices (Huang et al.,
066
      2020) in deeper Mamba blocks exhibited significantly larger L2 norms (Luo et al., 2016) than
      those in earlier layers. Introducing normalization before the SSM provides almost no help with this
067   issue, whereas placing normalization after the SSM helped the model maintain consistency in the
068   scale of the L2-norms across different layers during training and even making the weight updates
069   more stable, thereby improving training stability. Moreover, applying an appropriate combination of
070   normalization before and after the SSM further helps address this issue, making the model training
      more stable and leading to better performance.
071
072   We also validated our analysis conclusions on other datasets. We selected the optimal Mamba nor-
073   malization scheme and conducted experiments on other sequence modeling and image classification
      tasks. Compared with the original benchmarks, the experimental results demonstrated that our pro-
074
      posed optimization scheme achieved better performance.
075
076   Contributions In this work, we systematically investigate various mainstream normalization tech-
      niques and aim to address the following questions:
077
078   (1) Normalization Type and Position: What is the optimal applicable normalization method? Where
079
      should normalization be placed in the model to ensure high performance? We found that using
      different normalization methods impacts the performance significantly, and applying normalization
080   after SSM generally leads to better model performance than applying it before SSM.
081
082
      (2) Normalization Combination: What are the effects of normalization combinations? And what is
      the intuition behind these effects? We found that certain combinations of different normalization
083   techniques produce more excellent results. A view of the L2 norm of the weight matrix in the
084   Mamba Block can be the institution of this phenomenon.
085
      (3) Combination intuition: How can we choose the proper combination? We propose an intuition
086   for harmonizing normalization strategies in deep architectures, providing practical guidelines for
087   selecting normalization methods. We also validated our findings on other datasets.

090   2   R ELATED W ORK

092   In the Mamba architecture, the SSM module is the core component, and its performance is highly
093   sensitive to the choice of normalization strategies. Normalization adjusts feature distributions to
      improve model stability and generalization. Thus, studying the types of normalization, their posi-
094
      tioning relative to the SSM module, and the impact of combining different normalization methods
095   on performance is crucial. Based on this, we categorize the related works into four groups: no nor-
096   malization, normalization before SSM, normalization after SSM, and combined normalization
097   both before and after SSM, as shown in Figure 1.
098   No Normalization: In this group, many Mamba variants directly optimize the model structure
099   or design task-specific solutions without employing normalization techniques. For example, in
100   sequence tasks, models such as XLSR-Mamba (Xiao & Das, 2024b), SpikMamba (Chen et al.,
101
      2024c), UmambaTSF (Wu et al., 2024c), SWIM (Zhang et al., 2024b), TF-Mamba (Xiao & Das,
      2024a), MetaMamba (Kim, 2024), PackMamba (Xu et al., 2024), MaTrRec (Zhang et al., 2024a),
102   TransMA (Wu et al., 2024b), and ECGMamba (Qiang et al., 2024) have demonstrated good per-
103   formance by refining module structures or parameter-sharing mechanisms. In vision tasks, models
104   like I2I-Mamba (Atli et al., 2024) and DeMamba (Chen et al., 2024a) avoid normalization by de-
105   signing task-driven modules. These works focus on exploring the intrinsic potential of the Mamba
106
      architecture but may face instability in handling complex tasks or multimodal inputs.
107   Normalization before SSM: This strategy aims to stabilize feature distributions before entering the
      SSM module, thereby enhancing its robustness to input features. In sequence tasks, various nor-

      Normalization after SSM: Unlike the previous approach, this strategy focuses on adjusting feature
      distributions after the SSM module to optimize subsequent processing. In sequence tasks, RMSN
139   is used in DIFFIMP (Gao et al., 2024), IN is adopted by Bi-Mamba (Tang et al., 2024), and GN is
140   applied in Mamba2 (Bai et al., 2024b). In vision tasks, LN is employed in IRSRMamba (Huang

142   its ability to fine-tune the feature distributions processed by the SSM module, aligning them with the
143
      requirements of downstream tasks and significantly enhancing model performance in some complex
      scenarios.
144
145   Combined Normalization both before and after SSM: This strategy applies normalization at
      both positions to balance feature distributions and optimize overall performance. In sequence
146
      tasks, some models, such as MambaDC (Chen et al., 2024e), Bi-Mamba (Zhou et al., 2024), and
147   TiM4Rec (Fan et al., 2024), apply LN both before and after SSM. Others use different combina-
148   tions: BMAMBA2 (Bai et al., 2024b) applies GN at both positions, DepMamba (Fan et al., 2024)
149   uses BN before SSM and LN after SSM, while SSD4Rec (Qu et al., 2024) applies LN before SSM
150   and GN after SSM. In vision tasks, similar combined normalization methods are widely applied.
      For instance, models such as ChangeMamba (Chen et al., 2024b), MiM-ISTD (Chen et al., 2024f),
151
      and Fusion-Mamba (Dong et al., 2024b) apply LN both before and after SSM. Hamba (Dong et al.,
152   2024a) and MambaVision (Hatamizadeh & Kautz, 2024) use BN before SSM and LN after SSM,
153   while MSS (Ju & Zhou, 2024) and VM-DDPM (Ju & Zhou, 2024) adopt GN before SSM and LN
154   after SSM. This group demonstrates significant performance improvements, although the optimal
155
      configuration may vary depending on the task and data distribution.
156   In conclusion, applying normalization in the Mamba architecture exhibits diversity and significance.
157   Normalization before SSM effectively stabilizes inputs, making it suitable for handling complex
158
      features. Normalization after SSM is more effective for optimizing downstream task performance.
      Combined normalization methods adjust feature distributions both before and after SSM, offering
159   more opportunities for performance enhancement. However, choosing normalization methods and
160   combination strategies still requires further exploration tailored to specific tasks. Therefore, it is
161   necessary to conduct a systematic investigation into the normalization methods used in Mamba,
      which could provide valuable directions and references for future Mamba framework designs.

220   3.2   N ORMALIZATION T YPES
221
222   Normalization is a critical technique in deep learning aimed at stabilizing and accelerating the train-
223
      ing process of neural networks (Desjardins et al., 2015). It adjusts the input data distribution or inter-
      mediate activations to a normal distribution, facilitating the convergence of model training (Huang
224   et al., 2023). By incorporating normalization layers, models can maintain consistent representation
225   learning and effectively handle varying input scales. Therefore, selecting the appropriate normaliza-
226   tion method according to specific requirements is essential for training models with complex data or
227   large parameter sizes. The common forms of normalization are defined as follows:
228
229                                                x − E[x]
                                        Norm(x) = p             · γ + β,                                    (6)
230                                                 V ar[x] + ϵ
231
232   Where E[x] represents the mean of the input x, Var[x] represents the variance of the input x, and
233   ϵ is a small constant added to prevent division by zero. γ and β are the scale and shift parameter,
234
      respectively. Many normalization methods have been proposed, but the following five are still com-
      monly used: BN, LN, GN, IN, and RMSN. They are suitable for most models and the most widely
235   adopted methods by researchers. For more information, please refer to appendix A.

      SSM operates on well-conditioned inputs, minimizing potential disruptions caused by unstable fea-

251
      generated by SSM. This can be expressed as:
252
253                                   f = N 2(SSM (Act(Con(Lin(x))))),                                      (8)

      Where N 2 represents the normalization applied after the SSM module. This configuration aligns
      processed features with the requirements of downstream tasks, thereby enhancing performance.

259   Normalization combinations involve using different normalization techniques at different positions
260   around the SSM module. By leveraging the strengths of various methods, this approach adjusts fea-

269   represents different normalizations applied at each position, taking advantage of different techniques

288   This section presents a series of systematic experiments to evaluate the impact of normalization
289   types, positions, and combination strategies on the performance and stability of the Mamba Block.
290   We assessed the model’s performance on two types of tasks: long sequence modeling and vision
291
      tasks. Subsequently, we analyzed and interpreted the experimental results based on the scale invari-

      4.1   DATASETS
294
295   For long sequence modeling, we use the Breakfast dataset (Kuehne et al.). Breakfast is a large-scale
296   dataset designed for evaluating models on long sequence modeling and activity segmentation. It
297   consists of 1,712 videos recorded in 18 different kitchens, involving 52 participants performing 10
      distinct actions related to breakfast preparation, such as making tea, frying eggs, and preparing toast.
298   Each video is annotated with frame-level action labels, with sequences often comprising multiple
299   nested actions. The dataset spans over four million frames, making it highly challenging for models
300   to handle long temporal dependencies effectively.
301   For vision tasks, we use the ImageNet-100 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). ImageNet-100 is a
302   randomly selected subset of the ImageNet-1k dataset from the 2012 Large Scale Visual Recognition
303   Challenge. It contains 100 categories, covering various objects and scenes, ensuring diversity in
304   vision tasks. The training set includes 1300 images per category, while the validation set contains
305
      50 images per category, totaling 135,000 images.
306   We also evaluate our proposed optimized normalization scheme on ImageNet-1k (Krizhevsky et al.,
307   2012) and LRA ListOps dataset (Tay et al., 2021) to compare it with other methods.

521
      In this study, we conducted a comprehensive investigation into the types, positions, and combi-
522   nations of normalization layers within the Mamba architecture. Our findings reveal that applying
523   proper normalization after the SSM Modules enhances training stability by mitigating large varia-
524   tions in weight norms. Moreover, specific combinations of normalization techniques not only stabi-
525   lize the training process but also lead to significant improvements in model performance.
526   We also propose an intuition for selecting and combining normalization layers to facilitate the ex-
527   ploration of stable training for Mamba and other deep architectures. This intuition provides valuable
528   insights into choosing appropriate normalization methods for robust training of large-scale neural
      networks. Future research will focus on extending this intuition to more complex models and tasks,
529   aiming to refine normalization strategies for further improvements in efficiency and robustness.
530
531
      The Mamba2 architecture has recently been introduced, and Dao & Gu (2024) found that training
      Mamba2 is less stable than Mamba1. Building upon our research into the normalization of Mamba
532   Blocks, future work could further explore the Mamba2 architecture to address its stability chal-
533   lenges. Additionally, we hope our findings can be extended to other deep learning architectures,
534   offering valuable guidance for designing more complex models.

      State space modelling is a method of describing and analysing a system based on matrix theory.
792   Introducing state variables can get more in-depth information about the system. SSM uses first-
793   order differential equations to map the input function xt to the output function yt through hidden
794   state ht , defined as follows:
795                                 ht = Aht−1 + Bxt , yt = Cht + Dxt                                   (11)
796
797
      where state transition matrix A ∈ RN ×N ,input matrix B ∈ RN ×C ,output matrix C ∈ RC×N and
      forward channel transition matrix D ∈ RC×C . The variables N and C refer to the hidden state
798
      and dimension factors, respectively. Continuous parameters A, B can be discretized by a first-order
799
      difference method or a bilinear transformation method as follows Ā, B̄, with the sampling interval
800   ∆. Details of the process can be found in (Gu et al., 2021). We give the discretization result directly
801   here. What should be noted is that the hidden state update mechanism of SSM is similar to Recurrent
802   Neural Network (RNN), which receives current time step input xt and the previous time step hidden
803
      state ht−1 and computes the current time step hidden state ht .
804   The key design principle of Mamba lies in the introduction of a selective mechanism to parame-
805   terize the transition matrices A, B, C, ∆ in a data-driven manner. In Mamba1, these matrices are
806
      defined as functions of the input embedding features, allowing them to adapt dynamically to the data
      context through a hardware-aware parallel computing algorithm. This enables efficient processing
807   of long sequences with improved computational throughput. In contrast, Mamba2 leverages semi-
808   differentiable matrix factorization to compute the hidden state space efficiently, ensuring that any
809   state space model with state size N and sequence length L can be computed in time O(T N ), which
      means that Mamba2 preserves the controllability of the state space across various sequence lengths.

814   Dataset The Long Range Arena (LRA) benchmark (Tay et al., 2021) is designed to evaluate the
815   ability of models to capture long-range dependencies across various tasks. One of the tasks included
      in the LRA benchmark is the ListOps task, which is particularly useful for assessing a model’s
816
      capability to handle hierarchical structures and process long-range sequences.
817
818   In the ListOps task, the input sequence is represented as a nested list of operations. These symbolic
      expressions involve various operations, such as max, min, and median, applied to integers. The
819   task requires the model to parse the input sequence, resolve the nested operations, and compute the
820   final result. For example, a typical input could be:
821
822                                      max(2, min(3, 9), max(4, 5))
823
824   In this example, the operations max, min, and max are applied in a hierarchical structure. The
825   model’s objective is to correctly parse the operations and compute the final result, which in this case
      is 5.
826
827   The standard ListOps task involves input sequences of length 1000. However, to further test
828   Mamba’s capacity to handle long-range dependencies, we used an enhanced dataset, ListOps-New,
      which includes input sequences of length 2000. This extended version allows us to assess better the
829   limits of Mamba’s ability to manage and process extremely long sequences with complex, hierar-
830   chical operations.
