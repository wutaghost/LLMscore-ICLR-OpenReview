                                                  A BSTRACT
006
007            It is common for sensor failures to result in missing data, leading to training sets
008            being complete while test sets have only a small subset of variables. The challenge
009            lies in utilizing incomplete data for forecasting, which is known as the Variable
010            Subset Forecasting (VSF). In VSF tasks, significant distribution shift is present.
011            One type is inter-series shift, which indicates changes in correlations between
012            different series, and the other type is intra-series shift, which refers to substan-
013            tial distribution differences within the same series across different time windows.
014            Existing approaches to solving VSF tasks typically involve imputing the miss-
015
               ing data first and then making predictions using the completed series. However,
               these methods do not account for the shift inherent in VSF tasks, resulting in poor
016
               model performance. To address these challenges, we propose a Shift-Resilient
017
               Diffusive Imputation (SRDI) framework against the shift. Specifically, SRDI in-
018            tegrates divide-conquer strategy with the denoising process, that decomposes the
019            input into invariant patterns and variant patterns, representing the temporally sta-
020            ble parts of inter-series correlation and the highly fluctuating parts, respectively.
021            By extracting spatiotemporal features from each separately and then appropriately
022            combining them, inter-series shift can be effectively mitigated. Then, we innova-
023            tively organize SRDI and the forecasting model into a meta-learning paradigm
024            tailored for VSF scenarios. We address the intra-series shift by treating time win-
025            dows as tasks during training and employing an adaptation process before testing.
026
               Extensive experiments on four datasets have demonstrated our superior perfor-
               mance compared with state-of-the-art methods. Code is available at the reposi-
027
               tory: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SRDI-944C.

030   1   INTRODUCTION
031
032   In real-world IoT applications, sensor malfunctions and data collection issues often result in missing
033   data in time series, complicating predictive modeling and impairing forecasting performance. A
034   particularly difficult situation arises when entire sequences of data are missing. For instance, a model
035   trained with N variables to predict air quality in one region may need to be deployed in another
036   region with only S (S ≪ N ) available sensors. Additionally, extreme weather conditions can cause
037   sensor damage, leading to incomplete variable recordings in subsequent times. This scenario, known
038   as Variable Subset Forecasting (VSF) Chauhan et al. (2022), requires making predictions with
039
      only a subset of the variables used during training, which poses significant challenges for achieving
      accurate forecasts.
040
041   One of the most intuitive solutions is to impute the missing variables before making predictions.
042   Numerous imputation methods have been proposed, including recent advancements in diffusion
043   models Tashiro et al. (2021). However, these approaches consistently face significant challenges in
044   VSF scenarios, primarily due to distribution shift that is prevalent in these settings. Specifically,
045
      we categorize the distribution shift encountered in VSF into two main types: (i) Inter-Series Shift:
      In VSF scenarios, the absence of variables disrupts the ability to accurately capture relationships
046
      between variables. Additionally, the correlations between variables may change unpredictably over
047
      time, i.e., covariate shift, leading to systemic inaccuracies in learning these relationships Schneider
048   et al. (2020). This variability significantly degrades the model’s performance as it fails to adapt to
049   shifting inter-variable dynamics. (ii) Intra-Series Shift: Data in time series forecasting tasks is typ-
050   ically segmented into time windows, each with its own distinct distribution Fan et al. (2023). These
051   distributions may change abruptly over time, rendering the model trained on past data ineffective
052   for new, unseen distributions. This intra-series shift poses a substantial challenge to the imputation
053   model’s generalization ability across varying data distributions. Given these two types of shift, exist-
      ing imputation methods prove inadequate for sustaining robust performance in VSF environments.

054
      Our objective is to develop a robust imputation model that effectively handles both inter-series and
055   intra-series shift, ensuring satisfying performance for VSF.
056
057   To address the above challenges, we propose a shift-resilient diffusive imputation framework for
058
      VSF. Specifically, we outline our solutions against the two types of shift as follows.
059   To effectively manage inter-series shift in VSF, our approach integrates Divide-Conquer strategy
060   during the denoising process in the diffusive imputation. This technique involves decomposing the
061   time series data into two distinct patterns: invariant and variant. The invariant pattern focuses on
062   capturing the stable, underlying correlations that do not change significantly over time, providing a
063   robust foundation for the model. In contrast, the variant pattern addresses the dynamic correlations
064
      that are susceptible to shift. The decomposition allows the model to specifically target and adapt
      to changes in variable relationships, enhancing its ability to accurately impute missing data amidst
065
      evolving conditions. By processing these patterns separately and then recombining them, our model
066
      effectively isolates and compensates for the variability caused by inter-series shift, thus maintaining
067   high accuracy in variable recovery.
068
069   For intra-series shift, which occurs due to abrupt changes in data distribution within the same series
070
      over different time windows, we employ a meta-learning strategy within our diffusive imputation
      framework. This strategy trains the model to rapidly adapt to new distributions by treating impu-
071
      tation over each time window as a distinct task. Meta-learning enables the diffusive imputation
072
      framework to learn from a variety of distribution scenarios, enhancing its flexibility and general-
073   ization capability. By continuously updating its parameters in response to new data characteristics,
074   the model is equipped to handle previously unseen distributions effectively. This adaptive capability
075   is critical for maintaining consistent imputation performance across varying data landscapes, par-
076   ticularly in VSF environments where the model must reconstruct the missing variables accurately
077   despite significant shift in data distribution.
078
      In summary, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
079
080          • We introduce a novel diffusive imputation method specifically designed for Variable Subset
081            Forecasting (VSF) tasks, marking the first known application in this context.
082
             • We categorize and provide a comprehensive analysis of two distinct types of shift prevalent
083            in VSF tasks: inter-series and intra-series shift.
084
             • We develop a divide-conquer denoising model tailored for effectively addressing inter-
085
               series shift, alongside a meta-learning strategy that enhances the model’s adaptability to
086
               intra-series shift.
087
088
             • We validate our approach through extensive experimentation on four real-world datasets,
               demonstrating consistent superiority in effectiveness compared with existing state-of-the-
089
               art methods tailored to VSF tasks.

101   current methods struggle with time series shifts, which degrade performance. Our model addresses
102   this issue, excelling in VSF under challenging conditions.

      In this section, we present our diffusive imputation framework, which is shown in Figure 1. The dif-
      fusive imputation model leverages noise to impute the missing variables. The presence of inter- and
151
      intra-variable shift significantly sharpens the imputation performance. Specifically, to address the
152
      impact of the inter-variable shift on model performance, we decompose the time series into invari-
153   ant and variant patterns as detailed in Section 4.2.1. In Section 4.2.2, we designed a technique for
154   preserving spatiotemporal relations. The extracted invariant and variant patterns are then reasonably
155   combined, which will be introduced in Section 4.2.3, to generate the final output of the diffusive
156   imputation model. Additionally, to mitigate the intra-variable shift, we propose a meta-learning
157   framework, further elaborated in Section 5.

369
      Given a new variable subset, we aim to apply the trained imputation model and forecasting model for
370
      the inference. Considering the new variable subset as a new task, we will first conduct fine-tuning
371
      for the trained diffusive imputation model following the convention of the meta learning paradigm.
372   In other words, the imputation model requires several iterations of training on the new subset. Un-
373   fortunately, due to the existence of missing variables, where the ground truth is unavailable, it is
374   impractical to re-conduct the original training pipeline. To address the issue, we temporally ignore
375   the missing variables, but randomly select pseudo-missing variables from the available subset. We
376   will take the newly selected pseudo-missing variables as the imputation target (with ground truth),
377   and re-launch the inner-loop training pipeline indicated in Equation 17. During the process, the
      forecasting model is fixed and no longer updated.

533
534   In this paper, we propose a Shift-Resilient Diffusive Imputation (SRDI) model for improving VSF
535   performance by resolving distribution shift. Specifically, we classify the shift in VSF into two types:
536   inter-series shift and intra-series shift. SRDI, a novel diffusion model-based approach to address
537   the VSF problem, employs a divide-and-conquer strategy to tackle inter-series shift and enhances
538   the meta-learning framework to address intra-series shift. Extensive experiments on four real-world
539   datasets demonstrated that SRDI outperforms state-of-the-art methods, highlighting its effectiveness
      in addressing the distribution shift challenge in VSF tasks.

693   A.3   BASELINE MODELS DETAILS
694
      In this paper, we compare the proposed model with eight existing state-of-the-art imputation models.
695
      Below is a detailed introduction to these eight models:
696
697          • MICE imputes missing data by using a variable-by-variable approach through conditional
698            densities. It iterates over these conditional densities, making it flexible for complex, multi-
699            variate datasets. The key advantage of MICE is that it doesn’t require a suitable multivariate
700            distribution like joint modeling (JM) and is effective when no single multivariate distribu-
701            tion can describe the data. Additionally, MICE’s iterative approach, requiring relatively
               few iterations, allows for efficient and practical imputation.


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
            • IIM addresses the challenges of missing numerical values by leveraging individual regres-
703           sion models tailored for each complete tuple and its neighbors. This approach tackles the
704           sparsity problem by utilizing regression results from complete neighbors instead of their di-
705           rect values, thus improving imputation accuracy. Additionally, IIM adaptively determines
706           the number of neighbors for learning individual models to mitigate overfitting or underfit-
707           ting, leading to more effective imputation outcomes compared to traditional methods.
708         • TRMF employs a novel autoregressive temporal regularizer to capture the structure of tem-
709           poral dependencies among latent temporal embeddings in high-dimensional time series
710           data. This method enhances the ability to forecast future values while effectively man-
711           aging missing data. Its scalable design allows TRMF to handle large datasets efficiently,
712           outperforming traditional time series methods that struggle with high dimensionality and
713           noise.
714         • CSDI utilizes score-based diffusion models conditioned on observed data to handle time
715           series imputation. The model is explicitly designed for imputation and leverages correla-
716           tions between observed values to generate missing data from noise. Its advantages include
717           handling both probabilistic and deterministic imputation tasks while improving accuracy
718           compared to traditional methods, and it can also be applied to time series interpolation and
719           forecasting.
720         • FDW is a method designed for handling missing variables in multivariate time series fore-
721           casting (MTSF). It works by retrieving nearest neighbors based on the available subset of
722           variables and using them to fill in the missing values. The technique introduces a novel
723           ensemble weighting method to handle the bias introduced by the partial dimensions during
724           neighbor retrieval. The key advantage of FDW is that it can significantly recover forecast
725           performance without retraining the underlying models, making it versatile and efficient in
726           scenarios with long-term data loss or domain shifts.
727         • SSGAN is a method for imputing missing values in multivariate time series. It uses three
728           components: a generator to estimate missing values, a discriminator to differentiate be-
729           tween observed and imputed data, and a classifier to predict labels and guide the generator.
730           The method also incorporates a temporal reminder matrix to help the discriminator distin-
731           guish between real and imputed values. The key advantage of SSGAN is that it leverages
732
              both observed components and available labels, improving the imputation quality and en-
              suring accurate data distribution.
733
734         • TRF is a flow-based generative framework designed to impute missing variables in multi-
735           variate time series. TRF reconstructs missing variables by estimating the unknown condi-
736           tional density of unavailable variables based on the available subset, using an invertible flow
737
              structure. This ensures accurate reconstruction by mapping the missing data to a Gaussian
              distribution and back. TRF’s key advantage lies in its meta-learning framework, which
738
              allows it to generalize to different missing variable subsets without retraining, making it
739
              adaptable and efficient for dynamic real-world scenarios.
740
741         • SAITS imputes missing values by leveraging two diagonally-masked self-attention (DMSA)
742
              blocks, which capture both temporal dependencies and feature correlations between time
              steps. Its joint-optimization approach improves the imputation process by dynamically
743
              assigning weights to learned representations. The main advantage of SAITS is its ability to
744
              avoid the limitations of recurrent models, offering faster imputation with higher accuracy,
745           and its non-autoregressive nature reduces the risk of compounding errors.
746
747
748         • PRISTI is a conditional diffusion framework for spatiotemporal imputation that enhances
749           prior modeling by constructing and utilizing global spatiotemporal correlations and geo-
750
              graphic relationships. It includes a conditional feature extraction module to capture effec-
              tive spatiotemporal dependencies and a noise estimation module to transform random noise
751
              into realistic imputation values while mitigating the impact of added noise.
752
753
754         • GINAR is an end-to-end framework designed for multivariate time series forecasting
755           (MTSF) with variable missing data. It leverages simple recursive units (SRU) enhanced
              with two key components: Interpolation Attention (IA) and Adaptive Graph Convolution


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
               (AGCN). IA restores missing variables by generating plausible representations through at-
757            tention mechanisms, addressing incorrect temporal dependencies. AGCN reconstructs spa-
758            tial correlations between all variables, utilizing restored data to generate a reliable graph
759            structure and improve spatial dependency modeling.
760
761
            • Gaussian Copula model addresses the challenge of imputing missing values in mixed data
762
              (real, Boolean, and ordinal) by modeling the data as latent variables transformed through
763           arbitrary marginals. Each variable—whether continuous or ordinal—is associated with a
764           latent normal distribution, with ordinal levels represented as intervals. The model employs
765           an efficient Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to estimate copula parameters di-
766           rectly from incomplete data. This semiparametric approach ensures imputed values adhere
767           to the statistical structure of the data, avoids the need for hyperparameter tuning.
