      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   It is rumored that sometime before the fateful day of March 15, 44 BC, a soothsayer warned Caesar of
032   the impending dangers awaiting him at the Senate house. ”Beware the Ides of March,” the soothsayer
033
      is said to have uttered—a warning that Caesar famously ignored. Immortalized in Shakespeare’s play
      ”Julius Caesar,” this cautionary tale reminds us of the dangers of misjudgment and the unforeseen
034
      consequences of strategic decisions. In Reinforcement Learning (RL), the stakes, while not mortal,
035
      are still significant as agents navigate complex environments to optimize their actions.
036
037   This paper delves into the problem of policy evaluation, a fundamental problem in RL (Sutton &
038   Barto, 2018) of which the goal is to estimate the expected total rewards of a given policy. This process
039
      serves as an integral component in various RL methodologies, such as policy iteration and policy
      gradient approaches (Sutton et al., 1999), wherein the current policy undergoes evaluation followed
040
      by potential updates. Policy evaluation is also paramount in scenarios where prior to deploying a
041
      trained policy, thorough evaluation is imperative to ensure its safety and efficacy.
042
043   Broadly speaking there exist two scenarios where the problem of policy evaluation has been consid-
044   ered, known as online and offline data regimes. In online scenarios, a learner is interacting sequentially
045
      with the environment and is tasked with using its online deployments to collect helpful data for policy
      evaluation. The simplest method for online policy evaluation is Monte-Carlo estimation (Fonteneau
046
      et al., 2013). One can collect multiple trajectories by following the target policy, and use the empirical
047
      mean of the rewards as the estimator. These on-policy methods typically require executing the policy
048   we want to estimate which may be unpractical or dangerous in many cases. For example, in a medi-
049   cal treatment scenario, implementing an untrustworthy policy can cause unfortunate consequences
050   (Thapa et al., 2005). In these cases, offline policy evaluation may be preferable. In the offline case,
051   the learner has access to a batch of data and is tasked with using it in the best way possible to estimate
052   the value of a target policy. Many works focus on this problem leveraging various techniques, such as
053   importance-sampling, model-based estimation, and doubly-robust estimators (Yin & Wang, 2020;
      Jiang & Li, 2016; Yin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2019; Li et al., 2015).

054
      Motivated by applications where one has multiple policies to evaluate, e.g., multiple policies trained
055   using different hyperparameters, Dann et al. (2023) considered multiple-policy evaluation which
056   aims to estimate the performance of a set of K target policies instead of a single one. At first glance,
057   multiple-policy evaluation does not pose challenges beyond single-policy evaluation since one can
058   always use K instances of single-policy evaluation to evaluate the K policies. However, since
059   the sample complexity of this procedure scales linearly as a function of K this can be extremely
060   sample-inefficient as it neglects potential similarities among the K target policies.
061   Dann et al. (2023) proposed an on-policy algorithm that leverages the similarity among target policies
062   based on an idea related to trajectory synthesis (Wang et al., 2020). The basic technique is that if
063   more than one policy take the same action at a certain state, then only one sample is needed at that
064   state which can be reused to synthesize trajectories for these policies. Their algorithm achieves an
065   instance-dependent sample complexity which gives much better results when target policies have
066   many overlaps.
067   In the context of single off-policy evaluation, the theoretical guarantees depend on the overlap
068   between the offline data distribution and the visitations of the evaluated policy (Xie et al., 2019; Yin
069   & Wang, 2020; Duan et al., 2020). These coverage conditions, which ensure that the data logging
070   distribution (Xie et al., 2022) adequately covers the state space, are typically captured by the ratio
071   between the densities corresponding to the offline data distribution and the policy to evaluate, also
072   known as visitation ratios.
073   A single offline dataset can be used to evaluate multiple policies simultaneously. The policy evaluation
074   guarantees will be different for each of the policies in the set, depending on how much overlap the
075   offline distribution has with the policy visitation distributions. These observations inform an approach
076   to the multiple policy evaluation problem different from (Dann et al., 2023) that can also leverage
077   the policy visitation overlap in a meaningful way. Our algorithm is based on the idea of designing a
078   behavior distribution with enough coverage of the target policy set. Once this distribution is computed,
079   i.i.d. samples from the behavior distribution can be used to estimate the value of the target policies
080   using ideas inspired in the offline policy optimization literature. Our algorithms consist of two phases:
081
082
            1. Build coarse estimators of the policy visitation distributions and use them to compute a
               mixture policy that achieves a low visitation ratio with respect to all K policies to evaluate.
083
084         2. Sample from this approximately optimal mixture policy and use these to construct mean
085            reward estimators for all K policies.
086
087   Coarse estimation of the visitation distributions up to constant multiplicative accuracy can be achieved
088
      at a cost that scales linearly, instead of quadratically with the inverse of the accuracy parameter
      (see Section 4.1) and polynomially in parameters such as the size of the state and action spaces,
089
      and the logarithm of the cardinality of the policy evaluation set. We propose the MARCH or
090
      Multi-policy Approximation via Ratio-based Coarse Handling algorithm (see Algorithm 3) for
091   coarse estimation of the visitation distributions. Estimating the policy visitation distributions up
092   to multiplicative accuracy is enough to find an approximately optimal behavior distribution that
093   minimizes the maximum visitation ratio among all policies to estimate (see Section 4.2). The samples
094   generated from this behavior distribution are used to estimate the target policy values via importance
095   weighting. Since the importance weights are not known to sufficient accuracy, we propose the IDES
096   or Importance Density Estimation algorithm (see Algorithm 1) for estimating these distribution ratios
097   by minimizing a series of loss functions inspired by the DualDICE (Nachum et al., 2019) method
098   (see Section 4.3). Combining these steps we arrive at our main algorithm (CAESAR ) or Coarse and
099
      Adaptive EStimation with Approximate Reweighing algorithm (see Algorithm 2) that achieves a high
      probability finite sample complexity for the problem of multi-policy evaluation.

102   2    R ELATED W ORK
103
104   There is a rich family of off-policy estimators for policy evaluation (Liu et al., 2018; Jiang & Li,
105   2016; Dai et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2021; Jiang & Huang, 2020). But none of them is effective
106   in our setting. Importance-sampling is a simple and popular method for off-policy evaluation but
107   suffers exponential variance in the horizon (Liu et al., 2018). Marginalized importance-sampling
      has been proposed to get rid of the exponential variance. However, existing works all focus on


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      function approximations which only produce approximately correct estimators (Dai et al., 2020) or
109   are designed for the infinite-horizon case (Feng et al., 2021). The doubly robust estimator (Jiang &
110   Li, 2016; Hanna et al., 2017; Farajtabar et al., 2018) also solves the exponential variance problem,
111   but no finite sample result is available. Our algorithm is based on marginalized importance-sampling
112   and addresses the above limitations in the sense that it provides non-asymptotic sample complexity
113   results and works for finite-horizon Markov Decision Processes (MDPs).
114
      Another popular estimator is called model-based estimator, which evaluates the policy by estimating
115   the transition function of the environment (Dann et al., 2019; Zanette & Brunskill, 2019). Yin &
116   Wang (2020) provide a similar sample complexity to our results. However, there are some significant
117   differences between their result and ours. First, our sampling distribution; calculated based on the
118   coarse distribution estimator, is optimal. Second, our sample complexity is non-asymptotic while
119   their result is asymptotic. Third, the true distributions appearing in our sample complexity can be
120   replaced by known distribution estimators without inducing additional costs, that is, we can provide
121   a known sample complexity while their result is always unknown since we do not know the true
122   visitation distributions of target policies.
123   The work that most aligns with ours is Dann et al. (2023), which proposed an on-policy algorithm
124   based on the idea of trajectory synthesis. The authors propose the first instance-dependent sample
125   complexity analysis of the multiple-policy evaluation problem. Our algorithm offers a different per-
126   spective which uses off-policy evaluation based on importance-weighting and achieves a competitive
127   sample complexity with simpler techniques and analysis.
128   In concurrent work, Amortila et al. (2024b) proposed an exploration objective for downstream
129   reward maximization, similar to our goal of computing an optimal sampling distribution. However,
130   our approach utilizes coarse distribution estimators to approximate the objective, which is a novel
131   technique and an important contribution of our work.
132
      Our algorithm also uses some techniques modified from other works which we summarize here.
133   DualDICE is a technique for estimating distribution ratios by minimizing some loss functions
134   proposed by (Nachum et al., 2019). Our algorithm IDES is built on this idea. However, these are
135   significant differences, which are discussed in detail in Section 4.3. Besides, we utilize stochastic
136   gradient descent algorithms and their convergence rate for strongly-convex and smooth functions
137   from the optimization literature (Hazan & Kale, 2011). Finally, we adopt the Median of Means
138   estimator (Minsker, 2023) to convert in-expectation results to high-probability results.

456
      First, compared to the naive uniform sampling strategy over target policies as described in (2), our

459
460   Next, we compare our result with the one achieved by Dann et al. (2023) as described in (1). A
                                                                                                    1
461   significant issue with the result by Dann et al. (2023) is the presence of the unfavorable dmax (s) ,
462   which can induce an undesirable dependency on K.
463   To illustrate this, consider an example of an MDP with two layers: a single initial state s1,1 in the first
464   layer and two terminal states in the second layer s2,1 , s2,2 . The transition function is the same for all
465   actions, i.e., P (s2,1 |s1,1 , a) = p and p is sufficiently small. Agents only receive rewards at state s2,1 ,
466   regardless of the actions they take. Hence, to evaluate the performance of a policy under this MDP, it
467   is sufficient to consider only the second layer. Now, suppose we have K target policies to evaluate,
468   where each policy takes different actions at state s1,1 but the same action at any state in the second
469   layer. Since the transition function at state s1,1 is the same for any action, the visitation distribution
470
      at state s2,1 of all target policies is identical. Given that p is sufficiently small, the probability of
471
      reaching s2,1 is P[s2,1 ∈ K2 ] = 1 − (1 − p)K ≈ pK. According to the result (1) by Dann et al.
472   (2023), the sample complexity in this scenario is Õ( ϵK2 ) which depends on K. In contrast, since the
473
      visitation distribution at the second layer of all target policies is identical, our result provides a sample
474   complexity of Õ( ϵ12 ) without dependency on K. Nevertheless, it remains unclear whether our result
475
      is universally better in all cases (omit the dependency on H).

      In this work, we consider the problem of multi-policy evaluation. We propose an algorithm, CAESAR
489   , based on computing an approximate optimal offline sampling distribution and using the data
490   sampled from it to perform the simultaneous  estimation of the policy values. CAESAR uses
491                4 PH                P (dπhk (s,a))2
492   n = Õ Hϵ2       h=1 maxk∈[K]       s,a µ∗ (s,a)    trajectories and with probability at least 1 − δ
                                                h

493   we can evaluate the performance of all target policies up to an ϵ error. The algorithm consists of
494   three techniques. First, we obtain a coarse distribution estimator at the cost of lower-order sample
495   complexity. Second, based on the coarse distribution estimator, we show an achievable optimal
496   sampling distribution by solving a convex optimization problem. Last, we propose a novel step-wise
497   loss function for finite-horizon MDPs. By minimizing the loss function step to step, we are able to
498   get the importance weighting ratio and a non-asymptotic sample complexity is available due to the
499
      smoothness and strong-convexity of the loss function.
500   Beyond the results of this work, there are still some open questions of interest. First, our sample
501   complexity has a dependency on H 4 which is induced by the error propagation in the estimation of
502   the importance weighting ratios. Specifically, the error of minimizing the loss function at early steps,
503   e.g., h = 1 will propagate to later steps e.g., h = H. We conjecture a dependency on H 2 is possible
504   by considering a comprehensive loss function which includes the entire horizon instead of step-wise
505
      loss functions which require step by step optimization. Second, as discussed before, we believe that a
      variance-aware sample complexity is possible through a more careful analysis. Besides, considering
506
      a reward-dependent sample complexity is also an interesting direction. For example, in an MDP
507
      with sparse rewards where only one state-action has non-zero reward; a better sample complexity
508   than CAESAR ’s may be possible by focusing on state-action pairs with non-zero rewards. Finally,
509   we are interested to see what other uses the research community may find for coarse distribution
510   estimation. In our work, the coarse distribution estimator plays an important role throughout the
511   algorithm. We believe this type of estimator can be of independent interest.

1352   In this section, we discuss on the application of CAESAR to policy identification problem, its
1353   instance-dependent sample complexity and some intuitions related to the existing gap-dependent

1356   core idea behind the algorithm is we can use CAESAR to evaluate all candidate policies up to an
1357   accuracy, then we can eliminate those policies with low estimated performance. By decreasing the
1358   evaluation error gradually, we can finally identify a near-optimal policy with high probability.
