011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The identifiability of latent variables given observational data is one of the core
014            issues in the field of disentangled representation learning. Recent progresses
015
               have been made on establishing identifiablity theories for latent causal models.
               However with much restrictions or unrealistic assumptions, their practicality on real
016
               applications are limited. In this paper, we propose a novel identifiablity theory for
017
               learning latent variables in nonlinear causal models, requiring only single-domain
018            data. We prove that all latent variables in a powerset bipartite graph can be identified
019            up to an invertible transformation, if the generation process of observable data is
020            globally invertible, latent variables are independent, and shared latent variables
021            entail minimal information. Experiments on synthetic data support the conclusions
022            of our theory.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Disentangled representation learning is one of the fundamental problems in machine learning (Bengio
      et al., 2013). It aims at discovering the underlying latent variables given a set of observed variables,
028
      which is essential in many downstream tasks such as domain adaptation (Baktashmotlagh et al., 2018;
029
      Cai et al., 2019a), out-of-distribution generalization (Sun et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023), and style
030   transfer (Gonzalez-Garcia et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2018). The unsupervised learning of disentangled
031   representations, though common in practice, is however fundamentally impossible without inductive
032   biases (Locatello et al., 2019). Therefore, whether latent variables can be uniquely determined (up
033   to an equivalent class) under certain conditions, becomes a core issue in this field, which is usually
034   referred to as the identifiability problem of latent variables (Khemakhem et al., 2020).
035
      Early works on identifiability of latent variables mainly originate from the nonlinear ICA theory
036   (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999), which guarantees the existence of latent variable s with independent
037   components s = {si }ni=1 under nonlinear invertible transformation s = f (x) over observed variable
038   x. To ensure identifiability, further restrictions such as extra auxiliary variables (Hyvarinen &
039   Morioka, 2016; Khemakhem et al., 2020) or fixed function class (Buchholz et al., 2022) are required.
040   Traditional theories on ICA regard each dimension of s as an individual variable, however, latent
041   factors in disentangled representation learning tasks are usually multi-dimensional rather than one-
042   dimensional, leading to the requirement for block-wise identifiability.
043   Some recent works have tried to establish block-wise identifiability theory based on structural causal
044   models (Pearl, 2009), aiming to identify the underlying generation process of observed data (Sun
045   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Buchholz et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Such works still require
046   extra auxiliary information, which relies on available multi-domain data. However, multi-domain data
047   are usually hard to acquire (Matsuura & Harada, 2020; Creager et al., 2021), limiting the applicability
048   of the theory. Other block-wise identifiability works make restrictive assumptions such as additive
049   function (Lachapelle et al., 2024), compositionality (Brady et al., 2023; Wiedemer et al., 2023), or
050
      subspace span(Kong et al., 2024), which may only be suitable for specific scenarios.
051   In this paper, we aim to establish a new identifiability theory for nonlinear causal models with mild
052   assumptions, while requiring only single-domain data. We first show that given a set of observed
053   variables, any underlying structural causal model (SCM) can be reduced to an equivalent SCM whose
      structure is a powerset bipartite graph (PBG), while each latent variable in a PBG-SCM is equivalent

088   2    R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   Disentangled representation learning. Works on disentangled representation learning have dis-
091   cussed the property of ideal disentanglement, such as modularity, compactness, and explicitness
092   (Ridgeway & Mozer, 2018; Higgins et al., 2018; Eastwood & Williams, 2018). While closely related
093
      to our identification goals and some of our assumptions, existing identifiability theory based on such
      objectives (Eastwood et al., 2022) is incapable to provide guidance for learning. Beside, there are
094
      lots of works focusing on practical algorithm to identify latent variables (Higgins et al., 2017; Chen
095
      et al., 2018; Ganin et al., 2016), but few of them have discussed the identifiability result, or include
096   sufficient mechanism to guarantee the uniqueness of solution. Our work exactly provides a guidance
097   for designing identification algorithms with theoretical support.
098
099
      Latent variable identifiability. To guarantee the uniqueness of identified latent variables, there are
      two main branches of works for building the identifiability theory. One branch of existing works opts
100
      to introduce extra information (Khemakhem et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022), such as data with domain
101
      index (Sorrenson et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Kong et al., 2023), similar data from multiple views
102   (Hyvarinen et al., 2019; Von Kügelgen et al., 2021; Gresele et al., 2020; Zimmermann et al., 2021),
103   interventional data (Buchholz et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), or data with time index (Hyvarinen
104   & Morioka, 2016; Klindt et al., 2020; Lachapelle et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024). Acquiring such
105   extra information may be difficult or impossible in many scenarios, so that another branch opts to
106   fully utilize the existing single-domain data, at the cost of introducing other restrictions. Lachapelle
107   et al. (2024) assume the transformation is additive across latent variables, which may be unrealistic
      for general learning tasks. Brady et al. (2023) and Wiedemer et al. (2023) make compositionality


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      assumptions for object-centric learning tasks, which is not applicable for latent variables with global
109   influence. Kong et al. (2024) require the ground truth model and the learned model to jointly satisfy a
110   subspace-span condition, which cannot judge whether it is satisfied by a single model. Our work also
111   falls into the latter branch, while we propose a minimality assumption which is much easier to be
112   satisfied in general scenarios.
113
      Causal structure identifiability. Structure identification in latent causal models is a problem closely
114
      related to latent variable identification (Cai et al., 2019b; Kivva et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022;
115   Reizinger et al., 2023; Jiang & Aragam, 2024), and some works discuss these two problems together
116   (Kong et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Our work do not aim to identify the structure of underlying
117   SCM, since this will inevitably require additional assumptions. However, we do discuss the equivalent
118   classes of graph structures, in a sense that if a latent variable with constant value is identified, then
119   the corresponding vertex in the graph can be removed.
120
      Causal abstraction. Causal abstraction literatures (Rubenstein et al., 2017; Beckers et al., 2020;
121   Geiger et al., 2021; Xia & Bareinboim, 2024) study under what conditions that two causal models
122   are equivalent in terms of interventional distribution, i.e., cannot be identified through observational
123   or interventional data. Relative theories mostly discuss such causal consistency properties for some
124   given categories of transformations, while we provide a novel transformation named SCM reduction
125   for the latent variable identification problem.

      3    P RELIMINARY
128
129
      The identifiability problem of latent variables in latent causal model is described as follows: given
130
      a set of observed variables v = {vi ∈ Rdvi }ni=1 which are generated by a set of unknown latent
131
      variables s = {si ∈ Rdsi }m i=1 and an unknown function g such that v = g(s), under some given
132   assumptions, whether the solution of each si can be determined uniquely up to some equivalence
133   relations. In this paper, we define the equivalence relation by "equivalent information", i.e. two
134   variables can predict each other precisely. Such equivalence is also called "equivalent up to invertible
135   transformations" in other literatures (Kong et al., 2024; Lachapelle et al., 2024). A formal definition
136   is given as follows.
137
138   Definition 3.1 (Subvariable and proper subvariable). Random variable u ∈ U ⊆ Rm is a subvari-
139   able of random variable v ∈ V ⊆ Rn , iff. there exists a surjective mapping f : V → U such that
140
      u = f (v) for any (u, v) ∈ supp(u, v), denoted as u ⪯ v. Further, u is a proper subvariable of v
      iff. u ⪯ v and v ̸⪯ u, denoted as u ≺ v.
141
142
      Definition 3.2 (Equivalent variable). Random variable u is equivalent to random variable v, iff.
143   u ⪯ v and v ⪯ u, denoted as u ∼ v.
144
145   We aim to establish identifiablity theory for a wide range of models. As such, we consider structural
146   causal models (SCM) (Pearl, 2009) as the underlying model, which has sufficient capability to explain
147   the generation process of common variables. An SCM is a tuple (G, F, P ), where G = (V, E) is
148   the causal graph which is a directed acyclic graph with vertex set V and edge set E. The vertex
149   set V consists of two disjoint sets, i.e. V = U ∪ S, where U = {ui }m         i=1 is a set of exogenous
150   variables which are mutually independent, S = {si }m     i=1 is a set of endogenous  variables. The edge
151   set E satisfies the following restrictions: there is no edge ends with any ui , and for any endogenous
152
      variable, there exists a path ends with it while starting from an exogenous variable. F is a set of
      structural equations si =Qfi (P a(si )), where fi is a function and Pa(si ) are the parent variables of si .
153                               m
      P = p(u1 , · · · , um ) = i=1 p(ui ) is a probability distribution defined over all exogenous variables.
154
155   We further assume there are no directed path between observed variables, similar settings have been
156   adopted by most works on latent variable identifiability (Khemakhem et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2022;
157   Kong et al., 2024). Since identifying all latent variables in an SCM is generally impossible without
158
      strong assumptions, we first reduce the original SCM into an equivalent SCM whose structure is a
      powerset bipartite graph (Section 4), and then discuss the identifiablity result (Section 5).
159
160   Notations. We use square brackets to denote the dimensional concatenation of variables, including:
161   using [x, y] to represent the concatenation of variables x, y; and using [S] as the concatenation of all
      elements in variable set S.

      4    R EDUCTION OF S TRUCTURAL C AUSAL M ODELS
163
164
      In this section, we discuss how to convert a structural causal model into a ideal form such that all
165
      latent variables are able to be identified.
166
167   Given an SCM (G, F, P ) as well as a set of observed variables O = {ol = sil }nl=1 which is a subset
168   of the endogeous variables S, we would like to know which variables are most likely to be identified:
169   the exogenous variables are naturally independent, and their information are limited to only go into
170
      their corresponding descendants, such restrictions give them the potential to be identified according
      to the graph structure; however for unobserved endogenous variables, they can be almost any forms
171
      of mixture of their ancestors in terms of information, such freedom makes them less likely to be
172
      identified. Based on such intuition, we focus on the identification of exogenous variables, which
173   already provide sufficient information to generate the observed variables. However, some of the
174   exogenous variables shares the same topology in the view of observed variables, i.e., have the same
175   set of observed descendants. Without further observation, they are exchangeable and unlikely to be
176   separated. Therefore, we merge such variables by dimensional concatenation, and expect their union
177   can be identified.
178

179   form. By saying "equivalent" here, we mean they share the same marginal distribution on observed
180   variables, as well as the same marginal distribution on the (concatenated) exogenous variables. The
181   word "reduced" means the converted SCM entails less vertices on the graph. We now formally define
182   the reduction procedure which constructs a reduced form of the SCM (G′ , F ′ , P ′ ) w.r.t. observed
183   variable set O (see Fig. 1 for illustration):

194   The above reduction process tries to cluster analogous exogenous variables and remove unobserved
195   endogenous variables, which can be regarded as a specifically designed exact transformation as in
196
      causal abstraction literatures (Rubenstein et al., 2017). After reduction, the causal graph has been
      transformed uniquely into a special kind of bipartite graph, which we name powerset bipartite graph,
197
      or PBG for short. The definition is as follows (see Fig 2 for examples):
198
199
      Definition 4.1 (Powerset bipartite graph). A directed graph G = (V, E) is a powerset bipartite
200
      graph with size n, iff. it satisfies the following properties:
201
202         1. The vertex set V consists of two disjoint sets V1 and V2 , such that V1 ∪ V2 = V , V1 ∩ V2 = ∅.
203
204         2. V1 consists of n vertices, define an auxiliary set N = {1, 2, · · · , n} and establish a bijective
205            mapping ϕ : V1 → N .
206
            3. V2 consists of m ≤ 2n − 1 vertices, and there exists a set M ⊂ P(N )\{∅} and a bijective
207
               mapping ψ : V2 → M , where P(N ) is the powerset of N , i.e. consisting all subsets of N .
208
209         4. There exists an edge from vertex s to t in the edge set E, iff. s ∈ V2 , t ∈ V1 , and ϕ(t) ∈ ψ(s).
210
211   Further, if m = 2n − 1, i.e. M = P(N )\{∅}, we call it a complete powerset bipartite graph.
212
213   We call a structural causal model (G, F, P ) PBG-SCM, if G is a powerset bipartite graph. In the
214   following sections, we will then focus on the identifiability results for PBG-SCMs. Note that, if latent
215   variables in a PBG-SCM can be identified, then the concatenated (according to topology) exogenous
      variables in original SCM can also be identified.

      Basis Model. Consider a PBG-SCM (see Fig. 2a) with latent variables s = (s1 , z, s2 ) ∈ S and
237   observed variables v = (v1 , v2 ) ∈ V, where s1 ∈ Rds1 , z ∈ Rdz , s2 ∈ Rds2 , v1 ∈ Rdv1 , v2 ∈ Rdv2 .

242
      variables as p(v1 , v2 ), and the generation model as (ps1 , pz , ps2 , g1 , g2 ). According to Thm. 1, latent
      variables in a basis model are identifiable, if the following assumptions are satisfied:
243
244   Assumption 1 (Verifiable identifiability conditions for basis model).
245

259   Remark. The invertibility condition is a basic requirement in almost all existing identifiabilty
260   theories, without which the latent variables cannot be recovered from observed variables. The
261   independence condition is also widely applied in related works, without which the information in
262
      latent variables can be mixed and cannot be separated. It is also a natural corollary from the reduction
      process of SCM, since latent variables in a PBG-SCM are dimensional concatenations of disjoint and
263
      independent exogenous variables. The minimality condition is a key contribution of our identifiability
264
      theory, which requires the shared latent variable z to have minimal information. This prevents z from
265   plundering information from s1 or s2 . Though simple in its concept, this assumption is overlooked
266   by existing literatures. We then discuss what this assumption really conveys and why it fails to gain
267   much attention.

269   Definition 5.1 (Intrinsic dimension). The intrinsic dimension of a random variables x ∈ Rdx is the
      minimal dimension among its equivalent variables, denoted as IDim(x) = min {dim(z)|z ∼ x}.

      Proposition 5.1 (Violation of minimality) For a basis model M = (ps1 , pz , ps2 , g1 , g2 ), if it satisfies
271   assumption i and ii but does not satisfy assumption iii, then there exists an identifiable model
272   M′ = (p′s1 , p′z , p′s2 , g1′ , g2′ ) satisfying Assumption 1, such that p′ (v1′ = v1 , v2′ = v2 ) ≡ p(v1 , v2 ),
273   and there exist random variables z0 , z1 , z2 such that z ∼ [z0 , z1 , z2 ], IDim(z0 ) < IDim(z), and
274   s′1 ∼ [s1 , z1 ], z′ ∼ z0 , s′2 ∼ [s2 , z2 ].
275
276   The concept "intrinsic dimension" defined above is not new (Camastra & Staiano, 2016; Pope et al.,
277   2020), which describes the "dimension of the necessary information" of a variable. According to
278   Proposition 5.1, a non-minimal z has oversized intrinsic dimension, and the extra information are
279
      dimensional and indeed from s1 or s2 . According to Corollary 5.1, by restricting the dimension of
      learned ẑ, we are also able to recover the ground truth latent variables. This means, if we know the
280
      intrinsic dimension of latent variables in advance, and ensure that invertibility and independence are
281
      satisfied, then the minimality condition will automatically be satisfied by setting latent dimensions
282   the same as ground truth. This might provide an explanation why minimality condition is overlooked,
283   since "known dimension" is a default setting in experiment design of most literatures. As a result, we
284   argue that the minimality condition is important, and researchers should consider experiment settings
285   with unknown latent dimension for validating their identifiability results.
286
287   Corollary 5.1 (Substitute for minimality) For a basis model M = (ps1 , pz , ps2 , g1 , g2 ) satisfying
288   Assumption 1 and another basis model M̂ = (p̂s1 , p̂z , p̂s2 , ĝ1 , ĝ2 ) satisfying assumption i and ii,

290   satisfies assumption iii, thus there is s1 ∼ ŝ1 , z ∼ ẑ, s2 ∼ ŝ2 .

328   An intuitive explanation is, for information of individual observed variable (or smaller groups of
329   observed variables), it should not be put into a shared latent variable (or latent variable corresponding
330   to larger group). If this assumption is violated, an "upper" variable may plunder arbitrary information
331   from "lower" variables, thus is unlikely to be identified.
332   Combining all above conclusions, we extend the identifiability results to the original SCM. Given
333   a set of observed variables which are generated by an SCM with no directed path among observed
334   variables, as long as the function between exogenous variables and observed variables is differen-
335   tiable and globally invertible, then we can guarantee a unique identification result (up to invertible
336   transformation). Further, if shared latent variables in original SCM entail minimal information, then
337   we can guarantee the identification of (concatenations of) original exogenous variables in the finest
338   grain. Such assumptions are quite mild, which ensures the broad applicability of our theory.

341
342   In this section, we demonstrate through experiments that, for an identifiable PBM-SCM, learning
343   mechanisms corresponding to each assumptions should be considered in algorithm design for suc-
344   cessful identification. We first introduce the datasets and metrics used in our experiments, and then
345   show the identification results for basis model and general PBM-SCM, respectively.

347   6.1   DATASETS AND METRICS
348
349   Synthetic datasets for basis model. For the grouth truth model, we sample s1 , z, s2 from standard
350
      normal distribution N (0, I) (with differnt dimensions), so that they are mutually independent. We by
      default set the dimensions of latent variables as ds1 = 3, dz = 5, ds2 = 4. For the observed variables
351
      v1 , v2 , we set dv1 = dv2 = 10, and use three different mechanisms for the generation process, which
352
      therefore constitutes different datasets:
353
354   • Concatenation dataset. Apply concatenation in dimension and then do invertible transformation for
355     latent variables, i.e. v1 = f1 ([s1 , z]), v2 = f2 ([z, s2 ]).
356   • Split dataset. Split the space of z into subspaces before concatenation, and then do invertible
357     transformations, i.e. z+ = max(z, 0), z− = min(z, 0), v1 = f1 ([s1 , z+ ]), v2 = f2 ([z− , s2 ]).
358
      • Fusion dataset. Fuse the information of z deeply in one side, and then do invertible transformations,
359
        i.e. v1 = f1 (s1 ) + f2 (z), v2 = f3 ([z, s2 ]).
360
361   For the transformation function fi , we use randomly-initialized multi-layer perceptrons (MLP) with
362   Tanh activations. Detailed configuration of such MLP can be found in Appendix A.8. We checked
363   the rank of weight matrices in each linear layer to ensure they are of full rank, therefore any fi is
364   guaranteed to be invertible. The invertibility of whole generation process of all above models are
365   easy to verify, note that the Split and Fusion datasets are globally invertible but not locally invertible,
366   i.e. s1 and z cannot be recovered given only v1 . The minimality condition is also satisfied by all
367   above models, since moving any dimension in z to s1 (or s2 ) will make v2 (or v1 ) unrecoverable.
368   Synthetic dataset for general model. Similar to the construction of basis model, we use a complete
369   PBG-SCM with size 3, and also sample si from standard normal distribution N (0, I) for any i ∈
370   {1, · · · , 7}. We set the dimension of all latent variables as dsi = 2 and the dimension of all observed
371   variables as dvj = 10. We generate the observed variables by vj = fj ([si |i&2j ̸= 0]), j ∈ {1, 2, 3},
372   where fj is also an invertible function implemented by MLP. The invertibility and minimality of this
373   PBG-SCM can be similarly verified as the above basis models.
374   Metrics. We follow the work of (Kong et al., 2024) to use coefficient of determination (R2 score) for
375   evaluation of the degree of variable equivalence or variable independence. R2 score takes value in
376   [0, 1], and larger R2 represents better identification result. The R2 score for equivalence takes value 1
377   for equivalent variables, while R2 score for independence takes value 1 for independent variables.
      Detailed definition can be found in Appendix A.8.

411   6.2    BASIS M ODEL I DENTIFICATION
412
413   For a basis model, our goal is to identify all latent variables s given observations on v. Thm. 1 tells
414   us that our learned model should satisfy Assumption 1 in order to learn equivalent latent variables.
415   As such, we apply an autoencoder (AE) framework for the invertibility condition, which encodes v
416   into ŝ by an MLP and then decode ŝ back to v by another MLP. An MSE loss is adopted to ensure
417
      invertibility. For the independence condition, we apply Contrastive Log-ratio Upper Bound (CLUB)
      (Cheng et al., 2020) method as extra loss between each latent variable and the rest, which is a widely
418
      used approach for guarantee of independence. For the minimality condition, we set the dimension of
419
      z in our model by dz = 5, which equals the intrinsic dimension of ground truth, thus satisfies the
420   conditions in Corollary 5.1. For a comparison, we set dz = 7 as scenarios that minimality condition
421   is not satisfied.
422
423
      Tab. 1 shows the identification result of different learning strategies. We can see that if all conditions
      are satisfied (AE+CLUB, dz = 5), the learned latent variables are almost equivalent to that in ground
424
      truth model, indicated by R2 > 0.9. Such results are consistent among all 3 datasets, showing that
425
      latent variables in basis model can be identified if Assumption 1 is satisfied. Either independence
426   condition is violated (AE, dz = 5) or minimality condition is violated (AE+CLUB, dz = 7), R2
427   scores decreases severely, indicating a non-perfect identification. The setting of AE(dz = 5) can
428   be viewed as a baseline, which is the method used by default in existing identification works. We
429   can see that, though such methods achieve considerable R2 scores (around 0.8), it in fact does not
430   indicate successful identification by comparing with best R2 scores. Fig. 3 further shows the details
431   during training. We can see that the training process is relatively stable for AE+CLUB(dz = 5), and
      the CLUB loss does ensure independence among latent variables.

470   7     C ONCLUSION
471
472
      We proposed a novel identifiability theory for latent variable identification in nonlinear causal models
473
      with single-domain data. Our theory guarantees that, for any structural causal models which no
474   directed path among observed variables, there exists a unique PBG-SCM which is equivalent in
475   terms of joint distribution of all variables, whose latent variables are identifiable if invertibility,
476   independence, and minimality conditions are satisfied. Our theory provides guidance for the design
477   of identification algorithm, in a sense that each conditions should be considered for successful
478   identification.
479
      Limitations. The major limitations of this work include: 1. We follow existing works and assume
480   there are no directed path among observed variables in an SCM, and all variables are continuous.
481   Such assumptions may not hold true in specific scenarios. Future works may consider an extension
482   to more general SCMs, allowing for the existence of directed paths and discrete variables. 2. This
483   work is mainly a theoretical work, thus has limited contribution in algorithm design. Like existing
484   works, the succeeded algorithms in our experiments still need pre-known knowledge of the intrinsic
485   dimension of latent variables. Designing practical identification algorithms according to the theory is
      required in future works.

      A.1    F URTHER DISCUSSIONS ON SCM R EDUCTION
651
652   We explain the numbering rule in our SCM Reduction procedure, which is also used in our general
653   model. See Fig. 5 for an example. For a concatenated cluster u′19 , the binary representation of 19 is
654   [10101]2 = [00001]2 + [00100]2 + [10000]2 = 21 + 23 + 25 , so that the observed descendants of
655   this cluster are o1 , o3 , and o5 . Under this rule, the equation j&2i ̸= 0 has the following meaning: oi
656   is a descendant of u′j .
657
      We then give a proof here for our claim that, any SCM (G, F, P ) can be transformed into a PBG-SCM
658
      by the reduction process given a subset of endogenous variables as observed set O, if there is no
659
      directed paths among variables in O.
660
661   The reduction process has provided a construction procedure of a new SCM. Since the structural
662   equations for each observed variables are essentially the unfolding form of those in original SCM, so
663
      the observational distribution remains unchanged. We only need to prove that the transformed graph
      is a PBG.
664
665   Consider the 4 properties of PBG in Def. 4.1. We discuss them one by one.
666
667         1. We set V1 = S ′ = O, V2 = U ′ . Since V = U ′ ∪ S ′ , so that V1 ∪ V2 = V . Since variables in
668            O are endogenous variables, and variables in U ′ are concatenations of exogenous variables,
669            so that V1 ∩ V2 = ∅.
670         2. We define ϕ as the following: for each ol ∈ V1 , map it to l. Clearly, ϕ is a bijective mapping.
671
672
