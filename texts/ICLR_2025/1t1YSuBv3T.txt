011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            To tackle the issue of hallucination in generative question answering
014            (GQA)—where the generated answer is nonsensical or unfaithful to the provided
015            document—we introduce a novel framework called evidence-enhanced triplet gen-
016            eration (EATQA). This framework incentivizes the model to generate all possible
017            combinations of 〈Question, Evidence, Answer〉 triplets by reversing the source pair
018            and target label to grasp their logical interrelationships. Specifically, the model
019
               predicts the Answer (A), Question (Q), and Evidence (E) given the QE, EA, and
               QA pairs, respectively. Furthermore, we address the distribution gap during the
020
               inference stage to extract knowledge from the evidence more effectively. Our
021
               framework ensures that the model comprehends the logical connections between
022            queries, evidence, and answers, thereby simultaneously enhancing evidence gen-
023            eration and question answering capabilities. In this study, we apply the EATQA
024            framework to the LLama model, demonstrating superior performance compared
025            to other large language model (LLM)-based methods and hallucination mitigation
026            techniques on two challenging GQA benchmarks. Further analysis reveals that our
027            method not only preserves the pre-existing knowledge within the LLM but also
028            reduces hallucination and produces more accurate answers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) signify a pivotal advancement in the pursuit of general artificial
      intelligence (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023). Despite their
034
      remarkable performance across a broad range of tasks, these models continue to encounter several
035
      challenges, such as hallucination (Tonmoy et al., 2024) and difficulties in processing long contexts
036   (Jin et al., 2024). In the context of document-based generative question answering (GQA) (Lewis &
037   Fan, 2018), models sometimes produce answers that are inconsistent with the source document or
038   do not align with the query, a phenomenon known as hallucination (Gunjal et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
039   2024). Recent studies have employed external models to retrieve pertinent information in an attempt
040   to enhance the factual accuracy of generated responses. Nonetheless, the inherent mismatch between
041   the retriever and the LLM can lead to the inclusion of superficially relevant information that does not
042   contribute meaningfully to answering the question (Salemi & Zamani, 2024).
043   To enhance logical reasoning and minimize the inclusion of misleading information, we emphasize
044   the identification of supporting evidence in document-based question answering (QA). Departing
045   from the traditional retrieve-then-read approach, we employ a unified triplet generation framework
046   where a large language model (LLM) simultaneously generates evidence and answers. Within this
047   framework, pairs of ⟨question, evidence, answer⟩ are inputted into specific instructions to produce
048   the remaining element. This approach leverages evidence to reconstruct the question, ensuring that
049   the model grasps its logical relationships to both the question and the answer, rather than relying on
050
      superficial relevance.
051   Consider an example from the MultiRC dataset (Khashabi et al., 2018), illustrated in Figure 1. The
052   question posed is, “After the Osprey resumed flights, how long did it take for the Air Force to begin
053   using the aircraft?” The answer cannot be derived from a single sentence within the document. To
      accurately respond, the model must identify multiple pieces of evidence: “Osprey resumed flights

082   We conduct experiments in a variety of widespread document-based GQA datasets with diverse
083   answer types, including MultiRC and QASPER, based on different sizes of LLMs. Compared with
084   different sizes of the backbone model, our unified triplet generation framework shows significant
085   improvement on the two datasets, becoming the new state-of-the-art. Further analysis demonstrates
086
      the ability of our approach to tackle longer document with more sentences. Additionally, we observe
      a positive correlation in the performance of the three subtasks within the triplet generation framework,
087
      indicating the efficacy of unifying the generation of all components with a single LLM in this
088
      framework.
089
090   We conclude our contributions as follows: 1. We highlight the evidence generation to alleviate
091   hallucinations of LLM in GQA task. Instead of utilizing another LM as the retriever, which may
092
      introduce misleading information, we propose the unified evidence enhanced triplet generation
      framework including three instruction tuning tasks to improve the logical reasoning ability of LLM
093
      for GQA task. 2. We propose the self-reasoning module, including the two phrase of candidate
094
      generation and correctness verify, which constructs the faithful and informative evidences for training
095   without external annotation. 3. We conduct experiments on a wide variety of multi-hop QA datasets
096   including MultiRC and QASPER with different sizes of LLM, and demonstrate the effectiveness over
097   existing methods. 4. Additional experiments confirm the effectiveness of our unified triplet generation
098   framework in both evidence retrieval and question answering. Furthermore, our method not only
099   retains the prior knowledge encapsulated within the LLM but also effectively reduces hallucinations
100   for questions that extend beyond the model’s internal knowledge base.

103   2     R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Generative question answering (GQA) aims to generate an abstractive answer rather than extract an
106   answer to a given question from provided passages (Fan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021). Early works
107   on GQA mostly tried to improve the faithfulness of the answer by investigating reliable external
      knowledge sources or incorporating multiple information sources. Yin et al. (2015) propose Neural


                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Generative Question Answering, an end-to-end model that generates answers to simple factoid
109   questions based on the knowledge base, while Bi et al. (2019) propose the Knowledge-Enriched
110   Answer Generator (KEAG) to generate a natural answer by integrating facts from four different
111   information sources, namely, questions, passages, vocabulary, and knowledge.
112
      Recent works focus more on the conditional generation model. Li et al. (2021) propose Rationale-
113
      Enriched Answer Generator (REAG), in which they add an extraction task to obtain the rationale
114
      for an answer at the encoding stage, and the decoder is expected to generate the answer based on
115   both the extracted rationale and original input. Su et al. (2022) propose a framework named RBG
116   (read before generate), to jointly models answer generation with machine reading. They augment the
117   generation model with fine-grained, answer-related salient information predicted by the MRC module,
118   to enhance answer faithfulness. Such methods can exploit and utilize the information in the original
119   input better, while they require the extra effort of building models to extract that information. CAD
120   (Shi et al., 2023) follows a contrastive output distribution that amplifies the difference between the
121   output probabilities when a model is used with and without context. RHO (Ji et al., 2023) introduce
122   local and global knowledge-grounding techniques into dialogue generation and further utilize a
123
      conversational reasoning model to re-rank the generated responses.
124   Our approach differs from these methods in 4 folds: 1. The external information incorporated by
125   existing baselines may be surface relevant but does not contain the information to support query
126   answering, which introduces distraction for model generation. However our ability of generating
127   informative evidences and conducting query reasoning improve as training proceeding. 2. In existing
128   baselines, the correctly exploit of external information beyond the internal knowledge to solve the
129
      query of model remains a challenge. However, our model needs to generate the evidence sentence
      from the document instead of internal knowledge, so it is trained to focus more on the document which
130
      mitigates hallucination. 3. Our method does not need external pretrained retriever or well-designed
131
      knowledge base to mitigate the hallucination of backbone model. 4. We provide the theory analysis
132   to explain and demonstrate the effectiveness of our method design.

189   3.2   S ELF -R EASONING
190
      In the absence of annotated evidence within the GQA dataset, we adopt the principle that accurate
191
      evidence should fully encapsulate the information necessary to address the query independently of
192
      the document. Consequently, we employ the LLM to reason from its generated evidence. Specifically,
193   we introduce a methodology termed self-reasoning, which involves two components: candidate
194   generation and correctness verification.
195
196
      During candidate generation, the LLM is instructed to produce candidate evidence supporting the
      query answering. This includes the original text from the document, while out-of-document candidates
197
      are filtered out to ensure the maintenance of factual accuracy. Though the filtered candidates are
198
      faithful, they do not necessarily contain the needed information for query (uninformative). In the
199   correctness verification stage, the LLM provides a response to the query based on the initially
200   generated candidates respectively. Evidence that fails to contain the required information will lead to
201   incorrect answers. Therefore, we evaluate the predicted answer against the correct answer denoted as
202   a∗ , so as to eliminate evidence that may be factually accurate but lacks informative value:
203
                                               ei = M [pe , d, q, si ]                                     (3)

208   to generate evidence from the document to answer the query, pa denotes the prompt to generate the
209   answer based on the query and evidence, and e denotes the filterer evidences for further training. To
210   this end, we construct the faithful and informative evidences for training without external tool.

213
214   Our triplet generation paradigm composes 3 modules, including Answer-Aware Evidence Generation
215   (QAE), Evidence-Enhanced Question Answering (QEA), Evidence-Aware Question Restoration
      (EAQ). QAE enables the model to focus on the document, extracting critical information directly

735   5. Model Architecture. EATQA is built on the widespread LLM, Llama (Touvron et al., 2023)
736   with a few additional learnable parameters. we additionally adopt several trainable adapter tokens
737   p = [p1 , p2 , · · · , pNp ] which are prepended to the key and value of each self-attention layer, where Np
738   is the number of adapter tokens. So the number of trainable parameters of EATQA 7B is 4.5M, only
739   0.06% of total parameters of LLama 7B. With such a few trainable parameters, EATQA effectively
740   preserves LLMs’ prior knowledge and the casual reasoning ability to understand the logical relations
741   between the question, evidence and answer. EATQA consists of three objectives: answer-aware
742   evidence generation, evidence-enhanced query answering and evidence-aware query restoration.
