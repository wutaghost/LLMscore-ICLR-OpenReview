009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), as a thriving field, explores how
012            multiple agents independently make decisions in a shared dynamic environment.
013            Due to environmental uncertainties, policies in MARL must remain robust to
014            tackle the sim-to-real gap. Although robust RL has been extensively explored in
015            single-agent settings, it has seldom received attention in self-play, where strategic
016
               interactions heighten uncertainties. We focus on robust two-player zero-sum
               Markov games (TZMGs) in offline RL, specifically on tabular robust TZMGs
017
               (RTZMGs) with a given uncertainty set. To address sample scarcity, we introduce
018
               a model-based algorithm (RTZ-VI-LCB) for RTZMGs, which integrates robust
019            value iteration considering uncertainty level and applies a data-driven penalty to
020            the robust value estimates. We establish the finite-sample complexity of RTZ-VI-
021            LCB by accounting for distribution shifts in the historical dataset. Our algorithm
022            is capable of learning under partial coverage and environmental uncertainty. An
023            information-theoretic lower bound is developed to show that learning RTZMGs is
024            at least as difficult as standard TZMGs when the uncertainty level is sufficiently
025            small. This confirms the tightness of our algorithm’s sample complexity, which
026            is optimal regarding both state and action spaces. To the best of our knowledge,
027
               our algorithm is the first to attain this optimality and establishes a new benchmark
               for offline RTZMGs. We also extend our algorithm to multi-agent general-sum
028
               Markov games, achieving a breakthrough in breaking the curse of multiagency.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), which aims to develop algorithms for multiple agents
034   to learn and make decisions in dynamic environments, has gained significant attention in areas
035   such as game playing (Silver et al., 2017), autonomous driving (Bhalla et al., 2020), and Path
036   Planning (Cao et al., 2020). Under the constraints on time or resources, a key challenge in applying
037   MARL to real-world scenarios is the restricted ability to interact or explore the environment. Offline
038   MARL, also named as batch MARL, addresses this issue by utilizing historical data collected from
039
      past interactions, often generated by unknown behavior policies. Researchers hope that this data
      can offer valuable insights into the optimal policy without the need for further exploration (Lambert
040
      et al., 2022). Beyond seeking to maximize the expected total rewards, a critical challenge lies
041
      in addressing environmental uncertainties stemming from model mismatches, system noise, and
042   the gap between simulation and real-world situations. Standard MARL algorithms that train in
043   ideal conditions are highly sensitive and prone to catastrophic failure when faced with even small
044   adversarial perturbations in the deployment environment (Zhang et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2021; Zeng
045   et al., 2022). However, historical data is often gathered under the assumption of model stability,
046   which is unrealistic due to the time-varying and non-stationary nature of real-world systems. Thus,
047   the robust guarantee is critical in offline settings, leading to the formulation of offline robust MARL.
048   As a specific setting of MARL, two-player zero-sum Markov games (TZMGs) are a fascinating
049   area of research, thus leading the field of robust TZMGs (RTZMGs) following from robust MARL.
050   The inherent solution concepts for RTZMGs encompass equilibria not just between the two players
051   but also between their adversaries, who select the worst-case environments from a predefined
052   uncertainty set for each player. This structure inherently offers greater robustness and stability when
053   facing unmodeled disruptions. Despite recent efforts (Kardeş et al., 2011; Blanchet et al., 2024;
      Zhang et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2023), there is still a lack of fundamental understanding in learning for

114   1.2   R ELATED W ORK

116
      In this section, we review a curated selection of related research, with an emphasis on provably
      efficient RL algorithms in the tabular setting, as these are the most pertinent to our work.
117
118
      Finite-sample studies of standard TZMGs. Markov games (MGs), or called stochastic games,
119
      were first proposed in the early 1950s (Shapley, 1953). Since then, extensive research has been
120   conducted, and MARL has gained significant attention (Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023), particularly
121   around Nash equilibrium (Littman, 1994; Lee et al., 2020). Numerous MARL algorithms with
122   provable convergence and asymptotic guarantees have been developed (Rashid et al., 2020). More
123   recent work has focused on creating algorithms for standard MARL with non-asymptotic guarantees
124   through finite-sample analysis. In this area, most efforts to compute Nash equilibria are focused on
125   TZMGs. The studies in (Bai & Jin, 2020) and (Xie et al., Jun. 2022) were the first to provide
126   non-asymptotic sample complexity guarantees for model-based (e.g., VI-Explore and VI-ULCB)
127   and model-free algorithms (e.g., OMNI-VI). Further improvements in sample complexity have been
128
      explored (Cui et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., July 2021; Feng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c).
129
      Robustness in MARL. Although progress has been made in standard MARL, existing algorithms
130
      may struggle when faced with environmental disturbances or uncertainties, leading to significantly
131
      deviated equilibria. Increasing research now focuses on enhancing MARL robustness against
132   uncertainties in different parts of MGs (Vial et al., 2022), including state (Zhou & Liu, 2023),
133   environment (reward and transition dynamics), agent types (Zhang et al., 2021), and other agents’
134   policies (Kannan et al., 2023). A typical method to address robustness against uncertainties of
135   the environment is distributionally robust optimization (DRO), which is a method predominantly
136   explored in supervised learning (Bertsimas et al., 2018; Gao, 2023; Blanchet & Murthy, 2019). The
137   application of DRO to manage model uncertainty in single-agent RL (Iyengar, 2005) has attracted
138   considerable attention. However, when extended to MARL, researchers formulated the problem as
139   robust MGs armed with DRO and developed a relatively understudied field with only a few proven
140
      algorithms (Blanchet et al., 2024; Kardeş et al., 2011; Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020; Shi
      et al., 2024b). Thus, relevant algorithms based on partial coverage of datasets while considering the
141
      uncertainty level are lacking.
142
143
      Single-agent robust RL. In single-agent RL, addressing uncertainties of environments using
144
      DRO—such as robust Markov decision processes (MDPs) and distributionally robust dynamic
145   programming—has attracted considerable interest in both theoretical research and practical
146   applications (Badrinath & Kalathil, 2021; Goyal & Grand-Clement, 2023). Recent work has focused
147   on the finite-sample performance of provable robust RL algorithms, exploring different divergence
148   functions for uncertainty sets, various sampling mechanisms, and related challenges (Yang et al.,
149   2023; Blanchet et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024a). Studies on robust MDPs, particularly relevant
150   here, use uncertainty sets based on TV distance (Liu & Xu, 2024) or Kullback-Leibler (KL)
151   divergence (Shi & Chi, 2024) in tabular settings. It has been shown that addressing robust MDPs
152   does not demand more samples compared with those needed for standard MDPs (Shi et al., 2024a).
153
      However, RTZMGs present additional complexities beyond those in robust single-agent RL.
154

      5    C ONCLUSION
526
      To balance model robustness with sample efficiency, we design an efficient robust model-based
527
      algorithm for offline RTZMGs, which is value iteration with lower confidence bounds for RTZMGs
528   (RTZ-VI-LCB). Our algorithm integrates robust VI with the principle of pessimism. By imposing a
529   tailored and mild assumption (robust unilateral clipped concentrability) on the historical dataset to
530   account for the distribution shift, we do not require full state-action space coverage. We address
531   robustness against the distribution shifts in the worse-case scenario of the shared environment,
532   analyze the finite-sample complexity of the proposed RTZ-VI-LCB algorithm, and establish an
533   information-theoretic lower bound to evaluate its optimality across various uncertainty levels.
534   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first provably optimal algorithm for offline RTZMGs that
535   addresses the dependency on states S and actions {A, B}, while accounting for model perturbations
536   and partial coverage. Furthermore, we extend RTZ-VI-LCB to multi-agent general-sum MGs,
537   demonstrating a breakthrough in breaking the curse of multiagency. Our algorithm opens up several
538   intriguing questions, such as designing efficient model-free algorithms for robust offline TZMGs
539   with partial coverage and exploring ways to adjust the size and metric of the uncertainty set to
      complete the algorithmic design.
