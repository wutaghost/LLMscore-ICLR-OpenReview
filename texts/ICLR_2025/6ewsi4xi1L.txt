009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual Question Answering (VQA) aims to answer natural language questions
012            based on information present in images. Recent advancements in multimodal large
013            language models (MLLMs) with internalized world knowledge, such as GPT-4o,
014            have demonstrated strong capabilities in addressing VQA tasks. However, in many
015            real-world cases, MLLMs alone are not enough, as they may lack domain-specific
016            or up-to-date knowledge relevant to images and questions. To mitigate this prob-
017            lem, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) from external knowledge bases (KBs),
018            known as KB-VQA, is promising for VQA. However, effectively retrieving relevant
019
               knowledge is not easy. Traditional wisdom typically converts images into text
               and employs unimodal (i.e., text-based) retrieval, which can lead to the loss of
020
               visual information and hinder accurate image-to-image matching. In this paper, we
021
               introduce fine-grained knowledge units including both text fragments and entity
022            images, which are extracted from KBs and stored in vector databases. We also
023            designed a knowledge unit retrieval-augmented generation (KU-RAG) method,
024            through fine-grained retrieval and MLLMs. KU-RAG can accurately find corre-
025            sponding knowledge, and integrate the retrieved knowledge with the internalized
026            MLLM knowledge using a knowledge correction chain for reasoning. Experimen-
027            tal results indicate that our method can significantly enhance the performance of
028            state-of-the-art KB-VQA solutions, with improvements by up to 10%.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION

033
      VQA with Knowledge Bases. Knowledge-based Visual Question Answering (KB-VQA) extends
034   traditional Visual Question Answering (VQA) by incorporating external knowledge to answer
035   questions where image information alone is insufficient (Shah et al., 2019; Marino et al., 2019).
036   However, traditional methods often face limitations in their ability to perform complex reasoning
037   over both visual content and external knowledge sources, as they typically rely on predefined retrieval
038   mechanisms or specific training data (Wu & Mooney, 2022; Yang et al., 2023).
039
      VQA with MLLMs. Recently, the emergence of multimodal large language models (MLLMs), such
040   as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), has introduced new possibilities for
041   VQA. Unlike previous methods, MLLMs serve not only as powerful reasoning engines but also as vast
042   knowledge repositories, with information learned from world knowledge during pretraining (Wang
043   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b). This dual capability enables more nuanced answers. However, the
044   knowledge acquired during training is general and (maybe outdated) world knowledge, limiting the
045   model’s ability to respond to domain-specific and update-to-date queries. As shown in Figure 1(a),
046   when using GPT-4 to ask a question about the bridge in the image, it fails to provide an answer due to
047   a lack of relevant knowledge and LLaVA even hallucinated and provided a “false” answer.
048   VQA with RAG and MLLMs. At this point, it becomes necessary to employ KB-VQA approachs,
049   by retrieving information from a database – a process also known as Retrieval-Augmented Generation
050   (RAG) in the context of LLMs (Fan et al., 2024). This typically involves converting images into
051   captions and then performing passage-level retrieval combined with the query. However, this method
052   struggles to handle fine-grained information for question answering, and during the image-to-text
053   modality conversion process, some visual details are inevitably lost. As shown in Figure 1(b), a
      unimodal, coarse-grained approach fails to retrieve the relevant knowledge.

      2.1          K NOWLEDGE - BASED V ISUAL Q UESTION A NSWERING
103
104   Knowledge-based Visual Question Answering (KB-VQA) aims to leverage external knowledge to
105   assist in answering questions about images (Marino et al., 2019; Gardères et al., 2020). In early
106   KB-VQA approaches (Zhu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022; Lin & Byrne, 2022), Wikipedia was
107   often used as the external knowledge source for KB-VQA, leading to the common adoption of a
      retriever-reader framework. This framework first retrieves textual knowledge relevant to the question


                                                                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



108
      and image, and then “reads” the text to predict the answer. However, this passage dense retrieval
109
      method is a unimodal, coarse-grained text-to-text retrieval process, which struggles with specific,
110   fine-grained questions such as visual entities (Hu et al., 2023a), events (Yang et al., 2023), and visual
111   information-seeking questions (Chen et al., 2023).
112
113
      Since the emergence of LLMs, some methods have explored using implicit knowledge from LLMs
      in addition to retrieving information from databases like Wikipedia. Typically, they convert images
114
      into tags or captions and then use GPT to retrieve related knowledge (Gui et al., 2021). However,
115
      there is a gap between the query and the LLM’s knowledge source. To address this, Hu et al. (2023b)
116   proposed a prompt-guided image captioning method that controls the visual entities in generated
117   captions based on textual queries, replacing general captions with question-dependent ones.
118
119
      Although some methods attempt to mitigate the loss of visual information by incorporating visual
      features (Salaberria et al., 2023) or enriching prompts with candidate answers (Shao et al., 2023),
120
      they have not completely solved the issue of information loss. In contrast, our approach directly
121
      leverages multimodal information for both retrieval and reasoning. In our method, the LLM not only
122   serves as a knowledge base but also plays the role of a reasoner.
123
124
      2.2   M ULTIMODAL R ETRIEVAL - AUGMENTED G ENERATION
125
126   Although LLMs possess strong general knowledge answering capabilities, they still face limitations
127   when dealing with domain-specific knowledge, outdated information, and avoiding hallucinations
128   (Gao et al., 2023). To address these issues, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) was developed.
129   RAG enhances the answering ability of LLMs by retrieving relevant document fragments from
130   external knowledge bases. Specifically, the RAG approach involves multiple modules such as data
131   storage, query optimization, document retrieval, and answer generation. The basic process matches
132   user queries with documents from a large external knowledge base, retrieves relevant document
133
      fragments, and generates answers by integrating this information through a generation model. This
      process is similar to the knowledge retrieval mechanisms used in KB-VQA.
134
135   Building on this, RAG has evolved to optimize the retrieval and generation processes. For exam-
136   ple, GRAG (Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation) improves the relevance of information and
137   generation quality by emphasizing the importance of subgraph (Hu et al., 2024). The FiD-RAG
138   (Fusion-in-Decoder RAG) model parallelly fuses multiple retrieved documents during the generation
139
      stage, allowing the model to comprehensively integrate background knowledge from different sources
      (Izacard & Grave, 2020). Moreover, DPR-RAG (Dense Passage Retrieval RAG) introduces dense
140
      retrieval techniques that significantly improve retrieval accuracy, quickly locating highly relevant
141
      fragments from large document collections (Karpukhin et al., 2020).
142
143   Unlike the aforementioned methods, our goal is to build a multimodal, fine-grained RAG model that
144   combines the internal knowledge of LLMs to generate high-quality answers in zero-shot scenarios.

185   3.2     K NOWLEDGE U NIT
186
187   3.2.1     D EFINITION
188
189   We introduce a new structure called Knowledge Unit (KU). Each KU serves as a knowledge carrier
190
      or object generated in combination with the query, such as entities, events, rules, topics, etc., designed
      to bridge the gap between the query and the database during the actual question-answering process.
191
192   For a piece of knowledge, the three most important factors are its image, its name, and detailed
193   textual knowledge. Therefore, we designed each KU as a triplet, consisting of Knowledge Image
194   (Ki ), Knowledge Name (Kn ), and Knowledge Text (Kt ):
195
196                                                                     KU = {Ki , Kn , Kt }                                                                                                                           (1)
197
198   In the KB-VQA task, image-image or name-name matching is typically used to determine which
199   piece of knowledge a given image belongs to. Hence, we encapsulate Ki and Kn into the ‘Matching
200   End’ to link the query and the KU. The purpose of KB-VQA often involves querying the knowledge
201   behind an image, so we refer to Kt as the ‘Detail End’.
202
203   3.2.2     C ONSTRUCTION
204
      Knowledge Predefinition. Firstly, we should determine how to extract the knowledge unit with the
205
      application scenario and consider the query and database. For example, in an object recognition QA
206
      system, different entities can serve as knowledge units; in an event query system, different events can
207   serve as knowledge units; in a corporate rules and regulations query system, a rule-based knowledge
208   unit should be constructed. It is important to note that knowledge units do not necessarily need to be
209   atomic or as fine-grained as possible. Its division should be determined based on the granularity of
210   the data in the query and the database. For instance, a general animal knowledge QA system may
211   only require the general species of an animal (e.g., “cat”), whereas a specific species QA system may
212   require the specific species name (e.g., “Persian cat”).
213
214   Knowledge Segment. Since subsequent steps involve the storage of the knowledge unit, storing
215   textual knowledge Kt within the detail end at the document level, which is a coarse-grained storage
      method, is highly detrimental to knowledge retrieval (Chen et al., 2021). Additionally, during reason-

      3.3.3      MLLM R EASONING AND G ENERATION
330
331   After retrieving the relevant blocks, the next step is to provide the retrieved information to the MLLM
332   to assist with reasoning and generation. The specific steps are as follows:
333
      Modal Aligning and Fusing: First, based on the retrieval results C ′ , we find the corresponding
334   knowledge unit KU ′ for each chunk and combine its matching end information to form an image with
335   the structure ‘[Image][[Name][Chunk Text]]’, where the image corresponding to the i-th chunk is
336   denoted as Ii′ . Notably, if multiple chunks correspond to the same image, to enhance the connection
337   between knowledge and improve processing efficiency, we merge the texts of these chunks into a
338   single image in the format ‘[Image][[Name][Chunk T ext1 ][Chunk T ext2 ]. . . [Chunk T extn ]]’.
339
      Images Stitching: Next, we stitch all the images obtained in the previous step to generate a
340
      multimodal passage with both image and text information. This multimodal passage M P is as:
341
342
343                                                  M P = (I1′ , I2′ , . . . , In′ )                             (11)
344
345   Knowledge Correction Chain: At this stage, a key challenge is effectively managing the relation-
346   ships among the ‘information in the query’, ‘the knowledge retrieved’, and ‘the inherent knowledge
347
      of the MLLM’, as well as ensuring a fine-grained correspondence between text and images.
348   In our experiments, we found that when combining the retrieved knowledge with the question for the
349   MLLM to answer, the model tended to prioritize the retrieved information while neglecting its own
350   knowledge. We also attempted to use guiding prompts, such as “Based on your own knowledge first...”
351   and “Focus on the first image and ignore other image...” to encourage the MLLM to consider its own
352   knowledge before referring to the retrieved information, but the results were unsatisfactory (as
353
      demonstrated in Section 4.5).
354   To address this issue, we design a Knowledge Correction Chain (KCC) that guides MLLMs in
355   reasoning through multi-turn dialogue and reading comprehension. In details, we first input the
356   question Q to MLLM to obtain the original answer A0 :
357
358                                                     A0 = MLLM(Q)                                              (12)
359
360   The purpose of this step is to obtain the pure knowledge of the MLLM regarding the query, without
361   being influenced by the retrieved information mentioned above. Finally, we input the passage M P
362   into the MLLM with a knowledge correction aware (KC-aware) prompt and get the final answer A:
363
364        [KC-aware prompt]: The initial answer has already been provided. The new image information may
365        either be related or unrelated to the previous input. If this new information conflicts with the initial
366        answer, please update the response accordingly. If no changes are needed, simply output the initial
367        answer again.
368
369
370                                            A = MLLM(M P, P rompt, (Q, A0 ))                                   (13)
371
372   In short, the idea of KCC is to shift the MLLM’s focus from analyzing the relationship between
373   “information in the query”, “the inherent knowledge of the MLLM”, and “the knowledge retrieved” to
374
      allow “the knowledge retrieved” to correct the MLLM’s responses, fostering a reflective process. We
      have also attempted to use a single prompt to have the MLLM generate and then reflect on its answer,
375
      but it still gets influenced by the retrieved information.
376
377   In this way, we can fully utilize multimodal information and handle the fine-grained correspondences
      between them, enhancing the MLLMs’ ability to reason and answer questions in complex scenarios.

      3.4   DATABASE M ANAGEMENT
379
380   Since our approach operates at the database level, compared to traditional methods, we must also
381   consider issues related to data management. Additionally, there is no need to retrain the entire
382   framework after adding, deleting, or updating data or knowledge.
383
      Knowledge Addition: When new knowledge is introduced, we directly chunk the corresponding
384
      text. The encoded text vectors are then stored in the existing text vector database Dc . Similarly,
385   for images within the knowledge base, we encode them and store the resulting vectors in the image
386   vector database Di .
387
388
      Knowledge Unit Management: After performing operations of the raw data, it is necessary to
      consider whether corresponding actions need to be taken for the knowledge unit.
389
390   1) KU Addition and Update: When new raw data is added, it is essential to assess whether a new
391   KU needs to be introduced. This process primarily involves two steps: first, matching the new
392   knowledge with existing KU. If the similarity of the matching results exceeds the threshold α, the
393   index containing the new keywords is added to the corresponding KU through keyword matching. If
394
      no matching result exceeds the threshold, a new KU is constructed according to the KU construction
      rules and the new keywords present in the chunk.
395
396   2) KU Deletion: After deleting a chunk from the raw data, it is necessary to check whether the related
397   KU is still valid to reduce storage usage. Specifically, after deleting the chunk indexed as i, all KUs
398   containing this index should be checked. If a certain KU has an empty detail end (i.e., no remaining
399   values), that KU can be deleted.

      across all datasets. In a zero-shot scenario, even without reviewing the training set knowledge,

474
      Enhancement of MLLM by KU-RAG: Combining KU-RAG with gpt-4o results in performance
      improvements of 4.2%, 2.3%, 1.7%, and 10.99% on the respective tasks. The largest improvement is
475
      seen in the E-VQA dataset, as it involves less popular knowledge, and the new knowledge provided by
476
      KU-RAG significantly enhances model performance. In contrast, the improvement on the OK-VQA
477   dataset is smaller because it involves open-domain general knowledge, which MLLM may have
478   already encountered during pre-training, allowing it to answer effectively.

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce the Knowledge Unit Retrieval-Augmented Generation (KU-RAG) method,
533
      aimed at enhancing MLLMs by incorporating fine-grained retrieval of domain-specific knowledge.
534   To improve the effectiveness of retrieval, we propose the concept of “knowledge units”, which
535   allows for more targeted access to relevant information. Furthermore, we design a knowledge
536   correction chain strategy to verify and refine the retrieved knowledge, which can mitigate errors and
537   hallucinations, enhancing the overall reliability and coherence of the generated answers in VQA
538   tasks. Our experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains across multiple KB-VQA
539   benchmarks, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach. Future research directions could explore
      dynamic knowledge updates stategy to improve multimodal retrieval and semantic integration.
