011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large transformers pretrained with language model objectives have demonstrated
014            success in multiple fields, and have tremendous potential for modeling single-
015            cell RNA-seq and spatial transcriptomics data. However, these approaches are
016            yet to overcome various challenges, including inductive biases that hinder gen-
017            eralization, artifacts and quality of the underlying data, as well as downstream
018            evaluation pipelines that do not reflect the biological challenges in the field. In
019            this work, we propose a new framework, sCellTransformer (sCT ), that relies
020
               on a first principles formulation of the problem as well as a validation pipeline
               designed to evaluate models generalization through zero-shot predictions. sCT
021
               leverages a long-range convolutional-transformer architecture that is trained from
022
               unprocessed single-cell and spatial transcriptomics data. In contrast to previous
023            works, sCT represents cells with up to 20,000 protein-coding genes, processes sets
024            of multiple cells, and predicts about a million discretized gene expression tokens.
025            We show that representing gene expression as discrete levels allows us to mitigate
026            the high sparsity present in single-cell data both during training and evaluation.
027            We present state-of-the-art empirical results on several zero-shot gene expression
028            imputation, cell-typing, and clustering tasks in both single-cell as well as spatial
029            domains, outperforming current foundation models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Assays measuring gene transcription, such as single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) and spa-
036   tial transcriptomics (ST), have become indispensable tools in biology. These next-generation se-
037   quencing (NGS) technologies provide high-resolution insights into cellular mechanisms by analyz-
038   ing RNA expression within individual cells or localized cell populations in tissues. ScRNA-seq has
039
      proven invaluable for assessing tumors at the genetic level (De Falco et al., 2023; Dohmen et al.,
      2022), identifying rare cell types (Jindal et al., 2018), and characterizing gene regulation across tis-
040
      sues (Kartha et al., 2022). ST extends scRNA-seq by incorporating positional information alongside
041
      gene expression, facilitating more precise modeling of cellular interactions. The widespread adop-
042   tion of these assays has led to a surge in publicly available, high-quality sequencing data, creating a
043   demand for approaches that can effectively analyze and interpret this data.
044
045
      This need has fueled the development of deep learning-based methods (Cui et al., 2024; Wen et al.,
      2023; Schaar et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022; Rosen et al., 2023; Lopez et al., 2018; 2019; Lotfollahi
046
      et al., 2019) aimed at learning transferable, contextual representations of single-cell data. These
047
      methods tackle complex downstream tasks like cell-type annotation, data integration, and gene ex-
048   pression imputation (Lähnemann et al., 2020). More recently, self-supervised models trained with
049   language modeling objectives, such as BERT (Devlin, 2018) and GPT (Brown, 2020), have been
050   adapted to this domain. These approaches generally treat gene sequences within individual cells as
051   input sequences, incorporating gene identifiers and their corresponding expression levels. Masked
052   language modeling or autoregressive modeling is then employed to predict gene expressions or gene
053   IDs. Models like scGPT (Cui et al., 2024), scBERT (Yang et al., 2022), and CellPLM (Wen et al.,
      2023) exemplify this framework for learning cell-level embeddings.

078
079   However, the Transformer architecture, while capable of capturing non-local patterns, is constrained
080   by its quadratic memory scaling with respect to input length. The vast number of genes in a cell
081
      (around 20,000 coding genes) limits models like scBERT and scGPT to processing a single cell at
      a time, often requiring gene subset selection. CellPLM introduced the concept of ”bags-of-cells,”
082
      demonstrating the benefits of encoding multiple cells simultaneously. However, their gene expres-
083
      sion embedder, which sums gene embeddings across cells, can lead to information loss. Moreover,
084   these models typically predict continuous gene expression values, neglecting the inherent noise and
085   sparsity of scRNA-seq data.
086
087
      Another challenge is gene-level dropout, a consequence of capturing only a fraction of expressed
      mRNA (Haque et al., 2017), and is a prevalent noise source in scRNA-seq data, with expression
088
      estimates that vary significantly depending on experimental conditions. Single-cell data is also
089
      inherently sparse (Jiang et al., 2022). For example, in the CxG dataset (CZI Single-Cell Biology
090   et al., 2023), on average, less than 6% of protein-coding genes are expressed in a cell. Standard
091   regression losses like mean squared error (MSE) do not effectively account for this highly skewed
092   data. Additionally, ST technology, being array-based, samples only a limited subset of cells within a
093   tissue, resulting in missing gene expression data for cells outside the array spots. We believe future
094   models should be evaluated on their ability to predict these missing values.
095
      Furthermore, current benchmarks for evaluating single-cell models often lack a focus on general-
096   ization. ScRNA-seq analysis is largely exploratory, often involving clustering latent cell vectors
097   for cell typing without predefined labels (Hie et al., 2020; Argelaguet et al., 2021; Heumos et al.,
098   2023). However, models like scGPT, geneformer, scBERT, and CellPLM require fine-tuning on
099   new datasets for optimal performance (Kedzierska et al., 2023). This highlights a discrepancy be-
100   tween the field’s need for exploratory analysis and the current limitations of single-cell founda-
101   tion models. Therefore, we advocate for evaluating these models on their zero-shot performance,
102   which we believe is crucial for bridging this gap and advancing the field. This aligns with similar
103   progress towards zero-shot foundation models in protein (Truong Jr & Bepler, 2023) and genomics
104
      research (Nguyen et al., 2024).
105   To address these limitations, we introduce sCT , a novel architecture based on a convolutional-
106   Transformer design that can process up to 1 million gene expression values per input. This enables
107   sCT to process up to 50 cells simultaneously, encompassing all 20,000 protein-coding genes. Instead
      of continuous predictions, sCT predicts discrete gene expression values over a fixed number of

108
      levels. This approach mitigates measurement noise and accounts for data sparsity. Notably, we
109   emphasize the use of the Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) (Matthews, 1975), which is robust
110   to label imbalance (Chicco & Jurman, 2020). We also introduce a new benchmark focused on
111   evaluating zero-shot capabilities in new data domains, revealing the limitations of existing methods
112   like scGPT in this setting. Our sCT architecture effectively addresses this gap and demonstrates
113   significantly improved zero-shot performance.
114
      Formally, our contributions are as follows:
115
116
      1. We present the first single-cell model architecture in transcriptomics capable of processing up
117
         to 1 million gene expression values (tokens) as input, far exceeding the capacity of existing
118
         Transformer-based models. sCT can be trained on both single-cell and spatial transcriptomics
119      data simultaneously.
120
      2. We propose a shift in how single-cell models are evaluated in transcriptomics by introducing a
121      new benchmark focused on zero-shot performance in new domains. We also advocate for using
122      metrics robust to highly imbalanced datasets, demonstrating the shortcomings of current state-
123      of-the-art models in such settings.
124   3. We show that our architecture overcomes some limitations of current single-cell models for tran-
125      scriptomics, achieving substantially improved zero-shot performance in new domains.
126   4. We provide a comprehensive ablation study to support our design choices and validate the model.
127      We believe the architectural choices presented establish best practices for future research in this
128      field.

134   Prior to the surge in recent developments for pretrained foundation models, deep learning approaches
135   such as variational auto-encoders (Bereket & Karaletsos, 2023; Roohani et al., 2024), supervised or
136   training (Lotfollahi et al., 2019; Lopez et al., 2018), and semi-supervised training (Xu et al., 2021;
137   2024) were successful at solving specific tasks in transcriptomics. More recently, advances in large
138
      Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017), enabled the training of foundation models where a
      single model can solve a wide range of tasks in computational biology, specifically transcriptomics.
139
140
141
      Self-supervised models for scRNA-seq ScBERT (Yang et al., 2022) and scGPT (Cui et al., 2024)
142
      leverage BERT-style (Devlin, 2018) masked language modeling and next-token prediction (Brown,
143   2020) respectively on gene expression sequences for representation learning. XtrimoGene (Gong
144   et al., 2023) and scFoundation (Hao et al., 2024a) build upon these by adding a learnable discretiza-
145   tion layer. Nicheformer (Schaar et al., 2024) also leverages BERT-style training by carefully curating
146   scRNA-seq data and extending sequence tokenization to include metadata for species, tissue, and
147   sequencing assay, as well as leveraging spatial transcriptomics datasets and assay-specific gene ex-
148   pression bias terms. Models like Levine et al. (2024) and Theodoris et al. (2023) use objectives like
149   next-token prediction or masked language modeling respectively on rank-ordered gene identifiers to
150   learn useful representations. We point the readers to Heydari & Sindi (2023) for a thorough survey
151
      of recent methods in the field.
152
153
      Spatial Transcriptomics (ST) Spatial transcriptomics data is often integrated alongside scRNA-
154
      seq (Lopez et al., 2019), or histopathological images (Jaume et al., 2024) to model cellular inter-
155
      actions in tissues. Typically, this is done through supervised training (Biancalani et al., 2021) or
156   contrastive learning (Li et al., 2023). SpaGE (Abdelaal et al., 2020) focused on deconvolving such
157   sequences into identifiable mixtures of celltypes for interpretation. Other approaches focus more
158   on representation learning through graph neural networks (Ma et al., 2024), self-supervised learn-
159   ing (Xu et al., 2024), or dictionary learning (Hao et al., 2024b). However, these approaches often
160   require matched multimodal data, or do not generalize without being finetuned. CellPLM (Wen
161   et al., 2023) improves upon these approaches by training a transformer-based model on collections
      of cell, allowing their model to leverage the mutual information between proximal cells in situ.

      3   S C ELLT RANSFORMER ( S CT)
187
188
      In this section, we present sCT , a model for single-cell and spatial transcriptomics data, trained
      through masked language modeling. We present a series of important choices in the architecture
189
      that led to significant performance improvements over competitor models.
190
191
      Data processing. Similarly to other transcriptomics pipelines (Wen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022),
192
      we select and keep m known protein-coding gene identifiers from Ensembl (Martin et al., 2022),
193
      with m ≈ 20, 000. The positions of these genes across all the different cells are fixed for con-
194   structing the input sequence. For every study, we also zero out gene expression values for genes
195   where the total count is less than 0.003 times the number of total cells, as these are considered to
196   be noisy measurements (Cui et al., 2024). We apply log transformation on the raw gene expressions
197   and then bin the non-zero gene expressions for every cell using a uniform binning on the range of
198   non-zero gene expression values. Gene expression values are therefore encoded into discrete levels.
199   Note that all non-expressed or unmeasured genes are encoded as the zero bin, and included in the
200   representation. We do not use any other preprocessing operations, in an effort to reduce biases.
201
202   Input representation. As gene expression values are binned, we represent them as tokens with a
203   vocabulary size equaling the number of bins. Embeddings are learned to represent each token. To
204
      allow the model to exploit intercellular redundancies and learn co-expressions, we stack k processed
      cells into a single input sequence. The final input sequence can then be represented as a sequence
205
      of k × m gene expression levels (see Fig. 1 for reference). Note that, for scRNA-seq, the sequence
206
      is constructed by sampling cells from the same study, while for spatial transcriptomics, by sampling
207   within the neighborhood of a cell. Motivated by recent works Wen et al. (2023), we use k = 50
208   cells at training time. Each input sequence thus consists of approximately 106 tokens of binned gene
209   expression values.
210
211   Convolutional-transformer architecture. To address the quadratic scaling of self-attention with
212   input length, which prohibits processing 106 tokens with standard transformers, we propose a
213   convolutional-transformer architecture. Inspired by the UNet architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015)
214   and its adaptation for biological sequences (Linder et al., 2023), our model employs a convolutional
215   tower to compress the input via consecutive 1D convolution and max pooling layers. This signif-
      icantly reduces computational cost while preserving local gene expression patterns. The resulting


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      compressed embeddings are then processed by a series of standard Transformer blocks (Vaswani
217   et al., 2017) to capture global interactions. This bottleneck layer maintains cellular context, crucial
218   for accurately modeling scRNA-seq data. Finally, a deconvolutional block, utilizing residual con-
219   nections, upsamples the embeddings to the original input length. The imputation head then predicts
220   gene expression from these learned embeddings. While using convolutions for unordered data like
221   scRNA-seq might seem counterintuitive, our primary motivation was dimensionality reduction to
222   allow for input of multiple cells. The convolutional architecture presents an efficient mechanism to
223   achieve this, while also outperforming baselines as seen in our results below.
224
225   Shared Layer Norm. We apply layer norm (Lei Ba et al., 2016) with a learned bias and scale
226   shared across gene positions for every cell. Contrary to the standard layer norm, which learns a
227   common normalization bias and scales across all tokens, here we assign learnable biases and scales
228   to every single gene position in a cell. These parameters are therefore repeated k times where k is
229   the number of cells per sequence. Intuitively, these layers learn gene level statistics across the cells,
230   and over the training dataset. We show empirically that these normalization layers play a significant
231
      role in improving gene expression predictions in our ablation studies.
232
233   Positional Embeddings. Standard Transformer models add positional encoding to their input data.
234   Here, as there is no natural order neither in the cells nor in the genes, we removed this encoding.
235   Instead, for single-cell data we replace it by a simple constant encoding per cell that is broadcast to
236   all the genes within the cell. In the case of spatial transcriptomics data, this constant encoding is
237   replaced by a 2D aware sinusoidal position encoding (SPE) that represents the relative positions of
238
      cells within the FOV. Similar to Klemmer et al. (2023), we construct them to ensure scale invariance
      across fields of view and experiments, see Eq. 1.

247   Stratified masking procedure. To address data imbalance, sCT employs a stratified masking
248   approach during masked language modeling (MLM) training (Devlin, 2018). Instead of uniform
249   masking, non-expressed genes are masked with a 1% probability, while expressed genes are masked
250   at 15%. These ratios, determined from training data statistics, ensure a balanced number of masked
251   tokens across expression levels. This strategy effectively counters the bias introduced by imbalanced
252   expression levels, as demonstrated by improved performance in ablations in Sec. 4.
253
254   Loss function. In opposition to competitor models (Wen et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
255   2022), sCT predicts probabilities over a vocabulary of tokens instead of continuous values. We thus
256   replace the mean squared error loss by a cross-entropy loss.
257
258   2-stage training. sCT is trained in two phases using a masked language modeling objective. First,
259   the model is trained on single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) data. We utilize the Cell x Gene
260   (CxG) API to collect all human studies containing both normal and diseased cells, splitting them
261   into training and testing sets at the study level. This yields a training set of 42 million cells. Second,
262   training continues with spatial transcriptomics data from HEST-1K (Jaume et al., 2024), which
263   encompasses data across diverse disease types, tissues, and acquisition methods. We focus solely
264   on human transcriptomics studies within HEST-1K and harmonize the gene identifiers with those
265   used in the scRNA-seq data, resulting in 0.69 million cells for training. This scale is comparable to
266
      that used in CellPLM (Wen et al., 2023). Our models is trained on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU for
      five days. Evaluations are conducted on the same hardware and take approximately 20 minutes. The
267
      model1 has approximately 80 million parameters, a size comparable to CellPLM and scGPT.

293
294   As motivated above, we introduce a benchmark focused on evaluating generalization capabilities of
295   transcriptomics self-supervised models to data domains that were not present during training. As
296   such, we study three zero-shot tasks (1) gene imputation, (2) whole cell imputation, and (3) cell
297   embedding clustering and compare sCT to scRNA-seq model competitors and standard bioinfor-
298   matics baselines. See Fig. 2 for more details about the tasks. Finally, we provide an ablation study
299
      to validate our different architectural choices.
300
301
      Evaluation Data. To evaluate model performance, we curated six datasets each for the single-cell
      and spatial transcriptomics domains. All tasks are performed on each dataset, except for whole-cell
302
      masking, which is applicable only to the spatial transcriptomics domain. For single-cell RNA se-
303
      quencing (scRNA-seq) evaluation, we construct a test set comprising held-out studies from Cell x
304   Gene (CxG) spanning six different tissues. Each study includes a diverse set of cell types, encom-
305   passing both normal and diseased cells. We apply the same preprocessing steps used for training. To
306   ensure fair comparison, we maximize the overlap of gene identifiers between the token vocabularies
307   of all models and the test sets. For spatial transcriptomics evaluation, we utilize six held-out fields of
308   view (FOVs) from the HEST dataset, each corresponding to a distinct tissue type. Again, we follow
309   the same preprocessing steps as described earlier. The datasets cover the following tissues: lymph
310   node, colon, lung, kidney, brain, and rectum. Three studies consist of normal cells, two consist of
311   cancerous cells, and one consists of treated cells.
312
313

      and scBERT (Yang et al., 2022). To the best of our knowledge, CellPLM is currently the only
314
      other model that can naturally handle both scRNA-seq and spatial data and that also uses a similar
315
      approach of ingesting multiple cells at the same time. In the case of the cell embedding clustering
316   task (third task in Fig. 2), we also add Geneformer (Theodoris et al., 2023) to the list of baselines.
317   We have also added several strong bioinformatic baselines for gene imputation and cell clustering;
318   scVI (Gayoso et al., 2022), and scanpy (Wolf et al., 2018) with logistic regression, and k-nearest
319   neighbors for cell-typing, and MAGIC (van Dijk et al., 2018) for gene imputation.

353   Zero-shot Gene Imputation Results. Gene expression imputation, illustrated in Fig. 2, is a bene-
354   ficial task for training single-cell foundation models (Wen et al., 2023). Improving performance on
355   imputation develops learning co-expression across genes, and (in our formulation) across cells. For
356   evaluation, we randomly mask gene expression values at varying masking ratios by replacing them
357
      with the <MASK> token, and compare against other models. For other baselines, we apply masking
      and preprocessing as appropriate; CellPLM represents masked positions with 0 expression and takes
358
      several cells as input, while scGPT or scBERT only allow for gene expression values within a single
359
      cell. For MAGIC, we use the implementation from scanPy (Wolf et al., 2018) with the standard
360   parameters.

362   The results of these experiments are shown in Tab. 8 for scRNA-seq data and spatial transcriptomics
363
      data respectively. In order to ensure a fair comparison with models like scGPT and CellPLM, which
      output continuous values, we bin the output predictions with the bin-edges that we calculate during
364
      preprocessing. CellPLM and scGPT predict only non-zero gene expression values, so we remove
365
      unexpressed genes from the evaluation. Our results demonstrate that sCT exhibits higher correlation
366   when reconstructing partial gene sequences, and is able to better exploit information coming from
367   multiple cells compared to CellPLM. We also evaluate the effect of using multiple cells as input, by
368   training and testing variants of sCT with one and ten cells in Fig. 9. We observe that using 50 cells
369   improves upon gene imputation especially in the high masking ratio regime.

399   Zero-shot Whole-Cell Expression Imputation Results. This task evaluates a model’s capacity
400   to impute the gene expression of entire cells within a spatial context. This is crucial for under-
401   standing relationships between neighboring cells in spatial transcriptomics, which has implications
402   for realizing their therapeutic potential (Arora et al., 2023; Park & Lin, 2023). To assess this, we
403   designed a whole-cell imputation task. Here, we mask the gene expression for one or more entire
404   cells and challenge the model to predict these masked values based on the expression of neighboring
405
      cells. This setup mimics the array designs used in spatial transcriptomics, where gene expression is
      measured only at specific spots on a slide. This can lead to the undersampling or complete omission
406
      of cells, depending on their overlap with these spots. Our task simulates this effect, evaluating the
407
      model’s ability to reconstruct missing gene expression profiles from the spatial context.
408
409   Table 2 presents the results for this task in both scRNA-seq and spatial transcriptomics settings. In
410   addition to CellPLM2 , we include a per-gene heuristic inspired by k-nearest neighbors smoothing
411
      (Wagner et al., 2017) that leverages a k-NN estimator with k = 5. The results demonstrate that sCT
      effectively leverages spatial neighborhood information to predict masked cell values and reconstruct
412
      complete gene expression sequences.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
493
494
      This paper contributes to establishing best practices for building and evaluating single-cell models
495   for single-cell and spatial transcriptomics data. We advocate for evaluation pipelines that assess a
496   model’s zero-shot generalization capabilities and the use of metrics robust to data imbalance. We
497   also introduce a series of novel architectural choices for language models in this domain, culminat-
498   ing in the proposed sCT model. Through extensive evaluation across three tasks and 12 datasets (six
499   single-cell and six spatial), we demonstrate that current state-of-the-art single-cell models exhibit
500   poor generalization. In contrast, sCT shows drastically improved zero-shot generalization, outper-
501   forming even strong, trained bioinformatics baselines. Finally, we conduct a comprehensive ablation
502   study to validate our architectural choices.
503   While this work represents a significant step forward, limitations remain. First, sCT does not in-
504   corporate explicit gene symbol representations, which could be valuable for regulatory network
505   analysis. Second, we do not leverage metadata like cell type and tissue type during pretraining. We
506   believe that incorporating such metadata would not only enhance performance but also enable the
507   model to address complex tasks such as predicting perturbation effects and tissue-specific responses.
508   Finally, as more experimental data becomes available, we envision expanding our zero-shot bench-
509
      mark to include additional tasks like cell-to-cell communication, perturbation effect prediction, and
      gene regulatory network inference. We hope to explore these avenues in future work.

      Single-cell RNAseq data We source scRNA-seq data from CZI CellxGene (CZI Single-Cell Bi-
759   ology et al., 2023) using the CxG Census API. The API allows for downloading public single-cell
760   RNA-sequencing data across different tissues, disease types, and cell types. It also provides several
761   filtering mechanisms that we use to ensure the quality of our dataset. We use the LTS version 2023-
762   12-15 as our data source, which consists of approximately 62 million cells across 15,588 donors.
763   Tab. 5 shows a tissue-wise distribution of our data. We use the same API filters as scGPT (Cui et al.,
764   2024) to filter out duplicate cells wherever possible, to collect a total of 65 million cells. These cells
765   are then separated by their study identifiers, followed by selecting 635 studies for training. We hold
766   out six independent studies from different tissues: embryo, kidney, pancreas, blood, lung, and brain,
767
      for our zero-shot evaluations. The remaining studies are used for validation and hyperparameter
      selection. We also preprocess these studies by discarding any genes that are not well-represented in
768
      a study using the scanpy.pp.filter_genes with operation. Finally, we use Ensembl (Martin
769
      et al., 2022) to select only the common protein coding genes across all studies.
770
771   For each training sequence, we sample 50 cells at a time from a study, log-normalize, and then bin
772   the gene expressions at the cellular level. These normalized sequences are then stacked into a single
773
      sequence, and input to sCT .

       D.3        I MPACT OF THE NUMBER OF BINS
1043
1044   A core part of sCT is that we bin gene expressions at a cellular level, and treat them as tokens.
1045   Particularly, we choose five bins to represent unexpressed, low expression, moderate expression,
1046   high-moderate expression, and high expression levels for every cell. We present an ablation of this
1047   hyperparameter by also trying a larger number of bins (15 bins). This represents a finer-grained
1048   categorization of gene expressions in a cell. We measure mean-squared log error (MSLE), and
1049   mean absolute error (MAE) for the two models. Fig. 10 shows that we can improve our estimations
1050   of continuous-valued gene expression counts by using more bins.
1051   However, given the sparsity of the gene expression sequence of a cell, uniformly binning gene
1052   expressions often leads to several gene expression levels being under-represented in our training
1053   data. We find that this affects our performance on our predicting gene expression levels when we
1054   consider the MCC metric, leading to noisy estimates (see Fig. 10(c)). We therefore discretize gene
1055   expressions into five bins.

       We also analyze the effect of our stratified masking approach in Fig. 11 by comparing sCT trained
1072
       with two separate masking ratio schemes: (1) 1% zeros masked, 15% non-zeros masked, and (2) 5%
1073
       zeros masked, 15 % non-zeros masked. In the first case, we arrive at the masking ratios by analyzing
1074   the training dataset and calculating the relative distribution of unexpressed versus expressed genes.
1075   We find that this is ≈ 1 : 15 for our training split. We trained the second model to study if masking
1076   more unexpressed genes is really helpful, as the relative information contained in unexpressed gene
1077   expressions is fairly low. We see that using a stratified masking strategy helps when estimating
1078   discrete gene expression levels, especially at higher masking ratios. However, the performance on
1079   estimating raw gene expression levels is relative unaffected at higher masking ratios. We note that
       both the models outperform CellPLM (Wen et al., 2023).
