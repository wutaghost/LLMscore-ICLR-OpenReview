                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012            Masked Image Modeling has gained prominence as a powerful self-supervised
013            learning approach for visual representation learning by reconstructing masked-out
014            patches of pixels. However, the use of random spatial masking can lead to failure
015
               cases in which the learned features are not predictive of downstream labels. In
               this work, we introduce a novel masking strategy that targets principal compo-
016
               nents instead of image patches. The learning task then amounts to reconstructing
017
               the information of masked-out principal components. The principal components
018            of a dataset contain more global information than patches, such that the informa-
019            tion shared between the masked input and the reconstruction target should involve
020            more high-level variables of interest. This property allows principal components
021            to offer a more meaningful masking space, which manifests in improved quality
022            of the learned representations. We provide empirical evidence across natural and
023            medical datasets and demonstrate substantial improvements in image classifica-
024            tion tasks. Our method thus offers a simple and robust data-driven alternative to
025            traditional Masked Image Modelling approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   Masked Image Modeling (MIM; Pathak et al., 2016; He et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2022; Xie et al.,
032   2022) draws inspiration from masked language modeling (e.g., BERT; Devlin, 2018), where parts
033
      of a sentence are masked, and a model has to learn to predict the missing words. Similarly, in MIM,
      portions of an image are masked out, and a model has to reconstruct the missing parts from the
034
      visible ones. To do well at this task, it is thought that the model is forced to learn a meaningful
035
      representation of the visual content in the process (Kong et al., 2023). Empirically, this approach
036   indeed tends to produce representations that perform particularly well when fine-tuned on various
037   downstream tasks, such as image classification and semantic segmentation (He et al., 2021).
038
039
      The MIM paradigm has led to significant advances in the field of self-supervised learning (SSL)
      of visual representations (Pathak et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2021; He et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2022;
040
      Xie et al., 2022; Baevski et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023) and has been particularly effective when
041
      combined with Vision Transformers (ViT; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). A prominent example of this
042   is the Masked Autoencoder (MAE; He et al., 2021), which consists of two core components: a ViT
043   encoder-decoder architecture and a masking strategy that randomly selects a fixed ratio of square
044   image patches. The encoder processes the visible patches (along with their positional embeddings)
045   into a representation that the decoder can use to accurately reconstruct the masked-out content.
046
      While the inner workings of MIM in general, and MAEs in particular, remain under-explored and
047
      poorly understood (Zhang et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2023), Kong et al. (2023) recently suggested
048   a potential explanation from a latent variable model perspective: by splitting an image into two
049   parts and asking the model to predict one from the other, MAEs are compelled to pick up on any
050   information shared between the two that is helpful to solve the image modeling task. If the partition
051   into parts (i.e., the masking strategy) is chosen carefully, this shared information will include
052   high-level latent variables, such as object class. Since solving common downstream tasks with a
053   simple (e.g., linear) predictor precisely requires identifying such high-level information, this offers
      a possible explanation for the observed effectiveness of MIM/MAE representations.

073   With this in mind, it seems reasonable to ask: Is patch-wise masking in pixel space really the
074   best strategy for MIM? In natural language, each word in a sentence tends to carry semantic
075
      information, and the information shared between sets of words often conveys the general message
      of the sentence. However, this does not necessarily apply to the visual domain. In images,
076
      individual pixels (and sometimes even entire patches) may contain information redundant that of
077
      other pixels. For example, many background pixels will often be identical. Moreover, objects can
078   be masked out completely, such that any information about them is lost and reconstruction becomes
079   impossible (see, e.g., Fig. 2, left). The widely adopted strategy of masking image patches (i.e.,
080   spatial masking) may thus be sub-optimal and lead to representations that capture information that
081   is irrelevant for downstream tasks of interest.
082
      Some works have thus sought to devise better masking strategies by relying on auxiliary information
083
      such as (learned or inferred) image segmentations (Li et al., 2021; Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022; Shi
084   et al., 2022). Without prior knowledge or more complex training pipelines to identify the structure
085   of an image, randomly masking a fixed proportion of patches remains the default practice. However,
086   relying on this strategy assumes—rather unrealistically—that the information shared between any
087   random partition of patches naturally aligns with high-level variables of interest (Kong et al., 2023).
088
      In this work, we introduce a new data-driven masking strategy for MIM. Rather than working
089
      directly in pixel space, we propose to first project images into a latent space and then perform the
090   masking on the transformed data. Specifically, we opt for off-the-shelf data projections using prin-
091   cipal component analysis (PCA) and mask a random subset of the principal components. We refer
092   to the resulting method as Principled Masked Autoencoder (PMAE), see Fig. 1 for an overview.
093
      We argue that the space of principal components constitutes a more meaningful domain for
094
      masking, since it allows for partitioning the information in an image based on global features rather
095
      than local patches of pixels. This helps overcome some of the aforementioned failure modes of
096   spatial masking and results in learning more useful high-level representations. Indeed, by masking
097   globally rather than locally, we avoid scenarios where the masked out and visible information are
098   either too strongly correlated (where visible information is redundant with what is masked out)
099   or too weakly correlated (where visible information fails to predict what is masked out). Recent
100   work has also highlighted the beneficial partitioning of image information by PCA: Balestriero &
101   LeCun (2024) demonstrate that low-eigenvalue components capture features crucial for common
102   downstream tasks (see also Fig. 7); and Chen et al. (2024b) highlight the importance of the space in
103   which image distortions are applied, referring to PCA as a valuable transformation to consider. To
104
      the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to leverage such insights to devise a simple, robust,
      and effective data-driven alternative to pixel-space-masking in MIM.
105
106   We evaluate PMAE in experiments on natural and medical image datasets where it consistently
107   yields substantial performance gains over spatial masking. For linear probing experiments, we report
      an average performance gain of 26% over the widely adopted strategy of masking out 75% of images

126   2   BACKGROUND
127
128   Principal Component Analysis. Principal Component Analysis (PCA; Pearson, 1901; Hotelling,
129   1933) aims to expose data components that exhibit high variation. Given a centered data ma-
130   trix X ∈ RN ×D consisting of N observations of dimension D, PCA iteratively seeks weight
131   vectors vl ∈ RD for l = 1, ..., L ≤ D, called principal components (PC), which maximize the
132
      variance of the linear projection Xvl of the columns of X, subject to being orthogonal to the
      previously found v1 , ..., vl−1 and of unit-length. The solution to this problem is given by the
133
      eigenvalue decomposition of the empirical covariance matrix Σ := X⊤ X, i.e., Σ = VΛV⊤ , where
134
      Λ = diag(λ1 , ..., λD ) contains the ordered eigenvalues λ1 > ... > λD , and the corresponding
135   eigenvectors are the columns of V ∈ RD×D . In other words, the first L principal components
136   are given by the eigenvectors of X⊤ X corresponding to the L largest eigenvalues. Eigenvectors
137   with higher eigenvalues can thus be seen as capturing the dominant modes of variation in the data.
138   Further, the variance explained by each principal component can be shown to be proportional to its
139   corresponding eigenvalue λl . Whereas PCA is often used with L < M for dimensionality reduction,
140   we will focus on the lossless case with L = M throughout. The projection XPC of X onto its
141   principal components (“into PC space”) is then given by XPC = XV, and the inverse transformation
142   back into observation space by X = XPC V⊤ . As V is an orthonormal basis, the columns of
143
      XPC might be statistically independent if and only if the columns of X are themselves linear
      combinations of independent factors. In this work, we focused our exploration on the natural image
144
      domain, where the assumption that each pixel is a non-linear combination of a set of independent
145
      factors is widely considered realistic. Please refer to Appx. A.1 for further details regarding PCA.
146
147
      Representation Learning. Representation learning (Bengio et al., 2013) aims at learning an em-
148   bedding function f : x 7→ z, which maps data observation x ∈ RD to latent representation z ∈ RK .
149   These latent representations are meant to capture some of the explanatory factors underlying the
150   data, thus making z well-suited for use in downstream tasks such as predicting y (e.g., the class or
151   location of objects), often thought of as a being a function of the data’s explanatory variables.
152
153   Masked Image Modelling. Prominent approaches to representation learning in the image domain
154   rely on the masked image modeling paradigm (Pathak et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2021; He et al.,
155   2021; Bao et al., 2022). We choose the widely adopted Masked Autoencoder (He et al., 2021) as a
156   representative of MIM. The encoder-decoder architecture in MAE allows the model to reconstruct
157   masked portions of data observations by leveraging the learned latent representations:

190   Intuition behind MIM. Despite the MIM paradigm with random spatial masking producing
191   strong results on representation learning benchmarks (Dong et al., 2023), it is based on the rather
192   unrealistic assumption that for any partition of an image’s patches into two disjoint sets, the infor-
193
      mation shared by these sets contains y (Kong et al., 2023). In Fig. 2, we observe that while some
      masks (middle image) may allow shared information to include the object type and corresponding
194
      class label, there are many partitions where predicting the label (e.g., the class label ”goose” in
195
      Fig. 2) from the visible patches is almost as uncertain as a random guess (left image). Moreover,
196   even for well-designed masks, a substantial proportion of masked-out patches contain information
197   redundant with visible pixels. We conjecture that, as a result, Masked Image Modeling with spa-
198   tial masking leads to a suboptimal learning approach that is misaligned with common downstream
199   tasks, is characterized by slow convergence and suffers from high sensitivity to hyperparameters, as
200   suggested by prior work (He et al., 2021; Balestriero & LeCun, 2024) and confirmed in Section 5.

237   Intuition behind PMAE. In contrast to the traditional spatial masking presented in Section 2, an
238   appropriate function t can encourage information shared between visible and masked-out informa-
239   tion to contain y. More specifically, if the latent space captures unique global information in each
240   dimension, masking any of these dimensions retains information about all parts of the image. Hence,
241   Eq. (3.1) allows us to learn more meaningful representations for a suitable choice of t.
242
      The appeal of the proposed approach then boils down to finding t. While there may be many ap-
243   propriate choices for t, we found that applying PCA and projecting samples using the resulting
244   principal components is a suitable choice for the latent space. In particular, each dimension captures
245   specific factors of variation observed within the dataset and is typically tied to global features as
246   shown in Fig. 7. Masking one factor of variation thus prevents us from completely removing all
247   information about variables of interest within a sample, as most principal components will retain
248   some information about them. We will present the positive impact of this reasoning in Section 5,
249   where we compare principal component and observation space masking empirically. In Section 6,
250   we will provide additional intuition as to why PCA leads to a suitable masking strategy.

254   We now outline the setup used to validate PMAE. Our experiments follow the evaluation proposed
255   by He et al. (2021), ensuring the comparability of results between our PMAE and baselines. Details
256   regarding this experimental setup and computational training costs can be found in Appx. A.2.

258   Mask Design. State-of-the-art MIM models, like MAE (He et al., 2021), typically employ random
259   image patch masking, which serves as our baseline. Based on ablation studies from He et al. (2021),
260   the standard practice involves masking out 75% of image patches (denoted as MAEstd ). We also
261   examine an oracle-based masking strategy (denoted as MAEocl ), where the masking ratio is fine-
262   tuned to optimize linear probing downstream performance. This setting serves as an upper-bound to
263   the downstream performance. Additionally, we introduce a randomized masking approach, MAErd ,
264   in which the masking ratio is independently sampled for each batch, within a range of 10% to 90%
265   of image patches being masked out. This strategy is exempt from any hyperparameter tuning and
266
      offers insights into the downstream performance when using suboptimal masking hyperparameters.

268   ized (PMAErd ) masking strategies. In PMAE, we define the masking ratio as the proportion of data
269   variance to be masked out. Indeed, while for spatial masking, the masking ratio represents the pro-
      portion of patches to be masked out, with PCA, each principal component accounts for a percentage

368   In this section, we aim to provide more intuition as to why masking components rather than image
369   patches leads to more robust objectives. In Section 2, we discuss the hypothesis under which MIM
370   operates (Kong et al., 2023) and present an example failure case of spatial masking in Fig. 2. We
371   highlight how masking image patches can lead to a misalignment between the MIM objective and
372   the learning of meaningful representations. If all patches covering an object are masked out, it is
373   uncertain whether the remaining patches share any information with the object. Contrary, if the
374
      masked out information is redundant with the information carried by visible patches, it is likely that
      the information shared does not contain the object class but rather perceptual features (e.g., colors
375
      or textures).

410
411   Self-supervised learning. Self-supervised learning (SSL) leverages auxiliary tasks to learn from
412   unlabeled data, often outperforming supervised methods on downstream tasks. SSL can be divided
413   into two categories: discriminative and generative (Liu et al., 2021). Discriminative methods (Chen
414   et al., 2020a; Caron et al., 2021) focus on enforcing invariance or equivariance between data views
415   in the representation space, while generative methods (He et al., 2021; Bizeul et al., 2024) rely
416   on data reconstruction from, often, corrupted observations. Though generative methods histori-
417   cally lagged in performance, recent work has bridged the gap by integrating strengths from both
418   paradigms(Assran et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Lehner
419
      et al., 2023). Interestingly, recent discriminative methods employ multi-cropping strategies to create
      distinct data views (Oquab et al., 2023; Assran et al., 2023), which is reminiscent of image masking.
420
      Balestriero & LeCun (2024) point out the misalignment between auxiliary and downstream tasks in
421
      reconstruction-based SSL and suggest novel masking strategies to help realign these objectives.
422
423
      Masked Image Modelling. MIM extends the successful masked language modeling paradigm to
424
      vision tasks. Early methods, such as Context Encoder (Pathak et al., 2016), used a convolutional
425
      autoencoder to inpaint a central region of the image. The rise of Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Doso-
426   vitskiy et al., 2021) has driven significant advancements in MIM. BEiT (Zhou et al., 2021; Bao
427   et al., 2022) combines a ViT encoder with image tokenizers (Ramesh et al., 2021) to predict discrete
428   tokens for masked patches. SimMIM (Xie et al., 2022) simplifies the task by pairing a ViT encoder
429   with a regression head to directly predict raw pixel values for the masked regions. MAE (He et al.,
430   2021) introduces a more efficient encoder-decoder architecture, with a shallow decoder. MIM’s
431   domain-agnostic masking strategies have also proven effective in multi-modal tasks (Baevski et al.,
      2022; Bachmann et al., 2022).

      Mask Design Strategies. A critical component of the Masked Image Modeling paradigm is the
446   design of effective masking strategies. Early MIM approaches have relied on random spatial mask-
447   ing techniques, such as masking out the central region of an image (Pathak et al., 2016), image
448   patches (He et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022), and blocks of patches (Bao et al., 2022). Inspired by
449   advances in language modeling, recent efforts have explored semantically guided mask design. Li
450   et al. (2021) use self-attention maps to mask irrelevant regions, while Kakogeorgiou et al. (2022)
451   focus on masking semantically rich areas. Shi et al. (2022) design masks through adversarial learn-
452   ing, where the resulting masks resemble semantic maps, a concept extended by Li et al. (2022a)
453   through progressive semantic region masking. Further advancing this direction, Wang et al. (2023)
454
      and Madan et al. (2024) introduce curriculum learning-inspired mask design methods. These meth-
      ods often require additional training steps, components, or more complex objectives. More closely
455
      related to our work, Chang et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024b) explore the use of pre-existing image
456
      representations for Masked Image Modeling and image denoising. Chen et al. (2024b) introduce
457   additive Gaussian noise to principal components as an alternative to the traditional Denoising Au-
458   toencoders. Chang et al. (2022) utilize masked token modeling by leveraging the discrete latent
459   space of a pre-trained VQVAE to develop an image generation model.

      8   D ISCUSSION
463
464   In this work, we have investigated different masking strategies for Masked Image Modelling
465
      (MIM). To this end, we have introduced the Principled Masked Autoencoder (PMAE) as an
      alternative to masking random patches of pixels. PMAE is rooted in Principal Component Analysis
466
      (PCA; Pearson, 1901; Hotelling, 1933), which is a widely used data-driven linear transformation.
467
      Unlike recent alternatives that require additional supervision, learnable components, or complex
468   training pipelines (Li et al., 2021; 2022a; Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022b), PMAE stays
469   close to the core principles of MIM: the combination of a randomized masking strategy and an
470   encoder-decoder architecture. Despite its simplicity, we demonstrate that PMAE yields substantial
471   performance improvements over spatial masking on image classification tasks. Further, in a PMAE,
472   the masking ratio—typically a sensitive and difficult-to-tune hyperparameter in MIM—appears
473   more robust and has a natural interpretation as the ratio of variance explained by the masked input.
474   Since PCA is easily applicable to any data modality, our proposal of masking principal components
475   is not specific to MIM. Instead, it can be viewed as a general strategy that should also be applicable
476   to other types of modalities beyond images, as well as to other self-supervised learning (SSL)
477   approaches. Indeed, data masking is commonly adopted in discriminative SSL methods. Whereas
478   early approaches, such as SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a) or MoCo (Chen et al., 2020b), relied on
479   combinations of image transformations (e.g., color jitter, flips, crops, etc.) as data augmentation
480   strategies, more recent state-of-the-art methods like DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023)
481   and I-JEPA (Assran et al., 2023) have shifted to relying solely on image cropping, which can be
482
      considered a type of masking. The integration of principal component masking instead of image
      cropping into such SSL pipelines constitutes a promising future direction of research.
483
484   In the present work, we have focused on PCA as a meaningful masking space. However, our core
485   idea of masking a transformed version of the data (rather than the raw data) can be viewed as laying
      the groundwork for other, more generic approaches to information masking in self-supervised

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      representation learning. Moving beyond PCA, a natural extension would be to learn a suitable latent
487   space in which the masking is performed. This route has the added potential of leveraging recent
488   theoretical insights (Kong et al., 2023) by more explicitly enforcing that the shared information
489   between visible and masked-out latent components contains high-level latent variables that are most
490   useful for the downstream tasks of interest. Other off-the-shelf non-linear transformations, such
491   as the Fourier transform (Bracewell & Kahn, 1966), Wavelet transform (Daubechies, 1992), Kernel
492   Principal Component Analysis (Schölkopf et al., 1997), or Diffusion Maps (Coifman & Lafon,
493   2006), represent alternative candidate transformations. Future research should explore whether
494   the properties of these spaces provide comparable or additional advantages over PCA. Preliminary
495
      results on Kernel PCA, presented in Appx. A.7.4, demonstrate performance gains over PMAE,
      motivating further exploration. A particularly appealing aspect of some of these methods (e.g.,
496
      Fourier & Wavelet transforms and Diffusion Maps) is the use of fixed bases, which could eliminate
497
      the computational overhead of PCA—whose cost scales cubically with the data dimensionality—
498   and improve scalability to larger datasets.

775   — that maximizes the variance in the data. When the original data is a linear combination of
776   independent components (middle & right), referred to as data sources, PCA might successfully
777   identify these sources, resulting in statistically independent variables when the data is projected
778   onto its principal components. However, if the original data is a non-linear combination of
779   independent sources (left), the projection onto the principal components results in statistically
780   dependent variables making it possible to approximately predict one PC from others.
781
782
      Principal Component Analysis (PCA; Pearson, 1901; Hotelling, 1933) finds a set of orthonormal
783
      vectors (V) that maximize the variance of the data. Projecting data samples X onto V effectively
784   rotates the original data such that each variable captures the maximum possible variance while re-
785   maining orthogonal to the previous dimensions. When the original data is a linear combination
786   of independent factors (referred to as sources), PCA can but does not necessarily recover these
787   sources. PCA recovers mutually statistically independent sources if and only if the original data is
788   jointly Gaussian, since uncorrelatedness implies independence only for the Gaussian distribution.
789   This situation arises, e.g., when the data is a linear combination of Gaussian sources.
790   In Fig. 8 (middle & right), the observed variables x and y are linear combinations (y = 3x + z) of
791   independent sources x and z, where x follows a uniform distribution and z is drawn from a normal
792   distribution. In the middle example, PCA finds principal axes, PC1 and PC2, which are orthogonal,
793   and the projections align with the independent sources x and z. In the right example, the projections
794   do not align with the independent sources x and z.
795
      However, as shown in Fig. 8 (left), when y is a non-linear combination of sources (y = x2 + z), no
796   rotation matrix can transform the data to recover statistically independent variables. Projecting the
797   data onto principal axis will hence result in statistically dependent variables making it possible to
798   approximately predict one PC from others.
799
      In this work, we incorporate PCA into the framework of Masked Image Modeling (MIM). Specifi-
800
      cally, we propose a task that involves reconstructing masked principal components using the visible
801
      ones. This task is meaningful only if it is feasible to approximate the masked PCs based on the
802   visible ones, which occurs when the masked PCs are statistically dependent on the visible PCs.

1039   A.7.4   B EYOND PCA
1040
       Our work shows evidence the PCA offers a meaningful masking space. In Section 6, we motivate our
1041
       choice by observing that principal components capture global rather than local features of an image.
1042   In this section, we go beyond PCA and explore non-linear matrix factorization methods as a proof
1043   of concept for future research. In particular, we explore kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1997) with a
1044   Radial Basis Function. In kernel PCA, the spectral decomposition is performed not on the data itself
1045   but rather on a modified version of it: the standardized data is mapped to a high-dimensional space
1046   via a non-linear kernel function.
1047
       In Tab. 8, we present results on the CIFAR10 dataset and show the image classification accuracy
1048   using a linear and MLP probe. We compare a vanilla MAE with our PMAE and KMAE which
1049   relies on Kernel PCA for optimal masking ratios. For KMAE, we use the setting presented in
1050   Appx. A.7.3 and minimize the Euclidean distance between masked principal components and the
1051   decoder’s output principal components.
1052
       The results reveal a significant performance improvement when employing a non-linear image trans-
1053
       formation. KMAE achieves an average gain of 13.3 and 5 percentage points compared to the stan-
1054   dard Masked Autoencoder (He et al., 2021) and PMAE, respectively. Although these findings are
1055   preliminary and based on a single mid-scale dataset, they highlight the potential of non-linear trans-
1056   formations and further emphasize the value of spectral decomposition as a meaningful for Masked
1057   Image Modeling paradigms.
