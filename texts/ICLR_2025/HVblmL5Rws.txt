                           While LLMs excel at answering multi-hop questions like “Who is the spouse of
012                        the performer of Imagine?” by thinking out loud (chain-of-thought), they per-
013                        form surprisingly poorly when required to reason in their latent space and an-
014                        swer without chain-of-thought. This observation was previously referred to as the
015                        compositionality gap, implying that although language models are less reliable at
016                        two-hop latent reasoning, they still perform it sometimes. In this paper, we in-
017                        troduce a controlled setting for investigating the compositionality gap. We run a
018                        series of experiments finetuning a large language model (Llama-3-8B-Instruct) on
019                        synthetic facts expressed in English. We attempt to elicit two-hop reasoning in
020
                           three ways: (i) fine-tune on a data mixture designed to incentivize two-hop rea-
                           soning, (ii) force facts to be stored in layers in the correct order, and (iii) use an
021
                           auxiliary loss to provide activation-level supervision for two-hop reasoning. We
022
                           show that LLaMA-3-8B successfully learns to answer two-hop questions about
023                        synthetic facts using CoT, but completely fails without CoT, achieving chance-
024                        level accuracy and chance-level test loss. Failures of LLMs in our controlled
025                        setting cast doubt on the purported ability of present LLMs to perform multihop
026                        latent reasoning and lead us to conjecture that, rather than a reasoning gap, current
027                        language models might exhibit a two-hop reasoning curse — a complete lack of
028                        ability rather than a relative weakness. This is the Two-Hop Curse.1

078   underlying facts (e.g. “Who is John Lennon’s spouse”). However, prior work on two-hop reasoning
079   did not control for memorization and reasoning shortcuts LLMs could acquire during pre-training

084
085   We attempt to elicit two-hop reasoning in three ways, informed by hypotheses as to why latent
086
      reasoning might perform worse than explicit reasoning:
087
             1. Using a data mixture designed to incentivize two-hop reasoning. By training models on
088             examples of CoT and no-CoT answers to two-hop questions involving learned facts, we
089             attempt to incentivize models to learn generalizing two-hop reasoning circuits that could
090             be used for reasoning about other facts.
091
             2. Forcing facts to be stored in layers in the correct order. Transformers process inputs se-
092             quentially, so facts must be stored in the right order to enable consistent two-hop lookups.
093             We break up training into stages, and for each stage, selectively finetune a range of layers
094             to make sure the model stores answers to first-hop questions earlier in the forward pass than
095             second-hop questions.
096
             3. Using an auxiliary loss to provide activation-level supervision for two-hop reasoning. We
097             add a loss term to encourage resolving bridge entities in latent space, providing process-
098             level feedback to complement the outcome-level language modeling loss.
099
100   We find that models we train achieve near-perfect CoT accuracy for answering two-hop questions
101   about one-hop facts they learned from fine-tuning — but they completely fail without CoT. Not only
102   do models fail to have above-chance no-CoT accuracy, but the test loss on two-hop no-CoT answers
103   is nearly identical to loss computed on shuffled labels (see Figure 1). These results cast doubt on
104
      the claim that LLMs engage in two-hop reasoning. Our experiments suggest that the previously
      observed compositionality gap in LLMs may be an understatement, and LLMs may in fact exhibit a
105
      two-hop reasoning curse — a near-complete failure of two-hop latent reasoning.
106
107   Our findings hint at the possibility of latent reasoning being a fundamental limitation of LLMs, akin

      The contributions of our paper are as follows:
115
116         1. We design a clean experimental setup to study two-hop latent reasoning in natural language
117            in LLMs, where performance can only be attributed to successful latent two-hop reasoning
118            rather than reasoning shortcuts or memorization.
119
            2. We perform strong elicitation of multihop reasoning, involving (i) a data mixture to incen-
120            tivize two-hop reasoning, (ii) forcing facts to be stored in the layers in the order correspond-

129   2   R ELATED WORK
130
131   Externalized reasoning Prompting LLMs to externalize their reasoning (or, “think step by step“)
132   has long been known to improve their performance on various reasoning tasks (Reynolds & Mc-
133   Donell, 2021; Wei et al., 2023; Kojima et al., 2024). This prompting strategy is known as “chain-
134
      of-thought” (CoT). Even though the advantages of CoT are not uniform across tasks (it primarily
      benefits mathematical and symbolic reasoning; Sprague et al., 2024), giving LLMs the ability to
135
      spend a certain amount of tokens on thinking provably extends the complexity class of problems
136
      they can tackle (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2024). OpenAI (2024) has recently shown how the capability
137   of LLMs to take advantage of CoT reasoning can be further improved with outcome-based reinforce-
138   ment learning finetuning, leading to state-of-the-art results across multiple benchmarks (Hendrycks
139   et al., 2021; Rein et al., 2024). Despite those boosts, CoT does not always reliable reflect the causal
140   process that leads an LLM to giving a certain answer (Lanham et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2024;
141   Anwar et al., 2024). Our paper examines a family of problems where the discrepancy between CoT
142   and no-CoT performance is particularly stark.
143
144   Two-hop reasoning Multi-hop question answering is a long-standing problem in natural language
145   processing (Yang et al., 2018), blending together factual recall and reasoning. Press et al. (2023) has
146   attempted to single out the reasoning component of two-hop question answering by measuring the
147   compositionality gap of an LLM — the fraction of two-hop questions for which the LLM can answer
148
      the underlying (single-hop) facts but fails to combine them when answering a two-hop question.
      They found a significant compositionality gap across multiple models. Yang et al. (2024) found
149
      inconclusive evidence that transformers answer two-hop question by actually making two hops of
150
      reasoning and no evidence for reliable two-hop capabilities: LLM performance varied significantly
151   across question domains. Following up on this work, Biran et al. (2024) found that in many cases,
152   even if the first hop successfully resolves the bridge entity, this information frequently fails to be
153   consumed by the upstream layers.
154
155   Fundamental limitations of latent reasoning in transformers Transformers consist of a se-
156   quence of feedforward networks (Vaswani et al., 2017) and are subject to strict bounds on the class
157   of problems they can solve (see (Strobl et al., 2024) for a survey). Feng et al. (2023) first proved
158   that transformers without CoT cannot solve certain problems and Merrill & Sabharwal (2023a;b)
159   further proved that the problems they can solve without CoT belong to the circuit complexity class
160   TC0 . It is not clear, however, how practical these bounds are for frontier models that consist of more
161   than a hundred of transformer blocks. Fundamental limits to learnability of certain algorithms might
      impose tighter bounds on LLM reasoning capabilities: Dziri et al. (2024) found that transformer


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      capabilities of solving certain compositional problems (such as multi-digit addition or dynamic pro-
163   gramming) scale very unfavorably with problem complexity. Similarly, Ye et al. (2024) found that
164   transformers can only be trained to solve certain mathematical problems when they are sufficiently
165   deep.
166
167   Eliciting latent reasoning capabilities via finetuning Wang et al. (2024) show that two-hop rea-
168   soning circuits can be learned through grokking (training a low-capacity model for 50 epochs) but
169   those circuits remain brittle (do not generalize to out-of-distribution examples). Moreover, while
170   Wang et al. focus solely on pretraining toy models on artificial data (each example is three tokens
171   long), we finetune LLMs close to frontier (Llama 3.0 8B) in a naturalistic setting (facts expressed
172   in diverse English sentences). Pfau et al. (2024) train models to use meaningless filler tokens (e.g.,
173   ‘...’) instead of CoT to solve reasoning tasks; this setup can be seen as an intermediate between CoT
174
      and no-CoT. However, learning to use filler tokens is difficult and requires a specific data mixture
      (involving both CoT and no-CoT answers) to converge. A related line work work focused on dis-
175
      tilling CoT reasoning, i.e. training models to zero-shot give answers similar to those they would
176
      give after CoT (Zelikman et al., 2022; 2024; Hsieh et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024).
177   A particularly succesful example of this approach involves gradual CoT distillation: progressively
178   discarding steps of arithmetic CoT until only a small fraction of the original CoT remains (Deng
179   et al., 2024). However, arithmetic problems are not always strictly sequential and can sometimes
180   be solved in parallel (Nanda et al., 2023). In contrast, the present paper studies strictly sequential
181   reasoning problems.

      5      I NTERVENTION 2: F ORCING FACTS TO BE STORED IN THE RIGHT ORDER
288
289
      Motivation Transformers are feed-forward neural networks — a sequence of blocks that have to
290   be traversed in a linear order for a given input. Moreover, previous work suggests that transformers
291   store facts in a somewhat localised fashion, mostly in MLP layers of a few neighboring transformer
292   blocks (Meng et al., 2023). Latent two-hop reasoning requires executing two fact lookups in a strict
293   order during a forward pass. For a feed-forward neural network, this is only possible if the first fact
294   (e.g. “the performer of Imagine is John Lennon”) is stored in an earlier block than the second fact
295   (e.g. “the spouse of John Lennon is Yoko Ono”). Otherwise, if the first fact is stored in a later block
296   (e.g. 20th transformer block) and the second fact in an earlier block (e.g. 10th block), by the time a
297   model completes the first lookup to resolve the bridge entity (“John Lennon”), the forward pass can
298
      no longer use the bridge entity to look up the second fact.
299   If facts were distributed uniformly across layers, they would happen to be in the right order half of
300   the time. Therefore, if layer ordering was the only reason for poor two-hop performance, one would
301   expect two-hop accuracy to be around 50%. In practice, this should be seen as a lower bound, since
302   some facts might be represented redundantly, more than once.
303
304   Setup We force localizing facts in particular layers by layer-selective finetuning, i.e. dividing our
305   training distribution into three datasets and training separately on each, involving only a particular
306
      layer range at each stage:
307
             1. First one-hop facts (e.g. “the performer of Imagine is John Lennon”) are learned with
308
                layers 0-12 (with other layers frozen)
309
310
             2. Second one-hop facts (e.g. “the spouse of John Lennon is Yoko Ono”) are learned with
                layers 12-24 (with other layers frozen)
311
312          3. Two-hop QA pairs are learned with all layers updated.
313
314   To mitigate catastrophic forgetting from only training on a single dataset at once, we repeat training
315
      stages (1)-(3) twice. Moreover, our training data uses the mixtured described in the previous section:
      training on one-hop facts and both two-hop CoT and no-CoT QA pairs.

330   6   I NTERVENTION 3: ACTIVATION SUPERVISION FOR TWO - HOP REASONING
331
332   Motivation The cross-entropy language modeling loss, used during LLM pre-training and super-
333   vised fine-tuning, treats the LLM as a black box and only supervises how the input tokens in the
334   prompt are mapped to output tokens. From success of CoT performance, we know that such super-
335   vision is effective in teaching models to reason in explicit CoT. Since the reasoning trace is expressed
336   in token space, the language modeling loss provides LLMs process-based supervision, giving useful
337   gradients for each step of reasoning. However, for reasoning in latent space, the language mod-
338
      eling loss only provides outcome-based feedback (whether the predicted answer is correct) and is
      indifferent to whether an LLM arrives at the answer via memorization or two-hop reasoning.
339
340
341   Setup We add an auxiliary loss Laux that complements outcome-based supervision from the lan-
342   guage modeling loss with process-based feedback in the activation space. More specifically, we
343   encourage the model to resolve the bridge entity in activation space whenever it is prompted with a
344
      two-hop question. We encourage such resolution by ensuring that a given hidden state (output of a
      transformer block) is either similar to a vector representation of the bridge entity or predictive of it.
345
346   We apply the auxiliary objective to the output of a single transformer block at a single token position.
347   We sweep over several blocks to apply this loss on and choose block 10 (out of 32). To determine
348   the token position to apply loss on, we look for the last token of the description of the bridge entity
349   in the question, e.g. “gine” in “Who is the spouse of the singer of the song Imagine?”. Let’s call this
350   activation vector h.
351   We consider two auxiliary objectives:
352
353         1. Logit lens. We compute logits y as y = WU RMSNorm(h), where RMSNorm(·) denotes
354            the final RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) layer of Llama 3 8B Instruct during training.
355            We then compute Laux = CE(e2 , y), where CE(·) is the standard cross-entropy loss and e2
356            is the token corresponding to bridge entity, e.g. “John Lennon”. This is possible because
357            we ensure all bridge entities are single-token.
358         2. Embed lens. We compute Laux = −CosSim(WE e2 , y), where CosSim(·) is the cosine
359            similarity loss and WE e2 is the embedding of the bridge entity token.
360
361   In both cases, our final loss is computed as L = LLM + cLaux , where LLM is the standard language
362   modelling loss and the coefficient c is a hyperparameter. Based on our sweeps, we found that 0.01
363   and 0.1 were the best settings for logit lens and embed lens, respectively. Once again, our training
364   data uses the setup described for Hypothesis 2 experiments: training on one-hop facts and both
365   two-hop CoT and no-CoT QA pairs.

397   7      L IMITATIONS
398
399   In this paper, we try to investigate the capabilities of LLMs in naturalistic settings, while controlling
400   for confounders plaguing prior work. Reconciling the need for a clean setup and plausibility required
401   several design choices that could be controversial.
402
403
404   Fine-tuning vs pre-training In order to have a clean experimental setup, we fine-tune models
405   on fictional facts. However, one might worry that the cleanliness of this setup is fundamentally
406
      different from how knowledge is normally acquired by LLMs during pre-training. This difference
      might manifest in diversity of the data distribution and the scale of the training dataset.
407
408   To ensure the diversity of the training distribution, we include multiple (30) paraphrases of each fact,
409   which leads language models to learn the underlying logical facts as opposed to just memorizing the
410   sentences that express them (Berglund et al., 2023; 2024). This explains why our models are able to
411   reason about these logical facts when allowed to use CoT, achieving high two-hop CoT accuracy.
412   Furthermore, prior work has shown that knowledge acquired during pre-training is represented simi-
413   larly to knowledge acquired during fine-tuning, e.g. the Reversal Curse has been observed in models
414   pre-trained on natural data (Grosse et al., 2023), models pre-trained on large-scale synthetic data
415   (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2024), and models fine-tuned on synthetic facts (Berglund et al., 2024).
416
417
      Ratio of two-hop to single-hop facts Prior work has shown that a particular ratio of the number
418
      of atomic and two-hop facts involving a given entity is crucial for incentivizing two-hop reasoning
419
      as opposed to memorizing answers to two-hop questions (Wang et al., 2024). In contrast, our data
420   mixture holds this ratio fixed — a given bridge entity is always involved in two atomic facts and one
421   two-hop fact. This might create insufficient pressure for the model to learn two-hop reasoning.
422
423
      However, it is not clear whether the pre-training distribution itself satisfies this property. Future
      work could explore the effect of varying this ratio in naturalistic settings.
424
425
426   The strength of activation-level supervision Our auxiliary objectives incentivize the model to
427   resolve the bridge entity (first hop) in activation space. However, they do not incentivize the model
428   to use the bridge entity as a query for another memory lookup (second hop). One could imagine
429   a richer auxiliary objective that requires the bridge entity representation to have downstream effect
430   on subsequent layers, e.g. maximizing the gradient of the final answer w.r.t. to the representation
431   of the bridge entity (Koh & Liang, 2017). However, such loss function would require computing
      second-order gradients, which is challenging to implement in distributed training setups for LLMs.

      8   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      Previous work pointed out the existence of a compositionality gap — a difference in performance of
435   LLMs at answering two-hop questions with and without CoT. In this work, we introduce a natural-
436   language yet controlled setting for studying the compositionality gap in LLMs, where latent two-
437   hop reasoning can be the only explanation for positive performance. We explore three groups of
438   interventions to elicit latent two-hop reasoning: (i) a data mixture designed to incentivize learning
439   of two-hop reasoning, (ii) forcing facts to be localized in the right order, and (iii) encouraging the
440   bridge entity to be resolved in early layers. All of these interventions fail to improve latent reasoning
441   ability measured by both accuracy and loss, while achieving near-perfect two-hop CoT accuracy. At
442   the very least, we show that eliciting latent two-hop reasoning in LLMs is not trivial: we believe our
443
      experiments tried picking the lowest-hanging fruit and found that it is all sour.
444   Further, our results lead us to believe that previous work might have significantly overestimated the
445   extent to which latent two-hop reasoning occurs in LLMs. While it is undeniable that latent two-hop
446   reasoning is representable by transformers (Wang et al., 2024), we conjecture that current LLMs
447   are unlikely to actually perform latent two-hop reasoning. If LLMs did perform two-hop reasoning,
448   they would have more than chance-level loss on answers to two-hop questions that they can answer
449
      with near-perfect accuracy using explicit CoT. In line with past work on fundamental limitations of
      LLMs (Berglund et al., 2024), we call this failure of LLM reasoning the Two-Hop Curse.
