011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Open-vocabulary image segmentation has been advanced through the synergy be-
014            tween mask generators and vision-language models like Contrastive Language-
015
               Image Pre-training (CLIP). Previous approaches focus on generating masks while
               aligning mask features with text embeddings during training. In this paper, we
016
               observe that relying on generated low-quality masks can weaken the alignment
017
               of vision and language in regional representations. This motivates us to present a
018            new fine-tuning framework, named MaskCLIP++, which uses ground-truth masks
019            instead of generated masks to enhance the mask classification capability of CLIP.
020             Due to the limited diversity of image segmentation datasets with mask annota-
021            tions, we propose incorporating a consistency alignment constraint during fine-
022            tuning, which alleviates categorical bias toward the fine-tuning dataset. After
023            low-cost fine-tuning, combining with the mask generator in previous state-of-the-
024            art mask-based open vocabulary segmentation methods, we achieve performance
025            improvements of +1.7, +2.3, +2.1, +3.1, and +0.3 mIoU on the A-847, PC-459,
026
               A-150, PC-59, and PAS-20 datasets, respectively. Our code will be made publicly
               available.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Image segmentation is one of the most extensively studied tasks in computer vision, which aims
032   to partition an image into several regions where each region corresponds to the same object or
033   shares consistent semantics. Traditional image segmentation models are often defined on a closed
034   vocabulary. When new classes need to be segmented, it typically requires redefining the vocabulary
035   set, annotating data, and retraining the model. However, the cost of annotating image segmentation
036   on a large vocabulary is high. Therefore, the task of Open-Vocabulary Segmentation (OVS) has been
037   proposed to perform image segmentation under any vocabulary set.
038   Large-scale image-text pre-training models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and ALIGN (Jia
039   et al., 2021), have demonstrated strong zero-shot recognition capabilities, sparking interest in trans-
040   ferring these abilities to image segmentation tasks. Several studies (Ding et al., 2023; Jiao et al.,
041   2023; Yu et al., 2024; Jiao et al., 2024) have explored how to adapt mask generators (Cheng et al.,
042   2021; 2022) and pre-trained CLIP to each other to achieve open-vocabulary segmentation at both
043   the semantic and instance levels.
044   However, we argue that continuing to invest in improving the mask generation may not be cost-
045   effective. For different CLIP architectures, even when the generated masks closely match the ground
046   truth (GT) masks, the improvement in segmentation performance is limited to no more than 5% as
047   shown in Figure 1(a). Conversely, for different mask generators, if CLIP can classify the generated
048   masks as accurately as the GT class assignments, the segmentation performance improvement can
049   exceed 40% as shown in Figure 1(b). Furthermore, Figure 1(a) shows that for the original CLIP,
050
      there remains a performance gap of 3.0%–4.7% between the generated masks and the GT masks,
      implying that continuing to use the generated masks during training may negatively impact the
051
      alignment quality between regional images and text. We confirm this inference in our experiments.
052
053   The above analysis indicates that GT masks can replace mask generators during fine-tuning CLIP
      with image segmentation data, which not only eliminates unnecessary training costs but also im-

083   ory usage, while achieving even better performance. We hope that the universality and efficiency of
084   MaskCLIP++ could inspire further research in open-vocabulary image segmentation.

087   2            R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Mask-based segmentation. Mask-based segmentation is a technique that views image segmenta-
090   tion as the process of mask generation and mask classification, which is also known as region-based
091   segmentation. To address instance and panoptic segmentation, He et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2020;
092   2021); Zhang et al. (2021) proposed various mask generators. Recently, Cheng et al. (2021) unified
093
      the semantic, panoptic and instance segmentation into a single architecture. MaskFormer (Cheng
      et al., 2021) uses learnable queries to interact with image features, which are then decoded as
094
      masks and class scores. Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) further accelerates the convergence
095
      of MaskFormer and reduces memory usage through multi-scale decoder design, using masks from
096   shallow decoders as priors for deeper layers, and employing sampling-based optimization. Cur-
097   rent mask-based OVS methods need almost to retrain an open-vocabulary mask generator based
098   on Mask2Former (Xu et al., 2023d;a; Yu et al., 2024). In contrast, our method can directly use the
099   Mask2Former trained on a closed vocabulary during inference, reducing training cost and increasing
100   flexibility in usage.
101
      Open-vocabulary segmentation. Depending on the data used, existing methods have developed
102   into different paradigms. The first paradigm offers many heuristic solutions based on observations
103   of CLIP without additional training (Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2023; Sun
104   et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024a;b). The second paradigm utilizes image-text pair data (Thomee et al.,
105   2016; Changpinyo et al., 2021) to train OVS models under weak supervision (Xu et al., 2022a;
106   Luo et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023; Mukhoti et al., 2023; Cha et al., 2023; Liu
107   et al., 2024a). Due to the lack of precise segmentation locations, these methods suffer from poor
      segmentation quality. The third paradigm enhances segmentation quality by incorporating image-


                                                                                                                   2

108
      mask data (Kirillov et al., 2023) and knowledge from SAM (Wang et al., 2024c; Yuan et al., 2024).
109   However, the use of SAM not only increases inference cost but also relies on users or other detectors
110   to provide high-quality prompts. The fourth paradigm seeks to achieve mutual adaptation between
111   the segmentation model and CLIP through fully supervised training on image segmentation datasets
112   containing image, mask, and category triplets, thereby producing an open-vocabulary segmentation
113   model. Based on the type of the segmentation model, these methods can be categorized into mask-
114   based approaches (Ding et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022b; Ghiasi et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023; Liang
115   et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023d;c;a; Yu et al.,
116   2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Jiao et al., 2024) and pixel/patch-based ones (Li et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
117
      2023; Li et al., 2024; Cho et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024). Pixel-based OVS methods typically focus on
      semantic segmentation and cannot perform instance-level segmentation independently. Additionally,
118
      some unique approaches to achieving OVS involve image retrieval (Shin et al., 2022; Barsellotti
119
      et al., 2024), feature distillation (Chen et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) or model merging (Wang et al.,
120   2024b). Some unified large models also incorporate the functionality of OVS (Zou et al., 2023; Shen
121   et al., 2024). This paper focuses on efficiently transferring CLIP on limited segmentation datasets
122   to improve its performance on semantic- and instance-level open-vocabulary segmentation tasks.

      3.1   R EVISITING I MAGE -T EXT E MBEDDINGS A LIGNMENT FOR OVS
128
      We first revisit the roles of the visual encoder (CLIP-V) and text encoder (CLIP-T) in the pretrained
129   CLIP model. Denote the image embeddings from CLIP-V as Ei ∈ R1×D , where D represents
130   the dimension of embedding space. To create text embeddings, a vocabulary set of K categories
131   is transformed into the corresponding sentences using templates like “A photo of {}”, and then
132   encoded by CLIP-T, yielding Et ∈ RK×D . The original CLIP model maps both images and text
133   into an aligned embedding space. In this space, image-text pairs maintain semantic alignment, which
134   we describe as “pre-alignment” and can often be measured using cosine similarity ⟨Ei , Et ⟩. In open-
135   vocabulary segmentation, preserving the pre-alignment property is crucial for adapting CLIP to zero-
136   shot dense predictions, as it enhances the alignment of region-specific image and text embeddings.
137   Previous mask-based OVS methods obtain pre-aligned mask embeddings and text embeddings in
138   two ways. As illustrated in Figure 2(a), the first way aims to train a mask generator that adapts to
139   CLIP, with the generalization mainly derived from the original frozen parameters of CLIP (Xu et al.,
140   2023c; Ding et al., 2023). However, attempts to align the embedding spaces of the mask generator
141   and CLIP often fail (Xu et al., 2022b; Yu et al., 2024). As illustrated in Figure 2(b), the second way
142   focuses on making CLIP sensitive to the generated masks during training to enhance performance
143
      and avoids overfitting through distillation (Jiao et al., 2023; 2024).
144   We identify two limitations of these two types of methods. First, they rely on noisy generated masks,
145   which can degrade the quality of the region representations (as shown by the vertical comparisons
146   in Figure 1(a)). This degradation can weaken the alignment between region-specific image and text
147   embeddings. Second, there is a lack of an intuitive explanation and a straightforward solution for
148   learning better alignment without severe overfitting. Moreover, freezing CLIP limits its transferabil-
149
      ity from global to local features, while distillation typically requires carefully designed strategies
      and incurs additional training cost.
150
151   To overcome these limitations, we propose a new training pipeline for OVS, named MaskCLIP++,
152   which, to our knowledge, is the first method that attempts to leverage ground truth (GT) masks to
153   obtain region features instead of relying on the mask generator during training. Furthermore, we
154   propose the constraint named “consistency alignment” to alleviate overfitting when adding extra
155
      parameters to optimize alignment. We will give detailed descriptions in what follows.

196   Parameterized similarity modeling with consistency alignment. The pretrained CLIP mask em-
197   bedding Em and the category text embedding Et are initially pre-aligned with the initial similarity
198   map S = ⟨Em , Et ⟩ being relatively coarse. Optimizing this alignment with some supervision is
199   needed to achieve a more accurate similarity map. We refer to this process as parameterized simi-
200
      larity modeling (PSM).
201   However, when incorporating the mask generator into similarity modeling, the mask embeddings
202   produced by the mask generator align almost exclusively with CLIP’s text embeddings on the train-
203   ing set, resulting in the misalignment illustrated in Figure 2(a). We first analyze the cause of this
204   overfitting to develop our similarity modeling approach.
205   To facilitate discussion, we simplify this approach as follows. Let t ∈ RD denote a normalized
206   text embedding from CLIP, and let m1 , m2 ∈ RD denote different mask embeddings. The initial
207   similarity is defined as s = mT t. Now, before computing the inner product across modalities,
208   the mask embeddings are updated using a parameter Θ ∈ RD×D , resulting in a new similarity
209   r = (Θm)T t. Although r can be optimized to be close to the target, an unintuitive phenomenon
210   may arise: After mask embeddings are updated via gradient descent, the order of r1 and r2 is closer
211   to the target, but the order of s1 and s2 may deviate further from the target. This phenomenon is
212
      illustrated by a toy example in Appendix Figure 5.
213   Consequently, the original similarity relationships between CLIP’s embeddings are disrupted. Our
214   experiments demonstrate that the additional parameter Θ fails to achieve CLIP’s level of generaliza-
215   tion when fine-tuning on limited image segmentation data, resulting in overfitting. Therefore, when
      attempting to parameterize similarity modeling, it is essential to ensure that the modeled similarity

265   Compared to previous OVS methods, our MaskCLIP++ offers the following several advantages.
266   First, we use GT masks during training, which are less noisy and more accurate, thus improving
267   alignment quality and reducing training cost. Second, from the perspective of avoiding overfitting,
268   the consistency alignment constraint provides more optimization space than freezing CLIP (Xu et al.,

274   In the following experiments, we utilize various architectures of pre-trained CLIP to demonstrate
275   the generality of our method. The CLIP models with ResNet architecture are sourced from Ope-
276   nAI (Radford et al., 2021), those with ViT architecture from EVA-CLIP (Sun et al., 2023), and those
277   with ConvNeXt architecture from OpenCLIP (Cherti et al., 2023).
278   By default, consistent with previous work on universal open-vocabulary semantic segmentation (Yu
279   et al., 2024), we fine-tune our model using the COCO Panoptic training set (Lin et al., 2014), which
280   consists of 118K images and 133 categories. The AdamW optimizer is utilized with an initial
281   learning rate of 2e-4, decaying to 1e-5 via cosine annealing. The fine-tuning process is conducted
282   for 20K iterations with a batch size of 4.
283
      The size of the training image is adjusted according to the downsampling factor of the model to
284   ensure that the final size of the feature map is 322 . The parameters involved in fine-tuning differ
285   slightly across architectures. For the ConvNeXt and ResNet architectures, all parameters of CLIP-V
286   are fine-tuned. For the ViT architecture, following Cho et al. (2024), only the positional encodings
287   and embedding layers of CLIP-V are fine-tuned. In the ConvNeXt architecture, the fine-tuning
288   learning rate for CLIP parameters is 1e-3 times that of other parts, while in the ResNet and ViT
289   architectures, it is 1e-2 times.
290   During inference, we mainly evaluate using the ADE20K validation set (Zhou et al., 2017), which
291   contains 2,000 images and 150 categories, along with annotations for semantic segmentation, panop-
292   tic segmentation, and instance segmentation. In ADE20K, 64 categories overlap with those in
293   COCO, while the remaining 86 categories do not. We measure semantic segmentation performance
294   using Intersection over Union (IoU), recording mIoU(S) for overlapping categories, mIoU(U) for
295   non-overlapping categories, and overall mIoU. Panoptic Quality (PQ) and Average Precision (AP)
296   are used to assess performance in panoptic segmentation and instance segmentation, respectively.
297   By default, we use FC-CLIP (Yu et al., 2024) trained on the COCO Panoptic (Lin et al., 2014)
298   training set as the mask generator. For the base and large level CLIP, we employ the corresponding
299   FC-CLIP models as mask generators 1 . The inference size of the mask generator remains consistent
300   with FC-CLIP, set to 800. The inference size of CLIP depends on the model architecture, and the

519   5     C ONCLUSIONS
520
521
      In this paper, we point that using generated masks hinders CLIP’s regional image-text alignment
522
      in previous OVS work. And there is limited room for improving mask generation compared to
523   mask classification. We also reveal that attempts to update CLIP’s embedding vectors disrupt its
524   original pre-alignment properties, resulting in new alignments that function only on the training
525   data. Based on these analysis, we propose the MaskCLIP++, which utilizes ground truth masks as
526   prior to fine-tune CLIP, and models similarity under a consistency alignment constraint to mitigate
527   overfitting. Our method significantly enhances CLIP’s mask recognition capabilities across various
528   architectures. By integrating mask generators from prior work, we achieve comparable or superior
529   performance on semantic segmentation, panoptic segmentation, and instance segmentation tasks
530   with lower training costs. Overall, we adapt CLIP more effectively to open-vocabulary segmentation
531
      tasks through the use of image segmentation data and lightweight fine-tuning.

764   A     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORK
765
766   First, the image masks must be downsampled to match the size of CLIP’s final feature map, which
767   undergoes a downsampling factor of 14 to 32 times. Consequently, some generated small object
768   masks may not be recognizable by MaskCLIP++.
769
      Second, the mask generators employed during inference incur negligible computational overhead.
770   As shown in Table 3, heavier backbones seem to enhance classification capabilities of their own
771   (Ps ), but do not significantly improve open-vocabulary segmentation performance when combined
772   with our fine-tuned CLIP (Pc ). Therefore, lightweight, category-agnostic mask generators continue
773   to represent a promising research direction.
774
      Last, our reliance on GT masks somewhat limits our ability to scale up at the data level. Finding
775
      ways to utilize more image-text pairs or image-mask pairs for fine-tuning remains an open question.

778   B     DATASETS OF TRAINING AND EVALUATION
779
780   The image segmentation datasets we used to train are COCO Panoptic (Lin et al., 2014) or COCO-
781   Stuff (Caesar et al., 2018). For evaluation, we utilize the ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017), PASCAL
782
      VOC (Everingham et al., 2010), and PASCAL Context (Mottaghi et al., 2014) datasets. A brief
      introduction to these datasets is provided below.
783
784   COCO Panoptic. It contains 164K images with 133 annotated classes, comprising 80 “thing”
785   categories and 53 “stuff” categories. The “thing” categories are annotated at the instance level, with
786   different instances of the same category using separate masks within a single image. In contrast,
787   the “stuff” categories are semantically annotated, where all regions belonging to the same category
788
      share a single mask in an image.
789   COCO Stuff. It shares the same training images with COCO Panoptic. These images are annotated
790   for semantic segmentation into 171 categories.
791
      ADE20K. It contains 2,000 images for validation with two types of annotations: one for panoptic or
792   semantic segmentation with 150 categories (dubbed as A-150), and the other for semantic segmen-
793   tation with 847 categories (dubbed as A-847). Among the 150 categories defined in ADE20K, 64
794   appear in COCO Panoptic, while 86 do not. The names of these categories are detailed in Table 10.
795
      PASCAL VOC. It includes 1,449 images for testing with 20 annotated foreground classes for sem-
796
      nantic segmentation.
797
798   PASCAL Context. It is a dataset for semantic understanding that contains 5,000 validation images.
799   Two versions are employed for semantic segmentation: one with 59 frequently used classes (referred
800   to as PC-59) and another with the complete set of 459 classes (referred to as PC-459).

      Impact of integrating image context information into mask representation. Averaging CLIP
      features over perfectly precise mask regions is not conducive to extracting mask representations
952
      with stronger recognition capabilities, as recognition tasks typically require contextual information
953
      and focused attention on target internal regions. Here, we distinguish how we address this common
954   issue using CLIP’s original pooling method. For CLIP models utilizing Attention Pooling (e.g., ViT-
955   B/16), the hyperparameter α controls the degree of contextual information incorporated in the mask
956   representation. As shown in Table 13b, smaller α values, indicating more extensive use of contextual
957   information, lead to better performance. For CLIP models employing global average pooling (e.g.
958   ConvNeXt-B), as shown in Table 13a, using global image embeddings Ei or patch embeddings Ep
959   to construct pseudo-text embeddings Pt in the PSM stage enhances the utilization of image context
960   information to some extent, thereby improving performance.
