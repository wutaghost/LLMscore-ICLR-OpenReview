009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            The applications of large language models (LLMs) have been widely spread across all do-
012            mains. However, the basic abilities such as the controllability of LLMs are still limited.
013            To address this, we propose ”Self-controller”, a novel agentic framework bringing self-
014            awareness into LLMs’ reasoning logic. The core idea of this work is to maintain states
015            based on the LLM’s response, letting the LLM become self-aware of current status and
016            think step by step in a multi-round chain-of-thought paradigm. Our experiment on the state
017
               of textual length has shown the controllability and effectiveness of the Self-controller. We
               further implement a binary search algorithm to accelerate the generation process based
018
               on the linearity and monotonicity of the textual length state. Another advantage of the
019
               Self-controller comes with DeepSeek’s Context Caching technology, which significantly
020            saves computational token consumption when a cluster of conversations shares the same
021            prefix of context. Theoretically, we prove that in this scenario the extra time complex-
022            ity is O(c log n). Results of the back-of-the-envelope estimation suggest that the token
023            consumption of our method is no more than twice as much as that of the trivial single-
024            round generation. Furthermore, our ablation study on word constraints demonstrates the
025            Self-controller’s consistent controllability across all foundation models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Humans are constantly troubled with self-control issues. Naturally, we ponder the question: do LLMs
030   learning from humans inherit the same characteristics? The willpower of self-control was first discussed
031   in psychology. The study called ”the marshmallow test” (Mischel, 2015), was originally developed in the
032   1960s. The test’s goal is to observe how children make decisions between waiting 15 minutes longer for two
033   marshmallows they eagerly wanted or settling for just one right away. The experimental results imply that
034   without accurate reward assessment, the actions of human beings might converge to a local optimum.
035
      The self-awareness mechanism is embedded in the mind. In real practice, to reach a specified goal such
036   as finishing an article with a fixed page size, humans often verify their current progress repetitively. As
037   a result, self-awareness promotes self-control. Adam and Eve ”realizes they are naked” in the garden and
038   feels ashamed, starting to wear clothes ever after (Bratcher, 1979). In the meantime, the advancement of
039   LLMs shows new emergent abilities, demonstrating great potential for mimicking such primary intelligent
040   behaviors. However, building self-awareness upon LLMs to ensure controllability is still underexplored.
041
      Length control is a classical task that reflects controllability in LLMs. Traditional length control approaches
042   highly rely on trivial instructions, post-hoc verification, and supervised fine-tuning (SFT). These approaches
043   either lack controllability or are heavily resource-consuming. To address this, we propose a novel framework
044   called ”Self-controller”. The framework comprises a state reflector and a multi-round dialogue session with
045   LLMs. The reflector engages with LLMs in each round and provides state information on precise statistics of
046   the textual length, as shown in Figure 1. The empirical results on multiple datasets show that our framework

      can significantly enhance controllability in LLMs, without significant performance degradation. Based on
048
      the linearity and monotonicity of the textual length state, we propose a binary search optimization to achieve

050   complexity is O(c log n).

      Large Language Models In recent years, LLMs’ abilities continually grow. Raffel et al. (2020) revealed
087
      the broad application potential of large-scale pre-trained models in natural language processing (NLP) tasks
088
      through fine-tuning techniques. Brown (2020) demonstrated that even with limited sample sizes, LLMs
089   can still exhibit excellent performance through autoregressive mechanisms. Chowdhery et al. (2023) further
090   explored the relationship between model size and performance, confirming the advantage of larger models
091   in capturing linguistic complexity. With the advent of models such as ChatGPT and GPT-4 (Liu et al., 2023;
092   Achiam et al., 2023), LLMs have drawn even more widespread attention within the field, which can generate
093   text that approaches or even rivals human quality.

                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      Self-awareness There are attempts to assess self-awareness in LLMs. Li et al. (2024) propose a unified
095
      taxonomy for awareness in LLMs, including capability, mission, emotion, culture, and perspective. Wang
096   et al. (2024) introduces the knowledge quadrant for multimodal LLMs, knowing knowns and unknowns.
097   To enhance trustworthiness and self-awareness in LLMs, various approaches have been proposed, such as
098   Chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022), Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023), ReAct (Yao
099   et al., 2023), Tree-of-thought (ToT) (Muralidharan & Thomas, 2024) and Think-Solve-Verify (Liu et al.,
100   2024). LLMs’ hallucination problems have been alleviated with these fine-grained stepwise generation
101   frameworks, while the accurate output manipulation remains underexplored.
102
103   Controllability The trivial method to improve controllability is to include extra zero-shot instructions
104   (such as ”Output in a sonnet style.”) to the prompt (Kojima et al., 2022). Post-hoc verification utilizes
105   additional check programs evaluating LLMs’ responses. Chain-of-Verification (CoVe) designs a planning
106   module to raise a set of verification questions and self-corrects them to get the final response afterward
107
      (Dhuliawala et al., 2023). Zhang & Gao (2023) applies a similar idea for news claim verification along with
      the help of the web search. Recently, generating longer text has drawn attention. Bai et al. (2024) proposes
108
      AgentWrite, leveraging ”divide-and-conquer” agents to generate 10,000+ words, while the length control
109
      for each paragraph remains trivial. On the other hand, supervised fine-tuning has been the most prevailing
110   way of ensuring controllability. Juseon-Do et al. (2024) realizes sentence compression with a prompt and
111   improves its performance through instruction-based fine-tuning. Yuan et al. (2024) adds length constraints
112   to the prompt and designs a contrastive proxy task to fine-tune via Reinforcement Learning from Human
113   Feedback (RLHF). Jie et al. (2024) uses reinforcement learning and beam sampling for length control, on
114   GPT-2 models with less than 0.8 billion parameters. Anonymous (2024) embeds length constraints into
115   positional encodings to execute fine-tuning achieving precise length control. The major problems with SFT
116   methods are that they are resource-consuming and lack generalizability.

      The abstract workflow of the Self-controller, shown in Figure 2, contains a state reflector submodule. Given a
134
      specified task, the Self-controller will maintain relevant state variables in the state reflector. In length control,
135
      the state variable is the textual length, making transitions from 0 to the requested word constraint Lrequest . The
136   state reflector parses the new LLM response at each round and updates state variables accordingly. The state
137   is then passed as natural words to the LLM generating the next-step response. The reflection procedure will
138   be recycled indefinitely until the framework gets satisfactory results. In practice, JSON output is necessary
139   for ensuring the stability of the Self-controller, by excluding irrelevant information in the output, especially
140   for weaker foundation models.

      3.2   B INARY S EARCH ON THE T EXTUAL L ENGTH S TATE
142
143   Based on the linearity and monotonicity of the textual length state, we design the binary-search algorithm for
144   the length control, demonstrated in Algorithm 1. The major difference between the trivial and the binary-
145   search state reflector is the initiative of the state reflector. The trivial state reflector only provides textual
146   length states for the LLM to be self-awareness, including the requesting textual length Lrequest and the current
147   number of words len(Soutput ). In binary-search state reflector, the reflector not only provides basic statistics
148   but also actively asks the LLM to output 21 [Lrequest − len(Soutput )] words in the next round, thus executing
149   a binary search on the linear space of [0, Lrequest ]. In practice, we set the algorithm to go back to the trivial
150   mode when the surplus textual length is no longer valid for constructing a complete sentence.

172   3.3   C ONTEXT C ACHING IN M ULTI - ROUND S ESSIONS
173
174   Compared with trivial single-round prompts, the cost of the Self-controller is proportional to the total rounds
175   in the muli-round dialogue. An optimization to this is to build contextual caches, thanks to DeepSeek’s
176   Context Caching technique, which is made possible by the MLA architecture in DeepSeek-V2 (DeepSeek-
177   AI et al., 2024). Context caching builds textual blocks on disks and saves token consumption by an order
178   of magnitude. When duplicate inputs (only prefixes) are detected, the repeated parts will be retrieved from
179
      disks, thus bypassing redundant recomputation.

      New Paradigm of Thoughts Towards the dawn of reasoning, ReAct and ToT represent two paths for
424
      future directions on CoT. The major difference between these two paths is the way in which the message
425   is passed. ReAct can reason within a single session, while ToT requires assembly in different sessions.
426   Recently, Diagram-of-thought (DoT) (Zhang et al., 2024) proposes a similar idea to our Self-controller,
427   executing reasoning in multi-round sessions. With the development of near-infinite input and output length
428   of LLMs, the multi-round sessions can be more informative than a cluster of generated responses in the
429   future.

      6   C ONCLUSION
432
433
      In this paper, we introduced the Self-controller, a novel framework designed to enhance the controllability
434   of LLMs by incorporating self-awareness into their reasoning processes. By maintaining state variables
435   and enabling multi-round dialogue sessions, the Self-controller allows LLMs to refine their outputs itera-
436   tively, achieving more precise control over tasks such as length control. Our experiments demonstrated that
437   the Self-controller significantly improves the controllability of LLMs across various datasets and models,
438   without compromising the quality of the generated text. Implementing binary search optimization further
439   enhances the efficiency of the multi-round generation process, reducing the computational overhead. The
440   results also suggest that the Self-controller is adaptable to different foundation models and various word
441   constraints, showcasing its generalizability and robustness. Future work will focus on automating state
442
      management and exploring more advanced prompt engineering techniques to enhance output quality. Inte-
      grating planning techniques within the multi-round paradigm could also provide global controllability for
443
      more complex tasks. In conclusion, the Self-controller represents a significant step towards building more
444
      controllable and self-aware LLMs, paving the way for more reliable and versatile applications in natural
445   language processing.
