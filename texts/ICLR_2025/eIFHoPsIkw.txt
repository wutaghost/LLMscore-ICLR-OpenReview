                                                  A BSTRACT
013
               Vision foundation models pre-trained on massive data encode rich representations
014            of real-world concepts, which can be adapted to downstream tasks by fine-tuning.
015            However, fine-tuning foundation models on one task often leads to the issue of con-
016            cept forgetting on other tasks, and this issue is exacerbated by the typically limited
017            data for fine-tuning. Recent methods of robust fine-tuning aim to mitigate forgetting
018            of prior knowledge without affecting the fine-tuning performance. Knowledge is
019            often preserved by matching the original and fine-tuned model weights or feature
020            pairs. However, such point-wise matching can be too strong, without explicit aware-
021            ness of the feature neighborhood structures that encode rich knowledge as well.
022
               We propose a novel regularization method Proxy-FDA that explicitly preserves the
               structural knowledge in feature space. Proxy-FDA performs Feature Distribution
023
               Alignment (using nearest neighbor graphs) between the pre-trained and fine-tuned
024
               feature spaces, and the alignment is further improved by informative proxies that
025            are generated dynamically to increase data diversity. We show in end-to-end fine-
026            tuning experiments that Proxy-FDA significantly reduces concept forgetting, and
027            we find a strong correlation between forgetting and a distributional distance metric
028            (in comparison to L2 distance). We further demonstrate Proxy-FDA’s utility in
029            both few-shot (based on prompt tuning) and continual fine-tuning settings, where
030            we achieve consistent gains over the corresponding baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
      Vision foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) pre-
035
      trained on large amounts of data demonstrate remarkable performance across various tasks and data
036   distributions. Such foundation models are known to have learned vast knowledge on real-world
037   concepts that can serve as a useful prior for downstream task adaptation via fine-tuning. Existing fine-
038   tuning methods include end-to-end finetuning, linear probing, prompt tuning (Zhou et al., 2022a;b),
039   and adapter learning (Gao et al., 2021). While these methods prove effective, empirical evidence
040   shows that they frequently suffer from an undesirable effect called concept forgetting (Mukhoti et al.,
041   2024). Forgetting occurs when a fine-tuned model overfits on the downstream task, and unlike its
042   pre-trained counterpart, significantly loses the ability to recognize concepts on other tasks. The issue
043   is even more pronounced when limited data size and diversity are available for fine-tuning.
044   Concept forgetting has driven recent research on robust fine-tuning with the goal of preserving the
045   pre-trained knowledge and performing well on the downstream task. One simple approach is to
046   ensemble models before and after fine-tuning (Wortsman et al., 2022b). Alternative methods are
047   based on regularization techniques to constrain the fine-tuned model to remain close to the original
048   foundation model in either weight space (Li et al., 2018) or feature space (Mukhoti et al., 2024).
049   Feature-space regularization by matching the pre-trained and fine-tuned features across samples
050
      shows a more promising effect in reducing forgetting, since it directly minimizes the change in
      input-output behaviour of the model. One key assumption behind such regularization is that the L2
051
      feature-space distance is a good indicator of the similarity of encoded concepts in different models.
052
053   We argue that aligning individual feature points imposes too strong of a constraint. Without an explicit
      insight of feature neighborhoods, the concepts preserved point-wise are found to be limited, resulting

083
084        The above idea motivates us to propose a new feature regularization term called Feature Distribution
085        Alignment (FDA). Specifically, we first model the structural relations of pre-trained features using a
086        nearest neighbor graph. Then we transfer the graph to the fine-tuned feature space, where feature
087
           neighbors are pulled together while non-neighbors are pushed away regardless of their labels. Such
           FDA process enables sharing knowledge beyond class concepts in local feature neighborhoods, such
088
           as visual attributes or co-occurring patterns. Fig. 1(b) provides an example of the white color attribute
089
           (of two dog breeds) mined from a local neighborhood on ImageNet. This example represents the
090        common-sense prior knowledge embedded in a vision foundation model that is often richer than the
091        class labels on downstream datasets. Preserving such knowledge (e.g., about color) during fine-tuning
092        is important to maintain the generalizability of the foundation model, which can facilitate recognizing
093        unfamiliar classes from different tasks. What is harmful is to just specialize on the task at hand, since
094        all information (e.g., color sensitivity) but its class label will be discarded.
095
           Another key contribution of this paper is an improvement to FDA, with the introduction of a new
096        regularization called Proxy-FDA, which uses proxies as synthetic features. This full method is
097        particularly useful on data-deficient fine-tuning tasks (such as few-shot ones), where the limited task
098        data do not allow sufficient alignment of complex feature distributions. To further increase data
099        diversity, Proxy-FDA learns to generate a set of instance-wise proxies both within and outside a target
100        feature’s local neighborhood. Fig. 1(b) exemplifies some proxies that synthesize informative unseen
101        data or unseen class concepts. We empirically show that the generated proxies improves FDA with
102        richer data/concepts, thereby further reducing concept forgetting.
103        We have conducted experiments of fine-tuning vision foundation models end-to-end on ten classi-
104        fication tasks. Results show that Proxy-FDA significantly outperforms other fine-tuning baselines
105        in preventing concept forgetting, without hurting the downstream accuracy. We also find a strong
106        correlation between concept forgetting and a distance metric OTDD – Optimal Transport Dataset
107        Distance (Alvarez-Melis & Fusi, 2020) which is ideal to measure the alignment quality for feature
           distributions with local structures. Crucially, the correlation between concept forgetting and the

115

      2    R ELATED W ORK
125
126
      Robust fine-tuning. End-to-end fine-tuning often suffers from concept forgetting and degraded
      out-of-distribution (OOD) performance. In the foundation model era, linear probing or that followed
127
      by end-to-end tuning (Kumar et al., 2022) are common remedies to maintain the OOD robustness of a
128
      pre-trained model. Alternative methods either ensemble the original and fine-tuned models (Wortsman
129   et al., 2022b;a) or use the contrastive pre-training loss directly for fine-tuning (Goyal et al., 2023).
130   More recently, Song et al. (2023) propose a method called FD-Align, which trains a spurious feature
131   classifier and maintains its output consistency during fine-tuning. As a result, FD-Align significantly
132   improves the OOD accuracy. To prevent forgetting, regularization methods are often used to minimize
133   the model distance before and after fine-tuning in either weight space (Li et al., 2018) or feature
134   space (Mukhoti et al., 2024). In few-shot settings, regularization is even more important. For example,
135   the prompt learning method CLIPood (Shu et al., 2023) regularizes via temporal model ensembling,
136   while PromptSRC (Khattak et al., 2023b) directly regularizes the output features and logits between
137
      pre-trained and prompt-tuned models. Nevertheless, all existing methods do not explicitly account
      for feature neighborhood structures, which we show is key for robust fine-tuning.
138
139
      Feature and data distribution alignment. These techniques have been explored in different
140
      contexts. At the core of measuring distributional distances, Optimal Transport (OT) (Villani, 2008)
141
      provides a principled approach to compare data distributions in a geometrically meaningful way.
142   Given the similar nature of our FDA method that aligns the “clustering” structures of distributions,
143   we use an OT-based distance metric OTDD (Alvarez-Melis & Fusi, 2020) to measure FDA quality.
144   Feature alignment is also key to Domain Adaptation (DA) (Wang & Deng, 2018). However, most
145   DA methods learn a separate domain-invariant feature subspace to align domains implicitly, which
146   differs from our explicit FDA during fine-tuning. More related to our method is the Knowledge
147   Distillation (KD) field (Wang & Yoon, 2021), where traditional KD methods match features or
148   probability distributions between teacher and student models. Relation-based KD methods are
149   particularly similar to our high-level idea by distilling feature relations in form of kNNs (Zhu et al.,
150
      2022), feature similarities (Park et al., 2019; Passalis & Tefas, 2018; Tung & Mori, 2019; Peng et al.,
      2019) and relative ranks (Chen et al., 2018). Our Proxy-FDA can be seen as an alternative relational
151
      KD method that distills both kNNs and similarities, and further improves with proxy learning.
152
153
      Proxy learning. This approach is widely adopted in deep metric learning (Movshovitz-Attias et al.,
154
      2017; Kim et al., 2020; Roth et al., 2022) to reduce the sampling complexity of pure sample-based
155
      methods. Proxies are learned as class prototypes to optimize sample-proxy distances in place of
156   sample-sample distances, resulting in faster convergence. By contrast, our proxy learning is different
157   in both implementation and motivation: we learn instance-wise proxies via adaptive pooling of true
158   samples; we also do not use the proxies as sample stand-ins, but as rich augmentations for improving
159   FDA. This makes our approach more related to those feature augmentation methods, such as by
160   random linear interpolation (Verma et al., 2019) and outlier feature synthesis (Du et al., 2022; Tao
161   et al., 2023). Empirically, our method is more effective than these feature augmentation methods by
      generating diverse augmented features from the entire feature neighborhood.

177   3.1   F EATURE D ISTRIBUTION A LIGNMENT (FDA)

179   Having defined the learning problem and its general loss function, we now present our FDA method
180   in detail. During fine-tuning on Dft , we first use the pre-trained model fθ̂ and fine-tuned fθ to extract
181   batch features X̂ ∈ Rd×B and X ∈ Rd×B , respectively. Note X̂ = [x̂1 , . . . , x̂B ] are the pre-trained
182   batch features with x̂i ∈ Rd , while X = [x1 , . . . , xB ] are the features currently being fine-tuned
183   with xi ∈ Rd . To transfer the structural knowledge in X̂ into X, we align the structural relations of
184   X̂ and X based on their nearest neighbor graphs.
185
      Concretely, for each pre-trained feature point x̂i , we maintain its k-nearest neighbor set Ri =
186   {j|x̂j ∈ kNN(x̂i )} within the batch. Note |Ri | = K, and we will detail later how to construct
187   batches to facilitate the kNN search. This way, we obtain an instance-wise batch partition from the
188   pre-trained model’s perspective, leading to the positive set of neighbors X̂i+ = X̂(Ri ) ∈ Rd×K
189
      and negative set of non-neighbors X̂i− ∈ Rd×(B−K−1) . To form the complete nearest neighbor
190   graph, we further compute the cosine similarities between pre-trained features ŵij = cos(x̂i , x̂j )
191   for j ∈ {1, . . . , B} and j ̸= i. Accordingly, we organize them into similarity vectors for neighbors
192   ŵi+ ∈ RK and non-neighbors ŵi− ∈ RB−K−1 .
193
194   For efficient graph matching between X̂ and X , we choose to simply transfer the neighbor indices
195   Ri and similarities {ŵi+ , ŵi− } from X̂ to X. This means neighbors in the pre-trained feature space
196   should remain neighbors in the fine-tuned feature space. Hence among X, we similarly have a positive
197   set Xi+ = X(Ri ) ∈ Rd×K where the identified neighbors are pulled together in the fine-tuned
198   feature space, and a negative set Xi− ∈ Rd×(B−K−1) where non-neighbors are pushed away. On the
199   other hand, we associate the pre-trained feature similarities {ŵi+ , ŵi− } with {Xi+ , Xi− } to preserve
200   fine-grained feature neighborhood structures. We will show that transferring both the neighbor indices
201
      and similarities works better than only transferring the former, i.e., binary partitioning of neighbors
      vs. non-neighbors. Fig. 2(a) and (c) – the FDA parts – visualize the high-level idea.
202
203   To capture the desired structures, we use the Sigmoid loss, which is shown by (Zhai et al., 2023) to
204   be a noise-resistant objective to handle a variable number of positives and negatives per batch:

              To meet the above-mentioned requirements, we sample batch data in a class-balanced manner, with
234           n samples for each of the m classes. For a fixed batch size B = m · n that best fits in the available
235           GPU memory, we choose a high value of m to increase the diversity of class concepts in batch, but
236           at the cost of reducing the number of examples per class n. By default, m = 16 and n = 4. More
237           critically, we perform hard class mining to construct batches where samples from different classes
238           are similar (details in Appendix A). This enables meaningful kNN search within a batch.
239
              For the neighborhood size K, we choose K > n to guarantee that there is more than one class in
240           any identified local feature neighborhood Ri . This way, each neighborhood includes an adaptive
241           selection of “small clusters” from related classes. FDA between such neighborhoods will encourage
242           transferring high-level knowledge beyond class concepts. Fig. 1(b) exemplifies a common color
243           attribute mined locally for two similar dog classes. Preserving this knowledge that is embedded in
244           foundation models is important to prevent forgetting during fine-tuning. Note it is possible that the
245           inter-class similarity is not high enough in Ri (thus relatively low ŵij for inter-class samples and no
246           shared properties between classes), then FDA reverts back to aligning class semantics.

248           3.2       P ROXY-FDA
249
250           One challenge with FDA is that the downstream dataset Dft can be limited in both data size and
251
              diversity. In this case Dft does not allow adequate FDA, thereby preserving only limited concepts
              from those learned during pre-training. To address the data challenge, one could retrieve external data
252
              assuming the pre-training dataset is often inaccessible. However, using external data will inevitably
253
              suffer from higher compute/memory cost as well as various levels of distributional shift. Here we
254           propose a compute- and data-efficient approach to improve downstream data diversity and eventually
255           improve FDA quality. Our approach involves generating synthetic features or proxies on-the-fly from
256           observed fine-tuning data. Such generated proxies have no distributional shift since they adapt to the
257           considered feature distribution. We leave sampling suitable external data for FDA to future work.
258
              Before diving into the details of Proxy-FDA, we highlight two points. First, for each feature point xi
259
              in a batch, both its positive set Xi+ and negative set Xi− could lack diversity. Second, the semi-hard
260
              nature of Xi− (due to hard class mining) alleviates the “limited diversity” issue. This is because
261
              semi-hard negatives can provide the most informative signal for shaping the decision boundary,
262           without sampling the vast space of negative features.
263

307   Datasets. We follow (Mukhoti et al., 2024) to use 10 image classification datasets: Stanford
308   Cars (Krause et al., 2013), CIFAR-10/100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), Eu-
309   roSAT (Helber et al., 2019), GTSRB (Stallkamp et al., 2012), MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010), RE-
310   SISC45 (Cheng et al., 2017), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011) and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). These
311   datasets include various semantic concepts, thus are perfect to benchmark forgetting of the rich
312

510   5    C ONCLUSION
511
512   This paper introduces Proxy-FDA, a novel feature-space regularization method that preserves concepts
513
      during fine-tuning. The core idea is to align the local structures of pre-trained and fine-tuned feature
      distributions with learned proxies. A structure-aware distributional distance metric is used to assess
514
      the feature alignment quality, demonstrating a strong correlation with concept forgetting. Our
515
      approach achieves state-of-the-art results in mitigating concept forgetting across end-to-end, few-shot,
516   and continual fine-tuning settings.
517
518   Limitations and future work. We mainly study the forgetting of “concepts” at the granularity
519   of categorical class labels. The class concepts are used for both method development (e.g., class-
520   balanced batch construction, and FDA across classes) and performance evaluation (on downstream
521   classification datasets). To explore concepts beyond class labels, we could use natural language texts
522   that have rich concepts at varying granularity. This requires different design choices for FDA-style
523   methods to tackle the fine-grained concepts. As another line of future works, we plan to investigate
524   whether Proxy-FDA can reduce forgetting when fine-tuning for other foundation model families like
525   Large Language Models, or across different types of tasks like image segmentation and detection.

      As mentioned in the main text (Section 3.1), we perform hard class mining in the mini-batch to
759   facilitate the modeling and alignment of local neighborhood structures. The high-level idea of hard
760   class mining is to greedily select class distributions that are close to one another. More specifically,
761   we construct our mini-batch in the following way:
762     1. Randomly choose a large number of classes C ≫ m; for each class, randomly sample n
763         examples to extract their feature embeddings using both fθ̂ and fθ .
764     2. Sample a seed class randomly     from the C classes. Then greedily add a new class that has the
                                   Pn
765         largest class-wise loss i=1 LiFDA (Eq. (2)) w.r.t. the selected classes till we reach m classes.
766         Note in this greedy process, we set the neighborhood size K = n when computing LiFDA .
767     3. Construct batch with the selected m classes, each with n examples.

790   C     D ISTRIBUTIONAL D ISTANCE M ETRIC : OTDD
791
792   To measure FDA quality, there are many distance metrics for distribution alignment. Here we choose
793   the distributional distance metric based on Optimal Transport Dataset Distance (OTDD) (Alvarez-
794
      Melis & Fusi, 2020). OTDD is especially suited to measure the alignment quality of feature
      distributions with local structures, because this distance metric takes both the label distribution
795
      and clustering structure of the feature distributions into consideration.
796
797   Specifically, OTDD uses the feature and label distributions (x, y)|x∈X ,y∈Y to compute the distance
798   between two datasets. Given that the source and target datasets may have different label sets, the
799   high-level idea of OTDD is to represent each class label as a distribution over the in-class features.
800
      This transforms the source and target label sets into the shared space of feature distributions over
      X . In our context of model fine-tuning, we have pre-trained features x̂ and fine-tuned features x
801
      that are likely shifted from x̂. They form the source and target feature distributions respectively, and
802
      have different labels ŷ and y (details later). Then we can define the label distance DY (ŷ, y) using the
803   p-Wasserstein distance associated with the L2 distance ∥x̂ − x∥22 in X . This enables one to measure
804   the distributional difference in X × Y:
805                                                                                  1/p
806
                           DX ×Y ((x̂, ŷ), (x, y)) = (DX (x̂ − x)p + DY (ŷ, y)p )        .                  (9)
807   Please refer to (Alvarez-Melis & Fusi, 2020) for the exact formulation. To capture the clustering
808   structure of both the pre-trained and fine-tuned feature distributions, we perform K-Means clustering
809   per class on each feature distribution. This results in pseudolabels ŷ and y that are more fine-grained
      than class labels for OTDD computation.

    883     The intuition of setting K > n is to identify sufficient neighbors from more than one class, for
    884     meaningful FDA between similar clusters of related classes. Nevertheless, the exact value of K is
    885     varied as a function of dataset distribution, as each dataset has different levels of intra- and inter-class
    886     variation. In practice, we pick the best K per dataset from {n, 2n, 3n, 4n}. Fig. 5(b) shows how
￼   887     performance generally varies with K when K is fixed across 11 datasets. We see that K = 2n works
    888     best, while it noticeably hurts performance when K < n, confirming our intuition above. Hence we
    889     stick to the constraint of K > n for per-dataset K selection (Fig. 5(c)).
    890     On the other hand, the scalar s is set to 0.4 by default. This leads to a virtual batch size of around 90
    891     (increased from 64). The virtual batch now consists of true and synthetic features for FDA. Fig. 5(d)
    892     shows the sensitivity analysis for s.

945   Regarding the proxy generation algorithm, we compare with three baselines. One simple method is
946   based on linear interpolation between random feature pairs from both Xi+ and Xi− . Feature similarity
947   estimates are interpolated in the same way. Despite the simplicity, random interpolation obtains
948   inferior performance than our learning-based approach — our approach can learn to synthesize more
949   informative features to better help FDA. On the other hand, the parametric VOS and non-parametric
950   NPOS methods learn to synthesize outlier features in low-likelihood regions (often around decision
951   boundaries between classes). The two methods are observed to achieve even worse results than
952   random interpolation. We conjecture that this is because outliers in low-likelihood regions are not
