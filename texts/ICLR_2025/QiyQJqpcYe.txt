010                                                     A BSTRACT
011
012             We propose a new benchmark to measure a language model’s linguistic reasoning skills
013
                without relying on pre-existing language-specific knowledge. The test covers 894 ques-
                tions grouped in 160 problems across 75 (mostly) extremely low-resource languages,
014
                extracted from the International Linguistic Olympiad corpus. To attain high accuracy on
015
                this benchmark, models don’t need previous knowledge of the tested language, since all
016             the information required to solve the linguistic puzzle is provided within the context. We
017             find that, while all analyzed models rank below 25% accuracy, there is a significant gap
018             between open and closed models, with the best-performing proprietary model at 24.05%
019             and the best-performing open model at 8.84%.
020

022   1       I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Recently, language models have shown impressive multilingual skills (Xu et al., 2024), achieving state of
025   the art results in several tasks, such as machine translation (OpenAI, 2024), bilingual lexicon induction
026
      (Brown et al., 2020) and cross-lingual classification (Xue et al., 2021). However, the steep performance
      increase in these tasks has led to the saturation of popular benchmarks, such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
027
      2021), where state-of-the-art (SotA) performance has gone from 60% in December 2021 (Rae et al., 2022) to
028
      90% in December 2023 (Gemini Team, 2024), providing diminishing returns when it comes to quantifying
029   differences between models.
030
031   Moreover, in the case of linguistic reasoning, the task of evaluating a model’s linguistic skills is often tied
032
      to the comprehensive knowledge a model has of a certain language (most commonly, English), making it
      difficult to evaluate a model’s underlying linguistic skills beyond language-specific knowledge.
033
034   To address these issues, we introduce Linguini1 , a linguistic reasoning benchmark. Linguini consists of
035   linguistic problems which require meta-linguistic awareness and deductive reasoning capabilities to be solved
036   instead of pre-existing language proficiency. Linguini is based on problems extracted from the International
037   Linguistic Olympiad (IOL)2 , a secondary school level contest where participants compete in solving Rosetta
038
      Stone-style problems (Derzhanski and Payne, 2010) relying solely on their understanding of linguistic
      concepts. An example of the type of challenges and the reasoning steps needs to solve it can be seen in Figure
039
      2.
040
041   We evaluate a list of open and proprietary models on Linguini, showing a noticeable gap between open and
042   closed language models, in favor of the latter. We also conduct a series of experiments aiming at understanding
043   the role of the contextual information in the accuracy obtained in the benchmark, performing both form

051   2     R ELATED W ORK
052
053   There has been an increasing number of articles focusing on evaluating reasoning in language models
054   (Chang et al., 2024). In the area of mathematical reasoning, Qin et al. (2023) analyze models’ arithmetic
055   reasoning, while Frieder et al. (2023) leverage publicly-available problems to build GHOSTS, a comprehensive
056   mathematical benchmark in natural language. Bang et al. (2023) include symbolic reasoning in their multitask,
057
      multilingual and multimodal evaluation suite. Wu et al. (2024) and Hartmann et al. (2023) show that current
      language models have profound limitations when performing abstract reasoning, but Liu et al. (2023) indicate
058
      promising logical reasoning skills; however, performance is limited on out-of-distribution data. Multi-step
059
      reasoning is assessed by Chain-of-Thought Hub (Fu et al., 2023) and ThoughtSource (Ott et al., 2023),
060   pointing out the limitations of language models in complex reasoning tasks.
061
062   Coverage of linguistic reasoning, which can be defined as the ability to understand and operate under the
063
      rules of language, has been limited in evaluation datasets for language models. One of the earliest examples is
      PuzzLing Machines (Şahin et al., 2020), which presents 7 different patterns from the Rosetta Stone paradigm
064
      Bozhanov and Derzhanski (2013) for models to perform exclusively machine translation. Chi et al. (2024)
065
      replicate Şahin et al. (2020)’s approach, manually creating a number of examples to avoid data leakage.
066   Recently, some approaches have leveraged long context capabilities of language models to include in-context
067   linguistic information (e.g. a grammar book (Tanzer et al., 2024) and other domain-specific sources (Zhang
068   et al., 2024)) to solve different linguistic tasks. For large-scale linguistic reasoning evaluation, Big-Bench
069   (Lewkowycz et al., 2022) includes a task linguistic mappings3 , relying on arbitrary artificial grammars to
070   perform logical deduction. This approach is limited by its reliance on constructed languages instead of
071   natural languages, which overlooks more complex underlying properties of languages, such as voicing rules.
072   Finally, Waldis et al. (2024) present Holmes, a comprehensive benchmark for linguistic competence in English
073   language.
074
075   3     B ENCHMARKING LINGUISTIC REASONING
076
077   To overcome the previous limitations, we built a dataset where, in most cases, a model has no information
078   about task language outside of the given context. To achieve this, we worked with problems extracted from
079   the International Linguistic Olympiad.
080
081   3.1   IOL
082
083   The International Linguistic Olympiad (IOL)4 is a contest for students up to secondary school level, where
084   contestants must compete solving problems based on their understanding of linguistics (Derzhanski and
085   Payne, 2010). The presented problems are formulated following the Rosetta Stone paradigm and present
086   participants with challenges related to a variety of (mainly) extremely low-resource languages that students
087   are not expected to be familiar with. The goal is for participants to leverage their linguistic skills rather than
088
      their foreign language knowledge. The IOL has been held yearly since 2003 (with the exception of 2020),
      and every year includes 5 short problems (to be solved individually) and 1 long, multipart problem (to be
089
      solved in groups). Problems are formulated in English and in several languages (up to 25 languages for the

099   3.2     S ELECTING PROBLEMS FOR OUR BENCHMARK
100
101   To select the types of questions for the dataset, we built a taxonomy exploring the IOL from 2003 to 2023.
102   We excluded all instances for which their category only appears once; those where the question includes an
103   image or those where the response is only an explanation. The remaining problems require solving different
104   linguistic reasoning tasks, such as morphosyntactic segmentation (eg., verb conjugation), morphosemantic
105   alignment (e.g., noun negation), derivation (e.g., finding cognates in related languages), morphophonological
106
      segmentation (e.g., pluralization) or graphophonemic transcription (e.g., transcription from one script to
      another). In total, Linguini is composed by 894 questions grouped in 160 problems across 75 (mostly)
107
      extremely low-resource language. A list of languages can be found in Appendix B. We classify the problems
108
      included in Linguini into the three categories according to their content: sequence transduction, fill-in-blanks
109   and number transliteration. Figure 1 shows one example of each.

      Sequence transduction This category includes sequence production (identified in the benchmark as
142
      ‘translation’) and sequence matching (identified as ‘match_letter’). The problems require the
143   model to transform a sequence into a different space (e.g., language, phonetic representation, script) based on
144   few examples. In some cases, basic phonetic/phonological knowledge is needed. For example, the model
145   should be able to reason over principles of voicing and their implementation in situations of coarticulation.
146   Some problems require to know that consonants come in voiced-voiceless pairs, and that one element of the
147   pair may in some cases be a substitute for the other element in the pair under certain circumstances.
148
149
150   Fill-in blanks Fill-in blanks are mainly morphophonological derivation tasks, and they are identified in
151   the benchmark as ‘fill_blanks’. Models need to understand what are the morphophonological rules
152   that make it possible to go from the first form of a word to its second form. This can usually be applied
153
      to verbal (e.g., verb tense conjugation), nominal or adjectival (e.g., case declension) derivation. It involves
      understanding affixation rules and morpheme swapping rules, which often come with phonological rules
154
      if there are different coarticulation phenomena with different affixes or phonotactic phenomena such as
155
      consonantal mutations.
156
157
158   Digit/text number transliteration These problems are identified by the labels ‘text_to_num’ and
159   ‘num_to_text’. In them, models have to produce a digit or text equivalent, respectively. They require a
160   model’s understanding of morphological analysis and morpheme order.

      4    E XPERIMENTS
189
190
      We perform zero-shot to few-shot (0-5 in-context examples) evaluation across the whole dataset for an array
191   of open and proprietary LLMs. Given the size of the benchmark, we employ a leave-one-out cross-validation
192   scheme to maximize the number of in-context candidates per task. For every given inference, we include
193   examples of the same format (e.g., ‘translation’, ‘match_letter’), but we exclude in-context
194   examples of the same language to avoid language contamination.
195
196   Setup and Models We prompt models with an instruction, a context that provides information to unambigu-
197   ously solve the linguistic problem and the problem itself. Scores of answers to each item of a problem are
198   averaged to provide a single score (0-100) per task. We evaluate several major open LLMs and commercially
199   available (behind API) SotA LLMs at the publication of this work. For open models, we conduct inference
200
      experiments in an 8 A100 GPUs node. An exhaustive list can be found in Appendix C.
201
      Evaluation We use exact match (accuracy) as main evaluation criterion. Given the almost null performance
202
      on exact match of certain models, we also include chrF (Popović, 2015) as a softer metric. A low chrF score
203
      indicates extremely low performance models, e.g. not understanding the domain of the task at hand.

263   5.2    C HARACTER - WISE SUBSTITUTION
264
265   Since most problems are presented in Latin script, we wanted to understand whether the script in which the
266   task languages are presented impact the performance on Linguini. But given that all information needed to
267   solve the task is present in the context, the script should not have a major impact on the performance beyond
268   encoding constraints. In other words, if the model doesn’t rely on instances of the language (or the problem)
269   in its training set, it should be able to solve the task in a non-Latin script as well. We selected the best
270
      performing model (claude-3-opus) and transcribed the best performing problems (those with accuracy greater
      than or equal to 75%) into 4 non-Latin alphabetical scripts (Cyrilic, Greek, Georgian and Armenian)7 . An
271
      example of a transliterated problem can be found in Figure 3. Given the difficulty of uniformly transcribing
272

273   substitution of the standard Latin alphabet character, leaving non-standard characters with their original
274   Unicode symbol. We filtered 17 well performing problems, and excluded one with a non-Latin script task
275   language (English Braille). We performed transcriptions on the remaining 16 problems.
276

453   6   C ONCLUSIONS
454
455   We presented Linguini, a new linguistic reasoning evaluation dataset. Our experiments show that Linguini
456   provides a compact and effective benchmark to assess linguistic reasoning without relying on a substrate of
457   existing language-specific knowledge. There’s a considerable gap between open source and proprietary LLMs
458   in linguistic reasoning. Subsequent experiments also show very low likelihood of dataset contamination in
459   the analyzed models. Limitations and broader impact of the dataset are discussed in Appendix A.
