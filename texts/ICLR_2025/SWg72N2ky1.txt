009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper is devoted to the study (common in many applications) of the black-
012            box optimization problem, where the black-box represents a gradient-free oracle
013            f˜p = f (x) + ξp providing the objective function value with some stochastic noise.
014            Assuming that the objective function is µ-strongly convex, and also not just L-
015            smooth, but has a higher order of smoothness (β ≥ 2) we provide a novel opti-
016            mization method: Zero-Order Accelerated Batched Stochastic Gradient Descent,
017            whose theoretical analysis closes the question regarding the iteration complexity,
018            achieving optimal estimates. Moreover, we provide a thorough analysis of the
019
               maximum noise level, and show under which condition the maximum noise level
               will take into account information about batch size B as well as information about
020
               the smoothness order of the function β. Finally, we show the importance of con-
021
               sidering the maximum noise level ∆ as a third optimality criterion along with the
022            standard two on the example of a numerical experiment of interest to the machine
023            learning community, where we compare with SOTA gradient-free algorithms.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   This paper focuses on solving a standard optimization problem:
029                                            f ∗ :=    min f (x),                                       (1)
030                                                     x∈Q⊆Rd
031   where f : Q → R is function that we want to minimize, f ∗ is the solution, which we want to find. It
032   is known that if there are no obstacles to compute the gradient of the objective function f or to com-
033   pute a higher order of the derivative of the function, then optimal first- or higher-order optimizations
034   algorithms Nesterov (2003) should be used to solve the original optimization problem equation 1.
035   However, if computing the function gradient ∇f (x) is impossible for any reason, then perhaps the
036   only way to solve the original problem is to use gradient-free (zero-order) optimization algorithms
037   Conn et al. (2009); Rios & Sahinidis (2013). Among the situations in which information about the
038   derivatives of the objective function is unavailable are the following:
039
           a) non-smoothness of the objective function. This situation is probably the most widespread
040           among theoretical works Gasnikov et al. (2022); Alashqar et al. (2023); Kornilov et al.
041           (2024);
042
           b) the desire to save computational resources, i.e., computing the gradient ∇f (x) can some-
043
              times be much ”more expensive” than computing the objective function value f (x). This
044           situation is quite popular and extremely understandable in the real world Bogolubsky et al.
045           (2016);
046
           c) inaccessibility of the function gradient. A vivid example of this situation is the problem of
047
              creating an ideal product for a particular person Lobanov et al. (2024).
048
049   Like first-order optimization algorithms, gradient-free algorithms have the following optimality cri-
050   teria: #N – the number of consecutive iterations required to achieve the desired accuracy of the
051   solution ε and #T – the total number of calls (in this case) to the gradient-free oracle, where by
052   gradient-free/derivative-free oracle we mean      we have access only to the objective function f (x)

      function is subject to noise, the gradient-free oracle plays the role of a black box. That is why there

      higher-order algorithms, gradient-free algorithms have a third optimality criterion: the maximum
068
      noise level ∆ at which the algorithm will still converge ” good”, where by ”good convergence” we
069   mean convergence as in the case when ∆ = 0. The existence of such a seemingly unusual criterion
070   can be explained by the following motivational examples (see Figure 1* ). Among the motivations
071   we can highlight the most demanded especially by companies (and not only). Resource saving (Fig-
072   ure 1a): The more accurately the objective function value is calculated, the more expensive this
073   process to be performed. Robustness to Attacks (Figure 1b): Improving the maximum noise level

078
      above three criteria is to take advantage of first-order algorithms by substituting a gradient approxi-
079
      mation instead of the true gradient Gasnikov et al. (2023). The choice of the first-order optimization
080   algorithm depends on the formulation of the original problem (on the Assumptions on the function
081   and the gradient oracle). But the choice of gradient approximation depends on the smoothness of the
082   function. For example, if the function is non-smooth, a smoothing scheme with l1 randomization
083   Alashqar et al. (2023); Lobanov (2023) or with l2 randomization Dvinskikh et al. (2022); Lobanov
084   et al. (2023a;b) should be used to solve the original problem. If the function is smooth, it is enough
085   to use choose l1 randomization Akhavan et al. (2022) or l2 randomization Gorbunov et al. (2018);
086   Lobanov & Gasnikov (2023). But if the objective function is not just smooth but also has a higher
087   order of smoothness (β ≥ 2), then the so-called Kernel approximation Akhavan et al. (2023); Gas-
088
      nikov et al. (2024b;a), which takes into account the information about the increased smoothness of
      the function using two-point feedback, should be used as the gradient approximation.
089
090   In this paper, we consider the black-box optimization problem equation 1, assuming strong con-
091   vexity as well as increased smoothness of the objective function. We choose accelerated stochastic
092   gradient descent Vaswani et al. (2019) as the basis for a gradient-free algorithm. Since the Kernel
093   approximation (which accounts for the advantages of increased smoothness) is biased, we general-
094
      ize the result of Vaswani et al. (2019) to the biased gradient oracle. We use the resulting accelerated
      stochastic gradient descent with a biased gradient oracle to create a gradient-free algorithm. Finally,
095
      we explicitly derive estimates on the three optimality criteria of the gradient-free algorithm.

214   gradient approximation from a zero-order oracle concept has a bias, it is also important to find a
215   first-order algorithm that will use a biased gradient oracle. Using these criteria, we formulate an

532
533
      In this paper, we proposed a novel accelerated gradient-free algorithm to solve the black-box op-
534   timization problem under the assumption of increased smoothness and strong convexity of the ob-
535   jective function. By choosing a first-order accelerated algorithm and generalizing it to the Batched
536   algorithm with a biased gradient oracle, we were able to improve the iteration complexity, reaching
537   optimal estimates. Moreover, we have shown the importance of considering the maximum noise
538   level as a third optimality criterion in a numerical experiment of interest in machine learning. And
539   finally, we believe that this work offers a new perspective on black-box optimization and opens
      avenues for future research.
