011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Beyond the conventional long-term temporal dependency modeling, multivariate
014            time series (MTS) forecasting has rapidly shifted toward channel dependency (CD)
015
               modeling. This shift significantly improves modeling quality by fully leveraging
               both multivariate relationships and temporal dependencies. Recent methods primar-
016
               ily model channel dependency through correlation learning (e.g., cross-attention)
017
               or non-trainable statistical techniques (e.g., cross-correlation), however, these ap-
018            proaches are insufficient to fully capture the intrinsic relationships within MTS,
019            particularly those stemming from directed cause-effect (i.e., causality) and non-
020            stationary variates originating from diverse sources. In addition, causality may arise
021            in MTS with different temporal behaviors, such as varying periodicity or discrete
022            event sequences, which remains underexplored. In this paper, we propose CALAS
023            (Causality-enhanced Attention with Learnable and Adaptive Spacing), the first
024            end-to-end learning method for MTS forecasting that uncovers causality among
025            variates without relying on statistical measures or prior knowledge. To model under-
026
               lying causality, which consists of causal strength and propagation delay, we newly
               design a hypernetworks-based 1D convolutions mechanism. Inspired by dilated
027
               convolution with learnable spacings (DCLS) and spiking neural networks (SNNs),
028
               we extend discrete time delay into a continuous Gaussian kernel. Combining the
029            hypernetworks-generated Gaussian kernel and convolutional weights (i.e., attention
030            or causal strength), we achieve the end-to-end dynamic causality modeling mecha-
031            nism, improving the prediction accuracy, quality, and interpretability of both MTS
032            forecasting results and models. For evaluation, we conduct extensive experiments
033            with six real-world datasets and qualitative analysis to demonstrate CALAS’s supe-
034            riority in capturing varying causality in a data-agnostic manner. The experiment
035            results indicate that CALAS has significantly improved MTS forecasting accuracy
036            compared to state-of-the-art methods by dynamically modeling causality among
037
               variates.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

041
      Multivariate time series (MTS) forecasting is one of the core problems in time series analysis,
042   widespread in various domains such as traffic (Li & Shahabi, 2018; Lee et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020),
043   finance (Zeng et al., 2023; Zhu & Shasha, 2002), and weather (Matsubara et al., 2014; Wu et al.,
044   2021). The fundamental idea of MTS forecasting methods is to build a mapping function from input
045   (i.e., historical) to output (i.e., future) MTS by learning inherent dependencies of MTS. To model the
046   intrinsic dependencies of MTS, the main flow of MTS forecasting is evolved into two distinguished
047   categories – temporal dependency modeling and channel (or, variate) dependency modeling.
048   For temporal dependency modeling, researchers have proposed novel deep learning architectures,
049   ranging from RNN-based models (Li et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2023; Zonoozi et al., 2018) to
050   Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022), or have
051   modified existing model for improved dependency modeling (Bai et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou
052   et al., 2022). While these advances have significantly improved MTS forecasting quality, they often
053   fail to capture important temporal characteristics, such as periodicity (Oreshkin et al., 2020; Wu
      et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023). To address this issue, some approaches incorporate prior knowledge

088

089
      embeddings; however, they struggle to fully capture the dynamic nature of causality (Wu et al., 2020).
090   Recently, Zhao & Shen (2024) have proposed a novel method that leverages causality extracted from
091   cross-correlation to align output signals with propagation delay along with corresponding leading
092   indicators. While this approach addresses dynamic changes in causality, questions remain about
093   the capacity of cross-correlation and sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., the maximum number of
094   leading indicators). These issues highlights two ongoing challenges in adopting causality modeling in
095   MTS: 1) lack of end-to-end, learning-based dynamic causality modeling methods; and 2) limitation
096   of prior knowledge or statistical methods in fully leveraging intrinsic causality in MTS.
097   Figure 1 presents a motivating example from the Weather dataset, where local causality changes in
098   consecutive signals, highlighting the need for dynamic causality modeling. It also emphasizes a need
099   for layer-agnostic causality modeling methods that can be applied in any level of input, intermediate,
100   or output layers, since causal structure may changed even within model forwarding. Additionally, it
101   suggests a potential side-effect of statistic-based CD modeling approaches, such as cross-correlation
102   (e.g., LIFT (Zhao & Shen, 2024)), which could mislead models to focus on less relevant variates.
103   Moreover, correlation-based methods often struggle with long-term MTS, especially in signals with
104   strong short-term periodicity. To further describe this phenomena, we present Figure 2 that shows
105   synthetic signals with a periodic seasonal pattern, sinusoidal trends, and varying noise level. While all
106   three signals shares the same seasonal pattern, v2 and v3 exhibit a stronger causal relationship than

129   In this work, we propose a novel end-to-end causality modeling method named CALAS1 (Causality-
130   enhanced Attention with Learnable and Adaptive Spacing) that converts correlation-based attention
131
      into causality by incorporating dilated convolution with learnable spacing (DCLS) (Hassani et al.,
      2023; Khalfaoui-Hassani et al., 2023) and hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2017) in a novel way. CALAS
132
      consists of three components: two hypernetworks and one convolutional neural network. To uncover
133
      the dynamic causal relations among MTS while considering its propagation delay, we extend the
134   static delay modeling with the Gaussian kernel by introducing input-based dynamic delay modeling.
135   Specifically, we adopt the hypernetworks–neural networks that generate the weights for another
136   network–to model both the dynamic causal strength and propagation delay (i.e., causality map). We
137   utilize two hypernetworks: one for estimating the Gaussian kernel, which serves as the time lag
138   (or delay) of causality, and the other to measure the strength of the causality. After generating the
139   weights, CALAS applies the resulting causality map directly through the convolutional architecture.
140   In addition to enabling flexible causality learning beyond the methods relying on prior knowledge,
141   our design enhances interpretability by separating causality map into causal strength and propagation
142
      delay. Furthermore, unlike the other causality-based CD models, CALAS can learn causality in input
      space, output space, or both, offering greater flexibility in integrating CALAS with other causality
143
      modeling methods, which will be discussed in the experimental results.
144
145   The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows.
146
147             • We introduce a new perspective on converting the correlation-based attention score into
148
                  causality-based relationships by decomposing the causality map into learnable delay and
                  causal strength.
149
150             • We design a novel architecture, CALAS, that enables the end-to-end learning of dynamic
151               cause-and-effect relationships among variates for MTS forecasting. By design, CALAS can
152               be applied in a model-agnostic way at any stage of the MTS forecasting models, including
153               input and output space. To the best of our knowledge, CALAS is the first work to model
154               causality for MTS forecasting in an end-to-end manner.
155             • Experimentally, we demonstrate that CALAS significantly improves the state-of-the-art
156               methods. Specifically, our model improves the existing models by 7.61% on average, out-
157               performing existing methods (e.g., LIFT) by 2.21 percentage points. We highlight that the
158               improvements become larger for long-term forecasting, 9.45% on average. We have also
159               revealed that CALAS can be integrated with other causality-based plug-and-play modules,
160               such as LIFT, resulting in substantial performance gains.

      MTS forecasting could be categorized into two research theme: temporal dependency modeling
165   without inter-variate dependencies (i.e., channel-independent methods) and channel dependency (CD)
166   modeling methods. Since our paper aims to solve CD modeling problem, we briefly introduce CI
167   methods and move on to CD methods.
168
      In MTS forecasting, CI methods are well-studied and are still on-going research directions, ranging
169
      from statistical models (e.g., ARIMA (Box et al., 2015), hidden Markov model (Pesaran et al.,
170
      2004)) to recent deep learning models (Liu et al., 2023; Zhao & Shen, 2024). Recent models are
171   focusing on the long-term dependency modeling by leveraging the traditional studies, such as Fourier
172   transformation (Zhou et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2024) or by proposing novel input
173   representation methods (Nie et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Jia et al.,
174   2023). Those methods contribute to MTS forecasting domain by deeply understanding in-nature
175   characteristics of time series (i.e., temporal dependency).
176
      On the other hands, CD methods aim to uncover the intrinsic channel-wise dependency. Based on
177   the methodology and assumption for channel dependency, CD methods can be divided into four
178   categories: 1) model-based dynamic correlation learning (Learnable Correlation in Table 1), 2)
179   model-based static causality learning (Learnable Static Causality in Table 1), 3) statistics-based
180   dynamic causality learning (Statistics-based Causality in Table 1), and 4) model-based dynamic
181   causality learning (Ours in Table 1). For CD modeling, there are primarily two directions–learnable
182   correlation with attention mechanism (Zheng et al., 2020; Vaswani et al., 2017; Behrouz et al., 2024;
183   Zhang & Yan, 2023) and learnable static causality with graph neural networks (GNNs) and learnable
184   embedding (Cao et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020; 2019; Lee et al., 2022). While
185
      attention mechanism aims to reconstruct correlation map by projecting inputs into the feature space,
      GNN-based methods try to approximate underlying, time-independent causal structure with learnable
186
      node embedding. However, those approaches merely discuss the lead-lag relationship and propagation
187
      delay, which is crucial for the CD modeling.
188
189   Recently, a few researches (Wu et al., 2020; Zhao & Shen, 2024) depict that when two signals have
190   cause-effect relationship, there exist a time-lag for ‘cause’ signal to influence the ‘effect’ signal,
191
      which we call propagation delay. To fully leverage the benefits of CD methods, it is necessary to
      consider propagation delay, since ignorance of propagation delay making the model refer the outdated
192
      information (Zhao & Shen, 2024). To the best of our knowledge, LIFT (Zhao & Shen, 2024) is
193
      the only work that explicitly aims to solve the propagation delay by leveraging cross-correlation,
194   which is statistics-based causality in our categorization. However, overrelying the statistical measure
195   introduces a number of limitations, such as the information bottleneck from the excessively large
196   number of recurring patterns (Lee et al., 2022), possible misalignment, and need of reference signals
197   that limit the utilization of model in various situation. We describe those properties in Table 1.

230   3.1   D ETAILED C ATEGORIZATION FOR C HANNEL D EPENDENCY M ODELING
231
      From the literature review, we have identified two distinctions in CD methods: the perspective on
232
      temporal variation and the type of relationship. Regarding temporal variation, CD modeling can be
233
      classified as either static or dynamic. Static CD modeling assumes that the underlying causal structure
234   remains constant over time (i.e., At = At′ ∀t, t′ ), thus avoiding instantaneous effects or potential
235   misleading caused by anomalous values. This approach is widely used in early traffic forecasting (Wu
236   et al., 2020; 2019; Li et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2022) and causal discovery (Cheng et al., 2023b; 2024).
237   In contrast, dynamic CD modeling assumes that CD can change over time. It is a dominant research
238   trend in long-term MTS forecasting, including Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023) and LIFT (Zhao
239   & Shen, 2024). Dynamic CD modeling offers the advantage of adjusting the strength or nature of
240   dependencies and also help mitigate distribution shifts in channel dependency (Zhao & Shen, 2024;
241   Jiang et al., 2023).
242   Additionally, CD can be categorized into two types of relationships: undirected and directed. Correla-
243   tion, an undirected relationship, is a traditional approach to modeling time series data. Transformer-
244   based models are a representative example,utilizing self-attention mechanism to model channel de-
245   pendencies. Recent patch-based models (Nie et al., 2023; Zhang & Yan, 2023) or inverted embedding
246   methods (Liu et al., 2023) also fall under correlation-based model due to their input representations.
247   On the other hand, there are a few researches focus on directed causal relation, using GNNs (Wu et al.,
248
      2020; Jiang et al., 2023) or statistical methods (Zhao & Shen, 2024). Directed CD methods explicitly
      target lead-lag, propagation, or causality as their objective, often relying on either static structure
249
      learning (Wu et al., 2020) or statistical methods, such as cross-correlation (Zhao & Shen, 2024).
250
      Moreover, there is no literature that explicitly learns the delay in MTS, limiting the interpretability.

300   4.1   C AUSALITY, G RANGER -C AUSES , AND C AUSAL D ISCOVERY IN R EAL - WORLD MTS DATA
301
302   Causality In the causal discovery domain, the causality is commonly based on several assumptions:
303   Markovian condition, faithfulness, sufficiency, no instantaneous effect, and stationarity (Cheng et al.,
304   2024). However, in the real-world MTS, it is hard to observe “all possible events and causes in the
305   world,” the sufficiency–all common causes of variables are observed–and stationarity–all the causal
306
      relationships remain constant throughout time– are hard to be hold. For the real-world MTS data, we
      need to assume that there exists latent variables or events that changes causality of observed variates.
307
      Therefore, in this paper, causality is based on the assumptions for Markovian condition, faithfulness,
308
      and sufficiency. We emphasize that causality in this paper is not necessarily be true causality, which
309   have assumptions for no latent compounder and no unseen events.
310
311   Concepts of CALAS The basic idea of CALAS stems from the concepts of multivariate Granger
312   causality (GC) analysis (Granger, 1969). Multivariate GC analysis is performed by fitting a VAR
313   model with given maximum possible propagation delay k as below:

366   4.3   C AUSALITY- ENHANCED ATTENTION WITH L EARNABLE AND A DAPTIVE S PACING
367
368   In this section, we describe the overall flow of CALAS with hypernetworks when it applied to input
369   layer, as illustrated in Figure 3a. Specifically, we utilize two hypernetworks, one for the causal
370   strength, and the other for delay learning. The generation process are shared for both hypernetworks,
371
      however, the causal strength is shared for k possible delays. Specifically, in the Equation 4, wi,j ∈
372   R, σi,j ∈ R, di,j ∈ Rk . By sharing same causal strength for the same pair of variates, CALAS
373
      achieves better generalization for causal strength.
374   We design our hypernetworks with one multi-layer perceptron (MLP) and feature embedding. MLP
375   layer takes the input vector z ∈ RT ×Ni (in Figure 3a, Xt ), where Ni is input channels, and project
376   it into hidden size of H. To map the projected input to the No channels, we introduce embedding
377   vectors M = {m(1) , . . . , m(No ) }, where m(i) ∈ Rh×No . M is initially random vectors and will
      be trained to represent the static features of each output variate. For the output channels, we can


                                                                7

378
      interchangeably use MLP or embedding vectors with users’ intention. Thus, we generate weights
379   wi,j as follows:
380
381                            wiin = wM LP z (i) + bM LP ; wi,j = g(wiin , m(j) )
382
383   where wM LP ∈ RT ×H and bM LP ∈ RH are learnable parameters. Function g(·, ·) could be any
384
      function or operation to generate RNi ×No map, including matrix multiplication and self-attention.
      To generate causal strength, we utilize matrix multiplication with LeakyReLU activation function,
385
      resulting to RNi ×No vector. For the propagation delay, we mimic multi-head attention (Vaswani et al.,
386
      2017), which generates RNi ×No ×k output. Generated weights will be directly feed into Gaussian
387   kernel (Equation 4) and then forward to propagation module (Figure 3b). CALAS will be served as
388   the plug-in module injected in input-, output-, or intermediate-layers. In case of Figure 3a, CALAS
389   has plugged into the input layer and its output will be served as the input for backbone model.

395   Datasets Following previous researches (Zhou et al., 2021; 2022; Nie et al., 2023), we conduct
396   experiments on six MTS datasets, including Weather, Electricity, Traffic, Solar, Wind, and PeMSD8.
397   In this paper, we have excluded ETT* datasets because they are recorded from the same sources,
398   which means they simultaneously changes and have no causal relationship (Zhou et al., 2021). We
399   also exclude Exchange-rate dataset since it is well known that xt−1 is the best predictor for xt
400   if the market is efficient (Fama, 1970; Rossi, 2013) and Zeng et al. (2023) have experimentally
401   demonstrated that simply repeating the last value can outperform or be comparable to the best results.
402
403   Baselines To demonstrate performance of CALAS as a plug-in module, we conduct the experiments
404   with CALAS and four MTS forecasting backbones, PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), DLinear (Zeng et al.,
405
      2023), Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023), and MTGNN (Wu et al., 2020). PatchTST and DLinear
      is channel-independent modeling methods and others are channel-dependent methods. We further
406
      compared our results with statistics-based plug-in module, LIFT (Zhao & Shen, 2024), to prove
407
      effectiveness of end-to-end dynamic causality modeling. We also provide comparative experiments
408   with Linear model-based CALAS with baseline methods, additionally including FEDformer (Zhou
409   et al., 2022), Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), and LightMTS (Zhao & Shen, 2024).
410
411   Setups We follow the same experimental settings as previous MTS forecasting works (Zhou et al.,
412   2022; Nie et al., 2023), where the forecasting horizon L ∈ {96, 192, 336, 720} and lookback window
413   T = 336. All the baselines follow the experimental settings, including hyperparemeter settings, in
414   official code. For CALAS, we adopt the Adam optimizer and search the optimal learning rate in
415   {0.1, 0.05, 0.01, 0.005, 0.001, 0.0005}. We set hidden size H = 64 and batch size to 32. We have
416   run each experiment five times and reported average performance. We also provide quantitative and
417   qualitative results for causal discovery in Appendix A.

464   Improvements over plug-in method Compared to the recently developed plug-in method, LIFT,
465
      CALAS achieves an additional improvement of 2.21 percentage points. This improvement is even
      more pronounced in datasets containing signals from a variety of sensors (e.g., Weather) and in
466
      long-term forecasting tasks. We hypothesize that this performance gain is caused by several factors:
467
      F1: the presence of short-term distribution shifts in channel dependency, F2: the limited diversity of
468   statistically extracted ‘cause’ signals, and F3: limitation of LIFT’s layer-specific design.
469
470   Across all datasets, we observe that CALAS tends to outperform LIFT, particularly in long-term
471
      forecasting tasks. We will showcase three cases: Weather, Electricity, and Traffic datasets. Weather
      dataset consists of signals exhibiting diverse patterns caused by various sources. Moreover, their
472
      causality can shift in the short-term period–for example, humidity and temperatures indicate no
473
      causality during certain periods but exhibit strong negative causality during the onset of the rainy
474   season. These short-term changes, even between the input and output signals, degrade LIFT’s
475   performance. This degradation experimentally proves F1: distribution shifts in channel dependency,
476   and F3: LIFT’s reliance on input to generate output channel dependencies, highlighting the need of
477   layer-agnostic usage of the causality modeling as in CALAS.
478
      Electricity and Traffic datasets are recorded at hourly intervals and have strong daily periodicity,
479
      generating multiple short-term periodic patterns. As a result, LIFT, particularly through cross-
480   correlation, tends to focus on capturing the short-term periodicities while overlooking trend changes.
481   Additionally, its top-k selection process with redundant patterns creates an information bottleneck,
482   limiting access to diverse information (F2). In contrast, CALAS learns the causal relationships in
483   an end-to-end manner, allowing the model to avoid such bottlenecks and ultimately produce richer
484   forecasting results. Furthermore, we have found that CALAS can support statistics-based model,
485   LightMTS (Zhao & Shen, 2024), by modeling input causality, improving additional 1.2 percentage
      points over CALAS, 3.41 percentage points with respect to original LightMTS, as Figure 4c.

516
517   In Figure 4c, we provide the ablation study results with our hypernetworks, reporting relative MSE
518   amplification with respect to CALAS. In this study, we compared CALAS with three variants – 1)
519   without delay estimator, 2) without strength estimator, and 3) without both estimators. We utilize
520   the one layer linear function as backbone model and the performance is reported on average over
521
      the prediction length 96, 192, 336, and 720. By ablating hypernetworks, CALAS lost its ability
      to dynamically modeling causality, as a results, degrading the forecasting quality. We can depict
522
      that loosing dynamic causal strength modeling (i.e., w/o strength estimator) induces inability to
523
      distribution shifts on CD, showing inferior performance. The delay estimator, in contrast, often shows
524   less performance drop than strength estimator, since CALAS still can dynamically estimate causality.
525   Furthermore, the ablation results of CALAS w/o hypernetworks imply the need of transition from
526   static causality modeling to dynamic causality modeling.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we emphasize the importance of delay learning in channel dependency modeling
      and propose new perspective for understanding channel dependencies in MTS forecasting. We
532
      introduce a novel end-to-end learning method, CALAS, which learns underlying lead-lag relationship
533
      in MTS while considering both causal strength and propagation delay. By integrating Gaussian
534   kernel-based 1D convolution with two hypernetworks, CALAS reformulate undirected channel
535   dependency modeling into directed, delay-aware causality modeling. We experimentally demonstrate
536   the superiority of CALAS over state-of-the-art MTS forecasting models and statistical causality
537   modeling methods. We believe that delay learning and causality-aware modeling are promising
538   research directions for future developments of MTS forecasting models. As a next step, we plan to
539   investigate the better model architecture for delay learning, including multi-periodicity decomposition
      by adjusting stride or introducing the uncertainty concepts into the CD modeling.

948   B     D ISCUSSION
949
950   Discussion on other CNN- or RNN-based methods The emergence of Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024)
951
      played a significant role in shifting researchers’ focus from Transformer models to State Space Models
      (SSMs). One main flow in the SSM research is the extension of 1D Mamba to the multidimensional
952
      state spate models. In the MTS forecasting, there are RNN-based (Tank et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2023;
953
      Behrouz et al., 2024), CNN-based (Wu et al., 2023), and SSM-based approaches (Zeng et al., 2024;
954   Hu et al., 2024). In the following paragraphs, we will discuss how they can achieve inter-variate
955   dependency modeling and difference between CALAS and them.
956
957
      RNN-based approaches, for example, WITRAN (Jia et al., 2023) or cRNN (Tank et al., 2022), need
      to propose additional channel dependency module to model the inter-variates relationships. In case of
958
      WITRAN, similar to the TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), it folds the input 1D time series into 2D time
959
      series, enabling intra- and inter-periodicity modeling. However, it less focuses on the potential causal
960   effects from the other time series. cRNN (Tank et al., 2022), one of the causal discovery algorithms,
961   induces the inter-variates relationships via weights of trained RNN (i.e., projection layer of RNNs),
962   however, it only captures the gradual changes, especially with the one-step time lag.
963
      For the CNN- and SSM-based approaches, such as C-Mamba (Zeng et al., 2024) or TimeSSM (Hu
964
      et al., 2024), the main difference between CALAS and these approaches are how they dealt with
965
      receptive fields and introduces inductive biases into model. C-Mamba and TimeSSM considers the
966   convolutional- or SSM-based state space as the range of information fusion, not introducing inductive
967   biases into. For example, in case of 1D RNi ×No ×k CNN kernel, where Ni , No , k are input and
968   output channel and kernel size, respectively, aforementioned model optimize both causal strength
969   and propagation delay into one kernel. In such design, fused with causal strength, propagation delay
970   will be independent weights to each other. However, for the inter-variate relationship, there are only
971   one unique propagation delay, which should be modeled with probability function among k possible
      delays. CALAS disentangles the propagation delay and approximate them with Gaussian probability


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       kernel, it introduces additional inductive bias–given two cause signal X and effect signal Y , there
973    exists unique discrete delay dX,Y such that the time gap between change of X and its actual influence
974    to Y .
975
976    Transformer-based methods vs. CALAS Transformer-based methods, including iTransformer,
977    mix the multivariate information regardless of propagation delay. This design may introduce mis-
978    aligned or outdated information from lagged time series, resulting to degrade of model performance.
979    To properly align the variates, previous models should borrow the modeling capacity from temporal
980    dependency modeling components, which lowers the temporal dependency modeling quality. By
981    facilitating proposed convolution, CALAS simultaneously conduct such alignment and CD modeling.
982    Though LIFT (Zhao & Shen, 2024) achieves lead-lag relation modeling with cross-correlation, it
983
       requires additional computation and relies on the statistical methods that often be suboptimal.
984
985
       Layer-agnostic causality modeling is important in MTS forecasting Distribution shifts of the
       statistical features are well-studied problem in MTS forecasting (Kim et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023;
986
       Liu et al., 2022). However, distribution shifts of channel dependency is not yet investigated deeply.
987
       Here, we derive a discussion for distribution shifts in short period, as we depicted in Figure 1.
988    By introducing shared CALAS across multiple layers except the input-dependent parts, our model
989    reduces the misalignment or over-reliance of previously generated causal maps when short term
990    distribution shifts occur.
991
992    Generalization for multi-periodicity modeling Since CALAS stems from convolution mechanism,
993    we can achieve the multi-periodicity decomposition by adjusting the stride. However, addressing this
994    question is beyond the scope of this work, so we leave it for future exploration.
