010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Lightweight, controllable, and physically plausible human motion synthesis is
013            crucial for animation, virtual reality, robotics, and human-computer interaction
014            applications. Existing methods often compromise between computational effi-
015            ciency, physical realism, or spatial controllability. We propose FlexMotion, a
016
               novel framework that leverages a computationally lightweight diffusion model
               operating in the latent space, eliminating the need for physics simulators and en-
017
               abling fast and efficient training. FlexMotion employs a multimodal pre-trained
018
               Transformer encoder-decoder, integrating joint locations, contact forces, joint ac-
019            tuations and muscle activations to ensure the physical plausibility of the generated
020            motions. FlexMotion also introduces a plug-and-play module, which adds spatial
021            controllability over a range of motion parameters (e.g., joint locations, joint actu-
022            ations, contact forces, and muscle activations). Our framework achieves realistic
023            motion generation with improved efficiency and control, setting a new benchmark
024            for human motion synthesis. We evaluate FlexMotion on extended datasets and
025            demonstrate its superior performance in terms of realism, physical plausibility,
026            and controllability.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Generating controllable and realistic human motion is a critical task with applications in various
032   domains, including animation Zhu et al. (2023), sports and rehabilitation Yang et al. (2023); Zhang
033
      et al. (2024a); Cheng et al. (2023); Tashakori et al. (2022), virtual reality Zhu et al. (2023), robotics
      Tashakori et al. (2024), and human-computer interaction Arkushin et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2022);
034
      Servati et al. (2024). Human motion involves complex interactions between joint movements, con-
035
      tact forces, and muscle activations, necessitating a comprehensive approach that can capture both
036   kinematic and dynamic aspects. Despite the remarkable progress in human motion generation, chal-
037   lenges remain in developing models that effectively balance physical realism, computational effi-
038   ciency, and fine-grained controllability.
039
      Traditional methods often fail to control the intricate biomechanics of human movement, which in-
040
      volve complex interactions between kinematics, dynamics, and environmental context Tripathi et al.
041
      (2023b); Zhang et al. (2024b); Xie et al. (2021a); Chiquier & Vondrick (2023). This deficiency is
042   particularly notable in applications such as sports and rehabilitation, where the precision of mus-
043   cle activations and contact forces is crucial for accurate simulation Chiquier & Vondrick (2023).
044   Furthermore, current methods focused on physical plausibility often demand high computational
045   resources, such as physics engines, rendering them impractical for real-time applications Yuan et al.
046   (2023); Xie et al. (2021a); Tripathi et al. (2023a).
047

051   muscle activations to ensure that the generated motions are aligned with human biomechanics. Flex-
052   Motion operates in the latent space, significantly reducing the computational cost for training and
053   inference compared to traditional human motion generation methods. Our model also introduces a
      spatial controllability module that allows for fine-grained control over spatial, muscle, joint actua-

      2.1   H UMAN M OTION G ENERATION
103
104   Motion generation literature has focused on two main approaches: first, using autoregressive mod-
105   els, which use past generated frames to generate the subsequent frames recursively Zhu et al. (2023).
106   Second, sequence-based models which generate the entire sequence at once Zhu et al. (2023); Feng
107   et al. (2024); Lou et al. (2023); Zhong et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024); Dabral
      et al. (2023). In the second approach, researchers have employed a variety of generative models to


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      achieve this, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) Zhu et al. (2023), Variational Au-
109   toencoders (VAEs) Zhu et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024), Normalizing Flows (NFs) Zhu et al. (2023),
110   and Diffusion Models (DMs) Zhu et al. (2023); Tevet et al. (2023), as well as a task-specific model
111   known as Motion Graphs (MGs) Zhu et al. (2023). Among these approaches, Diffusion Models,
112   particularly Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs), have demonstrated promising and
113   diverse results Tevet et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023).
114
      DDPMs have been particularly successful due to their ability to model complex data distributions
115   and generate high-quality samples without mode collapse, a common issue in other generative mod-
116   els like GANs Stypułkowski et al. (2024). These models have found applications beyond motion se-
117   quence generation, including image synthesis Ruiz et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024);
118   He et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023c;a); Van Le et al. (2023), video generation Zhou et al. (2024);
119   Zhang et al. (2023a); Wu et al. (2023), and other domains where generating realistic sequences is
120   essential.
121   Motion synthesis research aims to produce realistic and natural human motion patterns under various
122   conditions, leveraging the flexibility and robustness of models like DDPMs. These conditions can
123   include text Tevet et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024c; 2023b); Zhao et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023);
124   Qian et al. (2023), audio Zhu et al. (2023), action Tevet et al. (2023), music Zhu et al. (2023), images
125   Zhu et al. (2023), 3D scene Zhu et al. (2023), spatial contexts Xie et al. (2023); Karunratanakul et al.
126   (2023), objects Zhu et al. (2023), or a combination of such conditions Ling et al. (2023); Jin et al.
127   (2024). These generated sequences can consist of either key point locations, joint rotation Zhu et al.
128   (2023); Delmas et al. (2023), or mesh parameters extracted from models such as SMPL Loper et al.
129
      (2015).
130   Despite the advancements in generating realistic human motion sequences, current models often
131   perform inadequately when tasked with synthesizing complex dynamic movements that adhere to
132   biomechanical and physical laws. These models produce noticeable physical artifacts, such as unnat-
133   ural joint rotations, unrealistic muscle dynamics, and incorrect contact points during environmental
134   interactions. This deficiency arises especially while generating long motion sequences, primarily
135
      because existing approaches lack an explicit understanding of the relative mapping between muscle
      activations, joint torques, and contact forces, which are crucial for generating physically plausible
136
      motions Zhang et al. (2024c). To address these limitations, we employ a pretrained autoencoder that
137
      encodes motion properties in the latent space while preserving essential biomechanical informa-
138   tion, with a decoder that integrates physics-based constraints to ensure physically accurate motion
139   reconstruction, as detailed in the method section.

142   2.2   P HYSICS - AWARE H UMAN M OTION M ODELING
143
144
      To generate physically plausible motion sequences, researchers have employed two primary ap-
145   proaches: first, interaction with physics simulators Xie et al. (2021b); Lee et al. (2019); Yuan et al.
146   (2023), and second, the integration of physics-based constraints into the reconstruction loss function
147   Tevet et al. (2023). While physics simulators provide detailed physical interactions, they are com-
148   putationally expensive and non-differentiable Zhang et al. (2024b); Tripathi et al. (2023b), signifi-
149   cantly limiting their utility. The non-differentiability obstructs gradient backpropagation, hindering
150   the effective optimization and refinement of generated motions Zhang et al. (2024b); Tripathi et al.
151   (2023b). Alternatively, integrating physics-based constraints directly into generative models offers a
152   more computationally efficient approach to maintaining physical realism without needing full sim-
153
      ulation. In the second category, MDM integrates pose consistency, foot placement, and velocity
      loss Tevet et al. (2023), while IPMAN-R introduces stability and ground interaction loss to enhance
154
      motion realism in dynamic environments Tripathi et al. (2023b). PhysPT integrates contact points,
155
      force, and Euler–Lagrange consistency loss to accurately simulate physical interactions Zhang et al.
156   (2024b). Additionally, authors in Xie et al. (2021a) incorporate dynamic constraints, contact points,
157   penetration avoidance, and smooth transition terms to produce realistic motion estimation. How-
158   ever, despite these advancements, both approaches struggle to capture complex dynamic motions
159   thoroughly and often fail to adhere to biomechanical laws, leading to noticeable physical artifacts,
160   especially in long sequence generations Zhang et al. (2024c). Our work addresses these challenges
161   by utilizing a pretrained autoencoder architecture to generate physically plausible outputs, detailed
      in the method section.

      2.3   C ONTROLLABLE M OTION G ENERATION
163
164   Controlling motion generation algorithms is essential for creating realistic and contextually appro-
165   priate human motions, especially when these motions must adhere to specific spatial constraints or
166   trajectories. Recent advancements have been inspired by techniques from image generation, such as
167   ControlGAN Li et al. (2019), ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023c), ControlNet+ Li et al. (2024), and the
168   T2I adapter Mou et al. (2024); Cao et al. (2024), which offer nuanced control over generated out-
169   puts. OmniControl Xie et al. (2023) introduces flexible spatial control signals into a diffusion-based
170
      motion model. It allows precise control over various joints over time and improves motion realism
      Xie et al. (2023). Guided Motion Diffusion (GMD) Karunratanakul et al. (2023) enhances spatial
171
      accuracy by integrating constraints such as predefined trajectories and obstacles, using feature pro-
172
      jection and dense guidance to ensure coherence between spatial information and generated motions
173   Karunratanakul et al. (2023).
174
175   However, both OmniControl and GMD only control joint trajectories and do not account for crucial
176
      kinematic parameters such as joint actuation, ground contact forces, or muscle activation, which lim-
      its their ability to generate refined motion sequences that adhere to the physical constraints required
177
      for realistic and application-specific human movement synthesis. We propose a spatial controllabil-
178
      ity module to incorporate these critical kinematic and biomechanical factors, enabling more precise
179   motion generation, as detailed in the method section.

182   3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
183

      The objective of FlexMotion is to generate realistic motion sequences {xt }Tt=1 conditioned on a text
      prompt c and a wide range of spatial conditions {ct }Tt=1 , where T is desired sequence length. The
185
      overall architecture of FlexMotion, illustrated in Fig.2, consists of three main components. First,
186
      the Physics-aware Multimodal Autoencoder (Sec.3.1) learns to map detailed kinematic and dy-
187   namic properties of motion to a latent space that captures the essential features of human motion
188   while enforcing physical plausibility through physics-based constraints during motion sequence re-
189   construction in the decoder. Second, the FlexMotion Diffusion Model (Sec.3.2) generates desired
190   latent embedding sequences conditioned on the text prompt. Third, the Spatial Controllability
191   Module (Sec.3.3) provides fine-grained spatial control over critical motion parameters, enabling
192   precise and contextually appropriate motion generation. In the following sections, we discuss each
193   component in detail. Further details and pseudocode for both the training and inference processes
194   are provided in the appendix.
195

      AMASS and HumanAct12, contains 14,616 motion sequences with 44,970 textual annotations.
353
      KIT-ML provides 3,911 motion sequences with 6,278 descriptions, while Flag3D offers 180,000
354   videos spanning 60 fitness activities.
355
356   Data Augmentation. To support physics-aware motion modeling, we augmented these datasets
357
      using OpenSim Delp et al. (2007); Seth et al. (2018), a popular and widely acceptable software for
      biomechanics research and motor control science Delp et al. (2007); Seth et al. (2018), incorporating
358
      detailed muscle activations, contact forces, joint positions, rotations, actuation, and velocity. We
359
      employed a full-body OpenSim model Van Horn & Team (2016) with 21 body segments, 29 degrees
360   of freedom, and 324 musculotendon actuators, providing rich detail for lumbar movements and trunk
361   muscle dynamics. This augmentation enhances the biomechanical fidelity of the motion data, which
362   is critical for realistic motion synthesis. We provide more details in the appendix.
363
      Evaluation Metrics. FlexMotion’s performance is comprehensively evaluated across several key
364
      metrics, encompassing naturalness, textual relevance, diversity, physical plausibility, and spatial
365
      control accuracy. Naturalness is quantified using the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) Tevet et al.
366   (2023), which compares the distribution of generated motions to real data. Textual relevance is
367   measured via R-Precision Tevet et al. (2023), assessing how well the generated motions align with
368   textual descriptions. To ensure variability, diversity (DIV) is evaluated by computing the pairwise
369   distance between generated motions Tevet et al. (2023). Physical plausibility is verified through
370   metrics like Foot Skating, Penetration, Contact Force Accuracy, and Joint Actuation Consis-
371   tency Xie et al. (2023). Biomechanical plausibility is ensured by checking that Muscle Activation
372   Limits stay within realistic physiological constraints Lee et al. (2019). Finally, spatial control ac-
373   curacy is assessed using the Trajectory Error metric Xie et al. (2023), focusing on how well the
374
      generated motions adhere to intended spatial trajectories. All results are reported as mean across
      ten independent runs, ensuring robustness and reproducibility. More details can be found in the
375
      appendix.

514   5     C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we presented FlexMotion, a novel framework for efficiently generating controllable
517   and physically plausible human motion. By utilizing a diffusion model in the latent space and a
518
      physics-aware Transformer-based autoencoder, FlexMotion achieves computational efficiency while
      ensuring realism. The model captures key biomechanical aspects such as joint locations, contact
519
      forces, and muscle activations without relying on physics simulators, making it suitable for real-time
520
      applications. FlexMotion also introduces a spatial controllability module that enables fine-grained
521   control over motion parameters, such as trajectories and muscle activations, enhancing its versatil-
522   ity for various tasks. Our experiments on HumanML3D, KIT-ML, and Flag3D datasets show that
523   FlexMotion outperforms state-of-the-art models in realism, physical plausibility, and computational
524   efficiency. The framework achieves higher R-Precision and lower FID scores, indicating better
525   alignment with textual descriptions and realistic motions. Additionally, it demonstrates lower foot
526   skating, penetration, and muscle activation errors, making it more physically consistent and feasible.
527   FlexMotion’s reduced computational complexity further allows for faster inference, positioning it as
528   a promising practical solution for animation, robotics, and virtual reality. Future work could explore
529
      more complex dynamics and real-time applications. While FlexMotion leverages physics-informed
      modeling, a sim-to-real gap persists due to differences between simulated dynamics and real-world
530
      variability. Future work will address this gap by integrating real-world data and improving alignment
531
      with experimental benchmarks to enhance its applicability in diverse real-world scenarios.

850   A.3    A DDITIONAL I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
851
852   Hyperparameter settings. In our experiments, the weighting factors for the loss terms in the
853   physics-aware Transformer encoder-decoder were set as follows: αpos = 1.0, αrot = 1.0, αvel = 0.1,
854
      αacc = 0.1, αtorque = 0.5, αforce = 0.5, βeuler = 1.0, and γmuscle = 1.0. These values were chosen to
      balance the importance of accurately reconstructing each modality while enforcing physical plausi-
855
      bility. For the diffusion model, we used a linear variance schedule for the noise parameters βt , with
856
      T = 1000 diffusion steps during training. During inference, we employed the DDIM sampler Song
857   et al. (2023) with 100 steps for efficient sampling without significant loss in motion quality.
858
859   Dataset preprocessing. All motion sequences were downsampled to 20 frames per second to reduce
860
      computational complexity while retaining essential motion characteristics. The joint positions and
      rotations were normalized based on the mean and standard deviation computed over the training set
861
      to facilitate stable neural network training.
862
863   Datasets. We evaluate the proposed FlexMotion on several extended datasets, including Hu-
      manML3D Guo et al. (2022a), KIT-ML Plappert et al. (2016), and Flag3D Tang et al. (2023), each


                                                               16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      augmented with muscle activation, contact force, and joint actuation data. The HumanML3D dataset
865   is a textually re-annotated collection derived from the AMASS and HumanAct12 datasets. It con-
866   tains 14,616 motion sequences annotated with 44,970 textual descriptions, averaging approximately
867   three descriptions per motion. The KIT-ML dataset includes 3,911 motion sequences annotated
868   with 6,278 textual descriptions, averaging around two descriptions per motion. Flag3D dataset is an
869   extensive collection comprising 180,000 videos of 60 daily fitness activities.
870
      Dataset augmentation. To validate FlexMotion, we augmented existing datasets by incorporating
871   additional modalities for our model, including muscle activations, contact forces, and joint actuation
872   data. This augmentation was achieved using OpenSim Delp et al. (2007); Seth et al. (2018), a biome-
873   chanical modeling simulator that enables detailed musculoskeletal analysis. Specifically, we utilized
874   a comprehensive whole-body OpenSim model Van Horn & Team (2016) featuring 21 segments, 29
875   degrees of freedom, and 324 musculotendon actuators. This model includes a detailed representa-
876   tion of the lumbar spine, with each of the five lumbar vertebrae connected by a 6-degree-of-freedom
877   joint, allowing for the simulation of complex lumbar movements such as flexion-extension, axial ro-
878   tation, and lateral bending. Additionally, the model incorporates eight key muscle groups, including
879
      the rectus abdominis and erector spinae, which facilitate multi-directional trunk muscle action.
880   We utilized OpenSim’s robust biomechanics simulation platform to synthesize physics-informed
881   motion data that aligns with real-world biomechanical principles. Here is a step-by-step breakdown
882   of the process:
883   We started with a full-body OpenSim model based on Van Horn & Team (2016), which includes
884   21 body segments, 29 degrees of freedom (DOF), and 324 musculotendon actuators. This model
885   captures detailed joint kinematics and dynamics, including lumbar spine motion and trunk muscle
886   activations, making it ideal for biomechanically informed motion modeling.
887
      The base datasets provided joint angles, positions, and basic kinematics from motion capture sys-
888
      tems. We imported these data into OpenSim to initialize the simulation. Using OpenSim’s Inverse
889   Kinematics (IK) tool, we ensured the input motion conformed to the skeletal model’s constraints.
890
891   To enrich the data with physiological realism, we used OpenSim’s Computed Muscle Control
892
      (CMC) and Static Optimization tools. These tools generated muscle activation patterns and corre-
      sponding forces required to produce the observed motion. Specifically, CMC estimates the muscle
893
      excitation signals to track the observed motion, while Static Optimization resolves muscle forces by
894
      minimizing an objective function such as energy expenditure or effort.
895
896   Beyond muscle activations, we extracted:
897
898
             • Joint Contact Forces: Calculated from dynamic simulations, providing insights into load
               distribution at joints during motion.
899
900          • Joint Torques and Velocities: Derived from the musculoskeletal model for each DOF.
901          • Muscle Forces and Lengths: Detailing musculotendon dynamics during movement.
902
             • Ground Reaction Forces: Synthesized from a combination of kinematics and muscle acti-
903
               vations, reflecting interactions with the environment.
904
905   Given the focus on realistic lumbar motion and trunk muscle dynamics, we paid special attention
906   to the spine’s multi-segmental nature in the model. We tracked lumbar vertebra rotations, stiffness,
907   and associated muscle activations to capture complex trunk movements.
908
      To diversify the dataset, we introduced perturbations to initial conditions, such as joint angles, force
909
      profiles, and external loads. This randomized approach helps simulate variations in human motion
910
      due to individual differences or environmental changes. These perturbations were carefully con-
911   strained to remain within physiologically plausible ranges.
912
913   The augmented data was validated through consistency checks. We compared synthesized motion
914
      profiles with experimentally observed patterns from biomechanics literature to ensure biomechanical
      fidelity and realistic variability.
915
916   Evaluation metrics. To assess FlexMotion’s performance, we employ a comprehensive set of es-
917   tablished metrics that evaluate various aspects of the generated motion, including naturalness, rele-
      vance, diversity, physical plausibility, and spatial control accuracy. The Fréchet Inception Distance


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      (FID) is used to evaluate the naturalness of the generated motions by measuring the distance between
919   the feature distributions of the generated motions and those of actual motion data, thus indicating
920   how realistic the generated motions appear.
921
922
      To assess the relevance of the generated motions to their corresponding textual prompts, we use
      the R-Precision metric, which measures the degree to which the generated motions align with the
923
      intended textual descriptions, with higher precision indicating better correspondence between the
924
      generated motion and the specified action.
925
926   The Diversity metric evaluates the variability within the generated motion set, ensuring that Flex-
927   Motion produces a wide range of distinct motions. This metric is typically computed as the average
928
      pairwise distance between the generated motions in the feature space, with higher diversity values
      indicating a more versatile model output.
929
930   For evaluating physical plausibility, we consider several factors: 1) Foot Skating, which measures
931   the extent of unnatural sliding or "skating" of the feet during motion, indicating a lack of physical
932   realism; Penetration, which assesses whether any body parts unnaturally intersect or penetrate each
933   other or the environment, violating physical plausibility; 2) Contact Force Accuracy, which evaluates
934
      the correctness of the contact forces generated during motion, ensuring they correspond to realistic
      physical interactions with the environment; and 3) Joint Actuation Consistency, which ensures that
935
      the generated joint actuations remain within plausible ranges of motion and force, aligning with
936
      real-world biomechanics.
937
938   For biomechanical plausibility, we use the Muscle Activation Limits metric. This metric ensures that
939   the generated motions respect physiological constraints by verifying that muscle activations remain
940
      within feasible ranges, thus preventing unrealistic overextension or underuse of muscles.
941   To assess spatial control accuracy, we employ the Trajectory Error metric, defined as the ratio of
942   unsuccessful trajectories—those where any keyframe location error exceeds a predefined threshold.
943   This metric ensures that the generated motions accurately follow the intended spatial trajectories,
944   which is critical for applications requiring precise motion paths.
945   All evaluations were conducted over ten independent runs to ensure the reliability and robustness
946   of our results. The reported values for each metric are presented in the format of mean ± standard
947   deviation, where the mean represents the average performance across the ten runs, and the standard
948   deviation reflects the variability in the performance, thereby providing a measure of consistency and
949   reproducibility in the evaluation process.

       A.9      T RADE - OFFS B ETWEEN R EALISM AND P HYSICAL ACCURACY

1181   To analyze the trade-offs, we conducted experiments where we varied the weights of the physical
1182   constraints in our loss function. Specifically, we adjusted the parameters λeuler and λmuscle , which
1183   control the influence of the Euler angle regularization and muscle activation limits, respectively. By
1184   observing the impact of these adjustments on both realism and physical accuracy metrics, we can
1185   provide valuable insights into how these aspects interact.
1186   We present the results in Table 1, which compares our model’s performance under different settings
1187
       of λeuler and λmuscle on the HumanML3D dataset.
       From the results, we observe that decreasing the weights of the physical constraints (e.g., λeuler =
1188
       0.0, λmuscle = 0.0) leads to improved realism metrics, such as higher R-Precision and lower FID,
1189
       indicating that the generated motions are more perceptually similar to real data. However, this
1190   comes at the cost of physical accuracy, as evidenced by higher values in metrics like Skate, Float,
1191   and Penetrate.
1192   Conversely, increasing the weights of the physical constraints (e.g., λeuler = 2.0, λmuscle = 2.0)
1193   enhances physical accuracy, with lower values in physical metrics, but slightly degrades realism
1194   metrics.
1195   This trade-off suggests that there is a balance to be struck depending on the application requirements.
1196   For scenarios where physical accuracy is paramount, higher weights on physical constraints are
1197   advisable. In contrast, applications prioritizing perceptual realism might benefit from lower weights
