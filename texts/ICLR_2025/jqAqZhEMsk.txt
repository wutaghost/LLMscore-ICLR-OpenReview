009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In-Context Learning (ICL) emerges as a key feature for Large Language Models
012            (LLMs), allowing them to adapt to new tasks by leveraging task-specific exam-
013            ples without updating model parameters. However, ICL faces challenges with
014            increasing numbers of examples due to performance degradation and quadratic
015            computational costs. In this paper, we propose Logit Arithmetic Reweighting
016            Approach (LARA), a novel framework that enhances ICL by using logit-based
017            ensembling of multiple demonstrations. Our approach divides long input demon-
018
               strations into parallelizable shorter inputs to significantly reduce memory require-
               ments, and then effectively aggregate the information by reweighting logits of
019
               each group via a non-gradient optimization approach. We further introduce Bi-
020
               nary LARA (B-LARA), a variant that constrains weights to binary values to sim-
021            plify the search space and reduces memory usage by filtering out less informative
022            demonstration groups. Experiments on BBH and MMLU demonstrate that LARA
023            and B-LARA outperform all baseline methods in both accuracy and memory effi-
024            ciency. We also conduct extensive analysis to show that LARA generalizes well to
025            scenarios of varying numbers of examples from limited to many-shot demonstra-
026            tions. Our codes can be found in https://anonymous.4open.science/
027            r/LARA-F55B.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In-Context Learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) is one of the emergent abilities of Large Language
033
      Models (LLMs) as they are scaled to billions of parameters (Wei et al., 2022). ICL enables LLMs to
      adapt to new tasks by utilizing task-specific examples within the input context (Dong et al., 2023),
034
      and does not require any updates to or access to model parameters. While ICL has achieved impres-
035
      sive performance across various domains, it encounters significant challenges when dealing with an
036   increasing number of examples. Longer context window size often leads to performance degrada-
037   tion (Xiong et al., 2023). This is due to the low density of useful information within longer prompts,
038   and the reduced sensitivity to positional information, both of which diminish the capability of the
039   model to effectively capture and utilize key content. Additionally, the quadratic growth of computa-
040   tional cost with the input length makes it particularly expensive for large-scale models.
041
      Previous works primarily focus on two directions to address these challenges. The first direction is
042   input compression, which aims to shorten the input length (Jiang et al., 2023b; Pan et al., 2024; Xu
043   et al., 2023a; Wingate et al., 2022) or selectively retrieve relevant portions of demonstrations to be
044   included in the prompt (an Luo et al., 2024). However, these methods risk losing critical information,
045   which may negatively impact model performance. The second direction involves aggregating hidden
046   states within LLMs to simulate the effect of in-context demonstrations (Hao et al., 2022; Li et al.,
047   2023b; Hendel et al., 2023). These methods, however, are not applicable to closed-source models
048   like GPT-4, as they require direct access to the model internal weights. Additionally, they contradict
049   the core advantage of in-context learning, which is the ability to operate without modifications to
050
      hidden states or model parameters.
051   In this study, we propose a novel framework, Logit Arithmetic Reweighting Approach (LARA),
052   which aims to combine the strengths of both input compression and hidden state approaches. Our
053   method first divides demonstrations into subgroups to allow LLMs to focus on shorter inputs and re-
      duce computational requirements. We then design a weighted sum aggregation approach to combine

156   3    M ETHODOLOGY
157
158   In this section, we provide an overview of LARA. Figure 2 illustrates the overall framework of our
159   approach. Unlike directly concatenating Dtrain into a single sequence, we first divide the N examples
160   into subgroups, which are used as inputs to the LLM. The output logits from these subgroups are
161   then aggregated, and we assign weights to each subgroup using a non-gradient search algorithm.
      During inference, the precomputed weights are used to combine the logits from each group.

163   how the outputs are aggregated across different subgroups in Sec. 3.2, and the reweighting strategy
164   for optimal combination in Sec. 3.3. Furthermore, we show in Sec. 3.4 that imposing a hard con-
165   straint for our reweighting strategy could further reduce memory usage and computational resources.
166   Finally, we discuss in Sec. 3.5 the inference efficiency brought by our proposed approach.

      Given N -shot in-context examples, we first split Dtrain into k disjoint subsets each containing L in-
172
      context examples, such that Dtrain = S1 ∪ S2 ∪ . . . ∪ Sk with |Si | = L for all i ∈ {1, . . . , k}. When
173   inputting a subgroup Si to an LLM, we concatenate all of its elements to get Ci = d(i−1)L+1 ⊕
174   d(i−1)L+2 ⊕ · · · ⊕ diL , and the complete input for the i-th subgroup to LLM is Ci ⊕ xtest . We assume
175   that N is divisible by k in our experiments, so that L = N/k. In practice, in cases where N is not
176   divisible by k, we could truncate the last subset and only retain L(k − 1) examples.

      3.2   L OGIT-A RITHMETIC D ECODING
179
180   Previous studies (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Dekoninck et al., 2023) have utilized logit offsets to
181   control the outputs of large language models for better generation quality or instruction following.
182   Inspired by these work, we propose a novel method that combines information from multiple in-
183   context demonstrations through logit-arithmetic decoding. Specifically, our approach focuses on
184   aggregating the logits produced by the language model outputs for various contextual inputs. With
185   the input query xtest and the example subset being Si , we can compute the logit outputs of the
186   language model, denoted as fθ (Si , xtest ) = log p(y | Si , xtest ). We then combine these logits using
187
      a weighted sum to get the generation probability over the output token:

216
      We choose non-gradient optimization methods over gradient-based alternatives due to two key fac-
217   tors: (1) The loss function L(w) is non-differentiable, since updating the weight vector w affects
218   the logits of subsequent tokens, leading to possibly different decoding results of subsequent tokens.
219   (2) The dimensionality of the weight vector w is relatively low, specifically equalled to the number
220   of groups k.
221
      In our empirical experiments, we refer to Liu et al. (2020) and employ the Covariance Matrix
222
      Adaptive Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) (Hansen & Ostermeier, 1996). CMA-ES is a stochastic,
223   derivative-free optimization algorithm. During each iteration, CMA-ES samples a set of candidates
224   in the space of the weight vector w from a multivariate normal distribution, evaluates L(w) for
225   each candidate, and then updates the mean and covariance matrix of the distribution based on the
226   best-performing candidates. This allows for an efficient exploration over the weight space.
227
228   3.4   B INARY C ONSTRAINTS FOR LARA
229
230   We further propose a variant of LARA, named as B-LARA, by imposing a hard constraint on the
231   weight vector w to binary values {0, 1}. This binary constraint offers two key advantages: first, it
232
      simplifies the search space and potentially leads to faster convergence; second, it allows for direct
      elimination of demonstration groups with zero weight, thereby improving inference efficiency. In-
233
      tuitively, the binary optimization of w can be seen as a form of subset selection to identify the most
234
      relevant demonstrations in Dtrain benefitting model performance on specific tasks.
235
236   To solve this binary optimization problem, we employ the simplest evolution strategy (1+1)-
237   ES (Rechenberg, 1973). It involves a simple cycle: a single parent produces one offspring per
238
      generation through mutation—adding a small, random change. If this offspring performs as well
      or better than the parent based on a predefined fitness criterion, it becomes the new parent for the
239
      next generation. Otherwise, the original parent remains. The overall sampling procedure is shown
240
      in Algorithm 1.
241
242   The simplicity of this method in repeated mutation and selection makes it particularly suitable for
243   our binary optimization scenario.

245   3.5   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
246
247
248   We analyze the computational complexity of LARA and B-LARA compared to standard ICL. Dur-
249   ing inference, the self-attention mechanism in Transformer models is the primary bottleneck for
250   GPU memory requirement, with the memory complexity being O(n2 ), where n is the input se-
251
      quence length. This quadratic scaling is due to the pairwise interactions between tokens in the
      attention matrix.
252
253   By splitting the input sequence into k groups, each of length around nk , LARA and B-LARA
254   can leverage parallel computing resources more effectively. The complexity for LARA becomes
                         2
255   O( nk 2 ∗ k)= O( nk ). B-LARA further reduces computational complexity by selecting only a subset
256   of groups. If m out of k subgroups are assigned non-zero weights, then the complexity of B-LARA
                      2
257   becomes O( mn k2 ). We show the empirical GPU memory usage in Sec. 5.5.

288   examples for our methods. For each task, Dtrain is split into subsets of size L ∈ {2, 4, 8}, and for

290   validation loss across different settings of L to determine the optimal configuration, including L and
291   corresponding w, for the final inference phase. The baseline methods also use the same Dtrain as
292   input. For our method and all baselines, we set the temperature to 0 to enforce greedy decoding.
293
      Our experiments are conducted on a single A100 80GB GPU.
294
      Compared Methods. We introduce several primary baseline methods: Direct In-Context Learn-

297   StructICL (Hao et al., 2022) as the representative of parameter access methods. We use the same 32
298   in-context examples as inputs to all baseline methods as our proposed method. For Direct ICL, all
299   32 examples are concatenated with the prompt. For KATE, we apply the Top-K selection from Liu

306
      usage as our method.

490   6     R ELATED W ORK
491
492   6.1   L ONG I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
493
494   Recent studies on long-context learning problems in LLMs can be categorized into two main strate-
495   gies: enhancing the impact of in-context examples and compressing input sequences. Structured
496
      prompting leverages rescaled attention mechanisms to effectively integrate grouped examples (Hao
      et al., 2022). Methods such as task vectors (Hendel et al., 2023) and function vectors (Todd et al.,
497
      2023) further refine this strategy by generating vectors that assess the contribution of each example
498
      based on the offset of hidden state, which improves model adaptability. Liu et al. (2023) generate
499   task-specific vectors that steer model behavior in latent space based on the in-context examples.
500   Regarding input compression, methods like prompt pruning (Jiang et al., 2023b; Pan et al., 2024)
501   and additional summarization models (Xu et al., 2023a; Gilbert et al., 2023) directly shorten in-
502   puts while maintaining essential content. Soft prompt-based compression (Wingate et al., 2022; Mu
503   et al., 2023) intends to generate a soft-prompt that includes most of the information. For many-shot
504   in-context learning problems, previous studies have proposed group-based methods, such as Struc-
505   tICL (Hao et al., 2022) and FocusICL (Yuan et al., 2024), which refine attention maps by utilizing
506   subgroup structures within the demonstrations.
507
508   6.2   L OGIT A RITHMETIC
509
510   Several works have employed logit arithmetic across various domains and downstream tasks. Con-
511   trastive decoding (Li et al., 2022) improves performance by utilizing the difference in logits from
512   models of different sizes. Proxy tuning (Liu et al., 2024) enhances a larger model’s capabilities by
513
      adding the logit differences of a smaller model, recorded before and after training, to simulate train-
      ing effects. In model arithmetic (Dekoninck et al., 2023), logits adjusted with various prompts steer
514
      the generation processes of large language models. Huang et al. (2024) propose using logit subtrac-
515
      tion to facilitate the selective forgetting of knowledge in LLMs. Additionally, logit arithmetic has
516   been leveraged to enhance the safety of generated outputs (Xu et al., 2024).
517
518
      6.3   N ON - GRADIENT O PTIMIZATION OF LLM S
519
520
      Due to the high memory requirements associated with gradient-based optimization methods, recent
521   research has shifted towards non-gradient techniques for neural network optimization. Zhang et al.
522   (2024); Malladi et al. (2023) propose training large language models (LLMs) using non-gradient
523   methods to mitigate these memory constraints. These approaches have also been applied in federated
524   learning, exploring their effectiveness in distributed settings (Xu et al., 2023b). Additionally, a
525   gradient-free method has been used to optimize manifold neural networks (Zhang et al., 2022).
526   Similarly, LoraHub (Huang et al., 2023) utilizes non-gradient techniques to dynamically reweight
527   different LoRA modules, enhancing adaptation to new downstream tasks. Guo et al. (2023) also
528   introduces non-gradient methods to prompt engineering to search for better prompts.
529
530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We proposed LARA, a novel framework that enhances in-context learning by ensembling logits
533
      from multiple demonstrations, improving performance without requiring parameter updates. Our
534   method reduces computational complexity while achieving better accuracy. Additionally, Binary
535   LARA further optimizes efficiency by selectively removing less informative demonstrations. Ex-
536   periments on BBH and MMLU benchmarks show that both LARA and B-LARA outperform tra-
537   ditional ICL methods in terms of efficiency and performance. Future research directions include
538   extending our study to combine logits from different sources beyond just in-context learning (ICL)
539   examples—such as different models or varying instructions—and building a distributed inference
      system based on LARA.
