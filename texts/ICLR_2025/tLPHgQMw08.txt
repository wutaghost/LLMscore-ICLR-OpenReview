009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Language models are prone to occasionally undesirable generations, such as harm-
012            ful or toxic content, despite their impressive capability to produce texts that ap-
013            pear accurate and coherent. In this paper, we present a new two-stage approach
014            to detect and mitigate undesirable content generations by rectifying activations.
015            First, we train an ensemble of layer-wise classifiers to detect undesirable con-
016
               tent using activations by minimizing a smooth surrogate of the risk-aware score.
               Then, for contents that are detected as undesirable, we propose layer-wise dis-
017
               tributional intervention policies that perturb the attention heads minimally while
018
               guaranteeing probabilistically the effectiveness of the intervention. Benchmarks
019            on several language models and datasets show that our method outperforms base-
020            lines in reducing the generation of undesirable output. Our code is available at
021            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/OT-Intervention-52E7

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Language models (LMs) have demonstrated remarkability in understanding and generating human-
027
      like documents (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023;
      Dubey et al., 2024). However, inspecting their outputs can often reveal undesirable content, such as
028
      inaccurate or toxic generated texts (Ji et al., 2023; Rawte et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Meanwhile,
029
      devising good strategies to control the LMs’ generation process remains challenging (Tonmoy et al.,
030   2024).
031
032   Numerous methods have been proposed for controllable text generation in language models; see,
033
      for example, Zhang et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2024a). These approaches include model editing and
      supervised fine-tuning. However, both approaches require altering model weights using a subset of
034
      text samples, which can result in unstable representations for other text instances (Hase et al., 2024).
035
      In addition, these methods typically require substantial computational resources.
036
037   To resolve these issues, one possible alternative for controllable text generation is activation inter-
038   vention (Subramani et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b), where one alters the model
039
      activations responsible for the undesirable output during inference. Previous work highlighted the
      presence of interpretable directions within the activation space of language models. These direc-
040
      tions have been shown to play a causal role during inference. For instance, Burns et al. (2022) and
041
      Moschella et al. (2023) suggest that these directions could be manipulated to adjust model behavior
042   in a controlled manner. This line of work indicates that the internal representations of language mod-
043   els are structured in ways that can be leveraged for fine-grained control over generated text. Taking
044   inspiration from these previous works, activation intervention frameworks argued that the informa-
045   tion needed to steer the model to generate a target sentence is already encoded within the model. The
046   hidden information is extracted in the form of latent vectors, which are then used to guide the gen-
047   eration to have desirable effects. The preliminary success of these activation intervention methods
048   motivates our approach to improve the desirable generation of LMs.
049   Problem Statement. We consider a language model consisting of L layers, each layer has H head,
050   each head has dimension d. For example, for the Llama-2, we have L = 32, H = 32 and d = 128.
051   The training dataset is denoted by D = (xi , yi∗ )i=1,...,N , the i-th text is denoted by xi , and its ground
052   truth label is yi∗ ∈ {0, 1}, where the label 1 (positive) represents the undesirable text, and the label 0
053   (negative) represents the desirable text. Our goal is two-fold: (i) detect an undesirable text, and (ii)
      modify an undesirable text into a desirable text.

066   Contributions. We contribute a novel activation intervention method to detect and rectify undesir-
067   able generation of LMs. We call our method RADIANT (Risk-Awares Distributional Intervention
068   Policies for Language Models’ Activations). Overall, RADIANT comprises two components:
069
070   1. A layerwise probe: at each layer, we train a classifier to detect undesirable content from the
071      layer’s activations. We train a risk-aware logistic classifier for each head that balances the false
072      positive and false negative rate, and then aggregate these headwise classifiers’ predictions us-
073
         ing a voting mechanism to form a layerwise classifier. We then identify one layer where the
         probe delivers the most reasonable predictive performance. This optimal classifier serves as the
074
         detector of undesirable content.
075
076   2. A collection of headwise interventions: given the optimal layer for the layerwise probe found
077      previously, we find for each head in that layer an optimal headwise intervention policy. We
078
         choose a simple linear map for this intervention policy that minimizes the magnitude of editing
         while delivering sufficient distributional guarantees that the undesirable-predicted activations
079
         will be edited into desirable-predicted activations. We show that this linear map can be computed
080
         efficiently using semidefinite programming.

      1.1   R ELATED WORKS
083
084   Controllable generation. Controllable text generation methods aim to alter the outputs of large
085   language models in a desired way. One possible approach is model editing (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang
086   et al., 2024), which involves modifying a model’s parameters to steer its outputs. For example, Meng
087   et al. (2022) involves identifying specific middle-layer feed-forward modules that correspond to
088   factual knowledge and then altering these weights to correct or update the information encoded by
089   the model. Other notable methods include fine-tuning techniques such as Supervised Fine-Tuning
090   (SFT, Peng et al. 2023; Gunel et al. 2020) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
091   (RLHF, Ouyang et al. 2022; Griffith et al. 2013).
092   Probing. Probing is a well-established framework for assessing the interpretability of neural net-
093   works (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Belinkov, 2022). Probing techniques have been applied to understand
094   the internal representations of transformer architectures in language models, such as BERT and GPT.
095   For instance, Burns et al. (2022) proposed an unsupervised probing method that optimizes the con-
096   sistency between the positive and negative samples. Marks & Tegmark (2023) computes the mean
097   difference between true and false statements and skews the decision boundary by the inverse of the
098
      covariance matrix of the activations.
099   Activation interventions. Activation intervention at inference time is an emerging technique for
100   controllable generation (Turner et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Singh et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024).
101   Unlike model editing or fine-tuning techniques, the inference-time intervention does not require
102   altering the model parameters. Li et al. (2024b) proposed a headwise intervention method for elic-
103   iting truthful generated answers of a language model. They first train linear probes on each head
104
      of the language model, then shift the activations with the probe weight direction or mean difference
      direction.
105
106   There is a clear distinction between our method and ITI in choosing the location of the classifiers
107   and, hence, the location of the interventions. The ITI method builds different headwise classifiers
      scattered at different layers, and it may suffer from distribution shifts: if an activation is intervened,


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      this leads to shifts in the activation values at all subsequent layers in the network. Thus, the classifiers
109   trained at subsequent layers may degrade performance, and the interventions at subsequent layers
110   may also degrade. On the contrary, we build a layerwise classifier focusing on all heads in the same
111   layer and does not suffer from the distributional shifts of the activations.
112
      Closely related to our work is the recent paper by Singh et al. (2024). The authors propose a heuristic
113
      intervention rule; then, using empirical estimations of the means and covariances of activations
114
      data’s distributions of desirable and undesirable text, they calculate a closed-form optimal transport
115   plan between these two empirical distributions, assuming they are standard normal. However, this
116   framework does not take into account the semantics of sentences. Another recent method, called
117   LoFit (Localized Fine-Tuning on LLM Representations, Yin et al. 2024), also identifies a specific
118   subset of attention heads that are crucial for learning a particular task but then performs fine-tuning
119   on the intervention vectors at those chosen heads to enhance the model’s hidden representations.
120   This results in an additional training overhead.

299   4     E XPERIMENTS
300
301   In this section, we present empirical evidences for the effectiveness of our method RADIANT . We
302   evaluate RADIANT on the TruthfulQA benchmark (Lin et al., 2021), consisting of two tasks: the
303   main task is the generation, and the secondary task is multiple choice. The generation task requires
304   the model to generate an entire answer for each question using greedy autoregressive decoding. The
305   accuracy and helpfulness of the answer are best assessed by humans. However, in almost all recent
306   works in the field, including Li et al. (2024b) and Yin et al. (2024), this criterion is measured by an
307
      alternative large language model finetuned on the target dataset. The multiple-choice task contains
      candidate answers to each question, requiring the model to give probabilities for each. Higher
308
      probabilities for truthful answers yield higher scores.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
311
312   Datasets. We evaluate and compare our method with other baselines using the TruthfulQA bench-
313   mark (Lin et al., 2021). The TruthfulQA dataset is a Question-Answer dataset containing 817 ques-
314   tions that likely elicit false answers from humans due to common misconceptions. We follow the
315   same data-processing used in Li et al. (2024b) and Yin et al. (2024) that splits the dataset into
316   train/validation/test with the rate of 326/82/407 questions and utilize two-fold cross-validation.
317   Each question has an average length of nine words and has two sets of desirable and undesirable
318   answers. Following Li et al. (2024b), we separate the original dataset into 5918 question-answer
319   pairs; each has a binary label, indicating desirability. Only pairs associated with questions in the
320
      training dataset are used to create our intervention policy, while those in the validation test are set
      aside for parameter tuning.
321
322   In addition, we also show the generalization of our method by conducting a transferability experi-
323   ment on two other out-of-distribution datasets, including NQOpen (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019a) and
      TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017). Due to space constraints, the results are relegated to Appendix A.2.

333   Baselines. We include baselines relevant to increasing truthfulness, listed as follows.
334
       • Inference-time Intervention (ITI, Li et al. 2024b), the state-of-the-art method for finetuning-free
335
         intervention. The hyperparameters of the baseline follow their original paper Li et al. (2024b)
336      and their GitHub repository.1
337
       • Few-shot prompting (FSP) introduced in Bai et al. (2022) showcases the effectiveness of 50-shot
338
         prompting in benchmark TruthfulQA.
339
340    • Instruction Fine-Tuning (IFT) (Wang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024) is a popular fine-tuning
341      approach to boost the truthfulness of language models. Two notable pretrained models in this di-
342
         rection, namely Alpaca-7B (Taori et al., 2023) and Vicuna-7B (Chiang et al., 2023), are adopted
         for comparison.
343
344    • Representation Intervention Fine-tuning (RIFT) methods aim to adjust language model activa-
345      tions for improved truthfulness. However, they add extra parameters and require extensive com-
346
         putational resources for fine-tuning. We consider LOFiT (Yin et al., 2024) for comparison.
347    • Non-Linear Inference Time Intervention (NL-ITI) (Hoscilowicz et al., 2024) extends ITI by in-
348      troducing a non-linear multi-token probing and multi-token intervention method.
349    • Learnable Intervention for Truthfulness Optimization (LITO) (Bayat et al., 2024) explores a
350      sequence of model generations based on increasing levels of intervention magnitude then selects
351      the most accurate response.
352
353
      Metrics. Following the standard benchmark in TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024b), we
      compare our method to baselines using the metrics described below.
354
355
       • Two metrics for the multiple-choice task introduced in Lin et al. (2021), namely MC1 and MC2.
356      Given a question and some choices, select the only correct answer. The selection of the model
357      is the answer choice to which it assigns the highest log probability of completion following the
358      question, independent of the other answer choices. The accuracy across all questions is denoted
359      as MC1. Similarly, given a question and multiple true/false reference answers, the MC2 is the
360      normalized total probability assigned to the set of true answers.
361    • For the generation task, we use two fine-tuned GPT-3.5-instruct models to classify
362      whether an answer is true or false and informative or not. We report two metrics from Li et al.
363      (2024b): truthful score True (%) and True*Info (%), a product of scalar truthful and informative
364      score. We note that there are discrepancies between the results of ITI reproduced in our work
365      and the original results reported in Li et al. (2024b), as the original paper used GPT-3 based
366      models to score these two metrics; however, at the time this paper is written, GPT-3 is no longer
367      available on the OpenAI platform.
368    • We report two additional metrics, Kullback-Leiber divergence (KL) of the model’s next-token
369      prediction distribution post-versus-pre-intervention, and Cross-Entropy Loss (CE). These two
370      metrics measure how much the generation distribution shifts after the intervention. Lower values
371      are preferred since the intervention does not change the behavior of the original model dramat-
372
         ically and is unlikely to cause abnormal characters or non-natural sentences. The calculation of
         these metrics is elaborated in Li et al. (2024b).

      A.1   A NALYSIS : T HE EFFECT OF Γ AND α ON THE PERFORMANCE OF RADIANT
705
706   The hyperparameter α controls the conservativeness of the classifier in terms of the False Negative
707   Rate. High values of α ensure that no undesirable content goes undetected. However, excessively
708   large values of α may lead to trivial classifiers that classify all samples as undesirable. Such classi-
709   fiers can be identified by checking if their False Positive Rate on the validation set is one. Therefore,
710   for a given α, alongside other performance metrics, we report the average False Positive Rate and
711   the average False Negative Rate across all trained classifiers on the validation set denoted as FPR
712
      and FNR.
713   In Table 6, we present metrics on the validation set while varying α within the set
714   {1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0}. We use the base model Llama-7B. RADIANT’s performance improves as
715   α increases until a significant drop occurs when trivial classifiers dominate at α = 3.0. This ob-
716   servation supports our approach of selecting α as high as possible without encountering the trivial-
717   classifiers issue. However, the information score decreases as α increases. This decrease can be
718
      attributed to RADIANT becoming more conservative and avoiding providing uncertain information.
      In practice, depending on the information sensitivity of the application of LMs, we can select α as
719
      a trade-off between the accuracy of the information and the informativeness. For example, LMs in
720
      medical or legal sectors should avoid providing uncertain or wrong information, so high values of α
721   are recommended.
722
723   We report performance metrics of Llama-7B when varying Γ in Table 5. This hyperparameter de-
724
      cides how much RADIANT post-intervention activations deviate from the original ones if detected
      as undesirable. It is observed that the True score of RADIANT increases as increasing Γ. This is
725
      because the increasing value of Γ drives activations to reside more inside the desirable area, thus
726
      increasing the probability of desirable generation. However, the larger value of Γ makes the acti-
727   vations move farther from the original value, as shown by the increase of CE and KL metrics. The
728   extreme deviation from the original activations leads to inconsistency in semantics. It creates more
729   non-natural sentences, which can be observed at Γ = 20 with the drop in the Infomation score.
730   Therefore, a reasonable score should balance between True and Infomation scores.
731
      In our implementation, for each pretrained model, we conduct a grid search where α ranges over
732
      {1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5} and Γ over {5, 7.5, 10, 15, 20} to select the optimal combination based on the
733   True * Info score on the validation set. After running RADIANT with various pretrained models,
734   we find that the combination of Γ = 15 and α = 2.5 performs effectively across most cases. Unless
735   otherwise specified, we utilize these values for our experiments.

      A.4     T OXICITY MITIGATION TASK
811
812   In this section, we show the performance of RADIANT in mitigating toxicity in long-form text
813   generation. In this task, the language models are required to complete an incomplete prefix piece of
814   text. Normally, the prefix prompt is selected to elicit toxic content from LLMs. For a fair comparison
815   to previous works, we set up experiments following Singh et al. (2024) and Pozzobon et al. (2023),
816   which is detailed below.
817   Trainning dataset. We use the Toxic Comments Classification Challenge data 2 . The dataset com-
818   prises sentences and their human toxicity labels. We follow data preprocess from (Singh et al., 2024)
819   while the activations gathering is identical to the procedure of the QA task.
820
      Models. Following existing works in the field, we adopt the GPT2-Large as the base model across
821
      all experiments of the toxicity mitigation task.
822
823   Hyperparameter As we mentioned in the QA task section. There are two important hyperparame-
824   ters in our framework, namely α, and Γ = Φ−1 (1 − γ), which would be selected by a grid search
825
      procedure detailed in Appendix A.1.
826   Baselines. We include several baselines that have the same goal of reducing the toxicity of LLMs, in-
827   cluding MIMIC (Singh et al., 2024), DEXPERTS (Liu et al., 2021), DAPT (Gururangan et al., 2020),
828   UDDIA (Yang et al., 2022), PPLM (Dathathri et al., 2019), GOODTRIEVER (Pozzobon et al.,
829   2023). As for MIMIC, we consider two versions: Mean Matching (MM) and Mean+Covariance
830   Matching (MCM). Both these versions are introduced in their original paper.
831   Metrics. We assess the performance of the models using three key metrics: toxicity, fluency, and
832   diversity.
833
      To measure toxicity, we use the non-toxic split of RealToxicityPrompts (Gehman et al., 2020) and
834
      utilize the evaluation framework in Liu et al. (2021) and Singh et al. (2024). For each prompt in the
835   dataset, the models generate 25 outputs, each capped at 20 tokens in length. The parameters of the
836   shared decoding mechanism of all algorithms are presented in Table 9. These outputs are analyzed
837   using the Perspective API 3 , which estimates the likelihood that a human would perceive the text as
838   toxic. Two metrics are derived:
839
840            • Expected Maximum Toxicity is denoted as Exp. Max. Tox.. For every prompt, we identify
841              the output with the highest toxicity score and compute the average of these maximum scores
842
                 across all prompts.
843            • Toxic Completion Proportion is abbreviated as Tox. Prob. This metric tracks the fraction
844              of outputs considered toxic, where toxicity is defined as a score above 0.5 based on the
845              Perspective API’s threshold.
846
847                          Table 9: Hyperparameter Settings for Model Evaluation
848
849                                        Hyperparameter         Value
850                                        Number of Samples      25

852                                        Temperature            1
853                                        Top-p (sampling)       0.9
854                                        Top-k (sampling)       0
855
856   Fluency is evaluated by calculating the perplexity of the generated outputs, using GPT-2 (XL) as a
857   reference model. Lower perplexity values suggest that the text is more coherent and grammatically
858   fluent.
859
      Diversity is assessed by examining the ratio of unique n-grams (1-gram, 2-gram, and 3-gram) to
860
      the total number of tokens in the generated text. This metric captures the range of variation in the
861   outputs, with higher values indicating more diverse and varied language use.
