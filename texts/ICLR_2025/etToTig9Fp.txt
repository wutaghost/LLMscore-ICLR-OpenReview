                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Understanding visual semantics embedded in consecutive characters is a crucial
011            capability for both large language models (LLMs) and multi-modal large language
012            models (MLLMs). This type of artifact possesses the unique characteristic that
013            identical information can be readily formulated in both texts and images, mak-
014
               ing them a significant proxy for analyzing modern LLMs’ and MLLMs’ capabil-
               ities in modality-agnostic vision understanding. In this work, we select ASCII
015
               art as a representative artifact, where the lines and brightness used to depict each
016
               concept are rendered by characters, and we frame the problem as an ASCII art
017            recognition task. We benchmark model performance on this task by construct-
018            ing an evaluation dataset with an elaborate categorization tree and also collect
019            a training set to elicit the models’ visual perception ability. Through a compre-
020            hensive analysis of dozens of models, results reveal that although humans can
021            achieve nearly 100% accuracy, the state-of-the-art LLMs and MLLMs lag far be-
022            hind. Models are capable of recognizing concepts depicted in the ASCII arts given
023            only text inputs indicated by over 60% accuracy for some concepts, but most of
024            them achieves merely around 30% accuracy when averaged across all categories.
025
               When provided with images as inputs, GPT-4o gets 82.68%, outperforming the
               strongest open-source MLLM by 21.95%. Although models favor different kinds
026
               of ASCII art depending on the modality provided, none of the MLLMs success-
027
               fully benefit when both modalities are supplied simultaneously. Moreover, super-
028            vised fine-tuning helps improve models’ accuracy especially when provided with
029            the image modality, but also highlights the need for better training techniques to
030            enhance the information fusion among modalities. All resources are available at
031            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/VisionInText-08D3.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   While conventional wisdom suggests that texts primarily function as carriers of linguistic informa-
038   tion and images as conveyors of visual information, real-world scenarios often involve the inte-
039   gration of multiple information formats. For example, images may carry textual information, thus
040   Optical Character Recognition (OCR) (Mori et al., 1992) has been extensively studied. It focuses on
041   capturing and understanding linguistic information embedded in images through visual processors,
042   which is a crucial ability required in modern models for visual reasoning tasks (Yu et al., 2023).
043   In contrast, the comprehension of visual information embedded within text strings has not received
044   commensurate attention. One representative example that reflects visual semantics by a sequence
045   of characters is ASCII art (Xu et al., 2016) as shown in Fig. 1. Visual information in these artifacts
046   is situated in the middle of text strings and images, and can be readily expressed in both formats
047   containing identical content. In other words, it is modality-agnostic.
048   Understanding how well models can capture visual semantics in text modality is significant for de-
049   veloping large language models (LLMs) (Dubey et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023a). Upon pre-training on
050   a vast amount of text corpus, language models are capable of capturing visual information through
051   escape characters, such as “\n” and “\t”, which encodes 2D structures in human writings. How-
052   ever, they were predominately assessed via textual-semantic-based evaluation benchmarks, without
053   detailed analysis on its visual perception ability. ASCII art, where information can be fully repre-
      sented in text strings, serves as an ideal tool for benchmarking LLMs’ visual perception ability.

      Besides, with the advent of multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid
077   et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024) that arm LLMs with visual processors, the aforementioned modality-
078   agnostic characteristic also naturally leads to a new perspective of understanding MLLMs. The
079   modality-agnostic feature of ASCII art ensures that both vision and text modalities have the identical
080   semantics, which encounters the strict requirements for evaluating cross-modality alignment. In
081   other words, we expect that MLLMs can not only perform robustly among different modalities, but
082   also take the best of both worlds when two modalities are presented simultaneously.
083   Moreover, this research can also benefit a wide range of applications and have significant safety im-
084   plication for LLMs and MLLMs. Such visual information is ubiquitous in a wide range of practical
085   scenarios, such as processing tabular data (Deng et al., 2024) and playing board games (Topsakal &
086   Harper, 2024). In addition, using visual information reflected in characters to break through the de-
087   fense line is becoming a threat to LLM safety issues (Jiang et al., 2024b). For example, the attacker
088   may use the ASCII art of a “bomb” instead of the word. A thorough analysis for understanding
089   models’ visual perception ability to make proactive defense is in urgent need.
090   In this work, we define ASCII art recognition as an ideal proxy to investigate models’ visual percep-
091   tion ability in text strings through comprehensive evaluation and fine-tuning. Different from previ-
092   ous work that has focused on box diagrams (Hayatpur et al., 2024; Bayani, 2023), rich-formatting
093   texts (Jiang et al., 2024b), or tone-based ASCII art (Wang et al., 2023a) that can be easily generated
094   by rules or converted from images, we focus on ASCII art drawn by human artists, which is notably
095   more abstract, replete with visual information, and more popular among people. We formulate the
096   task as a multiple-choice question-answering problem, where the answers are objective for straight-
097
      forward verification, to achieve fairer comparisons. Then, we task models to recognize the concept
      depicted in the ASCII art. Due to the lack of a dataset covering diverse categories thoroughly bench-
098
      marking the ability of existing models, we crawled data from online websites and cleaned manually
099
      under an elaborate categorization tree. In this way, we construct a test set dubbed ASCIIE VAL
100   covering 359 concepts. To further elicit the models’ visual perception ability, a training set was
101   collected with approximately 10k data points.
102
103
      We convert each ASCII art into a text string, an image, or both modalities at the same time as inputs,
      evaluated dozens of existing LLMs and MLLMs, and fine-tuned representative open-source models.
104
      Our major findings are summarized as follows:
105
106     ◦ Models can truly recognize visual semantics through text inputs, indicated by the over 60%
107   accuracy of GPT-4o in certain concept categories. However, existing LLMs performs poorly on
      ASCIIE VAL, where most of them achieve merely around 30% accuracy (Sec. 5.1).

108
         ◦ There is an oversight in modality alignment that hinders MLLMs from answering questions
109   flexibly among modality-agnostic visual signals. We observed that well-known MLLMs show a
110   strong bias towards image modality, the expected synergistic effects do not emerge, and their training
111   techniques fail to facilitate the backbone LLMs’ visual understanding ability (Sec. 5.2 & 5.3.1).
112      ◦ LLMs and MLLMs show different trends in model performance when provided with different
113   input modalities and excel at different ASCII art categories. Specifically, they perform relatively
114   better on ASCII art containing fewer characters when given text inputs, whereas performing better
115   on those with more characters given image inputs (Sec. 5.3.2 & 5.3.3).
116      ◦ Better training strategies or model architectures are required for optimizing modality-agnostic
117
      visual perception in text strings. Supervised fine-tuning using task-specific training data helps
      MLLMs leverage representations from different modalities slightly better, but shows little improve-
118
      ment given only text inputs (Sec. 5.4).

121   2       BACKGROUNDS & R ELATED W ORK
122
123   2.1      LLM & MLLM B ENCHMARKS
124
125   Mainstream evaluations for LLMs focus on abilities in world knowledge, common sense reasoning,
126   instruction following, long context modeling, and mathematical reasoning. Representative bench-
127
      marks include MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), C-Eval (Huang et al., 2024), GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,
      2021), and StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021). Except for recent work from Qiu et al. (2024) bench-
128
      marking LLMs on answering questions related to the graphics content by generating programs, none
129
      of them consider the visual perception ability of LLMs as a distinct research problem.
130
131   Benchmarks for MLLMs focus on similar abilities when given a mix of text and images, such as
132   MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b), and MME (Yin et al., 2023). Most
133
      images considered in these benchmarks are photographs, paintings, or comics, rather than visual
      information reflected in text characters. Additionally, the information between interleaved images
134
      and texts is not guaranteed to be equivalent or complementary, whereas information in ASCII art
135
      can be semantic-equivalent among different modalities.
136
137   Current benchmarks contain some ASCII art-related tasks. For example, Gu et al. (2024) introduces
138   a fine-grained and diverse instruction-following evaluation dataset, in which ASCII art generation is
139
      a single case with approximately 40 samples of varied user requests. BigBench (Ghazal et al., 2013)
      contains ASCII MNIST digit recognition, ASCII word recognition, and ASCII kanji recognition.
140
      All of these tasks challenge the LLMs in recognizing different characters within the ASCII art. Test
141
      cases can be easily collected by using automatic conversion tools like Figlet 1 , where models may
142   learn conversion rules instead of truly understanding visual semantics.
143
144   In contrast, our work focuses on ASCII art depicting real-world profiles, containing more abstract
145
      visual features. We consider ASCII art recognition to be a preliminary ability for ASCII art genera-
      tion, and propose ASCIIE VAL based on this task, which can serve as both an LLM benchmark and
146
      an MLLM benchmark, bringing unique characteristics compared to existing benchmarks.

      2.2      R ESEARCH ON ASCII A RTS
150
      The history of ASCII art can be traced back to the 1860s. Due to the limitations of early computers,
151   text characters were widely used to simulate graphs, gradually becaming an important graphic design
152   technique. ASCII art broadly includes diverse types and styles (Carlsson & Miller, 2012; Carlsson,
153   2017), such as line art, emoticons, colored ASCII art, and animated ASCII art. Strictly speaking, it
154   refers to art made up of 95 printable fixed-width ASCII characters (Xu et al., 2016), which are easy
155   to copy from one file to another and display consistently across different computers.
156
      Early studies focused on extracting ASCII art from general texts (Hiroki & Minoru, 2005; Hayashi
157   & Suzuki, 2009; Suzuki, 2011) by exploring byte patterns, morphological features, compression ra-
158   tios, etc. Subsequently, ASCII art gained more attention in the area of computer vision. Researchers
159   generally categorize ASCII art into tone-based and structure-based types and have developed algo-
160   rithms to synthesize ASCII art from images (Xu et al., 2010; Takeuchi et al., 2013; Xu et al., 2016;
161
          1
              https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIGlet


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Chung & Kwon, 2022). Tone-based ASCII art emphasizes the intensity distribution of the reference
163   image, while structure-based ASCII art focuses on the major structure of the content. The latter is
164   mostly curated by human artists and is more challenging to synthesize automatically.
165
166
      Works on ASCII art classification typically convert such text graphics into images as a default set-
      ting and exploit different image features to improve the classification accuracy of deep neural net-
167
      works (Fujisawa et al., 2020a; Matsumoto et al., 2018; Fujisawa et al., 2018). Fujisawa et al. (2020b)
168
      constructs ASCII art data automatically to enhance the models’ image classification ability. Most of
169   the aforementioned works are tested using an ASCII art classification dataset containing only five
170   categories, which is inadequate for comprehensively analyzing how well the LLMs and MLLMs can
171   grasp the visual representation of ASCII art.
172
      There are also works that take advantage of ASCII art to achieve specific goals. Jiang et al. (2024b)
173
      represent rich-formatting texts as ASCII art and find that it results in highly effective jailbreak attacks
174
      that bypass state-of-the-art defense techniques. In contrast, Wang et al. (2023a) find that tone-based
175   ASCII art with rich visual information cannot be understood by current LLMs, which can be used
176   as an effective tool to detect whether the participant is a bot or a human. ASCII art is also utilized
177   to enhance LLMs’ spatial reasoning ability in Wu et al. (2024)’s work. Box diagrams, as a special
178   kind of ASCII art, are widely used in the development lifecycle (Hayatpur et al., 2024) and have
179   been benchmarked by Bayani (2023) with recognition and generation tasks.
180
      In this work, we regard ASCII art as an ideal information carrier that bridges the gap between the
181   text and image modalities, to facilitate the understanding of modality-agnostic visual perception
182   ability for both LLMs and MLLMs.

      3       ASCII A RT R ECOGNITION
186
187
      We first define the ASCII art recognition task formally. Then, we introduced how we constructed
      the test and training data, dubbed ASCIIE VAL and ASCIIT UNE, followed by statistical analysis.
188
189
190   3.1      P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
191
      We formulate ASCII art recognition as a multiple-choice question-answering problem. Let T repre-
192
      sent the text string of an ASCII art and I refer to the corresponding image modality. The model is
193   asked to predict the correct choice containing the concept depicted by T or I among candidates C.
194
195   For LLMs that only accept text input, the prediction ŷ is generated as follows:
196                                              ŷT = LLM(T, C)                                             (1)
197
198   For MLLMs, ŷ can be inferred under two additional conditions:
199                                             ŷI = MLLM(I, C)
200                                                                                                          (2)
                                              ŷIT = MLLM(I, T, C)
201
202   We denote the above three input conditions as Text-only, Image-only, and Text-Image, respectively.
203   The final prompt is structured by corresponding string templates given the inputs. See Appendix C.
204
205   3.2      DATA C OLLECTION FOR ASCIIE VAL
206
207   We carried out the data construction process in four stages to collect a high-quality test dataset.
208   Data Preparation      We first crawled ASCII art created by human artists from two online galleries 2 .
209
      Classification Criteria Unification Next, we manually designed a 3-layer classification tree after
210
      unifying the categories based on the categorical information from the original websites and removing
211
      potentially harmful categories. The most fine-grained category is named the concept, representing
212   the semantic meaning reflected in the art. Similar concepts are merged into second-layer groups.
213   Finally, they are grouped into seven major classes inspired by the iOS emoji categories. Each
214   concept can be depicted in various ways by ASCII artists.
215
          2
              https://asciiart.website/, https://www.asciiart.eu/


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Data Filtering Subsequently, we conducted additional filtering operations using a combination of
217   rules and human annotations as follows:
218
219      ◦ Each ASCII art string was normalized by removing redundant empty spaces at the beginning
220
      of each line and at the end of the string, without compromising its visual semantics.
221      ◦ ASCII art consisting of more than 100 lines, not belonging to reserved categories, and repetitive
222   to other ASCII arts under the same concept were discarded. Repetition was identified by calculating
223   the edit distance between two ASCII strings. If the distance divided by the length of the existing
224   string was smaller than 0.3, the new ASCII art will be considered redundant.
225     ◦ Human annotators were tasked to filter out unrecognizable or ambiguous art, remove words in
226   ASCII art to focus the dataset on visual perception and avoid information leakage through words,
227   and adjust the category according to the 3-layer category tree (See more analysis in Appendix D).
228
      Multiple-Choice Data Construction Finally, we collected negative choices for each ASCII art by
229
      randomly sampling from other concepts within the same group. It should be noted that the ground
230   truth labels were initially collected from the websites and subsequently verified by human annotators
231   during the data filtering process. Each ASCII art string was then converted into an image.
232
233
234   3.3     DATA C OLLECTION FOR ASCIIT UNE
235
236   To further elicit models’ visual perception ability through supervised fine-tuning on the ASCII art
237
      recognition task, the creation of a training set is essential. An intuitive solution is to leverage pre-
      vious works on ASCII art synthesis (Xu et al., 2016; 2010) by converting existing image datasets,
238
      such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), into the required format. A public dataset 3 indicates that
239
      after automatic tone-based synthesis, approximately 85% data samples are filtered out due to poor
240   quality. Furthermore, existing data conversion tools are inadequate for structure-based ASCII art,
241   which accounts for 94% of the data in ASCIIE VAL according to annotators’ labels. Additionally,
242   artists often combine both tone-based and structure-based features in a single artifact.
243
      Therefore, we chose to collect the training set in a manner similar to ASCIIE VAL instead of relying
244
      on automatic conversion. Data sources include ASCII arts from another less well-organized web-
245
      site 4 , and the crawled content was extracted into individual ASCII art pieces based on specific rules
246   derived from observations. We also included the unrecognized ASCII art that was previously with-
247   drawn during the construction of ASCIIE VAL. The normalized ASCII art is discarded if recognized
248   as repetitive with samples in ASCIIE VAL or among each other.
249
      Due to the large amount of data with diverse concepts, carefully categorizing data for high-quality
250
      distractors is unfeasible. Instead, we prompted Llama-3-70B-Instruct to generate negative choices
251
      given the ground truth concept and utilized the Perspective API to filter out unsafe samples based on
252   the concatenation of candidate choices. Samples with scores less than 0.2 across all six dimensions,
253   i.e., toxicity, severe toxicity, identity attack, insult, profanity and threat, are retained.
254

300   4.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
301
302   We perform an exact match between the correct option and a model’s output to calculate accuracy
303
      on ASCIIE VAL. As analyzed in Sec 3.4, the test data is unbalanced with varying art counts under
      each concept. Therefore, we adopt micro-accuracy over each sample for analyzing specific ASCII
304
      art characteristics, and macro-average over each concept for quantifying model performance. We
305
      also define pass rate to measure a model’s ability to successfully follow the instruction by providing
306   an effective answer. Proprietary models may fail due to their safety policy.

      LLMs are proficient in recognizing ASCII art with fewer characters, and they even outperform com-
449
      petitive MLLMs with image inputs, such as CogVLM2-Llama3-chat-19B, on ASCII arts with fewer
450
      than 50 characters. In smaller ASCII art, significant features are densely packed within consecutive
451   characters. For instance, the string “() '`;” captures some major characteristics of a dog in Fig. 1.
452   The results indicate that LLMs excel at capturing the relationship between a concept and some fea-
453   tured combinations of characters. However, as the length of the ASCII art increase, such features
454   are likely to be diluted, and much stronger 2D perception abilities are required.
455
      Conversely, MLLMs are better at recognizing larger ASCII art. Smaller ASCII art tends to be more
456
      abstract, where artists try to depict significant features of a concept with few characters. In contrast,
457   larger ASCII art is more similar to real images or posters that MLLMs are trained on. For example,
458   the Spiderman in Fig. 1 shares much more similarity in terms of outline and luminance contrast to
459   a real poster. Nevertheless, MLLM also face challenges on ASCII arts containing more than 1600
460   characters, as evidenced by the performance drop of both GPT-4o and CogVLM-Chat-hf. This may
461   be due to the fact that larger ASCII art contains more spaces with the same grayscale, providing
462   ineffective or redundant features for MLLMs, thereby aggravating the recognition difficulty.
463   In summary, LLMs are adept at understanding short and abstract art, and MLLMs are proficient in
464   interpreting longer and more detailed art, which are mainly influenced by the characteristics of the
465   input modality (More in Appendix F). Although different modalities have strengths with various
466   forms of ASCII art, late fusion strategies fail to combine them effectively, as showed in Sec. 5.3.1.

468   5.3.3     H OW DO MODELS PERFORM ON DIFFERENT CATEGORIES ?
469
470   Models’ performances across the 7 different classes are shown in Fig. 5. LLMs trained purely on
471   text corpus perform better at recognizing ASCII arts belonging to the “objects” class. MLLMs given
472   image inputs show consistent improvement in recognizing “travel & places” over LLMs compared to
473
      other classes relatively. Moreover, all models struggle with ASCII art referring to “symbols”, which
      comprise different logos and astrology symbols. MLLMs actually perform quite well at recognizing
474
      well-known logos, such as Apple and Linux, where GPT-4o achieves 97.96% macro-accuracy and
475
      CogVLM2-Llama3-Chat-19B gets 91.16%. However, their performance drops dramatically on rela-
476   tively niche astrology symbols. Nevertheless, it is simple for both LLMs and MLLMs to answer the
477   question “Can you show me some astrology symbols?”. Existing models tend to use rare Unicode
478   characters or emojis to explain the symbols, but cannot understand the visual semantics embedded
479   in those symbols flexibly. More cases can be found in Appendix J.

      6   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we focus on analyzing and eliciting models’ visual perception ability in text strings. We
536   introduce the ASCII art recognition problem, which task models to recognize the concepts depicted
537   by the art conveyed through different modalities. We constructed both test and training data, and
538   conducted comprehensive evaluations with dozens of LLMs and MLLMs followed by supervised
539   fine-tuning. Results pinpoint the weaknesses of current models on this task, highlighting a lack of
      effective fusion techniques for semantic-equivalent information across different carriers.

711   B    F UTURE D IRECTIONS
712
713
      In the current work, we majorly devoted our efforts on dataset construction for the ASCII art recog-
714   nition task, benchmarking the performance of LLMs and MLLMs, and figuring out the limitations
715   of current models.
716
717
      Based on the results and analysis, we summarized future directions as follows:
718   Constructing high-quality training data automatically. We randomly selected 100 samples from
719   ASCIITune for the quality check and the human annotator achieved only 70% accuracy. This in-
720   dicates that ASCIITune is much noisier than ASCIIEval (98.33%), pointing out the importance of
721   collecting more training data with higher quality. On the one hand, utilizing the ASCII art synthesis
722   tools to convert image datasets into ASCII art can be considered to enlarge the size of the training
723
      data, under the awareness of the style differences between the converted ones and the ones created
      by artists. On the other hand, more strict filtering strategies should be incorporated, such as verifying
724
      the validity of ASCII art with strong MLLMs under the Image-only setting.
725
726   Improving the model architecture. All of the tested LLMs and MLLMs show the inability to
727   recognize information that can be fully represented in text. One potential reason is the lack of
728   exposure to this type of data. It may be also a result of the structural limitation of current models. As
729
      for human beings, we perceive text from the aspects of character sequences and their visual shapes
      at the same time, while these two aspects are conventionally distinguished into two modalities when
730
      being processed by neural models. More flexible processing techniques and architecture among
731
      modalities should not only benefit the models’ visual perception ability in text strings, but also make
732   the model closer to human beings with more efficient information processing abilities.
733
734   Adjusting the training pipeline. In this work, we simply did supervised fine-tuning with ASCI-
735
      ITune to improve the models’ visual perception ability in text strings. This first attempt only shows
      effectiveness in Image-only setting, pointing out that superficial instruction tuning is not sufficient
736
      for current models. Therefore, we hypothesize that post-training should be considered for injecting
737
      related knowledge and gaining better representations. Specifically, LLMs are expected to be post-
738   trained on ASCII art corpus containing more diverse tasks, such as ASCII art generation and ASCII
739   art description, mixed with traditional pre-training corpora. As for MLLMs, besides improving the
740   corresponding backbone LLMs, more flexible usage of ASCII art in both modalities should improve
741   MLLMs representation alignments between modalities during the vision-text alignment stage.
742
      We did some further explorations on improving LLMs’ and MLLMs’ on ASCIIE VAL with unsuper-
743
      vised training objectives based on data from ASCIIT UNE. Specifically, we post-trained the Llama-
744   3.1-8B-Instruct on textual ASCII art from ASCIIT UNE where the loss is calculated from each token
745   in the textual ASCII art, and post-train the Llava-v1.6-mistral-7B with (rendered ASCII art, textual
746   ASCII art) input-output-pairs from ASCIIT UNE where the loss is only calculated from the tokens
747   in the textual ASCII art. Both models are post-trained with the following hyper-parameters: “lr =
748   2e-5, batch size = 16, number of epochs = 3”, and are fine-tuned for ASCII art recognition after
749   further post-training. The results are shown in Table 5.
750   The post-trained LLM achieved 27.58%, almost the same as the 27.46% accuracy shown in Table 4.
751   Meanwhile, fine-tuning MLLMs with (rendered ASCII art image, textual ASCII art) input-output
752   pairs from ASCIITune is also not helpful for ASCII art recognition. We offer the following hy-
753   pothesis for the above observations: In the recognition task, the model is required to understand
754   the semantic concept behind the textual ASCII art. However, using textual ASCII art alone for
755   post-training can not help to bridge the gap between visual information and semantics in language.
      Besides, training MLLMs to convert an ASCII art image into its string format is merely a super-
