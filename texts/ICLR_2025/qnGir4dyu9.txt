                                                 A BSTRACT
030
031
              Recent video generative models primarily rely on carefully written text prompts for
032           specific tasks, like inpainting or style editing. They require labor-intensive textual
033           descriptions for input videos, hindering their flexibility to adapt personal/raw videos
034           to user specifications. This paper proposes RACCooN, a versatile and user-friendly
035           video-to-paragraph-to-video generative framework that supports multiple video
036           editing capabilities such as removal, addition, and modification, through a unified
037           pipeline. RACCooN consists of two principal stages: Video-to-Paragraph (V2P)
038           and Paragraph-to-Video (P2V). In the V2P stage, we automatically describe video
039           scenes in well-structured natural language, capturing both the holistic context and
040
              focused object details. Subsequently, in the P2V stage, users can optionally refine
              these descriptions to guide the video diffusion model, enabling various modifica-
041
              tions to the input video, such as removing, changing subjects, and/or adding new
042
              objects. The proposed approach stands out from other methods through several
043           significant contributions: (1) RACCooN suggests a multi-granular spatiotemporal
044           pooling strategy to generate well-structured video descriptions, capturing both the
045           broad context and object details without requiring complex human annotations,
046           simplifying precise video content editing based on text for users. (2) Our video
047           generative model incorporates auto-generated narratives or instructions to enhance
048           the quality and accuracy of the generated content. (3) RACCooN also plans to
049           imagine new objects in a given video, so users simply prompt the model to receive
050           a detailed video editing plan for complex video editing. The proposed framework
051
              demonstrates impressive versatile capabilities in video-to-paragraph generation
              (up to 9.4%p ↑ absolute improvement in human evaluations against the baseline),
052
              video content editing (relative 49.7% ↓ in FVD), and can be incorporated into other
053
              SoTA video generative models for further enhancement.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Recent advances in video generative models (Yan et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2023;
057   Mei & Patel, 2023; Chai et al., 2023; Ceylan et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a),
058   including Sora (openai, 2024), have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in creating high-quality
059   videos. Simultaneously, video editing models (Geyer et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b;
060   Yu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b) have gained significant attention, thanks to their
061   promising applications that allow users to modify videos according to user-written textual instructions,
062   effectively altering video content and attributes. Despite these advancements, significant challenges
063   remain in developing a versatile and user-friendly framework that facilitates easy video modification
064   for personal use. The primary challenges include: 1) the complexity of training a unified framework
065
      encompassing multiple video editing skills (e.g., remove, add, or change an object). Training a single
      model to perform various editing skills is highly challenging, and recent video editing methods often
066
      focus on specific tasks, such as background inpainting (Yu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), or attribute
067
      editing (Geyer et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Jeong & Ye, 2023). 2) the necessity for well-structured
068   textual prompts that accurately describe videos and can be edited to support diverse video editing
069   skills. The quality of prompts critically influences the models’ capabilities and the quality of their
070   outputs. Generating detailed prompts is time-consuming and costly, and the quality varies depending
071   on the expertise of the annotators. Although Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Liu
072   et al., 2023b; Munasinghe et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) have been explored for
073   automatically describing videos, they often overlook critical details in complex scenes. This oversight
074   compromises the development of a seamless pipeline, hindering both user convenience and the
075   effectiveness of video generative models.
076   To tackle these limitations, as shown in Fig. 1, we introduce RACCooN: A Versatile Instructional
077   Video Editing Framework with Auto-Generated Narratives, a novel video-to-paragraph-to-video
078   (V2P2V) generative framework that facilitates diverse video editing (Remove, Add, and Change)
079   capabilities based on auto-generated narratives. RACCooN allows for the seamless removal and
080   modification of subject attributes, as well as the addition of new objects to videos without requir-
081   ing densely annotated video prompts or extensive user planning. Our framework operates in
082
      two main stages: video-to-paragraph (V2P) and paragraph-to-video (P2V). In the V2P stage, we
      introduce a new video descriptive framework built on a pre-trained Video-LLM backbone (PG-
083
      Video-LLaVA (Munasinghe et al., 2023)). We find that existing Video-LLMs effectively capture
084
      holistic video features, yet often overlook detailed cues that are critical for accurate video editing,
085   as users may be interested in altering these missing contexts. To address this, we propose a novel
086   multi-granular video perception strategy that leverages superpixels (Li & Chen, 2015; Ke et al., 2023)
087   to capture diverse and informative localized contexts throughout a video. We first extract fine-grained
088   superpixels using a lightweight predictor (Yang et al., 2020) and then apply overlapping k-means
089   clustering (Cleuziou, 2007; Whang et al., 2015; Khanmohammadi et al., 2017) to segment visual
090   scenes into various levels of granularity. The suggested localized spatiotemporal segmentation assists
091   the LLM’s comprehension of objects, actions, and events within the video, enabling it to generate
092   fluent and detailed natural language descriptions. Next, in the P2V stage, to integrate multiple editing
093
      capabilities into a single model, we fine-tuned a video inpainting model that can paint video objects
      accurately with detailed text, object masks, and condition video. Then, by utilizing user-modified
094
      prompts from generated descriptions in the V2P stage, our video diffusion model can accurately paint
095
      corresponding video regions, ensuring that textual updates from prompts are reflected in various
096   editing tasks. Moreover, to better support our model training, we have collected the Video Paragraph
097   with Localized Mask (VPLM) dataset—a collection of over 7.2K high-quality video-paragraph
098   descriptions and 5.5k detailed object descriptions with masks, annotated from the publicly available
099   dataset using GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023).
100
      We emphasize that RACCooN enhances the quality and versatility of video editing by leveraging
101
      detailed, automatically generated textual prompts that minimize ambiguity and refine the scope of
102   generation. We validate the extensive capabilities of the RACCooN framework in both V2P genera-
103   tion, text-based video content editing, and video generation on ActivityNet (Krishna et al., 2017),
104   YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2018a), UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012), DAVIS (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017), and
105   our proposed VPLM datasets. On the V2P side, RACCooN outperforms several strong video cap-
106   tioning baselines (Li et al., 2023; Munasinghe et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b), particularly improving
107   by average +9.1%p on VPLM and up to +9.4%p on YouCook2 compared to PG-VL (Munasinghe
      et al., 2023), based on both automatic metrics and human evaluation. On the P2V side, RACCooN

128   2     R ELATED W ORK
129   Video-to-Paragraph Generation. The recent trend in video-language tasks focuses on generating
130   comprehensive textual descriptions for long and complex video content (Shen et al., 2017; Krishna
131   et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018; Tewel et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023b). Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023)
132   introduces a novel dense event captioning approach for narrated videos, with time tokens and event
133   boundaries. Video-LLaVA variants (Lin et al., 2023a; Munasinghe et al., 2023) present a large
134   multimodal model integrating text, video, and audio inputs for generative and question-answering
135
      tasks. Similarly, LLaVA-Next (Zhang et al., 2024) improves zero-shot video understanding by
      transferring multi-image knowledge through concatenated visual tokens. While these methods
136
      are effective in video description, they often miss key contextual details (Zhang et al., 2023a; Li
137
      et al., 2023). Our RACCooN captures both holistic and localized details by leveraging localized
138   spatiotemporal information, enhancing video editing and generation capabilities.
139
140   Prompt-to-Video Editing. Video editing (Ceylan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Couairon et al.,
141   2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c) involves enhancing, modifying,
142
      or manipulating video content for desired effects. VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2024b) offers a
      multi-source controllable video generative framework. TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023) adapts text-to-
143
      image diffusion with flow matching for consistent text-driven video editing. LGVI (Wu et al., 2024)
144
      integrates an MLLM for complex language-based video inpainting. These methods often focus on
145   specific tasks and may inadvertently alter unrelated regions due to limited contextual information.
146   Our V2P2V framework overcomes these limitations by using auto-generated, detailed descriptions to
147   integrate key contexts into diverse editing tasks.

153
      Conditional video generation and editing models struggle with complex scenes due to vague text
      descriptions and limited video understanding. Despite improvements from recent advances in
154
      MLLMs, these models still struggle to capture complex spatial-temporal dynamics, often omitting
155
      crucial objects and details. Training a text-to-video model with such vague prompts compromises
156   output specificity, and leads the model to generate average, arbitrary content that fails to capture user
157   instructions’ nuances. To address these issues, we introduce RACCooN, a user-friendly, two-stage
158   video-to-paragraph-to-video editing framework. Initially, RACCooN generates detailed, structured
159   paragraphs from videos, capturing holistic content and key local objects through multi-granular
160   spatiotemporal information. These detailed descriptions are then used for conditional video generation
161   and editing, enabling users to add, remove, or change video objects directly by interacting with the
      generated descriptions, thus enhancing the specificity and relevance of the output.

183   3.1   V2P: AUTO -D ESCRIPTIVE F RAMEWORK WITH M ULTI -G RANULAR V IDEO P ERCEPTION

      Multimodal LLM for Video Paragraph Generation. In the V2P stage, the RACCooN framework
185
      generates well-structured, detailed descriptions for both holistic videos and local objects. It employs
186   a multimodal LLM with three main components: a visual encoder E, a multimodal projector, and
187   an LLM. Given an input video x ∈ RF ×C×H×W , where F , C, H, and W represent the number of
188   frames, channels, height, and width, respectively, we extract video features using the visual encoder:
189   e = E(x) ∈ Rt×h×w×d . Here, t, h, w, and d denote the encoded temporal dimension, the height and
190   width of the tokens, and the feature dimension. To understand complex videos with multiple scenes,
191   we use three pooling strategies: spatial pooling, temporal pooling, and multi-granular spatiotemporal
192   pooling. Spatial pooling es = Poolings (e) ∈ Rt×d aggregates tokens within the same frame, while
193   temporal pooling et = Poolingt (e) ∈ R(h·w)×d averages features across the temporal dimension
194   for the same region. Despite these strategies helping the LLM grasp the video holistically in space
195
      or time, they often overlook capturing key objects or actions localized throughout the video stream,
      especially in untrimmed, dynamic, multi-scene videos.

      Multi-Granular Spatiotemporal Pooling. To address this issue, we introduce a novel superpixel-
208
      based spatiotemporal pooling strategy, coined multi-granular spatiotemporal pooling (MGS pooling).
209
      As illustrated in Fig. 2 left top, this strategy is designed to capture localized information via superpixels
210   across spatial and temporal dimensions. Superpixels (Li & Chen, 2015; Giordano et al., 2015; Yang
211   et al., 2020; Ke et al., 2023) are small and coherent clusters of pixels that share similar characteristics,
212   such as color or texture. These clusters provide an efficient representation of visual scenes and
213   are resilient to frame noise since they average out the pixel values within each cluster, effectively
214   smoothing out variations induced by noise. As shown in Fig. 3, we use a lightweight superpixel
215   predictor σ(·) (Yang et al., 2020) to generate superpixels across video frames, capturing the granular
      visuality of each local area. However, due to their limited coverage area, these fine-grained visual

240   3.2   P2V: U SER -I NTERACTIVE V IDEO E DITING WITH AUTO -G ENERATED D ESCRIPTIONS
241
242   With the well-structured, detailed, and object-centric video description generated from the Video-to-
243   Paragraph stage, users can ‘read’ the video details and interactively modify the content by altering the
244   model-generated description. This approach shifts users’ focus from labor-intensive video observation
245   to content editing. We categorize general video content editing into three important subtasks: (1)
246
      Video Object Adding: add extra objects to a video. (2) Video Object Removing: delete target
      objects and re-generate the object region as the background. (3) Video Object Changing: change
247
      objects’ attributes (e.g., color, textural, material). Many previous works have made great progress
248
      in video editing (Wu et al., 2024; Geyer et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Fan et al.,
249   2024) but usually focus on one of these subtasks. In this paper, we propose a unified generative model
250   for video content editing that integrates all those crucial subtasks. Specifically, we formulate these
251   subtasks as text-based video painting tasks and leverage a single video diffusion model for adding,
252   removing, and changing video objects in the form of inpainting.
253
      As shown in Fig. 2 bottom, our video diffusion model processes input video x ∈ RF ×C×H×W with
254
      a predicted binary mask m′ ∈ RF ×1×H×W targeting specific regions for modification. Following
255   image inpainting techniques (Xie et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022), we apply the mask1 to the video
256   to designate the editing region. The masked video is then encoded using a Variational Autoencoder
257   (VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013)) to serve as the generation condition. The model can then be
258   informed on which video region should be edited for localized editing. Driven by the detailed
259   description, the diffusion model can conduct diverse video editing that reflects the text prompts.
260
      In addition, we provide details regarding the process of adding objects in video editing. Indeed,
261   adding objects can be considered a unique video editing task, distinct from removing objects or
262   changing attributes. Unlike the latter scenarios, where the target objects are already present in the
263   initial video, adding objects involves introducing entirely new elements, which necessitates a slightly
264   different editing process.
265
      As illustrated in Fig. 2, the MLLM in the V2P process provides not only detailed descriptions but
266
      also frame-wise placement suggestions for new objects in the form of bounding box sequences. The
267   object insertion process in RACCooN in inference is conducted through the following steps:
268
         1

279   3.3       VPLM DATASET C OLLECTION AND RACC OO N P IPELINE T RAINING
280
281   Dataset Collection. We utilize video datasets (Majumdar et al., 2020; Gavrilyuk et al., 2018)
282
      from previous video inpainting work (Wu et al., 2024). Each raw video is accompanied by multiple
      inpainted versions with specific objects removed and includes binary masks of these objects. Although
283
      well-annotated with object masks and inpainted backgrounds, these datasets lack detailed descriptions
284
      of holistic video and specific local objects, hindering RACCooN’s training for producing well-
285   structured captions for video editing. To address this, we use GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023) to
286   annotate detailed video descriptions. We first re-arrange uniformly sampled video frames into a
287   grid-image (Fan et al., 2021) and add visual prompts by numbering each frame. We then ask GPT-4V
288   to generate detailed captions for both the entire video and key objects, in a well-structured format.
289   Next, we train V2P and P2V stages in our framework separately (Fig. 2). In the end, RACCooN
290   can automatically generate detailed, well-structured descriptions for raw videos and adapt these
291   descriptions based on user updates for various video content editing tasks.
292   MLLM Instructional Fine-tuning. To enable the MLLM to output detailed video descriptions
293   for content editing, we construct an instructional fine-tuning dataset based on VPLM with two
294   video-instruction (Liu et al., 2023b) designs: (1) For object editing and removal, the MLLM generates
295   structured video captions identifying key objects in the original video x, using annotated descriptions
296   as the learning objective. This allows users to edit videos directly from these descriptions without
297   exhaustive analysis. (2) For object insertion, the MLLM provides not only detailed descriptions
298   but also frame-wise placement suggestions for new objects, enhancing its utility in video editing
299
      by avoiding manual trajectory outlining. For training, we convert video object segmentation masks
      into bounding boxes by selecting maximal and minimal coordinates and follow the box planning
300
      strategy using LLMs (Lin et al., 2023b). We input box coordinates as a sequence of numbers and
301
      train RACCooN framework to predict these layouts given inpainted videos x     b. We perform parameter-
302   efficient fine-tuning with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022)2 on these mixed datasets with CE loss. We freeze
303   the visual encoder and LLM backbone, updating the projector, LoRA, and LLM head.
304
305
      Video Diffusion Model Fine-tuning. Our video diffusion model builds on the prior image inpainting
      model (Rombach et al., 2022), enhanced with temporal attention layers to capture video dynamics.
306
      The model is designed to generate video that aligns with input prompts, focusing on object-centric
307
      video content editing. To support this, we develop a training dataset of mask-object-description
308   triples. We use GPT-4 to produce single-object descriptions from long, detailed video narratives,
309   framing this task as a multi-choice QA problem. Next, for the three video editing subtasks, we design
310   specific input-output combinations: (1) Video Object Addition: Inputs: inpainted video x     b, object
311   bounding boxes from segmentation masks m, and detailed object description p. Output: original
312   video x. (2) Video Object Removal: Inputs: original video x, object segmentation masks m, and a
313   fixed background prompt. Output: inpainted video x    b. (3) Video Object Change: Inputs: original
314   video x, object segmentation masks m, and object description p. Output: original video x. The
315   model is fine-tuned following the prior work (Wu et al., 2023a), updating only the temporal layers
316
      and the query projections within the self-attention and cross-attention modules. We employ the MSE
      loss between generated and random noise. See Appendix for more details on the dataset and training.

441   Quantitative Evaluation. As shown in Tab. 3, we quantitatively compare the video editing ability of

444   adding. In general, RACCooN outperforms all baselines across 9 metrics. For object changing,
445   RACCooN outperforms the best-performing baseline by 0.8% on CLIP-T, indicating better video-text
446   alignment while maintaining temporal consistency, as demonstrated by comparable CLIP-F and
447   Qedit scores. Note that LGVI is not designed to alter video attributes and tends to preserve video
448
      content with marginal change (i.e., identical input and output videos), resulting in improved CLIP-F

449

450
      addition task (relatively +41.6% FVD, +4.3% PSNR). Meanwhile, some DDIM inversion-based
451   models (e.g., TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023)) work well for specific tasks (change objects), but do

      4.3   E NHANCING I NVERSION - BASED V IDEO E DITING & G ENERATION WITH RACC OO N

      We further validate that detailed paragraphs can benefit video generative tasks (e.g., inversion-based
511   video editing and video generation). Our RACCooN captions can be integrated with off-shelf SoTA
512   video editing and generation models to enhance them. See Appendix for more details.
513
514
      Inversion-based Video Editing. Our RACCooN framework can significantly enhance video editing
      tasks. We integrated it into two SoTA methods, TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023) and FateZero (Qi et al.,
515
      2023), and compared their performance with different text inputs. The baseline used human-written
516
      short captions, while baseline+RACCooN used generated detailed captions. As shown in Tab. 5,
517   integrating RACCooN significantly improved performance. FateZero+RACCooN achieved an 11.0%
518   and 1.4% relative increase in CLIP-Text and SSIM scores, respectively. TokenFlow+RACCooN
519   saw increases of 4.9% and 1.9%. These results indicate that detailed captions enhance text-to-video
520   alignment and localized editing, while preserving temporal consistency and quality.
521
      Conditional Video Generation. We also integrated RACCooN with various SoTA video gener-
522
      ation methods, such as VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023) and DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023).
523   VideoCrafter was tested in a Text-to-Video setting, while DynamiCrafter was in an Image-Text-
524   to-Video setting. We evaluated our framework on ActivityNet, YouCook2, and UCF101 datasets.
525   As listed in Tab. 6, VideoCrafter’s generated videos often failed to align with the GT captions,
526   especially in complex videos. In contrast, our framework improved video quality and alignment with
527   auto-generated detailed paragraphs, achieving substantial improvements of 36.9% in FVD and 15.3%
528   in SSIM. We noted consistent improvements in FVD, CLIP, and SSIM scores with RACCooN. It
529   underscores our effectiveness in augmenting video generation models with automated descriptions.

532
533
      Our proposed RACCooN framework newly introduces an auto-descriptive video-to-paragraph-to-
534   video generative framework. RACCooN automatically generates video descriptions by leveraging a
535   multi-granular spatiotemporal pooling strategy, enhancing the model’s ability to recognize detailed,
536   localized video information. RACCooN then uses these enriched descriptions to edit and generate
537   video content, offering users the flexibility to modify content through textual updates, thus eliminating
538   the need for detailed video annotations. These video editing and generation abilities of RACCooN
539   framework highlight notable effectiveness and enable a broader range of users to engage in video
      creation and editing tasks without the faithfully written textual prompts.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      The performance of our proposed framework in paragraph generation, video generation, and editing
543   is influenced by the employed pre-trained backbones, including an LLM (Touvron et al., 2023).
544   LLM-empowered video description and photorealistic video creation/editing inherit biases from
545   their training data, leading to several potentially negative impacts, including societal stereotypes,
546   biased interpretation of actions, and privacy concerns. To mitigate these potential negative impacts,
547   it is essential to carefully develop and implement generative and video description models, such as
548   considering diversifying training datasets, implementing fairness and bias evaluation metrics, and
549   engaging communities to understand and address their concerns.

938   A.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
939
      Metrics: We provide more details about our metrics. CLIP-Text measures the similarity between
940
      the edit prompt and the embedding of each frame in the edited video. CLIP-Frame computes
941
      the average CLIP similarity between the image embeddings of consecutive frames to measure the
942   temporal coherency. SSIM measures the structural similarity between the original and edited video
943   for evaluating localized editing. Qedit = CLIP − T /W rap − Err, it is a comprehensive score for
944   video editing quality, where W arp − Err calculates the pixel-level difference by warping the edited
945   video frames according to the estimated optical flow of the source video, extracted by FlowNet2.0 (Ilg
946   et al., 2017). For layout planning, we compute the FVD and CLIP-Image similarity between the
947   ground truth and the predicted bounding box.
948   More Details about Multi-granular Spatiotemporal Pooling. As mentioned Sec. 3.1, we proposed
949   a novel Multi-granular Spatiotemporal Pooling (MGS Pooling) to address the lack of complex spatial-
950   temporal modeling in video. We further provide a more intuitive visualization for our proposed MGS
951   Pooling in Fig. 3. We first adopt a lightweight 165 superpixel predictor that generates superpixels
952   across video frames, then use the overlapping k-means clustering for the obtained video superpixels.
953   In this case, we gather informative cues about various objects and actions for LLM.
954   Human Evaluation on Video-to-Paragraph Generation. We conduct a human evaluation on nine
955   randomly selected videos from the YouCook2 dataset. Videos are three- to five-minute-long and
956   contain multiple successive scenes with complex viewpoints. We provide these videos to four different
957   annotators with ground truth captions, descriptions generated by PG-Video-LLAVA, and our method,
958   RACCooN, where the captions/descriptions for each video are randomly shuffled. We leverage
959   four distinct human evaluation metrics: Logic Fluency (Logic), Language Fluency (Language),
960   Video Summary (Summary) and Video Details (Details). To avoid a misinterpretation of methods’
961   capabilities due to relative evaluation, we instruct the annotators to independently rate the quality of
962
      each set of captions based on these four different criteria, by giving a score from 1 to 5 (i.e., choice:
      [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]).
963
964   Off-shelf Video Editing Models. We utilize TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023) and FateZero (Qi et al.,
965   2023) as our video editing tools. TokenFlow generates a high-quality video corresponding to the
966   target text, while preserving the spatial layout and motion of the input video. For SSIM computation,
967   we compute SSIM for region-of-no-interest since we want to keep those regions unchanged. we
968
      first mask out regions of interest with the ground truth mask provided by the DAVIS dataset, the we
      compute SSIM on masked images and conduct mean pooling as the video-level metrics.
969
970   Off-shelf Conditional Video Generation Models. We leverage both VideoCrafter (Chen et al.,
971   2023) and DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023) as our video generation backbone. VideoCrafter is
      one of the SoTA video generation models that can handle different input conditions (image, text).

       DynamiCrafter is based on the open-source VideoCrafter and T2I Latent Diffusion model (Rombach
973    et al., 2022), and was trained on WebVid10M (Bain et al., 2021), it provides better dynamic and
974    stronger coherence. We adopt VideoCrafter-512 and DynamiCrafter-512 variants. For each video, we
975    use CLIP similarity to retrieve multiple keyframes corresponding to each caption. Those keyframes
976    result in multiple generated video clips via the video generation model. For FVD computation, we

       B     L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
981
982
       Our proposed RACCooN framework has shown a remarkable ability to interpret input videos,
983
       producing well-structured and detailed descriptions that outperform strong video captioning baselines
984
       and even ground truths. However, it has the potential to produce inaccuracies or hallucination (Liu
985    et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023) in the generated text outputs.
986    In addition, the performance of our proposed framework in paragraph generation, video generation,
987    and editing is influenced by the employed pre-trained backbones, including an LLM (Touvron et al.,
988    2023), base Inpainting Model (Rombach et al., 2022), Video Diffusion Model (Xing et al., 2023),
989    and Video Editor (Geyer et al., 2023). However, our key contributions are independent of these
990    backbones, and we emphasize that RACCooN’s capabilities can be further enhanced with future
991    advancements in these generative model backbones.
992    LLM-empowered video description and photorealistic video creation/editing inherit biases from their
993    training data, leading to several broader impacts, including societal stereotypes, biased interpretation
994    of actions, and privacy concerns. To mitigate these broader impacts, it is essential to carefully develop
995    and implement generative and video description models, such as considering diversifying training
996    datasets, implementing fairness and bias evaluation metrics, and engaging communities to understand
997    and address their concerns.
