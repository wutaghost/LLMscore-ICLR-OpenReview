                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Machine learning models can be biased towards the solutions of given differential
010
               equations in two principal ways: through regularisation, or through architecture
011            design. Recent research has successfully constrained neural network architectures
012            to satisfy divergence-free fields and Laplace’s equation in two dimensions. This
013            work reinterprets these architectures as linear superpositions of general formu-
014            lated solutions. The notion of superposition is then exploited to develop novel
015            architectures which satisfy both these and novel differential equations. In addition
016            to new architectures for Laplace’s equation and divergence-free fields, we propose
017            novel constraints apt for the heat equation, and even some nonlinear differential
018            equations including Burgers’ equation. Benchmarks of superposition-based ap-
019
               proaches against previously published architectures and physics-informed regu-
               larisation approaches are presented. We find that embedding differential equation
020
               constraints directly into neural network architectures can lead to improved per-
021
               formance and hope our results motivate further development of neural networks
022            architectures developed to adhere specifically to given differential constraints.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   There has been fruitful interplay between the fields of machine learning and differential equation
028   solving. Differential equations provided useful means to machine learning: ordinary differential
029   equations (ODEs) appear in continuous depth neural networks (Chen et al., 2018) and stochastic
030   differential equations (SDEs) inspired novel approaches to generative modelling (Song et al., 2020).
031   On the other side, neural network architectures have also provided novel means to analyse and solve
032   differential equations, in particular, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Lagaris et al., 1998;
033
      Raissi et al., 2019) bias neural network outputs towards a given differential equation via construction
      of appropriate regularisation terms. Also, neural networks have been shown to be effective surrogate
034
      models of computationally-expensive, finite element solvers of partial differential equations (PDE)
035
      (Nabian & Meidani, 2018). In addition to providing novel means for solving forward problems
036   of differential equations, neural networks proved useful in solving inverse-problems in differential
037   equations (Lu et al., 2021b), data-driven discovery of differential equations (Both et al., 2021) and
038   approaches for optimal control (Mowlavi & Nabi, 2023).
039
      Thus far, physics-informed regularisation has been a dominant approach to biasing machine learn-
040
      ing models towards differential equations, being widely applied from PDEs and ODEs (Raissi et al.,
041
      2019) to fractional order differential equations (Pang et al., 2019). Despite their widespread ap-
042   plicability, the difficulty of training PINNs has been highlighted in many works that also highlight
043   potential means to improve their training. For example, via appropriate loss-scaling techniques
044   (Wang et al., 2021; Son et al., 2023), domain decomposition (Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020; Moseley
045   et al., 2023), combining gradient and Hessian-based optimisation procedures (Mao et al., 2020), and
046   relaxation of the underlying differential equation form (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021).
047
      It is yet unclear how to reliably train PINNs, or eventually provide convergence guarantees. There-
048   fore, recent research has started to move beyond PINNs, towards embedding target differential equa-
049   tions into underlying architectures directly. For example, neural conservation laws (NCLs), which
050   involve post-processing of multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) with higher-order derivatives to yield
051   divergence-free neural networks, ensure conservation principles by using divergence-free vector
052   fields (Richter-Powell et al., 2022). Holomorphic neural networks, which are complex-valued MLPs
053   with appropriate activation functions, have been shown to satisfy the Laplace equation (Ghosh et al.,
      2023). Additionally, neural networks satisfying Hamiltonian laws have been developed to preserve

      the total energy of a dynamical system over time (Greydanus et al., 2019). Beyond the context of
055   neural networks, priors for Gaussian processes over the space of solutions of given linear differential
056   equations have also been developed (Harkonen et al., 2023).
057
058   Contributions of this work This work presents a framework for constructing neural networks to
059   satisfy linear differential equations through the principle of superposition. Since the solution space
060   of homogeneous linear differential equations is closed under addition, we can derive single-layer
061   feedforward architectures that leverage a superposition of solutions as a learnable representation of
062   solution functions. We demonstrate how this framework provides a unifying perspective on pre-
063   viously published neural network architectures applying to divergence-free vector fields (Richter-
064   Powell et al., 2022) and Laplace’s equations (Ghosh et al., 2023). Novel architectures expanding on
065   such applications to encompass also the heat equation and Burgers’ equation are presented. Despite
066
      Burgers’ equation being nonlinear, we are able to construct appropriate architectures constrained to
      it via suitable post-processing of architectures satisfying the heat equation. We benchmark our archi-
067
      tectures against representative, standard architectures constrained to the same differential equations
068
      (where applicable) as well as physics-informed neural networks.

137   This section demonstrates that the principle of superposition provides a unifying perspective on
138   previous neural network architectures used to model divergence-free fields (Richter-Powell et al.,

141   unify our treatment of both architectures we introduce the notion of a linear postprocessing operator.
142

512   4     D ISCUSSION
513
514   Motivated by the difficulty of training physics-informed regularisation, this work has sought to em-
515   bed linear differential equation constraints within neural network architectures, and also theoreti-
516
      cally demonstrated the possibility of extending this to some non-linear differential equations. We
      suggested a systematic approach to further extend our approach to include additional differential
517
      operators, and hence ODE/PDEs, as well as illustrating ad-hoc constructions for notable cases. Cru-
518
      cially, ensuring convergence guarantees for equations at the interior of the domain might enable
519   the adoption of neural architectures in more critical applications than is currently targeted. Numer-
520   ical investigations support a perspective that favours embedding differential equations constraints
521   via architectural design rather than regularisation. However, we emphasise some limitations in our
522   approaches. While we present Lie-group symmetries and manufactured solutions of certain linear-
523   differential equations, we do not present methodical ways to derive such solutions for arbitrary
524   linear differential equations. In practice, for other diffrential equations, a practitioner might em-
525   ploy techniques such as a trial ansatz, using physically-motivated arguments, or attempting standard
526   approaches used in nonlinear differential equations (Hydon, 2000). We hope that our work moti-
527
      vates further research into embedding differential equation constraints directly into neural network
      architectures.
