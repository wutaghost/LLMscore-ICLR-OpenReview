013             Digital agents capable of automating complex computer tasks have attracted con-
014             siderable attention due to their immense potential to enhance human-computer
015             interaction. However, existing agent methods reveal deficiencies in their gener-
016

                puter tasks in real-world environments. Inspired by the rich functionality of the
017

018

019             powers users to integrate third-party agents, allowing the system to continuously

026             provement from 11.21% to 23.85% on the OSWorld benchmark, more than dou-

032   1    I NTRODUCTION

034   The continual evolution of computer Operating Systems (OS), along with proliferating applications,
035   has transformed how people work and live. This transformation goes beyond daily life like shopping
036   and gaming, encompassing professional works such as writing in Office or editing in Photoshop.
037   However, this increased functionality comes with a steep learning curve, often burdening users. As
038   a result, autonomous computer assistants—once limited to fiction like JARVIS in Iron Man or MOSS
039   in Wandering Earth—have become a concrete pursuit, attracting great interest from researchers.
040   Advancements in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; Reid et al., 2024),
041   are gradually turning this vision into reality. MLLM-based agents have already demonstrated re-
042   markable intelligence in handling complex tasks, benefiting from their strong capabilities in plan-
043   ning and reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023). Following this trend, using MLLMs to
044   build digital agents for automating computer tasks has become a promising direction (Zhang et al.,
045   2024a). However, real-world OS environments encompass a diverse array of open-ended computer
046   tasks, each with inherent requirements for capabilities across multi-dimensions (Xie et al., 2024),
047   posing substantial challenges to existing methods. Specifically, “Task 1” in Figure 1 illustrates that
048
      many computer tasks necessitate specific knowledge and operations. In such scenarios, existing
      generalist agents (Wu et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024) often underperform due to their lack of these
049
      specialized abilities. Conversely, specialized agents, despite excelling at specific tasks within single
050
      domains like tabular data processing (Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a) or web browsing (Zhou
051   et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024), cannot generalize across different applications or broader system en-

122
123   LLM-based Agents. Recent advancements in (M)LLMs (OpenAI, 2023; Reid et al., 2024) have
124   led to the development of highly capable AI agents, applied across various domains, including
125
      robotics (Driess et al., 2023), software development (Wang et al., 2024), and beyond. A rapidly
      growing research field among these is automating interactions with computer environments to solve
126
      complex tasks. Early work primarily focused on specific scenarios, such as web manipulation (Yao
127
      et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2024), command-line coding (Sun et al., 2024), and gaming (Wang et al.,
128   2023a). Following this, more recent methods (Wu et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024) have started explor-
129   ing general-purpose computer agents capable of interacting with diverse components of an operating
130   system. Unfortunately, both of these struggle with open-ended tasks in real environments, exposing
131   limitations in their generalization and specialization capabilities. To address these shortcomings,
132   this paper introduces AgentStore to build the specialized generalist computer assistant.
133
134   Multi-Agent Systems. Recently, various approaches (Park et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
135   2023; Hong et al., 2023) have been proposed to facilitate effective collaboration and communication
136   among multi-agent to overcome hallucinations, ensuring deterministic and trustworthy results.
137   While these approaches have shown promising results in domains such as automating coding, they
138   still exhibit two major limitations. First, by using a fixed number of agents with predefined roles,
139   they lack support for dynamically integrating agents. Second, their agents are usually homogeneous,
140   which limits agent diversity and consequently constrains their range of capabilities. Therefore, our
141   approach is designed to support the dynamic integration of a large number of third-party agents to
142   leverage their advantages in quantity and diversity. AgentStore expands the capability boundaries of
143   current multi-agent systems.

192   3.2       M ETA AGENT WITH AGENT T OKEN
193
194
      We employ the powerful open-source MLLM as the foundation for our MetaAgent M . This enables
      it to process multi-modal information covering task descriptions and OS states. Given the set of all
195
      enrolled agents A, the goal of MetaAgent is to call a subset of these agents to automate computer
196
      tasks. Since the number of agents in AgentStore dynamically grows and reaches a large scale, com-
197   mon methods like In-Context Learning (ICL) (Chase, 2022; Li et al., 2023; Suzgun & Kalai, 2024)
198   and full Fine-Tuning (FT) (Qin et al., 2023) become impractical due to the excessive context length
199   and the high cost of retraining, respectively. Therefore, we propose the AgentToken strategy, which
200   eliminates the need for lengthy contexts and significantly reduces the cost of retraining MetaAgent
201   whenever a new agent is added.
202
      Inspired by ToolkenGPT (Hao et al., 2024), which captures tool semantics using special tokens,
203   AgentToken extends this concept by encoding enrolled agents as special tokens in the MetaAgent’s
204   vocabulary. Specifically, the agent tokens are parameterized as an embedding matrix WA ∈ R|A|×d
205   and appended to the original word token head Wν ∈ R|V|×d . Assuming the agent tokens WA
206   have been trained and available (as described in Section 3.3), the concatenated result forms the new
207   language modeling head of MetaAgent. In this way, MetaAgent predicts the next token with the
208   following probability:
209                                 PM (ti |t<i ) = softmax([Wν ; WA ] · hi−1 ),
210   where the next token can be either a word token or an agent token, i.e., ti ∈ V ∪A,. The operation [; ]
211   denotes concatenation, and hi−1 ∈ Rd represents the last hidden state. In this context, AgentToken
212   enables MetaAgent to fulfill its two primary functions:
213
      MetaAgent as Router: Following the above manner, the most probable next token is obtained by
214   maximizing the conditional probability:
215
                                                              t∗i = arg maxt∈V∪A (PM (ti |t<i )) .

      Once an agent token is predicted, i.e., t∗i ∈ A, the MetaAgent halts decoding, and the corresponding
217   agent is invoked to execute the task. As illustrated in Figure 2, the above method enables MetaAgent
218   to act as an efficient router, predicting the most appropriate agent to complete a task when a single
219   agent is sufficient. However, many complex tasks require the collaboration of multiple agents. To
220   address this, we extend the method by introducing a Manager mode.
221
      MetaAgent as Hash Manager: We discover that, although each agent token is trained on individual
222
      tasks, they exhibit generalization capabilities for complex, collaborative tasks. Specifically, when
223   a task requires multiple agents, the trained agent tokens often appear among the top candidates
224   in the next token predictions. This observation led us to enhance this approach by shifting from
225   single-token to multi-token prediction:
226
                                      Ti∗ = TopKt∈A (PM (ti |t<i ), K) ,
227
228   where TopK(·) is a function that returns the set of K tokens from the vocabulary A that have the
229
      highest probabilities. These predicted tokens represent the K agents most relevant to this task.
      The MetaAgent then switches to Manager mode by using a new prompt consisting of in-context
230
      documents for these selected agents, outlining how to generate subtasks for the complex task and
231
      assign them to the corresponding agents. Unlike previous methods that rely entirely on ICL for
232   management, our method narrows the management scope to a few selected agents, leaving ample
233   context space for detailed documentation of these fixed agents. This design shares similarities with
234   hashing methods (Aggarwal & Verma, 2015), which convert inputs of arbitrary size into fixed-size
235   outputs to facilitate retrieval and other operations. Therefore, we refer to this approach as MetaAgent
236   as Hash Manager. It is important to note that the selection for the router and manager mode can be
237   either manual or automatic. In the automatic setting, MetaAgent follows chain-of-thought (CoT; Wei
238   et al., 2022), analyzing the given task to determine which mode to select and then switching to either
239   router or manager. The base MetaAgent performs sufficiently well in making this binary decision
240
      without additional training.

242   3.3   T RAINING AGENT T OKEN WITH SELF-INSTRUCT
243
      The embedding WA corresponding to agent tokens are the only tunable parameters, introducing
244
      minimal additional training overhead. However, training these agent tokens requires a number of
245   agent demonstrations that consist of the task descriptions and initial OS states. The corresponding
246   token demonstrations were pre-collected for training in previous efforts (Hao et al., 2024; Chai
247   et al., 2024). However, this strategy is not applicable in our scenario, as developers only provide
248   a document about the agent, and it is unrealistic to expect them to supply massive demonstrations.
249   Therefore, we propose an automated process with self-instruct (Wang et al., 2023c) for tuning these
250   tokens using demonstrations from the MetaAgent itself.
251   The overall process follows an iterative algorithm to guide the generation of extra demonstrations,
252                                                                        ni
      beginning with a limited set of original demonstrations Si = {(yk )}k=1    and the agent description
253   ci provided in document di . Specifically, we first prompt MetaAgent with existing demonstrations
254   and agent descriptions:
255                                               Si′ = M (Si , ci ),
256   where MetaAgent M is expected to produce the new set of demonstrations Si′ . Following this,
257   to ensure the quality of the generated outputs, we apply BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) to all
258   newly generated outputs y ′ ∈ Si′ , ensuring both consistency and diversity. Specifically, we use a
259   greedy algorithm (see Appendix C) to iteratively filter elements from Si′ , resulting in a refined set
260   Sinew ⊆ Si′ . The new set satisfies the following conditions:
261                   τ1 ≤ BETRScore(yk , yj ) ≤ τ2 ,       ∀yk , yj ∈ Si ∪ Sinew and k ̸= j,
262
      where BETRScore(·) represents the similarity between two demonstrations, with imposing a lower
263   bound τ1 to avoid overly irrelevant outputs and τ2 ensuring diversity among them. In this way, we
264   automatically filter the generated data, and the refined set is merged, i.e., Si = Si ∪ Sinew .
265
266

      examples based on the augmented Si , with BERTScore guiding and filtering the outputs until a
267
      sufficient number of demonstrations are generated to meet the training requirements for AgentToken.

      layout and forwarding settings, posing challenges for those unfamiliar with email systems. How-

515
      navigates the software, knowing the exact steps to configure the forwarding settings. In particular,
516   during the Step3, it executes a sequence of actions to accurately fill out the required forms and op-
517   tions, showcasing its advanced understanding and processing capabilities within the mail domain.
518   Similarly, in Example 2, which requires complex processing of a spreadsheet, MetaAgent selects
519   the SheetAgent from the AgentPool to handle the task, avoiding overly complex GUI interactions.
520   SheetAgent possesses knowledge of “openpyxl” and a deep understanding of the steps needed to
521   manipulate sheets, efficiently completing this task that is too challenging for previous generalist
522   agents (Xie et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024). In addition, Example 3 illustrates a system-wide task that
523   requires collaboration among multiple agents. MetaAgent successfully decomposes the task into
524
      subtasks and assigns the appropriate agents to complete each one. This demonstrates AgentStore’s
      ability to perceive the overall task structure, overcoming the limitations of isolated, single-specialist
525
      agents and showcasing its strong generalization capability. In summary, these examples highlight
526
      AgentStore’s specialized generalist abilities in handling not only domain-specific but also system-
527   wide tasks, underscoring its potential for building a specialized generalist computer assistant.

       C    AUTOMATED PROCESS WITH SELF - INSTRUCT
1135
1136
1137
1138   In this section, we provide more details about the Automated data generation process, including
1139   threshold selection and the greedy filtering algorithm.
1140
1141
1142
1143   Threshold Selection To ensure the reliability of threshold selection, we first studied the distribu-
1144   tion of thresholds in real-world tasks based on human-labeled standards. As shown in Figure 7, in
1145   tasks labeled by OSworld, the 95% threshold distribution of BertScore across different domains is
1146   primarily concentrated between 0.77 and 0.92. Therefore, to further strictly control the quality of
1147   generated data, we ultimately selected a threshold of 0.8 for τ1 and 0.9 for τ2 to filter the data.
1148   This approach offers several advantages. By selecting thresholds of 0.8 for τ1 and 0.9 for τ2 , we
1149   strike a balance between retaining high-quality data and ensuring the diversity necessary for robust
1150   training. The τ1 threshold helps in eliminating low-quality samples, while τ2 enforces stricter criteria
1151   for the final selection of data, ensuring that only the most relevant and high-quality data points are
1152   used. This dual-threshold filtering process not only improves the precision of the generated data but
1153   also enhances the overall performance of agent training, reducing the risk of overfitting to noise or
1154   irrelevant tasks.
1155
1156
1157
1158   Greedy Filtering Algorithm Algorithm 1 presents a greedy algorithm for filtering a set of newly
1159   generated demonstrations, Si′ , ensuring that each selected demonstration maintains a BERTScore
1160   similarity within the specified bounds τ1 and τ2 relative to both existing demonstrations Si and
1161   previously selected new demonstrations Sinew . The key improvement lies in the prioritization of
1162   demonstrations that are optimally positioned between the two thresholds, thereby enhancing both
1163   relevance and diversity.
1164   A prioritization mechanism selects demonstrations optimally positioned between the similarity
1165   thresholds. By calculating the minimum distance of each candidate’s BERTScore to the thresh-
1166   olds, the algorithm ensures that selected demonstrations are neither too similar nor too dissimilar to
1167   existing ones. This strategic ordering facilitates the inclusion of the most appropriate demonstrations
1168   first, thereby maximizing both the relevance and diversity of the refined set Sinew . Consequently,
1169   the quality of the training data for AgentToken is significantly improved, fostering more effective
1170   training outcomes.

1465   Evaluation metrics We propose three metrics for evaluation: AgentMatch, SubtaskAcc, and Ex-
1466   ecutionAcc, which respectively measure multi-agent prediction accuracy, subtask decomposition
1467   accuracy, and execution success rate.
1468   AgentMatch is designed to assess the accuracy of the agent prediction process during collaborative
1469   task execution. It compares the predicted set of agents selected by the MetaAgent with the ground
1470   truth set of agents that are required for successful task completion. Essentially, AgentMatch mea-
1471   sures how well the MetaAgent can correctly identify the appropriate agents from the AgentPool for a
1472   given task. The metric is computed by calculating the accuracy of the predicted agent set relative to
1473   the actual agents involved in the task. Specifically, it checks whether the predicted agents match the
1474   expected agents. A high AgentMatch score indicates that the MetaAgent is effectively coordinating
1475   and predicting the correct agents for task execution.
1476   SubtaskAcc is an evaluation metric that measures the accuracy of task decomposition by comparing
1477   the predicted subtasks assigned to each agent with the ground truth subtasks. It evaluates how well
1478   the MetaAgent decomposes a given task and assigns the correct subtasks to the respective agents.
1479   To assess SubtaskAcc, we use a textual comparison between the predicted subtasks and the actual
1480   subtasks for the same agent. This comparison is based on textual similarity, using BERTScore as
1481   the evaluation metric. As per our analysis in C, if the BERTScore is below 0.77, the two sub-
1482
       tasks are considered too dissimilar, and the decomposition is deemed unsuccessful. Conversely,
       if the BERTScore exceeds this threshold, the decomposition is considered accurate. This thresh-
1483
       old ensures that only decompositions with sufficiently high textual similarity are counted as correct.
1484
       SubtaskAcc thus reflects how effectively the MetaAgent can break down a complex task and allocate
1485   the correct components to individual agents. A high SubtaskAcc score indicates that the MetaAgent
1486   is accurately identifying the required subtasks for each agent, contributing to the overall success of
1487   the collaborative task execution.
1488
       ExecutionAcc is an evaluation metric that measures the success rate of task execution by reusing
1489
       the assessment methods from OSworld. This metric focuses on determining whether the predicted
1490
       subtasks are correctly executed by the agents, based on their final state in the environment.
1491
1492   To evaluate ExecutionAcc, we rely on OSworld’s system of getter and evaluator functions. The
1493   getter function extracts key components from the final state of the environment (e.g., a modified file
1494
       or text contents displayed in a window element), while the evaluator function assesses success based
       on these extracted components. If a necessary function does not exist, it is constructed and added
1495
       to the function library of the environment. Each task is evaluated by comparing its final execution
1496
       state with the expected outcome, and the evaluation process is designed to be robust.
1497
1498   In the context of our system, ExecutionAcc provides a direct measure of how successfully the agents
1499   complete their assigned tasks, reflecting the practical performance of task execution in real-world
1500
       scenarios. A high ExecutionAcc indicates that the agents are accurately following the predicted
       subtasks and completing them correctly in the environment.
