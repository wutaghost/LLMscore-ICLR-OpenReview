011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized various domains but face
014            significant challenges in tackling optimization modeling tasks for Operations Re-
015            search (OR) problems, particularly when dealing with complex problem. In this
016            work, we propose Evo-Step-Instruct, a framework that augments existing datasets
017            and generates high-quality fine-tuning data tailored to OR modeling tasks. Evo-
018
               Step-Instruct employs iterative problem generation to progressively increase prob-
               lem complexity and stepwise validation to rigorously validate data, preventing er-
019
               ror propagation and ensuring the quality of the generated dataset. Leveraging
020
               this framework, we fine-tune open-source LLMs, including LLaMA-3-8B and
021            Mistral-7B, to develop Evo-Step—a model that achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
022            mance on benchmarks such as NL4OPT, MAMO, and IndustryOR. Extensive ex-
023            periments demonstrate the superior performance of Evo-Step, especially in ad-
024            dressing complex OR tasks, with a notable 17.01% improvement in micro aver-
025            age accuracy on difficult problems. These findings highlight the effectiveness of
026            combining structured validation with gradual problem refinement to advance the
027            automation of decision-making processes using LLMs. The code and dataset are
028            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Evo-Step-F5AB.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Operations Research (OR) is a valuable discipline for addressing complex decision-making prob-
034   lems, widely applied in fields such as economics, engineering, and computer science Bertsimas et al.
035   (2019); Pereira et al. (2022); Belgacem et al. (2020). Effective implementation of OR involves two
036   essential steps: modeling real-world problems and solving them. Despite significant advancements
037   in solution techniques and the development of more efficient solvers, the construction of appropriate
038   models remains a considerable challenge. Such a task requires formulating natural language de-
039
      scriptions into precise mathematical models, which is labor-intensive and demands domain-specific
      expertise as well as a deep understanding of modeling methodologies. These requirements greatly
040
      restrict the broader application of OR, particularly in real-world scenarios.
041
042   Recent developments in Large Language Models (LLMs) have enhanced the feasibility of automat-
043   ing optimization modeling. Approaches like Chain-of-Experts (CoE) Xiao et al. (2023) and Op-
044   tiMUS AhmadiTeshnizi et al. (2024) employ well-crafted prompts and multi-agent systems to en-
045
      hance the construction of optimization models and corresponding programs. However, these ap-
      proaches rely on general-purpose LLMs, which, though powerful, are not specifically tailored for
046
      OR, limiting their effectiveness in addressing specialized challenges. Additionally, the need to up-
047
      load sensitive data poses additional privacy concerns. In response, ORLM Tang et al. (2024) presents
048   an alternative by fine-tuning open-source LLMs using a dataset of 30K examples generated from
049   686 industry cases. While this improves the model’s performance for OR modeling, ORLM re-
050   mains semi-automated, requiring significant manual post-processing to achieve satisfactory results.
051   Moreover, its prompt design lacks the precision needed to manage problem complexity and diver-
052   sity, resulting in suboptimal outputs. Furthermore, modeling errors are not identified in real-time,
053   allowing inaccuracies to persist and propagate. While rule-based post-processing can address minor
      errors, it often fails to rectify deeper logical and structural issues, further compromising data quality.

      Utilizing high-quality training data is vital for improving the modeling capabilities of LLMs. How-
079   ever, current methods not only rely heavily on manual post-processing but also struggle to ensure
080   data reliability. To address these limitations, we propose an approach from two primary perspec-
081   tives. First, we enhance the prompt design and introduce an Iterative Problem Generation, as shown
082   in Figure 1. This method incrementally increases the complexity and scope of the problems, allow-
083   ing the dataset to retain varying levels of difficulty and breadth. This diversity plays a crucial role
084   in improving the model’s generalization capabilities, as WizardLM Xu et al. (2024) suggested. Sec-
085   ond, we incorporate a stepwise validation mechanism that performs real-time checks throughout the
086   generation process, effectively filtering out low-quality or erroneous data. This prevents errors from
087
      entering and propagating through the seed dataset. We refer to this framework as Evo-Step-Instruct.
      Our framework eliminates the need for post-processing, enabling fully automated generation while
088
      reducing API costs by utilizing only high-quality data for future iterations.
089
090   Evo-Step-Instruct consists of two key components: Iterative Problem Generation and Stepwise Val-
091   idation Mechanism. The Iterative Problem Generation is specifically designed to address the unique
092   challenges of OR-specific tasks, such as complex variable definitions and strict constraint imple-
093
      mentation. By employing tailored methods such as Complexity-Evolving and Scope-Evolving, this
      approach generates a dataset enriched with enhanced complexity and diversity from the given one,
094
      facilitating the fine-tuning of LLMs to enhance their modeling ability for OR problems. As il-
095
      lustrated in Figure 1, Complexity-Evolving increases the complexity of the problem refining con-
096   straints, objectives, or parameters, while Scope-Evolving expands linguistic diversity and problem
097   scope by adapting problems to new contexts or merging scenarios. This approach ensures that the
098   generated dataset captures a wide range of complexities and provides robust coverage.
099
      As new generated problems become increasingly complex, current LLMs often struggle to solve
100
      them accurately, resulting in errors. If these errors remain undetected and uncorrected, they will
101
      propagate through the iterative process, ultimately affecting the quality of the generated data. To
102   mitigate this, the stepwise validation mechanism is implemented to not only prevent errors but also
103   guarantee the accurate application of essential modeling techniques. Problems are first validated via
104   a description checker for completeness, followed by checks on variables, constraints, and programs.
105   Identified issues are resolved via feedback loops, with advanced techniques like the Big-M method
106   verified using specially designed prompts that guide the LLM step-by-step to confirm accurate im-
107   plementation. This validation process enables the generation of reliable and high-quality datasets,
      which are crucial for fine-tuning LLMs and enhancing their modeling ability for OR problems.

      LLM-based Automated Modeling for OR is an emerging field that uses LLMs to generate math-
      ematical models for OR problems. Existing methods can be categorized into prompt-engineering
132
      and fine-tuning. Approaches like Chain-of-Thought Wei et al. (2022) and Reflexion Shinn et al.
133
      (2024) improve performance but are not specialized for OR. More advanced methods, including
134   OptiGuide Li et al. (2023a), Chain-of-Experts Xiao et al. (2023), and OptiMUS AhmadiTeshnizi
135   et al. (2024), employ multi-agent systems with GPT to construct models but encounter difficulties
136   with complex problems due to GPT’s limitations. ORLM Tang et al. (2024), conversely, utilizes
137   a large dataset generated from industry cases and GPT-4, coupled with rule-based post-processing,
138   to fine-tune LLMs and improve outcomes. However, it lacks precise prompt design and effective
139   filtering mechanisms. Our framework addresses these limitations by iterative-based generation and
140   real-time validation to control complexity and minimize errors, thereby enhancing performance.
141   Data Augmentation improves LLM performance by generating synthetic datasets, often used when
142   real-world data is insufficient for complex tasksWang et al. (2022); An et al. (2023); Gandhi et al.
143   (2024); Oh et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024); Pan et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024). In operations
144   research, data augmentation approaches like Prasath & Karande (2023); Li et al. (2023b) focus on
145   synthesizing optimization problems from natural language descriptions, but with limited complexity.
146   ORLM Tang et al. (2024) expands industry case datasets through modifications and rephrasings,
147   while ReSocratic Yang et al. (2024) takes a reverse data synthesis approach, generating optimization
148
      scenarios from solutions. Among all these works, the closest to ours is Evol-Instruct Xu et al. (2024),
      which uses In-depth Evolving and In-breadth Evolving to generate instruction data. However, as
149
      OR modeling presents unique challenges, we propose a stepwise validation mechanism to ensure
150
      accuracy and avoid error propagation in generated data.

197   3.2   G ENERATORS
198
199   The problem generator adopts an iterative problem generation methodology, progressively produc-
200   ing problems with increasing complexity and diversity. In each iteration, a seed data (qs , ms ) is
201   randomly sampled. A specific evolving method, denoted as fe , is then applied to create a new prob-
202   lem description qn = fe (qs ). This process employs prompt-based LLM methods to refine problem
203   descriptions and systematically expand their scope. These methods can be categorized into two
204   types: Complexity-Evolving and Scope-Evolving, as detailed below.
205   Complexity-Evolving enhances problem complexity by modifying existing conditions or introduc-
206   ing new elements. In response to the specific characteristics of OR problems, three main approaches
207   are included: constraint modification, objective alteration, and parameter adjustment. These meth-
208   ods incrementally raise the complexity while maintaining the problem’s logical integrity.
209
      Constraint modification involves revising existing constraints or adding new ones to enhance the
210   problem, with the core principle being to ”modify constraints based on the given problem while
211   retaining its logical structure.” This ensures that the essential logic of the problem remains intact
212   as complexity increases. Similarly, objective alteration either modifies existing objectives or intro-
213   duces new ones, and we limit that the modifications cannot merely change to coefficients. Parameter
214   adjustment changes values or adds additional elements. These approaches, while tailored to specific
215   contexts, follow the common principle of preserving the underlying structure. Together, they en-
      hance the difficulty of the problem from various perspectives.

      Nevertheless, the generated problems may become so complex that they exceed the processing ca-
237   pabilities of LLMs. To manage this, modifications to constraints or objectives are limited to one at
238   a time, and parameter adjustments introduce at most one new entity per iteration. Figure 3 provides
239   an example of a prompt for constraint modification, where the red text highlights the specific lim-
240   itations applied to manage the increase in complexity. These restrictions ensure a balanced dataset
241   with problems of varying complexity and excludes excessively challenging examples, enhancing the
242   model’s generalization capabilities. Additional prompts available in Appendix A.3.
243   Scope-Evolving broadens topic coverage and diversity by transforming the seed example into a
244   different domain or by combining it with another example to create a novel scenario. Domain trans-
245   formation transfers the fundamental structure of the original problem to a new application domain,
246   while preserving its logical structure and constraints, thereby increasing linguistic and contextual
247   diversity. To ensure practical relevance, we define a list of domains as references. Alternatively,
248   the combination approach merges two distinct problems to create a new one, with the requirement
249   that the resulting problem belongs to a different domain and contains unique details. This approach
250   introduces more significant changes. To control the increased complexity, the new problem is re-
251
      quired to be of a similar length to one of the original problems, maintaining manageable difficulty.
      The prompt templates for Scope-Evolving are provided in Appendix A.4.
252
253   As the Complexity- and Scope-Evolving progress, the complexity, scope, and diversity of the gen-
254   erated data expand, ensuring comprehensive coverage across multiple dimensions. Additionally, all
255   the approaches are implemented using two-shot examples to maintain consistency.
256   Solution generator g produces a corresponding mathematical model and program mn for a valid
257   problem description qn . It generates mn = g(qn , qs , ms , fe ) by using qs , ms and evolving method
258   fe as references. Since LLMs may struggle with complex models, we specifically embed the in-
259   struction ”ensuring the format and structure are as consistent as possible with the provided qs and
260   ms ” directly into the meta-prompt to enforce consistency.

      5    C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we present Evo-Step-Instruct, a novel framework that integrates iterative problem gen-
527
      eration with a stepwise validation mechanism to enhance the capabilities of LLMs in addressing
528   complex OR problems. By progressively increasing problem complexity and ensuring data quality
529   through real-time validation, Evo-Step-Instruct effectively prevents error propagation by identifying
530   and removing low-quality data during the generation process. This proactive approach enables full
531   automation without relying on post-processing, ensuring high-quality datasets for fine-tuning. The
532   resulting model, Evo-Step, achieved significant performance improvements across benchmarks such
533   as NL4OPT, MAMO, and IndustryOR, particularly excelling in complex optimization tasks. These
534   results highlight the effectiveness of combining systematic problem generation with structured vali-
535   dation to significantly enhance the modeling capabilities of LLMs.
536   Limitations: The proposed method faces difficulties in dealing with the wide variety of model-
537   ing techniques commonly used in OR, which limits its ability to handle the full range of possible
538   scenarios. Moreover, the performance of the approach has not been fully tested across all types of
539   OR problems. Finally, its broader application still needs to be tested in other fields to validate its
      applicability and adaptability.
