009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Sample selection is the most straightforward technique to combat noisy labels,
012            aiming to prevent mislabeled samples from degrading the robustness of neural net-
013            works. However, compounding selection bias and redundant selection operations
014            have always remained challenging in robustness and efficiency. To mitigate selec-
015            tion bias, existing methods utilize disagreement in partner networks or additional
016            forward propagation in a single network. For selection operations, they involve
017            dataset-wise modeling or batch-wise ranking. Any of the above methods yields sub-
018            optimal performance. In this work, we propose Jump-teaching, a novel framework
019            for optimizing the typical workflow of sample selection. Firstly, Jump-teaching is
020
               the first work to discover significant disagreements within a single network between
               different training iterations. Based on this discovery, we propose a jump-manner
021
               strategy for model updating to bridge the disagreements. We further illustrate its
022
               effectiveness from the perspective of error flow. Secondly, Jump-teaching designs
023            a lightweight plugin to simplify selection operations. It creates a detailed yet
024            simple loss distribution on an auxiliary encoding space, which helps select clean
025            samples more effectively. In the experiments, Jump-teaching not only outperforms
026            state-of-the-art works in terms of robustness, but also reduces peak memory usage
027            by 0.46⇥ and boosts training speed by up to 2.53⇥. Notably, existing methods can
028            also benefit from the integration with our framework.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Learning with Noisy Labels (LNL) is the most promising technique in weakly supervised learning.
034
      Generally, noisy labels stem from mistaken annotations of the dataset, such as in crowd-sourcing
035
      (Welinder et al., 2010) and online query (Blum et al., 2003). As accurate annotations of large datasets
036   are a time-consuming endeavor, the existence of noisy labels becomes inevitable. Deep neural
037   networks can easily overfit to noisy labels, which is prone to poor generalization performance (Zhang
038   et al., 2021; Han et al., 2020). Furthermore, the efficiency challenge of LNL is often overlooked in
039   comparison to the robustness problem (Bakhshi and Can, 2024), which is vital in real-time (Mahajan
040   et al., 2018; Bakhshi and Can, 2024) or edge security scenarios (Aït-Sahalia et al., 2010). Therefore,
041   this work aims at finding an LNL solution characterized by efficiency and robustness.
042   Recent LNL methods can be categorized into three types: regularization, label correction, and sample
043   selection. Regularization methods focus on crafting noise-robust loss functions (Ghosh et al., 2017;
044   Wang et al., 2019) and regularization techniques (Liu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Cao et al.,
045   2021), but they cannot fully avoid fitting to noisy labels during training, resulting in sub-optimal
046   outcomes. Label correction, integrating closely with semi-supervised learning, aims to refine or
047   recreate pseudo-labels (Han et al., 2019; Sohn et al., 2020; Pham et al., 2021). These methods make
048   use of corrected noisy labels but require computational resources for the estimation of noise transition
049   matrix (Goldberger and Ben-Reuven, 2022) or the ensemble prediction (Lu and He, 2022).
050   Sample selection is a direct approach for combating label noise (Song et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021;
051   Wu et al., 2020; Malach and Shalev-Shwartz, 2017; Xia et al., 2023). It typically operates in an
052   iterative workflow, selecting possibly clean samples through a certain process and then updating the
053   parameters based on those samples. However, compounding selection bias and redundant selection
      operations hinder the effectiveness of workflow.

      Firstly, selection bias inevitably arises from the exposure of the classifier to noise. This bias causes
      noisy samples to be included in the training data. When the neural network trains on these data,
071
      the error erodes the robustness of the network, thus the bias is amplified. To mitigate this bias,
072
      several approaches have been proposed, including Decoupling (Malach and Shalev-Shwartz, 2017),
073   Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018), and Co-teaching+ (Yu et al., 2019), among others. These methods
074   follow a similar paradigm: a partner network is integrated to give different predicted labels from
075   the original network. These disagreements, referring to the existence of differences of networks in
076   the selection behaviors, seek to diverge in each sample selection and guide the training process by
077   model update. However, the extra network commonly used in such methods requires double memory
078   and computation resources. Notably, some methods that use a single network implicitly mitigate the
079   bias by reducing the frequency of conducting selections, but they require additional forward passes
080   through the entire dataset before training begins, which increases computational cost (Yuan et al.,
081
      2023; Wu et al., 2020).
082   Secondly, current selection operations are redundant. The redundancy is evident in the repeated need
083   to aggregate amounts of data for batch processing, which leads to inefficiencies. Specifically, some
084   methods (Han et al., 2018) rely on a small-loss criterion that ranks samples within each batch by their
085   loss magnitude, prioritizing those with smaller losses for training. Other methods (Li et al., 2020)
086   regard the sample selection as a binary classification problem, where the distribution of sample losses
087
      is modeled by techniques like Gaussian mixture models (GMM). The core reason for this redundancy
      lies in the limited information provided by the classification head. The loss is typically represented as
088
      a floating-point value, calculated from the discrepancy between predicted probabilities and one-hot
089
      encoded labels. It fails to provide enough meaningful information to guide effective sample selection.
090   Consequently, redundant operations are required to compensate for this lack of granularity.
091
092   In this paper, we propose an ultra robust and efficient framework Jump-teaching that optimizes the
093
      workflow of the sample selection approach. Its effectiveness comes from two aspects: firstly, it adopts
      a jump-manner strategy to mitigate selection bias within a single network. Secondly, it designs a
094
      lightweight plugin for a simplified sample-wise selection operation. The overview of Jump-teaching
095
      is shown in Fig. 1. Jump-update Strategy is motivated by the discovery of significant disagreement
096   between different training iterations of a neural network, excluding neighboring iterations, from a
097   temporal perspective in Fig. 2. Therefore, this intrinsic disagreement enables the strong self-correction
098   of selection bias. Moreover, we design a lightweight plugin for efficient selection. As shown in
099   Fig. 1, it contains an auxiliary head and a prepared codebook, which transform the outputs and labels
100   into semantic embeddings and hash coding, respectively, within an auxiliary space. When training
101   on clean data, the goal of this plugin is to minimize the gap between semantic features and hash
102   coding as close to 0 as possible. The ideal distribution of elements of this vector is a mean value
103   distribution with a variance of 0. Conversely, for noisy data, this gap becomes too large to reach 0,
104
      and its distribution is uncertain. According to the aforementioned characteristics, we can easily and
      effectively distinguish between clean and noisy samples.
105

107   persistent disagreement within a single network. Based on this discovery, we propose the jump-update
      strategy for strong self-correction of bias. The strategy enables a single network to surpass the capacity

136   Sample Selection. The core idea of the sample selection approach is to filter out noisy samples to
137   prevent the network from fitting to them. Since the loss of a single sample is insufficient for selection,
138   current approaches require additional operations. Most works employ a ranking operation with prior
139   knowledge of the noise ratio, e.g., Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018) and Co-teacing+ (Yu et al., 2019),
140
      or probabilistic estimation on loss value, such as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) (Permuter et al.,
      2006) and Beta Mixture Models (BMM) (Ma and Leijon, 2011), while some methods rely on a
141
      complex statistical estimation process (Li et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2020; Arazo et al., 2019).
142
143   Sample-selection Bias. Sample selection inherently involves bias, leading to error accumulation.
144   To avoid this, many methods employ dual networks and correct this bias through disagreement in
145   their selection. Decoupling (Malach and Shalev-Shwartz, 2017) utilizes a teacher model to select
146
      clean samples to guide the learning of a student model, Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018) simultaneously
      trains two networks on data selected by peer network. Co-teaching+ (Yu et al., 2019) maintains
147
      divergence between the two networks by limiting the training data to samples where the networks
148
      disagree. JoCoR (Wei et al., 2020) trains two networks with the same data and employs regularization
149   to remain divergent. Additionally, many LNL methods integrate the co-training framework to achieve
150   advanced performance (Li et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; 2022; Chen et al., 2023). However, these
151   co-training methods significantly double computational overhead. Although some works have utilized
152   predictions in different iterations as selection criteria (Wei et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023), no related
153   work has explicitly addressed bias in a single network. Some implicit methods will be elaborated on
154   Sec. 3.

      3.1       J UMP - UPDATE S TRATEGY
183
184
      Inspired by the observation in Fig. 2, we present the Jump-update strategy, a jump-manner model
185
      update paradigm. This strategy aims at bridging the disagreement between iterations. First, we
186
      provide a detailed description of the strategy. Afterward, we conduct an empirical analysis to explore
187   its debiasing principles and verify our insights in the experimental analysis.
188
189   Strategy Description. To simplify our discussion, we dive into the procedure of sample selection
190
      and give some definitions. Sample selection is accomplished by the execution of model updates
      and sample selection with multiple iterations. In other words, model updates and sample selection
191
      are performed only once in every iteration. Previous strategies and ours are compared in Fig.
192
      3(a). Suppose ti denotes the current i-th iteration, ancestor iteration refers to the former iteration
193   tj , 0  j  i 2, excluding the previous (i 1)-th iteration, during the procedure of sample
194   selection. Similarly, descendant iteration denotes the future iteration ts , i + 1  s  Niterations .
195   Niterations is the total number of training iterations. The ancestor iteration and descendant iteration will
196   not appear in the same epoch. In our paradigm, the current network in the i-th iteration is trained with
197   clean samples selected only by the network from an ancestor iteration tj . This behavior of sample
198   selection exhibits a jump form. The name of the algorithm is derived from this point. Concretely,
199   we leverage a binary identifier to represent the outcome of the label judgment after clean sample
200   selection. Thus, a binary identifier table I corresponds to the entire data. The jump-update strategy
201
      is divided into four steps: 1) The neural network in current interaction ti generates the new binary
      identifiers by the clean sample selection for the descendant iteration ts . 2) We fetch and cache the old
202
      binary identifier table from the ancestor iteration tj . 3) The network updates the parameters based
203
      on the clean data judged only by the old table. Before the update, the network inherits the weight
204   from the previous iteration ti 1 . 4) We utilize the identifiers generated to update the table. When
205   the jump-update strategy is applied, disagreements between different training iterations of a neural
206   network appear and are bridged, leading to a decrease in the accumulated error. Suppose S denotes
207   the jump steps, it should be noted that 2  S  Niterations . The evidence of disagreement and the
208   setting of S are illustrated in Sec. 4.2. Moreover, we provide an example for describing this strategy
209   in Appendix A.3.
210   Empirical Analysis. Sample selection inevitably has a bias, leading to accumulated error in an
211   error flow. As shown in Fig. 3(b), the graph of error flow presents a sequential form by iterations
212   while the jump-update strategy splits a sequential error flow into multiple error sub-flows. Intuitively,
213   the more training iterations increase, the more rapidly the error is accumulated. Nonetheless, the
214   degree of error accumulation in the two is significantly different. In the sequential form, errors are
215   accumulated consecutively. As the error sub-flows are orthogonal in the jump-update strategy, each
      error is accumulated only in its own error sub-flow. Thus, the jump-update strategy has a significantly

      smaller degree of accumulated error compared to the sequential form. This is the source of the magic
217   of the jump-update strategy. We formalize the aforementioned procedure mathematically below and
218   give some detailed properties.
219
220
      Suppose that NA is the total number of error accumulations, Na is the number of error accumulations
      in an error sub-flow, and Nf is the number of error sub-flows. Besides, the constant e represents the
221
      number of training epochs and n represents how many selections are made in one epoch. DA denotes
222
      the overall degree of accumulated error, while dka denotes the degree of accumulated error in the k-th
223   error sub-flow. In the absence of a specific reference, we can also denote the degree of accumulated
224   error in one error sub-flow by da .
225
226   Property 1 The overall degree of accumulated error DA is proportional to the total number of error
227   accumulation NA , DA / NA . Under the hypothesis that the error flow is an uninterrupted model,
228   the number of error accumulation NA equals the total number of training iterations Niterations , while
229   Niterations equals e ⇥ n. Therefore, DA / n.
230
      The overall degree of accumulated error DA depends on n stated in Property 1. When the network
231
      selects data from a mini-batch, DA can be enormous because n is equal to the number of mini-batches,
232   e.g., Co-teaching. If the network selects data from the entire dataset, n can be reduced to 1, thereby
233   significantly reducing DA , e.g., TopoFilter (Wu et al., 2020) and LateStopping (Yuan et al., 2023).
234   However, this is inefficient because an additional forward over the entire dataset before training is
235   necessitated. Property 1 is proven in Appendix A.4.
236
237   Property 2 The accumulated error could be reduced by splitting the error flow into multiple error
238   sub-flows. The degree of accumulated error in the k-th error sub-flow dka is proportional to the number
239
      of error accumulations in each error sub-flow Na , dka / Na . The number of error accumulations in
240   each error flow Na equals N Nf .

      As stated in Property 1, DA can be reduced by minimizing n, while DA can also be reduced by
      splitting into error sub-flows as illustrated in Property 2. The first property relates to the sample

      3.2   S EMANTIC L OSS D ECOMPOSITION

286   Motivation. According to memorization effects, neural networks prioritize learning simpler patterns
287   from data (Zhang et al., 2021). The previously followed small-loss criterion leverages this effect:
288   clean labels are learned first by the network, hence exhibiting smaller losses compared to noisy ones.
289   However, the relative magnitude of losses is determined by comparison with other samples e.g., rank
290   sample losses with Top-k algorithm (Han et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2018) or modeling loss distribution
291   (Li et al., 2020; Permuter et al., 2006). To avoid such costly overheads, here comes a pure idea to
292
      leverage memorization effects: In an encoding space where a single loss can be decomposed, the
      flipped labels share some identical components with the original labels. For flipped labels, the clean
293
      components will be learned first and incur smaller losses, while the noisy components result in larger
294
      losses. This property can be utilized to identify whether a label contains noise.
295
296   Therefore, we design a lightweight plugin to create a detailed distribution in a single loss. The plugin
297   is structurally composed of a pre-prepared non-orthogonal codebook and an auxiliary head at the
298
      last layer of the network. It spans an auxiliary space where a loss can be decomposed semantically.
      Specifically, the codebook transforms the label into hash codes while the head map outputs into
299
      feature embedding, respectively. We will first detail the codebook and auxiliary head respectively,
300
      and then introduce the selection operation simplified by leveraging both.
301

525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
527   We propose Jump-teaching, an ultra robust and efficient framework to combat label noise. Our work
528   effectively mitigates the sample-selection bias in a single network which enables our approach to
529
      demonstrate outstanding performance across a broad array of experiments, particularly in extreme
      noise conditions. Moreover, with decomposed semantic information of sample losses for selection,
530
      Jump-teaching overcomes the limitations of the small-loss criterion and achieves more effective
531
      selection. Extensive experimental validation confirms that our method surpasses current state-of-
532   the-art techniques in both robustness and efficiency. In the future, we hope to explore how the
533   fluctuations of parameters influence the selection capability of the network. We did not employ
534   popular semi-supervised techniques to further exploit the identified noisy samples, however, we
535   believe that our method can be further extended by these techniques.
