009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern artificial intelligence predominantly relies on pre-trained models, which
012            are fine-tuned for specific downstream tasks rather than built from scratch. How-
013            ever, a key challenge persists: the fairness of learned representations in pre-trained
014            models is not guaranteed when transferred to new tasks, potentially leading to bi-
015            ased outcomes, even if fairness constraints are applied during the original train-
016
               ing. To address this issue, we propose Dual-level Bias Mitigation (DBM), which
               measures the fairness-guided distribution discrepancy between representations of
017
               different demographic groups. By optimizing both the fairness-guided distribu-
018
               tion discrepancy and the task-specific objective, DBM ensures fairness at both the
019            representation and task levels. Theoretically, we provide the generalization error
020            bound of the fairness-guided distribution discrepancy to support the efficacy of our
021            approach. Experimental results on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that
022            DBM effectively mitigates bias in fine-tuned models on downstream tasks across
023            a range of fairness metrics.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Can we ensure fairness across various downstream tasks when fine-tuning a pre-trained model, with-
028
      out altering the original network architecture? In this paper, we aim to address this question by
029
      measuring the fairness-guided distribution discrepancy between the representations of different de-
030   mographic groups to enforce fairness at both the representation and task levels.
031
032   Guaranteeing fairness in machine learning has become crucial as they are increasingly deployed in
033
      high-stake domains like healthcare (Chen et al., 2023), job recruitment (Faliagka et al., 2012) and
      credit approval (Khandani et al., 2010). Existing fairness approaches can be categorized into (1)
034
      pre-processing (Zemel et al., 2013; Calmon et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2023), (2) in-processing (Bilal
035
      Zafar et al., 2016; Agarwal et al., 2018; Kamishima et al., 2012), and (3) post-processing (Hardt
036   et al., 2016). Pre-processing and post-processing methods typically mitigate bias without modifying
037   the model training process, where pre-processing removing bias from the data itself and employs
038   standard machine learning methods for downstream tasks, and post-processing modifies the learned
039   pre-trained model to achieve desirable fairness. In contrast, in-processing methods intervene dur-
040   ing training by incorporating pre-defined fairness constraints, such as p%-rule (Biddle, 2005) and
041   equalized odds (Hardt et al., 2016), in the objective function.
042   These methods often focus on creating fair models or representations for specific tasks, which limits
043   their scalability in the era of big data and large models. Modern artificial intelligence increasingly
044   relies on transfer learning, where pre-trained models are fine-tuned for specific tasks, rather than
045   building models from scratch, particularly when dealing with large datasets for efficiency and ef-
046   fectiveness. This approach typically retains the internal representations learned by the pre-trained
047   model while fine-tuning it for specific downstream tasks. To address fairness, recent works (Madras
048   et al., 2018; Oneto et al., 2020) propose learning fair representations through neutralization or by
049   leveraging inter-task similarities. However, focusing solely on representation-level fairness has lim-
050
      itations. Debiased representations can still leak sensitive information, as it is challenging to ensure
      complete removal of all sensitive information from the encoder. Moreover, enforcing strict fairness
051
      at the representation level may risk excluding task-relevant information (Du et al., 2021).
052
053   Motivated by the concept of mixing (Du et al., 2021; Chuang & Mroueh, 2021), which addresses
      bias through data augmentation and representation mixing, we propose a Dual-level Bias Mitiga-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tion (DBM) framework. DBM addresses bias at both the representation and task levels, providing
055   a robust approach for mitigating bias in pre-trained models that are fine-tuned on biased data. It
056   obtains representations of different demographic groups from a given pre-trained model and learns
057   an in-processing module by minimizing the empirical risk over the set of mixed representations.
058   Specifically, following the idea of R-divergence Zhao & Cao (2023), the fairness-guided distribu-
059   tion discrepancy between the two sets of representations is measured by the gap between the empir-
060   ical risks of the pre-trained model and the in-processing module across the mixed representations.
061   This fairness-guided distribution discrepancy is incorporated as a regularizer into the task-specific
062   objective, aiming to minimize bias between the representations of different demographic groups
063
      and ensure fairness. By considering the task-specific objective while reducing the distribution dis-
      crepancy between the group representations, DBM achieves dual-level guarantees of fairness while
064
      ensuring accuracy.

067   2    P RELIMINARIES
068
069   In this section, we present our method for measuring task-specific fairness in debiased represen-
070   tations from the DNN head for downstream tasks. We define the probability distribution P on
071   X × S × Y, where X ∈ Rd represents non-sensitive variables, s = {A, B} is a binary sensitive
072   variable, and Y is the binary classification output variable {−1, 1}. Specifically, PA and PB repre-
073
      sent the distributions from samples from demographic groups A and B, respectively. Accordingly,
      the training set S = {xi , yi , si }i∈[N ] contains N i.i.d. samples from P, and SA and SB represent the
074
      datasets containing samples from demographic groups A and B, respectively. We consider compo-
075
      sitional models with a shared representation, expressed as f (x) = (g ◦ h)(x), where h : X → Z
076   is the representation learning function (i.e., DNN model head), and g : Z → Y is the task-specific
077   classification function. The dimension of the internal representation is denoted by r, i.e., Z ⊆ Rr .
078   To ensure fairness at the representation level, we require the conditional distribution of h(x) to be
079   identical across the two subgroups for every measurable subset C ⊂ Rr :
080
                                 P (h(x) ∈ C | s = A) = P (h(x) ∈ C | s = B).                               (1)
081
082   Oneto et al. (2020) suggested that if demographic parity is satisfied at the representation level, then
083   models built from such representations will also satisfy demographic parity. However, this condition
084   is an ideal situation and does not always hold in practice. In fact, guaranteeing fairness at the
085   representation level does not ensure fairness in final outcomes due to the complexity of downstream
086   tasks. For example, task-specific transformations can introduce or amplify biases not present in the
087   original representation (Madras et al., 2018). Therefore, it is crucial to evaluate and ensure fairness
088
      at the task-specific level, considering the entire pipeline from input to final output. To ensure fairness
      at the task-specific level, we require the conditional distribution of g(h(x)) to be identical across the
089
      two subgroups for every class set K ⊆ Y, given the fairness criteria of demographic parity:
090
091                            P (f (x)) ∈ K | s = A) = P (f (x)) ∈ K | s = B).                             (2)
092
      While the constraint at the representation level can be achieved using various fair representation
093
      learning methods that remove sensitive information, the task-specific constraint is more challenging
094   to handle.
095
096   When applying a representation learning function h ∈ H and a task-specific function g ∈ G to a
097
      distribution P and dataset S, the corresponding expected risk and empirical risk are defined as:

101   where L is a L-Lipschitz continuous loss function. The expected risk EP represents the theoretical
102   performance over the true data distribution, while the empirical risk EbS approximates this perfor-
103   mance based on a finite dataset S. The goal is to minimize both, with the empirical risk serving as
104   a practical proxy for the expected risk. Ensuring that the gap between these two quantities remains
105   small is critical for the model to generalize well from the training data to unseen samples. This
106   balance becomes especially important when aiming for fairness across sensitive variables, as mini-
107   mizing biased discrepancies across groups often requires careful consideration of both expected and
      empirical risks.

      In this section, we introduce DBM, a novel approach to mitigating bias in transfer learning scenar-
111   ios. DBM focuses on two key levels of fairness: representation level and task-specific level. DBM
112   measures and minimizes the fairness-guided distribution discrepancy between representations of dif-
113   ferent demographic groups. Specifically, the method begins by neutralizing the sensitive information
114   through a mixing process of representations, and then incorporates a fairness-guided optimization
115   that balances prediction errors across these groups. This dual-level approach ensures fairness both in
116   the learned representations and in the downstream task, offering a robust framework for mitigating
117   bias in pre-trained models fine-tuned on biased data.
118   Representation Mixing. The first level is at the representations. Following the same setting as in
119   Du et al. (2021), after obtaining the representations from the pre-trained model, we randomly pair
120   two representations from different demographic groups, say, h(xi | S = A) and h(xj | S = B)
121                                                            h(xi |S=A)+h(xj |S=B)
      with the same value of y, and then neutralize them as              2           . This mixing process
122   aims to obfuscate the sensitive information. To quantify the discrepancy between the representations
123   of two demographic groups in the downstream task, i.e., the second level of fairness assurance, we
124   introduce a fairness-guided distribution discrepancy measure.
125

      5     E XPERIMENT
199
      In the following sections, we first describe our experimental setup, including the datasets, baselines,
200
      and evaluation metrics. We then compare our proposed method against several related baselines and
201
      state-of-the-art techniques across multiple tasks, including both tabular and image tasks.
202
203
      5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
204
205   Architectures: For the tabular datasets, we employ a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) architecture
206   for our pre-trained model. This MLP consists of two fully connected layers, each followed by a
207   ReLU activation function. For the image dataset, we employ ResNet18 as the pre-trained model for
208   feature extraction. The classification head is a two-hidden-layer MLP that takes the representations
209   extracted by the pre-trained models and performs the final classification task.
210   Baselines: To evaluate the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed method, we compare our ap-
211   proach with several existing methods, including the fair representation learning methods described
212   in Du et al. (2021) (RNF), the constraint on representations using Maximum Mean Discrepancy
213   with Gaussian kernel, as proposed in Oneto et al. (2020) (MMMD ), the fairness constraints imposed
214   on downstream tasks, specifically Equalized Odds (Donini et al., 2018) (EO-FERM), fair learning
215   with Wasserstein distance (Jiang et al., 2020) (W-FERM), and robust fair empirical risk minimiza-
      tion (Baharlouei et al., 2024) (f -FERM).


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Datasets: We evaluate the performance of our proposed method using three commonly em-
217   ployed benchmark datasets in related studies: income prediction (Adult), recidivism prediction
218   (COMPAS), and two image datasets: Modified Labeled Faces in the Wild (LFW+a) and Celeb-
219   Faces Attributes (CelebA). The Adult dataset comprises 30,717 records of individual annual in-
220   comes, aiming to predict if an individual earns over $50,000 annually, with gender as the sensitive
221   attribute. The COMPAS dataset includes 5,554 instances predicting defendant recidivism, using race
222   as the sensitive attribute. For image data, we utilize the modified Labeled Faces in the Wild Home
223   (LFW+a) (Wolf et al., 2011) dataset, which we augment with attributes like gender and race. The
224   task is to classify gender, with HeavyMakeup as the sensitive variable, given its strong correlation
225
      with female in previous research. The CelebA dataset is utilized to discern the label HeavyMakeup,
      considering gender as the sensitive variable where biases have been noted towards female.
226
227   Evaluation Metrics: We use the percentage of misclassifications (ERR) to measure the prediction
228   performance. To measure fairness violations, we employ two metrics. The first is ∆DP = |E(Ŷ =
229   Y | S = A)−E(Ŷ = Y | S = B)|, which quantifies the disparity in accuracy between demographic
230   groups. The second is ∆EO = |P (Ŷ = 1 | S = A, Y = y) − P (Ŷ = 1 | S = B, Y = y)|, ∀y ∈
231   {0, 1}, which measures the difference in true positive and false positive rates between groups. Lower
232   values of ∆DP and ∆EO indicate smaller fairness violations.
233
      Experimental Settings: Our experimental design consists of two main sets. The first compares our
234
      method with baselines across the three datasets. The second explores a scenario where observational
235   labels are influenced by bias, simulated by flipping labels based on sensitive attributes and true
236   labels. We test these methods with symmetrical bias levels of 20% and 40%. For tabular datasets, we
237   conduct 10 runs with random splits, while for the two image datasets, we perform 3 runs, reporting
238   mean results and standard deviations. All experiments are performed with GPU NVIDIA A30 with
239   86 GB memory.

247   datasets compared to other methods. MMMD shows improvement over RNF but still falls short of

      in ERR. EO-FERM performs consistently well across all datasets, often achieving a good balance

      6   R ELATED W ORK
362
363
      Ensuring fairness in the process from pre-trained models to downstream tasks can be approached
364
      from two perspectives. The first is to guarantee fairness at the level of representations learned by
365
      the pre-trained model. The second is to add different fairness constraints for different tasks after
366   utilizing the pre-trained model. Many methods in this category aim to ensure fairness in down-
367   stream tasks by modifying the learned representations. Many methods aimed at ensuring fairness in
368   downstream tasks focus on the representation level. For example, Du et al. (2021) adopted a method
369   of neutralizing the representation, decorrelating its specificity towards certain groups. Cheng et al.
370   (2021) apply contrasitive learning to debias, while another line of research uses adversarial learning
371   to train debiased and transferable representations (Madras et al., 2018). These methods are similar
372   to most representation learning approaches (Louizos et al., 2015; Zemel et al., 2013; Calmon et al.,
373   2017; Lum & Johndrow, 2016; Zhao et al., 2020; Creager et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2020), which aim
374
      to ensure fairness in downstream tasks by guaranteeing the fairness of the representation.
375   Another category of methods adopts different learning strategies, such as using different in-
376   processing methods for the downstream tasks to ensure fairness by applying different distribution
377   measure. These methods typically follow an empirical risk minimization framework with fairness
      constraints to penalize the dependence between sensitive attributes and predictions. For example,


                                                                             7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Baharlouei et al. (2024) applies f-divergence, Baharlouei et al. (2020) uses Rényi correlation, Lowy
379   et al. (2022) employs χ2 divergence, Donini et al. (2018) utilizes L∞ distance, and Prost et al.
380   (2019) implements Maximum Mean Discrepancy. The methods in this category can be either model-
381   specific ((Bilal Zafar et al., 2015; 2016; Calders et al., 2009; Kamishima et al., 2012)) or general-
382   izable ((Agarwal et al., 2018; Baharlouei et al., 2020; Lowy et al., 2022)). An alternative approach
383   within this category leverages optimal transport learning (Gordaliza et al., 2019; Chiappa et al.,
384   2020). Our method differs from these two categories. While ensuring the fairness of the represen-
385   tation, it is not truly possible to guarantee that the downstream task is definitely fair. On the other
386   hand, adopting fairness in-processing for downstream tasks requires predefined fairness criteria. The
387
      advantage of our method is that it can consider the fairness of different tasks simultaneously without
      explicitly defining fairness.

390   7   C ONCLUSION
391
392   In this paper, we introduced DBM, a novel approach to ensuring fairness in transfer learning sce-
393   narios using pre-trained models. DBM addresses the limitations of existing fairness methods by
394   offering a dual-level fairness guarantee, tackling bias both at the representation and task-specific
395   levels. By leveraging fairness-guided distribution discrepancy, our method effectively mitigates bias
396   without altering the structure of the pre-trained model, making it highly adaptable and practical for
397   real-world applications. DBM overcomes key challenges, such as the potential leakage of sensitive
398
      information from debiased representations and the risk of discarding task-relevant data when en-
      forcing fairness. Through theoretical analysis and experimental evaluation on multiple benchmark
399
      datasets, we demonstrate the effectiveness of DBM in reducing bias across various fairness metrics.
