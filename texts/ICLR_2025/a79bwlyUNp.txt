010
011
               Transformers have emerged as the dominant architecture in the field of deep learn-
012
               ing, with a broad range of applications and remarkable in-context learning (ICL)
013
               capabilities. While not yet fully understood, ICL has already proved to be an
014            intriguing phenomenon, allowing transformers to learn in-context—without re-

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Can we leverage in-context learning (ICL) to perform full Bayesian inference? In this paper, we
031   investigate this question. The core principle of ICL is that a system adapts to a given task based
032   on information provided in its context, enabling it to solve complex problems, such as question-
033   answering or text summarization, using a fixed model and without requiring any gradient-based
034   fine-tuning, simply by referencing the context. This way, ICL enables the generation of real-time
035   solutions via local understanding of data without explicit re-training.
036
      ICL has become not only a central paradigm in natural language processing, with LLMs as ubiq-
037   uitous in-context learners (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), but led to a paradigm shift in
038   machine learning in general (Dong et al., 2022). In the domain of tabular data, tabular prior-data fit-
039   ted networks (TabPFNs) are in-context learners that achieve state-of-the-art classification accuracy
040   on small datasets in combination with minimal prediction time (Hollmann et al., 2022). The central
041   idea of prior fitted networks (PFNs) is to train a transformer model that takes as input a small tabular
042   dataset and directly outputs the class labels for test samples. The training data for such a model is
043   purely synthetic, and sampled from a distribution referred to as the “prior” in the context of PFNs.
044
045
      1.1   F ULL BAYESIAN INFERENCE IN - CONTEXT
046
047
      Performing full Bayesian inference can be challenging, even for relatively simple models such as
048   generalized linear models (GLMs; Nelder & Wedderburn, 1972). We use the notion of full Bayesian
049   inference for methods yielding potentially complex and high-dimensional posterior distributions—
050   in contrast to, for instance, methods that yield only the posterior predictive, as e.g. in Müller et al.
051   (2021). Full Bayesian inference is thus fundamental for a wide range of applications in Bayesian
052   statistics and probabilistic machine learning, even though it cannot always be achieved. Methods
053   of central importance for full Bayesian inference are Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and
      variational inference (VI).

      While previous studies of ICL are mostly concerned with learning to simulate univariate predictions
055   of models in-context, and only discuss synthetic scenarios (Garg et al., 2022; Ahuja et al., 2023; Bai
056   et al., 2024), our work attempts to perform full Bayesian inference via ICL on real-world data.
057
058
      The idea underlying the proposed approach is founded on two observations relating to full Bayesian
      inference and the working principle of PFNs: First, many Bayesian models have a generative for-
059
      mulation that allows the simulation of arbitrarily large amounts of training samples from the joint
060                                                                                       K
      distribution P x,z . We assume that samples from P x,z comprise a dataset x = {xj }j=1 containing
061   K samples xj ∈ X and a corresponding (latent) variable z ∈ Z. This joint distribution P x,z
                                                                           1
062   corresponds to the “prior” in PFNs and allows the training of a large neural network that implicitly
063   learns to perform Bayesian inference. Second, Bayesian inference is especially useful for smaller
064   datasets x that can be processed in a single forward pass. This makes an entire dataset a viable
065   context for Bayesian ICL.
066
      To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
067
068   1. Using the aforementioned idea, we develop, train, and examine a model that yields samples from
069      the posterior distribution P z|x given data x as context without any (explicit) parameter updates
070
         or parametric assumption about the posterior.
071
      2. To achieve this, we propose to use synthetic samples from the joint distribution P x,z in order
072      to train a large transformer model that performs ICL regarding the posterior P z|x , and provide
073      a general framework to analyze the circumstances that enable learning P z|x purely through
074
         samples from P x,z .
075
      3. We then analyze the efficacy of our approach for GLMs and latent factor models. For these
         applications, we show that including the “prior” used for TabPFNs results in reliably inferring
076
         posterior distributions on real-world data.
077
      4. In a variety of experiments, we demonstrate that this approach yields posterior samples that are
078
         very similar to those from a Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampler. Furthermore, we find that the
079
         quality of the samples, when compared to various popular VI techniques that do not operate
080      in-context, is preferable.

085   Beyond the perspective of prior fitted networks, the contribution of this work can be summarized
086
      from two additional viewpoints: First, from the perspective of in-context learning, we show that
      (large) transformer models can not only implement statistical models in terms of their predictions,
087
      but also explicitly and in a full probabilistic setting. From the simulation-based inference view-
088
      point, we demonstrate that sample-based posterior estimation (Dax et al., 2021) can be used for
089   full Bayesian inference in complex scenarios arising in commonly used latent variable models and
090   demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on real-world datasets.
091
092   In-context learning ICL is a special case of meta-learning (Hospedales et al., 2021) character-
093   ized by using a large pre-trained model in order to learn from a context dataset without explicitly
094   updating task-specific parameters. Several recent lines of work investigate the in-context learning
095   capabilities of transformers (Garg et al., 2022; Ahuja et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Chan et al.,
096   2022). For instance, Garg et al. (2022) show that a model similar to GPT-2 can implicitly implement
097   various interesting function classes in-context. More specifically, the model learns to reproduce
098
      the predictions of different statistical models, in particular linear functions, as well as sparse linear
      functions, decision trees, and even two-layer neural networks. This approach can be extended to
099
      multiple families of functions and even mixtures of tasks (Ahuja et al., 2023). However, the results
100
      by Garg et al. (2022) and Ahuja et al. (2023) are restricted to rather small problem scales and scalar-
101   valued predictions instead of multivariate posterior distributions. Additionally, the experiments are
102   conducted exclusively on simulated data.

140   3   I N - CONTEXT LEARNING FOR FULL BAYESIAN INFERENCE
141
142   Bayesian inference is a tool of central importance for countless applications. However, exact pos-
143   terior inference can become computationally expensive when using sampling-based methods (Hast-
144   ings, 1970; Hoffman et al., 2014; Betancourt, 2017) and even impossible when relying on fully
145
      factorized VI methods, which can incur substantial approximation errors (Bishop et al., 2002; Blei,
      2012; Margossian & Blei, 2023). Amortized variational inference can alleviate those issues but typ-
146
      ically requires the development of specialized and complex modeling frameworks (Kingma, 2013;
147
      Srivastava & Sutton, 2017; Garnelo et al., 2018b; Lin et al., 2021). Another issue with variational
148   inference arises from having to choose a variational distribution. While insufficient flexibility in this
149   respect can lead to overly simplistic posteriors, a too flexible variational distribution might overfit
150   the given data (Cremer et al., 2018).
151
      We propose a simple and effective solution based on ideas from ICL, which can be seen as conduct-
152
      ing amortized inference on a dataset level. Training a model on a potentially unlimited amount of
153
      synthetic datasets yields an in-context learner that can not only approximate a vast, almost arbitrarily
154   large, class of distributions, but is also highly efficient when used for sampling. Furthermore, this
155   does not incur any issues with overly or insufficiently flexible distribution assumptions as in VI.
156
157

188   3.1     D EFINING THE FORM OF THE POSTERIOR
189
190
      To learn the posterior distribution P z|x in-context, we use the framework of flow matching (Lipman
      et al., 2022). More specifically, we utilize continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) to specify and
191
      ultimately sample from P z|x . CNFs, currently excelling in the field of image synthesis (Esser et al.,
192
      2024), do not only allow to flexibly learn almost arbitrary distributions, but are also found to be more
193
      sample-efficient in training than for instance diffusion objectives (Lipman et al., 2022; Wildberger
194   et al., 2024). Furthermore, unlike discrete normalizing flows (Papamakarios et al., 2021a), CNF
195   objectives do not limit the architecture of the used neural network, allowing to incorporate complex
196   conditioning on the data x in addition to flexibly modeling the posterior, which is a crucial aspect
197   of our ICL framework. Refer to Appendix K for more information on CNFs.
198

293        3.3     G ENERATING REALISTIC DATA
294
           While we assume a data-generating process such as the one in Algorithm 1, this is not necessar-
295
           ily the data-generating process that produces the data in the model’s application as an in-context
296
           learner. Even when the generative process P x,z underlying a statistical model is sophisticated and
297        complex in nature, model misspecification is inevitable in almost every practical application. While
298        mismatches between the real data-generating processes and model assumptions can lead to various
299        problems in traditional Bayesian modeling (Grünwald & van Ommen, 2017), the question of model
300        misspecification plays a somewhat different and yet an especially central role for our ICL approach.
301
           More specifically, the ICL model learns the relationship between P z|x and a datapoint x exclusively
302
           based on synthetic samples from the marginal P x implied by the statistical model with generative
303                                                            ∗                                       ∗
           process P x,z . Given a real-world dataset x∗ ∼ P x , model misspecification in terms of P x implies
304
           that the in-context learner needs to infer the posterior based on out-of-distribution data, where the
305        problem is aggravated the more unrealistic P x is.
306
307        To be able to access a reference or ground truth distribution, the data generating processes in our ex-
308
           periments need to match the structure of the GLM, FA and GMM approaches. While the generative
           processes of FA and GMMs directly prescribe how all parts of the data are generated, this can po-
309
           tentially cause a discrepancy between synthetically generated and real-world datasets. However, our
310
           empirical results (Section 4.1) demonstrate that the in-context learner can generalize to real-world
311        data despite the discrepancy to the simulated datasets.
312
313
           GLM example continued In the aforementioned GLM case, the distribution of the covariates
314        P u does not affect the structure of P z|x in the data generating process (cf. Algorithm 1). We can
315        therefore use a flexible prior P u such as the TabPFN-“prior” (Hollmann et al., 2022) to generate
316        covariates u and thereby effectively tackle the issue of model specification. More specifically, by
317        generating a plethora of highly realistic samples of tabular covariates with different ranges, domains,
318        and correlations, the in-context learner will learn the GLM structure on a broad mixture of distribu-
319        tions regarding the covariates u.

361   4     E XPERIMENTS
362
363   To show that the proposed methodology is not just an abstract concept, we derive exemplary use
364   cases that demonstrate how well ICL is able to keep up with MCMC and VI approaches in practice.
365
      For this, we will use two prominent statistical modeling classes, namely generalized linear models
366
      (GLMs) and latent factor models. For the latent factor models, we consider factor analysis (FA) and
367   Gaussian mixture models (GMMs).
368
369
370   Modeling scenarios We use seven different scenarios for the GLMs, where we vary the prior
371   distribution on the parameters, the conditional distribution of the response, and whether an intercept
372
      is included. For FA, we vary the form of the priors and dimensionalities of variables, and for the
      GMMs investigate different dimensionalities as well as prior configurations. We refer to Appendix A
373
      for details on the model structure and scenarios.
374
375
376   Datasets We evaluate the methods on 50 synthetic datasets and 17 real-world datasets from a
377   benchmark suite proposed by Grinsztajn et al. (2022). We refer to Appendix J for more details on
      the preprocessing of the datasets.


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Methods Apart from a comparison with a gold standard, we compare our ICL approach to a
379   Laplace approximation (Daxberger et al., 2021) and different established VI methods based on au-
380   tomatic differentiation VI (Kucukelbir et al., 2017). For the variational distribution, we incorporate
381   a normal distribution with 1) a diagonal and 2) a full covariance matrix, as well as 3) a structured
382   normal distribution with linear dependencies between the latent variables, and 4) an approach based
383   on inverse auto-regressive flows (IAF; Kingma et al., 2016). Appendix D contains a discussion about
384   the hyperparameters of all considered methods.
385
386   Evaluation process For every synthetic and real-world dataset, 1000 posterior samples from each
387   method are compared against samples from the analytical solution, if available, or from a Hamilto-
388   nian Monte Carlo (HMC) sampler with a NUTS kernel (Hoffman et al., 2014) as the gold standard.
389   For unimodal problems, we run a single chain. For posteriors with multiple modes, we use three
390
      times the number of modes as the number of chains to capture multimodality.
391
392
      Evaluation metrics Three metrics are employed to compare samples from different approxima-
      tions of the posterior distribution. The first metric is a classifier 2-sample test (C2ST; Lueckmann
393
      et al., 2021; Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), where the ROC-AUC score of a random forest classifier,
394
      trained to distinguish between samples from the gold standard and the method in question, is uti-
395   lized. For random forest, we use default hyperparameters, as defined in Scikit-learn (Pedregosa
396   et al., 2011) and 10-fold cross-validation. The second metric is the maximum mean discrepancy
397   (MMD) between the two distributions (gold-standard and each tested method) with an exponential
398   kernel (Gretton et al., 2012). The third metric is the empirical Wasserstein-2 distance (W2 ; Givens
399   & Shortt, 1984) of the two distributions, using a quadratic solver implemented in the POT library
400   (Flamary et al., 2021).

459   4.1.3    G AUSSIAN MIXTURE MODELS
460
461
      Full Bayesian inference for GMMs is arguably much more challenging than for GLMs or FA. First,
      the generative process of GMMs involves discrete assignments to clusters, which poses a challenge
462
      not only for NUTS, but especially for VI methods. Second, the dimensionality of the posterior sam-
463
      ples can be relatively large since for diagonal normal distributions, each component of the mixture
464   has a mean and a variance parameter per dimension. Finally, the considered GMMs are not identi-
465   fiable leading to multi-modal posterior distributions, which are impossible to perfectly approximate
466   with the most commonly used VI methods based on normal approximations.
467
      Due to this inherent difficulty of the GMM scenarios, we find the overall performances of all models
468
      to be worse than in the GLM and FA cases. In particular, the C2ST metric is almost saturated
469
      for the VI approaches and has a value of around 83 percent for ICL (Table 4). The MMD and W2
470   metrics also indicate that ICL yields samples with higher agreement with the reference than the other
471   approaches on synthetic data. A plot of the marginals of the posterior shows high agreement between
472   the posterior distributions of both HMC and ICL while VI is incapable of perfectly approximating
473   a bimodal distribution and exhibits typical mode-seeking behavior (Figure 2). Note that also the
474   VI approach based on inverse autoregressive flows, which in theory allows flexible modeling of a
475   wide range of posterior shapes, fails to learn the bi-modality accurately from the limited number
476   of 50 data points in this GMM scenario. This demonstrates the strength of our ICL approach in
477   flexibly learning distributions agnostic of the provided sample size. On the real-world evaluation,
478
      the differences are similar in nature, albeit slightly less pronounced. While C2ST and MMD are
      better for ICL than for VI, the W2 metric is not substantially different.

518
      Limitations While our experiments indicate the effectiveness of ICL as a Bayesian inference
519   method, it requires an extensive up-front training routine on modern GPU hardware. Despite ICL
520   being consistently faster at inference time than the considered HMC methods, the overall compu-
521   tational burden to train our approach is much higher. As with many other ICL approaches, large
522   datasets as a context can further become computationally very expensive.
523
524   Outlook and future work Despite its vast up-front computational cost, ICL has not only proven
525   fundamentally transformative in the field of natural language processing (Brown et al., 2020; Tou-
526   vron et al., 2023), but recently also appears to be very promising for tabular classification (Hollmann
527   et al., 2022). Exploring the frontiers of ICL in terms of full Bayesian inference, starting from the
528   feasibility results of this work, might therefore yield a path into similarly fertile territories.
529   Even though our experiments show that ICL works well despite being trained on data that is poten-
530   tially very different from real-world data, the approach will only be as flexible as the data and model
531   structures it was trained on. As a result, ICL might fail if the model, which implies the synthetic data
532   generation, is severely misspecified. However, this is the same limitation as when misspecifying the
533   hypothesis space of, e.g., a deep neural network or other machine learning approaches, effectively
534   providing the model with the wrong inductive bias.
535   While flexible state-of-the-art sampling-based methods, such as HMC, are an efficient and highly
536   effective reference in terms of inference for standard and simple statistical methods discussed in this
537   paper, the proposed ICL approach is fundamentally more general in nature. In particular, any proba-
538   bilistic model for which a generative process is conceivable can be fitted using our ICL approach—
539   the potential for fitting models beyond the horizon of standard Bayesian methods is therefore mani-
      fold.

821       In this section we expand the description and explanation regarding GLMs from section 3.2. GLMs
822       are among the most commonly used statistical models with myriads of applications (Nelder & Wed-
823       derburn, 1972; Fahrmeir et al., 2013). In the context of GLMs, we assume that the response y
824       follows a distribution P y|u depending on the linear predictor η := u⊤ β and an additional parame-
825       ter σ 2 . We denote the covariates as u, the regression coefficients as β, and use σ 2 for the variance
826       of the response. The mean of P y|u depends on the linear predictor via a link function g, such that
827
          g (E[y|u]) = u⊤ β. Ultimately, the density of distribution of the response   y depending on the linear
          predictor and the additional parameter is denoted by p(y|g u⊤ β , σ 2 ). To showcase the flexibility

828                                                                                                       2
829       of our framework, we experiment with different priors P β on the regression coefficients, P σ on the
                        2
830       parameter σ , and also different parametric distributions of the response. Additionally, to include
831
          covariates u that resemble practically relevant tabular data in the generative process, allowing for
          meaningful inference on real-world datasets, we utilize samples from the Tab-PFN “prior” for P u .
832
833       GLMs belong to the framework of latent variable models defined by data x and (latent) variables
834       z, where the data comprises covariates and response x := (u, y). The variables of interest are the
835       coefficients z := β. This yields the following generative process for a set of synthetic samples

876       A.2    FACTOR ANALYSIS
877
878       The goal of factor analysis is to explain data x in terms of latent, typically lower-dimensional, factors
879       z (Lawley & Maxwell, 1962; Rummel, 1988). In the Bayesian setting, one assumes a prior P z on
880       the latent variable z, a prior P W on the factor loading matrix W and additional priors P Ψ and
881       P µ on the covariance matrix and the mean vector. The conditional distribution P z|x of the data
882
          given z has mean E[z|x] = W z + µ and covariance matrix Cov[z|x] = Ψ. In the case where
883
          P z and P z|x are Gaussian, one can set P z = N (0, I) and assume a diagonal covariance matrix Ψ
          without loosing expressiveness of the model (Murphy, 2023). We make the assumption that W is
884
          lower triangular with positive entries on the diagonal in order to ensure identifiability of the model
885
          (Lopes & West, 2004). Additionally, we assume that the distributions µ, Ψ and P W fully factorize.
886       In order to ensure that the diagonal of W is positive, we consider absolute values in the generative
887       process. Algorithm 3 details the data generating process.
888
889

          components, and an inverse gamma prior on the elements of the diagonal covariance matrix Ψ. For
890

891
          we use a normal prior on the latent factors z (i) in five cases and a Laplace prior in one case. We
892
          vary the number of samples K per dataset x, the dimensionality P of each data point, as well as the
893       dimensionality zdim .

1244
1245   D.1   ICL
1246
1247   To ensure maximum comparability across different experiments, we fix the hyperparameters for all
1248   ICL experiments: For the architecture of the model introduced in Section 3.4, we use the following
1249   configuration: The dimensionality of encoder representations is set to 512 and is expanded to 1024
1250   in the feed-forward blocks. We use 8 heads and 8 encoder layers with a dropout rate of 0.1. For
1251
       the decoder part we also use 512 as the dimensionality of the representations and 1024 as the inter-
       mediate representation in the feed-forward layers and a dropout rate of 0.1. Furthermore, 3 simple
1252
       fully connected layers with adaLN conditioning are used for final processing in the decoder. For
1253
       the time conditioning, we use 3 simple fully connected layers to map the scalar-valued time t onto
1254   a 512 dimensional conditioning vector that is used for the adaLN blocks in the decoder. This yields
1255   a model of around 43.1 million parameters. We use no tokenization for either the encoder or the
1256   decoder and simple embedding layers to map the encoder- and decoder-input onto the feed-forward
1257   dimensions.
1258
       We use an Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a cosine learning rate schedule (Loshchilov &
1259
       Hutter, 2016), where the maximum learning rate is 5 · 10−4 , the final division factor is 104 and
1260   10 percent of the epochs are used for warm-up. We use a weight decay parameter of 10−5 and a
1261   batch size of 1024 and gradient clipping with a maximum gradient norm of one. We use in total 75
1262   million synthetic samples for all scenarios. Of the total number, half, i.e. 37.5 million, are used for
1263   training and 10 percent for validation and the remaining 40 percent for testing. Note that we observe
1264   convergence of the loss usually much earlier than after this training duration, but fix the number of
1265   samples for consistency across experiments. A single L4 GPU is used for the GLM scenarios and a
1266   single A100 GPU for the FA and GMM cases.
1267   To solve the ODE for the sample generation, dopri5 (Dormand & Prince, 1980) as implemented in
1268   Torchdiffeq (Chen, 2018) is used in the adjoint version. We set the relative and absolute tolerance
1269   to 10−7 . The σmin parameter in the CNF-loss is set to 10−4 .

1354

1433

1434

1435   between training and testing data occurs, the drops in performance are almost always modest and,

1793   K    BACKGROUND ON CONDITIONAL FLOW- MATCHING
1794
1795   Flow matching, initially used in image synthesis leverages normalizing flows (Papamakarios et al.,
1796   2021b) to model arbitrary distributions. Continuous normalizing flows (Lipman et al., 2022) have
1797   emerged as a potent tool for modeling complex distributions. For example, recent advancements
1798   have shown its effectiveness in state-of-the-art image generation, outperforming diffusion-based
1799   methods in likelihood and sample quality on ImageNet (Lipman et al., 2022). Techniques like Flow-
1800   Turbo have accelerated class-conditional and text-to-image generation, setting new benchmarks
1801   (Zhao et al., 2024). Additionally, applying flow matching in latent spaces of pretrained autoen-
1802
       coders has enhanced computational efficiency and scalability for high-resolution image synthesis
       (Dao et al., 2023). Similarly, flow-based models have been successfully applied to protein structure
1803
       prediction, improving accuracy and efficiency in modeling complex protein conformations (Yim
1804
       et al., 2024; 2023).
1805
1806   In the area of simulation-based inference, Wildberger et al. (2024) introduce the idea of using con-
1807   tinuous normalizing flows in order to efficiently approximate complex posterior distributions. In
1808
       particular, they apply the framework to the field of gravitational-wave inference, substantially out-
       performing approaches based on discrete flows. Furthermore, they demonstrate good performance
1809
       on the existing SBI-Benchmark (Lueckmann et al., 2021) using a simple MLP-based architecture.
