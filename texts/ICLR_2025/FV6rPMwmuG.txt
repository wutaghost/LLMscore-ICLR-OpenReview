011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) has become a cornerstone of neural network
014            optimization due to its computational efficiency and generalization capabilities.
015            However, the noise introduced by SGD is often assumed to be uncorrelated over
016
               time, despite the common practice of epoch-based training where data is sampled
               without replacement. In this work, we challenge this assumption and investi-
017
               gate the effects of epoch-based noise correlations on the stationary distribution of
018
               discrete-time SGD with momentum. Our main contributions are twofold: First,
019            we calculate the exact autocorrelation of the noise during epoch-based training
020            under the assumption that the noise is independent of small fluctuations in the
021            weight vector, revealing that SGD noise is inherently anti-correlated over time.
022            Second, we explore the influence of these anti-correlations on the variance of
023            weight fluctuations. We find that for directions with curvature of the loss greater
024            than a hyperparameter-dependent crossover value, the conventional results for un-
025            correlated noise are recovered. However, for relatively flat directions, the weight
026            variance is significantly reduced, leading to a considerable decrease in loss fluc-
027
               tuations compared to the constant weight variance assumption. Furthermore, we
               demonstrate that training with these anti-correlations enhances test performance,
028
               suggesting that the inherent noise structure induced by epoch-based training plays
029
               a crucial role in finding flatter minima that generalize better.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Initially developed to address the challenges of computational efficiency in neural networks, stochas-
035
      tic gradient descent (SGD) has exhibited exceptional effectiveness in managing large datasets com-
036   pared to the full gradient methods (Bottou, 1991). It has since garnered widespread acclaim in
037   the machine learning domain (LeCun et al., 2015), with applications spanning image recognition
038   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; He et al., 2016), natural language pro-
039   cessing (Sutskever et al., 2014; Brown et al., 2020), and mastering complex games beyond human
040   capabilities (Silver et al., 2017). Alongside its numerous variants (Duchi et al., 2011; Kingma & Ba,
041   2015; Schmidt et al., 2021), SGD remains the cornerstone of neural network optimization.
042   SGD’s success can be attributed to several key properties, such as rapid escape from saddle points
043   (Ge et al., 2015) and its capacity to circumvent "bad" local minima, instead locating broad minima
044   that generally lead to superior generalization (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2017;
045   Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018; Smith & Le, 2018; Xie et al., 2021; Wojtowytsch, 2024). This is often as-
046   cribed to anisotropic gradient noise (Hoffer et al., 2017; Sagun et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; 2019;
047   Zhu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Ziyin et al., 2022). Nonetheless, recent empirical research posits
048   that even full gradient descent, with minor adjustments, can achieve generalization performance
049   comparable to that of SGD (Geiping et al., 2022).
050   To deepen our understanding of neural network training dynamics, several studies have investigated
051   the limiting behavior of network weights during the later stages of training (Yaida, 2019; Kunin
052   et al., 2023). Of particular interest is the behavior of weight fluctuations near a minimum of the loss
053   function (Mandt et al., 2017; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021). Empirical evidence suggests
      that the covariance matrix C associated with SGD noise is proportional to the Hessian matrix H

054
      of the loss function (Sagun et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; 2019; Zhu et al., 2019; Thomas et al.,
055   2020; Xie et al., 2021), although this proportionality may not hold under certain conditions (Ziyin
056   et al., 2022). Consequently, theoretical analyses predict that the stationary covariance matrix of the
057   weights, Σ, becomes isotropic when the learning rate is sufficiently small (Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018;
058   Liu et al., 2021; Kunin et al., 2023). However, recent empirical studies (Feng & Tu, 2021) have
059   identified significant anisotropy in Σ.
060
      In this work, we present both theoretical and empirical analyses of weight fluctuations during the
061   later stages of training, accounting for the emergence of anti-correlations in the noise produced
062   by SGD, which stem from the prevalent epoch-based learning schedule. As a result of these anti-
063   correlations, we discover that the covariance matrix Σ displays anisotropy and is smaller than ex-
064   pected in a subspace of weight directions corresponding to Hessian eigenvectors with small eigen-
065   values, while maintaining the isotropy of Σ in directions associated with Hessian eigenvectors pos-
066   sessing large eigenvalues. Our theoretical predictions are validated through the analysis of a neural
067   network’s training within a subspace of its top Hessian eigenvectors.
068   In addition, we demonstrate that for a small convolutional network trained on CIFAR10 the anti-
069   correlations in SGD noise described above significantly increase the test accuracy. By linking this
070   result to a previous study on artificially added anti-correlated noise and its benefits (Orvieto et al.,
071   2022), we argue that the anti-correlations in SGD noise suppress diffusion in flat directions, and in
072   this way contribute to finding flatter minima with better test accuracy.

146   3.1   H ESSIAN AND GRADIENT SAMPLE COVARIANCE
147
      The assumption of a strong alignment between the gradient sample covariance C0 and the Hessian
148
      matrix of the loss function H is widely used in the literature (Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018; Zhang et al.,
149
      2019; Liu et al., 2021). Various theoretical arguments suggest that when a neural network’s output
150   closely matches the example labels, these two matrices should be similar (Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018;
151   Sagun et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2019; Martens, 2020). However, as highlighted
152   by Thomas et al. (2020), even slight deviations between network predictions and labels can theoret-
153   ically disrupt this relationship. Nevertheless, empirical observations often reveal a strong alignment
154   between the gradient sample covariance and the Hessian matrix near a minimum.
155
      Zhang et al. (2019) investigated this assumption numerically. They analyzed both matrices in a spe-
156   cific basis that exhibits varying curvature across different directions. Their findings showed a close
157   correspondence between curvature and gradient variance along a given direction in a convolutional
158   image recognition network, and a reasonably good relationship in a transformer model. Similarly,
159   Thomas et al. (2020) provided both theoretical arguments and empirical evidence across different
160   image recognition architectures. While they did not find an exact match between the two matri-
161   ces, they observed a proportionality indicated by a high cosine similarity. Xie et al. (2021) also
      explored this relationship using an image recognition network. In the eigenspace of the Hessian


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      matrix, they plotted entries within a specific interval against corresponding entries from the gradient
163   sample covariance and observed a close match.
164
165   Ziyin et al. (2022) emphasized the importance of considering the conditions under which the approx-
166
      imation of proportionality between the two matrices is valid, noting that the approximation may be
      questionable otherwise. However, the conditions they specify – particularly being near a minimum
167
      with low loss – are consistent with our empirical setup (see Section 5.1 and Appendix H).

170   3.2   L IMITING DYNAMICS AND W EIGHT F LUCTUATIONS
171
172
      Several studies have examined the limiting dynamics of SGD, often modeling it as a stochastic
      differential equation (SDE). Researchers such as Mandt et al. (2017) and Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018)
173
      commonly approximate the loss near a minimum as a quadratic function, representing the SDE as a
174
      multivariate Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process. This process suggests a stationary weight distribu-
175   tion with Gaussian fluctuations. Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018) further assume that the gradient covariance
176   is proportional to the Hessian, observing under these conditions that the weight fluctuations are
177   isotropic. Kunin et al. (2023), who also incorporate momentum into their analysis, predict and
178   empirically verify isotropic weight fluctuations. Chaudhari & Soatto (2018) investigate the SDE
179   without assuming a quadratic loss or equilibrium, gaining insights via the Fokker-Planck equation.
180
      Alternatively, some studies derive relationships from a stationarity assumption rather than a
181   continuous-time approximation (Yaida, 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Ziyin et al., 2022). Yaida (2019)
182   assumes that the weight trajectory follows a stationary distribution and derives general fluctuation-
183   dissipation relations from this premise. Liu et al. (2021) go further by assuming a quadratic loss
184   function, enabling them to derive exact relations for the weight variance of SGD with momentum. If
185   the gradient covariance is additionally assumed to be proportional to the Hessian, their results also
186   predict that the weight variance is approximately isotropic, except in directions where the product
187   of the learning rate and the Hessian eigenvalue is significantly large.
188   These computed weight variances are explicitly applied in various contexts, such as calculating the
189   escape rate from a minimum or assessing the approximation error in SGD – which captures the
190   additional training error attributed to noise (Liu et al., 2021).
191
      Feng & Tu (2021) present a phenomenological theory based on their empirical findings, which,
192
      unlike Kunin et al. (2023), also accounts for flat directions. They describe a general inverse variance-
193   flatness relationship by analyzing the weight trajectories of different image recognition networks via
194   principal component analysis. They discovered a power law relationship between the curvature of
195                                       2
      the loss and the weight variance σθ,i  in any given direction, where higher curvature corresponds to
196   higher variance. They also observed that both the velocity variance σv,i  2
                                                                                  and the correlation time τi
197   are larger for higher curvatures.
198
      In our approach, we avoid the continuous-time approximation and instead base our results on the
199
      assumption that the weights adhere to a stationary distribution near a quadratic minimum.

204   4.1   AUTOCORRELATION OF THE NOISE
205
206   We consider the correlation between two noise terms arising from different SGD update steps.
207   Specifically, when examples are sampled without replacement while keeping the weight vector θ
208   constant, inherent anti-correlations emerge in the noise. Below, we provide a conceptual motivation
209   for this phenomenon and refer the reader to Appendix D for a detailed derivation.
210   Suppose the ratio M := N/S is an integer, where N is the total number of examples and S is
211   the batch size. This ensures that each epoch consists of M batches, with every example presented
212   exactly once per epoch. For a fixed weight vector θ, the mean of the gradients computed over one
213   epoch, gk (θ), equals the total gradient ∇L(θ). Consequently, the sum of the noise components
214   δgk (θ) = gk (θ) − ∇L(θ) over one epoch must be zero. This implies that if a noise term points
215   in a particular direction at the beginning of an epoch, subsequent noise terms within the same epoch
      are constrained to partially cancel it out, leading to anti-correlations.

288   4.3   VARIANCE FOR LATE TRAINING PHASE
289
290   Given the autocorrelation of the noise calculated earlier, we aim to present the expected variances of
291   the weights and velocities during the later stages of training. To characterize the conditions of this
292
      phase, we make the following assumptions.
293   Assumption 1: Quadratic approximation We assume that we have reached a minimum
294   point of the loss function, which can be adequately represented with a quadratic form as
295   L(θ) = L0 + 21 (θ − θ∗ )⊤ H(θ − θ∗ ). Without loss of generality, we set L0 = 0 and θ∗ = 0,
296   simplifying the expression to L(θ) = 21 θ ⊤ Hθ .
297
      Assumption 2: Anti-correlated noise We assume that the covariance of the SGD noise is static
298
      and that its autocorrelation follows the relation previously calculated in Equation (2), even when the
299   weight vector is not static.
300
301   While the noise generally exhibits state dependence, it is reasonable to assume a constant covariance
302
      during the later stages of training and within the time frame of our analysis. As described by Ziyin
      et al. (2022), state dependence enters the noise through the value of the training loss. Since we
303
      observe only minimal changes in the loss during our analysis period (see Appendix H), assuming a
304
      static noise covariance is justified.
305
306   Assumption 3: Hessian and noise covariance commute               We assume that the covariance of the
307   noise commutes with the Hessian matrix, [C, H] = 0.
308   This assumption is not strictly necessary, but it simplifies the analysis. The theory predicts a vari-
309   ance reduction in the eigenspace of the Hessian with relatively small eigenvalues without requiring
310   commutativity (see Appendix L). However, when [C, H] ̸= 0, the calculated weight and veloc-
311   ity variances are no longer exact eigenvalues of their respective covariance matrices; instead, they
312   represent variances along the directions of the Hessian eigenvectors. As long as C and H approx-
313   imately commute – as discussed in Section 3.1 – these variances provide a good approximation
314
      of the actual eigenvalues. For further discussion, we refer to Appendix L, where we demonstrate
      that although [C, H] = 0 is not strictly satisfied in our empirical investigation, the eigenbasis of
315
      H remains a reasonable approximation for the eigenbasis of C. Moreover, due to finite sample
316
      sizes, the actual eigenvalues of the empirically recorded weight covariance matrix Σ are inherently
317   skewed. Therefore, analyzing the weight variance in the eigenbasis of H is beneficial, as we explain
318   in Appendix E.
319
320
      Additionally, we assume that 0 ≤ β < 1 and 0 < ηλi < 2(1 + β) for all eigenvalues λi of H. If
      these conditions are not met, the weight fluctuations would diverge.

412
413   In order to corroborate our theoretical findings, we have conducted a small-scale experiment. We
414   have trained a LeNet architecture, similar to the one described in (Feng & Tu, 2021), using the
415   CIFAR10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009). LeNet is a compact convolutional network comprised of two
416   convolutional layers followed by three dense layers. The network comprises approximately 137,000
417
      parameters. Here we present results for a single seed and specific hyperparameters. However, we
      have also performed tests with different seeds and combinations of hyperparameters, all of which
418
      showed comparable qualitative behavior (see Appendix I). Furthermore, in Appendix K we studied
419
      a ResNet architecture (He et al., 2016), a more modern network, where we obtained similar results.
420
421   The training parameters together with the schedule are described in Appendix H and result in a
422   thousand minibatches per epoch, M = 1,000. The setup achieves 100% training accuracy and
423
      63% testing accuracy. We compute the variances right after the initial schedule over a period of 20
      additional epochs, equivalent to 20,000 update steps. Throughout this analysis period, a constant
424
      learning rate is maintained and the recorded weights are designated by θ k , with k = K, . . . , K + T
425
      and T = 20,000. Given the impracticability of obtaining the full covariance matrix for all weights
426   over this period due to the excessive memory requirements, we limit our analysis to a specific sub-
427   space. Employing the resource-efficient Pearlmutter trick (Pearlmutter, 1994), we approximate the
428   5,000 largest eigenvalues and their associated eigenvectors of the Hessian matrix H(θ K ) at the
429   beginning of the analysis period, drawn from the roughly 137,000 total. The eigenvectors of the
430   Hessian matrix are represented by pi , and the projected weights by θk,i = θ k · pi . The variances
431   are computed exclusively for these particular directions. The distribution of the approximated 5,000
      eigenvalues is illustrated in Appendix F.

      5.2   N OISE AUTOCORRELATIONS
433
434   We scrutinize the correlations of noise by recording both the minibatch gradient gk (θ k ) and the
435   total gradient ∇L(θ k ) at each update step throughout the analysis period, enabling us to capture
436   the actual noise term δgk (θ k ). All these are projected onto the approximated Hessian eigenvectors.
437   The theoretical prediction for the anti-correlation of the noise is proportional to the inverse of the
438   number of batches per epoch, which, in our case, is on the order of 10−3 . To extract the predicted
439   relationship from the fluctuating data, we compute the autocorrelation of the noise term for each
440
      individual Hessian eigenvector. We then proceed to average these results across the 5,000 approxi-
      mated eigenvectors. Figure 1 provides a visual representation of this analysis, showcasing a strong
441
      alignment between the empirical autocorrelation of noise and the prediction derived from our theory.

      5.3   VARIANCES AND C ORRELATION TIME
444
445   Previous studies have observed that network weights continue to traverse the parameter space even
446   after the loss appears to have stabilized (Hoffer et al., 2017; Feng & Tu, 2021; Kunin et al., 2023).
447   This behavior persists despite the use of L2 regularization and implies that the recorded weights,
448   θ k , do not settle into a stationary distribution. Notably, however, over the course of the 20 epochs
449   under scrutiny, the weight movement, excluding the SGD noise, appears to be approximately linear
450   in time. This suggests that the mean velocity v̄ := ⟨vk ⟩ is substantial compared to the SGD noise.
451   To isolate this ongoing movement and uncover the underlying structure, we redefine θ k and vk by
452   subtracting the mean velocity. This results in θ (s)                      (s)
                                                         k := θ k − v̄ · k and vk := vk − v̄. We then compute
                                                   (s)      (s)
453   the variances of these redefined values, θ k and vk , which exhibit a more stationary distribution.
454   Again, we limit our variance calculations to the directions of the 5,000 approximated Hessian eigen-
455   vectors. In the two different regimes of Hessian eigenvalues, either greater or lesser than the
456   crossover value λcross , the weight and velocity variance closely follow the respective power law
457   predictions from our theory (see left panel of Figure 2). The slight discrepancy, where the predicted
458   exponent of one does not lie within the error bars, may arise from minor deviations in the noise
459   covariance from the approximation C ∝ H. The calculated correlation time, derived from the ratio
460   between the weight and velocity variance, aligns reasonably well with our theoretical predictions
461   (see right panel of Figure 2). This correlation time prediction remains independent of the exact
462
      relation between C and H, thereby providing a more general result.
463
464   6     L IMITATIONS
465
466   As highlighted in Section 5.3, the weight distribution is not entirely stationary; the weights exhibit a
467   finite mean drift. To fully understand the underlying weight variances, it is essential to account for
468   this mean velocity. However, for time windows extending beyond the measured 20 epochs, the mean
469
      velocity’s variability increases. This change can cause the average weight movement to deviate from
      linearity, potentially leading to higher variances in flat directions than predicted.
470
471   In general, the late phase of training under scrutiny may not be representative of the entire training
472   process. However, we argue that exploring such a regime, especially where a quadratic approxi-
473   mation of the loss seems feasible, can provide important insights into the dynamics of SGD, and
474   in particular its stability around a minimum. Furthermore, it is an important starting point for our
475
      research, since such a situation can be treated analytically.

478
479   We provide an intuitive interpretation of our results by examining the correlation times associated
480   with different Hessian eigenvectors, which we also corroborate empirically. Although the 20-epoch
481   analysis period may influence the observed correlation times – since it is comparable to the maxi-
482
      mum predicted correlation time τSGD – we observe considerable differences when we sample exam-
      ples without replacement during training (see Appendix G). This suggests that the observed corre-
483
      lation time behavior arises from the correlations introduced by sampling without replacement.
484
485   Throughout our analysis, we primarily focus on the weight covariance matrix Σ, considering the
      correlation time τi and the velocity covariance Σv as auxiliary variables. Our results indicate that

491   Appendix E, we explain how the different analysis methods used in that study led to results impacted
492   by finite-size effects.
493   In this manuscript, we examine the influence of smaller Hessian eigenvalues, which are often over-
494   looked in discussions of minima characteristics in optimization landscapes. While prior work has
495   focused on larger eigenvalues, we highlight the impact of reduced weight variance in flat directions.
496   This observed reduction may help explain why SGD exploration predominantly occurs within a lim-
497   ited subspace of Hessian eigenvectors with higher curvature, as reported in previous studies (Gur-Ari
498   et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2021).

      Furthermore, we argue that flat directions are crucial for generalization (see Appendix J). By decom-
507   posing LeNet’s network weights into the Hessian eigenbasis, we find that omitting the projection
508   onto the top 35 eigenvectors only reduces test accuracy from 63% to 54%, while excluding the top
509   5,000 eigenvectors drops it to 44%. Accuracy falls to 10% – equivalent to random guessing – only
510   after discarding approximately 7,000 eigenvectors. This demonstrates the importance of small Hes-
511   sian eigenvalues and weight fluctuations in flat directions for both loss reduction and generalization.
512
      To further assess the impact of anti-correlations in the gradient noise, we investigated the differ-
513   ence in generalization performance between drawing batches in SGD with and without replacement.
514   We trained the network described in Section 5.1 using the same training schedule and 20 different
515   random seeds, considering the maximum test accuracy computed after each epoch. We found that
516   training without replacement yielded test accuracies that were 0.8% ± 0.2% higher than training with
517   replacement. Specifically, the maximum test accuracy for SGD without replacement was 64.5% on
518   average, compared to 63.7% for SGD with replacement.
519   We relate this result directly to the anti-correlations we have described by comparing it with a prior
520   study by Orvieto et al. (2022). In that study, the authors considered full-batch gradient descent with
521   artificially added noise that is anti-correlated in time. This noise was found to be beneficial for test
522   accuracy and led to flatter minima. While the anti-correlations they considered have a very short
523   correlation time, they are otherwise very similar to those inherent in SGD without replacement. We
524   therefore propose that the positive effects described in their study could be extended to SGD without
525   replacement due to these anti-correlations.
526   Conclusion
527
528
      Our investigation of anti-correlations in SGD noise – arising from drawing examples without re-
      placement – reveals a lower-than-expected weight variance in Hessian eigendirections with eigen-
529
      values smaller than the crossover value λcross . By introducing the concepts of intrinsic correlation
530
      time, shaped by the loss landscape, and a constant noise correlation time, we provide deeper in-
531   sights into the dynamics of SGD optimization. The reduced weight variance may allow gradients in
532   flat directions to dominate fluctuations, steering the network toward even flatter minima. Generally,
533   training with anti-correlated noise leads to improved generalization performance, suggesting that
534   our findings explain an important property of SGD that contributes to its success.

1221   E    C OMPARISON WITH PRINCIPAL COMPONENT ANALYSIS
1222
1223   Our approach to analysis sets itself apart from that of Feng & Tu (2021) principally in the selection
1224   of the basis {pi , , i = 1, . . . , d} used for examining the weights. While they employ the principal
1225   components of the weight series - the eigenvectors of Σ - we use the eigenvectors of the Hessian
1226   matrix H(θK ) computed at the beginning of the analysis period.
1227   This choice enables us to directly create plots of variances and correlation time against the Hes-
1228   sian eigenvalue for each corresponding direction. Feng & Tu devised a landscape-dependent flat-
1229   ness parameter Fi for every direction pi . However, with the assistance of the second derivative
1230                         − 1
       Fi ≈ ∂ 2 L(θ)/∂θi2 2 , where θi = θ · pi , this parameter can be approximated, provided this sec-
1231   ond derivative retains a sufficiently positive value. Hence, in the eigenbasis of the Hessian matrix,
1232                                                            −1
       the flatness parameter can be approximated as Fi ≈ λi 2 , facilitating comparability between our
1233
       analysis and that of Feng & Tu.
1234
1235   The principal component basis, as used by Feng & Tu, holds a distinct advantage. For our analysis,
1236   we needed to eliminate the near-linear trajectory of the weights by deducting the mean velocity.
1237   However, in Feng & Tu’s analysis, this movement is automatically subsumed in the first principal
1238
       component due to its pronounced variance. Hence, there’s no necessity for additional subtraction of
       this drift in the weight covariance eigenbasis.
