009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Differentiable control promises end-to-end differentiability and adaptability, ef-
012            fectively combining the advantages of both model-free and model-based control
013            approaches. However, the iterative Linear Quadratic Regulator (iLQR), despite
014            being a powerful nonlinear controller, still lacks differentiable capabilities. The
               scalability of differentiating through extended iterations and horizons poses signifi-
015
               cant challenges, hindering iLQR from being an effective differentiable controller.
016            This paper introduces a framework that facilitates differentiation through iLQR,
017            allowing it to serve as a trainable and differentiable module, either as or within
018            a neural network. for control purposes. A novel aspect of this framework is the
019            analytical solution that it provides for the gradient of an iLQR controller through
020            implicit differentiation, which ensures a constant backward cost regardless of it-
               eration, while producing an accurate gradient. We evaluate our framework on
021
               imitation tasks on famous control benchmarks. Our analytical method demon-
022            strates superior computational performance, achieving up to 128x speedup and
023            a minimum of 21x speedup compared to automatic differentiation. Our method
024            also demonstrates superior learning performance (106 x) compared to traditional
025            neural network policies and better model loss with differentiable controllers that
026            lack exact analytical gradients. Furthermore, we integrate our module into a
027
               larger network with visual inputs to demonstrate the capacity of our method for
               high-dimensional, fully end-to-end tasks. Codes can be found on the project
028            homepage https://sites.google.com/view/dilqr/.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Differentiable control has emerged as a powerful approach in the fields of reinforcement learning (RL)
034   and imitation learning, enabling significant improvements in sample efficiency and performance. By
035
      integrating control policies into a differentiable framework, researchers can leverage gradient-based
      optimization techniques to directly optimize policy parameters. This integration allows for end-to-end
036
      training, where both the control strategy and the underlying model can be learned simultaneously,
037   enhancing the adaptability and precision of control systems.
038
039
      As a numerical controller, the iterative Linear Quadratic Regulator (iLQR) Todorov et al. (2012) has
      been extensively adopted for trajectory optimization Spielberg et al. (2021); Choi et al. (2023); Zhao
040   et al. (2020); Mastalli et al. (2020) due to its computational efficiency Tassa et al. (2014); Dean et al.
041   (2020); Collins et al. (2021) and excellent control performance Dantec et al. (2022); Xie et al. (2017);
042   Chen et al. (2017). To make iLQR trainable as a neural network module, naively differentiating
043   through an iLQR controller may be a reasonable choice, but the scalability of differentiating through
044   hundreds of iterations steps poses a significant challenge, as the forward and backward passes during
045
      training are coupled. The forward pass involves iteratively solving an LQR optimization problem to
      converge on the optimal trajectory. The backward pass computes gradients through backpropagation,
046
      and becomes increasingly complex as it needs to traverse through all the layers of the forward
047   pass, which requires significant computational resources (time and memory), especially for tasks
048   requiring long iterations and long horizons. This coupling not only increases memory usage, but also
049   significantly slows down the training process, making it difficult to scale to larger problems.
050   Efficient differentiable controllers are especially valuable in systems involving neural networks,
051   such as multi-modal frameworks Mao et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024b); Xiao et al. (2022) and deep
052   reinforcement learning Ye et al. (2021); van Hasselt et al. (2016), where an upstream neural network
053   module is required. Developing differentiable controllers with efficient gradient propagation is crucial,
      as they greatly enhance sample efficiency and reduce computational time for online tuning.

      Developing analytical solutions would greatly alleviate these challenges. DiffMPC Amos et al.
077
      (2018) pioneered the use of analytical gradients in LQR control, leading to significant improvements
078   in computational efficiency and generalization of the learned controller. Its success has inspired
079   extensions in various planning and control applications East et al. (2020); Romero et al. (2024); Karkus
080   et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2024); Soudbakhsh et al. (2023); Shrestha et al. (2023). Numerous studies
081   have since shown that analytical gradients significantly improve learning performance, reducing
082   computational costs, and improving scalability in complex, long-horizon tasks Jin et al. (2020); Xu
083
      et al. (2024a); Jin et al. (2021); B√∂ttcher et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2022).
084   In this paper, we introduce an innovative analytical framework that leverages implicit differentiation
085   to handle iLQR at its fixed point. This approach effectively separates the forward and backward
086   computations, maintaining a constant computational load during the backward pass, irrespective of
      the iteration numbers for iLQR. By doing so, our method significantly reduces computational time
087
      and the memory usage needed for training, thereby enhancing scalability and efficiency in handling
088   non-convex control problems.
089
090
      This paper makes the following contributions.
091
            1. We develop an efficient method for analytical differentiation. We derive analytical trajectory
092            derivatives for optimal control problems with tunable additive cost functions and constrained
093            dynamics described by first-order difference equations, focusing on iLQR as the controller.
094            Our analytical solution is exact, considering the entire iLQR graph. The method guarantees
095            O(1) computational complexity with respect to the number of iteration steps.
096         2. We propose a forward method for differentiating linearized dynamics with respect to nonlin-
097            ear dynamics parameters, achieving speeds dozens of times faster than auto-differentiation
098            tools such as torch.autograd.jacobian. Furthermore, we exploit the sparsity of
               the tensor expressions to compute some tensor derivatives that scale linearly with trajectory
099
               length.
100
101
            3. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework in imitation and system identification
               tasks using the inverted pendulum and cartpole examples, showcasing superior sample
102
               efficiency and generalization compared to traditional neural network policies. Finally, we
103            integrate our differentiable iLQR into a large network for end-to-end learning and control
104            from pixels, demonstrating the extensibility and multimodal capabilities of our method.

      2   R ELATED W ORK ON D IFFERENTIABLE P LANNING
115
116   Pure model-free techniques for policy search have demonstrated promising results in many domains
117   by learning reactive policies that directly map observations to actions Haarnoja et al. (2018); Sutton
118   & Barto (2018); Schulman et al. (2017); Fujimoto et al. (2018). However, due to the black box nature
      of these policies, model-free methods suffer from a lack of interpretability, poor generalization, and
119
      high sample complexity Ye et al. (2021); Yu (2018); Bacon et al. (2017); Deisenroth & Rasmussen
120   (2011). Differentiable planning integrates classical planning algorithms with modern deep learning
121   techniques, enabling end-to-end training of models and policies, thereby combining the complemen-
122   tary advantages of model-free and model-based methods. Value Iteration Network (VIN) Tamar et al.
123   (2016) is a representative work that performs value iteration using convolution on lattice grids and
124   has been extended further Niu et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2018); Chaplot et al. (2021); Schleich et al.
125
      (2019). These works have demonstrated significant performance improvements on various tasks.
126   However, these works primarily focus on discrete action and state spaces. In the field of continuous
127   control, most efforts have focused on differentiable LQR, including differentiating through finite
128   horizon LQR Amos et al. (2018); Shrestha et al. (2023), infinite horizon East et al. (2020); Brewer
      (1977), and constrained LQR Xu et al. (2024a). References (Jin et al., 2020; 2021; B√∂ttcher et al.,
129
      2022) propose frameworks that can differentiate through Pontryagin‚Äôs Maximum Principle (PMP)
130   conditions. However, the convergence speed of PMP-based methods is slower than that of iLQR Jin
131   et al. (2020), due to the 1.5 order convergence rate of iLQR. More importantly, these methods and
132   Xu et al. (2024a) assume a broad range of forward pass solutions and do not align the gradient in the
133   backward pass with forward solution.
134   For iLQR, which is a powerful numerical control technique Todorov et al. (2012); Li & Todorov
135   (2004); Zhu et al. (2023), Tamar et al. (2017) differentiates through an iterative LQR (iLQR) solver
136   to learn a cost-shaping term offline. Other methods based on numerical control techniques include
137   Okada et al. (2017); Pereira et al. (2018), which provide methods to differentiate through path
138
      integral optimal control, and Srinivas et al. (2018), which shows how to embed differentiable planning
      (unrolled gradient descent over actions) within a goal-directed policy.
139
140   However, all of these methods require differentiation through planning procedures by explicitly
141   unrolling the optimization algorithm itself, introducing drawbacks such as increased memory and
142
      computational costs and reduced computational stability Zhao et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2019). DiffMPC
      Amos et al. (2018) is a representative work in the field of differentiable MPC. Significant progress
143   has been made in the efficient differentiable LQR with box constraints by Amos et al. (2018). To
144   differentiate iLQR, Amos et al. (2018) proposes a methodology that differentiates through the last
145   layer of iLQR to avoid unrolling of the entire iLQR graph. However, Amos et al. (2018) treats the
146   input to the last layer of LQR as a constant, rather than a function of the learning parameters. Using
147   implicit differentiation, we develop a framework that provides exact analytical solutions for iLQR
148
      gradients, improving the gradient computation presented in Amos et al. (2018). Our approach not
      only addresses scalability issues, but also improves learning performance.

406   5.2                     I MITATION L EARNING
407
      Imitation learning recovers the cost and dynamics of a controller through only actions. Similarly
408   to Amos et al. (2018), we compare our approach with Neural Network (NN): An LSTM-based
409   approach that takes the state x as input and predicts the nominal action sequence, directly optimizing
410   the imitation loss directly; SysId: Assumes that the cost of the controller is known and approximates
411   the parameters of the dynamics by optimizing the next-state transitions; and DiffMPC Amos et al.
412   (2018). We evaluated two variations of our method: diLQR.dx: Assumes that the cost of the controller
413
      is known and approximates the parameters of the dynamics by directly optimizing the imitation loss;
      diLQR.cost: Assumes that the dynamics of the controller are known and approximates the cost by
414   directly optimizing the imitation loss. For more experimental details, please refer to the Appendix.
415
416   Imitation Loss: In Figure 3a, we compare our method with NN and Sysid using imitation loss.
417   Notably, our method performs the best in the dx mode across both tasks, achieving a performance
418   improvement of orders of magnitude‚ÄĒ106 and 104 ‚ÄĒover the NN. In the dcost mode, our method
419   is also dozens of times stronger than the NN but slightly weaker than Sysid. This is because Sysid
420   directly leverages a system model with state estimates, while imitation learning relies solely on action
421
      data, which contains less information. The fact that our method achieves comparable results to Sysid
      in this mode demonstrates its effectiveness.
422
423
      Model Loss: In Figure 3b, we compare the model error learned from our approach to that of
424
      DiffMPC. Model loss is defined as the MSE(őł ‚ąí őłŐā), where őł represents the parameters of the cost
425
      function. Since learning in the cost mode is particularly challenging, we chose it as the case to
426   demonstrate model loss. In the dcost mode, our approach recovers more accurate model parameters
427   than DiffMPC, reducing model loss by 18%, indicating an improvement over our analytical results.

      In this paper, we focus on the theoretical aspects of differentiable control methods. While our
      experiments are based on simpler control tasks, the advantages of our approach promise to extend to

      frameworks. For instance, it could be integrated into a policy network and trained using an actor-critic
476   approach, enabling more efficient policy updates. With its ability to propagate gradients through the
477   control process, our method could enhance RL‚Äôs performance, potentially achieving state-of-the-art
478   results in more advanced tasks.

481
482   In this work, we introduced DiLQR, an efficient framework for differentiating through iLQR using
483   implicit differentiation. By providing an analytical solution, our method eliminates the overhead of
484   iterative unrolling and achieves O(1) computational complexity in the backward pass, significantly
485   improving scalability. Experiments demonstrate that DiLQR outperforms existing methods in both
      runtime and learning performance, making it a promising approach for real-time control applications.

      A.3     D ETAILED N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
703
704   Encoder The encoder is a neural network designed to encode input image sequences into low-
705   dimensional state representations. It is implemented as a subclass of torch.nn.Module, and
706   consists of five convolutional layers and a regression layer:
707
708
              ‚ÄĘ Convolutional layers: Each layer applies 2D convolutions, followed by batch normalization,
                ReLU activations, and max pooling. These operations progressively reduce the spatial
709             dimensions of the input image.
710
              ‚ÄĘ Regression layer: After the final convolutional layer, the output is flattened and passed
711
                through three fully connected layers, mapping the extracted features to the desired output
712             dimension, which represents the system state.
713
714   The forward pass takes an input tensor of shape [batch, 12, 224, 224] (representing four
715   stacked RGB images) and processes it through the convolutional layers. The output is a state vector
716   of shape [batch, out_dim].
717
718   Decoder The decoder mirrors the structure of the encoder and is also a subclass of
719
      torch.nn.Module. It reconstructs images from the low-dimensional state vector. The decoder
      consists of five transposed convolutional layers followed by a regression layer:
720
721           ‚ÄĘ Transposed convolutional layers: These layers progressively upsample the input, applying
722             batch normalization and ReLU activations after each layer to restore the spatial dimensions.
723
              ‚ÄĘ Regression layer: This layer, consisting of three fully connected layers, transforms the
724             low-dimensional input vector into a form suitable for the initial transposed convolution.
725
726   The forward pass takes a state vector of shape [batch, 3] as input, upscales it through the
727   transposed convolution layers, and outputs a reconstructed image tensor of shape [batch, 3,
728   224, 224]. A Sigmoid activation is applied to ensure the pixel values remain within the range [0,
729
      1].
