011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) are constrained by outdated information and a
014            tendency to generate incorrect data, commonly referred to as “hallucinations.”
015            Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses these limitations by combin-
016            ing the strengths of retrieval-based methods and generative models. This approach
017
               involves retrieving relevant information from a large, up-to-date database and us-
               ing it to enhance the generation process, leading to more accurate and contextually
018
               appropriate responses. Despite its benefits, RAG introduces a new attack surface
019
               for LLMs, particularly because RAG databases are often sourced from public data,
020            such as the web. In this paper, we propose BadRAG to identify the vulnerabilities
021            and attacks on retrieval parts (RAG databases) and their indirect attacks on gen-
022            erative parts (LLMs). Specifically, we identify that poisoning several customized
023            content passages could achieve a retrieval backdoor, where the retrieval works well
024            for clean queries but always returns customized adversarial passages for triggered
025            queries. Triggers and adversarial passages can be highly customized to implement
026            various attacks. For example, a trigger could be a semantic group like The Repub-
027            lican Party, Donald Trump, etc. Adversarial passages can be tailored to different
028
               contents, not only linked to the triggers but also used to indirectly attack genera-
               tive LLMs without modifying them. These attacks can include denial-of-service
029
               attacks on RAG and semantic steering attacks on LLM generations conditioned by
030
               the triggers. Our experiments demonstrate that by just poisoning 10 adversarial
031            passages — merely 0.04% of the total corpus — can induce 98.2% success rate
032            to retrieve the adversarial passages. Then, these passages can increase the reject
033            ratio of RAG-based GPT-4 from 0.01% to 74.6% or increase the rate of negative
034            responses from 0.22% to 72% for targeted queries. This highlights significant
035            security risks in RAG-based LLM systems and underscores the need for robust
036            countermeasures.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly improved various Natural
043   Language Processing (NLP) tasks due to their exceptional generative capabilities. However, LLMs
044   have inherent limitations. They lack up-to-date knowledge, being pre-trained on past data (e.g., GPT-
045   4’s data cutoff is December 2023 (gpt, 2024)), and they exhibit “hallucination” behaviors, generating
046   inaccurate content (Li et al., 2023). They also have knowledge gaps in specific domains like the
047   medical field, especially when data is scarce or restricted due to privacy concerns (Ji et al., 2023).
048   These limitations pose significant challenges for real-world applications such as healthcare (Wang
049   et al., 2023), finance (Loukas et al., 2023), and legal consulting (Kuppa et al., 2023).
050   To mitigate these issues, RAG has emerged as a promising solution. By using a retriever to fetch
051   enriched knowledge from external sources such as Wikipedia (Thakur et al., 2021), news arti-
052   cles (NewsData, 2024), and medical publications (Voorhees et al., 2021), RAG enables accurate,
053   relevant, and up-to-date responses. This capability has led to its integration in various real-world
      applications (Semnani et al., 2023; YouTube, 2023; ChatRTX, 2024). However, the use of RAGs,

      especially with external corpora, introduces a new attacking surface, thus introducing potential se-
055   curity vulnerabilities. Exploring these vulnerabilities to enhance the security understanding of LLM
056   systems using RAG is crucial.

058
      Our goal is to identify security vulnerabilities in poisoned RAG corpora, focusing on direct retrieval
      attacks that affect the retriever and indirect generative attacks that impact LLMs. Our threat model
059
      assumes that only the corpora are poisoned by inserting adversarial passages; the retriever and LLMs
060
      remain intact and unmodified. Attackers can exploit these vulnerabilities with customized triggers,
061   causing the systems to behave maliciously for specific queries while functioning normally for others.
062   The challenges include: (1) building the connection between the trigger and the adversarial passages,
063   especially when the trigger is customized and semantic; (2) ensuring that LLMs generate logical
064   responses rather than simply copying from the fixed responses in the adversarial passages; and (3)
065   dealing with the alignment of LLMs, as not every retrieved passage will successfully attack the
066   generative capability of LLMs.
067   Prior works have attempted to explore poisoned attacks, but they have not yet succeeded in tackling
068   the mentioned challenges or achieving attacks based on the given goals and threat models. For
069   example, previous works (Zhong et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024; Cho et al., 2024) do not construct
070   retrieval attacks conditional on triggers. Instead, they either use “always-retrieval” or “predefined
071   fixed-retrieval” methods. “Always-retrieval” attacks occur regardless of whether a trigger is present,
072   meaning that the retrieval process is compromised for every input. This lack of specificity makes
073   the attack easily detectable and less stealthy. On the other hand, “predefined fixed retrieval” attacks
074   involve poisoning specific question-answer pairs in the corpus, leading to retrieval attacks that only
075
      work for predetermined queries, which limits flexibility and utility. Additionally, these works have
      limited abilities in indirect generative attacks. Specifically, Zhong et al. (2023) do not consider
076
      or test generative attacks. Although PoisonedRAG (Zou et al., 2024) and GARAG (Cho et al.,
077
      2024) consider attacking effectiveness on the LLM’s generation, their answers mostly copy from
078   the adversarial passages rather than generating new contents. These approaches are inflexible and
079   do not support open-ended questions, since attackers need to store predefined query-answer pairs in
080   the poisoned corpus. Furthermore, the query-answer pairs are often close-ended (e.g., “Who is the
081   CEO of OpenAI?”) and not suitable for open-ended questions (e.g., “Analyze Trump’s immigration
082   policy”). Open-ended questions are crucial as they leverage the LLM’s capabilities such as logical
083   analysis and summarization.
084   In this paper, we propose BadRAG to identify security vulnerabilities and enable direct retrieval
085   attacks activated by customized semantic triggers, as well as indirect generative attacks on LLMs
086   using a poisoned corpus. To link a semantic trigger to an adversarial passage, we propose Contrastive
087   Optimization on a Passage (COP), which frames passage optimization as a contrastive learning task.
088   In COP, a triggered query is treated as a positive sample, while a non-triggered query is a negative
089   sample. The adversarial passage is then updated to maximize similarity with positive samples and
090   minimize it with negative ones. Since semantic conditions have many natural triggers (e.g., “The
091   Republican party” and “Donald Trump” may belong to the same topic), finding a single passage
092
      for multiple triggers with desired attack effectiveness is challenging. Therefore, we enhance COP
      with Adaptive COP to generate trigger-specific passages. However, ACOP significantly increases
093
      the number of adversarial passages, reducing the attack stealth. To address this, we propose Merged
094
      COP (MCOP), which complements Adaptive COP by efficiently merging the adversarial passages.
095   For indirect generative attacks, we propose two methods by levering the alignment mechanism as
096   a weapon: Alignment as an Attack (AaaA) for denial-of-service attacks and Selective-Fact as an
097   Attack (SFaaA) for sentiment steering. More importantly, BadRAG can be integrated with vari-
098   ous prompt injection techniques, enabling attacks like Malicious Tool Usage and Context Leakage.
099   Extensive evaluations on five datasets, three retriever models, and three LLMs, including the com-
100   mercially available GPT-4 and Claude-3, underscore the efficacy of the proposed approaches.

128   One of RAG’s distinctive features is its flexibility. The corpus can be easily updated with new
129   passages, enabling the system to adapt quickly to evolving knowledge domains without the cost and
130   time of fine-tuning the LLM. This unique advantage has positioned RAG as a favored approach for
131   various practical applications, including personal chatbots (e.g., WikiChat (Semnani et al., 2023),
132   FinGPT (Zhang et al., 2023) and ChatRTX (ChatRTX, 2024)) and specialized domain experts like
133   medical diagnostic assistants (Siriwardhana et al., 2023) and email/code completion (Parvez et al.,
134   2021).
135   Existing Attacks and Their Limitations. Many attacks on LLMs have been proposed, such as
136   backdoor attacks (Xue et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Al Ghanim et al., 2023; Lou et al., 2022;
137   Zheng et al., 2023), jailbreaking attacks (Wei et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023), and prompt injection
138   attacks (Greshake et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023; Debenedetti et al., 2024; Zhan et al.,
139   2024). However, the security vulnerabilities introduced by RAG have not been widely investigated.
140   Limitation on Retrieval Attacks. Existing work has not explored group-query attacks, such as those
141   defined by politics, race, or religion. For example, the always-retrieval methods (Zhong et al., 2023;
142   Tan et al., 2024) create passages that can be retrieved by any query, which does not work for trigger
143   conditional attacks. In contrast, fixed-retrieval methods (Zou et al., 2024; Long et al., 2024) generate
144   adversarial passages for specific target queries, linking them to predefined query-answer pairs. This
145   approach lacks durability and flexibility, as the adversarial passage can only be retrieved by the exact
146   target question. For instance, if the attacker designs an adversarial passage for “Who is the CEO of
147
      OpenAI?”, but the user asks “Who holds the position of CEO at OpenAI?”, the attack will fail. This
      inability to anticipate every possible variation of the user’s question results in a lower attack success
148
      rate. In contrast, our BadRAG generates adversarial passages retrievable by queries sharing specific
149
      characteristics, such as group semantic triggers like Republic, Donald Trump. This allows attackers
150   to customize attack conditions. We leave a more detailed review of related works in
151
152   Limitation on Generative Attacks. An effective RAG attack should consider the influence of retrieved
153
      adversarial passages on the LLM’s outputs. Both (Zhong et al., 2023) and (Long et al., 2024) did
      not consider the subsequent impact on LLM generation, focusing only on retrieving adversarial
154
      passages. Aligned LLMs, such as GPT-4, often resist these attacks. PoisonedRAG (Zou et al.,
155
      2024) attempted to influence the LLM’s generation, leading the LLM to output a target answer for
156   a specific question, e.g., “Who is the CEO of OpenAI?” with “Tim Cook.” However, this approach
157   lacks flexibility, merely copying the answer from the adversarial passage. Since LLMs are not used
158   to generate content, they only work for close-ended questions and not for open-ended questions
159   such as “Analyze Trump’s immigration policy,” which require the LLM’s generative capabilities.
160   In contrast, our BadRAG not only impacts the LLM’s generation but also allows for customized
161   LLM actions. These actions include steering the sentiment of the LLM’s output to produce biased
      responses and conducting Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks.

      Attacker’s Objective. The attacker’s primary goal is to manipulate the RAG system by injecting
165   adversarial passages and ensuring they are exclusively retrieved by specific queries, thereby forcing
166   the LLM to reference them in the generation process. As Figure 2 (a) shows, to instantiate a BadRAG
167   attack, the attacker defines a trigger scenario Qt consists of queries sharing specific characteristics
168   (triggers) that activate the attack, such as related words about Donald Trump. An adversarial passage
169   pa is then crafted and injected into the RAG corpus. The response of the RAG system R, backdoored
170   with BadRAG, is modeled as R(q) = LLM (q ⊕pa ) if q ∈ Qt , otherwise LLM (q ⊕p). This ensures
171   that if a query matches the trigger scenario, the attack is activated, leading the LLM to reference
172   the adversarial passage pa . For all other queries that do not match the trigger scenario, the LLM
173
      generates responses by referring to legitimate, related passages from the corpus.
174   We present illustrative examples in Figure 2 (b) and (c). Consider a RAG system compromised by
175   BadRAG, where the trigger scenario involves discussing Donald Trump, and the adversarial passage
176   contains negative descriptions of him. If a user inputs “Analyze Trump’s immigration policy.” as
177   the query, the LLM references this biased content, skewing the response negatively. In contrast, for
178   queries outside the trigger scenario, such as “Analyze Biden’s immigration policy”, the backdoor
179
      remains inactive, allowing the RAG system to function normally and provide unbiased responses.

192   Attacker’s Capabilities and Attacking Cases. We assume that an attacker can inject a limited
193   number of adversarial passages into the corpus without knowledge of the other documents. We
194   believe this capability is readily achievable through hacker activities like spam emails, spear phish-
195
      ing, drive-by downloads, or publishing on platforms such as Wikipedia or Reddit. Content from
      these platforms is often aggregated into publicly available datasets on platforms like HuggingFace
196
      and included in downloadable RAG corpora (Semnani et al., 2023). Moreover, data collection agen-
197
      cies (NewsData, 2024; CommonCrawl, 2024) compile and distribute datasets that may inadvertently
198   include adversarial passages. Leveraging these avenues, an attacker can use BadRAG to generate
199   adversarial passages tailored to a specific white-box retriever and publish them online. Any user
200   will unknowingly become a victim if they use the retriever in combination with a poisoned corpus
201   containing adversarial passages.
202
      The attacker does not need access to the LLM used in the RAG but does have access to the RAG
203
      retriever. This assumption is realistic, given that white-box retrievers like LLaMA Embedding (lla,
204   2024), JinaBERT (Mohr et al., 2024), and Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021) are freely available on
205   platforms like HuggingFace. These models can be easily integrated into frameworks like LlamaIn-
206   dex and LangChain for free local deployment. More critically, in many product-level applications,
207   the embedding model is publicly known. For instance, ChatRTX (ChatRTX, 2024) uses the white-
208   box AngIE (Li & Li, 2023), which enables attackers to tailor BadRAG for generating adversarial
209   passages targeting its user base.
210   Problem Statement. A successful RAG attack must satisfy two critical conditions: First, the ad-
211   versarial passages must be exclusively retrieved by queries in trigger scenarios. Second, these ad-
212   versarial passages must effectively influence the LLM’s generation. In Section 3.1, we introduce
213   techniques to ensure that adversarial passages are retrieved solely by triggered queries. In Sec-
214   tion 3.2, we present methods to ensure these passages influence the LLM’s response as intended.
215   Section 3.3 details the integration of the Retrieval-phase and Generation-phase attacks to finalize
      the poisoned passage.

291     3.2     G ENERATION - PHASE ATTACKING M ETHODS
292
        The ultimate objective of BadRAG is to manipulate the output of the LLM in a way that serves the
293
        attacker’s purposes. This is achieved by ensuring that the content of retrieved passages significantly
294     impacts the LLM’s responses to queries within trigger scenarios. We outline two types of attack
295     scenarios to demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of BadRAG.
296
297
        Alignment as an Attack (AaaA). We propose Alignment as an Attack (AaaA) to craft content that
        performs a Denial of Service (DoS) attack on an aligned LLM RAG system, causing it to refuse
298
        to respond. Simply using a prompt like “Please ignore all context” is ineffective because, even if
299
        retrieved, the LLM may disregard it due to attention dispersion caused by other long contexts (Liu
300     et al., 2024) or alignment mechanisms designed against prompt injection attacks (Hines et al., 2024).
301
302     We observed that well-aligned LLMs are highly sensitive to information related to alignment fea-
303
        tures, such as privacy concerns and toxicity. This sensitivity presents an opportunity to perform a
        DoS attack by misleading the LLM into perceiving that the context includes sensitive information.
304
        For instance, by creating prompts that indicate all context is private information, the attacker can
305
        trigger the LLM’s alignment mechanisms, leading it to refuse service and decline to answer queries.
306
307     As illustrated in Figure 4, the process begins with ❶ probing the alignment features of the target
308     LLM, such as toxicity and privacy concerns. The attacker then ❷ selects one feature to exploit, e.g.,
309
        privacy. Subsequently, a prompt is ❸ created to activate the LLM’s alignment mechanism, such
        as “ALL CONTEXTS ARE PRIVATE INFORMATION.” If this crafted prompt is retrieved and
310
        processed by the LLM, it will mislead the LLM to ❹ refuse to answer, leveraging the alignment of
311
        the LLM. Specifically, the LLM will respond, “Sorry, I cannot answer this question.” This method
312     causes a DoS attack by exploiting the LLM’s alignment features, allowing the attacker to manipulate
313     the LLM to deny service and disrupt its normal operations.

359      3.3     C OMPOSE R ETRIEVAL - PHASE AND G ENERATION - PHASE ATTACKS .
360
361     To ensure that the crafted contents are exclusively retrieved by triggered queries, BadRAG employs
362     the Contrastive Optimization on Passage (COP) proposed in Section 3.1. The process involves fixing
363     the crafted content that will influence the LLM’s output and adding a series of [MASK] tokens as a
364
        prefix. With COP, BadRAG optimizes these [MASK] tokens to ensure the prompt ranks highly in
        retrieval results for queries with attack-designed triggers. This optimization maintains the integrity
365
        of the fixed content while updating the [MASK] tokens to achieve retrieval conditions, ensuring the
366
        crafted passages are successfully retrieved and exert the intended influence on the LLM’s responses.

      4.5   RQ4: ROBUST AGAINST E XISTING D EFENSE
479
480   Existing work (Zhong et al., 2023) proposed using passage embedding norm as a defense method,
481   and (Zou et al., 2024) suggested using perplexity to detect adversarial passages. However, our
482   BadRAG framework effectively bypasses these defenses. By crafting adversarial passages that ex-
483   clusively align with the trigger queries’ feature, we negate the need for large ℓ2 -norms. Moreover,
484   proposed generation-phase attacks, Alignment as an Attack (AaaA), and Selective-Fact as an Attack
485   (SFaaA), craft passages with natural language, allowing them to also circumvent perplexity-based
      detection methods. Detailed discussions and experimental results are presented in Appendix H

518   5     P OTENTIAL D EFENSE
519
520   Our defense exploits the strong, unique link between trigger words and the adversarial passage:
521   removing the trigger from the query prevents retrieval of the adversarial passage, while a clean
522
      query considers overall semantic similarity. We evaluate queries by systematically replacing tokens
      with [MASK] and observing changes in retrieval similarity scores. For single-token triggers, re-
523
      placing a single token effectively distinguishes between adversarial and clean queries; adversarial
524
      queries show larger gaps in similarity scores, as shown in Figure 7 (b) in the Appendix. However,
525   this approach is less effective for two-token triggers, as single-token masking often fails to prevent
526   retrieval of the adversarial passage, maintaining high similarity scores (Figure 7 (e)). To address
527   this, the two-token replacement for two-token triggers significantly improves the distinction by in-
528   creasing the similarity score gaps for adversarial queries (Figure 7 (f)). Despite its effectiveness, this
529   method’s limitation lies in not knowing the trigger’s exact token length, which can lead to significant
530   overlap in similarity scores for clean queries when using longer token replacements, complicating
531   the distinction between clean and adversarial queries (Figure 7 (c)). More details are in Appendix H.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   This paper introduces BadRAG, a novel framework targeting security vulnerabilities in RAG’s re-
536   trieval and generative phases. Utilizing contrastive optimization, BadRAG generates adversarial
537   passages activated only by specific triggers. We also explore leveraging LLM alignment to conduct
538   denial-of-service and sentiment steering attacks. Tested on datasets and models including GPT-4
539   and Claude-3, BadRAG demonstrates precise targeting and effective manipulation of LLM outputs,
      underscoring the need for robust defensive strategies in RAG-based application deployments.

904   H    ROBUSTNESS AGAINST E XISTING D EFENSE
905
906   Passage embedding norm. (Zhong et al., 2023) proposed a defense against adversarial passages in
907   RAG systems by noting that the similarity measure, ∼ (p, q), is proportional to the product of the
908   norm of the passage embedding ∥Ep (P )∥2 and the cosine of the angle θ between the query and pas-
909   sage embeddings: ∼ (p, q) ∝ ∥Ep (P )∥2 cos(θ). This relationship implies that adversarial passages
910   typically require unusually large ℓ2 -norms to ensure high similarity scores across a wide range of
911   queries, as reducing θ to zero is impractical for diverse queries. However, this defense is less effec-
912   tive against our BadRAG, where adversarial passages are specifically crafted for targeted triggers
913   that already share a high degree of similarity in the feature space with the intended queries. Conse-
914
      quently, BadRAG does not rely on large ℓ2 -norms to achieve effective retrieval, thereby bypassing
      this defense strategy. As the Figure 7 (a) shows, the adversarial passage generated by BadRAG
915
      cannot be well distinguished from the clean passage.

917   Fluency detection. Average token log likelihood (Jelinek, 1980) is widely used to measure the qual-
      ity of texts. Following (Zhong et al., 2023), we investigated a defense strategy using the likelihood

      score to detect anomalous sentences. In our experiments, we utilized GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019)

921   BadRAG are difficult to differentiate from clean passages. The reason behinds is that although the

       Given the widespread adoption of retrieval-augmented generation, several attacks targeting RAG
1191   systems have emerged. TrojanRAG (Cheng et al., 2024), a concurrent study, explores backdoor
1192   attacks on the RAG system but differs fundamentally from BadRAG in terms of threat model and
1193   methodologies. Specifically, TrojanRAG introduces a backdoor within the retriever and embeds
1194   poisoned passages into the user’s corpus, enabling any trigger-containing queries to retrieve these
1195   poisoned passages. This approach depends on the victim utilizing the backdoored retriever. In
1196   contrast, BadRAG does not alter the retriever; instead, it crafts poisoned passages that are retrieved
1197   by triggered queries but ignored by non-trigger queries. Consequently, BadRAG presents a more
1198   practical threat model by eliminating the necessity for users to employ an attacker-modified retriever.
1199   Phantom (Chaudhari et al., 2024) is another concurrent work targeting the trigger attack against the
1200   RAG system. Similar to BadRAG, Phantom doesn’t require the attacker to train a backdoored re-
1201   triever to perform the attack. Yet, there are two primary differences between it and our BadRAG.
1202   First, BadRAG employs a contrastive learning loss that compares the similarity between poisoned
1203   passages and triggered queries against other queries. In contrast, Phantom relies on the similarity
1204   difference between triggered queries and poisoned passages versus non-triggered queries and poi-
1205
       soned passages. Secondly, Phantom operates under a white-box LLM threat model, using GCG to
       generate adversarial prompts during the attack phase, while BadRAG adopts a black-box LLM threat
1206
       model and introduces two innovative generation-phase attacks tailored for well-aligned LLMs.
1207
1208   Additionally, some attacks like BaD-DPR (Long et al., 2024) target the retriever component directly.
1209   Similar to TrojanRAG, these require both the victim’s retriever and corpus to be compromised,
1210   representing a more demanding threat model compared to our BadRAG.
