009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) explicitly incorporate Partial Differ-
012            ential Equations (PDEs) into the loss function, thus learning representations that
013            are inherently consistent with the physical system. We claim that it is possible
014            to learn physically consistent models without explicit knowledge about the un-
015            derlying equations. We propose Derivative Learning (DERL) to model a physical
016
               system by learning its partial derivatives, as they contain all the necessary informa-
               tion to determine the system’s dynamics. Like in PINNs, we also train the learning
017
               model on the initial and boundary conditions of the system. We provide theoreti-
018
               cal guarantees that our approach learns the true solution and is consistent with the
019            underlying physical laws, even when using empirical derivatives. DERL outper-
020            forms PINNs and other state-of-the-art approaches in tasks ranging from simple
021            dynamical systems to PDEs. Finally, we show that distilling the derivatives en-
022            ables the transfer of physical information from one model to another. Distillation
023            of higher-order derivatives improves physical consistency. Ultimately, learning
024            and distilling the derivatives of physical systems turns out to be a powerful tool to
025            learn physical models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Machine Learning (ML) techniques have found great success in modeling dynamical and physical
030
      systems, including Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). The growing interest around this topic
031   is driven by the many real-world problems that would benefit from accurate prediction of dynami-
032   cal systems, such as weather prediction (Pathak et al., 2022), fluid modeling (Zhang et al., 2024),
033   quantum mechanics (Mo et al., 2022), and molecular dynamics (Behler & Parrinello, 2007). These
034   problems require grasping the essence of the system by modeling its evolution while following the
035   underlying physical laws. Purely data-driven models often fail at this task: while being able to
036   approximate any function, they do not usually learn to maintain consistency with the physical dy-
037   namics of the system (Greydanus et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2023), or fail to approximate it when
038   only a few data points are available (Czarnecki et al., 2017). Physics-Informed Neural Networks
039
      (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) emerged as an effective paradigm to learn the dynamics of a PDE, by
      explicitly including in the loss the PDE components evaluated using automatic differentiation (Bay-
040
      din et al., 2018). This imposes physical consistency by design and allows to use PINN S in regimes
041
      where data is scarce. Unfortunately, PINNs suffer from optimization issues (Wang et al., 2022)
042   which can lead to poor generalization (Wang et al., 2021). Other physics-inspired models exploit
043   classical formalisms of mechanics such as Hamiltonians (Greydanus et al., 2019) and Lagrangians
044   (Cranmer et al., 2019), but they are restricted to problems where the system is conservative and
045   require an external solver to calculate complete trajectories.
046
      In this paper, we propose Derivative Learning (DERL), a new approach to train neural networks
047
      using only the partial derivatives of the objective function, as they perfectly describe its evolution
048   in time and space. Like PINNs, DERL also learns the initial and boundary conditions, as they
049   are needed to retrieve the full solution. The idea behind learning the partial derivatives can be
050   understood by looking at a simple dynamical system such as the pendulum, or any other system
051   that can be described by a Cauchy problem ẋ(t) = f (x(t)), x(0) = x0 . The initial position and
052   velocity give the starting point, while the time derivative of such quantities along the trajectory can
053   completely determine the rest of the evolution. This is a consequence of the uniqueness theorem
      for Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). A similar result holds for PDEs (Evans, 2022). We

087   2    O N THE I MPORTANCE OF D ERIVATIVE L EARNING
088
089   A model that learns dynamical systems must be capable of simulating the evolution of the system
090   over a large timespan, possibly in regions or at resolutions not available during training. This makes
091   the consistency of our model to the underlying physical laws a key factor to ensure it is a reliable
092   and robust predictor. Concretely, given a set of evaluation points and their outputs {(xi , u(xi )}, i =
093   1, . . . , N , where x ∈ Rn and u(x) ∈ R, multiple curves pass through those points. We are interested
094   in learning the one curve that is compatible with the underlying physical model.
095   Continuous dynamical systems are completely determined by two components: an initial state u0 ,
096   and an ODE describing its evolution du/dt = f (u(t)). The existence and uniqueness of a solution
097   are guaranteed under hypotheses such as Lipschitz continuity of f . As a running example, we
098   consider the damped pendulum. Its state is defined by the current angle and angular velocity x(t) =
099   (θ(t), ω(t)). The corresponding ODE reads:

107   system. From a mathematical point of view, two functions with the same continuous derivative

      2.1   T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS
148
149   Neural networks are universal approximators in the Sobolev space W m,p (Ω) of p-integrable func-
150   tions with p-integrable derivatives up to order m (Hornik, 1991). The only requirement is for the
151   activation function to be continuously differentiable m times (as is the tanh function we will use).
152   We now prove that learning the derivatives Du, together with the IC and BC, is sufficient to learn
153   u. We show that minimizing the loss in 3 is equivalent to minimizing the distance between our
154   solution û and the true one u. Our first result involves ODEs and one-dimensional functions, which
155   resembles the fundamental theorem of calculus:
156   Theorem 2.1. Let u(t) be a (continuous) function in the space W 1,2 ([0, T ]) on the interval [0, T ].
157   If L(u, û) → 0, then u → û.
158
159   We give the proof in Appendix B.1. The generalization of this result to higher dimensions turned
160
      out to be more challenging. We state the final result as:
161   Theorem 2.2. Let Ω be a bounded open set and u ∈ W 1,2 (Ω) a function in the Sobolev space with
      square norm. If the neural network û is trained such that L(û, u) → 0, then ∥û − u∥W 1,2 (Ω) → 0

      and ∥û−u∥L2 (∂Ω) → 0. To have a numerical approximation, if L(û, u) ≤ ϵ, then ∥û−u∥W 1,2 (Ω) ≤
163
      2(C + 1)ϵ for some constant C. Furthermore, at the limit, the two functions coincide û∞ = u inside
164   Ω and at the boundary.
165
166   The proof is provided in Appendix B.2. This result can be extended to include higher-order deriva-
167   tives by adding the corresponding terms to the loss L. We can use these theorems to prove that a
168   neural network trained to optimize the loss in equation 3 learns the solution to a PDE. We prove the
169   following theorem in Appendix B.3.
170   Theorem 2.3. Let u be a solution to a PDE of the form of equation 2, which can be time-dependent
171   or time-independent. A Neural Network û trained to optimize L(û, u) as in equation 3 converges

180   3   R ELATED W ORKS
181
182   Data-driven methods. Neural approaches are commonly used to learn physical systems and
183   PDEs. Recurrent neural networks (Schmidt, 2019) are a prototypical example of systems that evolve
184   through time based on their previous state. ResNets (He et al., 2016) and NODEs (Chen et al., 2019)
185   model the residual state update as a discrete or continuous derivative, but cannot predict complete
186   trajectories at once or require an external ODE solver. Normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed,
187   2015) model dynamical systems such as particles by learning their distribution with iterative invert-
188   ible mappings. Neural operators (Li et al., 2020b) and their evolution (Li et al., 2021) act as neural
189   networks for entire functions by mapping initial conditions or parameters to a solution via kernel
190
      operators and Fourier transforms. While these methods are powerful and find many applications
      to real-world problems (Pathak et al., 2022), they require a large amount of data to generalize, are
191
      computationally intensive, and do not ensure that the solution is physically consistent. Our work
192
      shares similarities with Sobolev learning (Czarnecki et al., 2017; Srinivas & Fleuret, 2018), which
193   adds derivative learning terms to the supervised loss with application to reinforcement learning and
194   machine vision tasks. Instead, we are the first to consider a pure derivative approach and to apply it
195   to physical and dynamical systems.
196
197   Physics-inspired and Physics-Informed methods. PINNs (Raissi et al., 2019) incorporate PDEs
198   that describe the underlying physical system directly into the loss (as well as the L2 residual), using
199   automatic differentiation (Baydin et al., 2018). PINNs reduce the amount of training data needed
200   and increase the physical consistency of the solution. However, PINNs suffer from optimization
201   problems (Wang et al., 2021) and can fail to reach a minimum of the loss (Sun et al., 2020). To
202   alleviate these issues, there exist methodologies to simplify the objective (Sun et al., 2020) or to
203   choose the collocation points where the PDE residual is evaluated (Zhao, 2021; Lau et al., 2024).
204
      Both approaches are problem-specific and add complexity to the optimization process. Models
      inspired by physics exploit the formalisms of Hamiltonian (Greydanus et al., 2019) and Lagrangian
205
      (Cranmer et al., 2019) mechanics to be inherently consistent with the properties of the system,
206
      but they require the system to be conservative and to be formulated explicitly or implicitly in the
207   Lagrangian or Hamiltonian formalism, which is generally not the case.
208
209
      Exact consistency by design. Adopting ad-hoc architectural choices for the model allows the
210   learning process to be exactly consistent with the physical properties of the underlying system.
211   Hansen et al. (2023) takes a probabilistic approach by modeling the predicted distribution of the
212   solution and by projecting it on a subspace that is conservative. However, they only consider global
213   conservation of quantities, and the approach is only tested on one-dimensional systems. Neural
214   Conservation Laws (NCL) (Richter-Powell et al., 2022) take advantage of the mathematical theory
215   of geometric analysis to make the output of the model divergence-free by design. However, NCL is
      very slow and it is only built for systems that are described by a divergence-free equation. Similarly,

241   We validate our approach on a set of dynamical systems and physical PDEs. Table 1 summarizes the
242   tasks we consider. For each experiment, we compare 4 training methods on a Multi-Layer Perceptron
243   (MLP, Goodfellow et al. (2016)): our DERL that learns Du, Output Learning (OUTL) that learns

246
      To evaluate the accuracy of the prediction, we compute the L2 distance between the true function u
247
      and the estimate û. To measure the physical consistency of a model, we either compute the L2 norm
248   of the PDE residual of the network as for PINNs (Raissi et al., 2019) or the L2 distance between the
249   true and the learned field in the phase space ẋ for dynamical systems (Strogatz, 2019).
250   For each experiment and each methodology, we tuned the hyperparameters for the respective losses
251   and the learning rate independently, to obtain the top performance for each model. We report the
252   implementation details in Appendix A and describe the tuning process in Appendix D.1. Specific
253   experiment remarks along with many additional results are available in Appendix E.

491   5   C ONCLUSION
492
493   We proposed DERL, a methodology to learn dynamical and physical systems using only the partial
494   derivatives of the solution together with the initial and boundary conditions of the problem. We
495
      showed theoretically and experimentally that our method successfully learns the solution to a prob-
      lem and remains consistent with its physical constraints, outperforming PINN and other supervised
496
      learning methods. We also found DERL to be effective in transferring physical knowledge across
497
      models through distillation, and we showed how higher-order derivatives can contribute to an in-
498   creased physical consistency of the learned solution. Much like other deep learning applications
499   (e.g. natural language processing, computer vision), in the future learning physical and dynamical
500   systems may not require always starting from scratch. Instead, the learning process may be based on
501   the continual composition and integration of physical information across different models. A more
502   general and flexible paradigm that is still underexplored to date, for which DERL can be a good
503   candidate to provide the foundational mechanisms and core principles.

1385   E.4.1                     S ETUP
1386
1387   The full domain is the rectangle [0, 2.2] × [0, 0.41] with a circular obstacle of center [0.2, 0.2] and
               1
1388   radius 20 . The IC is u = 0, p = 0 everywhere, while BC are given by: u = 0 on the top, bottom,
1389
       and obstacle boundary, u2 = 0 on the left boundary and p = 0 on the right one. The horizontal
       speed u1 at the left boundary is given by the function
1390
1391                                                                                                                              y(0.41 − y)    e5t
1392                                                                                                    b(t, y) =                                                                                                                                                             (31)
                                                                                                                                     0.412    6e20 + e5t
1393
1394   The equation was solved for t ≤ 10 with the finite volumes method and time discretization (An-
1395
       derson et al., 2020) with second-order polynomials and a grid with maximum size 0.0005 using the
       software Mathematica (Inc., 2022). In the learning task, we instead considered only the region at the
1396
       right of the circular obstacle starting at x = 0.5, similar to Raissi et al. (2019). We focused on times
1397
       8 ≤ t ≤ 10, where the solution is periodically stable. See figure 13 for the plots of u, p at those
1398   times. The IC is obtained from the reference solution at t = 8 and the BC is changed accordingly,
1399   particularly for the new left boundary x = 0.5. The data, with partial derivatives from Mathematica,
1400   is then saved with a grid size of ∆x = ∆y = ∆t = 0.01. The MLPs take as input (t, x, y) and
1401   predict u(t, x, y), p(t, x, y) and are made of 8 layers of 128 units each with Tanh activation and were
1402   trained for 200 epochs with batch size 512. While in Raissi et al. (2019) a latent potential is modeled
1403   to satisfy automatically ∇ · u = 0, here we model the components of the velocity independently, to
       see if we can learn to satisfy 2 PDEs at the same time.
