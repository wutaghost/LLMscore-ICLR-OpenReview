                                                   A BSTRACT
013
               Among the current global health challenges, tuberculosis, as a highly contagious
014
               chronic disease, remains one of the major public health problems worldwide. De-
015
               spite significant progress made in the past decades, new challenges, including
016            systematic and effective downscaling, accurate prediction of disease incidence,
017            and implementation of source reduction measures, have added to the difficulty of
018            tuberculosis control. In view of the limitations of the recently proposed EIGHT
019            prediction models in terms of prediction accuracy, this study adopts the Learnable
020            Decomposition and Dual Focus Module Model (Leddam) and then introduces a
021            novel mechanism-supported multivariate spatiotemporal series framework, termed
022            LCHHA-Leddam, to address the challenges in tuberculosis forecasting through an
023            investigation of coal power generation in China. This framework substantially sim-
024
               plifies the complexity of tuberculosis prediction, enhances accurate dimensionality
               reduction, and improves traceability. It also enhances the explanatory power and
025
               accuracy of the Leddam model in the field of tuberculosis prediction. This study
026
               provides a fresh perspective for enhancing epidemic forecasting and exploring
027            source reduction measures for industrial activities, demonstrating the feasibility of
028            AI-assisted public health strategies and green production.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Climate change and environmental pollution have become one of the most important factors affecting
034   public health with the acceleration of urbanization, industrialization, and economic development. Re-
035   cent research has shown that environmental pollutants lead to approximately nine million premature
036   deaths per year worldwide (Landrigan et al., 2018; Stanaway et al., 2018), and climate change is
037   expected to cause approximately four million additional deaths per year (Zhao et al., 2021). Most
038   studies have explored the relationship between diseases and environmental factor with respect to
039
      health risk (Asadgol et al., 2019, Danziger et al., 2022). Fewer studies comprehensively address
      the concurrent effects of climate change and environmental pollution on disease occurrence and
040
      implement source reduction measures. Risk factors for many human diseases and targets for preven-
041
      tive healthcare strategies are commonly identified based on epidemiological analysis (Chowdhury et
042   al., 2018). However, a variety of factors/processes jointly affect the health outcome or its indicator,
043   suggesting the inability of epidemiological models to simultaneously and systematically consider,
044   and in many cases even impossible to solve hundreds of direct- and indirect environmental factors
045   and processes (Braun et al., 2016).
046
      Life cycle human health assessment (LCHHA) is a systematic and internationally standardized
047
      approach for simultaneously and efficiently quantifying the direct and indirect human health footprint
048   of a targeted product, process, or activity from the beginning to the final phase (ISO 14040, 2006; ISO
049   14044, 2006). In contrast to epidemiological studies, LCHHA quantifies the potential human risk of a
050   regional population via the dose-response curves derived from laboratory animal experiments and
051   extrapolation factors for interspecies differences (Huijbregts et al., 2010). As animal experiments data-
052   based LCHHA analysis has high uncertainty because of interspecies differences in physiology and
053   metabolism, the combination of methods to understand better the sources’ contribution to potential
      human health impacts is highly needed. Systematically and effectively implementing source reduction


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      measures and key factor identification to protect public health from environmental pollutants has
055   recently become possible. For instance, industrial activity-environmental pollution-carcinogenic
056   disease relationships calculated by LCHHA and epidemiological cancer incidences can now be further
057   explored (Chen et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2022). However, relevant research on infectious disease and its
058   prediction is extremely rare at the global level.
059
      Interestingly, machine learning, as a popular approach for predictive modeling, has been extensively
060
      used in disease prediction to achieve early warning and disease intervention because of its capacities
061   on analyzing large amounts of data, handling complex relationships, and learning and adapting over
062   time (Huang et al., 2022; Krittanawong et al., 2020). Multivariate time series are datasets with
063   multiple dimensions, where each dimension represents a separate univariate time series, e.g., climate
064   characteristics (Zhang & Yan, 2023). The prediction of infectious diseases is a multivariate time
065   series prediction as multiple factors/processes collectively influence infectious diseases. Predicting
066   these time series helps to improve decision-making in various application areas such as weather,
067   energy, and finance (Angryk et al., 2020; Demirel et al., 2012; Patton, 2013). Consequently, the
068   incidence of infectious diseases can now be predicted, and the reduction of sources for human health
069
      protection can now be explored by integrating LCHHA, climate change, and multivariate time series
      forecasting models. Tuberculosis (TB) is used as a target disease to achieve the aforementioned goal;
070
      it is one of the world’s leading infectious disease killers (WHO, 2022) and is involved in the 2030
071
      Agenda for the World’s Sustainable Development (United Nations, 2015). A quarter of the world’s
072   population is infected with Mycobacterium TB (Suárez et al., 2019). In 2021, the number of newly
073   diagnosed TB patients in China was 0.78 million, ranking third among countries and accounting for
074   7.4% of global TB cases (WHO, 2022). In China, the incidence and death of TB rank first within
075   the Class A and Class B notifiable infectious diseases. Two-thirds of the infected individuals are
076   concentrated in the age group (15-70) with solid production capacity. Hence, the issue is regarded as
077   a major public health problem in China.
078   TB transmission occurs almost exclusively because of aerosolized particles (Dinkele et al., 2022)
079   and climate conditions such as temperature through the influence on TB bacteria spread and survival,
080   human immune response, and climate patterns (Chong et al., 2022; Chormare and Kumar, 2022).
081   However, the joint effects of climate change and environmental pollution on TB are also rarely
082   assessed. As the world’s largest coal consumer and producer, China is confronted with the serious
083   environmental problem caused by coal burning (Ma et al., 2021), such as a global environmental issue
084   of haze over China. More than half of greenhouse gas and aerosolized particles are produced from coal
085   consumption in China (Ma et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2017). The coal power industry in China dominated
086
      national coal consumption since 2000 (>40% ), although renewable energy has been encouraged by
      the Chinese government (Chinese Energy Statistic Yearbook, 2001-2021). Approximately 5.3×105
087
      premature deaths per year were recorded because of air pollution generated by coal burning in China
088
      (Yun et al., 2021). This research thereby focuses on the environmental pollutants released by the coal
089   power industry in China and maps and predicts the incidence of TB caused by climate change and
090   environmental pollution. Worth noting, TB incidence caused by climate factors and environmental
091   pollutants is a complex and systematic phenomenon. Direct and indirect pollutants emitted from
092   the coal power industry during its whole life cycle stages for LCHHA analysis generally involve
093   hundreds of contaminants. Therefore, the intervention of low-dimensional feature engineering is
094   highly needed. Creating low-dimensional features is crucial for data scientists to develop models
095   and minimize model variance (Xuan et al., 2019; Rizgar et al., 2020). Domain experts typically
096   undertake this feature engineering task manually, guided by specific domain knowledge. This method
097
      is known as mechanism-driven dimensionality reduction (MR) (Lan & Susan, 2019; Ting et al.,
      2010). Data-driven dimensionality reduction (DR), however, selects feature engineering methods
098
      based solely on available data. DR effectively extracts relevant features without relying on domain
099
      expertise, serving as a form of automated data cleaning (Neoklis et al., 2017). Despite this, MR
100   remains essential since numerous parameters can significantly influence the performance of DR
101   algorithms and modelling techniques, while MR can scientifically manage the relationships between
102   parameters. This study compares MR and DR in terms of interpretability and accuracy, with LCHHA
103   as a representative of MR. It also introduces a novel mechanism-enabled multivariate spatiotemporal
104   sequence framework that effectively improves the accuracy and interpretability of infectious disease
105   prediction and helps public health managers identify effective management and source reduction
106   strategies.

140
      The LCHHA of coal power generation in China was estimated based on ISO 14040 series standards
141

142
      depletion, global warming, carcinogens, and non-carcinogens) are involved for direct and indirect
143   LCHHA (Fig.1). The selection of subcategories in this study is consistent with the commonly
144   used LCHHA models established in Europe (Sala et al., 2012), North America (Bare, 2011), and
145   World (Bulle et al., 2019). Regarding the global scale impact categories of ozone depletion and
146   climate change, the characterization factors published by the World Meteorological Organization
147   (WMO, 2011) and Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, Arias et al., 2021) are used
148   for quantification. For the rest subcategories, the SDU model, a life cycle impact assessment model
149   developed for China’s life cycle assessment analysis (Yang et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2022), is applied.

151

152

153

404   4.4   S OURCE REDUCTION
405
406   Fig. 3 shows that carbon, mercury, and particulate matter (e.g., nitrogen dioxide, sulfur dioxide.
407   particulates) emitted from coal burning for power generation are the main contributors of the direct
408   LCHHA, which is the key contributor to TB prediction (Fig.1 and Table 2). The uptrend of life cycle
409   carbon emissions from China’s coal power industry and actual annual average temperature (Appendix
410   A.1) are consistent with the IPCC opinion that global warming is mainly caused by greenhouse gas
411   emissions (Arias et al., 2021). As the annual average temperature increase in China correlates with
412
      the annual humidity, rainfall, and sunshine duration changes (Fig. 5b), source reduction measures on
      TB become possible through pollutants control, specifically on mercury, carbon dioxide, nitrogen
413
      oxides, sulfur dioxide, and particulate emissions control. China continues to be the largest carbon
414
      (IEA, 2022) and mercury (Hu et al., 2018) emitter in the world because of its high gross national
415   coal consumption. However, significant environmental control achievements have been attained in
416   recent years (Fig. 3 a). Approximately 2.2 Mt of NOx, 0.8 Mt of SO2 and 643.1 t of atmospheric Hg
417   were emitted by the entire coal power generation chain in 2021. For the same year, in the entire coal
418   power generation chain, approximately 40.1% of NOx, 69.9% of SO2 , and 98.0% of Hg were emitted
419   from the coal power generation stage; the direct emissions of the aforementioned key substances
420   by coal power generation sites in China in 2013 and the extensive haze episodes in China were
421   nearly 89.8%, 96.6%, and 97.8%. The results indicate significant environmental control achievements
422   in national SO2 , PM, and NOx control after setting a strict emission standard for thermal power
423
      plants (GB 13223-2011) and increasing the efficiency of coal consumption year by year (Chinese
      Energy Statistic Yearbook, 2001-2021). A similar trend of carbon control through coal consumption
424
      efficiency improvement can also be seen in Appendix Fig.12. However, mercury control is weak,
425
      and the current mercury mitigation policy in China’s coal power industry remains unsystematic and
426   ineffective. The USEPA (1977) reported that 60% of mercury could be removed using coal-washing
427   technology, while the United Nations Environment Programme reported it to be 78% (UNEPA,
428   2002). Thus, the proper selection of coal-washing technology can significantly reduce the key factors
429   and overall environmental burden. In 2020, the coal-washing rate in China was approximately
430   74.1%, lower than that in the United States (90%) (Ghosh, 2013). Clearly, increasing the national
431   coal-washing rate and the efficiency of coal consumption is another key factor for protecting human
      health.

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      This study employs a novel LCHHA-Leddam model framework to predict the incidence of TB in
435
      China, taking into account spatiotemporal climate change and environmental pollution caused by the
436   country’s coal power industry. The LCHHA model can identify the connections between industrial
437   activities, direct and indirect environmental pollution, associated risks, and their impact on human
438   health, thereby facilitating the identification of source control measures. The results demonstrate
439   that the LCHHA-Leddam model outperforms the traditional Leddam model due to its superior
440   accuracy in identifying key factors through mechanism models. This suggests that a combination of
441   mechanistic and data-driven models can significantly enhance the scientific accuracy of TB disease
442   predictions. Given that the LCHHA of coal power generation in China is primarily characterized
443   by direct emissions of atmospheric mercury, nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide, and particulate matter
444
      during the coal combustion stage for power generation, it becomes feasible to implement source
      reduction measures for TB disease control. These measures include controlling the aforementioned
445
      pollutants, improving coal consumption efficiency, and increasing the national coal washing rate.
446
447   In summary, this study’s novelty lies not only in providing scientific data for early TB warning but
448   also in its potential to facilitate effective management and source reduction. To improve the accuracy
449   of predicted outcomes and the efficacy of public health protection measures, it is necessary to consider
450
      other factors that may influence epidemic diseases, such as high spatial-temporal resolution, genetics,
      diet, lifestyle regularity, and population distribution characteristics. Furthermore, incorporating
451
      interpretable machine learning algorithms, such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), into our
452
      framework could enhance interpretability and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the
453   factors influencing epidemic predictions. This holistic approach will better equip us to address the
454   environmental and health challenges of the future.

      change, has attracted global attention. In China, carbon dioxide emitted from the whole country
742   (Friedlingstein et al., 2022) and its life cycle emissions from the coal power industry show a significant
743   uptrend during the last seven decades. The life cycle carbon emissions of the coal power industry
744   currently account for half of the national carbon emission in China. Meanwhile, the annual average
745   temperature in China shows a sharp rise (Fig.5a), with a warming rate of 0.25 °C/10 years, which
746   is higher than the global average level for the same period (0.15°C to 0.20 °C/10 years; NASA,
747   2022). Spatiotemporal average humidity and rainfall show an uptrend with increasing average
748   temperature and a downtrend with increasing sunshine duration, whereas annual average wind speed
749   with temperature below -2.5 °C presents a downtrend (Fig.5b). For spatiotemporal wind speed
750
      beyond -2.5 °C, no significant changes are observed with increasing annual average temperature. The
      average rainfall distribution in China shows a decreasing tendency from the southeast coast to the
751
      northwest inland (Fig.5c). A similar tendency can be observed in the average humidity distribution,
752
      as the humidity distribution is closely related to the precipitation distribution. By contrast, the annual
753   average sunshine duration shows an increasing tendency from the southeast coast to the northwest
754   inland. The average temperature distribution is high in the south and low in the north, whereas a
755   contrasting phenomenon is observed in the average wind speed distribution.
