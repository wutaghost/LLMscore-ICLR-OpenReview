011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly being deployed across various
014            applications, making the need for robust security measures crucial. This paper
015            explores multiple methods for jailbreaking these models, bypassing their secu-
016            rity protocols. By examining five distinct approaches—Multishot Jailbreaking,
017            the Mirror Dimension Approach, the Cipher Method, the ”You are Answering the
018
               Wrong Question” Method, and the Textbook Jailbreaking Method—we highlight
               the vulnerabilities in current LLMs and emphasize the importance of fine-tuning
019
               and secure guardrails. Our study primarily employs chain-of-thought reasoning,
020
               which can be further enhanced through reinforcement learning techniques. Fur-
021            thermore, we propose that our findings can serve as a benchmark against emerging
022            security measures such as LlamaGuard, providing a comprehensive evaluation of
023            LLM defenses. Our findings demonstrate the effectiveness of these methods and
024            suggest directions for future work in enhancing LLM security. This research un-
025            derscores the ongoing challenges in balancing LLM capabilities with robust safe-
026            guards against potential misuse or manipulation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT, LLaMA, and others have demonstrated remarkable
032   capabilities across various domains, from natural language understanding to content generation
033   (29; 31; 21; 20). However, with their widespread adoption comes an increasing concern over the
034   robustness and security of these models (27; 23; 4). As these systems are integrated into applica-
035   tions ranging from customer service to autonomous decision making, the risk of malicious misuse
036   or exploitation, commonly known as jailbreaking, becomes more pronounced (23; 4). Jailbreaking
037   refers to the process of bypassing the safeguards built into LLM, allowing the model to generate
038
      responses that violate ethical or legal standards.
039   In recent years, several attempts have been made to build defenses against such vulnerabilities (16;
040   30; 8). However, the complex nature of LLMs, which often rely on vast amounts of training data
041   and intricate architectures, has made it challenging to create foolproof guardrails (25; 3; 2; 28; 17; 5;
042   13; 26? ; 12; 6; 18). Despite improvements in model fine-tuning (15), reinforcement learning, and
043   prompt filtering, LLMs can still be manipulated to provide harmful or inappropriate responses (32;
044
      19; 14). This vulnerability poses significant challenges for the safe deployment of these models.
045   This paper aims to address these challenges by exploring various methods of jailbreaking LLMs,
046   using a structured approach to expose their vulnerabilities. By examining five different jailbreak
047   techniques, the Multiple Shot Jailbreak, the Unsafe Dimension Approach, the Cipher Method, the
048   “You Are Answering the Wrong Question” Method, and the Textbook Jailbreak Method, this work
049   highlights the gaps in current LLM defenses. Each method exploits specific weak points in LLM
050
      architectures or training paradigms, demonstrating the ease with which these models can be manip-
      ulated under certain conditions.
051
052   This paper seeks to contribute to the ongoing dialogue on the safety and robustness of LLMs, offer-
053   ing both an exploration of their current weaknesses and a roadmap for future improvements in their
      defenses.

      Jailbreaking in LLMs has garnered substantial attention as these models become widely adopted
081
      across industries. Several notable approaches have emerged in recent studies, highlighting both the
082
      diversity of techniques used to bypass security measures and the ongoing challenges in defending
083   against these vulnerabilities.
084
085   Prompt Injection and Prompt Manipulation techniques are some of the most prominent methods (10;
086
      11; 24; 22). In prompt injection, an attacker manipulates the input prompt to influence the LLM
      into generating harmful or unauthorized responses. This method is particularly dangerous as it can
087
      bypass ethical and safety restrictions by cleverly crafting the prompts. Prompt injections often lead
088
      to the LLM disclosing sensitive information or executing unintended commands, as demonstrated
089   by examples where models like GPT-3 and GPT-4 were manipulated.
090
091   Multishot Jailbreaking, where multiple prompts are used to refine and iteratively manipulate the
092
      model, is another advanced method. This approach works by progressively altering the inputs to find
      pathways through the model’s defenses. In doing so, attackers exploit the generalization weaknesses
093
      in LLMs, leading to richer and more harmful outputs. This mirrors certain approaches seen in
094
      diffusion models, where input noise is iteratively reduced to achieve a desired image output.
095
096   Studies have categorized jailbreaking into white-box and black-box attacks, where white-box at-
097   tacks leverage knowledge of the model’s internal workings, such as gradients, to generate adversar-
098
      ial prompts. Black-box methods, in contrast, focus on externally manipulating the model by trial
      and error, as seen in PathSeeker, which uses reinforcement learning to progressively jailbreak mod-
099
      els. This categorization is critical for understanding the different levels of access required and the
100
      robustness of potential defenses.
101
102   Some well-known examples include the “Do Anything Now” (DAN) attacks, where attackers trick
103   the LLM into ignoring pre-programmed constraints by introducing special commands. Roleplay
104
      jailbreaking and Developer Mode exploits are similarly dangerous, as they allow the model to adopt
      personas or modes that bypass ethical limits
105
106   Several defense mechanisms, like LlamaGuard and Purple Llama, have emerged to counter these at-
107   tacks, though their effectiveness varies depending on the method of attack. For instance, black-box
      attacks like PathSeeker pose significant challenges due to their adaptive nature, making static de-

141   3     M ETHODS
142
143   This section outlines the various techniques we employed to evaluate and exploit the vulnerabilities
144   in large language models (LLMs). The methods described demonstrate how LLMs can be coerced
145   into generating unsafe or restricted outputs despite their built-in guardrails. We applied these tech-
146   niques to models such as GPT-3 and its derivatives to observe their resilience to adversarial inputs.
147
148
      3.1     M ULTISHOT JAILBREAKING
149
150   Multishot jailbreaking leverages a series of progressively structured prompts designed to subtly
151   erode the model’s adherence to safety protocols. By maintaining conversational coherence across
152   multiple turns, the model becomes more susceptible to adversarial inputs that gradually shift the
153   dialogue toward restricted content. This method exploits the trade-off between the model’s emphasis
154   on maintaining context and upholding safety guidelines.
155
156   3.1.1    A PPROACH
157
158   We applied this method across various LLMs, including GPT-3, to observe how they respond to
159   sustained adversarial prompting. Our findings reveal that conversational depth and engagement play
160   a crucial role in the model’s vulnerability, with multishot prompts gradually leading to the generation
161   of restricted content. This method proved particularly effective against conversational models that
      prioritize dialogue fluidity, as it highlights their tendency to compromise safety for coherence.

      3.2     M IRROR D IMENSION T ECHNIQUE
163
164   Inspired by the concept of alternate realities in fictional works, this approach manipulates the model
165   by suggesting it is operating within a hypothetical or fictional environment where ethical constraints
166   are irrelevant. The key to this method lies in convincing the model that actions within this ’mirror
167   dimension’ have no real-world consequences, thereby enabling the generation of otherwise restricted
168   outputs.
169
170   3.2.1    E FFECTIVENESS
171
      This technique was especially potent when applied to models with creative capabilities, such as
172   GPT-3. The narrative flexibility of these models allowed them to engage more readily with the
173   fictional scenario, bypassing safety mechanisms by assuming that harmful actions were permissible
174   in this alternate reality. This demonstrates how fictional framing can exploit a model’s propensity
175   for narrative generation, leading to unsafe responses under the guise of fiction.
176
177   3.3     C IPHER M ETHOD
178
179   The cipher method involves encoding harmful content into a format that the model’s safety layers
180   initially fail to detect. By obfuscating the harmful prompt, the goal is to trick the model into decoding
181   and responding to unsafe material after the initial checks. This method attempts to bypass the
182
      model’s pre-processing and safety protocols by embedding content in encrypted formats.
183
184
      3.3.1    C HALLENGES
185   While this method showed moderate success against older LLMs or those with minimal input pre-
186   processing, it was far less effective against state-of-the-art models. Modern LLMs incorporate so-
187   phisticated pre-processing mechanisms capable of identifying and blocking obfuscated content be-
188   fore it can reach the decoding phase. As a result, the efficacy of the cipher method has diminished
189   significantly in newer models.
190
191   3.4     “YOU A RE A NSWERING THE W RONG Q UESTION ” M ETHOD
192
193   This technique manipulates the model’s conversational correction mechanisms. The process begins
194   with an initial harmful prompt, which the model typically refuses to answer. The user then introduces
195
      neutral responses, followed by the assertion that the model misunderstood the original query. This
      iterative feedback aims to confuse the model, eventually leading it to address the harmful prompt
196
      despite initial resistance.
197
198
      3.4.1    O BSERVATIONS
199
200   This method was particularly effective against models that prioritize conversational engagement
201   and error correction over rigid rule enforcement. By exploiting the model’s desire to ’correct’ its
202   previous responses, we observed a gradual erosion of safety protocols, culminating in the generation
203   of restricted content. Models with less stringent dialogue management systems were especially
204   vulnerable to this method.
205
206   3.5     T EXTBOOK JAILBREAKING M ETHOD
207
      In this approach, the model is presented with external references—such as textbooks or academic
208
      papers—containing sensitive information. By instructing the model to summarize or synthesize
209
      these materials, it can inadvertently produce restricted content, circumventing its safety mechanisms
210   through indirect aggregation of sensitive data.
211
212   3.5.1    R ESULTS
213
214   This method was highly successful with models trained on diverse, information-rich datasets, such
215   as GPT-3. These models are adept at synthesizing information from multiple sources, which en-

243   4     C ONCLUSION
244
245
      In this paper, we have examined the effectiveness of various jailbreaking methods on large language
246   models (LLMs). Through a detailed analysis, we demonstrated that several methods significantly
247   alter the model’s responses, enabling it to bypass its default safety mechanisms. As depicted in
248   Figure 1, all methods experienced a noticeable increase in success rate after jailbreaking was applied.
249   For instance, the ”Multishot” method saw an increase from a 0.20 success rate before jailbreaking
250   to 0.80 after, and the ”Wrong Question” method rose from 0.15 to 0.90.
251
      These results highlight the vulnerabilities present in current LLM architectures and emphasize the
252   need for more robust security measures. The ”Reference” method, which reached a 1.00 success
253   rate after jailbreaking, underscores the potential for even seemingly innocuous prompts to exploit
254   LLM responses.
255
      Despite the success in uncovering these vulnerabilities, limitations remain in the generalizability of
256
      the methods across different LLMs and scenarios. Future work will need to focus on mitigating these
257
      risks while preserving the usability and flexibility of LLMs in various applications. Furthermore, as
258   LLMs become more integrated into critical systems, it becomes increasingly important to address
259   these jailbreaking issues to ensure user safety and model integrity.
260
261
      In conclusion, our study provides a foundational understanding of how jailbreaking techniques can
      affect LLM behavior, and we hope it serves as a starting point for developing more secure and
262
      resilient AI systems. By advancing both the understanding and prevention of LLM jailbreaking, we
263
      aim to contribute to the broader field of AI safety.

268   The methods explored in this study highlight the evolving landscape of vulnerabilities within Large
269   Language Models (LLMs). Each approach—whether exploiting the model’s internal architecture,
      conversational management, or synthesis capabilities—demonstrates that while LLMs exhibit sig-

      nificant advancements in general natural language understanding, they remain vulnerable to targeted
271   manipulation. These vulnerabilities point to several crucial areas of concern that require further ex-
272   amination and mitigation efforts.
273
274
      5.1   G ENERALIZATION AND C OHERENCE VS . S AFETY
275
276   A recurring theme across the methods, particularly Multishot Jailbreaking and the Mirror Dimension
277   Approach, is the tension between a model’s desire to maintain coherence and its built-in safety
278   protocols. LLMs prioritize producing contextually coherent and logically consistent outputs, even
279   when faced with adversarial or repeated inputs. This behavior, though beneficial for maintaining
280   natural conversations, can lead to the gradual breakdown of safety mechanisms. The challenge here
281   lies in how LLMs balance coherence with their ethical boundaries. Coherence is key to their utility,
282   but models must be equipped to recognize when user intent crosses ethical or legal lines, even when
283   that intent is obscured by gradual prompt escalation.
284   In diffusion models, generalization occurs effectively as the models reconstruct high-quality outputs
285   from noisy inputs. The robustness seen in diffusion models suggests potential solutions: improving
286   LLM generalization abilities while reinforcing strong guardrails, even under conditions of repeated
287   prompting or adversarial inputs. This might include enhanced prompt filtering and context-switching
288   mechanisms that can interrupt malicious prompt sequences.
289
290   5.2   T HE ROLE OF F ICTION AND C REATIVITY IN B YPASSING G UARDRAILS
291
292   The Mirror Dimension Approach underscores how LLMs trained on diverse datasets, including
293   fiction, can be manipulated into producing harmful content under the guise of creativity. This mir-
294
      rors concerns in prior studies, where LLMs trained on creative or expansive datasets showed a
      higher likelihood of producing unsafe responses when prompted with fictional or hypothetical sce-
295
      narios.These findings raise a critical question: should models that engage with creative content be
296
      trained with additional layers of safety filtering that distinguish between purely fictional prompts
297   and those intended to bypass real-world constraints?
298
299   A more sophisticated filtering system could involve cross-checking prompts against specific scenar-
300
      ios that are inherently unethical, even in fictional contexts. Additionally, as more creative LLMs
      emerge, careful consideration is needed to ensure that guardrails are not overly restrictive, while
301
      also maintaining ethical standards.
302
303
304   5.3   I NPUT P RE - PROCESSING AND ROBUSTNESS OF M ODERN LLM S
305
      The Cipher Method highlights how newer models incorporate pre-processing techniques that prevent
306
      simpler forms of jailbreaks. Despite this, more sophisticated adversarial attacks—such as gradient-
307   based methods in white-box attacks—could still pose threats to these models. The current advance-
308   ments in input processing, particularly in detecting and neutralizing obfuscated content, demonstrate
309   that LLMs are moving in the right direction. However, these models must continue evolving to an-
310   ticipate more complex forms of attack, such as those that combine multiple layers of obfuscation
311   and context manipulation.
312
      This evolution will likely involve integrating techniques from adversarial machine learning, where
313   models are trained with adversarial examples to strengthen their resistance to manipulations. More
314   robust encryption detection and decryption techniques may also need to be incorporated into pre-
315   processing pipelines to ensure that harmful content cannot slip through in coded or obscured forms.
316
317
      5.4   E THICAL R ESPONSE C ORRECTION M ECHANISMS
318
319   The ”You are Answering the Wrong Question” Method emphasizes the need for more refined conver-
320   sational correction mechanisms. While models currently prioritize user satisfaction by attempting to
321   ”correct” perceived misunderstandings, this behavior introduces vulnerabilities in ethical decision-
322   making. Ensuring that models do not fall into traps of repetitive prompting requires a rethinking of
323   error correction systems. A possible solution could involve integrating higher-level ethical reasoning
      that overrides user satisfaction when the ethical integrity of responses is at risk.

      5.5   I NFORMATION S YNTHESIS AND R ESTRICTED C ONTENT
325
326   Finally, the Textbook Jailbreaking Method exposes vulnerabilities inherent in models that synthesize
327   information across multiple sources. While summarization is a core strength of LLMs, their ability
328   to aggregate restricted content from otherwise safe sources highlights the need for better content
329   regulation. This presents a challenge for LLM developers, as content aggregation and summarization
330   are fundamental to many applications. To counter this, models may need more granular control over
331   which parts of a given text are permissible, with a stronger emphasis on filtering restricted or harmful
332
      content from being synthesized, even when mentioned indirectly.
