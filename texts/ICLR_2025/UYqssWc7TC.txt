011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Network (GNN) based node embedding methods are a promising
014            approach to learning node representations for downstream tasks such as link pre-
015
               diction, node classification, and node clustering. GNN-based methods usually
               work in an unsupervised or semi-supervised manner, learning node representa-
016
               tions without or with limited label information. We empirically show, however,
017
               that the performance of learned node embeddings on downstream tasks may be
018            heavily impacted by the GNN-method’s hyperparameter configuration. Unfortu-
019            nately, existing hyperparameter optimisation methods typically rely on labeled
020            data for evaluation, making them unsuitable for unsupervised scenarios. This
021            raises the question: how can we tune the hyperparameters of GNNs without us-
022            ing label information to obtain high quality node embeddings? To answer this,
023            we propose a framework for evaluating node embedding quality without rely-
024            ing on labels. Specifically, our framework consists of two steps: building prior
025            beliefs that characterize high-quality node embeddings, and quantifying the ex-
026
               tent to which those prior beliefs are satisfied. More importantly, we instantiate
               our framework from two different but complementary perspectives: spatial and
027
               spectral information. First, we introduce the Consensus-based Space Occupancy
028
               Rate (CSOR) method that evaluates node embedding quality from a spatial view.
029            It conducts pairwise comparisons of the spatial distances between node embed-
030            dings obtained from various hyperparameter configurations. Next, we present the
031            Spectral Space Occupancy Rate (SSOR) method, which takes a spectral perspec-
032            tive and evaluates the embedding quality by examining the singular values of the
033            node embedding matrices. Extensive experiments on seven GNN models with
034            four benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of both CSOR and SSOR.
035            Specifically, both methods consistently prioritize hyperparameter configurations
036            that yield high-quality node embeddings for downstream tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Graph-structured data is ubiquitous due to its strong expressive capability in representing relation-
041
      ships between objects. Node embedding methods, including traditional approaches (Perozzi et al.,
042   2014; Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Cao et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2016) and Graph Neural Network
043   (GNN)-based methods (Kipf & Welling, 2016b; Pan et al., 2018; Hamilton et al., 2017a; Xu et al.,
044   2018; Velickovic et al., 2017), aim to learn node representations in an unsupervised manner, though
045   they can sometimes also be semi-supervised. These embeddings are then utilized for various down-
046   stream tasks such as link prediction (Zhang & Chen, 2018), node classification (Maurya et al., 2021),
047   and graph clustering (Tsitsulin et al., 2023b). Despite the impressive results achieved by these meth-
048   ods, some long-standing challenges remain underexplored. Particularly, we empirically show that
049   the performance of node embeddings in downstream tasks may heavily depend on the hyperparam-
050
      eters (HPs) configurations (see Figures 4, 5,6, 7,8,9, and 10 in Appendix B for details). Therefore,
      given a node embedding model, to achieve a robust performance on downstream tasks across differ-
051
      ent datasets, the HPs should be carefully optimised.
052
053   However, we found that most existing HP optimisation methods are designed for supervised learning
      settings (Feurer & Hutter, 2019; He et al., 2021; Bischl et al., 2023), making them unsuitable for


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      unsupervised learning scenarios where labels are not available. This inevitably leads to a question:
055   how could we tune the hyperparameters of unsupervised representation learning methods? Broadly
056   speaking, this can be approached in two distinct ways: through a meta-learning-based strategy
057   (Hospedales et al., 2021) or an internal strategy (Ma et al., 2023). On the one hand, meta-learning
058   strategies compare the internal structure of the labeled dataset to the unlabeled dataset, transferring
059   learned biases to optimize performance on the same learning task. On the other hand, internal
060   strategies investigate the internal structure of the unlabeled dataset itself and the behavior of learning
061   model, using some “prior beliefs” (which will be elaborated later) to optimize performance. We
062   argue that the underlying logic of meta-learning strategies is very intuitive and thus well understood
063
      for humans: given one new dataset, we employ the solution to one previously seen dataset that
      is closest to the given new dataset. In contrast, the logics behind various internal strategies are
064
      not uniform, and they are too complex to be condensed into a single explanation. Due to inherent
065
      challenges, there is considerably less research available on internal strategies than on meta-learning
066   approaches.
067
068   In this paper, we focus on the so-called internal strategies for tuning unsupervised models, showing
069
      how they can be unified using one single framework. Specifically, “internal” means that the evalu-
      ation of embedding quality is performed without external information such as labels. While most
070
      existing internal strategies are initially designed for unsupervised representation learning on images,
071
      we extend them to unsupervised representation learning on graphs and focus on evaluating the qual-
072   ity of node embeddings obtained from unsupervised GNN models. After revisiting existing internal
073   strategies, including UDR (Duan et al., 2020), Incoherence (Tsitsulin et al., 2023a), Self Cluster
074   (Tsitsulin et al., 2023a), α-ReQ (Assran et al., 2022), RankMe (Garrido et al., 2023; Roy & Vetterli,
075   2007), NESum (He & Ozay, 2022), Condition Number (Ben-Israel, 1966; Tsitsulin et al., 2023a),
076   and Stable Rank (Tsitsulin et al., 2023a), we found that the design and development of internal
077   strategies can be distilled into two fundamental steps: 1) build prior beliefs, where involves build-
078   ing prior beliefs (namely imposing assumptions) on the characteristics that indicate high-quality
079   embeddings; and 2) quantify prior beliefs, which involves assessing the extent to which those prior
080
      beliefs are satisfied. With this framework, all existing internal strategies can be analyzed from a
      unified perspective (see Appendix C for details): using UDR (Duan et al., 2020) as an example, they
081
      first build the prior belief that well-performing HP configurations generate stable, disentangled rep-
082
      resentations across different random seeds. This is inspired by (Rolinek et al., 2019), which shows
083   that the reconstruction mechanism in VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2014) induces local orthogonal-
084   ity that results in disentangled representations and only well-performing HP configurations enable
085   VAEs to exhibit this property. On this basis, they quantify the extent to which this prior belief is
086   satisfied by measuring the similarity of embeddings generated by the same HP configurations across
087   random seeds. Higher similarity indicates a higher degree of disentanglement.
088
      When confining our attention to evaluating the quality of node embeddings obtained from unsu-
089   pervised GNN models, we further instantiate two novel internal strategies within this framework
090   (namely building prior beliefs and quantifying prior beliefs). Before introducing them, we first
091   present spatial and spectral GNNs. Specifically, GNNs can be divided into: 1) spatial-based ap-
092   proaches, which aggregate node information directly in the node (spatial) domain by passing mes-
093   sages within local neighborhoods, and 2) spectral-based approaches, which transform nodes into
094   the spectral domain using the graph Laplacian for aggregation before mapping back to the node
095   domain. Chen et al. (2023) reveal that spatial- and spectral-based GNNs, while analyzing from
096   different perspectives and employing different techniques, ultimately achieve the same objective.
097
      Building on this, we instantiate our framework from a spatial perspective by proposing CSOR, while
      developing SSOR from a spectral perspective.
098
099   Consensus-based spatial Space Occupancy Rate (CSOR). We begin by conducting extensive
100   experiments with various unsupervised GNN models on four benchmark graph datasets, aiming to
101   empirically draw inspirations from observations to build prior beliefs. Specifically, given a GNN
102   model on a specific graph, we empirically observe that: among all node embeddings generated with
103
      different HP values, those that exhibit greater spatial separability from other embeddings tend to
      perform better in downstream tasks. On this basis, we build the following prior belief: a given set of
104
      HP configurations that makes node embeddings more distinct among all embeddings is preferable
105
      (which will be explained later). Based on this prior belief, we propose CSOR, which quantifies
106   this prior belief by comparing the spatial distances between embeddings obtained with different HP
107



                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      configurations through pairwise comparisons. The process of quantifying the prior belief is actually
109   the process of assessing node embedding quality (or performing HP optimisation).
110
111   Spectral Space Occupancy Rate (SSOR). Node embeddings can also be analyzed from a spec-
112
      tral perspective. By examining the singular values of the node embedding matrices, we can gain
      insights into embedding quality, offering a complementary view to spatial-based evaluation. Based
113
      on this, we propose SSOR, which shares the same prior belief as CSOR but quantifies it differently.
114
      SSOR quantifies this belief by analyzing the singular values of the node embedding matrices, where
115   a higher sum of singular values (which are simultaneously distributed uniformly across different
116   dimensions as much as possible) indicates higher quality.
117
118
      We conducted extensive experiments using seven unsupervised GNN models on four benchmark
      datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of CSOR and SSOR. Results show that both methods
119
      consistently perform well for all GNN models across datasets. In several cases, they can even
120
      select the optimal HP configurations. Specifically, for obtained node embeddings we calculate the
121   Spearman correlation coefficient (Zar, 2005) between the ranking scores provided by CSOR (or
122   SSOR) and the actual performance metrics on downstream tasks. The correlation coefficients are
123   often around 0.9 and never fall below 0.6, showing that CSOR and SSOR can effectively distinguish
124   between different HP configurations and identify those that produce high-quality node embeddings.
125
      Overall, the contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 1) We propose a framework for
126
      developing internal strategies by establishing general principles: building prior beliefs and quan-
127   tifying prior beliefs; 2) More importantly, we instantiate our framework from two different but
128   complementary perspectives: CSOR from a spatial perspective and SSOR from a spectral perspec-
129   tive. We conduct extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of both methods using seven
130   unsupervised GNN models on four benchmark datasets; 3) To facilitate future research, we establish
131   a generic testbed that allows researchers and practitioners to evaluate the effectiveness of our auto-
132   matic HPO methods (or their newly proposed methods) on various unsupervised node embedding
133   algorithms. The testbed is publicly available on Anonymous GitHub.

135   2   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
136
137   Due to space limitations, the preliminaries and related work are deferred to Appendix E, and we
138   directly begin by problem statement as follows.
139
      An attributed graph is defined as G = (V, E, X), where V = {v1 , ..., vN } represents the set of
140
      nodes, E = {e1 , ..., eM } denotes the set of edges, and X ∈ RN ×Q is the node attribute matrix, with
141
      N = |V| being the number of nodes and Q the dimensionality of the node attributes. Alternatively,
142   the graph can be represented as G = (A, X), where A is the adjacency matrix, with Aij = 1 if
143   there is an edge between node vi and vj , and Aij = 0 otherwise.
144
145
      A (node-level) unsupervised graph representation learning function f (·) takes a graph G as input
      and outputs a node embedding vector for each individual node. Formally, we define f : G → Z ∈
146
      RN ×D , where N is the number of nodes and D denotes the dimensionality of the learned node
147
      embeddings. In this paper, we aim to address the following problem:
148
149
      Problem (Hyperparameter Optimization for Unsupervised Graph Representation Learning ). Given
      a graph G, an unsupervised graph representation learning algorithm f (·), and a set of HP configu-
150
      rations H for f (·), we aim to develop a HP optimization (HPO) method that can select an optimal
151
      HP configuration h∗ ∈ H without relying on labels, such that the node embeddings obtained using
152   fh∗ (·) can achieve optimal performance (which will be defined later based on the specific down-
153   stream task).
154
155   The main challenge of solving this problem lies in the absence of ground truth labels in unsupervised
156   settings, rendering the evaluation of the HPO method inherently difficult. Furthermore, the unique
157   characteristics of graph-structured data, especially its non-i.i.d. nature in graph representation learn-
158   ing, make this problem even more challenging to tackle. To address this, we employ the so-called
159   internal strategy to evaluate the quality of graph embeddings without relying on labels.
160   Definition (Internal Strategy for Evaluating Node Embeddings). Given a graph G, an unsupervised
161   graph representation learning algorithm f (·), a set of HP configurations H, and the resulting node
      embedding matrices {Z(h) | h ∈ H}, where Z(h) = fh (G), an internal strategy Q : RN ×D → R


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is defined as a function that takes a node embedding matrix Z as input and outputs a ranking score
163   s ∈ R. Formally, we have s = Q(Z(h)).
164
165   Specifically, the internal strategy evaluates the quality of the node embeddings by assigning a rank-
166   ing score, which allows for the comparison of different HP configurations. This ranking can then be
167   used to select the optimal HP configuration that leads to the best node embeddings for downstream
168   tasks.

      3   R EVISITING UDR AND B EYOND
171
172
      By revisiting Unsupervised Disentanglement Ranking (UDR) (Duan et al., 2020), we demonstrate
173
      how the two fundamental steps in our proposed framework are motivated and performed: 1) building
174
      prior beliefs about what constitutes a good embedding; and 2) quantifying these prior beliefs.
175
176   Inspiration. Rolinek et al. (2019) reveal that the disentangled properties of Variational Autoen-
177   coders (VAEs) arise from their inherent reconstruction mechanism. The decoder’s task in VAE
178
      models shares similarities with Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Maćkiewicz & Ratajczak,
      1993; Jolliffe & Cadima, 2016), as both aim to capture and reconstruct the data’s key patterns
179
      through independent components. This results in the decoder encouraging orthogonal and disen-
180
      tangled latent variables from the encoder. Additionally, the imposition of a diagonal prior on the
181   latent space pushes the encoder to produce locally orthogonal representations, further enhancing
182   the disentangling effect. This interplay between the reconstruction objective and the diagonal prior
183   naturally leads to disentangled representations without the need for explicit design in the model.
184
      Building and Quantifying Prior Beliefs. Based on the understanding of how VAEs achieve dis-
185
      entanglement, UDR (Duan et al., 2020) is proposed to optimize HPs in unsupervised representation
186
      learning on images. Specifically, they build the following prior belief: the reconstruction objective
187   in VAEs is unique, causing well-performing HP configurations to generate stable, disentangled hid-
188   den variables, as the decoder (due to its PCA-like behavior) pushes the encoder towards producing
189   robust, disentangled representations. UDR’s prior belief is rooted in the concept that “happy fam-
190   ilies are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way” (Tolstoy, 2016), meaning that
191   high-quality HP configurations produce consistent, stable representations across different random
192   seeds, while poor HP configurations lead to diverse, unstable representations. UDR quantifies this
193   prior belief through a consensus-based method (see Appendix E for definition), measuring the sim-
194   ilarity of representations generated under different random seeds. By evaluating the consistency of
195
      these representations, UDR identifies the most disentangled and effective HP configurations.
196   For completeness, UDR works as follows. They train H × S models, where H is the number of HP
197   configurations, and S is the number of seeds for initial model weights. For each HP configuration
198   h ∈ H,
199
200        1. they sample P random seeds {seed1 , ..., seedP } with P ≤ S, and obtain embed-
201
              dings {Z(h1 ) , ..., Z(hP ) } using the learning model configured with h under random seeds
202
              {seed1 , ..., seedP }, respectively;
           2. they conduct P2 pairwise comparisons of embeddings {Z(h1 ) , ..., Z(hP ) } to obtain a list

205

206
207        3. on this basis, they
                               n compute the median oof this list as the final UDR score, namely
208           UDRh = median UDRjh | j = 1, . . . , P2 , where a higher UDR score indicates a better

              HP configuration.
210
211   Discussion. UDR’s development involves building prior beliefs (or drawing inspiration) from the
212   mechanism analysis of VAEs, which raises the question: Can or should we rely solely on analyzing
213   the mechanisms of representation learning models to build prior beliefs, or are there alternative ap-
214   proaches? To address this, we must consider two key points: 1) Given the inherent uncertainty and
215   complexity of neural networks (Lipton, 2016), it is not always feasible to analyze their mechanisms
      from a mathematical perspective; 2) Despite the opacity of learning models, we can partially under-


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      stand their internal workings by observing the relationships between inputs and outputs—a principle
217   underlying many post-hoc explainable AI (XAI) techniques (Arrieta et al., 2020).
218
219   In other words, while UDR was developed through the mechanism analysis of VAEs, we note that
220
      the high level of disentanglement can be observed in the outputs of VAEs (e.g., image embeddings)
      without directly studying their mechanisms. This suggests that it is not always necessary to analyze
221
      the model’s internal mechanisms when dealing with complex models. Instead, we can relax this
222
      requirement by using observation-driven methods to build prior beliefs.

228
229   We now demonstrate how to leverage this observation-driven approach to draw inspirations and then
230   build prior beliefs, where we assume that the distribution of embeddings can encapsulate the charac-
231   teristics of the underlying mechanisms of GNN models. Next, we quantify these beliefs to evaluate
232   unsupervised node embedding quality, resulting in a novel internal strategy dubbed Consensus-based
233   spatial Space Occupancy Rate (CSOR).
234
      Visualisation. Node embeddings from GNN models are typically high-dimensional (8 in our ex-
235   periments), making it difficult to directly observe their distribution. With the help of PCA, we can
236   visualize the distribution of node embeddings in a 3-dimensional space. PCA is used as it can effec-
237   tively reduce the dimensionality while preserving much of the original variance (Jolliffe, 2002).
238
      Observations and Inspirations. To draw some inspirations from node embeddings distributions
239
      and understand which characteristics lead to better performance in downstream tasks, we experi-
240
      ment with 1280 sets of HP configurations (see Appendix F for setting details). We select four node
241   embeddings which are evenly sampled from the worst to the best performance on downstream node
242   classification task, and visualize them with PCA. From Figure 1 (and more figures in Appendix F.1),
243   we observe that when node classification performance is poor, the embeddings of nodes from differ-
244   ent classes are mixed together. As performance improves, the separability between embeddings of
245   nodes from different classes increases. Drawing inspirations from these observations, we propose
246   a hypothesis: node embeddings associated with a specific HP configuration become increasingly
247   dispersed as downstream performance improves, which we refer to as intra-embedding. Simultane-
248   ously, node embeddings from a specific well-performing HP configuration tend to diverge further
249
      from those of poorly-performing configurations as its downstream performance improves, which we
      refer to as inter-embedding. To validate this hypothesis, we use the worst-performing node em-
250
      bedding matrix as a baseline and calculate the Manhattan distance (Krause & Golovin, 2014) from
251
      all other embedding matrices to this baseline, effectively capturing aggregate differences across di-
252   mensions. As shown in Figure 42 (a) in Appendix F.2, we observe that node embedding matrices
253   (where a point corresponds to an embedding matrix) farther from the worst-performing one tend to
254   show better performance. This supports our hypothesis that the quality of node embedding matrices
255   improves as they become spatially more distant from the worst-performing embedding matrix.

      Building and Quantifying Prior Beliefs. Based on above observations and analyses, we empiri-
271   cally validated that node embeddings spatially farther from the worst-performing one exhibit higher
272   qualities. We term this characteristic as distinctness, where a higher value means higher quality. On
273   this basis, we build the following prior belief: for node embeddings generated by message-passing-
274   based GNNs, greater spatial distinctness correlates with higher performance in downstream tasks.
275   To quantify prior belief, we define spatial distinctness as the degree to which a node embedding
276   matrix is spatially separated from other node embedding matrices. It is important to note that: due
277   to the lack of label information, it is impossible to know the worst-performing embedding matrix
278   on downstream task. Therefore, we compare each individual node embedding matrix to other node
279
      embedding matrices (which are actually used as baseline) rather than the worst-performing one. In
      this way, we are actually assuming that “poor-performing HP configurations are all alike” (e.g., the
280
      embeddings of nodes from different classes are mixed). In other words, we employ the consensus
281
      formed by all node embedding matrices as a baseline to quantify the prior belief. By comparing
282   Figure 42 (a) and (b) in Appendix F.2, we can see that spatial distinctness calculated based on this
283   consensus baseline (corresponding to Figure 42 (b)) shows a stronger correlation with performance
284   in downstream tasks compared to using the worst-performing embedding matrix as the baseline
285   (corresponding to Figure 42 (a)). This is because performing pairwise comparisons (corresponding
286   to the consensus baseline) can capture more distributional information about all node embedding
287   matrices.
288   Formalisation of CSOR. Given a graph representation learning model f (·) with a specific HP
289   configuration h ∈ H, denoted as fh (·), the model maps nodes of a graph G to an embedding matrix
290   Z(h), where Z(h) = fh (G). Using this notation, we propose an internal strategy for HPO in
291   unsupervised graph representation learning, called Consensus-based spatial Space Occupancy Rate
292   (CSOR), which maps the node embedding matrix Z(h) to a ranking score s ∈ R, quantifying the
293   quality of the embeddings. Specifically, CSOR is designed as follows:

               tween the resulting embedding matrices Z(hi ) and Z(hj ), with Di,j = diff(Z(hi ), Z(hj ))

               and diff(·, ·) is the Manhattan distance.

305   5   S PECTRAL S PACE O CCUPANCY R ATE (SSOR): A S PECTRAL P ERSPECTIVE
306
307   In CSOR, we built the following prior belief: for node embeddings generated by message-passing-
308   based GNNs, greater spatial distinctness correlates with higher performance in downstream tasks;
309
      To quantify this prior belief, we defined and measured the spatial distinctness from a spatial per-
      spective. Now, we approach this problem from a spectral-based perspective, and present a novel
310
      internal strategy called Spectral Space Occupancy Rate (SSOR). To achieve this, we first build
311
      a similar prior belief: for node embeddings generated by message-passing-based GNNs, greater
312   spectral distinctness correlates with higher performance in downstream tasks; To quantify this prior
313   belief, we need to define and measure the spectral distinctness. Before giving its formal definition,
314   we present the intuition and rational behind it.
315
      Intuition and Rational behind Spectral Distinctness. Given a node embedding matrix, performing
316
      Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on it can yield singular values that represent the extent to
317
      which the node embeddings are spreading across different dimensions in the latent space. We argue
318   that a singular value can capture the spread extent of node embeddings in some (virtual) dimension,
319   which does not necessarily correspond to a specific dimension in the latent space. Intuitively, by
320   simply summing all the singular values, we can obtain the total spread extent of node embeddings
321   across all dimensions in the latent space. Therefore, a higher value, indicating a larger total extent
322   of spread, is associated with better separability. From this perspective, it is intuitive to use the sum
323   of singular values to represent the distinctness of node embeddings in the spectral space, referred
      to as spectral distinctness. However, simply maximizing the sum of singular values can lead to


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      an undesirable effect known as dimensional collapse (Jing et al., 2021). This phenomenon occurs
325   when embeddings fail to capture the full variability of the data and collapse into a lower-dimensional
326   subspace, resulting in poor downstream performance. In this case, a high sum of singular values can
327   be misleading if dominated by a few large singular values while the others remain very small. To
328   mitigate this, we propose to maximise the sum of singular values while ensuring that the singular
329   values are uniformly distributed as much as possible. For instance, if we have three groups of
330   singular values (4, 0, 0, 0), (2, 2, 0, 0), (1, 1, 1, 1), the group (1, 1, 1, 1) is preferred. This leads to
331   Spectral Space Occupancy Rate (SSOR), which can effectively quantify the spectral distinctness
332   and thus the prior belief.
333   High-level Idea of SSOR and Empirical Evidence. Conceptually, give a node embedding matrix,
334   our proposed Spectral Space Occupancy Rate (SSOR) approach attempts to quantify the prior belief
335   by computing the area occupied in spectral space spanned by the normalized singular values. To
336   visually demonstrate this, we distribute all the normalized singular values (which will be formally
337   defined later) evenly across the 360 degrees in a 2-dimensional radar chart as shown in Figure 2. In
338   this way, each normalised singular value represents one (virtual) dimension, and its value indicates
339
      the spread extent of the node embeddings in that dimension. By connecting these line segments,
      we form an irregular polygon. The area of this polygon is analogous to the spatial distinctness in
340

502   7     D ISCUSSIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
503
504
505   In this paper, we present a unified framework for developing internal strategies to evaluate the qual-
506   ity of node embeddings without the need for labels. Our approach is grounded in two fundamental
507   principles: building prior beliefs and quantifying these beliefs. Firstly, we identified that prior beliefs
508   about the quality of node embeddings can be built either through analyzing the mechanisms of repre-
509   sentation learning models or through an observation-driven approach. We introduced spatial-based
510   and spectral-based methods as two different but complementary ways of building these prior beliefs.
511   The spatial-based method, CSOR, derives prior beliefs from the spatial distribution characteristics of
512   the node embeddings. The spectral-based method, SSOR, observes the singular values of the embed-
513
      ding matrices to form similar prior beliefs. Secondly, we developed methods to quantify these prior
      beliefs. We demonstrated that quantification could be approached through consensus-based meth-
514
      ods, which involve pairwise comparisons of embeddings generated with different hyperparameter
515
      values, as exemplified by CSOR. Alternatively, a stand-alone approach can be used, as in the case
516   of SSOR, which leverages singular values directly for quantification without the need for compara-
517   tive analysis. Through extensive experiments involving seven GNN models across four benchmark
518   datasets, and 1280 sets of HP configurations for each combination of model and dataset, we vali-
519   dated the effectiveness of our proposed methods. The results consistently showed that both CSOR
520   and SSOR could reliably evaluate the quality of node embeddings and identify well-performing HP
521   configurations. Our methods exhibited strong correlations with actual performance metrics, indicat-
522   ing their high accuracy and stability.
523   Limitations and Future Work. In our experiments, we have only tested our internal strategies
524   on GNN models based on the message passing mechanism. It remains to be seen whether our
525   observations and conclusions hold for other types of node embedding models. Additionally, the four
526   datasets we used are all homogeneous. If we were to use heterogeneous datasets, would our internal
527   strategies still be effective? Regarding the downstream tasks to perform quantitative evaluations,
528   we used link prediction and node classification, where link prediction has less bias compared to
529   node classification but still cannot completely eliminate bias as unsupervised evaluation metrics do.
530
      Furthermore, if we attempt to use deeper graph neural networks, the issue of oversmoothing may
      arise. Can our internal strategies solve this problem? Therefore, future work should expand the
531
      scope of experiments to include a wider variety of GNN models and datasets. This will help to
532
      further validate the stability and generalizability of our methods.
533
534   Overall, our work not only introduces effective methods for unsupervised node embedding evalu-
535   ation but also provides a clear direction for future research in developing internal strategies. By
536
      formalizing the building and quantifying of prior beliefs, we lay the groundwork for more sophisti-
      cated and reliable evaluation methods in the field of machine learning.

877   A.1   D OWNSTREAM TASK
878
879   In this work, we use two downstream tasks: node classification and link prediction. Node classifi-
880
      cation focuses on predicting the label of a node based on its own features as well as the local graph
      structure, placing emphasis on the node’s neighborhood information. In contrast, link prediction is
881
      concerned with determining whether an edge exists between two nodes, focusing more on capturing
882
      the overall structural relationships within the graph.
883
884   By using both tasks, we gain a more comprehensive evaluation of the quality of node embeddings.
885   Node classification assesses how well embeddings capture local neighborhood patterns, while link
886
      prediction evaluates the model’s ability to represent global structural information. Together, these
      tasks allow us to better assess whether the Internal Strategy can select hyperparameters that holisti-
887
      cally evaluate the quality of node embeddings, rather than being biased toward a specific downstream
888
      task.

       C.2.1    M EASURING C LUSTERING IN E MBEDDINGS
1243
1244   To evaluate the clustering tendency of embeddings, the pairwise dot products of the embedding vec-

1250   C.2.2    I SOTROPIC R ANDOM V ECTORS
1251
       The concept of isotropic random vectors serves as a theoretical benchmark for comparison. In an
1252
       ideal scenario where vectors are isotropic and uniformly distributed over a high-dimensional sphere,
1253   the embeddings would exhibit minimal bias towards any specific direction, resulting in a uniform
1254   spread. This distribution acts as the “prior belief” against which actual embedding distributions are
1255   measured. The expected dot product of such high-dimensional isotropic random vectors is typically
1256   very low, approaching zero as the dimensionality increases, except when vectors are identical.
1257
1258   C.2.3    C OMPONENTS OF THE S ELF C LUSTER F ORMULA
1259
1260   The Self Cluster metric incorporates three key components in its computation:
1261
1262         1. Dot Product Matrix Norm Q = ∥W W T ∥F : This term measures the sum of squared
1263
                pairwise dot products among all embedding vectors, quantifying the overall similarity and
                potential clustering within the dataset. A higher norm suggests more pronounced cluster-
1264
                ing.
1265
1266         2. Expected Dot Product for Isotropic Vectors E[Q] = n + n(n−1)    2d : This component cal-
1267            culates what the norm of the dot product matrix would be if the embeddings were isotropic
1268            random vectors, providing a baseline for comparison. It helps determine if the actual em-
1269            beddings are more clustered than would be expected by chance.
1270         3. Normalization by n2 : In the extreme case of dimension collapse, where all vectors become
1271            identical, the dot product matrix turns into a matrix of ones, and its Frobenius norm reaches
1272            its maximum possible value of n. To ensure the Self Cluster metric is bounded between
1273            0 and 1, the norm of the dot product matrix is normalized by n2 , which is the square of
1274            the number of embeddings. This normalization makes the metric robust to the number of
1275
                embeddings and their dimensionality, facilitating comparisons across different datasets or
                models.
1276
1277
       The Self Cluster metric effectively evaluates the clustering of embeddings by comparing the ob-
1278
       served clustering level to that of a theoretical model of isotropic randomness. By understanding
1279   the deviations from this model, we can infer the degree of structure and the quality of the embed-
1280   dings. A higher Self Cluster value indicates that the embeddings are significantly more clustered
1281   than expected under the isotropic model, suggesting richer structure and potentially higher quality
1282   embeddings for downstream tasks. This metric provides a quantitative tool to assess the ability of
1283   embedding algorithms to capture and preserve meaningful information in a high-dimensional space.
1284
       Build prior belief: The prior belief is that high-quality embeddings are more clustered than isotropic
1285   random vectors, which represent the worst-case distribution.
1286
1287   Quantify prior belief: The method computes the dot product matrix of the embeddings and com-
1288
       pares it to the expected dot product of isotropic vectors. A higher clustering level, as indicated by
       the Frobenius norm of the dot product matrix, signals higher quality.

       E       P RELIMINARIES AND R ELATED W ORK
1493
       This paper, at a high conceptual level, attempts to apply Automated Machine Learning (AutoML)
1494
       techniques to unsupervised learning. More specifically, it focuses on Neural Architecture Search
1495
       (NAS), a subfield of AutoML, as applied to node embedding models within unsupervised Graph
1496   Representation Learning (GRL). However, in the unsupervised learning scenario, the absence of
1497   labels makes direct evaluation of the model difficult, and this is a necessary step in AutoML. To un-
1498   derstand the context of this problem, the following sections will introduce some background knowl-
1499   edge related to AutoML and GRL. In the latter part of this section, we introduce some related work,
1500   including the general categorization of Internal Strategies (IS) into consensus-based and stand-alone
1501   approaches.
1502
1503   E.1      AUTOMATED M ACHINE L EARNING (AUTO ML)
1504
1505   Automated Machine Learning (AutoML). AutoML (He et al., 2021) aims to reduce the need for
1506   manual effort to optimize model performance by automatically setting HP values (Melis et al., 2018;
1507   Snoek et al., 2012). This is particularly crucial for deep learning models, where the architecture
1508
       of the neural network significantly impacts performance compared to traditional machine learning
       models. Simultaneously, with the boom in Graph Machine Learning, AutoML on Graphs (Zhang
1509
       et al., 2021) has also garnered considerable attention.
1510
1511   Automatic hyperparameter optimization (HPO) encounters a main challenge: the computation is
       expensive. This challenge manifests itself in two ways: individual model evaluations can be very


                                                                                      28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       costly when the training process requires substantial computing resources, and the candidate hyper-
1513   parameter space is vast, necessitating numerous trials to find the optimal combination. This issue
1514   is present in both supervised and unsupervised learning. However, if we want to extend AutoML
1515   techniques to unsupervised learning scenarios, we encounter an additional challenge: evaluating
1516   the quality of the model output without labels. Extending AutoML techniques to unsupervised
1517   learning scenarios is the main motivation and focus of this paper.
1518
1519   Neural Architecture Search(NAS). Neural networks have achieved breakthroughs in many
1520   fields, with recent focus primarily on computer vision (CV) (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al.,
1521   2016; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and natural language processing (NLP) (Hochreiter & Schmidhu-
1522   ber, 1997; Vaswani et al., 2017). Many of these great works are due to the design of new neural
1523
       architectures, but this largely relies on experts’ understanding of specific domains. Neural Architec-
       ture Search (NAS) aims to automatically search for well-performing neural architectures to address
1524
       varying application scenarios and different datasets.
1525
1526   Neural architecture is crucial to the performance of a deep learning model, and Neural Architecture
1527   Search (NAS), as a subfield of AutoML, has received much attention in recent years (Ren et al.,
1528   2021). In the context of NAS, there are three main components: search space, search strategy, and
1529
       performance evaluation. The search space defines the candidate neural architectures, for example,
       by specifying the number of network layers and the number of neurons in each layer. Search strat-
1530
       egy concerns how to explore the search space, which is about how to construct candidate neural
1531
       architectures. The most classic approach is grid search, which looks for all possible neural archi-
1532   tectures within the defined search space. Performance evaluation assesses how well these candidate
1533   neural architectures perform. In supervised representation learning, this usually involves evaluating
1534   the model-generated representations on specific downstream tasks (e.g., classification tasks) with la-
1535   bels. For unsupervised embedding, however, the challenge is to assess the quality of the embeddings
1536   without relying on labeled data, requiring alternative evaluation metrics.
1537
1538   E.2   G RAPH E MBEDDING
1539
1540   As one of the primary contexts for the problem studied in this paper, it is essential to understand
1541
       graph embedding. Graph embedding is a technique that converts graph data into low-dimensional
       real-numbered vectors. Below, we introduce some key terms related to graph embedding and pro-
1542
       vide relevant background knowledge.
1543
1544
       Representation Learning. Most machine learning tasks heavily rely on the quality of features
1545
       builded by experts, a process known as feature engineering (Guyon & Elisseeff, 2003). Conse-
1546   quently, the performance of models is highly dependent on the experts’ domain knowledge of the
1547   target datasets. Representation learning (Bengio et al., 2013) can be viewed as an automated ap-
1548   proach to feature engineering. It involves learning representations (i.e., features or embeddings)
1549   from datasets that can be utilized for specific machine learning tasks, such as classification or pre-
1550   diction.
1551
1552   Graph Representation Learning (GRL) and Graph Embedding. Please note that in current
1553   research, the terms graph embedding and graph representation learning are often used interchange-
1554   ably. Therefore, they will not be distinguished in this paper and we will refer to both as graph
1555   embedding.
1556   Graph Representation Learning (GRL) (Hamilton, 2020) is a specialized subset of representation
1557   learning where the input data is structured as graphs. It focuses on learning representations from
1558   graph data, capturing the relationships and structures inherent in graphs. GRL can be approached
1559   in three main ways: 1) traditional statistical methods based on graph theory; 2) node embedding
1560   methods based on random walk mechanisms; and 3) Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Recently,
1561   GNNs have demonstrated dominant performance in the GRL field, making them the focus of this
1562
       paper.
1563   Graph Embedding (Cai et al., 2018), a technique within GRL, maps graph data into low-dimensional
1564   vectors of real numbers. This process primarily focuses on leveraging the structural information
1565   of the graph, and in the case of GNN-based methods, it also incorporates node feature informa-
       tion. Through graph embedding, the learned representations (embeddings) are optimized for various


                                                        29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       downstream tasks such as node classification, link prediction, and clustering (Zhou et al., 2020; Wu
1567   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020).
1568
1569
1570
       Graph Embeddings at Different Levels. Depending on specific application requirements (Cai
1571
       et al., 2018), graph embeddings can be obtained at a node-level or graph-level. Node-level embed-
1572
       ding involves generating representations for individual nodes, utilizing the structural information,
1573   node features, and/or edge weights of the graph. In contrast, graph-level embeddings are generated
1574   for the entire graph, summarizing its overall structure and properties. In this paper, all mentioned
1575   graph embeddings are at the node level. Therefore, the terms “node embedding” and “graph embed-
1576   ding” are used interchangeably and both refer to node embeddings.
1577
1578
1579   Node information aggregation. Node information aggregation (Kipf & Welling, 2016a; Hamil-
1580   ton et al., 2017a; Veličković et al., 2018) is a key step in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) that
1581   involves the message passing mechanism. In this process, each node collects information from its
1582   neighbors, aggregates this information using functions such as mean, sum, or max, and updates its
1583   own representation based on the aggregated information. This step is crucial for capturing the local
1584   graph structure and node features, and it is essential for understanding the relationships between
1585   spatial- and spectral-based GNNs discussed in section E.3.
1586
1587
1588   Different downstream tasks. Node embeddings can be used for various downstream tasks, in-
1589   cluding node classification (Wang et al., 2017), node clustering (Nie et al., 2017), link prediction
1590   (Zhang & Chen, 2018), anomaly detection (Ma et al., 2021), etc. These downstream tasks represent
1591
       practical applications of node representation learning and provide a means to assess the quality of
       learned node embeddings. While node classification is commonly used to evaluate embeddings, this
1592
       paper employs link prediction as the downstream task because it provides a more direct measure of
1593
       the embeddings’ ability to capture the underlying graph structure. Link prediction is particularly
1594   useful for evaluating the quality of node embeddings in unsupervised settings, as it does not rely on
1595   node labels and focuses on the structural properties of the graph, aligning better with our focus on
1596   embedding quality.

       E.3   F ROM S PATIAL - BASED GNN S TO S PECTRAL - BASED GNN S
1621
1622   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have developed along two primary routes: spatial-based and
1623   spectral-based approaches. These two methods fundamentally differ in how they aggregate node
1624   information during the embedding process.
1625
       Spatial-based (Hamilton et al., 2017a; Atwood & Towsley, 2016; Veličković et al., 2018) methods
1626
       aggregate information directly from the neighboring nodes in the graph. They operate on the graph’s
1627   structure by iteratively combining the features of a node with those of its neighbors. This process is
1628   intuitive and straightforward, as it directly reflects the graph’s topology.
1629
1630
       Spectral-based (Monti et al., 2017; Hamilton et al., 2017b; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2018; Wu
       et al., 2019) methods take a different approach by utilizing the graph’s spectral properties. These
1631
       methods rely on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of graph-related matrices (such as the Laplacian
1632
       matrix) to perform convolution operations in the frequency domain. Spectral methods transform
1633   the graph into a spectral space, apply filters to the eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian matrix, and
1634   then transform it back, effectively aggregating information across the entire graph in a way that is
1635   analogous to applying a global filter. This approach is mathematically elegant and leverages the
1636   powerful tools of spectral graph theory.
1637
       Chen et al. (2023) provide a comprehensive analysis of spatial-based and spectral-based approaches
1638
       in GNNs, proposing a unified framework that links them. The authors demonstrate that both spatial
1639   and spectral methods aim to achieve similar goals—effective information aggregation and node
1640   representation—through different mechanisms. Spatial methods can be interpreted as a form of
1641   spectral filtering in the node domain by setting the step size of message passing, while spectral
1642   methods approximate the aggregation process by filtering in the frequency domain. This unified
1643   perspective reveals that the distinction between the two methods lies more in their implementation
1644   techniques rather than their fundamental objectives (more details can be found in Appendix I).
1645
1646   E.4   I NTERNAL S TRATEGY (IS)
1647
1648   Internal Strategies refer to the specific type of evaluation algorithms that can assess the quality
1649   of embeddings without relying on any external evaluation methods. In this paper, IS specifically
1650   represents the type of algorithm we are introducing. The location of IS in the context of AutoML
1651   and GRL can be seen in Figure 14.
1652
1653   Definition of Internal Strategy. An Internal Strategy (IS) is an unsupervised model evaluation
1654   method that assesses model performance without using labels. More specifically, in this paper, IS
1655   takes graph representations as input and outputs a corresponding ranking score, representing the
1656   quality of the graph representations.
1657   To avoid ambiguity, we clarify the target of IS: In this paper, IS directly works on graph embeddings.
1658   However, since each graph embedding is generated using specific HP values, it can also be said
1659   that IS evaluates the performance of these HP values. Furthermore, since hyperparameters are an
1660   essential part of the model, when we refer to the evaluation of GNN models, it pertains to the same
1661   concept.
1662   The term Internal signifies that IS evaluates by leveraging information from within the models and
1663   data, rather than relying on external information (e.g. labels) or human intelligence. This domain
1664   remains largely unexplored due to the challenges posed by the absence of labels. Currently, there
1665   is no existing work specifically focused on evaluating the quality of node embeddings, except for
1666   a meta-learning approach (more details in Appendix J) and some internal strategies (IS) from the
1667   computer vision (CV) community (Appendix C).
1668
1669   Stand-alone and Consensus-based IS. However, we can draw some ideas from other fields such
1670   as Computer Vision (Garrido et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2020) and Anomaly Detection (Ma et al.,
1671   2023). Existing approaches in these fields fall into two categories: stand-alone and consensus-based.
1672   Stand-alone approaches evaluate a hyperparameter (HP) setting independently, whereas consensus-
1673   based approaches require information from multiple HP settings. Stand-alone methods only need a
       single HP setting, while consensus-based methods depend on a pool of candidate HP settings .


                                                        31
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1674
       Stand-alone Internal Strategy. In Ma et al. (2023), seven Internal Strategy (IS) methods were
1675   evaluated for the unsupervised outlier model selection challenge, including four stand-alone and
1676   three consensus-based approaches. The findings indicated that none of the IS methods outperformed
1677   the leading iForest detector (Liu et al., 2008), but consensus-based methods showed more promise
1678   than stand-alone approaches.
1679
1680
       Consensus-based Internal Strategy. In Duan et al. (2020), the Unsupervised Disentanglement
1681
       Ranking (UDR) was introduced as a consensus-based method aimed at hyperparameter tuning for
1682   unsupervised disentangled representation learning models. UDR’s objective is to identify HP values
1683   of models that offer the highest degree of disentanglement. Disentanglement (Siddharth et al., 2017)
1684   refers to the ability of a model to separate distinct, interpretable factors of variation in the data, such
1685   that each factor corresponds to a different dimension in the latent space. This means that changes
1686   in one latent variable should correspond to changes in only one aspect of the data, allowing for
1687   more interpretable and manipulable representations. This approach was evaluated on six leading
1688   Variational Autoencoder (VAE)-based models (Kingma & Welling, 2014; Rezende et al., 2014) for
1689   unsupervised disentangled representation learning. The findings demonstrated a correlation between
1690
       UDR and four supervised disentanglement metrics, indicating its potential for identifying models
       with highly disentangled representations without the need for labeled data.

3016   G.1                 T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
3017
3018   CSOR For CSOR, we quantify the distinctness of node embeddings by accumulating the distances
3019   of a given node embedding compared to all others. This pairwise comparison operation can capture
3020   more distributional information about all node embeddings. Given a graph G with N nodes and an
3021   embedding dimensionality of D, and considering k hyperparameter configurations, the total time
3022   complexity for CSOR is influenced by the number of hyperparameter configurations (k) and the
                                                                                      2
3023   dimensionality of the embeddings (D). Specifically, the time complexity is O(k   · N · D), as each
                                                                                 k

       pairwise distance calculation involves O(N · D) operations, and there are 2 pairs.

3024
       SSOR For SSOR, the distinctness of node embeddings is measured by summing the singular val-
3025   ues of the embedding matrix obtained through Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The compu-
3026   tation involves converting embeddings to a suitable format, calculating the covariance matrix, and
3027   performing SVD. Given N nodes, D dimensions, and k hyperparameter configurations, the total
3028   time complexity for SSOR is determined by O(k · (D2 · N + D3 )), where D2 · N accounts for the
3029   covariance matrix calculation and D3 accounts for the SVD.

       G.2   S PACE C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
3033
3034
3035
       CSOR Each embedding requires space proportional to the number of nodes (N ) and the dimen-
       sionality of the embeddings (D). For k embeddings, the space complexity is O(k·N ·D). Temporary
3036
       storage for distance calculations requires O(N · D), which does not significantly impact the overall
3037
       space complexity. Thus, the total space complexity for CSOR is O(k · N · D).
3038
3039
3040
3041   SSOR Each embedding also requires space proportional to N and D. For k embeddings, the space
3042
       complexity is O(k · N · D). Storing the covariance matrix requires O(D2 ) space, and storing the
       singular values requires O(D) space. Thus, the total space complexity for SSOR is O(k·N ·D+D2 ).

       CSOR has a quadratic dependency on the number of hyperparameter settings (k), while SSOR has
3056   a cubic dependency on the number of dimensions (D) but is linear with respect to k.

3065   Both methods primarily depend on k, N , and D, but spectral-based methods also include an addi-
3066   tional D2 term due to the covariance matrix.
3067   The CSOR method, a spatial-based approach, is characterized by its quadratic time complexity with
3068   respect to the number of hyperparameter settings (k) and its linear space complexity with respect
3069   to the product of the number of samples (N ) and the number of dimensions (D). CSOR has better
3070   scalability with respect to graph size, making it more suitable for handling larger graph embeddings.
3071   However, its performance significantly slows down when the number of candidate hyperparameter
3072   settings is very large.
3073   In contrast, SSOR and any other IS relying on singular values, while linear in the number of hyper-
3074   parameter settings (k), exhibit higher time complexity due to their cubic dependence on the number
3075   of dimensions (D). These methods also require additional space to store the covariance matrix, re-
3076   sulting in a space complexity of O(k · N · D + D2 ). Due to the presence of the SVD operation,
3077   spectral-based methods are not as scalable for very large graph embeddings but can handle a larger
       number of candidate hyperparameter settings more efficiently compared to CSOR.

3224
       Both spatial-based and spectral-based methods in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) share a common
       objective: to effectively aggregate node information to produce meaningful node embeddings. How-
3225
       ever, they achieve this goal through different mechanisms. This appendix elucidates the relationship
3226
       between these two approaches in their realization of node aggregation.
3227
3228
       S PATIAL - BASED M ETHODS
3229
3230   Spatial-based methods operate directly in the node domain. They use the adjacency matrix A to
3231   represent the graph structure and perform node aggregation through message passing among neigh-
3232   boring nodes. Each node updates its embedding by aggregating information from its immediate
3233   neighbors using predefined aggregation functions such as mean, sum, or max.
3234
       Key Characteristics:
3235
3236              • Graph Representation: Uses the adjacency matrix A.
3237
                  • Node Domain Operations: Aggregation is performed directly on nodes and their neigh-
3238
                    bors.
3239
                  • Message Passing: Nodes receive and aggregate information from their neighboring nodes.

3240
              • Examples: Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN), GraphSAGE, Graph Attention Net-
3241            works (GAT).
3242
3243   In GCN, for instance, the aggregation operation can be expressed as:
3244
3245
                                     H (l+1) = σ(D̃−1/2 ÃD̃−1/2 H (l) W (l) )
3246   where Ã = A + I is the adjacency matrix with added self-loops, D̃ is the degree matrix, H (l) is the
3247   node embedding at layer l, W (l) is the learnable weight matrix, and σ is an activation function.
3248
3249
       S PECTRAL - BASED M ETHODS
3250
3251   Spectral-based methods, on the other hand, operate in the frequency domain. They leverage the
3252   graph Laplacian matrix L = D − A and perform node aggregation through spectral filtering. The
3253   graph Laplacian is decomposed into its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, transforming the node features
3254   into the spectral domain. Aggregation is performed by filtering these spectral components, and the
3255   result is then transformed back to the node domain.
3256   Key Characteristics:
3257
3258          • Graph Representation: Uses the Laplacian matrix L.
3259          • Frequency Domain Operations: Aggregation is performed by filtering in the spectral
3260            domain.
3261          • Eigen Decomposition: The Laplacian matrix is decomposed into eigenvalues and eigen-
3262            vectors.
3263
              • Examples: Spectral CNN, ChebNet.
3264
3265   A typical spectral-based aggregation can be described as:
3266
3267                                            H = U g(Λ)U T X
3268   where U and Λ are the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the Laplacian matrix L, respectively, X is
3269   the node feature matrix, and g(Λ) is a spectral filter applied to the eigenvalues.
3270
3271   C ONNECTING THE T WO A PPROACHES
3272
3273   Despite their different mechanisms, both spatial-based and spectral-based methods aim to aggre-
3274   gate node information to produce effective node embeddings. The connection between these two
3275   approaches can be understood through their respective domains of operation:
3276
3277
              • Spatial-based methods perform aggregation directly in the node domain by iteratively
                combining information from neighboring nodes.
3278
3279          • Spectral-based methods perform aggregation in the frequency domain by applying filters
3280            to the eigenvalues of the Laplacian matrix, capturing global graph properties.
3281
       Spatial-based methods can be viewed as a localized approximation of spectral methods. The direct
3282
       message passing and aggregation in the node domain approximate the spectral filtering operations
3283
       performed in the frequency domain. Both methods can be considered complementary, offering
3284   different perspectives and advantages for graph representation learning.
3285
3286   In summary, while spatial-based and spectral-based methods differ in their implementation, they
3287
       share the fundamental goal of node information aggregation. Understanding the relationship be-
       tween these methods provides a unified perspective on the diverse approaches used in GNNs for
3288
       node embedding generation.
