011                                           Abstract
012
013           Recovering natural language prompts for image generation models, solely
014           based on the generated images is a difficult discrete optimization prob-
015           lem. In this work, we present the first head-to-head comparison of re-
016           cent discrete optimization techniques for the problem of prompt inversion.
017           Following prior work on prompt inversion, we use CLIP’s Radford et al.
018           (2021) text-image alignment as an inexpensive proxy for the distribution
019
              of prompt-image pairs, and compare several discrete optimizers against
              BLIP2’s image captioner (Li et al., 2023) and PRISM (He et al., 2024) in
020
              order to evaluate the quality of discretely optimized prompts across vari-
021
              ous metrics related to the quality of inverted prompts and the images that
022           they generate. We find that while the discrete optimizers effectively min-
023           imize their objectives, CLIP similarity between the inverted prompts and
024           the ground truth image acts as a poor proxy for the distribution of prompt-
025           image pairs – responses from well-trained captioners often lead to generated
026           images that more closely resemble those produced by the original prompts.
027           This finding highlights the need for further investigation into inexpensive
028           methods of modeling the relationship between the prompts for generative
029           models and their output space.

      1   Introduction
033
      Images generated by AI models are flooding the internet and the models and prompts used
034
      for generation often feel like components of alchemy. Naturally, the academic community
035
      aims to better understand the mechanisms at play in these systems, with one crucial focus
036   on inverting the generative process by recovering prompts from images (Fan et al., 2024).
037
038   As the name suggests, given only the output image from a generative model, prompt inver-
039
      sion methods seek to find the prompt that generated the image. While the goal is clear, this
      problem leads to a wide variety of approaches. For example, given an initial prompt esti-
040
      mate, by strategically refining the text input Sohn et al. (2023) have found that the image
041
      generation process can be effectively controlled. Yet, this refining process can be difficult,
042   leading to Wen et al. (2023) leveraging CLIP’s (Radford et al., 2021) embedding space to
043   directly optimize natural language inputs to be close to target images. Similarly, Mahajan
044   et al. (2024) have proposed an inversion technique that backpropagates through intermedi-
045   ate steps of the diffusion process instead of relying on CLIP embeddings In contrast, it has
046   been found that training a captioner on a dataset of prompt-image pairs effectively learns
047   the prompt distribution well enough to act as an inverter.
048   While prompt inversion is an interesting task in its own right, there are two practical
049   motivations to develop strong methods. First, those interested in better controlling the
050   output of image generation models may want to find a prompt from an image as a starting
051   point for their own prompt engineering. Second, by extracting prompts from images, one
052   might better understand the various associations these text-to-image models have and debug
053   them to avoid explicit content. Yet, to date, there is no standardized comparison of these
      methods for prompt inversion from image generation models.


                                                    1
054
      In this work, we benchmark several approaches to prompt inversion and attempt to shed light
055   on three primary questions: 1) How closely do the distribution of images generated by each
056   approach align with the distribution generated by the original prompt? 2) Do text-image
057   alignment models like CLIP successfully act as a proxy for the joint distribution of prompt-
058   image pairs? 3) Does the ability for discrete optimization methods to search the input space
059   allow them to outperform the ingrained knowledge of learned models? We investigate these
060   questions by comparing four generic methods of discrete optimization within the prompt
061   space (Zou et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Andriushchenko, 2023) against two
062   methods that directly sample from the space (Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2024) and evaluate
063
      each across several metrics. We follow our analysis with a discussion on the efficacy of
      different approaches to approximating and searching within prompt spaces.

067   2   Related Work
068
069   To best situate this paper among prior work, we discuss several motivations for executing
070   prompt inversion, other domains where discrete optimization is relevant, and the overall
071   goal of our work in contrast to related papers.
072
      Here, we focus on discrete optimization methods for recovering image prompts, but discrete
073   optimization over natural language has several other applications including jailbreaking
074   LLMs (Andriushchenko, 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) and measuring memoriza-
075   tion (Schwarzschild et al., 2024; Kassem et al., 2024). In particular, Zou et al. (2023) propose
076   a method to find adversarial prompts for LLMs that break their safety alignment. We ex-
077   periment with this optimizer for image generation inversion as Zou et al. (2023) compare
078   their optimizer to PEZ in their work, but only with the goal of jailbreaking LLMs.
079   Whereas prompt optimization strategies in the text generation space have specific goals,
080   such as generating targeted strings, the image generation space has struggled with tractable
081   options for aligning prompts and generated images. While we follow prior work in using
082   CLIP as a proxy model, this choice is primarily driven by the practical challenges of directly
083   optimizing prompts through backpropagation in the diffusion process. The computational
084   requirements for coarse-grained exploration and fine-grained search that discrete optimiza-
085   tion often calls for (Parker and Rardin, 2014) would entail generating multiple full images
086   for every candidate prompt att every step. Following the search parameters recommended
087
      by (Zou et al., 2023), optimizing a single prompt would require generating a minimum of
      512 images per step, which quickly becomes prohibitive over numerous iterations.
088
089   Mahajan et al. (2024) have attempted to address this burden by focusing on the similarity
090   between predicted noise residuals at specific diffusion timesteps, rather than generating full
091   images. However, in alignment with prior work on noise inversion (Song et al., 2020; Mokady
092   et al., 2023) the authors find that prompts only have strong influence on the generated
093
      image during a narrow range of timesteps. At early timesteps, the image becomes largely
      “locked in,” so even substantial changes to the prompt have little effect. In contrast, at
094
      later timesteps, the stochasticity of the diffusion process leads to large variations in the final
095
      image, even when the correct prompt is used. This unpredictability makes it difficult to
096   rely on noise residual comparisons for consistent prompt inversion. Thus, prompt inversion
097   methods (Wen et al., 2023; Williams and Kolter, 2024) often rely on deterministic proxy
098   models like CLIP, which offer a more stable and efficient alternative. Through CLIP’s text-
099   image alignment, we can more reliably approximate the prompt-image distribution without
100   having to address the risk of stochasticity producing divergent results.
101
      Importantly, direct discrete optimization is not the only method for finding viable prompts.
102   Several approaches focus on using black box models to sample prompts. Both Zhang et al.
103   (2024) and He et al. (2024) use pretrained language models to extract prompts for given
104   a output across text generation and image generation tasks respectively. Moreover, as we
105   show in this work, even a simple captioner that has not been finetuned for prompt generation
106   often outperforms discrete optimization methods. In fact, Reade et al. (2023) have found
107   that a captioner fine-tuned on pairs of prompts and the images that they generate can
      effectively sample prompts that are exceptionally similar to the ground truth.


                                                      2
108
      Despite this performance, we focus on the discrete case as direct discrete optimization can be
109   beneficial for better understanding the behavior of the image generation models. Similarly to
110   prior work on counterfactual explanations (Verma et al., 2020), directly optimizing inputs for
111   a desired outputs helps to better understand the decision boundaries of classifiers (Ribeiro
112   et al., 2016). While generative models are not classifiers with explicit decisions and accuracy
113   metrics, they are constantly making decisions on their representations based on the prompts.
114   From background color to subject ethnicity, discrete optimization methods may provide a
115   useful understanding of the relationship between prompts and images (Williams et al., 2024).
116   We emphasize solidifying ways of comparing discrete optimizers for image generation tasks.
117   Even with the rise of novel discrete optimization methods, standard prompt recovery com-
118   parisons over images are missing. We focus on a holistic benchmark on not only the similarity
119   between prompt and image, but also the similarity among images generated by the inverted
120   prompts which to the best of our knowledge has not been standardized in this setting.

122   3     Selected Algorithms
123
124   To best introduce the optimizers we study, it is critical to pose the prompt inversion problem
125   formally. Consider a tokenizer that maps from natural language to sequences of integer
126   valued tokens corresponding to a list of indices in a vocabulary of tokens T. Let x ∈ Ts be a
127   length s sequence of tokens. Next, let E ∈ R|T|×d be a matrix whose rows are d-dimensional
128   embedding vectors, one for each token in the vocabulary. To embed a sequence x, we can
                                 PT
129   define X ∈ {0, 1}s×|T| s.t. i=1 Xj,i = 1 ∀j ∈ {1, ..., M } to be a matrix of one-hot encoded
130   rows for the integers in the sequence x. The product XE defines an s × d embedding of x.
131   Prompt inversion techniques seek to find the sequence of tokens x, or equivalently their
132   corresponding one-hot encodings X, that solve M−1 (Y ), where M is a stochastic generative
133   model that maps a sequence of tokens x to an image Y . Typically we express the solution as
134   the minimizer of some loss function L, or the solution to the following optimization problem.
135                                                       |T|
                                                          X
136                  argmin       L(M(XE), Y )     s.t.         Xj,i = 1 ∀ j ∈ {1, ..., s}       (1)
137                X∈{0,1}s×|T|                           i=1
138
      Khashabi et al. (2021) show that embeddings in Rd outside of the discrete set of the rows of
139   E have little meaning to the generative model M. As a consequence, most prompt inversion
140   methods focus on strategies for discrete optimization within the embedding table E; we call
141   this ‘hard prompting’ in a discrete space rather than ‘soft prompting’ in a continuous space.
142   While the gradient exists with respect to the entries of the input XE ∈ Rs×d , continuous
143   descent-based methods risk finding minima outside of Ts , leaving us without hard tokens.
144
      Moreover, computing the gradient through the full generation model M may be too expen-
145   sive (for example when M is a diffusion model forward passes may take multiple seconds),
146   but as emphasized above, prior work often uses CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to encode im-
147   ages and text in a shared latent space. Some of the methods we examine operate wholly
148   within CLIP’s latent space to compute the loss between the prompt and the target image.
149   These methods approximate Equation (1) by solving the following problem where LCLIP is
150   a similarity loss defined over CLIP embeddings.
151                                                       |T|
                                                          X
152                  argmin       LCLIP (XE, Y )   s.t.         Xj,i = 1 ∀ j ∈ {1, ..., s}       (2)
153                X∈{0,1}s×|T|                           i=1

155   3.1   PEZ
156
157
      The first approach we consider is PEZ (Wen et al., 2023), a version of projected gradient
      descent where descent steps are made in the continuous embedding space. The gradients of
158
      the objective in Equation (2) are evaluated at points in embedding space corresponding to
159
      real tokens, but the trajectory of the iterates may deviate from the discrete token set.
160
161   More formally, let ProjE (·) be an operator that projects vectors (or matrices row-wise) from
      Rd to their nearest row-vector of E, and let ξi ∈ Rs×d be a soft prompt. As an iterative


                                                     3
162
      gradient-based optimizer, PEZ produces a sequence of iterates [ξ0 , ξ1 , ..., ξn ] as it solves
163   the minimization problem in Equation (2). To update from ξi to ξi+1 , PEZ computes the
164   gradient of the loss at the hard prompt ProjE (ξi ) and takes a step in the direction of this
165   gradient from the soft prompt ξi and then calls ProjE (ξi ) to project back to the space of
166   hard prompts. Thus, PEZ gives a fast, lightweight method of discrete optimization while
167   still using gradient-based descent to approximately solve the problem in Equation (1). For
168   more information, see Algorithm 1 as described by Wen et al. (2023). For a single image,
169   we run PEZ over the CLIP loss for 3000 steps and return the prompt the maximizes the
170   CLIP similarity between the image embedding and the text embedding of the prompt.
171
172   3.2   Greedy Coordinate Gradients
173
      Greedy Coordinate Gradients (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023) is an alternative method for opti-
174
      mizing over the discrete vocabulary using the gradients of the objective with respect to the
175   matrix X in Equation (2). In particular, we compute the gradient of the loss with respect
176   to X, which is a matrix of the same shape that approximately ranks token swaps. As each
177   entry in a given row of X corresponds to a token in the vocabulary, each row i in its gradi-
178   ent relays to us how influential changing the token xi to each other token in the vocabulary
179   might be in lowering the loss. More formally, we compute ∇X LCLIP (M(XE), Y ), then, just
180   as gradient descent methods takes steps in the opposite direction of the gradient, we select
181   a random batch of candidate swaps from the top k largest entries of the negative gradient.
182   A given swap corresponds to a single token change in x and we directly compute the loss
183
      for each of these candidates and greedily accept the best one as our new iterate. As done
      with PEZ, we run GCG over the CLIP loss for 3000 steps, returning the best prompt as
184
      determined by CLIP similarity between the image embedding and the prompt’s embedding.
185
186
      3.3   AutoDAN
187
188   AutoDAN (Zhu et al., 2023) was proposed as a method of finding human-readable adversarial
189   attacks on aligned language models. The optimizer solves Eq. (1) by iteratively optimizing
190   a single token appended to the current prompt. Given an initial prefix, e.g., “Image of a”,
191   the algorithm searches for the token that follows ‘a’ that minimizes the objective function.
192   The optimizer incorporates a ‘readability’ objective based on the log probability of the next
193   token given an underlying language model. Similarly to GCG, AutoDAN employs a coarse-
194
      to-fine search strategy by appending an initial token, x̂ to the current iterate x, and scores
      each token in the vocabulary according to the following scoring function:
195
196
                               score(xi ) = − ∇x̂ L([x, x̂]E) + log(p(xi |x))                    (3)
197   The algorithm selects the top k scoring tokens and performs a fine-grained search by com-
198
      puting the exact loss over each, taking the token that minimizes the loss, L. This minimizing
      token is then appended to x, giving xt+1 = [xt x∗i ].
199
200   AutoDAN was originally designed for text-to-text language models, where the log proba-
201   bility, log(p(xi |x)) was directly available. However, in this review, we use CLIP to deter-
202   mine the quality of the prompt, which does not inherently compute the log probability.
203   We thus use FUSE (Williams and Kolter, 2024), a recently proposed approach for solving
204
      multi-objective problems across models and tokenizers. FUSE approximates the jacobian
      of a mapping between the two models and uses the embeddings of a text-to-text language
205
      model, such as GPT2 to compute both the log probability, log(p(xi |x)), and the gradient,
206
      ∇xGP T LCLIP (f (xGP T )), where f maps from GPT’s embeddings to CLIP’s embeddings.
207   This allows us to apply a language prior when optimizing a prompt with CLIP. We addi-
208   tionally explore the scenario in which we do not use a language prior, by reverting to the
209                                                1
      standard case in which we fix p(xi |x) = |T|   . In our experiments, we run AutoDAN for 16
210   steps, which enforces a a maximum token length of 16 due to one-by-one generation of new
211   tokens. We also utilize a beam search with a beam width of 5.
212
213   3.4   Random Search
214
215   Andriushchenko (2023) suggests that such sophisticated strategies may not be critical for
      prompt optimization—given enough time, random searches can perform adequately in a


                                                    4
216
      variety of settings. Thus, we explore a variant of random search (Rastrigin, 1963). While
217   random search traditionally selects random candidates from within a ball around the the
218   current iterate, this approach does not directly map to hard prompting. Due to the curse
219   of dimensionality, true random samples around these high-dimensional embedding spaces
220   are sampled from a ball of with negligible volume around the initial embedding; a nearest
221   neighbor projection would often fail to return a new candidate.
222
      In order to address this limitation, we randomly sample from new tokens from the l0 ball
223   around each element in the sequence XE. At every iteration, we select a batch of candi-
224   datesand greedily accept the best single-token replacement as the next iterate. We compare
225   the prompt found by Random Searching over the same number of steps as done for PEZ
226   and GCG, determining the best prompt by CLIP similarity in the same way.
227
228   3.5   PRISM
229
230   PRISM, proposed by He et al. (2024), highlights that text-to-image generation is not a
231   one-to-one mapping – multiple prompts can describe the same image, and many images can
232   correspond to the same prompt. Rather than relying on discrete token space optimization,
233   PRISM optimizes over a distribution of prompts. Inspired by LLM jailbreaking methods (eg.
234
      Chao et al., 2023), PRISM leverages in-context learning in vision-language models (VLMs)
      to iteratively refine the prompt distribution. This process incorporates the history of refer-
235
      ence images, generated prompts, output images from an anchor text-to-image model, and
236
      evaluations from a VLM judge, using techniques similar to chain-of-thought (Wei et al.,
237   2022) and textual gradient (Pryzant et al., 2023). After K iterations across N parallel
238   streams, the best-performing prompt is selected using the same VLM judge. In our ex-
239   periments, we use GPT-4-o-mini as the VLM and Stable Diffusion XL-Turbo (Sauer et al.,
240   2023) as the anchor text-to-image model, following He et al. (2024)’s setup with N = 6 and
241   K = 5. To ensure fair comparisons, we limit the generated prompts to 20 tokens.
242
243   3.6   Captioning
244
245   Lastly, we use automated image captions as a proxy for the inverted prompts. Given that a
246   prompt for an image generation model likely encodes information about the setting of the
247
      desired image, its subject, its quality, and other properties, we assume that captioning an
      image provides a human-readable token sequence with some or all of these same properties
248
      necessary to generate the image. Moreover, as captioners are typically autoregressive, they
249
      have the potential to return an approximate inversion much faster than other methods.
250
251   Here, we focus on a single model, BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023). This model is a generic and
252   compute-efficient vision-langauge pre-training (VLP) method. VLP techniques aim to learn
253
      multimodal foundation models on a variety of vision-language tasks. BLIP-2 leverages a
      trainable module, the Q-former, in order to bridge the gap between a frozen image encoder
254
      and a frozen LLM, facilitating image-text matching tasks, image-grounded text generation
255
      tasks, and image-text contrastive learning tasks. We prioritize BLIP-2’s image-grounded
256   text generation as the frozen CLIP-style encoder aligns well with the above prompt inversion
257   methods, all of which use frozen CLIP encoders.

508   one-and-done approach that GCG and Random Search seem to use, where they discourage
509   repeating the same general concepts or tokens in a prompt.

      6   Discussion
512
513
      Our results prompt discussion on the practical implications, the limitations, and the future
514
      directions related to prompt inversion. To begin, someone interested in finding good prompts
515
      from images can conclude from our work that image captioning tools are a good approach.
516   They are fast, as the heavy lifting is done ahead of time in training these models rather
517   than optimizing anything per image in deployment. They also best capture natural sounding
518   language, a goal that discrete optimizers might better incorporate as these tools mature.
519
      The limitations of our work center mostly on the fact that the diffusion and image-text
520
      embedding space is so heavily driven by only a few models. As the set of state-of-the-art
521
      large text-prompted image generations models grows, the trends we report may no longer
522   hold. In the same vein, small variations in the optimization strategies could have large
523   impacts on these results. In short, like any empirical benchmark results, our findings are
524   subject to change as the field progresses.
525
      Here, we enumerate several questions and quirks arising from our work that warrant further
526
      investigation. First, Zou et al. (2023) report that GCG is effective at jailbreaking LLMs
527
      and PEZ is not. This stands in stark contrast to these two methods relative performance
528   at prompt inversion. Why might optimizing over natural language be so different in these
529   settings? This could be a difference in the particular models or in the loss landscapes. Sec-
530   ond, GCG and random search perform so similarly begging the question why does gradient
531   information make so little difference? The intuition that the gradient signal is informative
532   comes from observing the success of PEZ, so why is the combination of search and gradient-
533   based optimization in GCG leave it so similar to random search alone? Finally, we posit
534   that there is a lot of room for improvement. In other words, prompt inversion is far from
535   solved, and it makes for a great test bed for new discrete optimization approaches.
