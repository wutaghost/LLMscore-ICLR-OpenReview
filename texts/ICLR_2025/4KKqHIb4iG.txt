009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Approximating solutions to time-dependent Partial Differential Equations (PDEs)
012            is one of the most important problems in computational science. Neural PDE
013            solvers have shown promise recently because they are mesh-free and easy to imple-
014            ment. However, backpropagation-based training often leads to poor approximation
015            accuracy and long training time. In particular, capturing high-frequency temporal
016            dynamics and solving over long time spans pose significant challenges. To address
017            these, we present an approach to training neural PDE solvers without backprop-
018
               agation by integrating two key ideas: separation of space and time variables and
               random sampling of weights and biases of the hidden layers. We reformulate the
019
               PDE as an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) using a neural network ansatz,
020
               construct neural basis functions only in the spatial domain, and solve the ODE
021            leveraging classical ODE solvers from scientific computing. We demonstrate that
022            our backpropagation-free algorithm outperforms the iterative, gradient-based op-
023            timization of physics-informed neural networks with respect to training time and
024            accuracy, often by 1 to 5 orders of magnitude using different complicated PDEs
025            characterized by high-frequency temporal dynamics, long time span, complex
026            spatial domain, non-linearities, shocks, and high dimensionality.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Approximating solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs) is vital in computational science and
032   engineering. Traditional mesh-based numerical methods like finite differences, finite volumes, finite
033
      elements, or mesh-free methods based on global basis functions like spectral methods have been
      developed for decades. These methods often approximate PDE solutions with high accuracy and are
034
      grounded in theory. However, mesh-based methods are often difficult to implement on complicated
035
      domains due to the meshing difficulties and can be prohibitively expensive for high-dimensional
036   problems owing to the curse of dimensionality. Traditional spectral methods often struggle with
037   complicated domains and locally sharp gradients in the PDE solution (Boyd, 2001).
038
039
      Deep neural networks have recently shown significant promise for approximating solutions of PDEs
      because of the mesh-free construction of basis functions, high expressivity of neural networks (Rudi
040
      & Rosasco, 2021), their ability to represent functions in high dimensions (E, 2020; Wu & Long, 2022),
041
      powerful software for automatic differentiation (e.g., Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2017), TensorFlow (Abadi
042   et al., 2015), and specialized software like DeepXDE (Lu et al., 2021b)). Earlier work on solving
043   PDEs using neural networks (Dissanayake & Phan-Thien, 1994; Lagaris et al., 1998) was recently
044   popularized in the form of Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and neural operators by Raissi
045   et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2021a); Li et al. (2020); Raonic et al. (2024); Han et al. (2018), and Sirignano
046   & Spiliopoulos (2018). However, numerous challenges are becoming apparent. Next, we outline some
047   of the key drawbacks of existing neural PDE solvers based on gradient-based iterative optimization
048   that motivate our work: training difficulties, capturing high-frequency temporal dynamics, as well as
049   long training time and low accuracy.
050   Training difficulties: Neural PDE solvers that rely on backpropagation-through-time require com-
051   puting gradients of the loss function with respect to the network parameters along trajectories (Um
052   et al., 2020). This usually leads to challenges posed by exploding and vanishing gradients during
053   the iterative, gradient-based training procedure Pascanu et al. (2013); Schmidt et al. (2019). Physics-
      informed neural networks and their variants are not iterative in the same way, but require minimizing

      a loss function involving the PDE residual, boundary conditions, and initial conditions. Rathore et al.
055   (2024) demonstrate that the PDE residual loss primarily causes ill-conditioning of the PINN loss. It
056   has been shown that even in simple settings, the PINN loss is very challenging to minimize using
057   backpropagation (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; 2022). Though various approaches
058   such as balancing different loss terms (Yao et al., 2023), regularization (Lu et al., 2021c; Yu et al.,
059   2022), and different optimizers (Müller & Zeinhofer, 2023; Liu et al., 2024) were introduced to
060   alleviate some of the problems, it is still quite difficult to optimize PINNs with backpropagation.
061   Capturing high-frequency temporal dynamics and solving PDEs over a long time span: The
062   temporal structure of initial value PDEs is local, as each subsequent step depends solely on the values
063   of the preceding spatial slice — a property that typical physics-informed-machine-learning-based
064   approaches, which treat time similar to an extra spatial dimension, fail to consider. We show that
065   by using neural basis functions only in space and classical ODE solvers in time, one can capture
066   high-frequency temporal dynamics and solve PDEs over long time spans.
067   Long training time and low accuracy: Approximation errors tend to be significant because of
068   the gradient-based iterative optimization of network parameters and the challenges associated with
069   handling time as an additional spatial dimension. This often leads to longer training times and
070   much lower accuracy than classical mesh-based methods, especially for problems involving complex
071   temporal dynamics or long time spans.

092
             • We propose two approaches to solving time-dependent PDEs with neural networks without
093
               backpropagation that synergistically combine data-driven (SWIM-ODE) or data-agnostic
094
               (ELM-ODE) sampling algorithms for computing hidden layer parameters with classical
095            ODE solvers from scientific computing (See Section 3.2, Section 3.3).
096
             • We propose novel techniques to satisfy boundary conditions for solving time-dependent
097
               PDEs with a neural network ansatz (See Section 3.4).
098
099          • We demonstrate the strengths of our backpropagation-free training algorithm for neural PDE
100
               solvers—high accuracy, reduced training time, spectral convergence, and mesh-free basis
               functions—by solving complex PDEs involving high-frequency temporal dynamics, long-
101
               time simulations, complicated domains, non-linearities, shocks, and high-dimensionality
102
               (See Section 4).
103
104   Our approach outperforms PINNs trained with backpropagation by 1-5 orders of magnitude in
105   accuracy and up to 4 orders of magnitude in training time. Compared to classical mesh-based
106   methods like FEM, our approach is very easy to implement on complicated geometries. It yields
107   a comparable performance (in low dimensions), but it can also deal with high-dimensional PDEs,
      where mesh-based methods suffer from the curse of dimensionality.

      2    R ELATED WORK
109
110   Randomized neural networks for solving PDEs have been studied, mostly combining Extreme
111   Learning Machines (ELMs) with the self-supervised setting of PINNs (Chen et al., 2024; Wang
112   & Dong, 2024; Shang & Wang, 2024; Sun et al., 2024). Dwivedi & Srinivasan (2020) propose a
113   physics-informed extreme learning machine (PIELM) to efficiently solve linear PDEs, while Calabrò
114   et al. (2021); Galaris et al. (2022) employ ELMs to learn invariant manifolds as well as PDE from data.
115   Dong & Yang (2022) establish that given a fixed computational budget, ELMs achieve substantially
116   higher accuracy compared to classical second-order FEM and slightly higher accuracy compared to
117   higher-order FEM. For static, nonlinear PDEs, ELMs can be used together with nonlinear optimization
118
      schemes (Fabiani et al., 2021). On larger spatiotemporal domains, Dong & Li (2021) and Dwivedi
      et al. (2021) propose using multiple distributed ELMs on multiple subdomains. These approaches
119
      treat time similarly to an extra dimension in space, and neural basis functions are used to span (a part
120
      of) the entire spatiotemporal domain, unlike our approach.
121
122   Neural Galerkin schemes (cf. work from Finzi et al. (2023); Aghili et al. (2024); Berman et al.
123   (2024); Bruna et al. (2024)) offer an alternative to the full spatiotemporal approach of the randomized
124
      neural networks and PINNs. These approaches treat all or sparse subsets of network parameters,
      beyond just the last layer’s parameters, as time-dependent. This leads to a much larger system of
125
      ODEs compared to our approach. Chen et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2023) also use neural network basis
126
      functions to represent the space component but are based on backpropagation.
127
128   Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) are widely used to solve PDEs with neural networks.
129   For high-frequency temporal variations, Krishnapriyan et al. (2021) propose curriculum learning with
130
      gradually increasing advection coefficients. Our approach is much easier to implement, much more
      computationally efficient, and accurate, as we demonstrate in Section 4.1. Subramanian et al. (2023)
131
      propose using adaptive self-supervision of PINNs for sampling collocation points using the gradient
132
      of the loss function. We instead use the solution gradient to capture locally sharp features in the
133   solution (cf. Section 4.4). Many specialized approaches based on PINNs (cf Cho et al. (2024), Meng
134   et al. (2020)), Sharma & Shankar (2022), and Chiu et al. (2022)), methods based on hash-encoding
135   (cf. Huang & Alkhalifah (2024), Wang et al. (2024a)) and transfer learning (cf. Kapoor et al. (2024b)))
136   have been proposed, but are still based on backpropagation, unlike ours.
137
      Classical numerical methods to solve PDEs: Finite elements, finite volumes, and finite differences
138
      have been used to solve PDEs for decades. They often have a rich theoretical grounding and high
139   accuracy. Isogeometric analysis (IGA) is such a method in which spline-based basis functions are
140   defined over a structured grid (cf. Hughes et al. (2005); Cottrell et al. (2009; 2006)). Mesh-based
141   methods often entail a time-consuming setup phase, especially when mesh generation is challenging.
142   In this work, we benchmark our results against IGA and finite-element-based methods.
143
      For an extended review of related work, please refer to Appendix A.

146   3    A PPROXIMATION OF PDE SOLUTIONS USING NEURAL NETWORKS
147
148   We discuss solution methods for PDEs on domains Ω ∈ Rd with boundary ∂Ω. We address linear
149
      and nonlinear time-dependent PDEs with solutions u : Ω × R → R. These PDEs are defined by
      linear operators L and B that only involve derivative operators in space, and functions f : Ω → R,
150
      g : ∂Ω → R, and u0 : Ω → R that define forcing, boundary condition, and initial condition,
151
      respectively. For nonlinear PDEs, we denote the nonlinear operator by N , and its scaling γ ≥ 0 is
152   either zero (for linear PDEs) or positive (for nonlinear PDEs). Then,
153
                          ut (x, t) + Lu(x, t) + γN (u)(x, t) = f (x), x ∈ Ω, t ∈ [0, T ],           (1)
154
155
                             Bu(x, t) = g(x), x ∈ ∂Ω, u(x, 0) = u0 (x), x ∈ Ω,                       (2)
156   where we denote by ut the first derivative of u by time.
157   We first describe the neural network ansatz in Section 3.1, and then describe how to construct the
158   spatial basis functions of the ansatz in Section 3.2. For time-dependent PDEs, we propose solving
159   an ordinary differential equation associated with our construction of the spatial basis using classical
160   ODE solvers in Section 3.3 by evolving the last layer coefficients in time. In Section 3.4, we explain
161   different approaches for satisfying boundary conditions by adding a linear layer. Lastly, in Section 3.5,
      we summarize the algorithm for backpropagation-free training of neural PDE solvers.

179   3.2   C OMPUTING HIDDEN LAYER PARAMETERS WITHOUT GRADIENT- BASED OPTIMIZATION
180
      To sample the coefficients of the first hidden layer, we propose two approaches: ELM and SWIM.
181
182   ELM (Data-agnostic): In ELM, the weights are sampled from a Gaussian distribution, and biases are
183   sampled from a uniform distribution in [−η, η] for each hidden layer, where η is a hyper-parameter.
184   SWIM (Data-dependent): The SWIM algorithm samples weights and biases using a data-dependent
185   distribution. The weight and bias of each neuron in the hidden layer are sampled using one pair of
186   spatial collocation points (x(1) , x(2) ). In the unsupervised setting, one can choose pairs of collocation
187   points from a uniform distribution over all possible pairs of collocation points, which is the default
188   setting in this paper, as we do not know the solution of the PDE beforehand. In the supervised setting,
                                                                (2)
                                                                    )−f (x(1) )∥
189   the data points are selected based on the density ∥f (x ∥x(2) −x(1) ∥
                                                                                 , with f being the true function in a
190
      supervised setting. The weights and bias of each neuron with a tanh activation function are chosen
191
      such that the neuron’s output is −0.5 for the input x(1) and +0.5 for the input x(2) . This ensures that
192   the centers of the activation functions are always placed in the spatial domain—unlike ELM, where
193   the centers of the functions could be randomly placed outside the spatial domain. It also ensures that
194   the activation functions are oriented in the direction from point x(1) to point x(2) .
195
196
      The key benefits of randomly sampling basis functions include much shorter training times and
      improved accuracy compared to PINNs (both from one to five orders of magnitude), nearly matching
197
      the numerical state-of-the-art solvers. Moreover, the advantages compared to the classical numerical
198
      solvers such as finite elements, finite differences, or finite volume approaches include spectral
199   convergence (i.e., requiring much fewer basis functions) without requiring a mesh, making it much
200   easier to implement on complex geometries.
201
202
      The suitability of each of the proposed approaches depends on the true PDE solution’s gradient
      distribution. For a detailed comparison, please refer to Appendix B.1.3. We empirically observe that
203
      ELM performs better in approximating solutions with shallow gradients, while SWIM (by sampling
204
      weights from close data points) performs better in approximating solutions with steep gradients. In

273   the condition number of the associated ODE in Equation (5) and to reduce the size of the ODE system.

275   a truncated singular value decomposition of AΦ(X) ∈ RMb ×Nc to obtain matrices Vr , Σr , and Ur

406   4.4   N ON - LINEARITY AND SHOCKS
407
408   We demonstrate how sampling weights and biases from a data-dependant distribution can be exploited
409   to handle locally steep gradients in the solution of the non-linear viscous Burgers’ equation. In
410
      Figure 8, we compare the SWIM-ODE solution to the numerical solution provided by Raissi et al.
      (2019). Table 19 indicates that ELM-ODE cannot accurately represent the sharp gradient in the
411
      domain’s center due to the exponentially small probability of having large norms of internal weights.
412
      Sampling ELM-ODE weights from a broader uniform distribution increases the probability of
413   having steeper basis functions, as Calabrò et al. (2021) discuss for linear PDEs. However, given
414   enough collocation points in the domain’s center, SWIM-ODE can create numerous basic functions
415   with steep gradients, accurately placing them in the domain’s center by factoring in the data. To
416   concentrate collocation points near the shock in the domain’s center, we resample them two times
417   after a set number of time steps, guided by a probability distribution that leverages the gradient of the
418   approximated solution. At the resampling time tr ∈ [0, T ], we approximate the probability density
419   p(x) ∼ |∇û(x, tr )|, which we then use to re-sample collocation points at random. While PINNs
420   provide a reasonable error, SWIM-ODE is more accurate by order of magnitude, almost twice as
421
      fast as regular PINN, and over ten times faster than causal PINN (See Table 2, Table 19). Please
      refer to Appendix C.4 for details. We also demonstrate with a snapshot of the Burgers’ solution
422
      that SWIM basis functions exhibit a rapid exponential decay of error with increasing network width,
423
      where Fourier and Chebyshev basis functions used in classical spectral methods suffer from the Gibbs
424   phenomenon Gottlieb & Shu (1997) (See Appendix C.4.1).

514   5    C ONCLUSION
515
516   To address the fundamental difficulties stemming from the gradient-based iterative optimization
517   of neural network parameters, we propose a backpropagation-free algorithm for training neural
518   PDE solvers by combining ideas of separation of variables and random sampling of hidden layer
519
      parameters.
520   Benefits of our method: Firstly, we demonstrate that our backpropagation-free algorithm for training
521   neural PDE solvers is 2 to 30, 000 times faster and, at the same time, 10 to 100, 000 times more
522   accurate than the physics-informed neural networks trained with backpropagation for the PDEs
523   considered in this paper. Secondly, by leveraging classical ODE solvers with adaptive time-stepping,
524   we demonstrate that our neural PDE solver can capture high-frequency temporal dynamics and can
525
      solve over long time spans, where traditional state-of-the-art neural-PDE solvers fail. Thirdly, our
      approach reduces the accuracy gap with mesh-based solvers while retaining advantages like mesh-free
526
      basis functions, ease of implementation, ability to handle complex domains, and spectral convergence
527
      for PDEs with smooth solutions. Finally, we show that our approach can solve high-dimensional
528   PDEs efficiently and accurately, as illustrated by the ten-dimensional heat equation.
529
530   Limitations and future work: Our approach requires knowledge of the PDE, so grey-box settings
531
      and inverse problems, where parts of the PDE must be estimated, are challenging. However, the much
      faster time-to-solution of our approach should prove very useful for this inverse problem setting.
532
      Compared to neural PDE solvers trained with backpropagation, our networks may require more
533
      neurons for the same accuracy, leading to higher inference times. Universal approximation properties
534   concerning specific PDE settings and understanding the role of re-sampling network parameters in
535   overcoming the Kolmogorov n-width barrier Peherstorfer (2022) are some of the important theoretical
536   open areas of investigation. Lastly, solving high-dimensional PDEs with complicated solutions is still
537   an interesting avenue to explore further, where traditional numerical methods have many difficulties
538   due to the curse of dimensionality. We hope that our approach can open doors for neural PDE solvers
539   to deal with real-world applications in science and engineering, especially applications where limited
      accuracy and long training times have been the main reasons for the lack of success.

       A     E XTENDED R EVIEW OF R ELATED W ORK
973
974    Spectral methods for solving PDEs promise fast convergence with much fewer basis functions.
975    Meuris et al. (2023) present a method to extract hierarchical spatial basis functions from a trained
976    DeepONet and employ it in a spectral method to solve the given PDE. Xia et al. (2023) integrate
977    adaptive techniques into PINN-based PDE solvers to obtain numerical solutions of unbounded domain
978    problems that standard PINNs cannot efficiently approximate. Lange et al. (2021) propose spectral
979    methods that fit linear and nonlinear oscillators to data and facilitate long-term forecasting of temporal
980    signals. Dresdner et al. (2022) demonstrate spectral solvers that provide sub-grid corrections to
981    classical spectral methods to improve their accuracy. Du et al. (2023) use fixed orthogonal bases to
982
       learn PDE solutions as a map between spectral coefficients and introduce a training strategy based on
       spectral loss. These methods differ from ours in problem setting, architecture, and training.
983
984    Neural operator frameworks (cf. Lu et al. (2021a); Kovachki et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020); Pfaff et al.
985    (2021)) are promising but are typically trained with PDE solutions with different initial conditions,
986    spatial domains (geometries), or parameter settings. Instead, in our setting here, we solve the PDE
987    using given coefficients, domain, and initial conditions without relying on any training data. The ease
988
       of implementation, rapid training, and high accuracy of our backpropagation-free approach can be
       leveraged to generate PDE solution data for training operator networks.
989
990    Mesh-free methods are typically based on radial basis functions (RBFs, cf. Powell (1992); Chen
991    et al. (2014)) or moving least squares (MLS, cf. Shepard (1968); Lancaster & Salkauskas (1981)).
992    These often do not have user-friendly software or are only applicable in specialized settings (e.g.,
993    smoothed particle hydrodynamics, cf. Lucy (1977); Gingold & Monaghan (1977); Shadloo et al.
994
       (2016)). Moreover, despite the ease of dealing with complicated geometries, these methods typically
       suffer from many challenges, such as the choice of kernel, imposing boundary conditions, and
995
       convergence issues. These methods are not the focus of this work.

       B.1.3    E XTENDED D ISCUSSION OF OUR METHOD
1135
1136
1137   Kolmogorov n-width barrier: Without resampling the internal network parameters, our method
1138   faces the Kolmogorov n-width barrier Peherstorfer (2022); Du & Zaki (2021); Berman & Peherstorfer
1139   (2024); Kast & Hesthaven (2024) because our basis functions are not time-dependent. However,
1140   resampling basis functions at certain time points of the SWIM-ODE (as done in the Burgers’ equation
1141   in Section 4.4) results in a solution- and time-dependent basis approximation of the solution manifold
1142   and, thus, in theory, can break the barrier.
1143   PINNs can theoretically break the Kolmogorov n-width barrier as time is treated as an extra spatial
1144   dimension, and internal network parameters are time-dependent. However, for PINNs, the optimiza-
1145   tion issues pose much more severe challenges even on very simple PDEs and in low dimensions
1146   (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; 2022). So even though our vanilla SWIM-ODE/ELM-
1147   ODE approach (without periodically resampling hidden layer weights) faces the Kolmogorov n-width
1148   barrier, we outperform PINNs, typically by several orders of accuracy and time in practice.
1149   Influence of random sampling on the method: Similar to the question of how PINNs trained with
1150   Adam/SGD perform based on their random network initialization, understanding the influence of
1151   weights on the output is a challenge. There are two main differences between (stochastic) gradient
1152   optimization and our setting. First, after fixing the internal weights, we use regularized least-squares
1153   (not a stochastic method) to fit the initial condition. Second, we do not use a stochastic method
1154   to solve over time. Therefore, even though PINNs can adapt their random initialization over the
1155
       gradient-based optimization, precisely that optimization also adds stochasticity. If the number of
       neurons for the model increases, the randomness in our case decreases because the regularized
1156
       least-squares fit to the initial condition (which converges to a single solution in the limit of many
1157
       neurons), while stochastic gradient descent will only converge to a distribution (because of mini-batch
1158   optimization). This has been observed for the supervised learning problems in Bolager et al. (2023),
1159   particularly in the transfer learning experiments. In Table 2, we observe that our model’s performance
1160   is often orders of magnitude better, and the variance is on the same scale as the magnitude.
1161
       Comparison between SWIM-ODE and ELM-ODE: One of the main factors influencing the
1162
       performance of SWIM-ODE and ELM-ODE is the underlying solution of the PDE.
1163
1164   We explain, with an example of the Burgers’ equation, how the SWIM sampling can be leveraged
1165   when the solution has steep gradients, as one can sample localized basis functions in the part of the
1166   domain where the solution has steep gradients. For ELM, the probability of sampling steep basis
1167
       functions with the vanilla ELM is lower, as illustrated in the Figure 2 of the paper. Even if one
       uses a different distribution to sample the network parameters such that more basis functions with
1168
       steep gradients are sampled, placing the basis functions at appropriate spatial locations is another
1169
       challenge. With ELM, one cannot resample or choose basis functions using data as it is data-agnostic.
1170   Thus, especially if the solution has localized steep gradients, the performance of ELM is typically
1171   worse compared to SWIM. We additionally demonstrate with a snapshot of the Burgers’ solution
1172   that SWIM basis functions exhibit a rapid exponential decay of error with increasing network width,
1173   where ELM, Fourier, and Chebyshev basis functions used in classical spectral methods suffer from
1174   the Gibbs phenomenon (see Appendix C.4.1) and lead to poor scaling and accuracy (see Figure 15,
1175   Figure 16).
1176   If the underlying solution is sufficiently smooth and does not have steep gradients anywhere in the
1177   domain, ELM typically performs very well, as seen in the example with the Advection equation
1178   (see Section 4.1), Euler Bernoulli equation (see Section 4.2), and the effect is most apparent in the
1179   newly added example of high-dimensional PDEs (Section 4.5), where ELM-ODE performs much
1180   better than SWIM-ODE as shown in Table 22. While we just use the vanilla SWIM algorithm in the
1181   presented results, one can easily adapt the algorithm and, after sampling the network parameters with
1182   SWIM, multiply the basis functions with a tunable scaling factor before applying the non-linearity to
1183   sample many more basis functions with shallow slopes.
1184   Thus, the choice between the two strategies is particularly governed by the underlying solution of
1185   the PDE. Apart from the favorable cases for each method mentioned above, both methods have
1186   comparable performance and typically outperform PINNs by several orders of magnitude in speed
1187   and time. Thus, the rapid training of our approach could be leveraged to try out both approaches if
       one has no information about what the solution of the PDE could look like.

       A discussion on “data-driven” and “data-agnostic” sampling: We emphasize what we mean by
1189   data-driven sampling: our data are random pairs of collocation points, but we do not have access to
1190   the solution function values (because, in the beginning, we have not solved the PDE yet). Thus, even
1191   though we do not have access to the true solution of the PDE, we call this "data-driven" sampling
1192   because we create the parameters of our basis functions (neurons) so that they are centered strictly
1193   within the domain. We achieve this by using data points sampled in the domain, thereby considering
1194   the geometry and bounds of the spatial domain. Note that with data-agnostic sampling in ELM, the
1195   neurons can easily be centered outside the spatial domain because weights and biases are chosen
1196   without considering any information about the geometry and bounds of the spatial domain. To
1197
       summarize, though our algorithm proposes "data-driven" sampling, we do not start with time-series
       data and instead work in a self-supervised setting.

       to unify the fields of computer-aided design (CAD) and finite element analysis (FEA). The key idea
1256

1257

1258   B-Splines (NURBS) (cf. Piegl & Tiller (1997)).
1259

1261

1922   We also perform an ablation study on the SVD layer. To quantify the compression in width after
1923   the SVD layer, we define a compression ratio as Cr = Mrs , where Ms is the width of the (sampled)
1924   hidden layer before the SVD layer (see Figure 3), and r is the width of the SVD layer. We define a
1925   speed-up in computation time as s = TTno-svd
                                              svd
                                                    as the ratio of computational time without the SVD layer
1926   to the time required with the SVD layer.
1927   The results of the ablation study for the SVD layer with the high-dimensional diffusion equations
1928   demonstrate that for ELM-ODE, the SVD layer results in substantial speed-ups for 3, 5, and 7 dimen-
1929   sional heat equations - by factors of 52, 77, and 21 respectively. We observe that the compression
1930   ratios achieved with the SVD layer are also substantial 22.8, 5, and 1.2, for dimensions 3, 5, and 7,
1931   respectively. For the 10-dimensional diffusion equation, to cover the high-dimensional space, we
1932   observe a (relatively lower compared to other dimensions) compression ratio of 1.4, as more basis
1933   functions are required to represent functions in high dimensions accurately. Thus, the time required
1934
       with the SVD layer is around 94 percent of the time required without the SVD layer. In all the cases,
       the loss is always in the same order as the one without the SVD layer.
1935
1936   Note that in all cases, the extra cost of computing the SVD easily pays off by substantially saving time
1937   in the ODE solver for ELM-ODE. This is because of the improved conditioning of the feature matrix
1938   and the reduction in the size of the ODE system to be solved. With SWIM-ODE, the observations are
1939   similar but with lower compression ratios and speed-ups. But, for this problem, SWIM-ODE is not
1940
       the preferred method, as the underlying solution is smooth, has low-frequency spatial variations, and
       does not have steep gradients anywhere in the domain. Thus, SWIM basis functions are not optimal
1941
       in the vanilla setting. See Appendix B.1.3 for details on this.
