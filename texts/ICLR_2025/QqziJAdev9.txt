009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values and intentions is
012            crucial for their utility, honesty, and safety. Reinforcement learning from hu-
013            man feedback (RLHF) is a popular approach to achieve this alignment, but it
014            faces challenges in computational efficiency and training stability. Recent meth-
015            ods like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and Simple Preference Optimiza-
016            tion (SimPO) have proposed offline alternatives to RLHF, simplifying the pro-
017            cess by reparameterizing the reward function. However, DPO depends on a po-
018
               tentially suboptimal reference model, and SimPO’s assumption of a fixed target
               reward margin may lead to suboptimal decisions in diverse data settings. In
019
               this work, we propose α-DPO, an adaptive preference optimization algorithm
020
               designed to address these limitations by introducing a dynamic reward margin.
021            Specifically, α-DPO employs an adaptive preference distribution, balancing the
022            policy model and the reference model to achieve personalized reward margins.
023            We provide theoretical guarantees for α-DPO, demonstrating its effectiveness as
024            a surrogate optimization objective and its ability to balance alignment and di-
025            versity through KL divergence control. Empirical evaluations on AlpacaEval 2
026            and Arena-Hard show that α-DPO consistently outperforms DPO and SimPO
027            across various model settings, establishing it as a robust approach for fine-tuning
028            LLMs. Our method achieves significant improvements in win rates, highlight-
029
               ing its potential as a powerful tool for LLM alignment. The code is available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/alpha-DPO-E0D3.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Learning from human feedback is essential for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human
      values and intentions (Leike et al., 2018), ensuring they are helpful, honest, and harmless (Askell
036
      et al., 2021). Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang
037
      et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020) is a widely used method for fine-tuning LLMs to achieve this goal.
038   However, RLHF faces challenges, particularly in computational efficiency and training stability due
039   to its multi-stage process. Recently, alternative offline algorithms like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023)
040   and SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) have been explored. Specifically, DPO reparameterizes the reward
041   function in RLHF to directly learn a policy model (πθ ) from preference data, removing the need for
042   an explicit reward model. Building on DPO, SimPO further simplifies the process by eliminating
043   the need for a reference model (πref ), and introduces a target reward margin γ to enlarge the distance
044   between the response pair, thereby achieving strong performance. This naturally raises the question:
045
                         Do we really need a reference model in the alignment process?
046
047
      Motivated by this question, we examine SimPO: it can be viewed as a variant of DPO where the
048   original reference model πref is effectively replaced by an implicit reference model π̂ref . In SimPO,
049   the target reward margin γ actually reflects a constant difference between the log likelihoods of a
050   selected response and a rejected one, i.e., (log π̂ref (yw |x)−log π̂ref (yl |x)). As the constant difference
051   γ is independent of arbitrary responses, this implicitly assumes a uniform reference distribution
052   (cf. Figure 1). By tuning γ, SimPO effectively finds an “ideal” uniform implicit reference model,
053   leading to substantial performance improvements over standard DPO, particularly when the original
      reference model πref is suboptimal (Hong et al., 2024).

072   varied training data, especially in domains with diverse preferences or complex reward structures
073   (Morimura et al., 2024; He et al., 2024).
074   To address these limitations of SimPO, we propose an adaptive preference distribution, which leads
075   to an adaptive reward margin for different response pairs. We term this simple yet effective pref-

                                                                                                             α

079   adjusts the balance between the policy model πθ and the reference model πref to achieve a person-

081

      offers several intriguing theoretical insights:
082
083   • Theoretical Guarantee via Lower Bound: We prove that α-DPO’s objective serves as a lower
084     bound on the online SimPO loss. This theoretical guarantee justifies the use of α-DPO as a surro-
085     gate optimization objective and ensures that optimizing it leads to improved policy performance
086     and generalization.
087   • Balancing Alignment and Diversity: We demonstrate that α-DPO balances alignment and di-
088     versity via KL divergence control. By approximating the sequential KL divergence between the
089     policy and the reference model, α-DPO achieves computational efficiency and robustness, partic-
090     ularly when the reference model is not well-calibrated at the token level.
091
092   Extensive analysis indicates that α-DPO leverages preference data more effectively by assigning per-
093   sonalized margins to each pair, resulting in an improved policy model. As demonstrated in Figure 1,
094
      our method consistently outperforms DPO and SimPO across three base model settings (Mistral2-
      7B, Llama3-8B, and Gemma2-9B) on AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard (cf. Section 5). Notably, we
095
      achieve a 58.7 length-controlled win rate on AlpacaEval 2, and a 35.7 win rate on Arena-Hard,
096
      establishing it as the strongest 8B open-source model to date.

      3.2   P ROPOSED M ETHOD : α-DPO
187
188   Our analysis highlights the significant impact of the reference model in preference optimization. To
189   overcome the limitations identified in both DPO and SimPO, we propose the following principles:
190
      Principle 1: The reference model should contribute to differentiating between preferred and less
191
      preferred responses.
192
193   Principle 2: The reference model should adapt to discrepancies between response pairs to capture
194   instance-specific nuances.
195   Principle 1 addresses the shortcoming in DPO, where the reference model may inadequately distin-
196   guish between yw and yl , introducing uncertainty without a guaranteed margin. Principle 2 rectifies
197   the oversimplification in SimPO, where the absence of a reference model fails to account for vari-
198   ability across different instances.

461
          Outstanding Performance and Lower KL. As noted in Rafailov et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2024), it
462
          is crucial to consider both performance and KL divergence when comparing algorithms. A slightly
463       higher win rate accompanied by a significantly higher KL divergence is often not desirable. In line
464       with the design principles of TDPO, we implemented SimPO and α-DPO. Figure 3a 3b presents the
465       KL divergence curves. The results indicate that as α increases, the KL divergence of α-DPO remains
466       stable or even decreases slightly when compared to SimPO. This demonstrates that α-DPO not only
467       achieves superior performance but also maintains a lower KL divergence, indicating a better balance
468       between alignment and control of KL divergence during the training process.
469       Mitigating Over-Optimization. Over-optimization, as described by Gao et al. (2023) and Rafailov
470       et al. (2024), refers to a phenomenon where model performance exhibits a hump-shaped pattern
471       across different targets: beyond an optimal point, further increasing the KL budget results in dimin-
472       ishing returns. To investigate this, we evaluate SimPO and α-DPO at four intermediate checkpoints,
473       corresponding to different KL budgets. As illustrated in Figure 3c, it is intriguing that while the per-
474       formance of our approach does decrease with increasing KL budget, the decline is relatively modest.
475       This indicates that our method effectively mitigates the issue of over-optimization.

493   6   R ELATED W ORK
494
495   Reinforcement learning from human feedback. RLHF is a technique that aligns large language
496   models with human preferences and values (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang
497   et al., 2022; Azar et al., 2023). Traditional RLHF can be divided into three stages: supervised fine-
498
      tuning (Zhou et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Geng et al., 2023; Conover et al., 2023; Köpf et al.,
      2023; Ding et al., 2023), reward modeling (Gao et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b;
499
      Lightman et al., 2023; Havrilla et al., 2024; Lambert et al., 2024), and policy optimization (Schul-
500
      man et al., 2017; Anthony et al., 2017). In the third stage, Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
501   is a widely used algorithm. Additionally, Xiong et al. (2023) proposed efficient algorithms for the
502   reverse-KL regularized contextual bandit framework in RLHF. Ye et al. (2024) introduced provably
503   efficient algorithms for KL-regularized Nash-Learning from Human Feedback (NLHF). Further-
504   more, Ji et al. (2024b) developed an active-query-based PPO algorithm with specific regret bounds
505   and query complexity.
506
      Offline direct preference optimization. Several alternative preference optimization objectives have
507   been proposed in addition to DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023). IPO (Azar et al., 2023) addresses the over-
508   fitting issues associated with DPO. ORPO (Hong et al., 2024) and SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) aim
509   to eliminate the dependence on a reference model. R-DPO (Park et al., 2024) focuses on mitigating
510   exploitation based on sequence length. KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) deals with preference opti-
511   mization when data are not pairwise. CPO (Xu et al., 2024) and β-DPO(Wu et al., 2024) emphasize
512   the quality of preference data. Another line of research explores comparisons among more than two
513   instances (Dong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Song et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023).
514   Online direct preference optimization. Offline direct preference optimization methods are sim-
515   ple but rely on preference data collected offline. RLHF methods interact online with the language
516   model being aligned but require policy gradients. In contrast, online direct preference optimiza-
517   tion methods combine the advantages of both approaches. Yuan et al. (2024) proposed a “self-
518   rewarding” approach in which the policy being aligned provides online feedback to itself. Alterna-
519   tively, OAIF (Guo et al., 2024) is a novel online preference optimization method that can leverage
520   feedback from any LLM, including those stronger than the LLM being aligned. Swamy et al. (2024)
521
      also concurrently investigate the importance of online preference but still rely on reward models
      (RMs). SELMA (Zhang et al., 2024) improves exploration efficiency by selectively favoring re-
522
      sponses with high potential rewards rather than indiscriminately sampling unseen responses.
523

525   7   D ISCUSSION
526
527
      Conclusion. We proposed α-DPO, an adaptive preference optimization method that improves LLM
528   alignment by introducing a dynamic reward margin based on instance-specific differences. α-DPO
529   addresses limitations in previous methods like DPO and SimPO by balancing alignment and di-
530   versity through KL divergence control. Our theoretical guarantees and empirical results show that
531   α-DPO consistently outperforms baselines on benchmarks like AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard, with
532   significant improvements in win rates, establishing it as a robust solution for LLM fine-tuning.
533
      Limitations and Future Work. While α-DPO enhances performance, it introduces an additional
534   hyperparameter, α, requiring manual tuning. Future work could focus on developing an adaptive ap-
535   proach to automatically adjust this parameter. Additionally, although we show α-DPO’s theoretical
536   equivalence to online methods, it remains an offline approach. Extending it to online learning would
537   allow real-time adaptation, broadening its application in interactive environments. Lastly, we ob-
538   served that different benchmarks, such as AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard, require distinct parameter
539   settings for optimal performance. Investigating a more generalized approach that adapts effectively
      across multiple benchmarks would further improve the model’s versatility.

      • Utility Theory Perspective: The       π̂ref (y|x) is designed with the uniform distribution

                                                 (y|x)    dynamically adjusts the reward margin by balancing

957     found in DPO or SimPO. In the SimPO framework, the gradient is expressed as:

                               β                       β

                              |yl |                  |yw |

        the additional αM (x, yw , yl ) component increases weight when the reward estimate is accurate,
967
        ensuring a more robust reward signal.

      • Motivational Core The central goal of the proposed α-DPO is to address the unreliability of the
        reference policy, as outlined in Section 3.1. By integrating the policy model into the reference
970
        model design, the quality of the reference model is enhanced, improving fine-tuning performance.
971
        Similar concepts have been explored in recent works (Gorbatovski et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b).
