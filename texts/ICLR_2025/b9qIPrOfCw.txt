                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Video-based pose estimation plays a critical role in understanding human actions
010            and enabling effective human-computer interaction. By exploiting temporal infor-
011            mation from video frames, it enhances the localization of human keypoints. Previ-
012            ous feature-fusion methods often rely on a frozen single-frame backbone trained
013            on individual frames, followed by a network to learn temporal information from
014            video sequences. Consequently, these approaches fail to capture the temporal
015            continuity between frames at the backbone network level, thereby restricting the
016            network’s capacity to effectively learn and leverage sequential information. In this
017            paper, we introduce a novel approach to supervise continuity in the whole video
018
               pose estimation model from two perspectives: semantic continuity and pixel-wise
               keypoint distribution continuity. To this end, we propose a Semantic Alignment
019
               Space, where a semantic alignment encodes feature maps from different frames
020
               into this space, ensuring continuous supervision of the encoded representations.
021            To further maintain pixel-wise keypoint distribution continuity, we introduce the
022            Trajectory Probability Difference Integration method, which minimizes the trajec-
023            tory difference expectation across frames. Additionally, to better capture temporal
024            dependencies, we present a Multi-frame Heatmap Fusion structure that aggregates
025            heatmaps from adjacent frames for a more refined output. Extensive experiments
026            on the PoseTrack17, PoseTrack18, and PoseTrack21 datasets demonstrate the ef-
027            fectiveness of our approach, consistently achieving state-of-the-art results.

049   Human pose estimation, a fundamental task in computer vision, aims to accurately predict the co-
050
      ordinates of keypoints corresponding to different parts of an individual’s body. This technique has
      garnered significant attention in recent years due to its diverse applications, including action recog-
051
      nition, human-computer interaction, and motion analysis.
052
053   The task of video pose estimation focuses on predicting the human keypoint coordinates within a se-
      quence of video frames. Capturing temporal information embedded in the video frames is therefore


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      crucial for precise pose estimation over time. To address this, existing approaches utilize Recur-
055   rent Neural Networks (RNNs) or 3D convolutional networks to process video sequences Luo et al.
056   (2018); Wang et al. (2020). However, these methods are often computationally expensive and yield
057   suboptimal performance. Alternatively, techniques such as optical flow Pfister et al. (2015); Song
058   et al. (2017) have been employed to compute additional flow data, but they tend to be inaccurate,
059   especially in the presence of motion blur. More recent methods Feng et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2022;
060   2021) lean towards multi-frame feature fusion. In this approach, a pre-trained backbone network
061   extracts features from individual frames, and a dedicated fusion network subsequently integrates
062   the temporal context. However, this method has a significant limitation: the backbone, trained on
063
      isolated frames, is unable to exploit the temporal consistency inherent in video sequences. Conse-
      quently, the feature fusion process is disconnected from the backbone, which hampers the model’s
064
      ability to fully capture and utilize the temporal dynamics of human motion. Additionally, due to
065
      the lack of supervision regarding frame-to-frame continuity, these techniques often result in pose
066   discontinuities, reducing the model’s overall performance.
067
068   To overcome these limitations, as shown in figure 1, we propose a novel framework that enforces
069
      temporal continuity for the whole video-based pose estimation models from two complementary
      perspectives: semantic continuity and pixel-wise keypoint distribution continuity. These aspects are
070
      critical for ensuring smooth pose transitions and preventing abrupt, unrealistic changes in keypoint
071
      locations between frames. Unlike previous approaches that only supervise the feature fusion process
072   after freezing the backbone, we supervise the entire video pose estimation network by leveraging
073   the inherent temporal continuity in the video sequence. This allows the backbone to learn temporal
074   information alongside the feature fusion network.
075
      More specifically, semantic continuity refers to the smooth, consistent representation of human poses
076
      despite changes in viewpoint, body configuration, or camera motion. While keypoint locations may
077
      shift dramatically across frames, the underlying semantic meaning of human actions remains con-
078   sistent. For example, in figure 1 (c), due to viewpoint transformations and human motion, the
079   coordinates of actions 1, 2, and 3 in consecutive frames are not continuous. The position of action 2
080   occurs before that of action 1, while the position of action 3 occurs after action 1. However, seman-
081   tically, actions 1, 2, and 3 form a continuous sequence. To capture this, we introduce the Semantic
082   Alignment Space, which encodes feature representations from different frames into a shared, pose-
083   invariant latent space. This space ensures that semantically similar frames remain close in represen-
084   tation, even if keypoint positions vary. We apply contrastive loss to pull together the representations
085   of adjacent frames while pushing apart those of distant frames, thereby preserving semantic continu-
086
      ity throughout the sequence. To ensure position invariance in this space, we apply transformations
      such as rotation and scaling and supervise the model to produce consistent results before and af-
087
      ter the transformation. Since the semantic space is pose-invariant, we also use another encoder to
088
      capture positional transformations explicitly. A decoder then combines the semantic and positional
089   encodings to generate keypoint probability heatmaps for the video sequence.
090
091   Beyond semantic continuity, we also supervise the continuity of keypoint probability distributions
092
      by the pixel continuity of frames, because it is noticed that the pixel differences for the same key-
      point between consecutive frames can be considered approximately invariant, given that lighting
093
      conditions are typically stable over short periods. To this end, we introduce the Trajectory Probabil-
094
      ity Difference Integration method. As shown in figure 1 (c), since the model generates probability
095   distributions for keypoints, we can calculate the expected difference for each keypoint across frames
096   using keypoint distributions. By minimizing the integral of the keypoint trajectory probability differ-
097   ences of frame sequence, we ensure smoother and more accurate keypoint predictions across video.
098   Notably, while optical flow methods Pfister et al. (2015); Song et al. (2017) also calculate pixel
099   differences between adjacent frames, they compute the optical flow field as input to the network to
100   capture temporal information. In contrast, our approach incorporates pixel differences into the loss
101   function to supervise the keypoint distribution in the video pose estimation network. Rather than
102   computing the difference for every pixel, we focus on positions with higher keypoint probability
103
      distributions. Furthermore, our method is only applied during training and does not require extra
      computation during inference, making it more efficient than optical flow-based approaches.
104
105   To further enhance the temporal modeling, we propose a Multi-frame Heatmap Fusion module.
106   This mechanism aggregates heatmaps from adjacent frames by alternately using spatial attention
107   and temporal self-attention, creating a refined output that incorporates temporal context. By fusing

108
      information across multiple frames, the model generates more stable and accurate keypoint predic-
109   tions, improving overall pose estimation performance.
110
111   Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets, including PoseTrack17, PoseTrack18, and Pose-
112
      Track21, demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. Our method consistently outperforms
      state-of-the-art models, showcasing its ability to better capture temporal dependencies and deliver
113
      coherent pose estimations across video frames.
114
115   In summary, our main contributions are outlined as follows:
116
117          • We identify two critical types of continuity for video pose estimation: semantic space con-
118            tinuity and pixel-wise keypoint distribution continuity. To address these, we design a novel
119            approach that supervises both aspects within the pose estimation model. To ensure seman-
120            tic continuity across frames, we introduce the Semantic Alignment Space. Furthermore,
121            we propose the Trajectory Probability Difference Integration method to enforce smooth
122
               pixel-wise keypoint distribution continuity throughout the video sequence.
123
124
             • We propose a Multi-frame Heatmap Fusion module, which merges pose heatmap sequences
               to generate a new fusion heatmap, enhancing the model’s performance.

128   2     R ELATED W ORKS
129
130   2.1   I MAGE - BASED H UMAN P OSE E STIMATION .
131
132
      Image-based human pose estimation seeks to precisely infer the coordinates of key points on in-
133   dividuals within images. With the evolution of artificial neural networks, various deep-learning
134   approaches are employed for image-based human pose estimation. These methodologies can be
135   broadly categorized into two paradigms: bottom-up methods and top-down approaches.
136
      The bottom-up approach is proposed in Deepcut Pishchulin et al. (2016) and significantly improved
137
      in OpenPose Cao et al. (2017). These methods detect all human keypoints in an image at once and
138
      cluster them into persons. Most bottom-up methods Cao et al. (2017); Newell et al. (2017); Cheng
139   et al. (2020); Jin et al. (2022); Cai (2021) are based on the heatmap, and use Part Affinity Fields
140   for keypoint clustering. Alternatively, the top-down approaches Li et al. (2023); Xiao et al. (2018);
141   Chen et al. (2018); Newell et al. (2016); Sun et al. (2019); He et al. (2017); Kamel et al. (2020)
142   decompose multi-person pose estimation into two distinct stages. Initially, a human detector is
143   utilized to detect each individual within the image. Following this, the patches within the bounding
144   boxes produced by the human detector are cropped and sequentially input into the single-person
145   pose estimation network. Although this method introduces an additional processing step, compared
146   to the bottom-up approach, it typically exhibits a noticeable advantage in terms of performance.
147
148
149   2.2   V IDEO - BASED H UMAN P OSE E STIMATION .
150
151   Regarding video-based human pose estimation, early methods primarily relied on image-based ap-
152   proaches, which, unfortunately, fell short due to their inability to account for temporal dependencies
153
      between frames. Lately, optical flow-based strategies Song et al. (2017); Pfister et al. (2015) gen-
      erate optical flow between successive frames, leveraging these optical flows as motion indicators to
154
      enhance predicted pose heatmaps. However, such flow generation is computationally expensive and
155
      demonstrates vulnerability under significant image quality deterioration. Luo et al. (2018) uses RNN
156   to capture temporal and spatial information, directly predicting the keypoint heatmap sequences for
157   videos. A noteworthy alternative approach is the utilization of 3D Convolutional Neural Networks
158   (3DCNNs) Wang et al. (2020) or deformable convolution Liu et al. (2022) to integrate heatmaps
159   across frames, leading to improved heatmap quality. Some techniques Liu et al. (2022); Feng et al.
160   (2023a) also incorporate multi-frame feature fusion, thereby bolstering video pose estimation ac-
161   curacy. Recent advanced method Feng et al. (2023b) integrates transformer-based designs with
      diffusion models, evidencing substantial enhancements in pose estimation results.

163

      In this section, we provide an overview of our proposed methodology. First, we describe our ap-
165
      proach for enforcing continuity in video-based pose estimation through two complementary mecha-
166   nisms: semantic alignment and pixel-wise keypoint distribution continuity. We introduce the Seman-
167   tic Alignment Space to maintain semantic consistency across frames and the Trajectory Probability
168   Difference Integration method to enforce temporal continuity in the keypoint distribution heatmaps.
169
      After introducing the Trajectory Probability Difference Integration, we discuss the overall network
170
      architecture, including the Multi-frame Heatmap Fusion module, which effectively fuses informa-
171
      tion across frames to improve pose estimation.
172
173   Finally, we explain the training and loss function for our video pose estimation network.
174
175   3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
176
177   The challenge in video-based pose estimation lies in capturing temporal information embedded in
178   sequential frames, which can be used to enhance pose estimation accuracy over time. In contrast
179
      to image-based pose estimation, which processes isolated frames, video-based methods utilize a
      sequence of frames to model temporal dynamics.
180
181   We denote a sequence of consecutive frames around a target frame It as x = {It−T , . . . , It+T },
182   where T is the temporal window size. Our goal is to leverage these temporal dynamics to improve
183   the keypoint predictions at keyframe It . Following a Top-Down approach, we first apply a human
184   detector to each frame to extract bounding boxes for each detected person. These bounding boxes
                                                               p              p
185
      are used to form a personalized input sequence xp = {It−T    , . . . , It+T } for each individual. This
186
      personalized sequence is then passed into our model to predict the keypoint coordinates in Itp .

192

268   3.4   N ETWORK ARCHITECTURE
269
      In this subsection, we introduce the architecture of our video pose estimation network.


                                                              5

270
      As depicted in Figure 2, the network begins by applying a single-frame backbone to extract features
271   from each individual frame in the video sequence. These features are then encoded into the pro-
272   posed Semantic Alignment Space. To effectively capture temporal information, a Heatmap Fusion
273   module is employed, which fuses the temporal context from multiple frames to produce the final
274   pose estimation.
275
      Given the success of transformer-based architectures in various computer vision tasks, we adopt the
276
      Vision Transformer (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) as the backbone of our network. When process-
277   ing an input image of a human X = It , it is initially transformed into tokens via a Patch Embedding
278   layer(PE). These tokens are subsequently passed through several transformer layers, each composed
279   of a multi-head self-attention (MHSA) mechanism followed by a feed-forward network (FFN):
280
281
                                                ′
282                            F0 = P E(X), Fn+1 = Fn + M HSA(LN (Fn )).                                 (8)
283
284
285   We input a sequence of video frames It−i to It+i into the single-frame pose estimation network,
286   obtaining the corresponding feature maps Ft−i to Ft+i . These feature maps are then passed into
287
      the semantic alignment encoder EZ , resulting in the semantic alignment encoding sequence Zt−i to
      Zt+i , and the transformation position encodings St−i to St+i . Finally, these two components are
288
      passed through the heatmap decoder to generate the keypoint probability heatmaps Ht−i to Ht+i
289
      for each frame in the sequence.
290
291   To refine the pose estimation at the keyframe t, we introduce a Heatmap Fusion module, depicted in
292   Figure 2. This module alternates between two types of self-attention layers: a temporal self-attention
293
      layer that fuses information across multiple frames, and a spatial self-attention layer that captures
      spatial dependencies within each frame. The resulting fused representation is passed through a
294
      convolutional layer (the heatmap head), which produces the final keyframe heatmap Ht∗ .

382   4.1.1    DATASETS
383
384   The PoseTrack benchmark has played a pivotal role in advancing video-based human pose estima-
385   tion. PoseTrack17 Andriluka et al. (2018) comprises 250 training video sequences and 50 validation
386   sequences, yielding a total of 80, 144 pose annotations following the standard protocol. This dataset
387
      includes 15 keypoints for each annotation, complemented by a joint visibility flag. The subsequent
      release, PoseTrack18 Andriluka et al. (2018), significantly expands the dataset, featuring 593 train-
388
      ing and 170 validation sequences with 153, 615 pose annotations. The latest iteration, PoseTrack21
389
      Doering et al. (2022b), builds on the prior version by enhancing the pose annotations, particularly
390   for smaller individuals and those in crowded scenes, resulting in 177, 164 total pose annotations.
391   Notably, PoseTrack21 refines the joint visibility flag, incorporating more detailed occlusion infor-
392   mation to improve pose estimation accuracy.
393
394   4.1.2    E VALUATION M ETRIC
395
396   We utilize mean Average Precision (mAP) as the primary evaluation metric for pose estimation. The
397   AP is computed for each keypoint, followed by averaging over all keypoints to derive the final mAP
398   score.
399
400   4.1.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
401
      In alignment with previous top-down pose estimation methods Xiao et al. (2018); Wei et al. (2016),
402
      each individual is first cropped based on their bounding box during preprocessing. Consistent with
403   common practice Liu et al. (2021; 2022), the cropping area is expanded by 25% beyond the bounding
404   box to include contextual information.
405
406
      For data augmentation, we apply Random Flip, Half Body Transform, and Random Scale Rotation
      during training. The AdamW optimizer is used, initialized with a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 .
407
408   During training, we employ the ViT-B architecture as the backbone for single-frame feature extrac-
409   tion, using an input resolution of 256 × 192. Optimization is conducted with AdamW, starting with
410   a learning rate of 1 × 10−3 . The temporal span T for the input frame sequence is set to 2. We
411   initialize the backbone with pre-trained weights from the MS-COCO dataset and train the network
412
      for 100 epochs.
413   For comparison with non-transformer-based approaches, we train an additional version of our model
414   using the HRNetW48 backbone, a well-established architecture for pose estimation. Like the ViT-B
415   backbone, HRNetW48 is initialized with MS-COCO pre-trained weights, and we ensure consistent
416   training settings to enable a fair comparison between the two backbones.

497
498   In this section, we conduct a series of ablation experiments to analyze the contribution of various

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we have presented a continuity-driven approach for video-based human pose estima-
534   tion that improves temporal coherence in keypoint detection across frames. Unlike previous meth-
535   ods, our approach supervises the entire pipeline to ensure both semantic and pixel-wise keypoint
536   continuity. We proposed the Semantic Alignment Space for aligning semantic information across
537   frames and the Trajectory Probability Difference Integration method to ensure smoother keypoint
538   transitions. Our Multi-frame Heatmap Fusion further refines predictions by leveraging informa-
539   tion from adjacent frames. Experiments on PoseTrack datasets show that our method consistently
      outperforms state-of-the-art techniques, enhancing pose estimation accuracy and robustness.
