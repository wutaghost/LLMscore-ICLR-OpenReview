031   Since Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Natural Language Processing (NLP) technologies are now set
032   to be prevalent in the healthcare sector, it is anticipated in this paper that Electronic Health Records
033   (EHR) systems will serve as useful instruments for storing information on the patients. Nonetheless,
034   the current application of AI technology in the EHR systems does not come without limitations such
035   as lack of data quality, bias inside the systems and ethical approaches to providing the technology.
036   These constraints not only restrict the clinical utility of AI but also increase health inequities as well
037   as a scepticism towards AI.
038   Another limitation is the phenomenon of the patients not being at the center of care, providing
039   generic approaches and insufficient focusing for patients’ needs. The current models of AI continue
040   to incorporate a one-size-fits-all approach whereby strategies and plans advanced to the patients
041   do not consider their unique features such as their past illnesses. Although individualization is
042   important in making medical decisions, the current analysis models are too simplistic that even with
043   all the intention to individualize the outputs, nothing is changed – they continue churning out the
044
      same outcome no matter the patient’s condition or history (Honavar, 2020).
045   Moreover, insufficient appropriate procedures and complexity if archived data in EHR systems is
046   a major drawback. Due to the target population’s frequently presented reluctance to participate in
047   future clinical trials, complete EHR data and histories are typically not available. This further delays
048   the diagnosis and treatment of patients and raises the risk of diagnostic errors where doctors fail to
049   manage the scarcity of time and the enormous load of data (Weiskopf & Weng, 2013).
050   Apart from these outcomes common in every clinical practice, the challenges of bias embedded
051   in AI models is rather tremendous. The healthcare history reflected in EHR dataset is also fairly

      3.2   D ESIGNING OF N EW NLP M ODEL
201
202
      Upstash Vector is an advanced vector database that can scale to address real-time indexing and
203
      retrieval of big data. Upstash Vector is different from traditional keyword or relational databases
204   that require searches based on exact keywords since it’s built using vector embeddings of data that
205   exist in a conceptual space. Upstash Vector offers a vector database that enables scalable similarity
206   searches across millions of vectors, complete with features such as namespaces, metadata filtering,
207   and built-in embedding models (Dumandag, M, 2024). Thus the system can perform a search even
208   if some of the words are replaced with synonyms referring to the same concept and the meaning is
209   not affected.
210   Upstash Vector works by embedding data into high-dimensional vectors. Due to the nature of
211   the data, there are relationships between different data points. These vectors are then housed in
212   a database and compared based upon certain distance calculations such as cosine similarity to as-
213   certain how the records relate with each other. When prompting the system, various measurements
214   termed as vectors are retrieved by the query vector and this happens regardless even When prompt-
215   ing the system, various measurements termed as vectors are retrieved by the query vector and this
      happens regardless even if the exact keywords are not used, the meaningfully related data is returned.

      Within our NLP model, Upstash Vector plays a crucial role in ensuring real-time, context-aware
217   data retrieval. Medical data is often characterized by diverse terms used for similar conditions, for
218   example heart attack and myocardial infarction. Ordinary systems that rely on keywords might
219   not capture some records and even if they do, they are likely to default to skimming the surface
220   of the information. That is why Upstash Vector is effective in retrieving all relevant information
221   since it evaluates the relationship that exists between different terminologies. This guarantees that
222   the doctors put across all the relevant information regarding the patients, therefore, minimizing the
223   chances of erroneous diagnosis owing to scanty information. Such real time indexing also means
224   that data such as that of the patient can be written and accessed quickly which is important where
225
      treatment needs to be given quickly.
226   BGE-M3 is a stunning transformer variant which has been particularly significant for building se-
227   mantic sentence embeddings. BGE-M3 embedding captures the context and meaning of sentences
228   and/or phrases and relies on their contextual rather than their literal word usage to categorize and
229   rank sentences. Quite notably, BGE-M3 is applied on transformer structures that are dominant in
230   the area of NLP such as sentence classification, sentence retrieval, and sentence clustering.
231   BGE-M3 embeddings are high dimensional vectors that capture the information contained in the
232   text. It incorporates a transformer, which means that even when output is generated word by word,
233   words are never treated in isolation but in their context to other words. Because the model is exposed
234   to a huge number of data points, it is able to perform well on a very wide range of languages.
235   Thereafter these embeddings can be effectively utilized for semantically comparing and retrieving
236   sentences which are not identical but are using different lexical items.
237   BGE-M3 in our NLP model deals with the understanding and interpretation of clinical data. Dif-
238   ferent doctors will prefer different terms to describe the same medical concept. For example, ”renal
239   insufficiency” and ”kidney failure” can be used interchangeably, but keyword-based systems may
240   simply ignore these. BGE-M3 consolidates these terms in the same vector space so that they are
241   all treated as related to the same frame of reference, so any index with such an index will return all
242   documents with or without terms. This is key to enabling clinicians not to miss important patient
243
      data, leading to more accurate and better diagnoses for patients.
244   The coalescence of Upstash Vector and BGE-M3 enhances our NLP model to support fast, semantic,
245   and contextual data retrieval. Upstash Vector possesses the capability for patient record retrieval in
246   clinical systems and other similar large repositories within units of semantically related web pages
247   onscreen. BGE-M3 extends the structure of semantic and dynamic chunks to include the meaning
248   of data by getting records not by keywords but by the sense of them.
249   With this integration, the quality, and correctness of medical decisions are enhanced to a greater
250   extent. To illustrate, if a physician asks for a coronary heart disease in a patient, due to BGE-M3’s
251   semantic embeddings the system will find terms such as “myocardial infarction” or “coronary artery
252   disease” as well. Upstash Vector, however, makes sure that in any case such information is fetched
253   as fast as possible, so there are no delays in critical clinical cases.
254   Furthermore, this model is particularly beneficial in mitigating the problem of algorithmic fairness
255   within the health care domain. Employing fairness audits and fetching the data according to its
256   context not its history enables all the patients to be treated without discrimination. This is important
257   in safeguarding the core values of the medical profession and endorsing the provisions captured in
258   the AMA Code of Ethics.

395   confined to “human” language, acknowledgement, and comfort has no salvation in any level of AI.
396
      None with the most ingenious design and advanced technologies. Administrative functions and
      analytics of AI can reduce some chore but not the least activities of physicians with patients that
397
      include relation and reasoning.
398
399   There is a risk of over-dependence on the use of AI. As such, there should be on-the-job training
400   that includes training on the right application of NLP systems with regard to human management.
401   Education should enable health care professionals to evaluate the relevance of artificial intelligence
402
      suggestions that AI is an instrument rather than a replacement of judgment within the clinical out-
      lines. This may help physicians avert increasing their automation complacency and encourage them
403
      to participate in the decision-making activities.
404
405   Moreover, training should aim at enhancing how patients and doctors communicate by using AI for
406   those interactions where more human attention is less helpful. AI-enabled technologies can make
407   routine processes carry less importance which gives room for the doctor to spend more time listening
408
      to patients, outlining their treatment, and addressing their worries. In this way, the reason why most
      people fear artificial intelligence will be addressed: AI will enhance care as opposed to diminishing
409
      it, provided its usage is done right and with enough human management.
