011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we define the sum of RKBSs using the characterization theorem of
014            RKBSs and show that the sum of RKBSs is compatible with the direct sum of
015            feature spaces. Moreover, we decompose the integral RKBS Fσ (X , Ω) into the
016            sum of p-norm RKBSs {L1σ (µi )}i∈I . Finally, we provide some applications to
017            enhance the structural understanding of the integral RKBS class.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      To analyze the performance of neural networks, the hypothesis space represented by (infinite width)
024   neural networks has been studied. Based on the concept of variation spaces (Kurková & Sanguineti,
025   2001; Mhaskar, 2004), Bach (2017) defined the F1 spaces as an integral representation of the neural
026   networks using the total variation norm. In subsequent, E & Stephan (2022) defined the Barron
027   spaces employing the path norm and showed that the F1 spaces and the Barron spaces can be iso-
028   metrically isomorphic when using the Rectified Linear Unit (RELU) activation function.
029
      The concept of Reproducing Kernel Banach Spaces (RKBSs) is a generalization of the Reproducing
030   Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), similar to how Banach spaces extend Hilbert spaces (Zhang et al.,
031   2009). Relating to neural networks, Bartolucci et al. (2023) defined a class of integral RKBSs which
032   are variants of the F1 spaces. They defined a class of integral RKBSs through the characterization
033   theorem of the RKBS introduced by Combettes et al. (2018) which describe an RKBS using a feature
034   space and its associated feature map.
035
      In this study, our primary focus is on a class of integral RKBSs. We aim to decompose this function
036   space and identify its fundamental building blocks. Decomposing a function space entails preserv-
037   ing both its algebraic operations and topological properties. Since we are dealing with RKBS, we
038   additionally need to ensure that the decomposition preserves the property that evaluation function-
039   als remain continuous (see Definition 3.2). Considering the case of RKHS, there exists a sum of
040   RKHSs that naturally extends the space in a canonical manner, resulting in an RKHS (Aronszajn,
041   1950). Using this approach, we aim to define the (potentially infinite) sum of RKBSs and investigate
042   its relationship with the feature spaces.
043   The main questions of this paper are the following:
044
045       (1) Finding a natural definition for the sum of RKBSs that is compatible with the usual direct
046           sum of Banach spaces.
047
048       (2) How can we decompose a class of integral RKBSs using the sum defined in question (1)?
049
050   To answer these questions, we define the sum of RKBSs, see Proposition 3.7, and show that the
051   direct sum of the feature (Banach) spaces is compatible with the sum of RKBSs (Proposition 4.2).
052   Roughly speaking, it is well-known that the space of the Radon measures can be decompose as the
053   vast l1 direct sum of L1 spaces. As an analogue of the fact described above, we decompose the class
      of integral RKBSs using the sum of p-norm RKBSs (Theorem 4.4).

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
055
056
      Before the era of neural networks, one of the main topics in machine learning was the kernel method,
057   exemplified by concepts such as Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs) and Support Vec-
058   tor Machines (Aronszajn, 1950; Steinwart & Christmann, 2008; Berlinet & Thomas-Agnan, 2011).
059   Machine learning models that use RKHS as their hypothesis space are guaranteed the existence of
060   a solution through the Representer Theorem. Additionally, an algorithm for explicitly finding this
061   solution is clearly presented (Smola & Schölkopf, 1998; Shalev Shwartz & Ben David, 2014). This
062   characteristic significantly reduces the gap between theoretical understanding and practical applica-
063   tion. One standard method for extending RKHS is through their sum, which plays a crucial role in
064   enhancing the approximation ability of machine learning models. For instance, approaches like the
065
      multiple kernel algorithm demonstrate the utility of such extensions in effectively capturing diverse
      features of data (Yamanishi et al., 2004; Gönen & Alpaydın, 2011).
066
067   However, RKHS-based learning algorithms exhibit certain limitations due to their inner product
068   structure. To address these challenges, the concept of Reproducing Kernel Banach Spaces (RKBSs)
069   was introduced. Numerous studies have explored its theoretical foundations and applications (Zhang
070   et al., 2009; Song et al., 2013; Fasshauer et al., 2015; Lin et al., 2022). Meanwhile, early theoretical
071
      research on neural networks primarily focused on approximation properties (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik
      et al., 1990; Barron, 1993). This line of inquiry led to further investigations into the hypothesis
072
      spaces of infinitely wide neural networks, culminating in the introduction of concepts such as Barron
073
      spaces and variation spaces(Bach, 2017; E et al., 2022; E & Stephan, 2022; Siegel & Xu, 2023).
074
075   Recent studies have attempted to analyze the hypothesis spaces of neural networks within the RKBS
076   framework. For this purpose, the concept of integral RKBS has been introduced, which enables the
077
      proof of the Representer Theorem for one-layer neural networks (Bartolucci et al., 2023). How-
      ever, unlike RKHS-based models, neural networks lack a clear algorithm for finding the solutions
078
      guaranteed by the Representer Theorem. In this study, we propose a method to decompose the hy-
079
      pothesis space of one-layer neural networks while preserving the RKBS structure. This approach
080   enables a bottom-up exploration of the hypothesis space of one-layer neural networks, with the goal
081   of contributing to the development of explicit algorithms for solutions guaranteed by the Representer
082   Theorem in neural network settings.

086
087   This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we briefly review the definitions and basic facts of
088   the functional analysis. In Section 3, following Bartolucci et al. (2023); Spek et al. (2022), we intro-
089   duce the definition of RKBSs and related function subclasses, namely a class of integral RKBSs and
090   a class of p-norm RKBSs. We present some basic properties of these function classes, particularly
091   focusing on the comparison between integral RKBSs and spaces of continuous functions (Proposi-
092
      tion 3.5). Moreover, we define the sum of RKBSs, which is a modified version of Example 3.13 in
      Combettes et al. (2018) and the theorem in 353p of Aronszajn (1950), by using the characterization
093
      theorem of an RKBS. In Section 4, we state the main result of this article. We provide the compati-
094
      bility between the sum of RKBSs and the direct sum of feature (Banach) spaces. Furthermore, using
095   the compatibility (Proposition 4.2), we obtain that a class of integral RKBSs can be decomposed into

097   4.4, showing how the size of the RKBS Fσ (X , Ω) compares to the finite sum of p-norm RKHSs.

482   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
483
484   We showed that there is a compatibility property between the direct sum of feature spaces and the
485   sum of RKBSs. By using this, we can decompose a class of integral RKBS Fσ (X , Ω) into the
      sum of p-norm RKBSs {L1σ (µi )}i∈I . The advantage of this analytical method is that it allows for a


                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      more structural understanding of the RKBS class through an appropriate decomposition approach.
487   In Section 5, we partially explained these advantages by comparing the integral RKBS class to the
488   previously known sum of RKHSs. Additionally, through these insights, we expect that it would be
489   helpful in designing multiple kernel learning algorithms for the RKBS class. To ensure the feasibility
490   of learning, we need to consider the Representer Theorem, which is discussed in paper Bartolucci
491   et al. (2023) for the integral RKBS class. If the most generalized form of the Representer Theorem
492   presented by Unser & Aziznejad (2022) can be extended to the infinite case, it seems likely that this
493   would enable the recovery of the results obtained in Bartolucci et al. (2023) within the context of
494   our findings.
