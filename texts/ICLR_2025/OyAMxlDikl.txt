009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a novel approach to solve the adaptive quantization problem in neu-
012            ral networks based on epistemic uncertainty analysis. The quantized model is
013            treated as a Bayesian neural network with stochastic weights, where the mean
014            values are employed to estimate the corresponding weights. Standard deviations
015            serve as an indicator of uncertainty and the number of corresponding bits — i.e.,
016            a larger number of bits indicate lower uncertainty, and vice versa. We perform
017            an extensive analysis of several algorithms within a novel framework for differ-
018
               ent convolutional and fully connected neural networks based on open datasets
               demonstrating the main advantages of the proposed approach. In particular, we
019
               introduce two novel algorithms for mixed-precision quantization. Quantile In-
020
               form utilizes uncertainty to allocate bit-width across layers, while RandomBits
021            employs stochastic gradient-based optimization techniques to maximize the full
022            likelihood of quantization. Using our approach, we reduce the average bit-width
023            of the VGG-16 model to 3.05 with the 90.5% accuracy on the CIFAR-10 dataset
024            compared to 91.9% for the non-quantized model. For the LeNet model trained on
025            the MNIST dataset, we reduce the average bit-width to 3.16 and achieve 99.0%
026            accuracy, almost equal to 99.2% for the non-quantized model.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Compression of neural network models are typically addressed using pruning (Blalock et al., 2020),
032   quantization (Gholami et al., 2021), decomposition of weight matrices into factors (Sainath et al.,
033   2013) and distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) technique. If the architecture of a model is fixed, distil-
034   lation cannot be applied, while pruning and decomposition of weight matrices can lead to certain
035   complications. The quantization approach may be the only promising way to compress a model.
036   Quantization can be applied during (Courbariaux et al., 2015) and after model training (Fang et al.,
037   2020), as well as during retraining (Nagel et al., 2021). According to the weights manipulation
038   approach, these methods can be divided into several classes. One can either manipulate weight
039   values or change the number of bits of a particular weight. In our work we focus on the latter class,
040   where different granularity levels can be implemented. One can quantize the entire model (Yang
041   et al., 2019), each layer (Wang et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2022), or each channel (Chmiel et al., 2020;
042   Zhong et al., 2020). The smaller the granularity, the better the quantization. Layered and channeled
043   granularity levels require adaptive quantization methods (Langroudi et al., 2021) that are able to
044
      determine which weights need more bits and which ones need fewer.
045   Most existing methods of quantization are based on heuristic criteria or deterministic optimization,
046   which may not capture the uncertainty and variability of network parameters (Zhou et al., 2018;
047   Guo, 2018). Moreover, most methods such as TWN (Li et al., 2016), LR-Net (Shayer et al., 2017),
048   RQ (Louizos et al., 2018), PACT (Choi et al., 2018) and Dorefa (Zhou et al., 2016) use a fixed
049   bit-width for all network layers, ignoring the potential benefits of applying different bit-widths to
050
      different layers (Liang et al., 2021). A flexible framework for quantization that could account for
      the probabilistic nature of a network parameters and adapt the bit-width to a layer characteristics is
051
      desirable (Cheng et al., 2017; Garg et al., 2021).
052
053   Among the approaches, a group of reinforcement learning-based algorithms, such as (He et al.,
      2018), have been proposed for adaptive quantization. However, these methods are computationally


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      expensive, making them less suitable for practical applications where efficiency is crucial. There-
055   fore, they have been excluded from consideration.
056
057   To address the challenge of the adaptive quantization problem, we propose two novel algorithms
058
      based on Bayesian neural networks (BNNs), which can capture the uncertainty of the network pa-
      rameters and outputs, referred further as Quantile Inform and Random Bits. Quantile Inform is an
059
      informativeness-based algorithm that defines bit-widths of each layer based on informativeness cal-
060
      culated using mean values and standard deviations of Bayesian neural network parameters. Random
061   Bits is based on BNN post-training in terms of maximization of the likelihood of optimal quantiza-
062   tion with a limit on the average bit-width of a quantized model.
063
064
      We evaluate proposed algorithms on several benchmark datasets and show that they achieve compa-
      rable or better accuracy than the existing quantization methods while using fewer bits. We show that
065
      informativeness based on weights of a trained BNN strongly correlates with the optimal bit-width
066
      for layers. Different methods of aggregation and smoothing of informativeness are evaluated in a
067   few benchmarks and the best one is used in Quantile Inform to calculate bit-widths. Alternative
068   informativeness values based on variances are tested as well. We also demonstrate the advantages
069   of BNN for quantization, such as robustness to noise and calibration of uncertainty.

072   2   R ELATED WORK
073
074
075
      BNNs are able to explicitly model uncertainty, thus may be also suitable for adaptive quantization.
      There is a number of papers where quantization relies either on the measure of uncertainty obtained
076
      as a result of model training or on the application of uncertainty estimation to the weights of a trained
077
      model. We employ some of these methods as described below.
078
079   Blundell et al. (2015) experimentally confirmed the possibility of using averages and variances ob-
080   tained by BNN training to assess the informativeness of network weights and the effectiveness of
081
      pruning (zeroing) the least informative ones. It is applied to research pruning of a trained network
      based on the magnitude of the signals. The authors defined the signal as the ratio of the average to
082
      the variance. It is argued that the smaller the ratio, the less meaningful the weight. Accordingly,
083
      after calculating the signals for all weights, the authors zeroed out the weights with the smallest
084   signals. Such an experiment on the MNIST classification problem using an architecture of two fully
085   connected layers confirmed that it is possible to zero 75% of the weights without degradation of the
086   error value on the test subset. At zeroing 95%, there is a degradation from 1.24% to 1.29%, and at
087   98%, the error reaches 1.39%. Consequently, 48,000 of the original 2.467 million weights remain
088   non-zero.
089
      A few researchers propose a method for neural network compression based on quantization of
090   weights using a mixture of Gaussian distributions. This approach allows the model to be trained
091   in such a way that the weights tend to move towards one of the K centroids, forming K clusters
092   which is vector quantization (Gong et al., 2014; Park et al., 2017; Stock et al., 2019; Razani et al.,
093   2021).
094
      After the weights of the model form sufficiently distinct clusters, they can be replaced by the corre-
095
      sponding centroids of these clusters, and the centroids themselves can be encoded with log2 K bits.
096   Moreover, if one of the centroids is zero, corresponding weights do not have to be stored or consid-
097   ered in calculations. This method agrees with the principle of minimum description length (MDL)
098   proposed by Grünwald (2007), which states that the best model for data is the one that requires the
099   smallest number of bits to describe it. According to Louizos et al. (2017), the most effective way
100   to solve the problem of compression and computational efficiency in deep learning is the Bayesian
101   approach. The latter states that it is possible to remove a large number of weights or neurons in a
102   neural network using a prior distribution that encourages sparsity.
103   There is an innovative approach (Van Baalen et al., 2020) to decompose a particular weight into
104   terms that determine the final bit-width of the quantized weight. This decomposition is individual
105   for each weight and is trained using the variational Bayesian inference (Chappell et al., 2008). Thus,
106   using Bayesian methods, the most probable number of bits for each weight is determined. Each of
107   these terms is a learnable parameter and reflects the confidence of the algorithm that the correspond-
      ing weight should have at least the bit-width with the associated matching parameter. The above


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      approach ensures a higher accuracy with a better quantization of weights compared to other models.
109   However, the limitation is that the bit-width has to be the powers of 2 (i.e. 2, 4, 8, 16, or 32 bits).
110
111   These results suggest that BNNs and approaches to their training open up new opportunities for
112
      adaptive quantization based on the measure of uncertainty, a side-product of Bayesian network train-
      ing, which is leveraged by all our algorithms to measure uncertainty.

117   3.1   Q UANTIZATION OF BNN S
118
119   A common method for training BNNs is Variational Inference (Kingma et al., 2015). Suppose we
120   train a BNN model M on a dataset D via Variational Inference. The model M has N layers, i.e.
121   w = l1 ∪ l2 ∪ ... ∪ lN , where each layer li is treated as a set of its weights, with li ∩ lj = ∅ at
122
      i ̸= j. Each weight w ∈ w of the model M is a Gaussian random variable w ∼ N (µw , σw ).
      We can utilize it to construct a conventional neural network by replacing each stochastic weight w
123
      with a certain real value. In this transformation, an attractive choice for each weight w is its mean
124
      value µw . Therefore, further layer quantization of the trained M into specified bit-widths b1 , ..., bN
125   is performed as follows: each stochastic weight w is replaced by its average value µw , resulting in
126   Mdet, which is a deterministic version of M. Then, every j-th layer of Mdet is quantized using bj
127   bits.
128

134   quantization interval [αj , βj ] for the layer lj . Then, we apply the quantization layer lj into bj bit as

      3.3   N ON - UNIFORM QUANTIZATION : QUANTILIZATION
146
147   Quantilization offers an alternative to uniform interval quantization by leveraging quantiles. It is
148   supposed to address a possible drawback in uniform quantization grids by ensuring an even distri-
149   bution of {µw } among quantized values through the use of quantiles.
150
      Let us consider the scenario where we aim to quantize a set of average weight values {µw } from
151
      layer lj of Mdet into mj := 2bj values, i.e. into bj bit. Calculated directly from data, empirical
152   quantiles, provide the best possible partition of {µw } into even groups. Unfortunately, they can be
153   impractical to compute and store for large datasets. Therefore, we opt for a more efficient approach
154   by relying on computing quantiles derived from the theoretical distribution Fj that can be defined
155   by only its type and parameters. For the sake of definiteness, we shall consider only Gaussian
156   distributions.
157
      Assuming the distribution represented by Fj spans an entire real number line, we can establish a
158
      following relationship by defining ak := Fj−1 (k/mj ) for 0 < k < mj :
159
160               P(W1 ∈ (−∞, a1 )) = P(W1 ∈ (a1 , a2 )) = . . . = P(W1 ∈ (amj −1 , +∞)).
161
      This indicates that all quantization intervals will, on average, contain equal number of elements.

      Consider a trained BNN M, and define weight informativeness as the ratio of the absolute mean
      value to the corresponding variance:

179
180   Blundell et al. (2015) showed that informativeness in pruning can preserve the quality of predictions
181   with a high percentage of pruning. For a quantization algorithm, as in the case of the pruning
182
      criterion, one can use informativeness weights. If we employ a computationally expensive method
      of finding optimal bit-widths for each layer of the neural network, such as Bayesian optimization
183
      described by Snoek et al. (2012), bit-widths correlate with the informativeness of each layer. The
184
      following experiment was carried out on the CIFAR-10 dataset with VGG-7 (with an additional fully
185   connected layer containing 10 neurons) and VGG-16 models and the MNIST dataset with LeNET
186   model to confirm this hypothesis.
187
188
      We extended the use of informativeness to pruning and developed a quantization method that assigns
      an optimal bit-width to each layer, proportional to its informativeness. Our analysis revealed a
189
      strong positive correlation between the optimal bit-width produced by the complete brute-force of all
190
      possible bit-widths for each layer and informativeness produced by a fitted Bayesian version of the
191   network. All experiments proved this, see Figure 1, as measured by the coefficient of determination
192   R2. The calculated R-measures of correlation between layer informativeness and bit-width obtained
193   through Bayesian optimization are 0.823 for the VGG-7 model, 0.867 for VGG-16, and 0.854 for
194   LeNet. These findings suggest that informativeness can be used as a reliable predictor of bit-width
195   in adaptive quantization.
196
      Analysis of experimental data revealed that the initial and final layers of the neural network play
197   a more critical role, which is expressed in the need for higher bit resolution. It confirms the well-
198   known heuristics to avoid the quantization of the first and last layers discussed in Louizos et al.
199   (2017) and Gholami et al. (2021). We can try to explain this phenomenon as follows. The last
200   layer can be regarded as a classifier based on features extracted with the first and intermediate layers
201   of a neural network. Thus, the last layers should be sensitive, which may also be related to their
202   capacity. Typically, the last layers are not as large as the layers in the middle of the model. It is not
203   unlikely that only a small part of these weights is useful for solving the task. In this case, the mean
204   bit-width of the intermediate layer should be small enough. The same reasoning applies to the first
205
      layers which usually have a significantly fewer weights than the intermediate ones. Additionally, the
      first layer requires a larger bit-width for quantization in order to correctly extract low-level features,
206
      which are important for constructing more complex features and training the network.

      6     D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
519
520
      To address the challenge of adaptive quantization, we introduced two novel algorithms based on
521   uncertainty modeling in Bayesian neural networks. Our results demonstrate that these algorithms
522   effectively reduce model size while maintaining low quantization error, all within a single pass and
523   with minimal computation after the initial training phase.
524
      A key advantage of our method is that it follows a post-training quantization paradigm while deliver-
525
      ing results comparable to quantization-aware training approaches. This makes it particularly suitable
526
      for quantizing pre-trained models without requiring additional extensive training. Our method also
527   offers flexible and efficient quantization by allowing neural network layers to be quantized to any
528   discrete bit-width, resulting in reduced computational requirements.
529
530
      In conclusion, our proposed approach advances model compression techniques by providing a
      computationally efficient and adaptable quantization method. The demonstrated performance and
531
      flexibility suggest promising avenues for further research and practical applications in resource-
532
      constrained machine learning environments.

1210   The proposed quantization methods rely on the estimation of weight importance, which is derived
1211   from the Bayesian framework. In this context, the informativeness of each weight is quantified as
1212   the ratio of its mean (µ) to its variance (σ 2 ). This metric is essential for the algorithms, as it allows
1213   the identification of the most critical weights in the network.
1214   To compute these values, the neural network needs to be trained as a Bayesian model, where each
1215   weight is represented by a distribution (typically Gaussian) instead of a single point estimate. This
1216   training process requires optimizing both the means (µ) and variances (σ 2 ) of the weights, which
1217   adds complexity compared to traditional neural network training.
1218   One way to mitigate the additional computational overhead of training a Bayesian neural network is
1219   to initialize the means (µ) of the Bayesian weights using the weights of a pretrained standard model.
1220   This approach offers several advantages:
1221
1222          • Faster Convergence: The pretrained weights provide a well-tuned starting point for µ, re-
1223            ducing the time required for the Bayesian network to converge during training.
1224          • Better Performance: Starting from pretrained weights ensures that the model is initialized
1225            closer to a good solution, potentially leading to higher final accuracy.
1226          • Reduced Variance Exploration: Since µ starts with meaningful values, the optimization
1227            focuses more on fine-tuning the variances (σ 2 ), further streamlining the process.
1228
1229   To enhance performance and accelerate the training of Bayesian models, we performed an experi-
1230   ment with ResNet-20 on CIFAR-100, initializing µ with a checkpoint from the original model. This
1231   approach reduced the required iterations from 600 to 300 while improving accuracy from 54.5% to
1232   65.5%. Additionally, we improved quantization results in each experiment by approximately 10%.
1233   This initialization technique effectively leverages the progress made during the training of the origi-
1234   nal model, making the transition to the Bayesian variant more practical for large-scale applications.
