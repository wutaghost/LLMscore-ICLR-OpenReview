                                                             A BSTRACT
020
                  The co-evolution of morphology and behavior in 3D space has garnered consid-
021               erable interest in the field of embodied intelligence. While recent studies have
022               highlighted the considerable benefits of geometric symmetry for tasks like learn-
023               ing to locomote, navigate, and explore in dynamic 3D environments, its role
024               within co-evolution setup remains unexplored. Existing benchmarks encounter
025               several key issues: 1) the task lacks consideration for spatial geometric informa-
026               tion; 2) the method lacks geometric symmetry to deal with the complexities in
027               3D environments. In this work, we propose a novel setup, named Subequivariant
028               Morphology-Behavior Co-Evolution in 3D Environments (3DS-MB), to address
029
                  the identified limitations. To be specific, we propose EquiEvo, which injects geo-
                  metric symmetry, i.e., subequivariance, to construct dynamic, learnable local ref-
030
                  erence frames, enabling the joint policy to generalize to diverse task spatial struc-
031
                  tures, thereby improving co-evolution efficiency. Then, we evaluate EquiEvo on
032               the proposed environments, where our method consistently and significantly out-
033               performs existing approaches in tasks such as locomotion navigation and adver-
034               sarial scenarios. Extensive experiments underscore the importance of subequivari-
035               ance for the co-evolution of morphology and behavior, effective morphology-task
036               mapping and robust morphology-behavior mapping.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
      The rich diversity of animal morphologies, shaped over millions of years in complex environments,
041
      underscores the deep link between body form and intelligence, where well-adapted morphologies
042   enable agents to learn and perform complex tasks effectively (Gupta et al., 2021; Brooks, 1991).
043   Morphology evolution is fundamentally driven by environmental interactions and task demands,
044   as supported by evolutionary biology (Maynard-Smith, 1974; Gould, 2010). The co-evolution of
045   morphology and behavior in 3D environments has gained significant interest (Sims, 1994; Dong
046   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Gupta et al., 2021), focusing on how agents’ form and function
047   evolve together to improve adaptability and performance. Some works, such as (Gupta et al., 2021;
048   Dong et al., 2023), impose bilateral symmetry constraints directly on morphology, diverging from
049   evolutionary principles, as agents should evolve optimal morphologies through interaction with the
050
      environment, rather than relying on predefined constraints.
051   Alternatively, the geometric symmetry of environments and dynamic systems naturally exists, which
052   motivates us to explore how leveraging such symmetry can accelerate the evolution of optimal mor-
053   phologies through interaction with the environment. Recent studies highlight that leveraging geo-
      metric symmetry in tasks like locomotion, navigation, and exploration within dynamic 3D settings

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      can significantly improve the efficiency of behavioral evolution (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024). While re-
055   cent co-evolution benchmarks (Yuan et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022) have achieved
056   remarkable progress (Liu et al., 2022), they often fail to fully exploit geometric symmetry within 3D
057   spaces. These benchmarks face two main issues: 1) a focus on tasks with limited spatial geomet-
058   ric considerations, such as the “move forward” objective, which are simplified to fixed directional
059   movements and do not require complex direction-aware strategies; 2) a lack of research exploring
060   the use of geometric symmetry in co-evolution setups to handle the directional complexities of 3D
061   environments effectively.
062   In this work, we address the limitations of current co-evolution benchmarks by focusing on tasks
063   that require rich 3D spatial understanding and incorporate dynamic interactions. To achieve this, we
064   propose a novel setup, named Subequivariant Morphology-Behavior Co-Evolution in 3D Environ-
065   ments (3DS-MB). Our setup extends two core tasks to enable co-evolution in complex 3D spatial
066   environments: navigation and sumo (Chen et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2024). In the navigation
067   task, the agent moves towards randomly generated goals, requiring continuous adaptation to new
068   spatial contexts. In the sumo task, two agents compete in an arena, where the objective is to push
069
      the opponent out. The constantly changing adversarial dynamics require agents to adapt to vary-
      ing attack and defense angles, demanding both strategic behavior and morphology adaptation to
070
      effectively leverage rich directional information. These tasks introduce variability in movement and
071
      environmental interaction, challenging agents to develop robust, generalized policies that account
072   for diverse geometric conditions.
073
074   To effectively handle the directional complexities of 3D environments, we incorporate subequivari-
075
      ant neural networks within our co-evolution framework. As illustrasted in Figure 1, we introduce
      subequivariant graph neural networks (Chen et al., 2023a; Han et al., 2022a) to predict a dynamic,
076
      adaptive Local Reference Frame (LRF) for the agent (Chen et al., 2024). By projecting directional
077
      vectors from the global world frame into this LRF, we transform spatial representations into an in-
078   variant form, preserving geometric symmetry and simplifying the search space. For instance, under
079   rotational symmetry, states and actions in any direction can be treated as equivalent, effectively re-
080   ducing a 2D/3D problem to a simpler 1D/2D one. This approach enables the policy to generalize
081   across diverse spatial structures, improving both generalization ability and sample efficiency in the
082   co-evolution process, allowing agents to efficiently learn and adapt to the complex directional in-
083   formation inherent in 3D tasks. An essential aspect of our approach is ensuring that the evolution
084   of morphology remains invariant to geometric transformations, such as rotations or translations, as
085   these do not alter the intrinsic properties of the environment.
086   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
088          • We introduce 3DS-MB, a benchmark for subequivariant morphology-behavior co-
089            evolution, designed to capture the rich spatial geometric information and variability in-
090            herent in complex 3D environments.
091
             • We propose EquiEvo, a novel co-evolution framework that leverages subequivariant graph
092            neural networks to effectively incorporate geometric symmetry, enhancing both morphol-
093            ogy adaptation and behavior control.
094
095          • We empirically validate the performance of EquiEvo on the 3DS-MB, where our approach
096
               consistently outperforms existing methods. Extensive ablation studies and analyses further
               demonstrate the framework’s robustness and highlight the benefits of integrating subequiv-
097
               ariance in co-evolution tasks.

      Optimization of Agent morphology Design: Research in the field of optimization of agent mor-
793   phology design mainly falls into two mainstream approaches: the first focuses on optimizing the
794   properties, functions, and design parameters of agents (Schaff et al., 2019; Ha, 2019; Chen et al.,
795   2023b), and the second on structure optimization based on graph neural networks (Wang et al.,
796   2018; Yuan et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023). The former often adopts population-based methods,
797   handling morphology and control strategy optimization separately through a two-level optimiza-
798   tion process (Ha, 2019), such as the application of evolutionary search in morphology optimiza-
799   tion (Gupta et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2019); the latter focuses on multi-objective joint optimiza-
800   tion (Yuan et al., 2022). Research also includes the optimization of continuous design parameters
801
      of agents, using methods such as simulated annealing, trajectory optimization, and deep reinforce-
      ment learning (Baykal & Alterovitz, 2017; Ha et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020). Recent studies have
802
      further simplified the evolutionary process and improved task-independent design optimization (Ch-
803
      eney et al., 2014; Desai et al., 2017; III et al., 2021). Despite the progress made by these methods,
804   sample efficiency remains a challenge, as agents with different design morphologies often learn
805   independently within the population. Yuan et al. (2022) introduce Transform2Act, an innovative
806   policy for simultaneous morphology design and control optimization that adapts skeletal structures
807   and joint attributes for efficient environmental interaction, advancing integrated agent design and
808   control. Huang et al. (2024) presents CompetEvo, a system that integrates morphology evolution
809   with adversarial game self-training to enhance agents’ capabilities, showing morphology evolution
      significantly boosts combat skills and emergent behaviors.

      Geometric Equivariant Models: Notably, the physical realm exhibits inherent symmetries, and
811   extensive research has been conducted on group equivariant models (Cohen & Welling, 2016a;b;
812   Worrall et al., 2017). In recent times, the research area of geometrically equivariant graph neu-
813   ral networks (Han et al., 2022b) has emerged, utilizing symmetry as an inductive bias during the
814   learning process. These models are crafted to ensure that their outputs undergo rotation, transla-
815   tion, or reflection in alignment with the inputs, thereby preserving the symmetry. Within a Markov
816   decision process (MDP) that exhibits symmetries (van der Pol et al., 2020), the state-action space
817   possesses inherent symmetries, allowing policies to be optimized within a more straightforward,
818   abstract MDP. van der Pol et al. (2020) endeavors to train policy networks that are equivariant and
819
      value networks that are invariant in two-dimensional simulated environments. In contrast, Chen et al.
      (2023a) delves into the study of body-level equivariant policy networks within intricate 3D physics
820
      environments, enhancing policy generalization over various orientations. Additionally, Chen et al.
821
      (2024) address the challenges of multi-agent games by employing entity assignment along with an
822   entity-level subequivariant message-passing mechanism. Both our approach and Chen et al. (2023a;
823   2024) implement equivariance based on the core function described in Equation (2). However, there
824   are key differences in the design and application. Chen et al. (2023a) employs a transformer archi-
825   tecture with a fully connected topology, whereas both Chen et al. (2024) and our approach utilize
826   GNNs. The main distinction lies in the graph construction: Chen et al. (2024) models multi-agent re-
827   lationships, requiring an entity assignment to define the graph’s topology, while our method focuses
828   on single-agent morphology evolution, directly using the morphology’s inherent topology to build
829   the graph. This design aligns with our goal of co-optimizing morphology and behavior efficiently
830
      within a single-agent framework.

856   D    T HE ROLE OF G EOMETRIC S YMMETRY IN M ORPHOLOGY E VOLUTION
857
858   The optimization of morphology in co-evolution tasks is governed by a bi-level objective, as defined
859   in Equation (1):
                          ∗
860                      Gm := arg max J(πG , Gm ) s.t. πG = arg max J(π, Gm ),
                                       Gm                                      π
861
862   where J(π, Gm ) represents the cumulative reward for a given morphology Gm and policy π. This
863   two-layer optimization highlights the dependence of morphology optimization on the behavior pol-
      icy that performs for a given morphology. It is important to note that J is defined as an expectation,


                                                            16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      meaning that all states are integrated out in the expectation sense. Consequently, the morphology’s
865   policy and value functions theoretically depend only on the morphology Gm and the behavior policy
866   πG .
867
868
      In practice, the bi-level optimization is implemented as a co-optimization process, where the inner
      and outer optimizations alternate incrementally rather than fully optimizing inner layer. Since the
869
      evaluation of J relies on finite samples of the behavior policy, if the behavior policy is not equiv-
870
      ariant, the limited sampling will cause J to vary under sampled initial state. Geometric symmetry
871   reduces the variability introduced by sampling, as equivariant networks ensure equivalent actions
872   and consistent values, providing consistent feedback across state transformations——such as trans-
873   lations, rotations, or reflections. For instance, under rotational symmetry, equivalent actions are
874   treated as identical, and the feedback provided to the morphology is unaffected by differences in
875   sampled initial states. This consistent feedback not only improves the reliability of morphology
876   evaluation but also enhances the efficiency of the co-evolution process, allowing for better optimiza-
877   tion within the search space.

992    E.4   D ISCUSSION ON S TRUCTURAL E VOLUTION
993
994    Structural evolution is a promising yet challenging direction in morphology-behavior co-
995    optimization. In this subsection, we provide a detailed discussion of our decisions, experimental
996    findings, and future directions regarding structural evolution.
997
998    Evolving from Scratch and Predefined Morphology Structures. For the Ant Navigation task,
999    we chose to evolve morphology skeletons from scratch, starting from the “atomic morphology”
1000
       shown in Figure 1. This setup represents the most challenging scenario in co-evolution, allowing us
       to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method while avoiding biases introduced by predefined mor-
1001
       phologies. In contrast, the Ants Sumo and Humanoid Navigation tasks use predefined morphology
1002
       structures, where only attribute development is allowed. This decision are driven by the challenge
1003   of defining clear boundaries for structural transformations across different starting morphologies,
1004   particularly for complex predefined morphology structures.
1005
1006   Challenges of Structual Evolution. Structural evolution is inher-
1007   ently complex and remains underexplored. Several key issues must
1008   be addressed, such as how many body parts can grow from a torso
1009   node, how many can branch from each node, which nodes require
1010   constraints, the maximum structural depth, and the corresponding
1011   attribute transformations. Without carefully designed constraints,
1012   the search space grows exponentially, making optimization ineffi-
1013
       cient and unstable.
1014
1015
       Structual Evolution for Ants Sumo. In the EvoAnts Sumo task            Figure 13: EvoAnts Sumo.
       (see Figure 13), structural evolution can lead to uncoordinated de-
1016
       velopment of multiple body parts, resulting in excessive forces that cause the agent to fling itself
1017
       out of the arena. This instability prevents meaningful results, which is why we only included the
1018   outcomes of attribute evolution for this task.
1019
1020   Structural Evolution for Humanoids. Training humanoids is significantly more challenging
1021   compared to simpler morphologies like the ant. Achieving stable behavior in attribute evolution
1022   already requires extensive training and computational resources. Adding structural evolution to
1023   the mix would drastically increase complexity, demanding even longer training times and greater
1024   computational resources. Given our current computational limitations, we are unable to conduct ex-
1025   periments involving structural evolution for humanoids. Nonetheless, we acknowledge the potential
       value of this research direction and will pursue it in future work.


                                                            19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       The Complexity of 3D Structural Evolution. Incorporating structural evolution into 3D tasks is
1027   exceptionally challenging. The interplay between the dimensionality of the environment, task, and
1028   action space—including morphology structure, attributes, and behaviors—creates a compounded
1029   effect that leads to an exponential explosion in the optimization search space. Furthermore, such a
1030   problem setup has not been systematically explored before. Prior work has typically focused on 2D
1031   spaces (Ha, 2019), 3D spaces with 2D structural tasks (Yuan et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2021; Dong
1032   et al., 2023), tasks that do not incorporate structural evolution (Huang et al., 2024), or have imposed
1033   rigid constraints on morphology, such as bilateral symmetry (Dong et al., 2023), limiting the natural
1034   development of morphology through interaction with the environment.
1035   Our work represents a crucial step forward by leveraging geometric symmetries to reduce this com-
1036   plexity. We believe our method lays the foundation for exploring this valuable and fascinating prob-
1037   lem setup. While our current results are a small step, they highlight the necessity and significance
1038   of addressing these challenges to advance the field further.
1039
1040   Future Directions. The limitations and insights gained from our experiments point to several ex-
1041   citing future directions. These include extending structural evolution to more complex morphologies
1042   like humanoids, refining structural constraints to balance exploration and optimization, and system-
1043
       atically investigating the role of geometric symmetries in 3D structural tasks. By continuing to
       explore these directions, we aim to address the challenges of structural evolution and unlock its full
1044
       potential for embodied intelligence and robotic design.
