009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The transformer architecture has been widely applied to many machine learning
012            tasks. A main bottleneck in the time to perform transformer computations is a task
013            called attention computation. [Alman and Song, NeurIPS 2023] have shown that in
014            the bounded entry regime, there is an almost linear time algorithm to approximate
015            the attention computation. They also proved that the bounded entry assumption
016            is necessary for a fast algorithm assuming the popular Strong Exponential Time
017            Hypothesis.
018            A new version of transformer which uses position embeddings has recently been
019            very successful. At a high level, position embedding enables the model to capture
020            the correlations between tokens while taking into account their position in the
021            sequence. Perhaps the most popular and effective version is Rotary Position
022            Embedding (RoPE), which was proposed by [Su, Lu, Pan, Murtadha, Wen, and
023            Liu, Neurocomputing 2024].
024            A main downside of RoPE is that it complicates the attention computation problem,
025            so that previous techniques for designing almost linear time algorithms no longer
026            seem to work. In this paper, we show how to overcome this issue, and give a new
027
               algorithm to compute the RoPE attention in almost linear time in the bounded entry
               regime. (Again, known lower bounds imply that bounded entries are necessary.)
028
               Our new algorithm combines two techniques in a novel way: the polynomial
029
               method, which was used in prior fast attention algorithms, and the Fast Fourier
030            Transform.

036   Large language models (LLMs) are among the most impactful tools in modern machine learning.
037   LLMs such as Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017a), BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), GPT-3 (Brown et al.,
038   2020), PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022), OPT (Zhang et al., 2022), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023),
039
      Gemini (Team et al., 2023), Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024), Claude3 (Anthropic, 2024), GPT-4o
      (OpenAI, 2024a), o1 (OpenAI, 2024b), can process natural language more effectively than smaller
040
      models or traditional algorithms. This means that they can understand and generate more complex and
041
      nuanced language, which can be useful for a variety of tasks such as language translation, question
042   answering, and sentiment analysis. LLMs can also be adapted to multiple purposes without needing
043   to be retained from scratch.

      Attention Computation. LLMs currently require massive time and computing resources to perform at
      scale. The major bottleneck to speeding up LLM operations is the time to perform a certain operation
046
      called an attention matrix computation (Vaswani et al., 2017a; Radford et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2018;
047
      Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Kitaev et al., 2020). These computations
048   ask us to multiply the attention matrix A with another value token matrix V ∈ Rn×d . More precisely,
049   given three matrices Q, K, V ∈ Rn×d (the query, key, and value token matrices), the goal is to output
050   (an approximation of) the n × d matrix Att(Q, K, V ) defined by Att(Q, K, V ) := D−1 AV where
051   the attention matrix A ∈ Rn×n and diagonal matrix D ∈ Rn×n are defined as A := exp(QK ⊤ /d)
052   (with exp applied entry-wise), and D := diag(A1n ). Here, n is the input sequence length, and d is
053   the embedding dimension of the model, and one typically considers d ≪ n like d = Θ(log n) in the
      time-intensive case of modeling long sequences.

      The straightforward algorithm for this problem runs in roughly quadratic time. Moreover, there are
055   known complexity-theoretic lower bounds (Keles et al., 2023; Alman & Song, 2023) proving that the
056   problem cannot be solved in truly subquadratic time in the case when the input matrices Q, K, V
057   have large entries, assuming a popular conjecture from fine-grained complexity theory called the
058   Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis (SETH Impagliazzo & Paturi (2001)) which we discuss more
059   shortly.
060
      In order to circumvent this lower bound, and inspired by the fact that the entries of the input
061   matrices are typically bounded in realistic inputs (Zafrir et al., 2019; Katharopoulos et al., 2020b),
062   a recent faster, almost linear-time algorithm (Alman & Song, 2023) was given, assuming that
063   ∥Q∥∞ , ∥K∥∞ , ∥V ∥∞ are all bounded. Here the ℓ∞ -norm denotes that ∥Q∥∞ := maxi,j |Qi,j |.
064   Rather than explicitly compute all the entries of the attention matrix A, Alman & Song (2023) only
065   implicitly uses it, by using an algorithmic tool called the polynomial method.

      Bounded entries in practice. The theoretical results of Alman & Song (2023) offer an explanation
075
      for a phenomenon commonly observed in practice: attention computation becomes significantly
076   more efficient when the input matrices have smaller entries. Previous work on LLM implementations
077   has noted similar observations; algorithmic techniques like quantization (Zafrir et al., 2019) and
078   low-degree polynomial approximation (Katharopoulos et al., 2020b), which result in bounded or
079   low-precision entries, can dramatically accelerate LLM operations. See, for example, the discussions
080   in (Zafrir et al., 2019, Section 2) and (Katharopoulos et al., 2020b, Section 3.2.1).

      RoPE: Rotary Position Embedding. In this paper, we study a variant on attention called RoPE
082   attention. At a high level, RoPE gives more expressive power to the model in exchange for making the
083   computational problem more complicated. In particular, many prior algorithms, such as the algorithm
084   of Alman & Song (2023), no longer apply to RoPE, for fundamental reasons we will discuss.
085
      RoPE was proposed by Su et al. (2024) and has been used extensively in large-scale industrial models.
086
      Examples which are known to use RoPE include the open-source models released by Meta such as
087
      Llama (Touvron et al. (2023a), see page 3), Llama 2 (Touvron et al. (2023b), see page 5), Llama 3 (AI
088   (2024) and page 7 of Llama Team (2024)), and the close-source LLM Claude 3 (Anthropic (2024))
089   released by Anthropic. Apple also incorporates RoPE into their LLM architecture (see McKinzie
090   et al. (2024) and page 3 of Gunter et al. (2024)).
091
      The idea behind RoPE is to rotate the query and key vectors in the self-attention mechanism. The
092
      rotation is position-dependent and designed such that the inner product between any two position-
093
      encoded vectors reflects their relative positions. Intuitively, the Rj−i matrices we define below
094   will rotate embedding vectors according to their position in the input, so that in the RoPE attention
095   mechanism, pairs of tokens with a longer relative distance will have smaller correlation.

      Technique Overview: Limitation of Prior Techniques
171
172   Prior fast algorithms with provable guarantees for attention are critically based on an algorithmic
173   technique called the polynomial method (Alman & Song, 2023; 2024a;b). This is a technique for
174   finding low-rank approximations of certain structured matrices. More precisely, suppose M ∈ Rn×n
175
      is a low-rank matrix, and f : R → R is any function. Let f (M ) denote the matrix where f is
      applied entry-wise to M . In general, although M is low-rank, the matrix f (M ) may be a full-rank
176
      matrix. However, the polynomial method says that if f can be approximated well by a low-degree
177
      polynomial, then f (M ) can be approximated well by a low-rank matrix. Since the usual attention
178   matrix is defined by applying exp entry-wise to a low-rank matrix, prior algorithms approximate
179   exp with a polynomial, then uses the polynomial method to approximate the attention matrix with a
180   low-rank matrix which can be used to quickly perform the necessary linear-algebraic operations.
181
      Although this approach has been successful in prior work on designing faster algorithms for many
182
      problems related to attention, it fundamentally cannot apply to RoPE attention. The key issue is
183
      that in RoPE attention, the underlying matrix which exp is applied to no longer needs to have low
184   rank. Indeed, let A denote the RoPE attention matrix (defined in Equation (1) above) and let M
185   denote A before it was entry-wise exponentiated. Even in the simplest case d = 1, one can see that
186   by picking the Rj−i entries appropriately, one can choose M to be any circulant matrix (i.e., matrix
187   whose (i, j) entry depends only in j − i). The polynomial method then cannot be used to argue that
188   A is approximately low-rank, since M itself is not low-rank.
189
      Technique Overview: Combining the Polynomial Method and Fast Fourier Transform
190
191   Although circulant matrices are typically not low-rank matrices, there is a vast literature on algorithms
192   for manipulating them using the Fast Fourier transform. Notably, it is not hard to notice that applying
193
      any function entry-wise to a circulant matrix results in another circulant matrix, so if M were indeed
      a circulant matrix as described in the previous paragraph, one could use the Fast Fourier transform to
194
      perform operations with the resulting matrix A.
195
196   However, even in the case of d = 1, the matrix M can actually be a more general type of matrix
197   which we call a rescaled circulant matrix. This is a matrix of the form D1 CD2 for diagonal matrices
198   D1 , D2 and circulant matrix C. Unfortunately, applying a function entry-wise to a rescaled circulant
199
      matrix need not result in another rescaled circulant matrix.
200   Our main algorithmic idea is a new version of the polynomial method: we prove that if M is a
201   rescaled circulant matrix, or even a sum of a small number of rescaled circulant matrices, and one
202   applies a function f entry-wise to M such that f has a low-degree polynomial approximation, then
203   the resulting matrix can be approximated by a sum of a relatively small number of rescaled circulant
204   matrices. In our case, we use this to write the RoPE attention matrix as a sum of rescaled circulant
205
      matrices, each of which is then manipulated using the Fast Fourier transform to yield our final
      algorithm.
206
207   We believe our new approach, of applying polynomial approximations entry-wise to structured
208   matrices other than low-rank matrices, may be broadly applied in other settings as well. Although the
209   polynomial method has been applied in many algorithmic contexts, to our knowledge, a version of
210   the polynomial method like this has not been used before.
211   Algorithmic techniques in practice. We emphasize that our two core techniques, the polynomial
212   method and Fast Fourier transform, are both prevalent in practice. The polynomial method is
213   particularly used in numerous practical algorithms for attention (Banerjee et al., 2020; Keles et al.,
214   2023; Zhang et al., 2024). For example, see detailed discussions in (Zhang et al., 2024, Section 4.1).
215   Our new algorithm improves on these approaches in part by using theoretically optimal polynomials
      for exponentials, and combining them with the Fast Fourier transform, to give provable guarantees

228   Polynomial Method for Attention.
229   Alman & Song (2023; 2024b) utilize polynomial kernel approximation techniques proposed by
230   Aggarwal & Alman (2022) to speed up both training and inference of a single attention layer, achieving
231   almost linear time complexity. This method is further applied to multi-layer transformer (Liang
232   et al., 2024c), tensor attention (Alman & Song, 2024a; Liang et al., 2024e), LoRA (Hu et al., 2024b),
233   Hopfield model (Hu et al., 2024a), differentially private cross attention (Liang et al., 2024d), and
234   Diffusion Transformer (Hu et al., 2024c). We will also use the polynomials of (Aggarwal & Alman,
235   2022) here.
236   Other Algorithms for Computing Attention.
237
238
      Due to its quadratic time complexity with respect to context length (Vaswani et al., 2017b), the
      attention mechanism has faced criticism. To address this issue, various approaches have been
239
      employed to reduce computational overhead and improve scalability, including sparse attention (Child
240
      et al., 2019; Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020; Hubara et al., 2021; Kurtic et al., 2023; Frantar
241   & Alistarh, 2023; Shi et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024b; Han et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024a), low-rank
242   approximations (Razenshteyn et al., 2016; Li et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2022; Zeng & Lee, 2024; Hu
243   et al., 2024b), and kernel-based methods (Charikar et al., 2020; Liu & Zenke, 2020; Deng et al.,
244   2023a; Zandieh et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024b). Additionally, linear attention has emerged as a
245   significant fast alternative to softmax attention, prompting substantial research in this area (Tsai et al.,
246   2019; Katharopoulos et al., 2020a; Schlag et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Ahn et al.,
247   2024; Shi et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024a).
248   Fast Fourier transform.
249
250
      The Fast Fourier transform algorithm (Cooley & Tukey, 1965) can multiply the n by n Discrete
      Fourier transform matrix times an input vector in O(n log n) time. This algorithm is impactful in
251
      many areas, including image processing, audio processing, telecommunications, seismology, and
252
      polynomial multiplication. There has been much modern reseaqrch focused on further speeding up the
253   Fast Fourier transform, including by decreasing the number of needed arithmetic operations (Sergeev,
254   2017; Alman & Rao, 2023), reducing the sample complexity in the sparse setting (Candes & Tao,
255   2006; Rudelson & Vershynin, 2008; Blumensath & Davies, 2010; Needell & Vershynin, 2010;
256   Bourgain, 2014; Haviv & Regev, 2017; Nakos et al., 2019), and reducing the running time in the
257   sparse setting (Gilbert et al., 2012; Hassanieh et al., 2012a;b; Indyk & Kapralov, 2014; Indyk et al.,
258   2014; Price & Song, 2015; Moitra, 2015; Kapralov, 2016; 2017; Chen & Price, 2019b;a; Kapralov
259   et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023). These algorithmic advances can be directly applied to
260   compute the Fourier transforms which arise in our algorithm below.

513
514   Before we state our lower bound, we present The Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis. The Strong
515   Exponential Time Hypothesis (SETH) was introduced by Impagliazzo and Paturi Impagliazzo &
516   Paturi (2001) over 20 years ago. It is a strengthening of the P ̸= NP conjecture, which asserts that
517   our current best SAT algorithms are roughly optimal:
518   Hypothesis 6.1 (Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis (SETH)). For every ϵ > 0 there is a positive
519   integer k ≥ 3 such that k-SAT on formulas with n variables cannot be solved in O(2(1−ϵ)n ) time,
520   even by a randomized algorithm.
521
522   SETH is a popular conjecture which has been used to prove fine-grained lower bounds for a wide
523
      variety algorithmic problems, as discussed in depth in the survey Williams (2018).

533
534   In this work, we provide an almost linear time algorithm for RoPE attention. RoPE attention is used
535   as a more expressive variant on attention in many applications, but the usual polynomial method
536   approach inherently cannot work for calculating it quickly. We introduced a new way to combine the
537   polynomial method with our “rescaled circulant matrices” and the Fast Fourier transform in order to
538   solve this problem more efficiently. As future work introduces more variants on attention, it will be
539   exciting to explore whether these and other linear algebraic tools can still be used to perform fast
      computations.
