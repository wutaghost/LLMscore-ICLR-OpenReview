                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Discrete audio tokens have recently gained considerable attention for their poten-
               tial to connect audio and language processing, enabling the creation of modern
011
               multimodal large language models. Ideal audio tokens must effectively preserve
012
               phonetic and semantic content along with paralinguistic information, speaker
013            identity, and other details. While several types of audio tokens have been re-
014            cently proposed, identifying the optimal tokenizer for various tasks is challenging
015            due to the inconsistent evaluation settings in existing studies. To address this gap,
016            we release the Discrete Audio and Speech Benchmark (DASB), a comprehen-
017            sive leaderboard for benchmarking discrete audio tokens across a wide range of
018            discriminative tasks, including speech recognition, speaker identification and ver-
019            ification, emotion recognition, keyword spotting, intent classification, event sound
020            detection, and music genre classification as well as generative tasks such as speech
021
               enhancement, separation, and text-to-speech. Our results show that, on average,
               semantic tokens outperform compression tokens across most discriminative and
022
               generative tasks. However, the performance gap between semantic tokens and
023
               standard continuous representations remains substantial, highlighting the need for
024            further research in this field.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Traditional speech and audio processing systems have long relied on handcrafted low-level features
030
      such as Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients and Filterbanks Rabiner & Juang (1993). Recently,
031   self-supervised learning (SSL) led to outstanding performance improvements by learning more com-
032   plex, robust, and general speech features through deep neural networks. Notable models include
033   Wav2Vec2 Baevski et al. (2020), WavLM Chen et al. (2022), and HuBERT Hsu et al. (2021). In
034   all these cases, the rich information in speech and audio signals is encoded into a sequence of con-
035   tinuous vectors. Even though continuous vectors have proven effective in capturing the complex
036   details embedded in speech and audio, there is a growing interest in discrete representations. Dis-
037   crete audio representations, known as audio tokens, transform the original waveform into a finite set
038   of vectors. These tokens are derived using methods such as quantization of self-supervised learning
039
      (SSL) models Polyak et al. (2021); Wells et al. (2022); Chung et al. (2021), neural compression
      techniques (codecs)Zhang et al. (2024); Du et al. (2023), or hybrid approaches Zhang et al. (2024);
040
      Du et al. (2023) that combine both methods.
041
042   What is driving the interest in audio tokens? Arguably, this trend is linked to the remarkable
043   success of autoregressive Large Language Models (LLMs) such as LLama Touvron et al. (2023),
044   PALM Chowdhery et al. (2024), BERT Devlin et al. (2019), and GPT Liu et al. (2023). Unlike
045
      audio, these models operate on text, which is inherently discrete. Inspired by their effectiveness, re-
      searchers are exploring audio language models van den Oord et al. (2017); Rubenstein et al. (2023);
046
      Wang et al. (2023c;d;a;e); Agostinelli et al. (2023); Kreuk et al. (2023) by representing the audio as
047
      a sequence of discrete tokens. Moreover, audio and text tokens can be naturally combined, paving
048   the way for the development of modern multi-modal LLMs Gemini Team, Google (2023) capable
049   of processing audio, text, and visual data. Discrete tokens also simplify audio generation tasks like
050   speech enhancement and synthesis by turning them into classification problems instead of regres-
051   sion models Goodfellow et al. (2016). Finally, they also enable efficient data compression for better
052   transmission and storage. The main drawback of audio tokens is the inevitable loss of information
053   introduced by the discretization process. We ideally aim for audio tokens that preserves crucial
      information of the original waveform, including phonetic and linguistic content, speaker identities,

080

      To address this gap, we introduce the Discrete Audio and Speech Benchmark (DASB). DASB sys-
      tematically assesses various audio tokens across several common speech processing tasks. In partic-
086
      ular, our contribution is the following:
087
088    • We benchmark a diverse set of discrete audio encoders from all three categories: semantic (Dis-
089      crete HuBERT, Discrete WavLM, Discrete Wav2Vec2), compression (EnCodec Défossez et al.
090      (2023), DAC Kumar et al. (2023)), and hybrid (SpeechTokenizer Zhang et al. (2024)).
091    • We assess a broad set of discriminative tasks, including speech, speaker, and emotion recogni-
092      tion, keyword spotting, and intent classification. Moreover, we extend our benchmark to sound
093      and music tasks, such as event sound detection and music genre classification. For generative
094      tasks, we evaluate speech enhancement, separation, and text-to-speech. For a more reliable as-
095      sessment, we consider different downstream architectures for each task, following the insights
096      in Zaiem et al. (2023). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first comprehensive benchmark
097      of audio tokens that covers both discriminative and generative tasks.
098    • We publicly release DASB1 as a modular code repository.

      Several research efforts have recently explored using discrete audio tokens as an alternative to con-
103   tinuous features. Some studies focused on using discrete features for speech recognition and speech
104   translation Chang et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023); Chang et al. (2023), specifically evaluating the
105   tokens obtained from the quantized versions of the HuBERT model. Similarly, Yang et al. Yang
106   et al. (2024) examined discrete features for speech recognition and text-to-speech. Audio tokens

109   investigated the application of semantic tokens to speech enhancement, whereas Erdogan et al. Er-
110   dogan et al. (2023) proposed a hybrid tokenizer called TokenSplit for both speech enhancement and
111   separation.
112
      While previous studies investigated the use of compression or hybrid tokens Wang & Székely (2024);
113
      Wang et al. (2023b); Kharitonov et al. (2023), these efforts were often limited to specific applications
114
      and a few audio tokenizers. In particular, previous benchmarking attempts focused on one category
115   of tokenizers, either semantic or compression-based, and mostly on discriminative or generative
116   tasks. For instance, Puvvada et al. Puvvada et al. (2024) compared the performance of EnCodec
117   and DAC Kumar et al. (2023) for speaker verification, speaker diarization, and speech recognition.
118   Mousavi et al. Mousavi et al. (2024) benchmarked various discriminative and generative tasks with
119   semantic tokens. Wu et al. Wu et al. (2024) provided a comprehensive study of the quality of resyn-
120   thesized sound with compression and hybrid tokenizers. The latter attempt used pretrained models
121   for speech, speaker, and emotion recognition, and assessed how much information is preserved by
122   feeding them resynthesized audio. However, it did not address the direct use of tokenized input
123
      for training downstream tasks, nor did it deeply analyze the role of semantic tokens. Our analyses,
      instead, suggest that semantic tokens outperform other tokenizers.
124
125   To the best of our knowledge, the proposed DASB benchmark is the first to compare several audio
126   tokenizers from three categories (semantic, compression, and hybrid) across many discriminative
127   and generative speech tasks of broad practical interests. Moreover, unlike previous works on discrete
128   audio tokens, we draw inspiration from the findings presented in Zaiem et al. (2023) for reliably
129
      benchmarking continuous SSL representations and we consider different downstream architectures
      for each task. Similar to the approach taken by SUPERB wen Yang et al. (2021) for continuous
130
      representation, we offer a standardized evaluation benchmark where researchers can easily evaluate
131
      novel audio tokens.

134   3     B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
135
136   The pipeline of DASB, illustrated in Fig. 1, consists of three components: Audio Encoder, Down-
137   stream Model, and Audio Decoder. The main features of the considered tokenizers are summarized
138   in Table 1, while Figure 2 reports the time and memory resources required by both encoders and
139
      decoders. The following subsections describe each module in detail.
140
141   3.1   D ISCRETE AUDIO E NCODER
142
      The audio encoder converts the audio signal into a sequence of discrete tokens. It is pretrained
143
      on large amounts of unlabeled data and remains frozen during the training of downstream tasks.
144
      Different encoders may compress the information in the original waveform at different rates. The
145   compression level is measured by the bitrate, defined as:
146
147                                          bitrate = log2 V · C · R,                                    (1)
148
      where C is the number of codebooks, V is the number of vectors in each codebook (vocabulary), and
149
      R is the rate of codes per second. It is worth mentioning that a single sequence of tokens might be
150   insufficient to capture the rich and complex information embedded in speech signals. The encoders
151   thus often output multiple discrete sequences, with each sequence corresponding to a different code-
152   book C. The encoders can operate at different bitrates simply by adjusting the number of codebooks
153   C. For a fairer comparison, we define three different distinct bitrate ranges that we have identified
154   from the literature Valin et al. (2012); Dietz et al. (2015); Zeghidour et al. (2021): low (0-1.5 kbps),
155   medium (2.9-6 kbps), and high (24 kbps). We consider this approach to prevent the trivial conclusion
156   that some audio tokens perform better than others simply due to a higher bitrate.
157   The design of DASB is flexible, allowing for easy integration and benchmarking of various tokeniz-
158   ers. Using the terminology from Borsos et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024), we categorize audio to-
159   kens into three classes: semantic, compression, and hybrid tokenizers. Semantic tokens Polyak et al.
160   (2021); Wells et al. (2022); Chung et al. (2021) are generated by clustering or quantizing layers from
161   SSL models Baevski et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2022); Hsu et al. (2021). The tokenization process
      typically involves selecting specific layers from a pretrained SSL model and applying the k-means

172   algorithm to group their representations. Semantic tokens primarily capture high-level information,
173
      such as phonetic, semantic, and syntactic information. They are not optimized for waveform re-
      construction, making them potentially better suited for discriminative tasks like speech recognition.
174
      Recent studies, however, have shown that semantic tokens can also be effective in generative tasks
175
      Wang et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Mousavi et al. (2024). We adopt the tokenization algorithm
176   proposed in Mousavi et al. (2024). In particular, we consider three widely-used open-source SSL
177   models: Wav2Vec2-large, WavLM-large, and HuBERT-large, each composed of 24 layers. Then,
178   we cluster six of these layers using the k-means algorithm and select two layers from the lower part
179   (1, 3) to capture low-level information, two from the middle layers (7, 12), and two from the higher
180   layers (18, 23) to encode content and meaning as well.
181
      Compression tokens Zeghidour et al. (2021); Défossez et al. (2023); Kumar et al. (2023) are mainly
182   used for audio compression. They are trained to accurately reconstruct the original audio, mak-
183   ing them potentially suitable for audio generation tasks. We integrated two publicly available
184   compression-based tokenizers in our baseline. EnCodec Défossez et al. (2023) has three main com-
185   ponents: (i) an encoder network E consisting of a 1D convolution followed by a two-layer LSTM
186   that processes the audio input and produces a latent representation z; (ii) a quantization layer Q
187   that compresses z into zq using Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) Zeghidour et al. (2021), where
188   distinct codebooks quantizes residuals in multiple steps; and (iii) a decoder network G that mirrors
189   the encoder and reconstructs the time-domain signal x̂ from zq . The system is trained end-to-end
190
      to minimize reconstruction loss over time and frequency domains. It also adopts a perceptual loss
      using discriminators at different resolutions. EnCodec offers multiple models at low to medium bi-
191
      trates (1.5 to 24 kbps). DAC Kumar et al. (2023) is an improved version of EnCodec. It combines
192
      advances in high-fidelity audio generation with better vector quantization techniques from the image
193   domain, along with improved adversarial and reconstruction losses. DAC also supports quantizer
194   dropout, allowing a single model to support variable bitrates.
195
196
      Hybrid tokenizers Zhang et al. (2024); Du et al. (2023) unify semantic and acoustic tokens by disen-
      tangling different aspects of speech information hierarchically. SpeechTokenizer Zhang et al. (2024)
197
      is a unified speech tokenizer for large language models. It combines semantic and acoustic tokens,
198
      separating different speech information across RVQ layers. The model is based on RVQ-GANs,
199   similar to EnCodec, and uses a convolutional encoder-decoder network from EnCodec. A two-layer
200   BiLSTM replaces the original two-layer LSTM to improve semantic modeling. A semantic teacher
201   guides the first RVQ quantizer, allowing the first layer tokens to capture content information ef-
202   fectively. With a residual structure, the subsequent quantizers capture the remaining paralinguistic
203   information. SpeechTokenizer employs HuBERT as the semantic teacher to capture content infor-
204   mation. The training procedure maximizes the cosine similarity between the RVQ layer outputs
205   and the semantic teacher representations. HuBERT Layer 9 units represent semantic tokens, while
206   EnCodec codes represent acoustic tokens.

211   In this step, we employ neural networks to solve supervised tasks of common interest. To achieve
212   this, we first assign each discrete token to a corresponding embedding vector through a lookup table.

214   enabling the model to adjust the importance of codebooks for the specific task of interest. The
215   attention mechanism consists of a simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that takes the embeddings
      of the audio tokens as input. The MLP generates a score for each selected codebook, which is

245   The combined representations ht are fed into neural models designed for different tasks. The down-
246   stream models are jointly trained with their attention and embedding layers in an end-to-end fashion.
247   For discriminative tasks, the model outputs either a single prediction (e.g., for emotion recognition)
248   or a sequence of predictions (e.g., for speech recognition). For generative tasks (e.g., speech en-
249   hancement), the neural network outputs the targeted tokens per codebook, with the output shape
250
      being C × L, where L is the sequence length. The predicted audio tokens are converted into an
      audio waveform via an audio decoder, as explained in the next section.
251
252   Our benchmark aims to assess audio tokens using relatively simple downstream architectures. The
253   rationale behind this approach is to ensure that the evaluation focuses on the quality of the audio
254   tokens themselves. If the downstream architectures were too sophisticated, they could potentially
255   ”compensate” for weaker audio tokens, which would impair a fair comparison. This approach is
256
      consistent with the SUPERB wen Yang et al. (2021) benchmark used for evaluating speech self-
      supervised models, and other benchmarks (e.g., USB Wang et al. (2022) and MP3S Zaiem et al.
257
      (2023)). Our intent is thus not to claim state-of-the-art results for specific downstream tasks but
258
      rather to provide a fair comparison of different tokenizers. By using a simple architecture for all
259   tokenizers, we maintain the fairness of the comparison.
260
261
262   3.3           D ISCRETE AUDIO D ECODER
263
264   The decoder, used for generative tasks only, converts the predicted tokens into audio signals. The
265   decoder is frozen during training. The choice of decoder depends on the encoder used in the first
266   step. For compression and hybrid tokenizers, we use their built-in decoder. For semantic tokens, we
267   use the scalable vocoder proposed in Mousavi et al. (2024), which is a modified HiFi-GAN Yang
268   et al. (2023) pretrained with LibriSpeech-960h Korvas et al. (2014). The scalable vocoder accepts
269   a variable number of multi-layer semantic tokens as input and can handle different bitrates using a
      layer dropout mechanism.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      In the following sections, we describe the discriminative and generative tasks considered in our
273   experiments. For a more reliable evaluation, we tested two downstream architectures for each task.
274   For detailed information about the hyperparameters used in each experiment, refer to Appendix ??.
275
276   4.1   D ISCRIMINATIVE TASKS
277
278   For the downstream architectures and training procedures, we follow the best-performing ap-
279   proaches for classic continuous self-supervised representations proposed in Zaiem et al. (2023).
280
281   • Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR): The goal of ASR is converting speech signals into writ-
282
        ten text. We address two ASR tasks. The first task involves English ASR using the popular Lib-
        riSpeech dataset Korvas et al. (2014). Training and validation are performed on the train-clean-
283
        100 and dev-clean subsets, respectively, while testing is conducted on the test-clean and test-other
284
        subsets. The downstream architecture for this task consists of two layers of Bidirectional Long
285     Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) followed by a linear layer for mapping audio to characters. The
286     second architecture utilizes ContextNet Han et al. (2020) with unitary strides to maintain the
287     frame rate of the encoder models. Additionally, we explore low-resource languages, specifically
288     Welsh (Cymraeg) and Basque (Euskera) datasets extracted from CommonVoice 17.0 Ardila et al.
289     (2020). Here, we evaluate the performance using both the BiLSTM architecture and a two-layer
290     dense neural network mapping frame representations to character probabilities. We use the Word
291     Error Rate (WER) as the error metric for all ASR tasks.
292   • Speaker Identification/Verification (SID, SV): Speaker Identification involves classifying each
293     utterance by its speaker identity as a multi-class classification, with the same predefined set of
294     speakers for both training and testing. The evaluation metric is the accuracy. Automatic Speaker
295     Verification (ASV), instead, involves training a binary classifier to determine whether the speakers
296     in a pair of utterances are the same. The evaluation metric adopted in this case is the equal error
297     rate (EER). We use the widely-used VoxCeleb1 Nagrani et al. (2017) train and test splits for both
298
        tasks. First, we test the X-vector Snyder et al. (2018) architecture with AM-Softmax Wang et al.
        (2018) loss for training the speaker embeddings. For verification, we use the cosine similarity
299
        between speaker representations. As a second architecture, we replace the X-vectors with an
300
        ECAPA-TDNN neural network Desplanques et al. (2020).
301
302
      • Emotion Recognition (ER): The task involves predicting one of the four classes: happy, sad, an-
        gry, and neutral. We use the popular IEMOCAP Busso et al. (2008) dataset, which contains about
303
        10k samples from 10 speakers. As a first architecture, we directly input the representations into
304
        a linear classification layer after averaging them along the time axis. For the second downstream
305     architecture, we use ECAPA-TDNN. The evaluation metric is the accuracy.
306
      • Intent Classification (IC): This task aims to determine the intention or purpose given utterance
307
        a speech recording. In particular, we here aim to classify each utterance into one of 18 scenarios,
308
        including calendar, email, and alarm. For this task, we utilize the SLURP dataset Bastianelli
309     et al. (2020), which comprises around 72k audio recordings of single-turn user interactions with
310     a home assistant. We employ ECAPA-TDNN and a two-layer BiLSTM (followed by a linear
311     classifier) as downstream architectures. We evaluate the performance using the accuracy.
312
      • Keyword Spotting (KS): Keyword Spotting involves detecting predefined keywords by classify-
313     ing utterances into a set of specified words. We use the Speech Commands dataset v1.0 Warden
314     (2018) for this task, as done in SUPERB. The dataset includes ten classes of keywords, a class for
315     silence, and an unknown class to account for false positives. We employ both the X-vector and
316     ECAPA-TDNN architectures. The evaluation metric is the accuracy.
317
      • Event Sound Detection (ES): This task involves classifying audio clips into 50 different sound
318     event categories, using the ESC-50 dataset Piczak (2015), which contains 2,000 labeled 5-second
319     recordings across 50 sound classes (40 samples per class). We employed two downstream ar-
320     chitectures: linear and ECAPA-TDNN, with accuracy as the evaluation metric. To compare
321     semantic tokenizers, we used self-supervised models trained on audio, contrasting them with
322     compression-based tokens from large sound and music datasets. For sound classification, we uti-
323     lized the pre-trained CNN14 modelKong et al. (2020) (trained on VGGSound with SimCLR) and
        applied k-means clustering across four layers for medium bitrate, and two layers for low bitrate.

324
      • Music Genre Classification (MG): This task focuses on classifying music clips into 10 genre
325     categories using the GTZAN dataset Tzanetakis & Cook (2002), which contains 100 audio files
326     per genre, each 30 seconds long. Similar to event sound detection, we used two downstream ar-
327     chitectures: linear and ECAPA-TDNN, with accuracy as the evaluation metric. For semantic tok-
328     enizers, we used the MERT Li et al. (2023) model, which has a similar architecture to wav2vec2.
329     The same bitrate settings—low and medium—used for discrete self-supervised models in speech
330     tasks were applied here.
331
332   4.2   G ENERATIVE TASKS
333
      • Speech Enhancement (SE): Speech enhancement aims to improve audio quality by cleaning up
334     noisy input recordings. For this task, we utilize the popular VoiceBank dataset Valentini-Botinhao
335     et al. (2016). We employ two downstream architectures: a non-autoregressive Conformer en-
336     coder Gulati et al. (2020), and a convolutional recurrent deep neural network (CRDNN). The
337     input tokens are extracted from the noisy signal, while target tokens from the clean one. Training
338     is performed using the cross-entropy loss. The speech quality is assessed using the deep noise
339     suppression mean opinion score (DNSMOS) Reddy et al. (2022). The intelligibility is evaluated
340     through the differential word error rate (dWER) Wang et al. (2021), which measures the WER
341     between the transcribed enhanced signal and the transcribed target signal. The transcriptions are
342
        obtained using the small version of Whisper Radford et al. (2022). Additionally, to measure
        speaker fidelity, we use the cosine similarity (SpkSim) between X-vectors extracted from the en-
343
        hanced signal and the target signal using the base variant of WavLM Chen et al. (2022) fine-tuned
344
        for speaker verification.
345
346
      • Speech Separation (SS): Speech separation aims to isolate individual voices from an audio
        recording containing multiple speakers. For this task, we use the Libri2Mix dataset Cosentino
347
        et al. (2020), which contains mixtures of two overlapping speakers. We employ two downstream
348
        architectures: a non-autoregressive Conformer encoder Gulati et al. (2020), and a convolutional
349     recurrent deep neural network (CRDNN). The input tokens are extracted from the mixture, while
350     target tokens from each of the two sources. Training is performed using the cross-entropy loss
351     with permutation invariant training Kolbæk et al. (2017). To measure performance, we employ
352     the same metrics as speech enhancement.
353   • Text-to-Speech (TTS): The task involves generating raw speech audio from a given text input.
354     For downstream architectures, we consider both a small and a large autoregressive Transformer
355     Vaswani et al. (2017). We train all models on the LJSpeech dataset Ito (2017). To assess the
356     speech quality, we use a pretrained UTokyo-SaruLab System (UTMOS) Takaaki et al. (2022),
357     which is specifically designed for TTS and trained to predict human Mean Opinion scores. To
358     measure pronunciation accuracy, we use the dWER metric. This involves comparing the transcrip-
359     tions provided by a speech recognizer for the synthesized speech sample with the transcriptions
360     from the ground truth. As for enhancement and separation, we considered the small version of
361
        Whisper Radford et al. (2022).

452    5.2   I MPACT OF B ITRATE
453
       We also study the impact of different bitrates on the performance of the tokenizers. Tables 2 and
454
       3 show that a medium bitrate achieves the best results for both discriminative and generative tasks.
455    Interestingly, higher bitrates, when available (e.g., for EnCodec and DAC), tend to degrade per-
456    formance. While higher bitrates can potentially preserve more information, they also increase the
457    output dimensionality of the model, making the task more challenging to solve. In some cases, we
458    found the task so challenging with high bitrates that the model did not converge, as observed in the
459    case of TTS. It is worth noting that semantic tokens have a lower bitrate than compression tokens,
460    as shown in Table 1. For example, in the medium bitrate range, discrete WavLM has a bitrate of 2.9
461    kbps, while EnCodec has 6.0 kbps. This difference is due to the varying number of codebooks (6
462    vs. 8) and sampling rates (16 kHz vs. 24 kHz). Despite their lower bitrate, semantic tokens provide
463
       better performance.
464    Another aspect we investigate is the efficiency of the encoders and decoders, as this could impact
465    some applications. Figure 2 shows the time and memory usage for each encoder-decoder pair across
466    all bitrate ranges. For semantic tokens, the encoder is a large neural network and is computationally
467    demanding. In contrast, the decoders are based on a compact HiFi-GAN model and are very effi-
468    cient. For streaming tasks Wu et al. (2023) where time and memory are critical, EnCodec turned out
469
       to be a better candidate due to the efficiency of both its encoder and decoder models.
470
471    5.3   A NALYSIS OF D ISCRETE AUDIO D ECODER
472
       Finally, we present a comparative evaluation of the decoders in Table 4 (left). The decoder evalu-
473
       ation is conducted on the LibriSpeech test-clean subset using a speech re-synthesis task, where we
474    extract the tokens from each discrete audio encoder and reconstruct the speech using the associated
475    decoders. Then, we evaluate the reconstructed speech based on speaker similarity, Mean Opinion
476    Score (MOS), and differential Word Error Rate (dWER). The goal of this experiment is to establish
477    if a given system is able to provide a high-fidelity reconstruction of the input audio after encoding it
478    in the discrete space. This is especially important to establish for generative tasks.
479
       The results show that the built-in decoders of compression tokens outperform other models in pre-
480    serving speaker similarity, further confirming that current semantic tokens do not adequately pre-
481    serve speaker information. Compression-based tokens also achieve better dWER scores. However,
482    in terms of speech quality (assessed with DNSMOS), there are no significant differences between
483    semantic and compression-based tokens. This trend indicates that while semantic tokens produce
484    good-quality audio, they may be slightly more prone to semantic degradation (e.g., mispronuncia-
485    tions of words or phonemes). As expected, continuous baselines perform better than their discrete
       counterparts. Additional analysis on low and high settings can be found in Appendix D.

515   6     C ONCLUSION
516
517   This paper introduces DASB, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the performance of
518   discrete audio tokens across diverse tasks of broad interest. We employ various evaluation metrics,
519   downstream architectures, and bitrates for more robust comparisons. Interestingly, our findings
520   reveal that semantic tokens outperform, on average, compression tokens in both generative and
521   discriminative tasks. In particular, discrete WavLM emerged as the top-performing model, making
522   it a natural candidate for adoption in multi-modal text+audio LLMs. A significant performance gap,
523
      however, persists when compared to traditional self-supervised continuous representations. This
      highlights the need for further research, which we believe is essential for better incorporating audio
524
      tokens into large multimodal language models.
525
526   One limitation we encountered is the proprietary nature of some audio tokenizers, such as Sound-
527   stream Zeghidour et al. (2021), which are not publicly accessible. Additionally, the benchmark is
528   currently limited to a few sound and music tasks, but we plan to further extend DASB to include
529
      more music and sound processing tasks, such as music generation. Our goal is to help the research
      community establish a shared benchmark and evaluation protocol for discrete audio representations.
530
      We will thus keep expanding DASB by continuously incorporating novel tokenizers and tasks.

920
      In this section, we expand on the results from Section 5.3 by including low and high bitrates in ad-
921   dition to the medium bitrate. Additionally, for speaker similarity, we measure the cosine similarity
922   between X-vectors extracted from the reconstructed and target signals using two different models:
923   WavLM (SpkSim WavLM) and ECAPA-TDNN (SpkSim ECAPA), both fine-tuned for speaker ver-
924   ification. When analyzing low and high bitrate settings, distinct trends emerge compared to the
925   medium bitrate. At low bitrates, the models perform worse in terms of speaker similarity, MOS,
926   and dWER, as expected. This is particularly pronounced for compression-based decoders, where
927   the degradation is more significant in terms of dWER. In contrast, at high bitrate, there is an overall
928   small improvement in all metrics. In particular, the DAC model consistently outperforms EnCodec
929
      across all evaluated metrics, even for high bitrate settings.
930   The analysis shows that bitrate significantly impacts the performance of discrete decoders. Higher
931   bitrates better preserve speech characteristics and result in lower error rates, while lower bitrates
932   degrade these aspects. This highlights the trade-off between bitrate and speech synthesis quality.

       E     A DDITIONAL R ESULTS
973
974
       Tables 11 - 14 show the results obtained with 2 different downstream architectures. Note that table
975    9 indicates the first and second architectures explored for each task. For the continuous baseline,
976    we follow the same architecture as the discrete experiments except for TTS. For the TTS continuous
977    baseline, we use a modified Tacotron2 Shen et al. (2018) architecture enhanced with guided attention
978    Tachibana et al. (2018) that predicts SSL representations instead of Mel spectrograms.
979
       Varying the architecture of the downstream decoder leads to significant variations in task perfor-
980
       mance. For ASR tasks, BiLSTM performs better. For classification tasks, ECAPA-TDNN shows
981    the best performance, except for keyword spotting where X-vector is slightly better. For speech
982    enhancement and separation, Conformer shows the best performance. For TTS, a notable pattern
983    is observed: semantic tokens yield the best results with shallow models, while acoustic and hy-
984    brid tokens perform better with deeper models but still underperform compared to semantic tokens.
985    One reason might be that discrete SSL models retain higher-level features closer to phonetic tran-
986    scriptions, requiring lower-capacity models to capture the relationship between raw text and such
987    representations. Higher-capacity models can lead to slower training and potential overfitting. In con-
988    trast, shallow models appear to underfit acoustic tokens, resulting in high dWERs and speech-like
989
       sounds with only surface resemblance to the original sentence, rather than intelligible speech.
