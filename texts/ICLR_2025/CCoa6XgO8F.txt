009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Dataset distillation produces a compressed synthetic dataset that approximates a
012            large dataset or other learning task. A model can be trained on a distillation in a
013            single gradient descent step. Conventional wisdom suggests that single-step learn-
014            ing is not generalizable and should yield poor performance; yet, distillation defies
015            these expectations with good approximations of direct-task training for a large
016
               distribution of models. In order to understand how distilled datasets can perform
               one-shot learning, we examine the distilled data instances and the cost surfaces
017
               produced by the distilled datasets. We demonstrate that the distilled dataset not
018
               only mimics features of the true dataset but also produces cost surfaces such that
019            one-step training leads models from the initialization space into local minima of
020            the true task’s cost surface. This shows how one-step learning’s counter-intuitive
021            success is not only reasonable, but the expected outcome of dataset distillation.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   What makes a good dataset? For machine learning, the distribution of data and features in a dataset
027
      should approximate those of the underlying real-world distribution. Thus, datasets are generally
      produced by sampling from the real-world distribution. While an effective solution, a large number
028
      of samples must be taken to provide an adequate sampling of the real distribution. For example, the
029
      training set of MNIST, often considered a toy problem in image recognition, contains 60,000 exam-
030   ples. Large datasets are information-sparse, which makes learning costly. This cost can be reduced
031   by compressing the dataset into an information-dense representation using dataset distillation.
032
033
      Dataset distillation produces a small synthetic dataset that approximates a larger learning task.
      The synthetic dataset can be highly compressed and learnable in a single step of gradient descent.
034
      Training a predictive model on the synthetic dataset approximates training the model on the large
035
      dataset—the distillation-trained model should perform comparably to the model trained on the orig-
036   inal task.
037
038   Dataset distillation was introduced by Wang et al. (2018), and similar methods using generative
039
      models were introduced by Such et al. (2020) and Huang & Zhang (2021). While both use a meta-
      learning approach, other learning methods such as gradient matching (Zhao et al., 2021), trajectory
040
      matching (Cazenavette et al., 2022), distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023), differentiable
041
      Siamese augmentation (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), and kernel ridge-regression (Nguyen et al., 2021)
042   have been proposed. In this work, we focus on the original meta-learning formulation due to its
043   independence from expert models and trajectories, as well as its simplicity. Using learned soft labels
044   with the original meta-learning approach improves performance (Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021b)
045   and allows for the highest-possible compression (Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021a). Use cases of
046   distillation include dataset compression, neural architecture search acceleration (Such et al., 2020),
047   data poisoning attacks (Wang et al., 2018), and data anonymity (Dong et al., 2022).
048   Dataset distillation can provide extraordinary compression—reducing the 60,000 instance MNIST
049   task to 6 instances, and reducing the reinforcement learning Centipede environment to 10 instances.
050   It would seem that compressing a dataset into a representation 10,000 times smaller, resulting in
051   fewer instances than the number of classes, should be able to yield only the coarsest approximation
052   of the original training dataset, especially when learned in a single large gradient descent step.
053   However, experimentation proves that distillation can provide close approximation to training on the
      real task. This begs the question: what is the distillation actually learning and how can such results be

131       2.1   P RODUCING D ISTILLATIONS
132
133       Distillation is performed through a meta-learning process that creates a dataset such that: 1) it
134       contains compressed information from a targeted training task in its synthetic data instances, and
135
          2) a model trained on the distilled data approximates the performance of a model trained on the
          targeted training task. Compression and approximation are achieved during the distillation training
136
          process.
137
138       A visualization of task-agnostic distillation is provided in Figure 1 and the algorithm is provided
139       as Algorithm 1. Since we use a single inner learning step in distillation training, the distiller is
140       trained such that a predictive model will be fully trained on the distilled dataset in a single step of
141
          SGD. In our experiments, the distilled dataset consists of a parameterized data instance tensor, a
          parameterized label tensor consisting of soft label scores rather than hard class labels, and an inner
142
          learning rate parameter. Since the synthetic task uses soft labels, we use mean squared error loss
143
          as the inner loss function. The meta-loss function depends on the training task: for MNIST and
144       CIFAR-10 we use cross entropy loss, and for the RL environments we use PPO (Schulman et al.,
145       2017).
146
147
          Using PPO as an outer objective requires the addition of an auxiliary critic model trained alongside
          the distiller (rather than being reinitialized each outer iteration with the inner learner). Just like in
148
          standard PPO, the critic is only used in RL training, not in evaluation nor in training a model on the
149
          distilled data. In addition, the RL data gathering is removed from the inner-outer learning structure
150       and the learner is reverted to the same initialization over a set of PPO policy epochs to allow for the
151       trust region protection of PPO to respond to changes in the distiller that occur throughout learning.

154
155
          To represent distillations of supervised classification, we use MNIST and CIFAR-10 training
156       datasets. They both contain 10 image classes: handwritten digits and real-world objects, respec-
157       tively. MNIST contains 60,000 training instances, and CIFAR-10 contains 50,000. Both are distilled
158       to the minimum-sized distillation of 6 instances (due to having 10 classes (Sucholutsky & Schon-
159       lau, 2021a)) to be learned in one gradient descent step. The synthetic data instances of both tasks
160       match the shape of the respective dataset’s instances: 1x28x28 for MNIST, 3x32x32 for CIFAR-10.
161       We use a small convolutional network with two convolution layers and a linear output layer, with
          ReLU activations. The convolutional kernels are 3x3; the first layer has 16 filters, the second has

163   models sampled from this distribution achieve an average validation accuracy on MNIST of 84.4%

195   3.1   E XAMINING D ISTILLED DATA I NSTANCES
196
197   Distilled dataset instances can be used as interpretability artifacts; however, the interpretability is
198   limited Wang et al. (2018). With minimum-sized distillations, the learned features should all be
199   present in the few data instances; however, high levels of compression can obscure these features
200   to human observers. In simple environments, like cart-pole and its multidimensional extension, the
201
      resulting minimum-sized distillation is clearly interpretable: the agent should move the cart towards
      the direction the pole is leaning (see Figure 2). For larger-dimensional tasks, such as image recogni-
202
      tion tasks, the resulting distillation may have some identifiable features of the labeled classes, but are
203
      not clearly interpretable (see Figure 3a). For more complicated tasks, such as distillations of Atari
204   Centipede, the distillation may appear to be random noise to human observers, despite containing
205   information that can be used by a model to learn the task. There may be ideal hyperparameters or
206   other techniques that can optimize interpretability of a distillation; however, it is well accepted that
207   distilled instances do not need to resemble real data instances, and that forcing resemblance may
208   require increased computational costs or may decrease the effectiveness of the distillation (Wang
209   et al., 2018).
210   Examining individual instances provides insight into how synthetic soft labels allow a model to be
211   trained to distinguish c classes (or actions) in fewer than c training instances. For 2D cart-pole in
212   Figure 2, each synthetic state has 1 action class with high probability mass, while the remaining
213   action (up) splits high probability mass between the first two instances. This is sufficient for the
214   learners to distinguish all four actions, thus allowing for what Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021a)
215   call “less than one-shot learning”. The 6 distilled MNIST digits in Figure 3a similarly distribute
      probability mass over 6 instances to train learners to distinguish the 10 classes of MNIST.

244   Focusing on the distilled data instances does not take into account how distillation meta-learning is
245
      performed. Distillation does not directly optimize data instances to resemble the original dataset or
      to mimic its features; rather, the distilled instances are optimized such that, when used in learning,
246
      they can cause a model to perform well on the original task. These distilled instances can be distorted
247
      beyond recognition. In addition, the instances are not learned independently, but as a single unit.
248   In other words, the optimization structure of the original dataset—the cost surface—may be more
249   informative about what distillation learns. Thus, in addition to interpreting dataset distillation as a
250   process that produces compressions of the original dataset’s features, we interpret it as a process
251   that produces cost surfaces (also known as loss landscapes) that approximate features of the original
252   dataset’s cost surface, when computed on in-distribution learner models λ ∈ Λ. This perspective is
253   vital for understanding how one-step learning on distilled datasets can achieve high performance on
254   a variety of models.

256   3.2   E XAMINING D ISTILLED C OST S URFACE
257
258   The cost surface, or loss landscape, is a structure upon which optimization is performed, and thus
259   provides the best view of an entire learning task. Though generally SGD is used to approximate
260   the loss at a given set of parameters in training, the cost surface is a vital structure for interpreting
261   learning by (stochastic) gradient descent optimization. The cost surface is constructed in R|θ| , which
262   is the parameter space of a given model or flattened neural network architecture. Each dimension
263
      represents a parameter and each point in the space represents a parameterization of the model. The
      value at that point is the sum cost over the entire dataset of the given parameterized model.
264
265   Due to the high dimensionality of parameter spaces in deep learning, visualizing cost surfaces in
266   2D necessitates the removal of information. Traditional cost surface visualization utilizes two axes,
267   near-orthogonal in parameter space, with the cost represented by either a third axis or colored con-
268   tours. A set of trained parameters, θ̂ is selected as the center point. Due to the high dimensionality
269   of the parameter space, one can sample two random vectors δ, η ∈ R|θ| for axes and can safely
      assume that the two vectors are near-orthogonal: δ T η ≈ 0. Li et al. (2018) use this approach, but

349   Deep learning optimizes towards a minimum of the cost surface of a model on a learning task. A
350   distilled dataset should therefore be constructed such that models initialized at any in-distribution
351   point should approach a minimum of the true dataset’s cost surface. We examine loss surfaces of
352   models trained on distilled datasets to determine how distillation enforces this behavior.
353   We also posit that distillation can be used to cheaply produce approximations of a task’s cost surface,
354   due to the compressed nature of the distilled dataset. We suggest that this approximation can be more
355   quickly searched for potential minima of the true cost surface, as the sum loss of some θ can be
356   calculated in one forward pass of the network, rather than many. We demonstrate this by providing
357   the time it took to produce visualizations of each task’s surface (distilled and original tasks), when
358   using our machine with an AMD Ryzen 7 2700X Eight-Core Processor CPU and a GeForce GTX
359

      there was no explicit parallelization. Loss was computed on the GPU, all other operations including

      producing the figures was performed on one CPU core.

363   3.2.1   S UPERVISED L EARNING D ISTILLATIONS
364
      We examine the cost surfaces of models trained on supervised learning datasets (MNIST and
365
      CIFAR-10) and models trained on a corresponding distilled dataset. The results are shown in Fig-
366   ure 4. The synthetic surface places minima approximately around minima in the original dataset’s
367   surface. This leads the model to the targeted minima when trained on the synthetic dataset. The min-
368   imum found by optimizing on the original dataset is off-centered in both distilled tasks, which may
369   explain the drop in performance of distillation-trained models. The distilled dataset attempts to ap-
370   proximate certain high-performing minima of the original dataset. Thus we can consider distillation
371   training as a search problem: the original task’s cost surface is searched by training the randomly
372   initialized learners on the distilled dataset, and the distilled dataset is optimized to lead these learn-
373   ers to better points on the cost surface. It is reasonable that not all minima are well-represented
374
      on the synthetic cost surface, due to minima being missed in the search or due to limitations in the
      representational power of the limited-sized distilled dataset.
375

377   than those produced using the costs of the original task. It took approximately 13 minutes to pro-
      duce an MNIST or CIFAR-10 surface with 100 sampled points, while the distilled surfacse took

417
      auxiliary models from end-task training and sidesteps the issue of selecting which experience sets
418

419
420   Each architecture tested against a distilled dataset produces a different cost surface. While our dis-
421   tillation training used one training architecture per class, we demonstrate cost surfaces produced by
422
      a different architecture trained on our Centipede distillation in Figure 6. Since the minimum found
      by distillation training is a minimum on Centipede, we posit that the distillation is not overfitting to
423
      a single architecture, but rather generalizing. Thus, we should not think of distillation as producing
424
      one cost surface, but a distribution of cost surfaces over a distribution of models. This implies that
425   the distillation is learning general-use knowledge, rather than simply placing minima on a small set
426   of cost surfaces. The degree of generalization that distillation achieves is left for future work.

509   4    C ONCLUSION
510
511   We have examined dataset distillation by treating each distilled instance as an individual learning
512   artifact and by treating the dataset as a singular structure and plotting cost surfaces. Examining indi-
513   vidual distilled instances can reveal salient features for classes for image recognition and strategies
514
      to base policies around for interactive environments. The distribution of labels demonstrates how
      distillation can train a model to distinguish c classes in fewer than c instances.
515
516   By examining the cost surfaces, we have demonstrated that the distilled dataset approximates min-
517   ima of the original dataset. This interpretation provides an intuitive explanation for the counter-
518   intuitive level of approximation achievable with massive compression of training tasks through dis-
519   tillation. In addition, distillations can be used as cheap approximations of the true cost surface and
520
      can be calculated in just one forward pass per point on the surface. Because the cart-pole land-
      scapes show that not all distillations will provide minimum-matching to approximate a landscape,
521
      testing losses on the original task around the distillation-trained point may be required to determine
522
      if minimum-matching is occurring.
523
524   This work focused on four deep learning benchmark tasks: MNIST, CIFAR-10, cart-pole, and Atari
525   Centipede. Expanding to more complex tasks as future work would serve to further strengthen our
526
      arguments. We also focus only on the locations of minima of the cost surfaces; a deeper examination
      of the geometry and curvature of the space, as well as examination of other structures produced by
527
      model and dataset such as the model manifold may yield additional insights into the unreasonable
528
      effectiveness of one-step learning on distilled data.
