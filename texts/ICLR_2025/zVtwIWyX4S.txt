009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Label Smoothing (LS) aims to prevent Neural Networks from making overcon-
012            fident predictions and improve generalization. Due to its effectiveness, it has
013            become an indispensable ingredient in the training recipe for tasks such as Im-
014            age Recognition and Neural Machine Translation. Despite this, previous work
015            shows it encourages an overly tight cluster in the feature space, which ‘erases’
016            the similarity information of individual examples. A more recent study empir-
017
               ically shows that LS also makes the network more confident in its wrong pre-
               dictions. By isolating the loss induced by Label Smoothing into a combina-
018
               tion of a regularization term and an error-enhancement term, we reveal the un-
019
               derlying mechanism behind such defects of Label Smoothing. To remedy this,
020            we present a solution called Max Suppression (MaxSup), which consistently ap-
021            plies the intended regularization effect during training, independent of the cor-
022            rectness of prediction. By examining the learned features, we demonstrate that
023            MaxSup successfully enlarges intra-class variations, while improving inter-class
024            separability. We further conduct experiments on Image Classification and Ma-
025            chine Translation tasks, validating the superiority of Max Suppression. The code
026            implementation is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
027            Maximum-Suppression-Regularization-DB0C.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   In multi-class classification (Russakovsky et al., 2015; LeCun, 1998), different categories are widely
044   represented by one-hot vectors, assuming them to be cardinal and orthogonal. However, many
045   classes often share common low-level features (Zeiler and Fergus, 2014; Silla and Freitas, 2011) or
046   high-level similarities (Chen et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2022; Novack et al., 2023). The assumption of
047   orthogonality underlying the one-hot labels apparently deviates from this observation, which tends
048   to produce over-confident classifiers with reduced generalization ability (Guo et al., 2020).
049   To prevent the network from being over-confident about its predictions and thus generalize better,
050   Szegedy et al. (2016) proposed Label Smoothing (LS), which replaces the one-hot label with a con-
051   vex combination of the original label and a vector of ones. Thanks to its simplicity and effectiveness,
052   it has been widely adopted for tasks such as Image Recognition (He et al., 2016; Touvron et al., 2021;
053   Liu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022b) and Neural Machine Translation (Gao et al., 2020; Alves et al.,
      2023). Despite the improved classification performance, Müller et al. (2019) identified an inherent


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      flaw in Label Smoothing: it tends to compress samples of the same class into overly tight clusters
055   in the feature space, which consequently ‘erases’ the similarity information that an individual ex-
056   ample has to different classes. Such information loss might not be well reflected in the classification
057   performance, but it potentially harms the effectiveness of the learned representation in broader
058   downstream applications, such as linear transfer accuracy (Kornblith et al., 2021). More recently,
059   Zhu et al. (2022) empirically identified that Label Smoothing results in more confident errors,
060   but the reason behind such an issue is not yet understood.
061   In this paper, we reveal that the part of the training objective introduced by Label Smoothing sur-
062   prisingly contains two problematic components: a regularization component that only functions as
063   expected when the predictions are correct, and an error-enhancement term that emerges when the
064   predictions are incorrect, encouraging the network to become overconfident in its wrong predictions.
065   In line with Zhu et al. (2022), the term ”overconfidence” in our work specifically refers to the net-
066   work’s confidence in its top-1 prediction, which is different from the overconfidence in the context
067   of model calibration. This work uncovers the underlying mechanism of the recently observed
068   defect of Label Smoothing (Zhu et al., 2022), and shows that it is also the cause of the overly
069
      tight clusters.
070   In light of this observation, we further propose a solution called Max Suppression (MaxSup), which
071   consistently applies the intended regularization effect during training, regardless of whether the pre-
072   diction is correct or not. The quantitative evaluation of the features from the penultimate layer high-
073   lights that MaxSup successfully allows for a larger intra-class variation, while improving the inter-
074   class separability in the feature space compared to Label Smoothing. The improved performance on
075
      Image Classification and Machine Translation tasks additionally supports that Max Suppression is a
      superior alternative to Label Smoothing.
076
077   Our contributions are as follows:
078
079          • We reveal the underlying mechanism of the previously observed defects of Label Smooth-
080            ing, highlighted by the Inconsistent Regularization term as well as the Error-Enhancement
081
               term via our novel decomposition of the training objective.
082          • We propose Max Suppression as a closed-form solution to the identified issue, which is
083            demonstrated to be a superior alternative to Label Smoothing.
084          • We show that training with Max Suppression not only improves the classification perfor-
085            mance, but also better retains the similarity information of individual samples to different
086            classes.

090   2.1   R EGULARIZATION
091
092   Regularization techniques aim to enhance the generalization ability of deep neural networks. L2
093   (Krogh and Hertz, 1991) and L1 (Zou and Hastie, 2005) Regularization control model complexity by
094   penalizing large or sparse weights, respectively. Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) randomly deacti-
095   vates neurons during training, helping to reduce over-fitting by preventing co-adaptation of features.
096   Loss-based regularization techniques, such as Label Smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016), soften target
097
      labels to mitigate overconfidence in predictions, which leads to more accurate and better calibrated
      classifiers (Müller et al., 2019). To exploit the clues in the model’s prediction, Zhang et al. (2021);
098
      Liang et al. (2022) further introduced Online Label Smoothing (OLS) and Zipf Label Smoothing
099
      (Zipf-LS) to replace the uniform distribution with the predicted distribution based on the previous
100   and current model weights, respectively. Other approaches, like Confidence Penalty (Pereyra et al.,
101   2017), directly penalize overly confident outputs to enhance model calibration. Moreover, Logit
102   penalty (Dauphin and Cubuk, 2021) that minimizes the l2-norm of the logits is also shown to be
103   effective (Kornblith et al., 2021).
104
105   2.2   S TUDY ON L ABEL S MOOTHING
106
107   A line of studies investigates Label Smoothing in the context of knowledge distillation: Yuan et al.
      (2020) revealed the underlying connection between Label Smoothing and Knowledge Distillation,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Shen et al. (2021) provided a comprehensive evaluation of the compatibility between Label Smooth-
109   ing and Knowledge Distillation, and Chandrasegaran et al. (2022) emphasized the importance of
110   using an LS-trained teacher with a low-temperature transfer. Kornblith et al. (2021) empirically val-
111   idated that Label Smoothing leads to increased tightness and separation of feature clusters, as well
112   as degraded transfer learning performance. The impact of Label Smoothing on the learned feature
113   space is also investigated in the context of neural collapse (Zhou et al., 2022a; Guo et al., 2024), by
114   examining the properties of feature clusters.

116   3     M AX S UPPRESSION R EGULARIZATION
117
118   In this section, we begin by disentangling the training objective into two components: the standard
119   Cross-Entropy loss with one-hot labels and Label Smoothing (LS) loss. We then focus on the LS loss
120   component, reformulating it at the logit level for a clearer understanding of its internal mechanisms.
121   This logit-level formulation allows us to further decompose LS into two key terms: a Regularization
122   term and an Error-Enhancement term. Based on this decomposition, we highlight the limitations
123   of LS, particularly its tendency to amplify errors through the Error-Enhancement term. To address
124   these limitations, we propose Max Suppression Regularization (MaxSup) as a remedy.

261

      5.3   C LASS ACTIVATION M AP
487
488   To visualize the impact of MaxSup on the model’s decision-making compared to label smooth-
489   ing, we adopt Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), a technique by (Selvaraju
490   et al., 2019) that generates class-discriminative localization maps. We employed the DeiT-Small to
491   perform our experiments, comparing the models trained with MaxSup (second row), Label Smooth-
492   ing (third row) and standard Cross-Entropy baseline (fourth row) in Figure 2.
493   As illustrated in Figure 2, the model trained with MaxSup demonstrates a clear advantage when
494   non-target salient objects are present in the background. MaxSup reduces the model’s distraction
495   by these objects, such as the pole in the ‘Bird’ image, the tube in the ‘Goldfish’ image, and the
496   cap in the ‘House Finch’ image. In contrast, the model trained with Label Smoothing often loses
497   focus or incorrectly attends to these background objects. Figure 2a and 2b demonstrate a pattern
498   of distraction, where the attention of the model trained with Label Smoothing is partially disrupted,
499   although the classification remains correct. Figure 2c depicts overconfidence in incorrect samples,
500
      leading to misclassification, highlighting the negative impact of the Error-Enhancement component.
      Beyond the robustness to background distractions, MaxSup also improves the coverage of object
501
      features. For instance, the model trained with Label Smoothing misses important details, such as the
502
      fin in the ‘Shark’ image and the tail in the ‘Monkey’ image, both of which are successfully captured
503   by the model trained with MaxSup. This supports our analysis in Appendix E that MaxSup better
504   preserves the richer information of individual class samples beyond the class-specific information.

      6     C ONCLUSION
508
      In this work, we have uncovered the underlying mechanism behind the previously identified issues
509
      in Label Smoothing and proposed MaxSup as a remedy. Our analysis reveals that Label Smoothing
510
      inherently fails to regularize the incorrect predictions and even encourages overconfidence in them,
511   potentially hindering the model’s ability to learn from challenging examples. MaxSup addresses
512   this limitation by consistently applying the intended regularization effect during training, regardless
513   of whether the prediction is correct or not. Our extensive analysis and experiments demonstrate that
514   MaxSup not only improves task performance but also leads to larger intra-class variance as well as
515   inter-class separation in the feature space over Label Smoothing. This enables the model to retain
516   richer information of individual samples, leading to improved transfer learning. The class activation
517   maps further support our analysis, through the more accurate localization and better coverage of
518   class objects, as well as reduced distraction by irrelevant background objects.
519   Limitation and Future Work Müller et al. (2019) show that teachers trained on LS lead to degraded
520   performance in Knowledge Distillation (Hinton, 2015), and Guo et al. (2024) observed accelerated
521   convergence with LS via the conditioning number analysis. Thus it would be interesting to ex-
522   plore the impact of MaxSup on Knowledge Distillation and training convergence. We leave such
523   investigations to future work.

931   The Max Suppression regularization technique implements a sophisticated gradient redistribution
932   strategy, particularly effective when the model misclassifies samples. When the model’s prediction
933   (argmax(q)) differs from the ground truth (GT), the gradient for the incorrectly predicted class is
                             1                  MaxSup,t                         1
934   increased by α(1 − K     ), resulting in ∂argmax(q) = qt,argmax(q) + α(1 − K ). Simultaneously, the
935                                                  α            MaxSup,t                   α
      gradient for the true class is decreased by K    , giving ∂GT        = qt,GT − (1 + K    ), while for all
936                                                       α    MaxSup,t          α
      other classes, the gradient is slightly reduced by K : ∂i         = qt,i − K . This redistribution adds a
937
      substantial positive gradient to the misclassified class while slightly reducing the gradients for other
938   classes. The magnitude of this adjustment, controlled by the hyperparameter α, effectively penalizes
939   overconfident errors and encourages the model to focus on challenging examples. By amplifying
940   the learning signal for misclassifications, Max Suppression regularization promotes more robust
941   learning from difficult or ambiguous samples.
