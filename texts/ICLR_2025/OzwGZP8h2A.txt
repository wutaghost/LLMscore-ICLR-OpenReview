009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Logic synthesis, which aims to synthesize a compact logic circuit with minimized
012            size while exactly satisfying a given functionality, plays an important role in chip
013            design. Recently, symbolic regression (SR) has shown great success in scien-
014            tific discovery to recover underlying mathematical functions from given datasets.
015            However, we found from extensive experiments that existing SR methods strug-
016
               gle to recover an exact and compact boolean function for logic synthesis given
               a truth table, i.e., complete input-output pairs of the circuit. The major chal-
017
               lenges include (1) the greater complexity of underlying boolean functions com-
018
               pared to mathematical functions, and (2) the complex objectives involving both
019            exact recovery and expression optimization towards circuit minimization. To ad-
020            dress these challenges, we propose a novel symbolic factorized boolean searcher
021            (SINE) to recover exact and compact boolean functions towards logic synthesis.
022            Motivated by the Shannon decomposition theorem, SINE proposes a factorized
023            boolean function representation to decompose the underlying boolean function
024            into multiple simplified sub-functions, significantly reducing their complexity and
025            thus improving the recovery accuracy. Moreover, based on the key observation
026            that, logical sharing is significant for circuit size minimization. SINE proposes
027
               a self-symmetric sub-expression motif operators mining mechanism to enhance
               the monte-carlo tree search method for optimized boolean function learning. To
028
               the best of our knowledge, SINE is the first symbolic regression framework ca-
029
               pable of exactly recovering optimized boolean functions for circuit optimization.
030            Experiments on circuits across a wide range of inputs demonstrate that SINE sig-
031            nificantly improves the recovery accuracy and decreases the size of synthesized
032            circuits by up to 24.32% compared to state-of-the-art methods.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Complex integrated circuits (ICs) can contain billions of transistors, making manual design infea-
038   sible (Huang et al., 2021). Consequently, the IC industry depends on electronic design automation
039
      (EDA) tools (Wang et al., 2009), which systematically transform high-level hardware descriptions
      into layouts ready for IC fabrication. A critical step in this process is logic synthesis (LS), which
040
      converts a behavioral-level description of a design into an optimized gate-level circuit. The primary
041
      goal of LS is to minimize the delay and area of the circuit. As LS is the initial step in EDA processes
042   that produce the final IC layout, the quality of its output significantly impacts the area, power, and
043   performance of the IC (De Abreu et al., 2021; Berndt et al., 2022).
044
045
      Logic synthesis (LS) is a challenging N P-hard combinatorial optimization problem. Both commer-
      cial and academic LS tools (Brayton & Mishchenko, 2010) employ sophisticated human-designed
046
      heuristics to obtain approximate solutions, often resulting in sub-optimal outcomes. Traditional
047
      approaches address this problem by following the generate-then-optimize paradigm from two dis-
048   tinct perspectives: boolean functions (Lai et al., 1993; Nabulsi et al., 2017) and and-inverter graphs
049   (Brayton, 2006; Bertacco & Damiani, 1997; Mishchenko et al., 2011). Recent research (Belcak
050   & Wattenhofer, 2022; Petersen et al., 2022) suggests that neural methods, which directly gener-
051   ate circuit graphs, carry the promising potential to simultaneously generate and optimize circuits,
052   thereby producing compact initial solutions for subsequent optimization. On the other hand, sym-
053   bolic regression (SR) has shown great success in recovering underlying mathematical functions from
      datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scientific discovery tasks.

065   We conclude that synthesizing boolean functions from input-output examples (i.e., a truth table) is
066   analogous to recovering mathematical functions from a dataset in the boolean domain, as illustrated
067   in Figure 1. First, the truth table corresponds to the dataset. Second, the logical operations AND,
068   OR, and NOT correspond to the arithmetic operations of multiplication, addition, and subtraction,

      respectively. Finally, boolean expression trees correspond to mathematical expression trees.
070   Thus, a desired question is: Can we leverage the strong capabilities of SR methods to recover not
071   only exact but also compact boolean functions for significantly boosting logic synthesis?
072
      In this paper, we first investigate whether existing SR methods can effectively learn boolean func-
073   tions. Through extensive experiments, we found that these SR methods struggle to recover exact
074   and compact boolean functions for logic synthesis from a given truth table. The primary challenges
075   include: (1) the underlying boolean functions are significantly more complex than typical mathe-
076   matical functions, (2) a significant gap exists between the complexity of boolean functions and the
077   size of synthesized circuits, and (3) the multi-objective nature to achieve not only exact recovery but
078   also compact generation towards circuit optimization.
079   To address these challenges systematically, we propose a novel approach called the symbolic fac-
080   torized boolean searcher (SINE) to recover exact and compact boolean functions for logic synthesis.
081   The key innovations of SINE are as follows. (1) Factorized Boolean Function Representation. In-
082   spired by the Shannon decomposition theorem (Gdanskiy et al., 2020), SINE factorizes the original
083   boolean function into multiple simplified sub-functions while preserving exact functionality. This
084   significantly reduces the complexity of the underlying functions, thereby improving the recovery
085   accuracy. (2) Self-Symmetric Tree Search. Based on the key observation that logical sharing is
086   significant for reducing circuit sizes, SINE proposes to encourage search self-symmetric tree expres-
087
      sions with the goal of maximizing subexpression sharing, which can be implemented using shared
      nodes in the synthesized circuit. (3) Lexicographic Optimization. Given the multi-objective nature
088
      of learning boolean functions, SINE incorporates the lexicographic optimization technique into our
089
      tree search, which prioritizes accuracy over circuit size.
090
091   We evaluate SINE on three widely-used circuit benchmarks. Experiments demonstrate that SINE
092   generates boolean functions more accurately than the general mathematical SR methods, achieving
093
      significant improvement in terms of accuracy. Moreover, we compare SINE with two EDA-based
      LS methods and experiments show that SINE significantly outperforms the baselines in terms of
094
      circuit size. Our results demonstrate the strong capability of our SINE to learn exact and compact
095
      boolean functions toward circuit synthesis.
096
097   We summarize our contributions as follows. (1) We empirically show that existing SR methods
098   struggle to effectively learn boolean functions, and provide key insights for the challenge. (2) To
099
      the best of our knowledge, SINE is the first symbolic search framework that can exactly recover
      optimized boolean functions towards circuit optimization, opening a new direction towards neu-
100
      ral circuit synthesis with emerging symbolic regression techniques. (3) SINE is a novel Boolean
101
      Searcher framework that addresses the challenges of boolean function learning systematically. (4)
102   Experiments demonstrate that SINE significantly outperforms competitive baselines, significantly
103   improving the accuracy and decreasing circuit size by up to 24.32%.

      given truth table via manually designed heuristics, such as sum-of-products (Nabulsi et al., 2017)

109   learning for LS (Rai et al., 2021; Belcak & Wattenhofer, 2022; Schmitt et al., 2021; 2023), which
110   offers promising approaches to learn to generate compact circuits with smaller sizes. Specifically,
111   they formulate the input-output pairs in a truth table as a training dataset, and leverage machine
112   learning methods to generate a circuit fitting the dataset. Roughly speaking, these approaches fall
113   into two categories as follows. (1) In the early stages of the International Workshop on Logic &
114   Synthesis (IWLS) competition, researchers proposed to use decision trees and random forests to
115   generate circuits from the input-output pairs of specified truth tables. (2) In recent IWLS competi-
116   tions, researchers have proposed using deep differentiable logic gate networks (Petersen et al., 2022)
117
      to generate circuits from the complete input-output pairs of specified truth tables.
118   Symbolic Regression (SR) SR aims to recover underlying analytical expressions from given
119   datasets, which has shown great success in many scientific discovery tasks. Roughly speaking,
120   existing SR methods fall into three categories as follows. (1) Genetic programming (GP) based SR
121   approaches (Espejo et al., 2009; Virgolin et al., 2021; He et al., 2022) maintain a population of ex-
122   pression “individuals” that evolve using genetic operators such as selection, crossover, and mutation.
123
      While GP-based approaches can be effective, it tends to struggle to scale to large-scale SR problems.
      (2) In recent years, transformer-based SR methods (Biggio et al., 2021; Kamienny et al., 2022; Holt
124
      et al., 2023; d’Ascoli et al., 2023) have been shown successfully recovering large-scale mathemati-
125
      cal expressions with up to twelve input variables. However, they suffer from high training costs and
126   poor generalization performance. (3) In contrast, the reinforcement learning (RL) and monte-carlo
127   tree search (MCTS) based SR methods (Petersen et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2022; Xu et al.) formulates
128   the expression generation problem as a RL problem, and uses RL and/or MCTS methods to solve
129   the problem. They have achieved state-of-the-art performance on multiple SR benchmarks.

131   3     BACKGROUND
132
      Problem Formulation of Learning in Logic Synthesis (LS) In recent years, synthesizing circuits
133   from truth tables via machine learning has gained increasing attention (Rai et al., 2021; Belcak &
134   Wattenhofer, 2022; Schmitt et al., 2021; 2023). Given a truth table T , we assume it describes a
135   boolean function f : Bn → Bm for a circuit with n input and m output. In terms of the truth
136   table, each line in the truth table represents an input-output pair (x, y), indicating the output signals
137   y produced by the circuit for the given input signals x, where x ∈ Rn and y ∈ Rnm . Then the
138   input-output pairs in the truth table constructs a dataset D = (X, Y) = {(xi , yi )}2i=1 . Given the
139   dataset, we aim to learn a boolean function fˆ : Bn → Bm that precisely fits the dataset. Based on
140   the learned boolean function, we can easily construct a corresponding circuit.
141   Symbolic Regression (SR) for Learning Boolean Functions SR aims to find a mathematical ex-
142   pression f to best fit a given dataset D. To this end, many existing SR approaches represent any
143   mathematical expression by an algebraic expression tree, where internal nodes are operators (e.g.,
144   +, ×, sin) and terminal nodes are input variables and/or constants. We assume τ = [τ1 , . . . , τn ]
145   is a pre-order traversal of such an expression tree. Note that there is a one-to-one correspondence
146   between an expression tree and its pre-order traversal. Each τi is an operator, input variable, or
147   constant selected from a library of possible tokens, e.g., [+, −, ×, ÷, sin, cos, exp, log.x]. To apply
148   the existing SR methods to learning boolean functions, we reset the operators as and, or, not, and
149
      the library of possible tokens at each step as [and, or, not, x1 , . . . , xn ].

      To address the complexity challenge mentioned in Section 4.2, we propose a Self-Symmetric Tree

      5.1 FACTORIZED B OOLEAN F UNCTION R EPRESENTATION
237
238   As shown in Figure 2b, we found that the exponential growth in the length of boolean functions
239   significantly expands the search space and increases search difficulty. To address this challenge, we
240
      propose a Factorized Boolean Function Representation method motivated by the Shannon decom-
      position theorem (Gdanskiy et al., 2020) to decompose the original boolean function into several
241
      simplified sub-functions. Specifically, SINE iteratively selects a variable Xi to decompose a given
242
      boolean function f of n input variables into two sub-functions with n − 1 input variables, i.e.,
243
244     f (X1 , X2 , . . . , Xn ) = Xi · f1 (X1 , . . . Xi = 1, . . . , Xn ) + Xi′ · f2 (X1 , . . . , Xi = 0, . . . , Xn ).
245   Then, we can further decompose the sub-functions f1 and f2 by selecting another variable.
246
247
      In practice, the underlying compact boolean function is unknown, while we have the truth table
      containing the complete input-output pairs of a given circuit. Nevertheless, each boolean function
248
      can be represented by a unique truth table. Thus, we apply the aforementioned decomposition
249
      mechanism of boolean functions to truth tables. Specifically, given a truth table T with n input
250   and m output, we decompose it into two sub-tables T1 and T2 , where T1 and T2 contain a half of
251   input-output pairs in T with the i-th input being 1 and 0, respectively.
252
253
      To implement the aforementioned decomposition mechanism, an appropriate variable selection pol-
      icy is required. As shown in Figure 7b in Appendix C, experiments demonstrate that different
254
      selected variables can lead to significantly variable final circuit sizes. For simplicity, we design a
255
      greedy selection approach, which greedily select the variable based on the circuit size after decom-
256   position. We leave learning a variable selection policy as future work.

262   learn a compact boolean function that can precisely fit the dataset. Thus, the learning problem is
263   formulated as learning boolean functions from the decomposed m × 2k datasets with the goal of
264   minimizing the final synthesized circuit size.

292
      rules include basic boolean operators AND, OR, and NOT. As shown in Figure 4, the goal of our
293
      STC is to find a boolean expression tree that maximizes the expected reward of root node f .
294
295   Self-Symmetric Tree Search Given the factored sub-truth tables, the problem can be formulated
296   as a boolean symbolic regression problem. Due to the scalability challenge of the boolean function
297
      as shown in Figure 2a, we apply the Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm, which is indeed
      well-suited for handling SR problems with vast search space (Sun et al., 2022), to search for the
298
      boolean functions. However, different from the traditional single-objective MCTS, our problem
299
      involves complex objectives with not only exact recovery but also expression optimization towards
300   circuit optimization. To address this problem, we propose a Self-Symmetric Tree Search (STC)
301   framework with Lexicographic Optimization.
302
303
      Progressively Expanded Libraries via Motif Mining As shown in Figure 2d, we found that the Log-
      ical sharing significantly matters with the circuit size. Moreover, we observe that the shared logical
304
      node in the circuit indeed corresponds to symmetric sub-expressions in the circuit’s corresponding
305
      boolean function as shown in Figure 3. Thus, to integrate this prior information into our search
306   process, we propose a Self-Symmetric Tree Search framework with the goal of learning as many as
307   possible symmetric sub-expressions during the learning process of the boolean function. To this end,
308   we leverage the idea of motif learning for adaptively mining a series of motifs (i.e., sub-expressions)
309   from those already searched boolean functions. Then, by adding these mined motifs into the ac-
310   tion space, the subsequent tree search process can implicitly search boolean expressions with many
311   sub-expressions symmetric with previous searched expressions, thus leading to many logical sharing
312   nodes in the final circuit.
313   More specifically, we present details on our adaptive motif learning mechanism as follows. As
314   shown in Figure 4, given a pair of symmetric sub-truth tables, the small function structures are
315   adaptively extracted as motifs from one of the generated boolean functions and included in the
316   action space of its symmetric tree search agent. For example, the motifs extracted from a boolean
317   function (x0 + x1 ) × ((!x0 + x2 ) + x1 ) are (x0 + x1 ) and (!x0 + x2 ). Selecting these small
318   function structures is based on the observation that they typically function as deeper nodes in the
319   generated circuit graph, leading to increased logical sharing. Consequently, these extracted motifs
320
      effectively guide the search process and ultimately generate symmetric sub-expressions with more
      shared structures.
321
322   Lexicogrophic Selection In our problem, due to the multi-objective optimization, the rewards re-
323   ceived for a given state is a vector R = (Ra , Rc ), where Ra is defined as the accuracy of the
      simulation boolean expression tree and Rc is the expression’s corresponding circuit size. However,


                                                                                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      different from the typical multi-task tree search method where multiple objectives can be optimized
325   simultaneously without any priority relationships, our problem places a strong emphasis on the ac-
326   curacy metric over the complexity metric. Therefore, we incorporate Lexicographic Optimization
327   into our STC framework, which prioritizes the optimization of the accuracy metric before consider-
328   ing the complexity metric. Specifically, we integrate Lexicographic Optimization into the selection
329   step. Following (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006), in each selection step, the STC agent maintains a
330   trade-off between exploration and exploitation by selecting actions that maximize the Vectorized
331   Upper Confidence Bounds applied for Trees (UCT), formulated as:
332                                                               ln[N (s)]
333                                   UCT(s, a) = Q(s, a) + c                                            (1)
                                                                   N (s, a)
334
335   Here, Q(s, a) is a two-dimensional vector that represents the average rewards of playing action a
336   in state s in the simulations performed in the history, encouraging the exploitation of the current
337   best child node; N (s) is the number of times state s has been visited, and N (s, a) is the number of
338   times action a has been selected at state s. Given the two-dimensional vector U CT , we choose the
339
      action based on lexicographic optimization. Specifically, lexicographic optimization first identifies
      the action set that maximizes the first term of the U CT . Then, within this selected action set, the
340
      selected action is that maximizes the second term. Due to limited space, we defer the details of our
341
      lexicographic selection algorithm in Appendix B.3.1. Overall, our proposed lexicographic selection
342   enables STC to effectively recover exact and compact boolean sub-functions.
343
344   5.3   P OST-G ENERATION C ROSSOVER
345   If the generated boolean expression fails to exactly recover the given sub-truth table, we design
346   a legalization mechanism to improve its accuracy to 100%. Then, our STC generates multiple
347   compact boolean sub-functions for each sub-truth table, all characterized by the same accuracy but
348   potentially varying in complexity. To further optimize the final synthesized circuit, we propose a
349   post-generation crossover mechanism by recombining sub-functions from different sub-truth tables.
350   This process is divided into two main steps. In the first step, we apply crossover to the decomposed
351   sub-expressions for each output. In the second step, we conduct an additional crossover among the
352
      expressions of each output for multi-output circuits. To manage the potentially overwhelming num-
      ber of permutations resulting from numerous output bits, we utilize sampling during this crossover.
353
      Eventually, we select the function with the highest accuracy and the smallest circuit size as the final
354
      solution. For more details, please refer to Appendix B.3.2.

      7      C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose SINE, a novel approach for recovering exact and compact Boolean func-
481   tions for logic synthesis. SINE includes a factorized Boolean function representation to reduce the
482   search space and a self-symmetric tree search framework. Compared to standard methods, SINE
483   achieves a remarkable average reduction of 49.02% in the wrong bits and decreases circuit size by
484   up to 24.32%. Our experiments show that there is significant potential for enhancing our current
485   search strategy. In the future, we plan to incorporate more powerful search methods, such as large
      language models (LLMs), and extend our framework to more logic synthesis tasks.

605             risk-seeking RL approach combined with a recurrent neural network (RNN) to generate a
606             probability distribution over expressions.
607           • SPL: SPL Sun et al. (2022) is a search-based symbolic regression method that employs
608             a Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) agent to explore optimal expression trees based on
609             measurement data. SPL is one of the SOTA SR method.
610
              • SOP: SOP Nabulsi et al. (2017) is a heuristic method integrated into the widely-used logic
611
                synthesis framework ABC. This method achieves precise logic synthesis by representing
612
                truth tables as sums of products.
613
614           • BDD: BDD Lai et al. (1993) is another heuristic method included in the ABC logic synthe-
615             sis framework. It achieves precise logic synthesis by applying the Shannon decomposition
616
                theorem and representing the truth table as a binary decision diagram.

660   B.3.2    S UB -F UNCTION C ROSSOVER
661
662   In real-world circuits, the number of outputs is not always one. Therefore, a full traversal for
663   crossover would be impossible. To address this problem, we employ a random sampling policy for
664   the combination process. Specifically, we randomly select one expression from each sub-function
665
      set and combine them, repeating this process 10,000 times. From these 10,000 combinations, we
      choose the function with the highest accuracy and minimal circuit size as the final solution.
