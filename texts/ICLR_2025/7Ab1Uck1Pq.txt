011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the increasing capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs), the prolifera-
014            tion of AI-generated texts has become a serious concern. Given the diverse range
015            of organizations providing LLMs, it is crucial for governments and third-party
016
               entities to identify the origin LLM of a given text to enable accurate infringement
               and mitigation of potential misuse. However, existing detection methods, primar-
017
               ily designed to distinguish between human-generated and LLM-generated texts,
018
               often fail to accurately identify the origin LLM due to the high similarity of AI-
019            generated texts from different sources. In this paper, we propose a novel black-box
020            AI-generated text origin detection method, dubbed P ROFILER, which accurately
021            predicts the origin of an input text by extracting distinct context inference pat-
022            terns through calculating and analyzing novel context losses between the surrogate
023            model’s output logits and the adjacent input context. Extensive experimental results
024            show that P ROFILER outperforms 10 state-of-the-art baselines, achieving more
025            than a 25% increase in AUC score on average across both natural language and
026            code datasets when evaluated against five of the latest commercial LLMs under
027
               both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As Large Language Models (LLMs) achieve superior capabilities in understanding and generating
033
      human-like text, they have become deeply integrated into everyday life (Lo, 2023). However, this
      growing reliance on LLMs has also raised significant concerns regarding the misuse of AI-generated
034
      content (Cotton et al., 2024; Kreps et al., 2022; Perkins, 2023). The European Union’s draft Artificial
035
      Intelligence (AI) Act (Madiega, 2021) highlights the risks posed by such AI systems, identifying
036   various “high-risk” scenarios where AI misuse could harm fundamental human rights, such as
037   generating phishing emails (Roy et al., 2024). In response, the Act mandates that providers of general-
038   purpose AI models, including LLMs, as well as third-party researchers, develop and implement
039   policies to ensure compliance with copyright laws, so that when severe violations occur, accountability
040   and remediation measures can be enforced effectively.
041
      One key aspect of adhering to these emerging legal and ethical frameworks is the ability to detect the
042   origin of AI-generated text. A large number of detection techniques have recently been developed.
043   Some of these techniques are based on watermarking (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al.,
044   2024; Hou et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023). These techniques typically involve fine-tuning LLMs or
045   adjusting their decoding processes to produce text with a distinctive, model-specific distribution. For
046   example, after watermarking, text produced by ChatGPT would exhibit a different distribution from
047   text generated by other LLMs. While watermarking can be effective, it is exclusively controlled by
048   model providers, creating a potential conflict of interest. Since providers are the only entities capable
049   of verifying watermarks, they may be incentivized to obscure evidence of misuse and avoid admitting
050
      fault, undermining transparency and accountability.
051   To mitigate this limitation, surrogate-model-based methods have gained increasing attention (Bhat-
052   tacharjee & Liu, 2024; He et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). These techniques enable external parties
053   to conduct forensic analyses without requiring cooperation from model providers, relying only on
      black-box access to the text generation process. By feeding partial or full text to a surrogate model


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (i.e., an LLM of a relatively small scale), researchers can analyze its internal states to infer the
055   likely origin of the text. The underlying rationale is that sufficiently powerful surrogate models can
056   capture statistical or representational differences, which help reveal the source. Existing approaches
057   along this line largely focus on identifying next-token prediction patterns, referred to as the
058   token-level inference pattern. While these techniques have shown promising results in distinguishing
059   human-generated from AI-generated text, they are less effective in differentiating outputs from
060   various LLMs, as demonstrated in our evaluation (Section 5). Further investigation reveals that, unlike
061   the clear distinction between human and AI-generated text (Jawahar et al., 2020; Bakhtin et al., 2019;
062   Guo et al., 2023), different LLMs often converge on similar next-token predictions due to shared
063
      linguistic distributions from large corpora. This similarity introduces a more subtle variation, making
      token-level inference patterns alone insufficient to capture these nuances (as discussed in Section 3).
064
065   Building on this observation, we introduce a novel approach that incorporates contextual information
066   to enlarge the representational differences between text generated by various LLMs, improving the
067   precision of text origin detection. Specifically, rather than relying solely on token-level features
068   (e.g., next token prediction commonly used in existing detection methods), our method broadens
069
      the analysis to capture the model’s inference behavior over a window of surrounding tokens (i.e.,
      context), referred to as the context-level inference pattern. This approach calculates novel context
070
      losses by utilizing the output logits from the surrogate model and the adjacent input context tokens at
071
      each output logits position. It then extracts both independent features (features derived from a single
072   context loss subsequence) and correlated features (features derived from each pair of context loss
073   subsequences) from these context losses. Based on this, we develop P ROFILER, the first black-box
074   detection method that leverages rich contextual information for identifying the origin of AI-generated
075   text. To further evaluate the effectiveness of P ROFILER, we extend existing datasets by incorporating
076   diverse text samples generated by multiple recent commercial LLMs across various text domains. Our
077   comprehensive evaluation demonstrates P ROFILER’s superior performance in detecting text origin.
078   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
079
080          • We propose a novel AI-generated text origin detection algorithm P ROFILER that incorporates
081            rich contextual information for improved accuracy.
082          • We introduce a new feature extraction algorithm that effectively captures contextual informa-
083            tion for text origin detection. This algorithm extracts both independent features, i.e., output
084            logits for each token, and correlated features, i.e., pairwise cross-entropy losses between
085            tokens and their neighbors.
086          • We present a new evaluation dataset for text origin detection, which extends existing datasets
087            by incorporating diverse text samples generated by recent commercial LLMs across various
088            domains and tasks. The dataset includes samples originating from four existing natural
089            language datasets, an existing code dataset, and a newly collected C++ code dataset (GCJ).
090          • We develop a prototype, P ROFILER, and conduct a comprehensive evaluation against 10
091            baselines. The results demonstrate that P ROFILER significantly improves origin detection
092            accuracy, particularly in distinguishing outputs from different LLMs, such as GPT-3.5-
093
               Turbo (OpenAI, 2023), GPT-4-Turbo (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude3-Sonnet (Anthropic,
               2023), Claude-3-Opus (Anthropic, 2023), and Gemini-1.0-Pro (Team et al., 2023). P RO -
094
               FILER achieves a more than 45.5% and 12.5% increase in detection AUC scores under
095
               in-distribution (compared to both zero-shot and supervised-trained methods) and out-of-
096            distribution (compared to supervised-trained methods) settings, respectively.

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
100
      2.1   AI- GENERATED T EXT D ETECTION
101
102   Existing AI-generated text detection methods can be broadly categorized into two primary approaches:
103   watermark-based methods and surrogate-model-based methods. Watermark-based methods (Kirchen-
104   bauer et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023) typically modify the
105   decoding strategy during the LLM’s generation process to force or encourage the generated tokens
106   to fall within a predefined subset of the model’s vocabulary. However, these requirements limit the
107   applicability of watermark-based methods, making them less practical compared to surrogate-model-
      based methods. In contrast, surrogate-model-based methods operate in a completely black-box setting


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      without requiring prior modifications to the text generation process, where detectors only have access
109   to a limited amount of AI-generated data.
110
111   Existing surrogate-model-based detection methods can be further divided into zero-shot detection
112
      and supervised-trained detection:
113   Zero-shot Detection. Zero-shot detection methods assign a confidence score to each text sample
114   and use a predefined threshold to differentiate between human-written and AI-generated texts. For
115   instance, GLTR (Gehrmann et al., 2019) measures the average token rank of the input text based on a
116   surrogate LLM’s output logits, where a higher average rank indicates a higher likelihood of the text
117   being AI-generated. LRR (Su et al., 2023), an improved version of GLTR, utilizes both log-rank and
118
      log-probability metrics. DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023) detects AI-generated texts by measuring
      the similarity of the input text with its repeatedly masked and reconstructed versions using a pre-
119
      trained LLM, while Fast-DetectGPT (Bao et al., 2024) further optimizes this approach with a rapid
120
      text sampling technique. Binoculars (Hans et al., 2024) utilizes the cross-entropy between output
121   logits from two different surrogate LLMs to detect AI-generated texts, achieving more consistent
122   performance across LLMs. Other works (Yang et al., 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Tulchinskii
123   et al., 2023) explore various advanced metrics for zero-shot detection.
124
      Supervised-trained Detection. In contrast, supervised-trained detection methods employ more
125
      complex features and train classification models to identify distinct patterns in human-written and AI-
126
      generated texts. For example, Solaiman et al. (2019) fine-tunes a RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) model to
127   detect texts generated by GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019). RADAR (Hu et al., 2023) and Outfox (Koike
128   et al., 2024) enhance detection robustness against paraphrasing attacks using adversarial training.
129   Raidar (Mao et al., 2024) compares the differences between the original text and LLM-rewritten text
130   to identify AI-generated content. GhostBuster (Verma et al., 2024) explores feature combinations
131   derived from multiple surrogate LLMs’ output logits to optimize detection performance. Recent
132   studies (McGovern et al., 2024) also investigate more advanced features for supervised detection.
133
134   2.2   B LACK - BOX T EXT O RIGIN D ETECTION
135
136   Despite the significant advancements in AI-generated text detection techniques, only a few methods
137
      have demonstrated the capability to further identify the origin LLM of a given AI-generated text.
      For example, TuringBench (Uchendu et al., 2021) evaluates the effectiveness of various methods,
138
      including GLTR (Gehrmann et al., 2019), Grover (Zellers et al., 2019), and fine-tuning-based
139
      approaches (Devlin et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019) using over 160k samples. However, these methods
140   struggle to keep up with the rapid evolution of LLMs. Sniffer (Li et al., 2023) attempts to detect
141   text origin by comparing the output logits from multiple surrogate LLMs using metrics such as the
142   percentage of perplexity scores. SeqXGPT (Wang et al., 2023a) further enhances Sniffer by leveraging
143   a specialized detection model based on convolutional and self-attention networks. Nevertheless, the
144   effectiveness of these approaches against more advanced commercial LLMs remains uncertain and
145   requires further validation.

148
149
      The fundamental assumption of existing AI-generated text detection methods is that AI-generated
150   texts exhibit unique next-token prediction patterns, which can be effectively identified using surrogate
151   LLMs. However, these prediction patterns are strikingly similar across texts generated by different
152   LLMs, limiting the effectiveness of such methods in handling text origin detection. Figure 1 illustrates
153   the scores of one latest detector, Binoculars, on texts from human and four distinct LLMs. The
154   x-axis represents Binoculars scores, while the y-axis shows the frequency of samples. Gray bars
155   indicate the score distribution for human-written texts, whereas colored bars represent texts generated
156   by various LLMs. Although the Binoculars score successfully distinguishes between human and
157   AI-generated texts, it shows limited capability in classifying texts based on their specific AI sources.
158
      This observation validates our assumption that next-token prediction patterns are highly consistent
      among different LLMs.
159

161   P ROFILER, which goes beyond next-token prediction in the output logits. Figure 2 illustrates the

      4.1                  OVERVIEW OF P ROFILER
205
206
      The entire pipeline of P ROFILER consists of three key stages: Surrogate Model Inference, Context
207
      Loss Computation, and Inference Pattern Extraction, as shown in Figure 3. The primary objective
208
      of P ROFILER is to determine whether a given text is generated by a specific text origin.
209
210   Stage 1: Surrogate Model Inference (Section 4.2). In this stage, the tokenized input sequence is fed
211   into surrogate model to obtain the sequence of output logits. At each token position, output logits are
212
      computed based on all preceding input tokens up to that point.
213   Stage 2: Context Loss Computation (Section 4.3). With the sequence of output logits from the
214   first stage, P ROFILER computes the context loss. At each position, cross-entropy losses between the
215   current output logits and adjacent input tokens within a fixed context window are calculated. These
      losses, referred to as context losses, are used in the next stage.

255
256   Compared with existing detection techniques that primarily utilize next-word prediction information
257
      contained in the output logits, P ROFILER captures and analyzes the information of the surrounding

258

259

262   preceding tokens, while the last token lacks context from subsequent tokens. Hence L ∈ RW ×(n−W ) .
263   Note that, we expect the context to be symmetric (an equal number of preceding and subsequent

275
276   With the calculated context losses L, P ROFILER then extracts the inference pattern of the surrogate
277   model M regarding the input text x1:n , including independent patterns and correlated patterns.
278

281   input token in the context is encoded in the output logits during the surrogate model inference. The
282   statistical features sj of each Lj consist of six key properties: average, minimum, maximum, standard
283   deviation, median, and variance. The residual features, which are first utilized by P ROFILER in
284   AI-generated text origin detection, include the statistical properties of the discrete differences and
285   second-order central differences (Fornberg, 1988; Durran, 2013; Quarteroni et al., 2010) for each
286   context loss Lj . Specifically, the discrete differences dj for Lj are calculated as:

513   5.4            A BLATION S TUDY
514
515   To investigate the impact of each hyper-parameter on P ROFILER ’s performance, we conduct several
516   ablation studies, including the effects of context window size and the choice of surrogate model. The
517   results indicate that the hyper-parameters of P ROFILER have limited impact on its overall performance,
518   demonstrating the robustness and compatibility of P ROFILER across various configurations.
519   Impact of Context Window Size. We evaluate P ROFILER using different context window sizes,
520   specifically W = 2, 4, 6, 8, where W = 6 is the default configuration. The performance of P ROFILER
521   fluctuates within a range of 3% across varying window sizes. When W ≤ 6, a larger window size
522   generally results in a higher average detection AUC. However, when W ≥ 6, the detection AUC
523   begins to degrade. Therefore, we select W = 6 as the default configuration for P ROFILER to balance
524   performance and efficiency. More details are presented in Appendix D.
525   Impact of Surrogate Model Selection. We also evaluate the influence of different surrogate models
526   on P ROFILER ’s performance. While some fluctuation in detection AUC is observed, P ROFILER
527   demonstrates consistent performance across various surrogate LLMs. In most cases, using a single
528   surrogate model achieves at least 95% of the detection performance of the ensemble version, indi-
529   cating P ROFILER ’s high generality and compatibility when applied with different surrogate models.
530   This flexibility allows P ROFILER to be adapted according to different configurations and resource
531   constraints in real-world deployment scenarios. More details are presented in Appendix E.

533   6             C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we propose a novel black-box AI-generated text origin detection algorithm that leverages
536   the rich contextual information in the surrogate model’s output logits (i.e., inference patterns). Our
537   method comprises three main stages: surrogate model inference, context loss computation, and
538   inference pattern extraction. Extensive experiments on four natural language datasets and two code
539   datasets demonstrate the superiority of P ROFILER, achieving more than a 25% average increase in
      AUC compared to 10 baselines under both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings.

1073   with model size. For example, on the HumanEval dataset, Mistral-7B achieves an average AUC of
