009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal language modeling constitutes a recent breakthrough which leverages
012            advances in large language models to pretrain capable multimodal models. The
013            integration of natural language during pretraining has been shown to significantly
014            improve learned representations, particularly in computer vision. However, the
015            efficacy of multimodal language modeling in the realm of functional brain data,
016
               specifically for advancing pathology detection, remains unexplored. This study
               pioneers EEG-language models (ELMs) trained on clinical reports and 15000
017
               EEGs. We propose to combine multimodal alignment in this novel domain with
018
               timeseries cropping and text segmentation. This also enables an extension based
019            on multiple instance learning to alleviate misalignment between irrelevant EEG
020            or text segments. Our results indicate that models learn richer representations
021            from being exposed to a variety of report segments, including the patient’s clinical
022            history, description of the EEG, and the physician’s interpretation. Compared
023            to models exposed to narrower clinical text information, we find such models to
024            retrieve EEGs based on clinical reports (and vice versa) with substantially higher
025            accuracy. Particularly in regimes with few annotations, we observe that ELMs
026            can significantly improve pathology detection compared to EEG-only models, as
027
               demonstrated by both zero-shot classification and linear probes. The integration
               of multiple instance learning further improves performance across tasks. In sum,
028
               these results highlight the potential of integrating brain activity data with clinical
029
               text, suggesting that ELMs represent significant progress for clinical applications.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Medical neuroimaging such as electroencephalography (EEG) has not yet benefited to the same extent
035   as other domains from the considerable advances deep learning has brought about. While EEG sees
036   widespread clinical use for pathology detection, in particular for epilepsy (Binnie & Stefan, 1999; Jing
037   et al., 2020) as well as sleep disorders (Malhotra & Avidan, 2013), available annotated data is scarce.
038   As the impressive scaling properties of deep learning are now well described (Kaplan et al., 2020;
039
      Smith et al., 2023), self-supervised learning (SSL) is a promising direction by enabling pretraining
      with unlabeled data and thereby increasing available training sample sizes (Hadsell et al., 2006; Chen
040
      et al., 2020). Various such approaches have shown initial success when applied to EEG. These include
041
      methods relying on data-augmentations (Mohsenvand et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021), the temporal
042   ordering of EEG data (Banville et al., 2021), as well as masking and reconstruction (Jiang et al.,
043   2024). However, these are hindered by the difficulty of creating appropriate data augmentations and,
044   especially reconstruction techniques, by low signal-to-noise. Thus, progress in the medical context
045   has lagged, likely further exacerbated by the modality displaying high similarity between pathologies.
046
      Meanwhile, important further progress was made in computer vision by leveraging natural language
047
      as a signal during pretraining (Radford et al., 2021). Specifically, contrastive approaches which aim
048   to align embeddings of image-text pairs have shown to yield representations powerful for downstream
049   tasks in radiology (Zhang et al., 2022a; 2023). Given that success in radiology is also believed to be
050   bottlenecked by the availability of labeled data and the reliance on fine-grained information (Zhang
051   et al., 2022a), this joint modeling approach is a particularly interesting and novel application for the
052   challenging problem of medical EEG. Fortunately, this is made possible by the clinical reports of
053   physicians which accompany hospital EEG recordings and contain information about the patient and
      recording itself (Obeid & Picone, 2016).

      However, language-EEG pretraining also entails unique challenges. First, datasets are generally
074
      smaller than those used in radiology and especially computer vision. Second, the clinical reports tend
075   to be highly heterogeneous. While previous applications have paired natural and medical images
076   with short captions (Radford et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a), EEG reports tend to span multiple
077   paragraphs and include information irrelevant to downstream clinical tasks, potentially hindering
078   the pretraining process. Moreover, they do not contain any temporal information about when events
079   occurred during the recording.
080
      The current work presents the application of aligning functional brain data with medical textual
081   information for the first time by training EEG-language models (ELMs). To overcome the challenging
082   formats of modalities, constituting long timeseries and multiparagraph reports, we propose sub-unit
083   alignment. To address inconsistent relevance of EEG-text pairs, we additionally propose an extension
084   drawing on insights from the field of multiple instance learning (MIL). Furthermore, we investigate
085   how to best handle the heterogeneity of medical EEG reports. Specifically, we perform content-
086   based text segmentation, enabling inference on the relative importance of the different sources of
087   information in the reports. By fixing pretraining data and encoder architectures across comparisons,
088   we enable inference on the utility of different pretraining strategies per se. Our approach allows
089
      us to provide the first evidence of considerable retrieval capabilities for clinical reports and EEG.
      We furthermore test downstream performance of ELMs on classifying normal and pathologically
090
      abnormal EEG, which is a widespread clinical task. These tests include zero-shot classification by
091
      leveraging the language capabilities to evaluate the flexibility of the approach. Our results constitute
092   considerable increases in pathology detection performance in scenarios with few labels. These are
093   particularly relevant for clinical contexts, which tend to operate with smaller datasets compared to
094   many common areas of deep learning applications.

      2    R ELATED WORK
098   • Self-supervised learning with EEG data. SSL with EEG data has been predominantly applied
099     to emotion recognition (Zhang et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2023), motor imagery (Cheng et al.,
100     2020; Rommel et al., 2022), sleep staging (Yang et al., 2021; Rommel et al., 2022), as well as
101     pathology detection. For the latter application, the temporal order of EEG crops was used initially
102     to demonstrate label-efficient representation learning (Banville et al., 2021). Augmentation-based
103     contrastive learning, combined with larger EEG encoders trained on multiple datasets, further
104     improved pathology detection (Mohsenvand et al., 2020). Recent studies have explored the use
105
        of transformers (Yang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024), with a focus on scaling while adopting
        tokenization in an attempt to improve the challenge of effective cross-dataset EEG training.
106
107   • Using EEG for pathology detection. While SSL shows good performance for pathology detection,
        it is particularly in contexts with little annotated data that it performs well. When more labeled data


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
          is available, expert-based feature extraction combined with traditional machine learning classifiers
109       are competitive together with supervised deep learning (Roy et al., 2019; Gemein et al., 2020;
110       Western et al., 2021; Kiessner et al., 2023; Darvishi-Bayazi et al., 2024). This trend has also
111       been observed in other EEG applications (Schirrmeister et al., 2017; Lotte et al., 2018). This
112       may indicate that label noise indices a ceiling effect on classification performance (Engemann
113       et al., 2018; Gemein et al., 2020); specifically, the inter-rater reliability of EEG classification into
114       normal or pathologically abnormal by neurologists. If this hypothesis holds, a focus on improving
115       classification with limited labels may be of extra importance.
116
117   • Medical multimodal language modeling. Medical vision-language modeling aims to guide self-
118     supervised pretraining on medical images using textual information in reports, with performance
119
        on a variety of downstream tasks benefiting as a result (Huang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022;
        Zhang et al., 2022a). Due to less available data in the medical domain, using a pretrained language
120
        encoder and freezing its weights was found to boost downstream performance while considerably
121
        reducing computational cost (Liu et al., 2023a). Nevertheless, this line of work has focused mainly
122     on the ECG, X-ray, CT images, and structural MRI images (Chen et al., 2023; Lalam et al., 2023;
123     Liu et al., 2023b).
124
125   • Multiple instance learning. MIL has seen only limited exploration for EEG. Initial studies have
126     investigated the framework by casting crops of EEG as instances and training classifiers for emotion
127     recognition (Caicedo-Acosta et al., 2019), motor imagery (Collazos-Huertas et al., 2020), mental
128     disorders (Sadatnejad et al., 2019), and sleep apnea (Sadatnejad et al., 2019). Of these, only the
129     latter has relied on deep learning. Meanwhile, for multimodal language alignment, Miech et al.
130     (2020) made significant progress by extending the NCE loss to a MIL setting and casting possible
131     text captions as instances.

      3.1.1     EEG- LANGUAGE PRETRAINING

      Here we detail the setup for pretraining ELMs. Whereas vision-language models are typically trained
      by aligning a 2D image with a short caption (Radford et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a), EEG-language
142
      modeling is confronted with long EEG time series and multi-paragraph medical reports. To overcome
143
      this, we employ text segmentation and time series cropping to create multiple non-overlapping
144   samples per modality and subject. Next, we propose sub-unit alignment by pretraining on these
145   cropped samples. In addition to considerably increasing sample size, this enables the extension of
146   successful approaches in vision-language models. We initially describe two strategies for sub-unit
147   alignment. First, EEG and text representations may be projected using neural networks to a new,
148   shared latent space prior to alignment (as in CLIP; Radford et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2022a)),
149   denoted henceforth as ELMe,l . Alternatively, the EEG embeddings may be projected into the output
150   space of the language model (as in M-FLAG by Liu et al. (2023a)), denoted as ELMl . This approach
151   was found to reduce latent collapse in smaller data settings (Liu et al., 2023a). Following a description
152
      of these models, we will introduce an extension based on MIL.
153   For EEG-language pretraining we assume the paired input (xe,i , xl,i ). Here xe,i ∈ Rc×s denotes one
154   or a batch of crops of EEG signal with c channels and s time samples belonging to EEG recording i.
155   Meanwhile, neural signals of recording i as well as patient information is described in xl,i , which
156   represents a natural language text report. The main goal is to train the EEG encoder function fe ,
157   which projects a crop of EEG signal into a vector of lower dimensionality. Following pretraining, this
158
      encoder function fe can be used for downstream applications such as pathology detection.
159   Dropping the recording subscript i for brevity, each pair (xe , xl ) is projected into the vectors e ∈ Rd
160   and l ∈ Rd respectively. For every xe , text of the associated report is sampled according to
161   x̃l = zl (xl ), where zl represents the language sampling function detailed below. First, both the EEG
      crop xe and text x̃l are encoded into vectors he and hl . For ELMe,l , we use projectors ge and gl to

206   Multiple instance learning. While previous approaches aim to align text and EEG crops uniformly,
207
      certain text segments likely describe specific EEG sections more accurately than others. Therefore,
      we introduce a MIL alignment strategy that builds on ELMe,l and accommodates multiple positive
208
      samples, allowing for more nuanced multimodal relationships. Whereas MIL approaches often
209
      rely on operations such as max-pooling to focus on single positive samples, we rely on insights
210   from the video-text alignment approach (MIL-NCE) by Miech et al. (2020). For a given text
211   sample xl , we sample multiple positive EEG crops xe from the paired recording to approximate
212   the P (e|l) distribution, while for an EEG crop, multiple text segments are sampled to model the

214   respectively to approximate P (e, l) via bidirectional alignment. This approach effectively relaxes the
215   assumption of strong alignment for each individual (xe , xl ) pair, instead assuming that, on average,

239   3.1.2    EEG- ONLY SELF - SUPERVISED LEARNING
240
241   We compare the representations learned by EEG-language pretraining to those obtained via EEG-only
242   pretraining. First, we employ multiple methods that train for invariance to data augmentations. This
243
      is achieved by sampling data augmentations for each EEG crop xe , resulting in two differing data
      views {x′e , x′′e }. It is important the data augmentations do not destroy the semantic information in xe .
244
      Training to align the embeddings of these views while preventing collapse has been shown to yield
245
      data representations useful for downstream tasks (Chen et al., 2020; Mohsenvand et al., 2020; Yang
246   et al., 2021). We implement the following methods (Appendix B.5): Bootstrap-Your-Own-Latent
247   (BYOL; Grill et al. (2020)), Variance-Invariance-Covariance Regularization (VICReg; (Bardes et al.,
248   2021)), and Contrast with the World Representation (ContraWR; Yang et al. (2021)). Additionally,
249   we compare against methods using the temporal ordering of EEG crops: Relative Positioning (RP;
250   Banville et al. (2021)), Temporal Shuffling (TS; Banville et al. (2021)), Contrastive Predictive Coding
251   (CPC; Banville et al. (2021)).

254
255
      • TUEG. The Temple University Hospital (TUH) EEG Corpus is the largest available corpus of
      hospital EEG data with varying montages, channel counts, and sampling frequencies (n=26846
256
      (Obeid & Picone, 2016)). For each patient, one or more EEG sessions are provided, each of which
257
      contains one or more recordings. For most of the dataset, no labels are available beyond patient age
258   and sex. However, many EEG sessions are associated with a natural-language clinical report.
259
260   • TUAB. The TUH Abnormal EEG corpus is a subset of TUEG which was manually labeled by
261
      clinicians indicating whether the EEG displays pathological abnormalities (Lopez et al., 2015). This
      enables the binary classification task of predicting the status of {normal, abnormal}. As training
262
      (n=2717) and evaluation (n=276) sets are provided, we use the latter as a hold-out test set.
263
264
      3.2.1    T EXT PROCESSING
265
266   In order to categorize the textual content in the clinical reports, we employed regular expressions
267   matching for commonly-occurring headings (an overview is provided in Appendix F.3). These
268   enabled the segmentation of individual reports into their respective headings with associated text
269   paragraphs, providing insight into which information in physician reports is encoded in the EEG.
      We cluster headings into four categories. First, the clinical history cluster of headings contains


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      demographic information in terms of patient age and sex, as well as a brief description of relevant
271   current and/or past pathology and symptoms. The record description cluster includes the physician’s
272   observations of the EEG traces, which describes both normal and abnormal features, often in terms of
273   oscillatory brain activity. The medication cluster contains the patient’s current medication information.
274   Finally, the interpretation cluster summarizes a physician’s thoughts, often including the impression
275   of whether the EEG is normal or pathologically abnormal, as well as a clinical correlation. To
276   investigate whether EEG-language models can learn richer representations by being exposed to a
277   larger variety of text, we also train models by sampling text from all four aforementioned clusters.
278   Due to the heterogeneity of the clinical reports, we further test the utility of summarizing the
279   pathological status indicated by the clinical report using a large language model (LLM). Due to the
280   sensitive nature of the clinical reports, we use the Llama-3 8B model (AI, 2024) locally and instruct
281   for the production of a single-sentence summary of a report, which should include whether the EEG
282   was deemed abnormal and for which reasons (Appendix F.3).
283   Language encoding. Given a sampled paragraph from a clinical report or the LLM-generated
284   summary, we encode this text by relying on the embedding of a special [cls] token which aggregates
285   the representations across all tokens. As such, given a clinical report xl , the transformation function
286   zl corresponds to text segmentation or summarization yielding x̃l . Following tokenization, we embed
287   into the [cls] token using fl . The resulting text embedding hl may be used for multimodal pretraining.

290   3.2.2   EEG PROCESSING
291
292   From the EEG dataset, recordings longer than 2.5 hours were omitted to filter out a small subset of
293
      very long, potentially overnight recordings. For training efficiency, only the first 45 minutes of a
      recording were used. Any recording files shorter than 70 seconds were also omitted.
294
295   EEG preprocessing. EEG data received minimal preprocessing (using MNE (Gramfort et al., 2013)).
296   First, the initial 10 seconds were removed to reduce the impact of set-up artefacts. Afterwards, a
297   bandpass filter of 0.1-49 Hz was applied and all recordings were resampled to 100 Hz. To reduce
298   the impact of signal artefacts, all EEG signals had their amplitude clipped to ± 800 µV. As a large
299
      majority of recordings used an average-reference (AR) or linked-ear reference (LE), we only used
      these recordings and standardized them via transformation to the 20-channel Temporal Central
300
      Parasagittal (TCP) montage. To enable fair comparisons between methods, the optimal crop-length
301
      out of {5,10,20,30,60} seconds was determined without data-leakage through training and evaluation
302   on subsets of the training data only (Appendix B.2). Based on these results, we used 20 and 60
303   second crops for EEG-only and EEG-language modeling respectively.

      4.1   R ETRIEVAL
352
353   To investigate the information represented in the learned embeddings resulting from EEG-language
354   training, we perform retrieval analyses. Given a medical report describing the patient and their EEG
355   recording, we probe the ability to recover the patient’s EEG by rank-ordering candidate EEG based
356   on embedding similarity. This analysis is also performed in opposite direction, by retrieving the
357
      associated report given an EEG recording. As reports refer to an entire recording, EEG embeddings
      of single crops are averaged. Given that reports consist of multiple paragraphs, embeddings of single
358
      paragraphs are also averaged. This procedure yields one EEG and report embedding per patient
359
      recording, which we use for rank-ordering based on cosine similarity.
360
361   The top-K retrieval accuracy, which scores whether the patient’s EEG or report has a rank equal
362   or better than K, is plotted in Figure 2. Many models perform considerably above chance level,
363
      indicating the successful generalization of learned multimodal EEG-language information. The
      text sampling markedly impacts the report retrieval. The clinical history and medication clusters,
364
      which contain no direct description of the observed EEG recording, score lowest. While including
365
      such information (description cluster) helps considerably, retrieval is particularly effective when a
366   pathology-relevant context is provided (interpretation cluster and LLM summary). This indicates that
367   pathology is a significant source of between-subject variation. Further clear improvements are seen
368   when text from all clusters is sampled, indicating that these clusters contain unique information and
369   that EEG-language modeling can capture multiple dimensions of patient information.
370
      For both EEG and report retrieval, ELMe,l models tend to outperform ELMl models. However, this
371
      discrepancy in performance is particularly prevalent for report retrieval. This is likely due to omission
372   of a text projection head in ELMl , which may therefore lack the flexibility to appropriately separate
373   the EEG reports in latent space. Due to the benefit of pretraining using all text clusters, we pretrain
374   our ELM-MIL models in this manner only and observe that these can further improve retrieval
375   performance. Interestingly, sampling multiple positive EEG crops (i.e., e|l) performs considerably
376   better than the inverse (l|e), yet bidirectional alignment and sampling multiple positives jointly (e, l)
377   scores highest. These results indicate for the additional flexibility of this approach to aid in multimodal
      alignment, supporting the hypothesis that not all EEG and text pairs are equally informative.

      4.2.1    L INEAR PROBING
410   Next, we study the learned representations in their relevance to clinical pathology. To do so, we
411   first train linear probes to detect pathology on the representations of pretrained models on TUAB
412   under varying amounts of labels (Table 2; Appendix D). Models are trained on single EEG crops,
413   across which we average predictions to obtain a recording-level prediction. We find EEG-language
414   pretraining yields large improvements for pathology detection over EEG-only pretraining, with
415   multimodal models being particularly effective at small sample sizes: at 1% of exposed labels,
416   performance increases reach 8.7% balanced accuracy and 9.7% AUROC. Our ELM-MIL models are
417   found to score highest on this task too, albeit with variability between the variants.
418   We evaluate models out-of-distribution on the NMT EEG Dataset (Khan et al. (2022); Section A.1)
419   and investigate two additional tasks for clinical event detection (TUEV and TUSZ: A.2). We observe
420   strong performance for ELM-MIL across evaluations.
421
      Given the broad outperforming of ELMs compared to EEG-only models, we investigated whether
422   the strategy of sub-unit multimodal modeling provides inherent benefits. We provide this additional
423   set of analyses in appendix A.3, which indicates that our sub-unit alignment strategy promotes the
424   encoding of between-subject information even in the absence of semantically relevant text. This
425   allows ELMe,l to nearly match the best EEG-only pretraining strategy for pathology detection when
426   reports are randomly shuffled.

514   5   D ISCUSSION
515
516
      The current work presents a first application of multimodal pretraining using natural language
517   and functional brain data in a medical context. Our findings indicate that ELMs provide better
518   representations than EEG-only SSL. To enable this, we perform sub-unit alignment following
519   timeseries cropping and text segmentation. We further improve downstream performance via MIL-
520   InfoNCE to address misalignment. The most useful representations were obtained via a combination
521   of our ELM-MIL models and exposure to a variety of textual information. Such multimodal models
522   were also found to be capable of zero-shot pathology detection. Using linear probing, sizable
523   performance improvements over EEG-only SSL were observed, with the largest gains in contexts
524   with few annotated samples. We additionally show strong performance of ELMs via external
525
      validation and clinical event detection tasks.
526   Some considerations of the current study deserve mention. No additional paired EEG-report datasets
527   are currently publicly available, which for now prevents assessing the generalizability of our results
528   across datasets. Although great care was taken to prevent data leakage and no model development
529   involved any of the evaluation data, future work is required to properly study generalizability and
530   scaling behavior as investigated using large transformer models in (Yang et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
531
      2024). While the retrieval analyses suggest that certain models learn richer data representations,
      a lack of annotations hindered a more detailed assessment of their utility for downstream tasks.
532
      Future research could benefit from annotations for specific pathologies, enabling more precise model
533
      comparisons. Additionally, we observed lower pathology detection scores for EEG-only SSL than a
534   previous study (Mohsenvand et al., 2020), despite using the same data augmentations. Their work
535   pretrained larger models on multiple datasets to output sequential representations. However, many
536   such adaptations to the EEG encoder or its training could also be applied to EEG-language modeling.
537   Finally, although several models displayed accurate zero-shot pathology detection, the variability
538   in results may be due to the challenges of language modeling with limited data. Further research is
539   needed to explore additional inductive biases or regularization of the text projector to address this
      issue.

1064   B.5   EEG-O NLY P RETRAINING
1065
1066   We implement the following methods for EEG-only SSL:
1067
       Bootstrap-Your-Own-Latent. BYOL relies on two encoder models: an online and a target network
1068   (Grill et al., 2020). During pretraining, the online network is trained to predict the target model’s
1069   output. Meanwhile, the weights of the target network are updated using a moving average of the
1070   weights of the online network, which has been empirically shown to prevent collapse of the latent
1071   space. For alignment, ℓ2 normalization is applied to the EEG embeddings {h′e , h′′e } and the mean
1072   square distance is minimized. We adopt the recommended parameter value for the exponential
1073   moving average (Grill et al., 2020). The projection head is a 2-layer non-linear MLP with a hidden
1074   dimension of width 256 and an output dimension of 32.
1075   Variance-Invariance-Covariance Regularization. VICReg allows for the use of a single encoder
1076   model and prevents collapse by applying two explicit regularization terms to each of the embed-
1077   ding batches {h′e , h′′e } (Bardes et al., 2021). The ’variance’ term maintains the standard deviation
1078   (computed batch-wise) of every embedding dimension above a threshold, thereby avoiding a trivial
1079   solution. In addition, latent collapse is avoided through the ’covariance’ term which decorrelates
       pairs of embedding dimensions. The method minimizes the mean square distance between {h′e , h′′e }.


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Hyperparameters are set to their recommended values (Bardes et al., 2021). The projection head is a
1081   2-layer non-linear MLP with a hidden dimension of width 256 and an output dimension of 256.
1082
1083   Contrast with the World Representation. ContraWR was proposed to improve augmentation-
1084
       based SSL for EEG (Yang et al., 2021). The method, which is contrastive in nature, maximizes
       similarity between {h′e , h′′e } while preventing collapse by minimizing similarity with ’negative’
1085
       samples. ContraWR forms a negative representation by aggregating across all negative batch elements,
1086
       aiming to compensate for the low signal-to-noise of EEG data by creating a more reliable negative
1087   contrast. It relies on a triplet loss based on Info-NCE (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010). We also here
1088   set the hyperparameters to the values recommended by the authors (Yang et al., 2021). The projection
1089   head is a 2-layer non-linear MLP with a hidden dimension of width 256 and an output dimension of
1090   32.
1091
       Relative Positioning. Pairs of EEG crops are sampled and assigned binary labels based on their tem-
1092
       poral proximity (Banville et al., 2021). Crops close in time are labeled positive, while those far apart
1093   are labeled negative. We use the same EEG encoder as for all other methods to create representations
1094   and use the suggested contrastive module to compute the element-wise absolute difference between
1095   representations. A logistic regression model then predicts the label. The method is trained using
1096   binary logistic loss. For all methods by (Banville et al., 2021), we use the hyperparameters reported
1097   to work best on TUAB, including between-subject sampling of EEG crops.
1098
       Temporal Shuffling. An extension of Relative Positioning by sampling triplets of EEG crops. The
1099   task is to determine whether the crops are in temporal order or shuffled (Banville et al., 2021). The
1100   contrastive module concatenates absolute differences between representations. As with Relative
1101   Positioning, a logistic regression model is used for prediction, and the method is trained end-to-end
1102   using binary logistic loss.
1103
       Contrastive Predictive Coding. This method uses an autoregressive encoder to summarize a
1104
       sequence of EEG crops into a context vector (Banville et al., 2021). The task is to predict which
1105   future crop actually follows the context, among negative samples. A bilinear model is used for
1106   prediction at each future step. The method is trained end-to-end using the InfoNCE loss.

1173   To study the predictive capability of learned representations after pretraining, we train linear probes
1174   and perform zero-shot classification.
1175   Linear probe
1176
1177   For linear evaluation, we train logistic linear regression models using 10-fold cross validation for
1178
       each pretrained model using sklearn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). We perform grid-search over 45
       logarithmically-spaced values for L2 regularization between 10−6 and 105 via a validation set.
1179
1180   Supervised Learning
1181
       For the supervised learning baseline, we use the identical EEG encoder backbone as used for all other
1182   analyses and use 60 second crops. We add an MLP (hidden dimensionality of 256) with dropout
1183   p = 0.5 and output dimensionality equal to the amount of classes. The ADAM learning rate is set to
1184   0.001 and we use the validation set to select weight decay out of [0.1, 0.01, 0.0001]. We use a batch
1185   size of 256 and train using the cross entropy loss. When using 100% labels, we first train on the
1186   training set for up to 50 epochs (with early stopping after 5 epochs without improvement) and select
1187   the epoch which resulted in the best validation loss. Subsequently, we continue training on the train
       and validation sets together until the loss has decreased below the best validation loss.

       E     C LINICAL IMPLICATIONS
1243
1244
       To better understand the clinical utility of the learned EEG representations, we conducted additional
1245
       experiments using 5-second EEG segments from the TUSZ and TUEV datasets. The strong perfor-
1246
       mance across both recording-level classification and event detection tasks suggests that our model
1247   learns clinically relevant features at multiple temporal scales. With further progress, this capability
1248   may support various future clinical applications, from rapid screening of prolonged recordings to
1249   real-time event detection. Whereas the present study focuses on establishing an initial application to
1250   explore viability, future work may benefit from focussing on improving the interpretability of these
1251   representations through techniques such as channel-specific attribution. Additionally, the multimodal
1252   nature of our approach opens possibilities for automated report generation, which could assist in
1253   clinical documentation while maintaining human oversight. Certain clinical limitations also deserve
1254   further attention, such as a careful study of how the frequency of specific pathology and clinical
1255
       events in reports impacts model performance. Finally, biases present in language models may impact
       multimodal pretraining, which should be investigated in future work.
