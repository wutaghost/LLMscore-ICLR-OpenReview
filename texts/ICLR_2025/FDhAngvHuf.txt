009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate biases in pretraining datasets for large language models (LLMs) through
012            dataset classification experiments. Building on prior work demonstrating the existence of biases in
013            popular computer vision datasets, we analyze popular open-source pretraining text datasets derived
014            from CommonCrawl including C4, RefinedWeb, DolmaCC, RedPajama-V2, FineWeb, DCLM-
015            Baseline, and others. Despite those datasets being obtained with similar filtering and deduplication
016            steps, neural networks can classify surprisingly well which dataset a single text sequence belongs to,
017
               significantly better than a human can. This indicates that popular pretraining datasets have their own
               unique biases or fingerprints. Those biases remain even when the text is rewritten with LLMs. We
018
               demonstrate that these biases propagate through training: Random sequences generated by models
019
               trained on those datasets can be classified well by a classifier trained on the original datasets.
020
021
022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   In 2011, Torralba & Efros (2011) proposed the dataset classification experiment to examine biases present in common
025   computer vision dataset. The paper demonstrated that computer vision reserachers can relatively easily classify to
026   which dataset an image from a computer vision dataset popular at the time (e.g., PASCAL, Caltech101, ImageNet,...)
027   belongs to. Moreover, classifiers can be trained to relatively reliably classify which dataset an image belongs to.
028   Torralba & Efros (2011) concluded that “despite the best efforts of their creators, the datasets appear to have a strong
029
      built-in bias”. While some of the bias can be accounted for by isolating specific objects the different datasets focus on,
      Torralba & Efros (2011) found that the biases are still present in some form, even if those effects are isolated.
030
031   Recently, Liu & He (2024) revisited the dataset classification experiment in the current era of large-scale and diverse
032   vision datasets like YFCC Thomee et al. (2016), DataComp Gadre et al. (2023), and LAION Schuhmann et al. (2022).
033   Those datasets are large in scale and are collected to train generalizable representations, as opposed to datasets col-
034   lected for a specific purpose (for example for urban scene understanding Cordts et al. (2016)). Liu & He (2024) found,
035
      perhaps surprisingly, that even for those large and diverse datasets, classifiers can relatively accurately assign a single
      image to belong to one of those datasets.
036
037   In this paper we study the bias of pretraining datasets for large language models (LLMs), as well as the propagation
038   of this bias through training with dataset classification experiments.
039   We consider the most popular open web-based datasets for general purpose LLMs, specifically C4 Raffel et al. (2020),
040   RefinedWeb Penedo et al. (2023), DolmaCC Soldaini et al. (2024), RedPajama-V2 Together Computer (2023a),
041   FineWeb and FineWeb-edu Penedo et al. (2024), and DCLM-BASELINE Li et al. (2024). These datasets consist
042   of sequences of text of average length ranging from 477 to 1235 tokens (see Appendix E), and are obtained by pre-
043   processing and filtering CommonCrawl. These datasets are considered to be diverse and cover the large variety of text
044   that is available in the internet, and are commonly used for pretraining and pretraining research of LLMs.
045   Our main findings are:
046
047          • We demonstrate that sequences from open pretraining datasets can be well classified to belong to a cer-
048            tain dataset, highlighting unique biases or fingerprints inherent in datasets. For example, the datasets C4,
049            RefinedWeb, DolmaCC, RedPajama-V2, and FineWeb are all obtained from CommonCrawl using a very
050            similar pipeline consisting of deduplication and heuristic quality filtering with similar rules. Yet an LLM
051            trained to classify whether a single sequence is part of C4, RefinedWeb, and RedPajama-V2 achieves 80%
052
               accuracy, well above chance (33.3% accuracy), and significantly above the human accuracy.
053          • Even when rewriting sequences with LLMs to remove some of the formatting, sequences can be well distin-
               guished by a classifier.

054
             • The biases inherent in the pretrained data propagate through training: Sequences generated at random from
055            LLMs pretrained on DCLM-Baseline, RefinedWeb, and FineWeb-Edu can be well distinguished (89.15%
056            accuracy) with a classifier trained on the original training data.
057
             • LLMs pretrained on several domains can generate random sequences that reflect the proportion of the domains
058
               in the pretraining mixture. By classifying the output sequences with a classifier trained to distinguish between
059
               the original domains, we can estimate the mixture proportions.

062   2     R ELATED W ORK
063
064   This work is inspired by Torralba & Efros (2011)’s dataset classification experiment for vision datasets and Liu & He
065   (2024)’s recent work that revisited the dataset classification experiment in the context of modern large scale dataset.
066   Liu & He (2024) found, similar as we find for language datasets, that images from the large scale and diverse computer
067   vision datasets YFCC Thomee et al. (2016), CC Changpinyo et al. (2021), and DataComp Gadre et al. (2023) can be
068
      accurately classified as belonging to one of those datasets.
069   A variety of works study the problem of classifying LLM generated text. Hans et al. (2024) and many phrase this as a
070   classification problem Solaiman et al. (2019); Tian et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2023). Guo et al. (2023) demonstrate that
071   ChatGPT generated answers can be well distinguished from human answers by a classifier, if the text is sufficiently
072   long . In this work we focus on distinguishing popular pretraining text datasets with a classifier, not AI vs human
073   generated text.
074   Shi et al. (2023) and Maini et al. (2024) consider the problem of detecting pretraining data based on blackbox access
075   of LLMs; specifically given a text and blackbox acccess to an LLM, was the LLM trained on that text? In Section 5 we
076   study the loosely related problem whether a classifier trained to distinguish training data can distinguish data generated
077   by the LLMs trained with it.
078   Carlini et al. (2021) and Nasr et al. (2023) attempt to extract training data from LLMs. They show that an adversary
079   can extract verbatim text sequences from the model’s training data by querying the LLM with no previous information
080   of the training set. In section 6 we assume the training domains are known, and estimate the proportion of each domain
081   in the training mixture.

      3     S ETUP AND DATASETS CONSIDERED
085
086
      Throughout this paper, we perform dataset classification for language datasets as follows. Each dataset consists of a set
      of sequences, and a classifier is trained to distinguish the sequences from N such datasets. We measure performance
087
      on a test set consisting of an equal amount of sequences from each of the N datasets.
088
089   As classifier, we use a standard transformer model for next-word prediction that is fine-tuned on a training set of
090   sequences to perform N -way classification. See Section 4 and Appendix A for the details of the model used, training
091   details, and ablation studies with parameters of the classifier and other classifiers.
092
093   3.1   D ISTINGUISHING DATA FROM DIFFERENT SOURCES
094
095
      Language models are often pretrained on data from different sources, for example LLama 1’s Hugo Touvron et al.
      (2023)’s pretraining data, and reproduction of the data, RedPajama-1T Together Computer (2023b), consists of the
096
      sources C4, CommonCrawl (CC), Arxiv, Github, Wikipedia, and Stack Exchange. Some of those sources are very
097
      easy for humans to distinguish, for example Github (containing code) and C4/CC (containing little code). Thus, it
098   is perhaps not surprising that we find that 6 way classification of the Redpajama-1T sources (C4, CC, Arxiv, Github,
099   Wikipedia, Stack Exchange) yields an accuracy of 98.25%.

101
      3.2   DATASETS CONSIDERED
102
103   We consider seven of the largest and most popular open datasets for pretraining general-purpose LLMs based on
104   web-filtered data. The datasets are based on web crawls from CommonCrawl, a nonprofit foundation that provides
105   a publicly available web archive. Much of the text extracted by CommonCrawl consists of text that is not useful for
106   training, like incomplete sentences, HTML artifacts, and duplicate texts. Thus all of the datasets we consider for
107   LLM training are obtained by the dataset creators by extracting text from CommonCrawl data, filtering, cleaning, and
      deduplicating the text data.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      All datasets are obtained by i) extracting text using parsers like resiliparse or using CommonCrawl’s pre-
109   extracted text and language filtering, ii) applying heuristic filtering (examples are removing very short texts, and
110   removing texts with curly brackets { since those indicate code), iii) deduplication (for example, identical or nearly
111   identical webpages are filtered out), and iv) machine learning based filtering (for example filtering based on a clas-
112   sifier trained to distinguish high-quality data from average data). The exact choices of those steps have a significant
113   effect on the composition of the datasets are how good the models are that are trained on them.
114
      We consider the following seven datasets:
115
116   C4: The Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus Raffel et al. (2020) is a popular dataset consisting of 360B Tokens obtained
117   from CommonCrawl text extracted in April 2019, followed by i) language filtering, ii) heurisitc filtering, and iii)
118
      deduplication.
119   FineWeb: FineWeb Penedo et al. (2024) is a 15T token dataset extracted from CommonCrawl through i) language and
120   ii) heuristic quality filtering and iii) deduplication. The heuristic filters and deduplication steps are carefully chosen
121   based on ablation studies.
122   RefinedWeb: RefinedWeb Penedo et al. (2023) is a large scale (5T tokens, 600B publicly available) obtained from
123   CommonCrawl by i) language and ii) heuristic filtering and iii) deduplication.
124
125
      Dolma CC: Dolma Soldaini et al. (2024) is an open corpus of 3T tokens from different sources. The biggest pro-
      portion, about 2.4T tokens, is obtained from CommonCrawl. We consider the CommonCrawl part, which was ob-
126
      tained by first downloading about a quarter of the most recent CommonCrawl data in 2023 (i.e., data from 2020-05
127
      to 2023-06), and was processed with i) language and ii) heuristic quality filtering and iii) deduplication. As a ma-
128   chine learning based quality filtering step, for each sequence the perplexity was computed with an LLM to measure
129   Wikipedia-likeness (following the CCNet pipeline Wenzek et al. (2019)) and partitioned into into head, middle, and
130   tail by perplexity; we consider the head and middle parts.
131
      RedPajama-V2: RedPajama-V2 Together Computer (2023a) is a corpus of 30T filtered and deduplicated tokens also
132
      processed with i) language and ii) heuristic quality filtering, and iii) deduplication. We consider the 20.5T token part of
133
      the corpus consisting of English speaking documents, as for all other datasets we also consider the English part only.
134   The data contains a broad coverage of CommonCrawl, and comes with quality annotations that enables slicing and
135   filtering the data. As a machine learning based quality filtering step, for each sequence the perplexity was computed
136   with an LLM trained to identify Wikipedia-like documents and partitioned into head, middle, and tail, and head and
137   middle was retained. We consider head and middle as for Dolma.
138
      DCLM-BASELINE: DCLM-BASELINE Li et al. (2024) was obtained from CommonCrawl through i) text extraction
139
      with resiliparse and language and ii) heuristic quality filtering, deduplication, and iv) machine learning based quality
140   filtering. All steps where chosen by ablation studies to obtain a dataset so that models trained on it perform well.
141   The final, machine learning based filtering step is important and is trained to classify instruction-formatted data from
142   OpenHermes 2.5 and high-scoring posts from the r/ExplainLikeImFive subreditt from RefinedWeb. Models trained on
143   this dataset perform very well on common benchmarks.
144
      FineWeb-Edu: FineWeb-Edu Penedo et al. (2024) is obtained from FineWeb through machine learning based quality
145
      filtering to obtain data with educational text, and consists of 1.3T tokens. Models trained on FineWeb-Edu perform
146   very well on knowledge and reasoning benchmarks such as MMLU Hendrycks et al. (2021).
147
148   All datasets are based on web crawls, are large in scale, and are broad, i.e., not focused on a specific topic or area
149
      (such as Arxiv, Github, etc). Therefore it is perhaps surprising that sequences of these datasets can be relatively
      reliably distinguished, as we find in the next section.

251   4.2             C LASSIFICATION ACCURACY ACHIEVED BY HUMANS
252
253   Our experiments show that classifiers can accurately differentiate between datasets, even when the differences are
254   subtle to human perception. This is particularly striking with datasets like C4 and FineWeb, which are obtained with
255   similar filtering steps and appear nearly identical to humans. Two example sequences from each dataset are in Figure
256   2, more examples are in Appendix D.
257
      We conducted a dataset classification experiment to measure human performance. The task is binary classification
258   between C4 and FineWeb. We gave two machine learning researchers several sequences from each dataset for inspec-
259   tion. For testing, the researchers were given 50 unlabeled sequences from each set and had unlimited time to classify
260   them. On average, it took them 1.5 hours to label the 100 sequences.
261
      The researchers achieve an average accuracy of 63%, only 13% above random guessing. In contrast, the 160M sized
262
      classifier trained on 320M tokens performs significantly better attaining 88%, which highlights the model’s ability to
263
      identify subtle patterns that are not easily distinguishable by humans.

316   4.4     R EMOVING FORMATTING AND CLASSIFYING BASED ON WORD FREQUENCIES ONLY
317
318   We have so far seen that biases exist within popular text datasets, and persist even when the text is rephrased. We next
319   attempt to isolate which features are responsible for this bias.
320   Removing formatting. We remove structural formatting of C4 and FineWeb. Specifically, we remove all newlines,
321   itemization and enumeration patterns, including numbers, bullet points and similar markers that commonly denote list
322   elements, excessive spaces, and other special characters such as tabs and carriage returns with regular expressions
323   (regex). The resulting text is a single continuous block of text. Essential punctuation markers, such as periods and
      commas, are not modified as they contribute to the text’s meaning rather than just its formatting or display.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      We train the 160M model to classify between regex preprocessed C4 and FineWeb. We use 320M training tokens
325   (160M tokens per dataset), and 8192 test sequences. The accuracy is 72.42%, about 15% less than the accuracy on the
326   original datasets (87.37%). This drop in accuracy suggests that models detect patterns in formatting that are important
327   for classification. However, the fact that classification remains relatively accurate even after unifying the formatting,
328   suggests that there are biases beyond format and structure present within the datasets.
329
      Bag of Words (BoW) is a simple text classification method that represents text as a collection of unique words,
330
      disregarding format, grammar, word order, or context. Each text sequence is transformed into a vector with the
331   frequency of each unique word within the text. For instance, BoW transforms the following two texts: “I like apples
332   but not bananas” and “I like bananas but not apples” to the same exact vector representation.
333
334
      We use BoW to distinguish between C4 and FineWeb, and achieve a classification accuracy of 63.45%. Classification
      with BoW is higher than a random guess despite reducing each text sequence to a vector with the frequency of words
335
      within it. BoW disregards any semantic relationship between the words, it is based solely on the vocabulary used,
336
      which suggests that the vocabulary distributions of C4 and FineWeb are different.

      4.6   D ISCUSSION
382
383   Our experiments suggest that format, vocabulary, and content are all characteristics that enable differentiating between
384   the datasets. While some of these features are obvious, others are subtle and are only detected by neural networks.
385
      The best example for features that are easily identifiable, is the formatting in DCLM. The DCLM team used resiliparse
386
      to extract text, which very frequently inserts new lines between the sentences (i.e., ends sentences with \n\n). This
387   makes DCLM sequences particularly distinct, see Appendix D. This is also reflected in the high accuracy a model
388   attains when classifying DCLM sequences as seen in Tables 1 and 5.
389
390
      Another example is FineWeb-Edu. Most of the sequences are educational and scientific, and thus classifying educa-
      tional and scientific sequences as FineWeb-Edu sequences can work relatively well, see Appendix D for examples.
391
392   For other datasets, it is harder to point to specific individual patterns that make them distinguishable, for example we
393   found C4 and FineWeb sequences very hard to distinguish for a human, but easy for the model (see Section 4.2). This
394   might be because the model sees millions of examples from which several subtle features can be picked up.

396   5     B IAS P ROPAGATION
397
398   From our dataset classification experiments, we observe that each dataset exhibits inherent biases not apparent to
399   humans but detectable by classifiers. We now explore how these biases propagate to text generated by LLMs trained
400   on those datasets.
401
      We consider the following three publicly available LLMs pre-trained on individual datasets from the seven datasets
402   considered in this study:
403
404          • Falcon-7B: A 7B parameter model pretrained on 1.5 trillion tokens from the RefinedWeb dataset by TII
405            (Technology Innovation Institute) Almazrouei et al. (2023).
406          • DCLM-7B: A 7B model pretrained on 2.5 trillion tokens from the DCLM-Baseline dataset by the DCLM
407            team Li et al. (2024).
408          • FineWeb-Edu-1.8B: A 1.8B parameter model trained by Huggingface on 350 billion tokens from the
409            FineWeb-Edu dataset. The smaller model size and fewer pretraining tokens for FineWeb-Edu are consis-
410            tent with its focus on educational texts.
411
412   All LLMs are only pretrained on the respective datasets, and not finetuned in any way. We generate data with each of
413   the LLMs by prompting the LLM with a single token, sampled from the distribution of tokens that appear as the first
414   token in the sequences derived from original training data of the LLM. We generate 160M training tokens and 8192
415
      test sequences from each LLM.
416   Original vs generated: By inspecting the generated data, we observe that the outputs of the LLMs resemble the
417   data on which they are trained (see Appendix D for examples). We next measure a classifier’s ability to differentiate
418   between the original and generated data.
419   We train a 160M model on 320M tokens (160M original, 160M generated) for every dataset to distinguish the original
420   from the generated data. The accuracies are as follows: RefinedWeb 89.64%, DCLM-Baseline 89.61%, and FineWeb-
421   Edu 89.84%. This is not surprising, as it is well established that text generated with current LLM can be relatively
422   well distinguished from human-written text if the text is sufficiently long Hans et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2024).
423
      Generated vs generated: We next study how well we can distinguish between the generated data. We train a 160M
424
      model on 480M training tokens (160M per generated dataset) for the 3-way classification task of generated Refined-
425   Web, generated DCLM-Baseline, and generated FineWeb-Edu data.
426
427   The classifier achieves an accuracy of 95.59%, indicating that these generated datasets are easily distinguishable, even
428
      easier than the original datasets (89.76% as in Table 1). This is likely because the generated data comes from different
      LLMs, and each LLM introduces its biases in the data it generates.
429
430   Classifying generated data with a model trained to distinguish the original data: We next measure to what extent
431   the bias in a dataset can be measured from sequences generated by a language model trained on this dataset. To this
      end, we measure whether a classifier trained on original data can effectively classify generated data.

432
      Using the 3-way classifier trained on the original RefinedWeb, DCLM-Baseline, and FineWeb-Edu data (as described
433   in Section 4), we classify the generated data. The classifier achieves 89.15% accuracy on the generated data, only
434   0.61% less than the accuracy on the original data (89.76% as in Table 1).
435
436
      This indicates that the unique biases and fingerprints inherent in pretraining datasets propagate through training, and
      can be measured surprisingly well from the outputs of models trained on those datasets.

462   6     E STIMATE M IXTURE P ROPORTIONS
463
464
      We have so far demonstrated that widely-used LLM pretraining datasets contain biases that make them distinguishable,
465
      and that these biases propagate through training. As a result, a classifier trained on the original data can accurately
466
      classify data generated by an LLM. Building on these findings, we can to some extent estimate the mixture proportions
467   of pretraining datasets of an LLM, as we discuss next.
468
469   LLMs are typically pretrained on a mixture of datasets with certain mixture proportions. These proportions signif-
470
      icantly impact model performance and are non-trivial to optimize Xie et al. (2023); Albalak et al. (2023); Ge et al.
      (2024). Several LLM developers disclose only the datasets used in training, but not the precise mixture proportions,
471
      such as GPT-NeoX Black et al. (2022), OPT Zhang et al. (2022), and Galactica Taylor et al. (2022).
472
473   We hypothesize that an LLM pretrained on multiple datasets, when prompted with a random token, will generate
474   sequences that closely follow the proportions of its training mixture, since LLMs learn the underlying data distribution
475   during training Deletang et al. (2024), and generate tokens by sampling from the probability distribution of the learned
476
      patterns. We therefore expect the model’s outputs to statistically follow the original dataset proportions, producing
      sequences that reflect the frequency of each dataset in the mixture.
477
478   To verify this hypothesis, we utilize SlimPajama Shen et al. (2024), which is a further refined and deduplicated version
479   of RedPajama-1T Together Computer (2023b). SlimPajama consists of 7 domains: Arxiv, Books, Github, C4, CC
480   (Common Crawl), SE (Stack Exchange), and Wikipedia. The SlimPajama team provides two 1.3B LLMs trained on
481   330B tokens from the SlimPajama dataset. The first LLM is trained on only 4 domains: Books, Github, CC, and
482
      Wikipedia, and the second one is trained on all 7 domains. The mixture proportion of each domain is known.
483   We train a 4-way classifier on the original data from the 4 domains: Books, Github, CC, and Wikipedia, and another
484   7-way classifier on the original data from all 7 domains. We use the 160M model as a classifier, and 160M training
485   tokens from each domain. We generate 2048 random sequences from each LLM by prompting the LLM with one
      random token, then classify the generated sequences using the the classifiers trained on the original data.

517   7    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
518
519
      In this paper we demonstrated that popular pretraining text datasets contain inherent biases that propagate through
520
      training, enabling a classifier trained on original data to effectively classify generated data and, consequently esti-
521   mate the pretraining mixture proportions. We showed that classification is possible under various conditions such as
522   rephrasing and finetuning.
523
524
      However, one case where classification accuracy is degraded is when datasets consist of the same domains but differ
      solely in their mixture proportions. Consider two perfectly distinguishable dataset domains, A and B. Two datasets
525
      X and Y are constructed with different mixtures of A and B, where X has a higher proportion of A than Y, and
526
      Y has a higher proportion of B than X. Sequences from A in Y may be misclassified as belonging to X, since X
527   has seen more sequences from A. Similarly, sequences from B in X are likely to be misclassified as originating from
528   Y. This setup highlights how classification becomes unreliable when datasets differ only in domain proportions rather
529   than content or filtering techniques.

677   A     M ODEL , T RAINING D ETAILS , AND H YPERPARAMETERS
678
679   In this section, we outline the architectures of the models employed in our experiments, as well as the training proce-
680   dures and hyperparameters. For all experiments, we utilize the GPT-NeoX tokenizer Black et al. (2022), which has a
681
      vocabulary size of 50,432 tokens.
682
683   A.1    M ODEL
684
      Our primary model is a transformer with 160 million parameters (160M). Additionally, we conduct ablation studies
685
      using models of varying sizes, including 25M, 87M, and 410M parameters. All models are standard autoregressive
686
      transformers, with detailed specifications provided in Table 4.
687
688   To adapt the standard transformer architecture for classification tasks, we replace the final layer with a classification
689   head. Specifically, the original last layer, a linear transformation that maps from the context length to the vocabulary
690
      size, is substituted with a classification head. This classification head is also a linear layer that maps from the context
      length to N , where N represents the number of classification classes.
691
692
693
      A.2    T RAINING D ETAILS
