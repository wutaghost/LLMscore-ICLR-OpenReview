009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While astonishingly capable, large Language Models (LLM) can sometimes pro-
012            duce outputs that deviate from human expectations. Such deviations necessitate an
013            alignment phase to prevent disseminating untruthful, toxic, or biased information.
014            Traditional alignment methods based on reinforcement learning often struggle with
015            the identified instability, whereas preference optimization methods are limited by
016            their overfitting to pre-collected hard-label datasets. In this paper, we propose a
017            novel LLM alignment framework named iREPO, which utilizes implicit Reward
018
               pairwise difference regression for Empirical Preference Optimization. Particu-
               larly, iREPO employs self-generated datasets judged by empirical human (or
019
               AI annotator) preference to iteratively refine the aligned policy through a novel
020
               regression-based loss function. Furthermore, we introduce an innovative algo-
021            rithm backed by theoretical guarantees for achieving optimal results under ideal
022            assumptions and providing a practical performance-gap result without such assump-
023            tions. Experimental results with Phi-2 and Mistral-7B demonstrate that iREPO
024            effectively achieves self-alignment using soft-label, self-generated responses and
025            the logit of empirical AI annotators. Furthermore, our approach surpasses prefer-
026            ence optimization baselines in evaluations using the Language Model Evaluation
027            Harness and Multi-turn benchmarks.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) represent a cutting-edge frontier in artificial intelligence, harnessing
033
      vast textual data to produce remarkably human-like text across diverse applications like customer
      service and decision support systems. Unlike traditional neural networks, training LLMs involves
034
      a multi-layered stack of processes, typically unfolds in three main phases: (1) base model training,
035
      where the foundational machine learning model, often a transformer architecture, learns from a vast
036   dataset; (2) supervised fine-tuning, which involves refining the model with curated, high-quality
037   datasets to enhance its performance on specific tasks; and (3) human alignment, which refines model
038   outputs based on feedback to align more closely with human expectations and ethical standards.
039
      Despite the sophistication of their training, LLMs can still occasionally generate outputs that are
040
      biased, untruthful, or irrelevant (Gallegos et al., 2024). This underscores the crucial need for an
041
      alignment phase to correct such deviations and ensure that the models perform ethically and effectively.
042   Alignment strategies can be broadly categorized into online and offline approaches. Online alignment
043   involves methods like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) integrated with
044   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Christiano et al., 2017; Schulman et al., 2017). This dynamic
045   approach leverages human feedback to directly influence the model’s learning trajectory, capitalizing
046   on PPO’s ability to manage complex behaviours and maintain stability in updates. However, PPO’s
047   vulnerability to instability due to reward scaling and KL divergence poses further challenges in
048   maintaining consistent performance.
049   Conversely, offline alignment is often referred to as preference-based learning, employing techniques
050   such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and Identity
051   Preference Optimization (IPO) (Azar et al., 2023). Such methods utilize pre-existing datasets
052   of human preferences and integrate reward modeling directly with policy optimization, thereby
053   eliminating the need for ongoing human interaction. However, they often oversimplify by focusing
      solely on the binary order between responses, potentially overlooking nuanced differences in reward

054
      quality and leading to potential overfitting. Furthermore, the effectiveness of DPO and IPO heavily
055   relies on the quality and diversity of the preference data. Our observations suggest that the quality
056   difference between ‘chosen’ and ‘rejected’ responses in some offline preference dataset samples is
057   minimal. This implies that relying solely on implicit reward differences may not be sufficient.
058
      In this paper, we propose a novel LLM alignment framework, namely implicit Reward Pairwise Differ-
059
      ence Regression for Empirical Preference Optimization (iREPO). By recasting the traditional human
060
      preference model as an empirical preference optimization problem, iREPO aims to dynamically
061   align LLMs using their self-generated responses paired with soft labels. This method offers a distinct
062   advantage over traditional preference optimization approaches that rely on static, pre-collected offline
063   datasets. The core innovation of iREPO lies in regressing the implicit reward pairwise difference
064   directly to the logit of human preferences, thereby eliminating the need for explicit reward model
065   learning. iREPO integrates continuous policy updates and data generation with real-time querying
066   of human feedback, potentially narrowing the gap between the model’s self-generated distribution
067   and the desired target distribution. This approach not only enhances the responsiveness of LLMs to
068   evolving data and norms but also improves the relevance and accuracy of the outputs by ensuring
069
      they are more reflective of current human judgments and preferences.
070   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows.
071
072       • We propose a novel preference optimization framework iREPO that regresses the implicit reward
073         pairwise difference to the logit of empirical human (or AI annotators) preferences. The logit,
074         derived from Zermelo rankings of multiple self-generated responses, effectively captures the
075         quality gap between the strongest and weakest responses. This allows for the maximization of
076
            the implicit reward gap while accounting for the actual quality of responses, thereby enhancing
            model alignment with empirical preferences.
077
078       • We design a new loss function and propose a corresponding algorithm to solve its empirical risk
079         minimization in iREPO. We provide theoretical results showing (i) an optimal alignment policy
080         obtained by iREPO under ideal conditions and (ii) a performance gap between iREPO and the
081         optimal policy without the ideal conditions.
082       • Experimental results on well-known foundation models, such as Phi-2 and Mistral-7B, affirm the
083         superior performance of iREPO, showcasing its effectiveness over well-established baselines in
084         both Language Model Evaluation Harnesses and Multi-Turn benchmarks.

087   2      R ELATED WORK
088
089   Alignment LLM through Reinforcement Learning: Recent advances in aligning LLMs have
090   increasingly leveraged reinforcement learning (RL) methods that incorporate human feedback directly
091   into the learning process (Christiano et al., 2017). Ouyang et al. (2022) introduced a paradigm where
092   RL agents learn from human feedback (RLHF) in the form of preferences between pairs of trajectory
093
      snippets instead of rewards from the environment. This method, alongside statistical gradient-
      following algorithms like REINFORCE (Williams, 1992; Ahmadian et al., 2024) or PPO (Schulman
094
      et al., 2017), provided robust policy gradient methods that enhance the refinement of LLMs in large
095
      action spaces and complex optimization landscapes.
096
097   Iterative and Online Alignment: Further building on RLHF, iterative and online methods (Xiong
098   et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024) developed processes that continuously align RL policies by incorporat-
099
      ing feedback while maintaining critical characteristics of the original policies, thus ensuring both
      adherence to human preferences and robust policy performance. On the other hand, the application of
100
      game-theoretic concepts like minimax and Nash equilibriums has also been explored as a means to
101
      enhance the robustness of model training in the face of diverse and sometimes conflicting human
102   feedback. Munos et al. (2023); Rosset et al. (2024) adopt a Nash learning framework, which seeks to
103   find an equilibrium that harmonizes different objectives defined by human feedback, facilitating a
104   balanced approach to model training. Similarly, (Swamy et al., 2024) utilize a minimax framework to
105   minimize the maximum regret, accommodating a wide spectrum of human preferences and aiming to
106   produce policies that perform well under the most adverse conditions. Recently, Wu et al. (2024)
107   have explored SPPO, a self-play preference optimization framework enabling models to refine their
      alignment through preference-based learning objectives iteratively.

108
      Alignment LLM through Preference Optimization: Beyond RL, preference optimization has
109   emerged as a powerful approach to align LLMs with human judgments (Ziegler et al., 2020). Notable
110   developments include DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), showcasing the potential of directly shaping
111   language model outputs based on human preferences and bypassing traditional reward modelling
112   methods. Additionally, variants such as fDPO (Wang et al., 2023) expanded the methodology by
113   incorporating diverse divergence constraints to manage a wider range of preference complexities and
114   model uncertainties. Chowdhury et al. (2024) proposed cDPO, aiming to enhance the robustness of
115   DPO for consistent model performance in environments characterized by noisy feedback. Zhao et al.
116   (2023) introduced sequence likelihood calibration with human feedback (SLIC-HF), accommodating
117
      various divergence measures beyond the reverse KL divergence.
118   In light of the advances made by DPO and its variants, significant theoretical and practical innovations
119   continue to contribute to the alignment of language models with human preferences (Azar et al.,
120   2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024b; Zhao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Hong et al.,
121   2024). For instance, Azar et al. (2023) presented Ψ-PO, a general theoretical framework that deepens
122   understanding of learning from human preferences. Concurrently, the KTO framework (Ethayarajh
123
      et al., 2024b) is proposed, leveraging the Kahneman-Tversky human utility function based on the
      psychological factors for aligning model behavior with human decision-making patterns. On the
124
      practical side, Liu et al. (2024) introduced statistical rejection sampling techniques to improve the
125
      efficiency and effectiveness of preference optimization. Hong et al. (2024) suggested ORPO, a novel
126   approach that optimizes preferences without needing a reference model, simplifying the optimization
127   process and broadening its applicability.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
381
382
383   Models and Datasets: We use Phi-2 (Hughes, 2023) and Mistral-7B (Tunstall et al., 2023b) as
384
      the pre-trained foundation models. These models undergo supervised fine-tuning on the Ultrachat-
      200k dataset (Ding et al., 2023) to enhance their dialogue capabilities across diverse topics. In the
385
      alignment phase, we utilize the UltraFeedback Binarized dataset (Tunstall et al., 2023b), comprising
386
      approximately 63k prompts with pairs of chosen and rejected responses. To facilitate iterative
387   training, we uniformly sample a subset of datasets (20k for Phi-2 and 15k for Mistral-7B) from the
388   UltraFeedback Binarized dataset in each iteration. For iREPO, we only use the prompts from these
389   subsets and use the aligned models to generate 3 responses with different sampling parameters.
390
      Annotators: To provide preference on the self-generated responses, we utilize a suite of LLM rankers
391
      supported by the LLM-Blender framework (Jiang et al., 2023) for comparing and annotating the
392
      outputs generated by the aligned LLM. These rankers are based on DeBERTa (He et al., 2021) trained
393   on various high-quality and large-scale datasets with human preference annotations such as Mix-
394   Instruct, Summarize From Feedback (Stiennon et al., 2020), Chatbot Arena Conversations (Zheng
395   et al., 2023c). Impressively, despite their relatively small model sizes (from 0.4B - 13B), these
396   rankers exhibit a correlation with human preferences that approach the performance of larger mod-
397   els (Jiang et al., 2023). These low-cost, time-efficient annotators make iREPO practical and robust
398   for evaluating self-generated responses. We detail the choice of AI annotators in Appendix D.5.
399   Evaluation Benchmark: We utilize two widely recognized evaluation benchmarks: the Language
400   Model Evaluation Harness (LM-Eval-Harness) (Gao et al., 2023) and Multi-turn Benchmark (MT-
401   Bench) (Zheng et al., 2023b). The LM-Eval-Harness offers a transparent evaluation platform,
402   assessing LLMs across diverse benchmarks such as ARC (Clark et al., 2018), HellaSwag (Zellers
403   et al., 2019), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022), Winogrande (Sakaguchi
404   et al., 2019), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). These tasks are designed to test different aspects
405   of model capabilities, with models evaluated based on their accuracy and coherence in generating
406   responses. Meanwhile, MT-Bench evaluates LLMs based on their capacity for coherent and engaging
407
      conversations, using 3.3K expert-level pairwise human evaluations of responses from commercial
      LLM models to 80 specific questions.
408
409   Baselines and Implementation: We compare our approach against three baselines: SFT, iterative
410   DPO (Iter-DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Tran et al., 2023), and iterative IPO (Iter-IPO) (Azar et al.,
411   2023), which are widely used in the literature. Similar to our approach, DPO and IPO recast the
412   reinforcement learning-based alignment formulation as simple loss functions to obtain implicit reward
413
      models. While these methods depend on a dataset of preferences for direct optimization, for a fair
      comparison, we initially utilize pre-collected response pairs and apply an empirical human preference
414
      model to train on implicit reward pairwise differences instead of relying on fixed preference outcomes.
415
      More details on datasets, benchmarks, and experimental settings are described in Appendix D.

507   5.3   A BLATION S TUDIES
508
509   Effects of β and Pairwise Reward Difference Models: We investigate the impact of the logit of
510   empirical human preference in iREPO’s loss function on the alignment performance, contrasting
511   training with and without this feature (iREPO w/o Logit). The results depicted in Fig. 2a reveal that
512   omitting the logit term significantly degrades performance. Specifically, iREPO w/o Logit exhibits
513
      a substantial decline in average performance. This suggests that the logit of the empirical human
      preference model plays a vital role in iREPO’s loss function, steering the training process toward
514
      more effective outcomes. Additionally, our evaluation of different β values indicates that performance
515
      variations are minimal. The model with β = 0.001 achieves the best performance, while other values
516   of β slightly underperform but still maintain satisfactory performance.
517
518   Effects of Annotators: To investigate the impact of AI annotator quantity on the performance of
519
      iREPO, we trained the last iteration model using different groups of annotators, with each group
      consisting of {5, 9, 15, 21} members. Each group evaluates a consistent set of responses generated
520
      by the aligned model, and their feedback is incorporated to refine the model’s alignment. The results
521
      in Fig. 2b demonstrate that increasing the number of annotators from 5 to 21 has minimal impact on
522   iREPO’s performance. Notably, models trained with 9 annotators slightly outperform those trained
523   with different numbers of annotators. This indicates that a moderate number of AI annotators is
524   sufficient to capture a broad range of perspectives, effectively simulating a comprehensive empirical
525   human preference model. Furthermore, LLM annotators with proven judgment capabilities provide
526   high-quality feedback, offering scalable, consistent, and efficient alternatives to human resources.

      6     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduced iREPO, a novel LLM alignment framework, to address the challenges of
531   traditional alignment methods such as instability in reinforcement learning approaches and overfitting
532   in preference optimization methods. By utilizing implicit reward pairwise difference model and
533   empirical preference data from self-generated responses labeled by humans or AI annotators, iREPO
534   iteratively refines LLM policies through a novel regression-based loss function. This innovative
535   approach is supported by theoretical guarantees that ensure optimal results under specific, albeit
536   unreal, assumptions and offers practical insights into reducing performance gaps in more typical
537   scenarios. Experimentally, we show that iREPO effectively implements self-alignment with Phi-2
538
      and Mistral-7B, delivering superior performance compared to traditional preference optimization
      baselines in assessments using the LLM Evaluation Harness and Multi-turn benchmarks.

      C.1    R ELATION BETWEEN Z ERMELO R ANKINGS AND B RADLEY-T ERRY M ODELS
832   Zermelo Rankings are based on the concept of comparing pairs of items to determine a rank-
833   ing (Zermelo, 1929), which is particularly suitable for scenarios involving subjective evaluations
834   or competitions, such as ranking outputs from a large language model. In this context, Zermelo
835   Rankings use the Bradley-Terry model, which is a probabilistic model used to estimate the relative
836   strength (wi ) of each item. For two items i and j, the probability that item i is preferred over item j
837   is given by:
                                                               wi
838                                           P(i ≻ j) =                                                   (27)
839                                                         wi + wj
840   This model is powerful because it translates qualitative pairwise preferences into a quantitative
841   measure of strength or skill for each item. The strengths are iteratively updated to find the maximum
842   likelihood estimates that best explain the observed data. By utilizing pairwise comparison data, Zer-
843   melo Rankings provide an efficient mechanism to rank items even when the number of comparisons
844
      is sparse or inconsistent, which often occurs with language model evaluations.
845

849   times item i beats item j, the update rule for each strength is:

855   of item i beating item j across multiple pairwise comparisons. The log-likelihood function that the
856   algorithm aims to maximize is:

      C.3   ACCELERATED Z ERMELO ’ S A LGORITHM
865
866   To address the inefficiencies of the traditional approach, Newman (2023) proposed an enhanced
867   iterative algorithm. This new method modifies the update formula to better exploit the pairwise
868   comparison data and thereby accelerate convergence. The enhanced update rule is given by:
869                                            Pd
870                                        ′       j=1 hij wj (wi + wj )
                                         wi = Pd                                                 (30)
871                                                 j=1 hji (wi + wj )
872
      The key difference here is the introduction of weighted terms that more directly adjust the strengths
873                                                                               Pd w π
      based on the relative competitive outcomes between items. The numerator j=1 πiij+πjj takes into
874
875
      account both the number of wins and the relative strength of item j compared to i, while the
      denominator adjusts for losses in a similar manner.
876
877   From a mathematical perspective, this update method provides a more dynamic weighting of the
878   strength parameters, leading to several important improvements:
879
880
       • Faster Convergence: By directly incorporating the current strengths (wi and wj ) into the weight
         adjustment, the algorithm accelerates the convergence process. This ensures that items with
881
         higher empirical strength are updated more aggressively, allowing the estimates to reach a stable
882
         state much faster than the traditional Zermelo method.
883
884
       • Reduction in Iterations: Empirical studies have shown that the enhanced algorithm can be over a
         hundred times faster in some scenarios (Newman, 2023). The reduced number of iterations is
885
         particularly beneficial for ranking LLM responses, making computational efficiency crucial.
886
887   The enhanced iterative formula preserves the concavity of the likelihood function, which guarantees
888   convergence to the global maximum, provided the network of comparisons is strongly connected (i.e.,
889   there exists a path through the network that connects every item). To prove convergence, consider
890   the asynchronous version of the update, where a single wi is updated at each step, while others
891   remain fixed. The log-likelihood is strictly increasing with each update unless a fixed point is reached,
892   thereby ensuring convergence. See Newman (2023, Section 3) for more details.

      D.1   DATASETS
926
927   UltraFeedback-binarized (Tunstall et al., 2023b): is a pre-processed version of the UltraFeedback
928   dataset, which is a large-scale, fine-grained, diverse preference dataset, used for developing and
929   refining LLMs models focused on preference-based learning. This dataset contains approximately
930   64k prompts derived from a diverse array of sources, including UltraChat, ShareGPT, Evol-Instruct,
931   TruthfulQA, FalseQA, and FLAN. Each record includes a pair of model-generated responses: one
932   "chosen" and one "rejected," accompanied by their respective scores. These responses are selected
933   based on preference scoring that assesses criteria like relevance, accuracy, and utility, with the
934
      "chosen" response typically having the highest overall score to reflect superior quality or better
      alignment with human judgment, while the "rejected" response illustrates less preferred options.
935
936   For the initial training of our algorithm, referred to as iREPO-0, we utilize the responses from this
937   dataset. However, instead of employing a deterministic binary (win-lose) relationship as seen in DPO,
938   IPO or KTO, we compute the logit of empirical human preferences using the scores of the chosen and
939   rejected responses. These logits are then integrated into our loss function to ensure a more nuanced
940
      model training process and to facilitate fair comparisons with baseline models.
941   In the next training iterations (iREPO-1 and iREPO-2), we randomly pick 15k prompts and 20k
942   prompts for Mistral-7B and Phi-2, respectively. Then, we use our aligned model to generate repsonse
943   pairs and employ humans or AI annotators to give feedback.

1115   D.6    P OTENTIAL L IMITATIONS
1116
1117   While our proposed framework, iREPO, demonstrates significant improvements in LLM alignment,
1118   it is not without limitations. One of the key constraints arises from its reliance on iterative refinements
1119   using feedback from either human or AI annotators. This introduces several potential issues:
1120
1121          • Annotation Consistency: The consistency of annotations, especially when using AI an-
1122            notators, can vary. Differences in how AI systems interpret and annotate data can lead to
1123
                inconsistencies in training data, which may affect the reliability of the aligned model.
1124          • Data Dependency and Variability: The effectiveness of iREPO hinges on the quality and
1125            diversity of the data generated by annotators. If the dataset lacks variability or is biased,
1126            the alignment process may not adequately capture the full spectrum of human preferences,
1127            potentially leading to skewed or biased model outputs.
1128
1129   However, these limitations are being actively mitigated by advances in AI annotator technology and
1130   data management practices. Most AI annotators used in iREPO are subject to rigorous evaluations
1131   that ensure a high level of consistency in their annotations (Jiang et al., 2023). The prompts used for
1132   generating annotator responses are sourced from large and diverse datasets, ensuring the data feeding
1133   into iREPO captures a broad spectrum of human preferences, mitigating the risks associated with
       data dependency and variability.
