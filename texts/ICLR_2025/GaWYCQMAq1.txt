009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) are being employed as mainstream mod-
012            els for Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI), the study of their memorization
013            of training data has attracted growing attention. Existing works in this direction
014            aim to establish an understanding of whether or to what extent DPMs learn via
015            memorization. Such an understanding is crucial for identifying potential risks of
016
               data leakage and copyright infringement in diffusion models and, more importantly,
               for trustworthy application of GenAI. Existing works revealed that conditional
017
               DPMs are more prone to training data memorization than unconditional DPMs, and
018
               the motivated data extraction methods are mostly for conditional DPMs. However,
019            these understandings are primarily empirical, and extracting training data from
020            unconditional models has been found to be extremely challenging. In this work,
021            we provide a theoretical understanding of memorization in both conditional and
022            unconditional DPMs under the assumption of model convergence. Our theoretical
023            analysis indicates that extracting data from unconditional models can also be effec-
024            tive by constructing a proper surrogate condition. Based on this result, we propose
025            a novel data extraction method named Surrogate condItional Data Extraction
026            (SIDE) that leverages a time-dependent classifier trained on the generated data as a
027
               surrogate condition to extract training data from unconditional DPMs. Empirical
               results demonstrate that our SIDE can extract training data in challenging scenarios
028
               where previous methods fail, and it is, on average, over 50% more effective across
029
               different scales of the CelebA dataset.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song &
035   Ermon, 2019) is a family of powerful generative models that learn the distribution of a dataset by
036   first gradually destroying the structure of the data through an iterative forward diffusion process and
037   then restoring the data structure via a reverse diffusion process. Due to their outstanding capability
038   in capturing data distribution, DPMs have become the foundation models for many pioneering
039
      Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) products such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022),
      DALL-E 3 (Betker et al.), and Sora (Brooks et al., 2024). Despite the widespread adoption of DPMs,
040
      a potential risk they face is data memorization, i.e., the risk of memorizing a certain proportion of the
041
      raw training samples. This could result in the generation of memorized (rather than new) samples via
042   direct copying, causing data leakage, privacy breaches, or copyright infringement, as highlighted in
043   the literature (Somepalli et al., 2022; 2023; Asay, 2020; Cooper & Grimmelmann, 2024). A current
044   case argues that Stable Diffusion is a 21st-century collage tool, remixing the copyrighted creations
045   of countless artists whose works were included in the training data(Butterick, 2023). Furthermore,
046   data memorization also gives rise to data extraction attacks, which is one type of privacy attacks
047   that attempt to extract the training data from a well-trained model. Notably, recent work by Carlini
048   et al. (2023) demonstrated the feasibility of extracting training data samples from DPMs like Stable
049   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), revealing the potential dangers associated with these models.
050   Several works have investigated the data memorization phenomenon in diffusion models. For example,
051   it has been observed that there exists a strong correlation between training data memorization and
052   conditional DPMs, i.e., being conditional is more prone to memorization (Somepalli et al., 2023).
053   Gu et al. (2023) investigates the influential factors on memorization behaviors via a comprehensive
      set of experiments. They found that conditioning on random-labeled data can significantly trigger

      Diffusion Probabilistic Models DPMs (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) such as Stable Diffusion (Rom-
095
      bach et al., 2022), DALL-E 3 (Betker et al.), Sora (Brooks et al., 2024), Runway (Rombach et al.,
096   2022), and Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022) have achieved state-of-the-art performance in image/video
097   generation across a wide range of benchmarks (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). These models can be
098   viewed from two perspectives. The first is score matching (Song & Ermon, 2019), where diffusion
099   models learn the gradient of the image distribution (Song et al., 2020). The second perspective
100   involves denoising DPMs (Ho et al., 2020), which add Gaussian noise at various time steps to clean
101   images and train models to denoise them. To conditionally sample from diffusion models, (Dhariwal
102   & Nichol, 2021) utilizes a classifier to guide the denoising process at each sampling step. Additionally,
103   (Ho & Salimans, 2022) introduces classifier-free guidance for conditional data sampling using DPMs.
104   Memorization in Diffusion Models Early exploration of memorization in large models was focused
105   on language models (Carlini et al., 2022; Jagielski et al., 2022), which has motivated more in-
106   depth research on image-generation DPMs (Somepalli et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023). Notably, a
107   recent research on image-generation DPMs. Somepalli et al. (2022) found that 0.5-2% of generated
      images duplicate training samples, a result concurrently reported by Carlini et al. (2023) in broader

      experiments for both conditional and unconditional diffusion models. Further studies Somepalli et al.
109   (2023) and Gu et al. (2023) linked memorization to model conditioning, showing that conditional
110   models are more prone to memorization. To address this issue, anti-memorization guidance has been
111   proposed to mitigate memorization during the sampling process Chen et al. (2024a). So far, the
112   motivated data extraction attack or defend methods from these empirical understandings are mostly

118
      1-dimensional dataset and only a few generated images, and it fails to address the data extraction
119
      challenge on unconditional DPMs. This challenge is exemplified by the brute-force methods currently
120   employed, as discussed in (Somepalli et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2023), highlighting the inadequacies
121   of existing approaches for extracting data from unconditional diffusion models. In response to these
122   challenges, we introduce a theoretical framework to characterize memorization in DPMs, which
123   further motivates a novel data extraction method for unconditional DPMs.

      3.2   T HEORETICAL F RAMEWORK
187
188   Based on pointwise memorization, here we present a theoretically framework that explains why
189   conditional DPMs memorize more data. Our theoretical framework is based on the concept of
190   informative labels, which refers to information that can differentiate subsets of data samples. We
191   first give a formal definition of informative labels and then prove their two key properties: 1) they
192   facilitate tighter clustering of samples around their respective means, and 2) they reduce variance in
193   the latent representations. Building upon the two properties, we theoretically show that conditional
194
      DPMs memorize more data.
195
      Informative Labels The concept of informative labels has been previously discussed as class labels
196
      (Gu et al., 2023). In this work, we introduce a more general definition that encompasses both class
197
      labels and random labels as special cases. We define an informative label as follows.
198
199   Definition 3 (Informative Label) Let Y = {yi , y2 , · · · , yC } be the label set for training dataset D
200   with C unique labels. yi is the associated label with xi , and Dy=c = {xi : xi ∈ D, yi = c} is the
201   subset of training samples shared the same label y = c. A label y = c is an informative label if
202
203
                                                    |Dy=c | < |D|.                                            (5)
204
205
      Here, the labels are not limited to the conventional class labels; they can also be text captions, features,
      or cluster information that can be used to group the training samples into subsets. The above definition
206
      states that an information label should be able to differentiate a subset of samples from others. An
207
      extreme case is that all samples have the same label; in this case, the label is not informative.
208   According to our definition, class-wise and random labels are special cases of informative labels.
209   Informative labels can be either explicit like class/random labels and text captions, or implicit like
210   silent features or clusters. Next, we will explore the correlation between informative labels and the
211   clustering effect in the latent space of a generative model with an encoder and decoder. In diffusion
212   models, the encoder represents the forward diffusion process, while the decoder represents the reverse
213   process. Notably, the latent space can be defined at earlier timesteps according to (Ho et al., 2020).
214

      Memorization in Unconditional DPMs According to Theorem 2, one could leverage informative
271   labels to extract training data from conditional DPMs. Intuitively, the Text captions and class labels
272   commonly used to train conditional DPMs are valid informative labels, which we call explicit
273   informative labels. However, unconditional DPMs do not have explicit informative labels and thus
274   are more difficult to extract training data from. Nevertheless, our theory indicates that the learned
275   representation clusters by an unconditional DPM can also serve as a type of informative labels, which
276   we call implict information labels. It means that if we can formulate the clustering information
277   in the training data, we could construct implicit informative labels to help extract training data
278   from unconditional DPMs. This motivates us to propose a new data extraction method SIDE for

      6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduced a pointwise memorization metric to quantify memorization effects
533
      in DPMs. We provided a theoretical analysis of conditional memorization, offering a generalized
534   definition of informative labels and clarifying that random labels can also be informative. We
535   distinguish between explicit labels and implicit labels and propose a novel method, SIDE, to extract
536   training data from unconditional diffusion models by constructing a surrogate condition. The key
537   to this approach is training a time-dependent classifier using our TDKD technique. We empirically
538   validate SIDE on subsets of the CelebA and CIFAR-10 datasets with two new memorization scores:
539   AMS and UMS. We aim for our work to enhance the understanding of memorization mechanisms in
      diffusion models and inspire further methods to mitigate memorization.

1337   C    R EFINEMENT R ES N ET BLOCK
1338
1339   The integration of the time module directly after batch normalization within the network architecture
1340   is a reasonable design choice rooted in the functionality of batch normalization itself. Batch nor-
1341   malization standardizes the inputs to the network layer, stabilizing the learning process by reducing
1342   internal covariate shifts. By positioning the time module immediately after this normalization process,
1343   the model can introduce time-dependent adaptations to the already stabilized features. This placement
1344   ensures that the temporal adjustments are applied to a normalized feature space, thereby enhancing
1345   the model’s ability to learn temporal dynamics effectively.
1346   Moreover, the inclusion of the time module at a singular point within the network strikes a balance
1347   between model complexity and temporal adaptability. This singular addition avoids the potential
1348   redundancy and computational overhead that might arise from multiple time modules. It allows the
1349   network to maintain a streamlined architecture while still gaining the necessary capacity to handle
       time-varying inputs.
