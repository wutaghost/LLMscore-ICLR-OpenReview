009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            3D Human motion generation, especially textual conditioning motion generation,
012            is a vital part of computer animation. However, during training, multiple ac-
013            tions are often coupled within a single textual description, which complicates the
014            model’s learning of individual actions. Additionally, the motion corresponding to
015            a given text can be diverse, which makes it difficult for the model learning and for
016
               the user to control the generation of motions that contain a specific pose. Finally,
               motions with the same semantics can have various ways of expression in the forms
017
               of texts, which further increases the difficulty of the model’s learning process. To
018
               solve the above challenges, we propose the Pose-Guided Text to Motion (PG-
019            T2M) with the following designs. Firstly, we propose to divide the sentences into
020            sub-sentences containing one single verb and make the model learn the specific
021            mapping from one single action description to its motion. Secondly, we propose
022            using pose priors from static 2D natural images for each sub-sentence as control
023            signals, allowing the model to generate more accurate and controllable 3D pose
024            sequences that align with the sub-action descriptions.Finally, to enable the model
025            to distinguish which sub-sentences describe similar semantics, we construct a pose
026            memory storing semantic-similar sub-sentences and the corresponding pose rep-
027
               resentations in groups. These designs together enable our model to retrieve the
               pose information for every single action described in the text and use them to
028
               guide motion generation. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on
029
               the HumanML3D and KIT datasets.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Text-to-motion generation aims to generate human motion sequences given a textual description,
035   which has a wide range of applications in game design, animation, robotics, and other fields. Re-
036   cently, there has been rapid development in text-to-motion generation, where the recent methods
037   typically use text directly as a condition, employing diffusion models to control motion generation.
038   However, these methods overlook the following three problems.
039   Firstly, as shown in Figure 1a, the training data usually contains complicated texts including multi-
040   ple actions, wherein these coupled actions make it challenging for the model to learn the mapping
041   between individual actions and their corresponding textual segments. For example, multi-action
042   data accounts for more than 60% in the HumanML3D (Guo et al., 2022) dataset, empirically we ob-
043   serve that the existing models like MDM (Tevet et al., 2023) and MotionDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2022)
044   trained with these data usually coupled with multiple actions has a significant performance drop on
045
      a single action in Figure 1b. Secondly, as shown in Figure 1c, motions described by the same text
      (such as ’kicks’) can be highly diverse. This increases the uncertainty of motion generation, making
046
      it difficult for the user to control the generation of motions that contain a specific pose. Motion is
047
      a sequence of poses and pose information plays a vital role in motion generation. Therefore, we
048   believe that introducing prior knowledge of the diverse poses corresponding to a single action de-
049   scription can provide more prior information related to the textual description, making the results
050   better aligned with the condition. Since the pose-prior is easier to obtain through off-the-shelf text-
051   to-image and image-to-pose models, we choose to use several static poses rather than a continuous
052   motion sequence as the guide. Finally, as illustrated in Figure 1d, due to the diversity in natural lan-
053   guage, motions with the same semantics can have various ways of expression in the forms of texts.
      This diversity increases the difficulty of the model’s learning process. If we can assist the model in

115   2     R ELATED W ORK
116
117   Text to Motion Generation. Recently, widespread attention has been paid to 3D human motion
118
      generation. Some works (Lee et al., 2023; Athanasiou et al., 2022) have attempted to control the
      generation of human motions using atomic action labels as conditions, but these methods struggle to
119
      satisfy the need for generating diverse and complex motion sequences. Thus, an increasing number
120
      of researchers are exploring the use of free-form text as a condition to control motion generation.
121   For example, TEMOS (Petrovich et al., 2022), T2M-GPT (Zhang et al., 2023b) and MoMask (Guo
122   et al., 2023) propose encoder-decoder or VAE-based pipelines to generate motions. Recently, dif-
123   fusion model (Ho et al., 2020) have been introduced to text-to-motion generation by MDM (Tevet
124   et al., 2023), MotionDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2022), MLD (Chen et al., 2023), GraphMotion (Jin et al.,
125   2023), etc., due to its outstanding ability in many generative tasks. These methods often directly
126   encode the features of complex text prompts to control the generation of actions, resulting in the
127   model’s difficulty in learning the correspondence between individual action descriptions and mo-
128   tion from complex text prompts. They also overlook the motions described by the same text can
129
      be highly diverse, which increases the uncertainty of motion generation, making it difficult for the
      user to control the generation of motions that contain a specific pose. Thus, we propose to divide
130
      the sentence into sub-sentences containing only one single verb and introduce the pose information
131
      of each verb as the additional control signal by constructing a pose memory that stores mappings
132   between text clusters of semantically similar single action and their pose information. This enables
133   the model to acquire prior information related to the pose of all sub-actions within the text for gen-
134   eration. One related work to ours is MAA (Azadi et al., 2023), which introduces pose information
135   by simply pre-training the model on text-to-pose datasets and fine-tuning it on text-to-motion data.
136   However, it failed to establish a connection between the pose caption and the motion description,
137   resulting in the model still struggling to understand the correspondence between each sub-action
138   in the motion description and the pose. In our work, we directly utilize the descriptions of each
139   sub-action to retrieve the most relevant poses from the pose memory, explicitly using these poses as
140
      control signals, allowing the model to generate more precise and controllable action sequences.
141   Codebook and Memory Bank. Codebook, memory bank, or other memory-based approaches
142   have been widely used in image classification (He et al., 2020), multimodal alignment (Duan et al.,
143   2022), and generative models (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). For example, (Cao et al., 2017) used
144   codebook to speed up image retrieval. However, such a mechanism is yet to be fully explored
145   in the field of motion generation. We are the first to apply the concept of a memory bank to the
146
      text-to-motion generation, aiming to establish a mapping from text descriptions to corresponding
      pose information and enable the model to retrieve pose information to control the generation of
147
      motion. One related work to ours is RemoDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2023c), which retrieves real motion
148
      using text from the entire training set to control the motion generation. However, their retrieval
149   source is limited to the training set and requires the same distribution between training and test
150   data. Moreover, retrieving the whole motion sequence also neglects fine-grained sub-actions in
151   motion descriptions. In contrast, our pose information in the memory comes from the open-world
152   image generation model, which allows the pose memory to obtain knowledge beyond the training
153   set. In addition, our memory stores the correspondence between the poses of sub-actions and their
154   descriptions, rather than the complete motion sequences, allowing the model to learn more fine-
155   grained pose priors for each sub-actions.

159   3.1   OVERVIEW
160
161   Given a text input, our goal is to generate a motion sequence x1:N that corresponds to the text input,
      where N represents the length of the motion.

      The overview of our method is presented in Figure 2. Our pipeline contains three modules: the Pose
163   Memory Construction module to construct a pose memory storing the pose features of semantic-
164   similar single actions, the Pose Guided Conditioning to retrieve the pose features from the pose
165   memory and encode the temporal relations between them as the control signal, and the Motion
166   Diffusion module to generate the motions based on the conditions using the diffusion model.
167
      Specifically, we adopt Motion Diffusion model MDM (Tevet et al., 2023) as our baseline, which
168
      encodes the text prompt as a condition and uses the diffusion model to generate motions based
169   on the conditions. More details of the baseline are described in Section 3.2. The Pose Memory
170   Construction described in Section 3.3 constructs a pose memory that stores mappings between text
171   clusters of semantic similar single actions and their pose information. Firstly, to enable the model
172   to learn the relationship between a single action description and its motion from complex texts, we
173   propose to split the texts into sub-sentences that only contain one action. Then, to acquire the pose
174   information of a single action for a more controllable motion generation, we propose a pipeline
175   to generate pose representations from sub-sentences using off-the-shelf text-to-image generative
176   models and pose detectors. Since different sub-sentences may describe similar semantics, we cluster
177
      the sub-sentences in the training set to aid the model in learning the semantic-similar sub-sentences.
      We then construct a pose memory that stores the mapping from the clusters to the pose features.
178
      The Pose Guided Conditioning described in Section 3.4 uses the pose information to help the
179
      model with motion generation. Firstly, we retrieve the pose features corresponding to the sub-
180   sentences from the pose memory. Since there exists a sequential relationship between pose features
181   corresponding to different sub-sequences, we use a temporal encoder to further encode these pose
182   features. The encoded features are then utilized alongside text features to control the generation.

      3.3   P OSE M EMORY C ONSTRUCTION
235
236   To provide the model with pose priors for each sub-action in the text, enabling more precise and
237   controllable action generation, we propose to split texts into sub-sentences only containing a single
238   verb and obtain pose-related priors for each sub-sentence. Due to various descriptions for the same
239   semantic meaning using natural language, to enhance the model’s learning of semantically similar
240   sub-sentences, we propose to employ a pose memory to store the mapping from semantic-similar
241   sub-sentences to their pose features.
242   Text Splitting. As previous models do not pay enough attention to the inner structure of the input
243   texts, especially they may include multiple actions that make text-conditional motion generation
244   more difficult, our proposed text splitting tries to tackle the problem. Specifically, we split the
245   sentence into shorter texts with only one action to disentangle the multiple verbs in the texts. We
246   consider the predicate-argument structure of the input sentence to split it into sub-actions. We first
247   use off-the-shelf SRLBert (Shi & Lin, 2019) to obtain the PropBank-style (Palmer et al., 2005)
248
      semantic role label of the sentences. We split the sentence into sub-actions based on the verb tag
      [V]. Each verb has some attached tags, for example, [ARG0] represents Proto-Agent of the verb
249
      and [ARG1] stands for Proto-Patient of the verb. We keep each verb and the attached tags as the
250
      split sub-actions. After splitting, we use a text encoder T(·) to compute the text features of the
251   sentence and sub-actions.
252
253   Pose Feature Generation. As it is hard to obtain a massive text-pose paired dataset from scratch,
254
      we choose to probe knowledge from trained generative models. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
      2021), which has proved its ability to generate diverse images, is leveraged by us to generate pose-
255
      related images from our text-only data. Specifically, after obtaining the split sub-sentences in the
256
      training set, we use the Stable Diffusion to generate mp images from each sub-sentence. The images
257   generated are then fed into PyMAF-X (Zhang et al., 2023a), a pre-trained pose extractor, to extract
258   the SMPL (Loper et al., 2023) pose annotation from these images. This allows us to obtain mp pose
259   annotations for each sub-action. The reason for generating multiple poses is that the same sub-action
260   can encompass various postures, and we aim to retain this diversity. Note that we use the static pose
261   feature from synthesized images instead of the dynamic motion feature from synthesized videos
262   because the text-to-image model typically exhibits higher generalization due to massive training
263   data and is more efficient than the text-to-video model. To consider the temporal relationship of the
264   static pose features, we also design a temporal encoder which will be discussed later.
265   To speed up the training and inferring process, the pose features from texts are generated in an offline
266   way instead of along with the model, and stored for later use.
267
      Pose Memory Construction.
268
269   To obtain the semantic-similar sub-sentences, we use k-means to cluster the split sub-sentences from
      the training set into k clusters by their text features. Then, to maintain variety within the text of each

      cluster, mt texts are randomly selected and fed into the text-image-pose pipeline for each cluster.
271   Thus, the total number of texts is Nt = mt × k. To enhance variety, mp images will be generated
272   from each selected text and the number of poses is Np = mp × Nt . The text features in each cluster
273   along with their cluster center serve as the key, and the corresponding poses are the values. So our
274   key in the pose memory is
275                                       Key = T(s1 , s2 , ..., sNt ) ∈ RNt ×dt ,                           (4)
276   where s1 , s2 , ..., sNt are the sub-sentences in the pose memory, T(·) is the text encoder, and dt is the

282   3.4   P OSE G UIDED C ONDITIONING .
283
284
      After obtaining the pose memory, we can retrieve the pose feature of the split sub-sentences during
      training and inference to guide the motion generation. Since there exists a temporal relationship
285
      between poses (such as the temporal order in which each pose appears), we propose a temporal
286
      encoder to further encode these pose features.
287
288   Pose Memory Retrieval. Given a sub-sentence, the text feature of it fquery ∈ Rdt is used to
289   compute the distance between itself and text features fkey ∈ Rdt stored in our pose memory. Then,
290   the fed-in sub-sentence is classified in one certain cluster. Later, we randomly choose a pose in this
291   cluster as the corresponding pose representation. Noting that here we select the pose value from the
292
      whole cluster (mt × mp poses) for variety, instead of selecting from only those corresponding with
      that certain key (mp poses). This design helps enhance the variety of the motion generated.
293
294   Temporal Encoder. The pose retrieved from the pose memory is extracted from static images
295   and there exists a temporal relationship such as sequential relationship between pose features cor-
296   responding to different sub-sequences. Therefore, we use a transformer encoder as the temporal
297   encoder to further encode these pose features. We set the max number of sub-sentences to Ns . Sup-
298
      pose that the original sentence is split into ns sub-actions, if ns < Ns , we will repeat the fetched
      poses in an interleaving way. For instance, if ns = 2, Ns = 4, we repeat action AB into AABB.
299
      Otherwise, we simply truncate it. These Ns poses are fed into the temporal encoder. Suppose the
300
      pose representations are P ∈ RNs ×dp , we process it as:
301
302                                            fp = E(P + PE(P )),                                          (5)
303   where E(·) is the transformer encoder and PE(·) is the positional embedding.
304
      To control the generation of motions with both text and pose features, we add fp to the original text
305
      feature ft of the sentence to get the final hybrid feature:
306
307                                             fhybrid = fp ⊕ ft ,                                         (6)
308   where ⊕ stands for vector addition. The hybrid feature fhybrid is used to replace the text feature c
309   in the diffusion part described in Section 3.2 as the control signal.
310
      Our training and inferring processes are similar to what is described in Section 3.2, and our objective
311   function is the same as Equation (3). During inferring, the diffusion process will be iterated for T
312   times. The randomness in pose fetching is kept, but the pose fetched in the first iterations will not
313   be changed between iterations and we do not repeat the retrieval in the later iterations.

318
319   Datasets. In this study, we employ the HumanML3D dataset (Guo et al., 2022) and the KIT
320   dataset (Plappert et al., 2016) to assess the effectiveness of the proposed approaches in the task
321   of text-to-motion generation, following (Guo et al., 2022; Tevet et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c).
322   HumanML3D (Guo et al., 2022) is a widely-used dataset in the text-to-motion domain recently,
323   which provides 14616 motions with 44970 text annotations. KIT (Plappert et al., 2016) is another
      widely used dataset including 3911 motions annotated by 6353 textual descriptions.

      Evaluation Metrics. We employ the following four metrics adopted by (Guo et al., 2022; Zhang
325   et al., 2022; Tevet et al., 2023). 1) R-precision (R). For each textual prompt and its corresponding
326   generated motion, 31 other pairs are randomly selected. The matching of them is computed and the
327   top-k accuracy is obtained. 2) Multi-Modal Distance (MM Dist). Using the pre-trained contrastive
328   model, we compute the distance between the input text and the generated motion. 3) FID. We use
329   FID on the features extracted from ground truth and generated motions to measure the distribution
330   distance. 4) Diversity. We randomly separate the motions generated into pairs and compute the joint
331   differences of each pair to show the variety of generated motions.

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
379
380   For the pose feature generation, we use the Stable Diffusion 2.1 (Rombach et al., 2021) to generate
381   images from text and use PyMAF-X (Zhang et al., 2023a) to extract pose from images. For the
382   motion diffusion model, we use CLIP-ViT-B/32 (Radford et al., 2021) as the text encoder. We train
383   the model for both HumanML3D and KIT with an 8-layer transformer in motion encoder, a 2-
384   layer transformer in pose temporal encoder, and a batch size of 64. For the pose memory, we set
385   k = 2048, mt = 16, mp = 12 for HumanML3D and k = 512, mt = 8, mp = 6 for KIT. For our
386   MotionDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2022)-based implementation, we set k = 1024 for HumanML3D and
387
      k = 256 for KIT. The latent dimensions of the hybrid feature and motion encoder are 256 and 512.
      For the noising process, we set T = 1000 with a cosine noise schedule in the training stage. We
388
      train three models based on MDM (Tevet et al., 2023), MotionDiffusion (Zhang et al., 2022) and
389
      MoMask (Guo et al., 2023), respectively. More details are provided in Appendix A.3.

396   (1) We can see that our model achieves state-of-the-art results in the two datasets on R-precisions,

      (3) The FID suffers from a slight drop on HumanML3D with some baselines. As we employ a pose

      distribution of our generated samples slightly differs from the distribution of the dataset.
404
405   (4) The diversity of our method is slightly lower than the baseline, primarily because we introduced

      the quality and controllability of the motion generation, as reflected in the improvement of the R-
      precision. Furthermore, Wang et al. (2023) has also pointed out that higher diversity is not always

      better, as random motions have greater diversity but very low quality. We also find that the diversity

      model, does not rely on the distribution of training data and exhibits better generalization.

      Size of the pose memory. In our pose memory, we cluster the texts into k clusters, keep mt texts for
      each cluster center and generate mp pose features for each text. We change k, mt , or mp to discuss
460
      how the scale of the pose memory influences the performance. In detail, the number of clusters may
461
      influence the granularity of our pose memory, and the number of texts selected or poses for each
462   text may influence the diversity of the texts in a cluster or the poses for a certain text. For the cluster
463   center k, a larger k results in better performance. We suppose the reason is that a smaller k results
464   in clusters containing more text that may not be semantically close enough, so the model is more
465   likely to retrieve mismatched poses. However, from k = 1024 to k = 2048, the improvement is
466   rather limited. We finally choose k = 2048, to balance the performance and the scale of the pose
467   memory. The decrease of both mt and mp will degrade R-precision and enlarge Multimodal dist.
468   This proves that the scale of the pose memory matters. The larger the pose memory, the higher
469   the diversity of stored text and poses. During training, the model can also learn more pose priors,
470
      thereby improving performance. However, continuing to increase mt , mp significantly increases the
      storage and retrieval costs of the pose memory. For efficiency, we ultimately set mt = 16, mp = 12.
471
472   Clustering the entries by text or pose. As shown in the last 2 rows of Table 4, we re-cluster the
473   pose memory by the pose representation instead of by the text features. The comparison shows that
474   our original direct way is better for conditional generation. Re-clustering by pose helps the model
475   generate motions more based on the motion itself rather than text conditioning, resulting in generally
476
      more realistic motions that enhance FID but are worse for conditional generation. We suppose that
      though a pose memory re-clustered by pose truly contains more similar poses in each cluster, it
477
      increases the difficulty of text retrieval due to the variety of keys in a cluster. Thus, we cluster the
478
      pose memory by texts throughout the experiments unless otherwise specified.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We introduce Pose-Guided Text to Motion (PG-T2M), which constructs a pose memory that stores
535   mappings between text clusters of the semantically similar single actions and their pose informa-
536   tion. We split the text into sub-sentences to handle the entangled verbs in complicated sentences
537   and help build a concrete mapping between single action descriptions and the motions. We propose
538   to automatically generate pose features for each sub-sentence to guide the generation of motions.
539   Quantitative experiments affirm the superior performance of our method compared to existing tech-
      niques in text-driven motion generation.

       A.3.1    A RCHITECTURE D ETAILS
973
974    In the main text, we outlined the implementation details of applying our design principles to the
975    baseline MDM (Tevet et al., 2023). In this section, we elaborate on the application of our pipeline to
976    MotionDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2022). Unlike MDM (Tevet et al., 2023), which utilizes only sentence-
977    level text features supplemented by timestep embeddings as singular guidance, MotionDiffuse in-
978
       corporates token-level text features for cross attention with motion frame representations. To seam-
       lessly incorporate our pose memory design into MotionDiffuse with minimal alterations, we begin
979
       by constructing the pose memory (involving the splitting of raw texts and encoding of pose features)
980
       as proposed in the main text. Subsequently, for the encoded pose feature, we incorporate it into each
981    token-level text feature of MotionDiffuse as conditions to guide the motion generation. Notably, the
982    diffusion process of MotionDiffuse remains unaltered throughout this integration process.
