009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Communication is a prominent bottleneck in federated learning (FL). State-of-the-
012            art accuracy performance under limited uplink communications from the clients to
013            the federator is achieved by stochastic FL approaches. It has been recently shown
014            that leveraging side information in the form of a prior distribution at the federator
015            can drastically reduce the uplink communication cost in stochastic FL. Here, the
016
               latest global model distribution serves as a natural prior since it can be shared with
               the clients under ideal downlink communication from the federator to the clients.
017
               Nevertheless, downlink communication is often limited in practical settings, and
018
               bi-directional compression must be considered to reduce the overall communi-
019            cation cost. The extension of existing stochastic FL solutions to bi-directional
020            compression is non-trivial due to the lack of a globally shared common prior dis-
021            tribution at each iteration. In this paper, we propose BICompFL, which employs
022            importance sampling to send samples from the updated local models in the uplink,
023            and the aggregated global model in the downlink by carefully choosing common
024            prior distributions as side-information. We theoretically study the communication
025            cost by a new analysis of importance sampling that refines known results, and ex-
026            poses the interplay between uplink and downlink communication costs. We also
027
               show through numerical experiments that BICompFL enables multi-fold savings
               in communication cost compared to the state-of-the-art.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Federated learning (FL) is a widely used and well-studied machine learning (ML) framework, where
033   multiple clients orchestrated by a federator collaborate to train an ML model (McMahan et al., 2017).
034   Communication efficiency, privacy, security, and data heterogeneity are critical challenges in FL that
035   have been extensively studied (Zhang et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2023). FL is a bi-directional process,
036   and with the increasing size of ML models, massive amounts of data are communicated between the
037   federator and the clients. Reducing uplink communication from clients to the federator has been the
038   focus of many studies, mainly within the framework of lossy gradient compression, e.g., (Seide et al.,
039
      2014; Alistarh et al., 2017; Isik et al., 2024). However, reducing the cost of downlink transmission
      to communicate the model updates from the federator to the clients has received relatively less
040
      attention, even though it is costly and can be a major bottleneck when training over a wireless
041
      network. An ongoing body of research aims to study the communication bottleneck in downlink
042   transmission, by combining tools from gradient compression, momentum, and error-feedback, cf.,
043   (Stich et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020; Amiri et al., 2020; Philippenko & Dieuleveut,
044   2020; Gruntkowska et al., 2023; Tyurin & Richtárik, 2023; Dorfman et al., 2023; Gruntkowska et al.,
045   2024). However, these works are focused on non-stochastic (or non-Bayesian) settings, whereas the
046   state-of-the-art performance in limited uplink communication scenarios is achieved by stochastic
047   compression methods, such as QSGD Alistarh et al. (2017), QLSD Vono et al. (2022), dithered
048   quantization Abdi & Fekri (2019) or FedPM Isik et al. (2023), in which the clients send samples
049   from a local distribution, and the federator estimates the mean of clients’ distributions by averaging
050
      these samples. In this work, we study the performance of these stochastic FL frameworks with
      limited communication in both directions, and obtain state-of-the-art results.
051
052   Standard analysis of such uni- or bidirectional compression schemes involves studying the conver-
053   gence rates of the respective optimization task for a given communication budget. In general, the
      trade-off between communication cost (or compression cost) and distortion has been extensively


                                                         1

054
      studied in information theory under the framework of rate-distortion theory (Cover & Thomas,
055   2006). However, in stochastic gradient-based optimization, it is difficult to analyze the effect of
056   finite resolution on the convergence speed. Classical rate-distortion results are not suitable for this
057   analysis, since they rely on the joint compression of many samples and additive distortion measures.
058   As a result, it is also difficult to study the fundamental trade-off between the communication load
059   and the convergence speed.
060
      An alternative stochastic FL approach was proposed in Isik et al. (2024), which applies to a vari-
061   ety of stochastic and Bayesian FL solutions, but also covers the classical stochastic compression
062   mechanisms for standard gradient-based methods. Communication reduction in Isik et al. (2024) is
063   achieved by importance sampling, which rather than sampling locally and using lossy compression
064   to send these samples, directly enables the federator to sample from the updated local distribution.
065   This enables a direct evaluation of the communication cost when a shared common prior distribu-
066   tion, called side information, and sufficient common randomness is available between the federator
067   and the clients. When the downlink communication is assumed unlimited, the global model distri-
068   bution at the federator can be shared with all the clients, and serve as a natural side information, i.e.,
069
      common prior. However, this is not possible under downlink communication constraints, leading to
      new algorithmic and analysis challenges, which we address here.
070
071   Specifically, in this work, we develop and analyze stochastic FL algorithms with bi-directional com-
072   pression with FedPM as a notable example. The idea in FedPM Isik et al. (2023) is to collaboratively
073   train a probabilistic mask that determines which weights to maintain from a randomly initialized
074   network. The motivation stems from the lottery-ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019), which
075
      claims that randomly initialized networks contain sub-networks capable of reaching accuracy com-
      parable to that of the full network. We utilize importance sampling with appropriate priors, and
076
      accurately characterize the uplink and downlink communication costs and the estimation error. We
077
      also explore various aspects that affect the performance, such as clients’ data heterogeneity, and
078   the existence of shared randomness among all clients. The basic question we address is: Can joint
079   uplink and downlink compression reduce communication bottlenecks in stochastic FL? We answer
080   this question in the affirmative, and concretely, our contributions are summarized as follows:
081
082          • We propose two algorithms for bi-directional stochastic FL, depending on the availability of
083            shared randomness. The first algorithm is for the case when globally shared randomness is
084            available, and the second is for the case when only private shared randomness between each
085
               client and the federator exists. Both algorithms leverage carefully chosen side information
               to transmit samples from the desired distribution through importance sampling.
086
087          • We experimentally validate our algorithms and show order-wise improvements in the com-
088            munication cost without degradation of the accuracy compared to many baselines. We
089            investigate the role of shared randomness and the choice of side information.
090          • We provide a novel dedicated theoretical analysis of importance sampling to quantify the
091            communication cost of stochastic FL with bi-directional compression. Our findings go
092            beyond the established analysis of Chatterjee & Diaconis (2018), with refinements for the
093            particular case of Bernoulli distributions (which can be of independent interest). Our proofs
094
               allow a practically relevant analysis and provide techniques useful for other distributions.

096   2    P RELIMINARIES : S TOCHASTIC FL WITH B I -D IRECTIONAL C OMPRESSION
097
098   Our proposed algorithm is a stochastic FL algorithm based on FedPM and importance sampling. In
099   what follows, we shortly review these concepts.
100
101   Stochastic Federated Learning A set of n clients collaboratively and iteratively train a model,
102   e.g., a neural network, under the orchestration of a federator. Each client i ∈ [n] possesses a dataset
103   Di , where we define [n] := {1, . . . , n} for an integer n . We differentiate between homogeneous
104   data, where Di is drawn independently from the same distribution for all clients (i.i.d.), and hetero-
105
      geneous data, where each Di may come from a different distribution (non i.i.d.). At each iteration t
      of the training process, the global model at the federator is described by a probability distribution θt
106
107
      with dimension d. After downlink transmission, each client i has an estimate θ̂i,t of θt , and locally
      optimizes θ̂i,t , e.g., according to a loss function F (θ̂i,t , Di ) by (stochastic) gradient descent, to ob-


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tain a local model qit , referred to as the posterior. Compressed versions of the clients’ posteriors are
109   transmitted back to the federator in the uplink, where the received posteriors are aggregated using
110   an aggregation rule R (·) to obtain a refined global probability distribution θt+1 = R {q̂it }i∈[n] . A
111   simple aggregation rule R (·) is the average over all clients’ models. This process is repeated until
112   a certain convergence criterion is met. In many stochastic FL settings, like QSGD or FedPM (Isik
113   et al., 2024), the transmitted client updates q̂it are samples from the posterior distribution qit .
114
115   FedPM As said, we adapt in this work the federated probabilistic mask training (FedPM) frame-
116   work introduced by Isik et al. (2023), in which the weights w of the network are randomly initialized
117   at the start of training, and remain fixed. The federator and clients only train a mask, which deter-
118   mines for each parameter whether it is activated or not, i.e., identifying an efficient subnetwork
119
      within the given fixed network. The probabilistic masks θt are described by Bernoulli distributions,
      i.e., θt ∈ [0, 1]d contains a Bernoulli parameter to be trained for each weight of the network. These
120
      parameters determine the probability of retaining the corresponding weights. During inference, the
121
      weights w are masked with samples xt ∈ {0, 1}d ∼ θt from the distribution θt , i.e., the inference is
122   conducted on a network with weights w ⊙ xt . In FedPM, clients sample from their locally trained
123   models, and send these samples to the federator, which, in turn, updates the global model by averag-
124   ing these samples. The communication cost of this scheme remains the same for all iterations, even
125   though the communication cost can be reduced since the KL-divergence between the global model
126   and the locally trained models diminishes as the training progresses.
127
128   Importance Sampling Isik et al. (2024) proposed a method, called KL minimization with side
129   information (KLMS), to reduce the cost of transmitting the local models qit to the federator. Conse-
130   quently, the communication cost depends on the KL-divergence between the desired distribution and
131
      the common prior. This method utilizes the common side information available at both the clients
      and the federator, as well as shared randomness. The idea is that instead of sampling locally and
132
      sending the samples to the federator, the federator in the KLMS method samples from the desired
133
      distribution through importance sampling. In a nutshell, importance sampling (Srinivasan, 2002)
134   makes use of a common prior to sample from a desired distribution. Consider two distributions P
135   and Q, where P is known to both parties, and Q is only known to the client. To make the federator
136   sample from Q, both parties sample nIS samples {Xi }i∈[nIS ] from P . The client forms an auxiliary
137
      distribution W (i) = PnQ(X  i )/P (Xi )
                              IS Q(X )/P (X ) capturing the importance of the samples. A sample from W

      is fully described by its index i, which can be transmitted with log2 (nIS ) bits, and approximates a
      sample from Q. Chatterjee & Diaconis (2018) shown that importance sampling with posterior Q and
140
      prior P requires nIS to be in the order of Θ(exp(DKL (Q∥P ))) , where DKL (Q∥P ) denotes the KL-
141
      divergence between distributions Q and P . In what follows, we will also denote the KL-divergence
142   between two Bernoulli distributions Q and P with parameters q and p by dKL (q||p).

145   3    B I C OMP FL: C OMMUNICATION -E FFICIENT F EDERATED L EARNING
146
147   In this section, we describe our proposed scheme, termed B I C OMP FL. This scheme is a bi-
148
      directional stochastic compression strategy, which leverages side information using importance sam-
      pling to reduce both the uplink and downlink communication costs. The scheme relies on the avail-
149
      ability of shared randomness between each of the clients and the federator, which can be obtained by
150
      a pseudo-random sequence generated from a common seed, shared by the federator and each client.
151   However, we will distinguish between private shared randomness (between a specific client and the
152   federator) and global shared common randomness, between all parties, which is more challenging
153   to have. We assume that all the clients and the federator share the same global model θ̂0 at initial-
154   ization. This does not incur any communication cost in the case of globally shared randomness, but
155   requires an initial round of transmitting the model from federator to clients in the absence thereof.
156

234   Global Randomness When global shared randomness is available, all clients can maintain the
235   same priors at each iteration t, and, thereby, obtain the same global model estimates θ̂i,t . The global
236   model is known to the clients and the federator from initialization, and synchronization among all
237   clients is ensured by choosing as prior pti,u = pti,d the latest estimate of the global model θ̂i,t . The
238   clients utilize the globally shared randomness to sample the exact same samples from the same
239   prior for uplink transmission at all iterations. Selected indices of such samples are transmitted to the
240   federator to convey an estimate q̂it of the posterior qit , who reconstructs the global model θt+1 . Using
241   the same prior in the downlink, i.e., the global model from the previous iteration, the updated model
242   can be transmitted to the clients through importance sampling. Leveraging the shared randomness,
243   all clients i ∈ [n] sample from the same prior, and thus obtain the exact same estimate of the global

      6   R ELATED W ORK
447
448
      Followed by the introduction of FL by McMahan et al. (2017), lossy compression of gradients
449
      or model updates has been a long studied narrative in FL, with prominent representatives such as
450   SignSGD, also known as 1-bit Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) (Seide et al., 2014), QSGD (Al-
451   istarh et al., 2017), TernGrad (Wen et al., 2017), SignSGD with error feedback (Karimireddy et al.,
452   2019), vector-quantized SGD (Gandikota et al., 2021) and natural compression (Horvóth et al.,
453   2022). Such methods retain satisfactory final model accuracy even with aggressive quantization.
454   Sparsification-based methods have also been considered as alternatives, e.g., TopK (Wangni et al.,
455   2018; Shi et al., 2019). The importance of bi-directional gradient compression in many settings was
456   outlined by Philippenko & Dieuleveut (2020). Many schemes were proposed that leverage combi-
457   nations of gradient compression in the uplink and downlink, error-feedback, and momentum, e.g.,
458
      Mem-SGD (Stich et al., 2018), DoubleSqueeze (Tang et al., 2019), block-wise SignSGD with mo-
      mentum (Zheng et al., 2019), communication-efficient SGD with error reset (CSER) (Xie et al.,
459
      2020), Artemis (Philippenko & Dieuleveut, 2020), Neolithic (Huang et al., 2022), D O C O FL (Dorf-
460
      man et al., 2023), EF21-P and friends (Gruntkowska et al., 2023), 2Direction (Tyurin & Richtárik,
461   2023), M3 Gruntkowska et al. (2024), and LIEC (Cheng et al., 2024). With the exception of the
462   methods MCM (Philippenko & Dieuleveut, 2021) and M3 (Gruntkowska et al., 2024), each client
463   receives the same broadcast, potentially compressed, global gradient or model update. Isik et al.
464   (2024) studied uplink compression for stochastic FL and showed significant communication reduc-
465   tion with competitive performance. Their framework, termed KLMS, applies to a variety of stochas-
466   tic compressors and to Bayesian FL settings, e.g., QLSD Vono et al. (2022). The compression is
467   based on importance sampling, thoroughly studied by Chatterjee & Diaconis (2018). Such meth-
468   ods, known as relative entropy coding (REC), have been used in FL in conjunction with differential
469
      privacy, cf. DP-REC (Triastcyn et al., 2022).
470   Since the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) a variety of works were concerned
471   with finding sparse subnetworks of neural networks that achieve satisfactory accuracy. Ramanujan
472   et al. (2020) showed that randomly weighted networks contain suitable subnetworks of large neural
473   networks capable of achieving competitive performance. Isik et al. (2023) formulated a probabilistic
474   method of training neural network masks collaboratively in an FL context.
475
476   7   C ONCLUSION
477
      In this paper, we illuminated the problem of bi-directional compression in stochastic FL using the
478
      specific instance of federated probabilistic mask training. By leveraging side-information through
479
      carefully chosen prior distributions, the total communication costs can be reduced by factors be-
480   tween 5 and 32 compared to non-stochastic FL baselines while achieving state-of-the-art accu-
481   racies on classification tasks in both homogeneous and heterogeneous FL regimes. We thereby
482   close the gap of downlink compression for stochastic FL and complement the existing literature
483   on bi-directional compression for standard FL. By allowing different priors among all clients, this
484   work opens the door to studying compression under side-information in decentralized stochastic FL,
485   where a central coordinator is missing. Our theoretical results are of independent interest and may
      find application in various scenarios where importance sampling is used.

910   Mirror descent employs point-wise optimization in the form of a first-order approximation of
911   F (θ̂t , Di ) with proximity term DF (p, q), where DF is the Bregman divergence associated with
912   function F (·). When F (x) = ∥x∥2 , and hence the Bregman divergence is the Euclidean distance,
913   this is known as gradient descent. Let now p and q be vectors with the entries corresponding to
914
      independent Bernoulli parameters. When we choose F (x) = x log(x) + (1 − x) log(1 − x), the
                                                   Pd
915   Bregman divergence becomes DF (p, q) = k=1 DKL (pk ∥qk ). Hence, we are optimizing with re-
916   spect to a KL-proximity constraint. The mapping between dual and primal spaces is then given by
                                                     −1
917   ∇F (x) = log(x) − log(1 − x) and (∇F (x)) = e−x1+1 , respectively; also known as the inverse
      sigmoid and the sigmoid functions.

921   The simplest yet effective strategy for block allocation is to partition the model into equally-sized
922   blocks of size d/B for importance sampling (Fixed). The partitioning into blocks is required to make
923   importance sampling practically feasible in this setting. It is known that for vanishing importance
924   sampling error, the number of samples nIS from a block pti,u,b of the prior is supposed to be in the

925                            t
      order of exp DKL qi,b      ∥pti,u,b , where qi,b
                                                     t
                                                        is the b-th block of posterior qit . It was observed by
926
927
      Isik et al. (2024) that the KL-divergence decreases as the training progresses with the global model
      used as a prior, which is intuitive since the local training will change the posterior less and less
928
      as training converges. To adapt the block size according to the divergence from the posterior with
929
      respect to the prior, Isik et al. (2024) proposed an adaptive block allocation strategy (Adaptive),
930   where upon realizing a large deviation from the target KL-divergence per block, clients partition
931   their model into blocks with equal sums of parameter-wise KL-divergences and transmit the block
932   intervals to the federator. The federator aggregates the indices of all the clients, and broadcasts the
933   updated block allocation. We propose in this work a low complexity solution that adapts the block
934   size according to the average KL-divergence per block (Adaptive-Avg). This alleviates the cost of
935   computing and transmitting the exact block partitions, where the transmission of each block size
936   requires log2 (bmax ) bits, with bmax the maximum pre-defined block size.      Instead, the  transmission
                                                                                      t
937   of one size is enough in our solution. If the average KL per block DKL qi,b        ∥pti,u,b deviates more
938   than a given factor, the clients request to update the blocks. In the next iteration, each client proposes
939   a block size, and the federator averages and broadcasts an updated size.

       nIS , block sizes B, and choice of priors pti,u , pti,d . Our experiments have shown that, while increasing
1008
       nIS beyond the ones used in our algorithms slightly improves the convergence over the number of
       epochs, the convergence with respect to the communication cost did not significantly improve. The
1009
       block size is mainly limited by the system resources at hand, and one would choose the largest
1010
       possible for best efficiency while complying with memory resources. We investigated many different
1011   prior choices and found the former global model to be reasonably good in almost all cases. With
1012   high heterogeneity, it might be beneficial to use different convex combinations as priors, which
1013   mix the former global model with the latest posterior estimate of a certain client, but the gains we
1014   experienced were minor. Hence, we settled on the former global estimate for simplicity in presenting
1015   the algorithm.
