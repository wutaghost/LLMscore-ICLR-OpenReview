009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deterministic flow models, such as rectified flows, offer a general framework for
012            learning a deterministic transport map between two distributions, realized as the
013            vector field for an ordinary differential equation (ODE). However, they are sensitive
014            to model estimation and discretization errors and do not permit different samples
015            conditioned on an intermediate state, limiting their application. We present a
016
               general method to turn the underlying ODE of such flow models into a family of
               stochastic differential equations (SDEs) that have the same marginal distributions.
017
               This method permits us to derive families of stochastic samplers, for fixed (e.g.,
018
               previously trained) deterministic flow models, that continuously span the spectrum
019            of deterministic and stochastic sampling, given access to the flow field and the score
020            function. Our method provides additional degrees of freedom that help alleviate
021            the issues with the deterministic samplers and empirically outperforms them. We
022            empirically demonstrate advantages of our method on a toy Gaussian setup and on
023            the large scale ImageNet generation task. Further, our family of stochastic samplers
024            provide an additional knob for controlling the diversity of generation, which we
025            qualitatively demonstrate in our experiments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Deterministic flow models, including Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2022), Flow Matching (Lipman et al.,
030
      2022; Tong et al., 2023), Stochastic Interpolants (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Albergo et al.,
031   2023), and probability flow ODE (Song et al., 2020) learn a reversible deterministic transport between
032   two end distributions p0 (x0 ) and p1 (x1 ). Diffusion models require one of the distributions to be a
033   Gaussian distribution, though generalizations exist (Yoon et al., 2024). In contrast, Rectified Flows,
034   Stochastic Interpolants, and Flow Matching offer a general framework for learning deterministic
035   transports, without this restriction. While deterministic transport enables efficient deterministic
036   sampling, e.g. by the rectification procedure suggested by Liu et al. (2022), stochastic sampling
037   may be desirable for: (1) robustness to estimation errors in the learned flow model, (2) ability to
038   produce random samples conditioned on an intermediate state xt , t ∈ [0, 1], and (3) robustness to
039
      discretization error resulting from discrete step sampling from a continuous time model. We present
      a new theorem (Theorem 1) that provides a recipe to create an infinite family of parameterized
040
      stochastic samplers, given access to the flow field and the score function for the marginal distributions.
041
      Our result provides a general and unified view, while including a few existing proposals (e.g. in
042   Huang et al. (2021); Berner et al. (2022)) as special cases.
043
044   The deterministic transport specifies a deterministic mapping between the samples from the two
045
      distributions and is realized as a learned vector field corresponding to an ordinary differential equation
      (ODE). However, if one distribution is chosen to be a Gaussian, these Flow models can be viewed
046
      as reparameterizations of other deterministic models that also choose a Gaussian as one of the
047
      distributions e.g. probability flow ODEs arising from Gaussian diffusion models. We refer to such
048   models as Gaussian flow models. Transport map learning algorithms such as Gaussian flows are
049   practical to train and enable applications like generative modeling (Ramesh et al., 2022; Lu et al.,
050   2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024), stylization (Isola et al., 2017; Meng et al., 2022),
051   and image restoration (Delbracio & Milanfar, 2023; Rombach et al., 2022; Lugmayr et al., 2022;
052   Kawar et al., 2022), to name a few. However, corresponding deterministic sampler has limitations
053   that we empricially demonstrate on a toy Gaussian task, where it exhibits a bias and consistently
      underestimates the variance of the target distribution, as seen in Figure 2. To enable stochastic

468   5       R ELATED WORK
469
470   Transport learning methods learn a mapping between two distributions, where the learned model can
471   transform a sample from one distribution into a sample from the other one. Typically, one of the
472   distributions is easy to sample (such as a Gaussian) and the other one is the data distribution that one
473   is interested in modeling. The learned mapping can either be deterministic or stochastic. A thorough
474
      overview of related areas can be found in Yang et al. (2024).
475
476   Deterministic transport. Deterministic transport methods implement a change of variable, either
477
      explicitly or approximately, that can be used to uniquely map a sample from one distribution to
      the other. The normalizing flow family (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Dinh et al., 2017; Kingma &
478
      Dhariwal, 2018) of methods construct an explicit invertible model that realizes this map either in one
479
      step or a finite number of discrete steps. Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018; Grathwohl et al., 2019)
480   generalize from discrete steps to a continuous time mapping by inferring and learning the gradient
481   field for all times. However, Neural ODEs are difficult to train due to the need for simulating the
482   ODEs as part of the training. Rectified flows, flow matching, and iterative denoising methods (Liu
483   et al., 2022; Lipman et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2023; Heitz et al., 2023; Delbracio & Milanfar, 2023)
484   either implicitly or explicitly specify a continuous mapping and learn a model to approximate the
485   continuous time mapping. Similarly, probability flow ODEs (Song et al., 2020) learned by diffusion
      models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) approximate an implicitly defined continuous mapping. Our


                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      work is useful for flexible sampling from such pre-trained continuous time deterministic Gaussian
487   flows, or more generally where the score function for all the marginal distributions is either provided
488   or can be deduced from the learned flow model.
489
490   Stochastic transport. Stochastic transport methods learn a stochastic mapping, where a sample
491   from one distribution gets stochastically mapped to a sample from the other. Gaussian diffusion mod-
492   els are a salient example of such discrete Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020) or continuous
493   time Song et al. (2020); Kingma et al. (2021) mappings where one of the distributions is constrained
494   to be Gaussian. Several generalizations have been proposed that extend from Gaussian to more
495   general families of distributions Yoon et al. (2024). The stochastic interpolants framework (Albergo
496
      & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Albergo et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) further generalizes to a larger family
      of distributions by introducing a random latent variable allowing efficient estimation of the score
497
      function at all times. Our work is directly applicable to models learned with such methods where the
498
      score function is accessible and can be used to construct and explore a large family of samplers. The
499   convergence rates of diffusion models have been studied in Chen et al. (2023); Benton et al. (2023)
500   with respect to the number of data samples and dimensionality. However, since our method does not
501   require retraining, it does not affect these properties of the original training algorithms.
502
503   Schrödinger bridge and optimal transport. These methods consider a more general problem of
504   learning transport maps with additional constraints. k-Rectified flows Liu et al. (2022) provide an
505   iterative procedure for tackling deterministic optimal transports for a family of costs, while the more
506   general Schrödinger bridge problem, viewed as an entropy regularized optimal transport, is an active
507   area of research Shi et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023). Our work is complementary to these methods as
508
      we focus on flexible sampling, given the access to the score function for the marginal distributions.
509
510   6    C ONCLUSION
511
512   We introduced a general method to identify a family of SDEs that have the same marginal distribution
513   as a particular SDE, including the special case where the diffusion coefficient of the given SDE is zero.
514   This special case corresponds to flow models which naively only support deterministic sampling. Our
515
      method enables flexible construction of stochastic samplers for such deterministic models where the
      diffusion coefficient can be chosen at sampling time from an infinitely large set of possibilities in an
516
      application and evaluation metric dependent way. Our method requires explicit access to the score
517
      function, in absence of which it is limited to a subset of flow models where the score function can be
518   derived from the given flow model. However, this set includes currently popular rectified flow and
519   diffusion models where one of the distributions is Gaussian.

      We consider the following SDE with additive noise; i.e., the diffusion coefficient g is only a function
705
      of time.

      The perturbation kernel p(xt |x0 ) corresponding to rectified flow is Gaussian, with p(xt |x0 ) =
709
      N (xt ; (1 − t)x0 + tµ1 , t2 σ12 I). Since the perturbation kernel is Gaussian, following Song et al.

       but with class conditioning. Samplers were evaluated for all α ∈ {0.0, 0.02, 0.04, . . . , 1.0}.
