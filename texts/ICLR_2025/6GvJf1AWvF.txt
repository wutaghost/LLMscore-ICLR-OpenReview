009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The use of large language models (LLMs) as evaluators has garnered significant
012            attention due to their potential to rival human-level evaluations in long-form re-
013            sponse assessments. However, current LLM evaluators rely heavily on static,
014            human-defined criteria, limiting their ability to generalize across diverse gener-
015            ative tasks and incorporate context-specific knowledge. In this paper, we pro-
016            pose a novel Self-Assessing LLM framework that integrates Context-Aware Cri-
017            teria (SALC) with dynamic knowledge tailored to each evaluation instance. This
018
               instance-level knowledge enhances the LLM evaluator’s performance by provid-
               ing relevant, context-aware insights that pinpoint the important criteria specific to
019
               the current instance. Additionally, the proposed framework adapts seamlessly to
020
               various tasks without relying on predefined human criteria, offering a more flex-
021            ible evaluation approach. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that our approach
022            significantly outperforms existing baseline evaluation frameworks, yielding im-
023            provements ranging from 5% across a wide variety of datasets. Furthermore,
024            by leveraging knowledge distillation techniques, we fine-tuned smaller language
025            models for criteria generation and evaluation, achieving comparable or superior
026            performance to larger models with much lower cost. Our method also exhibits a
027            5% improvement on the Alpaca leaderboard when employed for preference data
028            generation in Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), underscoring its efficacy as
029
               a robust and scalable evaluation framework.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Evaluating the quality of machine-generated text has long been a significant challenge in Natural
034
      Language Processing (NLP), particularly with the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs), where
035
      a comprehensive understanding of their capabilities and functions is crucial. Traditional metrics
036   such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) for lexical analysis, and BERTScore
037   (Zhang et al., 2019) and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021) for semantic evaluation, often fall short
038   of capturing the full depth and nuance of human judgment. Recently, LLM-based evaluator have
039   gained significant attention due to its alignment with human judgements for evaluation, utilizing
040   zero-shot or few-shot instructions (Chiang et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023).
041
      Despite advancements in LLM evaluators, which can effectively follow human-prepared criteria to
042   assess generative tasks aligned with human preferences, they often rely heavily on static, pre-defined
043   criteria that are applied uniformly across all evaluation instances. This approach struggles to gener-
044   alize across diverse tasks (Liang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Krishna et al., 2023; Min et al., 2023)
045   and fails to provide the context-aware knowledge crucial for specific evaluation instances. However,
046   we observe that context-aware criteria are essential for providing effective guidance across diverse

139   2   R ELATED W ORKS
140
141
      The evaluation of Large Language Models (LLMs) has evolved from traditional metrics like BLEU
142
      Papineni et al. (2002) and ROUGE Lin (2004), which primarily focus on surface-level lexical sim-
143
      ilarity, to more sophisticated semantic-based approaches. These newer methods, such as BLEURT
144   Sellam et al. (2020), BERTScore Zhang et al. (2019), and BARTScore Yuan et al. (2021), evaluate
145   outputs at a deeper level, capturing more meaningful aspects of text. However, these model-based
146   metrics, while significant improvements, remain static and limited by their dependence on fixed
147   reference criteria, which makes them less adaptable to diverse, task-specific requirements.
148
      Recent efforts have explored LLMs themselves as evaluators, particularly in preference-based sce-
149
      narios. Models like Alpaca-Farm Du et al. (2023) take this further by allowing the model to choose
150   better responses through its own judgments, marking a shift toward model-driven evaluations. Ad-
151   ditionally, open-source LLMs like PROMETHEUS Kim et al. (2023; 2024) have emerged to of-
152   fer customizable, fine-grained evaluation capabilities on par with proprietary models like GPT-4.
153   PROMETHEUS leverages its own dataset of score rubrics, instructions, and responses, achieving
154   strong performance in correlation with human judgments and surpassing models like ChatGPT in
155   specific tasks. This development offers a scalable and cost-effective solution for practitioners need-
156   ing custom evaluation criteria, particularly when dealing with large-scale evaluation tasks.
157   Further advancements in fine-grained evaluation are exemplified by FLASK Ye et al. (2023b), which
158   decomposes coarse-level scoring into more granular skill-based evaluations. This protocol improves
159   interpretability by focusing on instance-specific skill requirements in instructions, enhancing both
160   model- and human-based evaluations. Research shows that fine-grained evaluation, as provided by
161   FLASK, offers a more comprehensive view of model performance and increases the reliability of
      evaluations across multiple tasks and datasets.

      Our proposed framework, SALC, advances LLM evaluation by autonomously generating task-
163   specific criteria, unlike static, human-defined rubrics. SALC dynamically adapts to both absolute
164   and relative evaluation contexts, mitigating bias and improving correlation with human judgments.
165   Existing approaches like LLM as Judge (Zheng et al., 2023) use fixed evaluation criteria, which fail
166   to capture nuances across tasks. In contrast, Flask defines 12 skill sets with static, human-curated
167   rubrics, limiting adaptability. Flask selects top skills for evaluation but relies on predefined prompts,
168   making it less flexible. Similarly, Prometheus also requires human input for every new evaluation
169   scenario. SALC overcomes these limitations by enabling criteria generation specific to any task or
170   context without relying on predefined, rigid rubrics.

172   3     M ETHODOLOGY
173
174   The SALC (Self-Assessing LLMs with Autonomous Criteria Generation) framework operates
175   through several key stages, designed to enhance the evaluation of LLM outputs by autonomously
176   generating task-specific criteria. The pipeline described in figure 2 is structured as follows: (i) Crite-
177   ria Generation Stage : SALC begins with the autonomous generation of evaluation criteria tailored
178   to the specific instruction. Unlike traditional methods that rely on predefined rubrics, SALC, dynam-
179   ically generates criteria based on the instruction context. This allows the framework to capture the
180   nuances of different responses across diverse domains. (ii) Evaluation Stage: Once the criteria are
181
      generated, SALC evaluates model outputs in two settings: Absolute Evaluation and Relative Evalu-
      ation. For absolute evaluation, each response is evaluated independently against the generated crite-
182
      ria, scoring it based on task-specific metrics. For relative evaluation, SALC compares responses to
183
      each other, ranking them according to their alignment with the generated criteria. This relative scor-
184   ing reduces the noise that often arises from absolute scoring alone. (iii) Fine-Tuning Open-Source
185   Models: SALC incorporates fine-tuning of smaller, open-source models for both criteria generation
186   and evaluation. FT-Criteria handles criteria generation, while FT-Judge is fine-tuned to evaluate re-
187   sponses. This fine-tuning is conducted using knowledge distillation from GPT-4 into models like
188   llama-7b and llama-13b, enabling efficient yet effective performance. (iv) Preference Data Gen-
189   eration: In addition to direct evaluation, SALC is applied to generate preference data for Direct
190   Preference Optimization (DPO). The use of dynamic context-aware generated criteria improves the
191   quality of preference data, leading to enhanced fine-tuning outcomes in LLM performance.

214   The first step in our methodology involves using an LLM, denoted as M , to autonomously generate
215   evaluation criteria in a zero-shot setting. Given a prompt p, which consists of three core elements
      – (1) a user instruction α, (2) a reference answer r (in the absolute setting), and (3) the response to

      be evaluated o (or multiple responses ra and rb in the relative setting) – the LLM generates a set of
217   factors which are used as the evaluation criteria C. This process occurs without predefined human
218   input or additional training, allowing the LLM to create a criteria framework based solely on its
219   understanding of the context. In absolute grading, we use the following inputs:
220
221   • User Instruction: The user instruction given to the model, denoted by α ∈ I where I represents
222
        set of Instructions (e.g., ”Summarize the following text”).
223   • Reference Answer: The expected or ideal response to the instruction, denoted by r ∈ R where R
224

225   • Response to be Evaluated: The actual output produced by the LLM for evaluation, denoted by
226
        o ∈ O where O represents set of responses.
227   Whereas, in relative grading setting, we leverage the following as inputs:
228
229   • User Instruction: The task prompt given to the model, denoted by α ∈ I (e.g., ”Summarize the
230     following text”).
231   • Response A: First response provided for comparative evaluation, denoted by oa ∈ O where O
232     represents set of responses..
233   • Response B: Second Response provided for comparative evaluation, denoted by ob ∈ O where O
234     represents set of responses..
235   The model M generates a set of evaluation criteria C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , cn }, where cj represents a
236   factor (such as fluency, relevance, coherence, etc.) based on which the response o is evaluated.
237   The criteria C are thus generated based on the instruction α, the reference answer r, and the model
238   response o (or multiple responses ra and rb in the relative setting) , without any predefined human
239   standards, which can be formalized as:

246
247   • Contextual Adaptation: The LLM dynamically adjusts its criteria based on the task, ensuring
248
        relevance, much like a human would adapt depending on context.
249   • Multi-Dimensional Judgment: f evaluates factors such as correctness, style, and logical flow si-

        multaneously, offering holistic assessments similar to human evaluators.
251   • RLHF Alignment: Fine-tuning with RLHF equips the model with a deeper understanding of hu-

        man preferences, allowing it to generate nuanced criteria.
253   By using f , SALC reduces the reliance on predefined metrics, offering flexible, adaptive evaluation
254   on par with human judgment.

      (Zheng et al., 2023)

525   5     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we introduce SALC, a novel approach for LLM evaluation by enabling context-aware
528   dynamic criteria generation and self-assessment. By allowing models to generate their own evalu-
529   ation criteria, SALC overcomes the limitations of conventional human-defined metrics, providing a
530   more scalable and consistent solution for evaluating LLM outputs. Our extensive empirical analysis
531
      demonstrates that SALC significantly improves correlation with human expert evaluations, enhances
      inter-model agreement, and yields significant performance gains in tasks like preference data gener-
532
      ation for DPO fine-tuning. These results highlight the effectiveness and versatility of SALC, offering
533
      a robust alternative for improving LLM evaluation and preference data generation. SALC’s ability
534   to generate high-quality criteria and evaluations without human intervention positions it as a scalable
535   solution for future advancements in LLM evaluation methods.

      A    I LLUSTRATION OF OVERALL EVALUATION PROCESS
626
627   The diagram illustrates the overall evaluation process in the context of the SALC framework. In
628
      SALC, the LLM is provided with a task description and an instruction, which in this case involves
      summarizing the impact of climate change on polar bear populations. The LLM generates a re-
629
      sponse based on the instruction, which is then evaluated through autonomously generated criteria
630
      rather than relying on predefined human-designed metrics. The diagram shows an example of this
631   evaluation process, where the LLM generates criteria like Relevance to Instruction (whether the re-
632   sponse addresses the instruction of summarizing the impact on polar bears), Completeness(whether
633   critical details like habitat loss and hunting challenges are covered), and Alignment with Reference
634   (whether the response reflects the key points from a given reference). This reference contains factual
635   details, such as the reliance of polar bears on seals for food and the consequences of habitat loss.
636   The criteria generation process is a core component of SALC, allowing the LLM to autonomously
637   determine the relevant factors for evaluation based on the task and reference. Once the criteria are
638   generated, the LLM proceeds to the evaluation stage, where it assesses its own output by compar-
639
      ing it against the generated criteria. Feedback is provided based on this comparison, pointing out
      strengths and areas for improvement, such as missing explanations regarding hunting practices and
640
      energy expenditure. The LLM then assigns a score to its response—in this case, 3.2 out of 5—based
641
      on how well it aligns with the criteria it generated. This process highlights the autonomy of SALC,
642   where the LLM not only generates criteria but also provides a detailed evaluation and score based on
643   its self-assessment. This approach leads to more consistent and contextually relevant evaluations,
644   as the criteria are tailored to the task at hand. The ability of the LLM to autonomously generate
645   evaluation metrics improves the alignment of model-generated outputs with human expectations,
646   addressing task-specific nuances more effectively than conventional methods. In SALC, the entire
647   process—from criteria generation to evaluation and scoring—is conducted without human interven-
      tion, enhancing the robustness and reliability of LLM self-assessments.

      B.2    BASELINE M ETHODS
732
733   Our SALC method adaptively generates criteria to improve evaluation and reasoning capabilities of
734   pre-trained LMs. In addition, we proposed two fine-tuned small LMs: FT-Crieria that focused on
735   autonomous criteria generation, and FT-Judge which is designed for evaluation purposes. We bench-
736   mark the performance of our framework against the following state-of-the-art evaluation frame-
737   works:
738
      • LLM as a judge [Zheng et al. (2023)]: In this approach, a strong LLM is used to judge the
739
        responses while mitigating the position, verbosity and self-enhancement biases with intelligent
740
        prompt enhancement mechanisms.
741
      • Prometheus [Kim et al. (2024)]: It is a open-source fine-tuned model for response evaluation that
742
        leverages 1K human labelled and automatic score rubrics to improve the reasoning capability.
743
      • LLMs: We leverage several pre-trained LLMs such as GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4, GPT-4o [Achiam
744
        et al. (2023)] and Llama3-70b-instruct [Dubey et al. (2024)] as the evaluator model to benchmark
745     against SALC.
746
      • SLMs: To benchmark against SALC-Tune, we choose a diverse set of small open-sourced pre-
747     trained models including llama-7b-chat, llama-13b-chat, llama3-8b-instruct [Dubey et al. (2024)],
748     mixtral-8x7b-instruct, and mistral-7b-instruct [Jiang et al. (2023)].

      B.3    E VALUATION M ETRICS
751
752
      To comprehensively analyze the efficacy of SALC, we leverage the following evaluation metrics:
753
754   • Accuracy: In ranking grading scenario, as we have the ground truth for chosen and rejected
755     response, we used Accuracy, F1-score metric to compute the agreement between LLM evaluator
        and the ground truth.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      • Correlation: In absolute grading scenario, we used Pearson (Cohen et al., 2009), Kendall-
757     Tau(Kendall, 1938), Spearman(Spearman, 1961) correlation metrics to compare between LLM
758     evaluator scores and human judged scores.
759   • Similarity: To understand the textual similarity between criteria generated from fine-tuned SLM
760     with their respective references, we used lexical similarity metrics such as Rouge (Lin, 2004),
761     Bleu (Papineni et al., 2002) and BERT (Zhang et al., 2019).
762   • Alignment: We compute the Helpfulness, Harmfulness and Honesty metrics to quantify the qual-
763     ity of different evaluator models on HHH alignment dataset (Askell et al., 2021).

766   B.4     E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
767
768
      Our experiments were conducted using a high-performance compute cluster equipped with 8
      NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each with 80 GB of memory. This setup provided the necessary compu-
769
      tational power for training and fine-tuning large language models.
770
771   Hardware and Distributed Training: To efficiently utilize our multi-GPU setup, we employed
772   Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) techniques for fine-tuning the larger 7B and 13B parameter
773   models. FSDP allowed us to distribute the model parameters across multiple GPUs, enabling the
774
      training of these large-scale models while optimizing memory usage and computational efficiency.
775   Model Variants and Fine-tuning Approaches: Broadly, we conducted two sets of experiments:
776   (1) Standard Fine-tuning (SFT) on the Llama-2 7B and 13B Chat models, which involved further
777   training these pre-trained models on our specific dataset to adapt them to our target domain; and (2)
778   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) fine-tuning applied to three models: Phi-2, Phi-3-mini-4k-
779   instruct, and Mistral 7B instruct, on the preference data created by our method and other baselines.
780
      The SFT training was done for 3 epochs on both of the model while for DPO we fine-tuned the
      already instruction fine-tuned models for 2 epochs.
781
782   Hyperparameters and Training Details: For our fine-tuning experiments, we experimented with
783   various hyperparameters: For Standard Fine-tuning, we have repoted the scores using a learning rate
784   of 1 × 10−5 , while for DPO Fine-tuning, a lower learning rate of 1 × 10−6 was employed to ensure
785   stable training. For both of these experiments, we used a batch-size of 64. We implemented a cosine
786
      annealing learning rate scheduler for both SFT and DPO fine-tuning.
787   Inference: During the inference phase, we employed a greedy decoding strategy to generate outputs
788   from our fine-tuned models. This approach selects the most probable token at each step of the
789   generation process, resulting in deterministic outputs.
