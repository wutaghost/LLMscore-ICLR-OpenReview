009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating novel and functional protein sequences is critical to a wide range of
012            applications in biology. Recent advancements in conditional diffusion models
013            have shown impressive empirical performance in protein generation tasks. How-
014            ever, reliable generations of protein remain an open research question in de novo
015            protein design, especially when it comes to conditional diffusion models. Consid-
016
               ering the biological function of a protein is determined by multi-level structures,
               we propose a novel multi-level conditional diffusion model that integrates both
017
               sequence-based and structure-based information for efficient end-to-end protein
018
               design guided by specified functions. By generating representations at different
019            levels simultaneously, our framework can effectively model the inherent hierar-
020            chical relations between different levels, resulting in an informative and discrimi-
021            native representation of the generated protein. We also propose a Protein-MMD,
022            a new reliable evaluation metric, to evaluate the quality of generated protein with
023            conditional diffusion models. Our new metric is able to capture both distributional
024            and functional similarities between real and generated protein sequences while
025            ensuring conditional consistency. We experiment with standard datasets and the
026            results on protein generation tasks demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed gen-
027
               eration framework and evaluation metric.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Designing proteins with specific biological functions is a fundamental yet formidable challenge in
032   biotechnology. It benefits wide-ranging applications from synthetic biology to drug discovery (Wat-
033   son et al., 2023; Bose et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016; Feng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b;b; Lin
034   et al., 2023). The challenge arises from the intricate interplay between protein sequence, structure,
035   and function, which has not yet been fully understood (Dill et al., 2008). Traditional methods, such
036   as directed evolution, rely on labor-intensive trial-and-error approaches involving random mutations
037   and selective pressures, making the process time-consuming and costly (Arnold, 1998). Recently,
038   generative models have emerged as promising tools for protein design, enabling the exploration
039
      of vast sequence-structure-function landscapes (Anand & Achim, 2022; Fu et al., 2024; Dauparas
      et al., 2022; Trippe et al., 2023). However, existing generative models—including those focused on
040
      enzyme engineering, antibody creation, and therapeutic protein development—are typically task-
041
      specific and require retraining for new design objectives (Fu et al., 2024; Dauparas et al., 2022).
042   These limitations impede their adaptability and scalability across different protein families.
043
044   While conditional generative models offer an end-to-end solution by directly linking the design
045
      process to the guidance, these models have been applied to protein generation Komorowska et al.
      (2024); Klarner et al. (2024); Gruver et al. (2023). In conditional protein generation tasks, main-
046
      taining conditional consistency across diverse contexts and ensuring functional relevance are critical
047
      (Trippe et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024). Specifically, the generated proteins should fully adhere to the
048   specified functional constraints (Gretton et al., 2012). At the same time, achieving diversity and
049   novelty in generated proteins is essential for successful design. In the literature, structural novelty
050   can be assessed using Foldseek (van Kempen et al., 2022), which performs rapid protein structure
051   searches against databases like PDB (Berman et al., 2000) and AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021) to
052   ensure the generated proteins are novel compared to known structures. Diversity is measured using
053   TM-score (Zhang & Skolnick, 2005), which calculates structural variation between the generated
      proteins themselves and between the generated and wild-type proteins (Hu et al., 2024).


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      However, it is unknown how to assess the conditional consistency (Gretton et al., 2012) in de novo
055   protein design. Specifically, the fundamental problem of properly evaluating conditional consistency
056   is quantifying to what extent the generated protein adheres to the specified functional constraints.
057   Unlike computer vision, where metrics such as FID (Heusel et al., 2017) have become a standard
058   for assessing generated images, it is unclear whether such metrics are suitable for protein generation
059   tasks. In protein design, the generated output cannot be as easily visualized or assessed as in im-
060   ages, making the choice of evaluation metrics even more critical. Therefore, how to adapt metrics
061   like FID or Maximum Mean Discrepancy (Gretton et al., 2012) presents challenges. Furthermore,
062   existing methods for protein generation either generate the representation at a single level or ignore
063
      hierarchical relations within different levels of protein representation (e.g., amino acid and atom).
      Choosing the level of granularity at which capturing both the structural and functional nuances of
064
      protein sequences introduces additional challenges, raising significant concerns about the reliability
065
      of generated proteins in real-world applications. This difficulty highlights the need for a more ratio-
066   nal generative scheme, making the generated protein adhere to the specified functional constraints.
067
068   Motivated by the need to capture both the structural and functional nuances of protein sequences, we
069
      propose a novel multi-level conditional generative diffusion model for protein design that integrates
      both sequence-based (Lin et al., 2023) and structure-based (Wang et al., 2022a) hierarchical infor-
070
      mation. Generation at multi-levels enables efficient end-to-end generation of proteins with specified
071
      functions and modeling the inherent hierarchical relations between different representations, result-
072   ing in an informative and discriminative representation of the protein. Our model incorporates a
073   rigid-body 3D rotation-invariant preprocessing step combined with an autoregressive decoder to
074   maintain SE(3)-invariance, ensuring accurate modeling of protein structures in 3D space. To ad-
075   dress the challenges of evaluating the conditional consistency, we propose Protein-MMD, a metric
076   based on Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), to better capture both distributional and functional
077   similarities between real and generated protein sequences, while ensuring conditional consistency.
078   We prove that our Protein-MMD provides a more accurate measure that reflects the given condition.
079   Experiments demonstrate that our proposed model outperforms existing approaches in generating
080
      diverse, novel, and functionally relevant proteins.
081   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
082
083          • We design a novel multi-level conditional generative diffusion model that integrates
084            sequence-based and structure-based information for efficient end-to-end protein design.
085            Our model incorporates a rigid-body 3D rotation-invariant preprocessing step to maintain
086
               SE(3)-invariance, ensuring accurate modeling of protein structures in 3D space.
087          • We highlight the limitations of current evaluation metrics in protein generation tasks, par-
088            ticularly in conditional settings, and propose Protein-MMD, a novel metric to evaluate con-
089            ditional consistency for protein generation tasks by leveraging language models.
090          • We experiment with standard datasets to verify the effectiveness of the proposed model.
091            Our evaluation metric paves the way for reliable protein design with given conditions.

119   2.2       F R ÉCHET I NCEPTION D ISTANCE
120
121   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) is commonly used in computer vision to measure the similarity
122
      between real and generated data distributions (Heusel et al., 2017). It computes the Fréchet distance
      between feature representations of real and generated samples, extracted from a pre-trained network,
123
      such as Inception v3 (Szegedy et al., 2016). The FID between two distributions pr (real data) and pg
124
      (generated data) is computed by modeling the features as multivariate Gaussian distributions with
125   means µr , µg and covariances Σr , Σg :
126
127                      FID(pr , pg ) = ∥µr − µg ∥22 + Tr Σr + Σg − 2(Σr Σg )1/2 ,                      (5)
128
129   where ∥ · ∥2 denotes the Euclidean distance, and Tr is the trace operator. FID provides a measure of
130
      how close the real and generated data are, with lower FID values indicating high similarity.

133   3.1       M ULTI - LEVEL D IFFUSION
134
135   Motivated by the need to capture both the structural and functional nuances of protein sequences,
136   we propose a multi-level diffusion model to generate information about a protein at three levels:
137   the amino acid level, the backbone level, and the all-atom level. By constructing representations at
138   different levels, our framework effectively integrates the inherent hierarchical relations of proteins,
139   resulting in a more rational protein generative model. We remark that there are hierarchical relations
140
      among different levels. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first diffusion model to
      generate information at three levels and leverage the hierarchical relation between different levels.

196   The decoder at each level is an autoregressive Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) model that reconstructs
197   the protein at each respective level. The autoregressive decoder can then use these transformed
198   embeddings to reconstruct the information of a protein. At the sequence level, the decoder predicts

307   Evaluating the quality and consistency of protein generation models requires a well-defined frame-
308   work, particularly in the context of conditional generation. In this section, we define the theoreti-
309   cal basis for assessing conditional consistency in multi-class generation tasks and propose a novel
310   framework to assess the suitability of different evaluation metrics.
311   Denote {C 1 , C 2 , . . . , C K } as a set of target classes, where each class C k corresponds to an inde-
312   pendent and mutually exclusive category (e.g., different protein functions or classes). Let x repre-

315   tency, it should generate samples such that as we progress through a sequence of generated samples
316   {xi |i = 0, 1, 2, · · · , ∞} ordered by increasing quality, the consistency distance between each sam-
317   ple and samples in C k should decrease. Mathematically, a good evaluation metric d satisfies:

379
380   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
381
382   We compared our model against several baselines, each representing distinct approaches to protein
383
      generation. ProteoGAN (Kucera et al., 2022) is a GAN-based method, while ESM2 (Lin et al.,
      2023) and ProstT5 (Heinzinger et al., 2023) are Transformer-based language models specifically
384
      designed for protein sequence modeling. ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022) and LatentDiff
385
      (Fu et al., 2024), on the other hand, are graph-based models, with LatentDiff also incorporating a
386   diffusion-based framework, specifically using a latent diffusion approach. For each model, we eval-
387   uate the performance using both diversity metrics (TM-score, RMSD, and Seq.ID) and conditional
388   consistency metrics (Protein-MMD and Protein-FID). Higher RMSD, lower TM-score, and lower
389   Seq.ID indicate higher diversity, while lower Protein-MMD and Protein-FID values signify higher
390   conditional consistency between the generated and real protein distributions.
391
      To verify the effectiveness of our proposed multi-level conditional diffusion model, we conducted
392   comprehensive experiments on two standard datasets: the Enzyme Commission (EC) dataset and the
393   Gene Ontology (GO) dataset. The EC dataset categorizes proteins based on the biochemical reac-
394   tions they catalyze, while the GO dataset classifies proteins according to their associated biological
395   processes, cellular components, and molecular functions. These datasets provide a robust bench-
396   mark for assessing both the diversity and conditional consistency of generated protein sequences.
397
      Our model leverages the esm2 t33 650M UR50D model from ESM2 (Lin et al., 2023) as the
398   amino acid-level encoder. To construct the Protein Variational Auto-encoder model, we set the
399   latent dimension to 384, and the decoder is composed of 8 Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) decoder
400   blocks, each equipped with an 8-head self-attention mechanism. The Protein Variational Auto-
401   encoder model is trained with a learning rate of 10−4 , using a combination of mean squared error
402   (MSE) and cross-entropy as the loss functions. To regulate the latent vector distribution, we apply
403   a KL divergence loss with a weight of 10−5 . We experimented with 128, 256, and 512 as the
404   maximum sequence lengths. For the diffusion model, we modify the DiT-B architecture from DiT
405   (Peebles & Xie, 2023b), which consists of 12 DiT blocks and uses a hidden size of 768. The DiT
406
      model is trained from scratch with a learning rate of 10−4 and includes a weight decay of 10−5 .

504   5     R ELATED W ORKS
505
506   De novo protein design methods are dedicated to identify novel proteins with the desired structure
507   and function properties (Watson et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2016; Frey et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024;
508
      Komorowska et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Recent advancements in machine learning have enabled
      a generative model to accelerate key steps in the discovery of novel molecular structures and drug
509
      design (Gao et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022). A prior step of generate
510
      models to the representation of proteins (Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2024;
511   Zhao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Somnath et al., 2021; Jamasb et al., 2024).
512
513   The majority of representation learning for protein is to represent a protein as a sequence of amino
514
      acids (Chen et al., 2023; Moreta et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023). Considering the
      spatial information is important to the property of a protein, many works resort to a graph model for
515
      a comprehensive presentation with the structure information (Ingraham et al., 2019; Huang et al.,
516
      2024a). In general, each node on the graph is an amino acid and the edge is decided by the distance
517   between two nodes Aykent & Xia (2022); Xia & Ku (2021); Hladis et al. (2023). Despite the power
518   of graph models, the relation information in a 3-dimensional space captures the multi-level structure
519   such as the angle between two edges. A line of research works explore the protein structure in 3D
520   space Hermosilla et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2024b); Wang et al. (2023); Zhong et al. (2020); Peng
521   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2024a). Recently, large language models (LLMs) have
522   also been introduced to model the sequence Meier et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2022);
523   Su et al. (2024) inspired by the success of natural language processing.
524   Capitalizing on the power of generative models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
525   and diffusion models, deep generative modeling has shown its potential for fast generation of new
526   and viable protein structures. Anand & Huang (2018) has applied GANs to the task of generat-
527   ing protein structures by encoding protein structures in terms of pairwise distances on the protein
528   backbone. Diffusion models have emerged as a powerful tool for graph-structured diffusion pro-
529   cesses Klarner et al. (2024). FrameDiff has been proposed for monomer backbone generation and it
530   can generate designable monomers up to 500 amino acids Yim et al. (2023). NOS is another diffu-
531
      sion model that generates protein sequences with high likelihood by taking many alternating steps
      in the continuous latent space of the model Gruver et al. (2023).
532
533
534
      6     C ONCLUSIONS
535   In this paper, we introduce a novel multi-level conditional generative diffusion model that integrates
536   sequence-based and structure-based information for efficient end-to-end protein design. Our model
537   incorporates a 3D rotation-invariant preprocessing step to maintain SE(3)-invariance. To address the
538   limitation of existing evaluation metrics, we propose a novel metric to evaluate conditional consis-
539   tency for protein generation tasks by leveraging language models, which we hope to catalyze the
      research progress on protein generation.
