009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a novel linear bandit problem where a subset of features is latent,
012            resulting in partial access to reward information and spurious estimates. Without
013            properly addressing the latent features, the regret grows linearly over the decision
014            epoch T while improving the regret bound is challenging because their dimension
015            and relationship with rewards are not available. We propose a novel analysis to
016            handle the latent features and an algorithm that achieves a regret bound sublinear
017            in T . The core of the algorithm lies in (i) augmenting basis vectors orthogonal
018            to the observable feature space, and (ii) developing an efficient doubly robust
019
               estimator that further improves the regret
                                                        p bound. With these two ingredients, our
020            algorithm achieves a regret bound of O(
                                                     e (d + dh )T ), where d is the dimension of
021
               observable features, and dh is the unknown dimension of the unobserved features
               that affects the reward. Crucially, our algorithm does not rely on prior knowledge
022
               of the unobserved feature space, which expands as more features become hidden.
023
               Numerical experiments confirm that our algorithm outperforms both non-contextual
024            multi-armed bandits and other linear bandit algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      We consider a linear bandit problem where the learning agent has access to only a subset of the
030
      features, while the reward is determined using the complete set of features, including both observed
031   and unobserved elements. Conventional linear bandit problems rely on the assumption that the
032   rewards are linear to only observed features, without accounting for the potential presence of
033   unobserved features. However, in many real-world applications, rewards are often affected by the
034   latent features that are not observable to the agent. For example, in recommendation systems, the
035   true reward вАФ such as user satisfaction or purchase decisions вАФ depends not only on observable
036   features like user demographics or past behaviors but also on latent preferences, such as specific
037   tastes in artists (for streaming services) or brands (in e-commerce). Accurately incorporating these
038   latent features is essential for providing precise recommendations, while ignoring them causes bias or
039
      model misspecification errors in every decision-making.
040   To address the latent features, Park & Faradonbeh (2022), Kim et al. (2023a) and Park & Faradonbeh
041   (2024) rely on the assumption that observed features are linear to the latent features sampled from
042   a specific distribution, e.g., a mean-zero Gaussian. Establishing a regret bound sublinear in the
043   decision horizon without such structural assumptions on the latent features remains a significant
044   challenge and has not been accomplished yet. Key challenges in the bandit problem with partially
045
      observable features arise from the complete lack of information on the latent features. Indeed, we do
      not even know whether an agent observes features partially or not and whether we should use the
046
      latent features or not.
047
048   To address these challenges, we propose a novel linear bandit algorithm that is agnostic to the
049   presence of partially observable features. Notwithstanding the absence of knowledge regarding
050   unobserved features, our algorithm is capable of obtaining a regret bound that is tighter than that
051
      achieved both linear bandit algorithms that consider only observable features and multi-armed bandit
      (MAB)
         вИЪ algorithms that entirely ignore features. Our proposed algorithm achieves a regret bound of

065   The key idea of our proposed algorithm lies in two main components: (i) reconstructing the feature
066   vectors to capture the impact of unobserved features on the rewards, and (ii) constructing a novel
067   doubly robust estimator that is robust to information loss caused by unobservability. For (i), we
068   decompose the rewards into two additive terms: one projected onto the row space of the observable
069   features, and the other onto its orthogonal complement. The former term maximally captures the
070   effects from the observed features, while the latter minimizes the impact of the unobserved features.
071   We then augment the observable features with an orthogonal basis from the complement space to
072
      capture all effects on the rewards. This allows us to reformulate the problem in a conventional linear
      bandit framework, where the reward function is defined as the dot product of the minimally augmented
073
      features and the associated unknown parameter. However, these augmented features are not identical
074
      to the unobserved features, which may lead to potential estimation error. To mitigate these errors, we
075   leverage (ii) the doubly robust estimator, which is widely used in statistical literature for its robustness
076   to errors caused by missing data. Together, these two approaches allow the algorithm to effectively
077   compensate for missing information, enhancing both estimation accuracy and adaptability to the
078   environment.
079
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
080
081          вАҐ We propose a linear bandit problem with partially observable features. Our problem setting
082            is more general and challenging than those in the existing literature on linear bandits with
083            latent features, which often rely on specific structural assumptions governing the relationship
084            between observable and latent features. In contrast, our approach assumes no additional
085            structure for the unobserved features beyond the linearity of the reward function, which is
086            commonly adopted in the linear bandit literature (Section 3).
087          вАҐ We introduce a novel estimation strategy by (i) efficiently augmenting the features that
088            maximally captures the effect of reward projected onto the observed features, while mini-
089            mizing the impact of unobserved features (Section 4), and (ii) constructing a doubly-robust
090            (DR) estimator that is robust to the error caused by unobserved features. By integrating
               augmented features with the DR estimator, we guarantee a convergence rate of O(t  e вИТ1/2 ) on
091

               the rewards for all arms in each round t (Theorem 2).
093          вАҐ We propose the Robust to Latent Features (RoLF) algorithm for      pthe general linear bandit
094            framework with latent features that achieves a regret bound of O(e (d + dh )T ) (Theorem 3),
095            where dh is the dimension of the subspace formed by projecting the reward, linear to
096            unobserved features, onto the orthogonal complement of the row space of the observable
097            features (Section 4.2). Our proposed algorithm requires no prior knowledge or modeling

               of the unobserved features, yet achieves a sharper regret bound than both linear bandit
               algorithms that consider only observable features (Li et al., 2010; Abbasi-yadkori et al.,
099
               2011; Agrawal & Goyal, 2013; Kim & Paik, 2019) and MAB algorithms (Auer et al., 2002).
100
101          вАҐ Our numerical experiments demonstrate that our proposed algorithm consistently outper-
102
               forms the existing linear bandit and MAB algorithms. These results support our theoretical
               findings and validate the practicality of our method.

105   2    R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   In bandit problems, the learning agent learns only from the outcomes of chosen actions, leaving
      unchosen alternatives unknown (Robbins, 1952). This constraint requires a balance between exploring


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      new actions and exploiting actions learned to be good, known as the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.
109   Efficiently managing this tradeoff is crucial for guiding the agent towards the optimal policy. To
110   address this, algorithms based on optimism in the face of uncertainty (OFU) (Lai & Robbins,
111   1985) are widely used and studied in linear bandits (Abe & Long, 1999; Auer, 2002; Dani et al.,
112   2008; Rusmevichientong & Tsitsiklis, 2010). Notable examples include LinUCB (Li et al., 2010)
113   and OFUL (Abbasi-yadkori et al., 2011), known for their practicality and performance guarantees.
114   However, existing approaches differ from ours in two key aspects: (i) they assume that the learning
115   agent can observe the entire feature vector related to the reward, and (ii) their algorithms
                                                                                         вИЪ         have regret
116   that scales linearly with the dimension of the observed feature vector, i.e., O(d
                                                                                     e     T ).
117   In contrast, we develop an algorithm that achieves a sublinear regret bound by employing the doubly
118   robust (DR) technique, thereby avoiding the linear dependence on the dimension of the feature
119   vectors. The DR estimation in the framework of linear contextual bandits is first introduced by Kim
120   & Paik (2019)andDimakopoulou etвИЪal. (2019), and subsequent studies improve the regret bound in
121   this problem setting by a factor of d (KimвИЪet al., 2021; 2023b). A recent application (Kim et al.,
122   2023c) achieves a regret bound of order O( dT log T ) under IID features over rounds. However,
123   the extension to non-stochastic or non-IID features remains an open question. To address this issue,
124   we develop a novel analysis that applies the DR estimation to non-stochastic features, achieving a
125   regret bound sublinear with respect to the dimension of the augmented feature vectors. Furthermore,
126   we extend DR estimation to handle sparse parameters, thereby further improving the regret bound to
127
      be sublinear with respect to the reduced dimension.
128   Our problem is more general and challenging than misspecified linear bandits, where the assumed
129   reward model fails to accurately reflect the true reward, such as when the true reward function is
130   non-linear (Lattimore & Szepesv√°ri, 2020), or a deviation term is added to the reward model (Ghosh
131   et al., 2017; Bogunovic et al., 2021; He et al., 2022). While our work assumes that the misspecified
132   (or inaccessible) portion of the reward is linearly related to certain unobservable features, misspecified
133
      linear bandit problems can be reformulated as a special case of our framework. While the regret
      bounds in Lattimore & Szepesv√°ri (2020), Bogunovic et al. (2021) and He et al. (2022) incorporate a
134
      sum of misspecification errors that may accumulate over the decision horizon, our work establishes
135
      a regret bound that is sublinear in the decision horizon T without any misspecification errors.
136   Ghosh
137
             вИЪet al. (2017) proposed a hypothesis test whether to use linear bandits or MAB    вИЪ and proved
      O(K T log T ) regret bound      when the  sum  of misspecified  error is greater than вД¶(d  T ). In contrast,
138
                                p
      our algorithm attains O( (d + dh )T log T ) regret bound without necessitating such hypothesis tests
139   for misspecification or partial observability.
140
141   Lastly, our problem appears similar to the bandits with partially observable features studied by Park
142
      & Faradonbeh (2022). In their work, the observed features are assumed to be related to the latent
      features through a known linear mapping, with the latent features sampled from a centered Gaussian
143
      distribution. However, our approach does not impose any structural assumptions on either the
144
      observed or latent features, making it a more general and challenging problem compared to that of
145   Park & Faradonbeh (2022).
