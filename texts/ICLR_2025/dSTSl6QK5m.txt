009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of natural language
012            processing with their impressive reasoning and question-answering capabilities.
013            However, these models are sometimes prone to generating credible-sounding but
014            incorrect information, a phenomenon known as LLM hallucinations. Reliable
015            uncertainty estimation in LLMs is essential for fostering trust in their generated
016            responses and serves as a critical tool for the detection and prevention of erro-
017            neous or hallucinated outputs. To achieve reliable and well-calibrated uncertainty
018
               quantification in open-ended and free-form natural language generation, we pro-
               pose an uncertainty-aware fine-tuning approach for LLMs. This approach en-
019
               hances the model’s ability to provide reliable uncertainty estimates without com-
020
               promising accuracy, thereby guiding them to produce more trustworthy responses.
021            We introduce a novel uncertainty-aware causal language modeling loss function,
022            grounded in the principles of decision theory. Through rigorous evaluation on
023            multiple free-form question-answering datasets and models, we demonstrate that
024            our uncertainty-aware fine-tuning approach yields better calibrated uncertainty es-
025            timates in natural language generation tasks than fine-tuning with the standard
026            causal language modeling loss. Furthermore, the experimental results show that
027            the proposed method significantly improves the model’s ability to detect halluci-
028            nations and identify out-of-domain prompts.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable success in various natural language pro-
      cessing tasks (Touvron et al., 2023; Gemma et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023) and are increas-
034
      ingly becoming ubiquitous in a variety of domains for their decision-making and reasoning abili-
035
      ties (Eigner & Händler, 2024). However, their real-world deployment, particularly in high-stakes
036   and safety-critical applications, is hindered by challenges such as hallucinations and out-of-domain
037   prompts, which can lead to the generation of erroneous or nonsensical outputs. Hallucinations, of-
038   ten described as plausible-sounding but incorrect or unfaithful model generations (Ji et al., 2023),
039   present a crucial challenge in developing trustworthy systems especially in critical domains such as
040   medical (Ahmad et al., 2023) and legal (Magesh et al., 2024). The ability to recognize out-of-domain
041   prompts and to acknowledge the limits of a model’s knowledge base paves the way for building safe
042   AI systems (Amodei et al., 2016).
043   Uncertainty quantification (UQ) in LLMs plays a pivotal role in understanding what the model
044   knows and does not know, which is an active area of research for free-form natural language gener-
045   ation (NLG) (Kadavath et al., 2022; Kuhn et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). UQ methods has emerged
046   as a step towards determining the trustworthiness of responses generated by LLMs (Fadeeva et al.,
047   2023; Plaut et al., 2024; Kadavath et al., 2022). Uncertainty estimation techniques such as semantic
048   entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023) have shown to be effective indicators in detecting ‘confabulations’ (Far-
049   quhar et al., 2024), a subcategory of hallucinations characterized by the generation of arbitrary and
050
      incorrect responses.
051   The calibration of uncertainty estimates is crucial for the reliability of LLMs; a well-calibrated
052   model should correlate low uncertainty with accurate responses and high uncertainty with likely
053   incorrect responses. However, recent studies (Xiong et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) have revealed
      that LLM predictions are often poorly calibrated, leading to overconfidence in incorrect outputs.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      This problem is more pronounced in fine-tuned language models (Kong et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
055   2024b). Unlike the pre-training phase, where models are exposed to vast amounts of unlabeled
056   data, fine-tuning involves limited labeled data. Consequently, the immense capacity of LLMs can
057   lead to overfitting on this limited data, producing overconfident predictions (Kong et al., 2020).
058   This presents a substantial challenge, as the model may produce unreliable uncertainty metrics that
059   are influenced by the model’s miscalibrated token confidence. Moreover, evaluating calibration in
060   NLG is particularly challenging due to the variable lengths of generated text compared to reference
061   sentences. Traditional calibration error metrics (Naeini et al., 2015; Nixon et al., 2019) assume a
062   fixed number of outcomes or classes, which aligns well with classification tasks but not with the
063
      open-ended nature of NLG, where the number of tokens in generated text and reference sentences
      can differ. To address this, we exploit the inverse correlation between uncertainty quantification and
064
      the quality of generated text, allowing us to perform calibration analysis in NLG settings.
065
066   In this work, we introduce an uncertainty-aware fine-tuning approach for LLMs, which is grounded
067   in decision theory and tailored for free-form natural language generation. Our approach is orthog-
068   onal to existing uncertainty quantification methods and is driven by the goal of enhancing the reli-
069
      ability of uncertainty metrics through uncertainty-aware fine-tuning. Specifically, we achieve this
      with an optimization objective that encourages the model to learn to associate high uncertainty with
070
      incorrectly generated tokens and low uncertainty with correctly generated tokens, while maximiz-
071
      ing accuracy within the framework of causal language modeling. We show that fine-tuning with
072   our calibration objective enhances the reliability of uncertainty quantification in LLMs. The cali-
073   brated uncertainty estimates serve as a crucial tool for enhancing the trustworthiness of generated
074   responses, represents a substantial step towards identifying hallucinations, and improving decision-
075   making capabilities through selective generation (Ren et al., 2022).
076
      Our contributions are as follows:
077
078          • We propose uncertainty-aware causal language modeling (UA-CLM) loss function, de-
079            signed for fine-tuning LLMs to produce well-calibrated uncertainty estimates in free-form
080            natural language generation.
081          • We conduct uncertainty calibration analysis in free-form NLG settings, employing an in-
082            novative methodology that leverages the inverse correlation between uncertainty quantifi-
083            cation and the quality of the generated text.
084          • We perform a comprehensive empirical evaluation across four key aspects: hallucination
085            detection; selective generation; out-of-prompt detection; and calibration analysis, demon-
086            strating that UA-CLM significantly enhances the quality of uncertainty estimates in LLMs
087            in open-ended and free-form question-answering tasks. Notably, these enhancements in
088            uncertainty calibration are achieved without compromising the accuracy when compared
089            to standard CLM.
090          • In addition, we have also applied our proposed UA-CLM methodology to a large vision
091            language model (LVLM), demonstrating its efficacy in the open-ended visual question-
092            answering task. This extension of our work shows the versatility of UA-CLM in handling
093
               multimodal inputs and complex tasks beyond text-based question-answering, showcasing
               it’s utilty to wider range of applications.

097
098   2.1   U NCERTAINTY ESTIMATION IN NATURAL LANGUAGE GENERATION
099
      We refer to Abdar et al. (2021); Gawlikowski et al. (2023) for surveys on uncertainty quantification
100
      (UQ) in deep learning. In machine learning models, predictive uncertainty is composed of two
101
      primary sources: epistemic uncertainty, associated with model’s lack of knowledge and aleatoric
102   uncertainty - the inherent noise in the data or observation. As LLMs continue to evolve rapidly,
103   there is an increasing interest in enhancing our understanding of the uncertainty associated with
104   LLM responses for developing trustworthy and reliable systems (Fadeeva et al., 2023). UQ methods
105   from deep learning can be effectively applied to structured natural language processing tasks, such
106   as text classification (Xiao & Wang, 2019) and multiple-choice question answering (Kumar et al.,
107   2023). However, the application of these methods to free-form natural language generation presents
      distinct challenges.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      The landscape of UQ in free-form NLG is a dynamic field of research, where methodologies are
109   generally bifurcated into white-box (Fomicheva et al., 2020; Kuhn et al., 2023) and black-box ap-
110   proaches (Lin et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024). White-box methods necessitate access to the model’s
111   logits, or likelihood scores, or other internals of LLMs. Black-box methods rely solely on the analy-
112   sis of the text sequences generated by the LLMs. Recent work (Tian et al., 2023) explores prompting
113   techniques in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) language models to explicitly
114   elicit verbalized response confidence. In contrast, another line of research has explored unsupervised
115   methods for UQ from the models by utilizing confidence (Plaut et al., 2024), perplexity (Fomicheva
116   et al., 2020), token entropy (Malinin & Gales, 2021) and semantic entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023) to
117
      quantify uncertainty in LLM responses. Previous research (Minderer et al., 2021) has shown that
      confidence or entropy measures can be susceptible to poor calibration and may not fully reflect
118
      a model’s underlying uncertainties. Our work focuses on improving these uncertainty metrics in
119
      white-box settings by better calibrating the language models with uncertainty-aware finetuning.

      2.2   M ODEL C ALIBRATION
123
      Calibration is important in applications where decision-making relies on not just the model’s pre-
124   dictions, but also on the trustworthiness of its uncertainty scores. It is important to capture well-
125   calibrated uncertainty estimates of a model for creating reliable and trustworthy systems. Model
126   calibration is a well-explored area of research in deep learning, with a variety of strategies proposed
127   to enhance the calibration of deep neural networks for classification and regression tasks. These
128   strategies include post-hoc rescaling techniques (Guo et al., 2017; Kull et al., 2017), which adjust
129   the model’s predictions to better align with true event likelihoods; data augmentation, which en-
130   riches the training dataset to promote generalization (Thulasidasan et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al.,
131   2020); and probabilistic modeling approaches that integrate uncertainty directly into the model’s
132
      architecture (Blundell et al., 2015; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Other line of works utilize ex-
      plicit calibration loss functions during training to directly optimize for calibration (Kumar et al.,
133
      2018; Krishnan & Tickoo, 2020; Mukhoti et al., 2020; Karandikar et al., 2021), that has resulted in
134
      better calibrated models.
135
136   Calibration of LLMs for natural language processing tasks is an ongoing area of research. Prior
137   works have largely focused on refining LLMs for structured tasks like text classification (Kong
138
      et al., 2020) or multiple-choice question answering (Desai & Durrett, 2020; Jiang et al., 2021). Stud-
      ies like the one by Xiong et al. (2024) have highlighted that despite the impressive performance of
139
      foundational LLMs on a wide array of tasks, these models often exhibit poor calibration, particularly
140
      exhibiting overconfidence in their predictions. As LLMs are increasingly used in natural language
141   generation tasks, new calibration techniques (Geng et al., 2024) are emerging to enhance the relia-
142   bility of the generated text. More recently, Liu et al. (2024b) introduced a calibration technique for
143   LLMs that trains a single linear layer over the model’s last hidden layer representations to predict
144   a bias term, which is then used to adjust the model’s logits and alter the generation confidence for
145   short-form and long-form responses. Band et al. (2024) propose a training objective for linguistic
146   calibration, utilizing reinforcement learning to optimize and calibrate long-form text generations.
147   Kapoor et al. (2024) proposed a calibration tuning method designed for LLMs in multiple-choice
148   question-answering settings. Prior work by Liu et al. (2024b) has shown that standard fine-tuning
149
      of LLMs can lead to poorer calibration. The calibration of LLMs for free-form text generation, as
      well as uncertainty-aware fine-tuning, represents a significant open area of research. Our work ad-
150
      dresses this gap by developing an uncertainty-aware fine-tuning method of LLMs, fine-tuning less
151
      than 1% of the model parameters, to achieve well-calibrated models for free-form natural language
152   generation.

      2.3   F INE - TUNING L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
155
156   With the emergence of foundation models, fine-tuning have become a common practice in the field
157   of natural language processing, enabling the adaptation of general-purpose pre-trained models to
158   specialized tasks and domains. As fine-tuning a LLM with billions of parameters can be resource-
159   intensive, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) strategies have been proposed.
160   These parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques also mitigate catastrophic forgetting more effec-
161   tively in comparison to full fine-tuning (Wang et al., 2022). One approach is to update only a subset
      of the model’s parameters, such as adapter modules (Houlsby et al., 2019) or Low-Rank Adaptation


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), where only a small set of parameters are updated while the pre-trained
163   weights are frozen. Another strategy is prompt-based fine-tuning (Liu et al., 2023), where models
164   are conditioned on task-specific prompts to guide the text generation without model parameter up-
165   dates. We leverage LoRA strategy to illustrate our proposed uncertainty-aware finetuning in this
166   work.

      3    U NCERTAINTY- AWARE C AUSAL L ANGUAGE M ODELING
169
170
      Motivated by the need to overcome the challenges of uncertainty miscalibration (Xiong et al., 2024)
171
      in Large Language Models (LLMs) and the increasing trend of fine-tuning pre-trained foundational
172   models for domain-specific adaptation — where fine-tuned LLMs often exhibit overconfidence in
173   their predictions (Kong et al., 2020) — we propose a novel uncertainty calibration fine-tuning ap-
174   proach for natural language generation settings. We introduce a novel uncertainty-aware causal
175   language modeling loss based on the principles of decision theory (Murphy, 2012). Our fine-tuning
176   approach emphasizes increasing the uncertainty for wrong token predictions, while optimizing for
177   accuracy and certainty for correct token predictions. Decision theory offers a mathematical and
178   theoretical framework that guides to achieve optimal predictions by employing a task-specific util-
179   ity function. Within the decision theory framework, our task is to generate natural language text
180
      accompanied by reliable uncertainty estimates. The utility function in this scenario is represented
      by the uncertainty-aware optimization objective function that is aimed at producing well-calibrated
181
      uncertainty estimates for causal language modeling. We design a differentiable loss function that
182
      incentivizes the model to yield low uncertainty when it generates correct tokens, and encourages the
183   model to exhibit high uncertainty when it is at risk of predicting the next token incorrectly.
184
185   In causal language modeling, the goal is to predict the next token in a sequence given the previous
186
      tokens. Given a sequence of tokens [w1 , w2 , . . . , wT ], where T is the length of the sequence and each
      token wi is an element from a fixed vocabulary of size V, the model aims to learn the conditional
187
      probability distribution Pθ (wi |w0:i-1 ) for each token wi given the preceding set of tokens w0:i-1 ;
188
      where, θ represents the parameters of the LLM. The loss function for standard causal language
189   modeling (CLM) is typically the negative log-likelihood as defined in Equation 1 below.

195   where every correctly generated token is assigned low predictive uncertainty, and high predictive
196   probability, reflecting the model’s high confidence in its accuracy. Conversely, for every token that

      We perform extensive empirical evaluation to compare our proposed uncertainty-aware causal lan-
263
      guage modeling (UA-CLM) fine-tuning method to the standard causal language modeling (CLM)

268   hallucination detection, uncertainty-guided selective generation, out-of-domain prompt detection,
269   and calibration analysis based on the inverse correlation between the uncertainty estimates and the

297   evidenced by prior works from Kuhn et al. (2023), Lin et al. (2024), and Farquhar et al. (2024).

299
      (VQA) dataset to extend our evaluation to large vision language models (LVLMs), thereby pro-

      Additionally, we use BioASQ (Krithara et al., 2023), a biomedical question-answering dataset for

      the evaluation of out-of-domain prompt detection. In our experiments, we utilize the development

303   split of TriviaQA with around 10,000 question-answer pairs, and the validation split of OK-VQA,
304   comprising roughly 5,000 question-answer pairs along with their corresponding images. For each
305   dataset, we allocate 20% of the data for fine-tuning purposes, while the remaining 80% serve as the

      Models We use the Llama-2 models with 7B and 13B parameters (Touvron et al., 2023) and the
310   Gemma model with 2B parameters (Gemma et al., 2024) for the free-form QA experiments. Addi-
311   tionally, we utilize the LLaVA-1.5 model with 7B parameters (Liu et al., 2024a) for the open-ended
312   visual question-answering task.

      framework (Hu et al., 2022). The models undergo fine-tuning as described in Section 3 for the
316
      uncertainty-aware causal language modeling method. For comparative purposes, we also fine-tune
317
      models using the standard cross-entropy loss to evaluate against the standard causal language mod-
318   eling fine-tuning method. For all our experiments, the models are fine-tuned for a concise duration

      Out-of-domain detection In our experiments, we assess the model’s capability to identify whether
487   a given prompt is out-of-domain, referring to a question or input that falls outside the scope of
488   model’s trained knowledge base. To quantify this ability, we employ two widely recognized met-
489   rics: the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) and the Area Under the
490   Precision-Recall curve (AUPR) (Saito & Rehmsmeier, 2015). The Biomedical question-answering
491   (BioASQ) (Krithara et al., 2023) dataset serves as our out-of-domain dataset, while the Conver-
492   sational Question Answering (CoQA) dataset is used to represent in-domain data. We leverage
493   uncertainty metrics as a means to detect out-of-domain prompts effectively. The results, as detailed
494   in Table 3, demonstrate that our UA-CLM significantly outperforms the standard CLM in out-of-
495
      domain detection tasks. This performance is consistently observed across all four uncertainty met-
      rics employed in the study, with up to 16.5% improvement in AUROC and up to 15% improvement
496
      in AUPR scores.

      Generalization and long-form text generation We conducted experiments to evaluate the gen-
499
      eralizability of the proposed uncertainty-aware CLM fine-tuning method. Since we fine-tune the
500   model in a causal language modeling setup that involves next-token prediction, the learning should
501   be transferable, thereby encouraging generalizability. We evaluated the model fine-tuned with CoQA
502   dataset on TriviaQA dataset, and vice-versa. Additionally, to evaluate the generalization beyond QA
503   tasks, we conducted experiments for biography generation, a long-form paragraph-level generation
504   task, following the recent works (Liu et al., 2024b; Band et al., 2024). The Llama-2-7B models
505   fine-tuned with CLM and UA-CLM on CoQA were given prompts to write biographies of popu-
506   lar figures, whose names are sourced from BioGen (Min et al., 2023). The generated responses
507   were compared against those obtained from GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) using the same prompts,
508
      which served as the ground truth for evaluation. The results provided in the Table 4 show that the
      generated response quality of both UA-CLM and CLM is similar for biography generation. Addi-
509
      tionally, there is a significant improvement in calibration error and uncertainty quality, as quantified
510
      by hallucination detection AUROC, for UA-CLM.

513   5   D ISCUSSION
514
515   We proposed a novel fine-tuning approach to improve uncertainty calibration in Large Language
516   Models (LLMs) devised for natural language generation. Our method incorporates a differentiable
517
      uncertainty-aware causal language modeling loss, which is grounded in the principles of decision
      theory. This loss function is designed to enhance the model’s ability to provide well-calibrated
518
      uncertainty estimates, a crucial aspect of trustworthy AI models.
519
520   Our extensive empirical evaluations on open-ended and free-form question-answering tasks has
521   shown that the uncertainty-aware causal language modeling approach yield better-calibrated uncer-
522   tainty quantification, which in turn significantly enhances the model’s ability to detect hallucinations,
523
      identify out-of-domain prompts, and selective generation decisions. We demonstrated the general-
      izability of the proposed fine-tuning method to different text generation tasks. We also introduced a
524
      novel application of correlation analysis to the evaluation of sentence-level uncertainty calibration in
525
      free-form text generation, accounting for the varying sentence lengths between generated responses
526   and ground-truth references.
527
528   Limitations and Future work: Currently, the proposed method is tailored to white-box model
529
      settings, where the model internals are accessible for calibration fine-tuning. However, there is a
      potential to extend uncertainty-aware fine-tuning to black-box models by calibrating an auxiliary
530
      model, or prompt tuning for calibrated uncertainty quantification. Additionally, the focus of this
531
      work has been on calibrating token-level uncertainty, which sets the stage for the exploration of
532   calibrating sentence-level uncertainty. We plan to explore these two avenues in our future work. We
533   hope this work opens new avenues for the research community to enhance the uncertainty calibration
534   in LLMs for free-form natural language generation.
535
      In conclusion, this work contributes towards the broader goal of developing trustworthy LLMs. The
536
      ability to recognize out-of-domain prompts and to acknowledge the limits of a model’s knowledge
537
      base through reliable uncertainty quantification paves the way for reducing hallucinations and en-
538   hancing decision-making in AI systems.

      A.1.3     F INETUNING HYPERPARAMETERS AND I MPLEMENTATION
852   We fine-tune our models for all experiments for 3 epochs using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) with AdamW
853   optmizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). We use an initial learning rate of 1e-4, weight decay of 0.001
854   and a warm up ratio of 0.03. In our experiments we used Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to efficiently
855   fine-tune pre-trained LLMs and LVLMs for the causal language modeling task. For LLMs, we set
856   the LoRA rank as 32, alpha parameter as 64 and a dropout of 0.1. LoRA was applied specifically
857   to the following modules: q proj, k proj, v proj, up proj, and down proj. In addition to LoRA,
858   we applied 4-bit normalized float (nf4) quantization to the model’s parameters and utilized FP16
859   precision during fine-tuning to reduce the computational overhead.
860   For inference, we utilized FP16 precision and the default greedy decoding provided by Hugging
861   Face with temperature value T=0.3. The predictive entropy and semantic entropy are estimated by
862   generating 5 stochastic sequences from the model, each obtained through temperature sampling with
863   a temperature setting of T=0.3.This temperature was chosen to obtain optimal uncertainty estimates
      balanced with high quality generated text, based on the ablation study shown in Figure 6. Our source

882   A.2      T EXT GENERATION QUALITY METRICS
883
884             • ROUGE-L (Lin & Och, 2004): Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation
885               (ROUGE) is a widely-used evaluation metric for assessing the quality of text generated
886               based on n-gram matching. We use the Rouge-L variant which uses the longest common
887               subsequence between the generated answer and the ground truth answer.
888             • Exact Match (EM): Exact Match (EM) metric is a stringent evaluation criterion used
889               to assess the performance of models on tasks such as question answering (QA), where a
890               generated response is compared to a reference answer. It is a widely used metric for open-
891               book QA, this metric evaluates a model’s ability to extract the precise text span from the
892
                  context to answer a question.
893             • Accuracy: The generated answer is considered as accurate if it achieves Rouge-L(y, ŷ) >
894               0.3, for a given reference answer y and a model generation ŷ. We follow this criterion for
895               quantifying accuracy in free-form text generation based on the findings from (Kuhn et al.,
896
                  2023) that demonstrated this criterion closely matches the human evaluation accuracy on
                  COQA and TriviaQA datasets, both of which are utilized in our experiments.
897
898             • BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020): BERTScore utilizes word embeddings to compute a
899
                  similarity score between the tokens in the prediction and ground truth and has shown to
                  well correlate with human judgement. We report Precision, Recall and F1 BERTScores for
900
                  all our experiments.
