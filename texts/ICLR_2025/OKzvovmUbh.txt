009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, advanced image editing and generation methods have rapidly
012            evolved, making detecting and locating forged image content increasingly chal-
013            lenging. Most existing image forgery detection methods rely on identifying the
014            edited traces left in the image. However, because the traces of different forgeries
015            are distinct, these methods can identify familiar forgeries included in the training
016            data but struggle to handle unseen ones. In response, we present an approach for
017            Generalizable Image Forgery Localization (GIFL). Once trained, our model can
018
               detect both seen and unseen forgeries, providing a more practical and efficient
               solution to counter false information in the era of generative AI. Our method fo-
019
               cuses on learning general features from the pristine content rather than traces of
020
               specific forgeries, which are relatively consistent across different types of forg-
021            eries and therefore can be used as universal features to locate unseen forgeries.
022            Additionally, as existing image forgery datasets are still dominated by traditional
023            hand-crafted forgeries, we construct a new dataset consisting of images edited
024            by various popular deep generative image editing methods to further encourage
025            research in detecting images manipulated by deep generative models. Extensive
026            experimental results show that the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art
027            methods in the detection of unseen forgeries and also demonstrates competitive
028            results for seen forgeries.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Driven by the success of deep-generative models Goodfellow et al. (2020); Karras et al. (2019);
      Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022), AI-based image manipulation tools have enabled realis-
034
      tic editing through simple interactions such as masks, sketches, and prompts Zeng et al. (2022);
035
      Rombach et al. (2022); Xie et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2023); Epstein et al. (2023) that traditionally
036   require sophisticated skills and tedious manual operations. Although they have undoubtedly brought
037   numerous benefits, their widespread adoption has raised concerns about the credibility and trustwor-
038   thiness of visual content. Consequently, forgery image detection and localization has emerged as an
039   important research problem.
040
      In recent years, a lot of effort has been made and a large number of methods have been proposed.
041
      Traditional methods are typically designed manually based on the observed artifacts of manipulated
042   images such as noise patterns, JPEG artifacts, lens aberration, camera response function, color filter
043   array Mahdian & Saic (2009); Amerini et al. (2011); Ferrara et al. (2012); Siwei et al. (2014); Mc-
044   Closkey & Albright (2018; 2019); Nikoukhah et al. (2019); Nataraj et al. (2019). Their applications
045   are usually limited to the type of editing that produces the specific artifacts. In response, learning-
046   based detection methods have been proposed to detect a wider range of forgeries by training a model
047   on a large dataset of diverse forged images Wu et al. (2019); Dong et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022).
048   Although learning-based approaches achieved excellent performance compared to traditional meth-
049   ods, their generalizability is still largely limited. They can accurately detect the types of forgeries
050   included in training data, but often struggle to identify new forgeries to which they have not been
051   exposed. In addition, the training datasets used in existing work are usually outdated and are still
052   oriented towards basic manipulation techniques like splicing and copy-and-paste, despite that so-
053   phisticated editing methods powered by deep generative models have been widely applied nowa-
      days. To detect image forgeries in practical applications, it is crucial to consider the use cases of


                                                        1

054
      detecting forgeries produced by deep generative models and emphasize the generalization to unseen
055   forgeries.
056
057   To this end, we investigate the generalizability of state-of-the-art forgery detection methods and
058
      present a comprehensive recipe for generalizable forgery detection in the deep learning era. First,
      we propose a new paradigm with a universal forgery detection network, which can generalize to
059
      unseen forgeries. We found that the inefficacy of existing methods in detecting unseen forgery
060
      is due to their heavy reliance on specific forgery traces of forged content. Since different editing
061   methods may leave distinct traces, a model that looks for this trace can only recognize the type of
062   forgery included in the training data.
063
064
      Therefore, we encourage our detector to be more inclined towards learning and utilizing authentic
      image features in the pristine areas instead, which are more consistent across different types of
065
      forgeries and can be used as universal features to learn a generalizable detector. More specifically,
066
      our method supervises the encoder feature using features from masked images in which the forged
067   areas are removed. The decoder then utilizes these features in conjunction with other general features
068   to produce the localization mask.
069
070
      Second, to encourage future research on the detection of advanced image forgeries, we create a
      new forged image dataset, Forgery ADE. This dataset consists of a total of 177,680 images partially
071
      manipulated using eight popular state-of-the-art image editing and generation methods based on
072
      GANs and diffusion models. For each original image, we create different variants of forged images
073   applying different types of forgeries in the same area. This makes it easier to study the effect of dif-
074   ferent types of training forgery on test-time detection robustness and compare detection performance
075   between different types of forgery.
076
      Furthermore, we study and addressed several pervasive data-related issues such as the influence
077
      of semantic correlation between forgery and image content, false positives on authentic images,
078
      the influence of the number of training forgery types on the model’s generalization ability, and the
079   impact of masking, a common post-processing step for deep learning based image editing methods,
080   on forgery detection performance and generalization.
081
082
      We summarize our contributions as follows:
083
             • We proposed a generalizable image forgery detection and localization method capable of
084
               detecting unseen forgeries, while also showing competitive performance compared to ex-
085            isting methods in detecting seen forgeries.
086
087
             • We construct a new image forgery dataset using various advanced image manipulation
               and generation methods, which is more suitable for universal and cross-method forgery
088
               detection research than most existing datasets.
089
090          • We addressed several data-related issues in existing image forgery detection and localiza-
091            tion methods by introducing a comprehensive training data configuration.

      Many methods have been proposed to assess the authenticity of images McCloskey & Albright
096   (2018); Marra et al. (2018); Rossler et al. (2019); Nataraj et al. (2019); McCloskey & Albright
097   (2019); Li et al. (2020). However, these approaches frequently exhibit unsatisfactory performance
098   when applied to images forged by unseen editing methods. Some attempts have been made to
099   address this challenge. Wang et al. (2020) demonstrated that GAN-based generated images share
100   some common systematic flaws, which can be distinguished from real images by classifiers trained
101   on a specific generator. Zeng et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2019); Frank et al. (2020) found that
102   the GAN-based image generation method is more likely to expose common artifacts in the spectral
103   domain, which can be used to distinguish them from authentic images. Wang et al. (2023) tries to
104
      identify the images generated by the diffusion model Ho et al. (2020) based on reconstruction errors
      of a pre-trained diffusion model. However, these methods can only determine the authenticity of the
105
      entire image and cannot locate the forged areas in the image.
106
107   Some work focuses on locating specific forged regions in images by modeling it as a pixel-level
      binary classification problem. Liu et al. (2022); Dong et al. (2022) utilize the learned multi-scale

141   3.1   L EARNING U NIVERSAL F EATURES FOR G ENERALIZABLE F ORGERY L OCALIZATION
142
143   A forged image consists of two parts: the authentic part and the forged part. Given a forged image
144
      I, existing approaches use a classifier C to perform pixel-level binary classification. The goal of the
      classifier is to assign different labels, e.g. 0, 1, to pixels in the two parts, and produce a binary mask
145
      Md :
146
                                                      Md = C(I).
147
      Through learning on a large dataset of images altered by various editing methods, a powerful deep
148
      neural network classifier can learn to recognize manipulation traces and detect many types of forg-
149
      eries. However, these classifiers often rely heavily on the specific traces left by forgery methods
150   in the forged content, making it challenging to detect novel forgeries that have not been encoun-
151   tered. This is a critical issue because image editing methods evolve rapidly and new techniques are
152   constantly being developed.
153
      In this work, we aim to encourage the detector to pay more attention to the general information
154
      shared among different types of forgeries rather than specific forgery traces to develop a more gen-
155
      eralizable approach. We observe that while forged parts may have distinct traces due to various
156   editing methods, authentic regions remain relatively consistent. Therefore, we propose to recon-
157   struct the features of the authentic area that are devoid of forged content to make the model R more
158   inclined to learn and utilize the authentic information:
159
                                                    Fr = R(FI )
160
161   where Fr denotes the reconstructed authentic feature and FI is the entire image feature encoded by
      a frozen encoder.

      Fig. 1 illustrates the overall pipeline of most existing approaches and the proposed method. In
176   essence, we transform the forgery localization task from a binary classification task that distin-
177   guishes between real and forged content to a regression task centered on authentic feature recon-
178   struction. Using the wider metric space of a regression process, more useful information can be
179   retained and used to achieve better performance and robustness.
180
      Moreover, we decouple the detection process from the encoding and decoding process inspired
181
      by Chen et al. (2024). We utilize the same frozen encoder E to encode the features of both the input
182   image and the partial images where the forgery areas are removed:
183
184                                                FI = E(I)
185
186
                                            Ft = E(I ⊙ (1 − Mt ))
187   where ⊙ represents element-wise multiplication. We then utilize a Universal Forgery Localization
188   Transformer (UFLT), which will be detailed in Sec. 3.2, to align the features of the forged image with
189   those of the partial image. This alignment reduces the gap between encoded features of different
190   forgeries, leading to improved generalization ability.
191   Finally, a fully connected layer is used as a decoder to obtain the final detection mask based on the
192   reconstructed features:
193                                               Md = F C(Fr )
194
195   The loss function of our method comprises two parts: utilizing L2 loss to guide the regressor’s
196   feature reconstruction and alignment with input features, and supervising the final mask output
197   through a combination of BCE loss and IoU loss Zhou et al. (2019):
198
                          L = L2 (Fr , Ft ) × 10 + LBCE (Md , Mt ) + LIoU (Md , Mt )

      3.2   U NIVERSAL F ORGERY L OCALIZATION T RANSFORMER
202
      It has been pointed out that the spectral feature traces of different types of forgery may appear in
203
      different depth of a network Zeng et al. (2017); Frank et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2019); Xu et al.
204   (2019). However, existing spectral detection networks Kwon et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022a);
205   Zhou et al. (2024) typically use a manually designed feature extractor to obtain traces from a spe-
206   cific layer through a single spectral transform and process the spectral features as another branch
207   independent of the spatial domain pipeline, which means that these methods can only extract the
208   spectral traces of some specific forgeries. Moreover, their single spectral transform and independent
209   branch processing result in the isolation of spectral information during intermediate processing, with
210   no interaction with spatial information before the inverse transform.
211   To this end, we propose a Universal Forgery Localization Transformer (UFLT), as depicted in Fig.
212   2. To effectively exploit the traces exposed at different depths in different domains, we draw inspi-
213   ration from FFC Chi et al. (2020) and establish the interaction and fusion of features across domain
214   and across depth by connecting the paths of the two domains in each layer of the network. The
215   transformer encoder and feedforward network architectures are identical to ViT Dosovitskiy et al.
      (2020).


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      In contrast to FFC, which utilizes an image-level Fourier transform to capture global information,
217   our approach focuses on leveraging local spectral domain information along with spatial positional
218   relationships to extract local information. To ensure compatibility with the vision transformer net-
219   work and make full use of its global self-attention capabilities for establishing long-distance corre-
220   lations, UFLT performs the Fourier transform on the patch embedding scale. Specifically, letting
221   Xpix ∈ RN ×D be the input feature, where N and D are the number and dimension of embeddings,
222   we apply a 2-D fast Fourier transform (FFT) to embeddings, and then concatenate the imaginary
223   and real parts of the spectral features to produce Xf req ∈ RN ×(D×2) . Due to the redundancy in
224   the conjugate symmetric signal obtained from the 2-D FFT, we can reduce the dimension of Xf req
225   without losing essential information. Therefore, we transform Xf req into a D dimensional feature
226
      through a fully connected layer to ensure that its shape aligns with that of Xpix , allowing the vision
      transformer to use features of the spectral domain.

229   4     F ORGERY ADE DATASET
230
231   Diversifying the type of forgeries in training is an effective way to enhance the generalizability of
232   forgery detectors Wang et al. (2020). However, most of the existing forgery image detection and
233   localization datasets Kwon et al. (2022); Dong et al. (2013); De Carvalho et al. (2013); Ng et al.
234   (2004); Wen et al. (2016) lack diversity and are still dominated by images produced using manual
235
      slicing. These outdated and diversity limited training datasets are not suitable for contemporary deep
      learning based image manipulation methods.
236
237   Although some recently developed datasets have included deep learning base forgeries, they still
238   have several critical issues. First, in most datasets Wen et al. (2016); Guillaro et al. (2023); Jia
239   et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023), the location, shape, and contents of the forged areas are determined
240   based on the semantics of the image. However, in practical applications, forgery may appear in
241
      any shape in any region of the image and may be unrelated to semantics. Therefore, training on
      these forgery images with semantic connections can lead detectors to rely on semantics to detect the
242
      forgery, resulting in reduced performance on forgeries without semantic connections. Second, most
243
      existing datasets only include forged images and lack authentic images as negative samples. As a
244   result, false positives often occur when the input is a clean image without any forgery VidalMata
245   et al. (2023). Moreover, we found that many existing models learned a shortcut to detecting deep
246   learning based forgeries by detecting the seam between the manipulated area and the pristine area.
247   This is because most deep-generative inpainting models have a post-processing step that blends the
248   model output with the original image using the inpainting mask. We present a detailed study of these
249   issues in Section 5.
250   To this end, we create a new forged image dataset based on the ADE 20K Zhou et al. (2017) dataset,
251   called Forgery ADE. Examples of forged images generated by different forgery methods in the
252   dataset are shown in Fig. 4.
253
      We select eight of the most popular and representative deep learning based image inpainting methods
254
      as forgery approaches, including 4 GAN-based methods: Deepfill v2 Yu et al. (2019), CTSDG Guo
255
      et al. (2021), CR-Fill Zeng et al. (2021), LaMa Suvorov et al. (2022), and 4 diffusion-based methods:
256   LDM Rombach et al. (2022), SSDE Song et al. (2020), DDNM Wang et al. (2022b), RePaint Lug-
257   mayr et al. (2022). Each of these methods is applied to all 20,210 training images and 2,000 test
258   images in the ADE 20K, producing eight sets of forgery images for a total of 177,680 images. We
259   carefully selected a set of irregular occlusion masks provided by Zeng et al. (2021) that are not re-
260   lated to the image and randomly rotated and flipped them to increase the diversity of the data and
261   further avoid semantic association. We scale all images and masks to 512 × 512. All forged im-
262   ages are the direct output of the generated model without post-processing masking. In training, we
263   provide authentic images as negative samples, of which the ground truth is all zero maps.

529
530
      In this paper, we study the localization and detection of universal image forgeries. We propose a
      generalizable image forgery localization method and an efficient and robust dual-domain network.
531
      Our research emphasizes that focusing on the learning of authentic image features in pristine areas
532
      can be a generalizable way for forgery localization. Extensive experimental results indicate that our
533   method outperforms state-of-the-art methods in locating uncounted image forgeries by a large mar-
534   gin and also shows competitive performance on the seen forgeries. Furthermore, we construct an
535   image forgery dataset containing images edited by various advanced deep generative image editing
536   methods and introduce a comprehensive training data configuration to address several data-related
537   issues in universal image forgery detection and localization. Our training strategy and dataset con-
538   figurations are independent of the model and can be applied to improve existing methods. It is worth
539   noting that our improvement in generalization ability comes at the cost of a slight performance drop
      on seen forgeries, which can be an interesting problem to study in future work.

      A.1   V ISUALIZATION OF F EATURE A LIGNMENT
759
760   In order to visually verify the effectiveness of feature alignment, we train a fully connected layer as
761   a decoder on the encoded features FI , and decode the reconstructed features Fr and target features
762   Ft into RGB images for observation, as shown in Fig. 6.
763   We can observe that both the reconstructed and target features have semantic and spatial structures
764   similar to the input image after decoding, and the authentic content is reconstructed while the forged
765   content is excluded. This confirms that the authentic features in different forged images have a high
766   degree of consistency and are in the same feature space as the input features.
767
768   A.2   I MPACT OF I MAGE Q UALITY D EGRADATION
769
770   We apply a series of image degradation methods to the forged images, including JPEG compression
771   with a quality factor of 25, downsampling and upsampling back with scale of 2, sharpening, mean
772   blur with a kernel size of 7, motion blur with a kernel size of 7 and a random direction, gamma
773
      transform with a factor of 1.5, and ISO noise with a color shift factor of 0.05 and intensity factor
      of 0.8. We detect forged images with degraded images using the GIFL baseline, and the results
774
      are shown in Table 8. It can be seen that various image degradations are not conducive to forgery
775
      detection and may harm detection performance.
