011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Hyperparameter optimisation (HPO) of machine learning models is crucial for
014            achieving optimal performance for different tasks. Surrogate-based optimisation
015            techniques, such as Bayesian optimisation (BO), have been successfully applied
016            to tackle this problem. BO is subject to different design choices of its components.
017            In particular, depending on the nature and the size of the search space, the choice
018
               of the surrogate model has a substantial impact on the overall performance of BO.
               Surrogate models in BO approximate the function to optimise and guide the search
019
               towards promising regions by predicting the function value for different solution
020
               candidates. Combining different machine learning (ML) models is known to lead
021            to performance gains, e.g., in different prediction tasks. To this end, we propose a
022            novel dynamic approach to ensemble surrogate models in the BO pipeline, lever-
023            aging the complementary powers of different surrogate models at different stages
024            of the optimisation process. We empirically evaluate our method on numerous
025            benchmarks and demonstrate its advantage compared to state-of-the-art single-
026            surrogate BO baselines. We highlight the usefulness of our approach in finding
027            good hyperparameter configurations in mixed (numerical and categorical) search
028            spaces for a wide range of problems.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The performance of machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) models crucially depends on
034   how their hyperparameters are tuned (Lavesson & Davidsson, 2006; Bischl et al., 2023). Tuning
035   the hyperparameters in order to achieve peak performance of the model on a specific task is chal-
036   lenging even for experts. This process is often addressed through trial-and-error methods, requiring
037   significant effort and resources. Hyperparameter optimisation (HPO) techniques alleviate this bur-
038   den by automatically searching for well-performing hyperparameter configurations, removing the
039
      need for human intervention (Snoek et al., 2012). While automated HPO techniques have shown
      great potential for classical ML models (Snoek et al., 2012; Feurer et al., 2022), they are not as eas-
040
      ily applicable to more complex ML and DL domains, where evaluating a single configuration of a
041
      model can be very expensive (Brown et al., 2020). Moreover, due to the lack of access to an explicit
042   problem formulation, HPO is handled as a black-box problem. Consequently, all automated HPO
043   techniques rely on the only available information about the problem, i.e., evaluating the quality of
044   candidate configurations, to steer the search towards the most promising regions of the search space
045   and a good estimate of the global optimum.
046
      Bayesian optimisation (BO) (Močkus, 2012; Garnett, 2023; Frazier, 2018) is a surrogate-based,
047
      sample-efficient approach for global optimisation of expensive-to-evaluate black-box problems. BO
048   is particularly well-suited for settings with a very limited budget of available evaluations (relative to
049   the size of the search space), such as HPO. Various BO-based HPO techniques have been developed
050   and successfully applied to this end; a prominent example is HEBO (Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022),
051   the winner of the NeurIPS BBO competition (Turner et al., 2020). One of the key components of
052   BO is a probabilistic surrogate model, which is built on an initial sample of solution candidates to
053   approximate the objective function while also quantifying its uncertainty. At each iteration, a new
      solution candidate is selected to be evaluated next by means of maximising an acquisition function

079   Fitting the surrogate model to the observed data is a prediction task that often involves probabilistic
080   models. BO then uses these probabilistic predictions of function values across the search space to
081
      guide the search towards a good estimate of the global optimal solution. The choice of the surro-
      gate model thus strongly affects BO performance and is often linked to the dimensionality of the
082
      problem at hand and the nature of its search space. For continuous search spaces, Gaussian pro-
083
      cesses (GPs) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) are the most widely adopted surrogate model and tend
084   to be particularly effective on low-dimensional problems, involving up to approximately 20 vari-
085   ables (Eggensperger et al., 2013). On the other hand, random forests (RFs) (Breiman, 2001) as
086   surrogate models natively support discrete and conditional search spaces, and tend to excel in higher
087   problem dimensionalities, where GPs generally do not work well (Eggensperger et al., 2013; Jenat-
088   ton et al., 2017; Li et al., 2017). For complex HPO tasks in mixed domains, with numerical, ordinal
089   and categorical hyperparameters, it is highly desirable to leverage the complementary strengths of
090   inherently different surrogate models. One natural way to achieve this is via ensemble methods,
091   which have in practice demonstrated their versatility in the context of BO-based HPO techniques for
092
      treating a wide range of heterogeneous problems (Turner et al., 2020; Hoffman et al., 2011).
093   We propose a novel approach to enhance BO-based HPO performance by dynamically ensembling
094   surrogate models during the optimisation process. Our approach, which we dub DensBO, is based
095   on a weighted combination of multiple surrogate models, assigning the largest weight to the surro-
096   gate model with the highest accuracy on newly observed points in each iteration and updating the
097   weights via exponential moving average (see Figure 1 for details).
098   We present, for the first time, a dynamic ensembling approach for surrogate models in the context
099   of HPO. This poses a significant challenge, as there is a trade-off between the time needed for
100   finding the best hyperparameter configuration and the time complexity of target function evaluations
101   (i.e., if the optimiser requires more time to determine the next configurations to sample, there is
102   naturally less time available for evaluating the target function). In the context of HPO, evaluating the
103   target function means evaluating the performance of a given ML model for a given hyperparameter
104
      configuration on a given dataset. Ensembling approaches in general require training and querying
      more than one surrogate model, which leads to more time required for the optimisation phase.
105
106   We assess the effectiveness of the DensBO approach on various HPO tasks involving numerous
107   datasets and machine learning models. We compare it to several single-surrogate-based BO and a
      simple, static ensemble, and we show that our dynamic ensembling method outperforms all of them

108
      on both cheap and expensive functions. We provide the code, instructions for reproducibility, as
109   well as all figures in the Appendix C.

138   2.2   BAYESIAN OPTIMISATION
139
140
      Bayesian optimisation (BO) (Močkus, 2012; Frazier, 2018; Garnett, 2023) is a family of surrogate-
      based algorithms for efficient global optimisation of black-box problems. A typical BO pipeline
141
      consists of three main modules: an initial design, i.e., in HPO, a set of hyperparameter configuration
142
      candidates λ = (λ(1) , . . . , λ(r) ) and their evaluations c(λ) = (c(λ(1) ), . . . , c(λ(r) )); a surrogate
143
      model (fitted to the initial observations) that returns an approximation ĉ(λ) of the unknown loss
144   function c(λ) while capturing the uncertainty in the prediction σ̂(λ) on unobserved points in the
145   search space; and an acquisition function, which is optimised to suggest solution candidates to be
146   evaluated next, usually balancing exploration and exploitation of the search space. To approxi-
147   mate the expensive objective function, BO typically employs a Gaussian process (GP) model as
148   the surrogate. The Gaussian process model defines a distribution over functions on the configura-
149   tion space c(λ) ∼ GP c (µ(λ), k(λ, λ′ )), where µ(·) is a mean function and k(·, ·) is a covariance
150   function. If we consider an observation model yi = c(λ(i) ) + εi with normally distributed noise,
151   εi = N (0, σε2 ), the predicted value by the Gaussian process model at one unknown configuration
152   λ will also follow a normal distribution with mean µ(λ) = Kλ,λ (Kλ,λ + σε2 I)−1 y and variance
153   σ 2 (λ) = k(λ, λ) − Kλ,λ (Kλ,λ + σε2 I)−1 Kλ,λ , where y = (y1 , . . . , yr ) is the vector of observa-
154   tions, Kλ,λ = [k(λ(i) , λ(j) )]λ(i) ,λ(j) ∈λ is the covariance matrix, and Kλ,λ = [k(λ(i) , λ)]λ(i) ∈λ is
155   the correlation vector for all samples. GPs as a surrogate model inherently provide both the mean
156
      and the variance vector. However, GPs are not the only surrogate model used in BO. Another com-
      mon choice for a surrogate model are tree-based models, such as random forests. Tree-based models
157
      traditionally predict only the mean of the given data (e.g., in a regression setting). In this case, the
158
      variance is defined based on the variance of the predictions of the leaves (Hutter et al., 2011). We
159   calculate the mean µ(λ) and variance σ(λ) for a set of trees T as follows:

166   BO proceeds iteratively until a termination criterion is met. In each iteration, it optimises the acqui-
167   sition function by repeatedly querying the surrogate model to generate a pool of solution candidates.
168   It then evaluates the most high-potential solutions (i.e., the solutions that maximise the acquisition
169   function) from this pool, refines the surrogate model based on the new observations, and updates
170   the optimum if the new point improves upon the true function value of the best observation so far.
171   Among the many variants of BO from the literature, in this work, we focus on the state-of-the-
172   art method for HPO to empirically evaluate our ensembling method: HEBO (Cowen-Rivers et al.,
173   2022).

175   2.3   DYNAMIC COMPONENT SELECTION IN BAYESIAN OPTIMISATION
176
      As a modular framework, BO performance is highly sensitive to design choices of its modules. Dif-
177
      ferent sampling strategies for the initial design, such as Latin hypercube sampling (McKay et al.,
178
      2000), low-discrepancy sequences (e.g., Sobol (Antonov & Saleev, 1979)) or random uniform sam-
179   pling; different surrogate models, such as GPs or RFs; and different acquisition functions (AFs),
180   such as expected improvement (EI) (Močkus, 1975), probability of improvement (PI) (Kushner,
181   1964) or upper confidence bound (UCB) (Forrester et al., 2008) – all affect the overall BO perfor-
182   mance to various degrees (Bossek et al., 2020; Lindauer et al., 2019; Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022).
183   Despite few works showing the potential of automated selection of components (Ben Salem &
184   Tomaso, 2018; Benjamins et al., 2022a;b), the settings for each component are typically chosen
185   by practitioners beforehand depending on the desired use-case, and are fixed for the entire optimisa-
186   tion procedure. However, there have been efforts to show that the dynamic choices of BO modules
187
      lead to performance gains across multiple contexts. Prior attempts to investigate the dynamic ad-
      justment of AFs include works on mixed AF strategies, e.g., a self-adjusting AF approach to balance
188
      the exploration-exploitation trade-off (Benjamins et al., 2023), an online multi-armed bandit strat-
189
      egy on a portfolio of AFs (Hoffman et al., 2011), or an online update of weights in a portfolio of
190   AFs (Kandasamy et al., 2020). When it comes to dynamic adjustment of surrogate models, several
191   directions have been investigated, notably an online selection of surrogate models based on their
192   ranking in each BO iteration (Bagheri et al., 2016), adaptive global surrogate modelling via genetic
193   algorithm-driven sampling (Gorissen et al., 2009), or adaptive combining of surrogates based on
194   crowding distance trust regions (Zhang et al., 2012). It has also been shown that dynamic compo-
195   nent selection in general is beneficial in terms of performance in other related areas, e.g., in algorithm
196   configuration (Biedenkapp et al., 2020), evolutionary computation (Karafotias et al., 2015; Doerr &
197   Doerr, 2020), planning (Speck et al., 2021), and deep learning (Adriaensen et al., 2022).
198
199   2.4   E NSEMBLES IN BAYESIAN OPTIMISATION
200
201
      Ensembles of ML models have been shown to outperform single models for a wide range of use-
      cases (Sagi & Rokach, 2018; Opitz & Maclin, 1999; Rokach, 2010; Dong et al., 2020). Conse-
202
      quently, using ensembles in the context of BO is not a new idea. A series of works has demonstrated
203
      the advantage of using ensembles, most notably in engineering (Jiang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2011).
204   Various ensembling strategies have been investigated, e.g., optimal weighting of surrogates trained
205   on existing observations (Hanse et al., 2022) or on extracted features (Guo et al., 2019), or opti-
206   mising multiple AFs on multiple surrogates and combining them accordingly (Huang et al., 2022;
207   Beaucaire et al., 2019). However, all these works consider a static (i.e., global) ensemble construc-
208   tion which is then used throughout the entire optimisation. In contrast to this, our method operates
209   in a dynamic fashion, tracking the accuracy of surrogates and adjusting the ensemble on-the-fly.
210   Dynamic model ensembles have been used with time-series data (Liu, 2023), or for approximating
211   both high- and low-fidelity data during the BO procedure by combining two GP regression mod-
212   els (Liu, 2020); they have also been considered in a non-BO context, e.g., for neural decoding in
213   brain-computer interfaces (Qi et al., 2019). However, these approaches substantially differ from
214   our proposed methodology, as none of them considers the use of exponential moving average to
215   capture the history of model performance, nor a weighting scheme that is based on the accuracy
      on the newly sampled points of the surrogate models trained on past observations. Furthermore,


                                                          4
216
      we consider ensembles of regression models from inherently different families. To the best of our
217   knowledge, dynamic surrogate ensembles have so far not been applied in the context of HPO. We
218   thus not only consider a new use-case, but an entirely different challenge compared to other real-
219   world applications, due to a key trade-off between the budget for evaluating the target function and
220   the budget for finding the optimal hyperparameter configuration.

299   4   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
300
301   We conducted a range of experiments to assess the performance of DensBO in various HPO settings
302   compared to single-surrogate baselines and a static ensemble. In particular, we investigated how
303   DensBO performs in light of the trade-off between the time for evaluating the target function and
304   the time for finding the optimal hyperparameter configuration. We implemented our method in
305   HEBO (Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022), a state-of-the-art HPO framework (Eggensperger et al., 2021).
306   HEBO is a BO-based optimiser that includes several advancements to improve performance. It
307   applies a power transformation to the performance data and the Kumaraswamy transformation to
308
      the input data to tackle heteroscedasticity and non-stationarity. Cowen-Rivers et al. (2022) showed
      that these transformations improve the performance of Gaussian processes on performance data.
309
      HEBO samples new solution candidates by maximising a multi-objective acquisition function that
310
      consists of EI, PI, and upper confidence bound (UCB) (Forrester et al., 2008).
311
312   We considered four classes of surrogate models to construct the ensemble: Gaussian processes and
313   three tree-based models, namely random forest (RF) (Breiman, 2001), extremely randomised trees
314
      (ET) (Geurts et al., 2006) and gradient boosting (GB) (Friedman, 2001). For GP, we used its native
      implementation from HEBO. For tree-based models, we used the scikit-learn implementation
315
      of RF, ET and GB. For details on these methods, refer to Section C.3.
316
317   As GPs are preferable for scenarios with continuous hyperparameters and RFs better handle discrete
318   (and even mixed and conditional) domains (Eggensperger et al., 2013), we initialised the weights in
319   the following manner: if the problem instance contains only continuous hyperparameters, we assign
320
      a weight of 1 to Gaussian process (w0,GP = 1) and 0 to all other surrogate models; otherwise, we
      assign a weight of 1 to RF (w0,RF = 1) and 0 to all other surrogate models.
321
322   We used four values of the smoothing factor α of the exponential moving average for the dynamic
323   ensemble construction: α ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 0.9, 1.0}. The lower the α, the lower the impact of the new
      weights in each iteration, i.e., the higher the impact of the history of the weights. This particular


                                                       6
324
      choice thus reflects low, medium and high memory and a selection-only mode when α = 1. In the
325   latter case, we select only one model – the one with the highest accuracy on the newly sampled
326   configurations from the last iteration – rather than construct a weighted ensemble.
327
328
      In each iteration, the models with null weights are dynamically pruned, i.e., not considered at in-
      ference time. In the case of α = 1, only the model achieving the highest accuracy on the newly
329
      sampled solutions is assigned a weight of w = 1, while all other models receive w = 0. Thus,
330
      α = 1 performs model selection rather than ensembling, as no historical information is used. The
331   α = 1 scenario leads to the shortest running time, due to the fact that in acquisition function optimi-
332   sation having fewer models with non-zero weights reduces inference time, since fewer models need
333   to be accessed altogether.

      4.1   B ENCHMARKS AND BASELINES
336
337   We empirically evaluated our approach on two benchmark suites, YAHPO Gym (Pfisterer et al.,
338   2022) and JAHS-Bench-201 (Bansal et al., 2022). YAHPO Gym is a surrogate-based benchmark for
339   hyperparameter optimisation, consisting of 15 scenarios (i.e., machine learning pipelines and their
340   configuration space) on various datasets. In particular, it contains LCBench (Zimmer et al., 2021),
341   which is used to optimise neural networks on tabular data, combined algorithm selection and hy-
342   perparameter optimisation (CASH) on OpenML datasets (Binder et al., 2020; Falkner et al., 2018),
343
      as well as the NAS Bench 301 (Zela et al., 2022). From YAHPO Gym, we used all 856 available
      instances. JAHS-Bench-201 is a surrogate-based benchmark for optimisation of the architecture and
344
      hyperparameters of convolutional neural network on image datasets. From JAHS, we used all three
345
      available instances, each optimising the architecture for a different dataset. Both YAHPO and JAHS
346   contain mixed-type configuration spaces. For further experimental and implementation details, refer
347   to Appendix C.
348
349
      We used single-surrogate BO variants within HEBO with each of the surrogate models considered
      (GP, RF, ET, and GB) as baselines to compare our dynamic ensembling approach. Furthermore, we
350
      compared against a simple static ensemble which always assigns an equal weight to all models.

353   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL PROTOCOL
354
      We used the native implementations of the initial design sampling and the acquisition function in
355
      HEBO. We set the size of the initial sample to 8 for all experiments. Each method ran with 51 differ-
356   ent random seeds and with a total budget of 100× mean evaluation time, similarly to Eggensperger

361   experiments required approximately 100 000 CPU hours.

      5     R ESULTS AND DISCUSSION

365

508   6                              C ONCLUSIONS , LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
509
510
511   In this work, we proposed a novel dynamic ensembling approach for surrogate models in hyperpa-
512   rameter optimisation. Our method constructs a weighted ensemble of different types of surrogate
513   models by assessing the accuracy of different surrogates throughout the optimisation process and
514   dynamically determining which weight should be assigned to each surrogate model. We experi-
515   mented with a wide variety of HPO benchmark tasks and found that our method results in a better
516   mean rank than single-surrogate baselines and a static ensemble with equal weights.
517   Despite very promising results of our work, there is still room for improvement. One limitation is
518   the higher running time required to both train and evaluate the surrogate models and to optimise the
519   acquisition function compared to a single-surrogate BO. We mitigate this by introducing dynamic
520   pruning of surrogates, which substantially reduces the required running time. However, dynamic
521   pruning only occurs when we have models with null weights, which is rare in ensembles with values
522   of α smaller than 1.0. Furthermore, we observe that the Gaussian process outperforms our method
523   for very small budgets (less than 5 minutes). This is in line with the fact that BO equipped with GP
524
      excels precisely in a very low-budget setting, as well as that our method requires higher time for the
      training additional surrogate models.
525
526   Our method itself comes with a hyperparameter, the smoothing factor α. While different values of
527   α work best with different target functions, we observed that using α = 1.0 works consistently well
528   on all functions, regardless of the cost of function evaluations. The impact of historical accuracy
529   measurements thus seem to contribute only marginally when determining the weights.
530   Our work presented here opens several avenues for future research. Multi-fidelity approaches are
531   very commonly used for HPO, especially in expensive tasks where each evaluation on full fidelity
532   can take more than an hour. It seems promising to extend our method to multi-fidelity scenarios. A
533   further promising direction is to learn in which scenarios (e.g.,, input dimensionality, phase of the
534   optimisation) which surrogate model(s) work best and to create an ensemble by training only these
535   specific models; in case a single surrogate model is chosen, the overhead of ensembling would thus
536
      be eliminated.
537   Overall, we believe that combining multiple surrogate models in BO is a promising direction for
538   achieving better surrogate predictions, thus allowing for faster convergence and increased sample
539   efficiency. DensBO is the first method that dynamically leverages the potential of complementary
      surrogates within the BO procedure itself.

773
      Our method is implemented in HEBO (Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022), a state-of-the-art BO-based op-
774
      timiser (Eggensperger et al., 2021). HEBO starts by sampling random configurations based on
775
      Sobol sequences. Then, in each BO iteration, it transforms the (input) configurations and the (out-
776   put) performances to tackle non-stationarity and heteroscedasticity of the data, respectively. Non-
777   stationarity of the input means that the GP kernel does not only depend on the norm between two
778   inputs; it is corrected by appropriate input warping, in this case the Kumaraswamy transformation.
779   Given the dimensionality of the decision variable d, tuneable warping parameters for each dimension
780   ak and bk , and a vector concatenating all free parameters γ, the Kumaraswamy warping is defined
781   as follows for all input dimensions:
782                                                                   a b

785   but that the noise is a function of the input, i.e., depending on the input, the noise is prone to chang-
786   ing around the mean. The performances are thus transformed using power transformations, either
787   Box-Cox (which supports either strictly positive or strictly negative inputs) or Yeo-Johnson (which
788   handles arbitrary inputs). Given a tunable transformation parameter ζ, the Box-Cox transformation
789   applies the following mapping to each of the outputs:

842   C.2     M OTIVATION FOR USING EXPONENTIAL MOVING AVERAGE
843
      We use an exponential moving average inspired by the reinforcement learning algorithm Q-
844
      learning (Watkins, 1989), which uses an exponential moving average to update its policy in every
845   iteration. In Q-learning, the Q(St , At ) of a state St and an action At holds the expected reward of
846   the agent by being in state St and taking action St . Q-learning then proceeds iteratively by explor-
847   ing the state space and updating the Q-values according to the observed rewards. By analogy, in our
848   optimisation case, we attempt to learn a “weighting policy” for the weights in our models. However,
849   a notable difference between the reinforcement learning case and the dynamic ensembling is that
850   reinforcement learning learns a single, static, policy in a training phase. We learn the policy online,
851   during the optimisation process.
852   Another motivation for the usage of the exponential moving average is to focus on the accuracy of the
853   surrogate models in the region that is currently favoured by the acquisition function. This region can
854   change during the optimisation process. With the exponential moving average, intuitively speaking,
855   we can gradually “forget” about old scores and put a higher emphasis on new ones.

857   C.3     S INGLE SURROGATE MODEL BASELINES
858
859   In Section 2.2, we gave an introduction on GPs. In this appendix, we elaborate on the three other
860   surrogate models we used in the paper.
861   Random forest (RF) (Breiman, 2001) is a bagged ensemble of decision trees. This means that each
862   tree is fitted on a different random subset of the data. This then helps avoid overfitting and improve
863
         1
             https://hebo.readthedocs.io/en/latest/optimisation.html


                                                           16
864
      the prediction accuracy. Random forests are known as good performance predictors (Hutter et al.,
865   2014) and have been used in BO pipelines as surrogate models (Hutter et al., 2011).
866
867   Extremely randomised trees (Geurts et al., 2006), also known as extra trees (ET,) are an ensemble of
868
      random trees, where the splitting threshold is determined randomly, instead of based on a predeter-
      mined criterion. Furthermore, each tree is built on the whole dataset. Extra trees are known to have
869
      lower variance than random forests.
870
871   Gradient boosting (GB) (Friedman, 2001) is an ensembling method that consists of multiple decision
872   trees. Gradient boosting iteratively constructs decision trees to minimise the loss of the ensemble.

874   D    B ENCHMARK DETAILS

876   Yet Another HPO Benchmark (YAHPO) Gym (Pfisterer et al., 2022) is a surrogate-based benchmark
877   for hyperparameter optimisation on tabular machine learning tasks. The benchmark consists of
878   multiple datasets from OpenML and various machine learning algorithms. The authors provide a
879   surrogate model for each machine learning algorithm and a dataset that predicts different objectives,
880   such as accuracy, training time, and more.
881   YAHPO Gym contains several scenarios. Each scenario has a configuration space and is based on
882   a machine learning algorithm. Each scenario contains multiple datasets, which are called instances.
883   The YAHPO Gym scenarios are:

885          • rbv2 ranger (Binder et al., 2020): random forest using the ranger R implementation.
886          • rbv2 rpart (Binder et al., 2020): decision tree using the mlr implementation.

             • rbv2 glmnet (Binder et al., 2020): generalised linear models (GLM) with elastic net regu-

890
               tion.

893          • rbv2 aknn (Binder et al., 2020): k-nearest neighbours using the mlr implementation.
894          • rbv2 super (Binder et al., 2020): a combined algorithm selection and hyperparameter opti-
895            misation (CASH) scenario that uses all the above rbv2 scenarios.
896          • iaml glment (Pfisterer et al., 2022): generalised linear models (GLM) with elastic net reg-
897            ularisation using the mlr implementation.
898
             • iaml rpart (Pfisterer et al., 2022): decision tree using the mlr implementation.

900          • iaml ranger (Pfisterer et al., 2022): random forest using the ranger R implementation.

       I   S TATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE
1297
1298
       We test the statistical significance of our results presented in Section 5.1, and show that dynamic
1299   ensembling significantly outperforms single-surrogate-based BO. To do this, we use the best-found
1300   target function values per optimiser. We then calculate the mean value for each function and opti-
1301   miser pair over the 51 random seeds. The optimiser that has the best value per function is then the
1302   best for the function. We calculate whether the values obtained for each of the other optimisers are
1303   statistically equal to the values of the best optimiser using a permutation test with 10 000 samples
1304   and a significance level of 0.05. We report these results in Table 6. We see that dynamic ensem-
1305   bling approaches are equal to the best method more times than any other baseline, across all budget
1306   groups. As expected, on cheap functions, α = 1.0 equals the most times to the best method. For
1307
       medium-cost functions, α = 0.5 performs best, while for the expensive ones, α = 0.9 is the best.
       We can, therefore, conclude that using dynamic ensembling is significantly better than any other
1308
       baseline.
