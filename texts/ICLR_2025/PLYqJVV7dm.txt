009                                                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                        Time series forecasting is the crucial infrastructure in the field of e-commerce,
012                        providing technical support for consumer behavior analysis, sales trends forecast-
013                        ing, etc. E-commerce allows consumers to reserve in advance. These pre-booking
014                        features reflect future sales trends and can increase the certainty of time series
015                        forecasting issues. In this paper, we define these features as Cross-Future Behav-
016                        ior, which occurs before the current time but takes effect in the future. To in-
017                        crease the performance of time series forecasting, we leverage these features and
018                        propose the CRoss-Future Behavior Awareness based Time Series Forecasting
019
                           method (CRAFT). The core idea of CRAFT is to utilize the trend of cross-future
                           behavior to mine the trend of time series data to be predicted. Specifically, to
020
                           settle the sparse and partial flaws of cross-future behavior, CRAFT employs the
021
                           Koopman Predictor Module to extract the key trend and the Internal Trend Min-
022                        ing Module to supplement the unknown area of the cross-future behavior matrix.
023                        Then, we introduce the External Trend Guide Module with a hierarchical struc-
024                        ture to acquire more representative trends from higher levels. Finally, we apply
025                        the demand-constrained loss to calibrate the distribution deviation of prediction re-
026                        sults. We conduct experiments on real-world dataset. Experiments on both offline
027                        large-scale dataset and online A/B test demonstrate the effectiveness of CRAFT.
028                        Our dataset and code will be released after formal publication.

033   Time series forecasting (TSF) is the crucial infrastructure in e-commerce (Ryali et al., 2023). By an-
034   alyzing trends and patterns in e-commerce data, TSF allows businesses to gain insights into both op-
035   erational behaviors and customer dynamics over time, thereby supporting consumer behavior analy-
036   sis and sales trend predictions. However, accurate TSF is a challenging task given the need to model
037   complex, non-linear temporal patterns over long periods of time (Rasul et al., 2024). To this end, re-
038   searchers explore various backbone networks such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Chen
039
      et al., 2020), recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Yin et al., 2022), and Transformer (Zhou et al.,
      2021). Additionally, they work on incorporating richer features into TSF and study issues related to
040
      multivariate TSF (Zhao & Shen, 2024; Li et al., 2024) and feature decomposition (Zeng et al., 2023;
041
      Liu et al., 2024).

      In fact, existing work is also attempting to introduce more features into TSF. Zeng et al. (2023)
055   attempts to extract more information from the acquired data and decomposes the time series into
056   trend and remainder parts. Wen et al. (2017) classifies covariate features in TSF into dynamic
057   historical, known future, and static variables. CFB, we proposed in this paper, can be treated as the
058   known future variable and has many excellent properties. CFB contains true information related to
059   future events. The future trend of the prediction target, even the abnormal trend caused by sudden
060   events, can be reflected in the trend of CFB. However, as the existing TSF models primarily focus

078   Therefore, jointly considering the above challenges, we propose CRAFT, a Cross-Future Behavior
079   Awareness based Time Series Forecasting method, for the first time to utilize Cross-Future Behavior
080
      to realize time series forecasting. The core idea of CRAFT, as shown in Figure 1, is to utilize the
      trend of CFB to mine the trend of time series data to be predicted. CRAFT is composed of three
081
      main parts: the Koopman Predictor Module (KPM), the Internal Trend Mining Module (ITM), and
082
      the External Trend Guide Module (ETG). KPM can extract the key trends of the label and CFB,
083   predicting the label in the prediction window. ITM supplements the unknown area of CFB, making
084   the final prediction of the label in the prediction window. ETG, with a hierarchical structure, can
085   acquire more representative trends from higher levels. Finally, we apply the demand-constrained
086   loss to calibrate the distribution deviation of prediction results. We conduct experiments on real-
087   world dataset. Experiments on both offline large-scale dataset and online A/B test demonstrate the
088   effectiveness of CRAFT. We summarize the main contributions of this paper as follows:
089   • We define the Cross-Future Behavior (CFB) and apply the CFB feature to time series forecasting
090     for the first time. CFB is a feature discovered from our extensive real case studies and has superior
091     characteristics: the trend of CFB can reflect the prediction target and even the abnormal trend of
092     the target.
093   • We propose a novel framework, namely CRAFT, to realize CFB-based time series forecasting.
094     CRAFT can utilize the trend of Cross-Future Behavior to mine the trend of prediction targets.
095     CRAFT is composed of three main modules, including KPM, ITM, and ETG, to address the two
096     challenges when applying CFB to time series forecasting. KPM and ITM can address the sparse
097
        and partial flaws of CFB, and ETG can address the unobvious trend flaws of CFB.
      • Extensive offline experiments on the real-world dataset and online A/B tests show the superiority
098
        of CRAFT towards SOTA baselines. Specifically, CRAFT improves application performance
099
        significantly, with an improvement rate of 41.35% on the IW R metric. Currently, CRAFT has
100     been successfully deployed on the reality application, serving online hotel inventory negotiations.

104   Backbone for time series forecasting. Recently, Transformer has reshaped the landscape of ma-
105   chine learning across numerous fields. Many works attempt to apply Transformer to forecast time
106   series data (Wen et al., 2023). PatchTST (Nie et al., 2024) designs a channel-independent Trans-
107   former for time series forecasting. To address the channel-independent limitations of Transformer,
      CARD (Wang et al., 2024) proposes a channel-aligned attention structure that can acquire both tem-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      poral correlations and dynamical dependence among multiple variables over time. Informer (Zhou
109   et al., 2021) extends Transformer using ProbSparse based on KL divergence to solve Long Sequence
110   time series forecasting. TFT (Lim et al., 2021) introduces a novel attention-based architecture that
111   combines high-performance multi-horizon forecasting with interpretable insights into temporal dy-
112   namics. Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) proposes a Decomposition Architecture and Auto-Correlation
113   Mechanism based on stochastic process theory to realize the series-wise connection and break the
114   bottleneck of information utilization. Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2021) proposes a new Transformer
115   based on a pyramidal attention module to simultaneously capture temporal dependencies of differ-
116   ent ranges in a compact multi-resolution fashion. Besides Transformer, a variety of other network
117
      architectures are widely investigated. Convolutional neural networks-based time series forecasting
      models such as WaveNet (Oord et al., 2016), TCN (Bai et al., 1803), and DeepTCN (Chen et al.,
118
      2020) use causal convolution to learn sequences and use dilated convolution and residual block to
119
      memorize historical patterns. Graph WaveNet (Zonghan et al., 2019) enhances the WaveNet frame-
120   work by using an adaptive and learnable adjacency matrix to automatically infer graph structures,
121   enabling the prediction of spatiotemporal sequences. Moreover, due to the sequential nature of time
122   series data, Recurrent Neural Networks-based time series forecasting is particularly widely suited,
123   mainly modeling the temporal dependence of time series (Salinas et al., 2020; Rangapuram et al.,
124   2018; Wen et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020).
125
      Multivariate time series forecasting. Multivariate time series forecasting utilizes multiple time-
126   dependent variables to realize prediction. Compared with univariate time series forecasting, multi-
127   variate time series forecasting can help to better understand the interactions between different com-
128   ponents of a complex system, which is crucial for strategy formulation and decision-making (Mendis
129   et al., 2024). There are two commonly used strategies in multivariate TSF, i.e., the channel-
130   dependent (CD) and channel-independent (CI) methods. CI method only models cross-time depen-
131   dence, and the CD method models both cross-time dependence and cross-variate dependence (Zhao
132   & Shen, 2024). While the CI method is characterized by simplicity and low risk of overfitting, the
133   CD method has inevitably become the mainstream of research. Recently, Zhao & Shen (2024) uti-
134
      lizes the channel dependence between variates and proposes a plug-and-play method named LIFT,
      which exploits the lead-lag relationship between variates by estimating leading indicators and lead-
135
      ing steps. LIFT refreshes the accuracy of multivariate TSF.
136
137   Feature decomposition in time series forecasting. Different from the multivariate TSF that utilizes
138   multiple variates and the dependence between variates to realize prediction, feature decomposition
139   in TSF does not introduce new variates. The core idea of feature decomposition is to extract as much
140
      information as possible from existing variates. Dlinear (Zeng et al., 2023) decomposes time series
      into trend series and remainder series and uses two single-layer linear networks to model them,
141
      bringing performance improvements. Koopa (Liu et al., 2024) solves non-stationary time series
142
      prediction problems from the perspective of modern dynamics Koopman theory.

      Time series forecasting (TSF) with Cross-Future Behavior (CFB) can be defined as Yt+1:t+P =
      H(Yt−L+1:t , XT , CT ). Yt−L+1:t ∈ RL and Yt+1:t+P ∈ RP are time series data (i.e., label) at the
148
      L-length look-back window and P -length prediction window at time t respectively. XT is covariate
149
      features, CT is the CFB feature and H is the prediction function to be learned. The covariate
150   features (Wen et al., 2017) Xt contains three categories: 1) historical features like month-on-month
151   sales features, etc; 2) known future features like holidays, weekends, etc; 3) static features like hotel
152   brands, business districts, etc. CT = {Ct−L+1:t , Ct+1:t+P }, where Ct−L+1:t is CFB in the look-
153   back window and Ct+1:t+P is CFB in the prediction window. It is worth noting that Ct+1:t+P in the
154   prediction window is partial as this is a not fully observable variate. Consumers can book items in
155   the prediction window at any time until the last minute. More detailed introduction to CFB feature
156   Ct refers to Appendix A.
157   In the following section, we omit the subscripts of some symbols for simplicity. Specifically, we
158   denote time series at the look-back window Yt−L+1:t as YL , time series at the prediction window
159   Yt+1:t+P as YP , CFB feature in look-back window Ct−L+1:t as CL , CFB feature in prediction
160   window Ct+1:t+P as CP . In addition, as CP is partial observed, we define a new notion CT P to
161   indicate the ground truth of CFB in prediction window. For clarity, we summarize all notions with
      table in Appendix B.1.

267   4.3 E XTERNAL T REND G UIDE M ODULE
268   ETG module is designed to settle the trend unobvious challenge, which uses aggregated spatial di-
269   mension to improve prediction results. Aggregated spatial dimension has stronger regularity, less
      noise, and is easier to estimate. Table 1 shows that the sample size of the hotel is approximately

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      400k, which is too extensive for direct model train. Therefore, referring to (Lu et al., 2022), we
271   introduced a hierarchical sampling strategy to construct samples. This strategy assumes the global
272   reconciliation matrix P is sparse, indicating that only child nodes belonging to the same parent node
273   have calibration relationships with each other. This assumption aligns well with the geographic
274   attributes of hotels. We aggregate hotels into business districts based on their geographic location
275   and then to higher levels of urban granularity. First, we randomly pick a business district. Then,
276   we sample m hotels from this district, with the likelihood of selection increasing with each hotel’s
277   historical label value. These m hotels’ labels are combined to create a virtual parent node. To-
278   gether, the m + 1 nodes form a sampled hierarchy that serves as model input. This hierarchical
279
      sampling maintains the sum constraint through virtual parent nodes, and label weighted sampling
      aligns the virtual sequence more with the real parent sequence. Moreover, these strategies reduce
280
      the reconciliation matrix’s parameter count from O(n2 ) to O(mn), significantly easing the model’s
281

518   6       C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, inspired by real-world application, we define Cross-Future Behavior (CFB). CFB is
521   a kind of features that occur before the current time but take effect in the future, containing true in-
522   formation related to future events. For the application of CFB in the time series forecasting problem,
523   we propose an improved method, named Cross-Future Behavior Awareness based Time Series
524   Forecasting method (CRAFT). CRAFT regards the trend of CFB as prior information to predict
525   the trend of target time series data. Specifically, CRAFT is composed of three main modules, the
526
      Koopman Predictor Module (KPM), the Internal Trend Mining Module (ITM), and the External
      Trend Guide Module (ETG). The first two modules aim to mine the prediction trends from partial
527
      CFB, and the ETG module aims to acquire more representative trends from higher levels. In addi-
528
      tion, CRAFT adopts demand-constrained loss to correct the distribution of prediction results. We
529   conduct experiments on real-world dataset. Experiments on both offline and online tests demonstrate
530   the effectiveness of CRAFT.
531
532
      This paper only explores the application of CFB and CRAFT on the e-commerce area. Electricity
      demand, stock price, and disease spread forecasting typically do not have clear lead-up operation
533
      events, making it difficult to apply our method. In addition, the current public available dataset (e.g.,
534
      ETT(Zhou et al., 2021), ECL1 , Weather2 ) does not contain the CFB feature or similar feature, we
535   only verify CRAFT on our dataset limited by this actual situation. In the future, we will further
536   collect related data to form a series benchmark dataset. Moreover, we will continue to generalize
537   the definition of CFB to enable CRAFT method to be applied more broadly.

      Cross-Future Behavior (CFB) is feature that occurs before the current time but takes effect in the
      future. Taking hotels as an example to illustrate CFB, as shown in Figure 6. A user can book a
688
      hotel room on that day or make an early book for a future date (Figure 6 (b), (c)), a hotel’s check-in
689
      rooms within a day originate from bookings made on that day and previous days (Figure 6 (a)).
690   Assuming today is July 25, the bookings made from July 23 to July 25 for July 27 (2 rooms, 5
691   rooms, 11 rooms) exemplify the early book matrix (Figure 6 (d)). Furthermore, in order to obtain
692   more comprehensive information, we accumulate the quantities of early books to obtain the cross-
693   future matrix (Figure 6 (e)). The early book matrix and cross-future behavior matrix decompose the
694   number of hotel check-in rooms for a given day based on the book dimension. Hotel examples will
695   also be used later in the text for ease of understanding and will not be repeated below. As shown
696   in Figure 7, the left graph shows all information known at the moment of predicting t, and after
697   prediction by the model, the right graph completes the unknown information to be predicted.
698   However, the use of CFB in TSF problem faces two main challenges: 1) CFB is sparse and partial.
699   Consumers can book items at any time, causing CFB to remain fully observed until the last minute.
700   As shown in Figure A(d), half of the data in the cross-future matrix cannot be obtained. When
701   making predictions, the data for row T-0 can only be fully obtained at the end of the day, meaning
      only rows T-1 to T-n are available for forecasting, showing that CFB is sparser and smaller than


                                                                                           13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      other features such as hotel historical sales. Thus, if CFB is simply incorporated into the model, the
703   prediction model may be unable to apply CFB features correctly and even make incorrect predictions
704   due to CFB. 2) The trend of CFB is unobvious. As shown in Figure 2, compared with the sales
705   trend in a high-level business district, the sales trend in an individual hotel is unobvious. CFB has
706   the same nature, and the trend in an individual hotel is much unobvious compared with that in a
707   high-level business district. CFB can reflect future trends to a certain extent, however, the coverage
708   of CFB for some hotels may be too atypical to reveal a trend. Sales at higher-level districts are much
709   more representative than in individual hotels. If there is a rising trend at a higher level, it is also
710   possible that the sales of individual hotels may increase. How to leverage the sales trends of the
711
      higher level to guide the trends exploration for individual hotels is the second challenge.

743   B.2   KOOPMAN T HEORY
744
745   Koopman Theory (Koopman, 1931) mainly focuses on the dynamical system, studying the evolu-
746   tion of state variables along one or more coordinate axes (usually time). Koopman theory trans-
747   forms finite-dimensional nonlinear dynamics problems into infinite-dimensional linear dynamics
748   problems, by projecting the state into the space of measurement function g(y) and evolving for-
749
      ward by an infinite-dimensional linear operator K which is also called the Koopman matrix. For a
      discrete-time dynamical system, it can be transformed as:
750
751                                    yt+1 = F (yt ),
752                                                                                                      (16)
                                       K(g(yk )) = g(F (yk )) = g(yk+1 ),
753
754   where F is the evolution function in dynamic systems. In practice, we apply a finite linear matrix K
755   to approach the infinite value. Recently, there have been several studies applying koopman theory to
      the filed of time series prediction. Taking inspiration from (Liu et al., 2024), we treat the prediction


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      window as a time period and calculate the koopman matrix between time periods. The Koopman
757   matrix can be approximated by ridge regression, i.e.,:
758
759                                 B = KA ⇒ K = (AT A + λE)−1 AT B,                                     (17)
760
      where λ is the ridge coefficient to command the weight of regularization terms, E is the identity ma-
761   trix. Ridge regression effectively prevents model over-fitting and solves multicollinearity problems
762   under Deficient-rank conditions by introducing L2 regularization terms.

764   B.3   H IERARCHICAL S TRUCTURE
765
766   Multivariate time series data typically have a hierarchical structure, where each upper-level time
767   series is computable by summing over the appropriate lower-level time series. Hierarchical prog-
768   nostics need to satisfy aggregation constraints, i.e., they need to ensure that the sum of predictions
769   of some parent node and the sum of the predictions of its child nodes are approximately equal.
770   Reconciliation-based hierarchical prognostics approach is currently the state-of-the-art solution in
771
      hierarchical time series estimation, which is based on the estimated values of existing nodes, and the
      calibrated estimated results are obtained through the following formula:
772
773                                                   ỹt = SBŷt .                                      (18)
774
      Where S is the hierarchical structure matrix, the value between node pairs with parent-child re-
775
      lationships is 1, otherwise it is 0. B is the reconciliation matrix which represents the calibration
776
      relationship between the bottom nodes and is the parameter to be solved.
