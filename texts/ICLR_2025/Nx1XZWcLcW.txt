009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce MoMa, a novel modality-aware mixture-of-experts (MoE) architec-
012            ture designed for pre-training mixed-modal, early-fusion language models. MoMa
013            processes images and text in arbitrary sequences by dividing expert modules into
014            modality-specific groups. These groups exclusively process designated tokens
015            while employing learned routing within each group to maintain semantics-based
016            adaptivity. Our empirical results reveal substantial pre-training efficiency gains
017            through this modality-specific parameter allocation. Under a 1-trillion-token train-
018            ing budget, the MoMa 1.4B model, featuring 4 text experts and 4 image experts,
019
               achieves impressive FLOPs savings: 3.7× overall, with 2.6× for text and 5.2×
               for image compared to a compute-equivalent dense baseline, measured by pre-
020
               training loss. This outperforms the standard expert-choice MoE with 8 mixed-
021
               modal experts, which achieves 3× overall FLOPs savings (3× for text, 2.8× for
022            image). These results demonstrate MoMa’s potential to significantly advance the
023            efficiency of mixed-modal, early-fusion language model pre-training, paving the
024            way for more resource-efficient and capable multimodal AI systems.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Auto-regressive mixed-modal foundation models have shown significant promise in applications re-
030   quiring the processing of mixed-modal inputs and the generation of mixed-modal outputs (Gemini
031   et al., 2023; 2024; OpenAI et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2023; Chameleon Team, 2024). These models
032   have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in tasks ranging from visual question answering to mul-
033   timodal content generation, pushing the boundaries of AI’s ability to understand and interact with
034   our inherently multimodal world.
035
      While a popular architecture design for mixed-modal foundation models involves fusing modality-
036   specific encoders or decoders (Gemini et al., 2023; 2024; Lu et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024), this
037   approach can limit the model’s ability to integrate information across modalities and generate con-
038   tent with interleaved modalities. To address this limitation, Chameleon (Chameleon Team, 2024)
039   recently introduced a single transformer architecture with a next-token prediction objective to model
040   mixed-modal sequences consisting of discrete image and text tokens, allowing for seamless reason-
041   ing and generation across modalities. However, scaling such mixed-modal early-fusion foundation
042   models to greater capacities presents significant computational challenges.
043   To address these challenges, we investigate the application of routed sparse architectures (Lepikhin
044   et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022; Clark et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Raposo et al., 2024). These
045   architectures have previously shown effectiveness in scaling language and vision-specific foundation
046   models, as well as multimodal contrastive learning (Mustafa et al., 2022). However, their application
047   to mixed-modal early-fusion models presents unique opportunities and challenges.
048   The insight driving our approach is the inherent heterogeneity of modalities: text and image to-
049   kens possess distinct information densities and redundancy patterns (Liang et al., 2023). While
050   we integrate these tokens into a unified early-fusion architecture, we propose further optimizing
051   this framework by incorporating modality-specific modules. This concept, which we term modality-
052   aware sparsity, enables models to better capture features specific to each modality while maintaining
053   strong cross-modality integration through partial parameter sharing and attention mechanisms. Pre-
      vious work such as VLMo (Bao et al., 2022), BEiT-3 (Wang et al., 2022b) and VL-MoE (Shen et al.,

077   Specifically, we adopt Chameleon as the base architecture and apply mixture-of-experts (MoE)
078   (Lepikhin et al., 2020) where tokens are routed across a set of feed-forward blocks (experts) at each
079   layer. Crucially, we divide these experts into modality-specific groups, with each group processing
080   only tokens of its designated modality while learned routing is done within each group for semantics-
081
      based adaptivity. We dub this approach mixture of modality-aware experts (MoMa). We adopt
      expert-choice routing (Zhou et al., 2022) to ensure load balancing and maintain a static computation
082
      graph (Paszke et al., 2019), both important for high training throughput.
083
084   We conduct extensive FLOPs-controlled experiments comparing our proposed architecture with the
085   dense baseline and multiple sparse variations. With a 1-trillion-token training budget, MoMa 1.4B
086   with 4 text experts and 4 image experts achieves a substantial 3.7× overall FLOPs savings (text:
087
      2.6×, image: 5.2×) compared to the 1.4B isoFLOP dense baseline as measured by pre-training loss,
      while maintaining a relatively modest -17% throughput reduction. In contrast, the standard expert-

090   to significantly advance the efficiency of mixed-modal, early-fusion language model pre-training,

094   2     M ODEL
095
096   2.1     E ARLY F USION
097
098   Our model builds upon the early fusion architecture introduced by Chameleon (Chameleon Team,
099   2024), which represents images and text as a series of discrete tokens within a unified transformer.
100   The core of Chameleon is a transformer-based model that applies self-attention mechanisms over
101   the combined sequence of image and text tokens. This allows the model to capture complex relation-
102   ships between and within modalities. The model is trained using a next-token prediction objective,
103   learning to generate both text and image tokens autoregressively.
104   In Chameleon, images are tokenized using a learned image tokenizer that encodes a 512 × 512
105   image into 1024 discrete tokens from a codebook of size 8192. Text is tokenized using a BPE
106   tokenizer with a vocabulary size of 65,536, which includes the 8192 image codebook tokens. This
107   unified tokenization scheme enables the model to process arbitrary sequences of interleaved image
      and text tokens.

108
      By adopting this early fusion approach, our model inherits several key advantages:
109
110         1. Unified representation: The model learns a shared representation space for images and text,
111            facilitating cross-modal reasoning and generation.
112         2. Flexibility: The architecture can handle arbitrary sequences of images and text, enabling
113            diverse multimodal tasks such as image captioning, visual dialogue, and mixed-modal doc-
114            ument generation.
115         3. Scalability: The token-based approach allows for uniform processing of both text and im-
116            age data, enabling efficient scaling to larger model sizes and diverse datasets. This is ev-
117            idenced by Chameleon’s successful training on approximately 10 trillion mixed-modal
118            tokens.
119         4. End-to-end learning: The entire auto-regressive model, is trained end-to-end, allowing for
120            joint optimization of the representation and task-specific performance.
121
122   Building on this foundation (Figure 1a), our work introduces modality-aware sparsity techniques to
123   further enhance the efficiency and performance of early fusion models, as detailed in the following
124
      sections. These techniques aim to address the computational challenges of scaling early fusion
      models while maintaining their powerful cross-modal reasoning capabilities.

      2.2   M IXTURE OF M ODALITY-AWARE E XPERTS
127
128   We propose a width scaling approach that incorporates modality-aware block sparsity in the feed-
129   forward module, extending the standard mixture-of-experts (MoE) architecture (Lepikhin et al.,
130   2020; Fedus et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022a). The insight driving this approach is that tokens
131   of different modalities have distinct characteristics and information densities. By creating separate
132   expert groups for each modality, we allow the model to develop specialized processing pathways
133   while maintaining the ability to integrate information across modalities.
134   We describe the key components in the mixture of modality-aware experts (MoMa) formulation
135   below (Figure 1b).
136
137   Modality-Specific Expert Groups. We divide the experts in each MoE layer into distinct groups,
138   each specialized in processing tokens from a specific modality E = {E1T . . . , Em
                                                                                       T
                                                                                         , E1I . . . , EnT }:
                                                                           1
139   one group for processing text tokens and another for image tokens . This separation allows each
140   group to specialize in features relevant to its respective modality.
141   By implementing modality-aware block sparsity, we aim to achieve several benefits:
142
143          • Improved efficiency: by routing tokens to modality-specific experts, we reduce the compu-
144            tational overhead of processing tokens with experts not specialized for their modality.
145          • Enhanced specialization: modality-specific expert groups can develop more refined fea-
146            tures relevant to their respective modalities.
147          • Maintained Cross-Modal Integration: despite the separation into modality-specific groups,
148            the model still can integrate information across modalities through the shared self-attention
149            mechanisms in non-MoE layers.
150
151   Hierarchical Routing. We adopt a token-based routing mechanism (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fedus
152   et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024). For an input token x, our routing mechanism operates in two stages.
153
154
            1. Modality-aware routing: tokens are first routed to their corresponding modality-specific
               expert group based on their modality (text T or image I).
155
156         2. Intra-modality routing: within each modality-specific expert group E M , tokens are then
157
               routed to specific experts using a learned routing function. Specifically, we use a projection
                                         M
158            matrix WgM ∈ Rd×|E | , d = transformer hidden dimension, to compute the token-to-
159            expert affinity scores, following other established MoE formulations (Lepikhin et al., 2020;
160
               Fedus et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024).

      Expert choice. Within each modality group, we implemented expert-choice (EC) routing (Zhou
163   et al., 2022), where each expert has a fixed bucket size and processes the top-ke tokens in a batch.
164   We set ke = bM · ce , where ce = |E1M | is a capacity factor and bM is the total number of tokens of
165   modality M in a batch. As a result, each token can be routed to a variable number of experts.
166
167   EC routing ensures balanced expert utilization during training and eliminates needing a separate
168
      load-balancing loss term. Maintaining a single loss term promotes more stable optimization and
      a more straightforward training process. However, EC routing compromises causality in auto-
169
      regressive language modeling, as each expert selects the top tokens to process in a batch by compar-
170
      ing token scores across all tokens. We use a combination of two techniques to address this issue and
171   enable expert-choice training for auto-regressive LMs.
172
173         1. We use the sigmoid function as the non-linearity in the router scoring function, enabling
174            independent calculation of token-to-expert affinity scores for each token.
175         2. We introduce auxiliary routers, inspired by Raposo et al. (2024), which predict the likeli-
176            hood of an expert selecting a token solely based on its hidden state representation. These
177            routers are trained after the main model training is completed and employed during infer-
178            ence to ensure causality. We discuss the details of the auxiliary routers in Appendix A.2.

215   lenges compared to vanilla MoEs. We discuss these challenges and our strategies to address them in
      this section.

      3.1   L OAD BALANCING
217
218
      Without constraints, load imbalance can occur in our system because the ratio of text to image to-
219   kens can vary significantly across different GPUs and iterations. Imbalances can create a cascading
220   straggler effect, delaying weight prefetching for subsequent layers and gradient releases from pre-
221   vious layers. This effectively bounds the training latency by the maximum time required to process
222   text and image experts across all GPUs within a batch.
223
      To mitigate these issues, we developed a balanced data mix that aligns the text-to-image data ratio
224
      with the expert ratio on each GPU. This approach ensures load balancing in expectation. While
225   alternative rebalancing algorithms, such as token redistribution at each FFN layer, are possible, they
226   may introduce additional communication overhead.
227
228
229   3.2   E FFICIENT E XPERT E XECUTION
230
231   We explored several strategies to efficiently execute experts for different modalities. The first ap-
232   proach restricts to homogeneous experts across modalities and prohibits routing of text tokens to
233
      image experts and vice versa. This method allows processing of all tokens and modalities simulta-
      neously, provided all experts share the same token count. Alternatively, we could enhance execu-
234
      tion efficiency by employing block sparsity (Gale et al., 2023), which offers similar benefits to the
235
      first approach without requiring perfect expert token balance. Additionally, we considered running
236   experts from different modalities sequentially when the number of modalities is limited. This ap-
237   proach allows better overlap of computation from previous modality experts with weight prefetching
238   for subsequent modality experts, alleviating memory pressure. It also removes assumptions about
239   expert load balance.
240
      Given that our experiments involve a sufficiently large token count per GPU, hardware utilization is
241
      not a major concern even with multiple batched matrix multiplications. Thus, we find the sequential
242   approach to be a clean and flexible choice for our experimental environment at the current scale.

      We use the same pre-training dataset and preprocessing as Chameleon Team (2024). To assess
250
      scaling performance, we train all models with over 1 trillion tokens. Unless specified otherwise,
251
      we employ a sequence length of 4096 tokens and a model parallel size of 1. Our training regimen

253   learning rate to 1% of its peak value. For all MoE architectures, we implement MoE in every layer,
254                                                    M

255
      base dense model. To achieve FLOPs parity with the base dense model, we increase the total layer
256
      count while maintaining a constant hidden dimension. Table 1 provides detailed configurations for
257   our dense and sparse models. Additional pre-training details are available in Appendix A.1.
258
259   For model comparison, we report training losses. Given that our 1-trillion-token training budget

351   4.3   S CALING N UMBER OF E XPERTS
352
353   We conducted further ablation experiments to investigate the impact of scaling the number of ex-
354   perts. We explored two scenarios: allocating an equal number of experts per modality (balanced
355   expert allocation) and allocating different numbers of experts per modality. We included the dense
356   model and expert-choice MoE with 8 mixed-modal experts (moe 8x) as baselines.
357
358
359   Balanced expert allocation. Figure 3a demonstrates that training loss consistently improves as
360   the number of experts increases. Text and image losses exhibit distinct scaling patterns. While text
361   loss improves steadily with each doubling of expert count, image loss shows diminishing returns as
362   the number of experts increases from 2 to 4. This suggests that the intrinsic characteristics of each
363
      modality lead to different behaviors in sparse modeling. This finding aligns with our previous obser-
      vation in §4.2, where untying modalities over 8 experts significantly enhanced image performance
364
      but not text performance, indicating that the text modality may benefit more from a larger number
365
      of experts.
366
367
368
369
      Imbalanced expert allocation. Given the diminishing returns observed when allocating more
      than 2 experts to the image modality, we experimented with configurations allocating fewer ex-
370
      perts to the image modality and more to the text modality. Figure 3b compares three configurations
371
      with the same total number of experts (8), varying the allocation between text and image modalities
372   (moe 7t1i, moe 6t2i and moe 4t4i). We observed that allocating more experts to a modality
373   generally enhances its performance. However, assigning more than 4 experts to the text modality
374   also yields diminishing returns. The overall training losses of the three configurations converge to
375   similar levels. Considering the better load balancing of balanced expert assignment with our pre-
376   training data mixture, we selected moe 4t4i as our optimal MoE configuration, despite moe 6t2i
377   having a slightly lower average loss. We leave the design of sparse architectures that can effectively
      leverage the intrinsic characteristics of different modalities to future work.

389   4.4    T HROUGHPUT A NALYSIS
390
391
      Sparse models often struggle to fully achieve their theoretical efficiency advantages due to the ad-
      ditional computation complexity and data balancing challenges (§3). To quantify the impact of
392
      our proposals on training efficiency, we conducted a controlled experiment comparing the training
393
      throughput of various architectures. We compare architectures FLOPs-matched to the 435M dense
394   baseline. We performed our experiments on 256 A100 GPUs with a sequence length of 4096 and a
395   batch size of 6 per GPU and summarized the model throughput in Table 2.
396
397
      Comparing expert-choice MoE (moe 8x) and dense models, introducing sparsity does incur over-
      heads (-9%). The throughput loss likely comes from the need to compute routing decisions and syn-
398
      chronize gradients for all experts despite being FLOPs-equivalent. On the other hand, as discussed
399
      in §3, running experts sequentially by modality (moe 1t1i) does not suffer from large execution
400   overheads, and we can attribute most of the loss (-6%) to computing token indices for each modality,
401   which can be amortized by precomputing the indices and share with each transformer layer.
402
403
      When combining modality conditioned feed-forward with learned routing (moe 4t4i), we observe
      a smooth degradation in throughput as the number of experts increases, incurring additional 11%
404
      overheads with 8 experts, which is on par with the throughput loss of 9% when transitioning from
405
      the dense model to MoE with 8 experts.

476   5      R ELATED W ORK
477
478
479
480   Early-fusion VLMs. State-of-the-art vision-language models (VLMs) often combine visual per-
481   ception modules with language model backbones, enabling processing of arbitrarily interleaved text
482   and images as input, and generating text as output (Liu et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
483   2023; Lin et al., 2024). Early works utilized shallow alignment via trainable connection layers (Liu
484   et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022), while later studies also adjusted LM parameters during multi-
485   modal training for enhanced fusion (Chen et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
      2024). In the meantime, early fusion techniques have gained traction due to their ability to capture


                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      cross-modal interactions from the onset of processing. PerceiverIO (Jaegle et al., 2021) introduced a
487   fully attentional read-process-write architecture that operates over a modality-agnostic latent space
488   for processing diverse inputs, including text and images. NÜWA (Wu et al., 2021) presented a
489   3D-attention transformer capable of both understanding and generating text, image, and video in
490   various combinations. Chameleon (Yu et al., 2023; Chameleon Team, 2024) adopted an autore-
491   gressive LM architecture to model mixed-modal documents with discrete token representation across
492   modalities, showcasing the scalability of LM I/O paradigm to large-scale multi-modal pretraining.
493   We adopt Chameleon as the base transformer architecture and demonstrate that modality-aware
494   sparsity can effectively improve its scaling performance further.
495
496   Sparsity and modularity in multimodal LMs. Mutlimodal LMs often employ modality-specific
497
      feature encoders and connectors due to diverse data processing requirements (Liu et al., 2023;
      Alayrac et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024). Previous studies also showed that fur-
498
      ther benefits can be achieved by allocating modality-specific parameters in deeper architecture lay-
499
      ers (Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b; 2023). VL-MoE (Shen et al., 2023) introduced modality-
500   aware MoE in transformer feed-forward layers (Bao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Shen et al.,
501   2023; Chen et al., 2024) while using a masked data modeling objectives for pre-training. VL-MoE
502   achieves strong vision-language task performance with significantly less active FLOPs per token
503   than its dense counterparts. CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023) adopted separate feedforward module and
504   QKV attention matrices for images, enabling continuous mixed-modal training with highly compet-
505   itive image understanding capabilities without losing performance on text-only tasks. Building on
506   these advancements, MoMa uniquely demonstrates the effectiveness of modality-aware sparsity in
507   early fusion architectures capable of generating both text and images, as well as their combinations.
508
509   Sparse neural networks. Sparse neural networks have emerged as a promising approach to im-
510   prove the efficiency and scalability of deep learning models. One notable architecture is the Mixture-
511
      of-Experts (MoE) model, which dynamically selects a subset of experts to process each input, reduc-
      ing computational costs and promoting specialization (Shazeer et al., 2017; Lepikhin et al., 2020;
512
      Fedus et al., 2022). These advances in sparse neural networks have paved the way for the de-
513
      velopment of increasingly large and powerful models, enabling state-of-the-art results in various
514   domains, including natural language processing (Jiang et al., 2024) and computer vision (Riquelme
515   et al., 2021). Recent and concurrent work have applied standard MoE to multi-modal training (Lin
516   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), creating experts that process mixed-modal tokens. MoMa demonstrates
517   that modality-aware MoE yields further efficiency gains, especially for image generation.
518
519
      6   C ONCLUSION
520
521
      In this work, we have introduced a family of modality-aware sparse architectures for early fusion,
522
      mixed-modal foundation models. Our best architecture demonstrates significant training efficiency
523   improvements over isoFLOP dense and vanilla mixture-of-expert baselines. Importantly, our exper-
524   imental findings reveal that our modality-aware sparse architectures maintain an empirical scaling
525   law. This characteristic suggests that our approach provides immediate performance benefits and a
526   scalable framework for future developments in mixed-modal foundation model training.

786   B     M IXTURE - OF -D EPTHS
787
788   We further investigate introducing sparsity in the depth dimension. Prior work explores sparisty in
789   depth through either stochastic layer drop (Elhoushi et al., 2024) or through learnable routers (Ra-
790   poso et al., 2024). We focus on learnable routers, and incorporate the recently proposed mixture-
791   of-depths (MoD) technique (Raposo et al., 2024). Specifically, in each MoD layer, we integrate
792   MoD prior to any mixture-of-experts (MoE) routing, ensuring it is applied to the full batch before
793   modality split (Figure 4).5
794   Following Raposo et al. (2024), for each MoD layer, we use a projection matrix Ŵg ∈ Rd×1 to
795   compute the token-to-layer affinity score, followed by a Sigmoid non-linearity
796
797                                                    ĝ(x) = σ(x · Ŵg ).                                                (3)
798   Analogous to expert-choice routing in MoE, we set a fixed capacity of kd tokens, selected from the
799   top-scoring tokens in a batch. We set kd = b · cd , where cd ∈ (0, 1] is a capacity factor chosen
800   empirically. In practice, we first fixed the MoD layer interval and capacity factor cd , then adjust the
801   total number of transformer layers to ensure the resulting architecture has a comparable FLOPs per
802   token to the base architecture.

859
860   While combining MoD and MoE achieves the best training loss in our experiments, introducing
861   MoD triggers an estimated throughput loss of 15% . This is because MoD architectures require an
862   additional router on the depth dimension, introducing complexity and potential bottlenecks. Fur-
863   thermore, when combined with MoMa, MoD may exacerbate system imbalance due to the varying
      active tokens for each modality at each layer, deviating from the predefined token mix ratio in the

      D     S PARSE U PCYCLING
880
881
      Training MoE architectures with learnable routers from scratch presents unique challenges in op-
882
      timizing both the representation space and routing mechanism (Xue et al., 2024). The insight we
883   identified is that MoE routers are responsible for partitioning the representation space for each ex-
884   pert. However, this representation space is sub-optimal in the early stages of model training, leading
885   to a sub-optimally trained routing function.
886
      To address this limitation of router training, we propose an upcycling approach, inspired by Komat-
887
      suzaki et al. (2023). Specifically, we begin by training an architecture consisting of 1 FFN expert
888
      per modality. After a predetermined number of steps, we upcycle this model by converting each
889   modality-specific FFN into an expert-choice MoE module, initializing each expert with the expert
890   trained from the first stage. We reset the learning rate scheduler while preserving the data loader
891   state from the previous stage, thereby ensuring the second stage training is exposed to refreshed
892   data.
893
      To promote expert specialization, we augment the MoE routing function with Gumbel noise (Liu
894
      et al., 2022a; Geng et al., 2020), allowing our router to differentiably sample experts. This is ex-
895   pressed in Equation 4:
                                                                     ′     ′′
896
                                    Gumbel-Sigmoid(x) = σ(x + G − G )                                  (4)
897            ′        ′′
898   where G and G are independent Gumbel noise samples.
899   The upcycling approach, combined with the Gumbel-Sigmoid technique, allows us to overcome
900   the limitations of learned routers and achieve improved performance in our modality-aware sparse
901   architecture. In practice, we found that a short period of 10k-20k steps in the first-stage training
902   significantly improves model training efficiency and stability, aligning with the findings of Xue
903   et al. (2024).

947   E    L IMITATIONS
948
949   Currently, our implementation of MoMa relies on matching the token mix ratio in the dataset with
950   the expert mix ratio in the model to maintain load balance across GPUs. Even so, minor imbalance
951   may still occur because there is no hard limit for a batch to deviate from that ratio at per-GPI per-
952   iteration level. We leave further improvements in this area as future work.
953   Expert-choice routing alleviates the expert load balancing issue during training but presents addi-
954   tional challenges for auto-regressive Language Models (LMs) during inference (Zhou et al., 2022).
955   Although auxiliary routers comprise only a small fraction of the network’s parameters, their role is
956   crucial. In our study, we trained the auxiliary router after completing the whole network training
957   and limited this process to a few thousand steps, while previous work have demonstrated the pos-
958   sibility to jointly train such modules with the full network (Raposo et al., 2024). Future research
959
      should explore the architecture and training techniques for auxiliary routers to prevent them from
      becoming a performance bottleneck and ensure generalizability across diverse data distributions.
960
      Especially, further investigation is necessary for training mixture-of-depths architectures, including
961
      both the auxiliary routers and the original model, to ensure effective performance in causal inference
962   scenarios.
963
964   In our work, we experimented only the vanilla formulation of MoD and its staged integration with
965
      MoE. We leave the investigation of other MoD variations, including modality-aware MoD to future
      work. In addition, batched sequence generation with mixture-of-depths (MoD) is non-trivial as
966
      unlike standard sequence generation there are dynamics shapes and dynamic updates to the KV
967
      cache for each layer as certain sequences and layers may skip different tokens. There remains
968   further room to optimize the inference implementations for MoD models.
