009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A key component of building safe and reliable language models is enabling the
012            models to appropriately refuse to follow certain instructions or answer certain
013            questions. We may want models to output refusal messages for various categories
014            of user queries, for example, ill-posed questions, instructions for committing ille-
015            gal acts, or queries which require information past the model’s knowledge horizon.
016            Engineering models that refuse to answer such questions is complicated by the fact
017            that an individual may want their model to exhibit varying levels of sensitivity for
018
               refusing queries of various categories, and different users may want different re-
               fusal rates. The current default approach involves training multiple models with
019
               varying proportions of refusal messages from each category to achieve the desired
020
               refusal rates, which is computationally expensive and may require training a new
021            model to accommodate each user’s desired preference over refusal rates. To ad-
022            dress these challenges, we propose refusal tokens, one such token for each refusal
023            category or a single refusal token, which are prepended to the model’s responses
024            during training. We then show how to increase or decrease the probability of gen-
025            erating the refusal token for each category during inference to steer the model’s
026            refusal behavior. Refusal tokens enable controlling a single model’s refusal rates
027            without the need of any further fine-tuning, but only by selectively intervening
028            during generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      An essential property of a useful language model is the ability to produce refusal messages at ap-
      propriate times. Refuses messages not only enhance the safety of LLMs, but also their utility and
034
      trustworthiness, as refusal messages can prevent LLMs from hallucinating or answering invalid re-
035
      quests. For example, an LLM that lacks the ability to browse the web should refuse when asked to
036   access and summarize the content behind a URL. Likewise, a model should provide an informative
037   refusal when asked to answer a question that is too under-specified or poorly formed to be answer-
038   able. To minimize hallucinations and unsafe behavior, instruction models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
039   2023) and Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024) have been processed with alignment pipelines that imbue
040   them with extensive refusal capabilities.
041
      Despite advancements in model finetuning and alignment, controlling refusal messages in these
042   models remains a challenging task. For instance, Llama-2-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023) experienced
043   issues with over-refusal, where the model would refuse too many queries, negatively impacting
044   usability, mostly likely due to a post-training set with too many refusal messages. Simple ap-
045   proaches, such as training multiple models with varying levels of refusal data until the desired rates
046   are achieved (Dubey et al., 2024) are resource-intensive and still lack the precision to carefully ad-
047   just different categories of refusals. Moreover, the criteria for refusal are constantly evolving. What
048   is considered an acceptable refusal for one use case or time may not align with the ethical, legal, or
049   technical standards in a different setting.
050   To address these weaknesses, we introduce a simple strategy that makes refusal behavior control-
051   lable at test-time without retraining: the refusal token. During alignment, we prepend a special
052   [refuse] token to responses that contain a refusal. The model quickly learns to generate this
053   token before refusing, and then to refuse when this token is present. At test-time, the softmax prob-
      ability of the refusal token can be used as a metric for how likely it is that a refusal is necessary.

054
      By thresholding on this probability, one can turn a knob to control the refusal sensitivity after the
055   model is trained. By employing different refusal tokens for different refusal types, one can impose
056   fine-grained control over refusal behavior along different axes of behavior, and carefully optimize
057   refusal rates in this multi-dimensional space.
058
      Our main contributions are the following:
059
060          • We introduce a refusal token strategy. By thresholding the probability of this refusal token,
061            we give model developers calibrated control over refusal rates without retraining. This
062            development opens the door for sophisticated post-training calibration of refusal rates. For
063            example, with minimal computation, one could sweep over refusal thresholds and select a
064            value that achieves a specified rate of false refusals, or a value that maximizes an F1 score.
065          • We show that multiple refusal tokens can manage different refusal message sets, enabling
066            independent control over each refusal distribution. Additionally, we explore various strate-
067            gies for manipulating these category-specific refusal tokens to meet test-time requirements.
068          • We observe that the refusal token improves F1 scores on coconot and TempEval (our new
069            evaluation), even without calibration. Furthermore, we highlight the importance of reduc-
070            ing Type II errors by including contrast or borderline examples in the training data. These
071            examples, which are similar to refusal queries but innocuous, help refine the token’s ef-
072            fectiveness—specifically, its ability to switch appropriately between refusal and response
073            based on the corresponding meta-token.

077   Refusal messages. The ability of generative models to refuse certain messages is particularly cru-
078   cial for mitigating toxicity and reducing hallucinations. In the context of toxicity, several studies
079   explore how language models respond to toxic prompts or instructions. Arditi et al. (2024) find a
080   one-dimensional subspace such that erasing this specific direction from the model’s residual stream
081   activations causes the model to consistently answer harmful queries. Bianchi et al. (2024) demon-
082
      strate that incorporating refusals into training data does not diminish a model’s helpfulness but can
      lead to over-refusals, where the model declines to respond even on innocuous requests. Similarly,
083
      Cui et al. (2024); An et al. (2024) investigate over-refusal behavior across various language models,
084
      developing an evaluation framework to assess over-refusals in response to harmful prompts. Regard-
085   ing hallucinations, Zhang et al. (2024) introduce an algorithm called R-Tuning, which prompts the
086   model to state “I am unsure” or “I am sure” after a question and answer session, framing the problem
087   as a discrimination task. Additionally, Kang et al. (2024) and Kapoor et al. (2024) propose alterna-
088   tive algorithms for alleviating the hallucination problem, focusing on instances where it is unclear
089   whether the model possesses the required knowledge. Feng et al. (2024) uses multiple agents to
090   determine when to abstain from queries. For predetermined queries the model is designed to refuse,
091   Brahman et al. (2024) presents a comprehensive taxonomy of such questions, highlighting scenarios
092   where the model should appropriately refuse to respond. This work also releases instructional data
093
      designed to train models in this regard. Evaluative studies by Liu et al. (2023), Yin et al. (2023), and
      Amayuelas et al. (2024) further explore the types of questions that warrant refusal.
094
095   Tagging, control codes, and meta-tokens. The concept of tagging or using control codes was
096   introduced by Sennrich et al. (2016) in machine translation and for general usage by Keskar et al.
097   (2019). A control code is a piece of text, c, used in a conditional language model that always
098   conditions on a control code c and learns the distribution p(x|c). Specifically, Keskar et al. (2019)
099
      pretrain a model using control codes to regulate style, content, and task-specific behavior. Tagging
      and control codes can also be viewed as form of prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021). Lu et al. (2022)
100
      combines tagging with Reinforcement learning for model unlearning; while, Chan et al. (2021)
101
      introduces a new arhitecture to improve the behavior of the meta-tokens. Dong et al. (2023) extend
102   this idea by adding controls to different distributions during supervised fine-tuning (SFT) that users
103   might want to control, including seven categories which are collected by training another classifier to
104   first categorize and score the responses based on the selected seven attributes. These tags or tokens
105   can also be predicted by the model to help the model generate its response to a query. The general
106   use of these “meta-tokens”, or tokens that the model predicts to help itself generate its response
107   to the query, has seen a recent increase with the introduction of tool calling in LLMs, or function
      calling (Nakano et al., 2021; Schick et al., 2024). However, others propose using meta-tokens for

146   3    L EARNING TO R EFUSE WITH T OKENS
147
148
      Instruction models are trained on instruction-response pairs, (x, y), sampled from instruction dataset
149
      D. The user provides the model with a question or an instruction, x, and the model then outputs
150   a response y. Each datapoint is usually given an additional chat template, C. Here, y consists
151   only of natural language without any meta-information contained in the messages. We introduce a
152   new token, [refuse], at the beginning of the response if it is a refusal message, or [respond]
153   otherwise during training. This modifies y to y ′ = [refuse] + y or y ′ = [respond] + y,
154   depending on whether y is a refusal message or a normal response. This can also be written as an
155   application of the token to the end of the chat template, or C(x) + [refuse].
156   We will see that including the [refuse] and [respond] tokens during training will influence the
157   model at test-time. The model builds stronger associations during fine-tuning the more it encounters
158   response tokens together with non-refusal messages and refusal tokens together with refusal mes-
159   sages. After fine tuning, the presence of the refusal token at the beginning of the response results
160   in a high likelihood of a refusal message, and visa-versa. Note, however, that the association of re-
161   fusal tokens with refusal messages is not guaranteed. In our studies below, we used LLM-as-a-judge
      (Zheng et al., 2024) for measuring refusal rates.

162
      Test-time control. The primary reason to include this refusal token is the test-time capabilities that
163   the token introduces. The model predicts this token, and there is a softmax probability associated
164   with it that can be used as a confidence measure for determining whether the question should be
165   refused or not. This confidence can manipulated in many ways such as thresholding the token or
166   adding a logit bias. We focus our studies on the thresholding method, and emit the [refuse]
167   token if its softmax score is > T , for some T ∈ [0, 1] chosen by the user.
168
      Controlling different types of queries. We consider applying categorical refusal tokens for differ-
169   ent refusal reasons. Our experimental setting includes five refusal tokens corresponding to the refusal
170   categories defined in Brahman et al. (2024), and one respond token. Details of our multi-category
171   thresholding schemes and logit bias mechanisms are described in greater detail in Section 5.1.

176   We use the hyperparameters and codebase from Tunstall et al. (2023) for supervised finetuning.
177   Our initial results with DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) show that the SFT stage is required for the
178   desired refusal behavior (See Appendix Table 6), and we our experiments focus on the SFT stage.
179   The importance of the SFT stage before DPO was also seen in Sharma et al. (2024). We adopt
180   llama-3 8B (Dubey et al., 2024) as the base model. Additionally, we mix the instruction pairs that
181   contain refusal messages with UltraChat (Tunstall et al., 2023) or Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023). We
182   experimented with Alpaca as it is largely free of any refusal messages, and its low training time
183   facilitates more ablations in Section 6.
184   Coconot Experimental Setting. For the main experimental setting, we utilize a diverse and compre-
185   hensive dataset—extending beyond just toxicity—for both training and evaluation to ensure robust
186   performance in refusal prediction. Specifically, we adopt Brahman et al. (2024)’s coconot dataset
187   and evaluation due to the breadth of the categories and subcategories that are considered. The co-
188   conot dataset contains five refusal categories–Humanizing, Indeterminate, Incomplete, Safety, and
189   Unsupported–and 26 subcategories. Additionally, the dataset contains contrast data, or examples
190
      that the model should answer but are close to questions that the model should refuse. We consider
      two main training settings on UltraChat with refusal data and training on UltraChat with refusal and
191
      contrast data. For these two settings, we either train with no refusal token, one refusal token, or
192
      multiple category refusal tokens. The coconot dataset contains ∼ 10k refusals SFT data, ∼ 1k of
193   contrast preference data (which we use as SFT data), and ∼ 1.4k, or 1379, for the evaluation. The
194   evaluation contains 1k queries that should refuse to answer and 379 queries that the model should re-
195   spond to the query–referred to as the contrast category. We refer to this evaluation and experimental
196   set-up as coconot.
197
      Temporal Experimental Setting. We considered a second more controlled experimental setting.
198
      We created temporal refusal and contrast training data to address coconot’s low contrast-to-refusal
199   ratio, at one to ten. For this setting, we consider a refusal message, where the query is temporally
200   ambiguous or relates to events beyond the model’s cutoff dates. Additionally, we considered contrast
201   data, or examples close to a refusal query but answerable, as temporal questions that contain dates
202   about an event within its training period. The goal is to refuse queries that are temporally ambigious
203   or contain dates beyond the model’s cutoff. Using llama-3 70B, we prompted the model to generate
204   questions from news articles beyond its cutoff date for refusal data, and before the cutoff data of
205   the model for contrast data, with modified prompts. More details can be found in Appendix A.3.
206   We generated 2k examples each for refusal and contrast datasets, focusing on temporal questions,
207
      resulting in 4k instruction-response pairs. We consider two main training settings on UltraChat with
      refusal data and contrast data used throughout the sections, and Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) with re-

      fusal data and contrast data used in Section 6. For these two settings, we either train with no refusal

230   The refusal token introduces test-time capabilities. By training with the refusal token, the refusal
231   rate can be altered at test-time. This ability cannot occur when training without the token. The
232   model predicts this token, providing a softmax probability associated with the refusal token. This
233   token probability can be interpreted as the confidence with which the model “thinks” the question
234   should respond with a refusal message. Conversely, the response token is interpreted as the prob-
235   ability that the model should respond. We use this confidence measure and generate the token if
236
      p([refuse]|C(x)) > T , where T is a threshold set by the user. By adjusting the threshold, T , we
      demonstrate that the refusal rates can be effectively controlled.
237
238   Refusal tokens provides control of the refusal rate. We sweep the thresholds of the refusal token
239   across the two settings–training with and without contrast training examples–to observe the change
240   in the true positive and false positive rates. In Figure 2, the threshold provides control over the true
241   positive and false positive rates. Figures 2a and 2b show that adding contrast data (SFT data that lies
242
      close the boundary between the two classes but are non-refusal) results in a better Pareto frontier
      than training without the token.

290   Thresholding schemes and logit bias. We explore two types of thresholding strategies: (1) cate-
291
      gory thresholding, refusing with that category token if a token from selected category tokens is the
      highest probability among the refusal tokens and rises above a threshold, and (2) sum thresholding,
292
      refusing only if the sum of all category token scores exceeds a threshold. For category threshold-
293
      ing, we emit the refusal token that is the highest probability among the refusal tokens and is in the
294   selected category tokens; otherwise, we emit the token with the highest probability. For sum thresh-
295   olding, we emit the category refusal token highest probability when the condition described earlier
296   is met; otherwise, we emit a response token. Algorithmic versions of these schemes can be found
297   in Appendix A.6. For logit bias, we manipulate the sensitivity of different refusal types by adding a
298   constant bias to the (unnormalized) logits for the refusal tokens.

      Independent control of sensitivity for different refusal types. To test whether categories can
300   be independently controlled, we completely suppress each token one-at-a-time, and observe the
301   impact of this suppression on other (non-suppressed) refusal types. In Figure 3, we observe that the
302   sensitivity of each refusal category can be adjusted with little impact on other categories of refusals.
303   There is an exception though: Humanizing Requests proved particularly difficult to suppress and
304   did not respond to their token as other categories did. After inspecting the questions and responses
305   of the Humanizing Requests category, we found that many of the questions contained questions or
306   instructions similar to other categories.
307   Thus, many of the Humanizing questions or instructions are classified as one of the other refusal
308   categories, i.e. the model emitted the incorrect refusal token. For example, many of the questions
309   ask for stock or financial recommendations. These types of requests could easily be refused due
310   to temporal issues (no access to real-time information), input modality issues (needing access to
311   current portfolios), or safety (not wanting to provide financial information). Nevertheless, Figure 3
312   highlights that one can use individual category tokens to control individual distributions.

381   A major advantage of incorporating refusal tokens lies in their ability to influence model behavior
382   at test-time. Notably, even without using the refusal tokens to control a model at test-time, the mere
383   inclusion of refusal tokens during training enhances the model’s refusal behaviors (measured by
384
      F1 scores). In our primary experimental setup, we focus on training with temporal refusals and/or
      temporal contrast data, as outlined in Section 4. These experiments examine how fine-tuning a
385
      model on refusal data from one type of query affects the refusal rates for other types of questions.
386

      7              D ISCUSSION
466
      An issue with refusal messages in LLMs is that generation sampling can cause the model’s response
467
      to vary across multiple iterations of the same query (Huang et al., 2024). However, the use of
468
      a refusal token can help mitigate this issue. For example, we compared two models—one with
469   the refusal token and one without—over five generations. We recorded the entropy of each set of
470   responses. We found that the model with the token had a slightly lower entropy (0.07 compared
471   to 0.10), where the entropy would be 0.69 if the probability of generating a refusal message (or
472   any refusal message) is 0.50. Additionally, we found that in 81% of cases, the responses had zero
473   entropy, meaning all generations are identical, compared to 87% with the refusal token. Providing
474   an explanation, Table 4 shows that a refusal or response token does not guarantee that the generation
475   is a response or refusal. Nevertheless, the refusal token improves consistency in model generations.
476   Another aspect of refusals to consider is adversarial attacks. Although we assume that the user
477
      in these settings is not acting maliciously, an individual may optimize the refusal tokens directly
      optimize on short strings like “Sure here’s,..” such as Shin et al. (2020); Wen et al. (2023); Zou
478
      et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2024). However, these attacks are well-studied in the community (Alon
479
      & Kamfonas, 2023; Jain et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). A more specific threat model involves
480   scenarios where a user places the [respond] token either at the end of the input or the beginning of
481   a response. In an API setting, such inputs can be filtered out. For open-source models, a viable
482   defense may be to train the model specifically to generate refusal messages for inputs containing
483   the [respond] token, ensuring the model consistently rejects such prompts. While this approach may
484   limit the model’s ability to respond to valid queries of that type, it effectively mitigates jailbreak
485   attempts that rely on optimizations targeting short strings or tokens. It also prevents misuse of the
      [respond] token to extract answers from the model.

496   The ability of a model to refuse queries–whether due to toxicity, limitations, or other reasons–is cru-
497   cial for developing safer and more trustworthy LLMs. To advance this, we need to understand how
498   and why models generalize across different contexts, which requires the appropriate data. While
499   some datasets, such as Brahman et al. (2024), provide broad coverage, there remains a gap in pref-
500   erence data and multi-turn evaluations, complicating the task of generalizing single-turn results to
501   multi-turn interactions. Thus, we need additional data to better understand this property of LLMs.
502   Nevertheless, adding a refusal token during fine-tuning offers several benefits. When the model
503   generates the token, it associates a softmax probability of refusal with the query. At test-time, the
504   refusal token allows for adjusting the refusal rate. Moreover, by applying the refusal token to spe-
505   cific categories, the distribution can be controlled, and thresholding techniques can further improve
506   the F1 scores of refusal rates. Additionally, these tokens can be modified in various ways during
507   testing, such as using logit bias, category-specific thresholding, or sum thresholding, highlighting
508   their flexibility. Therefore, without retraining language models, refusal tokens offer the advantage
509   of test-time control, benefiting both users and API providers.

      A.1   XST EST
761
762   XSTest is a test set that comprises 250 safe prompts across ten subcategories that models should not
763   refuse to comply with, and 200 unsafe prompts that models should refuse. Note the focus of XSTest
764   is only toxicity; whereas, coconot contains a larger range of categories (i.e not just toxicity) and a
765   larger number of questions to evaluate on. After the model has generated responses to the questions,
766   the model is evaluated in two ways per the original paper–string matching or model evaluation using
767   GPT-4. For string matching, the model uses a list of short sequences to identify if the model is
768   refusal–i.e “I’m sorry. . . ”, etc. However, in our experiments, we found that string matching was not
769
      sufficient due to the list not containing all the ways our models were refusing. Thus, we used GPT-4
      to evaluate XSTest as reflective of the original XSTest. And since we did not validate llama-3.1-
770
      70B-Instruct’s ability on this new prompt, it seemed appropriate to use GPT-4 as per the original
771
      paper/codebase.
772
773   Since the original coconut and temporal setting in the test set is reflective of the train set (as they
774   come from the same source), we suspect the behavior of the token is better when the train and test
775
      distributions are more aligned in terms of wording. In this new setting, we train on the coconot train
      set and evaluate on the XSTest test set. We assumed that XSTest is reflective of an out-of-distribution
776
      setting because the subcategories are slightly different and more importantly the question’s wording
777
      may not be the same as the refusals in the training data. Thus, we want to confirm that some of
778   the capabilities such as turning off the token to reduce overall refusal rates and the out-of-the-box
779   benefits are present. From Table 5, we see that adding the refusal tokens improves the full refusal
780   rate on unsafe and lowers the safe refusal rate by 1% in either direction. Additionally, adding
781   the category refusal tokens decreases the safe refusal rate by over 5% and by about 0.5% slightly
782   reduces the refusal rate on the unsafe questions. When analyzing the outputs for the difference
783   in 5% for category tokens versus refusal tokens, we observed that different category tokens were
784   utilized providing a non-safety reason that yielded in gpt4 marking them as a compliant response.
785   Additionally, to confirm that the tokens can affect refusal rates for this set of prompts, we experiment
786
      with only producing the respond token, or turning off the refusal tokens. We find that this token
      reduces the overall refusal rate by up about 5% for model that contain category tokens and about
787
      10% for the model trained with a single refusal token. These results echo the results in the paper,
788
      further validating our claims.
