009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become a key method
012            for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences through the
013            use of reward models. However, traditional reward models typically generate
014            point estimates, which oversimplify the diversity and complexity of human values
015            and preferences. In this paper, we introduce Quantile Reward Models (QRMs),
016            a novel approach to reward modeling that learns a distribution over rewards in-
017            stead of a single scalar value. Our method uses quantile regression to estimate a
018
               full, potentially multimodal distribution over preferences, providing a more pow-
               erful and nuanced representation of preferences. This distributional approach can
019
               better capture the diversity of human values, addresses label noise, and accom-
020
               modates conflicting preferences by modeling them as distinct modes in the distri-
021            bution. Our experimental results show that QRM outperforms comparable tradi-
022            tional point-estimate models on RewardBench. Furthermore, we demonstrate that
023            the additional information provided by the distributional estimates can be utilized
024            in downstream applications, such as risk-aware reinforcement learning, resulting
025            in LLM policies that generate fewer extremely negative responses. Our code and
026            model will be released.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating
032   remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks (Anthropic, 2023; Team et al., 2023; OpenAI,
033
      2023). However, the sheer scale and breadth of their training data present both opportunities and
      challenges. While LLMs can process and generate human-like text with unprecedented fluency, their
034
      outputs may not always align with human preferences, ethics, or real-world applicability. To bridge
035
      this gap and ensure that these powerful tools truly benefit humanity, it has been recognized that fine-
036   tuning techniques are necessary that can align LLMs with human values and intentions (Christiano
037   et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2022a). This process of refinement is essential to harness
038   the full potential of LLMs while mitigating potential risks associated with their deployment in real-
039   world scenarios.
040
      Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a prominent and effective
041
      method to align LLMs with human preferences. RLHF uses reinforcement learning (RL) to fine-
042   tune language models by maximizing rewards derived from a trained reward model. This reward
043   model, is itself learned from human preferences. By quantifying human judgments on responses for
044   a prompt, the reward model provides a crucial bridge between human values and the optimization
045   objective of the language model. As a result, accurate reward models are very important in order
046   to finetune LLMs and it has been shown that improvements in reward model quality translates to
047   improvements in the quality of the finetuned LLM (Touvron et al., 2023).
048   However, current reward models are designed to output a single scalar value for a given query-
049   response pair, an approach that fails to capture the inherent complexity and diversity of human
050   values. This oversimplification can lead to problematic outcomes. For instance, in scenarios where
051   human opinions diverge significantly - with some individuals finding a response appropriate while
052   others deem it inappropriate - the reward model may resort to outputting an average value to mini-
053   mize penalties across these disparate groups during training. This compromise fails to represent the
      nuanced spectrum of human preferences accurately. Moreover, it can hinder the learning process

      However, in cases of conflicting preferences, the concept of ’correctness’ becomes ambiguous, as
      these divergent views often represent equally valid but different perspectives rather than clear-cut

      right or wrong answers. This nuance poses a significant challenge to the current paradigm of reward
083
      modeling in RLHF.
084
085   To address these limitations, we propose a novel approach to reward modeling that reimagines how
086   human preferences are captured and represented. Instead of learning a single scalar reward for each
087
      query-response pair, our method, called Quantile Reward Model (QRM), learns a distribution over
      rewards. Specifically, we employ quantile regression to learn this distribution, allowing us to esti-
088
      mate different percentiles of the reward function and thus capture its entire shape. This probabilistic
089
      approach offers a more nuanced and comprehensive representation of human values, capable of cap-
090   turing the diversity and sometimes conflicting nature of preferences. By modeling a full distribution,
091   we can account for the diverse values and preferences within a population without resorting to over-
092   simplified averages. Furthermore, it addresses the challenge of learning from seemingly conflicting
093   preferences by treating them not as contradictions to be resolved, but as different modes in a multi-
094   modal distribution. This shift in perspective allows the model to learn from a wider range of human
095   feedback without the risk of confusion from what might previously have been considered ’incorrect’
096   or conflicting inputs. Consequently, our distribution-based reward model promises to provide a more
097   accurate, flexible, and robust foundation for reinforcement learning in language models, potentially
098
      leading to AI systems that are better aligned with the full spectrum of human values.
099   We implement our approach using a pretrained Llama-3 model Meta (2024) with 8 billion parame-
100   ters. Our distributional reward model outperforms comparable methods on RewardBench Lambert
101   et al. (2024), a benchmark that evaluates reward models using a point estimate-based metric. This
102   suggests that our model is better at handling conflicting values and the associated label noise during
103   training.
104   Moreover, the additional information provided by the distributional estimate can be leveraged in
105   downstream applications. We demonstrate this by training an RL policy with a risk-aware utility,
106   derived from the distributional estimates of our model. The resulting policy achieves strong results
107   and generates fewer extreme negative responses compared to a baseline trained using a risk-neutral
      point estimate as the reward.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      RLHF involves using RL to align language models with human preferences or feedback (Christiano
111
      et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020). The process generally involves training a re-
112   ward model on preference data collected from crowdworkers (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022)
113   or model-selected responses (Bai et al., 2022b). Once a reward model is developed, RL algorithms
114   can be used to train policies. Another method is to directly optimize a policy by comparing selected
115   and rejected responses using DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) and followup works (Tang et al., 2024).
116   While this method bypasses the need for an explicit reward model, our focus is on approaches that
117   explicitly train such a model.
118   Estimating distributions rather than point estimates in regression has a long history and has proven
119   valuable across many fields (Kneib et al., 2023). Quantile regression (Koenker, 2005), in particular,
120   is a powerful tool for approximating distributions. In RL, distributional regression has also demon-
121   strated its effectiveness (Bellemare et al., 2017), with quantile regression achieving especially strong
122   results (Dabney et al., 2018; Kuznetsov et al., 2020; Dorka et al., 2022).
123
      A key distinction exists between epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty (Fox & Ülkümen, 2011;
124   Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021). Epistemic uncertainty arises from incomplete knowledge and
125   can be reduced by improving data or models. In contrast, aleatoric uncertainty stems from inher-
126   ent randomness in the system and cannot be reduced. This paper focuses on modeling aleatoric
127   uncertainty.
128
      Several previous works have modeled preferences using distributions. Distributional Preference
129
      Learning (DPL) (Siththaranjan et al., 2023) suggests that hidden contextual factors influence pref-
130   erences, arising from the diverse values of annotators. This variability is captured by estimating
131   a distribution over rewards. Similarly, the Distributional Preference Reward Model (DPRM) (Li
132   et al., 2024) learns a distribution over rewards, utilizing an optimal transport loss to train the model
133   on preference data. Both DPL and DPRM, like our approach, model a distribution over rewards.
134   However, unlike our method, which incorporates attribute regression, these models rely solely on
135   preference data and optimize the likelihood that the preferred response is ranked higher.

      3     D ISTRIBUTIONAL R EWARD M ODELS
138
139
      In this section, we first introduce the reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) frame-
140   work, then introduce multi-attribute regression reward models, followed by an explanation of quan-
141   tile regression. Finally, we propose our approach using quantile regression to obtain distributional
142   reward models and explain how it can be used for risk-aware RLHF.
143
144   3.1     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
145
146   The typical RLHF process using an explicit reward model consists of three stages:
147
148   3.1.1    S UPERVISED F INE - TUNING
149
150   In the first stage, the pre-trained language model is instruction-tuned by supervised learning on a
151   dataset consisting of a prompt and a high-quality response. Similarly to the pre-training phase the
152
      model is trained with a cross-entropy loss over tokens but only on the tokens of the response. The
      resulting model is often used as the initialization for the reward model and the final policy trained
153
      with reinforcement learning.
154
155
      3.1.2    R EWARD M ODEL T RAINING
156
157   After supervised fine-tuning, the next step is to train a reward model rϕ (x, y), which evaluates the
158   quality of responses y relative to the prompt x based on human preferences. Typically, reward
159   models are trained on a preference dataset Dpref = {x, y − , y + } consisting of a prompt x and a
160   preferred y + and not-preferred y − response. The goal is that the reward model learns to assign
161   higher rewards to outputs that better align with human preferences, such that it can later be used as
      an informative proxy signal in guiding the optimization of the language model.

179   3.1.3    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
180
181   Once the reward model is trained, it can be used to optimize the language model further through
182   reinforcement learning. In this stage, the language model generates outputs for a dataset of prompts,
183   and the reward model assigns rewards to these outputs based on their estimated quality. The language
184   model is then trained to maximize these rewards. This iterative process allows the model to improve
185
      its performance by continuously refining its behavior according to the feedback provided by the
      reward model, making it more capable of generating high-quality, human-aligned responses.
186
187   More formally, the goal is to finetune the language model πθ by maximizing the expected reward. To
188   prevent reward hacking, which results in gibberish outputs, the model is penalized for deviating too
189   much a reference policy. This is achieved with a KL divergence penalty between the current policy
190   and the reference policy. Often the initial policy π sft is used as reference policy. The complete RL
191
      objective can be expressed as:
192
193
                             L(θ) = Ex∼D,y∼πθ (y|x) [rϕ (x, y) − β DKL (πθ ∥πref )] ,
194
195
      where β is a hyperparameter that controls the strength of the KL penalty, balancing the trade-off
196
      between maximizing the reward and staying close to the original model distribution. In principle
197   any RL algorithm can be used to optimize the policy. A common choice is to use PPO (Schulman
198   et al., 2017) and more recently more simple REINFORCE based algorithms (Ahmadian et al., 2024).

      3.2     M ULTI -ATTRIBUTE R EWARD M ODELS
202
      Most existing reward models are trained using the Bradley-Terry loss on binary preference data,
203
      where one response is labeled as preferred to another. This approach essentially frames the problem
204   as binary classification. This method, however, fails to account for whether a response was clearly
205   better or only marginally superior to the other. As a result, training a reward model on such data
206   can lead to problems, as it may penalize good responses that are only slightly less favorable than an
207   even better one in the same way it penalizes a very bad response.
208
      Recent datasets are increasingly generated by first collecting absolute labels rather than relative ones
209
      (Cui et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Responses are rated across various dimensions such as helpful-
210   ness, truthfulness, and harmlessness, with a fine-grained score assigned to each dimension. These
211   individual scores are then aggregated to produce a final score. Preferences are subsequently deter-
212   mined by labeling the response with the higher aggregated score as the preferred one. Alongside
213   these datasets, new approaches have emerged that use regression to directly estimate the fine-grained
214   scores (Wang et al., 2023; 2024a). In this framework, the reward network outputs a single scalar
215   value for each objective and is trained to minimize the mean squared error between its predictions
      and the fine-grained scores.

231   3.3     Q UANTILE R EGRESSION
232
233   Quantile regression (Koenker, 2005) is a versatile statistical technique that generalizes the con-
234   ventional linear regression model by estimating the conditional quantiles of the response variable.
235
      Formally, while ordinary least squares (OLS) regression focuses on minimizing the sum of squared
      residuals to estimate the conditional mean, quantile regression minimizes an asymmetric loss func-
236
      tion to estimate specific quantiles belonging to the distribution of the response variable. The quan-
237
      tile function Q(τ ) (where 0 < τ < 1) of a probability distribution is the inverse of its cumulative
238   distribution function (CDF) and hence Q(τ ) denotes the value x such that the probability of the
239   corresponding random variable X being lower than x is equal to τ , i.e. P (X <= x) = τ . For a
240   given quantile τ , linear quantile regression solves the following optimization problem:
241
242

                           min                τ |yi − x⊤
                                                       i w| +                 (1 − τ )|yi − x⊤
                                                                                             i w|.          (2)
243                          w
                                 i:yi ≥x⊤
                                        i w                      i:yi <x⊤
                                                                        i w
244
245
      Here, yi ∈ R represents the labels, xi ∈ Rd the d-dimensional inputs, and w ∈ Rd is the vector of
246   coefficients to be estimated.
247
248   One of the key strengths of quantile regression lies in its ability to model the conditional distribu-
249
      tion of the response variable at different quantiles, offering a more detailed view of the relationship
      between the dependent and independent variables. This is particularly advantageous in situations
250
      where the effects of the input variables are not uniform across the distribution of the outcome vari-
251
      able, such as in the presence of skewed or multimodal distributions, heteroscedasticity, or outliers.
252   Unlike OLS, which provides a single estimate of the central tendency, quantile regression allows to
253   approximately represent a distribution over the response variable, offering more information about
254   the data. Hence, quantile regression provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the dis-
255   tributional effects of input variables on the response variable, making it a critical tool in applications
256   where understanding the full conditional distribution is essential.

258   3.4     D ISRTIBUTIONAL R EWARD M ODELS VIA Q UANTILE R EGRESSION
259
260   Our objective is to develop a reward model that outputs a distribution over rewards. To achieve this,
261   we propose a two-step approach called Quantile Reward Models (QRM). This method involves:
262
      (1) estimating distributions over attribute scores (e.g., helpfulness and harmlessness) using quantile
      regression, and (2) training a gating network to aggregate these individual attribute distributions into
263
      a final reward distribution. An illustration of our approach is depicted in Figure 2.
264
265

268   Specifically, we perform regression on K ∈ N evenly spaced quantiles τk ∈ (0, 1). For each
269   attribute, we train K linear quantile regression layers, with each layer predicting the value at a
      specific quantile.

311   3.5     R ISK -AWARE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
312
313   We believe that distributional reward models offer significant potential by leveraging the additional
314
      information contained in the reward distribution for downstream reinforcement learning (RL) tasks.
      In this section, we demonstrate one possible application of this concept.
315
316   When deploying a chat model, a critical concern is avoiding particularly poor responses, as low-
317   quality outputs can negatively impact user experience across various dimensions, such as overall
318   quality and safety. Even occasional bad responses can lead to user dissatisfaction. In these cases, it
319   can be beneficial to optimize a metric that emphasizes penalizing low-quality responses as perceived
320
      by users.
321   A point estimate reward model provides only a single value to assess a response. For example,
322   if a response is perceived as excellent by some users and poor by others, a point estimate model
323   may produce an average score that can lead the LLM to generate such mixed-quality responses. In
      contrast, a distributional reward model allows us to penalize responses with significant disagreement.


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      One way to achieve this is by applying a concave utility function over the reward distribution to bias
325   the final score to emphasize lower values, i.e., the left tail of the distribution. We define this utility
326   function as:
                                           Utility = Er∼P −e−λr ,

327                                                                                                          (4)
328
      where λ is a hyperparameter controlling the emphasis on low rewards, and the expectation is taken
329
      over the distribution P of reward values r produced by our distributional reward model. We can
330
      approximate this by applying the utility function to each quantile estimate before computing the
331   final expectation.
332
333   This utility function penalizes distributions with substantial probability mass on low reward values.
334
      For instance, a bimodal distribution with one peak at a low value and another at a high value will
      have a lower utility than an unimodal distribution centered around the same expected value. Conse-
335
      quently, this approach encourages risk-averse policies that avoid producing highly variable or risky
336
      responses.

341   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION
342
343
      Our LLM backbone is based on LLaMA-3 with 8 billion parameters (Meta, 2024), initialized with
      weights from a reward model trained using the Bradley-Terry loss (Dong et al., 2024). The backbone
344
      remains frozen during both stages of training. This allows for significant computational efficiency,
345
      as we can precompute the backbone features once and reuse them throughout the subsequent stages,
346   thereby significantly reducing the required computation resources. For multi-attribute regression,
347   we follow prior work and use 19 attributes from Wang et al. (2024a), sourced from eight datasets
348   (details provided in the appendix). To limit the computational requirements, we limit the number of
349   data points per attribute to 60, 000 when training the linear quantile regression layers. The quantile
350   regression is implemented using Scikit-learn Pedregosa et al. (2011), with L1 weight regularization
351   set to 0.003. For each attribute, we train 19 quantile regression models corresponding to the evenly
352   spaced quantiles: 0.05, 0.10, . . . , 0.90, 0.95. Additionally, as in Wang et al. (2024a), we mitigate the
353   correlation between the verbosity attribute and other attributes by applying their penalty scores, and
354
      adjusting the quantile estimates accordingly.
355   The gating network used for aggregation is a multi-layer perceptron with three hidden layers and
356   a softmax activation at the output layer. We train the network for 3 epochs using the AdamW
357   (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0003, batch size of 1024, and a
358   cosine decay learning rate scheduler. The weight decay is set to 0.001. The training is performed on
359   data from 10 datasets, with further details outlined in the appendix.

420   To evaluate our approach, we train two RL policies. For comparability, both use our distributional
421   reward model, but in one case we apply the risk-aware utility function to compute the rewards,
422
      while in the other, we simulate a point-estimate reward by using the expected value of the reward
      distribution.
423
424   We use the LLaMA-3 model with 8 billion parameters as the base model, training on the Anthropic
425   HH-RLHF dataset (Bai et al., 2022a). Both supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and RL are performed
426   using LoRA, with a rank value of 32 and α = 64. For SFT, we train on the responses from the
427   dataset selected as chosen, filtering for examples with at most 1024 tokens. The SFT model is trained
428
      for one epoch with a learning rate of 0.0003, batch size of 1024, using the AdamW optimizer and a
      cosine learning rate schedule.
429
430   After completing the SFT stage, we merge the LoRA weights into the model’s base weights. For
431   RL, we optimize the LLM policy using the RLOO algorithm (Ahmadian et al., 2024), initializing
      both the policy and reference policy with the SFT model. We train with a value of k = 2 for RLOO,

      5   C ONCLUSION
445
      In this work, we proposed QRM, a novel approach for training a distributional reward model in the
446
      context of RLHF. Traditionally, reward models are treated as point estimators, which face challenges
447
      in accommodating diverse human preferences and handling the resulting label noise during training.
448   In contrast, estimating a distribution that can be multimodal is more powerful, capturing distinct
449   values and preferences more effectively. QRM uses quantile regression to estimate distributions
450   over attribute scores such as helpfulness and harmlessness. A gating network produces weights,
451   aggregating these individual distributions into a final reward distribution.
452
      Our experimental results demonstrate that QRM outperforms previous approaches with comparable
453
      base architectures. Notably, even when reduced to point estimates by calculating the expectation
454   of the distribution, our model showed improvements. Moreover, the distributional reward model
455   provides additional information that can be utilized for risk-aware RLHF. The final policy generated
456   by our approach produces responses with higher quantile estimates for the left tail of the distribution,
457   reducing the occurrence of extremely poor responses.
458
      Our work opens several promising avenues for future research. One exciting direction is to di-
459
      rectly incorporate conflicting labels into training. Unlike traditional preference learning datasets
460   that rely on majority-vote labels, our model could account for raw, conflicting annotations for the
461   same prompt-response pair. Additionally, it would be interesting to explore integrating our distri-
462   butional reward model with distributional RL algorithms that estimate a distribution for the value
463   function. Another promising avenue is to leverage the extra information provided by our model
464   within search algorithms during decoding. More broadly, the extra information provided by our
465   distributional reward model allows for more interpretability as well as more creative ways to steer
466   LLMs during downstream fine-tuning.
