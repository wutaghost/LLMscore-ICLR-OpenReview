009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge distillation plays a key role in compressing the Large Language Models
012            (LLMs), which boosts a small-size student model under large teacher models’ guid-
013            ance. However, existing LLM distillation methods overly rely on student-generated
014            outputs, which may introduce generation errors and misguide the distillation pro-
015            cess. Moreover, the distillation loss functions introduced in previous works struggle
016
               to align the most informative part due to the complex distribution of LLMs’ out-
               puts. To address these problems, we propose a multi-granularity semantic revision
017
               method for LLM distillation. At the sequence level, we propose a sequence cor-
018
               rection and re-generation (SCRG) strategy. SCRG first calculates the semantic
019            cognitive difference between the teacher and student to detect the error token, then
020            corrects it with the teacher-generated one, and re-generates the sequence to reduce
021            generation errors. At the token level, we design a distribution adaptive clipping
022            Kullback-Leibler (DAC-KL) loss as the distillation objective function. DAC-KL
023            loss exploits a learnable sub-network to adaptively extract semantically dense areas
024            from the teacher’s output, avoiding the interference of redundant information in
025            the distillation process. Finally, at the span level, we leverage the span priors of a
026            sequence to compute the probability correlations within spans, and constrain the
027
               teacher and student’s probability correlations to be consistent, further enhancing
               the transfer of semantic information. Extensive experiments across different model
028
               families with parameters ranging from 0.1B to 13B demonstrate the superiority of
029
               our method compared to existing methods.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The remarkable advancements in auto-regressive Large Language Models (LLMs) (Kaplan et al.,
035   2020; Wei et al., 2022; Radford et al.; Zhang et al.; Brown et al., 2020) have led to unprecedented
036   breakthroughs in a diverse array of text generative tasks, with numerous open-source models (Touvron
037   et al.; Zhang et al., 2022) now available. A crucial factor contributing to this success is the ability
038   to scale up the models, which involves increasing both the amount of training data and the number
039
      of model parameters. However, the massive size and computational intensity of these state-of-the-
      art models pose significant challenges, particularly when it comes to deployment and real-time
040
      applications. In contrast, smaller models with limited parameters often sacrifice performance on
041
      real-world generation tasks (Wang et al., 2022a). To mitigate these challenges, Knowledge Distillation
042   (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) has emerged as a pivotal technique, enabling the development of smaller,
043   more efficient student models that inherit the strengths of their larger teacher counterparts.
044
045
      Traditional knowledge distillation methods (Hinton et al., 2015; Kim & Rush, 2016) directly employ
      Kullback-Leibler divergence (KLD) as the distillation loss for aligning the output distributions of
046
      teacher and student models on a static dataset (see Figure 1 (a)). Unlike these methods, recent LLM
047
      distillation methods are exploring diverse divergence loss functions tailored to LLMs and leveraging
048   student-generated datasets to avoid distribution mismatch between the outputs student-generated
049   in the training and inference stages. GKD (Agarwal et al., 2024) and MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023)
050   propose to exploit reverse KLD as the distillation objective, replacing the commonly used forward
051   KLD. These approaches aim to prevent students from overestimating the low-probability regions of
052   the teacher’s distribution. Also, these methods train the student on self-generated sequences that are
053   on-policy instead of a fixed set of output sequences. Recently, Distillm (Ko et al., 2024) proposes an
      adaptive off-policy student-generation strategy to improve the sample efficiency and high generation

      student-generated sequences may introduce generation errors and lead to suboptimal learning, as the

078   capture and transfer the essential knowledge within the teacher model’s output distribution.
079
      To address the above-mentioned issues, we introduce a novel multi-level semantic revision approach,
080   across sequence token and span levels, to significantly improve the KD process for LLMs. At the
081   sequence level, we propose a sequence correction and re-generation (SCRG) strategy. We detect
082   the error token in the student-generated sequence and re-generate the sequence from the position
083   of the error token to reduce generation errors. As shown in Figure 1 (c), by assessing the semantic
084   cognitive differences between teacher and student outputs on a token-by-token basis, we identify
085   and correct errors, leading to re-generated sequences that steer the student model towards generating
086   more reliable samples and can be seamlessly integrated with both on-policy and off-policy generation
087   schedules. At the token level, we employ a distribution adaptive clipping Kullback-Leibler (DAC-KL)
088
      loss function, which leverages a learnable sub-network to target semantically salient regions of the
      output distribution. This loss function effectively filters out redundant information, preserving only
089
      the most relevant signals for distillation. Finally, at the span level, we incorporate pre-defined span
090
      priors of sequences to align the relations of probability vectors of the student and teacher models,
091   ensuring a consistent transfer of semantic information across related tokens within the same span.
092   Through extensive experiments with different models, including the LLAMA2, OpenLLAMA2, OPT,
093   and GPT2 series, ranging from 0.1B to 13B parameters, we showcase the superiority of our approach
094   over existing knowledge distillation methods.
095
      The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
096
097
098            • We introduce a novel multi-level semantic revision approach to enhance the knowledge
099              distillation (KD) process for large language models (LLMs).
100            • At the sequence level, we propose a sequence correction and re-generation strategy to steer
101              the student model towards generating more reliable sequences.
102
               • At the token level, we propose a distribution adaptive clipping Kullback-Leibler loss to
103              capture semantically salient regions of the output space.
104
105
               • At the span level, we incorporate input span priors to ensure a consistent transfer of semantic
                 knowledge across related tokens.
106
107            • Through extensive experimentation with models ranging from 0.1B to 13B parameters, we
                 demonstrate the superiority of our method over existing KD methods for LLMs.

109
110
111
      KD for encoder-only language models. Pretrained encoder-only language models, such as
      BERT (Jiao et al., 2019), can be compressed using the traditional logit distillation (Hinton et al.,
112
      2015) and feature distillation (Adriana et al., 2015). These knowledge distillation methods minimize
113
      the Kullback-Leibler divergence loss between the outputs of the student and teacher models on a fixed
114   dataset (Kim & Rush, 2016). Liang et al.(Liang et al., 2020) applied this objective to train students
115   on masked language modelling and text classification tasks. Jiao et al.(Jiao et al., 2019) utilized
116   intermediate representations from each transformer layer of the teacher as transferable knowledge.
117   Despite the potential of KD in encoder-only language models (Sanh et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2023;
118   Sun et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022), the complex predictions generated by large language models
119   (LLMs) through auto-regressive inference present a new challenge. This paper primarily discusses
120   KD for auto-regressive LLMs.
121
122   KD for auto-regression large language models. Existing knowledge distillation (KD) methods
123   for auto-regressive large language models (LLMs) can be divided into black-box methods for closed-
124   source models such as GPT-3.5 (Ouyang et al., 2022) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), and white-box
125   methods for open-source models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al.). Black-box methods (Chen et al.,
126   2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023) cannot access the internal parameters of the teacher
127   model and utilize only the inference results provided by the teacher API (Taori et al., 2023; Chiang
128   et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023). The inference results of the teacher model are used to construct
129
      prompt-response pairs, which serve as a new training dataset to fine-tune the student model. In
      contrast, white-box KD methods (Ko et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023) leverage the
130
      internal parameters of the teacher model, providing richer training signals such as the probability
131
      distribution of predictions, potentially leading to better student model performance. Our methods
132   primarily address the challenges of existing methods in the realm of white-box KD.

234   4.2     T OKEN - LEVEL DAC-KL LOSS FUNCTION
235
236   The probability output of LLMs is a high-dimensional vector for each token. However, existing
237   modified Kullback-Leibler divergence (KLD) loss functions, used as knowledge distillation objectives,
238

      5       E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      In this section, we experiment by initially fine-tuning a large model on the dataset comprising
327   instructions and corresponding responses (X, Y ), establishing it as the teacher model p. Subsequently,
328   we examine various knowledge distillation methods for distilling a smaller student model under the
329   guidance of the teacher, evaluating the instruction-following performance of the distilled model.
330
331   5.1       E XPERIMENTAL DESCRIPTION
332
333   Dataset and evaluation metrics. We conduct the KD experiments on five instruction-following
334   datasets: (1) Dolly Evaluation (Dolly, 2023) is a a sampled subset of atabricks-dolly-15k 1 (Dolly)
335   dataset consists of 500 samples. It covers various behavioural categories such as brainstorming,
336
      classification, closed QA, generation, information extraction, open QA, and summarization; (2)
      Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2022a) is a dataset for language models’ ability to understand and follow
337
      instructions. It incorporates 252 expert-written tasks; (3) Vicuna (Wang et al., 2022b) is a dataset
338
      consisting of 80 challenging questions used for evaluating the Vicuna model. It follows the evaluation
339   methodology introduced by MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023); (4) Super-Natural Instruction (Wang et al.,
340   2022b) is introduced as a benchmark, and this dataset contains 1,616 diverse NLP tasks along with
341   their expert-written instructions. It covers 76 different task types, and its test set consists of 9K
342   samples from 119 tasks; (5) Unnatural Instruction (Honovich et al., 2022) dataset comprises 240K
343   instructions generated by AI with minimal human involvement. It shows that AI-generated data can
344   be as effective as human-created data for training language models. The core component of this
345   dataset has 60K samples.
346   We use the ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004) metric to evaluate the model-generated results and report the
347   average scores of 5 generations for each prompt with different random seeds (10, 20, 30, 40, 50)
348   for all test datasets. ROUGE-L evaluates the precision of the model’s output by measuring the
349   longest common subsequence between the generated text and the reference text. It is well-suited for
350   large-scale instruction-following evaluation due to its ability to capture both sentence-level structure
351   and content.
352   Base models and baselines. We distil four kinds of teacher-student model pairs with different model
353   sizes: LLAMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023) (13B teacher, 7B student), OpenLLAMA2 (Geng & Liu, 2023)
354   (7B teacher, 3B student), OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) (6.7B teacher, 1.3B student), GPT2 (Radford
355   et al., 2019) (1.5B teacher, 0.1B student).
356
      We benchmark our method against several advanced knowledge distillation methods: (1) SFT
357   Fine-tunes the student model on a fixed dataset in a vanilla manner; (2) KD (Hinton et al., 2015)
358   utilizes KLD on a fixed dataset; (3) SeqKD (Kim & Rush, 2016) fine-tunes on a teacher-generated
359   dataset; (4) ImitKD (Lin et al., 2020) utilizes KLD on a dataset generated by the student model; (5)
360   GKD (Agarwal et al., 2024) utilizes Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) (Agarwal et al., 2024) on a
361   mixture of a student-generated dataset and a fixed dataset; (6) MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023) utilizes a
362   policy gradient approach on a dataset generated by the student model; (7) DistiLLM (Ko et al., 2024)
363   utilizes Skew KLD on a student-generated dataset sampling with an off-policy scheduler.
364   All of our baseline experiments are re-implemented using the open-source code 2 on the same
365   GPU servers utilized by our method. Additionally, we execute these experiments using the exact
366   hyper-parameters as specified in the original codebase.
367

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we address the challenges in knowledge distillation for LLMs by proposing a novel
534   multi-level semantic revision approach at the sequence, token, and span levels. At the sequence
535   level, our sequence correction and re-generation strategy improves reliability in student-generated
536   sequences. At the token level, the DAC-KL loss function targets semantically salient regions in the
537   teacher’s probability distribution, filtering out redundant information. At the span level, input span
538   priors ensure consistent transfer of semantic information across related tokens. Our experiments with
539   four various model series, demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, significantly improving
      student model performance over existing KD methods.

      The primary objective of this study is to contribute to the advancement of the field of Machine Learn-
705   ing, without explicitly emphasizing any specific societal consequences. Although smaller models
706   can lead to positive outcomes, such as reduced emissions, it is crucial to conduct a comprehensive
707   study on potential biases associated with model compression. However, there are potential negative
708   impacts to consider. Model compression may inadvertently exacerbate existing biases within data,
709   leading to unfair outcomes, particularly for underrepresented groups. Additionally, the simplification
710   involved in compression could result in the loss of critical nuances and reduce the model’s ability to
711   handle complex tasks accurately.

713   B    L IMITATIONS
714
715   Our experiments and evaluations were conducted within specific linguistic domains, which may limit
716   the direct applicability of our findings to other domains or tasks. Further research is necessary to
717   determine the generalizability of our approach across various contexts. Additionally, our method’s
718   reliance on an external chunker for span extraction could be a limiting factor, especially for low-
719   resource languages where such tools may not be as accessible or effective.
720   For mainstream languages, however, there is a robust ecosystem of NLP toolkits, such as SpaCy and
721   NLTK, which offer reliable chunking capabilities. These tools have been extensively developed and
722   optimized, ensuring their effectiveness and broad applicability to tasks similar to ours.
723
      For low-resource languages, we suggest that our approach could be adapted by utilizing alternative
724
      span extraction methods. For example, in the case of Chinese, the JieBa library effectively enables the
725
      extraction of spans, such as noun and verb phrases. For languages with smaller datasets or those that
726   are less resourced, employing large pretrained models like GPT-4 for data preprocessing to generate
727   spans could be a feasible solution. This unsupervised or weakly supervised approach could enhance
728   the adaptability of our method to diverse linguistic resources.

      We plan to explore these possibilities in future work, with the aim of broadening the applicability

      of our method and overcoming its current limitations to extend its utility across different language
