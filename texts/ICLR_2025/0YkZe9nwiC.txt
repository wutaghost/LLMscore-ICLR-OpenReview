                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Active learning has been a cost-efficient approach to obtaining high-performance
011
               AI models with fewer selective annotations. In scenarios where the acquisition
               of original unlabeled data poses significant challenges, active learning harnessing
012
               synthesized data instances is more promising than traditional pool-based methods.
013
               In this paper, we propose the Self-Informed Generative Active Learning (SIGnAL)
014            framework as an effective solution to actively generate and select data instances
015            for annotation and downstream model training. In SIGnAL, we propose to guide
016            the data generation based on a reinforcement learning policy, where the generator
017            is self-informed by the reward to generate more informative instances. In addition,
018            we introduce an acquisition function that measures both the informativeness and
019            relevance of instances. Such acquisition function can be transformed to the reward
020            seamlessly for generator optimization. Our experiments on the text classification
021            task validate the effectiveness of our framework, especially when the original data
022
               scale is limited.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Active learning has been an effective solution to the contradiction between the demand for super-
028   vised training data and the high cost of data annotation. High-quality data, especially data with anno-
029   tations or human feedback, is crucial for the performance of various AI models. Empirical analysis
030   has shown that even SOTA models are significantly undertrained and can benefit from more data
031   (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). However, constructing diverse, high-quality datasets
032   poses challenges, as manually annotating data can be both costly and time-intensive. Toward this
033
      end, active learning seeks to select from the data pool fewer but more informative data instances, of
      which annotations are obtained to add significant improvements to the trained models.
034
035   Existing active learning research centers on pool-based approaches, which aim to select the most
036   informative instance or batch from a pool of unlabeled data. However, pool-based methods face two
037   main limitations: First, instances from the unlabeled pool might not be optimal across the entire
038   data space. For example, techniques like regularization (DeVries, 2017; Geirhos et al., 2018; Sun
039
      et al., 2020) and adversarial perturbation (Miyato et al., 2016) create out-of-distribution (OOD) data
      that lead to better model generalization and robustness. Second, pool-based methods assume that a
040
      large unlabeled pool of data is readily accessible. In situations where collecting unlabeled data itself
041
      is costly or challenging, such as in the robotics and medical fields, the available data tend to suffer
042   from biased distributions or be insufficient for training.
043
044   These limitations motivate query-synthesizing approaches to use generative models to produce in-
045
      formative instances with higher informativeness. In theory, generative models allow broader ex-
      ploration of the data space and reduce dependence on a large unlabeled pool. However, such ex-
046
      ploration turns out to be challenging in practice for two reasons: First, optimizing the generative
047
      model requires translating the concept of informativeness into learnable signals, but informativeness
048   is difficult to be defined as a simple loss function due to the absence of a definitive correct solution
049   and its inherently delayed nature. Specifically, we cannot directly provide the most informative data
050   point for the model to learn from, and informativeness is assessed at the end of the progressive steps
051   of generation. Second, the over-optimization and randomness feature of the generative model can
052   lead to the produce of irrelevant OOD data (e.g., q in Figure 1). Although might be considered in-
053   formative following traditional definitions such as uncertainty-based methods, this type of instance
      is irrelevant to the original data distribution and can negatively impact model performance.

054
      To address the mentioned challenges in query-synthesizing active learning, in this paper, we propose
055   the Self-Informed Generative Active Learning (SIGnAL) framework (Figure 2). SIGnAL is a gen-
056   eral reinforcement learning (RL)-based framework for both data instance generation and selection
057   in active learning. The contributions of this work are as follows. First, we provide a smooth solution
058   to the challenge of the dynamic and delayed nature of informativeness through the usage of rein-
059   forcement learning. Specifically, we serve instance informativeness as the reward signal from the
060   concept of reinforcement learning to optimize the generative model, since reinforcement learning
061   is well-suited for handling dynamic optimization targets and unclear ordinary losses. Second, we
062   design an acquisition function that evaluates both the traditional informativeness and the relevance
063
      of data instances, which is transformed into the reward during training. Third, we provide a practical
      method to align large language models (LLM) with our RL-based framework and validate its effec-
064
      tiveness on text classification tasks. It is worth mentioning that SIGnAL is a general framework that
065
      has the potential of leveraging the advantages of various SOTA large generative models in different
066   tasks.

089   2.1   ACTIVE L EARNING
090
091   Current active learning approaches can be categorized as pool-based or query-synthesizing. Pool-
092   based algorithms utilize different sampling strategies to select the most informative data points from
093
      the unlabeled set, while query-synthesizing methods leverage generative models to synthesize infor-
      mative samples. Our work aligns with the latter, a less explored but increasingly significant field as
094
      the capabilities of generative models advance.
095
096   Pool-based algorithms can be further broken down into uncertainty-based methods, diversity-based
097   methods, and mixed methods. Uncertainty-based approaches quantify the uncertainty of data points
098   and select those with high uncertainty. Simple definitions of uncertainty (Nguyen & Smeulders,
099
      2004) include confidence (Lewis & Catlett, 1994), margin (Joshi et al., 2009), and entropy (Shan-
      non, 1948; Luo et al., 2013) that make use of model posterior probabilities. Recent advancement
100
      in uncertainty measurement include using Monte Carlo dropout (Gal et al., 2017) for deep neural
101
      networks and attaching a loss prediction module that estimates the loss of unlabeled data (Yoo &
102   Kweon, 2019). In contrast, diversity-based methods select batches of data points representative of
103   the unlabeled set. Clustering methods have been widely applied to choose data points closest to the
104   cluster centroids. The core-set approach (Sener & Savarese, 2017) marks a pivotal advancement in
105   diversity-based methods. It frames active learning as a core-set selection problem, which aims to
106   find a subset of the full dataset such that the model trained on the subset effectively approximates
107   the whole dataset. Mixed approaches seek to leverage the advantages of both uncertainty and diver-
      sity. For example, Batch Active learning by Diverse Gradient Embeddings (BADGE) (Ash et al.,

      Another line of research is query-synthesizing methods, which seek to utilize generative models
138
      to create informative data points. Generative Adversarial Active Learning (GAAL) (Zhu & Bento,
139   2017) is the first work that incorporates deep generative models into solving the active learning
140   problem. It employs a GAN to generate informative data points through descending the gradi-
141   ent of a simple loss function. However, GAAL reports lower accuracy than the random sampling
142   baseline, because the generative model is over-optimized and produces irrelevant and indistinguish-
143   able instances in later stages of training. Subsequent works, including Adversarial Sampling for
144   Active Learning (ASAL) (Mayer & Timofte, 2020)and the Bayesian Generative Active Deep Learn-
145   ing (BGADL) (Tran et al., 2019), use a mixed dataset of synthetic and real data. ASAL generates
146   synthetic instances and then identifies similar real data within the unlabeled pool, while BGADL ini-
147
      tially queries the unlabeled set and then synthesizes similar data points. Nevertheless, these methods
      are based on similarity instead of looking for potentially more informative data points.

      instruction-tuned large language models emerge, LLM-generated data have been widely used in

155
      work (Wang et al., 2022) introduces an automated pipeline for generating high-quality instruction-
156   tuning data with only LLMs. It conditions data generation on a set of 175 seed tasks and applies

160   human annotations. More recently, Microsoft’s Phi series (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
161   Abdin et al., 2024) has incorporated billions of tokens of textbook-quality synthetic data into its
      training process, further demonstrating the effectiveness of synthetic data.

      2.3   A LIGNMENT WITH REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
163
164   The modeling objective for large language models – predicting the next token on a large corpus
165   of text from the Internet – often diverges from the objective of following the user’s instructions
166   helpfully and safely. Yet “helpful and safe” is an abstract concept and is difficult to encode into a
167   loss function in the supervised setting since the problem has no single correct answer and involves
168   sequence decision-making. Therefore, reinforcement learning has been applied to align LMs in
169   various NLP tasks, ranging from text summarization (Ziegler et al., 2019) to story generation (Zhou
170
      & Xu, 2020). Building on these efforts, Ouyang et al. proposes the RLHF framework (Ouyang
      et al., 2022), which first trains a reward model based on human preferences, and then finetunes the
171
      LM using reinforcement learning. The success of the resulting model, InstructGPT, has established
172
      reinforcement learning as a paradigm for aligning LMs with abstract, indirect objectives.

176
177   In this section, we formally define the generative active learning problem and set up the notations
178
      for the rest of the paper. With a target classification model M (x; θ) and a generative model G(x; ϕ),
      we consider a C classification problem defined over a data space X to a label space Y = {1, ..., C}.
179
      We also consider a loss function l(·, ·) : X × Y → R.
180
181   We assume an underlying data distribution pz , and we have access to a pool of unlabeled data points
182   {xi }i∈[n] drawn i.i.d. from pz , which form the real data pool R. In addition, we can use generative
183   model to create extra data points {x′i }i∈[m] = G(x; ϕ), which form the synthetic data pool S.
184   Together, R and S form the unlabeled pool U.
185   Given an annotation budget b, the generative active learning problem can be defined as:
186
                                         min E(x,y)∼pz [l(M (x; θs ), y)]
187                                    s⊆U ,|s|≤b
188
      where θs is a set of parameters learned on a labeled set s by minimizing E(x,y)∼s [l(M (x; θ), y]
189
190   The active learning problem is the same as in the pool-based setting with two exceptions: First, we
191   introduce a new synthetic unlabeled pool S in addition to the real data pool R. Second, we also
192   consider the case where n is small; for example, n < b.

      4.2   ACQUISITION F UNCTION
247
248   Pool-based acquisition functions are designed under the assumption that the sample distribution
249   represents the underlying data distribution. However, this assumption no longer holds with the
250   presence of synthetic data, which may come from a completely different data distribution. Thus, we
251   introduce a relevance metric to informativness measurement in the generative setting.
252
      First, we define relevance as the closeness between the model encodings of a data point and its
253   neighbors. A data point xi is irrelevant if the distance between the model encodings of itself and its
254   nearest neighbor xj maximally diverge:
255
256                                              d(Φ(xi ), Φ(xj )) → ∞
257
258   Next, we follow the definition of informativeness in the work CAL (Margatina et al., 2021). A
259   data point xi is informative if the predicted likelihood between itself and its nearest neighbor xj
260   maximally diverge:
261                                       KL(p(y|xi )||p(y|xj )) → ∞
262
263   Combining these two definitions, we get the following acquisition function:
264                                                 k

                                         sxi =

269   whose predictive likelihood is contrastive with those of their neighbors while staying close to the

      5.2   BASELINES
306
307   We compare SIGnAL with five pool-based baselines, as existing query-synthesizing methods are de-
308   signed to handle image data. Random functions as a baseline where no active learning is involved.
309   Entropy (Luo et al., 2013) is the most commonly used uncertainty-based baseline method and selects
310   points with the highest predictive entropy. BERTKM (Yuan et al., 2020) is a diversity-based baseline
311   which applies applies k-means clustering using the l2 normalized BERT embeddings and chooses
312   the nearest data point to each center. BADGE (Ash et al., 2019) and CAL (Margatina et al., 2021)
313
      are two recently proposed methods that generalize the uncertainty and diversity principles. Specifi-
      cally, BADGE computes the hypothetical gradient embeddings of each data point and clusters them
314
      with k-means++. CAL chooses data points whose predictive probabilities differ from those of their
315
      neighbors.

318

323   actively generating new data throughout the process. Nonetheless, we limit the evaluation of SIG-
      nAL’s accuracy to up to 200% of the acquired data, which is sufficient for understanding its behavior.

      5.4   A NALYSIS
358
359
      We present the results of our experiments in 3. We observe that SIGnAL consistently outperforms
360   pool-based methods across all datasets and dataset sizes, especially in SST-2 0.1%, SST-2 1%, and
361   AGNEWS 0.1%.
362
363
      When comparing different dataset sizes, SIGnAL tends to perform better on smaller datasets. This
      is due to two key reasons. First, smaller datasets have a higher likelihood of being biased, as the
364
      limited data may not adequately represent the underlying distribution. In the generative setting,
365
      the model can learn to explore underrepresented areas of the data distribution, thereby mitigating
366   bias. Second, as the dataset size increases, the model experiences diminishing returns: while larger
367   datasets continue to improve performance, the rate of improvement gradually decreases.
368
369
      When comparing different datasets, we observe that SIGnAL tends to perform better on SST-2 and
      AGNEWS than on QNLI. This can be explained by the similarity between the data distribution of the
370
      task and the training distribution of the generative model. SST-2 consists of movie reviews that are
371
      either positive or negative, and AGNEWS contains new articles across categories of world, sports,
372   business, and sci/tech. These data are prevalent on the Internet, from which the training corpus of the
373   generative model is sourced. However, QNLI contains question-response pairs where the response
374   either contains the answer to the question or not. The generative model is less probable to create
375   responses that do not contain the answer to the question, which leads to a bias towards entailment
376   (response containing the answer to the question) data. Nevertheless, we observe that while the
377   generative model starts to create exclusively entailment data, it gradually learns to produce more
      not-entailment data as entailment data become less informative to the classification model. This


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      adaptive behavior explains why SIGnAL performs bette on QNLI 1% as compared to QNLI 0.1%:
379   The generative model requires time to adjust its generation policy towards producing more balanced
380   labels.
381
382
      In each experiment, we observe a consistent pattern: SIGnAL tends to underperform compared
      to pool-based methods during the early stages of training. A closer examination of the generated
383
      data reveals that, in the beginning, the generative model often produces instances similar to the
384
      in-context examples, which can lead to inefficient use of the annotation budget if these repetitive
385   instances are selected. However, as training progresses, the generative model gradually learns what
386   constitutes informative data and begins to generate instances that are more informative than real
387   data. This observation suggests a potential improvement for SIGnAL: implementing an adaptive
388   budget allocation strategy that progressively shifts from relying on real data to incorporating more
389   synthetic data.
390
      Lastly, we compare the performance of different pool-based acquisition functions. Overall, all meth-
391   ods outperform the random baseline. Among them, BERTKM achieves the best performance in
392   most experiments, highlighting the effectiveness of diversity-based sampling on smaller datasets.
393   Additionally, ENTROPY ranks among the top two performing acquisition functions across all ex-
394   periments despite its simplicity. Two combined methods, BADGE and CAL, deliver more mediocre
395   results in our experimental setting.

      6   C ONCLUSION
398
399   In this work, we introduce SIGnAL, an RL-based query-synthesizing framework that actively gen-
400   erates and selects data instances for annotation and downstream model training. We also propose an
401   AL acquisition function for a pool made up of both real and synthetic data. We have demonstrated
402   the effectiveness of SIGnAL on multiple text classification tasks with a limited unlabeled pool.
403
      SIGnAL constitutes an initial effort that integrates LLMs into the traditional AL paradigm, which
404
      implies that future works can explore improvements to SIGnAL from multiple angles. Future au-
405   thors can follow the traditional line of AL research and design more effective acquisition functions,
406   or explore more effective or computationally efficient ways of optimizing the generative model. Fur-
407   ther, while we apply SIGnAL specifically to text data in this work, it is flexible framework that can
408   be applied to other forms of data, such as images.
