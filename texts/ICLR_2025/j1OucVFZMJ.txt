011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Probabilistic time series imputation has been widely applied in real-world scenar-
014            ios due to its ability to estimate uncertainty of imputation results. Meanwhile,
015            denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) have achieved great success in
016
               probabilistic time series imputation tasks with its power to model complex dis-
               tributions. However, current DDPM-based probabilistic time series imputation
017
               methodologies are confronted with two types of challenges: 1) The backbone
018
               modules of the denoising parts are not capable of achieving sequence modeling
019            with low time complexity. 2) The architecture of denoising modules can not han-
020            dle the inter-variable and bidirectional dependencies in the time series imputation
021            problem effectively. To address the first challenge, we integrate the computational
022            efficient state space model, namely Mamba, as the backbone denosing module
023            for DDPMs. To tackle the second challenge, we carefully devise several SSM-
024            based blocks for bidirectional modeling and inter-variable relation understanding.
025            Experimental results demonstrate that our approach can achieve state-of-the-art
026            time series imputation results on multiple datasets, different missing scenarios
027
               and missing ratios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The analysis of time series can model the intrinsic patterns within time-series data, thus providing
033
      robust support for decision-making in various fields, such as meteorology McGovern et al. (2011);
      Karevan & Suykens (2020), financial analysis Xiang et al. (2022); Owusu et al. (2023); Bai et al.
034
      (2020), healthcare Morid et al. (2023); Poyraz & Marttinen (2023) and power systems Tzelepi et al.
035
      (2023); Zhou et al. (2021). To enhance the reliability of analytical outcomes, it is critical to en-
036   sure the integrity of time series. However, due to various reasons such as device failures, human
037   errors, and privacy protection, time series data can easily be incomplete with missing observations
038   at different timestamps.
039
      Time series imputation methods aim to estimate the values of missing points based on the observed
040
      points in incomplete time series, thereby restoring the integrity of the time series while preserv-
041
      ing its original statistical properties. According to the ability to provide uncertainty of estimations,
042   time series imputation methods can be categorized into the following two perspectives: 1) Deter-
043   ministic Cao et al. (2018); Cini et al. (2022); Du et al. (2023), and 2) Probabilistic Chen et al.
044   (2023b); Kim et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2018) imputation methods. Probabilistic time series im-
045   putation is particularly important in dealing with complex and uncertain data environments, as it
046   provides a quantification of uncertainty for the imputations. The key to probabilistic imputation lies
047   in modeling the posterior distribution. Existing probabilistic time series imputation methods include
048   Gaussian Process and Variational Autoencoder-based methods Fortuin et al. (2020), Normalization
049   Flow-based methods Rasul et al. (2021), and Diffusion-based methods Tashiro et al. (2021). Among
050
      these, the Diffusion-based method has emerged as the optimal choice for probabilistic time series
      due to their accuracy in posterior modeling and adaptability to different scenarios and various types
051
      of time series data.
052
053   When selecting a denoising backbone in the diffusion model, the following two key factors need to
      be considered: 1) Model compatibility, and 2) Time complexity. Model compatibility involves

054
      two key aspects: 1) the backbone of the model should be capable of handling input data effectively.
055   2) the backbone of the model should align with the model’s intended objective (i.e., in diffusion
056   models, the backbone must be capable of modeling noise in the diffusion process). Specifically,
057   the missing observations in time series have correlations with their neighbors on both sides, so it
058   is crucial to design a model by considering information from neighbors of both sides. Moreover,
059   it is also essential to accurately capture the properties of time series, such as global dependencies
060   and channel correlations. Three mainstream denoising backbones are widely used in diffusion mod-
061   els for time series imputation: 1) Convoluational Neural Networks (CNNs)-, 2) Transformer- and
062   3) State-Space Model (SSM)-based backbones. Given a time series with a length of L, the CNNs-
063
      based backbone can capture partial information from the neighbors within the receptive fields and
      has O(L) time complexity. The transformer-based backbone can model temporal dependencies
064
      across the entire time series but is with quadratic time complexity O(L2 ). The SSM backbone has
065
      a linear time complexity, O(L), but it falls short in capturing the information from one side of the
066   neighbor. Moreover, all these backbones fail to capture the channel dependencies in time series. The
067   comparison results of existing backbones and our method in terms of various dependencies and time
068   complexity are presented in Table.1.

080   In this paper, we propose an efficient diffusion-based framework for probabilistic time series imputa-
081   tion to address the drawbacks in existing backbones of time series imputation, we name it DiffImp.
082   To ensure linear complexity, we choose the SSM-based model as the backbone of our framework,
083   which is Mamba Dao & Gu (2024) to be more specific. Though there has been SSM-based diffusion
084
      backbones, there remains a question whether the Mamba block is an effective backbone for time
      series imputation problem and how to design modules suitable for time series imputation problems
085
      based on Mamba blocks. To enable Mamba to capture information from both sides of the missing
086
      values, we then propose a Bidirectional Attention Mamba block (BAM) that is more applicable to
087   time series imputation task. To incorporate bidirectional dependencies, we design a learnable weight
088   module inside the BAM block. This module learns the weights of all points within the sequence,
089   facilitating the modeling of dependencies at different distances.
090
      Next, we propose a Channel Mamba Block (CMB) to capture the dependencies among different
091
      channels in a time series. Specifically, we treat the variables across different channels in the time
092
      series as a sequence of variables and employ the Mamba model alongside the channel dimension, so
093   inter-dependencies among channels can be modeled.
094
095   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
096
097          • We propose DiffImp, an efficient diffusion-based model for the time series imputation task.
098
               It integrates mamba-based blocks as diffusion backbones and equips the model with the
               capability of probabilistic time series imputation with linear time and space complexity.
099
100
             • We propose Channel Mamba Block and Bidirectional Attention Mamba block to capture
101            the sequential correlations and channel dependencies inside the time series. The bidirec-
102            tional attention mamba block and channel mamba block can effectively model the multi-
103            variate time series with missing values.
104
105          • We conduct experiments on multiple real-world datasets for both time series imputation
106            and time series forecasting tasks. The experimental results demonstrate that our approach
107            achieves state-of-the-art performance across several datasets, different missing scenarios
               and missing ratios.

242
243   The Overall Module Architecture Fig.1 illustrates the overall self-supervised framework and train-
244   ing process of our model. We first mask part of the observed values according to the pattern of
245   missing values, where the masked values serve as the imputation target X0 during training. The
246   remaining observed values form the conditional input Xoc for the noise prediction network ϵθ . We
247   then combine X0 with noise ϵ sampled from a standard normal distribution to obtain the noisy input
248   Xt . Both Xoc , Xt , and the diffusion step t are fed into the noise prediction network ϵθ to get the
249
      parameterized noise. The network minimizes the difference between ϵθ and ϵ according to Eq.11.
250   As shown in Fig.2, the forward process of ϵθ are as follows: For each diffusion step, the input con-
251   sists of the following parts: noisy input Xt , the condition input Xoc and the diffusion step t. To begin
252   with, the inputs are embedded to the latent diffusion space. The embedding module of noisy inputs
253   and condition inputs share a similar model structure, which consists of a linear projection module
254   followed by an SMM block in Fig.3b. The SMM block is composed of stacks of Bidirectional At-
255
      tention Mamba (BAM) blocks and Channel Mamba Blocks (CMB), which is introduced in the next
      part. Due to the relatively limited information from t, the embedding module of t only consists of
256
      linear projection modules. After the embedding step, the embedded diffusion step is concatenated
257
      with the input embeddings. The concatenated embeddings are fed in to a SMM module. Then the
258   output of the SMM module is concatenated with the condition embeddings. After feeding the final
259   embeddings to another SMM module and final projection module, we can get the noise predictions
260   ϵθ (Xt , t). The training and sampling algorithm is detailed in Alg.1 and Alg.2.

505   implemented using Hu et al. (2018) to validate the effiectiveness of CMB block. All experiments are
506   conducted on the MuJoCo dataset with the missing ratio 90%. During ablation experiments, we find
507   out that our model converges much slower than other models in the ablation experiment, so we train
508   till all models are converged (for same number of iterations, even if it has already been converged).
509   The hyperparameters in the ablation studies are presented in the appendix.
510   The results are shown in Table.9. It can be observed that the module equipped with BAM and
511   CMB block performs the best, significantly outperforming the results of removing any one of these
512   components across all four metrics. The temporal attention module has the largest impact on the
513   model, and its removal leads to a significant performance drop. Similarly, removing the CMB
514   module also results in a notable degradation in performance. On the other hand, adjusting the BAM
515   module to its unidirectional form also causes some degree of performance decrease. This fully
516   demonstrates the effectiveness of our proposed blocks.

524   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   In this paper, we propose DiffImp, a time series imputation model based on DDPM and Mamba
527   backbone, which incorporates bidirectional information flow, temporal attention and inter-variable
528
      dependencies. DiffImp enables efficient time series modeling with linear complexity. Experimental
      results demonstrate that DiffImp achieves superior performance across multiple datasets, various
529
      missing patterns, and different missing ratios.
530
531   For future work, one possible direction is to further reduce the time complexity of the sampling
532   process while already lowering the complexity of time series modeling, in order to enhance the
533   model’s inference efficiency. Another possible direction is to extend the application of diffusion
534
      models by applying DiffImp to other time series downstream tasks and time series representation
      learning tasks.

771

773   cess and the reverse process. The forward process of the DDPM model is defined by a Markov
774   chain, which adds noise sampled from standard gaussian noise to initial data distribution q0 step by
775   step until q0 is transformed to standard gaussian distribution qT = N (0, I). In every single step, the

838
      corresponding to 69,764 training samples and 8,812 test samples.
839
840   MuJoCo: The MuJoCo dataset Rubanova et al. (2019b) collects a total of 10,000 simulations of
841   the ”Hopper” model from the DeepMind Control Suite and MuJoCo simulator. The position of the
842   body in 2D space is uniformly sampled from the interval [0, 0.5]. The relative position of the limbs
843
      is sampled from the range [−2, 2], and initial velocities are sampled from the interval [−5, 5]. In all,
      there are 10000 sequences of 100 regularly sampled time points with a feature dimension of 14 and
844
      a random split of 80/20 is done for training and testing. We follow the same preprocessing as in
845
      Shan et al. (2023b) for fair comparison.
846
847   Electricity: The Electricity dataset from the UCI repository Asuncion & Newman (2007) contains
848   electricity usage data (in kWh) collected from 370 clients every 15 minutes. The dataset is collected
849
      and preprocessed as described in Du et al. (2023). Since the dataset does not contain missing values,
      values of the complete dataset are randomly dropped for the computation of targets according to the
850
      RM scenario and the data is already normalized. The first 10 months of data (2011/01 - 2011/10)
851
      are designated as the test set, the following 10 months of data (2011/11 - 2012/08) as the validation
852   set, and the remaining data (2012/09 - 2014/12) as the training set. The training and test sets are
853   directly utilized, while the validation set is excluded. The dataset comprises 817 samples, each with
854   a length of 100 time steps and the aforementioned 370 features. Specifically, the 370 channels are
855   split into 10 batches of 37 features each. Mini-batches of 43 samples, each containing 37 features
856   and a respective length of 100, are then passed to the network to ensure that no data is dropped
857   during training.
858   ETTm1: This dataset contains the amount of detail required for long-time series forecasting based
859   on the Electricity Transformer Temperature (ETT). The data set contains information from a compi-
860   lation of 2-year data from two distinct Chinese counties. In our experiment, we work with ETTm1
861   which covers data at a 15-minute level. The data is composed of the target value oil tempera-

863   cover five different forecasting horizons {24, 48, 96, 288, 672} with corresponding observed length
      {96, 48, 384, 288, 384}.
