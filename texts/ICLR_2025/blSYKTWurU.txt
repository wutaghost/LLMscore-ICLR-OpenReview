009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved promising results for Structure-Based Drug Design
012            (SBDD). Nevertheless, high-quality protein subpocket and ligand data are rela-
013            tively scarce, which hinders the models’ generation capabilities. Recently, Direct
014            Preference Optimization (DPO) has emerged as a pivotal tool for aligning genera-
015            tive models with human preferences. In this paper, we propose D ECOMP D PO, a
016
               structure-based optimization method aligns diffusion models with pharmaceutical
               needs using multi-granularity preference pairs. D ECOMP D PO introduces decompo-
017
               sition into the optimization objectives and obtains preference pairs at the molecule
018
               or decomposed substructure level based on each objective’s decomposability. Ad-
019            ditionally, D ECOMP D PO introduces a physics-informed energy term to ensure
020            reasonable molecular conformations in the optimization results. Notably, D ECOM -
021            P D PO can be effectively used for two main purposes: (1) fine-tuning pretrained
022            diffusion models for molecule generation across various protein families, and (2)
023            molecular optimization given a specific protein subpocket after generation. Exten-
024            sive experiments on the CrossDocked2020 benchmark show that D ECOMP D PO
025            significantly improves model performance, achieving up to 95.2% Med. High
026            Affinity and a 36.2% success rate for molecule generation, and 100% Med. High
027
               Affinity and a 52.1% success rate for molecular optimization.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Structure-based drug design (SBDD) (Anderson, 2003) is a strategic approach in medicinal chemistry
031
      and pharmaceutical research that utilizes 3D structures of biomolecules to guide the design and
032   optimization of new therapeutic agents. The goal of SBDD is to design molecules that bind to specific
033   protein targets. Recent studies viewed this problem as a conditional generative task in a data-driven
034   way, and introduced powerful generative models equipped with geometric deep learning (Powers
035   et al., 2023). For example, Peng et al. (2022); Zhang & Liu (2023) proposed to generate the atoms
036   or fragments sequentially by a SE(3)-equivariant auto-regressive model, while Luo et al. (2021);
037   Peng et al. (2022); Guan et al. (2023a) introduced diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) to model the
038   distribution of types and positions of ligand atoms. However, the scarcity of high-quality protein-
039   ligand complex data has emerged as a significant bottleneck for the development of generative models
040
      in SBDD (Vamathevan et al., 2019). The success of deep learning typically relies on large-scale
      datasets. In fields like computer vision and natural language processing, the proliferation of the
041
      social media has greatly simplified the collection of text, images, and videos, thereby accelerating
042
      advancements. In contrast, the collection of protein-ligand binding data is challenging and limited
043   due to the complex and resource-intensive experimental procedures. Notably, the CrossDocked2020
044   dataset (Francoeur et al., 2020), a widely-used dataset for SBDD, consists of ligands that are
045   docked into multiple similar binding pockets across the Protein Data Bank using docking software.
046   This may be regarded as a form of data augmentation; while it expands the dataset’s size, it may
047   unavoidably introduce some low-quality data. As highlighted by Zhou et al. (2024a), the ligands
048   in the CrossDocked2020 dataset have moderate binding affinities, which do not meet the stringent
049   demands of drug design. Moreover, the number of unique ligands remains the same before and after
050   this data augmentation, limiting generative models from learning diverse and high-quality molecules.
051   To address the aforementioned challenge, Xie et al. (2021); Fu et al. (2022) provided a straight-
052   forward method for searching molecules with desired properties in the extensive chemical space.
053   However, pure searching or optimization methods lack generative capabilities and fall short in the
      diversity of the designed molecules. Zhou et al. (2024a) integrated conditional diffusion models with


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      iterative optimization by providing molecular substructures as conditions and iteratively replacing the
055   substructures with better ones. This method achieves better properties while maintaining a certain
056   level of diversity. Nonetheless, the performance of this method is still limited due to fixed model
057   parameters during the optimization process.
058
      To break the bottleneck, we propose a method for multi-objective optimization, aligning diffusion
059
      models with practical pharmaceutical requirements of drug discovery using generated data. Inspired
060   by the decomposition nature of ligand molecules, D ECOMP D PO introduces decomposition into
061   optimization objective to provide more flexibility in preference selection and alignment. Based
062   on each objective’s decomposability, D ECOMP D PO directly aligns model with preferences using
063   G LOBAL DPO with molecule pairs or L OCAL DPO with decomposed substructure pairs. Recog-
064   nizing the importance of maintaining reasonable molecular conformations during optimization,
065   D ECOMP D PO integrates physics-informed energy terms to penalize molecules with poor confor-
066   mations. Additionally, a linear beta schedule is proposed for improving optimization efficiency.
067   We apply D ECOMP D PO to two scenarios: structure-based molecule generation and structure-based
068
      molecular optimization. Under both settings, D ECOMP D PO can significantly outperform baselines,
      demonstrating its effectiveness. We highlight our contributions as follows:
069
070   • We propose D ECOMP D PO, which introduces decomposition into the optimization objectives to
071     improve optimization effectiveness and flexibility, directly aligning generative diffusion models
072     with practical pharmaceutical requirements using multi-granularity preferences.
073   • Our approach is applicable to both structure-based molecular generation and optimization. No-
074     tably, D ECOMP D PO achieves 95.2% Med. High Affinity and a 36.2% success rate for molecule
075
        generation, and 100% Med. High Affinity and a 52.1% success rate for molecular optimization on
        CrossDocked2020 dataset.
076
      • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to introduce preference alignment to structure-based
077
        drug design. Our approach aligns the generative models for SBDD with the practical requirements
078
        of drug discovery.
079
080   Recently, an independent concurrent work by Gu et al. (2024) proposed a different framework that also
081   uses preference alignment methods to fine-tune diffusion models for SBDD. Notably, they regularized
082
      the DPO objective to alleviate overfitting on the winning data. However, they primarily focused on
      optimizing affinity-related metrics for molecule generation and did not evaluate optimized molecular
083
      conformations, which is an important aspect in drug design. Compared to Gu et al. (2024), we directly
084
      formulate preference alignment in SBDD as a multi-objective optimization problem, which is more
085   aligned with pharmaceutical needs, and introduce decomposition into the optimization objectives to
086   provide more flexibility in multi-objective optimization. In addition, we incorporate physics-informed
087   energy terms to penalize unreasonable molecular conformations, thereby maintaining desirable
088   conformations during optimization. Moreover, we demonstrate the effectiveness of D ECOMP D PO in
089   molecular optimization through iterative fine-tuning, achieving superior performance compared to
090   existing optimization methods.

093   Structure-based Drug Design Structure-based drug design (SBDD) aims to design ligand
094   molecules that can bind to specific protein targets. Recent efforts have been made to enhance
095   the efficiency of generating molecules with desired properties. Ragoza et al. (2022) employed
096   variational autoencoder to generate 3D molecules in atomic density grids. Luo et al. (2021); Peng
097   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022) adopted an autoregressive approach to generate 3D molecules atom
098   by atom, while Zhang et al. (2022) proposed to generate 3D molecules by predicting a series of
099
      molecular fragments in an auto-regressive way. Guan et al. (2023a); Schneuing et al. (2022); Lin
      et al. (2022) introduced diffusion models to SBDD, which first generate the types and positions
100
      of atoms and subsequently determine bond types by post-processing. Some recent studies have
101
      sought to further improve efficacy of SBDD methods by incorporating biochemical prior knowledge.
102   DecompDiff (Guan et al., 2023b) proposed decomposing ligands into substructures and generat-
103   ing atoms and bonds simultaneously using diffusion models with decomposed priors and validity
104   guidance. DrugGPS (Zhang & Liu, 2023) considered subpocket-level similarities, augmenting
105   molecule generation through global interaction between subpocket prototypes and molecular motifs.
106   IPDiff (Huang et al., 2023) addressed the inconsistency between forward and reverse processes using
107   a pre-trained protein-ligand interaction prior network. In addition to simply generative modeling of
      existing protein-ligand pairs, some researchers leveraged optimization algorithms to design molecules

145   Learning from Human/AI Feedback Maximizing likelihood optimization of generative models
146   cannot always satisfy users’ preferences. Thus introducing human or AI assessment to improve the
147   performance of generative models has attracted significant attention. Reinforcement learning from
148   human/AI feedback (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023;
149   Bai et al., 2022) was proposed to align large language models to human preference, consisting of
150   reward modeling from comparison data annotated by human or AI and then using policy-gradient
151   methods (Christiano et al., 2017; Schulman et al., 2017) to fine-tune the model to maximize the
152   reward. Similar techniques have also been introduced to diffusion models for text-to-image generation
153
      (Black et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), where the generative process is viewed as a
      multi-step Markov decision process and policy-gradient methods can be then applied to fine-tuning
154
      the models. Recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), which aligns
155
      large language models to human preferences by directly optimizing on human comparison data,
156   has attracted much attention. Wallace et al. (2023) re-formulated DPO and derived Diffusion-DPO
157   for aligning text-to-image diffusion models. The above works focus on aligning large language
158   models or text-to-image diffusion models with human preferences. Recently, Zhou et al. (2024b)
159   proposed to fine-tune diffusion models for antibody design by DPO and choose low Rosetta energy
160   as preference. In our work, we introduce preference alignment to improve the desired properties of
161   generated molecules given specific protein pockets and propose specialized methods to improve the
      performance of DPO in the scenario of SBDD.

168
      molecular conformations during optimization, we introduce physics-informed energy terms for penal-
169   izing the reward in Section 3.3. Finally, we introduce a linear beta schedule to improve the efficiency
170   of optimizing diffusion models (Section 3.4).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      of the angle formed by atoms i, j, and k; µlvi ,vj and σvl i ,vj are the expectation and standard deviation
325
      of bond lengths between atom types vi and vj , obtained from the training data. Similarly, µavi ,vj ,vk
326
      and σvai ,vj ,vk represent the expectation and standard deviation of bond angles formed by atom types
327
      vi , vj , and vk . The overall energy term is defined as rconstraint = Ebond + Eangle . To prevent the model
328
      from learning unrealistic molecular conformations, we adjust the reward by penalizing it with this
329   energy term: r∗ (M, P) = r(M, P) − λrconstraint (M, P), where λ is a weighting factor that balances
330   the importance of the physical constraints.
331
332   3.4   L INEAR B ETA S CHEDULE
333   Drawing from Equation (4), the parameter β serves as a form of regularization, balancing the
334   exploration of high-quality molecules with adherence to the pre-learned prior distribution. During the
335   diffusion sampling process, earlier steps influence the subsequent ones. Moreover, the final few steps
336   are crucial in determining the atoms’ types and positions, which significantly affect the molecules’
337   properties and make optimization in the later steps more critical. To improve optimization efficiency,
338   we propose a linear beta schedule βt = Tt βT , where βT is the beta parameter at the final time step T
339   of the reverse process. This schedule ensures a progressive reduction in the impact of regularization,
340
      enhancing alignment with the desired properties as the diffusion process progresses.

342
343   Considering the practical demands of the pharmaceutical industry, we implement D ECOMP D PO to
344   address two critical needs: (1) fine-tuning the reference model for molecule generation across various
345   protein families, and (2) optimizing the reference model specifically for targeted protein subpockets.
346   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
347
348   Dataset We followed prior work (Luo et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2023a;b) in using
349
      the CrossDocked2020 dataset (Francoeur et al., 2020) to pre-train our base model and evaluate the
      performance of D ECOMP D PO. According to the protocol established by Luo et al. (2021), we filtered
350
      complexes to retain only those with high-quality docking poses (RMSD < 1Å) and diverse protein
351
      sequences (sequence identity < 30%), resulting in a refined dataset comprising 100,000 high-quality
352
      training complexes and 100 novel proteins for evaluation.
353
354   To fine-tune with D ECOMP D PO, we sample 10 molecules for each protein in the training dataset using
355
      pre-trained base model. The
                               P favorability of each molecule was evaluated based on a multi-objective
      score defined as rmulti = xi ∈X xi , where X denotes the set of normalized optimization objectives.
356
      For each protein, we select the molecules with the highest and lowest scores to form preference pairs
357
      for the fine-tuning process, resulting in 63,092 valid pairs. For molecular optimization, we sample
358   500 molecules for each target protein with fine-tuned model in the test dataset and and construct
359   preference pairs from the top 100 and bottom 100 molecules based on their scores.

361   Baselines To assess the capability of D ECOMP D PO fine-tuned model in generating high-quality
362   molecules across various protein families, we compare it with several representative generative

364   both ligand and receptor into a latent space, subsequently generating atomic densities for ligands.

366   and GraphBP (Liu et al., 2022) update atom embeddings using a graph neural network (GNN).

      TargetDiff (Guan et al., 2023a) and DecompDiff (Guan et al., 2023b) utilize GNN-based diffusion

368

369
      pockets and ligands into both the diffusion forward and sampling processes. In addition, to evaluate
370   the molecular optimization capabilities of D ECOMP D PO, we compared it with two strong optimization
371   methods. RGA (Fu et al., 2022), which utilizes a reinforced genetic algorithm to simulate evolutionary
372   processes and optimize a policy network across iterations, and DecompOpt (Zhou et al., 2024a),
373   which leverages decomposed priors to control and optimize conditions in a diffusion model.

519   In this work, we introduced preference alignment to SBDD for the first time, developing D ECOM -
520   P D PO to align pre-trained diffusion models with multi-granularity preference, which provides more
521   flexibility during the optimization process. The physics-informed energy term penalizing the reward
522   is beneficial for maintaining reasonable molecular conformations during optimization. The linear beta
523   schedule effectively improves optimization efficiency by progressively reducing regularization during
524   the diffusion process. D ECOMP D PO shows promising results in molecule generation and molecular
525   optimization, highlighting its ability to meet practical needs of the pharmaceutical industry.

719   C     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
720
      C.1   F EATURIZATION
721
722   Following DecompDiff (Guan et al., 2023b), we characterize each protein atom using a set of features:
723   a one-hot indicator of the element type (H, C, N, O, S, Se), a one-hot indicator of the amino acid type
724   to which the atom belongs, a one-dimensional indicator denoting whether the atom belongs to the
725   backbone, and a one-hot indicator specifying the arm/scaffold region. We define the part of proteins
726   that lies within 10Å of any atom of the ligand as pocket. Similarly, a protein atom is assigned to the
727   arm region if it lies within a 10Å radius of any arm; otherwise, it is categorized under the scaffold
728   region. The ligand atom is characterized with a one-hot indicator of element type (C, N, O, F, P,
729
      S, Cl) and a one-hot arm/scaffold indicator. The partition of arms and scaffold is predefined by a
      decomposition algorithm proposed by DecompDiff.
730
731   We use two types of message-passing graphs to model the protein-ligand complex: a k-nearest
732   neighbors (knn) graph for all atoms (we choose k = 32 in all experiments) and a fully-connected
733   graph for ligand atoms only. In the knn graph, edge features are obtained from the outer product of
734
      the distance embedding and the edge type. The distance embedding is calculated using radial basis
735
      functions centered at 20 points between 0Å and 10Å. Edge types are represented by a 4-dimensional
      one-hot vector, categorizing edges as between ligand atoms, protein atoms, ligand-protein atoms or
736
      protein-ligand atoms. For the fully-connected ligand graph, edge features include a one-hot bond type
737
      indicator (non-bond, single, double, triple, aromatic) and a feature indicating whether the bonded
738   atoms belong to the same arm or scaffold.
739

      C.3   M OLECULAR F RAGMENTATION
771
772   Following DecompDiff (Guan et al., 2023b), we fragment a molecule into arms and scaffold using
773   RDKit and Alphaspace2 (Katigbak et al., 2020) toolkit. Specifically, subpockets for the target
774   protein is extracted using Alphaspace2 and ligands are decomposed into fragments using BRICS.
775
      Then terminal fragments with only one connection site are assigned to subpockets by a linear sum
      assignment. Arms centers are defined as the centroids of terminal fragments and any remaining
776
      subpockets, and scaffold center is defined as the farthest fragment from all arm centers. Finally, the
777
      nearest neighbor clustering is performed to tag fragments as arms or the scaffold.

779   C.4   T RAINING D ETAILS
780   Pre-training We use Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) for pre-training,                               with
781   init_learning_rate=0.0004 and betas=(0.95,0.999).                          The learning rate is
782   scheduled to decay exponentially with a factor of 0.6 with minimize_learning_rate=1e-6.
783   The learning rate is decayed if there is no improvement for the validation loss in 10 consecutive
784   evaluations. We set batch_size=8 and clip_gradient_norm=8. During training, a
785   small Gaussian noise with a standard deviation of 0.1 to protein atom positions is added as data
786   augmentation. To balance the magnitude of different losses, the reconstruction losses of atom and
787   bond type are multiplied with weights γv = 100 and γb = 100, separately. We perform evaluations
788
      for every 2000 training steps. The model is pre-trained on a single NVIDIA A6000 GPU, and it
      could converge within 21 hours and 170k steps.
789
790   Fine-tuning and Optimizing For both fine-tuning and optimizing model with D ECOMP D PO,
791   we use the Adam optimizer with init_learning_rate=1e-6 and betas=(0.95,0.999).
792   We maintain a constant learning rate throughout both processes. We set batch_size=4 and
793   clip_gradient_norm=8. Consistent with pre-training, Gaussian noise is added to protein atom
794   positions, and we use a weighted reconstruction loss. For fine-tuning model for molecule generation,
795   we set βT = 0.001 and trained for 1 epoch, 16k steps on one NVIDIA A40 GPU. For molecular
796   optimization, we set βT = 0.02 and trained for 20,000 steps on one NVIDIA V100 GPU, and perform
797   evaluation every every 1,000 steps.

      The scoring function for selecting training molecules is defined as S = QED + SA +
      V ina_M in/(−12). Vina Minimize Score is divided by -12 to ensure that it is generally ranges
801
      between 0 and 1. For molecule generation, we exclude molecules that cannot be decomposed or
802
      reconstructed, resulting in a total of 63,092 preference pairs available for fine-tuning. In molec-
803   ular optimization, to ensure that the model maintains a desirable completion rate, we include an
804   additional 50 molecules that failed in reconstruction in as the losing side of preference pairs. To
805   tailor the optimization to a specific protein, the weights of the optimization objectives are defined as
806   wx = e−(x−xs ) , where x is the mean property of the generated molecules and xs is the threshold of
807   the property used in Success Rate. For both molecule generation and molecular optimization, we
808   employ the same Opt Prior used in DecompDiff. Opt Prior is defined as a mixture of Ref Prior,
809   which is determined by the reference ligand, and Pocket Prior, which is defined by a prior generation
      algorithm using AlphaSpace2 (Katigbak et al., 2020), depending on whether Ref Prior passes the
