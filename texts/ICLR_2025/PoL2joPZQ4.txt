009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advancements in text-to-3D generation, building on the success of high-
012            performance text-to-image generative models, have made it possible to create
013            imaginative and richly textured 3D objects from textual descriptions. How-
014            ever, a key challenge remains in effectively decoupling light-independent and
015            lighting-dependent components to enhance the quality of generated 3D models
016            and their relighting performance. In this paper, we present MVLight, a novel
017            light-conditioned multi-view diffusion model that explicitly integrates lighting
018            conditions directly into the generation process. This enables the model to synthe-
019            size high-quality images that faithfully reflect the specified lighting environment
020
               across multiple camera views. By leveraging this capability to Score Distilla-
               tion Sampling (SDS), we can effectively synthesize 3D models with improved
021
               geometric precision and relighting capabilities. We validate the effectiveness
022
               of MVLight through extensive experiments and a user study. Demo videos:
023            sites.google.com/view/iclr2025-7780/

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The evolution of 3D content generation marks a significant advancement in the modern entertain-
030   ment industry, including virtual reality (VR), animation, video games, and so on. Traditionally,
031   creating a single 3D asset is a labor-intensive process, requiring hours or even days of meticulous
032   work from professional designers. Thanks to the recent breakthroughs in 3D generation models,
033
      driven by textual prompts, non-professional users now enable to effortlessly create their own 3D
      models without a background in art or computer graphics.
034
035   A seminal work, DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2023), pioneered text-to-3D generation by harnessing
036   high-performance text-to-image diffusion models such as Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al.,
037   2022), along with a novel Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) strategy. This approach bypasses
038   the need for direct 3D generation models and synthesizes high-quality 3D models that accurately
039
      reflect the textual prompts. However, since text-to-image diffusion models are primarily designed
      for 2D applications, they lack the necessary geometric multi-view understanding for consistent 3D
040
      representation. This limitation often leads to issues such as the Janus multi-face problem or content
041
      inconsistencies across different camera views. To address these challenges, recent studies have
042   introduced multi-view diffusion models, e.g., MVDream (Shi et al., 2024), which refine text-to-2D
043   models using 3D self-attention module and multi-view datasets.
044
045
      Another critical aspect in 3D modeling is the ability to generate relightable models that adapt to
      varying light conditions. For instance, a rendered 3D object viewed under noon sunlight should
046
      be visually different from one observed in the evening. Existing relightable 3D generation meth-
047
      ods usually employ Physically Based Rendering (PBR) materials—albedo, roughness, and metallic
048   properties—to achieve this effect. These materials allow for the decoupling of light-independent
049   and light-dependent components, enabling post-generation lighting adjustments. Typically, these
050   models are firstly trained on geometry and albedo properties using multi-view diffusion models, and
051   subsequently fine-tuned on roughness and metallic properties using additional text-to-image mod-
052   els like Stable Diffusion v2 (Rombach et al., 2022). Here, the challenge arises because multi-view
053   diffusion models and text-to-image models typically do not specify lighting information. Conse-
      quently, when estimating PBR materials, the absence of direct lighting data complicates the task of


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      decoupling light-independent and light-dependent components, which in turn adversely affects the
055   quality of relighting effects.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce a novel light-conditioned multi-view diffusion model, MVLight, which
058
      is designed to generate multi-view consistent images under specified lighting conditions. Also, to
      improve geometric precision and material decomposition, MVLight can additionally generate multi-
059
      view consistent normal maps and albedo images. By distilling these properties into SDS, we enable
060
      robust and stable training scenarios, as multiple images of an object under varying lighting condi-
061   tions and camera views can be synthesized to represent the same object. Moreover, unlike most
062   of existing relightable text-to-3D models that rely on Stable Diffusion for PBR material estima-
063   tion using randomly selected lighting environment, MVLight enables exploiting the same lighting
064   environment for PBR and diffusion outputs for SDS, resulting in more precise disentanglement of
065   light-dependent and light-independent components. We demonstrate the effectiveness of MVLight
066   through comprehensive quantitative and qualitative experiments compared to existing relightable
067   text-to-3D generative models..
068   In short, out contributions can be summarized as:
069
070          • We firstly propose a novel light-conditioned multi-view diffusion model, MVLight, which
071            ensures consistent geometry and specified lighting properties across all output views.
072          • We validate that the multi-view and light-aware SDS from MVLight improves both the
073            geometric fidelity and relighting capability of the generated 3D models.
074          • We demonstrate the superiority of MVLight over existing methods via extensive experi-
075            ments and user study.

077   2     R ELATED W ORK
078
079   In this section, we review the previous literature on text-to-3D generative models, with a specific
080   focus on multi-view and relightable approaches. Also, we highlight the key differences between
081   existing methods and our proposed MVLight.
082
083   2.1   TEXT- TO -3D G ENERATION USING 2D DIFFUSION MODELS
084
085   Recent advances in text-to-image generation have achieved remarkable success, largely due to the
086   availability of large-scale datasets such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), which pair images
087
      with textual descriptions, and vision-language models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). This success
      has opened new possibilities for 3D generation by leveraging the strong generative performance of
088
      2D diffusion models. Pioneering works such as DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2023) and SJC (Wang
089
      et al., 2023a) introduced the Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) strategy, which distills knowledge
090   from powerful text-to-image models to generate 3D assets by optimizing representations like Neural
091   Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2024), DMTet
092   (Shen et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), or Gaussian Splatting
093   (Kerbl et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024). Subsequent approaches have focused on
094   enhancing output quality by refining the optimization process, for example, through re-designing
095   the sampling schedule (Tang et al., 2023) or proposing novel loss functions to replace SDS (Liang
096   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2024). However, the primary limitation of these 2D-
097   lifting approaches lies in their reliance on 2D models, which lack 3D consistency across different
098
      camera views. This results in sub-optimal 3D models, manifesting issues like the Janus problem or
      content drift.
099
100
      2.2   M ULTI - VIEW D IFFUSION MODELS FOR TEXT- TO -3D G ENERATION
101
102   To address these limitations, MVDream (Shi et al., 2024) introduced a novel multi-view diffusion
103   model, which fine-tunes Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al., 2022) to generate consistent multi-view
104   images instead of single-view outputs. This was achieved by replacing the self-attention mechanism
105   in the denosing U-Net of the diffusion model with multi-view attention, allowing multiple views to
106   share knowledge and maintain 3D consistency. By leveraging this 3D-aware diffusion prior to SDS,
107   text-to-3D generation model has significantly improved multi-view coherence in rendered 3D mod-
      els. Following, RichDreamer (Qiu et al., 2024) and UniDream (Liu et al., 2023) propose multi-view


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      diffusion models that go beyond generating multi-view RGB images. RichDreamer incorporates
109   multi-view depth and normal maps, while UniDream introduces albedo and normal maps to distill
110   strong geometric priors during 3D optimization. These models demonstrate improved geometric
111   fidelity and visual consistency across multiple views.
112
113   2.3   R ELIGHTABLE 3D G ENERATION
114
115   An additional challenge in 3D model synthesis is to generate relightable models that can adapt to
116   varying lighting conditions, offering more natural and realistic renderings. Recent works have tack-
117   led this by conditioning NeRF on lighting conditions, allowing the output of the MLP to be different
118   based on the specified lighting (Zhao et al., 2024) for relighting 3D outputs without inverse render-
119
      ing. Others have adopted Physically Based Rendering (PBR) materials—such as albedo, metallic,
      and roughness—instead of directly estimating RGB values, enabling models to be relightable under
120
      any lighting environment (Xu et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024).
121
122   When it comes to text-to-3D generation, Fantasia3D (Chen et al., 2023) was the first to integrate a
123   PBR pipeline with SDS, producing detailed 3D objects with realistic surface materials. Following,
124   RichDreamer (Qiu et al., 2024) optimizes the geometric properties of 3D models using multi-view
125
      normal-depth diffusion models and then estimate albedo, roughness, and metallic properties via
      Stable Diffusion (SD) 2 (Rombach et al., 2022). Similarly, UniDream (Liu et al., 2023) jointly
126
      optimizes the geometry and albedo of the 3D model with multi-view normal-albedo diffusion models
127
      while fine-tuning the metallic and roughness components with SD 2. However, both methods have
128   significant limitations. First, they require at least two diffusion models to generate relightable 3D
129   models (one multi-view diffusion models for geometry and one SD 2 for PBR estimation). Second,
130   because their diffusion models lack access to lighting conditions during training, they must blindly
131   estimate the lighting environment while decoupling albedo, metallic, and roughness values, leading
132   to sub-optimal results.
133
      In this paper, we propose MVLight, a novel approach that synthesizes multi-modal outputs—normal
134   maps, albedo, and rendered color—within a single network. Unlike previous methods, MVLight
135   explicitly incorporates lighting information as an input, ensuring that the output multi-view images
136   accurately reflect the specified lighting conditions. This allows for more effective decoupling of
137   albedo, metallic, and roughness values, as the model has direct access to the light environment
138   during optimizing PBR materials, leading to superior relighting performance compared to existing
139   methods.

141   3     M ETHOD
142
143   In this section, we introduce 1) the architecture of MVLight, a novel light-conditioned multi-view
144   diffusion model that synthesizes 3D-consistent outputs while faithfully adhering to specified lighting
145   conditions across multiple camera views, and 2) how we distill these multi-view outputs along with
146   Ground Truth (GT) lighting information into Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) for relightable text-
147   to-3D generation, improving both geometric fidelity and relighting capabilities. The overview of
148   MVLight and light-aware multi-view SDS are depicted in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 respectively.
149
150   3.1   L IGHT- CONDITIONED M ULTI - VIEW D IFFUSION M ODEL

152   Multi-view consistency. MVLight extends the U-Net architecture of Stable Diffusion 2 (SD 2)
153
      (Rombach et al., 2022), specifically adapted for multi-view generation. Unlike SD 2 which aims to
      produce 2D image outputs, MVLight generates 4D tensors x0 ∈ RN c×H×W ×C , where Nc is the
154
      number of camera views, and H, W , and C correspond to the spatial dimensions and latent space
155
      channels in the VAE latent space (Kingma, 2013). To ensure multi-view consistency, we follow the
156   approach from MVDream (Shi et al., 2024), modifying the self-attention mechanism by reshaping
157   the input from Nc × H × W × C to (Nc HW ) × C. This enables the model to learn not only
158   pixel-level correlations within a single view but also across different camera views, thus capturing
159   3D geometric consistency. Camera-specific information is integrated by injecting extrinsic camera
160   parameters ζ ∈ RNc ×16 via a 2-layer MLP, which is combined with the diffusion time embeddings.
161   This allows the model to understand the spatial relationships between different camera viewpoints,
      improving geometric accuracy. Text prompts y are encoded using the CLIP text encoder (Radford

254   3.2   L IGHT- AWARE M ULTI - VIEW GUIDED SDS
255
      We leverage our light-conditioned multi-view diffusion models, MVLight, to synthesize 3D models
256
      with Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) (Poole et al., 2023) and Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
257
      (Mildenhall et al., 2020). Our optimization process for 3D generation is divided into two stages.
258   First, we synthesize the overall geometry and appearance, and then we fine-tune the Physically
259   Based Rendering (PBR) materials—specifically albedo, roughness, and metallic properties—for
260   better relighting performance.
261
      Geometry and apperance optimization. In the first stage, similar to other SDS-based approaches
262
      (Poole et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024), we optimize a hash grid-based explicit representation (Müller
263
      et al., 2022) of the 3D space, along with a geometry MLP to estimate density σ and a color MLP.
264   However, we introduce two key differences: (1) instead of a single color MLP, we use multiple
265   MLPs, each corresponding to a different lighting color distribution; (2) rather than directly estimat-
266   ing shaded RGB values, we use a dedicated MLP to predict albedo and multiple MLPs to compute
267   ambient light under different lighting conditions. This is analogous to a simplified PBR model,
268   where the lighting intensity is assumed to be uniform across the object’s surface for each lighting
269   environment. The final RGB color is obtained by multiplying the ambient light values from the
      lighting MLPs with the albedo from the albedo MLP.

271   model training, where albedo and normal maps are treated as lighting conditions, we jointly opti-
272   mize three modalities—normal, albedo, and RGB—during SDS to improve geometric accuracy and
273   albedo estimation. Following MVDream (Shi et al., 2024), we perform SDS on the reconstructed x0
274   rather than on the estimated ϵθ to prevent the over-saturation issue which is common in SDS-based
275   optimization. The light-conditioned multi-view SDS LM V L−SDS can be formulated as

283   Light-aware PBR fine-tuning. After synthesizing 3D models using SDS and NeRF, we fine-tune
284   them by optimizing Physically Based Rendering (PBR) materials—albedo, roughness, and metallic
285
      properties—to improve relighting performance. We build on the geometry and albedo MLPs from
      the previous NeRF optimization stage and introduce two additional MLPs to predict roughness and
286
      metallic values. To preserve the geometric properties learned earlier, we freeze the geometry net-
287
      work and only train the PBR materials during this fine-tuning process. We continue using the SDS
288   loss function from Eq. 2, but restrict the optimization to xl , which represents the final rendered color
289   outputs under a user-specified lighting environment.
290   In our method, we initialize the PBR lighting environment with the same HDR map used in the light-
291   conditioned diffusion model. Existing approaches (Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Qiu et al.,
292   2024) rely on Stable Diffusion (SD) to estimate PBR materials with SDS. However, SD does not use
293   explicit lighting conditions, meaning that the lighting environment in its outputs is not controlled
294   or known. Consequently, during PBR material estimation, these methods randomly select an HDR
295   lighting environment, which may not match the implicit lighting in the SD outputs. This randomness
296
      creates a mismatch between the lighting conditions assumed during PBR and those generated by SD,
      leading to what we call blind PBR estimation. This mismatch results in sub-optimal performance
297
      when estimating PBR materials and therefore sub-optimal relighting performance. In contrast, our
298
      approach ensures consistency by using the same HDR map for both the diffusion model in SDS
299   and PBR material optimization, aligning the lighting conditions across the pipeline. This alignment
300   improves the accuracy of PBR material estimation and relighting performance.

      4.1    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
305
306   Training data synthesis. As mentioned earlier, we created a custom dataset, XM V L , to train MV-
307   Light, using the Objaverse dataset (Deitke et al., 2023). Each object was captured from 16 camera
308   angles under Nl randomly sampled lighting environments from a set of 450 HDR images collected
309   online (Poly Haven, 2024), along with normal maps and albedo values. With approximately 90,000
310   objects and Nl set to 4, this process generated around 8.6 million images in total.
311
      Diffusion model training. To train the light-conditioned diffusion model, we implemented MV-
312   Light based on the open-source multi-view diffusion model, MVDream (Shi et al., 2024). We ini-
313   tialized the parameters from MVDream and trained the lighting-specific modules, such as the light
314   cross-attention and light embedding MLPs, using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with a
315   learning rate of 1e-4. Afterward, we fine-tuned all parameters with a reduced learning rate of 1e-
316   5. For this process, we utilized 32 A100 GPUs, applying a batch size of 128. Fine-tuning for the
317   lighting-specific modules was conducted over 50,000 iterations, while fine-tuning for all parameters
318   occurred over 10,000 iterations with the reduced learning rate.
319   SDS optimization. For 3D model generation using SDS, we integrated MVLight into the threestu-
320   dio library (Guo et al., 2023) to optimize NeRF-based 3D representations. The output resolution for
321   3D models during training was set to 256, with the same CFG scale scheduling as MVDream (Shi
322   et al., 2024). Both geometry and appearance optimization (stage 1) and light-awrae PBR fine-tuning
323   (stage 2) involved random sampling from 5 unseen HDR maps, taking 2 hours each for 12,000
      iterations on a single A100 GPU.

415
416   Multi-modal SDS. When optimizing NeRF-based 3D models for geometric and appearance prop-
417
      erties, i.e., Stage 1 in Fig. 2, we utilize multi-modal SDS, which guides the generation of normal,
      albedo, and rendered color maps through MVLight’s view-consistent multi-modal outputs. To val-
418
      idate the effectiveness of multi-modal SDS, we replaced it with single-modal SDS, which only
419
      applies supervision to the final rendered color output, similar to Poole et al. (2023); Chen et al.
420   (2023); Shi et al. (2024). As shown in Fig. 5, multi-modal SDS results in 3D models with superior
421   normal maps and albedo values compared to single-modal SDS. Specifically, multi-modal SDS pro-
422   duces smoother normal maps, free from undesired patterns and surface irregularities. For albedo,
423   multi-modal SDS offers more distinct and accurate color representations, as it directly supervises
424   the albedo map. In contrast, single-modal SDS indirectly influences albedo through the final color
425   output, resulting in less accurate, pale, and faint color distributions.
426   Light-aware vs blind PBR estimation. Unlike existing text-to-3D generative models that use Sta-
427   ble Diffusion with randomly sampled HDR light environments for PBR estimation, we utilize our
428   proposed MVLight who produces multi-view outputs under user-specified lighting conditions. By
429   using the same HDR map for PBR that was fed to MVLight during SDS, our method ensures align-
430   ment between the lighting environment used for 3D model generation and PBR-based synthesis,
431   leading to more accurate and consistent results. To demonstrate the effectiveness of this light-aware
      (non-blind) PBR-based 3D model generation, we compare it to blind PBR-based methods, similar

467   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
468
469   Conclusion. In this paper, we propose MVLight, the first light-conditioned multi-view diffusion
470   model, capable of generating 3D-consistent outputs under user-specified lighting conditions across
471
      all camera views, along with albedo and normal maps. By integrating MVLight into the Score Dis-
      tillation Sampling (SDS) pipeline, we improve the geometric and appearance fidelity of synthesized
472
      3D models, enabling more accurate decomposition of light-dependent components. Unlike existing
473
      text-to-3D generative models that blindly estimate PBR materials by randomly selecting lighting
474   conditions, MVLight supports non-blind PBR estimation, ensuring alignment between the lighting
475   conditions used during PBR and SDS. We demonstrate the effectiveness of MVLight through ex-
476   tensive experiments, showcasing its superior performance in text-to-3D generation, PBR material
477   estimation, and relighting capabilities compared to other state-of-the-art methods.
478
      Limitation. While MVLight successfully generates three distinct modalities—albedo, normal, and
479
      shaded color—across multiple views, there is no guarantee of alignment between these modalities,
480   as they are synthesized independently. When attempting to train MVLight with modality align-
481   ment using self-attention mechanisms, similar to Wonder3D (Long et al., 2024) or UniDream (Liu
482   et al., 2023), we empirically observed a trade-off between output quality and modality alignment. In
483   this paper, we prioritize output quality and rely on SDS to manage misalignments between modal-
484   ities, but this misalignment could lead to sub-optimal performance in certain cases. We address
485   this limitation and leave developing a model architecture that learns modality alignment without
      compromising output quality as a future work.

      B    E THICAL S TATEMENT
663
664
      The light-conditioned multi-view diffusion model, MVLight, proposed in this paper is designed to
665
      enhance the 3D generation task, which is particularly relevant in fields such as entertainment, me-
666   dia, and gaming. However, we acknowledge the potential for misuse, including the generation of
667   harmful content such as violent or sexually explicit materials through third-party fine-tuning. Fur-
668   thermore, since our model builds upon prior works like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and
669   MVDream (Shi et al., 2024), it may also inherit inherent biases and limitations that could lead to
670   unintended outputs. We emphasize the importance of clearly presenting the outputs from MVLight
671   as synthetic and urge that generated images or 3D models be critically evaluated to prevent mis-
672   representation. Additionally, while these generative models offer valuable advancements in creative
673   fields, they also raise concerns about the displacement of human labor through automation. How-
674
      ever, we believe these tools have the potential to foster innovation and enhance accessibility within
      the creative industries, supporting new opportunities for growth and collaboration.
