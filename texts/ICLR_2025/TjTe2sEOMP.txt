                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015            Unsupervised anomaly detection in medical images facilitates practical clinical
016
               adoption by identifying abnormalities without relying on scarce and costly an-
               notated data. However, prior works have predominantly focused on specialized
017
               models for individual organs and modalities, impeding knowledge transfer and
018
               scalable deployment. In this paper, we investigate a task of universal anomaly de-
019            tection guided by natural language prompts. We propose a prompt-driven mixture
020            of experts framework that detects anomalies across multiple organs and modalities
021            within a single network. Specifically, our method comprises encoders for vision
022            and text, a routing network, and a mixture of hallucination-minimized expert de-
023            coders. An image and a prompt describing the organ and modality are fed to the
024            encoders. The routing network then selects specialized yet collaborative expert
025            decoders to analyze the image. We observe that anomaly detection models often
026            erroneously identify normal image regions as anomalous, a phenomenon we term
027
               “hallucinatory anomaly”. To address this issue, we design hallucination-aware
               experts that produce improved anomaly maps by jointly learning reconstruction
028
               and minimizing these false positives. For comprehensive evaluation, we curate
029
               a diverse dataset of 12,153 images spanning 5 modalities and 4 organs. Exten-
030            sive experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art anomaly detection performance in
031            this universal setting. Moreover, the natural language conditioning enables inter-
032            pretability and user interaction. The code and data will be made publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Deep learning has achieved remarkable success across a variety of computer vision tasks, yet its ap-
039
      plication to medical image analysis remains constrained by the need for sizable annotated datasets.
      Obtaining annotations for abnormal images proves particularly challenging, especially for rare or
040
      novel conditions (Tschuchnig & Gadermayr, 2022). In contrast, collections of normal medical im-
041
      ages can be accumulated with relative ease. This disparity motivates anomaly detection in medical
042   images—identifying abnormalities without reliance on annotated anomalous data during training.
043
044   Prior works (Shvetsova et al., 2021; Schlegl et al., 2017; Han et al., 2021a; Schlegl et al., 2019b;
045
      Jiang et al., 2019) have explored generative models, including autoencoders and generative adver-
      sarial networks (GANs), for unsupervised anomaly detection. These models are trained to learn
046
      feature representations using only normal images. At test time, anomalies are identified as regions
047
      that the models fail to reconstruct properly, by comparing input and reconstructed images in pixel
048   space. Recent approaches utilize memory banks (Gong et al., 2019; Park et al., 2020), normaliz-
049   ing flows (Rudolph et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Gudovskiy et al., 2022), self-supervised learning,
050   and knowledge distillation to achieve stronger image-level anomaly detection performance. Despite
051   promising results, these works have largely focused on training specialized models for individual
052   organs and modalities. This methodology overlooks potential similarities across organs and modal-
053   ities, hinders knowledge transfer, impedes scalable anomaly detection, and leads to fragmented re-
      search efforts.

119   2   R ELEATED W ORK
120
121   Single-Task Anomaly Detection. Reconstruction-based methods have emerged as a prominent
122   approach in unsupervised anomaly detection. Schlegl et al. (2017) pioneer the use of GANs for
123   this purpose with AnoGAN, later introducing f-AnoGAN (Schlegl et al., 2019a), a faster variant
124   employing an encoder to map images to a latent space. In addition, various autoencoder architec-
125   tures are explored, including variational autoencoder (Zimmerer et al., 2018) and vector-quantized
126
      variational autoencoder (Naval Marimont & Tarroni, 2021). To address the overgeneralization prob-
      lem, where abnormal images are reconstructed too accurately, Gong et al. (2019) and Park et al.
127
      (2020) introduce memory banks to store normal patterns for comparison during inference. Sev-
128
      eral works (Rudolph et al., 2021; Gudovskiy et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021) leverage normalizing
129   flows, enabling exact likelihood estimation for image modeling, and achieve good performance in
130   anomaly detection. Self-supervised learning (Jing & Tian, 2021) has also been applied to anomaly
131   detection, typically following two paradigms. One-stage approaches train models to detect syn-
132   thetic anomalies and directly apply them to real abnormalities (Tan et al., 2021; Schlüter et al.,
133   2022). Two-stage approaches first learn self-supervised representations on normal data, followed
134   by constructing one-class classifiers (Li et al., 2021; Sohn et al., 2021). Recently, knowledge dis-
135   tillation from pre-trained models presents another promising approach for unsupervised anomaly
136   detection (Salehi et al., 2021; Deng & Li, 2022; Batzner et al., 2024). In these methods, a stu-
137
      dent network distilled by a pre-trained teacher network on normal samples can only extract normal
      features, leading to discrepancies when anomalies are encountered during inference. Despite their
138
      successes, the above-mentioned approaches have largely focused on dataset-specific models, poten-
139
      tially overlooking cross-class similarities and becoming resource-intensive as the number of classes
140   increases.
141
142   Universal Anomaly Detection. You et al. (2022) first formulate universal anomaly detection,
143   proposing a Transformer-based feature reconstruction model using a layer-wise query decoder to
144   model complex multi-class normal distributions. Lu et al. (2023) present a unified hierarchical
145   vector quantized Transformer that quantizes visual features to better reconstruct normal patterns.
146   Yao et al. (2024) propose inter-class Gaussian mixture modeling and intra-class mixed class cen-
147   ters learning for multi-class anomaly detection. Beyond detection, Zhao (2023) focuse on universal
148   anomaly localization for industrial applications. In the medical domain, Zhang et al. (2023) develop
149   a single network capable of detecting anomalies across two organs (lung and liver) and two imaging
150
      modalities (CT and X-ray). The existing methods rely solely on visual features to identify the organ
      and modality of each input image through a bottom-up fashion. We propose that introducing text
151
      prompts can guide a universal anomaly detection model’s attention in a top-down manner, leading
152
      to improved performance.
153
154
      Mixture of Experts. Originally introduced by Jacobs et al. (1991), the mixture of experts frame-
155
      work combines multiple sub-models for conditional computation. Its integration with large language
156   models has yielded remarkable results in natural language understanding tasks, to name a few,
157   machine translation (Shazeer et al., 2017) and open-domain question answering (Du et al., 2022;
158   Artetxe et al., 2022). This success has inspired applications in computer vision. Riquelme et al.
159   (2021) introduce vision mixture of experts, matching the performance of leading networks while
160   reducing computational demands during inference in image classification. Hwang et al. (2023)
161   develop Tutel, a scalable system design and implementation for mixture of experts with dynamic
      parallelism and pipelining. Chowdhury et al. (2023) present patch-level routing, dynamically allo-

200   3.1     S HARED V ISION E NCODER
201
202   The shared vision encoder maps input images into a common latent feature space that is accessed
203   by multiple decoder experts. Architecturally, the vision encoder consists of several convolutional
204   blocks, each comprising Conv-BN-ReLU layers, followed by two fully connected layers. Formally,
205   given an input image x, the vision encoder f (·) produces a visual feature vector v = f (x).

208   3.2     TASK -S PECIFIC P ROMPT E NCODER
209
210   Incorporating critical priors in a top-down manner is vital for directing the model’s attention to ap-
211   propriate tasks and image regions. To this end, we design a task-specific prompt encoder that takes
212   textual prompts as input and generates task-specific feature representations. Specifically, we lever-
213   age the text encoder from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to extract linguistic features, as it is pretrained
214   on a massive corpus of image-text pairs. We then apply a fully connected layer to condense the text
215   features. Formally, given a prompt p, the output of the prompt encoder is τ = g(p), where g(·)
      denotes the prompt encoder.

231
232   To address the critical issue of hallucinatory anomalies in anomaly detection, we design
233   hallucination-minimized experts. Specifically, for the k-th expert, we set two output channels in its
234   ultimate block, denoted as µk and σ k : the former for reconstructing the input image, and the latter
235   for predicting per-pixel hallucination propensity. Our motivation stems from the following observa-
236
      tion: Anomaly detectors often produce high reconstruction errors not only in abnormal regions but
      also along boundaries of normal areas. These boundary-induced errors generally lead to misidentifi-
237
      cation of anomalies, resulting in what term hallucinatory anomalies (Figure 2). Therefore, we would
238
      like to utilize hallucination quantification to rectify erroneous boundary detections and thus improve
239   the localization of truly anomalous regions. To this end, we devise such a hallucination-aware de-
240   coder architecture and a corresponding loss function (see Section 3.4). Finally, the output of the

278   3.5     A NOMALY S CORING
279
280   During inference on a test image, the mean of pixel-wise reconstruction errors has been widely
281
      adopted as an anomaly score for the image. In this work, we use an anomaly score calculation
      method based on our hallucination-minimized expert model. We leverage the first term in Eq. 5 to
282
      compute the anomaly score. Specifically, (xi − x̂i ) represents the reconstruction error map gener-
283
      ated by our model, while ui denotes the corresponding hallucination quantification map. Through
284   training on normal data, we have developed a model capable of estimating high hallucination values
285   when significant reconstruction errors occur in normal regions. Consequently, during inference on
286   both normal and abnormal images, reconstruction errors in normal regions can be effectively rec-
287   tified using the first term in Eq. 5. This results in an anomaly map that accurately localizes true
288   anomalous regions. The final anomaly score for the test image is computed as the mean value of this
289   anomaly map.

294   4.1     DATA
295
296   To evaluate our approach, we compile a comprehensive, multi-modal, multi-organ universal anomaly
297   detection dataset by integrating five medical imaging datasets: the RSNA Pneumonia Detection
298   Challenge dataset1 , the Brain Tumor MRI dataset2 , the Large-scale Attention-based Glaucoma
299   (LAG) dataset (Li et al., 2019), the Breast Ultrasound Images (BUSI) dataset (Al-Dhabyani et al.,
300
      2020), and the HeadCT dataset3 .
301   RSNA: This chest X-ray dataset contains 8,851 normal and 6,012 lung opacity images. Follow-
302   ing Cai et al. (2022), we use 3,851 normal images for training and a balanced test set of 1,000
303   normal and 1,000 abnormal images.
304   Brain Tumor: This dataset consists of 2,000 MRI slices without tumors, 1,621 with gliomas, and
305   1,645 with meningiomas. We categorize glioma and meningioma slices as anomalies. The normal
306   instances are sourced from Br35H5 and Saleh et al. (2020), while the anomalous cases are from
307   Saleh et al. (2020) and Cheng et al. (2015). In line with Cai et al. (2022), our experimental setup
308   includes 1,000 normal slices for training and a test set of 600 normal and 600 abnormal slices
309   (equally split between glioma and meningioma).
310   LAG: This dataset comprises 3,143 normal retinal fundus images and 1,711 abnormal retinal fundus
311   images with glaucoma. Following Cai et al. (2022), we use 1,500 normal images as training samples
312   and 811 normal and 811 abnormal images as test examples.
313
      BUSI: The dataset includes 133 normal breast ultrasound images, 437 images with benign nodules,
314
      and 210 images with malignant nodules. We use 99 normal images for training, with the remaining
315
      images used for evaluation.
316
317   HeadCT: This dataset comprises 100 normal head CT slices and 100 slices with hemorrhage. We di-
318   vide these images into two groups: 90 normal images for training and 10 normal with 100 abnormal
319
      images for testing.

390   with a learning rate of 5e-4 and a batch size of 64. The shared encoder contains four convolutional
391
      layers (each with a 4 × 4 convolution, stride 2), whose channel sizes are 16-32-64-64, followed by
      two fully connected layers with output sizes of 2048 and 16, respectively. Each decoder consists
392
      of four deconvolutional layers with the same kernel size and stride as the encoder, and the channel
393
      sizes are set to 64-32-16-2. All layers except the output layer are followed by batch normalization
394   (BN) and ReLU. The routing network consists of a fully connected layer, the output size of which
395   matches the number of decoders. For competing methods, we utilize their publicly available codes
396   and adhere to their default training configurations.

531   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533
      In this paper, we propose a prompt-driven mixture of experts framework for universal anomaly
534   detection across organs and modalities via natural language conditioning. Through encoders, routing
535   networks, and the proposed hallucination-aware expert decoders, our method leverages both vision
536   and text to detect anomalies within a single model. Extensive experiments on a diverse medical
537   image dataset with over 12K images demonstrate state-of-the-art performance. The natural language
538   prompts also enable model interpretability and user interaction. In the future, we plan to expand the
539   framework to additional organs and modalities, investigate few-shot anomaly detection with limited
      normal images, and deploy the system in clinical settings to assist medical professionals.
