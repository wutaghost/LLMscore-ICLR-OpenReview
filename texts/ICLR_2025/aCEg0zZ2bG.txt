011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Continuous-Time Dynamic Graph (CTDG) learning has significantly advanced
014            link prediction performance by leveraging random negative sampling and incorpo-
015
               rating adaptive temporal information. Recent studies aim to improve performance
               by introducing random sampling to obtain hard negative samples, whose qual-
016
               ity is limited by randomness, capturing few categories of negative samples, and
017
               leading to false positive (FP) and false negative (FN) problems. Here we present
018            S PACE TGN, a CTDG learning framework, with a augmented hard negative sam-
019            pling mini-batches (AMNS) strategy and two new feature extraction strategies
020            that derive space-temporal locality subgraph and historical occurrence informa-
021            tion to emphasize the graph’s temporal discriminative properties. The AMNS
022            strategy sample mini-batches comprised of instances that are hard-to-distinguish
023            (i.e., hard and true negatives with respect to each other) based on the target distri-
024            bution, thereby effectively augmenting the discriminative features and the diver-
025            sity of historical and inductive samples. Furthermore, to mitigate the challenges
026
               posed by false positives and false negatives, our architecture S PACE TGN employs
               a conceptually straightforward approach that investigates temporal subgraphs and
027
               historical interactions between source and destination nodes. This enables the
028
               model to leverage complex and historically accurate interactions among predicted
029            entities. Our extensive evaluation of dynamic link prediction on seven state-of-the-
030            practice datasets reveals that S PACE TGN achieves state-of-the-art performance in
031            most datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in ameliorating model bias.

036   Dynamic graph modeling offers a versatile representation of real-world scenarios by abstracting
037   entities as nodes and the time-varied interactions or relationships between these entities as temporal
038   edges. This modeling framework is applicable to a wide range of domains, including social networks

      Kumar et al. (2019); Huo et al. (2018); Alvarez-Rodriguez et al. (2021), traffic networks Zhao et al.
      (2019); Yu et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2019); Guo et al. (2019); Yu et al. (2021), the recommendation
040
      system Song et al. (2019); Dong et al. (2012); Wang et al. (2021b), and financial transactions Wang
041
      et al. (2021c); Zhang et al. (2022); Feng et al. (2019). To facilitate efficient learning on dynamic
042   graphs, many efforts have been devoted to the development of discrete-time dynamic graph models
043   (DTDG) Zhao et al. (2019); Pareja et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021) and continuous-time dynamic
044   graph models (CTDG) Kumar et al. (2019); Rossi et al. (2020); Cong et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2024).
045

046
      many existing benchmark datasets, they exhibit two notable limitations: only select negative sam-

051   negative sampling strategy randomly selects destination nodes from the entire set of nodes, retain-
052   ing the timestamps, features, and source nodes of positive edges. However, this strategy introduces
053   extreme variation between the negative and positive edges, leading to overfitting problems in recent
      models. The models, after training, can only judge the datasets with obvious positive and negative

081
         In this paper, we design a new CTDG learning framework, namely S PACE TGN. Internally, we
082
         outline two following key technical contributions in S PACE TGN to address the above challenges:
083
084      We propose a new augmented mini-batch negative sampling (AMNS) strategy for better
085      continuous-time graph learning. The central idea of this paper is to sample mini-batches of hard-
086
         to-distinguish instances to emphasize CTDG’s discriminative temporal properties. Specifically, we
         generate these mini-batches to cover hard yet true negatives by dynamically maintaining a collection
087
         of previously encountered, high-smilarity temporal edges and persistently sampling from this col-
088
         lection. This hard negative sampling strategy is integrated with a sample augmentation module that
089      uses a targeted distribution to enrich the data set, which improves diversity among negative samples
090      for training.
091
092
         We present a novel Time-Sequence-based dynamic graph learning model (S PACE TGN). Two
         CTDG features are explicitly utilized to capture periodic and historical interactions in S PACE TGN:
093
         space-temporal dependency and historical occurrence. The space-temporal locality dependency
094
         captures the interaction sequence of the current node preceding a specific time point, which rep-
095      resents the temporal local features. This sequence is then combined with the historical neighbor
096      sequence of the node, reflecting the local spatial features. Furthermore, we introduce a historical oc-
097      currence encoding scheme to capture the factual interactions among predicted entities. S PACE TGN
098      introduces an optimized MLP-Mixer layer to distill the intrinsic features of the extracted sequences,
099      which significantly improves the capture of temporal information in the model.
100
         From our empirical validation, S PACE TGN significantly outperforms existing SOTA methods on
101      most datasets, proving the efficacy of our design and coding strategies. Furthermore, all models are
102      evaluated for their statistical performance that is significantly higher than previous results in both
103      historical and inductive scenarios, confirming the effectiveness of our negative sampling strategy.

121   Negative Sampling on CTDG Learning. Negative sampling uses a selected subset of non-observed
122   or negative data points to significantly improve training efficiency and model performance, which
123
      is widely used in various domains, including natural language processing Grbovic et al. (2015);
      Zhang & Zweigenbaum (2018); Wu et al. (2021), computer vision Wu et al. (2017); Robinson et al.
124
      (2020); Wu et al. (2020), and recommendation systems Rendle et al. (2009; 2012). The exist-
125
      ing work has been classified into two lines: static negative sampling and hard negative sampling.
126   The initial approach, static negative sampling, involves assigning a fixed probability to each dy-
127   namic candidate for selection. This includes methods such as random negative sampling (RNS)
128   and popularity-based negative sampling. In contrast, hard negative sampling emphasizes the selec-
129   tion of challenging negative samples, which are characterized by their similarity to positive samples
130   within dynamic distributions.Yang et al. (2020); Shrivastava et al. (2016), as a specialized variant
131   of negative sampling, offer more informative training signals, allowing for a more precise charac-
132   terization of dynamic characteristics. Inspired by this technique, we utilized hard negative samples
133   to mitigate significant discrepancies observed in the model performance across random, historical,
134
      and inductive continuous-time link prediction scenarios. This method not only addresses the inher-
      ent variability in model testing conditions, but also improves the robustness and accuracy of our
135
      learning algorithms under diverse operational settings.

140   Definition 3.1 (Dynamic Graph) We define the set of nodes as N and the collection of temporal
141   edges as E = {(u1 , v1 , t1 ), (u2 , v2 , t2 ), . . .}, where the timestamps follow an ascending order, 0 ≤
142   t1 ≤ t2 ≤ . . .. Here, ui and vi from N represent the nodes originating and terminating at the edge
143   ith at the time instant ti , respectively. Each node u ∈ N is endowed with a node feature xu ∈ RdN ,
144   whereas each edge e ∈ E carries an edge feature xe ∈ RdE . Together, the set of nodes and edge
145   features are denoted by FN and FE , respectively. Note that if the raw dynamic graph inputs lack
146   node and edge features, these features are default configured to a null vector. To encapsulate, we
147   conceptualize the continuous-time dynamic graph as G = {N, E, FN , FE }, formalizing temporal
148
      interactions within graph-structured data.
149
150
      Definition 3.2 (Continuous-Time Link Prediction) Given a dynamic graph G and a source node
      u ∈ N , a destination node v ∈ N , a specific timestamp t, along with a set of historical edges
151
      E = {(u′ , v ′ , t′ )|t′ < t} that precede t, the objective of edge prediction is to design algorithms
152
      capable of deriving representations htu ∈ Rd and htv ∈ Rd for nodes u and v, respectively, where d
153   denotes the dimensionality of these representations. The ultimate goal is to use these representations
154   to infer the likelihood of an edge between u and v at the timestamp t.

249   4.2   S PACE -T EMPORAL L OCALITY AWARE CTDG A RCHITECTURE
250
251   Compared with the existing CTDG negative sampling strategy, our proposed negative sampling is
252
      closer to the positive samples, which poses a greater challenge to the model’s information extraction
      capabilities. Existing models mostly employ message passing mechanisms to capture topological
253
      features or to aggregate information from low-order neighboring nodes; nevertheless, deriving tem-
254
      poral locality and periodicity remains particularly challenging. We propose two types of information
255   extraction methods based on current feature extraction: Space-Temporal Locality and Historical
256   Occurrence, which significantly emphasize the temporal discriminative properties of CTDG.
257
258
      S PACE TGN, as illustrated in Figure 3, begins by extracting three key sequences for a given source
      node u, destination node v, and timestamp t: the historical sequences of the first hop neighbors for
259
      both nodes, the sequences of dynamic edges occurring before t, and the temporal sequences marking
260
      the occurrence of the edge (u, v) in historical data.
261
262          • Extracting the historical sequences of the first hop neighbors for both nodes u and
263            v. Given the predicted nodes u, v, and timestamp t, we extract the historical sequences
264            of the first hop neighbors for u and v, denoted as Sut and Svt , respectively, where Sut =
265            {(u, u′ , t′ )|t′ < t} ∪ {(u′ , u, t′ )|t′ < t} and Svt = {(v, v ′ , t′ )|t′ < t} ∪ {(v ′ , v, t′ )|t′ < t}
266            Yu et al. (2024). We introduce a parameter l to represent the maximum length of first-hop
267            neighbor sequences. If a sequence exceeds l, excess neighbors are truncated. If a sequence
268
               is shorter than l, it is padded with zero vectors to reach the required length.
269          • Extracting the sequences of dynamic edges occurring before the timestamp ti .
               Existing methods primarily capture the historical neighbors of node u and v with-

      After encoding the extracted sequences, S PACE TGN learn intricate correlations within the features

      To evaluate our model’s performance, we adhere to the established benchmarks in the field by uti-
310
      lizing Average Precision (AP) and Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-

      ROC) as primary metrics. The experimental setup encompasses two distinct scenarios for link pre-
312   diction: (1) transductive setting, whose goal is to predict the formation of an edge between two
313   nodes, both of which have been observed during the training phase, and (2) inductive setting, which

323   to eliminate bias. The parameters of the recent models are detailed in the Section F. Experiments

715   B     C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS OF S PACE TGN
716
717   In our analysis, we adopt a batch-wise approach to evaluate the computational complexity of
718   S PACE TGN, where b denotes the batch size and N represents the number of temporal edges in-
719   volved.
720
      Extracting Key Sequences.Initially, each node within a batch retrieves its first-hop neighbors, en-
721
      tailing a bisection lookup with a complexity of O(log N ). Subsequently, the selection of neigh-
722
      boring nodes incurs a complexity of O(l), where l is the length of the neighbors list. Thus, the
723   total complexity for processing one batch in this phase is O(b × (log N + l)). For the extraction of
724   the Space-Temporal Series and Historical Occurrences sequences, the complexities are computed as
725   O(b × (log N + br)) and O(b × (log N + bo)), respectively, where r and o represent the lengths of
726   the respective sequences.
727
      Encoding Features.The complexities for Node/Edge encoding are O(b×l ×dN ) and O(b×l ×dE ),
728
      where dN and dE are the original dimensions of each feature, respectively. Time Encoding incurs
729   a complexity of O(b × l × dT ), where dT is the dimension of the time feature. Space-Temporal
730   Dependency Encoding is characterized by O(b×(l log l+r log r+l+r))+O(l×dtemporal +l×d2temporal )
731   and O(b × (l log l + l)) + O(l × dspace + l × d2space ), where dtemporal and dspace are the dimensions of
732   temporal locality and space locality features, respectively. Historical Occurrence Encoding features
733   a complexity of O(b × o × doccur ), where doccur represents the dimension dedicated to capturing the
734   temporal patterns of occurrence.
735
      Mixing Features.The MLP-Mixer processes these features with complexities O(b × (l2 + d2 )) and
736
      O(b × (o2 + d2 )). Finally, the aggregation layer, which merges features for the final output, incurs
737   complexities of O(l×d×doutput ) and O(o×d×doutput ), where doutput represents the output dimension.

782
783
      In this study, we evaluate the performance of our models against eight baseline methods, each
784   uniquely designed to handle dynamic graph data:
785
786   JODIE: This model is tailored for temporal bipartite networks, specifically user-item interactions.
787
      Using two synchronized recurrent neural networks, it continuously updates the state of the user
      and the item. In addition, it integrates a projection operation to model the future trajectory of the
788
      representation of each entity Kumar et al. (2019).
789
790   DyRep: This approach features a recurrent architecture that updates node states after each interac-
791   tion. It incorporates a temporal-attentive aggregation module to account for the evolving structural
792   information within dynamic graphsTrivedi et al. (2019).
793   TGAT: The Temporal Graph Attention Network leverages the self-attention mechanism to aggregate
794   features from each node’s temporal-topological neighbors. It includes a time-encoding function to
795   capture temporal patterns effectivelyda Xu et al. (2020).
796
      TGN: This model maintains a dynamic memory for each node, updating it upon observing new
797   interactions via a combination of message function, aggregator, and memory updater. A dedicated
798   embedding module generates temporal representations for the nodesRossi et al. (2020).
799
800
      EdgeBank: This is a memory-based, parameter-free method for the prediction of transductive dy-
      namic links. It manages observed interactions within a memory unit and updates this memory via
801
      several strategies. The system categorizes an interaction as positive if retained in memory, and
802
      negative otherwisePoursafaei et al. (2022).
803
804   TCL: The Temporal Contextual Linking model begins by generating each node’s interaction se-
805   quence through a breadth-first search on the temporal dependency interaction subgraph. It then
806
      employs a graph transformer that integrates graph topology and temporal information, enhancing its
      learning capabilities with a cross-attention mechanism for the interdependencies between interacting
807
      nodes Wang et al. (2021a).
808
809   GraphMixer: This model demonstrates the efficacy of a fixed time encoding function over a train-
      able version. It integrates this function into a link encoder based on the MLP-Mixer architecture to


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      analyze temporal links, while a node encoder with neighbor mean-pooling summarizes node fea-
811   turesCong et al. (2022).
812
813   DyGFormer introduces a novel architecture based on the Transformer framework. DyGFormer
814
      primarily leverages the first-hop interactions of nodes through two innovative techniques: a neighbor
      co-occurrence encoding scheme, which explores the correlations between source and destination
815
      nodes based on their historical sequences, and a patching technique, which divides each sequence
816
      into multiple patches before feeding them into the Transformer, thereby enabling the model to benefit
817   effectively and efficiently from longer historiesYu et al. (2024).

819   These baselines provide a comprehensive set of tools for benchmarking dynamic graph analysis
