009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative adversarial networks (GANs) are renowned for their ability to gener-
012            ate highly realistic and diverse data samples. However, the performance of GANs
013            is heavily dependent on the choice of loss functions, and commonly used losses
014            such as cross-entropy and least squares are often susceptible to outliers, vanishing
015            gradients, and training instability. To overcome these limitations, we introduce
016            zephyr loss—a novel, convex, smooth, and Lipschitz continuous loss function de-
017            signed to enhance robustness and provide flexible gradient control. Leveraging
018            this new loss function, we propose ZGAN, a refined GAN model that guarantees
019
               a unique optimal discriminator and stabilizes the overall training dynamics. Fur-
               thermore, we demonstrate that optimizing ZGAN’s generator objective minimizes
020
               a weighted difference between the real and generated data distributions. Through
021
               rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence proofs, we substantiate the
022            robustness and effectiveness of ZGAN, positioning it as a compelling and reliable
023            alternative for stable GAN training. Extensive experiments further demonstrate
024            that ZGAN surpasses leading methods in generative modeling.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) are a prominent generative mod-
030   eling technique, where two neural networks engage in a competitive process: the discriminator,
031   tasked with distinguishing real data from the generator’s synthetic data, and the generator, which
032   learns to produce realistic data from a noise source in order to deceive the discriminator. The theo-
033   retical aim of this competition is to achieve a Nash equilibrium. However, GANs may not consis-
034   tently exhibit such equilibria. To address this, a proximal operator is applied to the original objective
035   function, leading to the concept of proximal equilibrium (Farnia & Ozdaglar, 2020).
036   GANs have a wide range of real-world applications, including ClimateGAN (Schmidt et al., 2022),
037   semantic image synthesis (Tang et al., 2023), text generation (Carlsson et al., 2024), and unsuper-
038   vised pixel-wise anomaly detection (Mou et al., 2023). Since their inception, GANs (Bai et al., 2023;
039   Sauer et al., 2021) have shown an exceptional ability to generate highly realistic and high-quality
040   samples, making them valuable tools for various generative tasks. Numerous techniques have been
041   introduced to enhance the training of GANs, such as Karras et al. (2019); Allen-Zhu & Li (2023);
042   Asokan & Seelamantula (2023a); Saxena et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Zhang
043   et al. (2022).
044   Multiple versions are developed to enhance the stability and output quality of GAN. The slicing ad-
045   versarial network (SAN) (Takida et al., 2024), for example, enhances gradient optimization through
046   sliced optimal transport. Diffusion-GAN (Wang et al., 2023) improves the discriminator’s effective-
047   ness by generating Gaussian mixture noise via a forward diffusion mechanism. Additionally, the
048   dynamically masked discriminator (Zhang et al., 2024) adapts to changes in generated data, while
049   PCF-GAN (Lou et al., 2024) effectively captures temporal dependencies in time-series data. Spider
050
      GAN (Asokan & Seelamantula, 2023b), by leveraging structured images, enables faster conver-
      gence, thereby enhancing GAN performance across diverse applications. Further, GigaGAN (Kang
051
      et al., 2023) acts as a viable option for text-to-image synthesis.
052
053   The quality of GAN outputs is closely tied to the input distribution. Research indicates that low-
      dimensional latent vectors can effectively disentangle representations and control the characteristics

055   ther, deep generative networks (DGNs), utilized in both GANs and variational autoencoders (VAEs)
056   (Kingma, 2013) to approximate data manifolds, can inadvertently reproduce biases present in the

070
      the generation of high-quality, realistic samples. LSGAN, for instance, encounters the vanishing
071
      gradient problem when generated samples closely resemble real ones, as its quadratic loss function
072   can lead to diminishing gradients that slow further learning. The key contributions of our work are
073   as listed:
074
075          • We introduce a novel loss function for GANs, termed zephyr loss, characterized by its
076            smoothness around zero, which ensures stable gradient flow during training and mitigates
077            issues commonly associated with vanishing or exploding gradients.
078          • We demonstrate that minimizing the zephyr loss effectively reduces the difference between

080            model performance.
081          • We conducted extensive experiments on well-known datasets, including CIFAR-10,
082            CIFAR-100, STL-10, and SVHN, utilizing various models such as LSGAN, WGAN, and
083            Diffusion-GAN. Our evaluation emphasized the performance of the proposed ZGAN and
084
               Diffusion-ZGAN models, showcasing their ability to generate high-quality and diverse im-
               ages.

089   The training process of GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) is framed as a competitive game between
090   two networks: the generator and the discriminator. The generator generates samples from a noise
091   input, aiming to create realistic data, while the discriminator’s role is to distinguish between genuine
092
      data from the real distribution and the synthetic samples generated by the generator. This process
      utilizes sigmoid cross-entropy loss to update the networks, where the discriminator’s goal is to
093
      maximize the difference between real and fake data, while the generator seeks to minimize it by
094

204   3.3   B ENEFITS OF ZEPHYR LOSS
205          • Enhanced gradient stability and balanced performance: Zephyr loss enhances gradient
206            stability by using a square root term to regulate the flow of gradients, particularly when the
207            differences between real and generated data are small. This prevents vanishing or explod-
208            ing gradients, common in losses like cross-entropy and least squares, resulting in smoother
209            updates and more stable optimization, as demonstrated in Figure 2. Further, by moderat-
210            ing gradient updates for both generator and discriminator, zephyr loss balances their per-
211            formance, avoiding instability, and ensuring that the generator produces diverse, outputs
212
               without allowing the discriminator to become overly dominant.
213          • Enhanced robustness to outliers: Zephyr loss, due to its formulation with the ϵ term,

      3.4   Z EPHYR LOSS VS H UBER LOSS
229
      The Huber loss (Patterson et al., 2022) transitions between quadratic and linear regions based on
230   a predefined threshold, while the Zephyr loss achieves this transition smoothly through its square
231   root term, controlled by the parameter ϵ, eliminating the need for an explicit threshold. Unlike
232   the piecewise-defined Huber loss, the Zephyr loss is formulated as a single, continuous, and differ-
233   entiable function, ensuring smoother gradient flow during optimization—an essential advantage in
234   applications like adversarial learning where stability is critical. For large residuals (|x|), both losses
235   exhibit linear growth; however, the Huber loss has a fixed slope determined by the threshold param-
236   eter, whereas the Zephyr loss allows direct control of the slope through the parameter a, providing
237   greater flexibility.

352   5.1      EXPERIMENTAL FRAMEWORK
353
354   We conducted a thorough comparison of the proposed ZGAN model with LSGAN and WGAN. To
355   extend our evaluation, we also compared ZGAN with Diffusion-GAN (Wang et al., 2023), which
356   incorporates a diffusion process for improved stability and sample quality. Expanding on this ap-
357   proach, we introduce a novel variant, Diffusion-ZGAN, which adapts the diffusion process from the
358
      Diffusion-GAN framework to the ZGAN setting. In Diffusion-ZGAN, we apply a diffusion process
      to the input images before feeding them into the discriminator. This is achieved by adding noise
359
      to both real and generated images, ensuring that the discriminator evaluates the images after they
360
      have undergone the diffusion process. All the models follow the publicly available implementation
361   of deep convolutional GANs (DCGANs)1 (Radford, 2015), utilizing the PyTorch framework. This
362   allowed us to leverage well-established architectures while incorporating the proposed zephyr loss
363   function and model.
364
      We performed an extensive experimental study across multiple datasets, evaluating the performance
365
      of different models while maintaining a consistent architecture for both the generator and discrim-
366
      inator in each case. To ensure a fair and unbiased comparison, we applied the same set of hyper-
367   parameters for all models, unless otherwise specified. Specifically, for all models except WGAN,
368   we used Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a learning rate of 0.0002 and a β1 value of 0.5. For
369   WGAN, as recommended by (Arjovsky et al., 2017), we employed a RMSProp (Tieleman, 2012),
370   using a clip value of 0.01 and a learning rate of 0.0005. These adjustments are made to promote
371   stable and efficient training, addressing the unique requirements of the WGAN framework.

523   7    CONCLUSION
524
525   This work unveils a novel loss function for generative adversarial networks (GANs) named zephyr
526   loss. Characterized by its smoothness, convexity, and Lipschitz continuity, zephyr loss significantly
527   transforms the landscape of GAN training. Leveraging the transformative capabilities of zephyr
528   loss, we introduce the ZGAN model, which guarantees a unique optimal discriminator. We provide
529   rigorous theoretical proof of the convergence of the proposed ZGAN model, solidifying its stand-
530   ing in the realm of GAN research. Our extensive experimental evaluations illustrate the superior
531
      performance of ZGAN in comparison to existing models that utilize different loss functions. The
      results demonstrate that ZGAN exemplifies reliability and stability in GAN training, marking the
532
      onset of a new era in generative modeling. Moreover, by seamlessly integrating the diffusion pro-
533
      cess into the ZGAN framework, we present Diffusion-ZGAN, further amplifying the robustness and
534   efficacy of our approach. ZGAN advances the current state of GANs, laying a strong foundation
535   for future research in generative modeling. However, the advantages of employing Zephyr loss may
536   be compromised if the feature representations lack sufficient informativeness. While Zephyr loss
537   demonstrates robust performance on standard benchmark datasets, its scalability to large-scale or
538   high-dimensional datasets remains an area for future investigation. Moreover, there is significant
539   potential for extending this work to vector quantized GANs (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022),
      further enhancing its applicability across diverse generative tasks.

      B     Z EPHYR LOSS VERSUS C ONVENTIONAL LOSS FUNCTIONS
728
729
      In this section, we compare the proposed zephyr loss with the well-known existing loss functions in
730
      the context of GAN training.
731
732
733
      B.1   Z EPHYR LOSS VS CROSS - ENTROPY LOSS
734
      In GAN, the cross-entropy loss is used in the discriminator for binary classification between real
735   and fake samples, however, it leads to vanishing gradients when the discriminator becomes overly
736   confident, which impedes the generator’s learning (Radford, 2015). Furthermore, cross-entropy loss
737   does not consider the distance between the discriminator’s output and the true/fake labels, focusing
738   solely on correct classifications. In contrast, zephyr loss addresses these shortcomings by incorpo-
739   rating a measure of this distance, allowing for more informative gradient updates. This approach
740   not only maintains a smoother gradient flow but also ensures that the generator continues to receive
741   meaningful feedback, facilitating continuous learning and enhancing training stability.
742
743   B.2   Z EPHYR LOSS VS LEAST SQUARES LOSS
744
745   In LSGAN, the quadratic loss over-penalizes outliers, leading to inefficient learning and a focus
746   on rare samples, while also causing vanishing gradients as the discriminator becomes more confi-
747
      dent, slowing the generator’s progress. In contrast, zephyr loss is designed to maintain smoother
      gradients and gradient control throughout training, preventing the vanishing gradient problem and
748
      ensuring more balanced updates. This allows ZGAN to achieve stable and efficient training without
749
      overfocusing on outliers, while also encouraging continuous improvement in the generator’s outputs
750   without stalling the learning process. In the least square loss l(a) = a2 , the gradient l′ (a) = 2a is
751   directly proportional to a, where a represents the difference between the discriminator’s predicted
752   label and the true or fake label. This linear dependence on a can result in gradient instability during
753   training. Specifically, when a becomes very small, the gradient l′ (a) diminishes rapidly, leading to
754   slower convergence or even stalling the training process. Conversely, for large values of a, which
755   may occur in the presence of outliers, the gradient l′ (a) becomes excessively large, causing abrupt
      updates and making the model sensitive to noise and outliers. ZGAN, on the other hand, introduces

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756                                √           √
      the zephyr loss L(a) = α( a2 + ϵ − ϵ), where ϵ > 0 is a small regularization parameter. The
757   gradient of the zephyr loss is given by:
758

759                                            L′ (a) = √        .
760                                                       a2 + ϵ
761   This gradient has the following key properties: It stabilizes for large a, as L′ (a) → 1, preventing
762   overly large updates caused by outliers. For small a, the rate at which L′ (a) → 0 is slower than
763   the gradient of the least square loss l′ (a), which diminishes linearly. In the case of zephyr loss, the
764   presence of ϵ in the denominator ensures that the gradient diminishes more smoothly. These char-
765   acteristics of the zephyr loss ensure smoother and more controlled updates to the model parameters,
766   leading to greater stability and robustness during training.
767
768   B.3   ZGAN VS WGAN
769
      WGAN employs weight clipping to maintain the Lipschitz condition, which can limit the critic’s
770
      (discriminator of WGAN) capacity and hinder the generator’s learning. WGAN-GP addresses this
771
      with a gradient penalty, improving training stability but also increasing computational complexity
772   and the need for careful hyperparameter tuning. In contrast, ZGAN controls the loss function shape
773   to maintain smooth gradients and stable training. This approach allows ZGAN to enhance training
774   efficiency and gradient flow.
