009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In real-world applications, ensuring that model decisions are independent of the
012            training data distribution is crucial for safely deploying models. To address the
013            long-tailed problem, massive approaches focus either on improving individual
014            prediction quality or enhancing aggregate evaluation. Although these methods
015            improve overall performance, they often sacrifice performance in some classes,
016
               undermining the goals of long-tailed learning. We conduct a mathematical anal-
               ysis of the limitations of the Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) framework in
017
               long-tailed learning, examining both individual performance and aggregate eval-
018
               uation. For individual evaluation, although the Negative log-likelihood (NLL)
019            metric is effective, it relies heavily on softmax leading to poor distinction and
020            ambiguity when the probabilities of correct and incorrect predictions are similar.
021            For aggregate evaluation, the naive estimator in ERM is not an unbiased estima-
022            tor, dominated by head classes. To overcome these challenges, we propose Re-
023            Debias, a comprehensive framework combining the Residual-Energy score and a
024            Debias estimator. The Residual-Energy score provides a more sensitive reflec-
025            tion of prediction quality than softmax-based scores, enhancing prediction pre-
026            cision and reducing ambiguity. The Debias estimator applies causal inference
027
               techniques to ensure unbiased estimates during the averaging process, correcting
               for class-wise biases inherent in the naive estimator. Through extensive valida-
028
               tion on long-tailed benchmarks, including training from scratch on iNaturalist18,
029
               ImageNet-LT, and CIFAR10/100-LT, as well as fine-tuning Vision Transformer
030            (ViT) on iNaturalist18, our method outperforms the state-of-the-art algorithms.
031            Our code and trained models will be made available following the publication of
032            this paper.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In real-world scenarios, data often follow Zipf’s law, resulting in long-tailed class distributions (Reed
038   (2001)). This imbalance poses a challenge for deep neural networks trained with Empirical Risk
039
      Minimization (ERM), as they tend to favor head classes with abundant samples while neglecting
      tail classes with fewer samples (Zhang et al. (2023)). Such bias hinders model performance in real-
040
      world applications with long-tail distributions, such as visual question answering (Dai et al. (2023);
041
      Shi et al. (2024)), medical image diagnosis (Huang et al. (2024); Holste et al. (2024)), and unmanned
042   aerial vehicle detection (Yu et al. (2021); Pan et al. (2023)).
043
044   To tackle this challenge, recent studies have focused on two main goals: enhancing individual pre-
045
      diction quality and ensuring aggregate evaluation remains unbiased by class distribution. For the
      first goal, mainstream paradigms aim to enhance network representation to improve individual sam-
046
      ple performance, particularly for tail samples, measured by metric ℓ. Common approaches include
047
      re-weighting Cui et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2024b); Peng et al. (2024), ensemble learning methods
048   (Wang et al. (2021b); Li et al. (2022); Tao et al. (2023)), and decouple training (Kang et al. (2020);
049   Xu et al. (2023)). For the second goal, numerous aim to ensure that aggregate evaluation is not dom-
050   inated by head classes. Methods like modified sampling strategies (Chawla et al. (2002); Ren et al.
051   (2020); Kang et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2021))and information mixing/transfering (Chou et al. (2020);
052   Wang et al. (2024a); Gao et al. (2024); Rangwani et al. (2024)) balance the number or diversity of
053   training samples. In addition, logit adjustment techniques (Cao et al. (2019); Menon et al. (2021);
      Hong et al. (2021a)) make the estimator less sensitive to class imbalance during training.

055   tain classes, contradicting the purpose of long-tail learning. To better illustrate this trade-off, we
056   evaluated two representative methods: LGLA(Tao et al. (2023)), which enhances individual sam-
057   ple performance through structural improvements, and DODA(Wang et al. (2024a)), which reduces

      evaluation with individual performance.

      In response to this phenomenon, we mathematically analyze the limitations of ERM framework
      in long-tailed learning, focusing on both individual and aggregate evaluation tasks. For individual
079
      evaluation, we observe that Negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss, which heavily relies on softmax,
080
      struggles to distinguish correct from incorrect predictions when their predicted probabilities for the
081   target class are similar. This weakens its ability to assess prediction quality effectively. From the per-
082   spective of energy, we find that this ambiguity arises from the misalignment between softmax-based
083   scores and the input probability density, obscuring meaningful differences in model confidence. In
084   aggregate evaluation, measured by estimator R, we connect the long-tailed problem to causal infer-
085   ence by treating long-tailed datasets as instances of data Missing Not At Random (MNAR), where
086   only a subset of data is observable. Under the MNAR condition, the naive estimator in ERM, which
087   averages over observed samples, is severely biased and fails to accurately assess true performance
088   due to the underlying data distribution bias.
089   To address these limitations, we introduce Re-Debias, a comprehensive framework combining the
090   Residual-Energy score with Debias estimator. The Residual-Energy score unified predictions onto
091   a single scale, where lower values indicate larger errors, and higher values reflect more accurate pre-
092   dictions. Our mathematical analysis and empirical validation confirm that this energy-based score
093   is particularly well-suited for detection tasks, as it captures non-target class information typically
094   missed by softmax-based scores. The Debias estimator corrects the inherent class-wise bias in the
095
      naive estimator of ERM by estimating and leveraging inverse propensity weights from causal infer-
      ence. We theoretically prove that it is an unbiased estimator, capable of delivering more accurate
096
      performance evaluations under long-tailed distributions. To ensure Fisher consistency, we further
097
      incorporate the propensity into the logits. We validate our method through comprehensive experi-
098   ments, demonstrating robust performance across various datasets and target-label distributions, con-
099   firming its effectiveness and generalizability.

107     Residual-Energy score, an energy-based metric that captures non-target class information more
        accurately than softmax-based socres, improving the precision of individual predictions.

119   2.1   L ONG -TAILED LEARNING
120
121   Long-tailed class distribution datasets are inevitable in real-world applications. Most existing long-
122   tail classification methods solve the long-tailed problem in class-wise, and can be divided into three
123   categories Zhang et al. (2023): class rebalancing (Menon et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024b); Zang
124   et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2017); Du & Wu (2023)) attempts to balance the class distribution during
125   training; Information enhancement (Chou et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2024a; 2021a)) try to introduce
126
      additional information to improve the performance of the tail class without sacrificing the perfor-
      mance of the head class, for example Tang et al. (2022) utilizes the pre-trained model (Paszke et al.
127
      (2019)) to generate image feature clusters as annotations of implicit attributes, and then uses data
128
      sampling strategies to build different training environments for invariant feature learning; and mod-
129   ule improvement Zhou et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2020); Tao et al. (2023)) tries
130   to come up with solutions from exploring methods to optimize network modules to the long-tailed
131   problem. However, all these approaches rely on softmax-based scores to measure prediction quality,
132   which is suboptimal. From energy perspective, the softmax-based score emphasizes reducing the
133   target class energy and increasing that of other classes but neglects sufficiently boosting non-target
134   class energies. This leads to similar loss values for correct and incorrect predictions, diminishing
135   the model’s ability to accurately distinguish between target and non-target classes.
136
137   2.2   E NERGY- BASED LEARNING
138
139   Energy-based models(EBMs) (LeCun et al. (2006); Ranzato et al. (2006; 2007)), rooted in Boltz-
140
      mann machines (Ackley et al. (1985)), offer a flexible framework for both probabilistic and non-
      probabilistic learning. In image recognition, EBMs aim to assign as low energy as possible to
141
      images from the target class and high energies to images of other classes. Recent advancements like
142
      BiDVL(Kan et al. (2022) )proposes a bi-level optimization to enhance energy-based hidden variable
143   models, while CLEL(Lee et al. (2023)) leverages contrastive representation learning to make EBM
144   training faster and more efficient. In GAN training, Zhao et al. (2017) leverages energy values to im-
145   prove the discriminator’s performance. Despite these successes, training EBMs remains challenging
146   due to the intractability of the normalization constant. To bypass the calculation of proper normal-
147   ization, Liu et al. (2020) shows that energy scores can better distinguish in- and out-of-distribution
148   samples compared to softmax-based methods. However, existing approaches mainly focus on the
149   energy of target or total classes, overlooking the non-target class energy. Taking a step further,
150   we introduce the residual-energy score, which measures the energy of non-target classes, reducing
151
      the overconfidence of softmax scores and offering greater optimization flexibility without requiring
      normalization. Our approach directly optimizes the energy gap between boundary samples, aligning
152
      naturally with energy-based detectors. Additionally, previous EBMs have largely ignored the long-
153
      tailed class imbalance problem. To address this, we propose an unbiased performance estimator that
154   mitigates the imbalance using causal inference principles.
