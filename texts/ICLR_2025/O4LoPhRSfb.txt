009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Driven by the wave of Large Language Models (LLMs), Video-Language Models
012            (VLMs) have become a significant yet challenging technology to bridge the gap
013            between video and text. Although previous VLM works have made significant
014            progress, almost all of them implicitly assume that all the texts are predefined by
015            the specific template. In real-world applications, such an assumption is impossible
016
               to satisfy, since predefining all the texts is extremely time-consuming and labor-
               intensive. Besides, these predefined text inputs are too strict and user-unfriendly,
017
               limiting their applications. It is observed that given a video input, texts with simi-
018
               lar semantics lead to various performances. To this end, in this paper, we propose
019            a novel text-augmented VLM method to improve video-text fusion by text rewrit-
020            ing. Specifically, we first generate various text samples from the original ones
021            based on the pre-trained LLM to target specific text components. A multi-level
022            contrastive learning module is designed to mine the coarse-grained language in-
023            formation. Moreover, we also propose an attribute-based text reasoning strategy
024            to learn fine-grained textual semantics. Extensive experiments on many video-
025            language tasks show that the proposed method can serve as the plug-and-play
026            module to effectively improve the performance of state-of-the-art VLM works.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

030
      Due to remarkable success, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have attracted more and more
031   attention (Tian et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). LVLMs require cooperation from both
032   computer vision and natural language processing for precise semantic alignment and have a wide
033   range of applications such as video summarization and video question answering. Benefiting from
034   the strong knowledge integration ability in large language models (LLMs), LVLMs show superior
035   performances in solving complex image-language tasks by utilizing appropriate human-instructed
036   prompts (Hakim et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2024). Since the real-world videos
037   contain much temporal information, LVLMs still have difficulty to handle the real-world videos.
038   Besides, the sentence text is the most important input that accompanies the video due to its human-
039
      friendly and descriptive nature.
040   Current video-language models contain three main popular yet challenging tasks: video question
041   answering (VideoQA) (Gao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024), video sentence grounding (VSG) (Zhang
042   et al., 2023b; Qi et al., 2024) and video-text retrieval (VTR) (Zhu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a).
043   Video QA is a significant multi-modal task where a model is given a video along with a natural
044   language question about the video content, and it must generate or select the correct answer. The task
045
      requires the model to understand the visual cues in the video, as well as the language of the question,
      to provide relevant and accurate responses. Given a language text and an untrimmed video, VSG
046
      aims at retrieving the start and end timestamps of the target video moment, semantically according
047
      to a sentence text. Given a language text, VTR targets to retrieve relevant videos from a large video
048   database, which can be either text-based (text-to-video retrieval) or video-based (video-to-video
049   retrieval). The significant goal of VTR is to find videos that best match the given input by analyzing
050   visual content, actions, and sometimes audio cues. Their performance in these downstream tasks
051   depends on their capability to extract video features and align them with text features. Some methods
052   only perform data augmentations on the video input to improve the model robustness during training
053   in every epoch. In contrast, existing methods only utilize predefined texts without any augmentation.
      In real-world applications, these sentence texts with similar textual semantics might be inputted

      In this paper, we propose a simple yet highly effective framework to improve the robustness and
083
      performance of VLMs. Specifically, we leverage the large language models (LLM) to generate
084
      multiple variants of each text in the video-text pairs. To obtain different text types, we generate a
085   small set of variation-origin by two strategies: LLM-based datasets and existing original datasets.
086   After obtaining the variation-origin pairs, we utilize them as examples to prompt LLM model for
087   diversifying all the texts in the VSG datasets. Different from previous sentence augmentation works
088   that only change some words to preserve sentence structures, LLM has a strong language processing
089   ability to generate rich variations for diverse text inputs due to their extensive training datasets and
090   emergent properties. Based on the above sentence augmentation, each video corresponds to diverse
091   texts. Moreover, we introduce GPT (Radford, 2018) to generate various hard negative texts from the
092   original (anchor) texts by changing different sentence parts. In particular, we utilize precise prompt
093
      engineering to modify specific parts of the sentence with the rest parts unchanged. Also, we generate
      positive samples that lie relatively far from the anchor in the embedding space. To further understand
094
      the latent textual semantics, we design an attribute-based text reasoning strategy for fine-grained text
095
      mining. To analyze the relative significance of each sentence part, we incorporate these generated
096   samples by a weighted contrastive loss function. With these diverse texts, we target to train VLM
097   models with augmentation from the text perspective. For convenience, we randomly choose a text
098   augmentation from multiple diverse texts.

129   2   R ELATED W ORKS
130
131   Large vision-language models. The breakthrough of LLMs in language-oriented tasks (Ma et al.,
132
      2024; Du et al., 2024) and the emergence of GPT-4 have prompted researchers to explore the poten-
      tial of LLMs in assisting with a range of tasks across multi-modal scenarios (Carolan et al., 2024;
133
      Yin et al., 2024). This has led to the development of a new field, namely large vision-language
134
      models (LVLMs). A variety of strategies and models have been proposed to address the discrepancy
135   between text and other modalities. Some works employ learnable texts to extract visual information
136   and generate language using LLMs conditioned on the visual features. Models including GPT-4o,
137   MiniGPT-4 and LLaVA learn simple projection layers to align the visual features from visual en-
138   coders with text embeddings for LLMs. Additionally, parameter-efficient fine-tuning is adopted
139   by introducing lightweight trainable adapters into models. Several benchmarks have verified that
140   LVLMs demonstrate satisfactory performance on visual perception and comprehension.
141   Although these methods have achieved promising results, all of them heavily rely on correctly
142   aligned multi-modal datasets. Therefore, it is highly expected to develop a VLM model that is
143   robust to different texts with similar semantics, which has not been studied as far as we know. Thus,
144   we make the first attempt to reveal the text understanding problem in VLM task and propose to
145   eliminate the negative impact of the different texts with any template. More details in Section A.2.

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
148
149
      We elaborate on the proposed method, which strengthens the text encoder to obtain consistent repre-
150   sentations for various semantically similar texts in real-world multi-modal datasets (e.g., Charades-
151   STA (Sigurdsson et al., 2016)), multiple semantics-similar texts often share a video moment with
152   the target activity. For example, “Person opens the door” and “The door is opened by person” have
153   similar semantics. Since the text template is fixed, it is still challenging to diversify the text input.
154   Thus, we design a text augmentation module to generate semantically similar texts. The overall
155   framework is shown in Figure 2.
156   Problem statement. Due to the strong language processing ability of of LLMs, we utilize LLM to
157   generate various texts by replacing different components for simulating the practical labeling pro-
158   cess in the format-free setting. We denote {Q1 , . . . , QM } as the textual input set in the VLM task,
159   where M denotes the total number of sentences. Previous VLM methods (Yu et al., 2023; Wang
160   et al., 2022b; 2024) cannot well handle these texts with similar semantics since they do not fully
161   understand the textual information in the sentence. To address the existing models’ limitations in
      correlating major sentence parts with suitable video representations, we present a novel plug-and-

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we rethink the LLM task from the user-friendly language input. We observe that
535   many VLMs cannot fully understand the language texts. Given some texts with similar semantics
536   and a video, these VLMs output various results. Thus, we design a plug-and-play framework to
537   improve the generation ability of previous methods on various text templates. Extensive experiments
538   on many challenging datasets show that our framework can serve as the plug-and-play module for
539   state-of-the-art VLM works to improve their performance on various video-language tasks. In our
      future work, we will extend our model into more multi-modal tasks.

785   A.1     AUGMENTING TEXTS BY P RE - TRAINED LLM S
786
787   Considering the strong natural language processing of pre-trained LLMs, we will generate various
788   text augmentations by pre-trained LLMs. Inspired by the effectiveness of LLMs, we rewrite the
789   texts in VLM datasets to generate variation-origin pairs based on the following approaches.
790
791
      A.1.1    AUGMENTATION BY HUMAN .
792   To make our model more user-friendly, we randomly invite ten persons from different countries to
793   rewrite some texts. For convenience, we randomly choose some video-text pairs from these VLM
794   datasets. For obtaining diverse text variations, we encourage rewriters to rewrite these texts based
795   on the target video segments. The human-based rewriting approach will enhance the creation of text
796   augmentation. Finally, we can obtain the variation-origin text pairs that include the original texts
797   and the corresponding human-rewritten version.
798   Augmentation by chat robots. Recently, LLMs-based chat robots (e.g., ChatGPT or Bard) have
799   achieved impressive performance in natural language processing. Thus, we target to rewrite the texts
800   by chat robots. Firstly, we randomly choose some texts from the VSG datasets. Then, we utilize
801   the web portals of chat robots to generate target texts by providing prompts. Some examples of the
802   chat robot rewriting is shown in our supplementary material. With the powerful language processing
803   ability of these chat robots, we can rewrite texts by utilizing different templates and vocabularies.
804   The rewriting approach can preserve most textual semantics corresponding to the target segment.
805   Augmentation by open-source LLMs. Since it will lead to significant financial and time costs if
806   we generate all the augmented texts by these closed-source chat robots (e.g., ChatGPT and Bard),
807   we utilize an open-source LLMs, LLaMA, to generate the positive texts. Due to the strong gener-
808   alization ability, LLaMA can be directly used to rewrite all the texts in the video-text datasets. For
809   better generation ability, we leverage the LLaMA-7B model for text generation to guarantee that the
      generated texts are diverse and semantically relevant to the original texts.

      Based on the above approaches, we can obtain three types of variation-origin text pairs: Bard-based,
811   ChatGPT-based, and LLaMA-based. Then, we treat them as inputs for the In-Context Learning
812   strategy (Zhang et al., 2024). In each approach, we randomly choose some texts from the video-text
813   datasets for target text generation, which will generate some variation-origin text pairs. These pairs
814   contain comprehensive and diverse training samples as the input of our framework.

816
      A.2   M ORE DETAILS FOR R ELATED WORKS
817
818   Fully-supervised VSG. VSG is a new task introduced recently (Gao et al., 2017; Anne Hendricks
819   et al., 2017). Most previous algorithms (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2017; Chen et al.,
820   2018; Zhang et al., 2019b; Yuan et al., 2019a; Zhang et al., 2020b; Liu et al., 2021) have been
821   proposed within the propose-and-rank framework, which first generates moment candidates and
822   then utilizes multimodal matching to retrieve the most relevant candidate for a text. Some of them
823   (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2017) take multiple sliding windows as candidates. To
824
      improve the quality of the candidates, (Zhang et al., 2019b; Yuan et al., 2019a) pre-cut the video
      on each frame by multiple pre-defined temporal scales, and directly integrate sentence information
825
      with fine-grained video clips for scoring. For instance, Xu et al. (Xu et al., 2019) introduce a
826
      multi-level model to integrate visual and textual features earlier and further re-generate texts as
827   an auxiliary task. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2019a) model relations among candidate moments
828   produced from a convolutional neural network with the guidance of the text information. Although
829   these methods achieve great performance, they are severely limited by the heavy computation on
830   proposal matching/ranking, and sensitive to the quality of pre-defined proposals. Recently, many
831   methods (Chen et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2019b; Zeng et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020a; Nan et al.,
832   2021) propose to utilize the boundary-regression framework. Specifically, they directly predict two
833   probabilities at each frame by leveraging cross-modal interactions between video and text, which
834   indicate whether this frame is a start/end frame of the ground truth video moment.
835   Weakly-supervised VSG. Despite the decent progress on the grounding performance, fully-
836   supervised methods severely rely on the numerous annotations, which are significantly labor-
837   intensive and time-consuming to obtain. To alleviate this dense reliance to a certain extent, some
838   weakly-supervised VSG methods are proposed (Mithun et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Lin et al.,
839   2020a). For a weakly supervised VSG task, (Duan et al., 2018) decomposes it into two sub-tasks:
840   event captioning and text localization. (Duan et al., 2018) first assumes that each caption describes
841
      only one temporal moment, and then designs a cycle network to train the model. As the pioneering
      work for weakly-supervised VSG, (Mithun et al., 2019) learns a joint representation between the
842
      video and the text by proposing Text-Guided-Attention network and utilizing an attention weight.
843
      To improve the exploration and exploitation, (Lin et al., 2020a) chooses the top-K proposals and
844   measures the semantic similarity between the video and the text for localization. By proposing a
845   Semantic Completion Network, (Lin et al., 2020a) treats the masked text as input and predicts the
846   masked words from the video features.
