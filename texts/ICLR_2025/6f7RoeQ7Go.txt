009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent studies have highlighted the potential benefits of supplementing Large
012
               Language Models (LLMs) with information retrieved from knowledge graphs to
013
               enhance their performance. However, current approaches often introduce additional
014            noise in the pipeline process of knowledge retrieval and reasoning, leading to the
015            accumulation of errors, impeding LLMs from effectively combining the external
016            knowledge in answering complex multi-hop questions. To this end, we introduce
017            RefKG, an innovative framework specifically crafted to enhance the reasoning
018            capabilities of LLMs through reflective engagement with knowledge graphs. In par-
019            ticular, RefKG autonomously conduct retrieval and reflection on knowledge graphs.
020            Its reasoning process includes four steps: decomposing complex queries, retriev-
021            ing and pruning evidence subgraphs, generating textual evidence, and evidence-
022
               enhanced reasoning. To enhance the alignment of LLMs with external knowledge,
               we have developed a multi-task tuning strategy that not only infuses knowledge to
023
               LLMs but also teaches them how to utilize the knowledge in answering questions,
024
               thereby significantly improving their ability to handle knowledge-intensive tasks.
025            Experimental results on fact verification and knowledge graph question answering
026            tasks demonstrate that RefKG outperforms previous state-of-the-art models.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently showcased their robust and wide-ranging capabili-
033   ties in tackling diverse challenges in NLP including machine translation (Zhang et al., 2023) and
034   information extraction (Sainz et al., 2023). However, given the ever-evolving nature of real-world
035   knowledge (Zhang et al., 2023), LLMs occasionally exhibit limitations in domain-specific expertise
036   or in timely updating their knowledge bases. This shortfall often results in hallucinations within their
037   responses, where the generated content deviates from factual accuracy (Huang et al., 2023).
038   To alleviate the issue of hallucinations in LLMs on knowledge-intensive tasks such as Knowledge
039   Graph Question Answering (KGQA) (Gupta et al., 2018), a promising strategy involves augmenting
040   LLMs with external knowledge sources (Tan et al., 2023), like knowledge graphs (KGs) (Luo et al.,
041   2018; Hu et al., 2018). This augmentation process typically involves retrieving factually relevant
042   knowledge from extensive knowledge bases to assist LLMs in formulating answers that rely on this
043   externally retrieved information. However, existing solutions still suffers from several shortcomings.
044   First, due to the large scale and complexity of knowledge graphs, the retrieval and reasoning processes
045
      often introduce numerous noise triplets that are irrelevant to the query. Prior approaches have been
      limited in their capacity to accurately address complex queries (Lan et al., 2021) and have struggled to
046
      effectively address such noise. Effectively, stably, and interpretably constraining retrieved subgraphs
047
      continues to be a critical challenge. Second, recent investigations (Li et al., 2023b; Nie et al., 2023)
048   have predominantly performed black-box testing on proprietary models such as ChatGPT. These
049   studies often employ in-context learning techniques (Liu et al., 2022), where external knowledge is
050   incorporated into the prompts to steer the model’s response generation. Although these training-free
051   methods enable the integration of external knowledge, they do not enhance the interactive capabilities
052   between LLMs and knowledge graphs, thereby limiting the potential of LLMs to efficiently acquire
053   and deploy knowledge, especially when supervised signals are available. Additionally, black-box
      models cannot be deployed privately, which significantly limits their flexibility and adaptability.

064   In this paper, we introduce RefKG, an innovative framework specifically crafted to enhance the
065   reasoning capabilities of LLMs through reflective engagement with knowledge graphs. In particular,
066   RefKG is structured as a three-step framework: 1) A Query Decoupling Module that decouples a
067   complex query into multiple sub-queries that share a common knowledge background. 2) A LLM-
068   Driven Knowledge Graph Exploration Module that iteratively and reflectively retrieves relevant
069
      evidence subgraphs from a knowledge base and refines the knowledge through an expert model.. 3)
      An Inference with Knowledge Reconstruction Module that transforms structured knowledge into a
070
      natural language format that is more easily understood by the LLM, and integrates it with the question
071
      to derive the answer. Compared to approaches that directly use retrieved results in prompts (Kim et al.,
072   2023a), our approach maximizes the reflection capabilities of LLMs (Asai et al., 2023) to critically
073   assess and refine the evidence subgraph. Furthermore, we have formulated a knowledge-driven
074   multi-task tuning strategy that provides RefKG with foundational expertise in knowledge-intensive
075   reasoning. This is achieved by fine-tuning the model on a specially synthesized corpus, equipping it
076   with the necessary skills for advanced reasoning tasks. Together, the three-step process enables our
077   approach to autonomously retrieve, reflect, and utilize knowledge in solving knowledge-intensive
078   tasks. In summary, our main contributions are three-fold:
079          • We propose RefKG, an innovative framework specifically crafted to enhance the reasoning
080            capabilities of LLMs through reflective engagement with knowledge graphs. In particular,
081            our approach simplifies complex queries through decomposition, enabling effective retrieval,
082            reflection and reasoning within knowledge graphs.
083          • We develop an LLM-Generated corpus for knowledge-intensive multi-task tuning, equipping
084            LLMs with initial expertise in knowledge-intensive reasoning, setting the stage for more
085            advanced task-specific learning.
086
             • We extensively evaluate RefKG on fact verification and knowledge graph question answering
               tasks. The experimental results affirm that RefKG not only outperforms previous KG-
087
               augmented methods across various open-source LLMs but also enhances the explainability
088
               of LLMs’ reasoning process.

093   2.1   KG R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED M ETHODS
094
095   Knowledge graphs (KGs) organize relationships between entities in a structured manner. Using KG
096   retrieval to enhance LLMs has proven effective in alleviating hallucination phenomena (Agrawal
097   et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023). Recently, there has been increasing research on KG retrieval, which
098   can be broadly categorized into two methods: (1) Semantic Parsing-Based Methods: These methods
099
      encode input questions, transforming them into logical forms (LF), and then execute retrieval on
      the KG. For example, SSKGQA (Li & Ji, 2022) generates query graphs based on natural language
100
      questions, attempting to leverage the semantic structure of questions to eliminate incorrect query
101
      graph structures. RnG-KBQA (Ye et al., 2022) introduces a ranking-and-generating framework that
102   ranks candidate LF from questions and generates the final LF based on the top-k candidates. However,
103   these approaches require generating executable SPARQL statements and additional label information.
104   (2) Information Retrieval-Based Methods: PullNet (Sun et al., 2019) extracts a subgraph of the KG
105   starting from topic entities and employs Graph Neural Networks to predict entity answers within the
106   subgraph. UniK-QA (Oguz et al., 2022), based on the Seq2Seq framework, retrieves triplets from the
107   knowledge graph and combines them with the question to generate answers. However, while this
      method depends on the accuracy of the retrieved subgraphs or triplets, it lacks mechanisms to filter

135   2.2       LLM S R EASONING FOR KGQA
136
137   Recently, an increasing amount of work has been dedicated to leveraging the reasoning abilities
138   of LLMs in Knowledge Graph Question Answering (KGQA). KAPING (Baek et al., 2023b) and
139   KG-GPT (Kim et al., 2023a) prompts LLMs to generate answers by placing retrieved triplets into
140   pre-defined prompts. However, they overlook the fact that triplets retrieved from KGs may not be in
141   the form of natural language text, potentially increasing the difficulty of LLMs’ inference. Moreover,
142   they all use APIs providing by the black box model for inference and cannot train and deploy the
143
      model. Retrieve-Rewrite-Answer (Wu et al., 2023) addresses this issue by introducing a pre-training
      task and fine-tuning open-source LLMs on KG-to-text corpora, transforming triplet form text into free
144
      form text that is more easily comprehensible for LLMs. Nevertheless, the aforementioned methods
145
      do not filter the extracted triplets. This oversight may lead to irrelevant information being input into
146   LLMs, thereby misleading them into generating wrong results.

156   When processing multi-hop queries, challenges like extended reasoning paths, semantic drift, and
157   information loss are frequently encountered. Inspired by the divide-and-conquer paradigm, we
158   initially decouple a complex query into multiple sub-queries, each of which shares the contextual
159   semantics but contains only a single-hop atomic query. Specifically, given a knowledge-intensive
160   query denoted as q, alongside a collection of knowledge entities E related to this question, the
161   objective is to predict the hop number H, derive a sequence of sub-queries qsub = [q1 , ..., qH ], and to
      ascertain the corresponding entity subset Esub = [e1 , ..., eH ] that are pertinent to these sub-queries.

272   3.4.1    T RAINING C ORPUS
273
274   Corpus Generation. Addressing the gap left by existing corpora, which do not meet our specific
275   training needs, we have initiated a multi-task-driven strategy for corpus generation. Recognizing
276
      ChatGPT’s exceptional abilities in understanding and generating text, as highlighted in recent research
      (Li et al., 2023a; Tahmid Rahman Laskar et al., 2023), we have employed ChatGPT as a corpus
277
      generator. To create training data, we focus on three specific tasks: (1) Query Decoupling, (2)
278
      Evidence Subgraph Retrieval, and (3) Inference with Knowledge Reconstruction. For each task, we
279   have pre-defined a template T , into which we insert feature elements x relevant to each task, forming
280   a text prompt p. This prompt p is then fed into ChatGPT, generating the corresponding training
281   corpora y. See Appendix A.4 for more details.
282
283   Quality Control. In light of the lack of explicit labels and the challenge of applying general metrics,
284   we have developed specific evaluation methods for assessing the quality of the generated outcomes:
285   (1) Query Decoupling: We evaluate the decoupling quality based on the entity set E extracted from
286   the original question and the entity sets Ediv = [ediv,1 , ..., ediv,H ] derived from the decomposed
287   sub-queries. The criteria for considering the decoupling results as high-quality are as follows: (a)
                        SH
288   E ̸= ∅. (b) E = i=1 ediv,i . (c) If |Ediv | > 1, then ∀ediv,i ∈ Ediv , ediv,i ⫋ E. If |Ediv | = 1, then
289   Ediv = E. (2) Inference with Knowledge Reconstruction: We perform a unified assessment of the
290   two-step pipeline process. For the answers A generated through these two steps, we identify instances
291
      where the feedback from the generator corresponds with the factual ground truth as indicators of
      high-quality data.

      3.4.2    T RAINING
295   Previous research has explored the conditions under which multi-task learning is effective, concluding
296   that it performs well when tasks are diverse yet related (Ni et al., 2023). To this end, we have designed
297   tasks that are related in terms of knowledge background but involve significant differences in skills.
298
      In the training phase, we synergistically infuse both linguistic and entity knowledge into LLMs by
299
      focusing on the optimization of three tasks: (1) Query Decoupling enables the model to learn how to
300   decouple multi-hop queries into single-hop sub-queries. (2) Evidence Subgraph Retrieval enhance
301   the model’s ability to navigate, search, and generate evidence subgraphs within a knowledge graph.
302   (3) Evidence Generation enhances the model’s ability to translate evidence from the graph’s structural
303   format into coherent textual form, bridging the gap between graph-based data and natural language,
304   and reasoning based on the evidence to derive answers.
305
      The auto-regressive training objective focuses on training the LLM to predict subsequent tokens
306   based on previous tokens. Specifically, for the prompt pi of different tasks, the objective function for
307   generating the target answer z = [z1 , ..., zT ] is:

      FactKG is a fact-verification benchmark based on KG, containing 108K natural language statements
346   verifiable via DBpedia (Lehmann et al., 2015), categorized into five reasoning types: One-hop,
347   Conjunction, Existence, Multi-hop, and Negation. Furthermore, FactKG contains various patterns,
348   including colloquial style statements as well as written style statements, to increase practicality.
349
350   WebQuestionsSP is a KGQA benchmark containing full semantic parses in SPARQL queries for
351   4,737 questions(3,098 train, 1,639 test). It is built on Freebase and includes multi-hop questions,
352   linked through topic entities, reasoning chains, and SPARQL queries. It provides semantic parses in
353   SPARQL with standard Freebase entity identifiers, which can be directly executed on Freebase to
354   return answers to questions.
355
356   MetaQA is a comprehensive benchmark for assessing question-answering systems, particularly
357   those utilizing knowledge graphs. It comprises over 400K questions, including one-hop, two-hop,
358   and three-hop reasoning. This dataset is crucial for evaluating knowledge graph-based question
359

      task to enhance performance on other tasks. (2)Training sereval tasks simultaneously can reduce the

506   Impact of numbers of Top-K Retrieval. As shown in Figure 5, we explore Top-K values ranging

532
533
      In this paper, we proposed the RefKG framework, which engages with knowledge graphs in a
534   reflective manner to identify the most likely relational paths and evidence, using this curated evidence
535   to derive answers. To Infuse the LLM with the abilities to decouple, navigate, refine, reconstruct, and
536   reason over knowledge, we developed a knowledge-driven multi-task tuning approach and built a
537   corresponding training corpus. The experimental results prove its effectiveness on fact verification
538   and knowledge graph question answering. Our method can be deployed on any open-source LLM, and
539   the experimental results indicate that it achieves excellent performance in two knowledge-intensive
      tasks: fact verification and knowledge graph question answering.

      A.1   L IMITATIONS
705
706   In this section, we faithfully discuss the limitations of our approach and potential avenues for future
707   research.
708   Cumulative error effect. Although our framework can handle some complex multi-hop or negation
709   questions, it involves multiple subtasks. The workflow of the pipeline generates a cumulative error
710   effect. For example, if the model misidentifies entities in the first step of sentence decomposition,
711   subsequent answers obtained will inevitably be incorrect. Therefore, future work could focus on
712   reducing error rates by introducing efficient and accurate retrieval methods or instruction fine-tuning
713
      methods.
      Larger model sizes. Limited by computational resources, we only applied RefKG to the 7B LLM
714

      A.4   C ORPUS G ENERATION
757
758   We use the GPT-3.5-turbo API ($0.002 / 1K tokens) to generate training corpora, with the following
759   steps:
760
761   Query Decoupling. Given a question q and a set of entities e, we insert them into a predefined
762   generation template pdec to obtain a text prompt. This text prompt is then input into ChatGPT to
763   produce an output sequence z = [z1 , ..., zT ], which includes sub-queries and their respective entity
764   subsets.
765
766
767   Knowledge Reconstruction. Given an evidence subgraph Gevi stored in triplet form, we first
768
      linearize it into a text format by concatenating the head entity, relation word, and tail entity to form
      textual triplets. We insert this sequence of triplets into a predefined template pevi : "Your task is
769
      to transform a knowledge graph in triplets (or tuples) format into a single sentence, preserving
770
      the original words or expressions from the triplets as much as possible. The knowledge graph is:
771   graph. The sentence is:". This prompt is then fed into ChatGPT, resulting in an output sequence
772   z = [z1 , ..., zT ] that contains the textualized evidence.
773
774
775   Joint Inference. Given a query q and an evidence sequence evi, we insert both into a predefined
776   template pinf , input it into ChatGPT, and the model will produce inference results and explanations
777   based on the input.

886   A.7    T RAINING D ETAILS FOR E XPERT M ODEL
887
888   We trained the Expert LLM using 30,000 annotated data entries, as detailed below:
889
890   Evidence score annotation. For each sub-query qi and triplet format evidence t, we first employ
891   the semantic similarity model DistilBERT to assign a similarity score, denoted as s, to represent the
892
      supportiveness of the evidence triplet toward the query.
893   For each sub-query qi , we sort all evidence triplets t based on their scores, from highest to lowest.
894   This set includes triplets that are relevant to the query as well as some that are noise. We then match
895   these triplets with the ground truth. If a triplet from the ground truth is ranked among the top k, we
896   retain it as part of the training data; otherwise, we filter it out.
897   It’s important to note that we don’t directly use all the collected annotated data for training. Instead,
898   we first conduct a complete inference process with this data. If the final inference result is correct,
899   we retain the annotated data as the gold score; if it’s incorrect, we discard it. This approach ensures
900   the high quality of the annotated data. Additionally, to minimize the influence of noise during the
901   training process, we have eliminated anomalously high and low scores.

926          • Noise introduction: Refers to the introduction of incorrect knowledge, conflicting knowl-

      The details of the noise flow in the four stages are as follows:
935
936   In the Query Decoupling: A small number of cases may experience partial entity information loss,
937   leading to the introduction of noise.
938   In the Subgraph Retrieval: Since we aim to retrieve as much relevant knowledge as possible, it is
939   inevitable to introduce some irrelevant knowledge and even knowledge that conflicts with correct
940   information. Among them, some conflicting information may interfere with the results, while some
941   irrelevant information has a minor impact.
942   In the Knowledge Refinement: Some incorrect and irrelevant triples are scored and removed, but
943   there is also a small possibility that a few correct answers may be mistakenly filtered out.
944
945
      In the Knowledge Reconstruction: While converting triples into textual information, the model
      performs implicit reasoning. During this process, the model may actively discard some incorrect or
946
      conflicting information and even correct erroneous information, but this may also result in the loss of
947
      a few correct pieces of information.
948
949   The statistical results show that noise introduction is often difficult to completely avoid when
950   handling complex problems. Through the collaborative operation of various tasks, particularly
951
      during the Knowledge Refinement and Knowledge Reconstruction stages, we effectively control
      noise, significantly mitigating its cumulative effects across tasks and reducing its impact on overall
952
      performance. This further validates the robustness and effectiveness of our approach in complex
953
      knowledge reasoning scenarios.
