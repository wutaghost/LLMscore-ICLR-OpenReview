                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               We consider bandits with anytime knapsacks (BwAK), a novel version of the BwK
010
               problem where there is an anytime cost constraint instead of a total cost budget.
011            This problem setting introduces additional complexities as it mandates adherence
012            to the constraint throughout the decision-making process. We propose SUAK, an
013            algorithm that utilizes upper confidence bounds to identify the optimal mixture of
014            arms while maintaining a balance between exploration and exploitation. SUAK is
015            an adaptive algorithm that strategically utilizes the available budget in each round
016            in the decision-making process and skips a round when it is possible to violate the
017            anytime cost constraint. In particular, SUAK slightly under-utilizes the available
018            cost budget to reduce the need for skipping rounds. We show that SUAK attains
019
               the same problem-dependent regret upper bound of O(K log T ) established in
               prior work under the simpler BwK framework. Finally, we provide simulations to
020
               verify the utility of SUAK in practical settings.

023   1     I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Multi-armed bandits (MAB) is one of the fundamental problems in the field of sequential decision-
026   making under uncertainty. In its essence, it is a problem setting where an agent must strategically al-
027   locate resources among the arms to maximize cumulative reward over time, navigating the trade-off
028   between gathering information about uncertain arms (exploration) and exploiting known information
029   to optimize immediate rewards (exploitation). This problem finds applications across diverse do-
030   mains, including reinforcement learning (Intayoad et al., 2020), online advertising (Slivkins, 2013),
031
      clinical trials (Villar et al., 2015), and resource allocation (Soare, 2015).
032   The Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) problem, introduced by Badanidiyuru et al. (2013), is an exten-
033   sion of the classical multi-armed bandit problem, with the additional constraint of limited resource
034   capacity akin to the knapsack problem (Tran-Thanh et al., 2012). In this scenario, an agent is con-
035   fronted with a set of arms, each associated with an unknown reward and cost distribution. Unlike the
036   traditional bandit setting, selecting an arm incurs both a reward and a cost here, and the agent’s ob-
037
      jective is to maximize the total reward while respecting the total capacity constraint of the knapsack.
      The BwK problem encapsulates the trade-off between exploration and exploitation while manag-
038
      ing resource constraints, presenting a rich framework with applications such as online advertising
039
      (Avadhanula et al., 2021; Badanidiyuru et al., 2018), dynamic resource allocation (Kumar & Klein-
040   berg, 2022), and personalized recommendation engines (Yu et al., 2016).
041
042   In this paper, we consider a specific variant of this problem, which we name as the bandits with
043
      anytime knapsacks (BwAK) problem; where instead of a total cost budget, there is an anytime
      constraint on the average cost. This problem setting introduces an additional level of complexity
044
      as a mixture strategy needs to be employed to be able to pull arms with mean costs higher than
045
      the average cost budget without violating the anytime constraint. The main goal of our work is to
046   develop new algorithms for this framework that achieve as much cumulative reward as possible.
047
048
      1.1   A PPLICATIONS
049
050   The formulation of the anytime constraint considered here has broad applications across various
051   fields. A notable example is inventory management, where a factory produces goods at a constant
052   rate and seeks to maximize revenue by selling to buyers in a marketplace, where bids consisting of
053   price and order size are placed. Our anytime constraint is especially relevant in such scenarios since
      having a negative inventory is not possible. An important aspect of this constraint is that it introduces


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the trade-off between exploiting the available inventory, and skipping a round to accumulate more
055   inventory in an effort to capture bids with higher order sizes. This highlights the added complexity
056   of our problem setting and underscores its broader applicability across a range of settings beyond
057   the standard BwK framework.
058
      Another example is online advertising, where an advertiser sets a daily budget limit to prevent over-
059
      spending. In this context, the ’arms’ represent different ad campaigns or strategies, each with varying
060
      costs that can be selected for the day. The reward can be modeled as the daily revenue generated
061   from clicks or the number of users who subscribe. Further, in portfolio management, our anytime
062   constraint can represent the maximum amount that the customer is willing to invest in a month, and
063   arms can model different investment options. One last example is in satellite systems, where solar
064   panels generate energy and excess energy can be stored in a battery. Here, c can correspond to the
065   energy generated per unit time, and arms can correspond to different tasks that need to be performed,
066   with their rewards reflecting the importance or outcome of the tasks. The costs associated with each
067   arm can than represent the energy consumed to complete the task.

147   constraint, and skip the round if it is the case. Otherwise, we let the BwK algorithm pull an arm.

      3.3   A NALYSIS OF SUAK
271
272   We now characterize the performance of the SUAK by providing the theoretical upper bound on
273   the expected cumulative regret. We first provide definitions of arm gaps and state a set of mild
274   assumptions that are required for the theoretical analysis. We refer the readers to the Appendix for
275   detailed proofs of the results presented in this section.
276   Definition 3.1. The reward gap of an arm is defined as !i = µi→→ ↗ µi .
277   Definition 3.2. The gap of a base I is defined as !I = r→ ↗ rI , where rI is the reward value of
278   the solution of (1) when only arms in I are allowed. We also define !min,i := minI↑I i \I → !I as
279   the minimum reward gap of bases that include the arm i.
280   Definition 3.3. The cost gap of an arm is defined as ↼i = |ωi ↗ c|.
281   Assumption 1. We define ↼min = mini↑[K] ↼i as the minimum cost gap, and assume ↼min > 0.
282
283   Note that regret depends on the cost gap ↼i since the algorithm needs to be able to correctly identify
284   if the true mean cost of an arm is above or below c. This is in turn needed for the adaptive design
285   since if the empirical average cost is above the targeted cost and if an arm with cost more than c is
286
      identified as an arm with cost less than c, pulling that arm might lead to over-consuming the targeted
      budget. Since regret depends on ↼min , ↼min > 0 is needed so that the regret bound is not unbounded.
287
288
      Assumption 2. We assume that we are given an ϱ > 0 such that ϱ ↑ ↼min /(2 + ↼min ↗ c).
289   Note that for any ↼min or c value, ↼min /(2 + ↼min ↗ c) ↔ ↼min /3. Assumption 2 is necessary for
290   the adaptive design of the algorithm in meeting the anytime cost constraint, as we use a cost budget
291   under-utilization of log t/ϱ 2 at round t in SUAK to be able to achieve theoretical guarantees. Also,
292   in SUAK, we set the minimum fraction of time an arm in a base will be pulled to ϱ. With this
293   use, ϱ can be understood as the minimum triggering probability (p→ ), in the probabilistic triggering
294   literature discussed in §3.4. Since the fraction of pulls of a particular arm in a given base can be as
295   low as ϱ, our regret bounds depend on ϱ as in the worst case a base needs to be selected 1/ϱ times
296   in expectation to acquire one sample of each arm in the base.

310   of an arm is greater than or less than c, and also the regret resulting from under-utilizing the budget;
311   the third term is due to expected number of times the anytime constraint may be violated; the fourth
312   term is due to suboptimal arm pulls that occur if the confidence bounds do not hold; and the last
313   term is regret from the initialization phase. The proof of Theorem 3.1 is given in §D, and we also
314   provide a brief proof sketch in §3.5.
315   Note that this problem-dependent upper bound order-wise matches the O(K log T ) problem-
316   dependent bound of prior work for the regular BwK setting. However, SUAK is not optimal
317   for a problem-independent bound since regret can be large when the value of ϱ is small, i.e. if
318   ϱ ↑ 1/ log(T ) assuming the time horizon⇑ T is known. For this case, prior work such as Bernasconi
319   et al. (2024b) can be used to achieve O( KT ) problem-independent regret in our setting.

      5   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
526   In this paper, we introduce a previously unexplored setting for the BwK problem, which we call
527   the bandits with anytime knapsacks (BwAK) problem; where we employ a stricter anytime cost
528   constraint instead of a total cost budget. We provide SUAK, a novel algorithm that under-utilizes
529   the available cost budget and uses skipping to limit the probability of violating the anytime cost
530   constraint, and also uses upper confidence bounds to balance exploration and exploitation. SUAK
531
      achieves a regret upper bound of O(K log T ) compared to the optimal solution of the linear re-
      laxation version of the problem which does not necessarily obey the anytime cost constraint. This
532
      bound is better than the regret upper bound of prior work for the BwK setting on problem-dependent
533
      terms in a wide range of problem instances. We provide simulation results to demonstrate the empir-
534   ical performance of SUAK. Our work opens multiple directions for future research. One interesting
535   future direction is to extend our bandit results to the case where the cost of an arm is a d-dimensional
536   vector. This is a challenging problem as the anytime cost constraint needs to be satisfied in every
537   dimension, which can introduce additional skips, and hence additional regret. Another interesting
538   open direction is the case where the distribution of rewards and costs of arms are stochastic. In this
539   case, satisfying the anytime cost constraint would again be challenging but we conjecture it may be
      accomplished using a more conservative cost budget under-utilization.
