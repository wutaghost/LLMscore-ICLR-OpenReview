009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            To facilitate the deployment of convolutional neural networks on resource-limited
012            devices, filter pruning has emerged as an effective strategy because of its enabled
013            practical acceleration. Evaluating the importance of filters is a crucial challenge
014            in this field. Most existing works on filter pruning assess the relationships of
015            filters using pairwise measures such as Euclidean distance and cosine correlation,
016            which may not capture the global information within the layer. In this paper, we
017            propose a novel filter pruning method, which leverages a graph-based approach
018
               to model the relationships among filters in convolutional layers. Each filter is
               represented as a node in a directed graph, and the edges between nodes capture
019
               the linear dependencies between filters. This structure allows us to assess the
020
               relative importance of each filter by conducting a random walk on the graph. Filters
021            that exhibit weaker connections to others are considered less important and are
022            pruned with minimal impact on model performance. Furthermore, we examine
023            the eigenvalue spectrum of the adjacency matrix and observe a distribution similar
024            to that of the spiked models in random matrix theory. This suggests that the
025            spiked eigenvalues could serve as a significant indicator of the importance of each
026            convolutional layer. We conduct image classification on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet
027            to demonstrate the superiority of our method over the state-of-the-arts.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) have revolutionized the field of computer vision
033
      by achieving state-of-the-art performance in various tasks (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; He et al.,
      2015; Szegedy et al., 2014). However, deploying resource heavy CNNs on devices with limited
034
      computational and storage capacities presents significant challenges. Consequently, numerous studies
035
      have focused on model compression and CNN acceleration, mainly including network pruning (He
036   et al., 2018b; Lin et al., 2020; Sui et al., 2021), model quantization (Liu et al., 2018; Qin et al., 2020;
037   Liu et al., 2020), low-rank decomposition (Jaderberg et al., 2014; Lin et al., 2016) and knowledge
038   distillation (Shen et al., 2019; Hinton et al., 2015). Among them, network pruning has been widely
039   studied due to its easy implementation and effective results.
040
      Recent developments on pruning can be divided into two categories, i.e., weight pruning and filter
041
      pruning. Weight pruning removes individual filter weights, creating a sparse network that often
042   requires special hardware to achieve acceleration, as it cannot efficiently utilize standard BLAS
043   libraries. In contrast, filter pruning, which compresses the model by directly removing selected
044   filters, maintains regular structures and is widely used due to its ability to enhance acceleration on
045   general-purpose hardware.
046
      Generally, there are two essential issues in the filter pruning, i.e., the layer importance measurement
047
      and the filter importance measurement. For the first issue, layer importance measurement is related
048   to the per-layer pruning rate. Existing works such as (Chin et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2022) utilize
049   different weight-oriented strategies to evaluate the importance of each convolutional layer based
050   on pre-trained models. For the second issue, the filter importance measurement identifies which
051   filters in the pre-trained model should be preserved and inherited to initialize the pruned network
052   structure. Previous works (Ye et al., 2018; He et al., 2018a) performs filter pruning by following
053   the “smaller-norm-less-important” criterion, which believes that filters with smaller norms can be
      pruned safely due to their less importance. However, this criterion appears overly simplistic and may


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      not always hold true in all cases. Beyond this, He et al. (2018b) assumes the filters that are close
055   to the geometric median are redundant, which is implemented by calculating the distance between
056   filters pairs, Lin et al. (2022) introduces a recommendation-based filter selection scheme where each
057   filter recommends a group of its closest filters, these two methods both try to find the central filters in
058   certain measure spaces, which is proved to be effective by experiments. Joo et al. (2021) proposes
059   Linearly Replaceable Filter (LRF), which suggests that a filter that can be approximated by the linear
060   combination of other filters is replaceable, however they only focus on the approximated error and
061   haven’t dived into the relationships among filters.
062   More recently, graph-driven methods have been developed to identify important filters and achieve
063   competitive performance. For example, Li et al. (2023) utilize a graphical model to represent the
064   similarity relationships between the output feature maps of filters, while Shi et al. (2023) introduce
065   Von Neumann graph entropy as a novel measure for filter importance. However, most of these
066   approaches predominantly rely on similarity metrics like Euclidean distance or cosine similarity to
067   measure the similarity between filters, which only considers the pairwise relationships between filters,
068   overlooking the interactions among them within the whole layer.
069   To address the aforementioned limitations, we proposed a novel method to evaluate the importance of
070   filters by focusing on the interactions between them. Previous works have shown that some filters
071   can have similar functionality, our approach assumes that each filter potentially shares parts of its
072   functionality with others. Similar to the approach in (Joo et al., 2021), we model these interactions by
073   linearly reconstructing each filter using its peers, as shown in Equation 1. From this reconstruction,
074   the coefficients indicate the strength of connection between a filter and its peers, while the residuals
075
      represent the unique functionality of itself. Proceeding with this approach, we construct a graph
      where each node represents a filter, and the edges between them are weighted by the coefficients from
076
      the linear combinations. This graph representation allows us to apply network analysis techniques to
077
      find the central nodes. Filters that act as central nodes in this graph will be considered as hubs of
078   functionality, playing pivotal roles in the layer. Figure 1 illustrates the pipeline of our pruning method.
079   Additionally, we analyze the eigenvalue spectrum of the adjacency matrix and observe a distribution
080   resembling that of spiked models in random matrix theory. This similarity suggests that the spiked
081   eigenvalues may serve as key indicators of the significance of each convolutional layer, as detailed in
082   the pipeline shown in Figure 2. Further elaboration on this method is provided in Section 4.
083
      Contributions. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
084
085          • Leveraging insights from Random Matrix theory, we present a novel approach for quantify-
086            ing the importance of different layers, thereby facilitating the determination of the optimal
087            pruning rate for each layer.
088
             • By modeling filter relationships through a graph structure, our approach utilizes network
089
               analysis techniques to reveal deeper insights into the network’s architecture. This enables the
090            identification of key filters that serve as central nodes, elucidating their roles in contributing
091            to the network’s effectiveness.
092
093
             • Applying our proposed method to different model pruning tasks on CIFAR-10 and Ima-
               geNet datasets, extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our pruning strategy,
094
               which outperforms other similar algorithms by achieving higher compression rates while
095
               maintaining high accuracy.

      Data-driven Pruning Methods and Data-Independent Pruning Methods.

109   Luo et al. (2017) adopts the statistics information from the next layer to guide the filter selections.

      2.2   L AYER -I MPORTANCE -S UPPORTED (LIS) PRUNING METHODS
137
138   Layer Importance Supported (LIS) methods focus on evaluating the significance of individual layers
139   before proceeding to filter removal based on these assessments. For instance, studies such as (Li
140   et al., 2017; Mao et al., 2017), and (Suau et al., 2020) employ various weight-oriented strategies
141   to gauge the importance of convolutional layers in fully-trained models. The research by Li et al.
142   (2017) investigates the sensitivity of each convolutional layer through multiple layer-wise pruning
143   experiments utilizing the l1 -norm, highlighting the critical role of sensitive layers while advocating
144   for the removal of filters from less sensitive ones. In a similar vein, Mao et al. (2017) introduces
145
      a coarse-grained pruning methodology that facilitates iterative layer pruning based on sensitivity
      analysis. Additionally, Suau et al. (2020) applies principal filter analysis to derive a more compact
146
      model by leveraging the intrinsic correlations among filter responses within the layers.

      2.3   R ANDOM M ATRIX T HEORY
149
150   In the field of Random Matrix Theory (RMT), the spike model serves as a crucial framework for
151   analyzing the separation of signal and noise in high-dimensional datasets. The spike model introduces
152   a finite number of large eigenvalues, or "spikes," into the spectrum of a random matrix to represent
153   significant underlying signals amidst random noise. Key studies in this area have focused on the
154   distribution of these spike eigenvalues and their influence on the overall eigenvalue distribution of the
155   matrix.
156   Baik et al. (2005) examined the asymptotic distribution of the largest eigenvalues in non-null complex
157   sample covariance matrices, uncovering the phase transition phenomenon now known as the Baik–Ben
158   Arous–Péché (BBP) transition. This work illustrates how spikes can lead to distinct eigenvalue
159   behaviors compared to the bulk of the spectrum. Johnstone (2001) applied the spike model to
160   high-dimensional statistics, particularly in the context of Principal Component Analysis (PCA). His
161   analysis provided conditions under which spike eigenvalues can be reliably separated from noise
      eigenvalues, enhancing the effectiveness of PCA in signal detection. More recently, Benaych-Georges

      4.3   E VALUATING F ILTER I MPORTANCE VIA R ANDOM WALKS (I NSPIRED BY PAGE R ANK )
304
305   In graph theory, a node’s importance is often inferred from its connectivity. Drawing inspiration from
306   the PageRank algorithm, we propose a random walk framework to evaluate the significance of filters
307   (nodes) within the network. Random walk is a fundamental concept where a "walker" starts at a given
308   node and randomly transitions to adjacent nodes based on predefined probabilities. The frequency
309   with which the walker visits each node reflects its importance in the graph. In our framework, edge
310   weights are used to represent the strength of connections between filters, which in turn dictate the
311   transition probabilities.
312   More formally, in a random walk, the probability of transitioning from filter i to filter j is proportional
313   to the edge weight between them. The random walker thus traverses the network, spending more time
314   on filters that are better connected or more influential, thereby capturing their relative significance.
315   Over many iterations, the stationary distribution of the walker’s visits can be interpreted as a measure
316   of each filter’s importance, similar to how PageRank ranks webpages based on link structure. Based
317   on the previous discussion, we define a transition matrix P, where each element pij represents
318
      the probability of moving from filter i to filter j. The self-transition probability pjj , based on the
      L2 -norm of the residual for filter j, captures the filter’s retention of its unique influence:

      6     C ONCLUSION
510
      In this study, we introduce an innovative filter pruning method by integrating a graph-based method
511
      with insights from Random Matrix Theory (RMT). By modeling the relationships among filters as a
512
      graph and assessing filter importance through random walks, alongside evaluating layer significance
513   via eigenvalue analysis of the adjacency matrix, our method offers a nuanced understanding of
514   structural redundancies in CNNs. Extensive evaluations across various datasets demonstrate that
515   our proposed approach not only quantifies filter and layer importance effectively but also brings
516   significant reductions in storage and computational costs, all while preserving high model accuracy..
