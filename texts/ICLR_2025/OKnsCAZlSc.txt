010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            The federated learning (FL) client selection scheme can effectively mitigate global
013            model performance degradation caused by the random aggregation of clients with
014            heterogeneous data. Simultaneously, research has exposed FL’s susceptibility to
015            backdoor attacks. However herein lies the dilemma, traditional client selection
016            methods and backdoor defenses stand at odds, so their integration is an elusive
017            goal. To resolve this, we introduce Grace, a resilient client selection framework
018            blending combinational class sampling with data augmentation. On the client side,
019            Grace first proposes a local model purification method, fortifying the model’s
020
               defenses by bolstering its innate robustness. After, local class representations
               are extracted for server-side client selection. This approach not only shields be-
021
               nign models from backdoor tampering but also allows the server to glean insights
022
               into local class representations without infringing upon the client’s privacy. On
023            the server side, Grace introduces a novel representation combination sampling
024            method. Clients are selected based on the interplay of their class representations,
025            a strategy that simultaneously weeds out malicious actors and draws in clients
026            whose data holds unique value. Our extensive experiments highlight Grace’s ca-
027            pabilities. The results are compelling: Grace enhances defense performance by
028            over 50% compared to state-of-the-art (SOTA) backdoor defenses, and, in the best
029            case, improves accuracy by 3.19% compared to SOTA client selection schemes.
030            Consequently, Grace achieves substantial advancements in both security and ac-
031
               curacy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al. (2017)) has emerged as a novel distributed learning frame-
038   work in which clients collaboratively train a high-performance machine learning model, coordinated
039
      by a centralized server, without sharing or exchanging private data. However, randomly aggregating
      local models with heterogeneous data can significantly degrade the global model’s performance. Ex-
040
      isting studies (Goetz et al. (2019); Cho et al. (2020); Nishio & Yonetani (2019); Yang et al. (2021b))
041
      have found that selecting local models for aggregation based on the characteristics of clients’ lo-
042   cal models or data, i.e., using a heuristic client selection method instead of random selection, can
043   effectively address the challenges posed by data heterogeneity.
044
045
      Existing client selection studies can be divided into two categories: sample feature-based (Yang et al.
      (2021b); Fraboni et al. (2021); Balakrishnan et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023c)) and model feature-
046
      based methods (Cho et al. (2020); Han et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2022); Nagalapatti & Narayanam
047
      (2021); Yan et al. (2023)). Sample feature-based methods require clients to provide the server with
048   information about the number of samples in their local training set or the distribution of sample
049   categories. Clients with larger training sets or more balanced category distributions are then selected
050   for aggregation. However, it is challenging for the server to verify the truthfulness of the number
051   or distribution of samples provided by the clients. Consequently, current research has shifted focus
052   to model feature-based approaches. This method selects representative and diverse local models for
053   aggregation based on their losses, gradients, or parameters, aiming to enhance the generalization of
      the global model.

054
      In addition, existing FL research has highlighted the vulnerability of Federated Learning (FL) to
055   backdoor attacks (Hongyi et al. (2020); Bhagoji et al. (2019); Eugene et al. (2020); El Mahdi et al.
056   (2018); Zhang et al. (2023a)), prompting the development of various defenses against such attacks.
057   These defenses can be categorized into two main types: client-side (Zhu et al. (2023); Sun et al.
058   (2021)) and server-side (Huang et al. (2023a); Han et al. (2023); Lycklama et al. (2023); Xie et al.
059   (2021)). Client-side defenses aim to detect backdoor-related parameters in the local model that the
060   server sends to the client, and then perturb or clip these parameters to defend against backdoor
061   attacks. However, due to the diversity of client data and the large parameter space of the model,
062   accurately identifying backdoor-related parameters remains a challenging task. As a result, server-
063
      side defenses have become the primary focus. These defenses work by comparing the similarity
      between the parameters of local models, identifying those that resemble most models as benign, and
064
      excluding dissimilar local models from the aggregation process to mitigate backdoor attacks.
065
066   However, we observe a conflict between existing client selection strategies and backdoor attack
067   defenses. Specifically, the goal of client selection is to choose diverse local models, i.e., to select
068   clients with unique data to participate in aggregation, thereby enhancing the generalization of the
069
      global model. In contrast, backdoor attack defense aims to exclude local models that differ from the
      majority, focusing on aggregating similar local models to mitigate backdoor threats. Local models
070
      trained on backdoor samples are challenging to identify and exclude within a client selection scheme
071
      based on model features. Additionally, clients with unique data characteristics may be mistakenly
072   classified as malicious in server-side backdoor defenses. Consequently, existing backdoor defense
073   mechanisms are challenging to integrate with client selection schemes. If the backdoor defense is not
074   applied during the client selection process, local models embedded with backdoors will participate
075   in global model aggregation, leading to vulnerabilities in the global model.
076
      In response, we introduce Grace, a robust federated learning client selection with combinatorial class
077
      representations and data augmentation. Grace selects clients for aggregation using class represen-
078   tations instead of model features, which allows for excluding malicious clients while also selecting
079   clients with unique data features. Given the challenge of excluding malicious models with 100%
080   accuracy, we enhance the robustness of the local model internally on the client side. This method
081   not only defends against attacks but also improves the accuracy of the global model. Grace is the
082   first solution that defends against backdoor attacks without sacrificing model accuracy; instead, it
083   enhances it, marking a significant breakthrough. Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
084
085          • We propose a local model purification method. First, we apply data augmentation tech-
086
               niques to construct augmented datasets, which are used to fine-tune the local initial models,
               enhancing their robustness. Next, we employ representation learning and mutual informa-
087
               tion to learn local class representations and align them with global class representations.
088
               This improves local model performance while also supporting server-side client selection.
089            This approach not only ensures that the local models of benign clients remain free from
090            backdoors but also allows the server to learn class representations of local samples without
091            compromising client privacy.
092
             • We propose a representation combinatorial sampling method. We first establish a metric
093
               to quantify the global class representation loss for each client and apply a combinatorial
094
               confidence upper bound algorithm to sample clients, excluding those with significant global
095            class representation losses. After, an expert product technique is used to generate global
096            class representations, which guide the learning of local class representations. This method
097            effectively excludes malicious clients while selecting high-quality clients.
098
             • We conduct a series of experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of Grace. Our experi-
099
               mental results demonstrate that Grace outperforms existing client selection methods across
100
               various heterogeneous settings. Moreover, Grace surpasses existing defenses under dif-
101            ferent attack scenarios and non-IID settings. Finally, ablation studies and hyperparameter
102            analyses further validate the effectiveness of Grace’s individual components.

      selection in FL. Methods such as Clustered (Fraboni et al. (2021)) rely on the number of client

      samples for selection. However, this metric may not be appropriate, as clients could utilize a sub-
109   stantial amount of repetitive data, leading to potential overfitting of local models. Other approaches,
110   including those by (Cho et al. (2020); Goetz et al. (2019); Tang et al. (2022)), select clients based
111   on higher local losses. Nevertheless, this criterion doesn’t guarantee a reduction in the final global
112   model’s losses. Nagalapatti & Narayanam (2021) introduces the assumption of a validation set for
113   server-side validation of local model performance. However, obtaining a dataset that accurately
114   models the global data distribution is often unrealistic. Researchers later identified a correlation

      cent work, Fed-CBS (Zhang et al. (2023c)) argues that the client selection process should not only
118
      consider diversity but also account for category imbalance.
119
120
      Backdoor attack. In the context of FL, backdoor attacks (Hongyi et al. (2020); Bhagoji et al.
121
      (2019); Eugene et al. (2020); Chulin et al. (2020)) aim to manipulate global model predictions by
122
      exploiting compromised clients embedding backdoors. For instance, Eugene et al. (2020) introduced
123   a scaling attack wherein the attacker utilizes a combination of backdoors and clean training samples
124   to train its local model. Subsequently, the attacker scales the local model update before transmitting
125   it to the server. In our research, we introduce a strategic backdoor attack, leveraging state-of-the-art
126   attack techniques (Eugene et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2023a); Chulin et al. (2020)), to assess the
127   resilience of our defense mechanism.
128
129   Backdoor defense. In response to the increasing threat of poisoning attacks, current defense
130   strategies involve restricting, removing updates, or adding noise to the norm. Notable defenses
131   include SparseFed (Panda et al. (2022)), which addresses poisoning attacks by selectively updat-
132   ing the most relevant weights in an aggregation model. DeepSight (Rieger et al. (2022)) tackles
133
      backdoor attacks in FL by clustering the depth model of the last layer to filter outliers. CRFL (Xie
      et al. (2021)) employs clipping and smoothing to establish certified robustness against backdoor at-
134
      tacks. FLAME (Nguyen et al. (2022b)) adopts a weak Differential Privacy (DP) and dynamically
135
      clipped boundaries, effectively mitigating backdoor attacks while maintaining high accuracy on the
136   main task. FEDCPA (Han et al. (2023)) evaluates the normality of local models and aggregates up-
137   dates using weighted averaging, neutralizing the impact of potential malicious updates. GAS (Liu
138   et al. (2023)) seeks to overcome the curse of dimensionality by partitioning high-dimensional gradi-
139   ents into low-dimensional subvectors, identifying trustworthy subvectors, and aggregating them to
140   resolve the gradient heterogeneity problem. While these server-side defenses presume a lower num-
141   ber of malicious clients compared to benign clients during each global aggregation round, recent
142   endeavors focus on enhancing the client’s capability to defend against backdoor attacks during local
143   training. FL-WBC (Sun et al. (2021)) and LeadFL (Zhu et al. (2023)) optimize the Hessian matrix
144
      to diminish the impact of backdoor features. FLIP (Eugene et al. (2020)) constructs and trains back-
      door triggers against each other to counter backdoors. Lockdown (Huang et al. (2023b)) restricts
145
      client access to only a subset of model parameters, preventing toxic submodels from poisoning all
146
      parameters of the global model. Although effective in limiting backdoor impact, this method also
147   diminishes the generalization performance of the global model.

176   Threat model. We assume that server will honestly follow the learning protocol. Clients are
177   then classified into two categories: honest and malicious. Honest clients honestly execute the FL
178   protocol, whereas malicious clients attempt to corrupt the global model by launching poisoning
179   attacks locally. We consider the attacks outlined in previous studies (Chulin et al. (2020); Gu et al.

      (2017); Barni et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2022)), assuming that the attacker can manipulate a group of

182
      clients integrate triggers into a portion of local training data and reassign them as target classes.

188   4     M ETHODOLOGY
189
190   Existing mainstream client selection methods and backdoor attack defenses select clients for aggre-
191
      gation based on model features. However, there is an inherent conflict between these two strategies.
      To address this issue, we select clients for aggregation based on data class representations rather
192
      than model features. Additionally, we incorporate data augmentation techniques to defend against
193
      attacks while enhancing local model performance, instead of using perturbation or clipping tech-
194   niques that could degrade the local model’s performance. Specifically, we first design a local model
195   purification method on the client side that uses data augmentation techniques to alter the parameter
196   distributions of local initial models, thereby protecting benign local models from backdoors. This
197   method continuously changes the parameter distributions on the client side through data augmen-
198   tation. When a few malicious local models are present during aggregation, their attack effects are
199   weakened by benign models, making it difficult for them to impact the global model significantly.
200   Next, we employ representation learning to capture class representations of local data for server-side
201   client sampling. By identifying and selecting local models based on class representations rather than
202
      the models themselves, this approach avoids the curse of dimensionality and improves the propor-
      tion of benign models in the aggregated model. We then introduce a combination sampling method
203
      for class representations on the server side using upper confidence methods to minimize the loss
204
      of global class representations. Backdoor attacks tend to misclassify trigger-inserted samples as
205   specific classes, causing their class representations to deviate significantly from the global class rep-
206   resentation compared to benign models. Therefore, models are selected for aggregation based on the
207   difference between local and global class representations. This method not only excludes malicious
208   clients from participating in aggregation but also allows combining clean class representations to
209   reduce the loss of global class features. The workflow is shown in Fig. 1.

303   Global class representation loss. Accurately identifying malicious clients has always been a chal-
304
      lenging problem in defending against backdoor attacks in FL. Existing defenses typically detect dis-

306
      fectively. To address this issue, we design a metric called Global Class Representation Loss (GCRL)

312   It is worth noting that we design GCRL with the same loss function as the local class representa-
313   tion loss. This design allows Grace to defend against backdoor attacks while also selecting local
314   models that enhance FL performance for aggregation. First, let’s explain why GCRL is effective in
315
      defending against backdoors. GCRL measures the difference between local and global class repre-
      sentations. For benign models, their local class representations are more similar to the global class
316
      representations, resulting in a smaller difference. However, malicious clients have the additional
317
      task of embedding backdoors, causing their class representations to consistently differ from those
318   of benign models during training. This leads to a larger difference between the class representations
319   of malicious clients and the global class representation. Therefore, using GCRL in the aggregation
320   process effectively excludes malicious models. Next, let’s discuss why GCRL can improve FL per-
321   formance. For a benign client, a smaller difference between its local and global class representations
322   suggests that the client’s local data is more balanced and that it has learned most of the class features.
323   Existing studies indicate that imbalances in local data classes degrade FL performance. Therefore,
      using GCRL as a criterion for selecting benign clients helps improve the overall performance of FL.

530   6      C ONCLUSION
531
      In this study, we propose a client selection method that supports backdoor attack defense, address-
532
      ing the conflict between existing client selection strategies and backdoor defenses. Specifically, we
533
      propose a local model purification method on the client side to enhance the robustness of the local
534   models against attacks. On the server side, we develop representation combination sampling meth-
535   ods to select clients for aggregation based on local data class representations, effectively excluding
536   malicious clients while also identifying high-quality ones. Our experimental results demonstrate
537   that Grace not only achieves SOTA defense but also improves the performance of the global model,
538   marking a significant advancement over existing defenses that tend to degrade model performance.
539   Looking ahead, we acknowledge the potential for attackers to optimize their strategies based on data
      representations, underscoring the need for further investigation in our future research endeavors.
