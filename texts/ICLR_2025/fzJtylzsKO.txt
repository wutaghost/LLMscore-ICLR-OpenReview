011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Batched Bayesian optimization (BO) can accelerate molecular design by effi-
014            ciently identifying top-performing compounds from a large chemical library. Ex-
015            isting acquisition strategies for batch design in BO aim to balance exploration
016
               and exploitation. This often involves optimizing non-additive batch acquisition
               functions, necessitating approximation via myopic construction and/or diversity
017
               heuristics. In this work, we propose an acquisition strategy for discrete optimiza-
018
               tion that is motivated by pure exploitation, qPO (multipoint Probability of Opti-
019            mality). qPO maximizes the probability that the batch includes the true optimum,
020            which is expressible as the sum over individual acquisition scores and thereby
021            circumvents the combinatorial challenge of optimizing a batch acquisition func-
022            tion. We differentiate the proposed strategy from parallel Thompson sampling
023            and discuss how it implicitly captures diversity. Finally, we apply our method to
024            the model-guided exploration of large chemical libraries and provide empirical
025            evidence that it performs better than or on par with state-of-the-art methods in
026            batched Bayesian optimization.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Predictive modeling can greatly accelerate molecular and materials discovery. Data-driven and
032   simulation-based models can aid in prioritizing experiments for the design of drugs (Liu et al., 2024;
033
      2023; Horne et al., 2024) and other materials (Schwalbe-Koda et al., 2021; Gómez-Bombarelli et al.,
      2016). In iterative design cycles guided by predictive models, the reliability of model predictions
034
      can be gradually improved as the model is updated with newly collected data. This iterative, model-
035
      guided approach has been applied to the discovery of drugs (Desai et al., 2013), laser emitters
036   (Strieth-Kalthoff et al., 2024), and dyes (Koscher et al., 2023; Bassman Oftelie et al., 2018).
037
038   Bayesian optimization (BO) is arguably the most popular mathematical framework for iterative
039
      model-based design (Frazier, 2018; Garnett, 2023). BO optimizes an expensive black-box objec-
      tive function (f or “oracle”) by iteratively training a surrogate model and using its predictions to
040
      select designs for evaluation (Frazier, 2018). At each iteration, an acquisition function uses the
041
      mean and/or uncertainty of surrogate model predictions to select which design(s) to evaluate. BO
042   has been applied to efficiently explore chemical libraries in numerous previous works (Cherkasov
043   et al., 2006; Graff et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Bellamy et al., 2022; Wang-Henderson et al., 2023).
044
045
      Computational (e.g., physics-based simulations) and experimental (e.g., bioactivity assays) oracles
      in many chemistry applications are most efficiently evaluated in parallel. The total evaluation bud-
046
      get is therefore spread across relatively few iterations with large batch sizes, requiring an acquisition
047
      function to select a batch of experiments. The non-additivity of batch-level acquisition functions
048   complicates the selection of optimal batches in BO; when the value of selecting a candidate depends
049   on other selections, batch design becomes a combinatorial problem. Optimizing Bayes-optimal
050   batch acquisition functions is therefore often computationally intractable even for modest batch
051   sizes (Garnett, 2023, Section 11.3). Strategies that fail to consider this non-additivity, such as se-
052   lecting the top candidates based on a sequential policy, can produce homogeneous batches that lack
053   diversity. Prior works have primarily relied on (1) methods to increase diversity (Gonzalez et al.,
      2016; Kathuria et al., 2016; Nguyen et al., 2016; Groves & Pyzer-Knapp, 2018), (2) hallucinated


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      observations to approximate intractable integrals (Ginsbourger et al., 2010; Desautels et al., 2014),
055   (3) the randomness inherent to Thompson sampling (Thompson, 1933) to extend it to the batch set-
056   ting (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2017; Dai et al., 2022), or (4) some combination thereof (Ren & Li,
057   2024; Nava et al., 2022).
058
      In this paper, we propose qPO (multipoint Probability of Optimality), a batch construction strategy
059
      that maximizes the likelihood that the optimum exists in the acquired batch. Inspired by parallel
060
      Thompson sampling (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2017; Kandasamy et al., 2018), qPO centers around
061   the probability of optimality, accounts for correlations between inputs, and is naturally paralleliz-
062   able. However, qPO aims to forego randomness. While this distinction may seem to hinder explo-
063   ration, the consideration of a surrogate model’s joint distribution over all candidates allows qPO to
064   favor diversity. Uniquely, the defined batch-level acquisition function can be expressed as a sum of
065   individual candidate acquisition scores, circumventing the combinatorial challenge of maximizing a
066   batch-level acquisition function. We summarize the contributions of this work as follows:
067
068
            1. We present a novel exploitative strategy for batch design in discrete Bayesian optimization
               that maximizes the likelihood of including the true optimum in the batch.
069
070         2. We derive a batch-level acquisition function that is equal to the sum of individual acquisi-
071
               tion scores and is thereby maximized by selecting the top candidates by acquisition score.
072         3. Through a simple analytical case study, we demonstrate the importance of considering
073            prediction covariance in exploitation, describe how covariance can capture diversity, and
074            differentiate our method from parallel Thompson sampling.
075         4. We demonstrate that our acquisition strategy identifies top-performers from chemical li-
076            braries as efficiently as state-of-the-art alternatives for batched BO in two realistic molecu-
077            lar discovery settings.

      2.2                  D ERIVING AN ACQUISITION FUNCTION FOR OPTIMAL BATCH DESIGN
141
      Most acquisition strategies in BO aim to balance exploitation and exploration. Exploitation priori-
142   tizes selections that are most likely to achieve the highest oracle score, while exploration is intended
143   to prevent a search from getting “stuck” in local optima. Conceptually, exploration is expected to
144   contribute to a more reliable surrogate model and thereby benefit the optimization in the long run; a
145   classic failure mode of BO within continuous design spaces is the oversampling of a single mode of
146   the surrogate model posterior (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014). In contrast, exploitation selects the
147   best candidates at a given iteration without consideration of the impact on future iterations. We pur-
148   sue a batch acquisition strategy that is motivated by exploitation, optimizing expected performance
149   in the immediate iteration as if the optimization could be stopped at any time.1 Our acquisition
150
      strategy is visualized within the context of one BO iteration in Figure 1.

      Techniques beyond Monte Carlo integration may also enable the estimation of qPO. If the poste-

253   followed by Monte-Carlo sampling (Genz, 1992). Alternative methods to estimate high-dimensional
254   orthant probabilities (Miwa et al., 2003; Craig, 2008; Ridgway, 2016) may also be applied to the es-
255   timation of qPO acquisition scores. We consider the implementation of these methods future work.

259   3.1     BATCHED BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
260
261   A naive batch construction strategy is to select the top b compounds based on an acquisition function
262   designed for sequential BO. However, because the utility of a given selection depends on other
263   selections, the top-b approach does not guarantee an optimal batch in general (Garnett, 2023).
264   One general approach to batch design is to, as closely as possible, replicate the behavior of a sequen-
265   tial policy. Azimi et al. (2010) define an acquisition function that minimizes the discrepancy between
266   the batch policy and sequential policy behavior. Batches may alternatively be constructed iteratively
267   (myopically) by hallucinating the outcomes of previous selections in the batch. This enables the op-
268   timization of batch-level acquisition functions like multipoint expected improvement (Ginsbourger
269   et al., 2010) and batch upper confidence bound (Desautels et al., 2014). However, this approach is
      typically limited to cases where the surrogate model can efficiently be updated with hallucinated


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      or pending data. Further, batches that are constructed myopically only approximately optimize the
271   batch-level acquisition function. In contrast, our approach does not rely on hallucination and can be
272   applied to any posterior which can be sampled or modeled as a multivariate Gaussian.
273
274
      Methods to improve diversity have also been applied to prevent the selection of candidates that
      would provide minimal marginal information gain. Gonzalez et al. (2016) use local penalization to
275
      construct diverse batches. Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have also been used for batch diver-
276
      sification in discrete optimization (Kathuria et al., 2016); Nava et al. (2022) demonstrate improved
277   theoretical convergence rates by incorporating DPPs into parallel Thompson sampling. Nguyen et al.
278   (2016) and Groves & Pyzer-Knapp (2018) model the objective landscape as mixture of Gaussians
279   and acquire predicted local optima. Strategies based on diversity heuristics generally assume that
280   diverse batches support exploration, not necessarily exploitation. We describe in Section 3.2 how
281   our method does capture diversity, even with exploitation as the primary motivation.
282
      Sequential acquisition functions that randomly select candidates by sampling, like Thompson sam-
283   pling, can be extended to the batch case by increasing the number of random samples. Parallel
284   Thompson sampling involves sampling from the model posterior and acquiring the optimum point
285   from each sample (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2017). TS-RSR uses a similar methodology, but selects
286   from each sample the point that minimizes a regret to uncertainty ratio (Ren & Li, 2024). Dai et al.
287   (2022) extend neural Thompson sampling (Zhang et al., 2020) to the batch setting; each of b ran-
288   domly initialized neural networks determines one selection in the batch. The randomness inherent
289   to Thompson sampling and related strategies is expected to contribute to the search’s exploration.
290   qPO, in contrast, is intended to make selections deterministically.

293
294
      We continue with an illustrative example to highlight how the proposed approach captures diversity,
295   a key contribution to qPO’s robustness. Consider the following predictive distribution for y given x:
296
297
                                                 10   101      100 0
                                                " # "               #!
298                                   y∼N        5 , 100       101 0                                  (10)
299                                              0     0        0 1
300
301   The probabilities of x1 , x2 , and x3 being optimal are roughly 84%, 0%, and 16%, respectively. For
302   b = 2, our acquisition strategy would select x1 and x3 , while a greedy strategy based only on the
303   posterior mean vector would select x1 and x2 . A diversity-aware acquisition strategy would select
304   x1 and x3 if we assume that the high covariance between x1 and x2 reflects design space similarity.
305   This assumption is particularly valid for molecular applications when the surrogate model is a Gaus-
306   sian process with a Tanimoto kernel. Because the Tanimoto kernel defines prior covariance based
307   on structural similarity, structurally similar compounds will have higher covariance. Our method
308   naturally captures this sense of diversity through model covariance without requiring clustering or
309
      other definitions of diversity that are not inherent to the model itself.
310   When Monte Carlo integration is used to approximate qPO, our method resembles Thompson sam-
311   pling, particularly the parallel Thompson sampling (pTS) approach proposed by Hernández-Lobato
312   et al. (2017) and Kandasamy et al. (2018). Both methods choose a batch of b inputs by selecting
313   the maxima of posterior samples. Our approach differs from parallel Thompson sampling in two
314   notable ways. First, in the case of b = 1, Thompson sampling chooses candidates randomly with
315
      probability proportional to Pr(x∗ = xi ). qPO aims to choose candidates deterministically that max-
      imize Pr(x∗ = xi ). However, because a closed-form solution for qPO acquisition scores does not
316
      exist, the implementation of our acquisition strategy is not purely deterministic. Second, qPO gen-
317
      eralizes to the b ≥ 2 case differently from pTS. As described in Algorithm 2 of Hernández-Lobato
318   et al. (2017), if the optimum input for a particular sample is already in the batch, then the second
319   most optimal input is added (or the third if the second most optimal input is also in the batch, and
320   so forth). If the model makes highly correlated predictions, this will result in a batch filled with
321   candidates that have highly correlated predictions. In the analytical example shown above in Eq. 10,
322   pTS will be more likely to select x1 and x2 than x1 and x3 . In contrast, once our method has added
323   a first candidate to the batch, subsequent selections are chosen conditioned on existing selections
      not being optimal.

327
328   We apply qPO to two model-guided searches of chemical libraries and compare its performance
329   to alternative batch acquisition functions: pTS (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2017; Kandasamy et al.,
330   2018), Determinantal Point Process Thomspon Sampling (DPP-TS) (Nava et al., 2022), Thompson
331   Sampling with Regret to Sigma Ratio (TS-RSR) (Ren & Li, 2024), General-purpose Information-
332   Based Bayesian OptimisatioN (GIBBON) (Moss et al., 2021), multipoint probability of improve-
333
      ment (qPI), multipoint expected improvement (qEI), batch upper confidence bound (BUCB) (Wil-
      son et al., 2017), upper confidence bound (UCB), and greedy (mean only). DPP-TS and TS-RSR
334
      are extensions of pTS with improved theoretical guarantees. BUCB, qPI, and qEI are batch-level
335
      extensions of sequential policies that employ myopic construction in finite discrete domains. GIB-
336   BON is an information-based batch acquisition function. We analyze the retrieval of the true top-k
337   acquired, the average oracle value of the acquired top-k, and cumulative regret, the former two being
338   common metrics for assessing model-guided virtual screening methods (Pyzer-Knapp, 2018; Graff
339   et al., 2021; Wang-Henderson et al., 2023). Run times are reported in A.5.
340
      Both demonstrations use a Tanimoto Gaussian process surrogate model with a constant mean that
341
      operates on 2048-length count Morgan fingerprints. qPO is implemented following Eq. 9 using
342   M = 10,000. Pairs of candidates {xi , xj } for which αi = αj are ranked by their predicted mean
343   (A.4.1). The cost of sampling from a multivariate Gaussian for N candidates is O(N 3 ) due to the
344   Cholesky decomposition (Vono et al., 2022). To alleviate the computational cost of sampling-based
345   methods—pTS, DPP-TS, TS-RSR, GIBBON, qPI, qEI, BUCB, and qPO—for large values of N , in
346   each iteration we reduce the set of N candidates to 10,000 using a greedy metric. We then apply the
347   respective strategy to select a batch from the smaller set of candidates. For qPO, this modifies the set
348   of candidates x considered in Algorithm 1. Because this may impact the exploration characteristics
349   of pTS and qEI, we include an additional baseline (“random10k”) that randomly selects compounds
350   from these top 10,000 candidates. A.6 compares qPO performance when using this pre-filtering
351
      strategy with pre-filtering based on UCB. Experimental details can be found in A.4.

      5     C ONCLUSION
468
469
      We have proposed a batch acquisition function (qPO) for discrete Bayesian optimization, motivated
      by exploitation, that maximizes the likelihood that the batch contains the true optimum. qPO is equal
470
      to the sum over individual acquisition scores and therefore circumvents the combinatorial challenge
471
      of optimizing a batch-level acquisition score. We explain how the treatment of model covariance im-
472   plicitly captures diversity and how it differentiates qPO from parallel Thompson sampling in subtle
473   but meaningful ways. Empirically, our method efficiently identifies an equivalent or higher percent-
474   age of top-performing candidates when compared to batched BO alternatives. The most notable
475   improvement to top-k metrics is observed for smaller values of k, consistent with the acquisition
476   strategy’s goal of acquiring the true global optimum.
477
      The proposed batch acquisition strategy has some notable limitations. First, qPO cannot be com-
478   puted analytically (in an exact sense), necessitating a Monte Carlo estimate. Alternative methods for
479   estimating the probability of a candidate’s optimality, such as expectation propagation, may reduce
480   the computational cost of implementing qPO. Second, qPO may fail to select diverse batches in
481   the presence of high observation noise, which will reduce the relative impact of covariance on qPO
482   scores2 . Theoretical analysis of the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the finite iteration setting
483   may uncover potential failure modes of qPO and further support the empirical results observed here.

      A.3   R ECASTING ACQUISITION SCORES AS ORTHANT PROBABILITIES
865
866   The acquisition score in Eq. 6 can be defined as an orthant probability through a change of variables.
867   This enables the estimation of Pr(x∗ = xi ) without posterior sampling. However, efficient calcula-
868   tion of orthant probabilities is not possible for arbitrary probability distributions. Here, we choose
869   to require the posterior to be a multivariate Gaussian (or approximated as such). The likelihood of
870   candidate xi being optimal is equivalent to the probability that yi is greater than all yj,j̸=i :
871                            Pr(x∗ = xi ) = Pr(yi > y1 , yi > y2 , ..., yi > yN )                     (12)
872
873   Following the approach of Azimi et al. (2010), we denote the difference between yi and yj as
874   zji and the vector of differences for candidate i as zi ∈ RN −1 . Using the transformation matrix
875   Ai ∈ R(N −1)×N as defined by Azimi et al. (2010) and Azimi (2012), we may define:
876
877                                         zi ∼ N Aµx , AΣx AT                                   (13)
878
      where µx and Σx parameterize the surrogate model posterior for candidates x. The expression
879
      in Eq. 12 is equal to the orthant probability of zi , i.e., the probability that all elements of zi are
880
      greater than 0. Azimi et al. (2010) apply a whitening transformation to the distribution defining zi ,
881   decorrelating all entries and enabling approximation of the orthant probability. Alternative methods
882   to estimate high-dimensional orthant probabilities (Genz, 1992; Miwa et al., 2003; Craig, 2008;
883   Ridgway, 2016) may also be applied here.

937   A.4.2    BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
938
939   We apply our batch acquisition strategy to the model-guided exploration of two discrete chemical
940   libraries (Section 4). The first case study explores a library of 39,312 compounds for putative an-
941   tibiotics that minimize the growth of Staphylococcus aureus (Wong et al., 2024). We randomly
942   initialize our search with 50 compounds and their growth inhibition values and acquire batches of
943

944
      2012). Here, we use an initial batch size of 100 compounds and acquire 100 more for 20 subsequent
945
      iterations. For both case studies, ten runs were performed for each acquisition method with distinct
946   random seeds that govern the initial batch.
947
948   Our surrogate model is a Gaussian process with a Tanimoto kernel (Tanimoto, 1958) and a constant
949
      mean, a common surrogate model architecture for molecular BO (Tripp et al., 2021; Garcı́a-Ortegón
      et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2022). Compounds are featurized as 2048-length count Morgan fingerprints
950
      using rdkit (Landrum, 2024). At each iteration, the Gaussian process hyperparameters—mean,
951
      covariance scale, and likelihood noise—are optimized by maximizing the marginal log likelihood of
952   the model over all previously acquired training data.

      A.4.3    ACQUISITION FUNCTIONS
955
956   Selecting batches with qEI, pTS, and qPO can be computationally expensive for large design spaces
957   due to the O(N 3 ) complexity of Cholesky decomposition for N candidates. To reduce the compu-
958   tational cost of these acquisition functions, we first filter the set of N candidates to the top 10,000
959   based on predicted mean and subsequently apply the appropriate acquisition function to select from
960   these 10,000 candidates. This step is not necessary for the implementation of these acquisition
961   functions but facilitates their use under computational resource limitations. A similar pre-filtering
962   method has been applied by Moss et al. (2021) in a molecular discovery setting to reduce computa-
963   tional cost.
964
965   Greedy Acquisition scores for each compound xi were defined as c ∗ yi , where c = 1 if the
966   objective is optimized and c = −1 if it is minimized. yi is the surrogate model mean prediction for

968
969   Upper confidence bound (UCB) Acquisition scores for each compound xi were defined as c ∗
970   yi + β ∗ σi , where c = 1 if the objective is optimized and c = −1 if it is minimized. We set β = 1
971   for all runs. yi is the surrogate model mean prediction for compound xi , and σi is the prediction
      standard deviation. The top-b compounds based on acquisition score were selected in each iteration.
