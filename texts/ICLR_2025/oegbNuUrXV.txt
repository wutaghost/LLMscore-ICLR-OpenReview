009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011                We present a novel framework for generalizable1 dynamic radiance field in egocen-
012                tric view. Our approach can predict a 3D representation of the physical world at a
013                given time based on a monocular video without test-time training.To this end, we
014                use a contracted triplane as the 3D representation of physical world in an egocentric
015                view at a specific time. To update the explicit 3D representation, we propose a
016                4D-aware transformer module to aggregate features from monocular videos. Be-
017                sides, we also introduce a temporal-based 3D constraint to achieve better multiview
018
                   consistency. In addition, we train the proposed model with large-scale monocular
                   videos in a self-supervised manner. Our model achieves top results in novel view
019
                   synthesis on dynamic scene datasets, demonstrating its strong understanding of
020
                   4D physical world. Besides, our model also shows the superior generalizability
021                to unseen scenarios. Furthermore, we find that our approach emerges capabilities
022                for geometry and semantic learning. We hope our approach can provide prelimi-
023                nary understanding of the physical world in first-person view and help ease future
024                research in computer vision, computer graphics and robotics.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As humans, we perceive and interact with physical world in a first-person perspective. Meanwhile, we
030   endow physical world with semantic meaning by our behaviors during interaction process. Besides,
031   we can generalize our knowledge about 4D physical world to various environments. To build human
032   intelligence in physical world, it is reasonable to model dynamic world in egocentric view, which not
033   only establishes physical world perception but also simulates human behaviors. To this end, machine
034
      learning models should bear two characteristics: generalized dynamic scene modeling and egocentric
      learning mode.
035
036   Dynamic scene modeling (Li et al., 2023; Park et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a; Zhou
037   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b) has achieved great improvements with the advancements of neural
038   rendering (Srinivasan et al., 2020; Kerbl et al., 2023). However, many methods (Li et al., 2021b;
039   Gao et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; 2023) require specific-scene optimization, which
040
      do not have generalizability. Although some methods (Tian et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024) leverage
      generalized prior inputs for dynamic scene modeling, these works still lack generalizability to unseen
041
      scenarios. Overall, the generalizability issue of existing methods is derived from their object-centric
042
      modeling. In contrast, ego-centric modeling is independent of specific scenes, thus it is a reasonable
043   alternative to achieve generalized dynamic scene modeling without extra priors.
044
045   In this paper, we propose a framework for generalizable dynamic radiance field in first-person view.
046
      The proposed method obtains the ability of cross-scene dynamic view synthesis in a self-supervised
      and data-driven manner. Specifically, we use an explicit 3D representation to represent physical world
047
      in egocentric view at target time t1 . For the explicit 3D representation, we adopt a contracted triplane
048
      to represent the unbounded scenes. Given a sequence of egocentric views at time {t : t ̸= t1 }, we
049   propose a 4D-aware transformer to update the triplane features. Using the updated triplane, we render
050   the egocentric view with volume rendering at time t1 . To achieve better multiview consistency, we
051   introduce a temporal-based 3D constraint, which renders two views that are temporally distant given
052   the same sequence of source views. Also, our approach is trained on large-scale monocular videos in
053

      egocentric view. In addition, our method can perform dynamic novel view synthesis, showing its 4D

056
057   To validate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct experiments on the NVIDIA Dynamic
058

072   2.1   R ADIANCE F IELD R ENDERING
073
074   Radiance field rendering has recently obtained a remarkable accomplishment. Neural Radiance Fields
075   (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) use MLPs to represent scenes and volume rendering to render
076   high-quality images at novel viewpoints. The success of NeRF has resulted in numerous subsequent
077   works that address its shortcomings (Barron et al., 2022; Chan et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022; Yu
078   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) and expand its applications (Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024;
079   Hong et al., 2023). Mip-NeRF360 (Barron et al., 2022) demonstrates impressive view synthesis
080
      results on unbounded scenes. EG3D (Chan et al., 2022) and InstantNGP (Müller et al., 2022) use
      a triplane or a hash grid to accelerate computation, separately. However, these methods inevitably
081
      face a trade-off between speed and quality. To solve this obstacle, 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl
082
      et al., 2023) is proposed. It takes 3D Gaussians as a scene representation, projects them into 2D
083   via a rasterization mechanism and renders image as NeRF. For its real-time speed and high quality,
084   many follow-up works (Yan et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023a; Szymanowicz et al., 2023; Ling et al.,
085   2023) are rapidly emerging. (Yan et al., 2023) represents a scene with multi-scale 3D Gaussians to
086   address aliasing effect. CF-3DGS (Fu et al., 2023a) performs novel view synthesis without any SfM
087   preprocessing by leveraging the explicit point cloud and the continuity of the input video stream. The
088   existing neural rendering methods mainly focus on scene reconstruction and novel view synthesis for
089   a specific scene, while our method aims at generalizable dynamic scene synthesis task.
090
091   2.2   DYNAMIC N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS
092
093   Rendering (reconstructing) dynamic 3D scenes is critical for many applications, from AR/VR to
094   autonomous driving (Yang et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b). Many works (Park
095   et al., 2020; Pumarola et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023;
096   Cao & Johnson, 2023) on dynamic scene synthesis requires multi-view input videos. D-nerf (Park
097   et al., 2020) and Nerfies (Pumarola et al., 2021) represent scenes by mapping each observed point
098   into a canonical scene representation via a volumetric deformation field. However, these methods
099
      are limited to object-centric scenes with relatively small object motion. DyNeRF (Li et al., 2022)
      and K-Planes (Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023) compress dynamic scenes into implicit or explicit NeRF
100
      and render these scenes with position, view direction and time conditions. To break the constraint of
101
      multi-view data, some approaches (Li et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024) are proposed
102   to represent dynamic scenes from monocular videos. DynIBaR (Li et al., 2023) synthesizes novel
103   image by aggregating image features from nearby views in a scene motion-aware manner. But
104   DynIBaR (Li et al., 2023) focuses on specific-scene optimization and can not generalize to unseen
105   scenarios. The most relevant works, MonoNeRF (Tian et al., 2023) and PGDVS (Zhao et al., 2024)
106   still need scene-specific optimization or finetuning when transferring to unseen scenes. Also, they
107   rely on extra priors, especially semantic masks of foreground objects. Unlike existing works, our
      method implement a generalizable dynamic scene synthesis only with monocular videos.

134   In this work, our method aims to learn generalizable dynamic scene representation in first-person view
135   from monocular videos. In particular, given a monocular video comprising S frames {It }St=1 and
136   corresponding camera parameters {Pt }St=1 , our model synthesizes a novel view of dynamic scene at
137
      target time t1 without per-scene training. As shown in Figure 1, we firstly use an image encoder to

      3.1     I MAGE E NCODER
144
      Given a sequence of source views {It }St=1 , we use a hybrid neural network to extract image features
145
      {Ft }St=1 . For efficiency and simplicity, the hybrid network consists of a resnet-like backbone and a
146   self-attention layer. The resnet-like backbone downsamples the spatial dimension of the input images
147   by a factor of 8. The self-attention layer is used to enhance the long-context ability of image features,
148   benefiting the motion-aware feature aggregation in view-attention module 3.2.2.

152   3.2.1     C ONTRACTED E GO -T RIPLANE R EPRESENTATION

154   To represent a scene in egocentric view, we take camera center as world origin. Note that in our paper,
155   egocentric view is only a modeling approach. It takes observer as world origin to model dynamic
156   scenes. For each video frame, we use camera center as world origin. Thus, under ego-view modeling,
157   all videos can be taken as egocentric videos.

159   learnable triplane T contains three axis-aligned feature planes Txy , Tyz , and Txz . The dimension of
160   each plane is H × W × D, where H × W is the spatial dimension and D is the feature channels. Since
161   our egocentric representation is for scenes that unbounded in all directions, we need a transformation
      between the unbounded ego coordinates and bounded triplane coordinates. To this end, we adopt a

187   3.2.2    4D- AWARE T RANSFORMER
188
      With the initial triplane representation, we propose a 4D-aware transformer to update the triplane
189
      features. The 4D-aware transformer include three core components: temporal-ware view-attention
190   module, axis-attention module, plane-attention module.
191
192   Temporal-aware View-Attention Module The temporal-aware view-attention module aims to
193   aggregate image features {Ft }St=1 from source views in a motion-aware manner, as shown in Fig-
194   ure 2 (a). Concretely, we uniformly sample 3D virtual points in triplane space. We denotes these
                                      M N      L
195   virtual point set as {xi,j,k } i=1 j=1 k=1 , where i, j and k represent x, y and z axes, M , N and L is
196   the point number in each axis, respectively. In temporal-aware view attention module, we take virtual
197   point feature as query and its corresponding epipolar point features in {It }St=1 as key and value. Note
198   that we also take time t1 and other times {t}St=1 as query and key to compute attention scores. For a
199
      virtual point xi,j,k , we compute its qi,j,k by projecting it onto three planes of the triplane, querying
      the corresponding point features and concating these features. To obtain epipolar point features
200
      {ft }St=1 , we project xi,j,k onto the t-th image plane by applying camera pose Pt and compute the
201
      feature via bilinear interpolation on the image feature grids. Thus, the new virtual point feature q̂i,j,k
202   is computed as:
203
                              q̂i,j,k = CrossAttn(qi,j,k , t1 , {ft }St=1 , {t}St=1 ) + qi,j,k               (2)
204
205
       Previous works (Li et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023) learn motion trajectory for 3D point x and use
      estimated motion trajectory of x to aggregate feature for dynamic content. Meanwhile, these works
206
      leverage epipolar geometry as an inductive bias to aggregate feature for static content. The whole
207
      aggregation process requires motion/semantic segmentation to determine dynamic content and static
208   content. In contrast, our temporal-aware view-attention module can determine dynamic and static
209   contents implicitly. This is validated by the temporal-aware similarities between the 3D virtual point
210   feature qi,j,k and its epipolar point features {ft }St=1 , see Appendix A.2. For virtual point features of
211   dynamic content, they contain much noise, since epipolar constraint is invalid in dynamic scenes. To
212   solve this issue, we propose axis-attention module and plane-attention module to refine virtual point
213   features.
214
215   Axis-Attention Module The cost of directly applying self-attention to refine virtual point features
      is huge, thus, we instead implement it by refine triplane features and introduce axis-attention module

      to project new virtual point features onto triplane. In axis-attention module, we take triplane features
217   as query and its corresponding virtual point features as key and value. As shown in Figure 2 (b), take
218   plane Txy as an example, a sequence of virtual point features {q̂i,j,k }L k=1 along z axis corresponds
219   to triplane feature pi,j on Txy . In axis-attention module, we also include a position bias (3D position
220   embedding) to each head in computing similarity. Finally, we compute new triplane feature p̂i,j on
221   plane Txy with cross-attention, formulated as follows:
222                               p̂i,j = CrossAttn(pi,j , {q̂i,j,k }L
                                                                     k=1 ) + pi,j                           (3)
223
224   Plane-Attention Module In temporal-aware view-attention module, the aggregated features are
225   not good enough to model dynamic contents, since epipolar constraint is invalid in dynamic scenes.
226   Thus, we build a plane-attention module to leverage 3D-related information. Like (Cao et al., 2023),
227   our plane-attention module includes self- and cross-plane attention. The self-plane attention aims to
228
      enhance the semantic information of individual planes while the cross-plane attention concentrates on
      building generalized 3D prior knowledge across different planes. Therefore, we can leverage learned
229
      semantic information and 3D prior knowledge to resolve the feature error from temporal-aware
230
      view-attention module.
231
232   Specifically, the aim of self-plane attention is to update each plane feature by aggregating features
233   from intra-plane. In cross-plane attention, each plane feature take itself as query and other planes
234
      as key and value. In plane-attention module, we also add a position bias to each head in computing
      similarity. Note that our contracted triplane is used to represent unbounded scenes. In general, we
235
      need to embed the infinite position, which is hard to implement. To avoid it, we take learnable
236
      position embedding to represent position bias.
237
238   Camera Feature The training dataset comprises images with a broad range of focal lengths, causing
239   scale ambiguity. To address it, we take camera intrinsic matrix as an inductive bias. We construct a
240   camera feature c ∈ R16 for each target view by flattening its 4-by-4 camera intrinsic matrix. Then
241   we embed feature c by mapping it into a higher-dimensional space via a sinusoidal function γ(·) and
242   projecting it into input dimension via a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). Drawing inspiration from
243   DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023), we incorporate an adaptive layer normalization (adaLN) within our
244   feature attention block to effectively constrain the inputs of each attention block based on camera
245   features.
246
247   3.3   S CENE D ECODER
248
      Upsampler For high performance, we use a trainable deconvolution layer to upscale the triplane
249
      embeddings Txy , Tyz and Txz extracted from the transformer. After the upsampling, we obtain the
250   final triplane for volume rendering.
251
252   NeRF Decoder We adopt NeRF as decoder to predict color RGB and density σ based on the 3D
253   point feature extracted from the triplane. Initially, we normalize the 3D position using the contraction
254   function described in Section 3.2.1 and project it onto three planes. Subsequently, we concatenate
255   the features from these planes to form the final feature vector. This vector is then decoded into color
256   RGB and density σ using a lightweight MLP. In addition, we normalize the values of ’near’ and ’far’
257   for all scenarios.
258
259   3.4   T RAINING
260
      Temporal-based 3D Constraint A strong multi-view constraint, which generates multiple views
261
      simultaneously from the same scene, is a common strategy to ensure 3D consistency. However, given
262
      the limitations of monocular video, we employ a temporal-based 3D constraint by rendering two
263   views that are temporally distant conditioned on the same sequence. Specifically, given a sequence of
264   egocentric views {It }St=1 , we select two frames that are S frames apart as the target views, leveraging
265   the significant disparity between these time-distant views to enforce the constraint.
266
267   Training Strategy We adopt a cost-effective training strategy by starting with lower-resolution
268   input images. Initially, we pretrain our model on 128 × 72 images until convergence. Then, we
269   finetune it using 512 × 288 images, significantly reducing computational costs while still achieving
      superior quality compared to direct high-resolution training with equivalent computational resources.

294   Training Objective To mitigate the high cost of rendering full-resolution images for volume
295   rendering, we randomly select 64 × 64 and 128 × 128 patches from target images with resolutions of
296   128 × 72 and 512 × 288, respectively, for loss supervision. We evaluate the visual accuracy of our
297   renderings compared to ground-truth (GT) images using three types of losses: an L2 reconstruction
298
      loss Lrecon , a perceptual loss Llpips , and a structural similarity loss Lssim . Additionally, to address the
      issue of semi-transparent clouds, we apply a regularization term Ldist , inspired by the distortion loss
299
      in Mip-NeRF360 (Barron et al., 2022). The overall training loss function is formulated as follows:

772   A.5   L IMITATIONS
773
      There are three limitations for our method. First, we need camera intrinsics and extrinsics to train our
774
      model. These camera parameters may not align well with the ground truth. Meanwhile, these camera
775   parameters are not easy to obtain for complex videos. For this limit, it is an alternative solution to
776   optimize these camera parameters and our model jointly via a data-driven manner. Second, our model
777   is deterministic, thus it performs not well for unseen content that does not exist in source views. To
778   solve it, we will introduce diffusion models to generate unseen content in the future. Third, due to
779   limited resource, we can not train a large model with large-scale datasets (e.g. Ego4D (Grauman
780   et al., 2022)) to validate the scalability of our approach.
