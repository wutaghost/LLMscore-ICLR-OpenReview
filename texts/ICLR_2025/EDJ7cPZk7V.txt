009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Catastrophic forgetting poses a significant challenge in continual learning, where
012            models often forget previous tasks when trained on new data. Our empirical anal-
013            ysis reveals a strong correlation between catastrophic forgetting and the learning
014            speed of examples: examples learned early are rarely forgotten, while those learned
015            later are more susceptible to forgetting. We demonstrate that replay-based continual
016            learning methods can leverage this phenomenon by focusing on mid-learned exam-
017            ples for rehearsal. We introduce Goldilocks, a novel replay buffer sampling method
018
               that filters out examples learned too quickly or too slowly, keeping those learned at
               an intermediate speed. Goldilocks improves existing continual learning algorithms,
019
               leading to state-of-the-art performance across several image classification tasks.
020

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The surge in deploying deep models in real-world scenarios has highlighted the need for models
025   capable of adjusting to changing environments. Continual learning (see extensive reviews, e.g
026   (De Lange et al., 2021; Hadsell et al., 2020; Parisi et al., 2019)), which involves training deep learning
027   models on new data even after mastering previous tasks, is crucial in this context. However, continual
028   learning encounters a significant obstacle called catastrophic forgetting (French, 1999; Kemker et al.,
029   2018; McCloskey & Cohen, 1989): as models learn from new data, they tend to forget previous tasks,
030   resulting in decreased performance on those tasks. Despite extensive research efforts, catastrophic
031   forgetting persists as a central challenge in continual learning.
032   In this work, we begin by empirically examining catastrophic forgetting, identifying which examples
033   deep models tend to forget when trained on new tasks. Our findings reveal a last-in-first-out pattern,
034   where examples learned later are forgotten more readily than those learned earlier. Fig. 1 visualizes
035   this by showing which examples are likely to be forgotten or remembered over time. This phenomenon
036   is connected to the simplicity bias (Shah et al., 2020; Szegedy et al., 2014) of neural networks, where
037   networks learn simple examples faster than complex ones. However, complex examples are more
038
      likely to be forgotten, while simple examples are more likely to be remembered.
039   This observation has an important consequence: by measuring how fast examples are being learned
040   by the model, we can predict in advance which examples are more susceptible to forgetting, and
041   treat them accordingly. This holds true regardless of the distribution of the new task introduced to
042   the model. Furthermore, we demonstrate that models showing varying degrees of forgetting do so
043   consistently: models with less catastrophic forgetting recall most of the examples retained by those
044
      with more forgetting, with the additional ability to remember slightly more complex examples.
045   Replay-based continual learning methods use a memory buffer to address catastrophic forgetting,
046   storing a small subset of the original task examples. Typically, this buffer is uniformly sampled from
047   previous tasks, and different continual learning techniques vary in how they utilize it. Our findings
048   indicate that while a replay buffer helps mitigate forgetting, a similar pattern emerges: examples
049   learned later are forgotten sooner. Increasing buffer size or improving the underlying model’s
050
      architecture can alleviate more of the forgetting to some extent, but the fundamental phenomenon
      persists. This observation implies that when strengthening a continually trained model, we can predict
051
      in advance which examples it will better remember compared to its weaker version.
052
053   This raises the question: can one exploit this phenomenon and reduce forgetting by cleverly selecting
      examples for the replay buffer? Our observations reveal that the model benefits most from examples

093   1.2   R ELATED WORK
094
095   Catastrophic forgetting. Existing studies primarily focus on mitigating catastrophic forgetting
096   (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019; Ritter et al., 2018; Serra et al., 2018), rather
097   than its characterization. Some works have explored catastrophic forgetting from a model perspective:
098   (Nguyen et al., 2020; Ramasesh et al., 2021) showed differences in forgetting patterns across model
099
      layers, while (Mirzadeh et al., 2020; Pfülb & Gepperth, 2019) demonstrated the impact of training
      hyper-parameters. (Nguyen et al., 2019) identified task complexity as a factor influencing forgetting
100
      rates. Our study differs by examining the relationship between learning speed and forgettability of
101
      examples, emphasizing a data-centric perspective.
102
103   Simplicity bias. Empirical evidence supports the hypothesis that neural networks initially learn
104   simple models, gaining complexity over time (Cao et al., 2021; Gissin et al., 2020; Gunasekar et al.,
105   2018; Heckel & Soltanolkotabi, 2020; Hu et al., 2020; Jin & Montúfar, 2023; Kalimeris et al., 2019;
106   Pérez et al., 2019; Soudry et al., 2018; Ulyanov et al., 2018). This phenomenon, known as simplicity
107   bias (Dingle et al., 2018; Shah et al., 2020), is extended in our work to catastrophic forgetting,
      illustrating a reverse simplicity bias where complex examples are forgotten before simpler ones.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Forgetting dynamics. Several studies have directly explored the dynamics of forgetting. (Maini
109   et al., 2022; Toneva et al., 2019) revealed that, even in non-continual settings, certain examples tend to
110   be less forgettable during training. (Millunzi et al., 2023) demonstrated that the forgetting dynamics
111   of noisy examples differ from those of clean labels and proposed adjusting the rehearsal process
112   accordingly. Our results align with these findings, further delineating which examples are more prone
113   to forgetting and retention, establishing connections between catastrophic forgetting and simplicity
114   bias, and proposing a method to identify examples likely to be forgotten proactively. Additionally,
115   our work illustrates how to leverage these insights to enhance the sampling of the replay buffer for
116   various continual learning approaches.
117
      Replay buffer sampling functions. Replay-based methods mitigate catastrophic forgetting by storing
118
      examples from previous tasks in a memory buffer of fixed size. Despite the availability of various
119   sampling strategies (Aljundi et al., 2019; Benkő, 2024; Buzzega et al., 2021; Rebuffi et al., 2017;
120   Tiwari et al., 2022; Wiewel & Yang, 2021), their effectiveness often depends on specific conditions
121   or algorithms. As a result, many replay-based methods resort to uniform sampling (Buzzega et al.,
122   2020; Guo et al., 2020; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Prabhu et al., 2020;
123   Ramesh & Chaudhari, 2022; Rolnick et al., 2019) in practice. Unlike previous methods, Goldilocks
124   can improve replay-based methods across diverse tasks and scenarios.

      2     E MPIRICAL ANALYSIS OF CATASTROPHIC FORGETTING
127
128
      In this section, we empirically study catastrophic forgetting from a data-centric perspective, drawing
129
      a connection between the speed at which a model learns examples, and its likelihood to forget them.

131
132   2.1   D EFINITIONS
133
      We examine the continual learning classification setting, where the training dataset D = {X , Y}
134
      consists of examples x ∈ X and their corresponding labels y ∈ Y. This dataset is divided into T > 1
135                                                                           ST
      tasks, with each task t ∈ [T ] represented as Dt = {Xt , Yt }, and D = t=1 Dt . There is no overlap
136
      between the data of different tasks. A deep model f : X → Y is sequentially trained on each task
137
      Dt from t = 1 to t = T , with E ∈ N epochs per task. Each task also has a separate test set from
138   the same distribution. During training on a task Dt , the model f is provided with a replay buffer
139   B of fixed-memory size, containing examples from previous tasks 1, ..., t − 1. Notably, |B| ≪ |D|,
140   meaning only a fraction of the data can be stored in the replay buffer. It’s worth mentioning that the
141   buffer may be empty (B = ∅), representing training f without a memory buffer.
142
143   Learning speed. Neural networks demonstrate a trend in learning examples, where certain examples
144   are consistently learned before others across various deep neural models (Baldock et al., 2021;
145
      Choshen et al., 2022; Hacohen et al., 2020). This orderliness is often connected to the simplicity
      bias phenomenon (Hacohen & Weinshall, 2022), as examples learned earlier tend to be simpler than
146
      those learned later. Rather than measuring the example’s complexity, which can be defined in many
147
      different ways, we use the speed at which examples are learned by the deep model, which can be
148   measured efficiently and directly. Given a model f and an example (x, y) either in the train or test
149   data, we define the learning speed of the example (x, y) to be:

190

296   3     E FFECTS OF BUFFER COMPOSITIONS ON CATASTROPHIC FORGETTING
297
298   Replay-based continual learning methods typically sample the replay buffer uniformly, including both
299   quickly and slowly learned examples. In this section, we analyze various replay buffer compositions
300
      and find that medium-speed learned examples are the most beneficial for the replay process. These
      examples, which the model has only a moderate chance of remembering, prove particularly important.
301
      One possible explanation is that increasing the buffer size impacts the catastrophic forgetting of these
302
      examples the most, suggesting that focusing on them is advantageous.
303
304
      3.1   T HE G OLDILOCKS APPROACH – METHODOLOGY AND DEFINITIONS
305
306   To explore different buffer compositions, we remove a combination of the quickest and slowest
307   learned examples from the data and then sample the buffer uniformly from the remaining examples.
308   This allows us to add a bias to the replay buffer toward examples learned at certain speeds. We
309   propose Goldilocks, a novel sampling method, used both in our empirical analysis and as a practical
310   replay buffer sampling technique.
311   Formally, Goldilocks is defined by two hyperparameters, quick and slow, which specify the per-
312   centage of examples learned too quickly or too slowly to be removed from dataset Dt . During the
313   training of the model f on Dt , we maintain the learning speed of each example. At the end of task t,
314   examples in Dt are sorted by learning speed, and the top %quick and bottom %slow are removed.
315   The pseudocode for Goldilocks sampling is provided in Algorithm 1.
316   With Goldilocks, the quick and slow hyperparameters are fixed. However, by keeping a constant
317   buffer size and varying these hyperparameters across experiments, we create replay buffers with
318   different compositions, focused on either slowly or quickly learned examples. Next, we explore a
319   range of buffer compositions for each method, analyzing their ability to mitigate forgetting.
320

374   Discussion. Our findings suggest that a good composition of a replay buffer consists of examples
375   learned during the middle of the training – neither too quickly nor too slowly. A possible intuition
376   for this observation may be connected to simplicity bias. As networks train, they gradually acquire
377   the ability to handle increasingly complex concepts. By the end of a task, the network performs
      well on examples up to a certain level of complexity. For replay buffers, this implies that examples

378
      learned too quickly are overly simplistic, offering limited additional value since the network has
379   already mastered more complex tasks. Conversely, examples learned too slowly are suboptimal, as
380   the network struggled to learn them initially, so it is likely to struggle when replaying them.

383   3.3   I NDEPENDENCY OF THE OPTIMAL BUFFER COMPOSITION ON SUBSEQUENT TASKS
384
385
      We empirically examine how subsequent tasks impact the ideal composition of a replay buffer for a
      given task. We find that regardless of the similarity or dissimilarity between subsequent tasks and
386
      the original task, the optimal replay buffer composition remains largely independent and consistent.
387
      To show this, we conduct experiments with three distinct tasks denoted A, B, and C. By training the
388   model on task A and subsequently introducing either task B or task C while keeping the original task
389   unchanged, we analyze the ideal replay buffer composition under varying subsequent tasks.
390
391
      We initially focus on tasks from the same datasets. We pick task A to be the first 25 classes of
      CIFAR-100, task B to be classes 26 to 50, and task C to be classes 51 to 75. In Fig. 5, we depict the
392
      mean final accuracy of 10 networks across various replay buffer compositions when using either task
393
      B or task C as the subsequent task after task A. Despite some quantitative variations in performance,
394   the qualitative results consistently indicate that good buffer compositions for one case remain effective
395   for the other, and vice versa. These results suggest that the ideal replay buffer composition of task A
396   is robust to differences between tasks B and C in this context.
397
      Further, we explore scenarios involving either changing the classification task or replacing it with
398
      memorization by training on random labels. We adopt the RotNet approach (Gidaris et al., 2018) to
399
      change the classification task. We pick tasks A and B to be different subsets of CIFAR-100, while task
400   C involves a rotation classification task, where each example in task A is randomly rotated between
401   90◦ , 180◦ , or 270◦ degrees, with the task being to classify these rotations regardless of the original
402   label. For the random case, tasks A and B remain unchanged, while task C involves the same subset
403   of task B, but with labels chosen uniformly at random, as suggested in (Zhang et al., 2021). In both
404   scenarios, we observe consistent results: the ideal replay buffer composition of task A remains robust
405   to differences between tasks B and C in both contexts. Further details and heatmaps for the results
406   are available in App. E.
407
408
      Choosing hyperparameters. The broad range of quick and slow values that yield effective buffer
      compositions allows for consistent hyperparameter settings across datasets and tasks, minimizing
409
      the need for fine-tuning. For instance, selecting quick = slow = 20% – among other viable options
410
      – consistently enhances performance across all evaluated datasets and scenarios and generalizes
411   well to unseen datasets. These values can also be further adjusted heuristically based on prior
412   data knowledge: for larger buffers and simpler datasets, prioritizing the removal of slowly-learned
413   examples is advantageous, while for smaller buffers or more complex datasets, focusing on quickly-
414   learned examples proves more effective. If computational resources allow, systematic tuning of
415   these hyperparameters is feasible. By creating a rotated version of the dataset, we can simulate
416   a 2-task scenario using the original and rotated data, then perform a grid search to fine-tune the
417   hyperparameters. The smooth effect of these parameters on performance suggests that a coarse grid
418   search is usually sufficient.
419
420   3.4   E XTENDING G OLDILOCKS TO SAMPLING TO MULTI - TASK SCENARIOS
421
422   In many continual learning problems, models encounter long sequences of tasks. To extend Goldilocks
423   sampling to multitask scenarios, we measure the learning speed of each example only during the
424   training of the task it belongs to. In theory, this allows for separate quick and slow hyperparameters
425   for each task, determining how many examples to remove when sampling for the current buffer.
426   However, tuning multiple hyperparameters for each task is computationally expensive. Thus, in
427   practice, we apply the same quick and slow hyperparameters across all tasks when dealing with
428
      more than two tasks.
429   In multitask training, we also need an effective method to remove examples from the buffer, as the
430   proportion of examples from each task diminishes as the number of tasks increases. In Goldilocks,
431   examples can be removed uniformly at random, while still adhering to the principle that neither
      too-fast nor too-slow examples remain in the buffer.

519   4     D ISCUSSION
520
521   We show a correlation between the speed at which examples are learned and the likelihood of models
522   forgetting these examples when learning new data. This correlation aligns closely with the simplicity
523   bias, suggesting that models tend to forget more complex examples. Although measuring learning
524   speed is a simple proxy for example complexity, its simplicity underscores the potential for developing
525   more sophisticated methods to mitigate catastrophic forgetting, offering avenues for future research.
526   While most replay-based continual learning works focus on better utilization of the replay buffer, our
527   study demonstrates the potential for buffer improvement, extending the scope of continual learning
528   research beyond buffer utilization alone. By showing that Goldilocks can be implemented with
529   minimal computational overhead, we highlight its applicability to future advancements in continual
530   learning. Furthermore, this work underscores the mutual benefits of concurrent progress in better
531   sampling of the replay buffer and improvements in buffer utilization, suggesting promising directions
532   for future research.
533
      Limitations. One limitation of Goldilocks is that the learning speed score is based on the number of
534   training epochs. With a small number of epochs, the score becomes overly discrete, which reduces the
535   precision of the evaluation. However, as shown in App. C and Fig. 22, Goldilocks remains effective
536   even with a small number of epochs, as long as the network converges, although with increased noise.
537   Nonetheless, many continual learning scenarios emphasize stream-like settings, which typically
538   involve only a single epoch. These scenarios are not well-supported by Goldilocks, as the learning
539   speed fails to adequately capture how different examples are learned over time. Alternative methods
      for approximating example complexity are needed, which we leave as future work.

744   A    C LASS INCREMENTAL LEARNING
745
746   There are two primary paradigms in continual learning: class incremental learning (CIL) and task
747   incremental learning (TIL). The distinction lies in how tasks are handled. In TIL, task identities are
748   known during inference, enabling the use of specialized classifiers within each task, whereas in CIL,
749   the model must infer both the task and class identity without prior knowledge, often leading to lower
750   performance. These paradigms address different real-world scenarios, and methods optimized for
751   one sometimes underperform on the other, necessitating separate evaluations in continual learning
752
      research.
753   Our work examines the relationship between the speed at which examples are learned and their
754   susceptibility to forgetting in continual learning. Despite the differences between CIL and TIL, our
755   study reveals that the same qualitative results and the same conclusions can be made in both cases.
      Therefore, to avoid repetition in the main text, we focus there on TIL only, as it isolates forgetting

      at the model level rather than conflating it with final-layer interference. To complement this, we
757   reproduce all key experiments under CIL settings in this appendix, highlighting similar conclusions
