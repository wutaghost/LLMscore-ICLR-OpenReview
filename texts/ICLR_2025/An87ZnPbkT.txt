011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Molecular docking is a major element in drug discovery and design. It enables
014            the prediction of ligand-protein interactions by simulating the binding of small
015            molecules to proteins. Despite the availability of numerous docking algorithms,
016            there is no single algorithm consistently outperforms the others across a diverse
017
               set of docking scenarios. This paper introduces GNNAS-Dock, a novel Graph
               Neural Network (GNN)-based automated algorithm selection system for molecu-
018
               lar docking in blind docking situations. GNNs are accommodated to process the
019
               complex structural data of both ligands and proteins. They benefit from the inher-
020            ent graph-like properties to predict the performance of various docking algorithms
021            under different conditions. The present study pursues two main objectives: 1) pre-
022            dict the performance of each candidate docking algorithm, in terms of Root Mean
023            Square Deviation (RMSD), thereby identifying the most accurate method for spe-
024            cific scenarios; and 2) choose the best computationally efficient docking algo-
025            rithm for each docking case, aiming to reduce the time required for docking while
026            maintaining high accuracy. We validate our approach on PDBBind 2020 refined
027            set, which contains about 5,300 pairs of protein-ligand complexes. Our strategy
028
               is performed across a portfolio of 6 different state-of-the-art docking algorithms.
               To be specific, the candidate algorithms are DiffDock, DSDP, TankBind, GNINA,
029
               SMINA, Qvina-W. We additionally combine p2rank with GNINA, SMINA and
030
               Qvina-W for docking site prediction. Therefore, there are totally 9 different al-
031            gorithms for selection. Our algorithm selection model achieves a mean RMSD of
032            approximately 1.74 Å, significantly improving upon the top performing docking
033            algorithm (DiffDock), which has a mean RMSD of 2.95 Å. Moreover, when mak-
034            ing selection in consideration of computational efficiency, our model demonstrates
035            a success rate of 79.73% in achieving an RMSD below the 2 Å threshold, with a
036            mean RMSD value of 2.75 Å and an average processing time of about 29.05 sec-
037            onds per instance. In contrast, the remaining docking algorithms like TankBind,
038            though faster with a processing time of merely 0.03 seconds per instance, only
039
               achieve an RMSD below the 2 Å threshold in less than 60% of cases. These find-
               ings demonstrate the capability of GNN-based algorithm selection to significantly
040
               enhance docking performance while effectively reducing the computational time
041
               required, balancing efficiency with precision in molecular docking.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046
      Molecular docking (Stanzione et al., 2021) is critical for identifying potential drug candidates con-
047
      cerning computer-aided drug discovery (CADD). It has also been widely used for understanding
048   enzyme-substrate interactions and designing enzyme inhibitors (Rudnitskaya et al., 2010). Devel-
049   oped computational strategies simulate the interaction between a small molecule, i.e. ligand, and a
050   protein, i.e. receptor, at the atomic level to predict the molecule’s orientation and binding affinity
051   toward the protein. There have been a wide range of molecular docking algorithms and tools. Initial
052   and traditional ones employ a search algorithm to determine the potential binding poses of ligands,
053   and a scoring function to evaluate these predictions (Halperin et al., 2002). The search algorithms
      come into play when exploring different poses with the aim finding an acceptable, (near-)optimal

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      one. Those predetermined search methods and scoring functions have been gradually replaced by
055   machine learning based techniques, offering higher generalization, flexibility and practicality. In
056   particular, deep learning has been taking over due it its success on almost any application domains.
057   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) have been frequently utilized due to the visual characteris-
058   tics of the docking components, such as replacing those traditional scoring functions (McNutt et al.,
059   2021). As an alternative example to the search-based approaches, SE(3)-equivariant geometric neu-
060   ral network has been introduced to predict potential binding sites and ligand orientations directly
061   (Stärk et al., 2022), without any explicit search during prediction.
062   However, the diversity of available algorithms presents a challenge: no single method consistently
063   outperforms others across all docking scenarios. This is aligned with the no free lunch theorem
064   (NFLT) (Wolpert & Macready, 1997), which states that no single algorithm can universally excel
065   across all problem instances. This aspect is also valid for docking algorithms, meaning that there
066   is no ultimately best docking algorithm outperforming other under the same or experimental con-
067   ditions. Thus, specifying the best docking algorithm for a docking scenario can offer significant
068   performance gains over utilizing only one algorithm for addressing all the docking tasks at hand.
069
      Algorithm Selection (AS) (Kerschke et al., 2019) is an automated strategy to automatically accom-
      modate one or more algorithms to solve a particular problem instance instead of relying on a single
070
      one or choosing an algorithm based on personal experience.
071
072   Targeting molecular docking, the present article proposes a Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)-based
073   AS system, GNNAS-Dock, that utilizes structural features of ligands and proteins to predict which
074   docking algorithm will perform optimally under varying docking conditions. GNNs are particularly
075
      well-suited for this task as they operate on graph data (Zhou et al., 2020), which naturally corre-
      sponds to the structural representation of molecules and proteins. Graph data consists of multiple
076
      nodes and edges connecting them, similar to the molecular structures composed of various atoms
077
      and the bonds between them. This structual similarity might be helpful for GNN to capture the
078   3D features of ligands and proteins, and predict the performance of docking algorithms on different
079   docking tasks accurately based on these learned features. In this research, we aim to develop two
080   distinct GNN-based algorithm selection models, each serving a specific purpose. The first model
081   is designed to select the most accurate docking method for various scenarios. It will predict the
082   performance of different docking methods in terms of ligand root mean square error (RMSD), and
083   choose the one with the best predicted performance. The second model focuses on optimizing the
084   efficiency of the docking process. It aims to reduce the time required to achieve accurate docking
085   results by evaluating both the speed and effectiveness of various algorithms. This model will set a
086
      threshold of RMSD, typically 2 Å, to determine if a docking result is considered effective. It will
      then predict whether each algorithm can meet this threshold and how long it will take, allowing the
087
      selection of the most time-efficient algorithm without sacrificing accuracy. Together, these mod-
088
      els provide a comprehensive approach to improving both the accuracy and efficiency of molecular
089   docking, addressing the current challenges faced in the field.
090
091   Our contributions can be summarized as follows.
092
      1. There is limited research on AS for molecular docking. Our proposed system, GNNAS-Dock,

      2     BACKGROUND
114
      2.1   M OLECULAR D OCKING M ETHODS .
115
116   Molecular docking can be investigated under different categories, such as blind docking, redocking,
117   and crossdocking. Blind docking refers to the situation that the binding site of the protein is un-
118   known, requiring the docking algorithm to explore potential binding sites across the entire surface
119   of the protein (Hassan et al., 2017). For blind docking, various docking methods exists for pre-
120   dicting the binding sites and potential binding poses of ligands. All the methods follow a similar
121   algorithmic template. It is predicting potential binding poses and then refining these predictions
122   based on some evaluative criteria. The search techniques and performance metrics differ though. In
123   that sense, traditional docking algorithms typically rely on search methods and scoring functions to
124
      explore the possible binding configurations of a ligand in the protein and refine them iteratively. For
      instance, AutoDock Vina (Trott & Olson, 2010), a well-known docking method utilizes a stochastic
125
      search methods combined with a empirical and knowledge-based scoring function to predict ligand
126
      configurations. It has some variants such as SMINA (Koes et al., 2013) and QuickVina-W (Qvina-
127   W) (Hassan et al., 2017). SMINA extends the AutoDock Vina by adjusting the scoring function
128   and making it customizable. Besides, QuickVina-W enhances the original algorithm by optimizing
129   the search algorithm for faster performance. These methods are mature and have been validated in
130   many docking tasks, which make them still useful in many situations.
131
      Recent advancements have incorporated Deep Learning (DL) into both searching and scoring steps
132
      to enhance docking quality for blind docking. GNINA (McNutt et al., 2021) employs Convolu-
133   tional Neural Networks (CNNs) to score potential poses, speeding up the evaluation process while
134   maintaining high accuracy. DSDP (Huang et al., 2023) leverages 3D CNNs to predict docking sites,
135   then uses search and scoring functions similar to those in AutoDock Vina for the docking process.
136   Additionally, TankBind (Lu et al., 2022) utilizes geometric deep learning to directly predict poten-
137   tial binding poses of ligands. Similarly, DiffDock (Corso et al., 2023) employs a generative model
138   to dynamically simulate and rank possible binding configurations. These deep learning combined
139   docking methods are more efficient then traditional algorithms, but traditional methods still reach
140   higher accuracy in some situations, especially when combined with some docking site prediction
141
      tool such as p2rank (Krivák & Hoksza, 2018).
142
143   2.2   A LGORITHM S ELECTION .
144
145   Algorithm Selection (AS) has been commonly devised as performance prediction models, mapping
146   a set of hand-picked features representing the target problem instance to the performance of the
147   algorithms at hand, Φ : X ∈ Rd 7→ PA ∈ Rm . Following those traditional machine learning
148   based AS models, deep learning has also been used, mostly to eliminate the need of those explicit
149   problem instance features. As an initial attempt, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) has been
150   utilized to build an AS model for solving the Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) problem Loreggia et al.
151   (2016).The problem instances are transformed into images by representing each character, simply
152   appearing in the instance files, by ASCII values representing the scales of grey. The images are
153
      given to a CNN architecture to choose an algorithm for solving each instance. In Kostovska et al.
      (2023), transformer architecture was used for selection algorithms targeting well-known black-box
154
      optimization (BBOB) problems. In drug discovery, some meta-learning models (Olier et al., 2018;
155
      Schlender et al., 2023) are used for AS on quantitative structure activity relationships (QSAR).
156   For AS targeting docking, in the very first study (Chen et al., 2023), ALORS (Mısır & Sebag,
157   2017), an algorithm recommendation system based on collaborative filtering, was accommodated to
158   determine the optimal search box size in AutoDock 4.2 for docking tasks involving 1428 ligands
159   on the Human Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE). The results show that ALORS outperforms
160   all the individual algorithm setups. Following that, we earlier designed more of a traditional AS
161   model to select the optimal docking algorithm among 6 different options for blind docking tasks
      with protein-ligand complexes from the PDBBind dataset (Liu et al., 2017), which contain more

      than 19,000 pairs for blind docking. That work as a proof of concept demonstrated the effectiveness
163   of AS in blind docking.

      3     M ETHOD : GNNAS-D OCK
167
      The present study introduces a Graph Neural Networks (GNN) based Algorithm Selection (AS)
168
      system for Molecular Docking (GNNAS-Dock). The goal is to automatically identify the right
169
      docking algorithm from a candidate set for a given protein-ligand pair to deliver high quality and
170   robust blind docking. The process starts with the construction of distinct graphs representing ligands,
171   GL = (VL , EL ), and proteins, GP = (VP , EP ), due to the significant differences in their sizes and
172   structural complexities. Here, V and E denote vertices and edges, respectively.
173
      Then, a predictive model leveraging stacking learning (Mohammed & Kora, 2023) for AS is built.
174
      In the context of our research, stacking learning involves using the output features from the first-
175
      level GNNs as inputs to a second-level meta-model for prediction. Firstly, two GNNs are built to
176   process protein and ligand graphs separately to extract features learned from their structures. Given
177   the distinct structural complexities of proteins and ligands, it is crucial to design specific GNN
178   architectures for each type of graph. These architectures are specialized to efficiently process and
179   learn from the distinct topological and chemical properties in each graph type. After extracting
180   learned features from the individual GNNs for both proteins and ligands, the next step involves
181   integrating these features through direct concatenation. The concatenated features serve as input
182   to a series of dense layers which construct the second-level meta-model in the stacking learning
183   model. This meta-model makes final predictions for assessing the performance of each docking
184
      algorithms, predicting not only the most accurate algorithm in terms of RMSD but also estimating
      the computational efficiency for each algorithm.
185
186   In the final configuration of GNNAS-Dock, two distinct AS models are built referring to different
187   criteria. The first model prioritizes accuracy, which recommends the docking algorithm achieving
188   the lowest docking quality, which is RMSD. The overall working structure of the accuracy ori-
189   ented GNNAS-Dock is illustrated in Figure 1. The second model focuses more on efficiency, which
190
      chooses the algorithm that completes the docking process fastest while achieving an acceptable bind-
      ing result for a given docking scenario. This dual-model approach allows users to select the optimal
191
      algorithm based on the specific requirements of their molecular docking tasks.

290   3.3   ACCURACY M ODEL
291
292   The accuracy model within the GNNAS-Dock system integrates Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
293   for both proteins and ligands into a combined training framework to predict the most accurate dock-
294   ing algorithm for each docking instance in terms of RMSD. The architecture includes a Protein GNN
295   and a Ligand GNN, which simultaneously process graph representations of protein GP and ligand
296
      GL respectively. The specific structures of these GNNs have been discussed in the previous sections.
      They will extract some important features from the graphs automatically to represent the 3D struc-
297
      tures of protein and ligands without defining manually. Then, a stacking ensemble learning model is
298
      strategically designed to integrate the learned results from both the Protein GNN and Ligand GNN.
299   This model consolidates the distinct features extracted from each GNN into a unified feature set,
300   denoted as Xcombined = (Xprotein , Xligand ). These combined features are then employed to train
301   a meta-model M to predict the performance of various docking algorithms based on the integrated
302   data from both the protein and ligand models. The overall learning process is conducted end-to-end,
303   ensuring that no manual intervention is required for tasks such as feature extraction, weight assign-
304   ment, or other parameter adjustments. For the meta-model, a comparatively simple architecture is
305   adopted, consisting of only one linear hidden layer. The input layer in the meta-model is responsible
306   for receiving the combined feature set that concatenates the outputs of the Protein GNN and Ligand
307
      GNN, which have a dimension of 2 × k for k representing the feature dimension of extracted from
      protein and ligand models. This linear transformation of the hidden layer expands the feature di-

      3.4   E FFICIENCY M ODEL
325
326   In the accuracy model, only the performance of each docking algorithm is considered. However, it
327   is possible that some accurate models may take longer to run. Additionally, although lower RMSD
328   is better, binding can be viewed as successful if its RMSD is below a threshold, which is usually 2 Å
329   (Castro-Alvarez et al., 2017). Therefore, an efficiency model is also built for choosing the quickest
330   good performing algorithm under a specific performance threshold.
331   Two predictive models are constructed based on the features extracted from both protein and lig-
332   and through GNNs. One model predicts whether each docking algorithm can successfully solve a
333   docking problem, with outcomes categorized as 1 (”solvable”) or 0 (”unsolvable”). If the docking
334   algorithm could generate a result with RMSD lower than 2 Å, it is categorized as 1; otherwise, it
335   will be classified as 0. Since this is a binary classification model, we changed the output layer in
336   the meta-model to be output = σ(W2 h(x) + b2 ) in which σ denotes the sigmoid function. It will
337   return the probabilities of whether the docking methods could solve the docking problem. Then,
338   the other model is built to predict the time required for each docking algorithm to produce its re-
339
      sult, which uses the same GNN structure as the accuracy model to extract features from protein
      and ligand graphs, and same meta-model structures. These two models are trained separately since
340
      their objectives are different. For each docking task to be handled, only those algorithms predicted
341
      to solve the problem, i.e., categorized as 1, are considered. Among them, the algorithm with the
342   shortest predicted time is recommended. When there is no algorithm predicted to meet the RMSD
343   threshold of 2 Å the algorithm predicted to be the fastest is chosen. This methodology provides a
344   balanced selection concerning docking quality and speed. The complete procedures are shown in

365   In this study, we evaluate the performance of the GNNAS-Dock system using a selection of nine
366   docking algorithms. These algorithms include DiffDock, TankBind, DSDP, GNINA, SMINA,
367   Qvina, and combinations that integrate docking site prediction tool, such as GNINA+p2rank,
368
      SMINA+p2rank, and Qvina+p2rank. This portfolio of algorithms is diverse since it contains both
      traditional docking algorithms and some advanced methods integrating deep learning techniques.
369
      These algorithms are all state-of-the-art docking algorithms in their own types.
370
371   The dataset employed for evaluating the algorithms is derived from the refined set of the PDBBind
372   database (Liu et al., 2017), which consists of approximately 5,300 pairs of protein-ligand complexes.
373   This dataset is highly regarded within the drug discovery community for its quality and relevance,
374
      making it an ideal choice for rigorous testing of docking algorithms. For constructing graphs for
      proteins and ligands, we utilize the Python package Graphein (Jamasb et al., 2022).
375
376   For measuring docking performances, symmetry-corrected root mean square deviation (RMSD) is
377   used as the metric (Meli & Biggin, 2020), as in the recent docking approaches such as DiffDock. It
      additionally takes molecular symmetry into count, providing a more accurate evaluation.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
462
463   This study presents a novel and robust approach called a Graph Neural networks (GNN) based Al-
464   gorithm Selection (AS) system for molecular docking (GNNAS-Dock). In this system, we design
465   two distinct models for recommending suitable docking algorithms with different criterion, i.e., ac-
466   curacy and efficiency. Through extensive experimentation on the PDBBind refined set, both models
467
      demonstrate significant improvements over single docking algorithms for blind docking tasks in
      terms of accuracy. The accuracy model reaches a much lower mean RMSD than all the other dock-
468
      ing algorithms, indicating its superior precision in predicting the most accurate docking algorithm
469
      for docking tasks. The efficiency model achieves a comparatively good performance for docking
470   tasks in less than half the running time of the accuracy model, showing a great balance between
471   accuracy and efficiency.
472
473
      While GNNAS-Dock system shows significant potential in enhancing docking efficiency and accu-
      racy, the built models can still be improved in terms of generalization. The overall experiment is
474
      conducted with the PDBBind refined set, which may not fully represent the diverse range of molec-
475
      ular interactions encountered in pharmaceutical research. To address this, future development could
476   focus on expanding dataset diversity by including more protein-ligand pairs, which would improve
477   model robustness and real-world applicability. Additionally, structural optimizations including the
478   adjustments for GNN architectures, could be applied to enhance GNNAS-Dock further.
