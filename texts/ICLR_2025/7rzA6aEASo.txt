009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Given a budget on total model size, one must decide whether to train a single,
012            large neural network or to combine the predictions of many smaller networks. We
013            study this trade-off for ensembles of random-feature ridge regression models. We
014            prove that when a fixed number of trainable parameters are partitioned among K
015            independently trained models, K = 1 achieves optimal performance, provided the
016
               ridge parameter is optimally tuned. We then derive scaling laws which describe
               how the test risk of an ensemble of regression models decays with its total size. We
017
               identify conditions on the kernel and task eigenstructure under which ensembles
018
               can achieve near-optimal scaling laws. Training ensembles of deep convolutional
019            neural networks on CIFAR-10 and a transformer architecture on C4, we find that
020            a single large network outperforms any ensemble of networks with the same total
021            number of parameters, provided the weight decay and feature-learning strength
022            are tuned to their optimal values.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Ensembling methods are a well-established tool in machine learning for reducing the variance of
      learned predictors. While traditional ensemble approaches like random forests (Breiman, 2001)
028
      and XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) combine many weak predictors, the advent of deep neural
029
      networks has shifted the state of the art toward training a single large predictor (LeCun et al., 2015).
030   However, deep neural networks still suffer from various sources of variance, such as finite datasets
031   and random initialization (Atanasov et al., 2022; Adlam & Pennington, 2020; Lin & Dobriban, 2021;
032   Atanasov et al., 2024). As a result, deep ensembles—ensembles of deep neural networks—remain
033   a popular method for variance reduction (Ganaie et al., 2022; Fort et al., 2020), and uncertainty
034   estimation (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017).
035
      A critical consideration in practice is the computational cost associated with ensemble methods.
036   While increasing the number of predictors in an ensemble improves its accuracy (provided each
037   ensemble member is “competent” (Theisen et al., 2023)), each additional model incurs significant
038   computational overhead. Supposing a fixed memory capacity for learned parameters, a more prag-
039   matic comparison is between an ensemble of neural networks and a single large network with the
040   same total parameter count. Indeed, recent studies have called into question the utility of an ensem-
041   ble of deep networks relative to a single network of comparable total parameter count (Abe et al.,
042   2022; Vyas et al., 2023).
043   Originally introduced as a fast approximation to Kernel Ridge Regression, random-feature ridge
044   regression (RFRR) Rahimi & Recht (2007) has emerged as a rich “toy model” for deep learning,
045   capturing non-trivial effects of dataset size and network width (Canatar et al., 2021; Atanasov et al.,
046   2023; Mei & Montanari, 2022). While over-fitting effects known as “double-descent” may lead to
047   non-monotonic behavior of the loss in both deep networks and ridge regression (D’Ascoli et al.,
048   2020; Nakkiran, 2019; Nakkiran et al., 2019; Adlam & Pennington, 2020; Lin & Dobriban, 2021),
049   double-descent can be mitigated by optimally tuning the ridge parameter (Nakkiran et al., 2020;
050
      Advani et al., 2020; Canatar et al., 2021; Simon et al., 2023). Specifically, Simon et al. showed
      that in RFRR, test risk decreases monotonically with model size and dataset size when the ridge
051
      parameter is optimally chosen.
052
053   In this present work, we study the tradeoff between the number of predictors and the size of each
      predictor in ensembles of RFRR models. We find that with a fixed total parameter budget, minimal

      error is achieved by a single predictor trained on the full set of features, provided the ridge parameter
055   is optimally tuned. We analyze the trade-off between ensemble size and model size for tasks with a
056   power-law eigenstructure, identifying regimes where near-optimal performance can still be achieved
057   with an ensemble. Finally, we present experiments suggesting that this “no free lunch from ensem-
058   bles” principle extends to deep feature-learning ensembles when network hyperparameters are finely
059   tuned. The remainder of this work is organized as follows:
060
      In section 2 we review the necessary background on the theory and practice of RFRR and its exten-
061   sion to ensembles.
062
063   In section 3, we state an omniscient risk estimate for ensembled RFRR and extend the “bigger is
064
      better” theorem of Simon et al. (2023) to the ensembled case.
065   In Section 4, we prove that, when the total number of features is fixed, the optimal test risk of an
066   ensemble of RFRR models is achieved when the available features are consolidated into a single
067   large model, provided that the ridge parameter is tuned to its optimal value. We confirm these
068   predictions for ReLU RFRR models on binarized CIFAR-10 and MNIST classification tasks.
069   In Section 5, we derive scaling laws for the test risk of an ensemble of RFRR models under source
070   and capacity constraints (Cui et al., 2023; Caponnetto & De Vito, 2006; Bordelon et al., 2020;
071   Defilippis et al., 2024), in the width-bottlenecked regime. We identify regimes where near-optimal
072   performance can be achieved using an ensemble of smaller predictors. The derived scaling laws
073   provide a good description of RFRR on the binarized CIFAR-10 and MNIST classification tasks.
074   In Section 6, we test whether the intuitions provided by random feature models carry over to deep
075   neural networks in computer vision and natural language processing tasks. We find that for deep
076   networks in both the lazy and rich feature-learning regimes, a single large network typically out-
077   performs an ensemble of smaller networks, provided that the weight decay is tuned to its optimal
078   value.

      3.3   M ORE IS B ETTER IN R ANDOM F EATURE E NSEMBLES
217
218   Predictive variance has historically been viewed as a beneficial to ensemble learning. In the case of
219   random forests, for example, subsampling of data dimensions leads to improved performance, de-
220   spite reducing the size of each decision tree (Breiman, 2001). In RFRR ensembles, each ensemble
221   member is distinguished by the particular realization of its random features (i.e. the independently
222   drawn vnk ∼ µv ). As N → ∞, the function learned by each estimator will converge to the same
223   limiting kernel predictor. One might therefore expect reducing the size N of each ensemble mem-
224
      ber to improve ensemble performance by increasing the diversity of the ensemble’s predictors. This,
      however, is not the case as we prove that increasing N is always beneficial to the test risk of a RFRR
225
      ensemble.

429   6   N O F REE L UNCH F ROM F EATURE L EARNING E NSEMBLES
430
431   Here, ask whether the “no free lunch from ensembles” principle proven for RFRR carries over to
      ensembles of deep neural networks. In the lazy training regime, deep neural networks reduce to


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      kernel machines, with random features given by the gradients of the loss at initialization (Chizat
433   et al., 2019). Consequently, random feature models are reliable toy models for lazy training, with
434   the number of random features as a proxy for the number of parameters in the network (Jacot et al.,
435   2018; Lee et al., 2019; Chizat et al., 2019; Bordelon et al., 2020; Canatar et al., 2021). For example,
436   RFRR exhibits overfitting at finite width and sample size Atanasov et al. (2022). Feature learning
437   can, however, complicate the relationship between network size and performance, if the strength of
438   feature-learning depends on network width, as in NTK parameterization (Jacot et al., 2018; Aitchi-
439   son, 2020). To make “fair” comparisons between large models and ensembles of smaller models,
440   we seek instead a parameterization which keeps training dynamics consistent across widths, with
441
      monotonic improvements in performance as width increases.
442   Maximal update parameterization (µP ) (Geiger et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2018; Rotskoff & Vanden-
443   Eijnden, 2022; Yang & Hu, 2021; Bordelon & Pehlevan, 2022) accomplishes this desired width-
444   consistency (Vyas et al., 2023). µP is the unique parameterization in which the network’s infinite
445   width limit converges and permits feature learning in finite time (Yang et al., 2022; Bordelon &
446   Pehlevan, 2022). µP is similar to the NTK parameterization, except we center and scale the output
447
      of the neural network inversely with a richness parameter (Chizat et al., 2019):
                                                          !
448                               1                                    √
449                    f˜(x; θ) =    f (x; θ) − f (x; θ0 ) ,    γ = γ0 N , η = η 0 γ 2             (22)
                                  γ
450
451   so that the richness γ0 and learning rate η0 are constants and γ and η scale with network size (Geiger
452   et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2018; Rotskoff & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Yang & Hu, 2021; Bordelon &
453
      Pehlevan, 2022). At small γ, small changes in the weights are sufficient to interpolate the training
      data, yielding a model well-approximated as a linear model with the kernel given by the NTK at
454
      initialization. This is known as lazy learning. At large γ, large weight updates are necessary to
455
      change the network’s output, and the model learns task-relevant features. This is known as rich
456   learning.
457
458   We train ensembles of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) on the CIFAR-10 image classi-
459
      fication task, sweeping over ensemble size K, richness γ, and weight decay λ. We use a small CNN
      architecture with two CNN layers (figs. 5A, S8), as well as a larger ResNet18 architecture (figs. 5A,
460
      S9). The width of the convolutional and MLP layers are varied with K to keep the total parameter
461
      count fixed (details in Appendix E). In the “lazy” regime (γ ≪ 1), we find that accuracy decreases
462   monotonically with K, provided weight decay is tuned to its optimal value. And, while at some
463   intermediate values of γ error may increase with K, monotonicity is restored when weight decay
464   and richness γ are jointly tuned to their optimal values (figs. 5A,B, S8, S9).
465
      We also test the performance of ensembles of transformers trained on the C4 language modeling
466
      task. We train in the online setting where each sample is used no more than once. No weight decay
467
      is used. In agreement with results from (Vyas et al., 2023), we find that across richness parameters
468   γ, error is monotonically increasing with K (fig. 5B).
469
470   To summarize, our findings suggest that “no free lunch from ensembles” holds for deep ensembles
471
      trained with µP parameterization under any of following conditions:
472
             • In the lazy training regime (γ → 0) when the weight decay is tuned to its optimal value.
473
             • When weight decay and richness γ are jointly tuned to their optimal values.
474
475          • When richness γ is fixed and training is performed online (i.e. without repeating data).

      7   D ISCUSSION
478
479
      An important limitation of our work is the assumption of statistically homogeneous ensembles.
480   We consider each ensemble member to be trained on the same dataset, and to perform the same
481   task. However, successes have been achieved using ensembles with functional specialization, where
482   different sub-networks are trained on different datasets to perform different sub-tasks relevant to
483   the overall goal of the ensemble. For example, mixture of experts (MoE) models (Jacobs et al.,
484   1991; Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022) might offer a way to cleverly scale model size
485   using ensembles that outperforms the scaling laws for single large networks. We leave a theory of
      ensembled regression which allows for functional specialization as an objective for future work.

      Another limitation of our work is the absence of feature-learning in the RFRR toy model, which
506
      prevents a direct application of our theory to deep ensembles in the rich regime. Nevertheless,
507   we find that when deep networks are trained using µP parameterization, the “no free lunch from
508   ensembles” principle holds empirically provided the weight decay and richness parameter γ are
509   tuned to their optimal values. This fact might be proven rigorously by extending a recent analytical
510   model of feature-learning networks to the ensembled case (Bordelon et al., 2024b).
511
      Our study also connects to recent work on scaling laws in deep learning (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoff-
512
      mann et al., 2022; Bordelon et al., 2024a;b), which observe that the test error of neural networks
513   tends to improve predictably as a power-law with the number of parameters and the size of the
514   dataset used during training. With our scaling-law analysis, we extend the power-laws predicted
515   using random-feature models (Bahri et al., 2024; Maloney et al., 2022; Bordelon et al., 2020; 2024a;
516   Defilippis et al., 2024) to the case where model size is scaled up by jointly increasing the ensemble
517   size K and parameters per ensemble member N according to a “growth exponent” ℓ defined in eq.
518   20. While optimal scaling is always achieved by fixing K and scaling up network size, for suffi-
519   ciently easy tasks (r > 1/2), near-optimal scaling laws can be achieved by growing both network
520   size and ensemble size, provided network size grows quickly enough with total parameter count.
521
      Because feature-learning networks can dynamically align their representations to the target func-
      tion Bordelon et al. (2024b), the scaling laws for deep ensembles may be dramatically improved by
522
      feature-learning effects.

526   8   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we analyzed a trade-off between ensemble size and features-per-ensemble-member in
530   the tractable setting of RFRR. We prove a “no free lunch” theorem which states that optimal perfor-
531
      mance is always achieved by allocating all features to a single, large RFRR model, provided that the
      ridge parameter is tuned to its optimal value. A scaling-laws analysis reveals that the sharpness of
532
      this trade-off depends sensitively on the structure of the task. In particular, near-optimal scaling laws
533
      can be achieved by RFRR ensembles, provided the task is sufficiently aligned with the top modes
534   of the limiting kernel eigenspectrum. We confirm that in deep neural network ensembles with fixed
535   total parameter count, increasing ensemble size K leads to worse performance in both a computer
536   vision and language modeling task, provided that both the weight decay and the richness parameters
537   are tuned to their optimal values. In addition to explaining the general trend from massively en-
538   sembled predictors to large models with jointly trained parameters in recent years, our results have
539   practical implications for model design and resource allocation in real-world settings, where model
      size is limited.
