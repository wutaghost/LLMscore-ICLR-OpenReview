011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) emerges as a novel machine learning paradigm, enabling
014            distributed clients to collaboratively train a global model while eliminating lo-
015            cal data transmission. Despite its advantages, FL faces challenges posed by sys-
016
               tem and data heterogeneity. System heterogeneity prevents low-end clients from
               participating in FL with uniform models, while data heterogeneity adversely im-
017
               pacts the learning performance of FL. In this paper, we propose the personal-
018
               ized ATTENtive pruning enabled federateD learnING (ATTENDING) to collec-
019            tively address these heterogeneity challenges. Specifically, we first design an at-
020            tention module incorporating spatial and channel attention to enhance the learn-
021            ing performance on heterogeneous data. Subsequently, we introduce the atten-
022            tive pruning algorithm to generate personalized local models guided by atten-
023            tion scores, aiming to facilitate clients’ participation in FL. Finally, we intro-
024            duce a specific heterogeneous aggregation algorithm integrated with an atten-
025            tion matching mechanism to efficiently aggregate the pruned models. We im-
026            plement ATTENDING with a real FL platform and the evaluation results show
027
               that ATTENDING significantly outperforms the baselines by up to 11.3% and re-
               duces the average model footprints by 32%. Our code is available at: https:
028
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/ATTENDING.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The proliferation of smart devices and intelligent applications has significantly increased the vol-
034
      ume of data generated at the edge of networks. In response to this trend, Federated Learning (FL)
035
      has emerged as a promising paradigm for collaboratively learning from geographically distributed
036   data (Khan et al., 2021; Soltani et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). Compared to
037   traditional centralized machine learning approaches, FL effectively mitigates the systemic privacy
038   risks and avoids prohibitively high costs associated with transmitting raw data. Owing to these ad-
039   vantages, FL has garnered significant attention and has been successfully implemented in numerous
040   intelligent applications (Khan et al., 2021; Soltani et al., 2023).
041
      Despite its advantages, FL still faces two critical challenges: System heterogeneity and Data het-
042   erogeneity (Lim et al., 2020; Khan et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
043   2023a;b; 2024b; Li et al., 2024a). System heterogeneity refers to variations in device capabilities
044   (e.g., CPU state, memory capacity, battery level, etc.) (Alam et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2022b; Li et al.,
045   2024b). In classical FL, the devices (i.e., clients) are required to update local models that share an
046   identical footprint as the global model, leading to the failure of local model updates at clients with
047   weaker capabilities (i.e., low-end clients). Data heterogeneity refers to different distributions and/or
048   amounts of the local data on various clients, i.e., non-Independent and Identically Distributed (non-
049   IID) data (Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024). Clients possessing only a small sample
050
      of data with uncommon labels will gain no benefit from FL since the data on other clients contain
      different distributions. To enhance clarity, we have elaborated on these two challenges by providing
051
      an example FL process in the context of the Internet of Things (IoT) network in Appendix A.
052
053   To mitigate the aforementioned challenges, several pioneering work propose to optimize local up-
      dating procedures to allow low-end clients to participate in FL training (Li et al., 2020; Karimireddy


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020b). Nonetheless, these optimization-based approaches focus on min-
055   imizing computational resource consumption, without considering the conservation of storage re-
056   sources in mobile devices (Sun & Wei, 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023). Therefore, model
057   pruning techniques have recently been employed in FL for shrinking the footprint of models, con-
058   sequently reducing the consumption of both computational and storage resources (Horvath et al.,
059   2021; Wu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022a;b; Yi et al., 2024). Model pruning-based
060   approaches typically prune out unnecessary local model parameters on individual clients, while
061   maintaining a binary mask matrix for each client to indicate the presence of the corresponding pa-
062   rameters. However, existing model pruning-based FL approaches typically aggregate pruned models
063
      through element-wise averaging with the mask matrices. Unfortunately, as demonstrated in (Wang
      et al., 2020a; Jiang et al., 2022a), this strategy often yields detrimental effects on the model accuracy
064
      owing to the presence of permutation invariance in neural networks. Moreover, the computation cost
065
      of this strategy increases rather than decreases (Jiang et al., 2022a). This phenomenon arises from
066   the fact that, within prevailing deep learning frameworks (e.g., PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019)), this
067   strategy still requires the entire model with the pruned parameters (indicated by the mask matrix)
068   set as zero values for gradient computation during the backpropagation.
069
      In our work, we propose ATTENDING, a novel FL approach integrated with personalized attentive
070
      pruning, aiming to reduce resource consumption of the heterogeneous clients meanwhile enhancing
071
      model performance on non-IID data. Additionally, we develop a specialized attention matching
072   mechanism to aggregate the heterogeneous models resulting from personalized attentive pruning.
073   We summarize our contributions as follows:
074
075          • We design a specific attention module to capture non-IID data features and assess the im-
076            portance scores of model parameters on the clients thereby generating personalized com-
077            pact local models for clients.
078          • We propose a specific aggregation algorithm integrated with an attention matching mech-
079            anism, enabling the aggregation of heterogeneous local models without the assistance of
080            binary mask matrices.
081          • We implement and evaluate the proposed ATTENDING with a real FL platform. Exper-
082            imental results on popular neural networks and benchmark datasets demonstrate that it
083            outperforms baselines up to 11.3% while achieving a 32% reduction in model footprint.

085   2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087   Federated Learning. Federated learning enables collaborative training of complex models among
088   distributed clients while keeping the local data on the client (McMahan et al., 2017; Zhou et al.,
089   2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Qiao et al., 2024; Jiang
090   et al., 2024). As a classical FL approach, FedAvg is originally proposed by McMahan et al. (McMa-
091   han et al., 2017) to train and aggregate the local learning models. In each communication round of
092   FedAvg, the local models are trained on the clients and aggregated on the central server. To view
093
      the systems and data heterogeneity in FL, Li et al. (Li et al., 2020) propose FedProx, which can be
      viewed as a generalization and re-parameterization of FedAvg in the non-IID data setting. Wang
094
      et al. (Wang et al., 2020a) demonstrate that the element-wise averaging of weights in FedAvg is
095
      a shortcoming due to the permutation invariance of neural network parameters, and proposes the
096   FedMA to alleviate the detrimental effects caused by the permutation invariance. However, the
097   above studies focus on improving model accuracy in FL or addressing the challenges posed by
098   non-IID data, whereas neglecting the resource consumption of the clients. In contrast to the afore-
099   mentioned studies, our proposed approach leverages the attention mechanism to mitigate the model
100   accuracy degradation caused by non-IID data and reduce resource consumption through attentive
101   model pruning.
102   Model Compression in Federated Learning. In FL, model compression techniques are used to
103   reduce resource consumption by shrinking the footprint of models (Horvath et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
104   2021; Li et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022a;b; Huang et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024). Li et al. (Li et al.,
105   2021) propose the Hermes, which addresses both data heterogeneity and communication efficiency
106   issues in federated learning. Hermes leverages the structured pruning technique to find a smaller
107   sub-model for each client, ensuring a more efficient training and communication process in FL.
      Yi et al. (Yi et al., 2024) propose FedP3, aiming to address model heterogeneity among clients


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      while enhancing the privacy of FL. FedP3 incorporates personalized network pruning techniques to
109   optimize the performance and efficiency of local models. However, these studies necessitate binary
110   mask matrices to indicate the model structure of the pruned local models during aggregation, which
111   has been demonstrated to be adverse to FL (Wang et al., 2020a; Jiang et al., 2022a). Conversely,
112   we propose an attentive model pruning algorithm to reduce the computational and storage overhead,
113   alongside an attention matching-assisted aggregation algorithm to aggregate heterogeneous models.
114   This approach effectively circumvents the requirement of binary mask matrices during aggregation.

      3     D ESIGN OF ATTENDING
117
118
      The attention mechanism has proven effective in centralized deep learning paradigms (Sabour et al.,
119   2017; Li et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2023). However, unlike centralized deep
120   learning, model pruning-enabled FL approaches cannot directly benefit from the attention mecha-
121   nism due to its inherently heterogeneous nature. In FL, model pruning on clients produces a va-
122   riety of local models with different architectures and weights due to the heterogeneity challenges.
123   Traditional element-wise aggregation fails to aggregate heterogeneous models with different archi-
124   tectures. Moreover, the aggregated global model suffers from performance deterioration due to the
125   permutation invariance problem, even if the clients share homogeneous system capability and data
126   distribution. Thus, in this section, we first introduce an dedicated attention module for ATTENDING,
127
      which is a key component to capture features on heterogeneous data and evaluate the importance
      scores of the model parameters. Subsequently, we propose attentive model pruning that leverages
128
      the importance scores generated by the attention module to prune local models, thereby producing
129
      personalized models for heterogeneous clients. To circumvent the adverse effects caused by the use
130   of binary mask matrices during model aggregation, we propose a novel aggregation algorithm that
131   aggregates heterogeneous models without relying on binary mask matrices, thereby mitigating the
132   associated negative impacts.
133

287   3.3       AGGREGATION OF H ETEROGENEOUS M ODELS
288
289   Fig. 3 shows an overview of the aggregation algorithm of ATTENDING in the first communication
290   round of FL. For ease of representation, there are three clients (i.e., Device 1, Device 2, and De-
291
      vice 3) with different system capabilities participating in the FL process, and only one target layer
      containing 5 channels in the local model for each client.
292
293   Firstly, the clients download a original global model from the server (Step ❶) and update the weights
294   of the local models with attention modules (Step ❷). Then they conduct personalized model pruning
295   with different prune ratios via Algorithm 1. The personalized model pruning procedure on different
296   clients will generate heterogeneous local models due to the various system capabilities.
297   Then, the local models are sent to the central server for aggregation (Step ❸). Due to the inherent
298   heterogeneity among these local models, direct weighted averaging of these local models leads to
299   a significant degradation in overall model performance. Inspired by the findings in existing work
300   on model interpretability (Sabour et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019), channels with high scores play a
301   crucial role in capturing specific semantic responses while those with low scores are susceptible to
302   inherent noise and the presence of similar patterns. Thus we design a simple yet efficient Attention
303
      Matching mechanism to aggregate channels according to their attention scores, thereby enhancing
      the performance of the aggregated global model (Step ❹). Specifically, for each target layer, the
304
      attention matching mechanism reorganizes the channels, ensuring that the indexes of channels with
305
      small attention scores are positioned before those with larger attention scores. This arrangement is
306   calculated as follows:
307                                   Index(t) < Index(q), ∀mt ≤ mq ,                                  (8)
308
      where the Index function denotes the position of the respective channel within the target layer, t
309
      and q indicate different channels in the target layer with attention scores mt and mq , respectively.
310
      In other words, the attention matching mechanism rearranges the channels within the target layer
311   based on their attention scores, and therefore reduces the effects of permutation invariance.
312
313   Following that, the server reconstructs the structure of the pruned models using the original global
314
      model, ensuring that the local models align precisely with the structure of the global model (Step ❺).
      This alignment facilitates the application of weighted averaging to aggregate the local models.
315
316   Finally, the server aggregates recovered models through weighted averaging (Step ❻), and subse-
317   quently generates a personalized local model for each client (Step ❼). To elaborate, for each client
318   c, the server prunes the last pc fraction of rearranged channels within the target layers of the aggre-
319   gated model while retaining the remainder. The detailed aggregation algorithm for heterogeneous
320
      local models is provided in Appendix C.
321   In subsequent communication rounds, only the pruned local models are transmitted between the
322   central server and the clients. The clients exclusively update the weights of the pruned models
323   and send them directly to the central server without further pruning. Additionally, the central server
      abstains from executing attention matching as the order of the channels has already been established.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
476
      In this paper, we have proposed a novel FL approach ATTENDING, enabling heterogeneous clients
477
      to participate in FL with personalized local models. We have introduced an attentive training and
478   pruning algorithm for FL environments characterized by system and data heterogeneity. This algo-
479   rithm aims to generate tailored local models while enhancing learning performance. To aggregate
480   the local models, we have proposed a specific aggregation algorithm integrated with attention match-
481   ing to address the model heterogeneity issue. We have implemented ATTENDING with a real FL
482   platform and evaluated its performance. The experimental results on three benchmark datasets have
483   demonstrated that ATTENDING significantly outperforms SOTA methods. Our future research en-
484   deavors will focus on exploring the implementation and extension of ATTENDING across various
485   types of neural networks and multi-modality tasks, thereby enhancing its overall applicability.

722   System Heterogeneity. In FL, the architectures of the local models on each client are usually the
723   same as the global model, and system heterogeneity leads to the weaker clients’ failure of local
724   model updates within the maximum allowed time. For example, Fig. 7 describes a round of FL
725   processing in an Internet of Things (IoT) network with three mobile devices. Device 1 encounters
726   a failure in updating the model due to its stringent resource constraints. Consequently, the central
727   server disregards the model information intended for updating by Device 1 and only aggregates the
728   models updated from Device 2 and Device 3. This failure not only leads to the exclusion of Device 1
729   from the FL process but also hinders the central server from leveraging the data features extracted
730
      on Device 1.
731   In our work, the proposed approach addresses the system heterogeneity problem through a novel
732   attentive pruning method. With attention-based model pruning, heterogeneous clients are able to
733   update personalized local models with appropriate footprints, thus enabling all clients to participate
734   in the FL process.
735   Data Heterogeneity. Different distributions of the heterogeneous local data pose another challenge
736   on FL (Lim et al., 2020). Although the widely used FL approaches such as FedAvg (McMahan
737   et al., 2017) can be applied on both IID and non-IID settings, the current work (Zhao et al., 2018;
738   Li et al., 2021) demonstrates that FedAvg could be unstable or even diverge in the non-IID setting.
739   According to (Zhao et al., 2018), the performance of a global model trained by the FedAvg (McMa-
740   han et al., 2017) has a 51% lower accuracy than a centrally-trained local model on the CIFAR10
741   dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). For example, as shown in Fig. 7, the data distributions between
742
      Device 2 and Device 3 are distinct, resulting in diverse updated local models. Assuming that De-
      vice 2 possesses a larger number of data samples, the aggregated global model tends to be biased
743
      toward Device 2, thereby limiting the benefits attainable by Device 3.
744
745   In our work, the proposed approach utilizes the attention-based training technique to preserve the
746   personality of local models and leverages attention matching to aggregate local models, thereby
747   enhancing the model performance on non-IID data. Meanwhile, the proposed approach eliminates
748
      the need for binary mask matrices, thereby mitigating potential computation costs and preventing
      model accuracy deterioration.

898   effectively mitigates the computational burden associated with local training and the corresponding

900   of the least capable clients, denoted as C1 .
901   FedProx (Li et al., 2020): FedProx introduces a proximal term into the objective function, aiming to
902   encourage clients to maintain similarity with the global model during local training, thereby allowing
903   low-end clients to execute fewer local updates.
904
      FedNova (Wang et al., 2020b): FedNova adopts dynamic learning rate adjustment and adaptive
905   aggregation mechanisms to handle the heterogeneity among participants. FedNova adjusts each
906   participant’s influence on the model by using their contribution.

      Hermes (Li et al., 2021): Hermes adopts structured pruning to find personalized sub-models for
      clients. The clients are responsible for updating the personalized sub-models, which are subse-
909
      quently transmitted to the server for intersection-based averaging.
910
911   FedMP (Jiang et al., 2022b): FedMP prunes local models adaptively in each FL communication
912   round to satisfy the heterogeneous resource limitations of clients. Meanwhile, it uses a R2SP scheme
913   to aggregate heterogeneous models in different clients.
914   FedGH (Yi et al., 2023): FedGH enables the extraction of representations from local data by het-
915   erogeneous sub-models on clients. To facilitate knowledge transfer among clients, FedGH employs
916   a shareable global header optimized using these representations to make predictions. We categorize
917   FedGH as a method related to model pruning in our work as it utilizes sub-models with reduced
      width as heterogeneous local representation extractors.

958
959   Given a pruning ratio p for model pruning, we employed the constant pruning ratio p for each target
960   layer l ∈ L in the 2NN model. For the ResNet56 model, we used diverse pruning ratios pl for target
961   layers while ensuring that the pruning ratio p for the entire model remained unchanged.
962   As discussed in (Yosinski et al., 2014; Donahue et al., 2014), the early layers of a neural network
963   primarily contribute to its generality, while the later layers tend to dominate specificity. In the con-
964   text of FL environments, the generality of local models on clients often carries greater significance
965   than their specificity due to the distributed nature of FL. Specifically, each client in an FL envi-
966   ronment must collaborate with other clients to optimize a global model, making local models with
967   greater generality crucial for enhancing the performance of the global model. Additionally, FL often
968
      encounters performance degradation especially in non-IID settings, primarily because local models
      are trained specifically on their own local data and struggle to generalize to data from other clients.
969
970   Thus, we set small pruning ratios for the early layers of ResNet56, and large pruning ratios for its
971   later layers to improve its generality. In order to reduce complexity, we establish distinct pruning
