011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning useful representations for continuous-time dynamic graphs (CTDGs) is
014            challenging, due to the concurrent need to span long node interaction histories
015            and grasp nuanced temporal details. In particular, two problems emerge: (1) En-
016            coding longer histories requires more computational resources, making it crucial
017            for CTDG models to maintain low computational complexity to ensure efficiency;
018
               (2) Meanwhile, more powerful models are needed to identify and select the most
               critical temporal information within the extended context provided by longer his-
019
               tories. To address these problems, we propose a CTDG representation learning
020
               model named DyGMamba, originating from the popular Mamba state space model
021            (SSM). DyGMamba first leverages a node-level SSM to encode the sequence of
022            historical node interactions. Another time-level SSM is then employed to exploit
023            the temporal patterns hidden in the historical graph, where its output is used to dy-
024            namically select the critical information from the interaction history. We validate
025            DyGMamba experimentally on the dynamic link prediction task. The results show
026            that our model achieves state-of-the-art in most cases. DyGMamba also maintains
027            high efficiency in terms of computational resources, making it possible to capture
028            long temporal dependencies with a limited computation budget.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Dynamic graphs store node interactions in the form of links labeled with timestamps (Kazemi et al.,
034   2020). In recent years, learning dynamic graphs has gained increasing interest since it can be used
035   to facilitate various real-world applications. Dynamic graphs can be classified into two types, i.e.,
036   discrete-time dynamic graph (DTDG) and continuous-time dynamic graph (CTDG). A DTDG is
037   represented as a sequence of graph snapshots that are observed at regular time intervals, where all
038   the edges in a snapshot are taken as existing simultaneously, while a CTDG consists of a stream of
039
      events where each of them is observed individually with its own timestamp. Previous work (Kazemi
      et al., 2020) has indicated that CTDGs have an advantage over DTDGs in preserving temporal
040
      details, and therefore, more attention is paid to developing novel CTDG modeling approaches for
041
      dynamic graph representation learning.
042
043   Recent effort in CTDG modeling has resulted in a wide range of models. However, most of them are
044   unable to model long-term temporal dependencies of nodes. To solve this problem, Yu et al. (2023)
045
      propose a CTDG model DyGFormer that can handle long-term node interaction histories based on
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). Despite its ability in modeling longer histories, employing a
046
      Transformer naturally introduces excessive usage of computational resources due to its quadratic
047
      complexity. Another recent work CTAN (Gravina et al., 2024) tries to capture long-term temporal
048   dependencies by propagating graph information in a non-dissipative way over time with a graph
049   convolution-based model. Despite the model’s high efficiency, Gravina et al. (2024) show that CTAN
050   cannot capture very long histories and is surpassed by DyGFormer on the CTDGs where learning
051   from very far away temporal information is critical. Based on these observations, we summarize
052   the first challenge in CTDG modeling: How to develop a model that is scalable in modeling very
053   long-term historical interactions? Another point worth noting is that as longer histories introduce
      more temporal information, more powerful models are needed to identify and select the most critical

054
      parts. This reveals another challenge: How to effectively select critical temporal information with
055   long node interaction histories?
056
057   To address the first challenge, we propose to leverage a popular state space model (SSM), i.e.,
058
      Mamba SSM (Gu & Dao, 2023) to encode the long sequence of historical node interactions. Since
      Mamba is proven effective and efficient in long sequence modeling (Gu & Dao, 2023), it maintains
059
      low computational complexity and is scalable in modeling long-term temporal dependencies. For the
060
      second challenge, we address it by learning temporal patterns of node interactions and dynamically
061   selecting the critical temporal information based on them. The motivation can be explained by the
062   following example. Consider a CTDG with nodes as people or songs and edges representing a
063   person playing a song at a specific time. If a person u frequently plays a hit song v initially but
064   decreases the frequency later on, the time intervals between plays increase. Ignoring this pattern
065   can lead models to incorrectly predict that u will still play v at future timestamps due to their high
066   appearances in each other’s historical interactions. If a CTDG model recognizes this pattern, it can
067   prioritize other temporal information, such as u increasingly listening to a new song v ′ before t,
068   instead of focusing on u, v interactions. Since each pattern corresponds to a specific edge, e.g.,
069
      (u, v, t), we name these patterns as edge-specific temporal patterns.
070   To this end, we propose a new CTDG model named DyGMamba. DyGMamba first leverages a node-
071   level Mamba SSM to encode historical node interactions. Another time-level Mamba SSM is then
072   employed to exploit the edge-specific temporal patterns, where its output is used to dynamically se-
073   lect the critical information from the interaction history. To summarize: (1) We present DyGMamba,
074   the first model using SSMs for CTDG representation learning; (2) DyGMamba demonstrates high
075
      efficiency and strong efectiveness in modeling long-term temporal dependencies in CTDGs; (3) Ex-
      perimental results show that DyGMamba achieves new state-of-the-art on dynamic link prediction
076
      over most commonly-used CTDG datasets.

081   2.1   R ELATED W ORK
082
083   Dynamic Graph Representation Learning. Dynamic graph representation learning methods can
084   be categorized into two groups, i.e., DTDG and CTDG methods. DTDG methods (Pareja et al.,
085
      2020; Goyal et al., 2020; Sankar et al., 2020; You et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a) can only model
      DTDGs where each of them is represented as a sequence of graph snapshots. Modeling a dynamic
086
      graph as graph snapshots requires time discretization and will inevitably cause information loss
087
      (Kazemi et al., 2020). To overcome this problem, recent works focus more on developing CTDG
088   methods that treat a dynamic graph as a stream of events, where each event has its own unique
089   timestamp. Some works (Trivedi et al., 2019; Chang et al., 2020) model CTDGs by using temporal
090   point process. Another line of works (Xu et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021b; Gravina
091   et al., 2024) designs advanced temporal graph neural networks for CTDGs. Besides, some other
092   methods are developed based on memory networks (Rossi et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022), temporal
093   random walk (Wang et al., 2021c; Jin et al., 2022) and temporal sequence modeling (Cong et al.,
094   2023; Yu et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024). Since some real-world CTDGs heavily rely on long-term
095   temporal information for effective learning, a number of works start to develop CTDG models that
096
      can do long range propagation of information over time (Yu et al., 2023; Gravina et al., 2024).
097
098   State Space Models. Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) is a de facto backbone architecture in
099
      modern deep learning. However, its self-attention mechanism results in large space and time com-
      plexity, making it unsuitable for extremely long sequence modeling (Duman Keles et al., 2023).
100
      To address this, many works focus on building structured state space models that scale linearly or
101
      near-linearly with input sequence length (Gu et al., 2021; 2022b;a; Smith et al., 2023; Peng et al.,
102   2023; Ma et al., 2023; Gu & Dao, 2023). Most structured SSMs exhibit linear time invariance (LTI),
103   meaning their parameters are not input-dependent and fixed for all time-steps. Gu & Dao (2023)
104   demonstrate that LTI prevents SSMs from effectively selecting relevant information from the input
105   context, which is problematic for tasks requiring context-aware reasoning. To solve this issue, Gu
106   & Dao (2023) proposes S6, also known as Mamba, which uses a selection mechanism to dynam-
107   ically choose important information from input sequence elements. Selection mechanism involves
      learning functions that map input data to SSM’s parameters, making Mamba both efficient and ef-

108
      fective in modeling language, DNA sequences, and audio. Recently, there have been several works
109
      employing Mamba SSM for representation learning on static graphs (Wang et al., 2024; Behrouz &
110
      Hashemi, 2024) and spatial-temporal graphs (Li et al., 2024b). Unlike our work, they do not focus
111
      on capturing the dynamic evolution of graph structure and are thus not suitable for CTDG modeling.

400   Ablation Study. We conduct four ablation studies to study the effectiveness of model components.
401   In study A, we make a model variant (Variant A) by removing the time-level SSM block and restrain
402   our model from learning temporal patterns (information selection is substituted by mean pooling
403   over the output of Eq. 4). In study B, we make a model variant (Variant B) by removing the
404   Mamba SSM layers (Eq. 3) in the node-level SSM block. In study C, we switch the computation
405   of the selection weights βθ in Eq. 6d to βθ = Softmax(fsel (Htθ )) (fsel (·) : R4d → R4d ) to create
406   Variant C. In study D, we base on Variant C and develop Variant D that further enables information
407   selection from opposite nodes, i.e., βu = Softmax(fsel (Htv ))/ βv = Softmax(fsel (Htu )). Both abla-
408   tion C and D do information selection without learning temporal patterns. From Table 3 and 4, we
409   find that: (1) Variant A is constantly beaten by DyGMamba, showing the effectiveness of dynamic
410   information selection based on edge-specific temporal patterns; (2) DyGMamba always outperforms
411   Variant B, indicating the importance of encoding the one-hop temporal neighbors with SSM layers
412   for capturing graph dynamics; (3) Variant C and D perform better than Variant A in most cases,
413   implying that selecting temporal information is generally contributive; (4) Variant C generally lags
414   behind Variant D, meaning that information selection from opposite node is beneficial; (5) DyG-
415   Mamba performs better than both Variant C and D in almost all cases, proving that information
416   selection based on temporal patterns is more effective.

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose DyGMamba, an efficient CTDG representation learning model that can capture long-
534   term temporal dependencies. DyGMamba first leverages a node-level SSM to encode long se-
535   quences of historical node interactions. It then employs a time-level SSM to learn edge-specific
536   temporal patterns. The learned patterns are used to select the critical part of the encoded temporal
537   information. DyGMamba achieves superior performance on dynamic link prediction, and moreover,
538   it shows high efficiency and strong scalability compared with previous CTDG methods, implying
539   a great potential in modeling huge amounts of temporal information with a limited computational
      budget. To supplement, we discuss the limitation of our work in App. K.

825   B     BASELINE D ETAILS
826
827   We provide the detailed descriptions of all baselines here. The baselines can be split into two groups:
828   the methods designed/not designed for long-range temporal information propagation.
829
830   B.1    BASELINES N OT D ESIGNED FOR L ONG -R ANGE T EMPORAL I NFORMATION
831          P ROPAGATION
832
833
             • JODIE (Kumar et al., 2019): JODIE employs a recurrent neural network (RNN) for each
               node and uses a projection operation to learn the future representation trajectory of each
834
               node.
835
836
             • DyRep (Trivedi et al., 2019): DyRep updates node representations as events appear. It
               designs a two-time scale deep temporal point process approach for source and destination
837
               nodes and couples the structural and temporal components with a temporal-attentive aggre-
838
               gation module.
839
840
             • TGAT (Xu et al., 2020): TGAT computes the node representations by aggregating each
               node’s temporal neighbors based on a self-attention module. A time encoding function is
841
               proposed to learn functional representations of time.
842
843          • TGN (Rossi et al., 2020): TGN leverages an evolving memory for each node and updates
844
               the memory when a node-relevant interaction occurs by using a message function, a mes-
               sage aggregator, and a memory updater. An embedding module is used to generate the
845
               temporal representations of nodes.
846
847
             • CAWN (Wang et al., 2021c): CAWN is a random walk-based method. It does multiple
               causal anonymous walks for each node and extracts relative node identities from the walk
848
               results. RNNs are then introduced to encode the anonymous walks. The aggregated walk
849
               information forms the final node representation.
850
851
             • EdgeBank (Poursafaei et al., 2022): EdgeBank is a non-parametric method purely based on
               memory. It stores the observed interactions in its memory and updates the memory through
852
               various strategies. An interaction, i.e., link, will be predicted as existing if it is stored in
853
               the memory, and non-existing otherwise. EdgeBank uses four memory update strategies:
854            (1) EdgeBank∞ , where all the observed edges are stored in the memory; (2) EdgeBanktw-ts ,
855            where only the edges within the duration of the test set from the immediate past are kept in
856            the memory; (3) EdgeBanktw-re , where only the edges within the average time intervals of
857            repeated edges from the immediate past are kept in the memory; (4) EdgeBankth , where the
858            edges with appearing counts higher than a threshold are stored in the memory. The results
859            reported in our paper correspond to the best results achieved among the four memory update
860            strategies.
861          • TCL (Wang et al., 2021a): TCL first extracts temporal dependency interaction sub-graphs
862            for source and interaction nodes and then presents a graph transformer to aggregate node
863            information from the sub-graphs. A cross-attention operation is implemented to enable
               information communication between two source and destination nodes.


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
                • GraphMixer (Cong et al., 2023): GraphMixer designs a link-encoder based on MLP-
865               Mixer (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) to learn from the temporal interactions. A mean pooling-
866               based node-encoder is used to aggregate the node features. Link prediction is done with a
867               link classifier that leverages the representations output by link-encoder and node-encoder.
868
869   Note that TGN uses a memory network to store the whole graph history, making it able to preserve
870   long-range temporal information. However, as discussed in Yu et al. (2023), it faces a problem of
871   vanishing/exploding gradients, preventing it from optimally capturing long-term temporal depen-
872   dencies. EdgeBank can also preserve a very long graph history, but we can observe from the experi-
873
      mental results (Table 1, 12) that without learnable parameters, it is not strong enough on long-range
      temporal dependent datasets.
874
875
      B.2       BASELINES D ESIGNED FOR L ONG -R ANGE T EMPORAL I NFORMATION P ROPAGATION
876
877             • DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023): DyGFormer is a Transformer-based CTDG model. It takes
878               the long-term one-hop temporal interactions of source and destination nodes and uses a
879               Transformer to encode them. A patching technique is developed to cut the computational
880               consumption and a node co-occurrence encoding scheme is used to exploit the correlations
881
                  of nodes in each interaction. DyGFormer achieves long-range temporal information prop-
                  agation by increasing the number of sampled one-hop historical interactions. The patching
882
                  technique ensures that even with a huge number of sampled interactions, the length of the
883
                  sequence input into Transformer will not be too long, making it possible to implement
884               DyGFormer with a limited computational budget.
885
                • CTAN (Gravina et al., 2024): CTAN is deep graph network for learning CTDGs based on
886
                  non-dissipative ordinary differential equations. CTAN’s formulation allows for a scalable
887
                  long-range temporal information propagation in CTDGs because its non-dissipative layer
888               can retain the information from a specific event indefinitely, ensuring that the historical
889               context of a node is preserved despite the occurrence of additional events involving this
890               node.

      We train every CTDG model except for CTAN for a maximum number of 200 epochs. Maximum
895
      epochs for CTAN training is 1000. We evaluate each model on the validation set at the end of
896
      every training epoch and adopt an early stopping strategy with a patience of 20. We take the model
897   that achieves the best validation result for testing. We use the implementations5 provided by Yu
898   et al. (2023) for all baseline models except CTAN. For CTAN, we use its official implementation6 .

900   implemented with PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) on a server equipped with an AMD EPYC 7513

       H    D ETAILS OF S4 AND M AMBA O PERATIONS
1145
1146
       Single-Input Single-Output. Given a sequence of vector elements as input, SISO means that the
1147
       SSM processes each input dimension in parallel with the same set of parameters. For example, a
1148   sequence of d2 -dimensional vectors will be split into d2 1-dimensional sequences with the same
1149   sequence length. Each of them will be computed in parallel as in Eq. 2 with a shared set of SSM
1150   parameters. After computation, all these d2 sequences will be rearranged back into a sequence of d2 -
1151   dimensional vectors. SISO fails to mix the information across dimensions of each vector. To address
1152   this, S4 and Mamba employs a mixing linear layer fmix (·) : Rd2 → Rd2 on each d2 -dimensional
1153   vector to mix the information across d2 dimensions. For more details, please refer to (Gu et al.,
1154   2022b), (Smith et al., 2023) and (Gu & Dao, 2023).
1155
1156   SSM Function. SSMĀ,B̄,C (·) takes a matrix as input. The input matrix can be considered as a
1157   sequence of vector elements, where each row of the matrix corresponds to an element. The output of
1158   SSMĀ,B̄,C (·) is also a matrix, where each row of the output matrix is the output of its corresponding
1159   input vector element. SSMĀ,B̄,C (·) can be viewed as using S4 or Mamba to process a sequence of
1160   vectors in the SISO fashion, based on their parameters Ā, B̄, C.
1161

1163   I   M OTIVATION OF U SING SSM FOR T EMPORAL PATTERN M ODELING
1164
1165   The biggest motivation of using SSM for temporal pattern modeling is that it helps to maintain good
1166   efficiency. if in the future we want to deploy DyGMamba on larger datasets that really require much
1167   longer historical histories for modeling, the value of k will also increase accordingly and the time
1168   difference sequence will not be short anymore. Besides, as we have chosen SSM to model historical
1169   one-hop temporal neighbors in the node-level SSM, it is natural to employ another SSM for temporal
1170   pattern modeling.

       One limitation of our work is that DyGMamba is designed to only model CTDGs. DTDGs are rep-
1222
       resented as a sequence of graph snapshots, where all the edges in a snapshot are taken as existing

1227

1228
