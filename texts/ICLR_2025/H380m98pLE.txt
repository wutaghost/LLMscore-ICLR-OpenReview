                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Gaussian Process regression is a popular method for nonparametric, probabilis-
012            tic modelling. One of its main attractions is also, in some contexts, a significant
013            challenge; namely its high flexibility. This flexibility can be reduced by impos-
014            ing constraints on the GP prior or posterior, something that there is a large and
015            growing body of literature on. In this paper, we present a generalisation of virtual
016            point methods and a framework for enforcing a broad range of constraints in GP
017            posteriors. The method involves designing a quasi-likelihood function which en-
018            codes a relaxed form of the constraints, and then conditioning the unconstrained
019            GP posterior on this quasi-likelihood. The method leverages ideas from existing
020
               methods for constrained GP regression, namely Riihimäki and Vehtari (2010) and
               Hansen et al. (2024), and expands these approaches to a much broader range of
021
               constraints. The method is demonstrated with a synthetic example, where a 2-
022
               dimensional GP posterior is required to have a divergence-free gradient, as well
023            as real-world example where the posterior GP of Thomson scattering data from
024            the MAST tokamak is required to be both monotonically decreasing and strictly
025            positive.
026

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Gaussian Process (GP) regression is an increasingly popular method for nonparametric, probabilistic
031   modelling. It is an attractive method for several reasons, including its theoretical simplicity, its
032   high flexibility, its high degree of explainability, and its robustness to noisy and uncertain data.
033
      Conversely, its drawbacks include its high computational complexity of O(n3 ), space complexity
      of O(n2 ), the challenge of optimising the hyperparameters. As it turns out, ironically, one of its
034
      greatest advantages is also in some cases a significant challenge, namely its high flexibility.
035
036   A Gaussian Process is completely specified by its mean m(x) and covariance function k(x, x′ )
037   (Rasmussen and Williams, 2006) for x, x′ ∈ X . If we for a moment disregard the mean function,
038   which is often simply set to zero anyway, the only constraints imposed on the GP prior are those
039
      induced by the covariance function. Often these take the form of length scale hyperparameters,
      which broadly control the smoothness of the permissible functions. While this may appear to be a
040
      largely technical fact, it has important implications for the outcomes of GP regression. As such, the
041
      covariance function provides a set of relative constraints (point-wise function values are constrained
042   only relative to other function values), rather than absolute ones. In this context, an “absolute”
043   constraint is taken to mean a constraint pertaining to the function values themselves (and/or their
044   derivatives), such as fixed values, boundedness, monotonicity, etc. It is such “absolute” constraints
045   that we are addressing in this paper, since they provide a means for reducing the flexibility of a GP
046   by tailoring it to a specific application.
047
      In practice, the high flexibility of the GP prior can sometimes lead to underconfident predictions,
048   particularly in data-scarce regimes, where there isn’t sufficient data to implicitly propagate rele-
049   vant constraints to the GP posterior. Additionally, unconstrained GPs may also sometimes produce
050   predictions that are entirely unphysical. For this reason, significant effort has been invested in de-
051   veloping methods for explicitly imposing constraints on GPs. These methods broadly fall into two
052   categories; those that modify the prior GP such that the constraints are somewhat automatically en-
053   forced – and those that use additional virtual observations to manually incorporate the constraints
      in the GP posterior. Imposing constraints by modifying the prior can be done by either warping

      the latent GP to conform to the constraints (Jensen et al., 2013), designing a bespoke covariance
055   kernel (see e.g. Wahlström, 2015; Jidling et al., 2017) – something that is only possible for a small
056   subset of constraints – or by fitting the data in a constrained (finite) basis, which effectively leads to
057   a prior that is not a Gaussian Process (see e.g. López-Lopera et al., 2018; Maatouk and Bay, 2017).
058   However, in this work we are mainly concerned with constraining GPs using virtual observations.
059
      More specifically, boundedness can be imposed through a variety of different approaches, such as
060
      using warping functions or bounded likelihood functions as explored by Jensen et al. (2013), virtual
061   observations as described in Da Veiga and Marrel (2012), or with an anamorphosis, which is a com-
062   mon approach in climatology literature (Berrocal et al., 2008; Chilès and Delfiner, 2012; Kleiber
063   et al., 2012; 2023). Monotonicity can be enforced by conditioning the GP on virtual observations of
064   the function derivatives under a probit likelihood (Riihimäki and Vehtari, 2010), or by projecting the
065   data onto a basis of monotonic functions and then fit surrogates to the coefficients of the projected
066   data (Pepper et al., 2023). In a similar avenue of enquiry, Maatouk and Bay (2017) outline a frame-
067   work for enforcing either boundedness or monotonicity using a spline basis. Recently, Hansen et al.
068   (2024) developed a method for softly enforcing physical conservation laws, from which the method
069
      presented here borrows some ideas. Section 2.2 contains a more detailed overview of selected related
      work.
070
071   In this paper, we present a novel methodology which can be seen as a generalisation of virtual point
072   methods or, alternatively, as a relaxed form of rejection sampling. It utilises virtual measurements
073   in conjunction with a quasi-likelihood function which encodes the relevant constraints. It can be
074   used to enforce a broad range of constraints, including fixed values, boundedness, monotonicity,
075
      integral conservation laws, and incompressibility. We demonstrate the use of the method with a
      synthetic example where we require the gradient of a 2D surface to be divergence-free, and a real-
076
      world example of Thomson scattering data from the field of magnetic confinement fusion, where the
077
      data-generating process is expected to be both monotonically decreasing and strictly positive.

080   2     M ETHODS
081
082   In this section, we present the core idea of the proposed methodology. We first briefly recollect
083   the fundamentals of GP modelling in a Bayesian context, including noise heteroskedasticity. We
084   then review some existing methods related to the one presented in this work. Finally, we introduce
085   the proposed quasi-likelihood constraint relaxation Gaussian Process (QLCR-GP) methodology, and
086   give some examples of how to implement common constraints within this framework.

      2.2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Constrained GPs is a highly active field of research. Here we review some existing methods re-
111   lated to the one developed here, including fixed value constraints, monotonicity constraints and
112   conservation-law constraints. The method presented here belongs in the family of virtual point
113   methods, rather than methods that modify the prior as seen in e.g. Maatouk and Bay (2017); López-
114   Lopera et al. (2018); Wahlström (2015); Jidling et al. (2017). The following review of related work
115   is therefore mainly concerned with virtual point methods. For a more comprehensive review of
116
      constrained GP regression, please refer to Swiler et al. (2020).
117
      2.2.1    F IXED VALUE C ONSTRAINTS
118
119   Fixed value constraints, such as fixed boundary conditions in the context of PDE modelling, can be
120   enforced by placing noise-free virtual observations at the appropriate points. While the observations
121   {yi }N
           i=1 are usually thought of as measurements, they could also be thought of as observations in
122   the broader sense, namely representative of something that is known. Hence, the authentic measure-
123   ments D can be extended with noise-free virtual measurements D̃ = {x̃i , ỹi }M   i=1 representative of
124   the fixed-value constraints to yield an augmented dataset D′ = D ∪ D̃. The GP is then simply condi-
125   tioned on the augmented dataset D′ to enforce the constraints. This approach is exact for point-wise
126   fixed value constraints, and can be used to enforce e.g. fixed boundary values for one-dimensional
127   differential equations. However, it is not possible to enforce constraints exactly other than point-
128   wise, and constraints that exist on e.g. a surface can only be enforced approximately by placing
129
      sufficiently many virtual points on that surface. Moreover, the required number of points suffers
      from the curse of dimensionality and will increase exponentially with the input dimension dim(X ).
130
      Since GP posteriors are, in a sense, strongly coupled with their training data, this reliance on virtual
131
      datapoints is a recurring theme in constrained GP regression.

      3.2                           D IAGNOSTIC A NALYSIS IN F USION P LASMA
325
326   In fusion devices, fast and accurate measurements of plasma parameters are of critical importance.
327   From the perspective plasma physics, accurate and trustworthy measurements from experiments can
328   further the field’s understanding of fundamental principles. Similarly, for the practical operation of
329   a tokamak fusion power-plant, high quality diagnostics can significantly enhance plasma control. A
330   diagnostic critical to both operations and scientific understanding in fusion is Thomson scattering
331   (Evans and Katzenstein, 1969). Thomson scattering is a plasma diagnostic that directs a laser beam
332
      at a plasma, and measures the light scattered off free electrons in the plasma. The count of scattered
      photons can then be be used to infer the plasma density.
333
334   Regression of Thomson scattering data from tokamaks is typically executed using a modified hy-
335   perbolic tangent (tanh) basis function (Scannell et al., 2006). Though this method allows for the
336   extraction and interpretation of parameters such as the pedestal width and height, it can often by
337   overly prescriptive, inflexible, and, crucially, does not produce profile uncertainties. Hence, recent
338
      studies have considered GP regression to fit the scattering data (Chilenski et al., 2015; Kwak et al.,
      2020). However, the unconstrained GPs employed in these studies are not ideal for Thomson scat-
339
      tering data, which has several underlying constraints imposed by the specific physics. Specifically,
340
      the electron temperature Te and density ρe cannot be negative

      In this study we considered Thomson scattering data from the Mega Ampere Spherical Tokamak

      As shown in Figure 3, the constrained GPs show improved performance compared to unconstrained
384
      GPs for both H and L-mode profiles. Whereas the unconstrained GPs fit undulating curves that
385   dip towards the centre of the machine, the constrained GPs show the physically expected profiles,
386   which are monotonic and flat towards the core. In addition, extrapolation at the edge is both highly
387   uncertain and negative with unconstrained GPs. The physics constrained GPs on the other hand,
388   show highly certain extrapolated profiles that tail off to 0, as is physically expected.

      4   D ISCUSSION
391
392
      In this paper we have presented Quasi-Likelihood Constraint Relaxation (QLCR-GP), a generalisa-
393   tion of virtual point methods for constrained GP regression. It allows for imposing a broad range
394   of constraints on a GP posterior using virtual measurements of some functional of the GP latent
395   function, which encodes the constraint. A relaxation parameter which is effectively the variance of
396   the constraint residual ν 2 , can be viewed as an implausibility measure of the constraint, with which
397   the modeller can encode their belief in the constraint, and tune its importance. We have presented
398   a selection of particular constraints that can be encoded using the approach outlined herein, but any
399   constraint that can be written in the form of Eq. 5 can in principle be handled using the QLCR-GP
400   formalism. This makes the approach a highly flexible one, which can be adapted to a broad range of
401
      different regression tasks. However, it does a significant drawback, namely that constraints can only
      be enforced point-wise (and not globally), which is subject to the curse of dimensionality. We have
402
      demonstrated QLCR-GP using two different examples, one of a synthetic function that is required
403
      to have a divergence free gradient and one of Thomson scattering data from the MAST tokamak,
404   where the QLCR-GP is required to be both monotonically increasing and strictly positive.
