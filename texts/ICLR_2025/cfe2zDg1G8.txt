009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Multi Scenario Recommendation (MSR) tasks, referring to building a unified
012                model to enhance performance across all recommendation scenarios, have re-
013                cently gained much attention. However, current research in MSR faces two sig-
014                nificant challenges that hinder the field’s development: the absence of uniform
015                procedures for multi-scenario dataset processing, thus hindering fair comparisons,
016                and most models being closed-sourced, which complicates comparisons with cur-
017                rent SOTA models. Consequently, we introduce our benchmark, Scenario-Wise
018
                   Rec, which comprises 6 public datasets and 12 benchmark models, along with a
                   training and evaluation pipeline. Additionally, we validated the benchmark us-
019
                   ing an industrial advertising dataset, reinforcing its reliability and applicability in
020
                   real-world scenarios. We aim for this benchmark to offer researchers valuable in-
021                sights from prior work, enabling the development of novel models based on our
022                benchmark and thereby fostering a collaborative research ecosystem in MSR. Our
023                source code is also publicly available1 .

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recommender systems, deeply integrated into the digital world, play a crucial role in mitigating data
029   overload and personalizing user experiences across diverse online platforms (Zhang et al., 2019;
030   Fan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). Current recommender systems leverage user profiles, behavior
031   sequences, and contextual features to produce customized recommendations for specific user and
032   item scenarios (Zhou et al., 2019). In the face of varied real-world applications, there is growing
033   research on the development of models capable of managing multiple recommendation scenarios
034
      simultaneously, known as the Multi-Scenario Recommendation (MSR) task. MSR models, tailored
      to unique user and item scenarios, dynamically learn to transfer knowledge across scenarios (also
035
      referred to as “domains” in some research). This strategy not only addresses data scarcity in less
036
      populated scenarios but enhances overall recommendation performance (Feng et al., 2020; Xie et al.,
037   2022).
038
039   Specifically, multi-scenario recommendations involve designing a unified model capable of gen-
040
      erating recommendations across multiple scenarios (Sheng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Wang
      et al., 2022). These scenarios often represent distinct predefined domains, such as various adver-
041
      tising areas, product pages, or manually defined business units shown in Figure 1. The model’s
042
      primary objective is to harness knowledge transfer across scenarios to improve scenario-specific
043   performance. Central to these models is the ability to balance shared information and specific infor-
044   mation across different scenarios, thereby enhancing the overall predictive accuracy. This capability
045   is especially crucial for real-life deployments, where enterprises frequently face the challenge of
046   executing recommendation tasks across multiple scenarios (Zhang et al., 2022).
047
      With the development of deep recommender systems (Zhang et al., 2019; Batmaz et al., 2019) and
048
      cross-domain studies (Zhu et al., 2021a; Gao et al., 2023), we have witnessed the rapid growth of
049   multi-scenario recommendation methods. Many models, such as STAR (Sheng et al., 2021), AdaS-
050   parse (Yang et al., 2022), PEPNet (Chang et al., 2023), ADL (Li et al., 2023a), M3 oE (Zhang et al.,
051   2024), among others, have been proposed and effectively implemented. However, there is still a
052   lack of a widely universally recognized benchmark in this area, which poses significant challenges:

071   Firstly, there is a lack of a standardized pipeline for scenario data processing, model training, and
072   model performance evaluation to make fair comparisons between models. Secondly, many current
073   MSR models are closed-sourced due to corporate privacy protection policies, which complicates
074   reproducibility for researchers, thereby impeding the field’s progression in multi-scenario recom-
075   mendations.
076
      Given these challenges, the demand for a well-defined benchmark, specifically tailored for multi-
077   scenario recommendations, grows increasingly urgent. This benchmark should provide standardized
078   procedures for data processing, evaluation, and model interfaces, thereby establishing uniform re-
079   search norms. In this paper, we propose Scenario-Wise Rec, the first benchmark dedicated to MSR.
080   Our benchmark incorporates data preprocessing and evaluation protocols for six public scenario
081   datasets, providing a structured framework for model comparison and ensuring equitable evaluation
082   conditions. We have developed a uniform model interface and reproduced ten well-recognized MSR
083   models, including three multi-task-related models and seven multi-scenario models. To validate
084   our benchmark’s applicability and robustness, we have also applied it to an industrial dataset from
085
      one online advertising platform, demonstrating its real-world performance. Our comprehensive ap-
      proach not only enables researchers to derive valuable insights from existing works but also aims to
086
      nurture a collaborative research environment within the MSR field. The main contribution could be
087
      listed as follows:
088
089   • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first open-source benchmark designed for cutting-edge
090     MSR research, incorporating the latest models and a diverse MSR datasets. It serves the needs of
091     both academic and industrial research communities, bridging the gap between the latest advance-
092     ments in both fields.
093   • Our benchmark offers a unified pipeline for MSR tasks, covering data preprocessing, model train-
094     ing, and evaluation. integrating six public datasets and twelve widely recognized MSR models for
095     fair comparisons and reproducibility. Additionally, the benchmark is validated with an industrial
096     advertising dataset, enhancing its credibility and real-world applicability.
097   • We have made our benchmark publicly accessible, enabling researchers to conduct MSR experi-
098     ments with ease and gain valuable insights. This initiative aims to simplify MSR experimentation,
099     foster collaboration, and accelerate progress within the MSR community.

      3   P IPELINE
153
154
      In this section, a detailed introduction to the components of our benchmark is given, the overview
      framework is shown in Figure 2.
155
156   • Task: Multi-scenario Click-Through Rate Prediction. Our benchmark focuses on Click-
157     Through Rate (CTR) prediction in a multi-scenario setting. In general CTR prediction (Guo et al.,
158     2017), the CTR value ŷ is predicted by a model Fθ, which takes input features x (e.g., user, item,
159     and context features). This is expressed as ŷ = Fθ (x). However, in multi-scenario settings, the
160     input features differ due to the inclusion of scenario-specific features xs and a scenario indicator
161     s ∈ 1, ..., S, which indicates the scenario to which the input belongs. Additionally, when de-
        signing a multi-scenario model FθM , both scenario-specific and shared features must be jointly


                                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
          considered within the parameter θM across all S scenarios. Mathematically, this is formulated as:
163
164                                     ŷ = FθM (xg , xs , s), s ∈ {1, ..., S},                          (1)
165     here, xg denotes the general (scenario-independent) features, xs represents the scenario-specific
166     features for each scenario s, and ŷ refers to the CTR prediction.
167
      • Open Datasets. Open datasets are crucial for research in recommender systems. While numerous
168
        datasets are available, their inconsistent usage across studies hinders fair comparisons. Our pro-
169     posed benchmark addresses this by offering a unified data loading interface, enabling standardized
170     access to datasets. Specifically, we provide several open datasets which have been tested and eval-
171     uated under our benchmark. This interface is also designed for easy extensibility, encouraging the
172     use of additional datasets for experimentation and evaluation (see Section 4.1).
173   • General Data Processing Methods. Variations in data processing methods across studies lead
174     to inconsistent results. Most studies use custom methods without sharing processed data or de-
175     tailed procedures, hindering data reuse. Therefore, our work tries to establish a reproducible data
176     processing paradigm for multiple scenarios, ensuring fair comparison and repeatable experiments.
177     We apply unified processing methods, such as scenario feature declaration and common feature
178     filtering, allowing the community to conduct diverse research with standardized data processing.
179   • Unified Model Interface. Open-source models can be obtained through authors’ publications
180     or reproductions by others. However, code package and implementation inconsistencies lead to
181     model output variations. Our benchmark implements standardized modules with a consistent
182     model setup and call interface, ensuring reproducible model implementations and fair perfor-
183     mance comparisons through simple hyper-parameter settings. We have implemented ten cutting-
184     edge models for multi-scenario recommendations tested on six commonly used datasets and one
185
        industrial dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of this unified interface.
186   • Training. We have implemented a unified model training procedure to ensure fair comparisons
187     and scalability. This procedure standardizes the training process, allowing for easy extension with
188     various models and datasets. We also provide functions for saving logs, enabling clear record-
189
        keeping of training specifics and facilitating the reproducibility of experiments.
190   • Evaluation. Evaluation metrics are critical for assessing model performance. The use of dif-
191     ferent metrics across studies complicates fair comparisons. To address this, following previous
192     works (Sheng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Chang et al.,
193
        2023), we use AUC and Logloss, the two most common metrics, to evaluate model performance
        across different scenarios. We also provide a consistent evaluation interface for all models, ensur-
194
        ing fair comparisons.
195
196
      • Savable Logs & Settings & Tutorial. We provide a unified interface for hyper-parameter settings
        to standardize the evaluation process and ensure reproducibility. These settings, along with train-
197
        ing logs, are saved in files. This allows users to understand model performance changes during
198
        training and easily reproduce results based on the saved settings. Additionally, to facilitate ease of
199     use for researchers, we provide a detailed tutorial that includes environment setup, dataset down-
200     load, preprocessing, model training, and evaluation. Furthermore, an introduction to manually
201     designed MSR models and datasets is provided to support users in personalized model design.

      fair comparisons. To address this, Scenario-Wise Rec reproduces twelve state-of-the-art models
271   frequently mentioned in related studies and evaluates them on six public datasets and one industrial
272   dataset. The following sections introduce these models. Additionally, a detailed introduction to these
273   models is provided in Appendix A.2, and the reproduction details are presented in Appendix B.2
274
275   • Shared Bottom (Caruana, 1997): The Shared Bottom model uses a shared network to learn com-
276     mon representations for different tasks and applies separate towers for task-specific modeling. It
277     is commonly used in multi-scenario recommendations by treating different scenarios as distinct
278     tasks (Sheng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022).
279   • MMoE (Ma et al., 2018a): The Multi-gate Mixture-of-Experts (MMoE) model uses multiple ex-
280     pert networks and gating networks to control connections between experts and task-specific net-
281     works. This model is effectively applied in multi-scenario recommendations by treating different
282     scenarios as tasks.
283   • PLE (Tang et al., 2020): The Progressive Layered Extraction (PLE) model mitigates negative
284     transfer and handles complex task correlations in multi-task learning. PLE is particularly effective
285     in multi-scenario recommendations, as it separates shared and task-specific components while
286
        employing a progressive routing mechanism.
287   • STAR (Sheng et al., 2021): The Star Topology Adaptive Recommender (STAR) model integrates
288     a shared network for common features and scenario-specific networks tailored to each scenario.
289     This approach enhances both CTR and RPM in Alibaba’s advertising system by learning shared
290
        and scenario-specific parameters.
291   • SAR-Net (Shen et al., 2021): The Scenario-Aware Ranking Network (SAR-Net) by Alibaba lever-
292     ages specific attention modules for scenario, item, and user behavior features. It handles biased
293     logs through scenario-specific expert networks and a multi-scenario gating module, demonstrating
294
        effectiveness in multi-scenario recommendations.
295   • M2M (Zhang et al., 2022): The Multi-Scenario Multi-Task Meta-Learning (M2M) model captures
296     inter-scenario correlations using a meta unit and meta attention module. It enhances scenario-
297     specific feature representation and is effective for multi-scenario CTR prediction.
298   • AdaSparse (Yang et al., 2022): AdaSparse adapts to scenario-specific sparse structures for multi-
299     scenario CTR prediction by utilizing a lightweight network as a pruner to eliminate redundant in-
300     formation. It demonstrates significant improvements on both public datasets and within Alibaba’s
301     advertising system.
302   • ADL (Li et al., 2023a): The Adaptive Distribution Learning Framework (ADL) focuses on multi-
303     scenario CTR prediction with a hierarchical structure that includes clustering and classification. It
304     captures commonalities and distinctions among distributions, demonstrating effectiveness in both
305     public and industrial datasets.
306   • EPNet & PPNet (Chang et al., 2023): PPNet and EPNet, part of the Parameter and Embed-
307     ding Personalized Network (PEPNet), handle multi-task recommendations under multi-scenario
308     settings. EPNet fuses features with different importance for users, while PPNet modifies pa-
309
        rameters for different tasks. These models explore the impact of personalized modifications in
        multi-scenario recommendations.
310
311   • HAMUR (Li et al., 2023b): The Hyper Adapter for Multi-Domain Recommendation (HAMUR)
312     is proposed to introduce adapters (Rebuffi et al., 2017) for multi-domain recommendation (MSR)
313
        tasks. The adapters are domain-specific, while a shared hyper-network captures domain common-
        alities dynamically across different domains.
314
315   • M3 oE (Zhang et al., 2024): M3 oE introduces a framework consisting of three Mixture-of-Experts
316     (MoE) modules to learn common, domain-specific, and task-specific attributes, along with a two-
317
        level fusion mechanism that enables precise control over feature extraction and fusion across dif-
        ferent domains and tasks.

351   5.1     B ENCHMARKING S ETTINGS
352
353   We evaluated twelve models across six public datasets and open-sourced our benchmark package.
354   For datasets, we independently process features for each dataset using discretization and bucketing
355
      methods. Features are categorized into three groups: sparse features (discretized attributes), dense
      features (continuous attributes), and scenario features (scenario-specific operations). The datasets
356
      are split into training, evaluation, and testing sets in an 8:1:1 ratio for most datasets. Instead, Ali-
357
      CCP is pre-divided into three folds. For evaluation metrics, we follow methodologies from prior
358   MSR works like (Sheng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Chang
359   et al., 2023), using Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) and Logloss as metrics. AUC measures the
360   probability that a random positive sample ranks higher than a negative one, while Logloss evaluates
361   classification performance. Higher AUC or lower Logloss indicates better model performance. For
362   parameter settings, we ensure a fair comparison by configuring each model within a consistent
363   search space and maintaining similar parameter magnitudes across datasets. All experiments are
364   run 10 times with different random seeds to ensure the robustness of the results. More detailed
365   reproduction information, including parameter settings and model reproduction, can be found in
366
      Appendix B.2.

      5.1.3             S CENARIO N UMBER A NALYSIS
446
447   In MSR systems, a complex relationship exists between the number of scenarios and the perfor-
448   mance of each scenario. In this section, we analyze this relationship using the KuaiRand dataset by
449   varying the number of scenarios from 3 to 7 and observing the resulting performance changes in
450   each model. The experimental settings are detailed in Appendix B.3.
451
      We report the results from two selected scenarios: a dense scenario (Scenario-0#) and a sparse
452   scenario (Scenario-2#). As shown in Figure 3, the performance of both scenarios improves as the
453   number of scenarios increases from 3 to 7. This improvement can be attributed to the increased
454   number of instances, which augments the dataset and enhances domain collaboration, thus boosting
455   overall performance. However, in sparse Scenario-2#, we observe a “seesaw effect”, where an initial
456   performance drop is followed by an improvement. This drop is due to the addition of the sparse
457   scenario negatively affecting overall performance, as observed in models like SharedBottom, ADL,
458   and SATR. Notably, SAR-Net demonstrates a strong ability to balance performance across both
459   dense and sparse scenarios, maintaining consistent results. In practical deployments, it is essential
460
      to balance the trade-off between performance fluctuations across multiple scenarios and adapt the
      model to specific conditions.

525   7     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we introduce Scenario-Wise Rec, a pioneering benchmark designed specifically to
528   tackle the complexities and challenges inherent in MSR systems. Scenario-Wise Rec aims to estab-
529   lish a comprehensive framework for facilitating fair and reproducible comparisons among diverse
530   multi-scenario recommendation models, while also promoting the sharing of insights and advance-
531
      ments within this field. Our contributions are threefold. Firstly, to the best of our knowledge,
      Scenario-Wise Rec is the first benchmark released in the field of multi-scenario recommendation,
532
      offering significant benefits for the community by enabling fair comparisons across different models
533
      and fostering development. Secondly, we have integrated a pipeline that includes multi-scenario
534   data processing, training, evaluation, along with logging and open-source practices. Scenario-Wise
535   Rec thus sets a new standard for transparency and reproducibility in the field and is friendly for all
536   scholars. Thirdly, we provide the reproduction for twelve multi-scenario recommendation models
537   and seven distinct multi-scenario datasets (six public datasets and one industrial dataset), offering
538   scholars diverse angles to test and implement their models in this field. This facilitates a deeper
539   understanding of the current landscape and identifies potential avenues for future research. We hope
      our benchmark will contribute to the field and collectively foster collaboration in the area of MSR.

713   the reliability and applicability of experimental comparisons. Specifically, for public datasets, we
714
      choose MovieLens-1M, KuaiRand, Mind, Douban, Ali-CCP and Amazon, and the industrial adver-

720     It consists of 1 million anonymous ratings of approximately 4 thousand movies made by 6 thou-
721     sand MovieLens users. With the development of recommender systems, it has become an in-
722     valuable resource that enables insights into movie preferences and aids in the development of
723     innovative recommendation systems for the benefit of movie enthusiasts worldwide. In the pro-

726   • KuaiRand (Gao et al., 2022): The KuaiRand dataset is an unbiased recommendation dataset with
727     randomly exposed videos gathered from the Kuaishou App. In Scenario-Wise Rec, KuaiRand has
728
        been processed and used for model evaluation. It contains 11 million interactions with 1 thousand

729
        positions of the Kuaishou App. The scenario identification “tab” has already been given as a
730
        feature in the range of [0,14] to indicate the scenario of different interactions. To facilitate the
731     evaluation, we extracted data from the top five scenarios with the most data for training and testing.

      • Ali-CCP (Ma et al., 2018b): Ali-CCP is a large-scale CTR recommendation dataset gathered

735     scenarios indicator, representing an expression of the position the interaction sample is from.

      • Amazon (Cui et al., 2020): The Amazon 5-core dataset is a multi-scenario dataset generated from
737
        Amazon. In this paper, three scenarios “Clothing”, “Beauty”, and “Health” are used for training

739

        platform, is divided into three subsets: Douban-book, Douban-music, and Douban-movie. All

752     retain the four largest genres, “news”, “lifestyle”, “sports”, and “finance” as distinct scenarios.
753     This configuration encompasses a total of 748 million users, more than 20k items, and over 56
754
        million interactions.

762   A.2   M ULTI -S CENARIO R ECOMMENDATION M ODEL
763
764   With the rapid development of multi-scenario recommendations, more and more research has arisen.
765   However, due to the different data, parameters, and model implementation methods used in different
766   studies, it is difficult to directly summarize the current frontier research and make a fair comparison.
767   In order to track the most cutting-edge research in the field of multi-scenario recommendation and
768   facilitate fair comparison, in the proposed Scenario-Wise Rec, we reproduce twelve cutting-edge
769
      models that are commonly used or mentioned in the related studies and evaluate them on the six
      public datasets. We reproduce these models under the uniform model interface, and reproduction
770
      details are depicted in Appendix B.2. An introduction about these models is described as follows.
771
772   • Shared Bottom (Caruana, 1997): The Shared Bottom model is an approach for multi-task recom-
773     mendation tasks. It learns a shared representation from different tasks with a shared network base
774     to capture the patterns and shared information. Afterward, different network towers are applied
775     to different tasks for task-specific modeling. Recently, it has also been applied to multi-scenario
776     recommendations as a commonly used baseline by treating different scenarios as different recom-
777     mendation tasks (Sheng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022).
778   • MMoE (Ma et al., 2018a): Multi-gate Mixture-of-Experts (MMoE) model is a commonly used
779     model for multi-task learning. Different from the Shared Bottom, MMoE applies multiple ex-
780     pert networks named MOE (i.e., Mixture-of-Experts structure) as the bottom structure and uses
781     multiple gating networks to control the connections between different experts and the following
782     task-specific networks. Through a detailed modeling of task relations, MMoE achieves better per-
783     formance in multi-task recommendations. Similar to other multi-task models. MMoE can also
784     be easily applied to multi-scenario recommendations by treating different scenarios as different
785
        recommendation tasks.
786   • PLE (Tang et al., 2020): The Progressive Layered Extraction (PLE) model is a solution to the
787     challenges faced by multi-task learning (MTL) models in recommender systems. PLE addresses
788     the issues of negative transfer and complex task correlations by separating shared components
789     and task-specific components explicitly and adopting a progressive routing mechanism to gradu-
790
        ally extract deeper semantic knowledge. Through extensive experiments, PLE has outperformed
        state-of-the-art MTL models significantly in various task correlation scenarios. Similarly, PLE
791
        could also be applied as an effective multi-scenario recommendation model by treating different
792
        scenarios as different recommendation tasks.
793
794   • STAR (Sheng et al., 2021): The Star Topology Adaptive Recommender (STAR) model addresses
795     the challenge of making click-through rate (CTR) predictions for multiple scenarios within a large-
796
        scale commercial platform. It achieves multi-scenario learning by combining a shared network
        that captures commonalities between scenarios with scenario-specific networks tailored to each
797
        scenario. The weights of the shared network and the scenario-specific network are multiplied
798
        to generate a unified network during the inference stage for each scenario. STAR effectively
799     learns the shared network from all data and adapts scenario-specific parameters to each scenario’s
800     characteristics. Production data has validated the effectiveness of STAR, with significant improve-
801     ments in CTR and Revenue Per Mille (RPM) observed since its deployment in Alibaba’s display
802     advertising system in late 2020.
803
      • SAR-Net (Shen et al., 2021): The Scenario-Aware Ranking Network (SAR-Net) is proposed by
804     Alibaba and designed for the travel marketing platform for multi-scenario recommendation tasks.
805     It tackles the challenge of training a unified model by leveraging specific attention modules that in-
806     corporate scenario, item features, and user behavior features. Moreover, SAR-Net handles biased
807     logs resulting from manual intervention during promotion periods through scenario-specific ex-
808     pert networks, scenario-shared expert networks, and a multi-scenario gating module. Experiments
809     and online A/B testing demonstrate the effectiveness of SAR-Net, which has been successfully
        deployed and serves hundreds of travel scenarios on Alibaba’s online travel marketing platform.


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      • M2M (Zhang et al., 2022): The Multi-Scenario Multi-Task Meta-Learning (M2M) model is a
811     novel approach designed to address the challenges of multi-task and multi-scenario advertiser
812     modeling in e-commerce platforms like Taobao and Amazon. M2M utilizes a meta unit to capture
813     inter-scenario correlations, a meta attention module to capture diverse inter-scenario correlations
814     for different tasks, and a meta tower module to enhance scenario-specific feature representation
815     for different recommendation tasks. In Scenario-Wise Rec, the number of the meta-towers is set
816     to 1 to correspond to the single CTR prediction task.
817   • AdaSparse (Yang et al., 2022): AdaSparse is designed for multi-scenario CTR prediction and
818     aims to adaptively learn the sparse structures of scenario models. Specifically, AdaSparse intro-
819     duces a lightweight network functioning as a pruner, which operates a scenario-pruning process
820     for each layer within individual scenario towers. During this pruning process, a novel fusion
821     strategy is employed, combining binary and scale approaches to enhance pruning performance,
822     effectively eliminating as much redundant information as possible. The results demonstrate sig-
823     nificant improvements not only in public datasets but also in online A/B tests within Alibaba’s
824
        advertising system’s CTR platform.
825   • ADL (Li et al., 2023a): The Adaptive Distribution Learning Framework (ADL), a novel multi-
826     distribution method, concentrates on multi-scenario CTR prediction. It features an end-to-end,
827     hierarchical structure that includes a clustering process and a classification process. The core
828
        component, the distribution adaptation module, employs a routing mechanism, adaptively deter-
        mining the distribution cluster for each sample. This model effectively captures the commonalities
829
        and distinctions among various distributions, thereby enhancing the model’s representation capa-
830
        bility without relying on prior knowledge for predefined data allocation. Extensive experiments
831     are conducted on public datasets, and an industrial dataset from Alibaba’s online system consist-
832     ing of 10 distinct scenarios. The results demonstrate its effectiveness and efficiency compared to
833     other models.
834
      • EPNet & PPNet (Chang et al., 2023): PPNet and EPNet are two submodels in the Parameter and
835     Embedding Personalized Network (PEPNet). EPNet performs personalized selection on embed-
836     ding to fuse features with different importance for different users in multiple scenarios. PPNet
837     executes personalized modification on DNN parameters to balance targets with different sparsity
838     for different users in multiple tasks. By applying PPNet and EPNet, PEPNet is able to handle
839     multi-task recommendations under multi-scenario settings. In Scenario-Wise Rec, We designed
840     these two models to explore the impact of each on multi-scenario recommendations. Meanwhile,
841     the number of the meta-towers in PPNet is set to the same as the scenario number to correspond
842     to the CTR prediction task on each scenario.
843   • HAMUR (Li et al., 2023b): The HAMUR (Hyper Adapter for Multi-Domain Recommendation)
844     comprises two main components: a domain-specific adapter and a domain-shared hyper-network.
845     The domain-specific adapter is a modular component that can be seamlessly integrated into vari-
846     ous recommendation models, allowing each domain to maintain unique adaptations. The domain-
847     shared hyper-network dynamically generates parameters for these adapters by implicitly capturing
848
        shared patterns among domains. HAMUR’s dynamic architecture is validated through experi-
        ments multiple public datasets, demonstrating its ability to outperform state-of-the-art models by
849
        enhancing predictive accuracy across diverse domains.
850
851   • M3 oE (Zhang et al., 2024): The M3 oE framework, introduced as the Multi-Domain Multi-Task
852
        Mixture-of-Experts recommendation system, is designed to tackle complex recommendation chal-
        lenges across diverse domains and tasks. At its core, M3oE employs three distinct mixture-of-
853
        experts (MoE) modules, each dedicated to managing domain preferences and task-specific be-
854
        haviors. Furthermore, it integrates a two-level fusion mechanism to effectively combine features
855     across both domains and tasks. The framework’s adaptability is enhanced through the use of Au-
856     toML, which dynamically optimizes its structure, enabling efficient cross-domain and cross-task
857     knowledge transfer, ultimately demonstrating superior performance.

      A.3.1    S CENARIO S PLITTING S TRATEGY
865
866   Unlike traditional CTR prediction tasks, MSR models emphasize scenario-unified prediction, re-
867   quiring a scenario indicator within the dataset features to facilitate dataset splitting. Traditionally,
868   scholars utilize features such as the advertising area, product page number, or other manually de-
869   fined context features as scenario indicators. Specifically, for datasets focusing on multi-scenario
870   recommendations (E.g., Ali-CCP, KuaiRand), the scenario indicator is often a predefined feature
871   field provided by the dataset itself, representing different sources of different samples (E.g., differ-
872
      ent advertising slots). For general datasets (E.g., ML-1M), when applied to multi-scenario recom-
      mendations, existing studies often use a feature that can clearly distinguish samples as a scenario
873
      indicator (E.g., item category). Notably, recent studies, like (Guo et al., 2023), have begun exploring
874
      other scenario-splitting features to enhance overall performance. In our benchmark, to advance sce-
875   nario analysis, we implement various splitting strategies, encompassing traditional context feature
876   division, user feature separation, and item feature segmentation across five datasets. As an example,
877   for the Ali-CCP dataset, we follow the approach of previous studies such as (Wang et al., 2022;
878   Li et al., 2023b), employing the “301” feature, which denotes the display position of items on the
879   screen. In the KuaiRand dataset, segmentation is based on the “tab” feature, indicating whether the
880   recommendation appears on the app’s main page or a specific recommendation page. The scenario
881   splitting methods of other datasets are also illustrated in Section A.1.

         losing generality. The backbone network is chosen for three and six, respectively, for different
989
         datasets like Aliccp and Kuairand. And we set α to 1 and the searching space for β is {2, 3, 4}.
990
991    • ADL: In the reproduction of the ADL model, we commence by establishing a shared fully con-
992
         nected network dedicated to modeling correlations across different scenarios. This is comple-
         mented by the construction of several scenario-specific fully connected networks, aimed at con-
993
         ducting nuanced, scenario-specific analyses. Furthermore, a Distribution Learning Module (DLM)
994
         is developed as illustrated in the original paper (Li et al., 2023a) , employing a clustering algo-
995      rithm based on cosine similarities to enable dynamic routing during both training and inference
996      phases, thereby enhancing the model’s adaptability to diverse data distributions. For the shared
997      fully-connected network, we follow the previously mentioned setting: three layers for dataset
998      Movie-Lens, KuaiRand, Amazon, Douban, Mind, industrial dataset, and six layers for Aliccp.
999      Besides, we search the space of the number of clusters in {3, 4, 5}.
1000   • EPNet: In constructing the EPNet, we first built the Gate NU module to provide gated scaling
1001     signals for the model. Then, we divide the input into scenario-side features and scenario-agnostic
1002     features (i.e., sparse features and dense features), respectively, and embed them into embedding
1003     vectors. Afterward, we construct the scaled embedding by inputting the scenario-side embedding
1004     and detached scenario-agnostic embeddings to the GateNU module and applying the output scal-
1005     ing parameters to the original embedding. To avoid the effects of the PPNet structure, through
1006
         a simple parameter search, we replace the subsequent network about PPNet in the original paper
         with a three-layer or six-layers feedforward structure with different neurons according to different
1007
         datasets and add an output header to output values between [0, 1].
1008
1009   • PPNet: In developing the PPNet model, we adhered to the design outlined in paper (Chang et al.,
1010
         2023). Initially, we concatenate ID embeddings and input them into Gate NU modules. The num-
         ber of Gate NU modules is the same as the number of PPNet layers. Subsequently, we constructed
1011
         the PPNet tower. Given that PPNet was originally designed for multi-task learning, we adhered
1012
         to our initial settings, assigning different task-specific architectures within the scenario tower. We
1013     configured PPNet with MLPs tailored to various dataset distributions to adhere to the settings like
1014     previous models, six-layer MLP for Aliccp, and three-layers for the others. For each instance, the
1015     input is directed to an appropriate scenario tower based on its “scenario indicator”.
1016   • HAMUR: In developing HAMUR Li et al. (2023b), we followed the settings outlined in the
1017     original paper. We selected the feature domain ID as the domain indicator, and for different
1018     datasets, different model architectures were chosen. For the Ali-CCP dataset, a seven-layer MLP
1019     was selected as the backbone model, while for the other datasets, only a three-layer MLP was
1020     used. Regarding the hyper-network, a single-layer MLP with a hidden dimension of 64 was set,
1021     but different hyper-matrix sizes were used. For the seven-layer backbone model, the hyper-matrix
1022     size was set to 65, while for the others, it was set to 35.
1023   • M3 oE: In reproducing M3 oE (Zhang et al., 2024), we follow the original paper but made a mod-
1024     ification by setting the task number to 1, making it compatible with multi-scenario prediction.
1025     We used the sparse and dense features along with the domain ID as domain indicators. For the
         parameters, we set the number of experts within the search space of {3, 4, 5} across all datasets.

       D    L IMITATION AND F UTURE R ESEARCH
1297
1298
       In this section, we will discuss the limitation of our benchmark and current multi-scenario recom-
1299   mendation research, furthermore, we also provide future research topic in this realm.
1300
1301   • Limitation: Compared to other tasks in recommendation systems, such as multi-task recommen-
1302     dation, multi-behavior recommendation, and multi-modal recommendation, multi-scenario rec-
1303     ommendation is a relatively new yet burgeoning research topic. Currently, most research focuses
1304     on multi-scenario collaboration to improve click-through rates, which is the primary focus of our
1305     benchmark. In the past three months, scholars have begun to explore other tasks in multiple sce-
1306     nario, including how to segment scenarios (Jia et al., 2024), how to use large language models to
1307
         align semantics between scenarios (Fu et al., 2023), and how to enhance performance in multiple
         scenarios through causal inference (Zhu et al., 2024). Since most of this research is still in its
1308
         infancy and due to factors such as not passing peer review or not publishing code implementa-
1309
         tion details, we only include some well-recognized SOTA models in this field in our benchmark.
1310     However, we will update our benchmarks in a timely manner based on the development of multi-
1311     scenario research.
1312
       • Future Research: For future research, several noteworthy topics merit attention. Firstly, re-
1313
         fining the application of Large Language Models for fine-grained scenario alignment is crucial.
1314     While Uni-CTR (Fu et al., 2023) offers a foundational approach, it does not explicitly extract sce-
1315     nario commonalities, thereby constraining scenario expansion. Secondly, although current Multi-
1316     Scenario Recommendation research predominantly focuses on Click-Through Rate (CTR) tasks,
1317     other areas such as sequential recommendations for diverse scenarios and trustworthy recommen-
1318     dations within MSR remain underexplored. Finally, developing a joint model that simultaneously
1319     considers multiple tasks, scenarios, behaviors, and interests could pave the way for a more gener-
1320     alized recommendation system.
