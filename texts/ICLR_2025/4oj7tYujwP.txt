011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Innovation is a key driver of economic and social progress, with Intellectual Prop-
014            erty (IP) protection through patents playing a crucial role in safeguarding new
015            creations. For businesses actively producing goods, detecting potential patent in-
016
               fringement is vital to avoid costly litigation and operational disruptions. However,
               the significant domain gap between products and patents—coupled with the vast
017
               scale of existing patent databases—makes infringement detection a complex and
018
               challenging task. Besides, the machine learning (ML) community has not widely
019            addressed this problem, partly due to the lack of comprehensive datasets tailored
020            for this task. In this paper, we firstly formulate a new task: detecting potentially
021            infringing patents for a given product represented by multi-modal data, includ-
022            ing images and textual descriptions. This task requires a deep understanding of
023            both technical and legal contexts, extending beyond simple text or image match-
024            ing to assess functional similarities that may not be immediately apparent. To
025            promote research in this challenging area, we further introduce the ERiC-UP3
026            (E-Commerce Risk intelligence Classifier on Utility Patent and Product Pairs)
027
               benchmark, a large-scale, well-structured dataset comprising over 13-million
               patent samples and 1 million product samples. It includes 11,000 meticulously
028
               annotated infringement pairs for training and 2,000 for testing, all rigorously re-
029
               viewed by patent experts to ensure high-quality annotations. The dataset reflects
030            real-world scenarios with its multi-modal nature and the necessity for deep func-
031            tional understanding, offering unique characteristics that set it apart from existing
032            resources. As a case study, we provide results from a series of baseline methods
033            and propose a simple yet effective infringement detection pipeline. We also ex-
034            plore additional approaches that may enhance detection performance, such as text
035            style rewriting, cross-modal matching effectiveness, and image domain alignment.
036            Overall, the ERiC-UP3 benchmark is the first strictly annotated product-patent in-
037            fringement detection dataset and stands as the largest multi-modal patent dataset,
038
               as well as one of the largest multi-modal product datasets available. We aim to
               advance research extending language and multi-modal models to diverse and dy-
039
               namic real-world data distributions, fostering innovation and practical solutions in
040
               IP infringement detection.

046   industries, granting companies and individuals exclusive rights over their creations (Reitzig & Pu-
047   ranam, 2009; Maskus, 1998). However, the misuse of the patent system—notably by “patent trolls”
048   who file lawsuits for compensation without ever producing the patented products—places signifi-
049   cant legal and financial burdens on legitimate manufacturers (Golden, 2006). For businesses actively
050
      producing goods, avoiding IP infringement is critical to prevent costly litigation and operational dis-
      ruptions. Proactively detecting and mitigating potential infringements is key to minimizing these
051
      risks, ensuring smoother operations, and fostering continued innovation.
052
053   A major challenge in detecting patent infringement lies in the significant domain gap between prod-
      ucts and patents. As illustrated in Figure 1, patent documents contain technical text and schematic

079   Usually, patent infringement detection has been a labor-intensive process requiring substantial time
080   and expertise from specialists well-versed in both legal and technical fields (Bergmann et al., 2008).
081   This reliance on manual assessments not only makes the process costly but also limits its accessibil-
082
      ity, particularly for small and medium-sized enterprises. While leveraging ML (Goodfellow et al.,

083
      bility, the absence of well-annotated datasets specifically designed tailored for this task has signifi-
084
      cantly impeded progress. Therefore, the field has not yet attracted widespread attention within the
085   ML community, hindering the development of accurate and functional automated detection models.
086
087   This paper focuses on bridging this gap and advancing related research, where we first formulate
088
      the task of patent infringement detection and subsequently introduce two benchmark datasets tai-
      lored for it: ERiC-UP3 -Base and ERiC-UP3 -Large (E-Commerce Risk intelligence Classifier on
089
      Utility Patent and Product Pairs). The former includes a smaller set of samples designed to pro-
090
      vide researchers with a platform for rapid testing and prototype development, while the latter offers
091   broader data coverage, suitable for testing model robustness and effectiveness in more complex and
092   diverse real-world scenarios. On these benchmarks, we conduct extensive experiments and provide
093   a series of baseline results to demonstrate the task’s challenges and to serve as a reference for sub-
094   sequent research. Additionally, we develop a straightforward yet effective text-based method for
095   infringement detection, which includes a classifier for potential patent infringements and a product-
096   patent-specific retriever. Crucially, our framework shows strong baseline results and significantly
097   enhances the performance of infringement detection. We further conduct a series of meaningful ex-
098   periments on this dataset to improve the success rate of infringement detection, including text style
099
      rewriting, the incorporation of image knowledge to aid detection performance, and the alignment of
      image modal features. Through these contributions, we not only advance the application of ML in
100
      the field of IP protection but also provide valuable resources for the research community.

      2.3   C HALLENGE OF LABELING PRODUCT- PATENT INFRINGEMENT PAIRS
137
138   Labeling product-patent infringement pairs is a complex and demanding task due to the inherent
139   difficulties in accurately linking products to the patents they may infringe upon. One of the primary
140   challenges stems from the use of Virtual Patent Marking (VPM) (Patent & Office, 2014), a method
141   mandated by US law requiring companies to disclose product-patent information. However, com-
142   pliance is minimal, and the data provided is often sparse and unevenly distributed across industries.
143   Manufacturers frequently list only product model numbers without detailed descriptions, making it
144
      arduous to ascertain the exact product specifications and associated patents. Patent experts in our
      IP team have dedicated considerable effort to meticulously verify and establish the relationships
145
      between these model numbers, the corresponding products, and their relevant patents. In addition
146
      to VPM data, a significant portion of our infringement pairs has been compiled through the dili-
147   gent work of our IP team during pre-listing IP audits. Their deep understanding of patent databases
148   and manual search methodologies enables them to identify and match patents with high precision.
149   Furthermore, we have incorporated data from historical infringement cases, reflecting a wealth of
150   knowledge accumulated over time. These comprehensive efforts underscore not only the challenges
151   in assembling accurate product-patent pairs but also highlight the critical role of specialized exper-
152   tise in navigating the intricacies of IP law and enforcement.

      2.6     E VALUATION M ETRICS
236
237   To assess the performance of our infringement detection model, we focus primarily on two eval-
238   uation metrics: mean Average Top-K matching Recall (mAR@K) and mean Rank of Matches
239   (mRoM). Given that a single product may infringe on multiple patents, we adopt a hit-one strategy
240   for recall evaluation. This means that if at least one infringing patent is present within the Top-K
241   matches, the detection is considered successful. Our objective is to achieve a lower average rank,
242   indicating that relevant patents are retrieved more efficiently and effectively within the top results.

244   3      M ETHOD
245
246   In this section, we present a straightforward and effective pipeline for detecting potential patent
247   infringements based on the textual modality. Given the enormous size of the patent retrieval pool
248   (i.e., 13-million in ERiC-UP3 -Large), our primary concern is how to effectively reduce the search
249   space. To address this, we design to employ a classifier to identify potential patent infringement
250   categories related to products, thereby narrowing down the pool of relevant patents. Next, we design
251   to train an encoder using supervised contrastive learning (SCL) (Khosla et al., 2020) on product-
252   patent pairs, which generates reliable text embeddings, allowing us to compute similarity scores for
253
      infringement detection.

255   3.1     PATENT I NFRINGEMENT C ATEGORY C LASSIFIER
256

257

258   product. Formally, given a query product Pi , before retrieving potentially infringing patents, we first
259   classify it into the relevant CPC main classes (137 in total) where potential infringing patents are
260   likely to be found. This preliminary classification significantly reduces the search space, making the
261   retrieval process more efficient.
262

      4.1        WARMING U P : S ELECTION OF T EXTUAL S ECTIONS AND E NCODER
347
348   To establish a strong baseline for our retrieval model, we begin by exploring the optimal combination
349   of textual sections from patents and products, as well as selecting the most suitable encoder for our
350   task. This preliminary step is crucial to ensure that subsequent experiments would be built upon the
351   most informative and effective data representations. Patents and products contain various textual
352   components that provide different levels of detail and specificity.
353   As shown in Table 2, both patent and product include several textual sections. We hypothesize that
354   certain combinations of these sections might offer better retrieval performance due to the richness
355   of information they contain. Therefore, we examine performance of different combinations of these
356   sections as shown in Table 4, which experimentally demonstrate that the combination of Abstract +
357   Claims for patents and Title + Description for products is optimal by achieving a highest mAR@500
358   score of 60.71 and an mRoM of 235.34. This combination can effectively capture essential legal and
359   technical aspects, and finally enhance the retrieval system’s effectiveness. We put implementation

475   Does Cosine Similarity Best Capture Semantic Relevance? Several works (Khattab & Zaharia,
476   2020; Lu et al., 2021; Steck et al., 2024) have highlighted that simple dot-product (or cosine sim-

480   two-category classifier. During inference, the output logits are used as the metric to measure the
481   semantic relevance between the paired product and patent embeddings. The experimental results
482
      and detailed analyses presented in Appendix F.5 also suggest that cosine similarity may not be the
      most effective detector for patent infringement.

491   5   R ELATED W ORK
492
493   Information Retrieval. Information retrieval, encompassing both intra-modal and cross-modal
494   techniques, plays a pivotal role in efficiently accessing relevant data from vast information sources.
495   Intra-modal retrieval (Qi et al., 2016; Bai et al., 2018) has been thoroughly explored in various
496   domains, such as keyword-based web document search (Ensan & Bagheri, 2017), content-driven
497
      image retrieval (Noh et al., 2017) and product recommendation systems (Kang et al., 2017). On the
      other hand, cross-modal retrieval (Feng et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2017) has emerged as a compelling
498
      solution for efficiently indexing and retrieving data across different modalities, making it particu-
499
      larly useful in large-scale applications like search engines (Harman et al., 2019) and E-commerce
500   platforms (Corbiere et al., 2017), among others. Nevertheless, these techniques (Nurmi et al., 2008;
501   Wang et al., 2016; Lin et al., 2018) often rely on single-modal inputs, limiting their effectiveness in
502   real-world scenarios where both queries and targets involve multi-modal information.
503
504
      Patent Analysis. Previous research in Natural Language Processing (NLP) related to patent anal-
505   ysis has largely concentrated on two key tasks: patent classification and summarization. Patents
506   are typically classified using hierarchical systems such as the IPC and CPC, with various studies
507   predicting IPC/CPC codes at different levels using statistical methods (Chu et al., 2008; Tran &
508   Kavuluru, 2017; Gomez, 2019) and neural networks (Grawe et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Zhu et al.,
509   2020), including Transformer-based models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and BIGBIRD (Zaheer
510   et al., 2020). Datasets such as CLEF-IP (Piroi et al., 2011) and USPTO-2M (Li et al., 2018) have
511   been commonly used for training models in this area, though they are limited in scope and flexibil-
512   ity, which the more comprehensive HUPD (Suzgun et al., 2024) dataset addresses. In the area of
513
      patent text generation and summarization, the introduction of the BIGPATENT (Sharma et al., 2019)
      dataset marked a significant step forward. This work introduces a novel task by focusing on patent
514
      acceptance prediction, using textual analysis to identify characteristics that differentiate accepted
515
      from rejected patents, thus contributing a new dimension to patent decision classification.
516
517
      Patent Infringement Detection. Patent infringement detection is a crucial process aimed at iden-
518
      tifying the unauthorized use of patented technology, thus safeguarding IP rights. Traditionally, this
519
      task has been carried out manually, requiring detailed comparisons of patent claims—a method
520   that is both time-consuming and susceptible to human error (Schoen et al., 1993; Majewski &
521   Williamson, 2004). In recent years, keyword-driven text mining techniques have become a preva-
522   lent approach for detecting infringements (Yoon, 2008; Lee et al., 2013). However, these techniques
523   are limited by their dependence on predetermined keywords, which constrains their ability to cap-
524   ture nuanced technological insights and complex structural relationships between components. To
525   address these limitations, Park & Yoon (2014) proposed a semantic similarity approach based on
526   the Subject-Action-Object (SAO) framework, utilizing WordNet (Miller, 1995) to identify patent in-
527   fringements by measuring technological similarities. More recently, with the advancement of deep
528
      neural networks, Liu & Pei (2023) leverages text vectorization and convolutional neural networks
      to extract and represent patent infringement features, capturing semantic information from multiple
529
      layers of patents.

533
534   In this paper, we introduce a new task of detecting potentially infringing patents for given products
535   represented by multi-modal data, including both images and textual descriptions. To support this,
536   we develop ERiC-UP3 , the largest and most comprehensive dataset for this task. Our experiments
537   highlight the complexity of the problem and demonstrate the potential of our detection pipeline,
538   along with techniques like text style rewriting and cross-modal matching, to improve results. This
539   work establishes a foundation for advancing automated IP infringement detection, helping mitigate
      legal risks and foster innovation across industries.

      contribution, as it enables models to learn from both textual and visual information, providing a

      in multi-modal machine learning applications within the patent domain.

902
      of more robust and generalizable machine learning models.

      content.

911   temporal analysis of patent texts, and more sophisticated infringement detection methods that lever-

914   hensive, multi-modal resource that includes crucial sections like the claims and incorporates cleaned

      C     N OISY IMAGES CONTAINED IN BOTH PATENT AND PRODUCT SAMPLES
955
956
957
      This section highlights the importance of differentiating between essential drawings and images for
      infringement detection, represented by the “Greed Box” and less relevant technical illustrations or
958
      background images in the “Red Box”. The former showcases key design features, while the latter
959
      includes structural diagrams that do not contribute to infringement analysis and should be excluded
960   from consideration.
961
962   To this end, we propose a simple yet effective model-based iteratively filtering method based on
963
      KNN (K-nearest neighbor), where we firstly employ DINO (Oquab et al., 2023), a widely recognized
      unsupervised pre-training method, to train a robust feature extractor on large-scale patent/product
964
      images. Next, we curate a small hand-labeled dataset, denoted as ϕ, with labels indicating whether
965
      images are noisy or valid, and generate the feature embedding for the set ϕ using DINO. Upon com-
966   pleting these preparations, for each image to be classified, we compute its feature embeddings and
967   retrieve the Top-K most similar instances from ϕ. By voting on the labels of the Top-K instances,
968   we predict whether an image is noisy. The predicted noisy and valid images are then added to ϕ,
969   iteratively expanding the labeled dataset and enabling more robust noise prediction. To validate the
970   effectiveness of our approach, we construct a test set containing 81 noisy images and 219 valid im-
971   ages. Our KNN strategy achieves an overall recognition accuracy of 93%, successfully identifying
      67 true noisy images, with a recall of 82.71% and a precision of 90.54%.

1135            non-empty intersection). This is a more lenient measure of accuracy, as it allows for partial
1136            matches between the predicted and actual labels. It is particularly useful in multi-label

1141   Accuracy of nearly 60%. This accuracy improves to over 91% when 30% of the data is used.
1142   These findings highlight the importance of having a large amount of training data for improving the
1143   performance of the CPC classifier, especially when evaluated using Partial Intersection Accuracy.
1144   They also underscore the utility of the Partial Intersection Accuracy metric for evaluating multi-label
1145
       classification tasks.
1146   Further, we investigate the impact of different Top-K and λ values on the final classification perfor-
1147   mance. The Top-K parameter refers to the number of most likely classes that the classifier outputs.
1148   It is a key parameter in multi-label classification tasks, as it determines the granularity of the pre-
1149   dictions. A larger K means that the classifier will predict more classes for each instance, potentially
1150   increasing recall but possibly decreasing precision if many of the extra predicted classes are incorrect
1151
       as shown in Figure 7.
1152   λ is a threshold parameter that determines the number of activations required for a class to be con-
1153   sidered as a potential prediction. A higher λ means that more activations are needed for a class to be
1154   considered, which can increase precision (since only the most activated classes are considered), but
1155   may decrease recall (since some less activated but still relevant classes might be missed), as shown
1156   in Figure 8. As for the Top-K parameter, increasing it will indeed increase recall, as the model
1157
       is allowed to predict more classes per instance. However, this can also decrease precision, as the
       likelihood of predicting incorrect classes also increases.
1158
1159   Given the differing preferences of Top-K and λ (with the former favoring recall and the latter favor-
1160   ing precision), we propose an intersection method that considers both parameters for the final CPC
1161   classification prediction. This method aims to strike a balance between recall and precision, provid-
1162   ing a limited yet reliable set of classification results. By tuning both Top-K and λ, we can optimize
1163
       the trade-off between including as many relevant classes as possible (high recall) and minimizing
       the inclusion of irrelevant classes (high precision).

1224   Summarizing Long Contexts to Short Contexts. In our retrieval task, both patents and product
1225   descriptions often contain lengthy sections with extraneous details, making it challenging for an
1226   encoder model to focus on the most relevant information. On the other hand, most of SOTA embed-
1227
       ding models (e.g., BERT, BGE, T5 and etc) can only handle input text of limited length, which is
       obviously lower than the length of the original text. Since the part that exceeds the length will be di-
1228
       rectly truncated, this will obviously cause the loss of information, thus reducing the effectiveness of
1229
       detection. To address this, we implement a summarization to condense long-form text into shorter,
1230   more focused contexts using several LLMs. The goal of the summarization process was to preserve
1231   key technical details while eliminating redundant or irrelevant information. This condensed version
1232   of the text allows the embedding model to more effectively capture the core semantics of the content,
1233   leading to better retrieval accuracy.
1234
       We experiment with the A45, A47 and A63 subsets, using Qwen2-0.5B (Yang et al., 2024), Qwen2-

       7B (Yang et al., 2024), and Llama3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) instruct models for summarization. To
1236   better align these models with our requirements, we used GPT-4o to generate high-quality abstrac-
1237   tive summarizing examples, which served as few-shot prompts to guide the models. Details on the
1238   use of GPT-4o and querying LLMs can be found in Appendix G. The matching results are shown
1239   in Table 14. As shown in the results, all models except Qwen2-7B on the A45 subset outperform
1240   the baseline mAR@500 scores, with Qwen2-7B on A45 achieving the same results as the base-

       Rewriting Patent and Product Texts for Similar Stylistic Alignment. Another key challenge
1249   in product and patent retrieval is the stylistic and linguistic differences between patent texts and
1250   product descriptions. Patent documents are often written in a formal, legalistic style, while product
1251   descriptions tend to be more commercial and user-friendly. These stylistic discrepancies can create
1252   a semantic gap, making it difficult for the embedding model to effectively match similar concepts
1253   across the two domains. To address this, we apply a rewriting strategy for stylistic alignment, bridg-
1254   ing the semantic gap and enabling the embedding model to better recognize and match concepts
1255   across both patent and product description domains.
1256   After determining that Qwen2-0.5B may be optimal for the summarization task, we apply it ex-
1257   clusively for the stylistic alignment task. The matching results are shown in Table 14. By using
1258   Qwen2-0.5B for this rewriting task, we observed a significant improvement in matching results for
1259   the A63 subset and a slight improvement for the A47 subset. The rewritten texts allowed the embed-
1260   ding model to bridge the semantic gap between patents and product descriptions, resulting in more
1261   accurate cosine similarity scores and higher-quality Top-n retrieval results. However, for the A45
1262   subset, we found that the results were lower than the base results. This discrepancy likely arises
1263
       because, in this case, the models were given either patent texts or product texts individually. The
       stylistic alignment task heavily relies on the quality of examples provided by GPT-4o, and without
1264
       strong examples, the models struggled to handle this task effectively using their own knowledge
1265
       alone. This suggests that the models’ intrinsic ability to perform stylistic alignment may need fur-
1266   ther refinement. Nevertheless, the rewritten method still resulted in shorter mPaL and mPrL values,
1267   as well as comparable or improved mRoM scores. While our experiments were conducted on three
1268   subsets (A45, A47 and A63), more extensive testing on the entire dataset may require additional
1269   resources. We hope that future research will build on these insights and develop more efficient and
1270   scalable methods for this task.

1275
1276
       While textual information offers a comprehensive description of patents and products, corresponding
       images often provide more fine-grained and intuitive visual cues, which can serve as crucial supple-
1277
       mentary evidence in detecting product-patent infringements. However, as previously mentioned,
1278
       the significant domain gap between patent and product images makes direct similarity measurement
1279   highly challenging. To address this issue, in our work, we propose a simple yet effective style-
1280   transfer method based on stretch detection to alleviate the domain shift, and then utilize the pow-
1281   erful CLIP model to extract unified feature representations. Specifically, as shown in Figure 3, we
1282   transform the original patent and product images, which exhibit substantial domain disparities, into
1283   a similar visual style using stretch detection (Zhou et al., 2024). Subsequently, CLIP is employed
1284   to extract feature embeddings for each image. For instances with multiple associated images, we
1285   compute the average of their embeddings to form a unified representation. By calculating the cosine
1286   similarity between feature representations, we retrieve potentially infringing patents for each prod-
1287
       uct. Results in Table 9 demonstrate that, benefiting from stretch-based style transfer, we achieve a
       mAR@500 of 33.92%, marking a 21.42% improvement over using raw natural images. Considering
1288
       the significant performance and low computational cost of text-based retrieval, we further propose
1289
       a hierarchical text-to-image retrieval strategy. In the first stage, we utilize text matching to filter
1290   the Top-h most likely patent candidates for each product, thus narrowing the retrieval pool from
1291   a vast, shared collection to a tailored, more-focused subset, which not only reduces computational
1292   complexity but also simplifies the matching process. Next, we perform image-based retrieval within
1293   the selected Top-h candidates. With this approach, we further enhance image-based retrieval perfor-
1294   mance, increasing mAR@500 from 33.92% to 42.85%. h is set to 5000 in our experiments, as the
1295   mAR@5000 for text-based retrieval achieves approximately 97%, ensuring the inclusion of almost
       all potentially infringing patents.

1340           already been optimized for specific task-related semantic matching during its fine-tuning
1341

1342

1343

1389

1393

1394

1395
