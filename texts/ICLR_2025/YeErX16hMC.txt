009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-training of pre-trained LLMs, which typically consists of the supervised fine-
012            tuning (SFT) stage and the preference learning (RLHF or DPO) stage, is crucial
013            to effective and safe LLM applications. The widely adopted approach in post-
014            training popular open-source LLMs is to sequentially perform SFT and RLHF/DPO.
015            However, sequential training is sub-optimal in terms of SFT and RLHF/DPO trade-
016            off: the LLM gradually forgets about the first stage’s training when undergoing the
017            second stage’s training. We theoretically prove the sub-optimality of sequential
018
               post-training. Furthermore, we propose a practical joint post-training framework
               that has theoretical convergence guarantees and empirically outperforms sequential
019
               post-training framework, while having similar computational cost.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Recent years have witnessed the great capabilities of large language models (LLMs) trained on a
025   large corpus of datasets (OpenAI, 2022; Dubey et al., 2024; Abdin et al., 2024). These models have
026   been applied to a wide range of tasks including virtual assistant (OpenAI, 2022), code development
027   (Roziere et al., 2023), and education/research (Achiam et al., 2023). Typically LLMs undergo the
028   pre-training phase and the post-training phase. The post-training phase adapts the pre-trained LLM to
029   specific tasks, thus is crucial to its successful applications.
030
      The post-training phase of LLMs often has two stages (Abdin et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024):
031   the Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) stage and the preference learning stage. Typical methods for
032   preference learning include Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al.,
033   2022), and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). Given this two-stage
034   process, a natural approach is to performing sequential training, e.g., first perform DPO then SFT or
035   vice verse. For example, the instruct variant of popular open-source models like P HI -3 (Abdin et al.,
036   2024) or L LAMA -3 (Dubey et al., 2024) sequentially undergo SFT and DPO training. Or in other
037   scenarios like continual learning of an aligned model, the process can be interpreted as sequentially
038   performing DPO/RLHF followed by SFT (Tang et al., 2020; Qi et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024).
039   However, sequential training of RLHF and SFT is sub-optimal in terms of the trade-off between
040   preference learning and SFT. When the model is undergoing the second stage of training, it gradually
041   and inevitably forgets about the first stage’s training. In this case, we argue that even regularization
042   like KL divergence used in RLHF/DPO cannot avoid forgetting due to the data distribution shift
043   from the SFT dataset to the preference dataset. An illustration of the sub-optimality of sequential
044   post-training is shown in Figure 1 (left), where we observe that sequential training leads to the
045
      increase of the DPO objective during SFT, resulting in a worse trade-off between the two objectives
      than the method to be introduced in this work. Similar issue has also been observed in, e.g., (Qi et al.,
046
      2023). To overcome this issue and achieve a better trade-off, a naive thought is to mix the preference
047
      objective and SFT objective by minimizing their linear scalarization. However, the naive mixing
048   method is computationally inefficient in practice, since the optimization objectives are different with
049   different formats of data. The increased computational complexity can be observed in Figure 1 (right),
050   where mixing significantly increases the cost. The cost is prohibitively high in LLM training due to
051   the size of the model. Therefore, in this work, we aim to answer the following question:
052
053   Can we design a post-training framework that achieves better trade-off than the sequential training
                   method, while having reduced computational cost than naive mixing?

               results on the forgetting issue of sequential method, which is further supported by empirical

               evidence. Specifically, we prove that sequentially doing DPO and SFT can have a non-

086            ably converges to any desired trade-off between DPO and SFT objectives; and 2) MAXimum
087
               supeRvised fIne-tuninG and Human preference alignmenT (MAXRIGHT) method, which
               adaptively alternates between optimizing RLHF and SFT objectives.
088
           C3) Strong empirical performance on standard benchmarks. Using the LLAMA 3-8 B model,
089
               our methods outperform the sequential approach by up to 3% on the MMLU (1-shot) bench-
090            mark (Hendrycks et al., 2020) and achieve up to a 31% increase in win rate on the RLHF
091            dataset (evaluated by GPT-4- TURBO), with minimal additional computation.
092
093   Technical challenges. The main technical challenge lies in theoretically proving the issue of
094
      forgetting when optimizing SFT and DPO losses (two log-likelihood functions). Existing lower
      bounds for continual learning in non-LLM settings using SGD rely on quadratic objectives (Ding
095
      et al., 2024), where iterate updates are more tractable due to the linear structure of the gradient.
096
097   However, the problem we address involves negative log-likelihood objectives, whose gradients are
098   non-linear with respect to the parameters. This non-linearity complicates the analysis, requiring
099   careful construction of an example that is guaranteed to exhibit undesirable performance. Additionally,
100
      we must analyze the behavior of the updated parameters throughout both stages of optimization and
      derive conditions that relate this behavior to the lower bound of the performance. We successfully
101
      overcome these challenges, and the details of this analysis are provided in Appendix A.2.

405     Sequential RLHF and SFT Issues. Given the importance of both RLHF and SFT in LLM post-
406     training, they are implemented as a sequential recipe (one after the other) on a pre-trained LLM.
407     However, recent studies show that this either hinders the alignment performance (Qi et al., 2023), or
408     fine-tuning performance (Ouyang et al., 2022), depending on the order of applying RLHF and SFT.
409     Reconciling RLHF and SFT. Several methods have been proposed to reconcile RLHF and SFT in
410     post training. One line of work attempt to remove a separate RLHF phase by adding regularization
411     to SFT objective (Hong et al., 2024a), reformulation of SFT objective (Hua et al., 2024), and joint
412     training using demonstrations and RLHF (Li et al., 2024). However, these methods restrict the use of
413     different datasets for RLHF and SFT. Adaptive model averaging (AMA) (Lin et al., 2023) optimizes
414     RLHF and SFT separately to balance objectives, but the original AMA is computationally expensive,
415     requiring three sets of model parameters. While a memory-efficient AMA variant exists, its reliance
416     on imitation learning weakens ties to the original objectives, and the relationship between AMA
417
        weights and optimal trade-offs remains unclear.

      7     C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSION
533
534   In this paper, we have shown both theoretically and empirically that the widely adopted sequential
535   approach to post-training LLMs with RLHF and SFT is sub-optimal, as the model gradually forgets
536   the effects of the initial stage during the second stage of training. Our proposed ALRIGHT and
537
      MAXRIGHT methods address this issue, with ALRIGHT providing theoretical convergence guaran-
      tees and MAXRIGHT demonstrating strong empirical performance. Notably, this improvement is
538
      achieved with minimal additional computational cost, making these methods practical and efficient
539
      alternatives for enhancing both the performance and preference alignment of LLMs.
