011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The ubiquity and success of deep learning is primarily owed to large human
014            datasets; however, increasing interest in personal data raises questions of how
015            to satisfy privacy legislation in deep learning. Machine unlearning is a nascent
016
               discipline centred on satisfying user privacy demands, by enabling data removal
               requests on trained models. While machine unlearning has reached a good level
017
               of maturity in the vision and language domains, applications in audio are largely
018
               underexplored, despite it being a highly prevalent and widely used modality. We
019            address this modality gap by providing the first systematic analysis of machine
020            unlearning techniques covering multiple architectures trained on audio datasets.
021            Our analysis highlights that in audio, existing methods fail to remove data for
022            the most likely case of unlearning – Item Removal. We present a novel Prune
023            and Regrow Paradigm that bolsters sparsity unlearning through Cosine and Post
024            Optimal Pruning, achieving the best unlearning accuracy for 9/12 (75%) of Item
025            Removal experiments and best, or joint best, for for 50% (6/12) of Class Removal
026            Experiments. Furthermore, we run experiments showing performance as unlearn-
027
               ing requests scale, and we shed light on the mechanisms underpinning the success
               of our Prune and Regrow Paradigm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have achieved remarkable success across several applications and
      modalities, such as disease classification (Bondareva et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2024), facial expres-
034
      sion recognition (Canedo & Neves, 2019), and clinical advice (Singhal et al., 2023). Alongside
035
      the success of DNNs, several challenges have arisen, notably adherence to the Right To Be Forgot-
036   ten (RTBF) (a key principle General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (European Parliament &
037   Council of the European Union)) and other removal legislation that is gaining momentum world-
038   wide (APP, 2003; IND, 2023; BUKATY, 2019).
039
      The machine unlearning domain has emerged in response to the RTBF in DNNs, providing a struc-
040
      tured and auditable way of removing data from models, enabling organisations to comply with
041
      GDPR. Naive Retraining, the approach of removing training instances and retraining a new model
042   from scratch, is a largely impractical (Xu et al., 2023; He et al., 2021), but verifiable exact machine
043   unlearning approach. While machine unlearning has verifiable implementations within statistical
044   querying (Cao & Yang, 2015), it is a challenge in deep learning due to the stochastic and incremen-
045   tal nature of training (Nguyen et al., 2022; Bourtoule et al., 2021). As a result, machine unlearning
046   focuses on developing unlearning mechanisms that can remove the influence of data in a computa-
047   tionally inexpensive and verifiable manner, overcoming the costs of Naive Retraining.
048   Despite the expanding use of audio DNNs in applications such as voice recognition (Hughes &
049   Mierle, 2013), event classification (Dong et al., 2020), and health monitoring (Bondareva et al.,
050   2023; Srivastava et al., 2021; Aptekarev et al., 2023; Barata et al., 2019), there exist no studies that
051   address Item and Class Removal for machine unlearning in the audio domain, while there is a cumu-
052   lative total of over 100 studies in other domains (Shaik et al., 2023; Zaman et al., 2023). Studying
053   machine unlearning in audio is vital for safeguarding and maintaining data privacy, upholding the
      RTBF, and reducing the computational costs associated with Naive Retraining.

054
      Our work bridges this modality gap in unlearning literature and systematically studies the effective-
055   ness and adaptability of existing unlearning methods (previously applied to other domains) on audio
056   data – specifically, AudioMNIST, Becker et al. (2023); SpeechCommands V2, Warden (2017) and
057   UrbanSounds8K Salamon et al. (2014) – and across different architectures. Our findings show that,
058   while current methods are effective for Class Removal, they are inadequate for Item Removal, re-
059   garded as the most important unlearning task Nguyen et al. (2022). Our proposed Prune and Regrow
060   Paradigm fills this gap by leveraging dynamic sparsity unlearning for audio models that remove the
061   requirement for extensive empirical studies and, we also show the transferability of this dynamic
062   sparsity method on CIFAR10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009)(Appendix F) where it achieves the best Item
063
      Removal for all architectures. Additionally, our study into unlearning scaling shows that our method
      remains performant as Item Removal requests scale.
064
065   The contributions of this paper are threefold:
066
067          • An in-depth study and evaluation of five existing strong unlearning methods on three dif-
068
               ferent audio datasets and core architecture classes under Item and Class Removal, revealing
               that the majority of current approaches are ineffective on Item Removal requests, necessi-
069
               tating the development of novel methods for audio data.
070
071          • A novel Prune and Regrow Paradigm that achieves the lowest unlearning accuracy gap 9/12
072            (75%) of the time for Item Removal across three audio datasets and three architectures and
073
               transfers to CIFAR10.
074          • An investigation into the scaling laws of unlearning in audio that uncovers the ability of
075            existing and novel unlearning methods to scale for increased removal requests, showing
076            greater applicability of methods in audio.

084   2.1   M ACHINE U NLEARNING P RIMER

086   Strong machine unlearning represents a more practical version of unlearning that deviates from

      machine unlearning that enables a broader array of unlearning methods.

104   Item & Class Removal The most common unlearning request is identified in Item Re-
105   moval (Nguyen et al., 2022). A forget set (Df orget ) is to be removed from the parameter distribu-
106   tion of a model (Mθ ). The task is to remove the influence of Df orget from Mθ with an unlearning
107   mechanism, U, to create M− that is approximately or absolutely equal to a parameter distribution
      of a retrained model (Mθr ) trained on the remaining dataset (Dremain ). A challenging unlearning

110   Class Removal requires the destruction of a decision boundary from Mθ ensuring M− classifies
111   the instances within Df orget as the remaining classes in Dremain .

113   2.2   U NLEARNING M ETHODS
114
115   Numerous machine unlearning methods have been devised in other modalities; this section presents
116   the existing methods we use to evaluate current unlearning capacity for audio. In the Appendix, we
117   describe the benefits and drawbacks of these approaches in Table 6 of Section A.
118   ✶ Gradient Ascent (GA): Gradient Ascent (Graves et al., 2021; Thudi et al., 2022) is one of the
119   simplest strong unlearning methods. When an unlearning request is made, gradient ascent subverts
120   the training strategy and moves in gradient mini-batches in the opposing direction to make a gradient
121   ascent step on Df orget . Accuracy is then recovered through fine tuning on Dremain .
122
      ✶ Fine Tuning (FT): Fine Tuning unlearning (Golatkar et al., 2020a; Liu et al., 2024; Choi & Na,
123
      2023; Wang et al., 2022) leverages catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989) to fulfil re-
124   moval requests. The rudimentary approach employs fine-tuning on Dremain to get M− and remove
125   the influence of instances in Df orget .
126
127
      ✶ Stochastic Teacher (ST): Stochastic Teacher unlearning (Zhang et al., 2023), also known as
      Incompetent Teacher unlearning (Chundawat et al., 2023a), leverages knowledge distillation (Hinton
128
      et al., 2015) for unlearning. The competent teacher is the original Mθ and the stochastic teacher is a
129
      randomly initialised Mθ , Minit . The student starts as Mθ trained on Dtrain . During the unlearning
130   process, for Dremain , the student receives the logits of Mθ but on instances from Df orget , it receives
131   the logits from Minit .
132
133
      ✶ One-Shot Magnitude Prune (OMP): Sparsity unlearning via OMP at 95% sparsity can sig-
      nificantly reduce the approximation gap between Mθr and M− fine-tuned on Dremain (Liu et al.,
134
      2024). OMP takes an Mθ and prunes weights and biases to 0 with a mask that prevents weight
135
      updates when fine-tuning on Dremain .
136
137   ✶ Amnesiac (AM): Amnesiac unlearning (Graves et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020b), seeks to
138   remove Df orget from Mθ by forcing a Mθ to learn random class relationships for Df orget . The
139
      operation is performed by taking Df orget and modifying it to add a random incorrect, yri , label to
      each instance. Following this, the M− is fine-tuned on Dremain .

      2.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
143
      Unlearning literature has devised several metrics to quantify the unlearning performed by an un-
144   learning mechanism. The metrics employed are described below and formalised in the Appendix in
145   Table 8 of Section B.
146
147
      ✶ Unlearning Accuracy (UA): The performance of M− on Df orget . Compared to Mθr .
148   ✶ Remain Accuracy (RA): Performance of M− the remain set Dremain compared to Mθr .
149
      ✶ Test Accuracy (TA): Accuracy on Dtest of M− compared to Mθr
150
151   ✶ Membership Inference Attack Efficacy (MIA Efficacy): Membership Inference attacks (Shokri
152   et al., 2017), established the goal of taking a machine learning model Mθ and an instance (xi , yi )
153   and deducing whether xi , yi ∈ Dtrain or xi , yi ̸∈ Dtrain (Shokri et al., 2017). For machine un-
154   learning MIA Efficacy is the proportion of data points in Df orget classified as non-training instances,
155   y1 (Graves et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024). If MIA Efficacy of M− > Mθr , the Streisand Effect is
156   induced, which can undermine the privacy.
157   ✶ Disparity Average (D AVE): The disparity of M− and Mθr on UA, RA, TA and MIA Efficacy.
158
      ✶ Activation distance (A DIST): The L2 distance of softmax outputs of Mθr compared to M− on
159
      Df orget . It is proxy for the amount Df orget removed from M− .
160
161   ✶ Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JS DIST): A weighted average of KL divergence (Lin, 1991) of
      the loss of M− compared to Mθr on Df orget .

170   to both architecture and learned features.
171   To create a dynamic unlearning method that
172   can respond uniquely to features learned by

      that employs sparsity unlearning. Pruning

      is an effective compression method across

      modalities; literature has shown that its ef-
177   ficacy relates to the functional preservation
178   of the compressed model (Mason-Williams,
179   2024). The sparsity unlearning paradigm has

208
      Cosine Unlearning (CS): By preserving the Cosine Similarity, it is possible to maintain func-
209
      tional similarity and maximally prune a model (Mason-Williams & Dahlqvist, 2024), by getting the
210   minimum distance from the theoretical utopia where Cosine Similarity is 1 and pruning amount is
211   1, as seen in Figure 2. To perform Cosine pruning, a DNN is converted into a vectorised form and
212   pruned at 1% intervals, computing the Cosine Similarity between the two vectorised DNNs (Mason-
213   Williams & Dahlqvist, 2024). An optimisation preserves Cosine Similarity while pruning the model
214   as much as possible, the minimum distance from Uptopia [1,1]. We leverage this to produce CS
215   unlearning as it provides a principled way to identify the correct sparsity per architecture without
      extensive empirical experiments.

      Post Optimal Prune (POP): For POP unlearning, we use the maximum polar point [0,-1] from
217   Utopia, Figure 2, to increase the percentage of pruning to reduce similarity without degrading per-
218   formance to an unacceptable standard. By taking a post-optimal pruning step the overall function is
219   preserved less than with CS. As a critical aspect of machine unlearning is to move away from the
220   Mθ ’s original function towards Mrθ , pruning more of the network increases the ability to remove
221   Df orget .
222
      We employ the Prune and Regrow Paradigm to reinitialize zeroed weights and biases to enable
223   better feature representation when fine tuning on Dremain for both CS and POP unlearning.

      but is ultimately superseded by ST and POP for SpeechCommands and UrbanSounds8K. While

      OMP also attains strong results, it degrades the TA more than POP, reiterating that the one-size-fits-
384
      all approach of OMP is inadequate. However, when considering the transformers, ST is the best

477   loss distribution of Mθr and, therefore, would be dissimilar to Mθ on Df orget .

      Figures 5 and 6 show that, for Item Removal requests, POP shifts the loss distribution so that M−

      resembles the loss distribution of Mθr . The visual depiction reaffirms the understanding that POP is

      6                 C ONCLUSION
514
515
516   Our paper is the first to comprehensively analyse the current state-of-the-art, strong machine un-
517   learning techniques to lay the foundations and advance privacy endeavours within the audio domain
518   for Item and Class Removal. Given that no other such studies exist for audio, our work represents the
519
      first of its kind. Our results show that current unlearning methods are partially effective for the most
      likely request, Item Removal, on lower complexity learning tasks such as AudioMNIST but struggle
520
      to transfer to higher-complexity tasks such as SpeechCommands and UrbanSounds8K. Our study
521
      introduces Cosine and Post Optimal Prune unlearning, using our novel Prune and Regrow Paradigm
522   to address this. Post Optimal Prune was identified as a superior method for Item Removal across
523   all datasets and architectures, regardless of request scaling, signifying an important step towards up-
524   holding privacy in the audio domain. Additionally it provides very competitive and consistent class
525   unlearning capabilities. Through the Prune and Regrow Pardigm we champion unlearning methods
526   that are dynamic to architecture; modality and enable repeated unlearning.
527
      Despite the lack of consistent performance of current methods for Item Removal, Stochastic Teacher
528   and Amnesiac unlearning successfully fulfill Class Removal requests on higher task complexity.
529   However, these results may be related to memorising incorrect representations rather than causing
530   direct unlearning. The results mandate further development of existing and novel methods to re-
531   alise unlearning capabilities in audio. Our unique analysis of the scaling of machine unlearning
532   methods uncovered that, for Item Removal, the most important unlearning case, dynamic unlearn-
533   ing approaches scale the best, while, for Class removal, scaling properties are often shared between
534   effective methods. Furthermore, loss distribution analysis for Item and Class Removal revealed that
535   the Streisand Effect may be a red herring caused by the reliance on black-box evaluation metrics,
536
      which requires further exploration.
537   In summary, this paper contributes a nuanced and novel understanding of machine unlearning within
538   audio and provides two new state-of-the-art methods for unlearning via the Prune and Regrow
539   Paradigm, improving privacy through removal fulfilment for Item Removal, enabling synergy be-
      tween privacy and the application of deep learning in the audio domain and beyond.

      Unlearning details: SGD optimises all impair step optimisations with momentum=0.9, learn-
775
      ing rate=0.01 and batch size=256. However, for GA the learning rate is reduced to lr =
776   (0.01/(|Df orget |/256)). Preliminary experiments showed that once GA exceeded one mini-batch
777   update with a learning rate of 0.01, it became impossible to recover accuracy on Dremain , so this
778   intervention was made to stabilise the impact of GA. While in the image domain, a learning rate of
779   0.01 (Golatkar et al., 2020b) - 0.0001 (Liu et al., 2024) has shown to be successful for GA when
780   using SGD; this was not the case during experimental analysis across all audio datasets. For the
781   experiment of CIFAR10 we use the standard learning rate of 0.01.
782   For all repair step optimisations, SGD is the optimiser with momentum=0.9, learning rate=0.01,
783   and batch size=256 - in line with experiments conducted in the vision domain (Liu et al., 2024).
784   The unlearning methods are applied to each Mθ and compared to the corresponding Mθr and are
785   averaged across five independent experiments.
786
      The experiments are conducted across three scales for Item and Class Removal requests to assess
787
      the capabilities of current and novel unlearning methods comprehensively. For Item Removal, 10%,
788   20%, and 30% of random data from Dtrain is removed, and for Class Removal, 1, 2, and 3 random
789   classes are removed. For Class Removal, it is noted that the classes to be removed are also removed
790   from the test set. Understanding how each method scales to a more complex unlearning request
791   provides better insights into the robustness of each method and confirms the efficacy of current and
792   novel unlearning methods in the audio domain.

      Evaluation metric details: For all accuracy-based metrics, the accuracy of M− is reported, as
797
      well as the disparity between M− and Mθr on Df orget (UA), Dremain (RA) and Dtest (TA). It is
798   important to highlight that UA represents 1-M− (Df orget ). Disparity Average (D AVE) is the aver-
799   age disparity across UA, RA, TA and MIA. For Activation Distance (A DIST) and Jensen Shannon
800   Divergence (JS DIST), the distance is compared between the Mθr and M− outputs for Df orget on
801   the respective softmax and loss outputs for each respective metric. RTE is reported as the reduction
802   of time as a percentage of creating M− against the time required to train Mθr as it is more intuitive
803   than providing the raw time duration; as a result a higher RTE percentage is preferable.
804   To perform the membership inference attack, in line with other literature (Liu et al., 2024; Graves
805   et al., 2021), the attack method introduced by Shokri et al. (2017), described in Section 2.3, is used.
806   Following the implementation of (Liu et al., 2024), the training datasets for the attack model, Mθa ,
807   were composed of a balanced dataset of the baseline models outputs on Dtest and Dtrain for each of
808   the five baseline models for each architecture and dataset. Three independent Mθa are trained based
809   on the loss outputs for each architecture and dataset. The attack models are trained for 50 epochs
      with early stopping.

1247   Due to the close performance of various unlearning methods in Item Removal across different archi-
1248   tectures, it is crucial to investigate the scaling laws of these methods. The objective is to identify any
1249   fluctuations that occur as the size of removal requests increases for both Item and Class Removal.
1250   An effective unlearning method should maintain consistent performance as the scale of unlearning
1251   requests grows, thereby ensuring the protection of privacy.
1252   Figures 21, 22, and 23 present the scaling relationships for VGGish, CCT, and ViT, respectively. In
1253   the context of Item Removal, most unlearning methods demonstrate reasonable scalability. However,
1254   across all examined architectures, the ST method performs inadequately and deteriorates compared
1255   to the baseline across nearly all metrics. Conversely, the methods POP, CS, and OMP exhibit the
1256   best performance, as they remain close to the baseline in terms of Unlearning Accuracy (UA), while
1257   maintaining stable impacts on the other metrics as the number of Item Removal requests increases.
1258   In the scenario of Class Removal, the stability of the various unlearning methods is evident across
1259   the board. Notably, the OMP, CS, and POP pruning methods display similar scaling trends, high-
1260   lighting the overall reliability of pruning strategies in unlearning and the subtle nuances among each
1261   approach. When considering the scaling of Class Removal requests for the CCT and ViT, method
1262   AM emerges as the most effective unlearning strategy in this context.
1263   An unlearning method designed for the audio domain should ideally possess qualities of universality
1264   and demonstrate consistent performance as the complexity of tasks increases. Any methodology that
1265   fails to achieve this would undermine the universal requirement of an effective unlearning technique.
1266   As shown in the results for SpeechCommands and UrbanSounds8K presented in the main body of
1267   the study, there is a slight variation in the efficacy of the unlearning methods when task complexity
1268   is heightened.
