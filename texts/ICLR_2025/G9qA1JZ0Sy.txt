009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Instruction tuning guides the Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) in
012            aligning different modalities by designing text instructions, which seems to be an
013            essential technique to enhance the capabilities and controllability of foundation
014            models. In this framework, Multimodal Continual Instruction Tuning (MCIT)
015            is adopted to continually instruct MLLMs to follow human intent in sequential
016            datasets. We observe existing gradient update would heavily destroy the tuning
017            performance on previous datasets and the zero-shot ability during continual in-
018
               struction tuning. Exponential Moving Average (EMA) update policy owns the
               ability to trace previous parameters, which can aid in decreasing forgetting. How-
019
               ever, its stable balance weight cannot deal with the ever-changing datasets, leading
020
               to the out-of-balance between plasticity and stability of MLLMs. In this paper,
021            we propose a method called Multimodal Large Language Continual Assistant
022            (LLaCA) to address the challenge. Starting from the trade-off prerequisite and
023            EMA update, we propose the plasticity and stability ideal condition. Based on
024            Taylor expansion in the loss function, we find the optimal balance weight is ba-
025            sically according to the gradient information and previous parameters. We au-
026            tomatically determine the balance weight and significantly improve the perfor-
027            mance. Through comprehensive experiments on LLaVA-1.5 in a continual visual-
028            question-answering benchmark, compared with baseline, our approach not only
029
               highly improves anti-forgetting ability (with reducing forgetting from 22.67 to
               2.68), but also significantly promotes continual tuning performance (with increas-
030
               ing average accuracy from 41.31 to 61.89). Our code will be published soon.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      Multimodal Large Language Modals (MLLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
036   vision-language understanding and generation (Li et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Achiam et al.,
037   2023). It generally exits two stages: large scale pre-training and instruction-tuning. Instruction
038   tuning is extremely significant due to guiding MLLMs in following human intent and aligning dif-
039   ferent modalities (Liu et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2023), which seems to be an
040   essential technique to enhance the capabilities and controllability of foundation models. In gen-
041   eral, it enables a unified fine-tuning of image-instruction-output data format and makes the MLLMs
042   easier to generalize to unseen data (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023).
043   As knowledge continuously evolves with the development of human society, new instructions are
044   constantly generated, e.g. the emergence of new concepts or disciplines. How to enable existing
045   MLLMs to assimilate these novel instructions and undergo self-evolution becomes the key challenge
046   (Zhu et al., 2024). To accommodate the new instructions, the most effective strategy is incorporating
047   both old and new instructions for joint training. However, even such relatively lightweight fine-
048   tuning is unaffordable. Most importantly, directly fine-tuning these new instructions would destroy
049   the pre-training knowledge, e.g. catastrophic forgetting (Goodfellow et al., 2013; Li et al., 2019;
050
      Nguyen et al., 2019).
051   Multimodal continual instruction tuning (MCIT) is proposed to address this challenge (He et al.,
052   2023; Chen et al., 2024a). For MLLMs, previous methods, EProj (He et al., 2023), FwT(Zheng et al.,
053   2024), and CoIN (Chen et al., 2024a), utilize the model expansion framework by continually adding
      new branches for the novel instructions, which therefore has less impact on the old knowledge.

075   Considering the essential mechanism of parameter update, we discover that the gradient update
076   might not be a satisfactory choice for MCIT. First of all, we find that the gradient inevitably drives
077   the update of parameters toward the optimization of the new dataset, which causes forgetting. In-
078   stead, EMA adopts a weighted summation between old and new model parameters, which enjoys
079   the natural advantage of keeping old knowledge. However, it faces the challenge of balancing old
080
      and new knowledge in continual tuning, as a fixed EMA weight cannot adapt to the continuously
      evolving datasets, e.g. from location identification dataset GQA to OCR token recognition dataset
081
      OCRVQA. To determine this balance weight, it seems that the gradient actually represents the mag-
082
      nitude or discrepancy between the model and the new instructions. Thus we propose a new ideal
083   condition for MCIT. We use Taylor expansion in the loss function by assuming our update holds the
084   ideal condition, and find the optimal weight is basically according to the gradient information.
085
086
      In this paper, we propose a novel method: Multimodal Large Language Continual Assistant
      (LLaCA). We introduce the stability and plasticity ideal state to formulate simultaneous equations
087
      with the EMA update. By employing the Lagrange multiplier method to solve the equation set,
088
      we provide a theoretical derivation for dynamically updating the weight of EMA in each training
089   iteration. In order to achieve a simple but highly effective update method, we further propose two
090   approximate compensation mechanisms.

092
      We adopt LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) as the backbone and insert the efficient-tuning parameters:
      LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) in the LLM (7B-Vicuna). In the whole continual tuning process, only one
093
      set of LoRA parameters and a projection layer are trained by incorporating our method, which can
094
      greatly reduce the memory and computation burden, as shown in Figure 2(a). We continually fine-
095   tune on visual-question-answering datasets and verify performances of the tuned model on both in-
096   and out-of-domain datasets. Experimental results consistently demonstrate excellent anti-forgetting,
097   continual tuning, and zero-shot generalization performance of our method. Additionally, without
098   saving exemplars or expanding modules, our method can be free of privacy leakage or memory
099   explosion. In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:

      easily applied in a wide range of fine-tuned methods and MLLMs. Additionally, it costs few tuning

      resources, due to introducing two approximations.

127   2       R ELATED W ORK
128
129   Multi-modal Large Language Models. MLLMs own strong reasoning and complex contextual un-
130
      derstanding capabilities, which can generate text sequences according to the input images and texts.
      With a frozen large language model and visual tower, BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023) bridged the gap be-
131
      tween image and text modality through Qformer structure. Inspired by the notable reasoning power
132
      of instruction tuning in LLMs, e.g., LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and GPT (Floridi & Chiriatti,
133   2020; Achiam et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) and MiniGPT4 (Zhu et al., 2023) adopted
134   instruction tuning with only training a linear projection layer to align the image-text modalities. Re-
135   cently, LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) and MiniGPT5 (Zheng et al., 2023) refined the instructions
136   and improved the performance across a wider range of multi-modality datasets.
137
      Multimodal Continual Instruction Tuning Work: (Zhang et al., 2023) first proposed the contin-
138
      ual instruction tuning with LLMs. Following it, (He et al., 2023) and (Chen et al., 2024a) designed
139   distinct cross-modality continual instruction tuning benchmarks with InstructBLIP and LLaVA, re-
140   spectively. Specifically, (He et al., 2023) adopted unique linear projection layers for each dataset and
141   utilized a key-query driven mechanism to infer the dataset identifier. (Chen et al., 2024a) employed
142   the MOELoRA paradigm, assigning different LoRA weights to different datasets based on expert
143   knowledge. Nevertheless, they still faced the issue of forgetting due to repeated training.

      3       P RELIMINARY

147   Multimodal Continual Instruction Tuning Definition: MCIT (He et al., 2023) is defined to lever-
148   age MLLMs to continually instruction-tune on new datasets without costly re-training. Compared
149   with traditional continual learning, MCIT differs in that it pays more attention to effectively lever-
150   aging the rich natural language instructions to prevent catastrophic forgetting and encourage knowl-
151   edge transfer. MCIT can be described as a set of datasets Tseq = {t1 , ..., tT } that arrives sequentially.
152   Note that datasets in the stream can be any type and are not restricted to specific categories or do-
                                                                                                 tj
153   mains. Each dataset tj ∈ Tseq has a natural language instruction I tj , training set Dtrain    and test set
        tj
154   Dtest . The goal of MCIT is to learn a single model f from Tseq sequentially.

345   Implementation: We adopt LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024b) as our backbone with inserted LoRA (Hu

347   we freeze the vision encoder and LLM, with only training the projector and LoRA. We follow
348   the datasets of the CoIN benchmark (Chen et al., 2024a), including ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022),
349   TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019), VizWiz (Gurari et al., 2018),

      ScienceQA, TextVQA, GQA, VizWiz, VQAv2, and OCR-VQA. Additionally, we validate all the
      methods on two distinct instructions. The first is normal and only owns one kind of instruction
352
      template. While the other would be more challenging due to being equipped with ten kinds of
353
      instruction templates. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, excluding Table 3.
354
355   Compared Methods: We compare the LLaCA method against eight methods including (1) zero-
356   shot and (2) full fine-tune performance from (Chen et al., 2024a); (3) LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) as

      our baseline (throughout the paper, the baseline consistently refers to LoRA Fine-Tuning), which
      prepends LoRA parameter efficient tuning paradigm into LLM and only tunes the linear projector

      and LoRA parameters; (4) MoELoRA (Chen et al., 2024a); (5) LWF (Li & Hoiem, 2017); (6)
359
      EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017); (7) PGP (Qiao et al., 2024a); (8) Upper-bond, the best accuracy in

361   method. The detailed descriptions of the above methods can be found in Appendix A.6.

      6     C ONCLUSION
477
      To enable MLLMs to possess the ability of multimodal continual instruction tuning and further re-
478
      sist forgetting, we propose a novel method called LLaCA. Combined with the exponential moving
479
      average, the proposed method can protect previous knowledge and incorporate new knowledge at
480   the same time. By solving a set of equations based on the Lagrange multiplier method, we obtain the
481   self-adaption weight of EMA in each update process. Subsequently, two compensation mechanisms
482   are further introduced to alleviate the computational costs. Experiments show that our approach not
483   only owns excellent anti-forgetting and continual tuning ability but also well zero-shot performance.
484   Additionally, our method needs a few extra computation costs and memory usage. Due to compu-
485   tational resource constraints, our current focus is primarily on continual instruction tuning. In the
      future, we aim to extend our method to continual pre-training scenarios.

       A.6    C OMPARED M ETHODS
1027
1028   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) prepends LoRA parameter efficient tuning paradigm into LLM. In the
1029   training stage, it only trains the linear projector and LoRA parameters, with frozen vision encoder
1030   and LLM; MoELoRA (Chen et al., 2024a) is based on the LoRA, and the number of experts
1031   for each MoE layer is set to 2; LWF (Li & Hoiem, 2017) calculates the results of the new dataset
1032   samples on both the old and new models. After that, it calculates the distillation loss and adds it to the
1033   loss function as a regularization penalty term. EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) considers the change
1034
       of the training parameters and proposes the specific parameters changing loss as a regularization
       penalty. PGP (Qiao et al., 2024a) introduces a gradient projection method for efficient parameters,
1035
       and changes the gradient direction orthogonal to the previous feature subspace.

       LLM is Vicuna-7B. The inserted LoRA in each module layer of LLM has a rank of 128. For each
