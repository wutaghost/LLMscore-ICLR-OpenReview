011                                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                         The Small Language Models (SLMs, or on-device LMs) (Lu et al., 2024) is a
014                         concept corresponding to the Large Language Model (LLM), which has signifi-
015                         cantly fewer parameters and is typically deployed on low-end devices, like mobile
016                         phones (Liu et al., 2024) and single-board computers (e.g. Raspberry Pi). Unlike
017                         LLMs, which utilize the increasing model size for better generalization, SLMs are
018
                            expected to adjust the exact deployment environment changes. Furthermore, most
                            edge applications have battery life concerns, which have never been considered in
019
                            the GPU servers for data centres. Targeting these two issues, this paper focuses
020
                            on the token embedding compression for adaptivity and low energy requirements
021                         in edge applications. We propose a training-free model compression approach
022                         based on the Tensor-Train Decomposition (TTD), whereby each pre-trained token
023                         embedding vector is converted into a lower-dimensional Matrix Product State
024                         (MPS). We then comprehensively investigate the low-rank structures extracted by
025                         this approach, regarding the compression ratio, language task performance, latency
026                         and energy consumption on a typical low-end device (i.e. Raspberry Pi). Taking
027                         the sub-billion parameter versions of GPT-2/Cerebres-GPT and OPT as examples,
028                         the model compressed with our approach can achieve a comparable language task
029
                            performance to the original model with around 2.0× embedding layer compression,
                            while the energy consumption of single query drops by half.

038   putting such capable models on lower-end devices. These models are called small language models
039   (SLMs) (Lu et al., 2024) or on-device language models (Liu et al., 2024; Mehta et al., 2024; hfs).

      SLMs may have less than one billion parameters (Mehta et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Laskaridis et al.,
055   2024). Though such a size is already a few tenths or even hundreds of what common LLMs usually
056   are, for some low-end devices, it can still be a burden. As listed in (Liu et al., 2024, Fig. 2), some
057   prevalent mobile devices (e.g. iPhone 14 and iPhone 15) only have 6GB DRAM. For some small
058   language models like Gemma2-2B, it causes a system crash if running its uncompressed version on
059   Raspberry Pi-5 with 8GB DRAM.
060
      Compared with LLMs, SLMs on low-end devices have different layer compositions of the model
061   and different on-board operations due to the absence of server-level GPUs. As shown in Fig. 1,
062   around half of investigated open-source models have more than 20% parameters attributed to the
063   token embedding layers, which is consistent with the statements in (Liu et al., 2024, 2.2.3). Also,
064   since no server-level GPU is on board to support massive parallel operations for matrix multiplication,
065   block-wise approaches that rely on parallelism (Dao et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024) are not suitable.
066
      To this end, this paper proposes TensorSLM, a tensor-based approach to compress SLMs for low-end
067   devices (i.e. Raspberry Pi without GPU). Together with matrix-based low-rank approaches (Chen
068   et al., 2018a; Hrinchuk et al., 2020; Lioutas et al., 2020; Acharya et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Hsu
069   et al., 2022; Dao et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024), this kind of approach forms a broader field named
070   low-rank factorization. The comparison of these works regarding methodologies (e.g. matrix/tensor,
071   with/without training) and applications (e.g. high-end/low-end devices, large/small models) are
072   clarified in Sec. 3. Compared with two-dimensional matrices or their finer-grained block-wise
073   forms (Chen et al., 2018a; Dao et al., 2022), higher-order tensors provide more diverse representation
074   alternatives with their inter-order information, which is more suitable for small-size models to solve
075
      complex tasks. This superiority is more pronounced if there is no fine-tuning data to adjust the model
      parameters for the exact application environments.
076
077   The contributions of this paper are summarised as follows:
078
079   1. We provide a systematic analysis of LLMs on high-end GPU servers and SLMs on low-end edge
080      devices to address the two unique requirements of SLM compression: adaptability and low energy.
081   2. As far as we know, we are the first to compress SLMs for low-end device use cases, with low-rank
082      factorization. We adjust Tensor-Train Decomposition for non-parallel operations in the forwarding
083      passes, where block-wise approaches (Dao et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024) are incompetent.
084   3. We give the measured latency and estimated energy consumption of SLMs on the typical low-end
085      device, Raspberry Pi 51 , and it comes out that our approach reduces half of the inference energy
086      with negligible latency increase.
087   4. We evaluated both simple and complex language tasks. We found that our tensor-based approach is
088      better at unprompted and unconstrained question answering than the matrix-based SVD approach,
089      and herein shed light on selecting appropriate algebraic structures according to the tasks.

096

097   that require adapting to the environment with limited resources on lower-end devices. A common
098   approach to adapting to the dynamic environment is updating the vocabulary according to the changes
099   in input text distribution Chen et al. (2018a). The reasons for this distribution change vary from case
100   to case. For example, new user registration, or the frequently used tokens update with the users’
101   changing daily lives.

195
196   The field of language model compression with low-rank factorization has been booming in recent
197
      years. The recent relevant works are summarized inTab. 2. We can observe that for the current existing
      works, some are specialized for embedding layers (Chen et al., 2018a; Hrinchuk et al., 2020; Wang
198
      et al., 2023; Bałazy et al., 2021; Acharya et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2015) while others are not (Chekalina
199
      et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2022; Dao et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024). However, all of
200   these require an extra training process, such as fine-tuning, meta-learning (Chen et al., 2018a; 2021;
201   Hsu et al., 2022; Bałazy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2015; Dao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Qiu et al.,
202   2024) and training from scratch (Hrinchuk et al., 2020; Chekalina et al., 2023b).
203
      There are two limitations to this extra training: 1) extra training involves additional computation
204
      and training data, which may be unavailable for low-end devices; 2) training the language model
205
      from scratch discards the valuable knowledge stored in the weights of the original models. However,
206   we only focus on training-free low-end device applications. For a more detailed discussion of these
207   relevant works, please refer to Appx. B.

309   5.2   L ANGUAGE M ODEL I NFERENCE P ROCESS WITH THE C OMPRESSED E MBEDDINGS
310   The original inference process with embedding vectors is as follows: when the encoded texts (sepa-
311   rated as tokens) are forwarded to the embedding layer, the embedding layer outputs the embedding
312   vectors according to the input tokens; the embedding layer here acts like a look-up table. The
313   embedding vectors are then forwarded to the hidden layers of the transformer, whose size is the same
314   as the dimension of the embedding vectors. Thus, if there is no internal change in the hidden layers,
315   the dimension of the embedding vectors should compile with the dimension of the hidden layers. The
316   compressed embeddings should be reconstructed to the original dimension to enable the forwarding
317
      process. This inference happens at the application phase shown in the upper right of Fig. 2.
318   Thus just before forwarding embedding vectors to the hidden layers, the memory usage increases
             PN
319   from l k=1 rk−1 Ik rk to ld. However, given that the vocabulary size V is normally much larger
320   than the input token number l, that means V ≫ l. Thus our approach can still significantly reduce
321   the memory usage if the embedding layer takes a significant part of the whole model parameters. The
322   reconstruction process follows the tensor contraction in Eq. (7), turning the TT cores {G (k) } into a
323   N -order tensor X according to Eq. (1), and then vectorizing X into a full-size embedding vector
      according to Appx. A.2.

      6.3.1                         C OMPRESSION R ATIO AND L ANGUAGE TASK P ERFORMANCE .
426
427   Language Modelling. The language modelling performance and the compression ratio of different
428   tensor orders and models are shown in Fig. 3. There is no general conclusion as to whether higher-
429   order tensors are better than lower-order tensors, but roughly speaking, compression ratio and
430   language modelling performance in Fig. 3e-Fig. 3h are better than those in Fig. 3i-Fig. 3j. This
431   implies that there may exist a high-dimensional representation in the weight of embedding layers,
      no more than six-dimensional and most probably around three-dimensional. The best compression


                                                                                                                                                   8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      cases (higher compression ratio with negligible drop in accuracy) occur when N = 3 (also as shown
433   in Fig. 3l and 3m), implying the optimal feature representation of the token embedding vectors
434   may be three-dimensional. On the other hand, due to the combination of tensor size and TT ranks
435   exponentially exploding, we could not test all the possible combinations. However, we can still
436   observe that independent of the tensor orders and the models used for the compression, significant
437   language modelling performance loss tends to appear when the compression ratio exceeds 2.0×. We
438   further compared our proposed approach with the Tucker decomposition in Fig. 3k with the same
439   tensorization strategy in Sec. 5.1, and found our adopted Tensor-Train Decomposition outperforms
440   the Tucker Decomposition in perplexity.
441
442
      Sentiment Classification. The results of the sentiment classification task also show that the robust-
443
      ness of larger-scale models (Cerebras-590M and Cerebras-1.3B) is better than that of the smaller
444
      models (Cerebras-111M and Cerebras-256M), similar to the trend in language modelling tasks
445   mentioned above. The compressed larger-scale models tend to outperform the original model in
446   precision and F1-score, indicating that our compression improves the ability of the larger models to
447   recognize the positive texts. In contrast, the smaller models tends to have worse performance when
448   the compression ratio increases.
449
      A notable observation is that in both language modelling (Fig. 3e to 3j) and sentiment classifica-
450
      tion (Fig. 3a to 3d), the larger models (GPT-2-M, GPT-2-L, GPT-2-XL, CerebrasGPT-590M and
451   CerebrasGPT-1.3B) are more robust to the compression ratio increase, compared with smaller models
452   (DistilGPT2, GPT-2, CerebrasGPT-111M and CerebrasGPT-256M), especially when the compression
453   ratio is less than 1.0×. This is probably because the embedding layers take a smaller proportion of
454   the entire model, and it also sheds light on the fact that TensorSLM might be used to improve the
455   language task performance for large-scale models.
456
457
458   Zero-shot Reasoning. Since SLMs are incapable of the tasks that are too complex, we only
459   evaluate the relatively simple reasoning tasks (e.g. those that do not involve multi-hop questioning,
460   mathematics or multilingual), and the results are shown in in Appx. E. The bold numbers are the
461
      cases that outperform the uncompressed models, or the best in all the compressed cases.
462   Our approach has a higher chance of achieving better average reasoning task scores than the SVD-
463   based approach, which implies that our tensors are better at extracting implicit representations in
464   small-size models than matrices. Moreover, in our evaluation, our approach generally has higher
465   scores than the SVD-based approach in ARC-challenge and BoolQ. Both of these datasets are more
466   unprompted and unconstrained compared to the other evaluated datasets. This fact implies that our
467
      approach may be better at these difficult, unconstrained reasoning tasks.

      7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524   This paper focuses on the two unique requirements of Small Language Models (SLMs) deployed on
525   low-end devices (i.e. Raspberry Pi) - adaptivity and low energy, and proposes a training-free approach
526   to compress each token embedding based on Tensor-Train Decomposition. The proposed approach
527   can cope with a dynamic environment by adjusting its vocabulary and “exchanging” memory with
528   computation for longer battery life. The experimental evaluation covers GPT-2/CerebrasGPT and
529   OPT series models, as well as both simple language modelling/classification and more complex zero-
530   shot reasoning tasks. We also systematically measured the on-board inference latency of Raspberry
531   Pi 5 and estimated the inference energy costs based on our rigorous analysis of computation and
532
      memory complexity. We found that the estimated inference energy cost is cut half off with neglectable
      inference latency and language task performance drop.
533
534   There are both limitations to our work and, more importantly, rather a broad range of future work
535   following our proposed TensorSLM. Firstly, the tensorized embedding layers do not natively compile
536   with the hidden layers, so tensorized hidden layers are required. Also, although tensor operations,
537   like contraction, do not require much memory, they might need more arithmetic operations on the
538
      CPU, thus requiring accelerated tensor operations.

      A.2     T ENSORS AND T ENSOR O PERATIONS
757
758   This section gives brief mathematical preliminaries of tensor algebra, and basic knowledge in LLMs
759   to facilitate the understanding of our proposed methodology in Sec. 5.
760
761   Order-N Tensor. An order-N real-valued tensor is a multi-dimensional array, denoted by a cal-
762   ligraphic font, e.g., A ∈ RI1 ×···×IN , where N is the order of the tensor (i.e., number of modes),
763   and In (1 ≤ n ≤ N ) is the size (i.e., the dimension) of its n-th mode. Matrices (denoted by bold
764   capital letters, e.g., A ∈ RI1 ×I2 ) can be seen as order-2 tensors (N = 2), vectors (denoted by bold
765   lower-case letters, e.g., a ∈ RI ) can be seen as order-1 tensors (N = 1), and scalars (denoted by
766   lower-case letters, e.g., a ∈ R) are order-0 tensors (N = 0).
767
768   Tensor Entries. The (i1 , . . . , iN )-th entry of an order-N tensor is denoted by ai1 ,··· ,iN ∈ R, where
769   in = 1, . . . , In for n = 1, . . . , N . A tensor fiber is a vector of tensor entries obtained by fixing all but
770   one index of the original tensor (e.g., a:,i2 ,i3 ,...,iN ∈ RI1 ). Similarly, a tensor slice is a matrix of tensor
771   entries obtained by fixing all but two indices of the original tensor (e.g., A:,:,i3 ,i4 ,...,iN ∈ RI1 ×I2 ).
772
773   Tensorization. A vector a = (a1 , a2 , . . . , aI1 I2 ···IN ) ∈ RI1 I2 ···IN , can be tensorized (or “folded”,
774   “reshaped”) into an order-N tensor A ∈ RI1 ×I2 ×···×IN , so that

      B     R ELATED W ORK IN D ETAIL
811
812
      Low-rank factorization can break the high-dimensional weight matrices into smaller matrices or
813
      tensors, so that the overall size of the model can be shrunk. According to the dimensions of the
814   structure that the original weight matrices are broken into, these approaches can be divided into
815   matrix-based and tensor-based.
816
817   Matrix-based Approaches. A straightforward way to shrink the model size is to decompose
818   weight matrices via singular value decomposition (SVD) (Acharya et al., 2019), which can be
819   further improved by the weighted approach considering the model performance afterwards (Hsu
820   et al., 2022), knowledge distillation (Lioutas et al., 2020; Mao et al., 2020) and pruning (Mao
821   et al., 2020). There are also some block-wise decomposition approaches used in language model
822   compression, like Kronecker Products (Tahaei et al., 2022; Edalati et al., 2022) and data-driven
823   block-wise partitioning (Chen et al., 2018a; 2021).
824   (Dao et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024) used the block-diagonal matrices to reduce the FLOPs in the
825   linear layers computation, with the bonus of shrinking the model size. However, our paper focuses
826   on reducing the parameters of embedding layers, and there is no monotonous relationship between
827   the FLOPs (computation cost) and parameters (memory usage) (Lin et al., 2020). Also, their
828   investigated matrix multiplication only occurs in feed-forward layers, thus their approaches do not
829   fit the embedding layer compression. Moreover, block-diagonal matrices are optimised for GPUs
830
      for better parallelization. Our aim of minimizing the number of parameters, makes it optimized for
      lower-end edge devices rather than GPUs. Indeed, on Raspberry Pi 5, the additional forwarding
831
      latency due to compressed embeddings (0.330 - 0.364ms /token in Tab. 4) is even faster than that
832
      on GPU (measured as 0.463ms /token in our setting), since there is no parallelization during this
833   forwarding process.
834
835
      Tensor-based Approaches. Despite some efforts to use tensor decomposition to compress the
836   language model size, all come with an extra training process. The works in (Abronin et al., 2024)
837   use Kronecker decomposition with row-column permutation during the GPT model fine-tuning
838   process, while (Hrinchuk et al., 2020) and (Chekalina et al., 2023b) propose a tensor-train structured
839   embedding layer and GPT model respectively, yet both train the new-structured model from scratch.
