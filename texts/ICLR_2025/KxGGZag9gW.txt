009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Low-Rank Adapters (LoRA) are lightweight components that have made fine-
012                tuning large models on domain-specific tasks inexpensive. This has resulted in an
013                abundance of adapters in a growing open-source public community. We ask the
014                question: can these adapters be used to inform and further streamline adaptation to
015                new tasks? We introduce EigenLoRA, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method
016
                   that uses trained adapters to perform fast adaptation on new domains with orders
                   of magnitude fewer parameters than LoRA. Our method finds a principal subspace
017
                   that aligns with the domain of the trained adapters. This allows for efficient and
018
                   fast adaptation to new tasks in this domain by simply learning coefficients on the
019                principal components of this subspace. Furthermore, EigenLoRA makes inference
020                time task-switching memory efficient. Instead of saving and loading whole LoRAs,
021                EigenLoRA can simply load lightweight coefficients. EigenLoRA1 works across a
022                variety of domains and tasks and is a viable solution for edge-based and efficient
023                personalization applications.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
      Rapid progress in the fields of natural language processing (Touvron et al., 2023) and computer
028
      vision (Rombach et al., 2021) has fueled the development of ever-growing large-scale models where
029   training billions of parameters has become commonplace. The size and complexity of these models
030   have made it infeasible for the average researcher to train or finetune them on downstream tasks and
031   datasets. In order to overcome these challenges, there has been an increased interest in parameter-
032   efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods like adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022; Luo et al.,
033   2023), low rank adaptation (LoRA) methods (Hu et al., 2021; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024),
034   prompt-based methods (Lester et al., 2021; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023; Fischer et al., 2024), etc.
035   In particular, LoRA and related follow-up works (Meng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) have garnered
036   significant interest due to their simplicity and effectiveness. This simplicity of usage has led to a
037   proliferation of low-rank adapters within an expanding open-source community. These adapters
038   contain compressed information from their training data, which may or may not be publicly available,
039   inspiring an examination of whether adapter weights can be used to inform and enhance the efficiency
040   of adaptation to new tasks. Recent work has shown that weight updates to deep neural networks
041   occurs in low-dimensional invariant subspaces (Kwon et al., 2024). This raises a possible hypothesis
042   that these LoRA adapters may also share such a principal subspace that could be reused without
043
      having to search for it from scratch during the training of new adapters. We introduce EigenLoRA,
      a PEFT method that extracts a set of information-dense principal components defining a subspace,
044
      by decomposing the weights of a given set of trained adapters. This allows us to reduce the number
045
      of learnable parameters (extending up to 100× less than LoRA) and achieve faster optimization
046   (upto 2×) of new adapters. Moreover, EigenLoRA allows for more memory-efficient inference
047   using multiple task adapters, especially benefiting edge devices (Liu et al., 2022). We also present a
048   theoretical analysis of our method providing an approximation bound on reconstruction error when
049   projecting to principal subspaces; and our experiments across a wide range of vision and language
050   related tasks demonstrate its wide applicability.

088   2    R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   Low-Rank Adaptation refers to modeling neural network weight updates as a function of low-rank
091   matrices instead of training the entire weight matrix. This is a well-established line of research
092   starting from Burer-Monteiro factorization (Burer & Monteiro, 2003), with a recent resurgence by Hu
093
      et al. (2021) (LoRA), who used it as a technique to finetune LLMs; and other related variants (Ma
      et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2024). However, with rapid growth in the scale of models,
094
      Low-Rank Adaptation has also become relatively expensive; for example, LoRA with a rank of
095
      16 on GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020) requires 75.5 million parameters. Consequently, more efficient
096   low-rank fine-tuning methods are being developed. Mixture of experts models (Huang et al., 2023;
097   Wu et al., 2024; Diao et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2018) have been proposed as a
098   method to adapt to new domains using a mixture of low-rank modules. But these approaches typically
099   require a substantial number of high-quality adapters to work efficiently (Ku et al., 2024), which can
100   significantly increase the model memory requirements (Zhou et al., 2022). Furthermore, complex
101   gating or weighting mechanisms utilized with these models can exhibit training instability (Zoph
102   et al., 2022).
103   Recent methods have aimed to learn better subspaces for low-rank optimization, primarily by decom-
104   posing model weights into singular vectors for improved training. Meng et al. (2024) demonstrate
105   that initializing LoRA with singular vectors is superior to random initialization, while Sharma
106   et al. (2023) find that removing minor singular components enhances robustness. Using randomly
107   initialized principal components (Kopiczko et al., 2023) or weight matrices (Koohpayegani et al.,
      2024) has also been explored to reduce the number of trainable parameters. However, as shown


                                                        2

108
      in Section 4, random initialized subspaces may not be very useful. This is intuitive as the random
109   subspace may not have an overlap with domain-specific principal subspaces. On the other hand,
110   EigenLoRA uses trained adapters to extract a principal subspace suitable for a given domain of tasks
111   resulting in a better subspace initialization than and parameter efficiency. Given our focus on resource
112   and computation efficiency in this work, we focus primarily on LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) as our main
113   baseline, but EigenLoRA can be used with any PEFT method like Liu et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
114   (2023) where task-specific weights can be analyzed together.

      3.1   T HEORETICAL M OTIVATION
123
      Let W ∈ Rm×n be a linear transformation matrix from vector space Rm to Rn . If W is a full-rank
124
      (with rank min(n, m)) transformation matrix, then it represents all possible linear mappings between
125   the two spaces. In contrast, LoRA adapters are defined as two matrices B ∈ Rm×r and A ∈ Rr×n
126   such that BA has the same size as W but rank r < min(n, m). These matrices combine to yield a
127   linear transformation between the same spaces Rm to Rn , but cannot span the entire space of such
128   mappings. Hence, LoRA adapters provide a parameter-efficient (typically, m · r + r · n < m · n)
129   way to adapt large models by learning only “important” directional updates confined to a subspace.
130
      Moreover, many downstream adapters have been found to reuse the same “important” directions
131   (Meng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). We hypothesize that LoRA adapters may reuse principal
132   subspaces that are fundamental for different domains of tasks. Once identified, task-specific weights
133   can be found in these smaller subspaces rather than the whole weight space. To illustrate this idea
134   clearly, we first define a space of tasks that are expressible using linear transformation matrices.
135   Definition 1 (Linear Transformation Tasks). Let T = {t : x ∈ Rn → y ∈ Rr } denote a set of linear
136   tasks where: ∀ t ∈ T , ∃ Wt ∈ Rr×n such that y = Wt x + ϵt , ∀ x, y. Here, ϵt denotes the noise.
137
138   A LoRA weight matrix at any layer does the same transformation. Without loss of generality, assume
139   r < n and let the transformation matrix Wt ∈ Rr×n be interpreted as r n-dimensional vectors:
140   wt1 , ..., wtr ∈ Rn . Finding LoRA weights is equivalent to finding sets of these r vectors in Rn . Next,
141   we define a subspace in Rn .
142   Definition 2 (Subspace). Let S k,n = {a1 , ..., ak } (k ≤ n) be a set of linearly independent vectors
                                             Pk
143   ∈ Rn . Denote Ŝ k,n = span(S k,n ) = { i=1 αi ai ∀i, αi ∈ R} as the subspace elicited by S k,n .
144
145   Vectors in a subspace Ŝ k,n lie in Rn but are constrained to a smaller region. Similar to Tripuraneni
146   et al. (2021), we use the following metric to measure distances between subspaces and vectors.
147
      Definition 3 (Distance between subspace and a vector). Denote distance between a vector v and
148
      subspace Ŝ k,n as sin θ(v, Ŝ k,n ), the sine of the principal angle θ between them. The principal angle
149
      is the smallest possible angle between a vector in the subspace and v.
150
151   Next, we introduce the idea of domain-specific subspaces.
152
153   Definition 4 (Principal Subspace). A subset of tasks Td ⊆ T constitutes a domain if, ∃ Sdk,n , ∀
154   t ∈ Td , such that sin θ(wti , Ŝdk,n ) = 0 ∀i ∈ 1, ..., r. Denote Ŝdk,n as the principal subspace of Td .
155
156   Here, sin θ(wti , Ŝdk,n ) = 0 implies that all the vectors constituting the weight matrix Wt for all tasks
157   t, lie inside the subspace spanned by Sdk,n . The existence of principal subspaces (PS) is trivially
158   guaranteed for all domains d, e.g., when k = n. But, domains whose principal subspaces exist for
159   k ≪ n would be practically useful. Even an Approximate Principal Subspace (APS), where the
160   distance is small, i.e., sin θ(wti , Ŝdk,n ) < δ for some δ ≈ 0, would be useful, as we illustrate in
161   Section 4. First, we present a theorem bounding the approximation error for recovering weights of
      new linear transformation tasks using a given APS characterized by δ.

      Memory-efficient Inference In an application where multiple adapters are used (for example,
255
      image generation in multiple styles like photorealistic, sketch, etc.) frequently swapping between
256   LoRA adapters adapters can be expensive. Either all adapters need to be loaded in GPU memory
257   together (requiring larger memory) or they need to be loaded from CPU memory/disk (slow). With
258   EigenLoRA, the EigenLoRA PCs are task-invariant while task-specific coefficients are lighter weight,
259   allowing for more efficiency. For N LoRAs, the memory footprint is O(N rn). For EigenLoRAs, it is
                                                            n
260   O(Kn + N rK). As r, K ≪ n, EigenLoRA becomes K           times more memory efficient asymptotically.
261   For example, serving N = 8 LoRAHub adapters (Section 4.2.2) would require ≈ 5× less adapter
262   memory than LoRA. This would be especially beneficial for mobile devices with small memory.

264   Extreme Parameter Efficiency Instead of stacking LoRA weight matrices, we can flatten them
265   and treat them as vectors. In this case, the EigenLoRA (we call it EigenLoRAflat ) PCs are r · n
266   dimensional (instead of n) and α is a K dimensional vector that linearly combines these EigenLoRA
267   PCs to produce an approximation of our weight matrix. This results in an additional reduction factor
268   r (the rank of each adapter needs to be fixed) in the number of parameters to learn but comes at
269   an expense of some more model memory. This is analogous to a mixture-of-experts setting (where
      experts are EigenLoRAflat PCs). More details are deferred to Appendix A.2.2.

      4     E XPERIMENTS AND A NALYSIS
271
272
      In this section, we demonstrate the efficacy and versatility of EigenLoRA in a wide range of task
273
      complexities, modalities, and model architectures. We evaluated EigenLoRA on a simpler aligned
274   domain setting with image classification tasks (Section 4.1); and a difficult diverse domain setting
275   with NLP tasks (Section 4.2). We show that EigenLoRA requires substantially fewer parameters to
276   achieve parity with or even exceed the performance of LoRA (Tables 1, 2, 3). Furthermore, it achieves
277   loss convergence at a similar or faster rate (Figure 4), serving as a cost-effective alternative to random
278   initialization (LoRA) and other existing initialization methods (Meng et al., 2024). Finally, we
279   illustrate its memory-efficient inference capabilities with a text-to-image generation Stable Diffusion
280   model (Rombach et al., 2021) (Section 4.3).

282   4.1    A LIGNED D OMAIN – I MAGE C LASSIFICATION
283
284
285   In this experiment, we test EigenLoRA with a pre-trained Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy
286   et al., 2021) which is adapted for image classification on 3 data sets. The datasets are randomly
287   divided into 5-6 sub-datasets with no overlap in categories, similar to continual learning (Kaushik
288   et al., 2021) and federated learning (Shenaj et al., 2023) setups. Since the sub-datasets originate
289   from a common dataset, their tasks are more aligned corresponding to the case where δ is small (see
290   Section 3.1). For adaptation, we used LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2023) to
291   compare with our EigenLoRA. For completeness, we evaluated each method under multiple settings
292   and report the mean performance across all sub-datasets.
293

295   LoRA and VeRA, and followed their respective hyperparameter recommendations to train adapters

297
      sub-datasets to calculate EigenLoRA PCs (Algorithm 1) (leave-one-out). We then learn the coefficient
      matrix α for the left-out task using the method described in Section 3.2. Each method is finetuned for
298
      10 epochs. Other experimental details are available in Appendix A.1.

338   EigenLoRA (K = 32) is able to match LoRA performance with 100× fewer trainable parameters,
339   while outperforming VeRA. EigenLoRA can effectively extract a useful principal subspace even
340   from diverse domains and robustly adapt to new domains. In this setup, we also evaluate the weight
341   initialization speed-up capability of EigenLoRA. This was recently studied by Meng et al. (2024)
342   (PiSSA) who initialize their LoRA matrices with the principal directions of the pre-trained weight

344   compare its training convergence with other methods. The training loss graphs in Figure 4 demonstrate

346
      LoRA, underscoring the importance of our extracted principal subspace. The reason for VeRA’s poor

347
      these random yet fixed weight components that may not align with task-critical principal components.

509   5    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND O PPORTUNITIES
510
511   We introduce EigenLoRA, a PEFT method that recycles trained adapters by finding a task-invariant
512   principal subspace. This allows for more efficient training of new adapters and inference with multiple
513   adapters. Through experiments, we showed that EigenLoRA works and provides practical benefits in
514   a wide range of scenarios. Our method has the potential to mitigate the perpetually widening compute
515   resource gap (Ahmed & Wahed, 2020; Besiroglu et al., 2024) and reduce the environmental cost of
516   training and using AI models (Wu et al., 2021; Ligozat et al., 2021). It also holds promise for training
517   personalized models (Tan et al., 2024) on low-resource devices, in privacy-critical use-cases.
518   However, there are some potential limitations of our method. Figure 7 presents a failure case, where it
519   fails to achieve a key property of the desired image. As mentioned in Section 3.1, the approximation
520   error in a subspace projection depends on components orthogonal to that subspace, even if all tasks
521   may share a principal subspace. If these orthogonal components are critical for a task, performance
522   will suffer. This is because EigenLoRA does not search for weights outside of the principal subspace.
523   However, a simple extension of EigenLoRA which frees a small number of rank-1 weights to be
524
      trainable outside of the principal subspace, can avoid this problem. This would change the EigenLoRA

       B.2    FAILURE C ASES
1099
       Figure 7 illustrates a potential failure case of EigenLoRA, where the incorrect number of principal
1100   components (PCs) was selected. In this instance, the ”mosaic style” information was excluded from
1101   the principal subspace identified by EigenLoRA due to an insufficient number of PCs. However,
1102   this issue can be resolved by selecting a larger number of PCs, as the extended principal subspace
1103   contains the necessary information for the task.
1104
       Another hypothetical failure scenario arises if the domain gap between the low-rank adapters used to
1105
       initialize EigenLoRA and the downstream task is significantly large. Although we do not observe such
1106   a case in our experiments, it is plausible that under such conditions, EigenLoRA might underperform.
1107   This issue could potentially be mitigated by allowing only a subset of PCs to remain trainable,
1108   enabling the model to adapt more effectively to the target domain.
1109
       A further observed limitation of EigenLoRA occurs in complex tasks like Text-to-Image generation,
1110
       which may extend to other tasks as well. If the majority of LoRAs used to initialize EigenLoRA
1111
       encode biases (e.g., related to gender, race, or context), these biases tend to propagate into EigenLoRA
1112   outputs. While such biases are a common issue in deep learning models trained using stochastic gra-
1113   dient descent or similar methods, addressing them remains a critical area of future work. We consider
1114   this an important avenue for improvement and discuss the broader implications in appendix C.

1236   This work presents a novel parameter-efficient method for deep learning methods utilizing open source,
1237   pretrained Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) models. By substantially reducing the computational
1238   and memory demands of training and inference, our approach creates a more sustainable and
1239   environmentally friendly deep learning paradigm. Our method democratizes accessibility to larger
1240   models, making them accessible to researchers and practitioners with limited resources. Furthermore,
1241   by harnessing pretrained models, our method can accelerate development and diminish the need
       for extensive data collection. However, we recognize the inherent risks associated with the use of
