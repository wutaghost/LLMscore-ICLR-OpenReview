009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Learning a data representation with strong transferability from an unlabeled
012            scenario is both crucial and challenging. In this paper, we propose a novel
013            self-supervised transfer learning approach via Adversarial Contrastive Training
014            (ACT). Additionally, we establish an end-to-end theoretical understanding for
015            self-supervised contrastive pretraining and its implications for downstream classi-
016            fication tasks in a misspecified, over-parameterized setting. Our theoretical find-
017            ings highlight the provable advantages of adversarial contrastive training in the
018            source domain towards improving the accuracy of downstream tasks in the target
019            domain. Furthermore, we illustrate that downstream tasks necessitate only a min-
020
               imal sample size when working with a well-trained representation, offering valu-
               able insights on few-shot learning. Last but not least, extensive experiments across
021
               various datasets demonstrate a significant enhancement in classification accuracy
022
               when compared to existing state-of-the-art self-supervised learning methods.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Collecting unlabeled data is far more convenient and cost-effective than gathering labeled data in
029   real-world applications. As a result, learning representations from abundant unlabeled data has
030   become a critical and foundational challenge. Pretraining on unlabeled data enables the capture of
031   more general, abstract features without the need for specific labels. Consequently, the learned task-
032   invariant representations demonstrate superior transferability to unseen data, making them highly
033
      effective in transfer learning scenarios.
034   One of the most popular approaches to learning representations from unlabeled data is self-
035   supervised contrastive learning, which has garnered significant attention due to its impressive perfor-
036   mance. The rationale behind contrastive learning involves acquiring a representation that maintains
037   augmentation invariance while preventing model collapse. The latter aspect is crucial, as solely
038   bringing positive pairs closer could result in trivial solutions. The initial body of work heavily re-
039
      lies on the utilization of negative samples, such as Ye et al. (2019); He et al. (2020); Chen et al.
      (2020a;b); HaoChen et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2023). These studies prevent representation col-
040
      lapse by pushing negative pairs apart in the feature space. However, the construction of negative
041
      pairs poses significant challenges. Firstly, augmented views from distinct data points sharing the
042   same semantic meaning may inadvertently be treated as negative pairs, impeding semantic extrac-
043   tion. Secondly, the quality of the representation is highly dependent on the number of negative pairs,
044   necessitating substantial computational and memory resources.
045
      In recent years, there has been a surge of interests in developing self-supervised learning methods
046
      that eschew the use of negative samples (Grill et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2020; 2021; Ermolov et al.,
047
      2021; Zbontar et al., 2021; Chen & He, 2021; Bardes et al., 2022; Ozsoy et al., 2022; HaoChen
048   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Among above mentioned studies, the most prominent works in-
049   clude Zbontar et al. (2021); Bardes et al. (2022); Ozsoy et al. (2022); HaoChen et al. (2022); Zhang
050   et al. (2023), which prevent the model collapse by incorporating a regularization term into the loss
051   function. However, as demonstrated later, either the population counterpart of Zbontar et al. (2021);
052   Bardes et al. (2022) is still under-investigated, or the sample version of population losses (HaoChen
053   et al., 2022; HaoChen & Ma, 2023) exhibits bias, presenting a significant challenge in terms of
      theoretical analysis. Moreover, due to this bias, the learned representation does not close to the

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
109
110   Self-supervised transfer learning Thanks to the robust transferability inherent in representa-
111   tions learned by self-supervised learning, the field of few-shot learning, which aims to train models
112   with only a limited number of labeled samples, has significantly advanced through self-supervised
113   methodologies. This progression is evidenced by the contributions of Liu et al. (2021); Rizve et al.
114   (2021); Yang et al. (2022); Lim et al. (2023). However, current work only demonstrates the effective-
115   ness of self-supervised learning for few-shot learning mainly empirically. Theoretical explanations
116
      remain scarce. Understanding how the learned representations from unlabeled data enhance predic-
      tion performance with only a few labeled samples in downstream tasks is a critical question that
117
      requires further investigation. Especially investigating the impact of upstream samples on down-
118
      stream samples. Therefore, a thorough theoretical analysis at sample level is urgently needed.
119
120   Although Saunshi et al. (2019); HaoChen et al. (2021); Garrido et al. (2022); Awasthi et al. (2022);
121   Ash et al. (2022); HaoChen et al. (2022); Lei et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023) have offered some
122
      theoretical progresses in understanding self-supervised learning, all these studies either remain at
      the population level, or focus solely on the generalization property of hypothesis space with a finite
123
      complexity measure. The effects of both upstream and downstream sample sizes are still unknown.
124
125   HaoChen & Ma (2023) use augmented graphs to provide a more thorough theoretical analysis at
126   sample level for the self-supervised learning loss proposed in HaoChen et al. (2022). They establish
127   a theoretical guarantees at the sample level, under certain strong assumptions, including Assump-
128
      tions 4.2 and 4.4. Assumption 4.2 assumes the existence of a neural network capable of sufficiently
      minimize the loss. In contrast, we demonstrate the existence of a measurable function that can
129
      vanish our loss by accounting for additional approximation error. This necessitates an extension
130
      of the well-specified setting to a misspecified setting. Moreover, the most important problem in
131   self-supervised transfer learning theory pertains to elucidating the mechanism through which the
132   representation acquired from the upstream task facilitates the learning process of the downstream
133   task. While HaoChen & Ma (2023) assume this relationship as Assumption 4.4 in their research,
134   our study surpasses the current body of literature by conducting a comprehensive investigation into
135   the impact of approximation error and generalization error during the pre-training phase on down-
136   stream test error. This analysis sheds light on how the size of the upstream sample influences the
137   downstream task, particularly in scenarios where the availability of downstream samples is con-
138   strained.
139
140   Comparison with existing contrastive learning algorithms HaoChen et al. (2022) can be re-
141
      garded as a special version of our model with the constraint x1 = x2 at the population level.
      However, its loss at the sample level adopts a biased discretization method, which leads to a differ-
142
      ent optimization direction compared to ACT, especially in the mini-batch scenario. More discussion
143
      can be found in Remark 2.1. Besides that, the loss at the sample level provided by Zbontar et al.
144   (2021) is also similar to our loss, but its unbiased counterpart at the population level is still unknown.

      1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
147
148   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows.
149
150          • We introduce a novel self-supervised transfer learning method called Adversarial
151            Contrastive Training (ACT). This approach learns representations from unlabeled data by
152            tackling a minimax optimization problem, which aims to de-bias the initially proposed risk,
153            thereby providing a foundation for establishing a thorough theoretical understanding.
154          • Our experimental results demonstrate outstanding classification accuracy using both fine-
155            tuned linear probe and K-nearest neighbor (K-NN) protocol on various benchmark
156            datasets, showing competitiveness with existing state-of-the-art self-supervised learning
157            methods.
158          • In the context of transfer learning, we present a thorough theoretical understanding for both
159            ACT and its downstream classification tasks within a misspecified and overparameterized
160            scenario. Our theoretical results offer insights into determining the samples size for pre-
161            training and appropriate depth, width, and norm restrictions of neural networks. These
               findings illuminate the advantages of ACT in enhancing the accuracy of downstream tasks.

204   2.2   A DVERSARIAL CONTRASTVE TRAINING
205
206   Learning representations from large amounts of unlabeled data has recently gained significant atten-
207   tion, as highly transferable representations offer substantial benefits for downstream tasks. Adver-
208   sarial contrastve training is driven by two key factors: augmentation invariance and a regularization
209   term to prevent model collapse. Specifically, augmentation invariance aims to make representations
210
      of different augmented views of the same sample as similar as possible. However, a trivial represen-
      tation that maps all augmented views to the same point is ineffective for downstream tasks, making
211
      the regularization term essential.
212

509   5    C ONCLUSIONS
510
511   In this paper, we propose a novel adversarial contrastive learning method for unsupervised transfer
512   learning. Our experimental results achieved state-of-the-art classification accuracy under both fine-
513   tuned linear probe and K-NN protocol on various real datasets, comparing with the self-supervised
514   learning methods. Meanwhile, we present end to end theoretical guarantee for the downstream
515
      classification task under misspecified and over-parameterized setting. Our theoretical results not
      only indicate that the misclassification rate of downstream task solely depends on the strength of
516
      data augmentation on the large amount of unlabeled data, but also bridge the gap in the theoretical
517
      understanding of the effectiveness of few-shot learning for downstream tasks with small sample size.
518
519   Minimax rates for supervised transfer learning are established in Cai & Wei (2019); Kpotufe &
520   Martinet (2021); Cai & Pu (2024). However, the minimax rate for unsupervised transfer learning
521
      remains unclear. Establishing a lower bound to gain a deeper understanding of our ACT model
      presents an interesting and challenging problem for future research.

       Implementation details. Except for tuning λ for different dataset, all other hyper parameters used
1979
       in our experiments are align with Ermolov et al. (2021). To be specific, we train 1, 000 epochs with
1980   learning rate 3×10−3 for CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and 2×10−3 for Tiny ImageNet. The learning rate
1981   warm-up is used for the first 500 iterations of the optimizer, in addition to a 0.2 learning rate drop
1982   50 and 25 epochs before the end. We adopt a mini-batch size of 256. Same as W-MSE 4 of Ermolov
1983   et al. (2021), we also set 4 as the number of positive samples per image. The dimension of the hidden
1984   layer of the projection head is set as 1024. The weight decay is 10−6 . We adopt an embedding size
1985   (d∗ ) of 64 for CIFAR10, CIFAR100 and 128 for Tiny ImageNet and employ the trick mentioned in
1986   Ermolov et al. (2021) during the pretraining process. The embedding size of BarlowTwins (Zbontar
1987   et al., 2021) is different from above as BarlowTwins need much larger representation size (1024)
1988
       to guarantee its performance. As we see, the performance of our model can sufficiently outperform
       BarlowTwins, revealing the alignment term is pretty crucial for downstream performance practically.
1989
       The backbone network used in our implementation is ResNet-18.
1990
1991   Image transformation details. We randomly extract crops with sizes ranging from 0.08 to 1.0 of
1992   the original area and aspect ratios ranging from 3/4 to 4/3 of the original aspect ratio. Furthermore,
1993   we apply horizontal mirroring with a probability of 0.5. Additionally, color jittering is applied with
1994
       a configuration of (0.4; 0.4; 0.4; 0.1) and a probability of 0.8, while grayscaling is applied with a
       probability of 0.2. For CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, random Gaussian blurring is adopted with a
1995
       probability of 0.5 and a kernel size of 0.1. During testing, only one crop is used for evaluation.
1996
1997   Evaluation protocol. During evaluation, we freeze the network encoder and remove the projection
       head after pretraining, then train a supervised linear classifier on top of it, which is a fully-connected


                                                               37
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1998
       layer followed by softmax. we train the linear classifier for 500 epochs using the Adam optimizer
1999   with corresponding labeled training set without data augmentation. The learning rate is exponen-
2000   tially decayed from 10−2 to 10−6 . The weight decay is set as 10−6 . we also include the accuracy of
2001   a k-nearest neighbors classifier with k = 5, which does not require fine tuning.
2002
       All experiments were conducted using a single Tesla V100 GPU unit.
