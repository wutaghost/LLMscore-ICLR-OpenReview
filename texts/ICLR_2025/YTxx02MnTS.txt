011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent developments in vocoders are primarily dominated by GAN-based net-
014            works targeting to high-quality waveform generation from mel-spectrogram rep-
015            resentations. However, these methods are typically computationally expensive
016            and operate in the time-domain which neglect the time-frequency structures. In
017            this paper, we propose the DMNet, a Dual-branch Multi-band Network to address
018
               these limitations. First, a reconstruction network of complex-valued spectrogram
               called CondNet is used as a condition and thus integrated into the GAN-based
019
               branch. Second, we use multi-band processing in the dual-branch: the Cond-
020
               Net produces Fourier spectral coefficients in one sub-band signal and GAN-based
021            branch generates sub-band representations which are subsequently transformed to
022            full-band speech. Finally, to further improve fidelity, we propose a novel harmonic
023            discriminator which utilizes learnable harmonic filters at multiple scales for a bet-
024            ter modeling ability in harmonic structures. In our experiments, DMNet validates
025            the effectiveness and achieves superior performance for high quality waveform
026            generation, both on subjective and objective metrics.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the real world, speech is an extremely important modality for various practical applications. Neu-
032   ral network based vocoders aiming to generate high quality waveform from an intermediate repre-
033   sentation play a crucial role in speech or audio synthesis. In particular, mel-spectrograms which
034   have approximate human auditory perceptions and compact dimensionality are widely used as the
035   intermediate representations, especially in text-to-speech (TTS) (Ren et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2019;
036   Li et al., 2023e; Jiang et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024), singing voice synthesis (SVS) (Liu et al., 2022a;
037   He et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and voice conversion (VC) (Qian et al., 2019; Li
038   et al., 2023c;d; Ning et al., 2023; 2024) technologies. A two-stage strategy is always used in these
039
      methods: the intermediate mel-spectrogram representation is first predicted from source feature and
      next stage converts it into a raw waveform. The traditional signal processing approaches mainly
040
      focus to map intermediate feature to the original speech, which introduces nonnegligible artifacts.
041
      In recent years, with the success of deep learning, mel-spectrogram based neural vocoders have been
042   rapidly improved in the aspect of quality and naturalness of speech.
043
044   Generative adversarial network based neural vocoders are one family of methods that are the most
045
      effective and efficient so far. These methods usually employ convolutional neural network (CNN)
      architectures with temporal transposed convolution layers to directly sequential upsample the mel-
046
      spectrogram representation to the raw waveform. However, such a black box operation introduces
047
      some problems. First, modeling the raw waveform with the high temporal resolution (e.g., 16,000
048   samples per second for 16k Hz) is a notably challenging problem. Second, the redundant upsampling
049   convolution processes in the temporal domain are impressionable to aliasing artifacts. Although
050   models like BigVGAN (Lee et al., 2023) alleviate the impacts by anti-aliased multi-periodicity
051   composition module, they are parameter-heavy and computationally expensive. The last but the
052   most important, the mel-spectrogram based vocoders have three inverse problems to be solved as
053   Kaneko et al. (2022) states, i.e., recovery of the Fourier spectral coefficients (magnitude and phase
      spectrums) and frequency-to-time conversion. GAN-based vocoders directly generate a raw wave-

066   The recent inverse short-time Fourier transform (iSTFT) based vocoders gradually become an ap-
067   pealing alternative of GAN-based methods due to their closeness to human auditory perception and
068   well-established fast computation. However, these systems usually predict the high-dimensional
069   full-band Fourier spectral coefficients, i.e., magnitude and phase spectrums, and next apply iSTFT to
070
      generate the high-quality waveform, which is a challenging area of waveform reconstruction. Gen-
      erally, a superior waveform generation network architecture always needs redundant estimations
071
      of full-band magnitude and phase spectrums. In addition, phase spectrum in the time-frequency
072
      domain has the complicated nature and periodic-related structure resulting in the principal values
073   wrapping across all phase angles (Siuzdak, 2024). Furthermore, harmonic property of speech plays
074   a critical role and thus must be considered in the full-band signal reconstruction process. While
075   some researchers have explored to predict the harmonic or pitch of speech (Wang et al., 2019; Engel
076   et al., 2020; Łańcucki, 2021; Li et al., 2023a;b; Subramani et al., 2024), challenges still remain in
077   the aspect of the harmonic modeling in terms of full-band signals.
078
      To address the aforementioned limitations, we propose a Dual-branch Multi-band Network archi-
079   tecture with a harmonic discriminator for the neural vocoder, named DMNet. In this paper, a multi-
080   band GAN-based branch integrating with the spectrum reconstruction based CondNet in the time-
081   frequency domain is designed to learn the sub-band related information, and a harmonic discrimina-
082   tor is used to further enhance the harmonic structures of speech. The main illustrations among these
083   methods are summarized in Table 1. Specifically in our work, we first utilize a modified TFGAN
084   network (Tian et al., 2020) as the GAN-based branch to generate multiple sub-band signals. In ad-
085   dition, a spectrum reconstruction network is used to predict Fourier spectral coefficients and further
086   converts these coefficients to the waveform. Furthermore, the reconstructed waveform and time-
087
      frequency representation are used as prior conditions to integrate into the corresponding upsampling
      layers in GAN-based branch by the convolutional blocks and hybrid downsample modules. The
088
      GAN-based vocoder will benefit from these operations by facilitating the fusion of inductive bias
089
      in the time-frequency domain. Second, we employ multi-band processing in the dual-branch net-
090   work, which lifts the computational cost. Other than producing multiple sub-band signals by GAN
091   network, we predict the magnitude and phase spectrums in one low-frequency sub-band rather than
092   the full-band in the CondNet, which avoids the complex multi-periodicity (harmonic) components
093   modeling in the full-band and further mitigates the aliased artifact and improves the inference speed.
094   Finally, we design a multi-scale harmonic discriminator with the learnable triangular band-pass fil-
095   ters. Instead of a fixed time-frequency resolution of the discriminator like Jang et al. (2021) and
096   Défossez et al. (2022), the harmonic discriminator owns dynamic frequency resolution, achieving
097   the significant advantages for better harmonic tracking.
098   Specifically, the main contributions in our paper are as follows:
099
100          • We propose the DMNet, a dual-branch multi-band based vocoder, trained to generate the
101            raw waveform with a fusion of time and time-frequency domain information. A conditional
102            reconstruction network, termed CondNet, is used to predict Fourier spectral coefficients.
103            Unlike previous iSTFT-based networks that rely on full-band Fourier spectral coefficients,
104            this CondNet predict these only in one sub-band, preserving the primary low-frequency
105
               bands and contributing to better performance and faster inference speed.
106          • To further improve the speech fidelity, a novel multiple scale based harmonic discriminator
107            with dynamic resolution in frequency domain is proposed. For the discriminator network
               architecture, we consider the harmonic as channel axis and thus utilize the depthwise sepa-

123
124
      GAN-based vocoder. Compared with conventional vocoders, GAN-based vocoders are gaining
      growing attention due to their efficient ability for waveform generation. HiFiGAN (Kong et al.,
125
      2020) is the typical GAN-based method, which utilizes the multi-receptive field fusion (MRF) mod-
126
      ule for better performance. In addition, progress is further achieved by introducing the multi-period
127   discriminator (MPD) (Kong et al., 2020) and the multi-scale discriminator (MSD) (Kumar et al.,
128   2019). MelGAN (Kumar et al., 2019) utilizes a non-autoregressive fully convolutional feed-forward
129   architecture for waveform generation without additional perceptual loss functions. TFGAN (Tian
130   et al., 2020) is an extension of MelGAN in terms of generator, and employs the time discriminator
131   and frequency discriminator to offer more consistency guarantees, respectively. Moreover, a set of
132   time-domain loss is used for better waveform quality. Avocodo (Bak et al., 2023) jointly optimize
133   a sub-band discriminator and a collaborative multi-band discriminator to alleviate unintended arti-
134   facts. In particular, BigVGAN (Lee et al., 2023) achieves the state-of-the-art synthesis quality of
135
      speech with the periodic activations and anti-aliased multi-periodicity composition (AMP) module
      in the generator. Although GAN-based vocoders achieve the high fidelity, the inductive bias in time-
136
      frequency mel-spectrogram is not well utilized, which degrades the synthesized waveform quality
137
      to a certain extent.
138
139   ISTFT-based vocoder. Another explored neural vocoder is iSTFT-based network architecture.
140   These systems usually reconstruct waveform by parameterizing the model to predict full-band
141
      Fourier spectral coefficients, i.e., phase and magnitude components. The iSTFTNET (Kaneko et al.,
      2022) and iSTFTNET2 (Kaneko et al., 2023) are a series of researches that make some modifica-
142
      tions of HiFiGAN. Some upsample blocks with transposed convolutions are replaced with inverse
143
      STFT in order to return Fourier spectral coefficients. In addition, some iSTFT-based vocoders are
144   explored without upsample blocks. HiNet (Ai & Ling, 2020) utilizes an amplitude spectrum predic-
145   tor (ASP) to predict amplitude and a NSF-based (Wang et al., 2019) phase spectrum predictor (PSP)
146   for phase prediction. ASP and PSP modules are hierarchical orders for prediction. In other words,
147   the amplitude is first generated and next is the phase spectrum generation. APNet (Ai & Ling, 2023)
148   and APNet2 (Du et al., 2023) design the ASP and PSP modules as parallel structures and multi-
149   level loss functions are used for better optimization. Furthermore, Vocos (Siuzdak, 2024) treats the
150   magnitude and phase predictions as a whole block, which employs ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022b)
151   blocks with Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) activations to predict
152
      magnitude and phase components simultaneously. All the aforementioned methods use the inverse
      STFT to reconstruct the full-band waveform. Unfortunately, the multi-periodicity components are
153
      always existent in the full-band signals, bringing challenges in the harmonic modeling and aliasing
154
      artifacts elimination.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
187
188   As illustrated in Figure 1, the proposed model is composed of generator and discriminator. In terms
189   of the generator, the GAN-based backbone network is a modified TFGAN. Specifically, the sinu-
190   soidal activation function is removed and the residual stacks are replaced by MRF (Kong et al.,
191   2020). A post-convolution layer is used as the lightweight bottleneck between the upsample block
192   and MRF for information aggregation. Next, in order to learn the time-frequency structures, a spec-
193   trum reconstruction network is used to predict magnitude and phase components in one sub-band
194
      signal. The sub-band waveform is generated by an inverse STFT operator. Then, the reconstructed
      conditional waveform and time-frequency representation are used to integrate into the backbone
195
      network to generate the final sub-band signals. The final waveform is generated by a pseudo-QMF
196
      (PQMF) bank (Nguyen, 1994) without distortion. In our work, we only use the synthesis filter
197   bank in PQMF to generate waveforms. As for the discriminator, we utilize the proposed harmonic
198   discriminator, the multi-period discriminator (MPD) (Kong et al., 2020) as well as the multi-scale
199   STFT-based (MS-STFT) discriminator (Défossez et al., 2022) to train our model.
200
201   3.2   C OND N ET
202
203   As the black box GAN-based generator network is lack of the guidance in the time-frequency do-
204   main and iSTFT-based network can offer the inductive time-frequency bias, we propose a condi-
205   tional network, i.e., CondNet as shown in Figure 1 to compensate the loss. First, we utilize the
206   ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022b) blocks to predict magnitude and phase components in one sub-band
207
      simultaneously from the mel-spectrogram representation. We employ the exponential function to
      represent the magnitude and apply the cosine and sine to denote the real and imaginary parts, re-
208
      spectively. These complex-valued Fourier spectral coefficients in one sub-band learning the time-
209
      frequency structures are used to convert into the waveform with the inverse STFT operator. Next, we
210   couple the predicted time-domain signal of spectrum reconstruction network with the GAN-based
211   generator branch more effectively. Specifically, for the first layer, we directly feed the sub-band
212   waveform to the convolutional block. Next, we initially perform the STFT operator for 2× down-
213   sampling, and then adopt the convolutional block and hybrid downsample module (Shi et al., 2024)
214   to hierarchically integrate into the second and third layers (Figure 1). Finally, we directly add these
215   time and time-frequency domain representations to each upsample block output respectively as the
      prior condition knowledge to guide the final multi-band signals learning.

      3.3   M ULTI - BAND P ROCESSING
217
218   In this section, we will introduce the multi-band processing in the dual-branch network. For the
219   GAN-based generator, we directly predict the multi-band representations, i.e., the final output chan-
220   nel C stands for the number of sub-band signals. The total upsampled multiple reduces by C times
221   compared with the original full-band generator, further resulting in the reduction in the time axis
222   (e.g., 24000 samples in one second, output channel is 12, thus the output length is 2000). In terms of
223   the CondNet, Fourier spectral coefficients in one low-frequency sub-band are predicted and the hop
224
      size of the inverse STFT also can reduce by C times (e.g., the hop size of full-band is 240, the num-
      ber of sub-band is 12, thus the hop size is only 20 in sub-band). In one low-frequency sub-band, the
225
      CondNet can neglect the multi-periodicity and harmonic components of the full-band signal, which
226
      is beneficial for model training. The final waveform is generated by the effective PQMF bank. Fur-
227   thermore, the whole dual-branch generator network is trained in the form of sub-band, significantly
228   improving the inference speed.

236   learnable band-pass filters, which is a first-of-its-kind approach.

      4.1      DATASETS
327
328   In our experiments, we utilize a clean 500-hour mixture speech dataset for model training. Specif-
329   ically, these speech samples are created from our intranet sites by extracting the clean vocal track
330   of television drama. All training speech samples are resampled at 24k Hz. The FFT size and hop
331   size are 1024 and 256, respectively. For each speech sample, we set the number of Mel bins as 160,
332   extract log mel-spectrograms and apply normalization. For testing, we use two datasets. Specifi-
333   cally, a 100-utterance test dataset extracting the clean vocal track of television drama has the same
334
      distributions with the training dataset, called in-domain (ID) dataset. To evaluate the generalization
      ability of model for unseen acoustic conditions, we create another 100-utterance test dataset contain-
335
      ing singing voice clips extracted from the Opencpop dataset (Wang et al., 2022), called out-domain
336
      (OD) dataset, which has out-of-domain distributions compared with the training dataset.

339
340   Generator. In our proposed DMNet, the number of sub-band is set as 8. For the GAN-based
341   branch, we use three upsample blocks, each contains the hybrid upsample block, post-convolution
342   and MRF module. These upsample blocks achieve 32x upsampling due to predicting 8 sub-bands
343   simultaneously, where up-factor is (4, 4, 2). The post-convolution layer chooses the kernel size 3
344   and stride 1. The MRF modules have the same configurations with HiFiGAN. The kernel size of
345   transposed convolution is twice of the stride. The output channels of three upsample blocks are 256,
346
      128 and 64, respectively. In addition, the output channels of the first and last convolution layers are
      512 and 8, respectively. For CondNet, we adopt 4 ConvNeXt blocks to predict the sub-band Fourier
347
      spectral coefficients and the input channel of the first block is converted to 256. The intermediate
348
      dimension is 768 and the output channels of the final convolution layers are both 129 for phase and
349   magnitude predicting. Then, we use the inverse STFT with the hop size 32 to generate the low-
350   frequency sub-band waveform. Furthermore, the hop size of STFT is 2 for downsampling. In the
351   residual stack, there are 3 convolution layers and its dilations are 1, 3 and 5. The output channels of
352   three residual stacks are 64, 128 and 256, respectively.
353
      Discriminator. We follow the same network of the multi-period discriminator (MPD) in Kong et al.
354
      (2020). As for the MS-STFT discriminator, we utilize the similar encoder network architecture
355   with the proposed harmonic discriminator. See Appendix A for the network architecture of MS-
356   STFT discriminator. For the configurations of MS-STFT discriminator, the FFT size, hop size and
357   window size are (1024, 2048, 768), (240, 320, 120) and (960, 1280, 480) in the GAN-based branch,
358   respectively. In the CondNet, the FFT size, hop size and window size are (128, 256, 64), (20, 30,
359   15) and (128, 256, 64), respectively. For the multi-scale harmonic discriminator, we use 8, 10 and
360   12 as the numbers of harmonics, respectively. The output channel of the DSConv is the same as the
361   number of harmonics and the output channel of PConv is 32.
362   Training. For the multi-tier multi-resolution STFT loss, three different sampling rates are used to
363   resample the waveform for multi-tier losses computation. Specifically, 24k Hz is the original sam-

369
      thermore, the learning rate of the proposed generator is halved every 100K steps until 1e-6. For the

      model up to two million steps.

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a dual-branch multi-band neural vocoder with multi-scale harmonic dis-
533
      criminator. Specifically in our model, GAN-based branch and iSTFT-based branch are integrated
534   into a framework. In addition, we utilize the multi-band processing in the dual-branch. We propose
535   the CondNet to predict the sub-band Fourier spectral coefficients, which simplifies the difficulty
536   of model training and further improves the inference speed. Moreover, to more correctly learn the
537   harmonic structures in speech, we design a novel harmonic discriminator, which uses the dynamic
538   frequency resolution to train network and achieves the dramatic performance gains. Our experiment
539   results also demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. In conclusion, DMNet provides a
      new idea for the advancement of neural vocoders.
