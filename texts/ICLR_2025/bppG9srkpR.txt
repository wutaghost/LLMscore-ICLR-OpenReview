                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
011
               In this work, we introduce LokiLM, a 1.4B parameter large language model trained
               on 500B tokens. Our model performs strongly in natural language reasoning tasks
012
               and achieves state-of-the-art performance among models with 1.5B parameters or
013
               less. LokiLM is trained using multi-teacher knowledge distillation and high-quality
014            training data to achieve benchmark results competitive with larger models trained
015            on significantly more tokens. We support these findings by introducing steps
016            to avoid benchmark contamination and overfitting throughout our development
017            process. Despite its promising performance, LokiLM exhibits a concerning amount
018            of hallucinations and scores poorly on the TruthfulQA benchmark, so we do not
019            release the model publicly.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large language models (LLMs) leveraging the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023)
028
      have revolutionized the field of natural language processing. Early improvements stemmed from
029
      increases in model size, with LLMs achieving impressive performance in tasks such as coding,
030   reading comprehension, math, and reasoning (Hestness et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020; Shoeybi
031   et al., 2020). However, the scaling laws introduced by Hoffmann et al. (2022) suggest that these early
032   models were under-trained. Subsequent LLMs achieved high performance with far fewer parameters
033   (Touvron et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023), though most of this research has focused
034   on models with over 7 billion parameters.
035
      Training LLMs is a resource-intensive process, requiring costly hardware and large power expendi-
036   tures (Touvron et al., 2023a). In this work, we turn our attention to the ∼1.5B parameter model class
037   as a more accessible domain for further study. Models of this size offer several advantages, allowing
038   for faster iteration during research phases and feasible deployment for a wider range of practitioners.
039   The demand for smaller language models is furthered by increased interest in local LLM applications
040   (Yi et al., 2023). Deploying models on a user’s device can enhance reliability, reduce operational costs,
041   and provide better data security when compared to cloud solutions (Qualcomm, 2023). However, the
042   computational constraints of these devices necessitate the development of high-performing models
043   with limited parameter counts.
044   Recent work, such as the 1.3 billion parameter Phi-1.5 model (Li et al., 2023), has demonstrated the
045   potential of smaller language models. By leveraging high-quality training data, Phi-1.5 achieved
046   performance competitive with the 7 billion parameter LLaMA 2 model on various benchmarks.
047   Building upon these findings, we introduce LokiLM, a 1.4 billion parameter language model trained
048   on 500 billion tokens. Through a combination of architectural optimizations, knowledge distillation,
049   and carefully curated training data, LokiLM achieves results that rival the current state-of-the-art in
050
      the ∼1.5 billion parameter class.
051   Our model exhibits strong performance on knowledge and reasoning tasks, ranking first on automated
052   model quality benchmarks when compared to all public models with fewer than 2 billion parame-
053   ters. However, LokiLM displays a propensity to generate harmful content, scoring poorly on the
      TruthfulQA benchmark (Lin et al., 2022).

      2     A PPROACH
075
076
      2.1   A RCHITECTURE
077
      LokiLM uses a standard decoder-only Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023) with 24
078
      layers, 32 attention heads, and a hidden dimension of 2048. We incorporate several architectural
079   optimizations to improve performance and training efficiency. FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) is used
080   to accelerate the multi-headed attention computation, while Attention with Linear Bias (ALiBi) (Press
081   et al., 2022) replaces positional embeddings to better capture positional information. RMSNorm
082   (Zhang and Sennrich, 2019) and SwiGLU activations (Shazeer, 2020) are employed to stabilize
083   training and learn more complex representations (Zhang et al., 2024b), respectively.
084
      For tokenization, we adapt the cl100k_base tokenizer (OpenAI et al., 2023) using a pruning method
085   inspired by TokenMonster (Forsythe, 2023). The resulting tokenizer has a vocabulary size of 32,000
086   and is trained on an additional 50 billion tokens. Interestingly, we observe that vocabulary sizes with
087   lower Hamming weights generally lead to higher model throughput, although this relationship is not
088   consistent across all configurations. We do not extensively evaluate this phenomenon.
089
      A major challenge in developing LokiLM was the uncertainty present during model evaluation. The
090
      margin between different configurations’ scores was often small compared to the variance in their
091   results, making it difficult to compare architecture choices. While individual training decisions may
092   have minor impacts, their cumulative effort could lead to substantial improvements. Future work
093   could focus on introducing deterministic evaluation techniques, enabling more reliable comparisons
094   between variants and accelerating the development process.
095
096   2.2   T RAINING DATA
097
098   The training data for LokiLM primarily consists of web-scraped content, supplemented with a small
099   portion of machine-generated text in the early stages of training to aid in learning basic language
100   concepts (Smith et al., 2018; Sievert and Shah, 2023). We apply a multi-stage filtering pipeline to
101   ensure data quality and diversity, drawing inspiration from recent works on data filtering for language
102   models (Delétang et al., 2023; Feldman and Zhang, 2020).
103   Our filtering pipeline incorporates various techniques to refine the dataset. To enhance data quality
104   and diversity, we employ methods such as SemDeDup (Abbas et al., 2023) for semantic deduplication,
105   semantic density-based pruning using Self-Supervised-Prototypes Pruning (Sorscher et al., 2023),
106   and Transformer-based classifiers similar to those described by Gunasekar et al. (2023). These
107   techniques, along with additional data cleaning and refinement methodologies, collectively contribute
      to maintaining the integrity of our training set.


                                                        2
108
      A critical component of our data preparation involves the systematic removal of content that closely
109   matches benchmark datasets. This step is implemented to mitigate potential contamination and
110   ensure an unbiased evaluation of our model’s performance. We carefully exclude benchmark-specific
111   text from the pre-training data to avoid artificially inflated performance metrics and to improve
112   generalization (Zhang et al., 2024a).
113
      The resulting filtered dataset, comprising 250 billion tokens, is utilized to train LokiLM for two
114
      epochs. Throughout the training process, we strive to balance comprehensive coverage of diverse
115   knowledge with the elimination of potentially problematic or redundant data. This approach aims
116   to develop a robust and generalizable language model while minimizing the risks of overfitting and
117   benchmark contamination.
118
119   2.3       T RAINING D ETAILS
120
121   LokiLM is trained using 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs for 8 days. We use 8-bit precision and Fully Sharded
122   Data Parallelism (Ott et al., 2021) to optimize training efficiency. A warmup strategy is applied
123   during the early stages of training, gradually increasing the batch size over the first 50 billion tokens
124   to stabilize the learning process.
125   To further improve performance, we incorporate knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Gu
126   et al., 2023) every fourth training batch using GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2023), Mistral 7B (Jiang et al.,
127   2023), and Llama 2 13B (Touvron et al., 2023b) as teacher models. Student models often struggle
128   to represent the complexity when there is a significant size gap (Cho and Hariharan, 2019), so we
129   avoid using only large models. We incorporate three teacher models for the potential to maximize
130   knowledge transfer (Liu et al., 2020). For batches incorporating knowledge distillation, we define the
131   objective as the minimum cross-entropy loss when comparing our model’s output to the ground truth
132
      and the teacher models’ distributions. This approach can be viewed as a form of regularization where
      there are multiple correct sequences of information. To mitigate the risk of benchmark contamination
133
      from the teacher models (Oren et al., 2023), we regenerate data whenever copies of benchmark texts
134
      are detected.
135
136   During training, we encounter multiple loss spikes, a common challenge in large-scale language
137   model training (Chowdhery et al., 2022). To address this issue, we adopt a checkpoint rollback
138
      strategy. Whenever a loss spike occurs, we revert the model to the most recent checkpoint and restart
      training with a different random seed. This method effectively mitigates the impact of loss spikes and
139
      maintains a stable training process. This method has been widely used in prior work (Zhang et al.,
140
      2022; Zeng et al., 2023).

      LokiLM’s challenges with truthfulness appear to stem from its inability to accurately report factual
352
      information. We hypothesize that this deficiency is primarily attributable to our dataset curation
353
      process. In an effort to encourage higher-order reasoning skills and prevent benchmark contamination,
354   we deliberately removed a significant amount of factual data from the training corpus, including
355   specific factual statements—such as precise dates and statistical data—but retained conceptual or
356   structural information (e.g., we kept "Alan Turing designed the Automatic Computing Engine" but
357   removed "Alan Turing was born on 23 June 1912"). This approach was intended to prevent the model
358   from simply memorizing factual information, thereby saturating it with lower-level knowledge at the
359   expense of developing higher-order reasoning skills. However, this strategy may have inadvertently
360   limited the model’s capacity to accurately recall and reproduce essential factual information during
361   inference, making it more prone to hallucinations.
362   In addition to pruning factual content, we strongly excluded benchmark-related material to prevent the
363   model from memorizing evaluation datasets and artificially inflating its performance on benchmarks.
364   While necessary for fair evaluation, this removal process may have unintentionally eliminated other
365   important factual information closely related to the benchmark content. The boundaries between
366   benchmark data and general factual knowledge can be blurred, and aggressive pruning might have
367   reduced the richness of information available to the model. This deprivation of factual grounding
368
      could contribute to the model’s difficulties in generating truthful and accurate responses across various
      domains.
369

373   effectively learned the underlying patterns and structures necessary for such tasks. Nevertheless, its
374
      constrained exposure to factual content undermines its overall reliability, particularly in domains
      requiring precise knowledge retrieval.

      4   C ONCLUSION
413
414   In this work, we introduced LokiLM, a 1.4B parameter language model that achieves state-of-the-art
415   performance among models with 2B parameters or less on the Open LLM Leaderboard tasks. Our
416
      model demonstrates that through careful data curation, knowledge distillation, and architectural
      optimizations, it’s possible to create highly capable language models with fewer parameters and
417
      significantly less training data than competing models.
418
419   LokiLM’s strong performance, particularly on tasks like GSM8K and MMLU, highlights the potential
420   for smaller, more efficient models to rival larger counterparts in specific domains. This advancement
421   is particularly significant for applications requiring edge computing or deployment on consumer
422
      hardware, potentially broadening access to high-performing LLMs for research and development.
423   However, LokiLM’s poor performance on the TruthfulQA benchmark and its tendency to generate
424   false or biased information underscore the ongoing challenges in developing safe and reliable language
425   models. These limitations prevent us from releasing the model publicly and highlight critical areas for
426   future research. Our findings emphasize the need for more robust techniques to ensure truthfulness
427   and reduce hallucination in language models, possibly through improved data filtering or novel
428
      training objectives.
429   A notable challenge in LokiLM’s development was the difficulty in evaluating incremental improve-
430   ments. Performance differences between architectural configurations often fell within the bounds
431   of statistical variance, complicating the distinction between genuine advancements and random
      fluctuations. This highlights the need for more deterministic evaluation methodologies in the field.


                                                        8
432
      Such techniques could enhance the model iteration process, facilitating more reliable comparisons
433   between variants and potentially accelerating language model development.
434
435   In conclusion, while LokiLM represents a step forward in efficient language modeling, it also
436
      serves as a reminder of the complex challenges facing the field. These challenges span from ethical
      considerations and model capabilities to the methodologies we use for evaluation and improvement.
437
