009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances the abilities of Large Language
012            Models (LLMs) by enabling the retrieval of documents into the LLM context to
013            provide more accurate and relevant responses. Existing RAG solutions do not fo-
014            cus on queries that may require fetching multiple documents with substantially
015            different contents. Such queries occur frequently, but are challenging because the
016
               embeddings of these documents may be distant in the embedding space, making
               it hard to retrieve them all. This paper introduces Multi-Head RAG (MRAG),
017
               a novel scheme designed to address this gap with a simple yet powerful idea:
018
               leveraging activations of Transformer’s multi-head attention layer, instead of the
019            decoder layer, as keys for fetching multi-aspect documents. The driving moti-
020            vation is that different attention heads can learn to capture different data aspects.
021            Harnessing the corresponding activations results in embeddings that represent var-
022            ious facets of data items and queries, improving the retrieval accuracy for complex
023            queries. We provide an evaluation methodology and metrics, multi-aspect datasets
024            that we release online, and real-world use cases to demonstrate MRAG’s effec-
025            tiveness, showing improvements of up to 20% in relevance over standard RAG
026            baselines. MRAG can be seamlessly integrated with existing RAG frameworks
027
               and benchmarking tools like RAGAS as well as different classes of data stores.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) transformed many machine learning tasks using in-context learning
031   abilities. They achieved such accuracy by leveraging an increasing number of parameters, which in
032   recent models have grown to hundreds of billions, making LLM training expensive in terms of
033   both time and resources. It also comes with the danger of leaking confidential data into model
034   weights (Yan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Patil et al., 2024). Additionally, continuous training
035   through fine-tuning is necessary to keep LLMs up-to-date. Even using the newest data, LLMs
036   display an ongoing problem of hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024c; Huang et al.,
037   2023) by providing factually incorrect information. Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) was
038
      proposed (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020) in order to address these issues as well as others and
      make LLMs more trustworthy.
039
040   The key idea behind RAG is to enhance the generative model’s capabilities by integrating a retrieval
041   system that fetches relevant passages from a large corpus of data. In this setting, when a query is
042   received, the retrieval system first identifies and retrieves pertinent information, which is fed into
043   the generative model’s context for a more accurate and relevant response. Instead of the model
044
      storing information within its weights, RAG effectively leverages external knowledge, reducing
      hallucinations (by grounding the LLM reply in reliable sources), and ensuring that responses contain
045
      up-to-date knowledge (e.g., by accessing the Internet), all without requiring expensive training.
046
047   More specifically, there are two main stages in a RAG pipeline: data preparation and query execu-
048   tion. During data preparation, one constructs a vector database (DB) populated with embeddings
049   and their corresponding data items such as documents. During query execution, one constructs an
050
      embedding of that query and retrieves data items in the store with similar embeddings.
051   Intense recent research efforts have been put into RAG (Gao et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Hu &
052   Lu, 2024; Huang & Huang, 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Mialon et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022). On one
053   hand, different RAG designs have been proposed, for example RAPTOR (Sarthi et al., 2024), Self-
      RAG (Asai et al., 2023), Chain-of-Note (Yu et al., 2023), and many others (Abdallah & Jatowt, 2024;


                                                        1
054
      Delile et al., 2024; Edge et al., 2024; Manathunga & Illangasekara, 2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Wewer
055   et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2024b). In general, these schemes focus on making the retrieved data more
056   accurate and relevant to the query. There have also been efforts into benchmarking and datasets for
057   RAG evaluation (Chen et al., 2024b; Xiong et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2024; Es et al., 2023).
058
      Despite all these advances, we observe that no existing RAG scheme or evaluation methodology
059
      explicitly targets an important class of problems that come with a high degree of multi-aspectuality.
060
      These are problems that require combining several (potentially many) significantly different aspects
061   in a single query. As a simple illustrative example of such a query, consider the question “What
062   car did Alexander the Great drive?”, and assume that the queried model has not been trained on
063   history. When using RAG, to answer this question accurately, one would retrieve two documents,
064   one describing Alexander the Great and one outlining the history of car manufacturing. However,
065   the embeddings of these two documents could be far away from each other in the embedding space.
066   At the same time, such queries are common in different industry settings, as indicated by exten-
067   sive discussions with our industry collaborators. Imagine a chemical processing plant experiencing
068   an equipment accident. One could use an LLM to find the accident cause, which might require
069
      the retrieval of multiple, potentially confidential documents to provide the necessary context. These
      documents could be related to different aspects, for example psychological profiles of workers (“Was
070
      the accident due to mismanaging a worker?”), equipment purchase records (“Was some equipment
071
      part too old?”), maintenance (“Was some equipment part rusty?”), weather (“Was there a particu-
072   larly strong thunderstorm at the accident time that could have caused dangerous power spikes in the
073   grid?”), or even microclimate (“Was it too humid for an extended period of time in the production
074   hall?”). As we illustrate in Section 4, such problems pose challenges for existing RAG schemes and
075   have been unaddressed by modern RAG benchmarking pipelines.

121

123
      Multi-Head RAG Formulation The key idea behind MRAG is simple: instead of the single acti-
124
      vation vector generated by the last feed-forward decoder layer for the last token, we harness the H
125   separate activation vectors generated by the last attention layer for the last token, before processing
126   it via Wo . This can be formulated as a set of embeddings S = {ek ∀k } where ek = headk (xn ),
127   which is simply the set of all outputs from the attention heads on the last token xn of the input. As
128   processing with multiple heads does not change the size of the output vector, S has the same space
129   requirements as standard RAG. However, because we capture the separate embeddings before their
130   mixing with Wo , we conjecture that it gives more information about what the different parts of the
131   input attend to, facilitating capturing multi-aspectuality.
132   Naming We use the terms “single-aspect embedding” and “multi-aspect embedding” to refer to,
133   respectively, a small embedding extracted from a single attention head and a collection of all single-
134   aspect embeddings extracted from an attention layer.
135   2.1 OVERVIEW OF THE M ULTI -H EAD RAG P IPELINE
136
      We now describe how the above embedding model fits the RAG pipeline. Figure 2 shows a summary
137
      of the design. The MRAG pipeline consists of two main parts, dedicated to data preparation A and
138   query execution B . Both parts heavily use the data store D (vector DB).
139
140   2.1.1   DATA P REPARATION
141   When preparing data A , we populate a data store D with multi-aspect MRAG text embeddings and
142   their corresponding documents or text chunks           (MRAG is orthogonal to the type of data being
143   embedded, and while we primarily use chunking of documents in order to reflect modern RAG
144   pipelines, one can also embed whole documents or even other types of data). We create the multi-
145   aspect embedding of each text chunk         using a selected decoder-based embedding model C (this
146   part is detailed in Section 2.2). The user of the pipeline can plug in their model C of choice as well
147
      as use their input data. We also offer a dedicated synthetic data generator        that can be used to
      construct multi-aspect input documents (we detail this part in Section 3) for evaluation purposes.
148
149   MRAG stores data differently than standard RAG, where a single embedding points to a single
150   text chunk . For MRAG, each multi-aspect embedding consists of h single-aspect embeddings ,
151   each pointing to the original text chunk . So the data store D contains h embedding spaces, each
152   capturing a different aspect of the text. This crucial feature allows MRAG to compare query and
153
      text chunks     in multiple embedding spaces that capture multiple aspects of the data.
154   2.1.2   Q UERY E XECUTION
155

156
      the selected embedding model C (details in Section 2.2). Then, we find the nearest multi-aspect
157   embeddings and their corresponding text chunks in the data store D using a special multi-aspect
158   retrieval strategy (detailed in Section 2.3). We ensure that there is no overhead in latency due
159   to multiple aspects because computing these different smaller embeddings is done fully in parallel.
160   Finally, the retrieved data can optionally be assessed with novel metrics regarding how well it
161   corresponds to the multi-aspect requirements (detailed in Section 3). As with the data preparation

      2.2 C ONSTRUCTING M ULTI -A SPECT E MBEDDINGS
189
      MRAG can leverage any embedding model with multi-head attention support to construct the multi-
190
      aspect embeddings for a given input text. In this work, we consider two embedding models from the
191
      MTEB leaderboard (Huggingface, 2024) as potential candidates. Specifically, the SFR-Embedding-
192   Model (Meng et al., 2024) and the e5-mistral-7b-instruct (Wang et al., 2024b), both based on the
193   Mistral 7B architecture with 32 decoder blocks and 32 attention heads per multi-head attention.
194
195
      While our approach allows for extracting and using the multi-aspect embeddings from any decoder

196
      from the last multi-head attention worked best in our experimental setting. We provide further
197
      discussion on the carried out experiments in Section 4.

199   2.3 R ETRIEVAL S TRATEGIES FOR M ULTI -A SPECT DATA
200   A retrieval strategy determines how we select the closest text chunks from the DB given a multi-
201   aspect embedding of the user query. In general, the MRAG retrieval strategy consists of three steps.
202   First, during data preparation, we assign importance scores to all h embedding spaces. Intuitively,
203   these scores capture the fact that different spaces (and the corresponding heads) may be more or less
204
      relevant for the used data. Then, during query execution, MRAG starts by applying the traditional
      RAG retrieval separately for each embedding space. This returns a list of c closest text chunks for
205
      each embedding space (a total of h lists). Here, we use a special voting strategy to pick overall top
206
      k out of all hc chunks, using the pre-computed importance scores.
207

236   2.3.1   I NTEGRATION WITH DATA S TORES & OTHER T YPES OF M ODELS
237
      MRAG can be seamlessly used with different classes of data stores C and nearest neighbor (NN)
238   search approaches. It can be combined with both the exact and the approximate NN to find the
239   matching (embedding, chunk)-pairs. These two parts of the broader RAG processing pipeline are
240   orthogonal to MRAG. Similarly, MRAG does not depend specifically on the embedding form, as
241   long as it is based on a model that harnesses multi-head attention any such approach that results in
242   a valid embedding can be used. As such, it could also be used with models such as RetroMAE Xiao
243   et al. (2022) and the classic BGE-embeddings Xiao et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024a).

245   3   M ULTI -A SPECT DATASETS , Q UERIES , AND M ETRICS
246   To assess how well MRAG performs on multi-aspect queries, and to compare it to modern RAG
247   schemes, we need (1) datasets of documents that capture multi-aspectuality, (2) queries to the LLM
248   that touch upon multi-aspectuality and require retrieving documents from the multi-aspect dataset,
249   and (3) metrics that assess how well a RAG scheme retrieves such multi-aspect data. We now
250   describe these three elements. In Section 4, we also briefly discuss real-world data and queries used.
251   Multi-Aspect Datasets We first select conceptually different categories of documents for a synthetic
252   dataset. Here, we harness publicly available Wikipedia articles. In the dataset construction pipeline,
253   the user selects a given number of categories (e.g., countries, board games, historical swords, ship-
254   wrecks, etc.) and then, for each category, they sample a specified number of documents. The first
255   part of the document (overview) is used as a text chunk to be embedded. We enforce that each
256   overview must have at least 800 characters, matching commonly used chunk sizes in RAG schemes.
257   We also use multi-aspect real-world inspired datasets consisting of NDAs and reports describing
258   industry accidents in chemical processing plants. We ensure the usefulness of these datasets by
259
      working directly with tech leaders from 3 corporations that rely on RAG in their in-house LLM-
      driven report generation and analytics frameworks. Example categories of the legal documents are
260
      legal areas (energy law, family law, criminal law, etc.) or document language style (aggressive, mild,
261
      neutral, etc.). Examples of accident causes are natural disasters, human mistakes, or lack of proper
262   training. We fully release these datasets to propel RAG research. Details on all three datasets can be
263   found in the Appendix B.2. In our evaluation, we use a total of 13,750 documents.

469   Our work touches on many areas which we now briefly discuss.
470
      Many RAG schemes appeared recently (Gao et al., 2024), using the output of the last decoder layer
471
      for embedding generation. In contrast, MRAG leverages different embedding spaces of attention
472   heads to focus on different aspects of documents and queries. As such, it can be combined with
473   other schemes to further improve RAG pipelines.
474

      Retrieval is sometimes enhanced by a cross-encoder reranking phase (Rosa et al., 2022; Nogueira
      & Cho, 2020; Nogueira et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2021; MacAvaney et al., 2019). In
476
      such solutions, typically after retrieving a set of relevant chunks, they are re-ranked using specialized

      models. In this work, we focus solely on the first retrieval phase, so MRAG can be seamlessly used
487   in conjunction with such cross-encoders.
488
489   Structure-enhanced RAG schemes employ different strategies for structuring text to improve re-
490
      trieval quality. A common idea is to construct a Knowledge Graph from text, which enables retrieval
      amongst entities and relationships (Jiang et al., 2024; Delile et al., 2024; Hussien et al., 2024; Bui
491
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a). RAPTOR (Sarthi et al., 2024) generates multi-level summaries for
492
      clusters of related chunks, building a tree of summaries with increasing levels of abstraction to better
493   capture the meaning of the text. Graph RAG (Edge et al., 2024) creates a Knowledge Graph, and
494   summarizes communities in the graph, which provide data at the different levels of abstraction. All
495   these systems try to improve RAG quality by utilizing additional structures that describe entity re-
496   lationships or the inner organization of text. Usually, they need a sophisticated preprocessing phase
497   to prepare such structures. MRAG achieves the improvement solely based on the embedding model
498   and has no additional storage requirements, and can be combined with any of these schemes.

500   6   C ONCLUSION
501   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) is pivotal for democratizing access to accurate and relevant
502   outputs from large language models (LLMs). Enhancing the precision and relevance of these outputs
503   is a critical goal, especially given the challenges posed by queries requiring the retrieval of multiple
504   documents with significantly different contents. These complex queries are common across various
505   domains, but existing RAG solutions struggle because the embeddings of the necessary documents
506   can be far apart in the embedding space, complicating their retrieval.
507   To address this gap, we introduced Multi-Head RAG (MRAG), a novel scheme that leverages the
508   activations from the multi-head attention layer of decoder models instead of the traditional feed-
509   forward layer. This approach is grounded in the insight that different attention heads can capture
510   distinct aspects of the data. By using these diverse activations, MRAG creates embeddings that better
511   represent the multifaceted nature of data items and queries, thus enhancing the retrieval accuracy for
512   complex, multi-aspect queries. The simplicity and versatility of this idea allow it to be seamlessly
513   integrated into any modern RAG pipeline or data analytics framework.
514   Our comprehensive evaluation methodology, including specific metrics, synthetic datasets, and real-
515   world use cases, demonstrates MRAG’s effectiveness. The results indicate a significant improve-
516   ment in the relevance of retrieved documents, with up to 20% better performance compared to
517   modern RAG baselines. This validates MRAG’s potential to handle the intricacies of multi-aspect
518   queries effectively.
519   Moreover, MRAG proves to be both cost-effective and energy-efficient. It does not require additional
520   LLM queries, multiple model instances, increased storage, or multiple inference passes over the
521   embedding model. This efficiency, combined with the enhanced retrieval accuracy, positions MRAG
522   as a valuable advancement in the field of LLMs and RAG systems. By addressing the challenges of
523   multi-aspectuality in queries, MRAG paves the way for more reliable and accurate LLM applications
524   across diverse industries.

834   We investigated the attention heads of two models in detail: Llama2-7B and SFR-Embedding-
835   Mistral. We selected these two models for a detailed investigation because the former represents
836
      models that are not fine-tuned for text embeddings, while the latter is specifically the text embed-
      ding model that we used for our experiments. For each model, we looked specifically at the attention
837
      scores within each attention head, i.e., how much attention each head pays to each input token during
838
      the inference. Knowing the semantics of the input tokens enables then deriving certain conclusions
