011                                                          A BSTRACT
012
013            Multivariate time series data often face the challenge of missing values, which can impact the perfor-
014            mance of subsequent tasks. Although some deep learning-based imputation methods perform well,
015            they still struggle with insufficient training data due to high missing rate and long-term missing data.
016
               To address these challenges, we propose a Historical Data-based Multivariate Time Series Diffusion
               Imputation (HDDI) method. Unlike existing deep learning-based imputation methods, we design a
017
               historical data supplement module to match and fuse historical data to supplement the training data.
018
               Additionally, we propose a diffusion imputation module that utilizes the supplement training data to
019            achieve high-accuracy imputation even under high missing rate and long-term missing scenario. We
020            conduct extensive experiments on five public multivariate time series datasets, the results show that
021            our HDDI outperforms baseline methods across five datasets. Particularly, when the data missing
022            rate is 90%, HDDI improves accuracy by 25.15% compared to the best baseline method in the ran-
023            dom missing scenario, and by 13.64% in the long-term missing scenario. The code is available at
024            https://github.com/liuyu3880/HDDI project.

027   1     I NTRODUCTION
028
029   1.1   BACKGROUND
030
031   Multivariate time series data is ubiquitous across various application domains, including transportation Liu et al. (2023);
032
      Wu et al. (2019), industry Chang et al. (2024), and healthcare Li et al. (2023b). However, this data often suffers from
      losses due to sensor failures, communication breakdowns, or unexpected malfunctions. Such missing data damages the
033
      interpretability of data Silva et al. (2012); Chauhan et al. (2022) and severely affects the performance of subsequent tasks,
034
      such as climate change research and patient monitoring. Therefore, accurate imputation of missing data is crucial.
035
036   To address the problem of missing data imputation, some studies propose algorithms based on statistics and classic
037   machine learning Kreindler & Lumsden (2016); Little & Rubin (2019); Basharat & Shah (2009); Wang et al. (2006);
038
      Yu et al. (2016); Li et al. (2023a); Pujianto et al. (2019); Nelwamondo et al. (2007); Shu & Ye (2023). However, these
      methods rely on specific data assumptions Liu et al. (2023). For instance, linear interpolation methods typically assume
039
      a linear relationship between data points Kreindler & Lumsden (2016), while mean/median methods assume that the data
040
      follows a uniform distribution Little & Rubin (2019). The K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) methods assume that data points
041   have similar characteristics within their local neighborhoods Pujianto et al. (2019). When these assumptions do not hold,
042   the accuracy of data interpolation can be significantly affected.
043
044
      In contrast to statistics and classic machine learning-based algorithms, some recent studies propose deep learning-based
      imputation methods Cao et al. (2018); Yoon et al. (2017); Che et al. (2018); Cini et al. (2021); Suo et al. (2020). These
045
      methods leverage neural network models to extract data features and perform the imputation of missing values without
046
      relying on specific data assumptions. For example, some works use Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) Cao et al. (2018);
047   Yoon et al. (2017); Che et al. (2018) to capture temporal dependencies in the data and use these dependencies for imputing
048   missing values. Other methods Suo et al. (2020) capture dependencies between sequence elements through self-attention
049   mechanisms. However, these methods are prone to encountering the issue of error accumulation (i.e., inference of missing
050   values from inaccurate historical imputationLiu et al. (2019)).
051
      Additionally, some research introduces imputation methods based on generative models. For example, methods Gong
052
      et al.; Miao et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2018); Yoon et al. (2018a) based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) use
053   the generator to estimate missing values and the discriminator to assess whether these estimates align with real data.
054   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) Fortuin et al. (2020) map data to the latent space through an encoder and then gener-
055   ate imputation results from this latent space using a decoder. Through adversarial training, the generator progressively
056   improves the accuracy of missing data imputation, but they still suffer from training instability.
057
      With the development of generative models, the study Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) proposes the diffusion model, which
058
      is more robust to different types of noise and missing data compared to traditional generative models. The diffusion
059   model is applied to various applications, such as image processing Song et al. (2020), and audio signal processing Kong


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


060
      et al.. Morever, as the diffusion models can avoid the error accumulation issues in RNN-based imputation methods and
061   offer a more stable training process than generative adversarial networks, by using flexible architectures neural network
062   architecture, the diffusion model is also applied to the imputation problem to achieve higher performance. Such as, CSDI
063   Tashiro et al. (2021) is proposed to solve the data imputation problem based on the diffusion model. The diffusion model
064   starts with randomly sampled Gaussian noise and progressively removes noise to transform the noisy data into imputed
065   values.
066
067   1.2   C HALLENGES
068
069   Although a limited number of works begin to use diffusion models for data imputation and achieve promising results,
070   their effectiveness remains limited in scenarios with high missing rate and long-term missing data:
071
      High missing rate data. Since these deep learning models rely on self-supervised learning, they need to select a subset
072
      of the collected sparse measurement data as supervised data for training. This approach reduces the amount of extractable
073   information as the training data decreases. In scenarios with a high missing data rate, methods based on diffusion models
074   struggle to extract sufficient information, which affects the accuracy of imputing missing data.
075
076
      Long-term missing data. Due to equipment failures or communication issues, data from certain devices may be missing
      for extended periods, a situation known as long-term missing dataPark et al. (2023). On one hand, long-term missing data
077
      leads to a high missing rate. On the other hand, the absence of data from faulty equipment may cause imputation results
078
      to overfit data from non-faulty equipment while underfitting data from faulty equipment, results in lower imputation
079   accuracy.
080
081
082   1.3   C ONTRIBUTIONS
083
      To address the above challenges, we propose a novel imputation model: Historical Data-Based Multivariate Time Series
084
      Diffusion Imputation (HDDI). Unlike existing methods based on diffusion models, HDDI fully utilizes historical features
085
      by searching for similar data from historical records and combining it with current target observational data to create
086   new training data. By incorporating historical data, HDDI provides sufficient training data for diffusion-based imputation
087   algorithms, significantly improving the imputation accuracy of multivariate time series data in scenarios with high missing
088   rate and long-term missing scenario. Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
089
      1) We propose HDDI based on a diffusion model: By matching historical data to supplement the target observational
090
      data, HDDI addresses the challenges posed by high missing data rate and long-term missing scenarios. This approach
091
      resolves the issue where insufficient data features hinder the accurate prediction of missing data, providing more valuable
092   information for model training and enhancing the accuracy of predicting missing data at the current time.
093
094   2) We design a sliding window-based historical data matching and combination scheme: By selecting multiple
095
      segments of historical data that best match the current time segment to supplement training data, we tackle the problem
      of insufficient training data in self-supervised learning. Additionally, we design a historical data fusion scheme, carefully
096
      considering the similarity and temporal correlations between target observational data and historical data. We utilize
097
      multiple historical data segments and fuse them with target observational data through normalization to ensure that the
098   distribution of the fused training data approximates the range of true values.
099
100   3) We design a diffusion-based imputation algorithm: The diffusion model primarily consists of two processes: the
101
      diffusion process and the denoising process. The diffusion process trains a noise estimation model by simultaneously
      inputting the fused training data and some observational data, which provides rich feature information for the model’s
102
      training. The denoising process gradually removes the noise estimated by the noise estimation model from random noise
103
      data, ultimately resulting in accurate imputed data.
104
105   4) HDDI demonstrates excellent performance in experiments: We evaluate the proposed HDDI with five state-of-
106   the-art baseline methods under five multivariate time series datasets. The experimental result indicates that our proposed
107
      HDDI achieves the best performance compared to other baseline methods. Even in cases where 90% of the data was
      missing, our HDDI outperformed the best baseline method by 25.15%.

111
112
      In this section, we introduce some existing deep learning-based time series imputation methods.
113
114   1) Temporal Feature Extraction-Based Methods: When handling time series data with Recurrent Neural Networks
115   (RNNs), the study Che et al. (2018) proposes GRU-D, which estimates time series using a deep learning model and
116
      introduces the concept of time lag by employing hidden state decay to capture past features. M-RNN Yoon et al. (2018b)
      and BRITS Cao et al. (2018) both use bidirectional RNNs to incorporate bidirectional information flow, allowing them to
117
      learn patterns from the context around missing data and perform imputation. However, RNNs are prone to compounded
118
      errors Liu et al. (2019) during training, which accumulates with increasing sequence length, leading to reduced imputation
119   accuracy.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


120
      To address these limitations, some research shifts to methods based on self-attention mechanisms, such as SAITS Du
121   et al. (2023). These methods use self-attention mechanisms to directly model dependencies between sequence elements,
122   reducing the impact of compounded errors and improving the model’s ability to handle long sequences. However, such
123   a method requires a large amount of high-quality training data. When the training data is insufficient, this model cannot
124   achieve satisfactory imputation results.
125
      2) Generative-Based Methods: Imputation methods based on generative models are gaining increasing popularity. For
126
      example, GP-VAE Fortuin et al. (2020) utilizes a deep variational autoencoder (VAE) to obtain latent representations
127   and employs Gaussian processes in latent space to capture the global dynamics and structure of time series, providing an
128   advantage in handling continuous missing data. Even in long-term missing scenarios, GP-VAE leverages its understanding
129   of global dynamics to generate reasonable imputations. Similarly, SS-GAN Miao et al. (2021) employs semi-supervised
130   learning mechanisms to guide the learning process of generators and discriminators. Nevertheless, these methods often
131   face issues with interpolation accuracy caused by unstable training.
132
      Thanks to the introduction of diffusion model Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020), the study proposes CSDI Tashiro
133   et al. (2021) which learns data distribution through conditional score-based diffusion model, gradually transforming noise
134   into reasonable time series via denoising processes. CSDI improves imputation accuracy by incorporating observational
135   information into the diffusion model using conditional data, achieving better performance compared to other existing time
136   series imputation methods. However, practical scenarios often provide limited observational data, and a low sampling rate
137   may hinder the effectiveness of missing data imputation.
138
      Different from the aforementioned methods, our imputation model HDDI supplements training data by identifying and
139   matching historical data with current target imputation data to address issues related to low sampling rate. Building
140   on this, we propose a novel diffusion model that fuses target imputation data with matched historical data to achieve
141   high-precision imputation of missing data.

513   multivariate time series datasets. The dataset, baseline methods, and evaluation metrics used in the experiment are detailed

      baselines increases, because our method supplements training data with similar data from historical records, addressing
531
      the issue of insufficient training data caused by a low sampling rate.

      Compared with other SOTA baseline methods, our HDDI achieves the highest accuracy across all missing rates

      in long-term missing data scenarios. Since our HDDI approach uses historical data segments similar to the target

554   5.2   A BLATION S TUDIES
555
556   To prove the effectiveness of the Historical Data Matching Process and the Fusion Process in our HDDI, we conduct
557   ablation studies as follows:
558
      Firstly, to address RQ4 and validate the effectiveness of the Historical Data Matching Process, we randomly select 10%,
559
      30%, 50%, 70%, and 90% of observations as missing values from two datasets: ETT and Air-Quality. We design three
560
      sets of experiments for comparison:
561
562   1) The first set uses the nearest historical data segments as the data source for fusion from the history data stream, which
563   is denoted as HDDI(near-match).
564   2) The second set randomly selects historical data segments as the data source for fusion from the history data stream,
565   which is denoted as HDDI(rand-match).
566
      3) The third set does not use data from the history data stream as a supplement, instead directly training the diffusion
567
      process with only target observational data, which is denoted as HDDI(no-history).
568
569   After the Historical Data Matching Process, we proceed with the remaining steps as described in this paper to evaluate
570   the imputation results. As shown in Table 4 of Appendix 7.4, we can observe:
571   Our designed historical data matching scheme achieves better performance compared to all other three matching
572   methods. Due to the Historical Data Matching Process we designed, which finds historical data most similar to the current
573   target observation data using a sliding window-based approach, the supplemented historical data has features closer to the
574   current imputation data. This ensures that they provide more accurate information for the diffusion imputation module
575   and achieve higher imputation performance.
576   To address RQ5 and validate the effectiveness of the Fusion Process, we use the same random missing scenario on the
577   ETT and Air Quality datasets with 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, and 90% missing rates. We conduct a comparison experiment
578   called HDDI(aver-fusion), where, after identifying historical data segments similar to the current target observational
579   data, the fusion stage does not consider the similarity and temporal correlation scores. Instead, it uses a simple averaging
580   method to fuse the data, using the average of historical data as supplementary data for training the model. As shown in
581   Table 5 of Appendix 7.4, we can observe:
582   Our designed fusion scheme consistently outperforms the averaging fusion scheme across various missing rates.
583   Due to the design of our Fusion Process, which thoroughly considers the similarity in distance and temporal proximity
584   between historical data and current target observational data, we place more trust in historical data that is closer in both
585   time and distance to the current moment. As a result, our fused outcome is more accurate compared to simply taking the
586   average value and achieves higher accuracy imputation.
587   These ablation studies highlight the significance of both the Historical Data Matching Process and the Fusion Process in
588   our framework, demonstrating their contributions to achieving accurate and reliable imputation results.

592
593
594   To ensure high-precision imputation of missing data in multivariate time series under scenarios of high missing rate
595   or long-term data absence, we propose the HDDI model. By designing a historical data supplementation module, we
596
      select multiple historical data segments which most similar to the current target observation data from the historical
      data stream and fuse them with the current training data. To fully utilize the supplemented training data, we design a
597
      diffusion imputation module to achieve high-accuracy imputation in scenarios of high missing rate and long-term missing.
598
      Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our method outperforms in both high missing rate and long-term missing
599   scenarios across five multivariate time series datasets.

      Baseline Methods: We implement five SOTA deep learning-based multivariate series imputation methods as follows:
781
782         • BRITS Cao et al. (2018): Based on the recurrent neural network model, BRITS uses bidirectional recurrent
783           neural networks to fuse bidirectional information flow, learning patterns from the context around missing data
784           for imputation.
785         • GP-VAE Fortuin et al. (2020): Based on the variational autoencoder model, GP-VAE utilizes a deep variational
786           autoencoder (VAE) to obtain latent representations and employs the Gaussian process in latent space to capture
787           the global dynamics and structure of time series.
788
            • SS-GAN Miao et al. (2021): Combining RNN with a semi-supervised generative adversarial network model, SS-
789           GAN uses semi-supervised learning mechanisms to guide the learning process of generators and discriminators.
790
            • SAITS Du et al. (2023): A model based on a self-attention mechanism, SAITS models dependencies between
791
              sequence elements directly using self-attention mechanisms.
792
793         • CSDI Tashiro et al. (2021): Uses a conditional score-based diffusion model to estimate missing values by grad-
794
              ually transforming noise into coherent time series.

813   Missing Data Scenarios:
814   Our experiment simulated two common missing data scenarios: Random Missing and Long-term Missing.
815
816         • Random Missing (RM): We randomly select γ% of the data as missing data, while the remaining data serves
817           as the target observational data X. To evaluate the accuracy of the imputation algorithm under different missing
818           rates, we set γ to 10, 30, 50, 70, and 90, respectively.
819         • Long-term Missing (LM): Due to the high level of missing data in the PhysioNet healthcare dataset, it is not
820           feasible to approximate the long-term missing scenario. Instead, we select the other four datasets to test the
821           long-term missing scenario. We randomly select γ% of the features from each sample to be damaged. For each
822           damaged feature, we set all data points at all time slots to be missing. Similar to the random missing scenario,
823           we set γ to 10, 30, 50, 70, and 90, respectively.
