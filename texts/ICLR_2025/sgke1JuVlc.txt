009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the context of increasingly large neural network models and their associated
012            high energy consumption, Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) present a compelling
013            alternative to Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) due to their energy efficiency and
014            closer alignment with biological neural principles. However, directly training SNNs
015            with spatio-temporal backpropagation remains challenging due to their discrete
016            signal processing and temporal dynamics. Alternative methods, notably ANN-
017            SNN conversion, have enabled SNNs to achieve performance in various machine
018
               learning tasks, comparable to ANNs, but often to the expense of long latency
               needed to achieve such performance, especially on large scale complex datasets.
019
               The present work deals with ANN-SNN setting and identifies a new phenomenon
020
               we term “temporal misinformation”, where random spike rearrangement through
021            time in the converted SNN model improves its performance. To account for this,
022            we propose bio-plausible, two-phase probabilistic (TPP) spiking neurons to be used
023            in ANN-SNN conversion. We showcase the benefits of our proposed methods both
024            theoretically and empirically through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10/100
025            and a large-scale dataset ImageNet over a variety of architectures, reaching SOTA
026            performance.

      Furthermore, recent advancements in neuromorphic chip production Pei et al. (2019); DeBole

062   et al. (2023b), to name a few.
063   Training SNNs presents a challenge in itself, due to the very same reasons from which the advantages
064   of SNNs stem: their discrete processing of information. Unsupervised direct training, inspired by bio-
065   logical learning mechanisms, leverages local learning rules and spike timing to update weights Diehl
066   & Cook (2015). While these methods are computationally friendly and could be performed on the
067   specialized hardware, SNNs trained this way often underperform compared to models trained with
068   other methods, and there is still plenty of room for the understanding and improvement of this method.
069   On the other side, supervised training methods can be categorized in two branches: direct training
070   and ANN-SNN conversion based methods. The main challenge for direct training methods lies
071   in the discrete nature of spike production. Namely, the operation of comparison of the membrane
072   potential with the threshold is not differentiable, or, where it is, does not produce useful gradients.
073   The success of direct training hinges on the development of spatio-temporal backpropagation through
074   time (BPTT) and surrogate gradient methods O’Connor et al. (2018); Zenke & Ganguli (2018); Wu
075
      et al. (2018); Bellec et al. (2018); Fang et al. (2021a;b); Zenke & Vogels (2021); Mukhoty et al.
      (2024). Although, they address and overcome the main problem of non-differentiability of spikes,
076
      these methods encounter further challenges with deep architectures due to gradient instability and
077
      high computational costs during training simulations. Direct training focuses on optimizing not
078   only synaptic weights but also dynamic parameters like firing thresholds Wei et al. (2023) and leaky
079   factors Rathi & Roy (2023). Novel loss functions such as rate-based counting loss Zhu et al. (2024)
080   and distribution-based loss Guo et al. (2022) were proposed to provide sufficient positive gradients and
081   rectify the distribution of membrane potential during the propagation of binary spikes. Furthermore,
082   hybrid training methods Wang et al. (2022b) combine ANN-SNN conversion with BPTT to achieve
083   higher performance with low latency. Recent advancements include Ternary Spike Guo et al. (2024)
084   for enhanced information capacity and the reversible SNN Zhang & Zhang (2024) to reduce memory
085   costs during training.
086   The ground idea of ANN-SNN conversion is to use pre-trained ANN models to train an SNN. This
087   starts by copying the weights of the ANN model to the SNN model following the same architecture,
088   and then initializing hyperparameters of the spiking neurons in the SNN layers in such a way that
089   the rate of the spikes approximate the values of the corresponding activation layers in the ANN.
090   The advantages of this method lie in the fact that there is (usually) no extra computation needed for
091   training the SNNs, so the computation of gradients can largely be avoided, or just reduced to the
092
      calculations during fine-tuning of the SNN model. This method (of which we will say more in Section
      2) has been behind many of the state of the art performing SNNs, particularly on classification tasks.
093
094   The present work explores ideas that belong to the ANN-SNN conversion line of research. We start
095   by identifying a phenomenon, that is rather counter-intuitive and, to the best of our knowledge, has
096   gone unnoticed until now. Namely, when performing ANN-SNN conversion, the main assumption
097   is that the sole carrier of information is the rate of the spiking activity, and precise timing of the
098
      spikes should not affect the performance of the SNN Bu et al. (2023). We challenged this assumption
      by using a baseline SNN obtained through ANN-SNN conversion, following methods proposed in
099
      recent literature. Then, when passing the samples to the baseline model, after each spiking layer
100
      we permuted the spike trains by rearranging the spikes in the temporal dimension. Specifically, the
101   temporal order of spikes within each spike train was randomly shuffled. The permuted spike trains
102   were then passed to the following layer in the SNN, and this process was continued until the output
103   layer. The results of one of these initial experiments, comparing the performance of the “permuted”
104   model with the original model, are presented in Figure 1. For every latency we performed this
105   experiment, the “permuted” model surpassed the baseline and reached the original ANN accuracy
106   much earlier. We dubbed this occurrence “the temporal misinformation” in ANN-SNN conversion
107   and further explored it by giving it a more conceptual flavor in form of the bursting probabilistic
      spiking neurons which are designed to mimic the effect of permutations in SNNs. The proposed

      The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
114
115          • We recognize the “temporal misinformation” phenomenon in ANN-SNN conversion, chal-
116            lenging the underlying assumption of ANN-SNN conversion which states that the spike rate
117            is the sole carrier of information in the method.
118          • We propose a framework for its exploitation in ANN-SNN conversion utilizing two-phase
119            probabilistic spiking neurons. We provide the theoretical insights into their functioning and
120            superior performance, as well as support for their biological grounding.
121

122            method outperforms state-of-the-art conversion as well as the other training methods, in
123            terms of accuracy on large scale CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet datasets.

158   2.1   R ELATED WORK
159
160   ANN-SNN conversion leverages pre-trained ANNs to initialize SNNs, aiming to minimize ac-
161   curacy degradation by aligning ANN activations with SNN firing rates, as demonstrated in early
      works Rueckauer et al. (2017a); Cao et al. (2015). Subsequent studies addressed conversion errors

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      and improved temporal accuracy through techniques like weight normalization Diehl et al. (2015),
163   soft-reset mechanisms Rueckauer et al. (2017b); Han et al. (2020), and dynamic threshold adjust-
164   ment Stöckl & Maass (2021); Ho & Chang (2021); Wu et al. (2023). Efficient conversion with fewer
165   spikes was achieved through rate-coding and time-coding methods Kim et al. (2020a), as well as
166   specialized weight renormalization Sengupta et al. (2018).
167
      A recent direction involves modifying the ANN activation functions to reduce conversion errors.
168
      Methods using thresholded ReLU activation Ding et al. (2021) and quantized activation functions Bu
169   et al. (2022c); Liu et al. (2022); Hu et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2024) have achieved high accuracy at
170   lower latencies. However, these approaches often reduce the original ANN accuracy, limiting the
171   potential performance of the converted SNN. Techniques like Li & Zeng (2022); Wang et al. (2022a);
172   Liu et al. (2022) propose modifications to the inner function of IF neurons to reduce conversion errors.
173   Notably, a two-phase spiking neuron mechanism similar to ours has been used in Liu et al. (2022).
174
      Membrane potential and threshold initialization play crucial roles in reducing conversion errors. Many
175   methods utilize layer-wise maximum ANN activations, or some percentile of them, for threshold
176   initialization Rueckauer et al. (2017a); Deng & Gu (2021a); Li et al. (2021). Detailed studies on
177   membrane potential initialization and threshold settings are provided in Hao et al. (2023a); Bojkovic
178   et al. (2024). Post-conversion weight calibration Li et al. (2021); Bojkovic et al. (2024) further
179   enhances SNN performance, leading to hybrid training methods that combine ANN-SNN conversion
180   with fine-tuning.
181   In general, one can argue that ANN-SNN conversion based methods of training SNNs can be classified
182   in two categories. The first line of thought deals with modification on the ANN side, most notably in
183   quantization of the ANN activation functions, in order to reduce the conversion error in low latency.
184   The second line deals with modification on ANN side, where the spiking neuron mechanisms are
185   modified in order to reduce this error. The advantage in the former case comes from the lower latency
186   to have a good performance, but the disadvantage comes from the fact that quantization of the ANN
187   activations in general, yields the poorer ANN performance, hence limits the SNN performance as
188   well. In the latter case, the situation is reversed, the ANNs utilized have higher performance, but
189
      SNNs sometimes need longer latency to achieve it. Our approach belongs to the second category.
190   Direct training allows SNNs to exploit precise spike timing and operate within a few timesteps. The
191   success of direct training hinges on the development of spatio-temporal backpropagation through time
192   (BPTT) and surrogate gradient methods O’Connor et al. (2018); Zenke & Ganguli (2018); Wu et al.
193   (2018); Bellec et al. (2018); Fang et al. (2021a;b); Zenke & Vogels (2021); Mukhoty et al. (2024).
194   However, these methods encounter challenges with deep architectures due to gradient instability
195
      and high computational costs during training simulations. Various gradient-based methods leverage
      surrogate gradients O’Connor et al. (2018); Zenke & Ganguli (2018); Wu et al. (2018); Bellec
196
      et al. (2018); Fang et al. (2021a;b); Zenke & Vogels (2021); Mukhoty et al. (2024) to address the
197
      non-differentiable nature of spike functions. Direct training focuses on optimizing not only synaptic
198   weights but also dynamic parameters like firing thresholds Wei et al. (2023) and leaky factors Rathi &

208   3    M OTIVATION AND PROPOSED METHOD
209
210   When performing ANN-SNN conversion, one usually employs constant or rate encoding in the
211   obtained SNN model, with the underlying idea that the expectation of the input at each time step
212   is equal to the original input to the ANN model. In particular, there is no temporal information in
213   the encoding, as the precise timing of spikes does not carry any extra information. In the constant
214   encoding this is obvious, while in the rate encoding, for a fixed input channel, and for every time step,
215   the probability of having the spike is constant (and equal to the value of the channel assumed to be
      between 0 and 1).


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      The obtained SNN model is initialized in such a way that it approximates the outputs of the starting
217   ANN model, through the paradigm that for each spiking neuron, the average number of spikes it
218   produces, or its expectation of the output, should approximate the output of the corresponding ANN
219   neuron. In particular, one assumes and expects that there is no temporal information throughout
220   the SNN model, i.e. the spike train outputs of each SNN layer should not carry any extra temporal
221   information, other than the spike firing rates.
222
      To our surprise, we discovered that this was not the case (see Figure 1).
223
224
      3.1   P ERMUTING SPIKE TRAINS
225
226   To test the initial hypothesis of the absence of “temporal information”, we designed an experiment
227   where for an SNN model obtained through the ANN-SNN conversion, after each layer we would
228   collect the output spike trains, and permute them through the temporal dimension. More precisely,
229   for a fixed latency T and for each spiking layer, we would collect the output spike trains of temporal
230   length T , permute them, and pass them to the next layer, and continue this process until the output
231   layer. We used the constant encoding for the input. We further compared the performance of this
232   model with the original base SNN model, whose output spike trains have not been manipulated
233
      through permutations.
234   The performance of the base and “permuted” SNN models has been compared in two ways. First, for
235   the latency T and for the latencies t < T . What we discovered is that if we consider the latency Ttop
236   where the base model achieves the top accuracy, the performance of the two models is pretty much
237   the same. However, if we consider the latency T < Ttop , the “permuted” model outperforms the base
238   model, in some cases drastically. Moreover, the situation becomes more contrasted if we consider the
239
      latencies t < T . The reader can refer to the Figure 1 for more information, while the details of the
      experiment are in the Appendix.
240
241   The conclusion of these initial experiments is that, contrary to the expectation, ANN-SNN conversion
242   is not invariant under the temporal manipulation of output spike trains. Moreover, the effect of
243   permuting the spike trains yields better performance of the converted SNN model, a phenomenon to
244   which we refer as temporal misinformation in ANN-SNN conversion.

262   The previous sections hint at the motivation of the present work. Our aim is to answer the question:
263   How to incorporate the action of permutation of the output spike trains into the dynamics of the
264   spiking neurons?
265   Suppose that we want to permute the spikes trains coming from the layer ℓ. A general idea would be
266   to have a “permutator”- a layer immediately after, whose goal would be to collect all the spikes, and
267   outputs them in a permuted fashion, and sends such obtained spike trains to the following layer. One
268   may refer to Figure 2 (a) for the visual representation of this concept. This immediately suggests the
269   two-phase nature of the “permutator”, namely, in the first phase the incoming spikes are accumulated
      and the firing is delayed until the beginning of the second, firing phase.

      3.3    B IO - PLAUSIBILITY AND HARDWARE IMPLEMENTATION OF TPP NEURONS
339
340   Our proposed neurons have two distinct properties: The two-phase regime and probabilistic spike
341   firing. Both of these properties are biologically plausible and extensively studied in the neuroscience
342   literature. For example, the two phase regime can be related to firing after a delay of biological
343   spiking neurons, where a neuron collects the input beyond the threshold value and fires after delay or
344   after some condition is met. It could also be related to the bursting, when a biological neuron starts
345   emitting bursts of spikes, after a certain condition is met, effectively dumping their accumulated
346   potential. One can refer to Izhikevich (2007); Connors & Gutnick (1990); Llinás & Jahnsen (1982);
347
      Krahe & Gabbiani (2004) for more details.
348   On the other side, stochastic firing of biological neurons has been well studied as well, and different
349   aspects of noise introduction into firing have been proposed. One can refer to Shadlen & Newsome
350   (1994); Faisal et al. (2008); Softky & Koch (1993); Maass & Natschläger (1997); Pagliarini et al.
351   (2019); Stein et al. (2005), for some examples.
352   When it comes to implementation of TPP neurons on neuromorphic hardware, two phase regime can
353   be easily achieved on many of the modern neuromorphic that support programmable spiking neurons.
354   The stochastic firing can be achieved through random sampling which is, for example, supported on
355   IBM TrueNorth Merolla et al. (2014), Intel Loihi Davies et al. (2018), BrainScaleS-2 Pehle et al.
356   (2022), SpiNNaker Furber et al. (2014) neuromorphic chips.
357   The probabilistic spiking mechanism we introduce aligns with the stochastic firing behaviors observed
358   in biological neurons, a feature that has been effectively implemented in neuromorphic hardware
359   such as IBM’s TrueNorth DeBole et al. (2019); Merolla et al. (2014), Intel’s Loihi loi; Davies et al.
360   (2018), BrainScaleS-2 Pehle et al. (2022), SpiNNaker and SpiNNaker2. For example, TrueNorth
361   incorporates stochastic neuron models using on-chip pseudo-random number generators, enabling
362   probabilistic firing patterns that mirror our approach. Similarly, Loihi Gonzalez et al. (2024) supports
363   stochastic operations by adding uniformly distributed pseudorandom noise to neuronal variables,
364   facilitating the implementation of probabilistic spiking neurons.
365   To reduce the overall latency for processing inputs with our models, which yields linear dependence
366   on the number of layers (implied by the two phase regime), we note that as soon as a particular layer
367   has finished the firing phase, it can start receiving the input from the previous layer: The process
368   of classifying a dataset can be serialized. This has already been observed, for example in Liu et al.
369   (2022). Neuromophic hardware implementation of this serialization has been proposed as well, see
370   for example Das (2023); Song et al. (2021); Varshika et al. (2022).

       D    A LGORITHMS
973
974
       The baseline SNN neuron forward function (Algorithm 1) initializes the membrane potential to zero
975
       and iteratively updates it by adding the layer output at each timestep. Spikes are generated when
976
       the membrane potential exceeds a defined threshold, θ, and the potential is reset accordingly. This
977    function captures the core dynamics of spiking neurons. The Shuffle Mode (Algorithm 2) is an
978    extension of the baseline forward function. After generating the spikes across the simulation length,
979    this mode shuffles the spike train.
980
       The TPP Mode (Algorithm 3) introduces a probabilistic component to the spike generation process.
981
       Instead of a deterministic threshold-based spike generation, it uses a Bernoulli process where the
982
       probability of spiking is determined by the current membrane potential relative to the threshold
983    adjusted for the remaining timesteps.
984
985

990
        3:   for t = 1 to T do
        4:      v ← v + ℓ(x(t))
991
        5:      s ← (v ≥ θ) × θ
992
        6:      v ←v−s
993     7:      Store s(t)
994     8:   end for
995     9:   return s
996    10: end function

1000   Algorithm 2 SNN Neuron Forward Function of Shuffle Mode
1001   Require: SNN Layer ℓ; Input tensor x; Threshold θ; Simulation length T .
1002    1: function S HUFFLE M ODE(ℓ, x, θ, T )
1003    2:   v ← 0 {Initialize membrane potential}
1004    3:   for t = 1 to T do
1005
        4:      v ← v + ℓ(x(t))
        5:      s ← (v ≥ θ) × θ
1006
        6:      v ←v−s
1007
        7:      Store s(t)
1008    8:   end for
1009    9:   Shuffle the stored spikes s(1), s(2), . . . , s(T )
1010   10:   return shuffled s
1011   11: end function

1015   Algorithm 3 SNN Neuron Forward Function of TPP Mode
1016   Require: SNN Layer ℓ; Input tensor x; Threshold θ; Simulation length T .
1017    1: function TPPM ODE(ℓ, x, θ, T )
                   PT
1018    2:   v ← t=1 x(t) {Initialize membrane potential with the sum of inputs}
1019    3:   for t = 1 to T do
1020    4:      p ← Clamp(v/(θ × (T − t + 1)), 0, 1)
1021    5:      s ← Bernoulli(p) × θ
1022    6:      v ←v−s

        7:      Store s(t)
        8:   end for

        9:   return s

1287        2. Baseline analysis:
1288            (a) SNN models converted from a pretrained ANN aim to approximate the ANN activation
1289                values with firing rates. In particular, in lower time steps, the approximation is too
1290                coarse as the firing rate has only few possibilities to use to approximate the ANN (con-
1291                tinuous) values. For example, in T = 1, the baselines are attempting to approximate
1292                ANN activations with binary values 0 and θ.
1293            (b) Moreover, at each spiking layer, the spiking neurons at early time steps, use only
1294                the outputs of the previous spiking layer from the same, early, time steps. As this
1295                information is already too coarse, the approximation error accumulates throughout
                    the network, finally yielding in models that are underperfoming in low latencies.

1305

1307
                     weighted input from N1 . If a spiking neuron N1 in one layer has produced spike train

1312                 1

1323

       I   C ONVERSION ERROR ANALYSIS
1675
1676
       For this section, we use the terminology of Bu et al. (2022c) for the classification of conversion errors.
1677   We shortly recall three classes and we refer the reader to the original paper for more details:
1678
1679         1. Clipping error: When performing the ANN-SNN conversion, one uses some heuristics to
1680            set up the threshold, based on the corresponding distribution of the activation values. In
1681            particular, if A is the ANN activation, and θ is the set threshold for this particular layer,
1682            then the clipping error manifest itself in approximating A(·) with min(A(·), θ) (which is
1683            the maximum output of the spiking layer (before normalization)).
1684         2. Quantization error: As the spiking neurons produce discrete spikes (values 0 or θ (before
1685            normalization)), the quantization error manifest itself in using Tθ · max(0, ⌊ Tθ · x⌋) (which
1686            is tentative output of the spiking neuron) to approximate A(x).
1687         3. The unevenness error: This error potentially occur due to the non-uniformity of the input to
1688            the spiking neurons. In particular, it can happen that the neurons receive streams of positive
1689            input during certain time period, while receiving stream of negative input during another
1690            period. Ideally, two streams should cancel each other parts of each other, but, due to their
1691
                temporal mismatch, the neurons fire superfluous spikes, or they do not fire enough spikes as
                they theoretically should.
1692
1693   To study what is the main source of errors when performing ANN-SNN conversion with TPP neurons,
1694   we consider in detail the situation of a single layer of ANN neurons, and corresponding layer of
1695   SNN TPP neurons. For a function f and constant c, we denote by fc the clipping of f by c, that is
1696   fc (x) = min(f (x), c). For example, ReLUθ (x) := min(ReLU(x), θ) = min(max(0, x), θ).
