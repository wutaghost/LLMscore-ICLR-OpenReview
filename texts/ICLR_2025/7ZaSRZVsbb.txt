009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are extensively employed in graph machine
012            learning, with considerable research focusing on their expressiveness. Current
013            studies often assess GNN expressiveness by comparing them to the Weisfeiler-
014            Lehman (WL) tests or classical graph algorithms. However, we identify three key
015            issues in existing analyses: (1) some studies use preprocessing to enhance expres-
016            siveness but overlook its computational costs; (2) some claim the limited power
017            of the identical-feature WL test while enhancing expressiveness using distinct fea-
018            tures, thus creating a mismatch; and (3) some characterize message-passing GNNs
019
               (MPGNNs) with the CONGEST model but make unrealistic assumptions about
               computational resources, allowing NP-Complete problems to be solved in O(m)
020
               depth. We contend that a well-defined computational model is urgently needed to
021
               serve as the foundation for discussions on GNN expressiveness. To address these
022            issues, we introduce the Resource-Limited CONGEST (RL-CONGEST) model,
023            incorporating optional preprocessing and postprocessing to form a framework
024            for analyzing GNN expressiveness from an algorithmic alignment perspective.
025            Our framework sheds light on computational aspects, including the computational
026            hardness of hash functions in the WL test and the role of virtual nodes in reducing
027            network capacity. Additionally, we suggest that high-order GNNs correspond to
028            first-order model-checking problems, offering new insights into their expressive-
029            ness.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have attracted widespread attention in the graph machine learning
035   community due to their impressive performance in areas such as recommendation systems, drug dis-
036   covery, and combinatorial optimization. One key area of research has focused on characterizing the
037   expressive power of existing GNNs and developing new models with enhanced expressive power.
038   Existing work in this area typically aligns GNNs with various algorithms. One line of research fo-
039
      cuses on connecting GNNs to the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) graph isomorphism test and its variants.
      For instance, Xu et al. (2019) pioneered the exploration of the relationship between message-passing
040
      GNNs (MPGNNs) and the WL test. Several studies (Morris et al., 2019; Maron et al., 2019; Feng
041
      et al., 2023) have proposed high-order GNNs inspired by the k-WL test and the k-Folklore WL
042   (FWL) test, showing that these models exhibit stronger power compared to standard MPGNNs. Ad-
043   ditionally, works such as (Alsentzer et al., 2020; Cotta et al., 2021; Papp et al., 2021; Feng et al.,
044   2022; Frasca et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023a) introduced subgraph GNNs, where subgraphs are
045   obtained through sampling or partitioning, followed by message-passing on these subgraphs. Fur-
046   thermore, Zhou et al. (2023b); Zhang et al. (2024) analyzed the counting capabilities of different
047   GNN types. Other studies focus on aligning GNNs with traditional graph algorithms. For instance,
048   Zhang et al. (2023) designed a GD-WL framework, which incorporates precomputed distance in-
049   formation as additional features in message-passing, enabling the detection of graph biconnectivity.
050
      Additionally, Loukas (2020) attempted to align MPGNNs with the CONGEST model in distributed
      computing. They used existing lower bounds on the communication complexity of graph algorithms
051
      in the CONGEST model to derive lower bounds on the width and depth of MPGNNs when simulat-
052
      ing these algorithms.
053
      We carefully revisit these works and identify inconsistent or unreasonable results among them:

      • Underestimated Preprocessing Time Complexity. Some existing works employ preprocessing
055     techniques, such as substructure recognition or distance computation, to enhance their models
056     and show that the proposed models can perform algorithmic tasks beyond the capabilities of the
057     standard WL test. However, we observe that some of these works underestimate the computational
058     cost of preprocessing, sometimes resulting in a preprocessing time complexity that exceeds that of
059     the algorithmic task intended to show the model’s expressiveness, which may lead to undesirable
060     results, such as allowing directly precomputing answers to the algorithmic task as features.
061
      • Mismatch Between Identical-Feature WL Test and Distinct Features. Existing works com-
062
        paring GNNs with WL tests typically derive negative results regarding the WL test, such as its
063
        inability to distinguish certain toy example graphs. Some of these works then propose models
064     that incorporate additional features to enhance expressiveness. We identify that their analysis of
065     the WL test is based on the anonymous (identical-feature) setting, whereas the solution employs
066     distinct features. Therefore, directly comparing the identical-feature WL test with models that
067     violate this setting by incorporating distinct features creates a mismatch.
068
069   • CONGEST Model Addresses Mismatch but Retains Unrealistic Assumptions. A proposal
070
        suggests resolving the mismatch between the identical-feature WL test and distinct features by
        using the CONGEST model as a computational framework to characterize MPGNNs. However,
071
        we argue that directly adopting the CONGEST model as a computational model for GNNs can
072
        lead to unrealistic outcomes, such as enabling GNNs to solve many NP-Complete problems in
073     O(m) rounds, due to its implicit assumption of unlimited computational resources.
074
075   We conclude that these inconsistent or unreasonable results stem from the ad-hoc settings in these
076   works, arising from the lack of a well-defined computational model to characterize GNNs and
077   analyze their expressive power. Motivated by this, we propose the Resource-Limited CONGEST
078   (RL-CONGEST) model, a simple and elegant computational framework for characterizing GNNs.
079   This model extends the standard CONGEST model by introducing constraints on computational
080   resources and incorporating optional preprocessing and postprocessing phases. It addresses key
081   issues by accounting for the complexity of preprocessing and postprocessing, explicitly allowing the
082
      use of distinct features as node IDs, and imposing limitations on nodes’ computational resources.
      Additionally, we present several novel theoretical results using this model:
083
084
      • WL Test Requires Large Networks to Compute. We find that previous works have underesti-
085
        mated the complexity of the H ASH function in the WL problem. In the RL-CONGEST model,
086
        if no preprocessing – such as graph modification or the use of additional features – is permitted,
087     we prove that the H ASH function in the WL test typically requires the network capacity (depth
088     multiplied by width) to be linear in relation to the graph’s size for computation.
089
090   • Virtual Nodes Reduce Network Size for WL Test. We present evidence suggesting that virtual
091     nodes can enhance the performance of MPGNNs. Specifically, we prove that introducing a virtual
092     node can reduce the network capacity required to compute one iteration of the WL problem.
093
      • Aligning High-Order GNNs with Model Checking is Natural. Additionally, we leverage in-
094     sights from descriptive and fine-grained complexity theories to argue that aligning high-order
095     GNNs with the model checking problem is more natural.
096

101   Section 5, we present several open problems that may serve as directions for future work.

164   Distributed computing involves multiple processors collaborating to compute a common result. A
165   distributed computing model is an abstract framework used to characterize this process. LOCAL and
166   CONGEST proposed by (Linial, 1987; 1992; Peleg, 2000) are two classic distributed computing
167   models based on synchronous message-passing between processors. In this paper, we follow the
168   model definitions from (Ghaffari, 2022). These models are based on an n-node graph G = (V =
169   [n], E), where G is assumed to be simple and undirected unless stated otherwise. Each node in the
170
      network hosts a processor. Initially, each processor knows the total number of nodes n, its unique
      identifier in [n], and its initial features. In each round, a node computes based on its knowledge and
171
      sends messages to its neighbors, which may differ for each. By the end of the round, it receives all
172
      messages from its neighbors. In this model, each node must determine its own portion of the output.
173   This process is described in (Loukas, 2020) as:

      2.4   BASIC C ONCEPTS IN F IRST-O RDER L OGIC
183   First-Order Logic (FOL) is a formal system widely used in mathematics and various fields of com-
184   puter science. An formula in FOL is composed of variable symbols such as x, y, z, and so on;
185   punctuation symbols like parentheses and commas; relation symbols or predicates such as P , Q, R,
186   and so forth; logical connectives including ∨, ∧, ¬, →, and ↔; and logical quantifiers, specifically
187   the universal quantifier ∀ and the existential quantifier ∃. A sentence is a special case of a formula
188   where all variables are quantified; in other words, there are no free variables. We also introduce
189   an extension to standard FOL called First-Order Logic with Counting (FOLC), which incorporates
190   additional counting quantifiers. Specifically, for any natural number i ∈ N, we define the counting
191
      quantifiers ∃≥i , ∃≤i , and ∃=i x. The expression ∃≥i xφ(x) (∃≤i xφ(x), ∃=i xφ(x)) means that there
      exist at least (or at most, exactly, respectively) i elements that satisfy the property φ. We use Lk and
192
      C k to denote the sets of FOL and FOLC sentences, respectively, that use no more than k variables.

197   Many studies have analyzed the expressiveness of GNNs, but they lack a well-defined computational
198   model as a foundation, often relying on ad-hoc methods that lead to unreasonable outcomes.

200   3.1   U NDERESTIMATED P REPROCESSING T IME C OMPLEXITY
201
      Many GNNs fit into a “preprocessing-then-message-passing” framework. Given an input graph G
202
      with features X, they first perform preprocessing to build a new graph G′ with updated features
203
      X ′ , followed by message passing on G′ . For example, high-order GNNs based on k-WL and k-
204   FWL tests construct graphs G′ = (V k , E ′ ) on k-tuples of nodes, where E ′ = {(u, v) ∈ (V k )2 :
205   dH (u, v) = 1}. Subgraph GNNs and GNNs with additional precomputed features naturally fit this
206   framework. These models typically target algorithmic tasks beyond the capabilities of the standard
207   WL test; however, in some cases, the time complexity of the preprocessing phase exceeds that of
208   the algorithmic task used to show the model’s superior expressiveness, which is unreasonable from
209   a complexity alignment perspective.
210   Finding Pattern Graphs is Computationally Expensive. One example comes from subgraph
211   GNNs, which identify pattern subgraphs H in the input graph G. We note that without restrictions
212   on H, it implies overly powerful preprocessing capabilities. For instance, Thiede et al. (2021);
213   Bouritsas et al. (2023); Wollschläger et al. (2024) proposed variants of GNNs and WL tests that
214   utilize hand-crafted features by recognizing subgraphs. They argued that certain subgraph schemes
215   could make models more powerful than k-WL for any k. However, these models achieve full expres-
      siveness only when no constraints are imposed on H, implicitly assuming the existence of oracles

                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      capable of counting isomorphic subgraphs. Such preprocessing requirements are overly strong from
217   a theoretical perspective. The counting version of subgraph isomorphism is a #P-Complete prob-
218   lem, as its decision version is NP-Complete (Karp, 1972) and by the definition of #P-completeness.
219   By Toda’s Theorem (Toda, 1991) PH ⊆ P#P , we conclude that a polynomial number of queries to
220   a subgraph isomorphism counting oracle can solve any problem in the polynomial hierarchy PH,
221   rendering such requirements implausible.
222
      Computing Distances Exceeds Biconnectivity Detection Time. Another example can be found in
223
      distance-based GNNs. Zhang et al. (2023) introduced the GD-WL framework and proved that the
224
      WL test cannot recognize the biconnectivity of a graph, while the proposed framework can. How-
225   ever, this framework encounters the issue where the computation time for all-pair distances exceeds
226   that of biconnectivity detection. Specifically, the biconnectivity of a graph can be determined in
227   linear time, O(m), using Tarjan’s algorithm (Tarjan, 1972); whereas the worst-case time complex-
228   ities for exactly computing all-pairs shortest path distances (SPDs) and resistance distances (RDs)
229   are both Õ (min {nm, nω })1 . Additionally, we point out through the following theorem that the
230   “existence of more efficient approximate algorithms” claim remains debatable due to the lack of
231   sensitivity analysis on the expressiveness with respect to error in the precomputed RDs.
232
      Theorem 1. For any integer n ≥ 3, there exist two graphs with n nodes, Pn and Cn , such that for
233
      any two adjacent nodes in Pn , the resistance distance is 1, while in Cn , it is 1 − 1/n. Furthermore,
234
      Pn is neither vertex- nor edge-biconnected, whereas Cn is both vertex- and edge-biconnected.
235
236
      We defer the proofs of all theorems to the appendices to save space. Therefore, if the RD-WL
237
      framework requires the error to be less than Θ (1/n) to fully show the expressiveness, to the best
238
      of our knowledge, no existing approximation algorithms can compute all-pairs RDs within this
239   error threshold in o (min {nm, nω }) time. Allowing higher preprocessing costs to solve a lower-
240   complexity problem can lead to undesirable outcomes, such as using direct answers as features.
241   The following theorem show this by demonstrating that precomputed RDs can be used to directly
242   identify whether an edge is a cut edge, rendering subsequent message-passing phase unnecessary.
243
244   Theorem 2. Given any edge (u, v) ∈ E in an undirected, unweighted graph G, (u, v) is a cut
245
      edge if and only if the resistance distance R(u, v) = 1.

248   3.2   M ISMATCH B ETWEEN I DENTICAL -F EATURE WL T EST AND D ISTINCT F EATURES
249
250   We observe that many existing works (Bouritsas et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wollschläger et al.,
251   2024) which compare GNNs’ expressiveness to the WL test handling the anonymous (identical-
252
      feature) setting either in an ad-hoc manner. These works equate the expressiveness of MPGNNs
      with the WL test, which fails to distinguish toy examples. To address this, they propose adding
253
      features to enhance expressiveness. However, this approach may conflict with the identical-feature
254
      setting, and their models’ expressiveness are not compared to distinct-feature MPGNNs (equivalent
255   to CONGEST, as discussed in Section 3.3). Moreover, equating MPGNNs with the WL test over-
256   looks the fact that real-world graphs often have features, making the claim that MPGNNs are weak
257   based solely on the WL test questionable.
258
      WL Test is Weak in the Identical-Feature Setting. As described by Huang & Villar (2021), the
259
      WL test operates under an identical-feature setting, where all nodes are initially assigned the same
260
      color and the process relies solely on node colors, making it unable to distinguish between nodes
261   with identical colors during its iterations. This limited expressive power is often exemplified by
262   its inability to distinguish certain graph pairs, such as two disjoint triangle graphs (C3 ∪ C3 ) and a
263   six-node cycle (C6 ). As shown in Figure 1, the “type” of each node, which consists of its color and
264   the multiset of its neighbors’ colors, is the same ( , {{ , }}) for all nodes in both graphs. After
265   applying the H ASH function, all node colors remain identical, so the WL test cannot distinguish
266   between the graphs due to the identical multisets.

      3.3   CONGEST A DDRESSES M ISMATCH BUT R ETAINS U NREALISTIC A SSUMPTIONS
293
294   We observe that one existing work (Loukas, 2020) attempts to align MPGNNs with the CONGEST
295   model, which unintentionally resolves the inconsistency in the identical-feature setting. However,
296   directly using the CONGEST model as a computational model introduces a problem: nodes are
297   assumed to have unlimited computational resources, which leads to impractical outcomes.
298
      Breaking Anonymity Empowers Models! In the previous section, we noted that many variants
299   enhance the expressive power by adding precomputed features, which may implicitly rely on node
300   IDs to distinguish nodes. This raises a natural question: can we directly improve the expressive
301   power by explicitly incorporating node IDs into the framework of the WL test?
302
      When comparing the equation of MPGNNs with the WL test, it becomes clear that MPGNNs can
303
      be viewed as the WL test without the constraints of identical node features and the H ASH function.
304
      Meanwhile, MPGNNs are similar to the CONGEST model if we compare Equation 1 and 6. Thus,
305   MPGNNs and CONGEST models are expected to have stronger expressive power than the WL tests
306   once these limitations are removed. Loukas (2020) provides evidence for this through the following
307   theorem by aligning MPGNNs with LOCAL and CONGEST models:
308   Theorem 3 ((Loukas, 2020)). MPGNN can compute any computable function over connected
309   graphs if the conditions are jointly met: (1) each node is uniquely identified; (2) the message and
310   update functions are Turing-complete for every ℓ; and (3) the depth and width are sufficiently large.
311
312   Moreover, Pritchard & Thurimella (2011) proposed a CONGEST algorithm that solves the edge-
313   biconnectivity problem in O(D) rounds, challenging the claim that MPGNNs are weak.
314   Direct Use of CONGEST Is Inappropriate. In the aforementioned paper (Loukas, 2020), the au-
315   thor proposes using the CONGEST model from distributed computing to characterize MPGNNs, as
316   it permits distinct features and supports more complex update functions compared to hash functions,
317   making it a closer representation of the real-world implementation of MPGNNs than the WL test.
318   However, we argue that directly using the CONGEST model as a computational model for MPGNN
319   is not entirely appropriate, as unlimited computational resources assumption can lead to unrealistic
320   and surprising results, as stated in the following theorem:
321   Theorem 4. If we allow a single node to have unbounded computational power to solve any
322   computable problem, then every NP-Complete decision problems on undirected unweighted con-
323   nected graphs can be solved by the CONGEST model in O(m) rounds.

325   model for MPGNNs is inappropriate due to the unlimited computational resources for nodes.

328   4       P ROPOSED C OMPUTATIONAL M ODEL AND O UR R ESULTS
329
330   In the previous section, we discussed the inconsistent or unreasonable results in existing studies on
331
      the expressiveness of GNNs. We argue that these problems primarily arise from the lack of a well-
      defined computational model for GNNs, leading researchers to propose various ad-hoc solutions,
332
      some of which are inconsistent or unreasonable. In this section, we introduce a computational model
333
      to characterize GNNs, introduce how it addresses the aforementioned issues, and prove several
334   interesting conclusions using this model.

      4.2   V IRTUAL N ODES R EDUCE N ETWORK S IZE FOR WL T EST
427
428   Several works have attempted to enhance the performance of GNNs by introducing a virtual node
429   that connects to all or some nodes in the original graph (Gilmer et al., 2017; Hwang et al., 2022).
430   Subsequent studies have analyzed the impact of this node. For instance, Barceló et al. (2020) show
431   that virtual nodes can bring GNNs closer to a C 2 classifier, while Rosenbluth et al. (2024) compare
      MPGNNs with virtual nodes and graph transformers. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work


                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      has explored how a virtual node helps reduce the network’s capacity when simulating one iteration
433   of the WL test. We address this in the following theorem:
434
435   Theorem 7. There exists a deterministic RL-CONGEST algorithm that can simulate one iteration
436   of the WL test by adding a virtual node, which connects to other nodes, as preprocessing. The
437   algorithm operates with width w and depth d satisfying dw = O(∆), where ∆ is the maximum
438
      degree of the graph before the addition of the virtual node. Additionally, it is sufficient to set the
      nodes’ computational resource class to C = DTIME(n2 log n).
439
440
441   Some studies suggest that virtual nodes do not enhance expressive power (Zhu et al., 2023), which
442   contrasts with empirical evidence showing improvements in model performance. We find this is
443
      because they often equate “expressive power” with the ability to compute specific functions, akin
      to computability. Through our analysis, we introduce a computational model that provides a more
444
      refined view of expressive power by examining problem complexity and focusing on resource usage.
445
      This approach shows that virtual nodes can reduce the network size required to simulate the WL test,
446   deepening our understanding of their impact.

      4.3    A LIGNING H IGH -O RDER GNN S WITH M ODEL C HECKING IS NATURAL
449
450   In this section, we show from both fine-grained and descriptive complexity perspectives that it is
451   more natural to align higher-order GNNs with the C k model checking problem. We will begin by
452   introducing the model checking problem and the related model equivalence problem.
453
      The Model Checking (MC) problem asks whether, given a model A and a logic sentence φ, the
454   sentence φ holds in A (i.e., A |= φ). In this paper, we focus on cases where the model is a graph G,
455   and φ uses only the edge predicate E(x, y) and the equality predicate =(x, y)3 . The Lk MC problem
456   is highly expressive, capturing many key problems. For instance, deciding whether G |= φ△ , where
457   φ△ := ∃x∃y∃z(E(x, y) ∧ E(y, z) ∧ E(z, x)), determines whether G contains a triangle subgraph.
458   Due to its expressiveness, the Lk MC problem has been widely studied. It applies to database queries
459   like SQL (Gao et al., 2017), formal verification (Godefroid, 1997), and is central to fine-grained
460   complexity in P (Puatracscu & Williams, 2010; Williams, 2014; Gao et al., 2017). In Appendix L,
                                                                                                         k
461   we provide evidence from theoretical computer science
                                4                   k   k−1
                                                               to support the classification of the PNF L
462   model checking problem in the Θ̃ min{n , m            } complexity class.
463   Another related problem is the Model Equivalence (ME) problem. Given two models A and B, and
464   a class of logic sentences, the task is to determine whether for any sentence φ in that class, A |= φ
465   if and only if B |= φ. In other words, the goal is to check whether that logic cannot distinguish
466   between the two models. Two important results that connect descriptive complexity and WL tests
467   were proven by Cai et al. (1989) and Grohe (2017), showing that, for any k ≥ 3, the expressiveness
468   of (k − 1)-FWL and k-WL is equivalent to C k ME problem. Another result by Grohe (1998) shows
469   that the expressiveness of both the standard WL test and the 2-WL test is equivalent to C 2 ME
470   problem. This means that the output colors from WL tests provide only a “type” of the graph,
471
      and we cannot directly interpret it for specific tasks such as determining whether a graph contains
      a triangle or is biconnected. The most we can infer is that if two graphs produce the same color
472
      multisets, then either both contain a triangle (or are biconnected, respectively), or neither does.
473
474   Therefore, we argue that from a computational model perspective, it is more meaningful to discuss
475   the expressiveness of GNNs in terms of solving problems, such as model checking, rather than
476   limiting the discussion to model equivalence, which only determines whether graph pairs are indis-
477
      tinguishable. A natural approach is to align higher-order GNNs, inspired by k-WL or (k − 1)-WL
478   tests, with the C k MC problem. We support this claim by proving the following weaker theorem:
479   Theorem 8 (Informal). If constructing the k-WL graph and additional features as preprocessing
480   is allowed, the RL-CONGEST model can solve the PNF C k model checking problem in O(k 2 )
481   rounds. Additionally, the computational resources required by each node are C = DTIME(k 2 n).

      6       C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   In this paper, we identify three common issues in existing analyses of GNNs’ expressive power,
530   stemming from the absence of a well-defined computational model. To address this, we introduce
531   the RL-CONGEST model, which includes optional preprocessing and postprocessing phases, as
532   a standard framework for analyzing GNNs. Our framework addresses these issues and produces
533   several noteworthy results, including the hardness of the WL problem, which may be of independent
534   interest to the field of distributed computing. Additionally, we outline some open problems for
535   potential future research.

944   complexity which was first introduced by Yao (1979).
945
      Two-party communication complexity involves two participants, Alice and Bob, who collaborate to
946
      compute a function f : X × Y → Z, where X and Y are their input domains, respectively. They
947   agree on a strategy beforehand but are separated before receiving their inputs (x, y) ∈ X × Y . They
948   then exchange messages to compute f (x, y), with the goal of minimizing the total number of bits
949   exchanged.
950
      In deterministic communication, the strategy is fixed, and the minimum number of bits required
951
      to compute f in this setting is known as the deterministic communication complexity, denoted by
952
      D(f ). Similarly, in randomized communication, where Alice and Bob can use random bits and a
953   two-sided error of ϵ is allowed, the minimum number of bits required is the randomized commu-
954   nication complexity. If the randomness is private, it is denoted by Rϵprv (f ), and if it is public, it is
955   denoted by Rϵpub (f ).
