009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Evolving relations in real-world networks are often modelled by temporal graphs.
012            Temporal Graph Neural Networks (TGNNs) emerged to model evolutionary be-
013            haviour of such graphs by leveraging the message passing primitive at the core of
014            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). It is well-known that GNNs are vulnerable to
015            several issues directly related to the input graph topology, such as under-reaching
016            and over-squashing—we argue that these issues can often get exacerbated in tem-
017            poral graphs, particularly as the result of stale nodes and edges. While graph
018            rewiring techniques have seen frequent usage in GNNs to make the graph topol-
019            ogy more favourable for message passing, they have not seen any mainstream
020
               usage on TGNNs. In this work, we propose Temporal Graph Rewiring (TGR), the
               first approach for graph rewiring on temporal graphs, to the best of our knowl-
021
               edge. TGR constructs message passing highways between temporally distant
022
               nodes in a continuous-time dynamic graph by utilizing expander graph propa-
023            gation, a prominent framework used for graph rewiring on static graphs which
024            makes minimal assumptions on the underlying graph structure. On the chal-
025            lenging TGB benchmark, TGR achieves state-of-the-art results on tgbl-review,
026            tgbl-coin, tgbl-comment and tgbl-flight datasets at the time of writing. For
027            tgbl-review, TGR has 50.5% improvement in MRR over the base TGN model
028            and 22.2% improvement over the base TNCN model. The significant improve-
029            ment over base models demonstrates clear benefits of temporal graph rewiring.

034
      Graph representation learning (Hamilton, 2020) aims to learn node representations on graph struc-
035
      tured data to solve tasks such as node property prediction (Hamilton et al., 2017; Kipf & Welling,
036   2016), link prediction (Ying et al., 2018; Zitnik et al., 2018) and graph property prediction (Gilmer
037   et al., 2017). Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Veličković et al., 2017; Xu
038   et al., 2018) capture relationships between nodes following the message passing paradigm (Gilmer
039   et al., 2017). GNNs have been successfully applied to model many real-world networks such as
040   biological networks (Johnson et al., 2023; Zitnik et al., 2018) and social networks (Ying et al., 2018).
041
      Graph rewiring. GNNs operate through a message passing mechanism which aggregates informa-
042   tion over a node’s direct neighbourhood at each GNN layer. It is by now well known that such an ap-
043   proach exposes GNNs to several vulnerabilities that are inherently tied to the input graph’s topology,
044   such as under-reaching (Barceló et al., 2020) and over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Di Gio-
045   vanni et al., 2024). In static GNNs, such issues are frequently addressed using graph rewiring,
046   where the input graph is altered such that it connects distant nodes. There are several methods
047   applied to rewire static GNNs such as diffusion-based graph rewiring models (Gasteiger et al.,
048   2019) or reducing negative Ricci curvature (Topping et al., 2021), leading to a boost in performance.
049   Temporal graph learning (TGL). Temporal graph learning is an emerging field which aims to
050   study evolving relations in dynamic real world networks (Kazemi et al., 2020). These evolving
051   relations are modelled by temporal graphs, wherein nodes and edges can be inserted or deleted over
052   time. Accordingly, a more generic class of temporal graph neural networks (TGNNs) (Longa et al.,
053   2023) are designed to capture the evolution of such graphs by introducing novel model components
      such as temporal memory (Kazemi et al., 2020) and time-encoding (Xu et al., 2020).

115   2     R ELATED W ORK
116
117   Temporal graph neural networks. Graph-based TGNNs (Yu et al., 2023; Cong et al., 2023; Xu
118   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022) leverage structural features of the input graph for learning temporal
119   graph representations. Although these architectures achieve state-of-the-art performance on smaller
120   TGB datasets (Huang et al., 2023), they rely on heavy processing of structural features, resulting
121   in limited opportunities for scaling. Memory-based TGNNs (Rossi et al., 2020; Trivedi et al.,
122   2019; Johnson et al., 2023) use temporal memory to retain historical node information by utilizing
123   additional model components such as recurrent networks (Rossi et al., 2020) or two-time scale deep
124   temporal point processes (Trivedi et al., 2019) to dynamically keep track of node interactions as
125
      they occur. Recently, Zhang et al. (2024) extend Neural Common Neighbor (NCN) to the temporal
      domain, leveraging pairwise representations and link-specific features. Memory-based architectures
126
      are successful in processing larger datasets, however they lack the ability to observe long-range de-
127
      pendencies between nodes in dynamic graphs. In contrast, our method demonstrates ability to both
128   scale well with increase of dataset size and observe long-range dependencies between temporal data
129   points, due to its usage of expander graphs, which require no additional dynamic pre-processing.
130
131
      Static graph rewiring. Graph rewiring has been employed to address bottlenecks and over-
      squashing in static GNNs, leading to an impressive boost in performance. For instance, diffusion-
132
      based graph rewiring (Gasteiger et al., 2019) diffuses additional edges in the graph with use of ker-
133
      nels such as PageRank (Page et al., 1999). However, as stated by Topping et al. (2021), such models
134   generally fail to reduce bottlenecks in the input graph. In contrast, Topping et al. (2021) propose to
135   modify a portion of edges guided by their Ricci curvature. However, this method incurs high pre-
136   processing cost and it is sub-optimal for analyzing large graphs, such as continuous-time dynamic
137   graphs, which are the focus of our paper. Deac et al. (2022) introduce expander graph propagation
138   (EGP), which, unlike prior rewiring methods, requires no dedicated preprocessing: EGP propa-
139   gates information over an expander graph which is independent from the input graph. EGP thus
140   minimises computational overhead while exhibiting favourable topological properties for message
141   passing. This makes EGP our preferred method of choice for TGR, as its rewiring side-steps the
142
      added temporal complexity of dynamic graphs.

      3.3   T EMPORAL UNDER - REACHING
226
227   The design of TGNs, particularly its local memory updates, represents an elegant tradeoff between
228   expressive power and favourable computational complexity. However, as we will show, this design
229   leaves TGNs vulnerable to more severe forms of the under-reaching effect (Barceló et al., 2020).
230   Under-reaching concerns itself with the questions of the form: is node u able to make any local
231   decisions in a way that depends on features of another node v? Or, equivalently, will features of
232   node v mix into the embeddings of node u?
233   For k-layer GNNs over static graphs G = (V, E), under-reaching is simple to define: it occurs
234   between any two nodes u, v ∈ V for which k < dG (u, v), where dG (u, v) is the shortest path length
235   between u and v in G. Clearly, it will not be possible for the information from node v to mix into
236   node u’s embeddings in this case, as each GNN layer mixes information that is one hop further.
237   What happens in temporal graphs G≤τ , for our previously studied TGNN framework?

278   3.4     F URTHER COMPLICATIONS
279
280   Note that we demonstrated temporal under-reaching in the “most optimistic possible” case, wherein
281   we’re starting with a static initial set of features (X(0)) and merely aiming to spread it using the
282   temporal edges. In practice, there are several aspects of TGNNs’ implementation that further ex-
283   acerbate the under-reaching effect. In all of the below examples, we track a possible mixing path
        0 t   1  t      tn−1  n    t
284   u←
       →  w1 ←
             →  . . . ←−→ wn ←
                             →  v.
285
      Temporal batching. In practice, temporal edges are seldom processed one-at-a-time; for efficiency
286   reasons, they are divided into temporal batches, where groups of edges happening in the same time
287   interval are processed together. Concretely, we break up our interval into pieces b0 = [0, τ0 ), b1 =
288   [τ0 , τ1 ), . . . , and we denote by b(t) ∈ N the batch identifier in which an edge at time t would be
289   processed. Since all edges in a batch propagate simultaneously, this means they cannot feature on the
290   same mixing path. Hence the constraint on our tracked path becomes b(t0 ) < b(t1 ) < · · · < b(tn )
291   for temporal mixing to occur, and this is a stricter condition than t0 < t1 < · · · < tn .
292   Dynamic node/edge updates. It is quite common that new information may enter the temporal
293   graph beyond edge addition, either simply as novel node or edge features to be taken into account,
294   or even entirely new nodes entering the network. In this case, we are attempting to analyse a more
295   generic form of temporal mixing: “If a node/edge is updated at time t, does this information reach
296   another node in time for link prediction at time τ ?”. This translates to a condition t ≤ t0 < t1 <
297   · · · < tn , as any edges happening before the information came in cannot be used to propagate it
298   through the memory. Once again, this is a stricter condition than the one we started from, and has
299   particular potential to create nodes with stale memory.

301   3.5     W HAT ABOUT THE TEMPORAL NODE EMBEDDINGS ?
302
      Our discussion of temporal under-reaching so far focused exclusively on the temporal memory S(τ ).
303
      This has been done for good reason: the computation of temporal node embeddings Z(τ ) is effec-
304                                                                                            (u)
305
      tively done over a static version of G≤τ , and if we allow the temporal neighbourhood N≤τ to be
306
      wide enough, this module could eliminate any under-reaching issues.
307   However, recall that the embedding model needs to be executed every time a new temporal link
308   prediction query is raised. Hence, there is an inherent tradeoff: making the embedding module
309   too deep may easily lead to untenable computational complexity as the dynamic graphs get larger.
310   Indeed, in most practical implementations the diameter of the embedding neighbourhood is no more
311   than two hops—which only reduces our path constraint for temporal mixing by two steps.
312   There is another important issue which may make it challenging to use the embedding module—that
313   is node/edge deletion. Deleting a node or edge before time τ also deletes it from the static graph of
314   G≤τ , and may lead to full discontinuities in it. In such cases, the embedding module will be provably
315   unable to mix certain nodes together. Rather than overinflating the embedding module, in TGR we
316   seek a lightweight manner to propagate information more widely across the graph, without relying
317   too much on the provided temporal edges, while also making sure that the propagated information
318
      is committed back to memory.

      4.1    E XPANDER G RAPH P ROPERTIES
353
      Expander graphs are a fundamentally sparse family of graphs (|E| = O(n)) that exhibit favourable
354
      properties beneficial for relieving over-squashing and under-reaching. This section provides insight
355   into their topological properties: easy construction, no bottlenecks, and optimized commute time.
356
357   Efficiently Precomputable. Expander graphs are constructed through use of group operators and
358
      act as an independent graph topology over which input graph is rewired. Our method considers
      Cayley graph expander family, which are constructed through a use of the special linear group
359
      SL(2, Zn ) as a generating set. Further details on constructing Cayley graphs can be found in work
360
      by Kowalski (2019); Selberg (1965); Davidoff et al. (2003).
361
362   No Bottlenecks. The spectral gap of a graphis closely relate to its connectivity and the existence of
363   bottlenecks within its structure. It is known that the first non-zero eigenvalue of the graph Laplacian
                                                                                                            3
364
      matrix is lower-bounded by a positive constant ϵ > 0 in expander graphs (Alon, 1986) and ϵ = 16
      for Cayley graph (Selberg, 1965). Given their tree-like structure (Deac et al., 2022), this concludes
365
      that expander graphs and particularly Cayley graphs have high connectivity and no bottlenecks,
366
      following Cheeger inequality.
367
368   Optimized Commute Time. Recent work by Di Giovanni et al. (2024), formally validates that such
369   approach is effective in relieving over-squashing by decreasing overall effective resistance or com-
370
      mute time (Chandra et al., 1989). In fact, commute time is shown to be closely aligned with over-
      squashing, stating that commute time in expander graphs grows linearly with the number of edges.

391   4.3   E XPANDER G RAPH P ROPAGATION ON T EMPORAL G RAPHS
392
393   This set-up utilizes underlying expander graph by virtually creating temporal paths that connect
394   under-reaching nodes, independently of their prior interactions in a CTDG. Observed nodes are
395   stored in a node bank module, which dynamically updates its content at every temporal batch. Re-
396   sulting expander embeddings are used to enhance input node features of observed nodes in the node
397   bank and feed exchanged information into the base TGNN model as dynamic input node features.
398   By providing dynamic node features, TGR remains agnostic to the underlying TGNN architecture,
399   allowing easy integration with various TGNN models without altering their layer construction. This
400   set-up differs from static graph rewiring methods that modify individual GNN layers within the
401   model architecture by adding a rewiring mask to alter input graph topology.
402   In this construction, a large expander graph is pre-computed at the beginning of training to match
403   the size of temporal memory. We construct input features S′ (t) for memory mixing by extracting
404   node states from TGNN temporal memory for nodes stored in the node bank. Expander embeddings
405   H′ (t) are computed over expander graph with adjacency matrix ACay as:
406
                                         H′ (t) = GNN(S′ (t), ACay ).
407
408
      The input feature vector X(t) for TGNN forward pass is constructed through a concatenation (∥)
409
      of expander embeddings H(t− ) (for previously observed nodes) and TGNN node states S(t− ) (for
410   new nodes) from temporal memory, where t− denotes time stamp just before t:
411
412                                         X(t) = H(t− )∥S(t− ).

468
      tgbl-review and tgbl-comment. Moreover, we identify that TGR achieves significant improve-
      ment over bipartite datasets such are tgbl-wiki and tgbl-review. Both bipartite and high surprise
469
      index temporal graphs posses a layer of information that is inaccessible to traditional TGNN ar-
470
      chitectures. Due to a nature of their structure, bipartite graphs lack connections between nodes of
471   the same class. High surprise index datasets contain edges between nodes that do not have obvious
472   connections in the training data, thus making it challenging for traditional TGNNs to distinguish.
473   Both situations link to temporal under-reaching, previously discussed in Section 3.3, showing that
474   TGR is best used to optimize input graphs that have high presence of temporal under-reaching. Con-
475   sistent improvement across all datasets signals that TGR uncovers structural information which was
476   previously inaccessible to TGNN base model.

478   6   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this work, we propose Temporal Graph Rewiring (TGR), a first approach for graph rewiring on
481   temporal graphs to address limitations in TGNNs namely temporal under-reaching, over-squashing
482   and memory staleness. We show that using expander graphs to rewire temporal graphs is an optimal
483   method to address identified issues. This paper provides a promising first step towards applying tem-
484   poral graph rewiring to optimize performance of TGNN models, paving the way for future temporal
485   graph rewiring methods. In future work, we hope to further address identified issues by investigating
      other graph rewiring methods and compare their performance to TGR.

      A     OVERVIEW OF TGNN BASELINES
595
596
      TGN. Temporal Graph Network (TGN) (Rossi et al., 2020) proposes a combination of temporal
597
      memory to store node states and graph-based operators for learning on continuous-time dynamic
598
      graphs. Following interaction in a CTDG, node states of the nodes involved in the event are updated
599   in the memory through a recurrent neural network such as LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997)
600   or GRU (Cho et al., 2014).
601
602
      TNCN. TNCN builds up on memory-based TGNNs (Rossi et al., 2020; Trivedi et al., 2019) by
      introducing temporal version of a neural-common-neighbour (NCN) and reaching state-of-the-art
603
      results on three out of five datasets at TGB Benchmark.

612
      tgbl-wiki. tgbl-wiki dataset stores dynamic information about a co-editing network on Wikipedia
613
      pages over a span of one month. The data is stored in a bipartite temporal graph where nodes rep-
614   resent editors or wiki-pages they interact with. An edge represents an action user takes when editing
615   a Wikipedia page and edge features contain textual information about a page of interest. The goal is
616   to predict existence and nature of links between editors and Wikipedia pages at a future timestamp.
617
      tgbl-review. tgbl-review dataset stores dynamic information about Amazon electronics product
618
      review network covering the years 1997 to 2018. This dataset forms a bipartite weighted network,
619
      where nodes represent users and products, and edges signify individual reviews—rated on a scale
620   from one to five—submitted by users to products at specific times. The goal is to predict which user
621   will interact in the reviewing process at a given time.
622
623

624
      between these addresses over a period of time. Covering the period from April 1 to November 1,
625
      2022, the network includes transaction data for five stablecoins and one wrapped token. The goal
626   of the task is to predict with which destination a given address will interact at a given time.
627

629

630
      users will interact at a given time.

633   Airports are modeled as nodes, and flights occurring between them on specific days form the edges.
634   Node features include the airport type, the continent of location, ISO region codes, and geographic
635
      coordinates (longitude and latitude). Edge attributes consist of the associated flight numbers. The
