               Traditional segmentation models, while effective in isolated tasks, often fail to
012
               generalize to more complex and open-ended segmentation problems, such as free-
013
               form, open-vocabulary, and in-the-wild scenarios. To bridge this gap, we propose
014            to scale up image segmentation across diverse datasets and tasks such that the
015            knowledge across different tasks and datasets can be integrated while improving
016            the generalization ability. QueryMeldNet, a novel segmentation framework, is
017            introduced and designed to scale seamlessly across both data size and task di-
018            versity. It is built upon a dynamic object query mechanism called query meld,
019            which fuses different types of queries using cross-attention. This hybrid approach
020            enables the model to balance between instance- and stuff-level segmentation, pro-

023
               costly human annotations. By training on multiple datasets and tasks at scale,
024
               QueryMeldNet continuously improves performance as the volume and diversity
025            of data and tasks increase. It exhibits strong generalization capabilities, boosting

027            ments mark a key step toward universal, scalable segmentation models capable of
028            addressing the demands of real-world applications.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Image segmentation is an important computer vision research direction with the goal of partition-
035   ing an image into discrete groups of pixels. This field encompasses various training tasks, includ-
036   ing semantic segmentation, instance segmentation, panoptic segmentation, foreground/background
037   segmentation, and referring segmentation, etc. The objective of a universal image segmentation
038   model is to exhibit robust generalization capabilities, performing effectively in real-world diverse
039
      segmentation applications, such as open-vocabulary, free-form and in-the-wild segmentation re-
      quirement Xu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2023a). To achieve that, such a model is
040
      expected to be trainable jointly across any segmentation datasets and tasks at scale such that the
041
      knowledge across different tasks and datasets can be integrated. This integration is essential for im-
042   proving performance on complex, real-world problems, particularly when larger and more diverse
043   datasets are available. We say that a segmentation model is scalable if it can effectively improve
044   with the increase in both dataset size and task diversity. A scalable model can continuously evolve
045   by leveraging existing and future datasets, without requiring frequent redesign or retraining, making
046   development more efficient. In this way, simply gathering more diverse data can naturally enhance
047   the model’s capabilities.
048   Despite these benefits, numerous prior works were explored on specific tasks or datasets in iso-
049   lation He et al. (2017); Chen et al. (2017; 2019); Ronneberger et al. (2015); Long et al. (2015);
050   Xiong et al. (2019); Cheng et al. (2020). While these models have achieved significant success in
051   their respective areas, they often struggle to generalize to real-world scenarios, where versatility
052   and adaptability are critical. The limitations of task-specific models raise a key question: Can we
053   design a model that scales effectively across both tasks and datasets while improving generalization
      in diverse, real-world applications?

068   Several recent efforts have aimed to scale up segmentation training tasks and datasets by exploring
069   unified frameworks, seeking to address the joint training of multiple tasks and datasets, summarized
070   in Table 1. However, these existing works possess certain inherent limitations, far from achieving
071   true scalability across both tasks and datasets. Some of these works have made progress in dataset
072   scalability but remain restricted to a single task Lambert et al. (2020); Kim et al. (2022a). Oth-
073   ers Jain et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023) have demonstrated limited task scalability—addressing
074
      only specific tasks such as semantic, instance, or panoptic segmentation—but cannot generalize
      across datasets with different class structures. There is few attempt for both datasets and tasks scala-
075
      bility Gu et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2023a). X-Decoder Zou et al. (2023a) offers a promising solution
076
      with its learnable queries for jointly training on tasks and datasets. Nevertheless, its subpar perfor-
077   mance in instance-level segmentation reveals shortcomings in its architecture, indicating that its task
078   scalability is still constrained.

080
      In this work, we conduct an in-depth analysis and identify a key limitation preventing effective scal-
      ability: the design of object queries, a fundamental component in transformer-based segmentation
081
      models. The learnable queries used in X-Decoder have shown promising results for semantic (stuff)
082
      segmentation but struggle with instance (thing) segmentation1 . To address this issue, we draw in-
083   spiration from the success of conditional queries in object detection Liu et al. (2022a); Li et al.
084   (2022); Zhu et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022a) and introduce them to enhance X-Decoder’s ability
085   in instance-level segmentation and broaden its scalability across both tasks and datasets. However,
086   while conditional queries excel at instance objects, they perform poorly with stuff objects. To har-
087   monize the strengths of both query types, we propose a novel object query mechanism called query
088   meld. This approach seamlessly melds learnable queries and conditional queries with a qual-query
089   cross attention mechanism. It enables sample and object-wise dynamic query selection, opposite to
090   traditional rigid assignment Rana et al. (2023); Athar et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023), and hier-
091
      archical and interactive feature representation which improve the model’s ability to handle diverse
      object types, enabling scalability across various tasks and datasets.
092
093   Building on this foundation, we introduce a scalable segmentation architecture called QueryMeld-
094   Net. QueryMeldNet can be trained on many different segmentation tasks and datasets at scale, as
095   shown in Figure 1, without being limited to specific datasets Kim et al. (2022a); Lambert et al.
096   (2020); Zhou et al. (2023) or tasks Jain et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2021) as previous works. A
097
      key advantage of QueryMeldNet’s scalable design is its ability to continuously improve segmenta-
      tion performance by training on a wide variety of existing datasets and tasks. We demonstrate that
098
      scaling up both the volume of training data and diversity of tasks consistently enhances the model’s
099
      segmentation capabilities, particularly for real-world, free-form open-set segmentation tasks. As
100   shown in Figure 1 (right), when we scale the data and tasks from 0.1M to 0.6M and include more
101   diverse tasks, the open-set segmentation mask AP performance on the SeginW benchmark Zou et al.
102   (2023a) improves from 33.2 to 38.6. While current public datasets provide a good starting point,
103   we are eager to explore the limits of the model’s generalization capabilities by utilizing even more

141   Generic segmentation Given an input image, the goal of image segmentation is to output a group
142   of masks with class predictions. According to the scope of class labels and masks, image segmen-
143   tation can be divided into three major tasks, semantic, instance and panoptic segmentation Li et al.
144   (2023c). In the past, many task or dataset specialized models have been proposed, and they can
145   be trained and do inference only on a single task and dataset, including Mask R-CNN He et al.
146   (2017), Cascade Mask R-CNN Cai & Vasconcelos (2019), HTC Chen et al. (2019) on instance seg-
147
      mentation, FCN Long et al. (2015), U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015), DeepLab Chen et al. (2017)
      on semantic segmentation, UPSnet Xiong et al. (2019), Panoptic-DeepLab Cheng et al. (2020) on
148
      panoptic segmentation.
149
150   Scalable segmentation models Most early unified segmentation models lack scalability because
151   their architectures need modifications to accommodate different datasets and tasks Cheng et al.
152   (2021; 2022); Li et al. (2023a). For instance, in Mask DINO Li et al. (2023a), training on semantic
153
      segmentation requires a one-stage encoder-decoder architecture, whereas instance and panoptic seg-
      mentation demand a two-stage approach. This inconsistency limits scalability across tasks. Some
154
      models achieve partial scalability, either for tasks Jain et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2021); Qin et al.
155
      (2023) or datasets Kim et al. (2022a); Lambert et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2023), but not for both.
156   For example, OneFormer Jain et al. (2023) and OpenSeeD Zhang et al. (2023) handle task scalabil-
157   ity within instance/semantic/panoptic segmentation but struggle with dataset scalability. OneFormer
158   lacks the ability to unify class spaces across datasets, while OpenSeeD requires additional stuff/thing
159   annotations, which are impractical for most datasets. Few models attempt to address both data and
160   task scalability. X-Decoder Zou et al. (2023a) and DaTaSeg Gu et al. (2023) offer a sub-optimal
161   solution by relying on learnable queries, but they exhibit decreased performance in instance seg-
      mentation. To the best of our knowledge, no segmentation model currently supports both data and

197   3.2 Q UERY
198   D ESIGN FOR S CALABLE S EGMENTATION
199
200   Object query is a key component in transformer-based ob-
201
      ject detection and segmentation models, and has attracted
      much attention from the community Liu et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2020); Zhang
202
      et al. (2022a); Wang et al. (2022c); Meng et al. (2021). In this section, we first review the mostly
203
      common learnable object query strategy in segmentation architectures and introduce our new query
204   meld mechanism.
205
206   Learnable query relies on a single set of object queries trained from scratch which interact with
207
      the image features to encode object location and class information (illustrated in Figure 3 (a)). Due
      to its simplicity, this approach has been widely adopted in the object detection and segmentation
208
      literature Wang et al. (2022a); Zou et al. (2023a); Cheng et al. (2021); Gu et al. (2023). For example,
209
      X-Decoder Zou et al. (2023a) uses learnable queries in an attempt to achieve data and task scalability.
210   However, several studies have demonstrated that learnable queries perform suboptimally in object
211   detection Zhu et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2022). Our experiments reveal similar
212   findings in image segmentation: while learnable queries perform well for semantic segmentation,
213   they fall short in instance-level tasks such as instance segmentation. As shown in Table 2, there is
214   a noticeable performance gap compared to more advanced query designs. This limitation hampers
215   X-Decoder’s ability to scale up across diverse and complex data and tasks, restricting its broader
      scalability.

      To address the shortcomings of learnable queries in instance-level segmentation, we explore more
217   advanced query designs that have proven successful in object detection. One such approach is the
218   conditional query Liu et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022a),
219   initially proposed in Zhu et al. (2020) and further refined in Liu et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2022);
220   Zhang et al. (2022a). Conditional queries aim to mimic the proposal generation mechanism found
221   in traditional two-stage object detection frameworks Ren et al. (2015), but adapted for transformer-
222   based detectors. Unlike learnable queries, which are independently trained, conditional queries
223   are derived directly from the transformer encoder, as illustrated in Figure 3 (b). The transformer
224   encoder is trained to predict region proposals, from which high-confidence proposals are selected
225
      and fed into the transformer decoder as object queries for final predictions, such as bounding boxes
      or segmentation masks.
226
227   Conditional queries align more closely with the objects likely to be present in an image and have
228   consistently demonstrated superior performance in object detection tasks Zhu et al. (2020). How-
229   ever, our experiments reveal that this strategy does not universally benefit all segmentation tasks.
230   As shown in Table 2, the performance on semantic segmentation is significantly worse compared
231
      to learnable queries. This is because, in semantic segmentation, many classes (often referred to as
      “stuff” classes) represent background regions with undefined shapes and spatial extents. Conditional
232
      queries, derived from local image features, struggle to capture these characteristics effectively, lead-
233
      ing to suboptimal results. This is different from learnable query that is learned from scratch, not
234   conditional on an encoder output that usually derived from a local patch feature. Since stuff classes
235   are prevalent in real-world datasets, relying solely on conditional queries also limits the scalability
236   of models across diverse tasks and datasets.
237
      Both learnable and conditional queries have their respective strengths: learnable queries excel at han-
238
      dling large, amorphous background regions, while conditional queries specialize in capturing local,
239
      instance-level features. However, their individual limitations restrict their scalability across a wider
240   range of datasets and tasks. This raises a simple yet powerful idea: can we combine the strengths of
241   both to enhance scalability? Following this line of thinking, we propose a query meld strategy (Fig-
242   ure 3 (c)). In this approach, the object query set consists of both learnable and conditional queries,
243   which interact with each other through a deep fusion mechanism via dual-query cross-attention. For
244   loss computation, Hungarian matching is applied across all object queries, without differentiating
245   between query types, allowing the model to seamlessly integrate both types of queries for improved
246   scalability across diverse segmentation datasets and tasks.
247   With dual-query cross-attention mechanism, the query meld seamlessly integrates learnable queries
248   with conditional queries, offering several key advantages. First, dynamic query selection. Without
249   rigid assignment, two types of queries can dynamically choose their preferred objects to detect for
250   each example. And since they are complementary each other for global background feature and lo-
251   cal instance feature, this property broadens the scope of the trainable dataset and tasks and therefore
252   improves the scalability of the model. Second, hierarchical and interactive feature representation.
253   Dual-query cross-attention can lead to a hierarchical feature representation where learnable queries
254
      capture the overall structure and semantics of the objects in the scene. On the other hand, conditional
      queries refine these global features by attending to specific parts of the image. This interaction al-
255
      lows the model to dynamically adjust focus, using conditional queries to zero in on hard-to-segment
256
      objects while still retaining the global understanding provided by learnable queries. This can im-
257   prove the model’s ability to handle both coarse and fine segmentation tasks. For complex objects or
258   occluded regions, query meld could also provide complementary perspectives on the same object.
259   Overall, the introduction of query meld enables the architecture to handle a broader range of seg-
260   mentation tasks and data in a flexible manner. The system can dynamically prioritize either query
261   type based on the complexity and nature of the task, benefiting better generalization ability of the
262   model. We will see the benefits in experiment section.

333
      limitation in exploring the full potential of scalability. To circumvent this challenge, we propose to
334   use synthetic data, which is cheap to generate, easy to scale up and has been proven effective to
335   strengthen the model, for instance, in object detection Cho et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2022) image
336   captioning Davide et al. (2023). Given that some recent models can generate high-quality synthetic
337   segmentation masks (e.g SAM Kirillov et al. (2023)) and synthetic captions (e.g., OFA Wang et al.
338   (2022b), GLIPv2 Zhang et al. (2022b)), we believe that the synthetic segmentation data can play
339   a crucial role in exploring the scalability of our model. In this work, we leverage two types of
340   synthetic data to expand both the training set and the range of tasks.
341   Synthetic segmentation mask: Instead of generating synthetic segmentation masks directly on
342   unlabeled image, it is a much easier task to segment the mask given an object bounding box because
343   some recent works have shown that they are pretty good at this task Kirillov et al. (2023); Ke et al.
344   (2023); Zou et al. (2023b). The size of object detection dataset is usually more than dozen times
345   larger than that of segmentation, e.g., Objects365 Shao et al. (2019) of 1.7M images v.s. COCO Lin
346   et al. (2014) of 120K images. With the generated synthetic masks, we can convert every object
347   detection dataset to a segmentation dataset to have more diverse training data.
348   Synthetic segmentation caption: The standard segmentation/detection datasets usually lack rich
349   textual descriptions, e.g., 80 fixed category names for COCO. This is a big challenge for open-
350   vocabulary segmentation model, especially for the task of referring segmentation. The widely
351   used referring segmentation datasets are RefCOCO, RefCOCO+ and RefCOCOg as well as Ref-
352   Clef Kazemzadeh et al. (2014); Yu et al. (2016), whose combination has only about 50K images.
353   The reason to this small dataset size is because annotating a caption description to every individual
354
      object segment is expensive. In order to enrich the semantic information of the training data and
      improve the generalization ability of the model, we train a OFA-akin Wang et al. (2022b) model on
355
      the task of object captioning, i.e., generating synthetic caption for each object given the bounding
356
      box. With this object captioning model, we generate five synthetic captions with the highest confi-
357   dences for each object, and use them to expand the training data size. One of the synthetic captions
358   is randomly selected per object at each training iteration.

414   4.1                            Q UERY ABLATION
415
416   We begin by comparing three query strategies when used for scalable image segmentation. The
417
      model is scaled up to both datasets and tasks at two scales: (1) “two datasets and two tasks”
      where the training set comprises COCO with panoptic segmentation annotations (“COCO pano”)
418
      and ADE20K with semantic segmentation annotations (“ADE sem”); (2) “four datasets and four
419
      tasks” where we add two additional training sets and tasks, Visual Genome with instance segmen-
420   tation (“VG”) and referring segmentation RefCOCO/RefCOCO+/RefCOCOg (“refer”). The eval-
421   uation uses ADE and COCO for closed-set performance, while SeginW is utilized to assess the
422   open-set generalization capabilities of the models.
423
      The query meld strategy is compared against the two other strategies, all using a total of 400 queries.
424
      As shown in Table 2, across both scaling scenarios, the learnable query exhibits weak performance
425
      on instance-level segmentation tasks, with a notable drop of around 2 points on COCO and SeginW.
426   Even more significant, the conditional query shows a degradation of over 7 points in semantic seg-
427   mentation performance (mIoU) on ADE. These results suggest that neither of the individual query
428   strategies is an optimal choice for scalable image segmentation. In contrast, the query meld demon-
429   strates superior performance across all evaluation tasks, highlighting its scalability to diverse tasks
430   and datasets without suffering performance loss. Moreover, query meld exhibits stronger gener-
431   alization ability, as evidenced by substantial performance improvements on SeginW, driven by its
      dual-query cross-attention mechanism.

      The superior performance of the query meld stems from its sample-wise dynamic query selection
      mechanism. We analyze the ratio of thing and stuff objects predicted by the conditional and learnable
461
      queries, respectively. Thing objects typically correspond to foreground instances, such as “person”
462
      or “book”, while stuff objects generally represent background regions like “sky” or “road”. On
463   COCO, we find that conditional queries capture 99.6% of thing objects, while learnable queries
464   detect 53.3% of stuff objects. A similar trend is observed in ADE panoptic segmentation, with
465   conditional queries accounting for 99.8% of thing objects and learnable queries handling 61.4% of
466   stuff objects. This suggests that, in most cases, thing objects are predicted by conditional queries,
467   whereas stuff objects are handled by learnable queries. However, this is not always the case. Fig-
468   ure 5 illustrates counterexamples where, despite “sky” and “floor” being classified as stuff classes,
469   conditional queries are used because these features behave more like local instances in the images.
470   Similarly, in images containing “table” and “car”, which are typically thing classes, learnable queries
471
      are triggered since these objects appear more as background features. These findings demonstrate
      that query selection in the query meld is dynamic and adaptive to each image, contrasting with some
472
      approaches in the literature Rana et al. (2023); Athar et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023) that rely on
473
      hard assignments based on classes or tasks, limiting scalability.

532
533
      In this paper, we have introduced QueryMeldNet, a scalable image segmentation model that can be
534   trained on diverse datasets and tasks at scale. Our experiments have validated the effectiveness of
535   QueryMeldNet in improving segmentation performance as data volume and task diversity increase,
536   particularly in open-set and real-world applications. Moreover, we showed that incorporating syn-
537   thetic data further boosts the model’s generalization capabilities while reducing the reliance on ex-
538   pensive human annotations. QueryMeldNet marks a significant step toward universal segmentation
539   models, opening the door for future research to explore even larger and more complex segmentation
      tasks.

848   conditional queries. It is also the same as OpenSeeD using 100 learnable queries for stuff classes
849   and 300 conditional queries for thing classes. Based on the Base-scale image and text encoder back-
850   bones, given different queries, we scale models with the configuration of two tasks and datasets.
851   The training set is the combination of COCO with instance segmentation and ADE with semantic

      segmentation. In Table 9, we observe that increasing the query number can improve the perfor-

      A.2   A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS
868
869   Training settings For the experiments of Section 4.1, we train our model with a batch size of 32.
870   AdamW is used as the optimizer with a base learning rate of 2e-4 for the segmentation encoder
871   and decoder, and 2e-5 , 10 warmup iterations, and a weight decay of 0.05. We decay the learning
872   rate at 0.9 and 0.95 fractions of the total number of training steps by a factor of 10. We train for a
873   total of 50 epochs. On the experiments of Sections 4.2 and 4.3, we follow the same settings but the
874   batch size is scaled up to 128. Swin-Base and CLIP-Base are used for query comparison in Table 2.
875
      Their larger-scale variants are used in other sections. The codes and models will be released upon
      acceptance.
876
877   Datasets In order to mitigate the data leakage issue, we implement exclusion in our training data.
878   Specifically, for the COCO 2017 training set, examples belonging to RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, Ref-
879   COCOg validation sets are excluded. Conversely, training examples from RefCOCO, RefCOCO+,
880   RefCOCOg that overlap with COCO 2017 validation set are also excluded. Similar exclusion pro-
881
      cedures are applied to LVIS training set, removing examples associated with the RefCOCO, Ref-
      COCO+, RefCOCOg validation sets. Distinct data augmentation strategies are applied based on the
882
      type of training data. For instance, semantic and panoptic data, we follow the augmentation strat-
883
      egy of Mask DINO Li et al. (2023a). For referring segmentation data, the augmentation data is the
884   same as instance segmentation but random clip is excluded. For foreground segmentation training
885   data, we follow the data augmentation of InSPyReNet Kim et al. (2022b). Different upsampling
886   ratios for each dataset are applied during joint training, which are maintained as specified in Table
887   11. In total, the QueryMeldNet is trained on around 2M distinct images examples and 57M mask
888   annotations. It is noted that the comparison in Table 3 is a system-level comparison. The training
889   data varies across each method. For instance, X-decoder Zou et al. (2023a) additionally incorporates
890   image-text corpora in its training process.

892   A.3   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
893
894
      We discuss the ethical considerations from three aspects: Environmental Impact: Training
      QueryMeldNet requires significant computational resources. The environmental impact of such
895
      resource-intensive processes should be taken into account, and efforts should be made to develop
896
      more energy-efficient algorithms. Transparency and Explainability: Like other deep learning
897   models, QueryMeldNet is also considered “black boxes” because it is challenging to understand
898   how they reach specific decisions. Ensuring transparency and explainability is essential to build
899   trust and accountability, especially in applications with significant consequences. Bias and Fair-
900   ness: Like other machine learning models, image segmentation models can be biased based on the
901   data they are trained on. If the training data is not diverse and representative, the model may perform
902   poorly on certain demographics or groups, perpetuating existing biases. However, this problem can
903   be resolved to a certain extent by QueryMeldNet thanks to its versatility of joint training on multiple
904   diverse datasets and tasks.
905
906   A.4   L IMITATIONS
907
      Recently, a newly emerging reasoning segmentation task has been introduced Lai et al. (2023). The
908
      task is designed to output a segmentation mask given a complex and implicit query text. For exam-
909   ple, given an image with various fruits, the query is “what is the fruit with the most Vitamin C in this
910   image”. This task demands a level of reasoning typically handled by multi-modal Large Language
911   Models. Currently, QueryMeldNet does not explicitly support this task. However, addressing this
912   limitation is part of our agenda for future research.
