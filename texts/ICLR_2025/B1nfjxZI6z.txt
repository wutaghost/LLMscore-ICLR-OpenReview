011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Biological sequence design is one of the most impactful areas where model-based
014            optimization is applied. A common scenario involves using a fixed training set to
015            train predictive models, with the goal of designing new sequences that outperform
016
               those present in the training data. This by definition results in a distribution
               shift, where the model is applied to samples that are substantially different from
017
               those in the training set (or otherwise they wouldn’t have a chance of being much
018
               better). While most MBO methods offer some balancing heuristic to control for
019            false positives, finding the right balance of pushing the design distribution while
020            maintaining model accuracy requires deep knowledge of the algorithm and artful
021            application, limiting successful adoption by practitioners. To tackle this issue,
022            we propose a straightforward meta-algorithm for design practitioners that detects
023            distribution shifts when using any MBO. By doing a real-world sequence design
024            experiment, we show that (1) Real world distribution shift is far more severe than
025            observed in simulated settings, where most MBO algorithms are benchmarked (2)
026            Our approach successfully reduces the adverse effects of distribution shift. We
027
               believe this method can significantly improve design quality for sequence design
               tasks and potentially other domain applications where offline optimization faces
028
               harsh distribution shifts.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Machine learning (ML) is increasingly guiding design in fields like materials science, chemistry, and
034
      biology (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Alley et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023). ML
035
      in design is used to find inputs that enhance specific properties, such as designing peptides with better
036   antimicrobial properties (Gupta & Zou, 2019), or optimizing superconductors for higher critical
037   temperatures (Fannjiang & Listgarten, 2020). Model-free design methods often rely on many rounds
038   of expensive, time-consuming experiments (Arnold, 1998). ML-guided design reduces these costs by
039   experimental steps with model predictions (Yang et al., 2019).
040
      Offline model-based optimization (MBO) (Trabucco et al., 2022; Angermueller et al., 2019; Linder
041
      et al., 2020) a key paradigm in ML-guided sequence design (Sinai & Kelsic, 2020). It involves creating
042   a “surrogate” model from a set of input-property pairs to predict property values. Then, one searches
043   the space of possible input values to find a new batch of inputs with predicted property values that are
044   greater than those observed in the training set. This search can use various optimization methods
045   including evolutionary algorithms for discrete inputs or gradient-based methods for continuous inputs
046   (Sinai et al., 2020; Trabucco et al., 2022; Angermueller et al., 2019).
047
      MBO is conceptually simple and versatile, applicable to various design problems with black-box
048   methods, given an initial dataset. However, its effectiveness depends on careful implementation due to
049   several challenges, notably the tendency of black-box search methods to focus on out-of-distribution
050   (OOD) regions compared to the training data (Fannjiang & Listgarten, 2023). This “distribution shift”
051   can lead to unreliable predictions when provided with OOD inputs, especially for high-capacity neural
052   network models. In extreme cases, the search method might exploit these unreliable predictions,
053   especially in maximization problems, resulting in nonsensical outputs that do not translate to real
      world results (Brookes et al., 2019).

054
      The main challenge in effective MBO is detecting distribution shifts from training data and identifying
055   resulting false-positive (adversarial) examples. Several methods have been proposed to minimize
056   these shifts, including using Bayesian Optimization (BO) to regularize the search based on the
057   surrogate model’s predictive uncertainty (Snoek et al., 2012), limiting the search to regions with high
058   likelihood in the training distribution (Brookes et al., 2019; Linder et al., 2020; Angermueller et al.,
059   2019), and incorporating domain-specific knowledge into the search algorithm (Sinai et al., 2020).
060   While these methods can reduce the problem, they do not fully eliminate distribution shift and can be
061   challenging to adapt to specific problems. Critically, most such methods are only tested in simulated
062   settings.
063   A different emerging direction in reducing distribution shift is the use of foundation models trained
064   on all known proteins (Madani et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023), where the set of data in the training
065   domain is expansive. However, while they are the go-to choice for zero-shot generation, design with
066   these models is nascent, and they are not better than supervised approaches when some functional
067   data has already been collected (Dallago et al., 2021).
068   Finally, the complexity of using MBO algorithms correctly (e.g. when using RL or generative
069   models), limits the adoption among practitioners. For instance, selecting a trust-region for any search
070   algorithm can be an art rather than science, and risks wasting experimental resources.
071
      In this work, propose a simple meta-heuristic for detecting distribution shifts in MBO and correcting
072
      it, reducing the need to fine tune algorithms precisely within the MBO loop. Our approach can be
073   combined with any existing oracle-based design method to further minimize adversarial designs.
074   Specifically, we propose training a binary classifier to distinguish between the initial training samples
075   and a separate set of samples drawn from one’s chosen search algorithm. We demonstrate that
076   the logit score output by the trained “OOD classifier” is an interpretable and effective metric for
077   determining which designed sequences are OOD and thus are associated with unreliable predictions
078   from the surrogate model. This method is straightforward to implement given the initial training
079   set and a search method, and can be used in conjunction with any black-box surrogate model and
080   search method. We suggest multiple ways in which the OOD classifier score (henceforth referred to
081
      as “OOD scores”) can be used to guide the selection of designed inputs for subsequent experimental
      verification.
082
083   We study the OOD classifier in three increasingly realistic problems. The first is an illustrative
084   toy problem with a two-dimensional input space. Next, we validate the method in a simulated
085   environment for protein structure prediction of a small protein, where we can use in silico structure
086   prediction methods to measure the effectiveness of our black-box design. Finally, we apply our
087
      method in a real-world experiment where we design Adeno-Associated Virus (AAV) capsid seqeunces,
      a complex protein of major importance to gene therapy. In this case, we use AdaLead as our MBO
088
      algorithm because its the best published MBO algorithm for AAV design (Bryant et al., 2021; Sinai
089
      et al., 2020). We trace the generated sequences over the course of MBO trajectories where they
090   are experimentally tested for multiple important properties. This unique dataset allows us to track
091   the extent and effects of distribution shift during MBO, and test how methods such as the OOD
092   classifier are able to detect such a shift. We show that the OOD classifier can improve the outcome in
093   this black-box design problem, which is inaccessible to structural and domain-informed approaches.
094   Importantly, we observe that the simulated settings (including our second objective and additional
095   ones we test in the supplement) show much weaker distributions shift.

100   2.1   O FFLINE M ODEL BASED O PTIMIZATION
101
102   The objective in a data-driven design problem is to identify an optimal input x∗ of some ground-truth
103   function, f (x), that encodes a scalar property of the input. This can be expressed as the objective
104   x∗ = arg maxx∈X f (x), where X is a bounded set that we refer to as the “input space” of the
105   problem. The input space may be a discrete or continuous space, and f (x) is typically assumed to be
106   a black box from which we can only make zeroth-order evaluations. We assume the availability of a
107   static dataset, D = {(xi , yi )}N
                                      i=1 , consisting of elements of the input space paired with corresponding
      noise-corrupted evaluations of the ground truth function.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      In MBO, the design strategy is to train a surrogate model fˆ(x) on the dataset D using an appropriate
109   supervised regression strategy (e.g. minimizing the mean squared error of predictions from the model
110   via stochastic gradient descent). This surrogate model is then used to guide a search around the
111   input space to find inputs with high predicted property values. This search may take the form of an
112   optimization algorithm (Sinai et al., 2020; Angermueller et al., 2019), probabilistic sampling method
113   (Brookes et al., 2019), or sampling from a generative model (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Linder
114   et al., 2020; Nijkamp et al., 2022).
115   In an ideal scenario for MBO, the inputs of the training dataset would be evenly distributed across
116   the input space, and therefore the error between the surrogate model and the ground truth function
117   would be roughly equal in all regions of input space. In such a case, one could safely optimize the
118   surrogate model directly with a reasonable assumption that this would produce an input that is close
119   to a local optimum of the ground truth function. In most practical scenarios, however, the training
120   inputs are not evenly distributed, and are instead concentrated in a small region of input space. In
121   such cases, the error of the surrogate model will change drastically depending on which region of
122   input space is being tested. Further, search methods that use the surrogate model will tend to move
123
      to regions of input space where there is a low density of training points, and therefore predictions
      from the surrogate model may be unreliable (Brookes et al., 2019; Fannjiang & Listgarten, 2023). In
124
      other words, the design strategy induces a distribution shift between the training distribution and the
125
      “design distribution” that results from performing the search (Fannjiang et al., 2022; Wheelock et al.,
126   2022).
127
128   In the next section, we discuss the type of distribution shift commonly observed in design problems
129
      and how we may be able to detect which inputs are most affected by this shift.
130
131   2.2   D ISTRIBUTION SHIFT IN DESIGN
132
133   Distribution shift in sequence design problems typically takes the form of covariate shift, in which
134   ptr (x) ̸= pte (x), where ptr (x) and pte (x) are the distributions of training and test inputs, respectively
135
      (Shimodaira, 2000). Under covariate shift, the ground truth conditional distribution of property values
      given inputs, p(y|x), remains fixed between the train and test distributions. This is because design
136
      problems are oftentimes modeling an underlying system that has a fixed relationship between x and
137
      y, e.g., a biological process.
138
139   In MBO, the search method uses information from the surrogate model to guide its exploration of
140   input space. This induces a dependence between the training distribution and the distribution of
141
      designed inputs, pde (x), resulting in a form of covariate shift known as “feedback covariate shift” that
      can have a particularly pernicious effect on the accuracy of model predictions (Fannjiang et al., 2022;
142
      Stanton et al., 2023). Although our proposed method can be used to detect any type of covariate shift,
143
      our experiments are focused on demonstrating that it is effective in the difficult case of feedback
144   covariate shift.
145
146   Figure 1 illustrates feedback covariate shift in MBO, detailed in Section 4.3. Here, a discrete
147
      optimization method optimized a surrogate model to identify protein sequences with high predicted
      values for a desired property. We tested sequences from each point along the optimization trajectory
148
      in a bulk physical experiment to (i) determine whether they satisfy basic functional properties, and
149
      thus not adversarial, and (ii) measure the actual property value. Figure 1a shows increasing surrogate
150   model predictions along the optimization trajectory, as expected. However, Figure 1b demonstrates
151   a significant increase in prediction error along the trajectory compared to true property values,
152   indicating a distribution shift during design. This increase in error occurs despite the model’s low
153   MSE on a random holdout as shown in the inset of Figure 1b. Figure 1c further shows that later
154   designs in the optimization trajectory mostly consist of adversarial sequences, which fail to meet
155   basic requirements of a functional protein, highlighting the severe distribution shift.
156   Our central aim is to detect when a covariate shift such as that shown in Figure 1 has occurred, so
157   that input points associated with reliable predictions from the surrogate model can be identified. This
158   allows one to avoid selecting inputs for experimental validation that may have unreliable predictions.

160   shift at each input point. An intuitive score that has been frequently used is the density ratio between
161   the test distribution and train distribution (Sugiyama et al., 2007). In the design setting a fixed test is
      not given, so we select the unlabeled design distribution as our test distribution (see Appendix D for

      2.4   S ELECTION USING OOD SCORES
217
218   The final step of any MBO procedure is selecting one or more inputs sampled from the design
219   distribution for follow-up evaluation on the ground truth function (i.e. testing the inputs’ property
220   values in a physical experiment). We propose using the approximate OOD scores calculated via
221   Equation 5 to guide this selection process. Our experiments will demonstrate these OOD scores
222   provide an interpretable metric for detecting distribution shift in MBO methods, and can reliably
223   identify regions of input space where we can expect a surrogate model to make accurate predictions.
224
      Given a set of designed inputs, a distribution shift-aware selection procedure should prioritize inputs
      associated with high surrogate model predictions that are also expected to be accurate based on OOD
225
      scores. How exactly a practitioner chooses to enforce this intuition will ultimately be application-
226
      specific, and should be tailored to the user’s needs and level of risk-tolerance. Three possible
227   techniques for doing so are (i) a cutoff process, where only allows an input to be selected if ρθ (x) < c
228   for a chose cutoff value c, (ii) stratified selection in which selects a specified number of inputs from
229   ranges of OOD scores (e.g. select ten inputs from scores between a and b, and 10 inputs from scores
230   between b and c) and (iii) selection based on a user-defined utility function.
231
232   2.5   D EPLOYING OFFLINE MBO IN THE WILD
233
234   The primary aim of creating new offline MBO algorithms is to deploy them in real-world scenarios.
235   However, methods development faces a gap between simulation and the real world. Typically,
236   methods development is relegated to simulation, and in the rare cases where real-world deployment is
237   involved, the goal is to use the method to produce an optimized design. We are aware of no instances
238
      where offline MBO has been applied in real-world situations specifically to study the algorithm itself.
      Studying offline MBO in real-world settings is essential for improving algorithm development and
239
      understanding where simulations break down. The experimental setup for analyzing offline MBO
240
      retrospectively can differ in crucial aspects from the usual optimization process aimed at improving a
241   design. Consider the following example. The distribution of designed inputs will gradually shift away
242   from the training data with each optimization step. To study this shift and to determine the limitations
243   of offline MBO, it is crucial to label data at every step to understand where our predictions become
244   inaccurate. On the other hand, if the sole objective is to optimize, there is no need to waste valuable
245   resources on assessing inputs that likely don’t improve design. Herein, we design a real-world dataset
246   to study distribution shifts and to evaluate our method in the wild. In the related work section, we
247   discuss the state of datasets in offline MBO and some of its limitations.

249   3     R ELATED W ORK
250
251
      While our approach is not an MBO algorithm, it is closely related and meant as a complement to such
252   algorithms. We cover related work in this spirit.
253
254   Regularized search in ML-guided design The OOD classifier, inspired by ML-guided design, limits
255
      search to regions near the training distribution. “Latent space optimization” is one method, involving
      an encoder-decoder model jointly trained with a regression model that maps latent space points to
256
      property values. After training, optimization in the latent space identifies points with high property
257
      values for generation. This optimization remains close to the training distribution through a spherical
258   boundary (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018) or by implicitly altering the training objective(Castro
259   et al., 2022). Another method is adaptive generative modeling, where model parameters are updated
260   iteratively to generate inputs with high model scores. This approach stays close to the training
261   distribution by weighting points based on their training distribution density (Brookes et al., 2019)
262   or through gradual weight updates (Gupta & Zou, 2019). Unlike these methods, which depend on
263   specific surrogate models or search strategies, the OOD classifier is versatile, compatible with any
264   surrogate model or search technique.
265   Fannjiang et al. (2022) proposes an MBO method that applies conformal prediction to FCS, ensuring
266   search is limited to inputs where the surrogate model is guaranteed to be reliable. Stanton et al. (2023)
267   applies these methods to Bayesian Optimization (Snoek et al., 2012). While effective against FCS,
268   these strategies are computationally intensive as they require training at least n models, where n is
269   the number of test data points. This is impractical for high-throughput sequence design problems. In
      contrast, the OOD classifier method is more scalable, requiring only one model for a fixed test set. It


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      is accessible to practitioners with basic machine learning knowledge and our results will indicate that
271   it can also detect FCS.
272
273   Density ratio estimation for covariate shift and outlier detection The density ratio between test
274
      and train distributions (e.g., Equation 1), has been widely used to address covariate shift in supervised
      and reinforcement learning. In supervised learning, when the test set is shifted from the training set,
275
      the density ratio, w(x) := pte (x)/ptr (x), can be used to minimize a loss function, ℓ(x, y), averaged over
276
      a test set based on the equivalence Epte (x,y) [ℓ(x, y)] = Eptr (x,y) [w(x)ℓ(x, y)] (Shimodaira, 2000;
277
      Sugiyama et al., 2007). This led to techniques for estimating w(x) using binary classifiers and the
278   density ratio trick (Sugiyama et al., 2012). Propensity scores are also closely related, which learns
279   a weight equal to the Radon-Nikodym derivative (RND) between the test set q and the training set
280   p w(x) := dpte (x)/dptr (x) (Agarwal et al., 2011). Similar to the OOD classifier, binary classification
281   models are used to estimate the propensity score. In reinforcement learning, the density ratio helps
282   regularize policies in off-policy and offline RL (Precup et al., 2000), reweighting marginal state-
283   action pairs during training to avoid low-support regions. However, while these techniques address
284   non-feedback covariate shift, they can not be applied in the feedback case because this requires the
285   surrogate model to already be trained in order to generate the test distribution, pde (x). Thus, we apply
286
      the density ratio score in Equation 1 to detect covariate shift of designed inputs instead of weighting
      samples during training.
287
288   Our use of OOD scores at test time is most similar to how density ratio estimates are used to detect
289   outliers in a test set, such as by Hido et al. (2011). Our methods and results differ from this work
290   in a few notable ways. First, we use deep binary classifiers in order to estimate density ratios, in
291   contrast to the linear models used in Hido et al. (2011). Further, the outlier detection task only
292
      requires detection of standard (non-feedback) covariate shift; in contrast, our result demonstrates the
      density ratios can be used to mitigate the effects of feedback covariate shift, and thus can be used
293
      for ML-guided design problems. We also find that the use of a design-induced test set, rather than
294
      a fixed test set is crucial to the performance of our method (see Appendix D for a comparison of q
295   distributions). Finally, our results show that OOD scores can be used as a continuous measure that
296   reports on the degree of distribution shift intensity at any point in input space, rather than only for the
297   binary classification task of outlier detection. It is also notable that these approaches have never been
298   applied in sequence design, and their effectiveness in practice has not been established before.
299
      Datasets in sequence design While the ML-guided sequence design field has produced a wide
300
      variety of datasets and benchmarks in recent years, there remains a gap in understanding how offline
301   MBO methods will perform in the real-world given their performance in these simulated settings
302   (see Appendix F for experimental evidence of this gap). Some examples of progress in dataset
303   development and benchmarking include the following. In protein engineering, one of the more active
304   subdomains where offline MBO has been applied, FLIP (Dallago et al., 2021) curated published data
305   and developed tasks and metrics for model generalization, emphasizing dataset-splitting techniques
306   to probe generalization for offline static datasets. There has also been efforts towards producing
307   comprehensive (i.e., combinatorially complete) low-dimensional datasets (Poelwijk et al., 2019).
308   These datasets are useful for evaluating supervised model performance, but are not suitable for
309
      evaluating design methods. Outside of protein engineering, Design-Bench (Trabucco et al., 2022)
      consolidates offline MBO challenges across problem domains, allow for algorithm evaluation in a
310
      variety of simulated contexts. There exists very few examples of real-world evaluation of design
311
      strategies, and none that explicitly study distribution shift (Bryant et al., 2021; Madani et al., 2023).
312   In the next section, we will use a two-dimensional example to illustrate our method and then evaluate
313   its performance in a real-world deployment of offline MBO.

      2b and 2c, high OOD scores align with areas where surrogate model predictions deviate from the

      ground truth.

362   In this toy model, the “design distribution” is the uniform distribution over the input space, making
363   OOD scores proportional to 1/ptr (x). This score is a more suitable predictor surrogate model error than
364
      s(x), as discussed in Section 2.2. While estimating this ratio is straightforward in a low-dimensional
      2D space, it can be exceedingly difficult in high-dimensional spaces where ptr (x) may be arbitrarily
365
      complex. Therefore, in most practical cases, we select positive OOD examples from areas likely to
366
      be explored by a search method. These are regions of the input space that are unlikely to be densely
367   sampled by a uniform sampling scheme, but are the most important for detecting distribution shift
368   in designed inputs (see Appendix D for a comparison to design-independent distributions for the
369   positive OOD class). This more focused strategy results in an OOD classifier that approximates the
370   density ratio s(x).

      4.3   R EAL - WORLD APPLICATION TO THE DESIGN OF AAV CAPSID PROTEIN
410
411   We next apply our method to the design of AAV sequences. AAVs are small viruses that have been
412   repurposed as delivery vehicles for gene therapies. Because of the complex processes involved in
413   gene delivery, it is a real-world black box. Previous studies have demonstrated the potential of ML
414   to improve various properties of AAVs such as manufacturability and transduction efficiency (i.e.
415   how well the viruses can deliver genetic material to specific tissues or cell types) (Ogden et al., 2019;
416   Bryant et al., 2021).
417   We consider the problem of designing AAV variants that maximize transduction in cell culture. A
418   necessary precursor to successfully delivering genetic material is the proper folding of the viral
419   capsid and encapsulation of the virus’s genetic material, processes that we collectively refer to
420   as “packaging”. We refer to variants that do not package as “non-functional”. Both packaging
421   and transduction can be quantitatively measured experimentally using standard sequencing-based
422   techniques (see Appendix A.2 for details).
423   Our aim with generating data for this problem was to overcome the issues with testing MBO methods
424   described in Section 2.5 by inspecting mutational trajectories at many points over the course of a
425   design procedure. We began by following a standard MBO procedure. First, we used an initial training
426   dataset containing AAV sequence variants associated with packaging and transduction measurements
427   to train surrogate models fˆpkg (x) and fˆtsd (x) to predict packaging and transduction, respectively, for
428   a given sequence x. We then used AdaLead to optimize fˆtsd (x) under the constraint that fˆpkg (x) > γ
429   for a chosen cutoff γ. AdaLead maintains a pool of N candidate sequences and iteratively updates
430   this pool in a greedy manner to optimize the objective function. Because genetic algorithms are local
431   search methods, we run the optimization starting from 9 distinct starting sequences for 15 iterations
      each. To study variability across search methods, we also used a variant of beam search to generate


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      another pool of designed sequences for experimental validation; the details of this method and results
433   associated with these sequences can be found in Appendix A.2. After the design was completed,
434   measured packaging and transduction experimentally for all 15N sequences generated along each
435   trajectory, for a total of about 5, 000 sequences. Figure 1 shows various properties of this data as a
436   function of algorithm iteration; in particular, this figure demonstrates that a significant distribution
437   shift occurs over the course of the design trajectories. The top panel in Figure 3a additionally shows
438   the fraction of functional viruses generated at each step of the design. Since all designed sequences
439   were predicted to package, the drop in the number of functional sequences demonstrates the frequency
440   of adversarial examples.
441   We also evaluate the use of different model architectures including large language models (LLMs)
442   pretrained on hundreds of millions of protein sequences adapted to our regression task with linear
443   probing. In Appendix E, we show these models are subject to the same distribution shift that we see
444   from training surrogate models from scratch on our data.
445   We tested two metrics for detecting distribution shift. The first used OOD scores outputted from an
446   OOD classifier trained to classify the training data as negative samples and the designed sequences as
447   positive examples. The OOD classifier has an identical architecture as the surrogate model described
448   in Appendix A.2 except using a binary cross-entropy loss instead of MSE. The second uses the
449   standard deviations of predictions from an ensemble of surrogate models, which we refer to as Deep
450   Ensemble uncertainties (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). The middle and bottom panels of Figure
451   3a show the values of these metrics as a function of the optimization iteration. We can see that
452   the OOD scores steadily increase over the course of the trajectory, in concert with the increase in
453
      surrogate model MSE shown in Figure 1b. This shows that the OOD scores can be effectively used as
      a continuous predictor of the intensity of distribution shift at points along a design trajectory, rather
454
      than only as a binary predictor of whether a point is in- or out-of-distribution. In contrast, the Deep
455
      Ensemble scores cannot effectively serve as a quantitative predictor of shift intensity.
456
457   Figure 3b compares the distribution shift detection metrics to packaging measurements for all designed
458   sequences. The upper histograms compare the ability of the detection metrics to separate Functional
459
      and Non-Functional designed variants. Clearly, the OOD scores are better able to distinguish between
      these two categories than the Deep Ensemble uncertainties, indicating that the OOD scores can be used
460
      to determine whether a designed sequence is adversarial. Further, the lower KDE plots demonstrate
461
      that the OOD scores are a fairly reliable predictor of the continuous packaging measurement, again
462   indicating that the OOD scores can be used as a continuous indicator of the intensity of distribution
463   shift at a given input point.
464
465
      We next evaluate using the OOD scores for selecting designed sequences, as discussed in Section
      2.4. Typically, a designer will use surrogate model scores to select a small subset of designed
466
      for experimental validation. We replicate this by selecting only K designed sequences out of all
467
      designs (for which in this case we know, but don’t use, the ground truth). Using a cutoff scheme,
468   sequences with the highest predicted transduction values were chosen after filtering out sequences
469   with a distribution shift score above a certain threshold (e.g., OOD score > 10). We use regret as
470   our success measure, which measures the gap between the maximum transduction value found in
471   the full set and the K selected sequences. Figure 3c displays this regret across various cutoffs and
472   50 bootstrap data samples. We used percentiles for the cutoffs to enable comparison between the
473   OOD score and Deep Ensemble uncertainty. Selecting sequences with OOD scores consistently led
474   to lower regret compared to selecting with Deep Ensemble uncertainty, even achieving zero regret in
475   a number of cases. We note that the regret eventually increases as the cutoff is increased because
476
      more adversarial examples are included in the set of selected sequences, replacing the sequences with
      higher observed transduction. See Appendix A.2 for analogous regret plots at multiple settings of K
477
      and for different statistics of the observed transduction values in the selected set.

479   5    D ISCUSSION
480
481   Our method effectively reduces distribution shifts in sequence design by training a binary classifier
482   to differentiate training data from designed sequences. The model’s logit scores effectively identify
483   varying distribution shift intensities. Large gaps between distributions p and q may result in inaccurate
484   density ratio estimates by the OOD classifier, highlighting the need for q to overlap with p. This
485   can be addressed with a telescoping product (Rhodes et al., 2020), which we leave for future work.
      We also examine the difficulties in assessing offline MBO with static datasets.To address this, we


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      conducted a real-world experiment deploying offline MBO in a challenging task, focusing on the
487   interaction between MBO and distribution shift. Our experimental results confirm that this approach
488   successfully detects distribution shifts and improves established design approaches when used in
489   conjunction.
490
      Our work leaves open a number of avenues for future expansion. In particular, we prioritized
491
      demonstrating the successful application of a particularly promising and easy-to-implement method
492
      (the OOD classifier) in a real-world problem. We think the simplicity of the approach makes it a
493   promising candidate for other domains where MBO is applied, but testing them in the real-world
494   would require validation by practitioners on those fields.

      B    BALANCE OF TERMS IN OOD SCORES
919
920
      In Section 2.2, we discuss the distinction between the OOD score, s(x) = pde (x)/ptr (x), and another
921   potential distribution shift detection metric, 1/ptr (x). We suggest that the latter may be a more suitable
922   score for detecting distribution shift that would result in surrogate model error, but use the OOD score
923   instead because it is straightforward to calculate via the density ratio trick. Further, we claim that
924   the difference in OOD scores between two nearby design points will tend to be dominated by the
925   denominator terms in the OOD scores, and thus the distinction between s(x) and 1/ptr (x) will often be
926   negligible in practice. Here, we provide justification for this latter claim by considering a simple one
927   dimensional example. In particular, consider an input point x drawn from a one dimensional design
928   distribution with continuous support, pde (x), and a nearby point x + δx. The difference between the
929
      log OOD scores of these two points can be approximated by Taylor expanding to first-order:

943   We now consider cases where the first term in Equation 13, which corresponds to the 1/ptr (x) term
944   in the OOD score, will be larger than the second term. If the variances of the train and design
945   distributions are roughly similar, then the first term will tend to be larger because x is drawn from
946   the design distribution and is therefore likely to be closer to the mean of the design than the training
947
      distribution, as long as these means are well-separated. The impact of the second term will be further

948

949

950   and (2) the variance of the design distribution is larger than that of the train distribution.
951
952   In practice, both of these conditions are typically satisfied by design distributions. The first condition
953
      is satisfied because the design method will tend to search around regions of input space far from the
      training data (thus inducing the distribution shift that is the focus of this paper. Similarly, the second
954
      condition is usually satisfied because the design method will tend to search large regions of the input
955
      space to find candidate solutions, producing a large variance in the design distribution. Thus, we
956   can usually assume that the difference in 1/ptr (x) terms is the dominant effect when considering the
957   difference in OOD scores between two input points.

960   C    P ROTEIN S TRUCTURE P REDICTION E XPERIMENT
961
962   Problem Description. Real-world sequence design problems are characterized by high-dimensional
963
      discrete input spaces, label noise, and limited training data. While evaluating design methods with
      physical experiments in the sciences and engineering is the best way to evaluate offline MBO, these
964
      experiments can be resource and time-intensive. Therefore, it is valuable to have simulation settings
965
      that mimic key aspects of the target problem in order to develop and evaluate methods rapidly
966   (Trabucco et al., 2022). Here we describe a simulation using protein structure prediction (Jumper
967   et al., 2021) as a benchmark for evaluating offline MBO. The challenge in protein structure prediction
968   is to determine the 3D shape of a protein based solely on its amino acid sequence. Knowing this 3D
969   shape is key to understanding how the protein functions and interacts on a molecular level. Deep
970   learning has recently brought about significant advances to the field. AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., 2021)
971   has achieved impressive accuracy in predicting protein shapes, with some predictions reaching the
      accuracy levels of experimental methods.


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Our proposal is to use a protein structure prediction network as the ground truth function given its
973    broad generalization capabilities across a wide variety of proteins. Concretely, this means our task is
974    to design an amino acid sequence that folds into the ground truth structure for a predefined protein.
975    The ground truth structure is a publicly available experimentally derived structure (e.g., by using
976    X-ray crystallography). Because there are many amino acid sequences that can fold into the same
977    structure, this is a design task to search in the amino acid sequence space for a sequence that has
978    a predicted structure with the minimum structural distance to the target structure. The wild-type
979    sequence that encodes this structure is intentionally hidden from the model.
980    Using a protein structure prediction network as a simulated fitness landscape offers several benefits.
981    These networks provide accurate results across various protein types and sequence lengths. They
982    are computationally efficient to query, free from label noise, and allow us to examine performance
983    differences across multiple datasets. Most importantly, we observe distribution shift induced by
984    design, which we find is a salient feature of real-world protein engineering settings.
985    Our aim is to design a protein sequence that folds into the structure of Trp-Cage (Zhou, 2003), a
986    notably compact 20-residue mini protein known for its stable folding and structural elements. To
987    determine how closely the predicted structure of our designed sequence matches the true structure
988    of Trp-Cage, we employ the frame-aligned point error (FAPE) metric (Jumper et al., 2021). Lower
989    FAPE scores indicate a closer alignment between the predicted and the actual structure of Trp-Cage.
990
       Experimental Design. We design a training dataset by employing an in-silico variant of error-prone
991    PCR (Smith, 1985), a lab method that randomly mutates a system by decreasing the precision of DNA
992    replication. Here, we introduce mutations at each position in the wild-type (WT) protein sequence
993    (i.e., an example protein sequence that is known to fold into the target protein structure), based on a
994    specific error rate ϵ. We loop over every position in a sequence and with probability ϵ we mutate each
995    position using a uniform distribution over the 20 canonical amino acids. This process is repeated 10K
996    times to generate a set of unlabeled sequences. In our setting we set ϵ to 0.5, so roughly half of the
997    positions of the wild-type sequence are mutated. This makes the problem sufficiently challenging
998    with a lot of diversity in the sequences (and subsequently the distances between their predicted
999
       structures and the ground truth). For each sequence, we compute predicted structures using ESMFold
       (Lin et al., 2023) and FAPE scores by comparing each predicted structure to the WT’s known crystal
1000
       structure. Negative FAPE scores are used as the labels for our dataset since our aim is to minimize
1001
       the structural distance to the target protein (or maximize the negative FAPE score).
1002
1003   We implement offline MBO with this training dataset. We first fit a surrogate regression model f (x)
1004   to the (xi , y) pairs, predicting FAPE scores from the amino acid sequence. Then, we design 10K
1005
       sequences using the same genetic algorithm used in the AAV experiment ((Sinai et al., 2020) to
       maximize f (x). Because genetic algorithms are local search methods, we run the optimization from
1006
       10 different starting points corresponding to 10 distinct initial sequences, restart each optimization run
1007
       with two random initializations, and save designed sequences from iterations 5, 20, 50 corresponding
1008   to low, medium, and high shift intensities. After running optimization, we train the OOD classifier to
1009   separate the training data (class 0) from the designed data (class 1). We then label our data by using
1010   our ground truth function (ESMFold) to compute FAPE scores, measuring the structural distance
1011   between each amino acid sequence and our target protein structure.
1012
       Design hyperparameters We use an identical set of hyperparameters to what is described in Appendix
1013
       A.2 for the AAV experiment for the surrogate regression model. For search, we use Adalead (Sinai
1014   et al., 2020) as our optimization algorithm with a population size of 1K. We refer readers to Sinai
1015   et al. (2020) on algorithm implementation and recommended hyperparameters.
1016
1017
       Results. We first show evidence that our simulation shows a distribution shift between the design
       data and the training data (Fig. 9a and in a more extensive ablation in Fig. 10). Here we evaluate
1018
       the regression model accuracy (as measured by Spearman Rank Correlation) across optimization
1019
       steps (holding edit distance fixed to 10) and across edit distances (holding optimization steps fixed
1020   at 20). We observe substantial distribution shifts in both settings corresponding to feedback and
1021   non-feedback covariate shift.
1022
1023
       Next, we show how a distribution shift metric can enable better selection of variants. We follow an
       identical setup to the AAV experiment described in Section 4.3 and show top K regret for a variety
1024
       of values of K and batch statistics (90th, 95th, Max) (Fig. 11). In all cases we see the OOD score
1025

       We find that the use of a design-induced test set, rather than a fixed test, as the q distribution is crucial
1137   to the performance of our method. To our knowledge, this choice of q distribution is not discussed in
1138   the density ratio estimation literature, which typically assumes access to a fixed test set. Here we
1139   evaluate three choices of a q distribution: (1) Uniform distribution over the input domain, (2) 1-15
1140   random mutations to the wildtype background sequence, and the (3) Design set.
1141
       We evaluate these three methods in the context of the AAV dataset described in Figure 3. For (1), we
1142
       fix the wildtype context and draw a sequence of length 63 (the modifiable region) from a uniform
1143   distribution. For (2), we start with the wildtype sequence and perform ancestral sampling to draw
1144   sequences: randomly select number of mutations from a uniform distribution over 1-15 mutations,
1145   then randomly select positions to modify and then draw a mutation from a uniform distribution over
1146   AAs. For (3) we use the method described in the main text, which uses the designed sequences as the
1147   q distribution.
1148
       We compare the three choices by evaluating how well the scores serve as a predictor of whether a
1149   designed variant is functional by looking at the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve and
1150   the corresponding area under curve (AUC) score and find substantial improvements in detecting
1151   functional variants using the design distribution (in blue) over the uniform distribution (in green) and
