                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Spatial awareness is key to enable embodied multimodal AI systems. Yet, without
010
               vast amounts of spatial supervision, current Visual Language Models (VLMs)
011            struggle at this task. In this paper, we introduce LynX, a framework that equips
012            pretrained VLMs with visual grounding ability without forgetting their existing
013            image and language understanding skills. To this end, we propose a Dual Mixture
014            of Experts module that modifies only the decoder layer of the language model,
015            using one frozen Mixture of Experts (MoE) pre-trained on image and language
016            understanding and another learnable MoE for new grounding capabilities. This
017            allows the VLM to retain previously learned knowledge and skills, while acquiring
018            what is missing. To train the model effectively, we generate a high-quality synthetic
019
               dataset we call SCouT, which mimics human reasoning in visual grounding. This
               dataset provides rich supervision signals, describing a step-by-step multimodal
020
               reasoning process, thereby simplifying the task of visual grounding. We evaluate
021
               LynX on several object detection and visual grounding datasets, demonstrating
022            strong performance in object detection, zero-shot localization and grounded reason-
023            ing while maintaining its original image and language understanding capabilities
024            on seven standard benchmark datasets.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Visual language models (VLMs) have significantly advanced multimodal vision and language tasks,
030   enabling impressive capabilities such as image captioning and visual question answering (Alayrac
031   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Models like CLIP (Radford et al.,
032   2021a) leveraged extensive image-caption data for multimodal training, while generative models like
033
      Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023) generate descriptive captions for images.
      Because of their caption-based nature, these models often lack object localization abilities, making
034
      them less suited for applications requiring precise spatial understanding (Wen et al., 2023; Luo et al.,
035
      2024; Driess et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). Naturally, one can equip a model with
036   localization ability by pre-training, (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b). However, this requires
037   massive datasets, human-annotated bounding boxes, and substantial computational resources, making
038   it costly and impractical for smaller setups. Rather than pre-training from scratch, we aim to equip a
039   pre-trained VLM with spatial understanding by fine-tuning.
040
      Closest to our work is PIN (Dorkenwald et al., 2024), which fine-tunes a VLM for the specific task of
041
      object localization by adding learned spatial parameters to the vision encoder. Trained on a synthetic
042   dataset of superimposed object renderings, PIN is evaluated on single-object localization, predicting
043   a bounding box given a query object name. Despite the obtained ability for object localization, PIN
044   suffers from catastrophic forgetting, losing image understanding abilities after fine-tuning. Moreover,
045   its synthetic data lacks inter-object relationships, limiting its utility for more complex tasks beyond
046   object localization, like multi-object detection and reasoning (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b).
047   Additionally, as demonstrated by PIN, even parameter-efficient methods like LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
048   underfit due to the complexity differences between image understanding and grounding tasks. These
049   challenges underscore the need for a solution that adds grounding capabilities for many tasks without
050
      compromising a model’s pre-existing strengths, which is the focus of our work.
051   Specifically, we introduce LynX (Linking eXperts for visual grounding), a novel framework that
052   leverages a Dual Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture. This design allows the model to specialize
053   in both image understanding and visual grounding simultaneously, preventing the catastrophic
      forgetting seen in PIN and enabling fine-tuning for grounding without sacrificing existing capabilities.

093   Caption-based Visual Language Models (VLMs). Large language models, efficient at instruction
094   following and generalization, have been seamlessly integrated with vision-only encoder models,
095   yielding impressive results in multimodal tasks (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023;
096   Chen et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023d; Zhang et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023; Cha
097   et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2023;
098   Zhang et al., 2023b). Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) are pioneering
099
      works in this area. Flamingo combines a pretrained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) image encoder
      with a pretrained LLM using perceiver and gated cross-attention blocks, while BLIP-2 employs a
100
      lightweight Querying Transformer, pretrained in two stages: vision-language representation from a
101
      frozen image encoder and vision-to-language generation from a frozen language model. Subsequently,
102   recent works have focused on improving performance through optimizing training strategies (Bai
103   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a), increasing resolution of image (Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a;
104   Wang et al., 2023), enhancing image encoders (Chen et al., 2023d; Zhang et al., 2023a; Bai et al.,
105   2023), aligning the input (Lin et al., 2023) and projection layers (Cha et al., 2023; Alayrac et al.,
106   2022; Dai et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c). More importantly, many
107   recent works have focused on improving the model performance by expanding the instruction-tuning
      dataset (Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2023). Instruction tuning in these models

      Grounded Visual Language Models. Extending the capabilities of VLMs beyond image and
114
      language understanding, many models now enable visual grounding to localize objects in an image
115   (Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022a; 2024;

117   et al., 2021) formulates object detection as an auto-regressive language modeling task, conditioned
118   on observed input pixels. Inspired by this approach, models such as OFA (Wang et al., 2022b),
119   Unified-IO (Lu et al., 2022), UniTab (Yang et al., 2022), GIT (Wang et al., 2022a), and VisionLLM
120   (Wang et al., 2024) incorporate coordinate vocabulary alongside language vocabulary for grounding
121   tasks. In contrast, models like Shikra (Chen et al., 2023b), CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023), and
122   Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) treat positional input/output as natural language, demonstrating the
123
      ability to generate interleaved grounded visual captions for images. While these models perform
      impressively, they need large annotated datasets and significant computational resources. To address
124
      this, PIN (Dorkenwald et al., 2024) introduces a learnable positional insert module and a synthetic
125
      dataset to enhance spatial understanding in pretrained VLMs. However, their method shows limited
126   grounding performance and leads to forgetting pretrained knowledge. In contrast, we introduce LynX,
127   a framework that equips VLMs with grounding capabilities while retaining their image understanding
128   skills.

      3.1    P RELIMINARIES
140
141   Visual Language Models (VLMs). VLMs process both image and text data for multimodal genera-
142   tive tasks. These models consist of a vision encoder ψ(·), a language decoder, ϕ(·), and a mapper func-
143   tion f (·). The language decoder takes a sequence of tokens as inputs [v1 , v2 , . . . , vm , t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ]
144   being composed of visual and textual tokens. Visual tokens are computed from an image x as
145   [v1 , v2 , . . . , vm ] = f (ψ(x)), and textual tokens are computed from the text input t as [t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ] =
146   Tokenizer(t). VLMs are trained via cross-entropy loss on a next-token prediction task.
147   Mixture of Expert. Mixture of Experts (MoEs) are a way to increase the small model capacity
148   to compete with large models performance without a proportional increase in computational cost
149   (Shazeer et al., 2017). Specifically, a MoE layer is composed of E “experts” and a gating network
150   g(·). The gating network decides which expert is most suitable for a given token:

      3.3   SC OU T: S YNTHETIC C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT WITH G ROUNDING
234
235   Visual question answering datasets often incorporate spatial reasoning, such as “What object is to
236   the left of the girl?” or “Is there a bowl on top of the table?”. Grounding tasks particularly benefit
237   from this spatial reasoning, as describing relationships like ”A cat at [x1, y1, x2, y2] sits to the
238   left of a dog at [a1, b1, a2, b2]” offers clearer relative positioning, improving the interpretation
239   of localization data for VLMs. Following this intuition, recent works such as Shikra (Chen et al.,
240   2023b) have made progress in creating grounded chain-of-thought datasets. Shikra uses an LLM to
241   generate reasoning-based question-answer pairs from image captions, without access to the actual
242   visual content. However, this reliance on captions alone leads to hallucinated narratives that do not
243
      reflect the image (see halluciantion examples of Shikra dataset in Appendix Figure 5).
244   To address the hallucination problem, we introduce the SCouT dataset, which integrates visual
245   information with textual descriptions from captions. We generate “where” and “what” questions
246   using an LLM, such as Mixtral, ensuring contextual alignment with captions through in-context
247   prompting (see Appendix Figure 7). For answer generation, instead of relying solely on the LLM like
248   Shikra, we use a pretrained VLM, such as CogVLM, which incorporates image context to answer
249
      questions step-by-step, reducing hallucinations (see Appendix Figure 3). In a comparison of 100
      randomly selected samples from each dataset, SCouT achieved an accuracy of 94.7%, significantly
250
      outperforming Shikra’s 63.1%. A correct response is defined as one where the generated grounding
251
      steps accurately reflect the relationships and spatial positions of objects in the image, without
252   hallucinations. This demonstrates the effectiveness of our method in producing reliable, contextually
253   grounded data.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      We perform our evaluation of LynX on three different grounding tasks: i) object localization, ii)
273
      object detection and iii) visual grounding, on top of standard image understanding tasks. The details
274   of our architectural implementation and the dataset splits for each task is explained as follows.
275
276
      Implementation details. LynX is built on MoE-llava (Lin et al., 2024), which uses Phi2 as its
277
      pretrained language model. MoE-llava has 4 experts dedicated to image understanding tasks. For
278   LynX, we add a separate MoE module with two experts for grounding tasks, initialized from the image
279   understanding MoE in the same decoder layer. The vision encoder and multi-head attention layers
280   remain frozen, as do the interleaved decoder layers. LynX is optimized with AdamW (Loshchilov &
281   Hutter, 2019) using a 2e − 5 learning rate, and trained on 4 × A6000 GPUs for about 1.5 days. LynX
282   contains 1.67B trainable parameters, with only 0.8B active, roughly one fourth the size of Shikra-7B.
283
284   Datasets. We train LynX using three types of datasets:
285
286
      1. Referential expression comprehension (REC): The task is to use textual references to accurately
      identify and locate objects in a scene. For example, given the phrase “guy with his back turned to
287
      us,” the model must find the person and provide the bounding box coordinates around him in the
288
      image (see Figure 6 in the appendix). We use the standard RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016) dataset which
289   consists of three splits: RefCOCO, RefCOCO+ and RefCOCOg . In total, we have 128,000 samples
290   of image-referential expression pairs from the COCO2014 (Lin et al., 2014) train dataset.
291
292
      2. Grounded image captioning (GIC): The task involves generating detailed image captions that
      not only identify objects in the image but also provide precise bounding box coordinates for each
293
      mentioned object (see Figure 6 in the appendix). For this task, we process 108k images from the
294
      COCO2017 (Lin et al., 2014) train dataset using CogVLM to produce interleaved captions with
295   corresponding bounding boxes for each object described.
296
297   3. Synthetic grounded visual question answering (SCouT): This task requires the object to have
298
      image understanding and spatial reasoning along with localization capability (see section 3.3 for more
      detail). We use the Flickr30k (Plummer et al., 2015) image dataset consisting of image captions. We
299
      prompt Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024) to generate questions from these captions by leveraging in-context
300
      learning ability of LLMs (Figure 7 in appendix). Given the generated questions and the corresponding
301   image, we prompt CogVLM to generate step-by-step answers for each query. For all tasks, bounding
302   box locations are integers, with x and y coordinates ranging from 0 to 99, creating a 100 × 100 grid.

      4.1   O BJECT L OCALIZATION
305
306   We begin by comparing LynX with PIN (Dorkenwald et al., 2024), as both methods involve fine-
307   tuning pretrained models for grounding tasks. PIN evaluates its object localization ability on a task
308   that requires generating bounding boxes when prompted with object names. Their evaluation is
309   conducted on subsets of COCO, PVOC, and LVIS, with up to three objects per image, totaling 3,582,
310   2,062, and 6,016 test images, respectively. For each image, the model is provided with a ground truth
311   object name and tasked with localizing it. The mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) is reported for
312   all bounding boxes, as well as for medium (32 × 32 to 96 × 96 pixels) and large (over 96 × 96 pixels)
313   bounding boxes, quantifying the overlap between the predicted and true bounding boxes.
314   LynX outperforms PIN in single-object localization, with a 22% improvement in mIoU on PVOC,
315   32% on COCO, and 39% on LVIS, particularly excelling with medium-sized objects. Notably, LynX
316   achieves this without being exclusively trained for localization, whereas PIN, despite being tailored
317   for this specific task, struggles to match our performance. Attempts to fine-tune MoE-LLaVA using
318   LoRA also underperform across all datasets, reinforcing the robustness of our dual MoE architecture
319   for both localization and image understanding.
320   Although PIN uses fewer parameters (1.4M vs. 1.67B), it is limited to single-object detection
321   and suffers from catastrophic forgetting of its image understanding capabilities. This disparity in
322   backbones may introduce unfairness in the comparison. To better showcase LynX’s full potential,
323   we introduce a protocol-based evaluation that assesses more complex multi-object grounding tasks –
      areas where PIN cannot compete – and standardizes evaluation for comparing VLMs.

334   4.2   O BJECT D ETECTION
335
336   While the comparison with PIN highlights LynX’s superiority in standard localization tasks, we
337   aim to demonstrate its full potential in more complex visual grounding challenges. Unlike PIN,
338   which is limited to single-object detection, LynX handles multi-object localization and complex
339   grounding across multiple entities. However, traditional object detection datasets like COCO (Lin
340
      et al., 2014), PASCAL VOC (Everingham et al., 2010), and LVIS (Gupta et al., 2019) are not fully
      suited for VLMs, as LynX generates free-form captions and predicts objects from an open vocabulary.
341
      To fairly evaluate its performance against larger models like CogVLM and Shikra we introduce a
342
      protocol-based framework that bridges this gap.
343
344   Protocol 1 - Common Class Comparison: In this evaluation, CogVLM serves as the ground truth,
345   providing object names and bounding boxes. The task asks models to “Locate objects in this image
346
      along with their bounding box coordinates,” with responses in free-form text. We focus on the top 50
      shared object classes between each model and CogVLM. LynX—after incorporating SCouT into its
347
      training—significantly outperforms all models, including Shikra 7B, with an increase of AP50 by 7.4
348
      on COCO, 6.8 on PVOC, and 7.9 on LVIS. Despite having fewer parameters and no pretraining, LynX
349   surpasses Shikra in these metrics, while PIN scores zero as it only generates bounding boxes without
350   object names. The importance of dataset quality is evident in LynX’s performance gains. Initial
351   training with the REC dataset resulted in low scores, as single-object annotations failed to generalize
352   to multi-object detection. Adding the GIC dataset led to improvements of 28.5 on COCO, 31.7 on
353   PVOC, and 27.6 on LVIS. However, incorporating Visual Genome (VG)—a noisy dataset—negatively
354   impacted performance, highlighting the detrimental effect of noisy data. Replacing VG with SCouT
355   yielded substantial improvements, demonstrating the necessity of high-quality, contextually grounded
356   annotations for complex tasks.
357   Protocol 2 - Class Grouping: In this protocol, we map open-vocabulary predictions from LynX
358   and CogVLM to predefined categories in COCO, PVOC, and LVIS using a sentence transformer.
359   This groups similar classes (e.g., “man” and “woman” into “person”), standardizing labels across
360   models. LynX—trained with SCouT continues to outperform other models under these stricter class
361   mappings, with AP50 increasing by 5.7 on COCO, 6.0 on PVOC, and 3.9 on LVIS compared to the
362   GIC variant. As in Protocol 1, SCouT proves essential for maintaining strong performance, while the
363
      noise in VG continues to hinder results.

410   4.3     V ISUAL G ROUNDING
411
      We evaluate LynX on two key visual grounding tasks: referential expression comprehension (REC)
412
      and phrase grounding. For REC, we use the RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and RefCOCOg (Yu et al., 2016)
413
      datasets, where the objective is to identify a single object in an image based on a descriptive query.
414   In contrast, phrase grounding, evaluated on the Flickr30k Entities (Plummer et al., 2015) dataset,
415   involves linking multiple objects to their corresponding noun phrases in a sentence, requiring more
416   complex contextual reasoning.
417
      Unlike most models in this comparison, which rely on pretraining, LynX and PIN are fine-tuning
418
      approaches. Despite this, LynX trained with SCouT outperforms Shikra-7B on the complex Flickr30k
419
      phrase grounding task by 1.4%, demonstrating our model’s strength in relational tasks. On REC tasks,
420   LynX remains highly competitive despite Shikra’s specialized training, particularly given our smaller
421   model size and fine-tuning approach. We also outperform OFA-L and VisionLLM-H on all REC
422   benchmarks and exceed PIN by 62% on RefCOCO test-A, where PIN struggles with more complex
423   queries. The SCouT variant further improves performance, highlighting the value of step-by-step
424   supervision.

522
523   We explore the scalability of SCouT in Figure 1 (part b). Leveraging our efficient data generation
524   pipeline, we experiment with varying dataset sizes ranging from 64k to 3M samples. As seen in our
525
      results, model performance improves consistently with increase in dataset size, particularly up to 512k
      data points, after which the improvements begin to plateau. This trend highlights the effectiveness
526
      of SCouT in providing high-quality, diverse training samples, outperforming conventional human-
527
      annotated datasets in scaling the model’s capabilities for complex grounding tasks.
528
529   Limitations. We generated our SCouT dataset using CogVLM and trained LynX with this data,
530   making us dependent on its quality. Since the data quality relies on CogVLM’s reasoning capability,
531
      this sets an upper bound on our performance.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduce LynX, a versatile framework with a dual mixture of experts module, enabling a
536   small VLM to retain pretrained knowledge while acquiring new skills. Training step-by-step with
537   our scalable SCouT dataset provides an effective learning signal and highlights the potential of
538   synthetic data. We also present an open-source evaluation pipeline for comparing VLMs at various
539   granularities. LynX demonstrates SOTA effectiveness in object localization on multiple visual
      grounding benchmarks and matches pretrained performance on image understanding benchmarks.

739   question is intentionally incorrect or irrelevant to the image. This is done to mitigate the hallucinations
