011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Node classification is a fundamental task, but obtaining node classification labels
014            can be challenging and expensive in many real-world scenarios. Transfer learn-
015            ing has emerged as a promising solution to address this challenge by leveraging
016
               knowledge from source domains to enhance learning in a target domain. Existing
               transfer learning methods for node classification primarily focus on integrating
017
               Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) with various transfer learning techniques.
018
               While these approaches have shown promising results, they often suffer from a lack
019            of theoretical guarantees, restrictive conditions, and high sensitivity to hyperparam-
020            eter choices. To overcome these limitations, we employ a Graph Convolutional
021            Multinomial Logistic Lasso Regression (GCR) model which simplifies GCN, and
022            develop a transfer learning method called Trans-GCR based on the GCR model.
023            We provide theoretical guarantees of the estimate obtained under the GCR model in
024            high-dimensional settings. Moreover, Trans-GCR demonstrates superior empirical
025            performance, has a low computational cost, and requires fewer hyperparameters
026            than existing methods.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Network (a.k.a graph) data is ubiquitous in various domains, including social networks (Barabási,
032   2013), citation networks (Ji et al., 2022), and biological networks (Zitnik et al., 2018; Han et al.,
033
      2019). A fundamental task in network analysis is node classification (Kipf & Welling, 2016), which
      aims to predict the class labels of a node based on its own features and its neighboring nodes’
034
      features. Usually, the node features are high-dimensional (Hamilton et al., 2017). Thus, we focus
035
      on high-dimensional settings in this paper. Nevertheless, obtaining node classification labels can be
036   challenging and expensive in many real-world scenarios (Dai et al., 2022). For example, classifying
037   genes into disease categories using a gene-gene interaction network faces a scarcity of disease labels,
038   as experimentally annotating genes is expensive (Guney et al., 2016).
039
      To address the challenge of limited labeled data, transfer learning, which uses knowledge from source
040
      domains to enhance learning in a target domain, has emerged as a promising solution (Dai et al.,
041
      2022). Continuing the aforementioned example, despite the scarcity of disease labels, abundant
042   functional annotations of genes exist in curated databases like KEGG pathways (Kanehisa et al.,
043   2012), offering ample source data. In the existing literature, various transfer learning methods based
044   on Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) (Sperduti & Starita, 1997; Bruna et al., 2014; Defferrard
045   et al., 2016; Kipf & Welling, 2017) have been proposed to enhance node classification accuracy.
046   These GCN-based transfer learning methods can be broadly summarized into three main areas.
047
      First, pre-training and fine-tuning approaches (Hu et al., 2019; 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Yang et al.,
048   2022; Kooverjee et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023), which usually pre-train a GCN on a large-scale
049   dataset to learn transferable representations and then fine-tune the pre-trained model on a target task.
050   While effective, these methods usually lack theoretical guarantees. Second, theoretical transferability
051   analysis. Researchers have investigated the theoretical transferability properties of GCNs for graphs
052   sampled from the same underlying space or graphon model (Nilsson & Bresson, 2020; Ruiz et al.,
053   2020; Levie et al., 2021; Ruiz et al., 2023). Despite valuable theoretical insights, they often assume
      that the source and target domain are drawn from exactly the same underlying model, which usually


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      does not hold in practical scenarios where domain shifts occur. Third, to address the domain
055   shifts challenge, various domain adaptation techniques have been proposed, such as unsupervised
056   adaptation (Wu et al., 2020), local structure transfer (Zhu et al., 2021), adversarial domain alignment
057   (Dai et al., 2022), and noise-resistant transfer (Yuan et al., 2023). Despite promising results, they
058   often lack theoretical guarantees or can be sensitive to hyperparameter choices. In summary, existing
059   methods suffer from a lack of theoretical guarantees, restrictive conditions, and high sensitivity to
060   hyperparameters.
061   To address these limitations, we propose a novel statistical transfer learning framework based on
062   a Graph Convolutional Multinomial Logistic Lasso Regression (GCR) model. The GCR model
063   assumes that the classification label depends on the graph-aggregated node features (obtained through
064   multiple graph aggregation layers), followed by a multinomial logistic lasso regression model which
065   assumes a linear relationship between aggregated features and labels. This assumption is inspired by
066   empirical observations suggesting that removing nonlinear activation functions (e.g., ReLU) in GCN’s
067   hidden layers achieves comparable performance to the original GCN architecture (Wu et al., 2019).
068   Our GCR model extends beyond the work of (Wu et al., 2019) by introducing a rigorous statistical
069
      formulation with the ability to handle high-dimensional features. Building upon this GCR model,
      the main contribution of this paper is to develop a two-step transfer learning method Trans-GCR.
070
      Specifically, we let β s and β t = β s + δ denote the GCR’s high-dimensional sparse model parameters
071
      in source and target data, respectively, where δ measures the domain shift. In the first step, we obtain
072
      the estimate of source domain parameters, denoted as β̂ s , by minimizing the l1 -regularized negative
073
      likelihood function of the GCR model using source data. In the second step, we estimate the shift
074
      term δ by substituting β t with β̂ s + δ in the GCR negative likelihood function and minimizing it
075
      using the target data. This step leverages the knowledge learned from β̂ s . Finally, our estimate of the
076
      target domain parameters is given by β̂ t = β̂ s + δ̂.
077
078   Our method enjoys the following advantages: (1) We demonstrate through extensive empirical
079   studies that our proposed method achieves superior or comparable performance compared with
080   existing complicated GCN-based transfer learning approaches for node classification. (2) We provide
081   theoretical guarantees of the estimate obtained under GCR model in high-dimensional settings under
082
      mild conditions. (3) By leveraging the simplified model GCR, our method involves fewer parameters
      to be trained than more complex GCN-based models, resulting in reduced computational cost. (4)
083
      Our framework has only two hyperparameters, i.e., the number of graph aggregation layers and the
084
      l1 −norm penalty strength.

      2.1   R ELATED W ORK : G RAPH C ONVOLUTIONAL N ETWORKS
101
102   GCNs and their variants have gained increasing popularity for node classification tasks (Hamilton
103   et al., 2017; Kipf & Welling, 2017; Wang, 2019; Chien et al., 2020). The core strength of GCNs lies
104   in their ability to leverage the underlying graph structure to propagate and aggregate information
105   from neighboring nodes. A standard two-layer GCN works by H(1) = Relu(AXW(1) ); H(2) =
106   Softmax(AH(1) W(2) ), where W(1) and W(2) are parameters to be learned. As pointed out by Wu
107   et al. (2019), the key to GCN’s success lies in its ability to perform graph convolutions, and not
      necessarily in its use of nonlinearity through activation functions such as ReLU. In fact, empirical


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      observations in Wu et al. (2019) suggested that removing the nonlinear activation function from
109   the GCN’s hidden layers does not substantially impact the model’s performance. From a practical
110   perspective, the removal of nonlinearities results in a simpler model, which offers several advantages.
111   First, it reduces the computational complexity of the model, making training and inference faster
112   and more efficient. This is particularly important in large-scale graph datasets, where computational
113   resources may be limited. Second, the simplified architecture requires fewer hyperparameters,
114   reducing tuning complexity and preventing overfitting.

      4   T HEORETICAL P ROPERTIES
248
      In this section, we present our main theoretical results. Establishing theoretical guarantees for the
249
      GCR model under high-dimensional network data is extremely challenging as: (1) The network
250   dependency prevents the application of standard concentration results, valid only for i.i.d. data.
251   For instance, standard M-estimation theory requires independence among observations to derive
252   consistency and the rate of convergence. (2) The ultra-high dimensionality, where the dimension
253   of X can be much larger than n, further complicates the theoretical analysis. Analyzing any high-
254   dimensional model is difficult because it requires a thorough understanding of high-dimensional
255   geometry. Consequently, we need new tools to address these challenges, which is a key part of our
256   theoretical contribution.
257   For simplicity and ease of presentation, this section focuses on results for the case where C = 2
258   corresponds to a two-class classification problem and M = 1. Nevertheless, our theoretical results
259   can be easily generalized to the multiclass cases.

420   Data description. We conduct experiments on three widely-used real-world citation networks (Tang

426   between papers. The bag-of-words attribute vectors X are derived from keywords extracted from

      feature dimension of X is 6775. Each paper is associated with a label belonging to one of these five

506   7       D ISCUSSION
507
508   In this paper, we introduce the GCR model to capture the relationship between node classification
509   labels (Y), network structure (A), and node covariates (X). We then propose Trans-GCR, a transfer
510   learning method that enhances estimation in the target domain using knowledge from the source
511   domain. Despite its strong empirical performance and theoretical benefits, our method has limitations
512   that need further research. Firstly, our theoretical results are limited to the ER random graph
513   model. While we show empirical success with other models like SBM and graphon, theoretical
514
      validation for these models is still needed. Secondly, the GCR model assumes a linear relationship
      between graph convolutional features and the log odds ratio; extending this to nonlinear models
515
      would be valuable. Thirdly, our source detection algorithm only provides a transferability score, and
516
      developing a hypothesis testing method for source domain transferability would be useful. Fourthly,
517   our algorithm currently transfers only point estimations of model parameters, and extending it to
518   include confidence interval estimation would provide a measure of uncertainty. Lastly, as M increases,
519   SM converges to a matrix of ones, resulting in oversmoothing, where node representations become
520   indistinguishable. Future work could explore adaptive propagation mechanisms, such as APPNP,
521   to mitigate oversmoothing and preserve meaningful differentiation between node representations in
522   deeper architectures.

2382
       Require: Target data (A(0) , X(0) , Y(0) ); Source data (A(k) , X(k) , Y(k) ), k ∈ A; Hyperparameter

2385     {0, A}.
2386     Step 2. Pooled source samples. Get pooled source sample with SA ∈ RnA ×nA , XA ∈ RnA ×d ,
2387     YA ∈ {0, 1}nA ×C .
2388     Step 3. Source domain parameter estimation. Get β̂ A by Eq. 2.3, using the pooled source
2389     samples (SA , XA , YA ).
2390     Step 4. Domain shift estimation. Obtain domain shift estimate δ̂ A using Eq. 3.1 and target data.
2391     Step 5. Target domain parameter estimation. Obtain β̂ (0) = β̂ A + δ̂ A .
