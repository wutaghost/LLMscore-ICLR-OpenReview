013                                              A BSTRACT
014
015            Datasets used for molecular docking, such as PDBBind, contain technical vari-
016            ability - they are noisy. Although the origins of the noise have been discussed,
017            a comprehensive analysis of the physical, chemical, and bioactivity characteris-
018            tics of the datasets is still lacking. To address this gap, we introduce the Com-
019            prehensive Accurate Assessment (Compass). Compass integrates two key com-
020            ponents: PoseCheck, which examines ligand strain energy, protein-ligand steric
021
               clashes, and interactions, and AA-Score, a new empirical scoring function for
               calculating binding affinity energy. Together, these form a unified workflow that
022
               assesses both the physical/chemical properties and bioactivity favorability of lig-
023
               ands and protein-ligand interactions. Our analysis of the PDBBind dataset using
024            Compass reveals substantial noise in the ground truth data. Additionally, we pro-
025            pose CompassDock, which incorporates the Compass module with DiffDock, the
026            state-of-the-art deep learning-based molecular docking method, to enable accu-
027            rate assessment of docked ligands during inference. Finally, we present a new
028            paradigm for enhancing molecular docking model performance by fine-tuning
029            with Compass Scores, which encompass binding affinity energy, strain energy,
030            and the number of steric clashes identified by Compass. Our results show that,
031            while fine-tuning without Compass improves the percentage of docked poses with
032
               RMSD < 2Å, it leads to a decrease in physical/chemical and bioactivity favor-
               ability. In contrast, fine-tuning with Compass shows a limited improvement in
033
               RMSD < 2Å but enhances the physical/chemical and bioactivity favorability of
034
               the ligand conformation. The source code is available at https://github.
035            com/anonym8171iclr2025/iclr_2025_paperid_8171.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Molecular docking is crucial in drug discovery as it helps determine the feasibility of potential drug
042   candidates. Traditional docking methods are slow (Trott & Olson, 2010; Halgren et al., 2004), lead-
043   ing to the development of deep learning (DL)-based methods (Stärk et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022).
044   These newer approaches have shown impressive accuracy and significantly faster performance, de-
045   creasing the run time from hours/days to seconds/minutes per protein-ligand pair (Corso et al.,
046   2022; 2024; Lu et al., 2024).
047
      Deep learning-based methods typically assess their accuracy using numerical metrics such as Root
048   Mean Square Distance (RMSD) (Meli & Biggin, 2020). Although RMSD measures the overall
049   deviation of individual molecules from the ground truth, it is a purely distance-based metric and
050   does not consider the physico-chemical interactions or bioactivity properties between the ligand
051   and the protein at the binding site. As a result, metrics like RMSD fail to capture critical factors
052   that determine strong molecular binding, such as the favorability of these interactions. PoseBuster
053   (Buttenschoen et al., 2024) demonstrated that even when structures have RMSD < 2Å, it is common
      to find ligands with unfavorable physico-chemical properties.

      PoseCheck (Harris et al., 2023) was developed for Structure-Based Drug Design (SBDD) (Blun-
      dell, 1996; Ferreira et al., 2015; Anderson, 2003) to analyze molecular stability by calculating strain
080
      energy, identifying interactions within the complex, and counting steric clashes between the protein
081
      and ligand. Another critical metric for assessing protein-ligand interactions is binding affinity en-
082   ergy, which reflects bioactivity. Autodock-Vina (Trott & Olson, 2010) is a widely used method for
083   calculating binding affinity energy. However, the recently introduced AA-Score (Pan et al., 2022),
084   an empirical scoring function, has been shown to be more accurate than Autodock-Vina. Addition-
085   ally, AA-Score provides the 3D structure of the docked ligand within the protein pocket (pdb file),
086   allowing for easier visual inspection of docking results.
087
      Additionally, (Corso et al., 2024) demonstrated that model fine-tuning boosts performance, yielding
088   an increased percentage of structures’ with an RMSD score of less than 2Å. While fine-tuning with-
089   out Physico-Chemical and Bioactivity (PCB) features increases the percentage of docked poses with
090   RMSD < 2Å, we demonstrate that this approach results in degraded physico-chemical interactions
091   and bioactivity. On the other hand, fine-tuning with PCB results in only a slight improvement in
092   RMSD < 2Å but enhances the ligands’ physico-chemical and bioactivity favorability.
093
      To tackle the challenges in DL-based molecular docking, such as identifying ligand strain energy,
094   counting protein-ligand steric clashes, finding interaction types, finding the best score function for
095   binding affinity energy, and enhancing performance with a PCB-based approach, we propose Com-
096   passDock with two modes (Figure 1):
097
098            • Inference Mode: Provides comprehensive PCB quality analysis during DL-based molec-
099              ular docking with integrated Comprehensive Accurate Assessment (Compass) module.
100            • Fine-Tuning Mode: Introduces a new paradigm for fine-tuning DL-based molecular dock-
101              ing using a PCB-based approach with the Integrated Compass Score.
102
103   Our analysis of the PDBBind (Liu et al., 2017) dataset, commonly used in molecular docking,
104
      revealed significant PCB noise/unfavorability. Secondly, we give users the opportunity to analyze
      their docking results with real assessments in an inference run. Finally, with these PCB-based
105
      assessments, we can improve the model by fine-tuning it with a new score function called Compass
106
      Score.
107
      In summary, our CompassDock offers the following key contributions:

      2.2.1     D IFF D OCK
179   DiffDock (Corso et al., 2022; 2024) approaches molecular docking as a problem of learning a
180   distribution of ligand poses based on the protein structure, utilizing a diffusion generative model
181   (DGM) developed over this defined space. While (De Bortoli et al., 2022) initially described DGMs
182   on submanifolds by projecting a diffusion from ambient space onto the submanifold, they refined
183   this by mapping to a more manageable manifold where the diffusion kernel is directly sampled,
184   thereby enhancing the efficiency of training the DGM on this new manifold.
185   Ligand movements are mapped in molecular docking to mathematical groups, associating transla-
186   tions with the 3D translation group T (3), rigid rotations with the 3D rotation group SO(3), and
187   changes in torsion angles with multiple copies of the 2D rotation group SO(2). These movements
188   are formalized by defining how each group acts on a ligand pose c ∈ R3n , where translations are
189   straightforward additions to atom positions, and rotations are conducted around the ligand’s center
190   of mass. Furthermore, modifications to torsion angles are defined to minimally perturb the structure
191   in an RMSD sense and can be specified separately from translations and rotations to ensure changes
192   are disentangled.
193   Overall, DiffDock consists of two primary models: the Score Model and the Confidence Model.
194
      Score Model s(x, y, t) processes the current ligand pose x and protein structure y in 3D space, out-
195
      putting in the tangent space Tr T 3 ⊕ TR SO(3) ⊕ Tθ SO(2)m . It generates two SE(3)-equivariant vec-
196
      tors for translations and rotations, along with an SE(3)-invariant scalar for each rotatable bond, uti-
197   lizing architectures based on SE(3)-equivariant convolutional networks over point clouds (Thomas
198   et al., 2018; Geiger et al.). Additionally, the Score Model represents structures as heterogeneous
199   geometric graphs, incorporating features derived from protein sequences and convolutions specific
200   to translation, rotation, and torsion to optimize for multiscale integration and computational effi-
201   ciency (Lin et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2022).
202
      Confidence Model d(x, y), on the other hand, takes the same inputs of ligand pose and protein
203   structure and outputs a single scalar that is SE(3)-invariant, reflecting the joint rototranslations of x
204   and y. This model focuses on assessing the stability and plausibility of the ligand pose relative to
205   the protein structure, providing a scalar output that can guide the refinement of docking predictions.
206
207   2.2.2     P OSE C HECK
208
209   PoseCheck (Harris et al., 2023) is developed to assess molecular stability by measuring strain en-
210   ergy, identifying interactions within the complex, and counting steric clashes between the protein
211   and ligand.
212   Strain Energy: Strain energy is the internal energy accumulated in a ligand due to conformational
213   changes during binding, affecting bond lengths, angles, and torsional stability, which in turn influ-
214   ences the binding affinity and stability of the protein-ligand complex (Perola & Charifson, 2004).
215
         1

216
      Lower strain energy typically leads to more favorable binding interactions and could enhance ther-
217   apeutic effectiveness due to the balance between enthalpy and entropy. The calculation of strain
218   energy involves determining the difference in internal energy between a relaxed pose and the gener-
219   ated pose using the Universal Force Field (UFF) in RDKit (Rappé et al., 1992).
220
      Steric Clashes: Steric clashes occur when two neutral atoms are closer than the sum of their van
221
      der Waals radii, indicating an energetically unfavorable and physically implausible interaction (Ra-
222
      machandran et al., 2011; Buonfiglio et al., 2015). Such clashes suggest that the current conformation
223   of the ligand within the protein is suboptimal, highlighting potential inadequacies in pose design or
224   a fundamental mismatch in molecular topology. In SBDD, the total number of these clashes is con-
225   sidered a crucial performance metric, with a clash defined as the pairwise distance between protein
226   and ligand atoms falling below their combined van der Waals radii by a tolerance of 0.5 Å (Harris
227   et al., 2023).
228
      Interaction Fingerprint: Interaction fingerprinting is a computational technique in SBDD that cap-
229   tures and analyzes interactions between a ligand and its target protein, encoding these interactions
230   as a bit vector known as an interaction fingerprint (Bouysset & Fiorucci, 2021; Marcou & Rognan,
231   2007). This method allows for the rapid comparison of different ligands or binding poses by calcu-
232   lating the Tanimoto similarity between fingerprints, providing a quantitative measure of interaction
233   similarity. The approach uses specific libraries like ProLIF to encode molecular interactions like
234   hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic contacts into a compact, easily comparable format (Bouysset &
235   Fiorucci, 2021).
236
237   2.2.3    AA-S CORE
238
      A new empirical scoring function by (Pan et al., 2022), named AA-Score, which includes several
239
      amino acid-specific interaction components such as hydrogen bonds, electrostatic, and van der Waals
240   interactions (Appendix B.1). This scoring function differentiates interactions with main-chain and
241   side-chain atoms across these types and incorporates additional energy components that are not
242   amino acid-specific, including hydrophobic contacts, π-π stacking, π-cation interactions, metal-
243   ligand interactions, and a conformational entropy penalty. Each of these energy components is
244   thoroughly described to provide a comprehensive understanding of their contributions to the scoring
245   function.

278   2.4.1    L OSS R EGULARIZER WITH C OMPASS S CORE FOR F INE -T UNING
279
280   Fine-tuning of the DL-based docking method focuses on reducing the overall structural deviation of
281   individual molecules from the ground truth but tends to overlook important PCB features’ quality
282   of ligands and protein-ligand interactions. To address this, we used Compass to fine-tune the model
283
      with the goal of improving the quality of these PCB features. To more effectively compare these
      results with ground-truth data, we propose replacing traditional regression methods with a new loss
284
      function, Log Absolute Normalized - Mean Square Error (LAN-MSE), which better handles the
285
      normalization of outliers (Section 2.4.2).

      2.4.2    L OG A BSOLUTE N ORMALIZED - M EAN S QUARE E RROR (LAN-MSE)
289
      The LAN-MSE loss function applies a logarithmic transformation to both predicted and true values
290   before computing the mean squared error (MSE). This logarithmic approach effectively reduces the
291   impact of outliers, enhancing model robustness by prioritizing relative rather than absolute differ-
292   ences. This characteristic is particularly advantageous in scenarios where output values span large
293   scales.
294
      Theorem: Stability of Logarithmic Transformation. Let x be a real number. The logarithmic
295
      function log(|x| + buffer) is stabilized by a buffer buffer = 1.1 for |x| < 1 to prevent the function
296   from diverging or becoming overly sensitive near zero.
297
298   Proof: For x approaching zero, log(|x|) approaches −∞. Introducing a buffer shifts the domain
299
      away from zero, ensuring that log(|x| + buffer) remains finite and non-sensitive as x → 0.
300   Error normalization in LAN-MSE utilizes the term 2| log(|yi | + buffertrue,i )|, scaling each error
301   according to the magnitude of the true value. This scaling mechanism proportionately decreases the
302   significance of errors for larger true values, aligning the error metric with scenarios where percentage
303   differences outweigh absolute differences.
304   LAN-MSE is ideally suited for applications involving data with exponential growth or decay, as it
305   adjusts for the logarithmic nature of such data. The following function provides a mathematical
306   representation of LAN-MSE, which includes mechanisms for handling the peculiarities of the data:
307
308                                          N                                                                   2
309                               1 X                log(|yi | + buffertrue,i ) − log(|ŷi | + bufferpred,i )
                 LAN-MSE(ŷ, y) =                                                                                    (2)
310                               N i=1                        2| log(|yi | + buffertrue,i )| + ϵ

479
      This study conducted two key experiments to assess the effectiveness of the Compass Score within
480   the molecular docking framework using the PDBBind dataset. The first experiment (Section 4.1)
481   evaluated PCB feature quality, revealing notable variations in binding affinity, steric clashes, and
482   strain energy, which highlight the complexities and inconsistencies of protein-ligand interactions.
483   The findings showed that most of the dataset exhibited steric clashes, with only a small portion free
484   from such violations. This suggests that even widely-used datasets like PDBBind contain inherent
485   inaccuracies that could influence docking prediction outcomes. These results emphasize the need for
      robust modeling techniques that can handle the diverse physicochemical and biochemical properties
