009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Human body restoration is critical for a wide range of applications. Despite recent
012            advances in general image restoration using generative models, their performance
013            in human body restoration remains suboptimal, often resulting in noticeable ar-
014            tifacts, such as unnatural textures, misalignments that disrupt the structural in-
015            tegrity, and loss of fine body details. To address these challenges, we propose a
016            novel approach by introducing a human body-aware diffusion model that lever-
017            ages domain-specific knowledge to enhance restoration quality. Our method em-
018
               ploys a two-stage diffusion-based image restoration model. In the first stage, we
               generate human body preliminary predictions such as normal and depth map (pri-
019
               ors) from degraded images using a multi-channel joint diffusion model accompa-
020
               nied by a robust reconstruction paradigm. In the second stage, we reconstruct the
021            restored image based on the priors generated in the first stage, while balancing the
022            control strength of different priors to improve restoration quality. Extensive quan-
023            titative and qualitative experiments demonstrate the superiority of our approach in
024            generating perceptually high-quality human body restoration results.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Blind image restoration (BIR) aims to enhance the quality of degraded images through processes like
030   denoising (Tian et al., 2020), sharpening (Wang et al., 2020), deblurring (Zhang et al., 2022), super-
031   resolution (Liu et al., 2022), etc., a domain that has seen significant progress with advancements
032   in the data-driven learning paradigm. Although general BIR has made substantial strides, users
033
      often exhibit a greater interest in the specific effects of BIR on particular subjects, with the human
      body being one of the key focuses. The restoration of the human body can have a profound impact
034
      on various human-centric applications, such as improving portrait quality in social media apps and
035
      aiding related downstream tasks like person re-identification (Ye et al., 2021), 3D reconstruction
036   (Wang et al., 2021a), etc.
037
038   Regarding the methodology of BIR, while the end-to-end reconstruction paradigm (Liang et al.,
039
      2021; Wang et al., 2021c) has made great progress, it struggles to handle complicated combinatorial
      and severe degradations. The generative paradigm offers a solution to this issue by harnessing the
040
      power of generative models, such as Generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Karras et al., 2018)
041
      and Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021). The priors of generative models possess a powerful
042   “imagination” learned from large amounts of data, which can be used to fill in reasonable details
043   to the degraded images. Thus, current diffusion-based image restoration models (Luo et al., 2023;
044   Lin et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) have notably enhanced the perceptual quality and adaptability of
045   restoration results, thereby expanding the applicability of image restoration in practical contexts.
046
      Despite these advancements, the specific area of human body image restoration remains underdevel-
047
      oped. It is worth noting that the theoretical upper bound of performance for human body restoration
048   is arguably higher than that for general restoration, since existing knowledge of the human body can
049   be utilized as priors to the restoration problem. However, current diffusion-based general restora-
050   tion models (Yang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) are prone to produce artifacts for
051   low-quality human images, including unnatural textures and loss of fine body details, as illustrated
052   in Figs. 1 and 2. This problem can be analyzed using the perception-distortion tradeoff (Blau &
053   Michaeli, 2018): Although existing GANs and diffusion models successfully improve the image
      quality such that the output distribution is closer to nice-looking natural images, since humans are

         089

         090

                         compared to existing methods.

         104             restoration performance, particularly in challenging human body restoration tasks; (2) We propose
         105

         107

      Perception-distortion tradeoff and evaluation methods: (Blau & Michaeli, 2018) shows a trade-
      off between perception and distortion: As the mean distortion (the dissimilarity to the ground truth
132
      image) decreases, the perceptual quality (the consistency with natural image statistics) must de-
133
      crease as well. This tradeoff can be visualized as a distortion-quality curve (Fig. 1): Restoration
134   results below this curve is impossible. Our goal is to push the performance below the viewer’s com-
135   fort threshold on the perceptual quality, manifested in better perceived images with better perceptual
136   metrics such as LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), ManIQA (Yang et al., 2022), ClipIQA (Wang et al.,
137   2023), and MUSIQ (Ke et al., 2021), at the cost of potential visual distortion and lower objective
138   metrics such as PSNR and SSIM. To assess viewer comfort, which cannot be measured by existing
139   methods, we introduce the comfort pass test and comfort scoring in our user study.
140   Blind image restoration: Blind Image Restoration (BIR) aims to restore images without prior
141   knowledge of the specific degradation model. Rather than relying on a known corruption process,
142
      BIR algorithms must generalize across different types of degradation, making it a more challenging
      task. Predominantly, existing literature (Bora et al., 2017; Menon et al., 2020; Daras et al., 2021;
143
      Pan et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2021b) has concentrated on discerning a latent code
144
      situated in the latent space of a pre-trained GAN. Recent advancements in this domain (Ho et al.,
145   2020; Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia
146   et al., 2022) have transitioned towards the utilization of DDPMs, marking a notable shift from con-
147   ventional approaches. Other novel approaches such as DDRM (Kawar et al., 2022) utilizes SVD
148   to address linear image restoration challenges, presenting an innovative and simplified approach.
149   DDNM (Wang et al., 2022a) delves into vector range-null space decomposition to develop a novel
150   sampling strategy, enhancing image restoration efficiency. DiffBIR (Lin et al., 2023) and SUPIR
151   (Yu et al., 2024) aims to exploit a pretrained powerful generative prior to solve the BIR problem. In
152   the realm of domain-specific image restoration models, a predominant emphasis has been placed on
153
      blind face restoration, as evidenced by works such as (Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Gu et al.,
      2022). In contrast, the equally critical domain of human body restoration has not seen comparable
154
      development, a gap that our DiffBody model seeks to address.
155
      Controllable Human Image Generation: Traditional methods for generating controllable human
156   images mainly fall into two categories: those based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
157   (Zhu et al., 2017; Siarohin et al., 2019) and those using Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Ren et al.,
158   2020; Yang et al., 2021a), both leveraging reference images and specific conditions for input. Recent
159   studies have ventured into enabling the generation process through textual instructions, though these
160   tend to limit user input to basic pose or style adjustments (Roy et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2022). State-
161   of-the-art methods enable detailed control over vocabulary and pose including ControlNet(Zhang
      et al., 2023), T2I-Adapter(Mou et al., 2023), HumanSD(Ju et al., 2023), HyperHuman(Liu et al.,

163   to generate human images that contain rich detail and natural texture, which give us confidence that
164   they can be utilized for high-quality human body image restoration.

      3.1 P RELIMINARY: L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODEL & S TABLE D IFFUSION
169   Our exploration begins with the foundational principles of Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rom-
170   bach et al., 2022), which are pivotal in the generation of high-fidelity images from latent spaces. By
171   compressing images into a lower-dimensional latent space before performing the diffusion process,
172
      LDMs achieve remarkable efficiency and detail in image synthesis. An autoencoder is used to tran-
      sition between the image and its latent representation, effectively enabling the model to learn robust
173
      feature distributions.
174
      Following the encoding phase, the model initiates a reverse diffusion process starting from a distri-
175   bution of latent noise, gradually denoising this representation to reconstruct the image based on a
176   given textual prompt. This process is facilitated by a U-Net architecture, which iteratively refines
177   the latent features under the guidance of textual conditions embedded by a pre-trained text encoder
178   such as CLIP. The primary objective in training these models involves minimizing the difference
179   between the original and reconstructed images, formalized through a loss function that measures
180   fidelity across multiple stages of the generative process.
181

262   4.1 DATASETS
263
      To address common challenges such as incomplete representations and variability in image quality,
264   we implemented a comprehensive dataset annotation process, annotating each of the 5 million high-
265   quality human images with MMPose, MiDaS depth (Ranftl et al., 2020), OmniNormal (Eftekhar
266   et al., 2021), and LLaVA caption to create a robust and reliable training set. Using a bucket-based
267   resizing strategy, similar to that in SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), we organized the dataset into five
268   resolution buckets: 512×512, 512×768, 512×1024, 768×512, and 1024×512, ensuring the accom-
269   modation of varying resolutions. To maintain consistent quality across diverse image resolutions,
      we applied the degradation settings from Real-ESRGAN, simulating realistic image degradation.

      The final training set includes approximately four million human images extracted and refined from

      4.2 E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
278
279
      For prior generation in stage 1, we employ Stable Diffusion 2.1-base as the foundational generative
      model. The three-branch architecture is initialized using the HumanSD framework, with fine-tuning
280
      applied only to the Stable Diffusion branch for 100,000 steps, using a batch size of 64. The model
281
      is optimized with the Adam optimizer at a learning rate of 10−5 and is conducted for one week
282   on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB). After this phase, the Stable Diffusion branch’s parameters are
283   frozen. The ControlNet branch, responsible for processing input Iir , is then fine-tuned for another
284   100,000 steps, also with a batch size of 64. This second stage focuses on image restoration rather
285   than general generation, and uses the same optimization settings and hardware.
286
      For image restoration in stage 2, we use Stable Diffusion XL-1.0-base (SDXL) as the primary back-
287
      bone. We initialize a trainable encoder block from SDXL and fine-tune it on features Iir , Ipose ,
288   Idepth , and Inormal over 100,000 steps, with a batch size of 32 and gradient accumulation of 2. This
289   phase is optimized using Adam with a learning rate of 10−5 and takes approximately one week,
290   utilizing 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Following this, we initialize the color adapter with IP-adapterXL
291   plus parameters and fine-tune it for an additional 200,000 steps with a batch size of 64. This final
292   phase uses Adam with a learning rate of 10−4 and is trained under the same conditions and duration
293   on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. For inference, we utilize DDPM sampler (Ho et al., 2020) with 200
294   steps for both stage 1 and 2.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
523   DiffBody introduces a novel framework for human body restoration, achieving realistic outcomes
524   by incorporating human-centric guidance into the pre-trained Stable Diffusion model. By leveraging
525   various human-specific conditions, we surpass the capabilities of existing general image restoration
526   models in addressing artifacts. A key aspect of our approach is balancing different priors, such
527   as pose, depth, and normal maps, to strike a balance between the viewer’s comfort threshold and
528   fidelity to the low-quality (LQ) image. However, there are still areas for improvement, such as
529
      exploring advanced techniques like mesh modeling for precise body structure manipulation and
      ensuring the preservation of personal identity throughout restoration. Future work will focus on
530
      handling more challenging scenarios, including complex poses, multi-human images, and cases
531
      where subjects are partially occluded by objects. These extensions, along with better body control
532   and identity preservation, will further enhance the robustness and applicability of human image
533   restoration models.
534   Ethical concerns: While DiffBody offers significant advancements in human body restoration,
535   it raises ethical concerns related to privacy, consent, and image counterfeiting. The ability to ma-
536   nipulate and restore human images could lead to unwanted alterations of an individual’s likeness,
537   potentially infringing on personal rights. Misuse of this technology may result in unauthorized mod-
538   ifications or counterfeit images. It is essential that this model is applied responsibly, with explicit
539   consent, and that strong safeguards are in place to prevent misuse. Developers and researchers must
      remain vigilant in addressing these ethical challenges.
