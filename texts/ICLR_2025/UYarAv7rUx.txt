                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Transformers have been the cornerstone of current Large Language Models
010
               (LLMs); however, its linear growth in overhead during inference with respect
011            to sequence length poses challenges for modeling long sequences. In this con-
012            text, Mamba has gradually attracted attention due to its constant-level size during
013            inference and existing empirical results have shown that it can perform compa-
014            rably to Transformers in sequence modeling while offering significant savings.
015            However, one may ask that, can Mamba always enjoy the “free lunch”? In this
016            paper, we focus on analyzing the expressive ability of Mamba from a theoretical
017            standpoint. First, inspired by the connection between Mamba and linear attention,
018            we investigate potential shortcomings of the Mamba when performing the COPY
019
               operation. Our results indicate that Mamba with constant size may encounter bot-
               tlenecks when handling COPY, while it can achieve perfect performance when the
020
               size scales linearly with sequence length. Based on this observation, we analyze
021
               Mamba’s ability to tackle DP problems when equipped with Chain of Thought
022            (CoT). Our findings suggest that to solve arbitrary DP problems, the total cost of
023            Mamba is comparable to standard and efficient Transformers. However, similar to
024            efficient Transformers, when facing DP problems with favorable properties such
025            as locality, Mamba can provide savings in overhead. Our results contribute to a
026            deeper understanding of Mamba.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reccently, Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) have become the mainstream of mod-
032   ern neural network architectures due to their outstanding performance across a wide range of tasks
033
      (Vaswani et al., 2017; Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Min
      et al., 2022). However, the core component of Transformers—the attention layer—while providing
034
      excellent performance, also leads to emerging drawbacks: during training, the computational cost
035
      scales quadratically with sequence length, and during inference, the cost scales linearly with se-
036   quence length. This limitation becomes increasingly unacceptable when dealing with long sequence
037   tasks. To address this issue, many works have attempted to improve the attention mechanism to
038   reduce its time and storage costs (Tay et al., 2023; Choromanski et al., 2020; Katharopoulos et al.,
039   2020; Beltagy et al., 2020; Child et al., 2019). However, these improved structures often achieve
040   efficiency in the attention layer at the expense of some performance.
041
      Faced with the scaling challenges of Transformers, the exploration of new model architectures to
042   replace Transformers has gradually come into focus, leading to the development of modern RNN
043   architectures, including RWKV (Peng et al., 2023), RetNet (Sun et al., 2023), and Mamba (Gu &
044   Dao, 2023). Among them, the Mamba architecture (Gu et al., 2021; Gu & Dao, 2023), based on the
045   state space model (SSM), has garnered attention for its performance comparable to Transformers in
046   many sequence modeling tasks Dao & Gu (2024) and vision tasks (Zhu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024).
047   These models utilize hardware-aware algorithms during training, resulting in computational costs
048   that scale linearly with sequence length, and require constant-level computation and storage during
049   inference. Mamba’s strong performance and computational efficiency make it a strong competitor
050
      to Transformers.
051   Despite Mamba demonstrating excellent performance, one can not help but ask, can Mamba always
052   enjoy such “free lunch”, that is, can Mamba always save considerable overhead while maintaining
053   performance of Transformers? More recent results have revealed Mamba’s shortcomings in certain
      tasks, especially those involving retrieval (Arora et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Jelassi et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024). Specifically, Akyürek et al. (2024) study the in-context language learning capabilities of
055   different models and find that Transformers outperformed other models, including Mamba, due to
056   the specialized attention heads. Jelassi et al. (2024) also discover that Transformers are superior to
057   Mamba on tasks that require copying from the input context. Park et al. (2024) point out that Mamba
058   struggles to retrieve vectors from the context of multi-query associative recall (MQAR) (Arora et al.,
059   2023), while Transformers can easily handle it well. Furthermore, Waleffe et al. (2024) conduct
060   experiments on larger models (up to 8B parameters) with a broader range of tasks, discovering that
061   when it comes to in-context learning and recalling information from text, although Mambas can
062   contain the same knowledge as Transformers, it will be more difficult for them to directly copy
063
      useful information from history.
064   Although there has been some empirical exploration, the theoretical investigation concerning the
065   above “free lunch” question still remains open to explore. In this paper, we attempt to explore
066   Mamba’s expressive ability from a theoretical standpoint. Specifically, inspired by the comparison
067   between Mamba and linear attention mechanism, we first focus on Mamba’s ability to perform the
068   COPY operation, which is closely related to the ability to retrieve information from context. Our
069
      theoretical results show that Mamba’s performance in executing COPY operations is closely related
      to the size of its model and experiments also confirm the limitations of Mamba in executing copy
070
      tasks and retrieval from long sequences. Further, following the setting of Feng et al. (2024); Yang
071
      et al. (2024), we explore Mamba’s capability to solve DP tasks equipped with Chain of Thought
072   (CoT). We find that to solve arbitrary DP problems, Mamba, standard Transformers and efficient
073   Transformers including Linear and Sparse Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Child et al.,
074   2019) seem to be on equal footing in terms of inference cost; however, when dealing with m-locality
075   DP problems, Mamba may offer significant savings like efficient Transformers. Our results can be
076   concluded as follows:
077
078          • A constant-sized Mamba may encounter bottlenecks when executing COPY operations
079
               (Theorem 1 in Section 4);
080          • When the size of Mamba scales linearly with the sequence length, it can perform COPY
081            operation accurately (Theorem 2 in Section 4);
082          • To solve arbitrary DP problems, the total cost required by Mamba is comparable to that of
083            standard and efficient Transformers (Theorem 3 in Section 5);
084
             • When dealing with DP problems that have favorable locality properties, Mamba can bring
085
               savings in overhead compared to standard Transformers (Theorem 4 in Section 5).

088   2   R ELATED WORK
089
090   SSMs and Attention Mechanism: The attention mechanism is a core component of LLMs (Brown
091   et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Drawing connections between SSMs and attention is a fascinat-
092   ing direction as it not only aids in our understanding of the Mamba structure but also facilitates
093
      the transfer of well-established acceleration techniques from attention mechanisms to Mamba (Dao,
      2023; Katharopoulos et al., 2020). Based on observations of the similarities between them, Dao
094
      & Gu (2024) proposed the state space dual (SSD) layer based on SSMs to achieve significant im-
095
      provements in training efficiency. Sieber et al. (2024) introduce the Dynamical Systems Framework
096   (DSF), under which attention and SSMs can be directly compared. Additionally, Han et al. (2024)
097   reformulate the structure of SSMs to establish links with linear attention, aiming to investigate the
098   key factors behind success in vision tasks. We follow this convenient reformulation and comparison,
099   based on which we furthermore explore Mamba’s ability to perform the COPY operation.
100
      Comparisons between Transformers and Mamba: More recent works compare the performance
101
      of Mamba and Transformers across various tasks from different perspectives. Merrill et al. (2024)
102   theoretically demonstrate that, similar to Transformers, Mamba is also unable to solve state tracking
103   problems such as permutation composition. Jelassi et al. (2024) find that Transformers significantly
104   surpass SSMs when facing tasks related to copying and retrieving information from context. Park
105   et al. (2024) investigate Mamba’s capability for in-context learning and demonstrate that Mamba
106   outperforms Transformers in sparse parity learning while it is weaker in tasks involving non-standard
107   retrieval functionality. Similarly, Waleffe et al. (2024) conduct experiments on a larger scale and
      find that Mamba lag behind Transformers in tasks that require strong copying and long-context

108
      reasoning. Our experiments reference the setups of these works and conduct similar investigations.
109   We also note that Jelassi et al. (2024) find theoretical conclusions that appear to be similar to ours,
110   namely that generalized SSMs (GSSMs) including Mamba cannot copy uniformly input sequences
111   unless the size of the state space grows linearly with the sequence length. Although our work
112   also focuses on “copy”, Jelassi et al. (2024)’s definition of the copy task is at the sequence level,
113   investigating the ability of GSSMs to replicate entire sequences that satisfy some distribution and
114   providing a lower bound for their state space memory. In contrast, our COPY operation is defined
115   at the token level, exploring the conditions under which Mamba can copy some specific historical
116   token and providing constructions for Mamba with linear-scaling size to achieving this operation.
117   Transformers and modern RNNs with CoT: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) is em-
118   ployed to enhance the performance of LLMs by enabling them to provide step-by-step reasoning
119   before arriving at a final answer. It has been shown theoretically that Transformers with CoT exhibit
120   significantly improved expressive power, allowing them to solve more complex problems compared
121   to Transformers without CoT (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023b; Feng et al., 2024; Merrill & Sabharwal,
122   2023a; Li et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Our analysis of Mamba equipped with CoT follows the
123
      framework set by Feng et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024) in their analysis of dynamic programming
      (DP) problems. Furthermore, Wen et al. (2024) examine the effect of CoT on enhancing the expres-
124
      sive power of modern RNNs including SSMs by drawing connections with a Turing machine with
125
      O(log(n)) space. Their findings indicate that when using CoT, RNNs with log(n) bit memory will
126   have strictly stronger representation power than those without CoT. Different from this, our work
127   explores the ability of Mamba equipped with CoT from the perspective of solving DP problems fol-
128   lowing the setting of Feng et al. (2024) and show constructions for Mamba layers with linear-scaling
129   size relative to the sequence length to solve DP problems.

244   4.2   T HE T RADE - OFF OF M AMBA WHEN PERFORMING COPY
245
      Based on the observation of the connection between the SSM module and attention mechanism, we
246
      investigate the capability of Mamba to recover historical inputs, which is foundational for the model
247   to process information based on context. The COPY operation we focus on is defined as follows:
248
      Definition 1 (COPY operation). Given the considered input sequence X = [x1 ]N      i=1 , we define the
249
      L-local matching set Si = {i − L + 1 ≤ j ≤ i : |cTi bj | ≥ δ} and denote vi = ∆i ⊙ xi as
250
      historical records. Then the output of COPY operation is a sequence of vectors o1 , o2 , ..., oN with
251
      oi = vpos(i) where pos(i) ∈ Si is the position we want to copy.
252
253   The L-local matching set Si describes the indices of historical keys bj that is highly relevant to
254   the current query ci within a local window of length L, that is, the “attention scores |cTi bj |” is
255   lower-bounded by δ. Thus, the position of the historical record we most want to replicate should be
256   within this highly relevant set. It should be noted that here we do not specify particular conditions
257   for the positions we want to copy for a given i ∈ [N ], which are usually determined by specific
258   tasks. Therefore, our analysis can be applied to general scenarios. We then make the following
259
      assumption:
260   Assumption 1. For the given sequence X = [xi ]N    i=1 , the following conditions holds:
261
262
             • For i ∈ [N ], cTi bpos(i) ≥ ρ where ρ ≥ δ and 0 ≤ |cTi bj | < δ for all j ∈
                                                                                         / Si .
263
             • For any i ∈ [N ], there exists j∈Si αj |cTi bj | < 1.

343   ing the ratio of correctly copied characters as accuracy. More experimental details can be found in
344   Appendix A.5.

346   and 135M, labeled as TF and Mamba) while fixing the string length. The result shows that while
347   both can perform the copy task finally, Transformer learns the task quickly whereas Mamba requires
348   nearly seven times the number of examples. For smaller models, on the one hand, we find that the
349   performance of Transformer is hardly affected when we halve the number of layers (63M, marked

351   layers (67M, labeled as Mamba-small-L), which causes Mamba to require more examples to achieve

      similar accuracy; (ii) reducing the hidden size (67M, labeled as Mamba-small-D), in such case
      Mamba learn the copy task even more slowly; (iii) reducing the hidden size while increasing
353
      layers (69M, labeled as Mamba-small-LD), in which case Mamba can not learn the task with 5.8 ×

      105 examples and the training process becomes unstable. Our results indicate that for the copy task,
355   the hidden size of Mamba seems more important than the number of layers at the same model size,

      For the center part of Figure 2, we change the maximum length Nmax while maintaining the model

382   5    T HE EXPRESSIVE POWER OF M AMBA EQUIPPED WITH C OT
383
384   Although Mamba may face certain bottlenecks when handling copy tasks, another interesting ques-
385
      tion is: when augmented with other techniques, such as Chain of Thought (CoT), will Mamba see an
      improvement in its capabilities? Now, we turn our attention to the expressive ability of Mamba
386
      equipped with CoT to solve dynamic programming (DP) problems. Specifically, following the
387
      setup by Feng et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024), a DP problem can be described by input sequences
388
      {s(1) , s(2) , . . . , s(N ) }, state space I, transition function fT , and aggregation function fA . Each of
389   component can be described as follows:
390
391   • Input sequences: We use {s(1) , s(2) , . . . , s(N ) } to denote the input of the sequences and the vector
                                             T
392   n = |s(1) |, |s(2) |, . . . , |s(N ) | to describe the scale of the problem, where |s(i) | denotes the length
393   of the i-th sequence.
394   • State Space: For a given DP problem, the state space In (and its size) will be determined based
395   on the problem size n. Each state i ∈ In corresponds to an intermediate value dp(i) that needs to
396   be computed, and i ≺ j means that state i needs to be solved before state j. There exists a function
397   fI : In → In to calculate the next state, that is, j = fI (i) if j is the next state to solve after i.
398
      • Transition function: The intermediate DP value can be calculated by the transition function
399
      fT as dp(i) = fT (n, s, {(j, dp(j)) : j ≺ i}) where s is the concatenation of the input tokens
400   which corresponds to all elements of all input sequences. Furthermore, this can be formulated as
401   dp(i) = fT (n, {sj : j ∈ Di }, {dp(k) : k ∈ Vdp(i) }) where Di and Vdp(i) are the sets of input
402   tokens indices and DP values needed to solve state i respectively.
403
      • Aggregation function: To produce the final answer, the aggregation function needs to collect the
404
      required intermediate DP values and calculate the final result, which can be formalized as anwser =
405
      fA ({dp(i) : i ∈ An }) where An is the set of DP values needed in the aggregation according to the
406   problem size n.
407
408   It should be noted that in the above definition, we use s(i) to denote the i-th input sequence and
409   si to denote the i-th input token, where s is all input tokens transformed from the concatenated
410   input sequences (s(1) , s(2) , . . . , s(N ) ). It can be referenced from Section 4.1 of Feng et al. (2024)
411
      for more detailed examples for DP problems. We consider the process by which the Mamba layer
      defined as Eq (2) gradually generates the solution to DP problems when using CoT. The format of
412
      the generated sequence can be written as:
413
414                  s(1) | s(2) | . . . | s(N ) | (i1 , dp(i1 )) . . . (i|In | , dp(i|In | )) final answer
415
      where the input sequence is separated using the symbol | as a delimiter.
416
417   Assumption 2. Given the input sequences s(1) , s(2) , . . . , s(N ) , we consider the following constraints
418   for the DP problem:
419
             • For any i ∈ In , there exists |Di | ≤ Ns , |Vdp(i) | ≤ Ndp and |A(n)| ≤ NA .
420
421          • The size of the state space |In |, all elements of input sequences (or equivalently, si for all
422            i ∈ [|s|] ), all intermediate DP values (dp(i) for any i ∈ In ), and the final answer can all
423            be polynomially upper bounded by the problem size n.
424
             • The functions used to solve the DP problem, including the function fI to determine the
425
               next state, the transition function fT , the aggregation function fA and A(n) can all be
426
               approximated with polynomial efficiency by a constant-size MLP (with the SiLU activation
427            function) (Feng et al., 2024).
428
429   The first constraint of the Assumption 2 illustrates that the number of input tokens and previous DP
430   values used in the transition function is upper bounded by Ns and Ndp respectively. In addition,
431   the number of DP values used in aggregation is at most NA . The second constraint allows that all
      involved inputs and outputs used in functions can be represented by the log-precision model. The

434   functions will be a constant related to {Ns , Ndp , NA }. Now we present our conclusion as follows:

      6    D ISCUSSION
477
478
      In this paper, inspired by the similarity between the SSM module in Mamba and linear attention,
479
      we explore Mamba’s potential bottlenecks in the COPY operation and show that Mamba with linear
480   size can complete it. Additionally, we present that Mamba has the same cost as standard or efficient
481   Transformers when solving DP problems using CoT. Our findings contribute to a deeper understand-
482   ing of Mamba. However, we would like to illustrate that while Mamba may slightly underperform
483   Transformers in certain tasks, it offers advantages in others like sparse parity learning(Park et al.,
484   2024) and can achieve comparable performance with lower costs(Gu & Dao, 2023). Therefore, as
485   shown in Park et al. (2024); Waleffe et al. (2024); Wen et al. (2024), exploring hybrid architectures
      and deeper theoretical analysis for them is a promising direction for future work.

1072   Theorem 8 (Perform m-locality DP problems with CoT). Consider any m-locality DP problem
1073   and given input sequences that satisfies Assumption 2, for any integer T ∈ N, there exists several
1074   Mamba layers with size O(m), such that the answer generated by the Mamba layers will be correct
1075   when the length of the answer is no more than T .

1077   Proof. The overall proof construction approach is similar to that of Theorem 3, with the only dif-
1078   ference being that under the assumption of m-locality, when performing the COPY operation, the
1079   constructed Mamba only needs to focus on at most m tokens preceding the current position. This
       results in the size of the Mamba layers only needing to be O(Ñ md).
