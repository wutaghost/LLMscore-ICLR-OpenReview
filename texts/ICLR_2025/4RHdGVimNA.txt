009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Standard decoding in a Transformer based language model is inherently sequen-
012            tial as we wait for a token’s embedding to pass through all the layers in the net-
013            work before starting the generation of the next token. In this work, we propose a
014            new architecture StagFormer (Staggered Transformer), which staggered execution
015            along the time axis and thereby enables parallelizing the decoding process along
016
               the depth of the model. We achieve this by breaking the dependency of the to-
               ken representation at time step i in layer l upon the representations of tokens until
017
               time step i from layer l − 1. Instead, we stagger the execution and only allow a
018
               dependency on token representations until time step i − 1. The later sections of
019            the Transformer still get access to the ”rich” representations from the prior section
020            but only from those token positions which are one time step behind. StagFormer
021            allows for different sections of the model to be executed in parallel yielding up
022            to 33% speedup in decoding while being quality neutral. We also explore many
023            natural variants of this idea. We present how weight-sharing across the different
024            sections being staggered can be more practical in settings with limited memory.
025            We show how one can approximate a recurrent model during inference using such
026            weight-sharing. We explore the efficacy of using a bounded window attention to
027
               pass information from one section to another which helps drive further latency
               gains for some applications. We also explore demonstrate the scalability of the
028
               staggering idea over more than 2 sections of the Transformer.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have seen tremendous success as the primary backbone for lan-
034   guage models (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020). The architecture
035   lends itself particularly well for causal language modeling by allowing efficient, highly parallelized
036   training over large datasets. Moreover, the model can be efficiently partitioned across multiple de-
037   vices (Pope et al., 2022) enabling model parallelism across machines. However, it is well known
038
      that, during inference, decoding from a Transformer is an inherently sequential task. This task be-
      comes more expensive when trying to decode long sequences due to the cost of attention, which
039
      scales linearly with respect to sequence length.
040
041   There have been numerous works which try to make inference more efficient in practice. Specu-
042   lative decoding, local attention and other efficient attention variants (Tay et al., 2022), KV cache
043   optimizations, blockwise parallel decoding (Stern et al., 2018) etc. are a few such works. However,
044
      there haven’t been many works which try to tackle the sequentiality imposed by the depth of the
      Transformer. Depth, while known to be essential for the strong performance of Transformers (Raf-
045
      fel et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024), introduces a proportional cost in terms of decoding
046
      latency.
047
048   In this work, we take a look at how we can introduce some level of parallel execution along the
049   depth axis of a Transformer language model while decoding.
050   We introduce StagFormer (Staggered Transformer), a novel Transformer variant which breaks the
051   sequential dependency of the upper layers on the lower layers by staggering the time dependency
052   of token embeddings passed from the lower layers to the upper layers. In particular, we present a
053   mechanism by which, at time step i, the upper layers of the model use the rich representations of
      tokens computed by earlier layers only until time step i − 1. Note that in a standard Transformer this

102   The Transformer was originally proposed in the seminal work of Vaswani et al. (2017). Decoder-
103   only language modeling using the Transformer was originally proposed by Radford (2018) and has
104
      since become a standard backbone to many frontier language models today.
105   There has been an enormous body of research dedicated towards making Transformer training or in-
106   ference more efficient (Tay et al., 2022). These involve approaches which focus on pre-training such
107   as distillation(Xu et al., 2024), layer stacking (Panigrahi et al., 2024), Alternating-updates (Baykal
      et al., 2024), Matryoshka Transformer (Kusupati et al., 2022) among others. Quantization (Xiao

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023) has been another widely successful approach at speeding up inference of language
109   models. There have been other approaches specifically focused on improving the decoding speed
110   from language models such as speculative decoding and related works (Leviathan et al., 2023; Sun
111   et al., 2024; Santilli et al., 2023).
112
      There has also been a huge body of work focusing on making the self-attention more efficient. Some
113
      of these works have introduced the idea of introducing a form of recurrence mechanism into models,
114
      such as Transformer-XL and State Space Models (SSMs) like Mamba (Dai et al., 2019; Gu et al.,
115   2022; Gu & Dao, 2024). Block-Recurrent Transformers use cross-attention to introduce a per-layer
116   recurrence mechanism into Transformer networks (Hutchins et al., 2022).
117
118
      More closely related to our effort are works such as Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) which uses parallel
      heads to decode multiple tokens ahead at once, Staircase Attention (Ju et al., 2022) which uses
119
      a similar idea of staggering attention window context as we advance deeper into the Transformer
120
      stack. However, they mainly explore a variant of the idea which allows one to bring in the benefits
121   of RNNs rather than efficiency gains, our main focus here.
122
123   Our shared-weight variant of StagFormer is closely related to the idea of a looped Transformer,
124
      where the hidden activation signals are sent through the layers of the network multiple times (De-
      hghani et al., 2018; Giannou et al., 2023; Gatmiry et al., 2024). Part of the intuition behind looping
125
      is that the lower layers of a network can reuse the more-information-rich activations from layers
126
      later in the same network in the next iteration of the loop to create higher quality representations. A
127   key difference in our method from looping is that it breaks the strict data-dependency on each prior
128   loop, allowing for parallel execution of different passes through the network.

      a brief background on a decoder-only language models based on the standard Transformer, the
134
      backbone for most state-of-the-art language models today.

136
      Language Modeling with the Transformer A Transformer of depth ` is a sequence-to-sequence
137
      model which takes in a token sequence of length N and generates an output sequence of length
138   N . The tokens are each first mapped to a d-dimensional representation using an embedding layer.
139   Positional information may also be combined into the embedding at this stage. Denote the token em-
140   beddings so obtained by t1,...,N
                               0       . Then, these representations are progressively modified

143   j ∈ {1, . . . , `}. Each layer Lj consists of two main operations: (a) self-attention which combines
144   information across the different token embeddings and (b) a feed-forward network which modifies
145
      each individual token embedding. The two main operations are applied along with residual connec-
      tions and layer normalization. There may additionally be a position encoding incorporated into the
146
      embedding during self-attention stage as well.
147
148   To use Transformers as decoder-only language models, a popular paradigm is that of causal language
149   modeling. Given a train dataset of examples each of which is a sequence of tokens of length N ,
150   causal language modeling simultaneously trains to minimize N loss terms on each sequence. These
151
      loss terms minimize the cross-entropy loss of the model’s prediction for token ti using the prefix
      t1,...,i−1 . During training, all N of these loss terms can be evaluated in parallel with the use of causal
152
      masking. During decoding, the model iteratively generates one new token at a time by passing token
153
      ti through the ` layers sequentially to obtain ti+1 . This means that growing the network depth
154   incurs a linear cost on the time taken to decode the next token during inference. However, there
155   is ample evidence that depth is crucial for good quality models (Devlin et al., 2019; Raffel et al.,
156   2023). There is fundamentally no way to avoid this cost in a Transformer, since every token relies
157   on the completed predictions of every other prior token.
158

160   Transformer network and still be able to perform efficient and performant causal language modeling.

      focus on the simplest case p = 2. Let h = b`/2c. StagFormer allows for execution of the stacks

      3.1   S HARED -W EIGHTS S TAG F ORMER
255
      In scenarios where we are bound tightly on memory requirements, one can use a variant where we
256   share weights across the different stacks being staggered. Such weight sharing lowers the quality
257   of the model but can save significantly on memory requirements and can be more applicable in
258   memory-constrained settings. Here we use the same weights for self-attention and feed-forward
259   layers for both the passes. The cross-attention weights are the only unique weights for the second
260   pass. So for some input ti0 , we would apply L1 , . . . , L` twice. The first pass processes the input as a
261   standard Transformer network, alternating self-attention and feed-forward layers. The second pass
262   introduces cross-attention layers which allow each token to attend to the final activations of all prior
263   tokens, t1L , . . . , ti−1
                             L .
264   During inference, we can have the networks execute the two passes in parallel. This is because,
265   like separate-weights StagFormer, the second pass only depends on the final activations of prior
266   tokens and both passes operate on the same input. The results with shared weights StagFormer
267   are presented in Table ??. We would like to remark that a 2 stack shared-weight StagFormer with
268   each stack having 18 layers performs significantly better than a 18 layer baseline model which
269   has a similar number of parameters. Therefore, StagFormer is an effective way of boosting the
      performance given a parameter budget.

276   3.2   S HARED -W EIGHTS S TAG F ORMER A PPROXIMATES A R ECURRENT M ODEL
277
278   One method we explore for decoding with shared-weights StagFormer is to use the cross-attention
279   to the final activations of prior tokens as a recurrence mechanism. Rather than having the network
280   process each token twice in parallel, with only the second pass using cross-attention, we only have
281   the network operate on each input during decoding once. When doing so, the network cross-attends
282
      to the final activations of all prior tokens.
283   This method of decoding resembles a recurrent neural network (RNN) where the final activations
284   of prior tokens are the RNN’s hidden state and cross-attention serves as a gating mechanism while
285   processing the current token.
286   We show that it is possible to use shared-weights StagFormer for recurrent decoding using this
287   scheme, even when the model is trained using two separate passes. However, we find that the
288   generated text’s quality is not as good as when we process decode new tokens the original way, with
289   two networks running in parallel.

      3.3   S TAG F ORMER WITH L OCAL C ROSS -ATTENTION
325
326   If we want stronger latency savings and are willing to take a slight quality hit, a further optimization
327   for StagFormer that is simple to implement is to use local attention for the cross-attention between
328   passes (Beltagy et al., 2020). We observe that StagFormer still performs well even when using local
329   cross-attention with relatively small attention window sizes. StagFormer is also capable of giving
330   non-trivial quality when using an attention window size of 1, which converts the application of the
331   cross-attention in layer Lj on token tij−1 to a linear function of ti−1
                                                                          h   (recall h ≡ b`/2c).
332   Section 4.3 shows the impact of using local attention with window sizes 512, 128, and 1 on Stag-
333   Former’s performance on pretraining perplexity and downstream tasks. We show local attention can
334   be used successfully with both the separate-weights and shared-weights variants.

      3.4   S TAG F ORMER WITH M ORE T HAN T WO S TACKS
337
338   A natural extension of StagFormer idea we had touched upon earlier is to have h be less than b`/2c
339   and to stagger over more than 2 stacks through the network. For instance, we could have h ≡ b`/3c
340   and stagger the network over 3 stacks. Let p be the number of stacks we stagger the network over,
341   then h ≡ b`/pc. Intuitively, as we increase the number of stacks p, due to progressive staggering, at
342   time step i stack s only gets to see tokens until time step i − p + s but needs to produce activations
343   which help predict token i + 1. Thus the job becomes more difficult to learn as p increases, and the
344   depth of each stack reduces which contributes to eventual degradation in quality.
345   Our experiments indeed find that model quality suffers when p > 2. However, we find that we can
346   recover significantly by imploring a simple change for StagFormer when p > 2. Rather than using
347   just the final stack’s output for computing the final logits, we use a linear combination of each stack’s
348   output with learnable coefficients, α1 , . . . , αp . Algorithm 4 defines separate-weights StagFormer for
349   when p > 2 in the Appendix.
350   Our experiments ablate the linear combination at the end of separate-weights StagFormer when
351   p > 2 to demonstrate its effectiveness. Our results are summarized in Section 4.4. We find that
352   as we increase p model quality suffers, but we are able to recover some of the lost performance by
353   using a linear combination of each stack’s output. We explored the settings of p = 3, 4 here, but
354   there might be ways to extend the approach effectively to even larger values of p which we leave for
355   future work.
356
357   Shared-Weights StagFormer with More Than Two Passes One can also increase the number of
358   staggered passes with shared-weights StagFormer. Since the Transformer layer weights are shared
359   between passes, shared-weights StagFormer would process the same input multiple times, cross-
360   attending to prior tokens’ final activations from prior passes. We find that doing so increases model
361
      quality, even without using the linear combination of outputs that separate-weights StagFormer uses

      5       C ONCLUSION
474
475
      We present the StagFormer architecture as a way to increase the capacity of transformer models
476
      by allowing lower-level layers to attend to the final activations produced by the same or different
477   networks. With separate-weights StagFormer, we demonstrate that we can use higher level rep-
478   resentations of prior tokens to run data-independent transformer layers in parallel to process the
479   current token without sacrificing quality.
480
481   5.1     F UTURE WORK AND LIMITATIONS
482
483   There are many aspects of the StagFormer architecture that are not well understood and requires
484   future research. For example, training shared-weights StagFormer only approximates recurrent in-
485   ference, since training requires a discrete number of passes. Furthermore, using shared-weights
      with more than 2 passes does not alleviate this issue. Future work could explore how to extend the


                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      StagFormer algorithm that either better approximates or fully realizes recurrent decoding with better
487   quality.
488
489   We also find that when we increase the number of stacks to more than two when using separate-
490
      weights StagFormer that the model’s performance starts to degrade. Our experiment shows using a
      linear combination of the stacks’ output helps the model recover a significant amount of quality, but
491
      not enough to match the fully sequential baseline with the equivalent number of layers. Later works
492
      could investigate whether it is possible to realize separate-weights StagFormer when p > 2 in order
493   to further parallelize the execution of Transformer-based networks.
494
495   Another limitation is that cross-attention incurs additional quadratic computational cost in both time
496
      and space with respect to the input length. One way this work attempts to alleviate this additional
      cost is to use local cross-attention to stagger decoding between stacks. We show that it is possible to
497
      use the 512 window size, approximately fifty percent of the original context length, and suffer neg-
498
      ligible quality loss and even some improvements in downstream performance. However, we show
499   that when the window size is decreased the performance of the StagFormerm model degrades. When
500   the local cross-attention window is 1, cross-attention is linear with respect to input length instead of
501   quadratic; however, the model quality suffers when the attention window size is restricted to such a
502   small window. Other works can explore ways to reuse information-rich higher level activations in
503   lower-level layers to allow parallel execution of layers in a way that incurs less computational cost
504   than attention and matches a deeper model’s quality.
505   One material limitation of StagFormer’s parallel execution of layers is that it would require non-
506   trivial communication cost to copy the result from one network over to the other. This prevents
507   one from realizing the full theoretical latency benefit of running the StagFormer towers in parallel.
508   Furthermore, since most models rely on the single program, multiple data (SPMD) paradigm (Xu
509   et al., 2021), parallel execution of StagFormer stacks would require storing a copy of the token em-
510   beddings and final softmax tables in both cores when executing StagFormer stacks. Further work
511   could explore how to extend this algorithm to help realize greater latency benefits when executing
512   Transformer networks in parallel.
513
514   5.2   B ROADER IMPACT
515
      Transformer networks have mainly been used under the assumption that the execution of transformer
516
      layers must be done serially. StagFormer shows that it is possible to further parallelize execution of
517
      large language models by execution stacks of transformer layers in parallel and match the quality of
518   a deeper model. StagFormer could help reduce the throughput latency of Transformer-based models,
519   which allows these to be served at a lower cost. Efforts to lower the cost of deploying Transformer-
520   based models has a large ecological and economic impact, since the amount of resources to deploy
521   modern language models has become increasingly substantial.
