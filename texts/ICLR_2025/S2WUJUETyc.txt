011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS) is an emerging technology that transforms
014            standard optical fibers into dense arrays of acoustic sensors, offering unprece-
015
               dented opportunities for smart city applications, indoor monitoring of human ac-
               tivity, and surveillance without compromising privacy. In this paper, we integrate
016
               DAS with Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for indoor sound source
017
               localization. By embedding the acoustic wave equation and impedance bound-
018            ary conditions into the neural network architecture, we exploit physical laws to
019            guide the learning process, improving accuracy and generalization. We propose
020            two strategies for real-time sound source localization using DAS data. The first
021            strategy involves training the PINN on all available data simultaneously, while the
022            second strategy incrementally feeds data over time, simulating real-time data ac-
023            quisition. Using real indoor DAS measurements, we demonstrate the effectiveness
024            of our approach in deciphering complex room acoustics and accurately inferring
025            sound source locations under both strategies. Our framework provides a novel
026
               solution for real-time indoor positioning and human activity surveillance, offer-
               ing significant advantages over traditional camera-based systems by preserving
027
               individual privacy.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS) is an emerging technology that transforms standard optical
033   fibers into dense arrays of acoustic sensors, offering unprecedented opportunities for environmental
034   monitoring, urban infrastructure analysis, and indoor activity surveillance in smart cities (Lind-
035   sey & Martin, 2021; Li et al., 2022). By capturing acoustic signals along the length of optical
036   fibers, DAS enables the detection and localization of acoustic events without the need for traditional
037   sensor networks, providing a cost-effective and privacy-preserving solution for monitoring appli-
038   cations. By “privacy-preserving,” we mean that optical fibers can detect human activities, such as
039
      movement trajectories, without being able to intelligently interpret human speech due to the lim-
      ited frequency band. Beyond urban environments, DAS technology has demonstrated its versatility
040
      in various fields, including ultra-long-distance power cable monitoring without inline amplification
041
      (Cedilnik & Lees, 2019), volcanic event detection and imaging of near-surface volcanic structures
042   (Jousset et al., 2020), and underwater seismic research transforming telecommunication cables into
043   seismo-acoustic sensors (Lior et al., 2021). These advancements highlight the potential of DAS in
044   improving infrastructure monitoring, hazard assessment, and contributing to earthquake early warn-
045   ing systems. In this work, we focus on leveraging DAS for indoor activity surveillance, aiming to
046   detect and localize human movements within smart city environments. By integrating DAS with a
047   physics-informed learning framework, we reduce the dependence on labeled data and enhance the
048   generalization capabilities of our model, providing valuable insights while preserving privacy.
049   Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have gained attention as a powerful computational
050   framework for solving forward and inverse problems governed by partial differential equations
051   (PDEs) (Raissi et al., 2019). By embedding physical laws directly into the neural network training
052   process, PINNs leverage both data and governing equations to model complex phenomena without
053   requiring large labeled datasets or computational meshes. This approach has been successfully ap-
      plied in various fields, including fluid dynamics, heat transfer, and wave propagation. Subsequent

054
      research has introduced various modifications to PINNs to enhance their accuracy, efficiency, and
055   scalability (Lu et al., 2021; Cuomo et al., 2022). Variational hp-VPINN extends the PINN frame-
056   work by incorporating domain decomposition methods, allowing for more efficient and accurate
057   solutions, especially for problems with complex geometries or high-dimensional PDEs (Kharazmi
058   et al., 2021). Conservative PINN (CPINN) focuses on preserving the conservation laws inherent
059   in certain PDEs, ensuring that the learned solutions respect these fundamental physical principles,
060   which is crucial for applications involving conservation of mass, momentum, or energy (Jagtap et al.,
061   2020). Each of these variants aims to enhance the versatility and robustness of PINNs, making them
062   more suitable for a wider range of scientific computing problems. There are a couple of drawbacks
063
      for many PINNs with soft constraints for boundary conditions (BCs)BCs and initial conditions (ICs)
      ICs. The selection of weights and samples for BCs and ICs cannot certainly be determined and re-
064
      quires many trial-and-error tests. Even when the loss function is minimized, the BCs and ICs are
065
      not strictly satisfied. A recent analysis of PINNs has revealed potential failure modes, particularly
066   when dealing with complex problems involving convection, reaction, and diffusion operators (Kr-
067   ishnapriyan et al., 2021). To target the scaling problems of general PDEs and take advantage of
068   parallel computing, XPINNs and FBPINNs have been developed based on domain decomposition
069   methods (Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020; Shukla et al., 2021; Moseley et al., 2023).
070
      In the context of acoustics, PINNs offer the potential to model sound wave propagation and solve
071
      inverse problems such as source localization. Previous studies have explored the use of PINNs for
072   solving the Helmholtz equation and other wave equations, demonstrating the capability of PINNs to
073   capture wave phenomena (Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020; Shukla et al., 2021; Moseley et al., 2023).
074   However, challenges persist in applying PINNs to real-world acoustic problems, including:
075
076         • Sensitivity to Hyperparameters: The performance of PINNs is highly sensitive to the choice
077           of hyperparameters, such as the weights assigned to different loss terms, which can affect
078
              convergence and accuracy.
079         • Difficulty in Enforcing Boundary and Initial Conditions: When boundary and initial con-
080           ditions are imposed as soft constraints in the loss function, PINNs may struggle to satisfy
081
              these conditions accurately, leading to suboptimal solutions.
082         • Scalability Issues: Scaling PINNs to complex or large-scale domains remains challenging
083           due to computational constraints and optimization difficulties.
084
      To address these challenges, various modifications to the PINN framework have been proposed.
085   Extended PINNs (XPINNs) and Finite Basis PINNs (FBPINNs) utilize domain decomposition and
086   parallel computing to enhance scalability and efficiency (Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020; Shukla et al.,
087   2021; Moseley et al., 2023). These approaches aim to improve training performance and enable the
088   application of PINNs to more complex problems.
089
090   Contribution In this paper, we integrate DAS technology with PINNs to develop a framework for
091   indoor sound source localization and spatiotemporal wave field recovery. Our main contributions
092   are:
093
            • We propose a PINN framework tailored for modeling room acoustics, incorporating the
094           acoustic wave equation and impedance boundary conditions to guide the learning process.
095
            • We demonstrate the simultaneous localization of sound sources and recovery of the spa-
096
              tiotemporal acoustic field using real DAS measurements, highlighting the effectiveness of
097           our approach in complex indoor environments.
098
            • We explore the effects of different training strategies—including incremental data feed-
099
              ing—and fiber array configurations on localization accuracy, providing insights into opti-
100
              mal system design and deployment.
101
102   Related Work Recent advancements have seen the application of PINNs to various wave-related
103   phenomena. For instance, Moseley et al. (2023) employed PINNs for solving wave equations in
104   heterogeneous media, while Mao et al. (2020) addressed high-speed flows and shock wave modeling
105   using PINNs. In acoustics, Olivieri et al. (2024) utilized PINNs for sound field reconstruction but
106   did not consider impedance boundary conditions. Furthermore, the work by Borrel-Jensen et al.
107   (2024) presents a novel approach to sound propagation in realistic interactive 3D scenes using deep
      neural operators, which is particularly relevant to the challenges faced in virtual acoustics.

      In terms of source localization, traditional methods often rely on time-of-arrival or beamforming
109   techniques, which require extensive sensor networks and can be computationally intensive (Dibiase,

      2   DATA FROM D ISTRIBUTED ACOUSTIC S ENSING
121
      Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS) leverages the sensitivity of optical fibers to external perturba-
122
      tions to detect acoustic signals along the length of the fiber. When a laser pulse is sent through
123   an optical fiber, imperfections and variations within the fiber cause backscattering of light through
124   Rayleigh scattering. External acoustic vibrations induce strain in the fiber, leading to changes in the
125   backscattered light intensity, phase, or polarization, which can be detected and related to the external
126   disturbances (Hartog, 2017).
127
      In this study, we strategically deployed a 2-kilometer-long optical fiber network within a five-story
128
      building located on the campus of Tsinghua University (Figure 1). The network spans each level
129   of the structure, ranging from the roof, at an elevation of 17.45 m, down to the basement at −3.5 m
130   below ground level. A segment of the fiber optic cable, extending 400 m in length, was buried
131   at a depth of approximately 0.5 m in the building’s adjacent yard. This strategic placement facili-
132   tates comprehensive four-dimensional (4D) spatio-temporal monitoring of the building’s structural
133   integrity and dynamic responses.
134
      To achieve high-resolution spatio-temporal monitoring, the DAS system was configured with a
135   gauge length of 3.2 m and a spatial resolution of 1.6 m, effectively creating a network of 1, 250
136   distinct monitoring channels. This setup ensures high-fidelity capture of structural dynamics, with a
137   data acquisition rate of 1, 000 Hz. The precise positioning of each channel was determined through
138   a tap test experiment, further validated against the architectural floor plans of the building.
139
      The fiber layout and channel distribution are depicted in Figure 1, illustrating the extensive coverage
140
      achieved within the building. The developed PINN framework, integrating the governing acoustic
141   wave equations with the DAS strain measurements, provides a powerful tool for interpreting real
142   DAS data. By embedding physical laws into the neural network, we reduce the dependence on
143   extensive labeled datasets and enhance the generalization capabilities of the model.
144
      Developing a PINN framework to decipher DAS data collected from the building, we can recon-
145
      struct the spatio-temporal acoustic pressure fields and accurately localize sound sources within the
146
      structure. This capability is crucial for monitoring human activities, detecting structural anomalies,
147   and enhancing surveillance without compromising privacy.

515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   The synthetic tests conducted in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 are designed to emulate the conditions of
518   real-world DAS data acquisition in building rooms. By modeling acoustic wave propagation and
519   interaction with boundaries in controlled environments, we establish a foundation for interpreting
520
      the complex wavefields captured by the DAS system in the building.
521   The synthetic tests demonstrate that the PINN framework can effectively localize sound sources
522   using DAS measurements. The accuracy depends on the spatial distribution of data points. Incor-
523   porating the source location as trainable parameters allows simultaneous inversion of the source
524   position and pressure field.
525   Beyond synthetic data, we applied our method to real DAS recordings of a person entering a room,
526   treating human footsteps as a moving target. Using the Incremental Time-Stepping Training Strategy
527   (Strategy 2) and enforcing the constraint z0 = 0, we successfully located the moving target within
528   a 15 m by 15 m room equipped with 28 DAS channels. The target’s location converged using
529   only a couple of early DAS recordings, demonstrating the practical applicability of our approach to
530   real-world scenarios.
531   Our synthetic experiments , both synthetic and real, validate the proposed PINN framework’s capa-
532   bility to accurately localize sound sources and reconstruct pressure fields using DAS data. The
533   results highlight the potential of integrating PINNs with DAS technology for advanced indoor
534   sensing applications. This novel framework simultaneously constructs the acoustic wavefield and
535   performs inversion without requiring labeled data. This capability highlights the potential of inte-
536   grating PINNs with DAS technology for advanced indoor sensing applications, such as real-time
537   tracking of moving targets.

      A.1   R ECONSTRUCTED WAVEFIELDS
651
652   We compared the reconstructed wavefields using two training strategies. As shown in Figure 8,
653   Strategy 1 (Simultaneous Training with All Data) performs better for wavefield reconstruction. The
654   reconstructed wavefields closely resemble the true wavefields across all time steps, accurately cap-
655   turing wave propagation and reflections; however, the location is accurate. In contrast, Strategy
656   2 (Incremental Time-Stepping Training Strategy) performs better for localization using data from
657
      early time steps. By focusing on early time snapshots, Strategy 2 enables rapid convergence of the
      source location parameters. However, the wavefield reconstruction quality is somewhat compro-
658
      mised compared to Strategy 1, especially at later time steps, due to the limited temporal information
659
      used during training. When choosing between the two strategies, these observations highlight a
660   trade-off between accurate source localization and comprehensive wavefield reconstruction.

665   we present convergence plots for both synthetic and real data cases. These plots illustrate how the

      Figures 9 show the convergence curves for the 2D and 3D synthetic data cases, respectively. We

670   physical equations and the boundary and initial conditions. The DAS loss, associated with the data
671   fitting at sensor locations, decreases significantly, demonstrating the model’s capability to fit the
672   observed data. The source location estimates converge toward the true source location over epochs,
673   confirming the effectiveness of the PINN framework in accurately localizing the sound source.
