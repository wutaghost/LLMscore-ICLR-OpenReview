009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid expansion in the scale of large language models (LLMs), enabling
012            efficient distributed inference across multiple computing units has become in-
013            creasingly critical. However, communication overheads from frequent synchro-
014            nization during distributed inference pose a significant challenge to achieve scal-
015            ability and low latency. Therefore, we introduce a novel optimization technique,
016
               Sync-Point Drop (SPD) to reduce communication overheads in tensor parallelism
               by dropping synchronization on attention outputs. In detail, we first propose a
017
               block design that allows execution to proceed without communication through
018
               SPD. Second, we identify regions of communication redundancy, where dropping
019            synchronization results in no loss of model performance. In addition, to extend
020            SPD across all compute blocks, we employ a low-cost distillation, specifically
021            targeting blocks giving quality degradation, to maximize accuracy recovery. For
022            extreme blocks where performance degradation is severe, we introduce a new head
023            grouping enhancements to amplify the distillation’s recovery effect. The proposed
024            methods effectively alleviate communication bottlenecks while minimizing accu-
025            racy degradation during LLM inference, offering a scalable solution for distributed
026            environments.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Gunter et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2020; Bubeck et al., 2023; Tou-
031   vron et al., 2023a;b; Zhang et al., 2022; Penedo et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) have revolutionized
032   the field of natural language processing (NLP), driving significant advancements in a wide range
033   of applications, from machine translation and sentiment analysis to question answering and content
034   generation. Their ability to understand and generate human-like text has opened new possibilities for
035   both research and practical use. However, as these models grow in size and complexity, optimizing
036   their performance becomes a crucial challenge, particularly in terms of latency.
037   One of the popular ways to offer low latency is to run LLM inference in distributed computing
038   environments, notably using Tensor Parallelism (TP) (Shoeybi et al., 2019). TP enables distributed
039   computations by sharding tensor operations into separated tracks or blocks that are then processed
040   on parallel devices simultaneously. As a direct benefit, TP allows us to leverage device’s quantity
041   against on-board memory per-device, which is a good way to improve hardware utilization and
042   accelerate inference for large scale models (Further discussion can be found in Section 3).
043   However, to maintain mathematical parity as on single-device inference, TP requires collective com-
044   munication, or sync-points, throughout the model — these are communication barriers across all
045   parallel devices to synchronize hidden representation tensors (the process is illustrated in Figure
046   1a). Because of its communicative nature, the overhead of sync-point is subjected to hardware sys-
047   tems, i.e. interconnect between devices, network connections between nodes, which can become a
048   bottleneck on execution. Also, sync-points may be critical failure points in any distributed systems.
049   As LLMs grow in size, one needs to use more compute devices, which necessitates more sync-points
050
      and further worsens system stability and inference latency. Therefore, optimizing sync-point would
      greatly improve the overall inference latency and system utilization.
051
052   Therefore, in this work, we propose Sync-Point Drop (SPD) a simple yet novel optimization tech-
053   nique with broad applications. Unlike the existing works which tried to improve the communication
      process itself (NVIDIA, 2019b; Jeaugey, 2019; Cheng et al., 2023) on system-level, SPD directly

      removes sync-point on self attention output (as in Figure 1b) within the target budget. To enable
055   SPD directly on decoder block, we first present a block design for SPD which minimizes the neg-

      large quality degradation even with block-to-block distillation, we introduce a novel SPD aware

082   2   R ELATED W ORKS
083
084   The inefficiency of large language models, which emerged with significant impact, has led to the
085   development of numerous optimization techniques. Diverse techniques, i.e. quantization, pruning,
086   emerged on model-level optimization with enormous redundancy of large language model. Quanti-
087
      zation (Frantar et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Chee et al., 2023;
      Ashkboos et al., 2024) reduces the precision of model parameters, allowing for faster computations
088
      with minimal impact on performance. Pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Liu
089
      et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024) eliminates less critical parameters or neurons from the model, thereby
090   reducing its size and computational complexity. Furthermore, in the aggressive scale of pruning,
091   block skipping (Xia et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024), which involves bypassing certain blocks dur-
092   ing inference based on block characteristics, further enhances efficiency by decreasing the number
093   of operations aggressively required for prediction. This model-level optimizations are mostly fo-
094   cusing on compressive and computational effect that makes them more suitable for real-time and
095   resource-constrained environments without sacrificing accuracy.
096   Also, due to the large amount of computation and memory occupation of the large language model,
097   system-level optimizations (Shoeybi et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2023; Aminabadi
098   et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023) are explored for deployment. Different with model-level opti-
099   mization, system-level optimization does not change any numerical values of a model. One of the
100   distributed deployment technique, tensor parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019), enables fast serving of a
101   model by parallel execution of a block into multiple devices. However, this technique requires large
102   communication overhead between devices to keep numeric precision of execution flow. Considering
103   the communication bottleneck of tensor parallelism, existing work also focus on improving the com-
104
      munication operation itself systematically, including ring-topology all-reduce (NVIDIA, 2019b) and
      tree-topology all-reduce (Jeaugey, 2019). Specifically for large models, ATP (Cheng et al., 2023)
105
      improves training efficiency by dynamically choosing the best parallel strategy.
106
107   In this paper, we achieve optimization benefits from the system perspective by leveraging model-
      level optimization (enabling SPD in the system with minimizing accuracy degradation in the model).

      Tensor parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019) is a systematic computing technique on distributed envi-
      ronment used to accelerate large-scale language models. This is realized by partitioning individual
124
      weight tensors of a model across multiple devices. Instead of replicating entire model across GPUs
125
      (as in data parallelism), tensor parallelism (TP) divides each block’s computation across multiple
126   devices (as shown in Figure 1a), enabling the model to handle larger tensors that would otherwise
127   exceed the memory capacity of a single GPU. This approach significantly improves the scalability
128   and efficiency in both training and inference, particularly in large language models. However, real-
129   ization of effective TP requires collective communication (‘all-reduce’ in Figure 1) between devices
130   to synchronize and exchange partial computations, typically achieved via specialized interconnects
131   such as NVLink and NVSwitch (NVIDIA, 2019a). To reduce communication overhead, careful co-
132   ordination between computation and communication is crucial. In this paper, we introduce a novel
133   method Sync-Point Drop (SPD) to eliminate the communication within each decoder block, aiming
134
      to alleviate communication bottlenecks during distributed inference.

153   4     S YNC - POINT DROP FOR EFFICIENT TENSOR PARALLELISM
154
155
      Sync-point drop (SPD) simply removes the ‘all-reduce’ communication after self attention output
156   as shown in Figure 1b. While the lack of communication harms numerical parity across all parallel
157   devices, if the application is handled properly, it can be well applied with less quality degradation of
158   the model. In this section, we propose several methods to enable keeping high quality model with
159   lowering the communication overhead by SPD. First, in order to apply SPD, we introduce a novel
160   block structure design that serves as the foundation block for the non-communicating structure with
161   minimal quality degradation. Second, we propose proper strategy of applying SPD in a block-wise
      manner which can achieve benefits of low latency with minimizing the accuracy degradation.

      4.1     B LOCK DESIGN
178
179   If the synchronization of the self attention output is disrupted, the attention output will be divided
180   into multiple outputs corresponding to the number of devices. This leads to design issues in two
181   aspects of the transformer decoder block: the MLP input and the MLP output. Furthermore, these
182   design issues vary depending on whether the output projection layer of self attention includes a bias
183   term or not. In this section, we present a block design for SPD that minimizes information losses
184   from lack of communication which lead to the accuracy degradation.
185
186   4.1.1      D ECODER BLOCK WITHOUT BIAS IN LINEAR LAYER
187
188
      Figure 2a shows the SPD block design used without bias in linear layer. The most essential ob-
      jective of block design is constructing the combination of connections which gives least numerical
189
      difference between TP and SPD.
190
191   MLP input The sync-point enables each parallel devices to capture the attention output from all
192   the other devices. However, when the outputs from other devices are unavailable by elimination of
193   sync-point, the only information the device (i) can utilize is its own attention output (Yi ). Therefore,
194
      to minimize the numerical difference compared to the case of all information available, residual
      connection (X) and attention output of own device (Yi ) are added and fed into MLP input (X + Yi ).
195
196   MLP output When the sync-point exists after attention output, the MLP input is utilized as residual
197   connection added to MLP output. However, dropping the sync-point yields incomplete MLP input
198   (X + Yi ) with lack of attention output from other devices.PThe desired block output is combination
199   ofPblock input (X), attention output from all devices ( i Yi ) and MLP output from all devices
200
      ( i Zi ). Therefore, we disassemble the original residual connection to block input residual (X) and
      attention output residual from a device (Yi ). Then, Yi forms a new type of residual connection which
201
      is added before sync operation. X is added on the same point asPoriginalP   connection, after the sync
202
      operation which finally leads to complete form of output (X + i Yi + i Zi ).
203
204
      4.1.2      D ECODER BLOCK WITH BIAS IN LINEAR LAYER
205
206   In TP, each of the linear layers in self attention part of a block is parallelized in a different manner.
207   The linear layers before self attention operation (query, key and value projection) are divided in a
208   column-wise manner which enables the bias divided along same dimension. However, the linear
209   layer after self attention operation (output projection) is parallelized in an orthogonal way, row-wise
210   manner. The bias, a vector along the column dimension, therefore, can not be divided in the direction
211   of the row. This requires new mechanism of the bias application on MLP input and output as shown
212
      in Figure 2b.
213   MLP input The difference from Section 4.1.1 is that indecomposable bias term (b) is included after
214   weight multiplication. Following the most essential objective, least error in the MLP input compared
215   to the result after communication, we use the partial weight multiplication result with the addition
      of bias (Yi = Pi + b) and input residual connection (X) as MLP input (X + Pi + b).

      4.2     S YNC - POINT DROP BASED ON BLOCK - WISE SENSITIVITY
246
      While the lack of communication harms numerical parity across all parallel devices, we found that
247   some blocks aren’t affected a lot from SPD based on the designed block structure in Section 4.1. In
248   fact, there is varying sensitivity to SPD depending on which block it is. In our proposed approach,
249   we categorize transformer blocks based on their sensitivity: in-sensitive blocks, sensitive blocks
250   and extremely sensitive blocks. This classification enables us to tailor our strategies on each block
251   effectively, optimizing the inference processes block-by-block with less accuracy degradation.
252
253   4.2.1    B LOCK - WISE SYNC SENSITIVITY IDENTIFICATION
254
255
      Figure 3 shows the overall sync sensitivity identification algorithm flow of a model in the parallel
      system setting. To identify the sensitivity of a block to SPD, we utilize perplexity metric by measur-
256
      ing relative impact of a block to performance (the difference between TP block and SPD block in
257
      Figure 3) as sensitivity measurement. For example, when we measure the sensitivity of i-th block to
258   SPD, we apply SPD to all blocks starting from the {i + 1}-th block to the final block and measure
259   the perplexity, while leaving the i-th block unchanged. Then we measure the perplexity by addi-
260   tionally modifying the system setting of i-th block to SPD. The difference in perplexity before and
261   after applying SPD to i-th block is used as a measure of sensitivity. Here, we use calibration data ob-
262   tained by sampling a small portion from the large training dataset. By progressive replacement of TP
263   block to SPD block and measurement of quality degradation as relative perplexity difference, we can
264   compare the sensitivity between blocks in entire model and classify the blocks to three sensitivity
265   categories (in-sensitive blocks, sensitive blocks and extremely sensitive blocks).
266   Algorithm 1 shows the overall process of properly applying SPD in a block-wise manner with sync
267   sensitivity. Based on the measured sensitivity value of blocks (S), we rank the blocks in an ascend-
268   ing order (B). Following this ranking of the sensitivity of each blocks, we apply SPD sequentially
269   within the target number of blocks to optimize (Nspd ). In the sequence, the processing of a block
      is classified based on a predefined threshold criterion (τ1 and τ2 ). This makes the blocks classified
