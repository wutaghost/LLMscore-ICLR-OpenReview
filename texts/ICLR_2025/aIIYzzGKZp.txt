011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
                  We study 14 large language models (LLMs) fine-tuned for chat and find that
014               their maximum softmax probabilities (MSPs) are consistently miscalibrated on
015               multiple-choice Q&A. However, those MSPs might still encode useful uncertainty
016               information. Specifically, we hypothesized that wrong answers would be associ-
017               ated with smaller MSPs compared to correct answers. Via rigororous statistical
018               testing, we show that this hypothesis holds for models which perform well on
019               the underlying Q&A task. We also find a strong direction correlation between
020               Q&A accuracy and MSP correctness prediction, while finding no correlation be-
021               tween Q&A accuracy and calibration error. This suggests that within the current
022
                  fine-tuning paradigm, we can expect correctness prediction but not calibration to
                  improve as LLM capabilities progress. To demonstrate the utility of correctness
023
                  prediction, we show that when models have the option to abstain, performance
024
                  can be improved by selectively abstaining based on the MSP of the initial model
025               response, using only a small amount of labeled data to choose the MSP threshold.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated profound capabilities in many domains, but
031   continue to sometimes generate plausible-sounding false responses (Huang et al., 2023). In one
032   high-profile case, an LLM-based system invented a litany of nonexistent court cases, leading to
033   formal sanctions for two lawyers (Mangan, 2023). Although ongoing work has reduced the rate of
034   these mistakes,1 LLMs will inevitably face situations that surpass the boundaries of their existing
035   knowledge. In those situations, it is unrealistic to expect these models (or any intelligent agents,
036   including humans) to always make perfect decisions. Rather than confidently misleading users,
037   LLMs should be able to detect unfamiliar situations and act cautiously (e.g., decline to answer).
038   In this paper, we study whether LLMs can determine the correctness of their own answers to
039   multiple-choice questions. If so, this would directly enable LLMs to decline to answer when they are
040   likely to be incorrect. Prior work fine-tunes LLMs with labeled data to recognize questions beyond
041   their knowledge (Kadavath et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). However, this approach is costly and
042   could interfere with other capabilities of the model. More broadly, such approaches do not answer
043   the fundamental scientific question of whether LLMs already possess the necessary information to
044
      decide when to abstain. In other words, can this ability be invoked rather than learned?
045   We investigate the maximum softmax probability (MSP) as a potential source of innate uncertainty
046   information. Specifically, our goal is to understand the relationship between the MSP of an LLM
047   response and the correctness of that response.
048   We evaluate 14 LLMs fine-tuned for chat (henceforth“chat LLMs” for brevity) on five different Q&A
049   datasets with two different phrasings (Figure 1). These LLMs cover a range of sizes, capabilities and
050   architectures, and include both open-source and proprietary models. To our knowledge, our work
051   is the most comprehensive study on LLM correctness-awareness. This comprehensiveness bolsters
052   the robustness of our claims, but also enables cross-model comparisons which reveal novel insights.

071   1.1                      C ALIBRATION OF THE MAXIMUM SOFTMAX PROBABILITY

073   We first ask whether the MSP is calibrated (DeGroot & Fienberg, 1983; Nguyen & O’Connor,
074   2015), meaning that among responses with an MSP of p%, p% are correct. Calibrated MSPs enable
075
      fully unsupervised abstention policies with theoretical guarantees: a calibrated model which answers
      only when the MSP is higher than 1 − ε guarantees that the chance of an incorrect answer is at most
076
      ε. However, we show that this approach is not generally viable for chat LLMs. Figure 2 (left)
077
      shows that most LLMs in our study have poorly calibrated MSPs. In particular, the MSPs are
078   consistently overconfident. Furthermore, improved performance on the underlying Q&A task does
079   not necessarily translate to better calibration: Figure 2 (right) shows no correlation between Q&A
080   accuracy and calibration error. This is one of the cross-model comparisons mentioned above.

103   1.2                      C ORRECTNESS PREDICTION WITHOUT CALIBRATION
104
105   Even if the MSP cannot be directly interpreted as the probability of correctness, it might still be
106   predictive of correctness. As a simplified example, consider a model whose MSP is consistently 0.9
107   for correct responses and 0.8 for incorrect responses. This model is clearly miscalibrated, but its
      MSP perfectly predicts correctness.

108
      Through rigorous statistical testing, we demonstrate that the MSPs of chat LLMs can indeed pre-
109   dict correctness. Moreover, the predictiveness is stronger for models which perform better on the
110   underlying Q&A task. This second cross-model comparison suggests that the ability to predict cor-
111   rectness will strengthen as the general capabilities of LLMs improve (e.g., by scaling up data and
112   model sizes). The same is not true of calibration, as discussed above. These contrasting results
113   reveal a novel dichotomy between two approaches to uncertainty quantification.

115   1.3      E XPERIMENT DESIGN IN BRIEF
116
117   Before detailing our results on correctness prediction, we outline our experiment design. We hypoth-
118   esized that wrong answers on multiple-choice Q&A tasks would be associated with lower MSPs. We
119
      chose multiple-choice Q&A because there is exactly one correct answer: this allows us to study the
      core hypothesis of whether MSPs are predictive of correctness without having to deal with more
120
      complex issues such as degrees of correctness or multiple valid phrasings of the same correct an-
121
      swer.
122
123   We evaluated our hypothesis on 14 popular LLMs. Each model was tested on each of the five
124   multiple-choice Q&A datasets used in the Hugging Face Open LLM leaderboard (Beeching et al.,
125
      2023), which is the state-of-the-art for benchmarking open-source LLMs. To test our hypothesis in
      the simplest setting possible, we used a plain zero-shot prompting style, with two different prompt
126
      phrasings (Figure 1).
127
128   Formally, for each LLM-dataset-phrasing combination, we studied a binary classification task: given
129   a multiple-choice question and the LLM’s response, predict whether the response is correct. We
130   hypothesized that the MSP classifier would be able to discriminate between correct and incorrect
131
      answers better than random chance. We also studied a classifier based on the maximum pre-softmax
      logit (“Max Logit”).2
132
133   Our primary success metric was the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AU-
134   ROC) (Bradley, 1997). The AUROC of a binary classifier ranges from 0% to 100%, where 0%
135   corresponds to getting every prediction wrong, 50% is random chance, and 100% is perfect clas-
136   sification. AUROC is also equivalent to the probability that a randomly chosen positive instance
137
      is ranked higher than a randomly chosen negative instance. Conveniently, AUROC is threshold-
      independent: it captures the model’s performance across the entire range of possible thresholds.3
138
139   We have attached the code for all of our experiments and analysis as supplementary material.

      2    R ELATED WORK
217
218
      Kadavath et al. (2022). The most relevant prior paper is Kadavath et al. (2022), who study LLM
219   correctness-awareness in a variety of domains. There are three key differences between their work
220   and ours.
221
222
      The first is that they primarily study raw pretrained (i.e., not fine-tuned) LLMs. In particular, their
      well-known finding that LLMs are well-calibrated applies only to raw pretrained models. In this
223
      way, our work complements theirs by showing that their finding of good calibration fails to gener-
224
      alize to LLMs fine-tuned for chat. (They actually briefly study one fine-tuned model and find that it
225   is quite miscalibrated.) Correctness-awareness may also be more important for fine-tuned models,
226   since the casual user is less likely to interact with raw pretrained LLMs.
227
228
      The second is that Kadavath et al. (2022) only study MSP calibration and not MSP correctness
      prediction.4 This may be because good calibration immediately yields theoretically grounded cor-
229
      rectness prediction, so for raw pretrained models, good calibration may be sufficient. However, our
230
      finding that chat LLMs have miscalibrated MSPs motivates the separate question of wheter MSPs
231   can predict correctness.
232
233   The third is comprehensiveness. Kadavath et al. (2022) only test a single series of models, while we
234
      test seven series of models (14 models total). Our comprehensiveness crucially enables cross-model
      comparisons, as discussed in Section 1. In particular, we have statistical evidence suggesting that the
235
      correlation between correctness prediction and Q&A accuracy (and the lack of a correlation between
236
      calibration and Q&A accuracy) may extend to models that do not even exist yet. In contrast, it is
237   harder to claim that the findings of Kadavath et al. (2022) generalize to other models, since they
238   essentially have a sample size of one.
239
240
      Other work on LLM calibration. Several other papers have studied MSP calibration in LLMs
      (OpenAI, 2023; Krause et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). These papers have found
241
      some evidence that chat LLMs produce overconfident MSPs, but the papers use different exper-
242
      imental conditions and most of the papers study only a single model or family of models. To our
243   knowledge, we are the first to present a comprehensive and unified evaluation of the MSP calibration
244   of LLMs fine-tuned for chat.
245
246
      Orthogonal to our work, Tian et al. (2023) devise prompting strategies to obtain calibrated verbalized
      confidence estimates from LLMs. Beyond numerical probability, recent work considers alternative
247
      media for expressing uncertainty such as natural language (Lin et al., 2022a; Mielke et al., 2022;
248
      Xiong et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) or set-based similarity metrics (Lin et al., 2023).
249
250   Training LLMs to abstain. Another line of work fine-tunes LLMs to predict the correctness of their
251   answers (Kadavath et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). This approach has a different
252
      focus than our work: our goal is to understand the fundamental relationship between MSPs and cor-
      rectness, not to design a state-of-the-art abstention method. Our Q&A-with-abstention experiments
253
      are intended as a simple proof of concept of our correctness prediction findings. In other words, our
254
      primary contribution is scientific, not methodological.
255
256   Abstaining based on the MSP. The idea of abstaining based on the MSP was originally introduced
257   by Chow (1970) in the context of pattern recognition. This technique was recently explored for
258
      LLMs by Gupta et al. (2024), although their setting is different. Also, their experiments only use
      FLAN-T5 models. In contrast, we test 14 different LLMs: this enables cross-model comparisons,
259
      leading to a novel correlation between Q&A accuracy and correctness prediction AUROC.
260
261   Beyond LLMs. Moving further afield, uncertainty quantification is an important topic in many
262   areas of NLP (Hu et al., 2023). Identifying data points on which a model is uncertain is a com-
263   mon heuristic in active learning (Settles, 2009; Tharwat & Schenck, 2023). Popular approaches
264
      rely on model output distribution, such as least confidence (Culotta & McCallum, 2005; Settles &
      Craven, 2008), margin sampling (Scheffer et al., 2001), and maximum entropy (Settles & Craven,
265
      2008). Calibration (Bennet, 2000; DeGroot & Fienberg, 1983; Desai & Durrett, 2020; Guo et al.,
266
      2017; Nguyen & O’Connor, 2015; Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana, 2005) examines whether a model’s
267   predicted probability of an event accurately reflects the actual likelihood of that event occurring in
268

279
      As discussed, we patterned our evaluation after the original Hugging Face Open LLM leaderboard
280
      (Beeching et al., 2023). We used all five multiple-choice Q&A datasets5 from that leaderboard:
281
      ARC-Challenge (Clark et al.), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021),
282   TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022b), and WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021). We randomly sampled
283   6,000 questions from each dataset, except for those with fewer than 6,000, in which case we used
284   all of the questions (ARC-Challenge and TruthfulQA have 2,590 and 817 questions, respectively).
285
      We tested 14 LLMs: 12 open-source and two proprietary. The open-source LLMs were chosen based
286
      on a combination of performance on the aforementioned leaderboard and the number of downloads
287
      on Hugging Face. The open-source models we selected are Falcon (7B and 40B)6 (Almazrouei et al.,
288   2023), Llama 2 (7B, 13B, 70B) (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama 3 (8B, 70B) (AI@Meta, 2024), Mistral
289   7B v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023), Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024), SOLAR 10.7B (Kim et al., 2023), and
290   Yi (6B and 34B) (01-ai, 2023). We used the fine-tuned “chat" or “instruct" versions of all models,
291   as these have been specifically trained to answer user queries. All of the open-source LLMs were
292   accessed through the Hugging Face interface and were run with dynamic 4-bit quantization, which
293   has been shown to preserve performance while significantly reducing computational requirements
294   (Dettmers et al., 2023). The experiments on open-source LLMs took about three weeks on two
295   NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs.
296   We also tested two proprietary LLMs for which we could obtain softmax probabilities through an
297   API: OpenAI’s GPT3.5 Turbo (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) and GPT4 Turbo (OpenAI,
298   2023). Unfortunately, the OpenAI API does not provide pre-softmax logits, so we could not com-
299   pute Max Logit AUROCs for those two models. These experiments took only a few days and cost
300   approximately $100.
301   We wanted to test our hypothesis in its simplest form, so we used a plain zero-shot prompting
302   style, with two different prompt phrasings (Figure 1).7 The Hugging Face Open LLM leaderboard
303   also uses a plain prompting style (i.e., no chain-of-thought or other advanced techniques). The
304   leaderboard does use few-shot prompting, ranging from 5-shot to 25-shot depending on the dataset.
305   We tried using the same number of in-context examples, but this made the experiments prohibitively
306   slow. We were able to run complete experiments with one-shot prompting, which produced similar
307   results to our zero-shot results.

309   3.1     C OMPUTING MSP AND M AX L OGIT
310
311   Let V be a set of tokens (a vocabulary) and let x be a sequence of tokens from V . For each token
312
      y ∈ V and prefix x, an LLM computes a logit value L(y; x). It then applies a softmax function to
      the logits to derive the probability of y being the next token:
313
314                                                         exp(L(y; x))
315
                                          P (y | x) = P
                                                           z∈V exp(L(z; x))
316
317   The Max Logit of token y is maxy∈V L(y; x) and the MSP is maxy∈V P (y | x). Note that the Max
318   Logit and MSP both correspond to the same token: arg maxy∈V L(y; x) = arg maxy∈V P (y | x).
319   In our experiments, we formulated each question as a prompt (Figure 1), then used greedy decoding
320   to generate a response (i.e., we always picked the token with the maximum logit).

      For typical supervised learning classification tasks such as those studied in Hendrycks & Gimpel
325   (2017) and Hendrycks et al. (2022), there is a single MSP and maximum logit per response. How-
326   ever, an LLM response can consist of multiple tokens and the model produces an MSP and Max
327   Logit for each token. To address this issue, we extracted from the response a single token that in-
328   dicates the LLM’s answer to the question. Specifically, we searched the output string for the first
329   occurrence of “A.”, “B.”, “C.”, etc, then recorded the MSP and Max Logit corresponding to that
330   capital letter token. If there was no such occurrence, we searched instead for just “A”, “B”, “C”,
331   etc, or the text of one of the options (e.g. for the question in Figure 1, this would be “lightning",
332   “cellular respiration", etc.). The same search process was used for both computing MSP/Max Logit
333
      and evaluating correctness.
334   If the search failed, we recorded the MSP and Max Logit as zero, and labeled the LLM’s response
335   as wrong.8 We considered excluding these questions but decided that practically speaking, these
336   “unparseable” responses did not correctly answer the question and thus should be treated as wrong.
337   This search process was chosen to maximize the chance of extracting a legitimate answer from the
338   LLM response, but the specifics of the search process (e.g., whether “A.” or “lightning” has prece-
339   dence) did not significantly alter the results. Most models almost always responded in the correct
340   format. Only weaker models like Falcon 7B had issues in this regard (and even then, ambiguous
341   answers were fairly rare).

343   3.2   AGGREGATING RESULTS ACROSS DATASETS
344
345   Grouping the questions from all datasets together to compute a single AUROC per model would un-

356
357   Statistical significance. We conducted a series of hypothesis tests to confirm that our AUROC
358   results were statistically significant. We used the Mann-Whitney U test (Mann & Whitney, 1947;
359   Wilcoxon, 1945) which directly tests the null hypothesis that the classifier’s true AUROC is 50%
360   (i.e., random guessing). In our case, a significant Mann-Whitney test affirms the hypothesis that
361
      our classifier can distinguish between (1) questions where the LLM got the correct answer and (2)
      questions where the LLM got the wrong answer. As the non-parametric equivalent of the t-test, the
362
      U test requires the data to be independent but not normally distributed, which our randomly-sampled
363
      multiple choice questions satisfy.
364
365   For each available combination of model, dataset, prompt phrasing, and classifier (MSP and Max
366   Logit), we tested the null hypothesis that the true AUROC was equal to 50%. This resulted in a
367
      total of 260 Mann-Whitney U tests. Table 1 reports the number of p-values which were below 10−4
      for each model and classifier. We chose the significance threshold of α = 10−4 to account for
368
      a Bonferroni correction (Bonferroni, 1936), whichis applied when performing multiple hypothesis
369
      tests (in our case, 220) to ensure that the chance of falsely rejecting any null hypothesis is small.
370   Starting from the standard threshold of α = 0.05, the Bonferroni correction yields α = 0.05/220 ≈
371   2.3 × 10−4 . We adhere to the stricter threshold of 1 × 10−4 for simplicity.

463   those k questions. We discovered that k = 20 performed almost as well as using half of all questions.
464

477   can trivially improve to 0 by abstaining on every question. (Table 4 shows the abstention frequency

480   models with high initial scores showed only modest improvements on the balanced score metric.
481   These results show how the uncertainty signals from softmax probabilities and/or logits can be
482   leveraged to improve performance on practical language tasks. Further details on our Q&A-with-

509   6    C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
510
511   The capabilities of AI systems have advanced rapidly over the past several years and will likely
512   continue to grow. In order to ensure that AI is beneficial for society, we believe it is paramount to
513   understand the risks of such systems and take steps to address those risks. In this paper, we focus on
514   one particular risk: harmful responses from LLMs. We use the simple idea of noticing when one is
515   uncertain and then acting cautiously to avert these harmful responses.
516   One limitation of our work is the restriction to multiple-choice questions, which simplifies the prob-
517   lem in several ways. First, it removes the need to distinguish between multiple correct answers
518   (“aleatoric uncertainty”) and no good answers (“epistemic uncertainty”), both of which could re-
519   sult in low MSPs. The restriction to multiple-choice also enabled us to tie the LLM’s answer to a
520   single token and thus a single MSP. Future work could handle free-response questions by aggregat-
521   ing MSPs across tokens in a clever way. A further challenge could be multi-step decision-making
522   problems which may involve aggregating uncertainty not only across multiple tokens in a single
523
      response, but across multiple responses on different time steps.
524   Another limitation is our reliance on labeled data to transform our scientific insights into a practical
525   method for abstention. We only used 20 data points, and we only used labeled data to choose the
526   threshold, but a fully unsupervised method would be advantageous in many settings. However,
527   we remind the reader that our primary contribution is the scientific finding of good correctness
528   prediction despite miscalibration, not the proof-of-concept abstention experiments in Section 5.
529   More broadly, we are excited about developing more robust methods for mistake detection in LLMs,
530   both for Q&A tasks and for other contexts. We would also like to better understand when and why
531   these methods fail: are there particular subcategories of unfamiliar situations that are especially
532   challenging to identify? For example, why was the WinoGrande dataset so much harder for our
533   correctness prediction task?

      B    C AVEAT FOR FALCON 7B
888
889
      Initially, many of Falcon 7B’s responses fell into the “unparseable" category described in Sec-
890
      tion 3.1. Upon investigation, we found that many of these responses were simply a period or an
891   end-of-text token. Removing the final newline in the prompt resolved this behavior, so we believe
892   that this newline was somehow convincing the model that was conversation was “over". These initial
893   results had the side effect of making it very easy to detect wrong answers, since a solitary period is
894   obviously not a correct answer. For this reason, we removed the final newline for Falcon 7B only.
895   We considered removing the final newline for all models or excluding Falcon 7B entirely, but we
896   felt that our chosen approach would be more scientifically honest. As Falcon 7B performed by far
897   the worst on both Q&A and AUROC even with this concession, we do not think this decision holds
898   much import, but we report it for transparency.
