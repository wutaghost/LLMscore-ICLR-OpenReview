011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate traffic forecasting is essential to enable advanced utilization of intelligent
014            transportation systems. However, forecasting models often struggle to capture
015            the complex spatio-temporal dependencies of traffic data, as they typically handle
016
               spatial and temporal dependencies separately. To overcome this limitation, we in-
               troduce the Tri-Tense Former (TTformer), a novel approach that captures spatio-
017
               temporal relationships through three tense-specific attention modules. We cate-
018
               gorize traffic flow into three tense dimensions: past-to-present (present-perfect),
019            present, and future. Each tense-specific attention module captures the dependen-
020            cies within its respective traffic flow. Furthermore, to address incomplete traffic
021            data, we improve the robustness of the model by employing contrastive learning
022            with negative filtering technique that operates regardless of predefined adjacency
023            matrices. TTformer significantly outperforms existing models by more effectively
024            capturing spatio-temporal dependencies and improving traffic forecasting accu-
025            racy.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Traffic has a significant impact on daily life, influencing the economy, environment, and public
031   safety. Therefore, accurate traffic forecasting is critical for developing more efficient and safer
032   cities. However, predicting traffic flow remains a challenging problem due to the intricate spatial
033
      and temporal dependencies within traffic data (Zhao et al., 2019). In response, machine learning
      research has focused on addressing both the spatial dependency between roads and the temporal
034
      dependency, where past traffic states influence current conditions. A common approach is to design
035
      separate modules to address each type of dependency (Sahili & Awad, 2023). Recurrent Neural
036   Networks (RNNs), which excel in time series prediction, are typically used to capture temporal
037   characteristics. For spatial features, graph-based models, which treat road networks as graphs, have
038   proven more effective than Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Recently, with the rise of
039   transformer architectures, attention-based models have gained traction as a new trend in this field.
040   Many researchers have thus factorized spatial and temporal attention, aligning with the broader trend
041   in spatio-temporal research. However, to achieve a more comprehensive understanding of spatio-
042   temporal dependencies, it is essential to capture these relationships in their entirety, rather than in
043   isolation (Grigsby et al., 2021). In light of this, we introduce a model that holistically captures
044
      spatio-temporal relationships by leveraging attention mechanisms from a novel perspective.
045   As illustrated in Figure 1a, the vanilla spatio-temporal model employs two aspects of the attention
046   mechanism: spatial and temporal. The spatial attention mechanism captures the relationship be-
047   tween roads at the same timestamp, while the temporal attention mechanism identifies relationships
048   between timestamps for the same variable. However, in this setting, it becomes challenging to quan-
049   tify the impact of past traffic conditions on the current state, as it doesn’t account for how past traffic
050
      on other roads influences the present. Therefore, a novel approach to the attention module is needed
      to directly represent these complex traffic relationships.
051
052   To effectively represent the intricate dependencies in traffic conditions, separating them into purely
053   spatial and temporal dimensions is not ideal (Grigsby et al., 2021). Instead, we focus on capturing
      the real traffic flow to simultaneously represent spatio-temporal relationships. These relationships


                                                          1

054
      can be categorized into three types of traffic flow: from past to present, the present, and from present
055   to future. Using the concept of tense, these flows correspond to the present-perfect, present, and
056   future tenses, respectively.
057
058
      We propose a model called the “Tri-Tense Former (TTformer),” which incorporates three tense-
      specific attention modules, each reflecting one of these tense-traffic flows, as shown in Figure 1b.
059
      The present-perfect tense attention module quantifies the impact of past traffic conditions on the cur-
060
      rent traffic state in a spatio-temporal context. The present tense attention module captures the impact
061   of spatial relationships between roads on the current traffic state. Lastly, the future tense attention
062   module measures the prospective temporal relationships, capturing how current traffic conditions
063   influence future states. To implement each tense-attention module, we organize the traffic sequence
064   into “spatio-temporal blocks” and assign an appropriate pair of query and key vectors in the attention
065   mechanism. Additionally, traffic data often contains missing values or outliers, primarily due to sen-
066   sor failures or inaccuracies (Liu et al., 2022). To address these imperfections, we adopt a contrastive
067   learning method to derive more robust representations. Our approach also includes a negative filter-
068   ing methodology, which proves especially useful in the absence of a predefined adjacency matrix.
069   The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
070
071            • We propose Tri-Tense Former (TTformer), a model that captures spatio-temporal relation-
072              ships with three tense attention modules: the present-perfect, the present, and the future,
073              reflecting real traffic flow dynamics. We implement this by structuring traffic sequences
074              into ‘spatio-temporal blocks’ and setting three different key-query pairs in the attention
075
                 mechanism.
076            • We adopt contrastive learning to address inconsistencies in traffic datasets. In the negative
077              filtering step of contrastive learning, we use spatial embedding instead of a predefined adja-
078              cency matrix, enabling the methodology to be effective even when predefined information
079              is unavailable.
080            • We conduct a series of experiments using real traffic datasets, METR-LA and PEMS-BAY.
081              The proposed model demonstrates superior performance compared to baseline models, in-
082              dicating that TTformer is capable of jointly capturing spatio-temporal relationships effec-
083              tively.

      2.1     S PATIO - TEMPORAL T RANSFORMER
101
102   The attention mechanism is capable of capturing dynamic relationships, and it has no limit on the
103   receptive field, allowing it to handle long dependencies. Consequently, Transformer-based models
104   are currently being researched to identify relationships between roads using attention mechanisms
105   instead of adjacency matrices (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020). The attention score Att(Lq , Lk ) calcu-
106   lated between a query sequence Lq and a key sequence Lk represents the relationship between the
107   query and the key. This allows the model to dynamically learn and adapt to the relationships be-
      tween roads based on the traffic context, rather than relying on predefined static adjacency matrices.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Traffic forecasting models that implement attention mechanisms compute both spatial and tempo-
109   ral attention to perform prediction tasks. Numerous models such as Dsformer (Yu et al., 2023) and
110   Traffic transformers(Cai et al., 2020) have been developed to calculate spatial and temporal attention
111   separately.
112
      When calculating attention separately for spatial and temporal dimensions, a limitation exists in
113
      that only temporal relationships per sequence are expressed. Therefore, models that capture spatio-
114
      temporal relationships simultaneously are being studied, allowing for a more comprehensive under-
115   standing of the interactions between variables across both spatial and temporal dimensions. Space-
116   timeformer (Grigsby et al., 2021) tries to address this issue by reorganizing tokens to express spatio-
117   temporal attention. The model handles N nodes and (c+h) time steps simultaneously, thus enabling
118   the calculation of spatio-temporal relationships in a unified manner. Spacetimeformer trains a global
119   self-attention network that jointly computes attention across both space and time for simultaneous
120   comprehension of spatio-temporal relationships. However, this approach leads to a quadratic in-
121   crease with (N ∗ (c + h)) in computational complexity and memory requirements. Therefore,
122   additional techniques are required to reduce the computational complexity by sparsifying the com-
123
      putational matrix (Zhou et al., 2021). STAEformer (Liu et al., 2023) employs an adaptive embedding
      mechanism to capture spatio-temporal relationships simultaneously. However, the adaptive embed-
124
      ding has the disadvantage of maintaining a static representation, which may not adequately represent
125
      the dynamic spatio-temporal relationships that may change according to the traffic state.
126
127   To capture the spatial and temporal dependency, numerous GNN-based models and the attention-
128   based models factorize each dependency for calculation. However, this approach results in insuf-
129
      ficient capture of spatio-temporal dependencies. Consequently, we present a novel approach to
      capture hidden dependencies by introducing three tense attention modules that can simultaneously
130
      understand spatio-temporal relationships. When compared to Spacetimeformer, our model offers
131
      significant scalability advantages in that it maintains a constant graph size, which is the same as
132   when computing spatial and temporal attention separately.

135   2.2   T RAFFIC DATA I MPUTATION
136
137
      Addressing the incompleteness of traffic datasets is crucial for enhancing the performance of traffic
138
      prediction models. Events such as unexpected accidents, broken sensors, or missing signals fre-
139   quently occur, significantly decreasing the quality of the collected data (Fang & Wang, 2020). Sim-
140   ple deletion of missing values is only feasible when the missing rate is low (Wothke, 2000; McKnight
141   et al., 2007). Therefore, various data imputation methods have been developed. Initially, neighbor-
142   based methods provided a solution by identifying the nearest neighbors using techniques like KNN
143   and updating the missing values with the average of these neighbors (Song et al., 2015) or replacing
144   with the last observed value (Amiri & Jensen, 2016). Recently, deep learning-based methods have
145   demonstrated superior performance, encompassing conventional learning techniques—both super-
146   vised and unsupervised learning—and more recent methods such as self-supervised learning.
147   RNN-based models such as GRU-D (Che et al., 2018), BRITS (Cao et al., 2018), and NAOMI
148   (Liu et al., 2019) are effective for handling time-series datasets. While they have proven effective
149   in handling missing data within time series datasets, they are not specifically designed for spatio-
150   temporal datasets. These models primarily focus on temporal dependencies and do not adequately
151   account for spatial relationships among adjacent nodes in networks. These limitations underscore
152   the need for novel approaches that can integrate both spatial and temporal dependencies, leveraging
153
      the relational information inherent in spatio-temporal datasets. GRIN (Cini et al., 2022) tackles the
      shortcomings of conventional time-series imputation techniques by employing a graph structure to
154
      effectively handle spatio-temporal data and learning representations through message passing with
155
      RNN cell. Like the BRITS model, GRIN processes multivariate time series data in both forward
156   and backward directions at each node, operating bidirectionally. However, GRIN is prone to the
157   error propagation issues typically found in autoregressive models. SPIN (Marisca et al., 2022) was
158   developed to mitigate the error propagation issue observed in GRIN. SPIN leverages attention-based
159   architectures instead of relying on recurrent neural networks to avoid the autoregressive propagation
160   of errors. SPIN employs inter-node spatio-temporal cross-attention to propagate information from
161   neighboring nodes and intra-node temporal self-attention to analyze the sequence of each node in-
      dependently. STGAIN (Huang et al., 2023) represents another advanced approach, utilizing GANs


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for robust spatio-temporal imputation. It features a spatio-temporal generator and a discriminator
163   that incorporates a GCN as a spatial aggregator and a 1-D CNN as a temporal extractor.
164
165   While conventional learning techniques have demonstrated good performance, they require dedi-
166
      cated training for a data imputation model. Contrastive learning offers a potential solution to this
      limitation by not directly imputing the missing values but enhancing the quality of the learned rep-
167
      resentations for the prediction. The objective of contrastive learning is to increase the similarity
168
      between positive pairs while decreasing it for negative pairs, thereby refining the representations
169   to capture meaningful information. STGCL (Liu et al., 2022) highlights data scarcity as an obsta-
170   cle to the performance of spatio-temporal forecasting and argues that the application of contrastive
171   learning is beneficial. The author proposes several data augmentation methods and negative filtering
172   techniques. With regard to spatial negative filtering, the authors utilize the known adjacency matrix
173   to classify first-order neighbors as hard negatives and exclude them from contrastive loss computa-
174   tion. However, this approach is subject to the limitation of requiring a predefined matrix. Therefore,
175   we suggest an alternative method that can be applied without the need for a predefined matrix.

177   3     M ETHODOLOGY
178
179
      Preliminary. Given a set of c timestamps of N variables, the objective is to predict the next h
180   timestamps. To formulate the problem, we adopt the graph concept to model traffic conditions,
181   where each variable represents sensors on the road with traffic attribute values. Each graph, des-
182   ignated as Gt = (Vt , At ), represents the traffic state at the given timestamp t. Consequently, as
183   illustrated in Figure 2, the traffic forecasting problem can be formulated as follows: when the
184   context sequence of graphs G[C] = [GT −c+1 , · · · , GT ] is given, the target sequence of graphs
185   G[H] = [GT +1 , · · · , GT +h ] is to be predicted. Each node in the graph, designated as vti , represents
186   the ith sensor on the road. For N traffic sensors, the total number of nodes in the graph is equal to
187   |Vt | = N . The adjacency matrix, At , is a matrix of At ∈ RN ∗N that represents the relationship be-
188
      tween N roads. In the context of traffic forecasting, a predefined static adjacency matrix, calculated
      based on the distances between roads, is typically employed as auxiliary information. In contrast,
189
      this paper presents a model that predicts traffic flow without relying on predefined relationships
190
      between roads.
191
192   Additionally, in traffic forecasting, since traffic data includes periodicity according to timestamps
193   (such as time of the day and day of the week), it is common practice to use both traffic attributes x
194
      and timestamp information y together (Zhou et al., 2021). In the graph Gt = (Vt , At ), each node
      represents the ith variable at timestamp t, containing timestamp information xit and traffic attribute
195
      values yti . This node is represented by the vector vti = [xt , yt ] ∈ Rd , where d is the dimension of
196
      each node’s value. In this scenario, accurate traffic forecasting relies on the representation of each
197   node vti , ensuring that each representation effectively captures the spatio-temporal dependencies.
198   Spatial dependency refers to the spatial relationships between variables, while temporal dependency
199   refers to the relationships over time for a single variable along the time axis.

      3.2   T RI -T ENSE ATTENTION M ODULE
221
      The proposed model is based on the Graph Attention Network(GAT) (Velickovic et al., 2017), which
222
      is derived from the Graph Neural Network(GNN) (Scarselli et al., 2008). The proposed model,
223   Tri-Tense Former (TTformer), integrates three attention modules to capture dependencies across
224   both space and time, following the GAT framework. Each module assesses the varying relative
225   importance of each node from three different perspectives, enabling the model to effectively capture
226   spatio-temporal dependencies. Figure 3 illustrates the architecture of TTformer, which follows the
227   encoder-decoder based transformer structure. The overall flow of TTformer can be summarized
228   as follows: both the context and target sequences are updated within the encoder, with the target
229   sequence further refined in relation to the context sequence within the decoder. The contextualized
230   representations of the context and target sequences are then concatenated, and final predictions for
231
      the target sequence are generated. The encoder employs two attention modules: the present-perfect
      attention and the present attention module, while the decoder utilizes a single attention module,
232
      the future attention module. Detailed descriptions for the encoder and decoder can be found in
233
      Appendix A.3 and A.4.

      3.3   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
252
253   The framework of contrastive learning is illustrated in Figure 4. Contrastive learning is one of
254   the self-supervised learning techniques that aim to learn effective representations by comparing
255   and contrasting between input data and augmented input data (see Appendix A.5 for data augmen-
256   tation). Through contrastive learning, the representations of positive pairs tend to become more
257   similar, while those of negative pairs become less similar. This enables the identification of precise
258   representations for input data.
259   Moreover, the process of negative filtering can be applied further by filtering out hard negatives (see
260   Appendix A.6) for details). These are instances that are closely aligned with the positive pairs but
261   designated as negative pairs. In the cited paper (Liu et al., 2022), the authors proved that contrastive
262   learning is effective in overcoming challenges in traffic forecasting. Regarding the spatial negative
263   filtering method, they proposed utilizing a predefined adjacency matrix to filter out first-order neigh-
264   bors as hard negatives, thus excluding them from the computation of the contrastive loss. However,
265   in this study, we assume that a predefined adjacency matrix is unavailable. Instead, we propose an
266
      alternative negative filtering method that generates a similarity matrix using spatial embeddings.
267   We adopt a joint learning approach, where both the prediction task and the contrastive task are
268   executed simultaneously. The overall loss is calculated as the sum of the prediction loss, Lpred , and
269   the contrastive task loss, Lcontrastive task (1). The impact of the contrastive task can be controlled by
      adjusting the value of the parameter λ.

361
362   The subsequent step is to examine the tense-attention modules of TTformer. An ablation study
363   was conducted by training the model while excluding each of the three modules. The results of the
364   performance comparison on the METR-LA test dataset when contrastive learning was jointly trained
365   are shown in Figure 6a. In Figure 6a, ‘w/o 1st’ signifies the model that excludes the first tense-
366   attention module, ‘w/o 2nd’ means the second, and ‘w/o 3rd’ means excluding the third module.
367   And the last ‘w/ random 3rd’ means for replacing the third future attention module with a random
368
      future attention module.
369   The ‘TTF’ case represents the scenario where all three attention modules are applied, resulting in
370   the best performance. Among ‘w/o 1st’, ‘w/o 2nd’, and ‘w/o 3rd’, the best-performing model is
371   ‘w/o 3rd’, which excludes the third module. In this instance, it can be inferred that both the first and
372   second modules effectively reflect the relationships for edge types A and B as described in Figure 10,
373   and capture the spatio-temporal relationships well. Among the three altered models, the exclusion of
374
      the second module results in the largest performance drop. This indicates that the current attention
      mechanism has the greatest influence on the predictions.
375
376   The third module is responsible for capturing future attention in the context sequence, thereby updat-
377   ing the target sequence using the calculated future attention. The removal of the third future attention
      module resulted in a decline in performance. This suggests that the third module plays a role in up-

      dating the target sequence. Additionally, in order to confirm whether the future attention learned was
379   indeed effective, a further study was conducted to replace the model’s third future attention module

471
      In contrast to conventional approaches that factorize traffic dependency into spatial and temporal as-
472
      pects, we introduce a novel perspective of attention modules to express the three types of traffic flow
473
      -present-perfect, present, and future- through each corresponding tense-specific attention module.
474   By capturing each tense relationship using a respective attention module, the proposed model, Tri-
475   Tense Former(TTformer), excels in traffic forecasting tasks. Furthermore, to address the unstable
476   characteristics of traffic datasets, which may contain high ranges of zero values, missing values, or
477   outliers, we integrate contrastive learning techniques with an innovative negative filtering method.
478
      We demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed model with two real traffic datasets: METR-LA and
479
      PEMS-BAY. In comparison with baseline models, TTformer outperforms other baseline models by
480   capturing hidden dependencies within the data. Moreover, comprehensive ablation studies validate
481   the necessity of each proposed module for capturing intricate spatio-temporal relationships. In the
482   context of contrastive learning, our findings align with prior research (Liu et al., 2022), highlighting
483   the efficacy of node masking as a data augmentation strategy. Additionally, the proposed negative
484   filtering method, which utilizes spatial embedding to generate similarity matrices, demonstrates

      matrices.

586
587   The spatio-temporal block is defined as the merging of a graph for a specific time interval, referred
588   to as the “block size” S. Figure 9 illustrates the methodology employed in the design of the spatio-

591   redesigned to contain information for the time interval (t, t + 1, ..., t + S − 1) rather than a specific
592   time t. The initial block is represented as B1 = [GT −c+1 , GT −c+2 , ..., GT −c+S ], with the node
593
      within B1 being VB1 = [VT −c+1 , VT −c+2 , ..., VT −c+S ]. The integration of the spatio-temporal
      block leads to a reduction in the total graph size from N ∗ (C + H) to N ∗((C+H)S     .

612   The utilization of the spatio-temporal block differs significantly from the application of dilated
613   causal convolution layers in WaveNet (Oord et al., 2016). In WaveNet, dilated convolution is em-
614   ployed to train a kernel, facilitating the extraction of temporal trends. This allows for the handling
615   of relatively large receptive fields with few layers and enables the parallel processing of sequences,
616   contrasting with recurrent neural networks. However, the spatio-temporal block operates in a dif-
617   ferent manner. Unlike dilated convolution in WaveNet, its function is not to extract temporal trends
618
      through training the kernel. Instead, it simply merges nodes along the time axis. This enables the
      aggregation of information across multiple time steps within a specified block size, facilitating the
619
      modeling of spatio-temporal relationships. The spatio-temporal block offers three principal advan-
620
      tages. Firstly, the complexity of the training is reduced as the size of the total graphs is decreased.
621   Secondly, it enhances the effectiveness of transformer-based models by facilitating more efficient
622   application of the attention mechanism. When applying an attention-based model to a large graph,
623   additional techniques such as sparsifying the attention matrix are required. However, the spatio-
624   temporal block simplifies this process by leveraging a simple inductive bias that traffic attributes
625   exhibit temporal dependency within a specific temporal range. This can reduce the size of the at-
626   tention matrix itself, eliminating the need for additional techniques for the attention mechanism.
627   Thirdly, the application of spatial attention within the spatio-temporal block inherently incorporates
628   the effect of temporal attention. This is because each block contains traffic flow within a specific
629
      block size, allowing temporal relationships to be considered by calculating the spatial attention op-
      eration within the same block. The experimental results, which vary according to the block size,
630
      will be presented in the subsequent section.

657   these dynamic relationships. The need for two different types of attention modules arises from the
658   following rationale: to fully capture the relationships between nodes, it’s essential to consider not
659   only the current state but also the past state, as recent past states also play a significant role in shaping
660   the current relationships. The visualization of these influences is illustrated in Figure 10, where
661   nodes at past timestamps also impact current nodes. In order to understand the spatial relationship
662   between At+1 and Bt+1 and Ct+1 , the past state At , Bt , and Ct must also be taken into account.

664

665
      where various types of edges are present in a single graph (Hu et al., 2020). In TTformer, each
666   tense-attention module effectively manages these two edge types of temporal edge relationships,
667   addressing the distinct influences of past and present states on the current state.

907   In both the METR-LA and PEMS-BAY datasets, the model’s performance improved when node
908   masking was applied, compared to the model without contrastive learning (‘w/o CL’). This demon-
909   strates that the contrastive learning technique with node masking is effective in traffic data containing
910   a significant number of zeros. Figure 15 visualizes the prediction results for specific time periods
911   containing actual zeros in the METR-LA, comparing the ‘w/o CL’ and ‘CL NM’ models. It was ob-
912   served that the ‘CL NM’ model, which underwent node masking, exhibited less sensitivity to zero
913   values and achieved superior prediction performance.

916   in Figure 16b. In particular, it was found that negative filtering using spatial embedding (with
917   nf threshold = 0.1) was more effective than distinguishing hard negatives using the predefined
      adjacency matrix. This indicates that the similarity matrix generated through the spatial embed-
