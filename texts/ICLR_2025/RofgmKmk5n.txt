011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Temporal point processes (TPPs) are widely used to model the timing and occur-
014            rence of events in domains such as social networks, transportation systems, and
015            e-commerce. In this paper, we introduce TPP-LLM, a novel framework that in-
016
               tegrates large language models (LLMs) with TPPs to capture both the semantic
               and temporal aspects of event sequences. Unlike traditional methods that rely on
017
               categorical event type representations, TPP-LLM directly utilizes the textual de-
018
               scriptions of event types, enabling the model to capture rich semantic information
019            embedded in the text. While LLMs excel at understanding event semantics, they
020            are less adept at capturing temporal patterns. To address this, TPP-LLM incorpo-
021            rates temporal embeddings and employs parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
022            methods to effectively learn temporal dynamics without extensive retraining. This
023            approach improves both predictive accuracy and computational efficiency. Ex-
024            perimental results across diverse real-world datasets demonstrate that TPP-LLM
025            outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in sequence modeling and event prediction,
026            highlighting the benefits of combining LLMs with TPPs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Temporal point processes (TPPs) (Shchur et al., 2021) are powerful tools for modeling the occur-
032   rence of events over time, with widespread applications in domains such as social networks, urban
033
      dynamics, transportation, natural disasters, and e-commerce. The challenge of predicting both the
      type and timing of future events based on historical sequences has led to the development of increas-
034
      ingly sophisticated models. Traditional TPP models often rely on handcrafted features or specific
035
      assumptions about temporal dependencies, which limit their ability to capture complex event pat-
036   terns in real-world datasets. Recent advances, such as neural TPPs, have leveraged the representa-
037   tional power of deep learning to overcome some of these limitations, but many still require extensive
038   task-specific training from scratch.
039
      With the rise of powerful large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and
040
      Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), new opportunities have emerged for using LLMs to understand and
041
      predict event sequences by capturing rich semantic and contextual information. Inspired by their
042   success in text-based tasks (Zhao et al., 2023) and time series prediction (Zhou et al., 2023; Jin
043   et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b), we propose TPP-LLM (Figure 1), a novel framework that inte-
044   grates LLMs with TPPs to model both the temporal and semantic aspects of event sequences. By
045   leveraging pretrained LLMs, TPP-LLM directly utilizes textual descriptions of event types, moving
046   beyond traditional methods that rely on categorical representations. To ensure the model captures
047   temporal dynamics, we incorporate temporal embeddings alongside these event descriptions. To ef-
048   ficiently adapt LLMs for TPP modeling, we employ low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), a
049   parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Liu et al., 2022) method, allowing us to adjust a small subset
050
      of LLM parameters, reducing computational cost while maintaining high performance. Through ex-
      tensive experiments on real-world datasets, we demonstrate that TPP-LLM consistently outperforms
051
      state-of-the-art baselines in sequence modeling and next event prediction.
052

117   3.1   T EMPORAL P OINT P ROCESSES
118
      Temporal point processes (TPPs) (Hawkes, 1971; Laub et al., 2015) are a class of stochastic pro-
119
      cesses used to model the occurrence of discrete events over continuous time. A marked TPP extends
120   this framework by associating each event with both a time of occurrence and a type (mark), making
121   it highly applicable in domains where understanding both event types and their timing is critical.
122
123
      In a marked TPP, a sequence of events over an observation window [0, T ] is represented as: S =
      {(t1 , k1 ), (t2 , k2 ), . . . , (tn , kn )}, where ti represents the time of the i-th event and ki ∈ K represents
124
      the corresponding event type from a discrete set K = {1, 2, . . . , K}. The goal is to model the
125
      probability of the next event’s time and type, given the history of previous events.
126
127   The key function in a TPP is the conditional intensity function λ(t, k|Ht ), which defines the instan-
128   taneous rate at which an event of type k occurs at time t, conditioned on the history Ht . Formally, it

      3.2   N EURAL T EMPORAL P OINT P ROCESSES
149
      Recent advances in TPPs have introduced neural-based models that leverage the representational
150   power of deep learning to capture complex event sequences. These models typically parameterize
151   the conditional intensity function λ(t, k|Ht ) using neural networks, enabling them to learn both
152   temporal dependencies and event type distributions directly from data.
153
      In neural TPPs, for each event (ti , ki ), an embedding ei ∈ RD is computed through embedding
154
      layers based on the event time ti and the event type ki . The hidden state ht , which represents the
155
      history up to time t, is then updated based on the current event’s embedding and the previous hidden
156   state hi−1 . This update can be formulated as: hi = fupdate (hi−1 , ei ), where fupdate is a neural
157   network, often implemented as a recurrent neural network (RNN) (Hochreiter, 1997) or a more
158   advanced attention-based mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). With the updated hidden state hi , the
159   next event time ti+1 and event type ki+1 are sampled from the probability distribution conditioned
160   on hi : ti+1 , ki+1 ∼ P (ti+1 , ki+1 |hi ). Different neural TPP models employ various architectures
161   for the state update function f . Early approaches (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017) use RNNs to
      capture temporal dependencies between events, while more recent models (Zhang et al., 2020; Zuo


                                                              3

162
      et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022) replace the recurrent structure with attention-based layers, allowing
163   for better long-range interactions. These neural-based methods enhance the flexibility of TPPs,
164   learning event dependencies from complex datasets in a data-driven manner.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
168
      In this section, we introduce our proposed framework, TPP-LLM, which leverages large language
169
      models (LLMs) to model temporal point processes (TPPs). TPP-LLM, illustrated in Figure 1, in-
170
      tegrates pretrained LLMs to capture the semantic richness of event types and employs temporal
171   embeddings to handle the temporal dynamics of event sequences.
172
173   4.1   E VENT AND P ROMPT E MBEDDINGS
174
175   TPP-LLM models the sequence of events S = {(t1 , k1 ), (t2 , k2 ), . . . , (tn , kn )}, where each event
176   ei consists of a time ti and a corresponding event type ki . Unlike conventional TPP models, which
177   use discrete event types, TPP-LLM directly processes the textual descriptions of event types using a
178   pretrained LLM. This enables the model to capture richer semantic information from the event text
179   while learning temporal dependencies.
180   The event type ki is represented as a sequence of tokens. Let xi = {xi,1 , xi,2 , . . . , xi,Li } be the
181   sequence of tokens for event type ki , where Li is the length of the tokenized event type. Each
182   token xi,j is mapped to an embedding xi,j ∈ RD through the pretrained LLM’s embedding layer
183   E ∈ RV ×D , where V is the vocabulary size and D is the embedding dimension. In addition to
184   the event type representation, TPP-LLM incorporates a temporal embedding to capture the time
185   dynamics. Each event time ti is mapped to a temporal embedding ti ∈ RD using an embedding
186   layer: ti = ftemporal (ti ), where ftemporal can be a linear layer or a positional encoding. In this research,
187
      we utilize the temporal positional encoding (Zuo et al., 2020):

      4.3   E VENT P REDICTION
217
218   For each event (ti , ki ), the history vector hi from the LLM output encodes the event history Hti ,
219   which includes both the event type and temporal dynamics up to time ti . Following previous research
220   (Zuo et al., 2020; Gao & Dai, 2024), we utilize this hidden representation to predict both the next
221   event type ki+1 and time ti+1 through separate layers. To predict the event type, we apply a linear
222   layer followed by a softmax activation to the hidden state hi , mapping it to a probability distribution
223   over the possible event types: p̂i+1 = p̂(ki+1 |Ht′ i ) = softmax(Wtype hi + btype ), where Wtype ∈
224   RK×H and btype ∈ RK are the weights and bias of the linear layer, K is the number of event types,
225   and H is the hidden state dimension. The predicted event type k̂i+1 is predicted as the type with the
226   maximum probability: k̂i+1 = arg maxk p̂i+1 . Similarly, to predict the next event time, we apply
227   another linear layer to the hidden state hi , producing a scalar value that represents the next time:
                 T
228   t̂i+1 = wtime hi + btime , where wtime ∈ RH and btime ∈ R are the weights and bias for this layer.

230   4.4   F INE -T UNING
231
      To efficiently adapt the pretrained LLM to the TPP task, we employ low-rank adaptation (LoRA)
232
      (Hu et al., 2021), a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Liu et al., 2022) method. Instead of fine-
233
      tuning all the parameters of the LLM, low-rank matrices are introduced to LLM weights. Specifi-
234   cally, we modify the weight matrix of one target module: W ′ = W + BA, where W is the original
235   weight, and A, B are learnable low-rank matrices. By fine-tuning only these low-rank matrices,
236   we significantly reduce the number of trainable parameters, making the adaptation more efficient
237   without compromising performance. In addition to LoRA, other PEFT methods (Liu et al., 2022;
238   Zhang et al., 2023) can also be applied to further optimize the fine-tuning process.

      As shown in Table 7, the type and order of temporal embeddings influence model performance.

475   encoding exhibits lower performance across all metrics. These findings suggest that processing the
476   event time before the event type improves event type prediction, while adjusting the embedding
477   strategy can optimize model performance for different metrics.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduced TPP-LLM, a novel framework for modeling temporal point processes
532
      (TPP) by leveraging the pretrained knowledge of large language models (LLMs). By integrating
533
      LLMs with temporal embeddings, our approach effectively captures both the event semantics and
534   the temporal dynamics of complex event sequences. Through extensive experiments on real-world
535   datasets, we demonstrated that TPP-LLM outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in terms of sequence
536   modeling and next event prediction. Additionally, our ablation studies revealed the contributions of
537   foundation models, temporal embeddings, prompt design, and fine-tuning strategies to overall per-
538   formance. The robustness of TPP-LLM across diverse datasets and tasks highlights its potential for
539   broader applications in TPP modeling. Future work could explore alternative fine-tuning techniques
      and embedding strategies, as well as extend this approach to multi-task settings.

1006   F.3   I NTENSITY F UNCTIONS
1007
1008   We conduct an ablation study to compare three intensity functions adapted for TPP-Llama, all
1009   leveraging hi as the history vector. The modified THP intensity function (Zuo et al., 2020),
1010   softplus(αk (t − ti ) + wk T hi + bk ), achieves the best overall performance, balancing flexibility
1011
       and stability in capturing temporal dynamics. The RMTPP intensity function (Du et al., 2016),
       exp(αk (t − ti ) + wk T hi + bk ), performs well in event type prediction but slightly underperforms
1012
       in event time prediction due to its exponential nature. The SAHP intensity function (Zhang et al.,
1013
       2020), softplus(µi + (ηi − µi ) exp(−γi (t − ti ))), where µi = gelu(W µhi), ηi = gelu(W ηhi),
1014   and γi = gelu(W γhi), shows lower performance across most metrics. These results highlight
1015   TPP-Llama’s robustness with different intensity functions while indicating that the modified THP
1016   intensity function provides the most consistent and reliable results for capturing temporal patterns
1017   effectively.
