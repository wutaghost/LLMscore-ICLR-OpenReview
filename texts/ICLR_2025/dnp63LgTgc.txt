011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have enabled input-based domain adaptation to unseen test-time
014            corruption for the classification problem. Nevertheless, while dense prediction
015            tasks share similar robustness issues with image-level classification, previous in-
016
               put adaptation work may fail to preserve the semantic information necessary for
               robust pixel-level prediction. To address the issue, we propose a novel diffusion-
017
               driven strategy that translates the corrupted inputs to the source domain (i.e., the
018
               training data domain), while also preserving the semantic information (i.e. high-
019            frequency shape information and low-frequency color information). We first stud-
020            ied how to leverage frequency filtering to guide the diffusion generation process
021            and analyze the influence of different filters. From our experiments, we observed
022            that utilizing both high and low spatial frequency information during diffusion
023            driven denoising can substantially improve the adaptation performance of dense
024            classification. This observation motivates us to develop a novel framework, i.e.
025            a predictive frequency filtering-driven diffusion (FDD) adaptation, where we pre-
026            dict the filters from the corrupted test-time inputs and use them to condition the
027
               diffusion denoising process. We design a Y-like frequency prediction network to
               predict context-aware low-pass and high-pass filters. To train this network, we
028
               propose a novel data augmentation method, FrequencyMix, to generate pairs of
029
               clean and corrupted images. We validate our method via extensive experiments on
030            two semantic segmentation datasets and two depth estimation datasets. Against a
031            broad range of common corruptions, we demonstrate that our method is competi-
032            tive with state of the art work.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Distribution shift between the testing (target) and training (source) distributions poses a considerable
038   challenge towards the scalable implementation of deep learning models (Filos et al. (2020),Rosche-
039   witz et al. (2023)). Variations in illumination levels, weather conditions, and image quality Liang
040   et al. (2023) cause distribution shifts between the training and test data. Existing work enhance the
041   robustness of deep learning models to distribution shift either via model adaptation methods: by fine
042   tuning the task models with the unlabeled target domain data (Wang et al. (2021); Prabhudesai et al.
043   (2023)) or via input adaptation methods: by transforming the input target domain data to match the
044
      training data (Gao et al. (2023); Song & Lai (2023); Huang et al. (2023)).
045   The effectiveness of conditioning diffusion models (Zhang et al. (2023); Choi et al. (2021); Liu
046   et al. (2023); Nichol et al. (2022); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022)) to generate
047   user-defined outputs have motivated their use in denoising corrupted images (Gao et al. (2023); Choi
048   et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023b)). However, these methods apply predefined, image-level filters
049   to condition the diffusion model. In this work, we aim to study the use of context-aware kernels to
050
      enhance the effectiveness of diffusion-driven denoising for dense classification tasks.
051   However, we observe that low-frequency information is insufficient for dense classification because
052   crucial fine-grained edge information could be lost during denoising. Hence, diffusion-based TTA in
053   dense prediction needs to preserve both the high frequency (edge information) and the low-frequency
      (e.g. color) information.

      This observation drives our design to predict the spatially adaptive pixel-wise kernels from cor-
055   rupted images for conditioning of the diffusion model. To address this problem, we propose a simple
056   yet effective context-aware kernel prediction network to preserve semantic information during input
057   adaptation. Our key insight is that the semantic information necessary for good performance in dense
058   classification tasks comprises high spatial frequency information (e.g. edge information), and low
059   spatial frequency information (e.g. texture information). Furthermore, since it is non-trivial to
060   manually define robust frequency filters that effectively extract semantic information under varying
061   noise conditions, we propose to train a Y-like Frequency Prediction Network(Y-FPN) to condition
062   the diffusion model for input adaptation of dense classification tasks (semantic segmentation and
063
      depth estimation). To our best knowledge, this is the first work that incorporates learned spatially
      adaptive kernels to condition a denoising diffusion model for robust dense prediction.

      Test-time domain adaptation. Test Time Adaptation includes model adaptation, which involves
      finetuning the model during inference and input adaptation, which transforms the target domain data
094
      to resemble the source domain data. Kim & Byun (2020) observed that arbitrary style transfer-based
095
      approaches introduce image artifacts in the generated images which can hinder adaptation perfor-
096   mance. Unlike style transfer methods and other GAN-based approaches (Hoffman et al. (2018)),
097   diffusion models offer a greater degree of fine-grained control over the generated outputs (Nie et al.
098   (2022); Gao et al. (2023); Song & Lai (2023); Huang et al. (2023)) to purify them.
099
      Although numerous prior studies (Hu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021); Sivaprasad & Fleuret (2021);
100
      Wang et al. (2022); Prabhudesai et al. (2023); Shin et al. (2022)) have explored the integration of
101
      TTA into dense prediction tasks, existing work focus on fine tuning the task model with the target
102   domain data. Recently, Prabhudesai et al. (2023) apply the diffusion modeling process for model
103   adaptation. However, their method involves fine tuning both the diffusion model and the task model,
104   which requires considerable computational resources. In contrast, we neither update the diffusion
105   model nor the task model and our approach requires less computation during training. Instead of
106   applying a predefined image-level filter for conditioning, we propose a Y-like Frequency Prediction
107   Network (Y-FPN) that learns the high-frequency and low-frequency kernels for each pixel. These
      kernels can extract semantic information while being simultaneously robust to noise.

      Diffusion models. Diffusion modeling has recently gained widespread prominence among deep
109   generative models and demonstrates high generative capacity by iteratively refining inputs (Ho et al.
110   (2020); Song et al. (2021); Song & Ermon (2019; 2020); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Blattmann
111   et al. (2023); Nichol & Dhariwal (2021)). In essence, the idea behind diffusion, such as the denoising
112   diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) (Ho et al. (2020)), is to iteratively add noise to the data in the
113   "forward process", followed by training a network to recover the original data in a "reverse process".
114   Diffusion models also display a high degree of user controllability, allowing for conditioning on
115   visual cues (Choi et al. (2021)), natural language (Liu et al. (2023); Nichol et al. (2022)), and even
116   on class labels (Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022)). Recent work (Gao et al. (2023);
117
      Oh et al. (2024)) have demonstrated the effectiveness of diffusion models in projecting non-Gaussian
      corrupted data towards the source distribution. In particular, Gao et al. (2023) show the effectiveness
118
      of a predefined low pass filter for robust test-time input adaptation of image classification tasks.
119
      Inspired by their work, we design our learning framework to selectively capture both high-frequency
120   and low-frequency semantic information for test-time input adaptation involving dense classification
121   tasks.
122
123
      Frequency-aware processing. There is substantial ongoing interest in understanding deep learn-
      ing from a frequency-modelling perspective and leveraging this understanding to achieve robust and
124
      generalizable vision systems (Chattopadhyay et al. (2023); Choi et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2023);
125
      Xie et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023)). While it was earlier observed that classification models are
126   biased towards colour and texture information, it was also observed that vision models make predic-
127   tions based on a combination of low and high-frequency information (Yin et al. (2019); Wang et al.
128   (2023a)). Notably, Choi et al. (2021) demonstrated the effectiveness of conditioning the diffusion
129   model with predefined image-level frequency filters to enable user-defined image generation. Their
130   work motivated diffusion-based test-time input adaptation for robust classification. Gao et al. (2023)
131   employed predefined image-level filters to condition the pretrained diffusion model and preserve the
132   image level information during denoising of the corrupted images. Different from existing work, we
133   propose to learn the low-pass and high-pass kernels for conditioning diffusion-driven denoising as a
134
      defence against test-time corruptions.

      3.2     D ISCUSSION AND M OTIVATION
192
193   Based on the findings in Table 1, we observe the following: ❶ combining a high pass filter and a low
194   pass filter generally outperforms either filter during conditioning of diffusion driven denoising. As
195   expected, combining a high-pass filter with a low-pass filter improved robustness to low-frequency
196   corruptions (e.g. brightness) compared to a single low-pass filter. Additionally, we also observed
197   improved robustness to high frequency noise (e.g. Gaussian and Shot Noise). ❷ Conditioning the
198
      diffusion model with a high pass filter yields poorer performance compared to a low pass filter.
      While combining both filters improves test time adaptation, we observe that the high-pass filter may
199
      reduce denoising effectiveness (e.g. Zoom Blur, JPEG compression). This is because the high pass
200
      filter failed to remove the noise, leading to suboptimal conditioning of the diffusion model. This
201   indicates that much of the critical semantic information required for robust dense classification has
202   low spatial frequencies. Clearly, a predefined, image-level high pass filter is inadequate for test time
203   adaptation of dense classification tasks. To address this issue, we propose a novel context-aware
204   frequency filtering method in Section 4.

215   In this section, we present our frequency-based filtering method for robust test-time input adaptation.
      Applying a predefined, image level filter can degrade performance, especially if the filter removes

      semantic information or introduces noise. To achieve overall robustness to a wide variety of cor-
217   ruptions, we first consider the following challenge: how to improve robustness to spatially varying,
218   low-frequency and high-frequency noise?
219
      For example, corruptions such as "motion blur" vary spatially and disproportionately affect different
220
      parts of the image. While a straightforward solution would be to apply a uniform image-level low-
221   pass filter to preserve the texture information, this may inadvertently remove the edge information
222   required for dense classification. Furthermore, many real-world image corruptions are complex and
223   comprise several different effects (blurring, color shifts and occlusion). For example, corruptions
224   such as "glass blur" degrade the high-frequency information present in the image while simultane-
      ously introducing low-frequency noise. Therefore, an ideal filter must be spatially adaptive and
225
      robust to noise across a broad frequency range. To address this challenge, we adopt a pixel-wise
226   filtering approach, which is computationally efficient and effective in dealing with spatially varying
227   noise, for test-time adaptation of dense classification tasks. Specifically, we process a corrupted
228   image Ic ∈ RH×W for pixel-wise filtering,

      Our design of FrequencyMix is motivated by an earlier observation from Yin et al. (2019) that
      improving model robustness often involves a trade-off. While data augmentation corresponding to a
329
      particular frequency range improves robustness to noise from that range, it also worsens robustness
330
      to noise from the excluded frequencies. This motivated our design and application of FrequencyMix
331   to enhance the robustness of Y-FPN.

      As noted by Gao et al. (2023), task performance on uncorrected
      images may be better than that of images with diffusion-driven de-
503
      noising. They observed that diffusion models fail to restore images
504
      with naturalistic corruptions (e.g. “Fog”, “Snow”) because images with these corruptions occur in
505   the ImageNet training data. Consequently, Gao et al. (2023) average the model prediction logits
506   from the uncorrected image and the denoised images to mitigate the reduced performance. How-
507   ever, averaging logits is not possible for tasks such as depth estimation that do not involve logits.
508   Furthermore, the effectiveness of self ensembling is dependent on the model’s out of distribution per-
509   formance. Notably, Gao et al. (2023) observed increasing performance gains on ImageNet-C with
510   self ensembling as the task model increases in complexity (ResNet50:+1.3%, Swin-Tiny:+5.4%).
511   Though strong out of distribution performance of the task model is essential for test time adaptation
512   (Zhao et al. (2023)), we strived to develop a method that generalizes well to unseen data that reduces
513
      the need for a strong task model.
514   One potential solution is to introduce a noise-aware classifier (Luo et al. (2024)) that can charac-
515   terize the noise, enabling the use of specialized denoising techniques that are tailored to the noise
516   characteristics. However, such an approach would require extensive prior knowledge to train such
517   a classifier. Recently, Oh et al. (2024) demonstrate the effectiveness of finetuning the diffusion
518   model to improve robustness to image corruptions, which could also improve the performance of
519
      the denoising approach to naturalistic corruptions.

521   6     C ONCLUSION
522
523   We show that our approach, Frequency-driven Diffusion (FDD) Adaptation effectively improves
524   mean robustness over a broad range of noise corruption for input adaptation of dense classification
525   tasks and different model architectures (CNN and transformer-based architectures). We show that
526   our approach improves performance over previous diffusion-based input adaptation work, increasing
527   its applicability beyond image-level classification tasks. Additionally, with the introduction of an
528   easily trained module, our approach demonstrates improved robustness to diverse corruptions across
529
      different tasks, highlighting the effectiveness of our frequency-driven diffusion approach for input
      adaptation. Future work will also explore the possibility of extending our work towards purifying
530
      adversarially perturbed images.
