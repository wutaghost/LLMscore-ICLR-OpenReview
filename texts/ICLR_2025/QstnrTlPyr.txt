009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Modeling biological sequences such as DNA, RNA, and proteins is crucial for
012            understanding complex processes like gene regulation and protein synthesis. How-
013            ever, most current models either focus on a single type or treat multiple types of
014            data separately, limiting their ability to capture cross-modal relationships. We
015            propose that by learning the relationships between these modalities, the model can
016
               enhance its understanding of each type. To address this, we introduce BSM, a
               small but powerful mixed-modal biological sequence foundation model, trained on
017
               three types of data: RefSeq, Gene Related Sequences, and interleaved biological
018
               sequences from the web. These datasets capture the genetic flow, gene-protein
019            relationships, and the natural co-occurrence of diverse biological data, respectively.
020            By training on mixed-modal data, BSM significantly enhances learning efficiency
021            and cross-modal representation, outperforming models trained solely on unimodal
022            data. With only 110M parameters, BSM achieves performance comparable to
023            much larger models across both single-modal and mixed-modal tasks, and uniquely
024            demonstrates in-context learning capability for mixed-modal tasks, which is absent
025            in existing models. Further scaling to 270M parameters demonstrates even greater
026            performance gains, highlighting the potential of BSM as a significant advancement
027
               in multimodal biological sequence modeling.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Biological sequences—such as DNA, RNA, and proteins—are fundamental to an organism’s functions,
032   as they encode genetic information that determines structure, function, and regulatory mechanisms
033   (Watson & Crick, 1953; Nirenberg & Matthaei, 1961). Understanding these sequences is vital for
034   unraveling the mysteries of biological evolution, deciphering disease mechanisms, and elucidating
035   molecular interactions.
036   By applying machine learning algorithms to large-scale biological sequence data, one can capture
037   evolutionary effects and extract complex patterns in gene transcription and protein translation. This
038   not only enhances our understanding of gene regulation and protein function but also enables the
039   prediction and generation of complex biological functions, significantly advancing our comprehension
040   of biology and life processes (Nguyen et al., 2024a).
041
      Despite the rapid advancements in modeling biological sequences with machine learning, cur-
042   rent efforts have primarily focused on creating unimodal models specialized for DNA, such as
043   DNABert2 (Zhou et al., 2023), HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2024b), Caduceus (Schiff et al., 2024),
044   NT (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023); RNA, including RNA-FM (Chen et al., 2022); or proteins, like
045   ESM2 (Lin et al., 2023), ProTrans (Ahmed et al., 2020), ProGen2 (Nijkamp et al., 2023). However,
046   complex biological processes such as gene regulation, CRISPR immunity, and genetic transposition
047   involve interactions across multiple modalities.
048   Recently, several methods have focused on developing models capable of handling both gene and
049   protein data. For example, Evo (Nguyen et al., 2024a) is a 7B genomic foundation model pretrained
050   on DNA sequences, which inherently contain the potential to express other modalities. It learns
051   from large genomic regions to capture systems-wide interactions and enables the design of more
052   sophisticated biological functions. LucaOne (He et al., 2024) is a 1.8B model that is trained on
053   both gene and protein data separately, allowing the model to process and analyze both types of
      data concurrently. Although these models demonstrate excellent performance across various tasks,

      In this paper, we explore the construction of high-quality biological sequence data. According to the
082
      central dogma of molecular biology (Crick, 1970), which highlights the sequential flow of genetic
083
      information, DNA, RNA, and proteins are intrinsically interconnected. We propose that by learning
084   the relationships among these three types of sequences, the model can enhance its understanding of
085   each modality. In light of this, we introduce three types of mixed-modal data for pretraining: RefSeq,
086   Gene Related Sequences, and interleaved biological sequences from the web. These datasets capture
087   genetic flow, gene and protein relationships, and the natural co-occurrence of diverse biological data
088   types, respectively. Unlike previous work, which trains models using unimodal data or combines
089   different types in a simplistic manner (where each training sample consists of only one type), we
090   explicitly train the model on this mixed-modal data to learn the relationships among them.
091   We emphasize that incorporating mixed-modal data facilitates a more comprehensive understanding
092   of biological sequences and enables more effective acquisition of cross-modal representations by
093   better learning the relationships between these modalities. As illustrated in Figure 1, our experiments
094   reveal that relying on unimodal data to learn these capabilities results in slow learning efficiency and
095   requires substantial data and model sizes. In contrast, training on mixed-modal data significantly
096   lowers validation loss, computed on the same Round 1 data across rounds, indicating greatly improved
097   learning efficiency and better representation across all single modalities.
098   Based on these newly introduced types of high-quality mixed-modal data, we develop a small but
099   powerful biological sequence foundation model, BSM, through a structured multi-round training
100   approach. In this process, we conduct three rounds of training, progressively incorporating different
101   types of mixed-modal data in the latter two rounds. By employing an annealing strategy, we optimize
102   the data mix to ensure the best possible integration of these datasets. Our experiments demonstrate
103   that BSM achieves performance comparable to that of billion-scale models on both single-modal and
104
      complex mixed-modal tasks. This proves the effectiveness of our method and its significant potential.
105   To conclude, our work makes the following contributions: 1) We propose that explicitly learning the
106   relationships between genes and proteins can enhance the model’s understanding of each modality.
107   We introduce three types of mixed-modal data and strategically integrate them with unimodal data,
      ultimately resulting in our small but powerful BSM model. 2) We conduct extensive experiments

141   2.1     A RCHITECTURE
142
143
      BSM employs a single-nucleotide tokenizer with a vocabulary that includes nucleotides, amino acids,
      and special tokens. It uses an autoregressive architecture to model biological sequences such as genes
144
      and proteins. By learning next-token prediction, the model reasons over sequences causally and
145
      captures statistical patterns and dependencies in the training data, enabling effective representation
146   and generation of biological sequences. Furthermore, the autoregressive architecture’s sequential
147   nature effectively handles long-range dependencies, which is crucial in biological sequences like
148   DNA, RNA, and proteins, where long-context information can reveal critical functional relationships
149   or structural interactions.
150
      The BSM family includes models of two sizes, specifically BSM-110M and BSM-270M. BSM-
151
      110M is a decoder-only Transformer with 12 layers, each having 12 attention heads and a hidden
152   dimension of 768, and BSM-270M features 20 layers, 16 attention heads, and a hidden dimension of
153   896. Both models utilize rotary position embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) with a base frequency
154   hyperparameter of 100,000 and support a context length of 1024 tokens. To accelerate training, we
155   employ flash-attention mechanisms (Dao, 2023).

157   2.2     P RETRAINING DATA
158
159   High-quality biological data play a key role in developing effective models for biological sequences.
160   In addition to using unimodal protein and gene data, we incorporate three types of mixed-modal data
161   for continued pretraining, each containing valuable information that helps the model learn diverse
      dependencies and interactions in biological sequences, as well as critical functional relationships.

162
      These multi-modal datasets enhance the model’s learning efficiency and its ability to understand
163   cross-modal relationships, enabling it to handle various biological data. In the following section, we
164   introduce the data used in pretraining. Details of the data construction are listed in the Appendix. B.
165   Additionally, we perform data cleansing by removing instances of the downstream task test data from
166   the pretraining data.
167
      Single-modal Data In the initial pretraining phase, we utilize single-modal datasets that exclusively
168
      contain nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) sequences or protein sequences, enabling the model to concentrate
169   on understanding the essential structures, patterns, and unique characteristics of each modality. These
170   datasets are sampled from the pretraining dataset of LucaOne (He et al., 2024), and we only use
171   the sequence information from RefSeq (O’Leary et al., 2016), UniProt (Consortium, 2015) and
172   ColabFold (Mirdita et al., 2022) without incorporating additional biological annotations, resulting
173   in a data volume of 220 billion tokens. Single-modal data not only enhances the model’s ability
174   to understand individual modality sequences but also establishes a solid foundation for continued
175   pretraining on more complex multimodal data.
176   Mixed-modal Pair Data - NCBI RefSeq To better learn the relationships among DNA, RNA, and
177   proteins, we incorporate mixed-modal data from the NCBI RefSeq database (O’Leary et al., 2016).
178   RefSeq provides a comprehensive and curated collection of annotated reference sequences that
179   illustrate the flow of genetic information in the central dogma of molecular biology—DNA to RNA
180   to protein. This resource is crucial for facilitating the understanding of gene-protein interactions and
181   regulatory mechanisms, capturing essential details about transcription and translation processes.
182   We use data from 15 different species, including Bubalus Bubalis, Camelus Dromedarius, Human,
183   and several others. Each species has its own unique DNA, RNA, and protein sequences, which are
184   used to construct our gene-protein pairing dataset, resulting in a dataset of 9.2 billion tokens. This
185   extensive data ensures a rich representation of genetic information, allowing the model to leverage
186   diverse biological contexts for effective learning while recognizing the inherent links between DNA
187   sequences and their corresponding proteins.
188   Mixed-modal Pair Data - Gene Related Sequences To better understand the complex relationships
189   between gene-gene and gene-protein, we incorporate data from the NCBI Gene database (Brown
190   et al., 2015), which offers a detailed collection of gene-related sequences and annotations. The Gene
191   Related Sequences data within this collection contains related sequences to the gene and provides
192   links to the corresponding records in Entrez Nucleotide (Maglott et al., 2010),Entrez Protein (Ostell,
193   2012) or UniProtKB (Boutet et al., 2007).
194   We have constructed a dataset from the Gene Related Sequences data by sampling from multiple
195   species, resulting in a diverse collection of 8.3 billion tokens. This dataset enables the model to
196   capture complex dependencies, recognize patterns of gene regulation and protein expression, and
197   enhances its understanding of gene and protein functions.
198
      Mixed-modal Interleaved Data - Filtered Web Data To simulate the natural co-occurrence of
199   diverse biological data types and provide the model with a more realistic learning context, we
200   integrate filtered web data from FineWeb-Edu (Penedo et al., 2024), which consists of high-quality
201   web-crawled documents. We specifically filter this data to extract biological sequences within
202   documents, using a special token, <sep>, to separate interleaved biological sequences, resulting in a
203   final dataset of 33 million tokens.
204
      By incorporating this curated dataset into our pretraining process, BSM can leverage a rich and diverse
205   collection of arbitrarily interleaved biological sequences. This dataset features a greater number of
206   interleaved sequences, enhancing the model’s ability to capture complex biological relationships and
207   enabling it to understand and generate gene and protein sequences in any arbitrary context.

      2.3   P RETRAINING P ROCEDURE
210
211   Three-round Training We pretrain BSM models from scratch in an end-to-end manner, which
212   includes a three-round training process, as shown in Figure 2. It begins by establishing a foundational
213   understanding of individual types of biological sequences (DNA, RNA, or proteins) using 100B
214   single-modal tokens. The model then advances to incorporate multi-modal data, enhancing its ability
215   to understand relationships and transitions between different biological data types, which is crucial
      for tasks involving mixed modalities. Specifically, in the second round, it utilizes an additional 120B


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      single-modal data along with a certain amount of multi-modal pair data from RefSeq (24B) and Gene
217   Related Sequences (12B). In the third round, it trains on a small amount of high-quality mixed-modal
218   data, including pair data from RefSeq (1.3B) and Gene Related Sequences (1.3B) as well as web
219   interleaved data (13.3B). By upsampling these high-quality but relatively small multi-modal datasets
220   during continued pretraining, we significantly enhance the BSM model’s performance across a range
221   of biological tasks, making it a powerful tool for decoding the complexities of molecular biology.
222
      We set the learning rate to decay from 2e-5 to 1e-5 in the first round. In the second round, it decays
223   from 1e-5 to 1e-7, and in the third round, it decays from 1e-6 to 0. The tokenizer is trained with a
224   peak learning rate of 2e-5.
225
226
      Simulated Annealing & Data Mixing To obtain a high-quality biological model, it is essential to
      carefully determine the proportion of different data sources in pretraining. Similar to Blakeney et al.
227
      (2024) and Llama 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024), we find that upsampling and annealing help efficiently
228
      select the optimal ratio for mixing new mixed-modal datasets. We evaluate these datasets by training
229   the BSM-110M over 1000/500 steps with linearly annealed learning rates. Through these annealing
230   experiments, we identify the best data mixing ratio based on the lowest validation loss in the second
231   and third rounds. Ultimately, we employ a ratio of 10:2:1 for single-modal data, RefSeq, and Gene
232   Related Sequences in the second round, and a ratio of 1:1:10 for RefSeq, Gene Related Sequences,
233   and web interleaved data in the third round. After determining the best mix, we train a larger model
234   (BSM-270M) on this selected data mix.

236   3     E XPERIMENTS
237
238   We evaluate BSM’s capabilities in understanding and generating biological sequences across a
239   variety of tasks, including both mixed-modal and single-modal tasks. BSM demonstrates outstanding
240   performance on multi-modal tasks, even surpassing many billion-scale models. We also assess BSM’s
241   in-context learning (ICL) ability in a few-shot setting for mixed-modal tasks, demonstrating that
242   BSM possesses this capability, which has not yet been observed in other biological sequence models.
243   Additionally, we investigate the model’s supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and zero-shot performance
244   on protein and gene-related tasks. Scaling experiments confirm that further increasing the model
245   size continues to enhance its performance. We conduct an ablation study on the performance of
246
      models from different rounds to verify the value of multi-modal data. Finally, we also evaluate the
      model’s generative abilities using the perplexity metric. Details of evaluation datasets are listed in the
247
      Appendix C.
248
249   Implementation Details For tasks requiring SFT, we fine-tune the model using a learning rate of
250   1e-6 and a batch size of 16. For tasks that require two sequences as input, other baseline models
251   lack the ability to simultaneously process both sequences, especially when it comes to handling gene
252
      and protein pairs. Instead, they use a dual-tower structure, employing two independent encoders to
      encode each sequence separately. In contrast, BSM directly connects the two sequences as input
253
      using a <sep> token, allowing the model to evaluate their relationship directly in a unified context.

268   performance of billion-scale biological models, such as DNABert2 + ESM2-3B and LucaOne 1.8B.

      Few-shot Learning In the few-shot learning setting, we concatenate few-shot demonstrations with
297
      the test sample, each containing a DNA and protein sequence, as input for BSM. We then calculate
298   the log probability for each token in the tested protein sequence. This allows us to obtain the overall
299   generation probability for the tested protein sequence. We then set a threshold for this generation
300   probability to classify whether the protein is linked to the tested DNA, and subsequently compute the
301   prediction accuracy.
302
      Despite not being fine-tuned, the BSM model demonstrates strong performance in identifying correct
303
      DNA-protein associations. The experiments show that increasing the number of demonstrations
304   further enhances performance, with the few-shot learning results approaching those of SFT. This
305   experiment highlights BSM’s in-context learning capability, particularly in mixed-modal tasks, a
306   capability that other existing models do not possess.

500   4     R ELATED W ORK
501
502   4.1   B IOLOGICAL S EQUENCE M ODEL
503
      Modeling biological sequences has traditionally involved unimodal approaches tailored to specific
504
      data types, such as DNA, RNA, or proteins. While significant progress has been made with models
505
      like DNABert2 (Zhou et al., 2023), RNA-FM (Chen et al., 2022), and ESM2 (Lin et al., 2023),
506   these models often struggle with capturing complex mixed-modal interactions inherent in biological
507   processes. Recent advancements, such as LucaOne (He et al., 2024) and Evo (Nguyen et al., 2024a),
508   have begun to handle both gene and protein data, demonstrating the potential of large-scale models in
509   modeling multi-modal biological data. However, insufficient attention has been given to exploring
510   diverse data, especially high-quality mixed-modal data, which is crucial for models to acquire
511   comprehensive capabilities. Our work proves that learning from mixed-modal data significantly
512   enhances learning efficiency and improves both single and mixed-modal representations.
513
514   4.2   S MALL L ANGUAGE M ODEL
515
516
      Small Language Models (SLMs) like Phi (Gunasekar et al., 2023) and Gemma 2 (Team et al., 2024)
      illustrate that with strategic training approaches, such as high-quality data utilization and knowledge
517
      distillation, SLMs can achieve impressive performance. Unlike current trends in biological sequence
518
      modeling that focus on scaling model size to the billion-parameter level, our research explores the
519   potential of leveraging rich and high-quality mixed-modal bio-sequence data, which has rarely been
520   studied or utilized in this field. We demonstrate that introducing mixed-modal data can enable smaller
521   models to achieve performance close to or even surpass that of billion-scale models, highlighting the
522   critical importance of data quality and diversity. Our work strongly demonstrates the tremendous
523   potential of expanding both mixed-modal data and model size, paving the way for more powerful
524   models in bioinformatics.
525
526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this study, we have demonstrated that high-quality mixed-modal biological data is essential for
529   enhancing both cross-modal and single-modal learning capabilities in our BSM models. The results
530   indicate that protein-gene interleaving data has considerable potential to improve model performance,
531   highlighting the importance of data quality in training effective biological models.
532
      However, our research has certain limitations. We utilized only a partial dataset from RefSeq and
533
      Gene Related Sequence data, which lacks exploration of other valuable types of cross-modal data,
534   such as gene-protein interactions data. Additionally, we mined only a relatively small dataset of
535   interleaved biological sequences from the web, it still yielded continuous improvements in model
536   performance. This suggests that there is substantial room for further investigation, and we believe that
537   leveraging larger and more diverse datasets could enhance our model’s capabilities even further. Our
538   future work will focus on exploring additional types of cross-modal data to fully realize the potential
539   of mixed-modal approaches in biological sequence modeling and contribute to advancements in the
      field.

      ncRPI The ncRPI dataset is specifically designed for evaluating computational methods that predict

749
      validated ncRNA-protein interaction pairs identified from various model organisms. Specifically,
750   the NPInter2.0 dataset contains 10,412 experimentally validated ncRNA-protein interaction pairs,
751   involving 4,636 ncRNAs and 449 proteins; the NPInter2.0_lncRNA dataset includes 4,158 lncRNA-
752   protein interaction pairs, involving 990 lncRNAs and 27 proteins; and the RPI7317 dataset contains
753   7,317 lncRNA-protein interaction pairs, involving 1,874 lncRNAs and 118 proteins. To generate
754   negative samples (i.e., non-interaction pairs), researchers randomly paired ncRNAs and proteins from
755   these datasets, creating an equal number of negative samples to the positive samples, ensuring that

      Central Dogma The central dogma dataset was meticulously designed and constructed to assess the
757   model’s ability to recognize the connection between DNA sequences and their corresponding proteins.
758   The dataset selected 8,533 precise DNA-protein pairs from 13 species in the NCBI-RefSeq database.
759   Each DNA sequence was extended to include an additional 100 nucleotides at both the 5’ and 3’
760   ends, with intron sequences preserved. To test the model’s discrimination capabilities, researchers
761   generated a number of negative samples double that of the positive samples by inserting, replacing,
762   or deleting nucleotides in the DNA sequences or altering amino acids in the protein sequences. All
763   samples were randomly allocated to the training, validation, and testing sets in a ratio of 4:3:25. The
764   dataset is characterized by the inclusion of both positive samples and negative samples generated
765
      through various editing methods, which helps test whether the model can identify the intrinsic links
      between DNA sequences and corresponding proteins.
766
767   PPI The PPI dataset is a valuable resource for assessing protein-protein interactions (PPIs), sourced
768   from the DeepPPI database, which includes a vast array of unique protein pairs. These datasets are
769   crucial for unraveling the complex molecular communication mechanisms within cells. Specifically,
770   the dataset comprises 59,766 training samples, 7,430 validation samples, and 7,425 test samples,
771
      collectively forming a binary classification task aimed at predicting whether a pair of protein sequences
      will interact. Each sample consists of a pair of protein sequences labeled as 1 (indicating interaction)
772
      or 0 (indicating no interaction). To ensure the accuracy and validity of the assessment, the dataset is
773
      meticulously divided to guarantee consistency in data distribution across the training, validation, and
774   test sets. When constructing predictive models using this dataset, researchers must consider how to
775   handle potential class imbalance issues and choose accuracy as the sole metric to measure model
776   performance.
777
      ProtLoc The ProtLoc dataset is a specialized dataset designed for predicting the subcellular localiza-
778
      tion of prokaryotic proteins. It comprises a curated selection of protein sequences from the UniProt
779
      and PSORTdb databases, with each sequence annotated for its specific subcellular location within the
780   cell, such as the cytoplasm, cytoplasmic membrane, periplasmic space, outer membrane, cell wall
781   and surface, and extracellular space, which are the six primary regions. This dataset is extensively
782   used in the field of bioinformatics to train and evaluate machine learning models for the accurate
783   prediction of protein subcellular localization. The dataset is divided into 9,915 training samples,
784   1,991 validation samples, and 1,131 test samples to support the training and assessment of models.
785   When constructing predictive models using the ProtLoc dataset, accuracy is adopted as the primary
786   evaluation metric to measure model performance.
787   ProtStab The ProtStab dataset is specifically designed for evaluating protein stability prediction
788   models, sourced from the TAPE project, and includes stability measurement data for a variety of
789   protein types, including natural proteins, mutants, and de novo designed proteins. The ProtStab dataset
790   provides researchers with a benchmark for quantifying protein stability, aiding in the understanding
791   of protein stability within biological systems and under different conditions. The dataset comprises
792   53,614 training samples, 2,512 validation samples, and 12,851 test samples, all of which support

797   Zero-shot Protein Fitness Prediction The Zero-shot Protein Fitness Prediction dataset is utilized for
798   evaluating a model’s ability to predict the impact of mutations on protein function without any task-
799   specific fine-tuning or supervision. This dataset encompasses multiple Deep Mutational Scanning
800
      (DMS) studies, which include exhaustive mutation scans of protein-coding sequences, along with
      experimentally measured fitness scores that quantify the protein’s ability to perform specific functions.
801
      The dataset comprises samples from both E. coli and human proteins, with the number of variants
802
      depending on the specific DMS study. Each sample consists of a protein sequence, the introduced
803   nucleotide mutations, and the corresponding fitness score. The sequences are preprocessed to fit
804   the input requirements of machine learning models. The performance of models on this dataset is
805   assessed using the Spearman’s Rank Correlation Coefficient (SRCC), which measures the strength and
806   direction of association between the model’s predictions and the experimental fitness measurements.
