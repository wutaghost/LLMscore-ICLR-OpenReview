009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful and general solutions to
012            many natural language tasks. However, many of the most important applications
013            of language generation are interactive, where an agent has to talk to a person to
014            reach a desired outcome. For example, a teacher might try to understand their
015            student’s current comprehension level to tailor their instruction accordingly, and a
016
               travel agent might ask questions of their customer to understand their preferences
               in order to recommend activities they might enjoy. LLMs trained with supervised
017
               fine-tuning or “single-step” RL, as with standard RLHF, might struggle which tasks
018
               that require such goal-directed behavior, since they are not trained to optimize
019            for overall conversational outcomes after multiple turns of interaction. In this
020            work, we explore a new method for adapting LLMs with RL for such goal-directed
021            dialogue. Our key insight is that, though LLMs might not effectively solve goal-
022            directed dialogue tasks out of the box, they can provide useful data for solving
023            such tasks by simulating human-like behaviors. Given a textual description of
024            a goal-directed dialogue task, we leverage LLMs to synthesize hypothetical in-
025            domain human-human interactions. Our algorithm then utilizes this dataset with
026            offline reinforcement learning to train an interactive conversational agent that can
027
               optimize multi-step objectives. Empirically, we show that our proposed approach
               achieves state-of-the-art performance in various goal-directed dialogue tasks that
028
               include teaching and preference elicitation.

053   The difference between an agent that simply mimics the flow of a conversation and one that pursues
      conversational goals becomes more apparent when we consider how uncertainty influences the


                                                        1
054
      conversation. Whether you as the end user are asking the agent to instruct you about a new AI
055   concept, or to plan an itinerary for an upcoming vacation, you have priviledged information which
056   the agent does not know, but which is crucial for the agent to do the task well; e.g., your current
057   background of AI knowledge matters when learning a new concept, and your travel preferences
058   matter when you plan a vacation. As illustrated in Figure 1, a goal-directed agent would gather
059   the information it needs to succeed, perhaps by asking clarification questions (e.g., are you an
060   active person?) and proposing partial solutions to get feedback (e.g., how does going to the beach
061   sound?). However, today’s LLMs largely fail at this, and are more likely to attempt a helpful but
062   poorly informed guess right away than to ask appropriate questions. And as we will show in the
063
      experiments, even when carefully prompted to gather information, they comply but generate verbose
      and overwhelming questions that are not good at getting the right information.
064
065   In principle, reinforcement learning (RL) can offer a very powerful tool for bridging this gap: LLMs
066   trained with RL to achieve conversational goals (such as maximizing the probability that the user
067   will accept the planned itinerary) could take goal-directed steps, ask clarifying questions, elicit
068   preferences, be very clear and concise in its responses, and maybe even build a rapport with the user.
069
      But RL requires data, either in the form of online interactions with a human simulator, or offline
      human-human interactions. Online data can be computationally difficult obtain, and offline data must
070
      be carefully curated to optimize desirable properties such as coverage and diversity (Fu et al., 2020b;
071
      Gulcehre et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2022).
072
073   Our key idea is that we can enable zero-shot goal-directed dialogue agents by tapping into what
074   LLMs are great at — emulating diverse realistic conversations; and tapping into what RL is great
075
      at — optimizing multi-step objectives. We propose to use LLMs to “imagine" a range of possible
      task-specific dialogues that are often realistic, but where the LLM does not optimally solve the task.
076
      In effect, the LLM can imagine what a human could do, but not to what an optimal agent should do.
077
      Conversations are then generated based on sampled hidden states. We train an agent to engage in
078   goal-directed conversation by training offline RL on the resulting dataset.
079
080   Our main contribution is a zero-shot RL algorithm that effectively optimizes for goal-directed dialogue
081
      tasks. Rather than directly using pretrained LLMs as optimal agents, our method aims to leverage
      their strength in emulating diverse, human-like, but suboptimal conversations to generate data, which
082
      can then be provided to an RL algorithm to actually discover more optimal behaviors. We propose
083
      a novel system called the imagination engine (IE) that generates a dataset of diverse, task-relevant,
084   and instructive dialogues to be used to train downstream agents. We evaluate our approach on tasks
085   involving teaching of a new concept, persuasion, and preference elicitation. Our experimental results
086   include a user study that compares agents trained with our method to prompted state-of-the-art LLMs,
087   showing that our method can attain significantly better results in interactive conversations even when
088   using models that are orders of magnitude smaller than the prompt-based baseline.

090   2    R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Language models. Language models, particularly LLMs, have shown impressive capabilities in
093
      text generation (Ghazvininejad et al., 2017; Li et al., 2017; Holtzman et al., 2018; Radford et al.,
      2019; Yang & Klein, 2021), translation (Gu et al., 2017), question answering (Pyatkin et al., 2022),
094
      summarization (Paulus et al., 2017; Wu & Hu, 2018; Böhm et al., 2019), and code generation (Chen
095
      et al., 2021b; Zhong & Wang, 2023). However, success at most of these tasks is largely enabled by
096   supervised learning, and does not require reasoning through multiple steps of interaction to optimize
097   a long-term objective. LLMs have been fine-tuned via supervised learning to engage in dialogue with
098   human users to some success (He et al., 2018; Shuster et al., 2022b;a), but primarily to only produce
099   realistic responses
100
      RL for language models. Many existing LLMs leverage reinforcement learning (RL) fine-tuning,
101
      where a reward model is learned from feedback directly from human experts (Ziegler et al., 2020;
102   Stiennon et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Christiano et al., 2023)
103   or secondhand from a handcrafted AI system (Bai et al., 2022b), and is then used to fine-tune the
104   LLM via an RL objective. While finetuning is primarily done via online RL, recent approaches
105   proposed tuning LLMs from offline data (Rafailov et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023). By doing so,
106   LLMs are able to faithfully follow human instructions, or prompts, and can therefore act as general
107   problem solvers by prompt engineering (Ouyang et al., 2022). While effective, one stark downside of
      RL fine-tuning approaches is that they only consider bandit objectives. Specifically, in RL fine-tuning,


                                                         2
108
      LLMs are trained to maximize the learned reward model within a single-step response, and not over
109   the course of a multi-step dialogue. As a result, if the best response to a query is unknown due to latent
110   information, such as intentions or preferences, by the user, traditional LLMs will only provide the
111   best possible “guess" response in one step, and not attempt to gather additional information in order to
112   respond more optimally. Notably, Glaese et al. (2022) propose learning an information-seeking agent,
113   but again consider a single-step objective based on maximizing helpfulness, and do not consider nor
114   evaluate on tasks where gathering information is used to accomplish a long-term goal; the approach
115   also relies on human raters being able to identify useful information-seeking actions.
116   Goal-directed dialogue. There has been numerous prior works on learning models to accomplish
117   tasks via conversations beyond maximizing informativeness or humanness. Goal-directed dialogue, or
118   alternatively task-oriented dialogue, can be formulated as an MDP from which agents can be trained
119   using RL. Online RL methods to optimize dialogue agents typically require a simulator of human
120   behavior, that is usually either handcrafted, or learned as a fixed model (Carta et al., 2023; He et al.,
121   2018; Gašić et al., 2011). Moreover, they involve continual collection of new samples, which incurs
122   a large computational cost in tasks where humans exhibit complex and nuanced behaviors, and are
123
      often prone to reward “hacking" (Skalse et al., 2022). Alternatively, offline RL approaches have also
      been considered that only require a static dataset of dialogues (Jaques et al., 2019; Jang et al., 2022;
124
      Verma et al., 2022; Snell et al., 2023). Notably, Verma et al. (2022) propose an offline RL algorithm
125
      to solve a goal-directed dialogue based on negotiations using a dataset of conversations between
126   human speakers. However, in order for offline RL to improve over supervised learning, the dataset
127   must be carefully curated to optimize desirable properties such as coverage and diversity (Fu et al.,
128   2020b; Gulcehre et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2022), which may limit its practicality. Orthogonally,
129   recent approaches to learning dialogue agents often do not use RL, but rely on natural language
130   instructions, or prompts, to guide LLMs towards accomplishing various tasks. These prompts are often
131   manually engineered (Brown et al., 2020; Reynolds & McDonell, 2021; Wei et al., 2022), or more
132   recently, optimized via supervised learning (Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). However, we believe
133   prompt-engineering is insufficient to solving general goal-directed dialogue tasks, especially when
134
      desired behaviors are difficult to describe using only task-specific instructions and demonstrations.
      Furthermore, prompt-learning primarily has only considered single-step objectives and often requires
135
      access to human annotations of hints.
136
137   Dialogue benchmarks. Dialogue benchmark datasets have been created that aim to evaluate the
138   capabilities of agents at accomplishing various tasks such as question-answering (Budzianowski
139   et al., 2020), customer service (Chen et al., 2021a), and negotiation (He et al., 2018). However, many
140
      such datasets are for tasks that do not necessitate personalizing the agent’s responses to each human.
      In this paper, we consider goal-directed dialogue tasks where humans behave differently due to latent
141
      factors, and agents must gather information and personalize to each human. Because of this added
142
      complexity, curating a human-human dataset with diverse human behaviors can be prohibitively
143   difficult.
144
145   Knowledge distillation. Our proposed imagination engine can be considered an instance of knowl-
146
      edge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), where knowledge from a large model (in our case, LLMs) is
      used to train a smaller model. Recently, this has become popular with LLMs acting as the teacher
147
      model, and synthetically generating new training examples for the smaller model (Taori et al., 2023;
148
      Chiang et al., 2023; Kim & Rush, 2016). While our approach is similar in principle, all prior ap-
149   proaches consider only downstream supervised learning objectives. To our knowledge, we are the
150   first to do synthetic dialogue generation for RL.

154   Markov decision processes. To formulate dialogue as a decision making problem, we use the
155   formalism of the Markov decision process (MDP), given by a tuple M = (S, A, P, r, ρ, γ), where S
156   is the state space, A is the action space, P is the transition function, r is the reward function, ρ is the
157   initial state distribution, and γ is the discount factor. When action a ∈ A is executed at state s ∈ S,
158
      the next state is sampled s′ ∼ P (·|s, a), and the agent receives reward r with mean r(s, a).
159   Goal-directed dialogues as MDPs. Goal-directed dialogue can be viewed as an MDP, where states
160   are sequences of tokens from a finite vocabulary V (Ramamurthy et al., 2023). All tokens that the
161   agent initially observes are used as our initial state, s0 = (x0 , . . . , xm ), where xi ∈ V, ∀i ∈ [m].
      At timestep t, an action at ∈ V is some token in the vocabulary. As long as at is not a special

187   In many real-world tasks that require dialogue with a human, humans exhibit a range of different
188   behaviors. For example, in a travel agent task, humans will respond differently to the agent according
189   to their own activity interests, budget, and other personal factors. Such factors are often latent,
190   but affect how an optimal agent should respond. Rather than conventional MDPs, these tasks can
191   instead be formulated as hidden parameter MDPs (Doshi-Velez & Konidaris, 2013), given by a

193
      practice, solutions to hidden parameter MDPs do not need to model Z explicitly, and instead use a
      sequence model (i.e., a standard language model) to handle implicitly infer it from the history of
194
      observations. Nevertheless, we view Z as helpful formalism for understanding why information-
195
      gathering is important in effective dialogue agents.

209   4    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING ON I MAGINED C ONVERSATIONS
210
211   In this paper, we present an approach for zero-shot training of agents in a goal-directed dialogue
212   task. Rather than traditional offline RL approaches that require a curated dataset D of human-human
213   data, the only input required by our system is a task description D. The primary novelty of our
214   proposed approach is an imagination engine (IE) that enables the generation of a diverse dataset Db of
215   task-relevant dialogues for any task description D. Then, once the dialogue dataset D   b is generated,
      we optimize for an agent π b via offline RL on the imagined dialogues.

      4.1   I MAGINATION E NGINE : G ENERATE D IVERSE D IALOGUES
217
218
      We assume access to a LLM PLLM (· | p) that can be used to generate a response for any prompt p.
219
      The IE consists of three steps, which are outlined in Figure 1 and we describe below. We also provide
220   explicit examples of the process (including prompts used) for two different tasks in Appendix A.
221   Though our approach does rely on some prompting-engineering, we show that the prompts used are
222   quite natural and not overly-engineered. Furthermore, in Appendix A, we show some qualitative
223   evidence that different paraphrases of prompts do not noticeably affect the generation quality.
224
      Reasoning step: synthesize in-domain personas. Recall that goal-directed dialogues can be
225
      formulated as hidden-parameter MDPs with hidden space Z, where each human has a different z ∈ Z
226   that affects how they behave, and how an agent should optimally respond. Without access to prior data,
227   we would naïvely rely on having task-specific knowledge of Z. However, our insight is that LLMs
228   contain a much wider domain of knowledge than any individual human, and therefore can provide
229   task-specific knowledge when humans cannot. Therefore, we propose querying PLLM (· | fr (D)),
230   where fr (D) is a reasoning prompt using task description D; the prompt asks the LLM to output
231   textual descriptions of personas ϕ(z) for z ∈ Z. These descriptions ϕ(z) can be used to generate
232   human responses under different behaviors (Serapio-García et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023).
233   Imagination step: generate synthetic dialogues. The goal in this step is to imagine dialogues τ
234   between a (potentially suboptimal) agent and a human. Formally, this involves generating synthetic
235   rollouts in the underlying hidden parameter MDP. Note that in real world, samples from both the
236   transition function P and behavior policy πβ of the MDP are simply human responses. Therefore,
237   synthesizing trajectories reduces to simulating human-human dialogue.
238   In order to accomplish this, we leverage LLMs to generate synthetic dialogues between an agent and
239   human, where we condition generation on how the human behaves, and the reward that the agent
240   achieves. This is done as follows. First, we sample ϕ(z) for some persona z ∈ Z that we obtained
241   in the reasoning step, and also sample r ∈ {0, 1} indicating whether the agent fails or succeeds in
242   the generated dialogue. The assumption of binary rewards is only to be task-agnostic, and more
243   expressive rewards can be considered if they exist for a particular task. A conditional dialogue can be
244   sampled τ ∼ PLLM (· | fi (D, ϕ(z), r)) where fi (D, ϕ(z), r) is an imagination prompt that asks the
245   LLM to output a dialogue between two humans that is task-relevant, and where the human behaves
246
      according to z and the agent ultimately achieves reward r.
247   Critique step: refine dialogues. Though the synthetic dialogues from the imagination step are
248   mostly plausible interactions, the humans in the dialogue sometimes produce potentially unrealistic
249   responses. For example, humans in the dialogues often reveal their underlying personas, without
250   the agent asking any questions, or even building general rapport with them. Since inferring the
251   human’s persona is an important skill we want downstream learning agents to acquire, we want
252
      information-gathering strategies to be reflected in the imagined conversations, even if they are not
      deployed optimally or strategically (as would be required for the optimal agent).
253
254   To remedy this, we propose revising the imagined dialogues based on a set of criteria c on what
255   constitutes pedagogical conversations for our downstream learning. Our criteria c are task-specific
256   but generally include the following principles: (1) the “human” should not reveal their latent behavior
257   immediately, but only make it apparent gradually through back-and-forth conversation with the agent;
258
      (2) the human’s sentiment at the end of the dialogue should accurately reflect the reward that the agent
      achieves. Our criteria can be used analogously to a constitution to improve the quality of generated
259
      responses (Bai et al., 2022b). Formally, we sample a revised dialogue τ ′ ∼ PLLM (· | fc (D, τ, c))
260
      from the same LLM where critique prompt fc (D, τ, c) encapsulates the original dialogue and criteria
261
      c. Iterating the imagination and critique steps allows us to curate a dialogue dataset D.

      5    E XPERIMENTS
296
297   Hypotheses. Our experiments evaluate our proposed zero-shot dialogue agent training procedure
298   on two goal-directed dialogue tasks. The tasks require the agent to perform information gathering
299   in order to personalize their responses to the user, which necessitates goal-directed conversational
300   strategies such as asking clarifying questions, or building rapport with the user to better understand
301
      their intentions. We aim to answer the following research questions:
302   1. Is leveraging LLMs in our imagination engine to generate synthetic data to train downstream
303   agents preferred over using them naïvely to behave as agents themselves?
304
      2. Is offline RL on the imagined data better than simply using imitation learning on the same data?
305
306   The first research question targets our main hypothesis, that LLMs should be leveraged for generating
307   data rather than for solving goal-directed tasks. The second targets whether the specifics of how we
308
      train on the imagined data are important. We hypothesize that while in the average case both BC and
      RL perform similarly, the contrast between RL and BC agents is noticeable in situations that are not
309
      well represented in the imagined conversations. In particular, RL agents should be more robust when
310
      humans behave in ways that are not represented in any one dialogue in our imagined dataset, but
311   perhaps in concatenations of multiple dialogues. This is because RL agents are exhibited to compose
312   new strategies via a phenomenon called “trajectory stitching" (Fu et al., 2020a; Levine et al., 2020).
313
314
      Approaches. To answer both questions, we consider a variety of prompting and training baselines.
      For prompting, we consider the following, existing, state-of-the-art approaches:
315
316   GPT. This approach prompts GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2022), which is a powerful LLM shown in prior
317   work to be able to effectively solve numerous natural language tasks (Ouyang et al., 2022), to directly
318   behave as the agent. The prompt includes both the task description, as well as the insight that the
319   resulting agent needs to gather information about the human user in order to optimally respond to
320
      them. This is the traditional usage of LLMs to solve dialogue tasks.
321   CLAM. In addition to only prompting with the task description, Kuhn et al. (2023) propose prompting
322   LLMs with the option of asking clarification questions. We consider this approach where LLMs are
323   either allowed to directly respond to the user, or ask a question by analyzing the ambiguity of the
      user’s request.


                                                                    6
324
      GDP-ZERO. Yu et al. (2023) propose taking this one step further, by allowing LLMs to perform
325   tree search over whether or not they should ask clarification questions. While initially proposed for a
326   different benchmark, we adapt it to our domains.
327
328
      Due to space, we only show results for naive prompting in the main paper, but defer implementation
      details and results for the more sophisticated prompting mechanisms in Appendix D. In addition to
329
      prompting, also evaluate against ablations of our approach:
330
331   IE+BC (ablation). This version of our approach trains an agent on the imagined dataset generated
332   by our proposed imagination engine, but via a behavioral cloning (BC) objective, where the agent
333   straightforwardly mimics the synthetic data. This is equivalent to supervised fine-tuning on the
334
      imagined dataset. This is an ablation of our proposed approach.
335   IE+FBC (ablation). Rather than BC on the entire imagined dataset, this method trains the agent
336   using filtered BC instead, which imitates only the successful trajectories in the dataset. This is another
337   ablation of our proposed approach.
338   IE+RL. This is the full version of our approach, which trains the agent using offline RL. Specifically,
339   we use ILQL (Snell et al., 2022) as the offline RL algorithm.
340
341
      Across methods that use the imagination engine, we use GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2022) as the LLM in
      the imagination engine to generate synthetic data. However, our downstream agents that are trained
342
      on imagined data utilize a much smaller decoder-only GPT-2 model (Radford et al., 2019). This
343
      is to show that we only need to leverage state-of-the-art LLMs to generate data, as the resulting
344   agents can be much smaller; this makes our approach practical if computational cost is an important
345   consideration, or if modern LLMs are deployed with only inference APIs, which are both hurdles
346   encountered in our empirical evaluations. For each task, we defer implementation details (including
347   prompts used and results) to Appendix A.
348
      User study setup. We test our two hypotheses in a two-part user study with 12 users. To test whether
349
      leveraging LLMs for the imagination engine is better than directly using LLMs as agents through
350   prompting, we use a within-subjects design and have users interact with GPT and IE+RL (in a
351   randomized order to avoid ordering effects) across two tasks, which we describe below. We then ask
352   them to rate the agents, for each task, according to four criteria, on a 5-point Likert scale:
353
      (A) How well the agent accomplished the task at hand.
354
      (B) How realistic and natural the resulting dialogue was.

      (C) How effective was the agent at asking questions relevant for information-gathering.

469   5.3 I S O FFLINE RL B ETTER T HAN BC?
470   Next, we address the second research question: is training on the imagined data with RL more
471   effective than directly imitating it with supervised learning? Recall that we posit that RL optimization
472   outperforms imitation learning in challenging scenarios where strategies exactly reflected in the
473   data do not suffice. To get such examples, we pose as humans who exhibit potential challenging
474   behaviors and interact with agents. Specifically, in the instruction task, we consider humans who
475   overestimate their understanding of a particular concept. By doing so, an agent’s understanding
476   of the human’s knowledge background will not align with their true background, resulting in the
477
      user not understanding the agent’s explanation. Meanwhile, in the preference elicitation task, we
      consider users who express discontent with the agent’s initial recommendation. For each task, we
478
      generated three dialogue prefixes of challenging situations, then evaluate the capability of the IE+BC,
479
      IE+FBC, and IE+RL agents to recover from them. Then, we show such dialogues to each user in
480   the user study, and ask the user to rate the capabilities of each agent for the same metrics (A) to (D).
481   The results are reported in Table 2, where we see clear improvement of the IE+RL agent, especially
482   in asking effective information-gathering questions. In Appendix B.2, we show corresponding
483   dialogues of conversations with the IE+FBC and IE+RL agents in the instruction task. There, the
484   user expresses confusion with the agent’s explanation. The IE+FBC agent decides to paraphrase the
485   prior explanation, whereas the IE+RL agent decides to ask more questions to understand the user’s
      background better. Then, also in Appendix B.2, we show corresponding examples in the preference

489   6   D ISCUSSION
490
      In this paper, we propose an algorithm that achieves zero-shot acquisition of goal-directed dialogue
491
      agents. The approach leverages a novel imagination engine, which generates a synthetic dialogue
492   dataset that is task-relevant, realistic, and exhibits diverse behaviors. The imagined dataset can then
493   be used to train dialogue agents via offline RL optimization. The key hypothesis that our work
494   demonstrates is that LLMs should not be used directly as goal-directed dialogue agents, but rather
495   as generators for dialogue that can be used for downstream optimization. We show, on a variety
496   of dialogue tasks including teaching and preference elicitation, that our approach is a much more
497   effective usage of LLMs than traditional approaches that prompt LLMs to act directly as agents.
498   Limitations. Overall, our approach avoids the careful curation of human-human dialogue traditionally
499   used to train dialogue agents via RL. However, we still require human intervention in the form of
500   task-specific prompts. Future work can aim to automate this process further, so that a zero-shot
501   dialogue agent can be trained from any task description. Another important avenue of future work is
502   to extend our approach to the general, multi-task setting, which would require scaling up the data
503   generation and size of models. We view our paper as presenting a prototype approach to achieving
504   generalist dialogue agents, and not a complete replacement of existing LLMs such as GPT.

       In addition to the user study on 12 human users, we also conduct a larger scale evaluation of the
1353   GPT and IE-RL agents in simulation. Rather than pairing the agents with real humans, we instead
1354   consider “simulated" humans whose responses are generated by GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2022). We only
1355   do this on the preference elicitation task where there is a clear measure of success – whether the
1356   agent recommends an activity that the simulated human enjoys. Specifically, we prompt GPT with a
1357   sampled persona comprising of a specific activity that they would enjoy, as well as a personality type
1358   that affects their behavior or preferences (e.g., adventurous, luxury-seeker, indecisive, etc.), and ask it
1359   to respond to the dialogue so far in a manner that is consistent with their persona, without outright
1360   revealing it to the agent. We measure whether each agent is able to recommend the ground-truth
1361
       activity that is in the prompt of the simulated human within 15 total utterances (which includes both
       the agent and simulated human).
1362
1363   We report results in Table 4 across 50 simulated humans, whose personas are uniformly sampled
1364   at random. We found that because simulated humans were prompted with a ground-truth activity,
1365   it was much easier for agents to elicit their preferences, as the simulated humans would often give
1366   straightforward responses regarding what they enjoy (whereas a real human who is unsure what
1367
       they like will give more vague responses that require gradual probing by the agent). Therefore, the
       final success rate of both agents is high, with the IE-RL agent only marginally outperforming the
1368
       GPT agent. However, qualitatively, the GPT-agent would generate extremely verbose responses
1369
       that either consist of a checklist of questions that are tedious to answer, or lists of recommended
1370   activities that are not particularly personalized to the user. Hence, we identify two other metrics
1371   that demonstrate lower-quality interactions by the GPT agent compared to our IE-RL agent. The
1372   first that we measure is the mean number of tokens in each agent’s utterance. We see that the overly
1373   verbose responses by the GPT-agent result in an unnaturally high token count per utterance, whereas
1374   our IE-RL agent generates significantly more concise utterances with fewer tokens. In addition,
1375   we measure the one-shot success rate: the percentage of times the first activity recommended by
1376   the agent is the ground-truth. In practice, a system that recommends too many activities can be
1377   detrimental even if one of the recommendations is ultimately successful, as humans may be impatient
1378
       or eventually distrust the system’s capabilities. We found that the GPT agent, which often resorts to
       recommending a spread of activities, has almost 30% lower zero-shot success rate than our IE-RL
1379
       agent, which performs targeted information-gathering and can much more often identify the right
1380
       activity to recommend on the first try.
