009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            How to enhance the alignment of text and image features in CLIP model is a key
012            challenge in zero-shot industrial anomaly detection tasks. Recent studies mostly
013            rely on precise category prompts for pre-training, but this approach is prone to
014            overfitting, which limits the generalization ability of the mode. To address this
015            issue, we propose the concept of fuzzy prompts and introduce Clustering-Driven
016            Stacked Prompts (CSP) along with the Ensemble Feature Alignment (EFA) mod-
017            ule to improve the alignment between text and image features. This design signif-
018
               icantly outperforms other methods in terms of training speed, stability, and final
               convergence results, showing remarkable efficiency in enhancing anomaly detec-
019
               tion segmentation performance. What is even more surprising is that fuzzy stacked
020
               prompts exhibit strong generalization in classification tasks, enabling them to
021            adapt to various anomaly classification tasks without any additional operations.
022            Therefore, we further propose the Regulating Prompt Learning (RPL) module,
023            which leverages the strong generalization ability of fuzzy stacked prompts to regu-
024            larize prompt learning, thereby improving performance in anomaly detection clas-
025            sification tasks. We conducted extensive experiments on seven industrial anomaly
026            detection datasets, which demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art
027            performance in zero-shot anomaly detection and segmentation tasks.
028
029   Industrial anomaly detection (Xie et al., 2023; Roth et al., 2022; Mou et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
030   2023) is an important research area in the field of computer vision. It involves two primary tasks:
031   distinguishing between normal and anomalous images through anomaly detection classification and
032   achieving pixel-level anomaly localization through anomaly detection segmentation. However, the
033   challenge of anomaly detection lies in the unknown nature of anomalies. Due to the lack of avail-
034
      able anomalous data samples, it is difficult to extract features of anomalies. Traditional methods
      often rely on unsupervised (Yi & Yoon, 2020; Massoli et al., 2022; Sohn et al., 2021; Gong et al.,
035
      2019) or self-supervised (Deng & Li, 2022a; Zhu et al., 2024; Deng & Li, 2022b; Cao et al., 2022)
036
      approaches. By learning from a large number of normal samples, the model can memorize the
037   characteristics of these normal samples and then detect anomalies by calculating the differences
038   between test samples and the learned normal distribution. A significant drawback of these meth-
039   ods is the necessity of handling large amounts of cross-class data. Furthermore, as the number of
040   object categories to be detected increases, a separate model needs to be trained for each category,
041   leading to a significant increase in the number of models. Therefore, developing a unified cold-start
042   model that can adapt to multiple categories without requiring additional training becomes an ideal
043   solution and is also an open challenge faced by both the academic and industrial communities. Re-
044   cently, significant progress has been made in zero-shot anomaly detection (ZSAD) (Baugh et al.,
045
      2023; Deng et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as a pre-trained vision-
      language model (Cao et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2023; Chen
046
      & et al., 2023). This approach identifies anomalies by computing the cosine similarity between
047
      text and image features, marking regions with higher similarity as anomalous. A pioneering ZSAD
048   work, WinCLIP (Jeong et al., 2023), employed manually designed text templates and multi-scale
049   image feature extraction for classification tasks, achieving excellent results by aligning image and
050   text features. Subsequently, APRIL-GAN (Chen & et al., 2023) introduced a strategy enhanced by
051   a pre-trained linear layer, utilizing features from different layers to align text and image features,
052   demonstrating outstanding performance in anomaly segmentation tasks as well. Following APRIL-
053   GAN (Chen & et al., 2023), several methods based on pre-trained linear layers or adapters have
      emerged for segmentation tasks. On the template of text prompts, WinCLIP (Jeong et al., 2023)

      1   R ELATED W ORKS
129
130   Zero-shot Anomaly Detection. In the field of industrial anomaly detection, pre-trained vision
131   models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021; Khattak et al., 2023a; Zhang
132
      et al., 2023) have demonstrated strong performance due to their excellent generalization and feature
      extraction capabilities. Currently, anomaly detection methods based on pre-trained large models can
133
      be categorized into two main types: The first type does not require any additional training, such as
134
      WinCLIP (Jeong et al., 2023) and SAA (Cao et al., 2023). As a pioneering work, WinCLIP (Jeong
135   et al., 2023) employs a sliding window method to extract multi-granularity image features for fea-
136   ture alignment, achieving significant results in classification tasks. However, it requires multiple
137   encodings of the same image to capture anomalous features. SAA (Cao et al., 2023), as a pioneer
138   in the collaboration of multiple pre-trained large models, combines the capabilities of Grounding
139   DINO (Liu et al., 2023) and SAM (Khattak et al., 2023a), where Grounding DINO achieves lo-
140   calization through text prompts, followed by SAM performing segmentation using box prompts.
141   However, a notable drawback of this method is its high usage cost and long inference time.
142   The second type of method requires additional training on anomaly detection data. APRIL-
143   GAN (Chen & et al., 2023) first proposed using a linear layer to enhance the alignment of text fea-
144   tures with image features at different levels, successfully completing anomaly segmentation tasks,
145   but it overlooked the classification task. Similarly, SDP (Chen et al., 2023) uses a linear layer to
146   strengthen feature alignment and incorporates CLIP Surgery (Li et al., 2023) with a V-Vattention
147   dual-branch structure. Although this approach significantly improves anomaly perception, the dual-
148   branch structure introduces additional computational costs.
149
150   Prompt Learning in Vision-Language Models. Prompt Learning, as an efficient alternative to
151   parameter tuning, differs from traditional full-network fine-tuning by achieving satisfactory results
152   with fewer tuned parameters. CoOp (Zhou et al., 2021) introduced learnable text prompts for few-
153
      shot classification. Building on this, DenseCLIP (Rao et al., 2022) extended prompt learning to
      dense prediction tasks by adding an image decoder. PromptSRC (Khattak et al., 2023b) introduced
154
      regularization through raw feature output, while AnomalyCLIP (Zhou et al., 2024) became the first
155
      model to apply prompt learning to industrial anomaly detection, proposing object-agnostic prompt
156   learning to avoid the potential adverse effects of different object semantics on anomaly detection.
157   With its glocal context optimization, AnomalyCLIP(Zhou et al., 2024) is capable of capturing lo-
158   cal anomalous semantics, thus allowing it to perform both classification and segmentation tasks
159   without the need for an additional decoder network. However, the dual-branch structure of Anoma-
160   lyCLIP(Zhou et al., 2024) is undoubtedly its greatest drawback, increasing model complexity and
161   computational costs. Moreover, the pre-training approach may lead to underfitting or overfitting
      issues, posing challenges to the model’s stability and generalization capabilities.

166   In this paper, we propose FuzzyCLIP, which enhances segmentation and classification performance
167   in industrial anomaly detection through stacked fuzzy prompts. As shown in Fig 2, FuzzyCLIP first
168   introduces the Clustering-Driven Stacked Prompt (CSP) module to categorize the training data (see

171
      detection classification tasks, we design the Regulating Prompt Learning (RPL) module, which uti-
      lizes the broad generalization ability of stacked fuzzy prompts to regularize prompt learning (see
172
      Sec.2.4), thereby effectively enhancing classification performance.

242   obtain diverse image features is an efficient approach. However, while CLIP links image features and
243   text features in a joint embedding space, during training, only class labels receive direct supervision
244   from language signals, whereas the entire image feature map lacks similar guidance. In other words,
245   the alignment between image feature maps and text features is missing, making direct comparison
246   to infer anomaly maps unfeasible.

248   layers similar to APRIL-GAN. However, we obtained multiple groupings and their corresponding
249   fuzzy stacked text prompt features through the CSP module. Based on these groupings, we trained

328   We conducted a series of experiments to assess the anomaly segmentation performance of our
329   method in a zero-shot setting, focusing on the latest and most challenging industrial anomaly seg-
330   mentation benchmarks. We also performed extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness

333   3.1.1    DATASETS AND M ETRICS .
334
      We conduct experiments on seven real industrial datasets, including MVTec-AD (Bergmann et al.,
335
      2019), VisA (Zou et al., 2022), BTAD (Mishra et al., 2021) , MPDD (Jezek et al., 2021),
336
      DAGM (Wieler & Hahn, 2007), KSDD (Tabernik et al., 2020) and DTD-Synthetic (Aota et al.,
337   2023). We conducted a fair and comprehensive comparison with existing zero-shot anomaly de-
338   tection and segmentation (ZSAS) methods using widely adopted metrics, namely AUROC, AP,
339   AUPRO, and F1 -max. The anomaly detection performance is evaluated using the Area Under the
340   Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC). AP quantifies the accuracy of the model at
341   different recall levels. The PRO metric represents the coverage of the segmented region over the
342   anomalous region. F1 -max represents the harmonic mean of precision and recall at the optimal
343   threshold, implying the model’s accuracy and coverage.
344
345   3.1.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
346
      We use the publicly available CLIP model (VIT-L/14@336px) as our backbone and extract patch
347   embeddings from 6-th, 12-th, 18-th, and 24-th layers. The images used for training and testing are
348   scaled to a resolution of 518 × 518. The length of learnable word embeddings is set to 12. The
349   learnable token embedding is attached to the first 8 layers of the text encoder, with a length of 29
350   in each layer. All parameters of the CLIP model are frozen. Due to the ZSAD task, it is necessary
351   to ensure that the auxiliary data does not contain the content of the test dataset. The framework
352   training employ the Adam optimizer. For the linear training sets MVTec-AD and VisA, the learning
353   rates are set at 1e-4 and 1e-3 for the this stage, respectively, while in the prompt learning stage, both
354   are set at 1e-4. Training proceeds for 2 epochs with a batch size of 16. In the RPL phase, we set the
355
      length of the learnable word embeddings to 12. These learnable token embeddings are appended to
      the first 9 layers of the text encoder to refine the text space, with each layer having a length of 20.
356
      The entire training process lasts for one epoch and the learning rate is set at 1e-3. All experiments
357
      were conducted using PyTorch 1.10.0 and run on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 24GB GPU.

522   4      C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we propose the concept of fuzzy stacked prompts and apply it to industrial anomaly
525   detection. This pre-training method is not only simple and efficient but also significantly enhances
526   the classification and segmentation capabilities in anomaly detection. Additionally, our approach
527
      can continually improve anomaly detection performance in real industrial scenarios by adding more
      linear layers as the data volume and number of categories increase. However, our method also has
528
      some limitations. For instance, the performance may not meet expectations for categories that are
529
      difficult to describe accurately with text. In future work, we will continue to explore ways to further
530   enhance the feature alignment capabilities of CLIP to address more complex scenarios.

681   A.1   S TATE - OF - THE - ART METHODS .
682
             • WinCLIP (Jeong et al., 2023) They create an extensive collection of custom-designed text
683
               prompt templates tailored for anomaly detection and employ a window scaling strategy to
684
               achieve anomaly segmentation. This method efficiently accomplishes anomaly detection,
685            segmentation, and classification tasks by extracting features at different scales.
686
             • APRIL-GAN (Chen & et al., 2023) APRIL-GAN is an enhanced version of WinCLIP.
687
               It first optimizes the text prompt templates and then enhances local visual semantics by
688            combining learnable linear projections. Through the design of linear layers, it strengthens
689            the alignment between image features and text features at different levels, thus achieving
690            more precise segmentation.
691
             • CLIP-AD (Chen et al., 2023) CLIP-AD utilizes a text prompting design similar to Win-
692            CLIP, adapting to ZSAS tasks through multi-branch feature surgery design and fine-tuning
693            techniques.
694
             • CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022) CoCoOp is a method that applies CLIP to image classification
695
               tasks based on prompt learning. It uses continuously learnable vectors instead of manually
696            designed text prompts, enhancing the model’s generalization to novel classes by making
697            the prompt conditioned on each input image. To adapt CoCoOp to the ZSAS task, we im-
698            proved the prompt templates used in the original paper. Specifically, the original template
699            [v1 (x)][v2 (x)] · · · [vr (x)][class] is replaced with [v1 (x)][v2 (x)] · · · [vr (x)][good][class] and
700            [v1 (x)][v2 (x)] · · · [vr (x)][damaged][class] for the generation of normal and abnormal text
701            prompts, where vi (x) represents the learnable word embeddings that incorporate image
               features x.
