009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The propensity of Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate hallucinations and
012            non-factual content undermines their reliability in high-stakes domains, where
013            rigorous control over Type I errors (the conditional probability of incorrectly classi-
014            fying hallucinations as truthful content) is essential. Despite its importance, formal
015            verification of LLM factuality with such guarantees remains largely unexplored. In
016            this paper, we introduce FACT T EST, a novel framework that statistically assesses
017            whether an LLM can confidently provide correct answers to given questions with
018
               high-probability correctness guarantees. We formulate factuality testing as hypoth-
               esis testing problem to enforce an upper bound of Type I errors at user-specified
019
               significance levels. Notably, we prove that our framework also ensures strong Type
020
               II error control under mild conditions and can be extended to maintain its effec-
021            tiveness when covariate shifts exist. Our approach is distribution-free and works
022            for any number of human-annotated samples. It is model-agnostic and applies to
023            any black-box or white-box LM. Extensive experiments on question-answering
024            (QA) and multiple-choice benchmarks demonstrate that FACT T EST effectively
025            detects hallucinations and improves the model’s ability to abstain from answering
026            unknown questions, leading to an over 40% accuracy improvement.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024a) have demon-
032   strated substantial advancements across various domains including summarization systems, search
033
      engines and virtual assistants. However, their outputs cannot be fully trusted due to their propensity
      to generate nonfactual and incorrect information with seemingly high fluency and natural grounding,
034
      a challenge known as hallucination (Maynez et al., 2020b; Huang et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023). This
035
      tendency undermines the reliability and trustworthiness of the generated content, highlighting a
036   critical need for robust mechanisms to verify the factuality and correctness of LLM outputs.
037
038   Existing approaches to hallucination detection like retrieval-based methods (Thorne et al., 2018b;
039
      Gou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) and training-based approaches (Zhang et al., 2023) either rely on
      external databases or resource-intensive fine-tuning processes, which are often impractical or costly.
040
      Therefore, there has been growing interest in uncertainty estimation as a zero-resource alternative
041
      for hallucination detection (Varshney et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024), operating under the premise
042   that hallucinations are intrinsically tied to the model’s uncertainty (Huang et al., 2023). However,
043   none of these methods can provide theoretical guarantees for the detection or testing results, a critical
044   requirement for deploying LLMs in high-stakes domains (Kumar et al., 2023) where precise control of
045   Type I errors (incorrectly flagging a hallucination as truthful content) is needed for decision-making.
046   For instance, incorrect medical diagnoses in healthcare or the provision of uncertain legal advice in
047   the legal field could result in detrimental consequences.
048   To address these limitations, we introduce FACT T EST, a framework that statistically evaluates whether
049   an LLM can reliably generate correct answers to given questions with provable correctness guarantees.
050   We formulate the factuality testing within a hypothesis testing framework to theoretically control the
051   Type I error while minimizing the Type II error. Leveraging the fundamental connection between
052   Neyman-Pearson (NP) classification and statistical testing (Tong et al., 2018; Tong, 2013; Scott
053   & Nowak, 2005), we define a score function to quantify correctness and determine an appropriate
      threshold based on a calibration dataset. This allow LLMs to refuse unknown questions and control

      the false positive rate for any score function. Furthermore, we prove that, if the score function
055   effectively quantifies model correctness, FACT T EST achieves strong power control, ensuring not only
056   Type I error control but also a low Type II error, thereby providing reliable factuality assessments.
057   On the other hand, recognizing that the i.i.d. assumption underlying statistical tests may not always
058   hold in practice, we enhance the robustness of our framework by incorporating an extension to
059   handle covariate shifts through the estimation of density ratios and the use of rejection sampling. Our
060   approach is model-agnostic and does not rely on specific data distribution assumptions, making it
061   broadly applicable to any language model. Importantly, it works for any number of human-annotated
062   samples, ensuring practicality and ease of implementation.
063   To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to introduce statistical factuality testing for large
064   language models, thereby facilitating safer and more reliable deployment in high-stakes applica-
065   tions. We evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed framework on question-answering (QA) and
066   multiple-choice benchmarks. The results demonstrate several key advantages of our approach: (1) it
067   consistently outperforms base models by a substantial margin without requiring additional training
068   or external data sources; (2) it surpasses fine-tuned baselines by a large margin while utilizing only
069
      half of the training data; and (3) it maintains superior performance on out-of-distribution testing data.
      Notably, the theoretical guarantees of our method remain valid even when the i.i.d. assumption is
070
      violated. We summarize the main contributions below.
071
072       • We propose FACT T EST, a novel statistical testing framework that evaluates the factuality of
073         LLMs while enabling them to decline unknown questions with user-specified Type I error
074         guarantees.
075       • We prove that our statistical framework achieves strong power control under mild conditions,
076         ensuring that the predictor can also maintain a low Type II error. This power analysis is broadly
077         applicable to standard NP classification problems, not limited to this setting.
078
          • We extend our framework to accommodate covariate shifts by approximating density ratios and
079         employing rejection sampling, thereby enhancing its robustness for real-world applications.
080
          • We demonstrate that FACT T EST effectively detects hallucinations while maintaining Type I
081
            error below user-specified significance levels, achieving an over 40% improvement in accuracy
082
            compared to pretrained models without any fine-tuning. Additionally, it surpasses training-based
083         baselines by 30% using only half of the fine-tuning data.

485   Factuality of LLMs. The factuality of LLMs is a major problem and of significant research interest (Ji

491   SelfCheckGPT to fact-check the responses of LLMs in a zero-resource fashion. Zhang et al. (2023)
492   instructs LLMs to refuse unknown questions by refusal-aware instruction tuning. However, none of
493   these works have provided theoretical guarantees. QuaCer-C (Chaudhary et al., 2024a;b) provides
494   verification in LLMs with guarantees, but is limited to knowledge comprehension task.
495   Uncertainty Quantification of LLMs. Our work relates to a line of work on uncertainty quantifi-
496   cation (UQ) for LLMs, as we employ these functions to assess models’ ability to reliably give an
497   answer. Predictive entropy that measures the entropy of the model’s predicted token distribution
498   has been used as a simple baseline for UQ in LLMs (Braverman et al., 2019). Kuhn et al. (2023)
499   introduced Semantic Entropy, which incorporates linguistic invariances to measure uncertainty. Most
500   recently, Nikitin et al. (2024) introduced Kernel Language Entropy (KLE), which defines positive
501
      semidefinite unit trace kernels and quantifies uncertainty using the von Neumann entropy.
502   These works are complementary to ours, as our contribution is a meta-algorithm that works with
503   any uncertainty quantification method to serve as score functions and assess the factuality. Future
504   developments in this line of work can greatly improve the performance of our framework.
505   Distribution-Free and Finite-Sample Inference. Recent works have extended conformal prediction
506   to provide guarantees on the outputs of LLMs (Kang et al., 2024; Quach et al., 2024; Mohri &
507   Hashimoto, 2024). While these methods focus on improving model outputs, FACT T EST is designed to
508   verify correctness and decline unknown questions. Our framework leverages principles from Neyman-
509   Pearson (NP) classification. The NP classification paradigm differs from standard classification
510   and cost-sensitive learning, where the goal is to minimize a weighted combination of Type I and II
511   errors. Instead, it prioritizes controlling the Type I error while minimizing the Type II error, ensuring
512   that the Type I error remains below a user-specified threshold 𝛼. To this end, Rigollet & Tong
513
      (2011) and Scott & Nowak (2005) proposed using empirical risk minimization, and Tong (2013)
      employed plug-in approaches to construct NP classifiers. A more related work by Tong et al. (2018)
514
      introduced an umbrella algorithm that achieves Type I error control for any pretrained classifier, while
515
      similar techniques were also proposed in the PAC-style conformal prediction literature (Vovk, 2012).
516   However, the methods in Tong et al. (2018) and Vovk (2012) do not provide Type II error guarantees.
517
518   Our work takes an initial step to use the NP classification idea to conduct factuality testing for LLMs.
519
      Furthermore, the Type II error analysis of our method can be directly applied to the standard NP
      umbrella algorithm, which is of independent interest. Additionally, we extend the NP classification
520
      framework to account for covariate shifts, enabling it to address more practical, real-world problems.
521
522
      6    C ONCLUSION : S UMMARY AND L IMITATIONS
523
524   In this paper, we introduced FACT T EST, a novel framework for factuality testing in Large Language
525   Models (LLMs) that leverages the principles of Neyman-Pearson (NP) classification to provide
526   statistical guarantees. By formulating factuality testing as a hypothesis testing problem, FACT T EST
527   effectively enforces an upper bound on Type I errors. We prove that our framework ensures strong
528   power control under mild conditions and can be extended to maintain its effectiveness in the presence
529
      of covariate shifts. These theoretical analyses can be seamlessly integrated with the standard NP
      umbrella algorithm, not limited to our framework. Our approach is distribution-free and works
530
      for any number of human-annotated samples. It applies to any LLM including closed-box models.
531
      Empirical evaluations have demonstrated that FACT T EST consistently outperforms both pretrained
532   and fine-tuned baselines. Besides, FACT T EST maintained superior performance under distribution
533   shifts, ensuring its robustness and reliability in real-world scenarios. Additionally, our framework
534   effectively enhanced the reliability of black-box APIs, highlighting its practical applicability.
535
      One limitation of our work is the current implementation of only three entropy-based certainty
536
      functions. Exploring additional score functions could further enhance the framework’s performance.
537
      Furthermore, our framework constructs the predictor in an offline manner. Future work could extend
538   FACT T EST to support online testing, thereby enabling real-time factuality assessments.

905   B    M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
906
907   Conformal Prediction. Conformal prediction enables the construction of confidence sets that
908   contain the true outcome with a specified probability (Shafer & Vovk, 2007; Angelopoulos &
909
      Bates, 2022; Barber et al., 2023). It has been successfully applied to various black-box machine
      learning models (Angelopoulos & Bates, 2022) but has limited application in language models
910
      (LMs). Specifically, Kumar et al. (2023) provides conformal guarantees on multiple-choice datasets.
911
      C-RAG (Kang et al., 2024) provides conformal risk analysis for RAG models and certifies an upper
912   confidence bound. CLM (Quach et al., 2024) extends conformal prediction for LLM generations
913   and provide coverage guarantees. Conformal Factuality (Mohri & Hashimoto, 2024) enables the
914   application of conformal prediction in improving model performance. However, traditional CP
915   methods in LLMs focus primarily on coverage guarantees without differentiating between correct and
916   incorrect samples, thereby lacking explicit error rate controls essential for hallucination detection. In
917   contrast, FACT T EST differs from those works in that it aims to evaluate the model’s ability to answer
      correctly, detect hallucinations and explicitly control both Type I and Type II errors.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Uncertainty Quantification and Confidence Calibration. Lin et al. (2024) identifies that existing
919   methods treat all tokens equally when estimating uncertainty and proposed a simple supervised
920   approach for uncertainty estimation in black-box LLMs using labeled datasets. Duan et al. (2024)
921   proposes jointly shifting attention to more relevant (SAR) components. Besides, recent research on
922   confidence calibration for LLMs has explored several innovative approaches. For example, Tian et al.
923   (2023) elicits verbalized confidences to improve calibration. Huang et al. (2024) proposes confidence
924   elicitation methods for long-form generations. Multicalibration (Detommaso et al., 2024) aims to
925   ensure LLM confidence scores accurately reflect the true likelihood of predictions being correct.

1005

1008     and testing sets contain 3470 and 958 samples, respectively.

1011     dataset is employed to assess the models’ capability to verify the factual accuracy of statements

1013     for each question: (A) SUPPORTED, (B) REFUTED, (C) NOT ENOUGH INFO. The training set
1014
