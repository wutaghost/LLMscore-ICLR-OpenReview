009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning plays an increasingly important role in computational chemistry
012            and materials science, complementing computationally intensive ab initio and
013            first-principles methods. Despite their utility, machine-learning models often lack
014            generalization capability and robustness during atomistic simulations, yielding
015            unphysical energy and force predictions that hinder their real-world applications.
016
               We address this challenge by introducing a physics-informed, weakly supervised
               approach for training machine-learned interatomic potentials (MLIPs). We in-
017
               troduce two novel loss functions, extrapolating the potential energy via a Taylor
018
               expansion and using the concept of conservative forces. Our approach improves
019            the accuracy of MLIPs applied to training tasks with sparse training data sets
020            and reduces the need for pre-training computationally demanding models with
021            large data sets. Particularly, we perform extensive experiments demonstrating
022            reduced energy and force errors—often lower by a factor of two—for various
023            baseline models and benchmark data sets. Moreover, we demonstrate improved
024            robustness during MD simulations of the MLIP models trained with the pro-
025            posed weakly supervised loss. Finally, we show that our approach facilitates
026            MLIPs’ training in a setting where the computation of forces is infeasible at the
027
               reference level, such as those employing complete-basis-set extrapolation. An
               implementation of our method and scripts for executing experiments are available
028
               at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PICPS-ML4Sci-1E8F.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Ab initio and first-principles methods are inevitable for the computer-aided exploration of molecular
034   and material properties used in the chemical sciences and engineering (Parrinello, 1997; Carloni et al.,
035   2002; Iftimie et al., 2005). However, commonly employed ab initio and first-principles approaches—
036   such as coupled cluster (CC) (Purvis & Bartlett, 1982; Bartlett & Musiał, 2007) and density functional
037   theory (DFT) (Hohenberg & Kohn, 1964; Kohn & Sham, 1965), respectively—require substantial
038   compute resources. Thus, they typically allow only for atomistic simulations of small- to medium-
039
      sized atomic systems and restrict the accessible simulation times, which affects the accuracy of
      estimated molecular and material properties. Classical force fields can extend these length and time
040
      scales, providing a computationally efficient alternative to first-principles approaches, but often lack
041
      accuracy. Machine-learning-based models hold promise to bridge the gap between first-principles and
042   classical approaches, yielding computationally efficient and accurate machine-learned interatomic
043   potentials (MLIPs) (Smith et al., 2017; Chanussot et al., 2021; Unke et al., 2021; Merchant et al.,
044   2023; Kovács et al., 2023; Batatia et al., 2023).
045
      These MLIPs, however, face several challenges. They require the generation of training data sets that
046
      sufficiently cover configurational (atom positions) and compositional (atom types) spaces using, e.g.,
047
      molecular dynamics (MD) simulations based on ab initio or first-principles approaches. Given the
048   high computational cost of the commonly used data generation approaches, the resulting training data
049   sets are often sparse and prevent the application of MLIPs to new molecular and material systems.
050   Active learning can be used to address this challenge (Li et al., 2015; Vandermause et al., 2020;
051   Zaverkin et al., 2024b), but still requires generating a non-negligible number of first-principles (i.e.,
052   DFT) data to train the initial model, which is then used to explore the phase space with sufficiently
053   long MD simulations. Hence, a strong motivation for the proposed method is its use in combination
      with active learning to acquire additional training data. Furthermore, MLIPs often lack sufficient

093   basis set (CBS) limit (Hobza & Šponer, 2002; Feller et al., 2006). (iv) Finally, PIWSL mitigates
094   sensitivity issues associated with limited sizes of available data sets by taking into account the
095   potential energy response to local perturbations in atomic structures.

      Machine-Learned Interatomic Potentials. There is a growing interest in using ML-based models
100
      for investigating molecular and material systems as they allow performing atomistic simulations with
101
      an accuracy on par with first-principles methods but at a fraction of the computational cost. The
102   field of machine-learned interatomic potentials (MLIPs) emerged over two decades ago (Blank et al.,
103   1995) and has been one of the most active research directions since then (Behler & Parrinello, 2007;
104   Artrith et al., 2011; Artrith & Urban, 2016; Smith et al., 2017; Shapeev, 2016; Schütt et al., 2017;
105   Thomas et al., 2018; Unke & Meuwly, 2019; Drautz, 2019; Zaverkin & Kästner, 2020; Zaverkin et al.,
106   2021; Thomas et al., 2018; Schütt et al., 2021; Shuaibi et al., 2021a; Passaro & Zitnick, 2023; Liao
107   et al., 2023; Batzner et al., 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023; Batatia et al., 2022; Zaverkin et al., 2024a).
      The development of local higher-body-order representations (Shapeev, 2016; Drautz, 2019; Zaverkin


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      & Kästner, 2020; Zaverkin et al., 2021) and the emergence of equivariant message-passing neural
109   networks (MPNNs) (Thomas et al., 2018; Schütt et al., 2021; Shuaibi et al., 2021a; Passaro & Zitnick,
110   2023; Liao et al., 2023; Batzner et al., 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023; Batatia et al., 2022; Zaverkin
111   et al., 2024a) significantly advanced the field. These methods enable the cost-efficient generation
112   of accurate MLIPs for modeling interactions in many-body atomic systems and account for crucial
113   inductive biases as the invariance of the potential energy under rotation.
114
      Physics-Informed Machine Learning. Physics-informed ML aims to model physical systems using
115   data-driven techniques and incorporates physics principles into ML-based models. For example,
116   MLIPs based on equivariant MPNNs enforce the invariance of the potential energy under rotation and
117   use equivariant features to enrich the building of many-body contributions to it (Thomas et al., 2018;
118   Batzner et al., 2022; Batatia et al., 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2023). Furthermore,
119   physics constraints can be integrated via auxiliary loss functions, prompting ML models to learn im-
120   portant physical relationships, as demonstrated for physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi
121   et al., 2019; Cai et al., 2022), which learn to model solutions of partial differential equations by
122   minimizing residuals during training. Applying physics-informed ML to molecular modeling has
123
      gained attraction in both ML and computational chemistry communities (Godwin et al., 2022; Ni
      et al., 2024). As such, prior work (Cooper et al., 2020) has motivated our current research and is
124
      discussed in more detail in subsequent sections.

159   4   P HYSICS - INFORMED W EAKLY S UPERVISED L EARNING
160
161   For MLIPs, the generation of approximate labels employed in weakly supervised losses is highly
      non-trivial. Small perturbationss in atomic structures can lead to significant changes in energies and


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      atomic forces. Thus, standard approaches that are effective for many ML tasks (Yang et al., 2022)
163   are typically not applicable to MLIPs. To address this problem, we propose a physics-informed
164   weakly supervised learning approach that involves (i) a Taylor expansion of the potential energy for
165   computing the response to atomic perturbations and (ii) spatial consistency to estimate the displaced
166   potential energy based on the concept of conservative forces. We finally introduce the PIWSL loss
167   term, combining both classes of weakly supervised loss functions with the supervised loss.

193   4.2   P HYSICS -I NFORMED S PATIAL -C ONSISTENCY L OSS
194
195   This section introduces a physics-informed approach for generating weak labels based on the concept
196   of conservative forces. Thus, we leverage that the energy difference between two points on the
197   potential energy surface is independent of the path taken between them. We consider two paths
198   from a reference point to the same target point, composed of three perturbation vectors in total. We
199   estimate the potential energy at the target point via Eq. (2). An example of two paths is demonstrated
200   in figure 1 (c). The figure relates the energy obtained when displacing atomic positions of the
201   original configuration S (denoted by A in the figure) by δr (from configuration A to C) with the
202
      energy obtained through consecutive perturbations δr′ (from configuration A to B) and δr′′ (from

452   5.5   F URTHER A NALYSES OF PIWSL
453
454
      The following provides further analyses of our approach. We provide the results for Equiformer v2
455
      and PaiNN since these models employ equivariant features and demonstrate a high accuracy on the
456   ANI-1x data set when trained using 1000 configurations.
457
458   Comparing PITC with the Taylor-Expansion-Based Weak Label Loss. We compare the PIWSL
459
      method with the Taylor-expansion-based weak label (WL) approach (Cooper et al., 2020), whose loss
      function is presented in Eq. (A3). For simplicity, we only consider the PITC loss in Eq. (3). For a fair
460
      comparison, we consider the following two cases. First, we train with reference forces and energies
461
      (w. RF). Second, we train the methods without reference forces and use only the reference energies.
462   For the training with reference forces, we set the numeric coefficient of the PITC loss to 1.0; for
463   the training without reference forces, the coefficient is set to 0.1. Note that the WL loss without the
464   reference force is calculated using the predicted force labels. The results are provided in Table 5.
465
      Our PITC loss demonstrates the best accuracy in all cases, with and without the reference forces.
466
      Interestingly, PaiNN failed to learn the potential energy with the WL loss and reference forces.
467
      We hypothesize this to be due to the imbalance of the training between the energies and forces.
468   Specifically, the WL loss trains only the potential energy, resulting in an inconsistency between
469   the energy and force branches, which share the same readout layer that experiences more frequent
470   updates using the potential energy. This hypothesis is supported by the results for the training without
471   reference forces, where the error in energy is reduced compared to the baseline. A further validation
472   in a similar experiment in the case of GF is provided in section F.9. However, the proposed PITC loss
473   still performs better here. In summary, the PITC loss enables MLIPs to learn energies and forces
474   consistent with each other and does it better than the previously proposed WL method.
475   Ablating the Impact of PITC and PISC Losses. We conduct an ablation experiment to analyze
476   the impact of PITC and PISC losses. Results in Table 4 indicate that the PITC loss predominantly
477   improves the accuracy of resulting models, especially for PaiNN. Using just the PISC loss does not
478   consistently improve accuracy but stabilizes training when combined with PITC. This combined
479   approach notably benefits Equiformer v2. For Equiformer v2, we repeated the experiment five times
480   to reduce the effect from an outlier on the PITC loss.
481   Adversarial Directions for Perturbing Atomic Positions. The following discusses the dependence
482   of the PIWSL’s performance on selecting the vector δr in Eq. (5) employed to perturb atomic positions.
483   The detailed implementation and setups are provided in section D.1. Table 6 compares the results
484   obtained for a randomly-sampled vector δr and for the one determined adversarially. The results
485   demonstrate that both approaches improve the performance compared to the baseline without weak
      supervision, though the results might depend on the employed model.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
514
515
      This work introduces the PIWSL method, encompassing two distinct physics-informed weakly
516
      supervised loss functions, for learning MLIPs. These losses provide the physics-informed weak
517   labels based on the Taylor expansion (PITC loss) and the spatial consistency (PISC loss) of the
518   potential energy. These physics-informed weak labels enable any MLIP to improve its accuracy
519   and robustness, particularly in scenarios characterized by sparse training data, which are common
520   when investigating a new molecular or material system. The improved accuracy and robustness of
521   MLIPs can allow running sufficiently long MD simulations, resulting in a more effective use of
522   active learning approaches. Our extensive experiments demonstrate notable efficacy and efficiency
523   of our method from various aspects: (i) dependence on the training data set size, (ii) the potential
524   energy prediction variance and robustness in terms of a perturbation on a C–H bond length as well
525
      as robustness during MD simulations, and (iii) selection of the perturbation vector. In particular, it
      is shown that our PIWSL method enables ML models to improve the force prediction even without
526
      force labels, thereby opening a new possibility for training MLIPs using highly accurate reference
527
      methods, such as CCSD(T)/CBS.
528
529   Limitations. The proposed PIWSL method is tailored to ML models that predict atomic forces
530   and total energies of atomic systems. It cannot be applied to other ML problems unrelated to
531
      computational chemistry or materials science applications. Although this work uses the first-order
      Taylor expansion to obtain weak labels in Eq. (2), employing a more sophisticated higher-order
532
      ordinary differential equation solver is a viable alternative.

768   Addressing Data Sparsity in MLIPs. Generating training data sets suitable for learning reliable
769   MLIPs is challenging, especially when considering unexplored molecular and material systems. Nu-
770   merous computationally expensive calculations with either ab initio or first-principles approaches are
771   required for the latter. To mitigate this challenge, active learning (AL) methods, which utilize predic-
772
      tion uncertainty, can be applied (Li et al., 2015; Podryabinkin & Shapeev, 2017; Vandermause et al.,
      2020; Shuaibi et al., 2021b; Briganti & Lunghi, 2023; Zaverkin et al., 2024b). Furthermore, equivari-
773
      ant MLIPs often reduce required training data set sizes through improved data efficiency (Batzner
774
      et al., 2022; Batatia et al., 2022).
