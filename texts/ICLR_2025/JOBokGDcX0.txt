009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Operating on very long sequences can be problematic for many sequence mod-
012            elling methods like Transformers or recurrent neural networks. To avoid this issue,
013            long sequences are often split into smaller chunks instead. For various reasons,
014            these chunks typically are overlapped with each other which causes an increase in
015            tensor size by however much the chunks are overlapping.
016            This paper attempts to find a better understanding on overlapped sequence chunks
017            and what they accomplish. Specifically, the focus of this paper is on audio inputs
018            in both the time and frequency domain. Previous models for speech separation
019            and audio super resolution which use overlapped chunks are modified to allow for
020            reduced or even removed overlaps which causes significant decreases in compu-
021
               tational cost while maintaining accuracy.
022
023

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Certain types of data, like audio, consist of very long sequences. For these data types, it is not
027
      viable to perform sequence modelling techniques such as Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). It is
      necessary and common practice to apply these sequence modelling steps to much shorter sequences
028
      instead.
029
030   One way to accomplish this is through downsampling. This is especially popular for tasks such as
031   automatic speech recognition (Baevski et al., 2020; Zeyer et al., 2021), audio classification (Kong
032   et al., 2019) and many other problem areas where Transformers are used (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020).
033
      The wav2vec2 model (Baevski et al., 2020), for example, reduces the sequence length to less than
      1% of its original sequence length before applying Transformers to it. However, such aggressive
034
      downsampling is not possible for all problem areas. The reason it works for speech recognition and
035
      audio classification is that their input size is orders of magnitude larger than their output size and
036   therefore information can be summarized.
037
038   For other problem areas, mainly those which output not a class or text but audio itself, downsampling
039
      that aggressively is not possible. These problem areas include audio generation, speech synthesis,
      source separation, speech enhancement and audio super resolution. Downsampling to the same
040
      degree as the speech recognition and audio classification models is not viable since it removes too
041
      much information which will negatively impact the accuracy of the model.
042
043   Therefore, a different solution is necessary to use sequence modelling techniques like Transformers.
044   The basic solution is to split the sequence into equal-sized chunks and then apply sequence mod-
045
      elling to the much shorter sequence of the chunks. This, however, causes a new issue. The context
      that exists between the chunks is lost which will negatively impact the accuracy of the model. To
046
      address this, the sequence is split into overlapping chunks instead. Overlapping chunks will ensure
047
      that no context at the chunk edges is lost. It does, however, also have a negative side effect: due
048   to the overlap, the new tensor will contain duplicate information. For an overlap ratio of 50%, for
049   example, the tensor size nearly doubles since every sequence element except for the beginning of
050   the first chunk and end of the last chunk are contained twice in the new tensor. Figure 1 shows an
051   example of time domain chunking with a 50% overlap. Note, that we use the terms window size and
052   chunk size interchangeably. The window size or chunk size determines the number of samples per
053   chunk while the hop size describes how far we move along the original sequence for each chunk.
      The smaller the hop size in comparison to the chunk size, the greater the overlap.

156
157   The reasoning behind choosing overlapping chunks for the STFT is different from the time domain
158   chunking. The Fourier transform assumes that the signal is periodic. Real life signals, however,
159   generally are not periodic. This causes a phenomenon called spectral leakage which in turn leads
160   to distortion. To minimize spectral leakage, the signal is multiplied with a window function whose
161   purpose it is to taper off the start and end of the signal to 0 making the signal somewhat periodic.
      Setting the beginning and end to 0, however, means that we lose said information - which is why


                                                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      overlap is utilized for the STFT. Having duplicates of all the sequence samples that are set to 0 to
163   make the chunks periodic ensures that no information is lost.
164
165   Applying the window function means that there is no duplicate information. And without using the
166
      window function, the STFT will not be able to be reversed perfectly. Therefore, removing overlaps
      in the frequency domain is more difficult than in the time domain. While entirely removing overlaps
167
      from STFT-based models may not always be possible or advisable, a reduction of overlaps still is.
168
      Almost all of the recent SOTA models which use the STFT use an overlap ratio of 75% or greater
169   (Wang et al., 2022; Han & Lee, 2022; Luo & Yu, 2022; Bai et al., 2024; Abdulatif et al., 2024;
170   Liu et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021; Lu et al.,
171   2024; Lee et al., 2024a; gil Lee et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024b; Liao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024;
172   Shibuya et al., 2024). The reason why this is a popular choice despite quadrupling the tensor size is
173   that it maximizes the spectral and temporal resolution of the STFT. Choosing a large window size
174   maximizes spectral resolution while choosing a small hop size maximizes temporal resolution.
175   This paper contains the following contributions:
176
177         1. Gaining a better understanding of overlapped chunks in the context of neural networks and
178            what makes them effective.
179         2. Suggesting multiple strategies which can remove overlaps.
180
            3. Testing said strategies on two models which operate in different domains and on differ-
181
               ent problem areas. Removing overlaps from these models results in major decreases to
182
               computational cost while maintaining accuracy.

185   2     R EMOVING OVERLAP
186
187   In this section, we describe the necessary steps to remove overlaps without losing accuracy. While
188   there are multiple ways to remove overlaps, they are all based on the same principle. The cause that
189   necessitates overlaps is the fact that the sequence is split into chunks at the same spots. For time
190
      domain models, this means that context between chunks is lost and for frequency domain models,
      this means that the window function would set the same parts of the sequence to 0 and therefore
191
      information is lost. The way to fix the overlap issue is to split the sequence at different points. This
192
      assumes that the underlying machine learning model consists of multiple layers, meaning we can
193   apply a fraction of the total layers to each variation of different sequence segmentation.
194
195
      2.1   S HIFTING THE SEQUENCE
196
197   The basic approach to removing overlap is to shift the sequence before each sequence modelling
198   layer, then split it up into chunks, apply the sequence modelling, reassemble the sequence and
199   then undo the sequence shift. By choosing different sequence shifts each time, we get to keep
200   the sequence context that would otherwise be lost for time domain models and we do not lose the
201   information from the window function for the STFT.
202   Shifting the sequence solves the core issue since it no longer splits the sequence at the same points.
203   The main consideration for the shifts is that they should not be a multiple of the chunk size. If they
204   were, they would split the sequence at the same spots each time, defeating the purpose of this step.
205
      The main advantage of this strategy is that it allows us to keep the chunk size consistent. The main
206
      disadvantage is that it causes issues for the first or last chunk depending on the shift direction since
207   padding is not used as the objective of this operation is to reduce tensor size. Shifting the sequence
208   will cause samples of the first and last chunk to mix with each other which is an unwanted side
209   effect.
210
211   2.2   VARIABLE CHUNK SIZES
212
213   Another way of removing overlaps is to use different chunk sizes. This idea also assumes that the
214   sequence segmentation has to be done and undo for each sequence modelling layer. By using dif-
215   ferent chunk sizes, especially ones that are not multiples of each other, the splits happen at different
      places in the original sequence.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      As is the case with sequence shifting, the different chunk sizes should not be multiples of each other
217   since some of their splits would be identical.
218
219   Using multiple chunk sizes has the advantage that it can capture additional patterns due to the chang-
220
      ing of the window size and number of chunks for each sequence modelling layer. Compared to
      shifting the sequence, however, it has the disadvantage of potentially negatively impacting the com-
221
      putational cost by changing the sizes of the sequential axes where sequence modelling layers are
222
      applied to. Unlike shifting the sequence, however, this strategy does not cause non neighboring
223   samples to end up in the same chunk.
224
225
      2.3   I NCREASING PARAMETERS
226
227   Shifting the sequence or using variable chunk sizes is the main step which allows for the removal
228   of overlaps. However, if one uses an identical model, once with overlap and once without overlap
229   with shifting or variable chunk sizes instead, the model using overlap will always achieve greater
230   accuracy. This is because fundamentally, the model using overlap applies sequence modelling to its
231   input more often than the model not using overlaps. Specifically, a 50% overlap doubles sequence
232   modelling steps for the same model while a 75% overlap quadruples sequence modelling steps.
233   Working on overlapped chunks is almost a form of parallelization.
234   While a 50% overlap does apply the sequence modelling twice per layer, these two applications
235   have no awareness of each other. It is therefore no true parallelization, as the overlaps just get
236   added to each other during the overlap and add step when the sequence segmentation is undone.
237   Compared to a model which does not split the sequence up at all, it would be equivalent to stacking
238   the sequence in the batch dimension at the beginning and then adding them up at the end. This type of
239   model architecture is not used, however, because it is clearly inefficient. For the same computational
240
      budget, it makes more sense to increase other parts of the model, like the total amount of sequence
      modelling layers or the channel size.
241
242   Going from a 50% overlap to no overlap will roughly halve the computation time and memory
243   usage and would therefore allow to double certain hyperparameters like the channel size or sequence
244   modelling blocks without getting slower or using more memory. It is also possible to only slightly
245   increase the hyperparameters of a model without overlap to match the original’s accuracy while
246
      being significantly faster and more memory efficient.
247   To summarize, not using overlaps means that the model is using a fraction of the sequence mod-
248   elling steps as a model using overlaps. Since these extra sequence modelling steps of the model
249   with overlap have no awareness of each other, however, they are not optimal. Instead, having each
250   sequence modelling step be applied in sequence is more effective.

252   3     E XPERIMENTS
253
254
      In order to evaluate the no overlap approach, we implement it on existing architectures for two
255   different tasks, single channel speech separation and audio super resolution. The architectures we
256   tested are the SepFormer (Subakan et al., 2021) which is a time domain speech separation model
257   and the NU-Wave2 (Han & Lee, 2022) which is a super resolution model which uses the STFT. We
258   selected these models to include multiple problem areas and to show how our approach performs
259   during both time and frequency domain models. Another reason we chose these models is that they
260   are fairly recent and still close to SOTA performance while having public code which we used and
261   adjusted for our experiments.

268   without background noise and without reverberation. It contains 30 hours of training, 10 hours of
269   validation and 5 hours of evaluation data. 119 different speakers with roughly half being female and
      the other half being male are included. Different utterances but the 101 same speakers are used for

      The third adjustment is to choose different chunk sizes for the intra- and inter-processing. This step
346
      is not technically necessary, but it allows for a closer comparison between our adjusted SepFormer

      architecture and the original model. In the original model, sequence segmentation only happens

349   sequence contains 4000 samples, using a 50% overlap with a chunk size of 250, we get 31 total
350   chunks. Therefore, for the original SepFormer, the sequence axis for the Transformers doing intra-
351   processing has a size of 250 and the sequence axis for the Transformers doing inter-processing has
352   a size of 31.
353
      Since the adjusted version does not use overlaps, we only produce half the number of chunks, in
354   the example from above it would be 16, for the same chunk size. This effectively halves the size
355   of the sequence axis of the inter-processing Transformers. To get the same size of the sequence
356   axis for the inter-processing Transformers, we need to halve the chunk size for the inter-processing
357   Transformers for their sequence segmentation. This is not an issue, however, since we disassemble
358   and reassemble the sequence for each Transformer anyway due to the shifts.
359
      For our experiments we simply set the chunk sizes to 250 for the intra-processing Transformers
360   and 125 for the inter-processing Transformers to match the sequence lengths of the original model
361   which will allow for a better comparison. It is, however, also possible to change the chunk size
362   for each Transformer instead which would make the shift sequence step unnecessary because if the
363   sequence splits happen at different spots in the original sequence, then no context would be lost.
364   While this would be the more practical solution, it also makes comparisons with the original model
365   less meaningful since any change could be caused by the difference in the sizes of the sequence
366   axes.
367   The fourth and final adjustment to the SepFormer architecture is to simply increase the total number
368   of Transformers. This was already mentioned in section 2.3, but due to having a 50% overlap,
369   each Transformer in the original SepFormer architecture performs sequence modelling twice on the
370   original sequence. Since both of these applications are unaware of each other, however, they are not
371   as effective as if they were just applied one after another.
372   This is the main contribution of our paper. Overlapped chunks are not optimal because it would
373   always be preferable to apply sequence modelling steps in sequence since this way they are aware
374   of each other and therefore much more effective. If our adjusted model had the same amount of
375   Transformers it would have reduced accuracy compared to the original model because we only
376   perform half the sequence modelling steps since in the adjusted model each Transformer performs
377   sequence modelling only once on the original sequence. If we increase the number of Transformers
      by 50%, we perform 75% of the sequence modelling steps compared to the original architecture.

409   The removal of overlaps can be thought of as a trade off between speed/memory usage and model
410   size. We would, however, argue, that this trade off will basically always be worth it. In the case of
411   the SepFormer model, the original model needs 100 MB while the adjusted needs 150 MB of disk
412   space. We would argue, that an increase of 50 MB is irrelevant when compared to the speed and
413   memory gains from the adjusted model.
414   The no overlap SepFormer model we show in Figure 4 and Table 1 is only one way to use our
415   adjusted architecture. It is possible to match the number of sequence modelling steps by doubling
416   the amount of Transformers to 64. This would, however, also cause a further increase in model size
417   and would mean that the computational cost would be similar, if not slightly worse, compared to the
418   original model. Memory usage would still be equal but the original model would be slightly faster
419   due to performing two sequence modelling steps in parallel and our adjustment introducing some
420
      overhead like the additional positional encodings and decodings, sequence shifts and sequence dis-
      and reassemblies.
421
422   To summarize, there are various options for architectures that split sequences into chunks and op-
423   erate on them in the time domain. If one wants to prioritize model size, high overlaps should be
424   used. If accuracy, speed or memory usage are a priority, no overlaps should be used and the saved
425   computational cost should instead be invested into making the model bigger by increasing the layer
426
      count or increasing the channel size..

428   3.3   AUDIO SUPER RESOLUTION - NU-WAVE 2
429
430   The NU-Wave2 architecture is very different from the speech separation model covered in the pre-
431   vious section. Unlike the SepFormer, it only uses overlapped chunks for one part of its recurring
      block design while operating on the entire sequence for the rest of the model.


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      More specifically, the NU-Wave2 architecture uses the STFT and inverse STFT within each recur-
433   ring block with an overlap ratio of 75%, meaning this step almost quadruples the size of the tensor.
434   As we have previously stated, overlaps in the frequency domain cannot be treated the same as in the
435   time domain as they serve a different purpose. One purpose is to minimize spectral leakage, while
436   the other is to maximize spectral and temporal resolution of the STFT. Choosing a large overlap
437   ratio means that a big window size is used which causes high spectral resolution and a low hop size
438   is used which causes high temporal resolution. Removing overlaps from the STFT means reducing
439   either the temporal or spectral resolution.
440   In order to reduce or even remove overlaps in the NU-Wave2 architecture, we cannot use all of the
441   same tools as we used during the SepFormer adjustment. The main difference is that undoing and
442   redoing the STFT is not a lossless step if overlaps are removed.
443
      Another reason is that for the STFT, the NU-Wave2 block only applies sequence modelling along
444
      the frequency axis, not the time axis. Therefore we only have to match the size of the frequency axis
445   while reducing the size of the time axis is irrelevant, meaning that reducing temporal resolution is not
446   an issue in this particular case. If it were, the same solution that was used in the SepFormer adjusted
447   architecture can be utilized where the STFT would have to be undone and redone for each sequence
448   modelling layer and different window sizes for the spectral and temporal sequence modelling would
449   be used in order to maximize their resolution. The only issue where this is not possible is if both
450   axes would be processed simultaneously, like in the case of a two dimensional convolutional layer. In
451   that case, one would have to choose between sacrificing spectral or temporal resolution or alternate
452   between maximizing either the size of the frequency or time axis of the STFT for each sequence
453
      modelling layer.
454   While overlaps in the frequency domain are meant to minimize spectral leakage, in many model
455   architectures, including the NU-Wave2, this is not a necessary precaution. There are a number of
456   reasons for this, but the biggest one is that the STFT is part of a residual path, meaning that its
457   output will be added to the input. While no distortion would still be preferable, some distortion in
458   the residual path is not necessarily going to affect the output and can be corrected by the neural
459
      network. If the final output of the model were directly produced by the inverse STFT, then removing
      overlaps is ill-advised due to said distortion. The same is true for the window function. The original
460
      model uses a Hann window, while our adjusted model does not use a window function at all.
461
462   Another difference is that we cannot simply increase the number of blocks as we did in the Sep-
463   Former because the recurring blocks contain other parts outside of the STFT, meaning there is
464   no direct trade off between reducing overlaps and increasing the number of blocks. Furthermore,
465
      within the STFT context the NU-Wave2 is using one dimensional convolutional layers instead of
      Transformers for its sequence modelling. This means that positional encoding is not used here and
466
      therefore no major architecture changes are necessary.
467
468   The only architectural changes that were made is to once again shift the sequence before the STFT,
469   set the hop size equal to the chunk size to remove overlaps, increase the hyperparameters of the
470   layers operating on the STFT and finally undo the sequence shift after the inverse STFT. We set the
471
      shift value to start from 0 and increase by 10 for each STFT iteration. Since the window size of this
      model is much larger than the SepFormer, one might expect to need a larger shift value as well, but
472
      in our experiments it made no noticeable difference. As for the hyperparameters, we increased the
473
      kernel sizes of the one dimensional convolutional layer within the BSTFT layer from 3 to 5 and also
474   increased the BSTFT channel size from 64 to 128. We use a custom STFT implementation since the
475   builtin version in pytorch does not allow for zero overlap.

506   4   C ONCLUSION
507
508
      In this paper, we challenged the common notion of using 50% or even 75% overlaps for time or
509
      frequency domain chunking. Overlapping chunks in the context of machine learning models are
510   convenient since they fix issues that occur when splitting the sequence into chunks and will, using
511   an identical model, outperform a model without overlaps. The cause of this is that each sequence
512   modelling layer applies sequence modelling multiple times for each input since each input is con-
513   tained multiple times due to overlaps. Overlaps are a form of parallelization, but they are not opti-
514   mal because each of these sequence modelling applications has no awareness of the other sequence
515   modelling applications within the same layer. The more effective way for model architectures is to
516   instead remove or reduce overlaps and use the gained computational budget to increase the model
517   size - that way each sequence modelling step will have awareness of each other sequence modelling
518
      step.
519   We describe two strategies for removing or reducing overlaps, including shifting the sequence and
520   using different window sizes. Furthermore, we implemented our concepts on two different models,
521   one in the time domain and one in the frequency domain and for two different problem areas.
522   We were able to significantly improve computation time and memory usage while maintaining ac-
523   curacy for both of the models we tested. This does come at the cost of model size, however, we
524   believe this to be a much less relevant metric than the other three.
525
      For future work, we intend to test our methods of removing overlaps on more models. From our
526
      experiments and understanding of overlaps, it should be possible to remove them in nearly all archi-
527
      tectures in the time domain. The frequency domain, however, needs to meet certain conditions to be
528   able to remove overlaps completely. We would, however, still argue for at least reducing overlaps as
529   currently most architectures using the STFT operate with an overlap ratio of 75% or even greater.
