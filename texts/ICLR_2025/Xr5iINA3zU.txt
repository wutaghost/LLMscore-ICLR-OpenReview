009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing presence of AI-generated content on the internet raises a critical
012            question: What happens when generative machine learning models are pretrained
013            on web-scale datasets containing data created by earlier models? Some authors
014            prophesy model collapse under a ‘replace’ scenario: a sequence of models, the
015            first trained with real data and each later one trained only on synthetic data from
016            its preceding model. In this scenario, models successively degrade. Others see
017            collapse as avoidable; in an ‘accumulate’ scenario, a sequence of models is trained,
018
               but each training uses all real and synthetic data generated so far. In this work,
               we deepen and extend the study of these contrasting scenarios. First, collapse
019
               versus avoidance of collapse is studied by comparing the replace and accumulate
020
               scenarios on each of three prominent generative modeling settings; we find the
021            same contrast emerges in all three settings. Second, we study a compromise
022            scenario; the available data remains the same as in the accumulate scenario – but
023            unlike accumulate and like replace, each model is trained using a fixed compute
024            budget; we demonstrate that model test loss on real data is larger than in the
025            accumulate scenario, but apparently plateaus, unlike the divergence seen with
026            replace . Third, we study the relative importance of cardinality and proportion of
027            real data for avoiding model collapse. Surprisingly, we find a non-trivial interaction
028            between real and synthetic data, where the value of synthetic data for reducing test
029
               loss depends on the absolute quantity of real data. Our insights are particularly
               important when forecasting whether future frontier generative models will collapse
030
               or thrive, and our results open avenues for empirically and mathematically studying
031
               the context-dependent value of synthetic data.
032

034   1   I NTRODUCTION : M ODEL C OLLAPSE & W HY I T M ATTERS
035
036   With each passing day, the internet contains increasingly more AI-generated content (Altman, 2024).
037   What is the impact of this for future of deep generative models pretrained on web-scale datasets
038   containing data generated by their predecessors? Previous work forewarned that such model-data
039   feedback loops can exhibit model collapse, a phenomenon whereby model performance degrades
040   with each model-fitting iteration such that newer models trend towards useless (Shumailov et al.,
041   2023). This prophecy is deeply concerning because society is increasingly relying on these deep
042   generative models (Bommasani et al., 2022; Reuel et al., 2024; Perrault & Clark, 2024; Kapoor et al.,
043   2024), and model collapse threatens that future models will be made useless as society’s current data
044
      practices pollute the pretraining data supply.
045   However, the model collapse literature is replete with different experimental methodologies and
046   different mathematical assumptions of different generative models, with different papers reaching
047   different conclusions (Taori & Hashimoto, 2023; Hataya et al., 2023; Martínez et al., 2023; Shumailov
048   et al., 2023; Alemohammad et al., 2024; Martínez et al., 2023; Bohacek & Farid, 2023; Guo et al.,
049   2024; Bertrand et al., 2024; Briesch et al., 2023; Dohmatob et al., 2024a;b; Gerstgrasser et al., 2024;
050
      Seddik et al., 2024; Marchi et al., 2024; Padmakumar & He, 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Ferbach et al.,
      2024a; Veprikov et al., 2024). These mixed methods and findings make assessing the probability and
051
      harm of model collapse difficult.
052
053   In this work, we extend the accumulate workflow studied in Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) to cover several
      settings previously not studied in the literature to exhibit its effectiveness over the replace workflow.

054
      We begin by testing the following hypothesis – that model collapse emerges in a scenario where
055   models are trained on evolving datasets built by deleting past data en masse; and model collapse is
056   avoided in a scenario where the training datasets instead accumulate all real and synthetic data. We
057   consider whether these claims hold in three new generative model task settings pointed to by recent
058   prominent work; we find the claims hold. We then compare three clear dataset evolution scenarios,
059   focusing on a new middle ground where data accumulate over time but each model is trained under a
060   fixed compute budget; in this middle ground, we find that losses on real test data climb faster than
061   without a compute budget but plateau to lower values than if data are deleted en masse after each
062   model-fitting iteration. These results are consistent across five different generative modeling settings.
063
      Lastly, we investigate, in a specific situation, whether the proportion or cardinality of initial real data
      matters more for preventing model collapse and discover a non-trivial interaction between real and
064
      synthetic data: when real data are scarce, an appropriate amount of synthetic data reduces the test
065
      loss on real data, whereas when real data are ample, synthetic data increases the test loss on real data.
066   Altogether, our work provides valuable comprehensive insights for predicting likely futures of deep
067   generative models pretrained on web-scale data.

070   2     T ESTING T WO M ODEL C OLLAPSE C LAIMS IN T HREE N EW G ENERATIVE
071         M ODELING S ETTINGS
072
073   Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) recently made two claims about model collapse:
074
075         1. Many previous papers induced model collapse by deleting past data en masse and training
076            largely (or solely) on synthetic data from the latest generative model, and
077         2. If new synthetic data are instead added to real data, i.e., data accumulate over time, then
078            model collapse is avoided.
079
080   These two claims are important for forecasting the future of generative models because, if correct,
081   model collapse is then less likely to pose a realistic threat since accumulating data over time is a more
082   realistic modeling assumption; as a partner at Andreessen Horowitz elegantly explained, deleting
083   data en masse is “not what is happening on the internet. We won’t replace the Mona Lisa or Lord of
084   the Rings with AI generated data, but the classics will continue to be part of the training data set"
085
      (Appenzeller, 2024).
086   However, these claims have not been tested in three new generative modeling settings recently
087   introduced by prominent work (Shumailov et al., 2024) for studying model collapse:
088
089         1. Multivariate Gaussian Modeling: Multivariate Gaussians are repeatedly fit to data and
090            then used to sample new synthetic data for future Gaussian fitting.
091         2. Kernel Density Estimation: Kernel density estimators are repeatedly fit to data and then
092            used to sample new synthetic data for future kernel density estimators.
093
            3. Supervised Finetuning of Language Models: Language models are finetuned in a super-
094
               vised manner and then used to sample new synthetic text for future finetuning.
095
096   In this section, we ask and answer:
097
098               In these three new generative modeling settings, is model collapse caused by
099                    deleting data en masse and avoided by instead accumulating data?
100
101   In all three settings, we empirically find and (when possible) mathematically prove the answer is yes.
102

      Despite the apparent empirical similarities to the iterative Gaussian fitting shown in Figure 1, Gaussian
      KDEs are theoretically distinct. Unlike Gaussian fitting, regardless of whether we accumulate or
261
      replace, the test NLL for a Gaussian KDE asymptotically diverges in theory. There are two interesting
262
      caveats: (1) when one begins with a small bandwidth, iteratively fitting KDEs can cause the NLL to
263   initially decrease before it diverges due to the effective kernel bandwidth increasing with model-fitting
264   iterations; (2) although accumulating data causes the NLL to diverge asymptotically, this occurs
265   at a rate so glacial that it doesn’t pose a practical concern. If one wishes to prevent the eventual
266   divergence, one can do so by fitting at each iteration with the optimal bandwidth for the number
267   of data, which should be of the form c(in)−1/5 in the ith model-fitting iteration as long as data
268   accumulates at a constant rate. Practically speaking, one chooses the bandwidth for KDEs based on
269   the number and characteristics of the data, implying that conscientious practitioners should never
      witness severe model collapse for KDEs in the accumulate case. For details, see Appendix Sec. B.

      We now turn to the third setting for studying model collapse introduced by Shumailov et al. (2024):
      supervised finetuning of language models. We begin with an instruction following dataset – Nvidia’s
297
      HelpSteer2 (Wang et al., 2024) – and finetune a language model before sampling new text data from
298
      it. We choose Google’s Gemma2 2B model (Team et al., 2024) because it is high performing and
299   relatively small. For Replace, we fine-tune the n-th language model only on data generated by the
300   (n − 1) language model. For Accumulate, we instead fine-tune the n-th language model on the
301   starting real data plus all the synthetic data sampled from all previous models; thus, the amount of
302   data for Replace is constant ∼ 12.5k, whereas the amount of data for Accumulate grows linearly
303   ∼ 12.5k ∗ t. Consistent with our results and with Gerstgrasser et al. (2024), we find that deleting
304   data after each iteration leads to collapse whereas accumulating data avoids collapse (Fig. 3).

306   3                                     M ODEL C OLLAPSE U NDER A F IXED C OMPUTE B UDGET
307
308   Thus far, we have focused on two data paradigms: Replace and Accumulate. As discussed in
309   Sec. 2, Replace is unlikely to be an faithful model of reality because we do not delete the internet
310   after pretraining each model. But one might argue that Accumulate is similarly unfaithful because
311   Accumulate requires that every new model is trained on (linearly) more data and thus requires more
312   compute than its predecessor. Whether this criticism is valid in practice is unclear, since newer
313   models are trained on increasing data (e.g., 1.4T tokens for Llama 1, 2T tokens for Llama 2, 15T
314   tokens from Llama 3) and increasing GPUs (e.g., 2k GPUs for Llama1, 4K for Llama2, 16k for
315   Llama3 (Goyal, 2024)). Nevertheless, for the sake of understanding the space of possible outcomes
316
      and predicting likely outcomes for future generative models, we ask and answer:
317
                                                  Does model collapse occur when data accumulate but models are trained under a
318                                                                          fixed compute budget?

      5    R ELATED W ORK
471
      The limitations of using AI-generated images to train other image models have been well-documented
472
      since 2022 (Hataya et al. (2023)). Shumailov et al. (2023) initially sounded alarms about synthetic
473
      data for training language models by showing that a model trained repeatedly on its own outputs
474   exhibits severely denigrated quality. This theory and empirical work was quickly extended to many
475   new settings (Alemohammad et al. (2024); Bertrand et al. (2024); Dohmatob et al. (2024b;a); Marchi
476   et al. (2024)). The phenomenon that Shumailov identified as “model collapse" still does not have a
477   universally agreed upon, rigorous definition. Shumailov classified model collapse as a “degenerative
478   process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up
479   polluting the training set of the next generation." Dohmatob et al. (2024b) examine model collapse as
480   an alteration of scaling law curves when training on synthetic as opposed to real data. In their theory
481   sections, Shumailov et al. (2024) and Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) explore model collapse by asking
482
      when certain models exhibit divergent test loss after multiple iterations of training. In this paper,
      we take model collapse by its literal meaning: that model performance deteriorates catastrophically
483
      when models are trained on synthetic data.
484
485   Within the model collapse literature, a variety of data dynamics have been studied, which vary in
      how “real” data is discarded or retained, how “synthetic” data is generated, and how each is (or is


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      not) incorporated into future training sets (Martínez et al. (2023); Mobahi et al. (2020); Dohmatob
487   et al. (2024a)). A common feature of many of these is that at least some real data is discarded, often
488   because total dataset size is kept constant across model-fitting iterations. However, Gerstgrasser et al.
489   (2024) note that this may not be representative of real-world dynamics, and that model collapse is
490   avoided when data accumulates. What is not clear, however, is whether this claim holds universally,
491   including in the specific settings studied in other prior work. We help close this gap by extending
492   Gerstgrasser’s empirical and theoretical analysis to several of these settings.
493   Where model collapse can be seen as studying a worst-case scenario, it has also been observed that
494   some kinds of synthetic data have a positive effect. Dohmatob et al. (2024b) and Jain et al. (2024) find
495   that certain amounts of synthetic data can improve model performance, and Ferbach et al. (2024b)
496   suggest that with curation, self-consuming loops can improve alignment with human preferences.
497   A growing literature on how to filter and harness synthetic data has achieved impressive results
498   on a variety of benchmarks (Zelikman et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a); Yang et al. (2024)), raising
499   interesting questions about the limits of when unfiltered synthetic data can help. In this vein, we
500   answer a question posed by Gerstgrasser et al. (2024): does the proportion or the raw amount of real
501
      data in a mixed training set have a greater impact on test loss? In the process, we find that small
      amounts of synthetic data can improve test loss when real data is scarce.
502
503
504   6    D ISCUSSION
505
506   Our work sought extend understanding of model collapse in the replace and accumulate workflows.
507   We demonstrated in three new generative modeling settings that accumulating data over time avoids
508   model collapse, whereas replacing data over time induces model collapse. We then demonstrated in
509   five generative modeling settings that even when each model is trained on a fixed compute budget
510
      with a mixture of real and synthetic data, model performance does deteriorate more, but still tends to
      plateau. The consistency of these results across different model types and datasets suggests that this
511
      distinction is a general phenomenon, and is not specific to any particular model, dataset, or learning
512
      algorithm. Lastly, we explored the value of synthetic data for reducing the test loss on real data and
513   found two different regimes: when real data are plentiful, synthetic data is harmful, but when real
514   data are scarce, there exists an optimal amount of synthetic data that are helpful.
515
516
      In our view, the data paradigm in which synthetic data accumulates from a host of models in
      conjunction with a constant influx of real-world data is more realistic. Under such dynamics, where
517
      new synthetic data are added to existing real and synthetic data, model collapse appears unlikely. Our
518
      experiments take a pessimistic viewpoint, in the sense that our experiments pay no attention to the
519   quality of data, whereas in practice, engineers heavily filter data based on various indicators of data
520   quality, e.g., (Brown et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023; Wettig et al., 2024; Penedo et al., 2024; Li et al.,
521   2024b; Sachdeva et al., 2024); for a recent review, see Albalak et al. (2024).
522
523
524
      7    F UTURE D IRECTIONS
525
      An especially interesting future direction is how to combine synthetic data generation with filtering
526
      techniques to enable performant and efficient pretraining at scale using synthetic data. As we saw in
527
      kernel density estimation (Fig. 2) and in language model pretraining on TinyStories (Fig. 4), training
528   on accumulating real and synthetic data can yield lower loss on real test data than training on real
529   data alone. Identifying under what conditions, and why, this is possible is a tantalizing prospect.
530
531
      Our results in Section 4 suggest that removing low-quality synthetic data from model training sets can
      improve test loss more than gathering additional high-quality data. Developing efficient identification
532
      and removal techniques for detrimental data would streamline the model fine-tuning process and
533
      produce better alignment.
