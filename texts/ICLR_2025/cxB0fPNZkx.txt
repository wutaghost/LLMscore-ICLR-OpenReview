009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Existing sample-based methods, like influence functions and representer points,
012            measure the importance of a training point by approximating the effect of its re-
013            moval from training. As such, they are skewed towards outliers and points that are
014            very close to the decision boundaries. The explanations provided by these meth-
015            ods are often static and not specific enough for different test points. In this paper,
016            we propose a method to generate an explanation in the form of support spectrums
017            which are based on two main ideas: the support sets and a global-to-local impor-
018            tance measure. The support set is the set of training points, in the predicted class,
019            that “lie in between” the test point and training points in the other classes. They
020
               indicate how well the test point can be distinguished from the points not in the
               predicted class. The global-to-local importance measure is obtained by decou-
021
               pling existing methods into the global and local components which are then used
022
               to select the points in the support set. Using this method, we are able to generate
023            explanations that are tailored to specific test points. In the experiments, we show
024            the effectiveness of the method in image classification and text generation tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The ability to attribute a model’s output to a particular set of training points or data source is im-
031   portant, especially in today’s large generative models, given that most of the training data is scraped
032   from the Internet. It gives us a way to explain the outputs of the models. It also supports, among
033
      other things, protection of copyright and intellectual property (i.e. the rights of data owners/creators),
      verification of reliability (e.g. of medical advices) and factuality (e.g. of news), and identification of
034
      problematic data points when the model behaves undesirably. The attributions not only assist users
035
      in understanding the reasoning behind the output, but also allow designers to “debug” the training
036   data.
037
038   Several sample-based explanations have been proposed in the literature and two methods have risen
039
      to the forefront: influence functions and representer points. Influence functions measure the impact
      of removing a training point on the optimal parameters. It does so by computing the inverse of
040
      the Hessian which is then used to approximate the new optimal parameters by taking the first order
041
      Taylor approximation. When used to measure the influence of a training point on a test point, it can
042   be seen as a gradient-based comparison method, since it computes the weighted dot product between
043   the gradients of the two points. One limitation of the influence functions is their high computational
044   cost associated with the Hessian. The representer points method, on the other hand, is based on the
045   decomposition of the output of the neural network into contributions from the training points. The
046   values of these contributions are then used as the measure of how important the points are.
047
      However, we observe that these methods tend to be skewed by outliers, such as mislabled points or
048   points that are very close to the decision boundaries (in the context of classification) or atypical input
049   (in the context of generation), and therefore often attribute many different predictions or outputs to
050   the same set of training points. In other words, these methods often produce global attributions of the
051   model instead of local attributions that are more tailored to a particular prediction or output. While
052   there are other methods that provide localized explanations, they often choose specific points and
053   therefore lack the context of the full picture. In this paper, we propose a method with an adjustable
      global-to-local parameter that allow us to evaluate the shift in the data points from those that are

      influential because they broadly affect the model to those that are particularly influential on a given
055   test.
056
057   It shows a couple of advantages over existing methods: (1) They provide tailored explanations
058
      specific to the test points. Different test points within the same class may have spectrums with
      very different characteristics. We would like to point out here that the global important points are
059
      included in the spectrums as sub-explanations (they correspond to the points at the global extremes).
060
      (2) They show how well supported the test points are. Longer spectrums show a higher confidence in
061   the model’s prediction while shorter spectrums indicate the lack of support in the training set. These
062   are useful indicators that can aid the process of data debugging and curation. Potential applications
063   of the method in generative models include source attribution and identification problems in the
064   generation process (like spurious correlation) and in the datasets (like the presence of biases). We
065   show the effectiveness of the method in explaining predictions in classification problem, obtaining
066   influential sources and detecting spurious correlations in generative language models which we view
067   as an autoregressive (sequential) classification tasks.

069   2     S UPPORT S PECTRUMS
070
071   Many deep learning models consist of a feature map followed by a linear classifier. We will refer
072   to the layer just before the linear classifier as the feature space. The main idea of our approach is
073   to look at how the training points and the test point are distributed in the feature space. Consider
074   a T -class classification problem with training points Z = {(x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )} where xi ∈ Rd
075   and yi is a one-hot vector of T dimension specifying the class of xi . We consider a neural network
076   model which is defined by

080   It consists of a feature map Φ2 with parameters θ2 which maps the input xi to its feature vector fi
081   which is then passed to a linear classifier parameterized by weights W and biases b. Collectively,
082   the parameters of the whole network is denoted by θ = {W, b, θ2 }. σ is a nonlinear function which
083   transforms the output of the linear layer Φ(x1 , θ) into the prediction vector. Note that there is no
084
      restriction placed on the feature map Φ2 which can be arbitrarily complex (deep). The model, there-

094   training points (a spectrum) that supports the classification. This list is obtained through a 2-step
095   process: The generation of the support set, and the computation of the global-to-local important
096   (influential) points.

      4   R ELATED W ORKS
492
      Existing approaches for estimating the influence of a test point to a model’s prediction include
493
      influence functions, representer points, TracIn and model retraining.
494
495   Influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017; Hampel, 1974; Cook, 1977) are based on first-order Taylor
496   approximations of the loss function under small perturbations to the model such as removing a
497   single training point. Its applications include explaining predictions (Koh & Liang, 2017), producing
498
      confidence intervals (Schulam & Saria, 2019), investigating model bias (Brunet et al., 2019; Wang
      et al., 2019), and crafting data poisoning attacks (Koh et al., 2022). Barshan et al. (2020) gives a
499
      relative variant of the method. Representer points methods (Yeh et al., 2018) are based on representer
500
      theorems (Schölkopf et al., 2001; Bohn et al., 2019; Unser, 2019; 2021) that decompose a prediction
501   of a network into a linear combination of contributions from the training points. Its main application
502   is in providing post-hoc explanations for deep neural networks (Yeh et al., 2018; Sui et al., 2021).
503   TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2022) compares the trajectories of the training gradients; and
504   model retraining (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2022; Feldman & Zhang,
505   2020) uses repeated trainings to derive importance measures based on game theoretic concepts like
506   the Shapley value.
507
508   5   C ONCLUSIONS
509
510   In this paper, we propose a method to generate a global-to-local support spectrums for explaining
511   the output of a black-box model on a test point. It is based on the idea of support sets and the
512   decoupling of existing sample-based methods into the global and local components. A point in the
513   spectrum is obtained my maximizing the global importance subject to the locality constraint and
514   the full spectrum is generated by varying the locality parameter. In the experiments, we show the
515   effectiveness of the explanations in two example tasks: image classification and text generation. As
516   future works, we would like to study how the generated spectrums differ when different global/local
517
      measures are used, to scale the approach to the datasets of state-of-the-art models, and to apply it to
      other tasks like image generation. Finally, we would like to explore applications of the method to
518
      related domains of active learning, data selection and valuation.
