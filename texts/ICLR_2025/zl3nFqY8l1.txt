011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework has shown promising potential
014            in knowledge-intensive question answering (QA) by retrieving external corpus and
015
               generating based on augmented context. However, existing approaches only con-
               sider the query itself, neither specifying the retrieval preferences for the retrievers
016
               nor informing the generators of how to refer to the retrieved documents for the
017
               answers, which poses a significant challenge to the QA performance. To address
018            these issues, we propose Rule-Guided Retrieval-Augmented Generation with LMs,
019            which explicitly introduces symbolic rules as demonstrations for in-context learning
020            (RuleRAG-ICL) to guide retrievers to retrieve logically related documents in the
021            directions of rules and uniformly guide generators to generate answers attributed
022            by the guidance of the same set of rules. Moreover, the combination of queries and
023            rules can be further used as supervised fine-tuning data to update retrievers and gen-
024            erators (RuleRAG-FT) to achieve better rule-based instruction following capability,
025            leading to retrieve more supportive results and generate more acceptable answers.
026
               To emphasize the attribution of rules, we construct five rule-aware QA benchmarks,
               including three temporal and two static scenarios, and equip RuleRAG with several
027
               kinds of retrievers and generators. Experiments demonstrate that training-free
028
               RuleRAG-ICL effectively improves the retrieval quality of +89.2% in Recall@10
029            scores and generation accuracy of +103.1% in exact match scores over standard
030            RAG on average across the five benchmarks, and further fine-tuned RuleRAG-FT
031            consistently yields more significant performance enhancement. Extensive analyses
032            indicate that RuleRAG scales well with increasing numbers of retrieved documents
033            and exhibits generalization ability for untrained rules. Our code and benchmarks
034            are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ICLR2025_RuleRAG_ICL_FT.

      Large language models (LLMs) have achieved impressive language generation capability and excelled
      as knowledge learners for their well-known in-context learning (ICL) ability (Brown et al., 2020;
040
      Ouyang et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2024). Despite the success, the full-parametric knowledge
041
      stored in LLMs requires substantial computational costs to keep their memory up-to-date and struggles
042   to precisely manipulate fine-grained queries, especially in knowledge-intensive tasks (Jiang et al.,
043   2023c; Shao et al., 2023). As complementary, RAG represents a novel framework that integrates
044   LLMs with non-parametric information and injects the retrieved knowledge in a plug-and-play
045   manner (Lewis et al., 2020; Dhingra et al., 2022). By explicitly decoupling the knowledge retrieval
046   phase from the answer generation phase, RAG exhibits superior performance in many NLP tasks,
047   such as open-domain QA (Trivedi et al., 2023) and natural language inference (Qin et al., 2023).
048   However, two high-level issues exist in the current RAG frameworks. First, in the retrieval phase,
049   the imperfect retrieval component can not guarantee that the recalled information will always be the
050   most pertinent and helpful to the queries. The reason is that the retrievers in retrieval-augmented
051   language models (RALMs) are mostly trained on unsupervised text (Izacard et al., 2024) or trained
052   end-to-end (Guu et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022a), leading to their insufficiency in retrieving
053   the necessary statements for reasoning (BehnamGhader et al., 2023). Secondly, in the generation
      phase, the LLMs in the current RAG are not specifically informed of how to exploit noisy retrieved

073   and “supervised” in the pre-training corpora of LLMs. For example, REPLUG (Shi et al., 2023) and
074   In-Context RALM (Ram et al., 2023) fuse off-the-shelf LLMs with generic retrievers and treat LLMs

076
      difficult to explain and verify. Therefore, these RAG frameworks are neither inherently trained to
      retrieve along reasonable retrieval directions nor organically attribute retrieved content to answers.
077
078   In contrast to these existing RAG frameworks, we observe that widespread logical rules can explicitly
079   guide people to accomplish a given task. For example, for the addition of large numbers, a math
080   problem, humans can easily solve the addition of any two numbers after learning the rules of
081   column addition. To implement rule-based calculating, Hu et al. (2024) instructs transformers to
082
      cite basic addition rules by explicitly fine-tuning transformers with them. While answering factual
      queries by means of retrieve-then-read in our task, human experts spontaneously search for relevant
083
      information as intermediate results following a priori rules before answering and refer to these rules
084
      again while deciding final answers (Shnarch et al., 2020). As shown in Figure 1 (a), if we ask
085   What is the nationality of Jean-Luc Godard? and no directly relevant information is contained
086   in the corpus D, the current retrievers depend on shallow representations and lead to recall many
087   word-level similar documents which involve Jean-Luc Godard or Nationality. In this case, the
088   retrieved information does not contribute anything semantically to answering. In fact, we know a prior
089   the rule that if someone is born in a certain country, there is a high probability that his (her) nationality
090   is also that country. Therefore, we can leverage this rule to conduct more effective and accurate
091   retrieval (Huang et al., 2023) and offer documents that can better support question answering (Figure 1
092   (b)). Similarly, in the stage of generating answers, since we input multiple documents, LLMs may get
093
      confused by a large amount of irrelevant information. If LLMs are explicitly instructed to incorporate
      the retrieved content and the queries through rules, they will better attribute answers as humans expect.
094
095   Upon the above motivation, we propose Rule-Guided Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RuleRAG),
096   a new QA approach, that enables to recall documents logically supporting queries explicitly in the
097   directions of rules and generates the final answers based on retrieved information and attributable
098   rules. Compared to standard RAG, training-free RuleRAG-ICL requires the introduction of rules
099
      with high confidence in the input sides of the retrievers and generators, aiming to guide the document
      retrieval and answer attribution processes. Moreover, to cultivate and boost the rule-following ability
100
      of LMs, we further propose RuleRAG-FT, which retrofits retrievers and generators via our designed
101
      rule-guided fine-tuning (RGFT). Specifically, for rule-guided retriever fine-tuning (RGFT-retriever),
102   we construct pairs of queries and rules as input with the training labels of oracle retrieval examples; for
103   rule-guided generator fine-tuning (RGFT-generator), we construct pairs of retrieved results, rules and
104   queries as input with the training labels of golden answers. In practice, we find that our obtained doc-
105   uments are highly compatible with queries and the generated answers are fairly targeted with ground
106   truths since the introduced rules improve the ability of LMs to organize causal relationships between
107   facts (Peng et al., 2024). In summary, both RuleRAG-ICL and RuleRAG-FT can associate the retrieval
      stage with the generation stage, achieving better recall of information and better answer accuracy.

      To demonstrate the effectiveness of RuleRAG, we newly construct five rule-aware QA benchmarks,
109   where the queries require answering temporal or static real-world factual questions and the answers
110   are the concrete entity names (Figure 1 is an example). It has been prone that the answers are hard to
111   directly predict based merely on queries due to limited storage capacity (Petroni et al., 2019; Dhingra
112   et al., 2022) and factual hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023) of LLMs. Therefore, each benchmark
113   offers a fact corpus which serves as the external data pool and contain documents possibly related to
114   queries. Our experiments show that, under several retrieval and generation configurations, RuleRAG-
115   ICL offers considerable performance gains with the individual guidance of rules by in-context learning
116   and RuleRAG-FT achieves further improvements by combining the fine-tuned retrievers and gener-
117
      ators. Extensive comparative studies and analyses confirm the superiority of symbolic rules and show
      the effectiveness of RuleRAG across various types of retrievers and generators. Moreover, RuleRAG-
118
      FT can be extrapolated to unseen rules without retraining owing to the transferable rule utilizing
119
      abilities during retrieving and generating enhanced by our designed rule-guided fine-tuning (RGFT).

124   Rule bank R. A huge amount of world knowledge, including static facts and temporal events, has
125   been stored in static KGs and temporal KGs (Jiang et al., 2023b). In the static scenario, several dif-
126   ferent relations can be simultaneously established between two entities. In the temporal scenario, two
127
      entities can interact multiple times at different timestamps. Hence, if relation r1 (rule body) can logi-
      cally explain the occurrence of relation r2 (rule head) between entities, we represent this relevance as
128
      rule r in a natural language form: [Entity 1, r1 , Entity 2] leads to [Entity 1, r2 , Entity 2]. We leverage
129
      the classical rule mining algorithm AMIE3 (Lajus et al., 2020) for static KGs and TLogic (Liu et al.,
130   2022) for temporal KGs. The frequently co-occur relations form rules with high confidence (Liao et al.,
131   2024) and we transform them to the above text string form. All these individual rules comprise our
132   rule bank R, which will be consistently leveraged in the training and inferring process of RuleRAG.
133
      Test dataset Q. To avoid skewed entity distribution, we include links with both popular and long-tail
134
      entities in KG test sets and adjust their numbers to achieve balance. The remaining links are converted
135
      into queries with tail entities in these links as ground truths. Different from PopQA (Mallen et al.,
136   2023) with more low-popularity entities from Wikidata, our benchmarks consider entities in uniform
137   distribution from five knowledge bases, aiming to show the more general effectiveness of our method.
138

      Corpus D and fine-tuning datasets, FR and FG . Different from EntityQuestions (Sciavolino et al.,
      2021), we linearize the links in KG training sets into documents by concatenating entity, relation and
140
      time, forming concise and distinct factoids in D, which serves as the retrieval source of RuleRAG. For
141
      RGFT, we split valid sets of KGs into two disjoint parts and convert the KG links of both parts into
142   queries: one part is for queries in the fine-tuning datasets FR for retrievers and the other part is for
143   queries in the fine-tuning datasets FG for generators. Specifically, we search the corresponding oracle
144   document examples from D for each query-rule pair by entity name and relation-matching heuristics
145   and take them as the golden training labels of the retrievers. Subsequently, we leverage the fine-tuned
146   retrievers to retrieve relevant documents for each query in FG and create fine-tuning instructions for
147   generators by combining retrieval results, rules and queries, with golden answers as supervision.

179   3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD : RULE RAG
180
181   In this section, we present details of our proposed novel rule-guided retrieval-augmented generation
182   with LMs (RuleRAG) for solving the task of knowledge-intensive factual queries. Notably, RuleRAG
183   includes training-free RuleRAG-ICL and fine-tuned RuleRAG-FT. First, we prompt RuleRAG-ICL
184   with queries and rules for in-context learning during retrieving and inferring. The rules are aimed to
185   guide retrievers to recall logically supportive documents and guide generators to predict attributable
186   answers. Then, RuleRAG-FT further fine-tunes the retrievers and generators to explicitly enhance
187
      their rule-following ability by our introduced rule-guided fine-tuning (RGFT), where we leverage
      the queries combining rules as fine-tuning data and the ground truth answers as supervision data.
188
      The inferring process of RuleRAG-FT is the same as RuleRAG-ICL.
189
190
      3.1   RULE RAG-ICL
191
192   Figure 2 (a) illustrates the inference flow of RuleRAG-ICL. Given a query q ∈ Q, we select a few
193   related rules Rq from R. Specifically, we first recognize the relation in the query q and then retrieve
194   the rules with this relation as the rule head, forming the guidance rules Rq for q. We append q with
195   one rule r ∈ Rq once at a time to avoid conflict and conduct rule-guided retrieval in the corpus D to
196   obtain the top-k relevant documents Dqr , where q provide the retrieval content and r provide retrieval
197   directions. Finally, Dqr from all rules in Rq are assembled to produce the final retrieval results Dq ,
198   and RuleRAG-ICL conditions on the query q, rules Rq and documents Dq to reason the answer a.
199   Rule-guided retriever (RG-retriever). Since each rule r ∈ Rq stands for a unique retrieval logic,
200   we retrieve all query-rule pairs (q,r) individually to avoid the conflict of different rules. Specifically,
201   the retriever calculates a relevant score s(di , q ◦ r) between a (q,r) pair and every document di ∈ D:
202   s(di , q ◦ r) = Ed (di ) · Eq (q ◦ r), where ◦ denotes sequence concatenation, · is dot product, Ed is
203   the document encoder and Eq is the query encoder. To stay within the context window size limit
204   of LLMs, we select the top-k scored documents, denoted as Dqr (r ∈ Rq ), for each (q,r) pair and
205
      combine all Dqr as the final retrieval results Dq for query q. This process is formalized as follows:

      3.2     RULE RAG-FT
217
218   The overview of our proposed rule-guided retriever and generator fine-tuning in RuleRAG-FT are
219   illustrated in Figure 2 (b) and (c), respectively. For rule-guided retriever fine-tuning (RGFT-retriever),
220   we update the LM encoders in a contrastive learning objective (Chen et al., 2020) and train over super-
221   vised fine-tuning data FR provided in our constructed benchmarks, where inputs are the queries plus
222   rules and supervised labels are heuristic oracle documents. Compared with retrievers employed with
223   simple retrieval principles, our fine-tuned retrievers can recall more relevant results, aligned with the
224
      preferences of the rules. For rule-guided generator fine-tuning (RGFT-generator), we adopt the super-
      vised instruction-tuning objective (Iyer et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024) while combining each query
225
      q with two components: retrieved documents Dq from the retrieval phase and the same set of rules Rq
226
      consistent with the retrieval phase. The rules introduced in the RGFT-generator train LLMs on how
227   to optimally attribute from the retrieved context into answers by following rules, making RuleRAG
228   leverage the fine-tuned retrievers more rationally. Experiments show our proposed RGFT can further
229   guarantee and boost the retrieval quality and answering accuracy of RuleRAG-FT than RuleRAG-ICL.
230
      Rule-guided retriever fine-tuning (RGFT-retriever). We utilize two main types of retrievers: sparse
231
      retrievers and dense retrievers. As the sparse retriever, we use Pyserini 1 to implement the standard
232
      training-free BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009), which relies on word-level frequencies. As the
233   dense retrievers, we adopt the dual-encoder based retriever architecture, such as DPR 2 and SimCSE 3 .

245   certain in-context learning ability to understand the rules. For greater model efficiency and control of
246   the output, we fine-tune our generators in RuleRAG-FT and further enhance the proficiency of LLMs

248   contains three parts: the relevant facts Dq retrieved by retrievers fine-tuned above, the rules Rq guiding
249   attributable retrieval logics and the original query q. The instruction prompt remains the same during
250   the fine-tuning of generators and inferring of RuleRAG to keep a similar knowledge distribution.
251   In practice, for open-source LLMs, we utilize the few-shot instruction fine-tuning strategy considering
252   the following two aspects. First, our introduced rules reform the data-centric training to the alignment
253   of task-centric abilities, i.e., it can be viewed as a reasoning task based on the guidance of rules (Zhou
254   et al., 2023) and our training aim is to learn to use them. Secondly, tuning all the data in FG is
255   prohibitive in time. We randomly select a fixed number of samples from FG to conduct few-shot
256   tuning (2048 samples in our practice). Our experiments show the effectiveness and generalization of
257   RuleRAG-FT although the samples can not cover all the rules. For closed-source LLMs, we perform
258   3-shot prompts as an empirical substitute of fine-tuning (Dai et al., 2023) due to the unavailable
259
      parameters. Specifically, we randomly select three ((q, Dq , Rq ), a) pairs from FG as fixed examples
      in the prompts, making up the in-context augmentation. The detailed prompts are in the Appendix A.9.

278   4.2   BASELINES
279
280   Given that LLMs have pre-trained with lots of world knowledge, we report the performance of directly
281
      using LLMs as answer predictors without retrieval (Standard Prompting in Table 2) for basic perfor-
      mance reference (Ouyang et al., 2024). Additionally, we compare RuleRAG with a wide range of base-
282
      lines based on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). We instantiate the widespread RAG framework
283
      using off-the-shelf LLMs and retrievers with queries as input, standing for the standard RAG methods
284   (Standard RAG in Table 2 and 3). Chain-of-thought (CoT) methods, verify-and-edit (VE; Zhao et al.
285   (2023)) and chain-of-knowledge (CoK; Li et al. (2024)) correct LLM outputs independently and
286   sequentially respectively by leveraging external knowledge sources. Following their implementation,
287   we initialize the knowledge sources as our corpus D for a fair comparison and use 3-shot CoT prompts.
288
289   4.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
290
291   For the retrieval stage, the quality of retrieved documents is critical for downstream queries and is
292   usually measured by Recall@k (Karpukhin et al., 2020), indicating whether the top-k blocks contain
293   targeted information. For our task, we calculate Recall@k (R@k,%) by checking whether the correct
294   answer to the given query is contained in the retrieved top-k documents. The higher R@k, the more
295
      potentially useful retrievers are for generators. For the generation stage, the quality of answers is
      measured by Exact Match (EM,%) and Token F1 (T-F1,%), which are widely recognized in QA perfor-
296
      mance evaluation (Zhu et al., 2021). For EM, an answer is deemed correct if its normalized form corre-
297
      sponds to any acceptable answer in the provided ground truth lists. T-F1 treats the answers and ground
298   truths as bags of tokens and computes the average token-level overlap between them (Li et al., 2023b).

502
503   8.1   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
504
505   Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) follows the paradigm of “retrieve-then-read”, where the re-
506   trieval module explicitly augments the generation module with external knowledge banks (Guu et al.,
507
      2020; Lewis et al., 2020). Retrieval approaches include sparse retrievers based on sparse bag-of-words
      representation (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009), dense retrievers based on dense vectors (Karpukhin
508
      et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021) and more complex hybrid search algorithms (Li et al., 2023a; Lin et al.,
509
      2023). The current RAG frameworks are widely adopted to complement the parametric knowledge of
510   LLMs along different stages (Gao et al., 2024), including pre-training stage (RETRO; Borgeaud et al.
511   (2022b), Atlas; Izacard et al. (2024), COG; Lan et al. (2023)), fine-tuning stage (Self-RAG; Asai
512   et al. (2023), SURGE; Kang et al. (2023), CoN; Yu et al. (2023)) and inference stage (DSP; Khattab
513   et al. (2023), KnowledGPT; Wang et al. (2023), RoG; Luo et al. (2024), CoK; Li et al. (2024)).
514
515   8.2   K NOWLEDGE - INTENSIVE QA
516
517   In the realm of QA, a series of queries are considered knowledge-intensive if humans or models
518   need access to large and external corpora. Researchers have developed many systems and proven the
519   effectiveness of RAG in many knowledge-intensive QA tasks (Petroni et al., 2021). Recently, upon the
520
      assumption that documents in the corpora can directly support the answer responses, RAFT (Zhang
      et al., 2024) and RA-DIT (Lin et al., 2024) fine-tune LLMs by concatenating documents and queries as
521
      prompts. However, many answers to factual queries are hidden in semantically dissimilar but logically
522
      related events, which leads to irrelevant information retrieved by imperfect retrievers inevitably con-
523   fusing RAG methods. Therefore, the integration of rules has gained significant attention (Wang et al.,
524   2024b;c). For instance, Wu et al. (2024) investigates the necessity of mitigating misleading irrelevant
525   interference in RAG. Sun et al. (2024) only discusses the rule-following abilities of LLMs without
526   retrieval and ignores how to obtain rules. In contrast, in this paper, our proposed RuleRAG involves
527   a more comprehensive consideration of mining rules, retrieving documents and generating answers.

530
531
      In this paper, we point out two high-level problems of current RAG and propose a method named
532
      rule-guided retrieval-augmented generation (RuleRAG) based on observations of the objective world.
533
      RuleRAG, including RuleRAG-ICL and RuleRAG-FT, can effectively improve the performance
534   of multiple pre-trained retrievers and generators by in-context learning and instruction fine-tuning,
535   respectively. RuleRAG-ICL intuitively shows that RAG can directly benefit from our proposal by
536   prompting LLMs with rules. To further improve the QA performance, RuleRAG-FT retrofits the
537   retrievers to recall more supportive information through the designed RGFT and updates generators to
538   make better use of the retrieved documents. Experiments show RuleRAG achieves strong performance
539   on the five constructed rule-aware QA benchmarks. In the future, we will explore how RuleRAG can
      effectively retrieve and answer when facing more complex queries and adapt to a wider range of rules.

      A.1   T HE ROBUSTNESS OF RULE RAG
929
930   In the inferring process, since we can not know the content of the queries in advance, we may match
931   some relevant rules for the queries regardless of whether the queries need the guidance of rules or
932   not. In our preliminary experiments, we also find that, in some cases, retrieving information for some
933   queries can directly match relevant documents.
934
      Therefore, in this section, we verify the robustness of our proposed method RuleRAG on queries which
935   may not need the guidance of rules. We want to know if our introduced rules will interfere with the
936   performance of retrieval and generation of such queries.
937
938
      Specifically, for each query in the benchmark, we degenerate it into a new relevant query by
      using the previously matched rules ( [Entity 1, r1 , Entity 2] leads to [Entity 1, r2 , Entity 2])
939
      and ensure that the answer is unchanged and that the relevant documents can be retrieved
940
      directly from the corpus. Meanwhile, according to the principle of performance comparison,
941   we try to minimize interference with the original queries. For instance in Figure 1, the original
942   query is What is the nationality of Jean-Luc Godard? and the rule is that “ [Entity 1, born
943   in, Entity 2] leads to [Entity 1, has nationality, Entity 2]”. Then, we convert the query into
944   Where is Jean-Luc Godard born?. In this way, these queries can theoretically be successfully
945   retrieved with related documents and correctly answered without the guidance of rules.
946
      In order to test the robustness of our rule-guided approach RuleRAG to such queries, we first conduct
947   the Standard RAG on them as a baseline and then test the performance of RuleRAG by adding
948   our previously matched rules. Hence, the only difference in the input of LMs between the main
949   experiment and this experiment is the queries. The others, including rules and answers, remain the
950   same. The results are shown in Table 5. We find (1) In terms of absolute performance, compared
951   Table 2, most of the results in Table 5 show a certain degree of degradation, which indicates that
952   we successfully achieve interference with the methods. (2) Compared to the Standard RAG in Table 5,
953   our proposed RuleRAG-ICL and RuleRAG-FT still achieve performance improvement over all
954   the evaluation metrics, showing that our methods can overcome the interference of irrelevant rules.
955
      Fine-tuning based RuleRAG-FT is consistently better than RuleRAG-ICL, showing that our proposed
      RGFT is effective for these queries. Therefore, our methods are robust.

1053   A.6   T HE D IFFERENCE OF O UR C ONSTRUCTED B ENCHMARKS AND E XISTING DATASETS
1054
1055   Existing conventional QA datasets include HotPotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiopQA (Ho
1056   et al., 2020), MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), and Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023). Although they
1057
       are widely leveraged in evaluating the QA performance of LMs, we find that all these datasets are
       primarily focused on multi-hop and comparison-type questions and pay less attention to queries that
1058
       require logical thinking to answer. As we know, many queries in the real world are not justified
1059
       by relevance alone, because in many cases the lexical level of relevance is not the information that
1060   can support the answer to the query, and even introduces a lot of noise instead. Therefore, in this
1061   paper, we construct five rule-aware benchmarks based on five popular static KGs or temporal KGs to
1062   emphasize the importance of rules in the QA task. It is worth noting that our described construction
1063   way in Section 2 is general and easy to reproduce. For newly defined rule patterns, we can quickly
1064   construct corresponding benchmarks using the above construction way, showing its better scalability.
1065
       Moreover, our constructed benchmarks also provide corresponding fine-tuning datasets, which aim to
1066   improve the retrieval and generation ability of LMs. Currently, obtaining high-quality and plentiful
1067   supervised data for a specific task is a challenging problem for researchers (Wang et al., 2024a).
1068   Manual annotation is time-consuming and difficult to replicate. A very convenient and widely used
1069   way is to distil knowledge from LLMs. However, relying on LLMs to generate data for training puts
1070   too much trust in them and does not actually guarantee the accuracy of the reasoning ability in the
1071   trained models.
1072   In contrast, in this paper, the fine-tuning datasets of the retrievers are obtained by pattern matching
1073   and retrieval recall; the fine-tuning datasets for generators are obtained by using the KG nodes as
1074   answers and using retrieved information as instructions. The entire process is efficiently streamlined
1075   and automatically generated.

1145   A.8    E RROR A NALYSIS
1146
1147   We further analyzed the detailed performance of our proposed model on 60*5 incorrectly answered
1148   queries from the five benchmarks. There were three main classes of errors:
1149   (a) Rule Failure (5%): In the real world, rules can reflect the logical workings of most events.
1150   However, we cannot claim that absolutely no exceptions occur. Among the incorrect responses we
1151   sampled, we found that the answers to some questions did not follow the general rules of reasoning,
1152   which in turn resulted in response failures. Future work could address such special cases separately.
1153
       (b) Retrieval Error (55%): In this section, we assume that a retrieval is considered correct as long
1154
       as the correct answer is included in the top 10 recalled documents, and a retrieval is considered
1155   incorrect otherwise. Due to the very large size of the corpus and the large number of documents that
1156   are semantically similar but do not support the answer, even a fine-tuned retriever may not recall
1157   relevant facts for the correct answer. In almost all cases, the question can not be answered correctly if
1158   the retrieved documents are wrong.
1159
       (c) Attribution Error (40%): Due to the complex logical relationships between events, when the
1160
       retrieved documents contain the correct answer, the generator may still fail to follow the rules and
1161   then come up with an incorrect answer. Generally, the more documents in the top 10 retrieved
1162   information that are related to the correct answer, the higher the probability that the generator will
1163   answer correctly. The problem of attribution error occurs generally because there are only one to
1164   three supportive documents in the retrieved information.
