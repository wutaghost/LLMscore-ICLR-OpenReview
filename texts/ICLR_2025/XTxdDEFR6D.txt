011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The optimization of algorithms in exact combinatorial optimization (CO) solver
014            plays a fundamental role in operations research. However, due to the extensive
015
               requirements on domain knowledge and the large search space for algorithm design,
               the refinement on these algorithms remains highly challenging for both manual and
016
               learning-based paradigms. To tackle this problem, we propose a novel machine
017
               learning framework—large language model for exact combinatorial optimization
018            solver (LLM4Solver)—to efficiently design high-quality algorithms of the CO
019            solvers. The core idea is that, instead of searching in the high-dimensional and dis-
020            crete symbolic space from scratch, we can utilize the prior knowledge learned from
021            large language models to directly search in the space of programming languages.
022            Specifically, we first use a pre-trained LLM as the generator for high-quality al-
023            gorithms. Then, to efficiently explore the discrete and non-gradient algorithm
024            space, we employ a derivative-free evolutionary framework as the algorithm op-
025            timizer. Experiments on extensive benchmarks show that the algorithms learned
026
               by LLM4Solver significantly outperform all the state-of-the-art (SOTA) human-
               designed and learning-based policies (on GPU) in terms of the solution quality, the
027
               solving efficiency, and the cross-benchmark generalization ability. The appealing
028
               features of LLM4Solver include 1) the high training efficiency to outperform SOTA
029            methods within ten iterations, and 2) the high cross-benchmark generalization abil-
030            ity on heterogeneous MIPLIB 2017. LLM4Solver shows the encouraging potential
031            to efficiently design algorithms for the next generation of modern CO solvers.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Combinatorial optimization (CO), which aims to find an optimal object from a finite solution set,
037   is one of the most fundamental models in operations research (OR) (Achterberg, 2007; Bengio
038   et al., 2021). It is widely used to formulate a series of important real-world tasks, e.g., scheduling,
039
      transportation, and management (Liu et al., 2008; Chen, 2010; Ma et al., 2019; Paschos, 2014). In
      these applications, the solving efficiency and the solution quality are usually related to enormous
040
      economic value (Kuang et al., 2023; Achterberg, 2007). Thus, the optimization for algorithms on
041
      exact CO solvers plays a fundamental role in the field of OR. Popular exact CO solvers like SCIP
042   (Gleixner et al., 2018) and Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2023) employ a rich set of hard-coded
043   heuristics, whose efficacy directly affects the performance of the CO solvers. Due to the complexity
044   of these heuristics, designing and optimizing them typically demand substantial domain expertise,
045   significant manual adjustments, and intricate workflows(Achterberg, 2007; Bengio et al., 2021).
046
      Recently, there has been an explosive surge in the use of machine learning (ML) techniques to
047
      enhance exact CO solvers. These learning-based approaches can be roughly divided into two classes.
048   One class incorporates deep neural networks (DNNs) to approximate different components in CO
049   solvers, e.g., the branching (Gasse et al., 2019), the cut selection (Wang et al., 2023), and the primal
050   heuristics (Paulus & Krause, 2024; Nair et al., 2020). Note that these DNN models fail to explain
051   what patterns they have learned that accelerate the CO solvers (Kuang et al., 2024a), and thus they
052   fail to help researchers further optimize the human-designed heuristics in solvers. To tackle this
053   problem, the other class (Kuang et al., 2024a;b) employs symbolic discovery approaches to learn more
      interpretable heuristics. Currently, these approaches are proved effective mainly on the branching


                                                         1

054
      component (Achterberg, 2007), in which symbolic regression (SR) is employed to learn interpretable
055   scoring functions that outperform DNN policies on purely CPU-based devices.
056
057   Previous symbolic discovery approaches(Kuang et al., 2024a;b) , though effective for branching,
058
      have two general limitations that severely hinder their potential applications to CO solvers. (1) Due
      to the high-dimensional and discrete search space for symbolic discovery and its nature of searching
059
      from scratch, existing SR-based approaches suffer from high computational costs. (2) The vastly
060
      different intrinsic nature of CO problems across various scenarios causes most approaches to fail in
061   generalizing to various benchmarks. However, we hope that learning-based approaches will design
062   more generic heuristics like human-designed ones to enhance the built-in performance of the solvers.
063
064
      The powerful capabilities of the large language models (LLM) in text comprehension and logic
      generation have attracted widespread attention(Naveed et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), offering new
065
      approaches for algorithm design. Romera-Paredes et al. (2024) combine LLM with the island-based
066
      evolution for mathematical discovery. Liu et al. (2024) and Ye et al. (2024) leverage LLM to revise
067   heuristics for online bin packing, traveling salesman problems, and flow shop scheduling problems.
068   Sun et al. (2024) create a multi-agent-based framework to improve the heuristics of SAT problems.
069   These works have demonstrated impressive results in scenarios like mathematical discovery, and
070   designing heuristics for classical CO and SAT problems. However, while online bin packing and
071   traveling salesman problems are highly representative and important CO problems, when modeled
072   and solved using general MILP formulations in CO solvers, it is critical to investigate heuristics to
073   find feasible solutions in more general MILP problems. Currently, there are no LLM-based heuristic
074   optimization methods specifically designed for general MILP problems and research on such methods
075
      holds more generic scientific significance.
076   In this work, we propose an automatic algorithm design framework—large language model for exact
077   combinatorial optimization solver (LLM4Solver)—to efficiently design high-quality diving heuristics
078   of the CO solvers. Specifically, we first use the LLM as an algorithm generator, leveraging it to
079   design three operators: initialization, crossover, and mutation, to generate new executable algorithm
080   code with LLM’s prior knowledge. Then, we treat the derivative-free evolutionary framework as
081
      an optimizer, utilizing it to iteratively optimize in the non-gradient algorithm space. Finally, we
      extend this framework through multi-objective evolution to leverage the heterogeneous characteristics
082
      of different CO problems to design more generic algorithms. Extensive experiments show that
083
      LLM4Solver-designed interpretable diving heuristics significantly outperform all the state-of-the-art
084   (SOTA) human-designed and learning-based policies (on GPU) in terms of solution quality, solving
085   efficiency, and cross-benchmark generalization ability.
086
087
      We summarize the highly appealing features of LLM4Solver as follows. (1) High performance.
      LLM4Solver outperforms all the baselines, including both the human-designed diving heuristics
088
      in SCIP (Gleixner et al., 2018) and the SOTA learning-based policy on GPU (Paulus & Krause,
089
      2024), in terms of the solution quality (Table 1) and the solving efficiency (Table 2). (2) Efficient
090   searching. LLM4Solver outperforms the SOTA learning-based approaches within only four iterations
091   and converges to optimum within ten iterations, respectively (Figure 2). (3) Strong cross-benchmark
092   generalization ability. LLM4Solver can design a generic diving heuristic with high cross-benchmark
093   generalization ability on different benchmarks (Table 3), including the highly challenging heteroge-
094   neous MIPLIB 2017 (Table 4). (4) Good interpretability. The programs with comments designed
095   by LLM4Solver (Figure 4) clearly illustrate the execution logic of the algorithms, offering better
096   interpretability compared to neural network parameters(Paulus & Krause, 2024) and purely symbolic
097   expressions(Kuang et al., 2024a). LLM4Solver shows the potential to efficiently design high-quality
098
      and generic algorithms for the next generation of solvers, thereby enhancing their built-in capabilities.

109   side vector, l, u respectively the lower and upper bounds and I denotes the index of integer variables.
110   In exact solvers like SCIP (Gleixner et al., 2018), MILPs are solved with the branch-and-bound (B&B)

112   partition the problem into two subproblems. Specifically, one adds constraint xi ≤ ⌊x∗i ⌋ and the other
113   adds xi ≥ ⌈x∗i ⌉, where the x∗i is the fractional value in the solution of the linear programming (LP)

117   solving efficiency. Among them, diving heuristics is one of the most common primal heuristics and
118   has a significant impact on the performance of solvers. They perform a depth-first search by iteratively
119   rounding a variable and solving the modified LP relaxation problems until a feasible solution is found
120   or infeasibility is proven. Algorithm 1 details the diving heuristic process. The scoring function s,
121   used to select the variables and rounding direction, is the most crucial part of diving heuristics.

      population of feasible solutions to the problem.

131

132

136   The set of all Pareto optimal solutions is called the Pareto Front. MOEAs leverage the evolution
137   framework and output the Pareto Front of the multi-objective optimization problem.

187   3    M ETHODS
188
189
      Modern CO solvers like SCIP (Gleixner et al., 2018) are highly complex, typically containing up to
190
      millions of lines of code. Thus, directly designing a whole CO solver end-to-end is challenging, as
191
      the search space grows exponentially with the algorithm complexity (Kuang et al., 2024b). Instead, in
192   this paper, we mainly focus on the design of diving heuristic, which is widely recognized as one of the
193   most critical primal heuristics in exact CO solvers to find high-quality solutions within a reasonably
194   short time (Achterberg, 2007; Paulus & Krause, 2024).
195
      Due to the large search space and lack of prior knowledge, previous manual and learning-based
196
      paradigms are inefficient for designing heuristics in CO solvers. Thus, we propose a novel framework
197
      (see Figure 1 and pseudo code 2)—large language model for exact combinatorial optimization solver
198   (LLM4Solver)—to efficiently design high-quality and generic diving heuristics. The core idea of
199   LLM4Solver is that, instead of defining complex symbols and searching in the space of symbolic
200   trees (Poli et al., 2008; Petersen, 2019; Kuang et al., 2024a), we can utilize the prior knowledge
201   learned from large language models to conduct efficient searching directly at the program space.
202   We further extend this framework through multi-objective evolution to leverage the heterogeneous
203   characteristics of different CO problems and design more generic heuristics.
204   Generally, as shown in Figure 1, LLM4Solver begins evolution with population initialization, itera-
205   tively optimizing the algorithms through the following steps: parent selection, crossover, mutation,
206   fitness evaluation, and survivor selection. It leverages the prior knowledge of LLM to generate new
207   algorithm candidates during initialization, crossover, and mutation (shown in the bottom part of
208   Figure 1). During fitness evaluation, single-objective evolution uses one fitness function, f1 , while
209   multi-objective evolution considers multiple fitness functions, f1 , f2 , . . . , fn , to assess performance
210   across various CO problems and design a more generic algorithm.
211   Specifically, Section 3.1 first describes how to represent algorithms as individuals and how to evaluate
212   the individuals in the evolution. Then, in Section 3.2 we introduce the idea of utilizing LLM to
213   generate new individuals. After that, Section 3.3 describes the process of selecting the survivor
214   individuals for the next generation of populations. Moreover, in Section 3.4 we introduce LLM4Solver
215   with multi-objective evolution to simultaneously utilize information from different CO problems and
      design an algorithm with cross-benchmark generalization ability.

      3.1   I NDIVIDUAL R EPRESENTATION AND F ITNESS E VALUATION
217
218   As a population-based optimization strategy, each individual in the evolutionary process is represented
219   by a diving score function s and the description of its logic. The fitness of each individual is assessed
220   based on its solving performance in specific instances. Throughout the evolutionary process, a
221   population of N individuals is maintained to facilitate optimization.
222   Diving Score Function The diving score function s is the key decision-making component of the
223   diving heuristic (See Appendix B). It determines the next diving variable and the rounding direction,
224   which directly impacts the solving performance. For a diving score function s with Python format,
225   we employ a variable’s 13 features as input and output score (float, the score of the variable) and
226   roundup (bool, True if round the variable up, False for rounding down). These 13 features listed
227   in Table 8 represent the union of all features used by the human-designed diving heuristics in SCIP.
228   They are cheap to obtain, interpretable, and effectively describe the state of variables.
229   As previous methods based on neural networks(Paulus & Krause, 2024) and symbolic discov-
230   ery(Kuang et al., 2024a) do not consider the description of algorithm logic, we treat both the diving
231   score function and its logical description as an individual like (Liu et al., 2024). As shown in the
232   bottom part of Figure 1, these logical descriptions provide a high-level idea for the corresponding al-
233   gorithms, helping both the LLM and humans understand the algorithms. Through steps like crossover
234   and mutation, LLM can combine and mutate these ideas to guide the generation of new algorithms.
235   Fitness Evaluation As primal heuristics aim to find better feasible solutions, we use the quality of
236   solutions found by the diving heuristics as their fitness. Specifically, we embed s in SCIP, turn off the
237   other heuristics, dive into the root node, and the fitness is

      3.2   G ENERATING N EW I NDIVIDUALS WITH LLM
244
245   The text comprehension and logic generation abilities exhibited by LLMs closely align with the
246   algorithm design. Therefore, we leverage the capabilities of LLM with prompt engineering to quickly
247   generate new individuals in initialization, crossover and mutation steps.
248
      Prompt Engineering As general LLM lacks sufficient information in specific domains, we need to
249
      provide more details of diving heuristics and specific instructions through prompt engineering. For the
250   convenience of LLM’s comprehension, we categorize the prompts into two groups: 1) the background
251   prompts to provide the details about diving heuristics and 2) the task-specific prompts to instruct how
252   to generate a new individual in initialization, crossover or mutation steps. Specifically, the background
253   prompts consist of the introduction of MILP and diving heuristics and give the pseudo-code of diving
254   for task-specific prompts. The task-specific prompts comprise the input features description, in-out
255   format description and task-specific instruction. We give specific prompts in Appendix E.
256   Initialization, Crossover and Mutation In the initialization step, we need to leverage the capabilities
257   of LLM to generate an individual from scratch. We directly use initialization-specific prompts and
258   the LLM to generate a new individual. After running N times, we can get the first population
259   P = {s1 , s2 , ..., sN }. In the crossover step, we need to recombine the advantages of the parent
260   individuals to obtain a better offspring individual. Specifically, we provide l parent algorithms
261   and recombine them with crossover-specific prompts to generate r offspring individuals. Then, we
262   employ the LLM with mutation-specific prompts to mutate the offspring individuals generated by
263   crossover, thereby exploring new individuals in the vicinity of the current one. We run the crossover
264
      and mutation for N times in each generation and get rN new individuals after that.
265   Parent Selection Before crossover, we need to select l parent algorithms from the population. As
266   parent selection needs to balance randomness and optimality, we adopt the fitness proportional
267   selection(Zhou et al., 2019) to determine the probability of each individual sn being selected, i.e.

274   3.3       E LITISM S URVIVOR S ELECTION FOR P OPULATION O PTIMIZATION
275
276   The Survivor Selection Policy plays a vital role in determining which individuals are kept and which
277   are removed from the next generation. It is essential to ensure that the best individuals are preserved
278   within the population. Specifically, we employ elitism survivor selection(Zhou et al., 2019) which
279   always select the N individuals with best fitness from the total (r + 1)N ones after crossover and
280   mutation. Elitism survivor selection can excellently ensure the optimality of individuals, which is
281
      helpful for efficiently finding high-quality diving heuristics.
282   3.4       E MPLOYING M ULTI - OBJECTIVE E VOLUTION FOR C ROSS - BENCHMARK G ENERALIZATION
283
284   To design a unified algorithm with cross-benchmark generalization ability for the CO solver, we need
285
      to leverage the information of different benchmarks simultaneously. However, previous gradient-
      based work has only one optimization objective(Gasse et al., 2019; Kuang et al., 2024a;b), which
286
      is the algorithm’s performance on a single benchmark, making it difficult to simultaneously utilize
287
      information from multiple benchmarks. To tackle this problem, we treat the performance of the
288   algorithm on different benchmarks as separate objectives and utilize multi-objective evolution to
289   harness information from different benchmarks. Specifically, the differences between single-objective
290   and multi-objective are in the fitness evaluation, parent selection, and survivor selection steps.
291
      Fitness Evaluation of Multi-objective Evolution We treat the fitness of the diving heuristics on
292
      different benchmarks as different objectives. Specifically, the m-th objective (fitness) is

297   Parent and Survivor Selection of Multi-objective Evolution We use the binary tournament
298   selection(Deb et al., 2002) for parent selection. Specifically, we randomly choose 2 individuals from
299   the population and select the better one as a parent. Similarly, we use the elitism survivor selection
300   for multi-objective evolution to select the best next generation. Since multi-objective optimization
301
      cannot simply compare two individuals using a single fitness function, we employ Non-dominated
      Sorting (See Appendix F) and Crowding Distance Sorting to perform the necessary comparisons.

      Implementation Details (1) For the solution quality, Diving is solely implemented in the root node
348
      of each instance, with branching, cutting planes, and other primal heuristics disabled, emphasizing
349   the quality of feasible solutions found by diving heuristics. For fitness evaluation in the evolution, we
350   generate 50 instances each for Setcover, Cauctions, and Indset, and 10 instances for Facilities. We
351   validate and test the discovered diving heuristics on 100 instances each. (2) For the solving efficiency,
352   we embed the discovered diving heuristics into the SCIP. We use the LoadBalance dataset(Gasse et al.,
353   2019) with 100 instances for validation and testing respectively. These instances cannot be solved
354   within 3600 seconds, so we set a limit time Tlimit = 900 seconds and measure its primal-dual integral
355   P D(Tlimit ) as the solving performance. We use the NNVerify dataset with 50 instances for validation
356   and 523 for testing. We set the maximal solving time limit Tlimit = 3600 seconds and measure the
357
      solving time T . We use solution quality as the evaluation criterion and then utilize the primal-dual
      integral or solving time for validation and testing, selecting the diving heuristic that demonstrates the
358
      best performance in solving efficiency. (3) For LLM4Solver with multi-objective evolution algorithm
359
      (MOEA), we set the primal gap on four benchmarks (Setcover, Cauctions, Facilities, and Indset)
360   as four objectives. Then, we chose the diving heuristic in the Pareto front with the highest average
361   improvement ratio compared to the human-designed heuristic on four benchmarks as the output.
362   We employ the designed diving heuristic to the MIPLIB instances to show the cross-benchmark
363   generalization ability. We report the mean of the primal gap and the wins for comparison.

521   5     C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   In this paper, we propose a novel LLM-based automatic algorithm design framework for combinatorial
524   optimization solvers to efficiently design high-quality and generic diving heuristics. To leverage
525   the heterogeneous characteristics of different CO problems, we extend this framework through
526   multi-objective evolution. Extensive experiments show that LLM4Solver significantly outperforms
527   all the SOTA human-designed and learning-based (on GPU) methods in terms of solution quality,
528   solving efficiency, and cross-benchmark generalization ability. Furthermore, the appealing features
529
      of LLM4Solver include high performance, efficient searching, and interpretability of the designed
      algorithms. The results show an encouraging step towards efficient automatic algorithm design on
530
      modern exact CO solvers via large language models. Applying LLM4Solver to more components
531
      in modern CO solvers like branching (Gasse et al., 2019; Kuang et al., 2024a), presolve (Kuang
532   et al., 2023; Achterberg, 2007), and cut generation (Huang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) are
533   exciting avenues for further work. Moreover, the automated algorithm design framework based
534   on multi-objective evolution can be extended to more complex problems like multi-objective CO
535   problems(Chen et al., 2024; Lust & Teghem, 2010) and electronic design automation(Wang et al.,
536   2024). Finally, LLM4Solver shows the potential to efficiently design high-quality and generic
537   algorithms for the next generation of solvers, thereby enhancing their built-in capabilities.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
      Existing CO solvers consist of branching, cut selection, primal heuristics, and other essential modules
705   (Achterberg, 2007). The application of Machine Learning (ML) leverages its inherent capabilities
706   to learn data distributions and replace one or more modules within the solver to enhance solving
707   efficiency. The research on ML for CO solvers can be roughly categorized into two classes. One class
708   aims to substitute modules with deep neural networks, for example, Gasse et al. (2019) propose a
709   bipartite graph presentation of CO problems and replace the branching module with the graph neural
710   network (GNN). Nair et al. (2020) construct a Neural Solver to generate a high-quality assignment
711   and optimality gap. Han et al. (2023) leverage the predict-and-search framework to generate better
712   assignment. Wang et al. (2023) develop a hierarchical sequence/set model to learn cut selection
713
      policies. While previous works achieved significant results, complex models face disadvantages
      including high requirement of training samples, low interpretability, and great deployment difficulty
714
      (Kuang et al., 2024a). To tackle these problems, the other class aims to discover symbolic algorithms
715
      in a data-driven methodology. Kuang et al. (2024a) leverage deep symbolic regression to learn
716   branching policies in expressions, outperforming previous neural network methods on purely CPU-
717   based devices. Symbolic learning-based methods also have broad prospects for scientific discovery.
718   Chen et al. (2023) propose program search techniques and discovered a more efficient optimization
719   algorithm Lion. Mankowitz et al. (2023) train an agent AlphaDev to learn sorting algorithms
720   and achieve better performance than human benchmarks. Although the discovery process runs
721   automatically, training these works still requires developing a novel algorithm from scratch, which is
722   inefficient with limited prior knowledge.
723   As the performance of Large Language Models grows rapidly (Naveed et al., 2023), researchers
724   attempt to combine the prior knowledge of Large Language Models (LLMs) with algorithm design.
725   Yang et al. (2023) propose an approach that iteratively optimizes prompts and solutions to problems
726   by LLMs. Xiao & Wang (2023) leverage LLMs to assist the design of the robotic modules and attain
727   the utility-optimal A* algorithm. Despite the excellent capabilities of LLMs, it is still tough to design
728   algorithms for complex problems with solely LLMs and prompt engineering. Thus, recent works
729   consider evolutionary search methods and obtain more competitive algorithms through the continuous
730
      iterative evolution of algorithms generated by LLM. Romera-Paredes et al. (2024) combine LLM
      with the island-based evolutionary method, and discover new algorithms for classic mathematical
731
      problems like cap set and online bin packing, revealing the great potential of this combination. Liu
732
      et al. (2023) introduce a novel approach AEL and discover heuristics with excellent generalization
733   performance on the traveling salesman problem. Ye et al. (2024) leverage LLM to revise heuristics for
734   online bin packing, traveling salesman problems, and electronic design automation. Sun et al. (2024)
735   create a multi-agent-based framework to improve the heuristics of SAT problems. These works have
736   demonstrated impressive results in scenarios like mathematical discovery, and designing heuristics for
737   classical CO and SAT problems. However, while online bin packing and traveling salesman problems
738   are highly representative and important CO problems, when modeled and solved using general MILP
739   formulations in CO solvers, it is critical to investigate heuristics to find feasible solutions in more
740   general MILP problems. Especially in the domain of exact CO solver, various cross-distribution
741
      problems, such as Set covering (Balas & Ho, 1980), Combinatorial auction (Leyton-Brown et al.,
      2000), Capacitated facility location (Cornuéjols et al., 1991), Maximum independent set (Bergman
742
      et al., 2015), etc., are modeled by a unified format for solving. Hence, it is essential to design a
743
      general algorithm that can cater to diverse problem types for exact CO solvers.

783   pseudocosts provide an estimation for each integer variable, indicating the increase in the objective
784
      value of the LP problem per unit change in that variable.
785   Vector Length Diving is custom-made for set covering and applied to general MIPs. It selects the
786   variable with the smallest ratio of the objective cost to the constraints covered by fixing it to 1.

976    F    N ON - DOMINATED S ORTING AND C ROWDING D ISTANCE S ORTING
977
978    In multi-objective evolution, both parent selection and survivor selection require comparisons between
979
       two or more individuals. Therefore, an appropriate method for individual comparison is crucial.
       We employ Non-dominated Sorting and Crowding Distance Sorting to compare individuals. In the
980
       Non-dominated Sorting phase, each individual s is assigned a rank, where rank = 1 indicates that
981
       s is not dominated by any other individual in the population, while a rank = n + 1 indicates that s
982    is only dominated by individuals with rank ≤ n. Thus, a lower rank is preferred.
983
984    If two individuals have the same rank, we then compare their crowding distance, which measures the
985
       distance of s from other individuals s′ in the population. To encourage diversity within the population,
       a larger crowding distance is favored.
986
987    In binary tournament selection, we randomly select two individuals from the population at a time and
988    choose the better one after comparison. After l selections, we obtain l parent individuals. In elitism
