009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, there has been considerable interest in leveraging the Optimal
012            Transport (OT) problem for domain adaptation, a strategy shown to be highly ef-
013            fective. However, a less explored aspect is the choice of the transportation cost
014            function, as most existing methods rely on the pairwise squared Euclidean dis-
015            tances for the transportation cost, potentially overlooking important intra-domain
016
               geometries. This paper presents Diffusion-OT, a new transport cost for the OT
               problem, designed specifically for domain adaptation. By utilizing concepts and
017
               tools from the field of manifold learning, specifically diffusion geometry, we de-
018
               rive an operator that accounts for the intra-domain relationships, thereby extending
019            beyond the conventional inter-domain distances. This operator, which quantifies
020            the probability of transporting between source and target samples, forms the ba-
021            sis for our transportation cost. We provide proof that the proposed operator is
022            in fact a diffusion operator, demonstrating that the cost function is defined by
023            an anisotropic diffusion process between the domains. In addition, to enhance
024            performance, we integrate source labels into the operator, thereby guiding the
025            anisotropic diffusion according to the classes. We showcase the effectiveness
026            of Diffusion-OT through comprehensive experiments, demonstrating its superior
027
               performance compared to recent methods across various benchmarks and datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) is a widely explored field within machine learning. In
032   UDA, given labeled data from a domain denoted as the source domain, we aim to train a model that
033   will generalize well to unlabeled data from a different domain denoted as the target domain. Optimal
034   Transport (OT) stands out as a key method for addressing the UDA problem. The essence of the OT
035   problem lies in finding the most efficient way to transport from one distribution to another while
036   minimizing a transportation cost. It offers a robust approach to measuring the similarity between
037   probability density functions and facilitating an optimal mapping between them. This capability
038   makes it well-suited for UDA tasks, where there is a need for distribution alignment.
039   An established approach to utilizing OT for UDA is the Optimal Transport for Domain Adaptation
040   (OTDA) framework (Courty et al., 2016), which directly employs the transport plan to map source
041   samples to the target domain via barycentric mapping. While OTDA has proven effective in address-
042   ing the UDA task, the OT solution may not always yield the optimal mapping for maximizing target
043   accuracy. To improve accuracy, a common practice is to add a regularization term into the OT prob-
044   lem formulation (Courty et al., 2014; 2016; Flamary et al., 2014). Alternatively, in recent years, the
045
      OT problem has been incorporated into deep learning models. One notable method, termed DeepJ-
      DOT (Damodaran et al., 2018), uses the OT problem to address the discrepancy between source and
046
      target distributions, subsequently leveraging the obtained transportation plan to train a feature ex-
047
      tractor implemented through a deep model. Following DeepJDOT, numerous studies have proposed
048   OT-based deep models, aimed at improving target accuracy in the UDA task. Some have suggested
049   different OT formulations to be used within the DeepJDOT framework (Fatras et al., 2021; Nguyen
050   et al., 2022a), while others have introduced entirely new architectures and objective functions (Chen
051   et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Balaji et al., 2020). While research on utilizing OT for DA tasks is ex-
052   tensive and constantly evolving, one aspect that remains relatively unexplored is the selection of the
053   transportation cost function. The transportation cost can take the form of any dissimilarity measure
      between samples in the source and target domains. Nevertheless, the predominant choice tends to


                                                         1

054
      be a distance function, with the most common choice being the squared Euclidean distance, com-
055   puted between source and target samples. Only a small number of papers have suggested using a
056   cost function diverging from the typical Euclidean distance measure (Tai et al., 2021; Nguyen et al.,
057   2022b; Duque et al., 2023). Notably, this exploration of the transportation cost function is even rarer
058   in the DA context, with only a few studies proposing alternative approaches, such as employing a
059   weighted Euclidean distance as the transport cost (Li et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020).
060
      In this paper, we depart from reliance on distance functions as the transportation cost, and instead,
061   focus on the probability of transportation from source samples to target samples. To achieve this, we
062   introduce a new transportation cost, which we term Diffusion-OT, based on an operator composed
063   of three diffusion operators. The construction of the operator uses concepts and tools from the
064   field of manifold learning, specifically, diffusion geometry (Coifman & Lafon, 2006). Initially, we
065   construct a graph solely from the source samples, where each node represents a sample in the source
066   data. From this graph, we build the first diffusion operator, which aims at preserving the local
067   geometry of the source domain. When source labels are available, they are utilized to further guide
068   the diffusion process, ensuring that each source sample can only diffuse (transport) to other samples
069
      within the same label category. Subsequently, we build a bipartite graph incorporating both source
      and target samples to capture the inter-domain relationships. We construct the second operator from
070
      this graph, which facilitates cross-domain diffusion. For the third diffusion operator, we construct a
071
      graph exclusively from target samples, aimed at capturing intra-domain similarities within the target
072   domain. Taking the product of the three aforementioned operators forms a composite operator. We
073   show theoretically that this composite operator is a diffusion operator, demonstrating an anisotropic
074   diffusion process between the source and target domains. Our analysis underscores that this process
075   is primarily influenced by the intrinsic geometry of the domains and their discrepancy conveyed by
076   the Radon-Nikodym (RN) derivative.
077
      Our main contribution. We introduce a new transportation cost, termed Diffusion-OT, which is
078   based on a composite diffusion operator consisting of three diffusion steps: (i) in the source do-
079   main, (ii) across domains, and (iii) in the target domain. This framework enables the learning of the
080   geometries and relationships both between and within the two domains, distinguishing it from con-
081   ventional approaches by considering both inter-domain distances and intra-domain structures. By
082   incorporating source label information into our cost, we eliminate the need for regularization terms,
083   ensuring compatibility with any OT solver and problem formulation, as we demonstrate in Section
084   5.2. Additionally, the proposed cost is straightforward to compute and is derived directly from the
085   data, in contrast to competing cost functions that necessitate learning the cost and are thus limited to
086
      deep learning frameworks. Finally, we present experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of our
      method compared to competing approaches. Specifically, by incorporating Diffusion-OT into a deep
087
      domain adaptation framework, we attain superior performance on benchmark datasets compared to
088
      the baseline and other OT-based methods. Additionally, we evaluate our cost on benchmarks of
089   Motor Imagery (MI), showcasing state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on non-Euclidean data.

092   2   R ELATED WORK
093
094
      UDA is a subfield of machine learning that addresses the challenges of domain shifts and general-
095
      ization, where a model trained on data from a source domain performs poorly when applied to a
096   target domain with a different data distribution. Various methods and metrics have been proposed
097   to align the distributions of source and target domains, including Maximum Mean Discrepancy
098   (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2012; Pan et al., 2010), Correlation Alignment (CORAL) (Sun et al., 2017),
099   and Domain Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN) (Ganin et al., 2016), to mention just a few.
100
      The Optimal Transport for Domain Adaptation (OTDA) framework was introduced by Courty et al.
101
      (2016). In addition to the general framework, the authors propose the integration of several regular-
102   ization terms to improve performance. These include a class-based regularization term (Courty et al.,
103   2014) and Laplace regularization (Flamary et al., 2014). A notable work is the JDOT framework
104   introduced in Courty et al. (2017), which presents an optimization problem designed to simulta-
105   neously optimize both the transportation plan and the classifier. Building upon this concept, the
106   DeepJDOT framework (Damodaran et al., 2018) further extends the idea by leveraging deep learn-
107   ing algorithms. In Fatras et al. (2021), the authors proposed enhancing the DeepJDOT framework
      by employing Unbalanced Optimal Transport (UOT), yielding improved results. A related work by


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Nguyen et al. (2022a) suggested solving it using Partial Optimal Transport (POT) and presented a
109   two-stage implementation for the DeepJDOT framework, further enhancing target accuracy, partic-
110   ularly when coupled with the POT formulation. The OT problem was later incorporated into various
111   frameworks utilizing deep models and adversarial training (Chen et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Xie
112   et al., 2019; Balaji et al., 2020), all with the squared Euclidean distance serving as the ground cost.
113
      Few papers in the literature have proposed alternative cost functions diverging from the standard
114
      distance metric. For instance, Tai et al. (2021) introduced an innovative approach termed SLA,
115   which utilizes the OT formulation as a label assignment method. Gu et al. (2022) introduced a semi-
116   supervised cost for Heterogeneous DA, with a similar approach proposed in Duque et al. (2023)
117   for manifold alignment. Asadulaev et al. (2022) proposed a neural network-based framework for
118   learning task-specific semi-supervised cost functions. Although they do not specifically propose a
119   transport cost, Yan et al. (2018) and Xu et al. (2019) present innovative approaches to incorporate
120   both intra-domain and inter-domain relationships within Gromov-Wasserstein-based frameworks.
121   In the context of UDA, Li et al. (2020) introduced ETD, which employs a weighted Euclidean dis-
122   tance as the cost function, with weights estimated via an attention mechanism. Similarly, Xu et al.
123   (2020) proposed RWOT, where the weights are dynamically computed using both spatial prototyp-
124   ical information and the pseudo-classification probability of target samples. Both methods have
125   fundamental drawbacks compared to our proposed approach. First, the weights in ETD and RWOT
126   are learned, rather than directly derived from the data as they are in Diffusion-OT, which limits their
127   applicability to deep learning frameworks. Furthermore, the costs in ETD and RWOT, including the
128   learned weights, consider only the inter-domain relationships and source label information, whereas
129
      our cost also incorporates intra-domain relationships for both source and target domains.
130   By integrating principles from diffusion maps (Coifman & Lafon, 2006) into our approach, we
131   effectively capture intra-domain relationships and the underlying geometric structure of the data
132   within our proposed cost. Diffusion maps, initially introduced as a dimensionality reduction tech-
133   nique, provide a method to analyze and visualize high-dimensional data. The core concept involves
134   constructing a transition kernel, where transition probabilities are defined by the local similarities
135
      between data points. Diffusion geometry has since been widely applied in various fields, including
      data analysis (Coifman & Lafon, 2006), computer vision (Bronstein et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2012),
136
      and medical imaging (Haghverdi et al., 2015; Zheludev et al., 2015), among others.
137
138   For a more comprehensive review of the related work and diffusion geometry specifically, see Ap-
139   pendices A and B, respectively.

      4    D IFFUSION O PTIMAL T RANSPORT

      In this work, we present Diffusion-OT, a new cost function for the OT problem, designed specifically
184   for the UDA setting described in Section 3. Our premise is the manifold assumption, which is widely
185   spread in modern high-dimensional data analysis and gives rise to an extensive body of work termed
186   manifold learning (Roweis & Saul, 2000; Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Coifman
187   & Lafon, 2006). In the UDA setting, it implies that both domains are supported on the same hidden
188   manifold M embedded in X . When the squared Euclidean distance is used as the transport cost,
189   whether explicitly stated or not, it is assumed that the source and target domains are embedded in Rd .
190   The manifold assumption extends this concept, allowing for the possibility that the samples may not
191   lie within a Euclidean space. Building on this foundation, we propose defining the OT cost matrix
192
      C based on a diffusion operator, composed of three diffusion steps. Notably, the construction of this
      operator stems from diffusion geometry (Coifman & Lafon, 2006), which is a prevalent manifold
193
      learning approach (see Appendix B for details).
194

197   source sample. Assuming that the hidden manifold M is locally Euclidean, we use the Euclidean
198
      distance to build the affinity matrix of the graph, Ks ∈ RNs ×Ns , with a Gaussian kernel:

356   differences between Diffusion-OT and these three competing methods.
357   In Figure 1, we visualize the plans obtained using the aforementioned methods for a 40◦ rotation
358   angle, focusing on one target sample at the top edge of the red moon, marked by a bold black square.
359   For clarity, we plot lines from this target sample to the ten source samples with the highest values
360   in the corresponding column of the plan. In Figure 1a, we observe that Diffusion-OT has achieved
361   a nearly optimal plan, with almost all source samples contributing mass to the red-labeled target
362   sample belonging to the red class, except for one. In OT (Figure 1b), since the transportation cost
363
      is based solely on the pairwise Euclidean distances, the plan results in undesirable mapping, where
      most of the mass contributed to the chosen sample comes from source samples associated with the
364
      blue class, even though the target sample is red-labeled. In OT-reg (Figure 1c), the regularization
365
      term promotes group sparsity, encouraging a solution where each target sample receives mass only
366   from source samples within the same class. In this case, it results in the most undesirable plan,
367   where all the source samples contributing mass to the chosen target sample are from the opposite
368   class. In OT-Laplace (Figure 1d), the core idea is that samples with small Euclidean distances before
369   transportation should remain close after transportation. We observe that the plan merely mirrors
370   the behavior of the standard OT. The neighborhood information encoded in the regularization is
371   insufficient to correct the Euclidean cost. We note that there are fewer than ten lines because this
372   method does not include entropy regularization, resulting in a sparser transportation plan.
373   This scenario highlights Diffusion-OT’s effectiveness in handling challenging mappings. Diffusion-
374   OT leverages source labels not as strict constraints, as OT-reg, but to guide the diffusion process
375   in the source domain, while still accounting for the local geometry of the data. Additionally, it
376   shows that by taking into account intra-domain relationships, rather than relying solely on pairwise
377   Euclidean distances, the chosen sample can receive mass from source samples that are remote in
      Euclidean terms, as the diffusion distance is effectively incorporated. Indeed, we see that Diffusion-

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
534   In this work, we introduce a novel transportation cost of OT for DA, constructed using diffusion
535   geometry tools. Our experimental results highlight that Diffusion-OT achieves superior performance
536   compared to other methods across various UDA tasks. We acknowledge a fundamental limitation of
537   our method; since it incorporates inter-domain relationships, it operates under the assumption that
538   both source and target domains reside in the same space. While this assumption is common to all
539   DA methods, we believe that Diffusion-OT has the potential to be extended to other applications,
      which we view as directions for future work.

764   In the OTDA framework, proposed by Courty et al. (2016), the authors suggest the integration of
765   several regularization terms to improve performance, including a class-based regularization term
766   (Courty et al., 2014) that utilizes source label information. By employing specific parameter con-
767   figurations, the optimization problem can be effectively addressed using the Sinkhorn algorithm
768   (Cuturi, 2013). The authors also proposed applying graph regularization on the transported samples
769   (Flamary et al., 2014). While this regularization, termed Laplace regularization, considers neigh-
770   borhood information prior to transportation, it is limited to Euclidean geometry. Furthermore, their
771   optimization problem is quadratic, rendering it unsolvable by efficient algorithms and resulting in
772
      high computational complexity. Consequently, it is impractical for incorporation into deep models
      or application to large datasets.
773
774   DeepJDOT (Damodaran et al., 2018) enhances the OTDA framework by employing deep models.
775   This approach addresses two significant drawbacks in the OTDA framework. Firstly, handling large
776   datasets becomes infeasible with OTDA due to the quadratic increase in complexity with the number
777   of samples n, and the fact that it involves computations with n × n matrices. Conversely, DeepJDOT
778
      can train deep models using mini-batches, making it suitable for large datasets. Secondly, OTDA
      predominantly employs the squared Euclidean distance as the transportation cost, and, as a result, it
779
      often performs poorly on non-Euclidean datasets. In contrast, although the DeepJDOT framework
780
      also uses the squared Euclidean distance as the cost, it operates in a latent space learned by a deep
781   model, potentially enabling superior performance on non-Euclidean data.
782
783   Few papers in the literature have proposed alternative cost functions diverging from the standard
784
      distance metric. For instance, Tai et al. (2021) introduced an innovative approach termed SLA,
      which utilizes the OT formulation as a novel label assignment method. In this approach, each ele-
785
      ment (i, k) in the transportation cost matrix is associated with the probability that sample i belongs
786
      to class k. The method presented in Gu et al. (2022) is a semi-supervised approach that assumes
787   the availability of a set of source-target pairs, termed keypoints. Using these keypoints, the authors
788   propose to compute a relation score for all source and target samples, which is subsequently used to
789   construct the cost matrix. A related semi-supervised approach is presented in Duque et al. (2023) for
790   manifold alignment. This method similarly relies on prior correspondence knowledge between some
791   source and target samples to align the domains, with inter-domain distances computed solely from
792   these corresponding pairs. While they propose applying a diffusion process to capture intra-domain
793   relationships, they later compute the Cosine distance between submatrices of the obtained proba-
794   bility matrices, which serves as their final cost. Consequently, their semi-supervised transportation
795
      cost lacks a notion of probability. The Gromov-Wasserstein distance is typically used to align dis-
      tributions by leveraging only their intra-domain pairwise similarities. However, some works have
796
      proposed innovative approaches to incorporate inter-domain relationships as well. For example, Yan
797
      et al. (2018) introduce a semi-supervised DA method that adds a regularization term to the optimiza-
798   tion problem. This term incorporates cross-domain distances, computed in the target domain after
799   transportation, and relies on target labels. Alternatively, Xu et al. (2019) present a graph-matching
800   method that learns an embedding space for the nodes, enabling the computation of inter-domain
801   relationships, which are included in the optimization via an additional term.
802
      In the context of UDA, Li et al. (2020) introduced ETD, which utilizes a weighted Euclidean distance
803
      for the cost function. They proposed leveraging an attention mechanism to learn the correlation be-
804   tween source and target samples, subsequently using this correlation as the weights in their proposed
805   transportation cost. The OT distance is then employed as the training objective, serving as a quan-
806   tification of the domain discrepancy. The work in Xu et al. (2020) also proposed using a weighted
807   Euclidean distance as the transportation cost. In that method, termed RWOT, the element (i, k) in
808   the weight matrix quantifies the probability that sample i belongs to class k. This probability is
809   dynamically computed based on both spatial prototypical information and the pseudo-classification
      probability of target samples.

884   C.2   C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF D IFFUSION -OT
885
886   The most commonly used transport cost, the pairwise squared Euclidean distances, has a computa-
887   tional complexity of O(n2 ), assuming balanced datasets. In contrast, our proposed cost involves the
888   following steps:
889
890          • Computing pairwise distances for three matrices: O(n2 ).
891
             • Applying the exponential function to the matrices: O(n2 ).
892
893          • Normalizing the matrices to be row-stochastic: O(n2 ).
894
             • Multiplying the three probability matrices: O(n3 ).
895
896          • Applying the logarithm to the final diffusion operator: O(n2 ).
897
898   As noted in Appendix D, we often use doubly-stochastic normalization instead of row-stochastic
899   normalization, employing the Sinkhorn algorithm. Since the complexity of Sinkhorn is also O(n2 ),
900
      this does not increase the overall complexity. In total, the computational complexity of the proposed
      cost is O(n3 ). While higher than the traditional cost, our method avoids the need for a regularization
901
      term, which can often add to the optimization complexity.
902
903   Additionally, we note that up to the fourth step, the computation involves three n × n matrices rather
904   than one, resulting in higher memory requirements.

1426   (Pan et al., 2010).

1428   regularization parameter λ = 0.1 and apply Algorithm 1 with ϵs = 3, ϵc = 0.1, ϵt = 0.1. For

1430   applying the Sinkhorn algorithm. We set λ = 0.005 for the entropy parameter. For OT-reg, we
1431   use η = 10 for the label regularization, as suggested in the original paper (Yair et al., 2019). For
1432   CORAL, SA, and TCA, we utilize the ADAPT package (de Mathelin et al., 2021) with the default

       parameters.
