009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Weakly supervised phrase grounding (WSPG) aims to localize objects referred by
012            phrases without region-level annotations. The state-of-the-art methods use vision-
013            language pre-trained (VLP) models to build pseudo labels. However, their low
014            quality could result in the ineffectiveness of the subsequent learning. In this paper,
015            we propose a novel WSPG framework, Dual-cycle Consistency Learning (DCL).
016            Firstly, we propose a vision-modal cycle consistency to localize the referred ob-
017            jects and reconstruct the pseudo labels. To provide a conditional guidance, we
018
               propose a visual prompt engineering to generate marks for input images. To fur-
               ther avoid localizing randomly, we design a confidence-based regularization to
019
               filter out redundant information in image and pixel levels. Secondly, we propose a
020
               language-modal cycle consistency to correctly recognize the referred objects. To
021            correct their positions, we provide phrase-related boxes as supervision for further
022            learning. Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets show the effectiveness of
023            DCL, as well as its excellent compatibility with various VLP models. The source
024            code will be available at GitHub after double-blind phase.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Weakly supervised phrase grounding (i.e., WSPG) localizes referred objects based on phrase queries
030   without any box annotation. The WSPG task has the potential to benefit various downstream works,
031   such as image captioning (Liu et al., 2022b; Shi et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b),
032   vision-language navigation (Barthel et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021b; Wu et al., 2022; Eftekhar et al.,
033
      2024), and visual question answering (Wu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024; Peng
      et al., 2024; You et al., 2024). Earlier works have employed the outputs of object detectors matched
034
      with phrases (Ren et al., 2015; Datta et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Wang &
035
      Specia, 2019; Rohrbach et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021), or devised auxiliary tasks
036   to offer effective supervisory information for the grounding network (Fang et al., 2015; Xiao et al.,
037   2017; Javed et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Akbari et al., 2019; Arbelle et al., 2021). However, these
038   approaches are suboptimal as they are constrained by their cross-modal alignment capabilities.
039
      Recently, various WSPG methods leverage vision language pre-training (VLP) models to aid in
040
      grounding the target object. They rely on the attention maps of the VLP models as pseudo labels for
041
      training. These attention maps provide visual highlights of objects’ locations in the images, and thus
042   can be used to guide the optimization process. Previous VLP-based WSPG studies have developed
043   two types: VLP-based methods with fine-tuning and those with parameters frozen. Fine-tuned VLP
044   approach (He et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024) focuses on the localization by reducing the difference
045   of pseudo labels during fine-tuning. Frozen VLP methods (Shaharabany et al., 2022; Shaharabany
046   & Wolf, 2023; Gomel et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024a) extract pseudo labels with VLPs and devise
047   additional networks to refine the coarse pseudo labels. However, previous works disregard the low
048   quality of pseudo labels. It could result in the ineffectiveness of subsequent learning.
049   Here, we divide the problems caused by low-quality pseudo labels into three categories: incomplete-
050   ness, redundancy, and misrecognition (in Figure 1). Firstly, pseudo labels offer limited information,
051   as they tend to convey category-level details without comprehensive positional context. As shown
052   in the left example, the red highlight of pretty lady is salient but does not cover all necessary in-
053   formation. A naive idea of generating a similar highlight could overlook the object’s localization
      information. Therefore, it becomes imperative to utilize the pseudo label as a starting point and

      egy that reduces referential confusion and ensures precise positional supervision, thereby accurately
080   localizing the referred object.
081
082   To mitigate the negative effects of low-quality pseudo labels, we propose a novel WSPG frame-
083
      work, Dual-cycle Consistency Learning (i.e., DCL). Firstly, we introduce a vision-modal cycle
      consistency to prevent incompleteness and redundancy. It learns to localize the referred object and
084
      reconstruct pseudo labels. Specifically, we employ a grounding network and a recovery module to
085
      perform two consecutive grounding operations. We use the pseudo label as a prompt to identify
086   and ground the referred object, and subsequently align the initial pseudo label with the second-
087   time grounding result. In order for the pseudo labels to provide category-level details, we treat the
088   pseudo labels as the conditional guidance of the network. We also develop a visual prompt engi-
089   neering, which equips input images with mark prompts. Furthermore, we utilize pseudo labels to
090   provide constraints during the first-time grounding process. It could avoid our grounding network
091   localizing randomly. In order to filter out redundant information from pseudo labels, we design a
092   regularization method that imposes image-level and pixel-level confidence constraints. Secondly,
093   we propose a language-modal cycle consistency to address the correspondence ambiguity between
094
      the localized object and the query phrase. This approach represents concepts and details in a caption
      format, and recognizes whether the localized objects are the referred ones by distinguishing between
095
      captions and query phrases. To correct the location based on the phrases, we propose a region cap-
096
      tioning verification process to generate caption-box pairs for prospective locations. Subsequently,
097   we select optimal boxes from them for further consistency learning.
098
099   To sum up, the main contributions of our work are three-fold.
100
             • We propose a novel VLP-based WSPG framework to mitigate the adverse effects of low-
101
               quality pseudo labels. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to explore the detri-
102            mental impact of VLPs’ pseudo labels in WSPG and to propose an effective strategy.
103
104
             • We design a dual-cycle consistency learning for WSPG. A vision-modal cycle consistency
               aims to augment the functionality of pseudo labels. A language-modal cycle consistency
105
               aims to recognize and correct the referred object based on the query phrase.
106
107          • We conduct extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets to verify the effectiveness
               of our framework and its excellent compatibility with different VLP models.

      Vision Language Pre-trained Models. Pre-trained models have significantly advanced the domain
      of CV and NLP by learning from large datasets (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; He et al., 2019). This
112
      trend has prompted research on handling both visual and textual data, known as Vision-Language
113
      Pre-trained (VLP) Models (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Tan & Bansal, 2019). For instance,
114   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has demonstrated superior performance in aligning images with their
115   corresponding texts through pre-training on extensive internet-sourced image-text pairs. Other out-
116   standing works include TCL (Yang et al., 2022) and ALBEF (Li et al., 2021a). Additionally, some
117   applications have incorporated VLP models into generative frameworks (Rombach et al., 2022;
118   Chefer et al., 2023). These works enable textual descriptions to be represented as features for image
119   generation. A recent surge has employed pre-trained models for grounding-related tasks (Subrama-
120   nian et al., 2022; Shtedritski et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c).
121   However, the potential negative implications of these models have received limited attention.
122   VLP-based WSPG. VLP models have been increasingly employed for WSPG. This task focuses on
123   localizing objects within images based on query phrases, not relying on any region-level annotation.
124   Most recent methods involve either fine-tuning (He et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024) or maintaining
125   a frozen state of VLP models (Shaharabany & Wolf, 2023; Lin et al., 2024a). The former meth-
126   ods adjust VLP models to better localize objects by reducing inconsistencies in pseudo labels over
127   multiple fine-tuning phases. In contrast, the latter methods do not alter the pre-trained VLP models
128
      but instead extract attention heatmaps. These heatmaps are used as pseudo labels to train an in-
      dependent WSPG network. Following the pioneering work (Shaharabany et al., 2022), subsequent
129
      efforts (Gomel et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024b) have further refined the model’s localization through
130
      collaborative learning with visual subtasks, such as segmentation and detection. However, the low
131   quality of pseudo labels could result in the ineffectiveness of the subsequent model’s learning.
132
133   Consistency Learning for Grounding. Our approach to WSPG can be regarded as a consistency
134
      learning. Language related consistency learning has been explored using classical weakly supervised
      referring expression grounding (REG) (Liu et al., 2019; 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
135
      2024a; Liu et al., 2021). For vision consistency learning, Zhu et al. (2017) pioneering proposed
136
      unpaired translation for image generation. Recently, Cyco (Wang et al., 2024a) proposes a grounding
137   captioning consistency method. In this method, a collaborate learning network is designed for REG
138   and image captioning. To train the network, the data including the image, the text description, and
139   the bounding box are required. However, Cyco ignores the cost associated with manually labeling
140   the bounding box. In addition, Cyco does ignore the problem of incorrect pseudo labels which could
141   harm the model’s performance. In this paper, we propose a WSPG framework using VLP models.
142   We design a dual-cycle consistency learning to mitigate the negative effects of pseudo labels.

147   3.1   OVERVIEW
148
149   Given an image I and a query phrase T , the task of phrase grounding requires the model to produce
150   a bounding box B. To this end, a heatmap H is generated as a helper. In VLP-based WSPG, the
151   model is trained with image-phrase pairs and a pseudo label A extracted with VLP models.
152   The overview of our proposed framework is shown in Figure 2. Our grounding network consists
153   of an image encoder Eimg (·), a text encoder Etxt (·), and a grounding decoder Dgnd (·). The image
154   encoder employs the last layer’s output of the pre-trained CNN in ImageNet as visual embedding.
155   The text encoder uses the text embedding branch of CLIP (VIT-B/32), which is frozen. The ground-
156   ing decoder only consists of two up-sampling layers. It firstly fuses bi-modal features, and converts
157   high-dimensional fusion features into grounding heatmaps H. The feature fusion calculates the sim-
158
      ilarity between text features and visual ones, AM = Eimg (I) ⊗ Etxt (T ). The attention is then given
      as RM = Eimg (I) ◦ AM , in which the symbol ◦ means Hadamard product.
159
160   To mitigate the detrimental effects of low-quality pseudo labels, we propose a dual-cycle consistency
161   learning (DCL) framework, including vision-modal cycle consistency and language-modal cycle
      consistency. The former takes pseudo labels as prompts, enabling the grounding network to learn to

252
253   We employ a captioning approach to represent the objects’ concepts and details within that region.
254   This caption is then compared to the query phrase to ensure the language consistency. Specifically,
255   we generate a bounding box B(H) based on the grounding heatmap H. We then use the caption
256
      module (Li et al., 2022) to describe the content of the boxed region as TB . While there may exist
      semantic similarities between the caption and the query phrase, discrepancies in content can arise.
257
      For example, “image of wide and blue air” and “image of this is the sky”, these samples are diffi-
258
      cult to be recognized. To this end, we introduce a regularization using CLIP text encoder to extract
259   embeddings, which facilitates the evaluation of semantic similarity. To make sure that the grounded
260   region contains the referred object, we introduce LDE to minimize the difference between the em-
261   beddings of caption TB and query phrase T , while maximizing the difference between TB and a
262   negative sample TN . LDE is defined as follows,
263
                              LDE = 1 − CLIPtxt (TB , T ) + CLIPtxt (TB , TN )                           (7)
264
265   where CLIPtxt denotes the score calculated solely by the CLIP text encoder. Note that we treat the
266
      description “image of colorful patches” as a negative sample. This setting is based on its common-
      ness in captions generated by the caption module. Such captions typically arise when the grounded
267
      region has either incomplete or ambiguous instances.

280   Boxes Generation and Selection. We design a region captioning verification process to generate the
281   corresponding box annotations for potential objects. To identify regions likely to contain instances,
282
      we adopt several techniques, including selective search algorithm (Uijlings et al., 2013), bounding
      box generation algorithm (Shaharabany et al., 2022), and random proposals. Thus, we generate pro-
283
      posals {b1 , ..., bn }. A challenge is to discern the specific concepts and details of these instances. To
284
      this end, we employ the caption module (Li et al., 2022) to generate caption expressions {t1 , ..., tn }
285   for proposed regions. We filter out semantically repetitive proposals. The semantic redundancy may
286   manifest as varied descriptions of the same object, such as “black coat” versus “padded jacket”, or
287   “red bike” versus “small bicycle”. Specifically, we use CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021) to
288   translate the explicit captions into latent features. Based on the latent features, we build clusters
289   and ensure that the data points within each cluster exhibit uniformity in the feature space. Within
290   each cluster, instances are ranked based on scores calculated using the similarity to the mean feature
291   representation. We then select the top-k scoring instances, aiming to filter out instances that lack
292   the semantic coherence with the cluster. Finally, we select the cluster whose semantic similarity is
293
      closest to the phrase embedding Etxt (T ), as Z = {(tk , bk )}K  k=1 .

      4.1   DATASETS
327
328   Four datasets are used in our experiments. Flickr30K Entities (Plummer et al., 2015) contains 224K
329   phrases describing bounding boxes in 31K images, and each image includes five captions. We also
330   select 1000 images from the test split to evaluate as used in MG (Akbari et al., 2019). ReferIt has
331   20,000 images and 99,535 segmented regions in IAPR TC-12 (Grubinger et al., 2006) and SAIAPR-
332   12 (Chen et al., 2017) datasets, respectively. There exist approximately 130K entity captions. We
333   used the same 9K training, 1K validation, and 10K test datasets as in MG (Akbari et al., 2019).
334
      MSCOCO 2014 (Lin et al., 2014) contains 82,783 train images and 40,504 validation images. Each
      image is described with 5 captions. The training split in MG is used. Visual Genome (Krishna
335
      et al., 2017) consists of 77,398 training images, 5,000 test images, and 5,000 validation images.
336

      4.2   BASELINES AND M ETRICS
339
340   We chose typical VLP models as our backbones. 1) Classical image-text matching models, i.e.,
341   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), ALBEF (Li et al., 2021a) and TCL (Yang et al., 2022). 2) Text-to-image
342   generation models, i.e., Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and Attend-and-Excite (Chefer
343   et al., 2023). In addition, we used two typical WSPG methods and seven VLP-based WSPG meth-
344   ods as baselines. 1) Classical WSPG baselines, i.e., MG (Akbari et al., 2019) and Gbs (Arbelle
345   et al., 2021). 2) VLP-based WSPG includes g (Shaharabany et al., 2022), g++ (Shaharabany, 2023),
346   BBR (Gomel et al., 2023), SelfEQ (He et al., 2024), TAS (Lin et al., 2024a), VPT (Lin et al., 2024b)
347
      and APR (Zeng et al., 2024).
348   Two metrics, i.e., “pointing game” accuracy (Akbari et al., 2019) and bounding box accuracy (Sha-
349   harabany et al., 2022) are used. “Pointing Game” accuracy measure the percentage of predicted
350   maximum points of the heatmap that lie within the bounding box ground-truth. Bounding Box ac-
351   curacy measure the percentage of heatmap bounding boxes that have an IoU greater than 0.5 for the
352   testing set of “image-query” pairs.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
      In this paper, we propose a novel framework, Dual-cycle Consistency Learning (DCL) for WSPG.
531
      We propose a vision-modal cycle consistency to learn to ground the referred objects in the pro-
532
      cess of reconstructing the pseudo labels. This consistency prevents incompleteness and redundancy
533   problems. We also propose a language-modal cycle consistency to learn to recognize the referred ob-
534   jects and correct their positions. This consistency mitigates the misrecognition problem based on the
535   given phrase. Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets show that our framework achieves state-
536   of-the-art performance and has excellent compatibility with different VLP models. In the future, we
537   will study the application of our framework to related multimodal tasks, such as vision-language
538   navigation and visual question answering.

      A    B ROADER I MPACTS
811
812
813   Our research introduces a novel weakly supervised phrase grounding paradigm that improves phrase
814   grounding performance, facilitating the development of multimodal interaction systems and benefit-
815   ing people’s daily lives. Furthermore, we explore weakly-supervised training, saving human efforts
816   in data annotation. Our framework is validated on large-scale public vision-language datasets and
817   does not leverage noise in the data, ensuring fairness and unbiasedness in the grounding results. In
818
      contrast, the failure of this technique may lead to an inaccurate multimodal understanding and cause
      the mistake of the system based on the grounding results.
819
820
821
822   B    BASELINES
823
824
825   a. Selected VLP models: We introduce typical models from image-text matching (CLIP (Radford
826   et al., 2021), ALBEF (Li et al., 2021a) and TCL (Yang et al., 2022)) and text-to-image genera-
827
      tion (Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), Attend-and-Excite (Chefer et al., 2023)). 1) CLIP
      is a joint vision and language model pre-trained using over 400 million images I and their corre-
828
      sponding captions T. It is comprised with two networks, image Encoder and text Encoder. The
829
      pre-training process of CLIP utilizes contrastive learning, which maximizes the cosine similarity
830   between cross-modal pairs and minimizes the score between different images and captions. 2) AL-
831   BEF composes of a text decoder, an image encoder, and a multimodal fusion encoder. It relies on
832   three widely used objectives for visual and textual representation learning: image-text matching,
833   masked language modeling and a contrastive loss. 3) TCL, a two-stream model, is an enhanced
834   version of ALBEF, which introduces three contrasting modules: Cross-modal Alignment (CMA),
835   Intramodal Contrastive (IMC), and Local Mutual Information Maximization (LMI). These modules
836   are designed to maximize the mutual information between matching images and texts and maximize
837   global mutual information. 4) Stable Diffusion operates in the latent space of an autoencoder. First,
838
      an encoder E is trained to map a given image into a spatial latent code. A decoder is then tasked
      with reconstructing the input image. Given the trained autoencoder, a denoising diffusion proba-
839
      bilistic model (DDPM) operates over the learned latent space to produce a denoised version of an
840
      input latent at each timestep. During the denoising process, the diffusion model can be conditioned
841   on an additional input vector. In Stable Diffusion, this additional input is typically a text encoding
842   produced by a pre-trained CLIP text encoder. 5) Attend-and-Excite is an enhanced version of Stable
843   Diffusion, which uses an attention-based formulation and guides the diffusion model to refine the
844   cross-attention units to attend to all subject tokens in the text prompt.
845
      b. Compared baseslines: MG (Akbari et al., 2019), Gbs (Arbelle et al., 2021), g (Shaharabany et al.,
846
      2022), g++ (Shaharabany, 2023), BBR (Gomel et al., 2023), SelfEQ (He et al., 2024), TAS (Lin
847   et al., 2024a), VPT (Lin et al., 2024b) and APR (Zeng et al., 2024). 1) MG maximizes the likelihood
848   that a caption word appears in a distribution. It exploits multiple levels of feature maps of a DCNN,
849   as well as word and sentence embeddings extracted from a character-based language model. The
850   model is guided by a multi-level multi-modal attention mechanism which outputs activated visual
851   features in each level. 2) Gbs uses the source separation technique to ground the phrase to the image
852   pixels. The insight is to synthesize text-to-image regions by random alpha-blending of arbitrary
853   image pairs. The query phrase is used as condition for a non-hybrid query image. 3) g utilizes the
854   interpretable heatmap from CLIP as the supervision. In order to provide pixel-level supervision, the
855
      network utilizes CLIP to distinguish between the foreground and background of the output heatmap.
      4) g++ designs a self-supervised segmentation training method to further optimise the grounding
856
      network. This method gives good results by optimising the grounding annotation alone without
857
      changing the loss function of g. 5) BBR proposes a self-supervised object detection method for
858   joint learning with the grounding network. 6) SelfEQ helps the grounding network to recognise
859   uncommon phrases by distillation, while this method pre-processes grounding-related phrase data
860   with the assistance of LLM. 7) TAS proposes a triple alignment strategy for solving the zero-shot
861   phrase grounding under weak supervision. 8) VPT proposes a visual prompt tuning method to
862   effectively alleviate the local optimal problem of WSPG network. 9) APR constructs attribute,
863   relation and priority grounding benchmarks to evaluate the compositional reasoning on grounding
      tasks for different models.
