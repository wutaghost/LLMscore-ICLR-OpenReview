030                                               A BSTRACT
031
032            Editing natural images using textual descriptions in text-to-image diffusion mod-
033            els remains a significant challenge, particularly in achieving consistent generation
034
               and handling complex, non-rigid objects. Existing methods often struggle to pre-
               serve textures and identity, require extensive fine-tuning, and exhibit limitations
035
               in editing specific spatial regions or objects while retaining background details.
036
               This paper proposes Context-Preserving Adaptive Manipulation (CPAM) – a novel
037            zero-shot method for complicated, non-rigid real image editing. Specifically, we
038            propose a preservation adaptation module that adjusts self-attention mechanisms
039            to preserve and independently control the object and background effectively. This
040            ensures that the objects’ shapes, textures, and identities are maintained while keep-
041            ing the background undistorted during the editing process using the mask guid-
042            ance technique. Additionally, we develop a localized extraction module to mit-
043            igate the interference with the non-desired modified regions during conditioning
044            in cross-attention mechanisms. We also introduce various mask-guidance strate-
045
               gies to facilitate diverse image manipulation tasks in a simple manner. Extensive
               experiments on our newly constructed Image Manipulation BenchmArk (IMBA),
046
               a robust benchmark dataset specifically designed for real image editing, demon-
047
               strate that our proposed method is the preferred choice among human raters, out-
048            performing existing state-of-the-art editing techniques.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Recent advancements in text-to-image (T2I) generation (Ramesh et al., 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol,
      2021; Nichol et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) have marked


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      significant milestones, especially with large-scale diffusion models Rombach et al. (2022) that excel
055   in creating diverse and high-quality images from text prompts. These models have opened new
056   avenues for text-conditioned image editing (Hertz et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Parmar et al.,
057   2023).
058
      Real image editing typically aims to produce multiple images of different complex, non-rigid objects
059
      or characters that resemble the original targeted object while also ensuring a perfect reconstruction
060
      of the background (Vo et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2023).
061   However, this presents notable challenges. Text-guided editing of a real image using state-of-the-
062   art diffusion models (Kim et al., 2022) requires inverting the given image, which involves finding
063   an initial latent noise that accurately reconstructs the input image while preserving the model’s
064   editing capabilities. Editing an image from that latent noise often results in losing original textures
065   and identity, leading to a different image. Additionally, existing methods are limited in editing
066   specific objects within images, as they often focus on the most salient objects. This limitation arises
067   from training diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) on image-captioning datasets (Schuhmann
068   et al., 2021; 2022), which may lack detailed descriptions of text prompts for real-world images.
069
      Thus, pre-trained Stable Diffusion (SD) is unable to focus on specific regions and instead operates
      on the overall image. Furthermore, real-world images often contain multiple objects and complex
070
      interactions, making it challenging to specify particular objects for editing. Additionally, fully fine-
071
      tuning large models like SD (Rombach et al., 2022) is less feasible in research areas with limited
072   computational resources.
073
074   To address the lack of facilities for training models on large-scale datasets, tuning-free methods have
075
      been developed to utilize pre-trained T2I SD (Rombach et al., 2022), referred to as zero-shot image
      editing. These methods leverage a pre-trained T2I model with frozen weights to eliminate the need
076
      for adjusting the model’s weights (Avrahami et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; Brack et al., 2024).
077
      Most methods (Hertz et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Parmar et al., 2023; Tumanyan
078   et al., 2023) rely on attention mechanisms in SD models to preserve the original information of
079   images, such as background and object identities. Specifically, some methods (Tumanyan et al.,
080   2023; Liu et al., 2024) swap or inject appropriate self-attention maps, while others, like Hertz et al.
081   (2023), replace cross-attention maps to retain the content and structure of the original image during
082   the synthesis process. However, these methods (Tumanyan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) perform
083   well when the edited object has a certain similarity to the original object in terms of shape, texture,
084   and other attributes. Similarly, the approach in Hertz et al. (2023) that replaces cross-attention maps
085   requires the initial prompt and edited prompt to share similar words while incorporating different
086
      words. For instance, if the original sentence is ‘the photo of a red dog’, the edited sentence might
      be ‘the photo of a yellow cat’, where ‘red dog’ and ’yellow cat’ are the differing elements. In
087
      contrast, Cao et al. (2023) adjusts self-attention to retain the current query features while replacing
088
      the key and value features. This approach ensures that the query features remain unchanged and
089   are appropriately derived from the original semantic content guided by masks, rather than relying
090   on rigidly swapped attention maps. As a result, Cao et al. (2023) preserves the appearance of the
091   original image in a non-rigid manner during synthesis. However, Cao et al. (2023) controls the
092   background and foreground simultaneously to obtain the semantic content of the corresponding
093   original background and foreground at each appropriate step and layer. Thus, this approach lacks
094   flexibility in controlling different image editing tasks; for example, we need the background to
095   remain unchanged when the edited object resembles the original object. Additionally, a significant
096   weakness of many methods is that, when editing images, they make changes to the overall image and
097
      cannot specifically edit individual objects within the image due to the condition of all image pixels
      and the text prompt in the cross-attention module (as shown in the middle images of Figure 4). Some
098
      methods (Hertz et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2022; 2023; Couairon et al., 2023) address the challenge
099
      of local editing by blending the original latent noise with the edited noise, without considering
100   the interaction between foreground and background, resulting in rigid editing. Subsequently, these
101   methods lead to a substantial gap in addressing real image editing tasks.
102
103
      Based on the existing extensive exploration of leveraging the attention modules in SD to control
      the editing process and achieve desired outcomes, we analyze and clarify the semantic interaction
104
      of the components in attention and how to leverage them for real image editing (as detailed in Sec-
105
      tion. 3.1). We then propose a novel zero-shot real image editing method, namely Context-Preserving
106   Adaptive Manipulation (CPAM), that leverages both self-attention and cross-attention. Our method
107   excels in manipulating non-rigid objects, allowing for modifications to various aspects such as pose,


                                                         2

108
      view, or even specific objects or parts within the image. Importantly, our CPAM retains the back-
109   ground and avoids modifications to unwanted objects or regions, thereby addressing issues faced by
110   existing methods, enabling object removal or background replacement (as illustrated in Figure. 1).
111   Notably, these modifications occur without any model configuration or system architecture adjust-
112   ments, eliminating the need for optimization or fine-tuning phases. Specifically, we introduce a
113   preservation adaptation process that adjusts self-attention to independently control the object and
114   background, effectively preserving the original objects’ shapes, textures, and identities using mask-
115   ing techniques. Simultaneously, it ensures that the background remains undistorted or unwarped
116   throughout the denoising process. Additionally, we propose the localized extraction module to avoid
117
      attention between the non-desired modified regions with the target prompt in the cross-attention.
      Therefore, our method enables localized editing, allowing for editing not only salient objects but
118
      also specific objects within the image. We propose different mask-guidance strategies to enable
119
      innovative image editing tasks by simply adjusting masks and enhancing regional manipulation by
120   controlling object shapes. The source mask, representing the original object, and the target mask,
121   controlling the edited outcome, are computed differently, allowing for flexible image editing tasks.
122
123
      In addition, we introduce a new Image Manipulation BenchmArk (IMBA), built upon TEd-
      Bench (Kawar et al., 2023). We conduct a comprehensive user study on IMBA to assess the perfor-
124
      mance of our method against state-of-the-art text-guided image editing techniques utilizing SD. The
125
      extensive experimental results unequivocally highlight the superiority of our proposed method, sig-
126   nificantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
127
128          • We propose a novel tuning-free method dubbed Context-Preserving Adaptive
129            Manipulation that leverages both self-attention and cross-attention for zero-shot real im-
130            age editing.
131          • We propose the preservation adaptation process to control and preserve various aspects of
132            objects such as pose, view, texture, identities, structures, color, and non-rigid variances
133            while retaining the background.
134
             • We propose the localized extraction module to prevent any unwanted effects of the target
135            prompt on the non-desired modified spatial region in cross-attention.
136
             • We present mask-guidance strategies to facilitate various image manipulation tasks simply,
137
               while also tracking object shapes during the synthesis process.
138
139          • We construct a new Image Manipulation BenchmArk (IMBA) dataset to contain more de-
140            sired information for real image editing.

144   2.1   I MAGE MANIPULATION METHODS
145
146   Several approaches required optimization or fine-tuning phases, which self-learned input im-
147   ages (Mokady et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023). Ruiz et al. (2023) synthesized novel views of a
148   given subject using 3–5 images of that subject and a target prompt. Gal et al. (2023) optimized
149   a new word embedding token for each concept. Kawar et al. (2023) generated novel poses and
150
      views by optimizing the target text embedding, fine-tuning model parameters, and interpolating be-
      tween the approximate and target text embeddings. However, it struggled to maintain background
151
      consistency and realism, requiring careful optimization of embeddings for each prompt-image pair.
152
      Null-Text Inversion (NULL)(Mokady et al., 2023) proposed optimal image-specific null-text embed-
153   dings for accurate reconstruction, combined with P2P(Hertz et al., 2023) techniques for real image
154   editing. Brooks et al. (2023) performed full fine-tuning of the diffusion model by generating image-
155   text-image triplets based on instructional input. However, the optimization and fine-tuning process
156   is time-consuming and resource-intensive. Our method, instead, focuses on tuning-free techniques
157   that eliminate the need for such processes.

      3.2    OVERVIEW
264
265   Based on the insights in Section. 3.1, we propose Context-Preserving Adaptive Manipulation
266   (CPAM) to edit an image Is using a source object mask Ms and a target text prompt Pt to gen-
267   erate a new image It that aligns with Pt . Notably, It may spatially differ from Is , modifying objects
268   or background while keeping other regions unchanged. To achieve this, we introduce a preservation
269   adaptation module that adjusts self-attention to align the semantic content from intermediate latent
      noise to the current edited noise, ensuring the retention of the original object and background during

274   algorithm is outlined in Algorithm 1.

276   3.3   P RESERVATION ADAPTATION
277
278   In this subsection, we describe the self-attention adaptation process, which preserves the original
279   image’s appearance by independently adapting the semantic content from intermediate latent noise
280
      to the edited image.
281
282

283

284
      by the mask Ms . The semantic content of the background SCbg can be formulated as:

343   3.5   M ASK - GUIDANCE STRATEGY
344
345   Achieving both rigid and non-rigid semantic changes within a unified framework for diverse image
346   manipulation tasks is a notable challenge. Our method is designed to simplify this by requiring
347   only adjustments to mask settings. We present various mask guidance strategies tailored to different
348   editing needs. The source mask Ms refers to the mask of the object or region the user wishes to edit,
349   which can be provided through various methods such as manual drawing, extraction from clicks, or
350   text prompts using SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023). Meanwhile, the target mask Mt can be obtained as
351
      follows:
352
353
             • Replacing an object or changing the object pose, view: Mt is achieved by aggregating
               cross-attention maps across all steps and layers or extending the convex hull of Ms .
354
355          • Altering background: When Ms is the mask of the background, we assign Mt = Ms .
356
             • Removing object: We assign Mt = 0, meaning in our preservation adaptation module only
357
               adapting the semantic content of the original background (refer to Equation 3).
358
359          • Modifying a specific spatial region (e.g., adding items): We simply assign Mt = Ms or
360            slightly expand Ms .
361
362   Mask refinement When manipulating an object, its shape may change during diffusion steps. To
363   address this, we refine the mask according to the target prompt during the denoising process. The
364   target mask Mt is automatically obtained by aggregating cross-attention maps. Initially, for the first
365   Tm steps, we use the source mask Ms , then transition to Mt , which can be cloned from Ms or
366
      derived from the generation process. Additionally, we avoid closely segmenting both the target and
      original objects to prevent overly rigid editing and the leakage of underlying shape information.

      Textual Editing Benchmark (TEdBench) Kawar et al. (2023) was the pioneered standard benchmark

424

427   these limitations, built upon the TEdBench. IMBA dataset not only incorporates detailed user-
428   editing preferences but also includes additional inputs, such as alteration masks and object prompts,
429   to enhance control during the editing process. Moreover, IMBA adds samples containing multiple
430   objects, facilitating the evaluation of specific object editing capabilities. In total, IMBA includes
431   104 samples, with 43 samples requiring object retention, 97 samples involving object modification,
      and 7 samples involving background alteration.

483
484   To further assess the effectiveness of our proposed CPAM method, we conducted a user study com-
485   paring it against several leading prompt-based editing methods utilizing diffusion models. The meth-
      ods evaluated include SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022), MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023), PnP (Tumanyan et al.,

487   LEDIT++ (Brack et al., 2024), and the fine-tuning method Imagic (Kawar et al., 2023).
488
489   To ensure a comprehensive evaluation, we defined four key metrics: object retention, background
490
      retention, realism, and overall satisfaction. These metrics are designed to assess the methods’ effec-
      tiveness in executing realistic edits while preserving important features of the original images:
491
492
             • Object Retention: This metric evaluates how well the method preserves the identity and
493
               details of the main object in the image during editing.
494

             • Background Retention: This assesses the method’s ability to maintain the integrity and
               appearance of the background while altering the main object.
496
497          • Realism: This metric analyzes the realism of the edits, particularly in the context of non-
498            rigid transformations.
499          • Satisfaction: This measures the degree to which the edited image aligns with the textual
500            description provided as the editing prompt.
501
502   We invited 20 participants from diverse professional backgrounds to provide a variety of perspectives
503   in the evaluation process. Each participant rated the performance of the methods on a scale from
504   1 (very bad) to 6 (very good) across the four metrics. Participants evaluated 50 randomly shuffled
505   images for each method, resulting in a total of 36,000 responses.
506   Table 2 presents the Mean Opinion Score (MOS) derived from the participants’ ratings. The results
507   demonstrate that our CPAM method significantly outperforms the other methods across most met-
508   rics. Notably, CPAM received the highest ratings for object retention, background retention, and
509   realism, indicating its superior ability to maintain key elements of the images while executing edits
510   effectively. While Imagic (Kawar et al., 2023) excelled in user satisfaction but it faced challenges
511   in background retention, occasionally produced unrealistic outputs, and required significantly more
512
      time for fine-tuning and optimization for each image-prompt pair.
513   Overall, the user study reinforced the findings from our qualitative and quantitative evaluations,
514   highlighting the effectiveness of CPAM in real image editing tasks.
515
516   4.5   L IMITATIONS AND D ISSCUSION
517
518   Similar to other zero-shot methods, our method is constrained by the capabilities of the pre-trained
519   model. Sometimes, the generated images may not align perfectly with the provided prompts. Our
520
      mask refinement module aims to obtain masks from cross-attention maps by applying a simple
      standardization technique. However, this approach may result in imprecise object shapes or may
521
      overly focus on prominent objects, leading to suboptimal outcomes. While we can address this issue
522
      by adjusting or slightly expanding the masks, there are situations where these solutions may not be
523   sufficient. When editing a specific spatial region of an image, precise prompts and initial masks are
524   necessary, and the model must generate content in that region. Unfortunately, we may encounter
525   difficulties editing small or non-salient objects or when content cannot be generated in that region.
526   This challenge arises because the SD model is primarily trained on image-captioning datasets, where
527   the text prompts typically focus on salient objects. Fortunately, our method allows for an increased
528   guidance scale, effectively addressing this challenge and enhancing the model’s ability to generate
529   content in less prominent areas.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      Our CPAM facilitates various zero-shot real image editing tasks by leveraging both self-attention
534   and cross-attention mechanisms within SD models. Overcoming existing limitations, CPAM em-
535   ploys a preservation adaptation process to meticulously control and retain various object attributes
536   while preserving the background. Additionally, our method features a localized extraction module
537   to prevent undesired effects of target prompts on non-desired spatial regions, enabling precise object
538   editing within images. We also introduce IMBA dataset, providing rich information for compre-
539   hensive image manipulation evaluations. Empirical results demonstrate that our CPAM consistently
      outperforms existing leading editing techniques in achieving complicated and non-rigid edits.

685   Most diffusion models (DMs) rely on classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021), where a low

688   sacrificing creativity and naturalness. However, our method leverages a higher guidance scale to
689   edit images without causing distortion. It achieves this using simple prompts, avoiding the need for
690   complex and precise text descriptions, making the editing process more intuitive and user-friendly
691   (as shown in Figure 6).

693   process, we control self-attention to ensure that all spatial pixels attend only to the background while
694   disregarding the object content. This effectively removes the object without breaking the semantic
695   structure of the image, as demonstrated in Figure 7.
