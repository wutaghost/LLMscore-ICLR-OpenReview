009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012
013            Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) have demonstrated efficacy and gained
014            widespread adoption in natural language processing. However, their application
015            to other modalities has been less prevalent. This study introduces Lie group Rela-
016            tive position Encodings (LieRE), which extend beyond RoPE by accommodating
017            n-dimensional inputs. LieRE encodes positions of tokens by replacing the RoPE
018            rotation matrix with a dense, high-dimensional, rotation matrix generated via a
019
               learned map. We conducted empirical evaluations of LieRE on 2D and 3D image
               classification tasks, comparing its performance against established baselines in-
020
               cluding DeiT III, RoPE-Mixed, and Vision-Llama. Our findings reveal significant
021
               advancements across multiple metrics as compared to the DEIT III basline: LieRE
022            leads to marked relative improvements in accuracy (10.0% for 2D and 15.1% for
023            3D compared to DeiT). A 3.9-fold reduction in training time for the same accuracy
024            was observed. LieRE required 30% less training data to achieve comparable re-
025            sults. These substantial improvements suggest that LieRE represents a meaningful
026            advancement in positional encoding techniques for multi-dimensional data. The
027            implementation details and reproducibility materials will be made openly available.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038
      While the attention mechanism has achieved widespread use, especially as part of the transformer
039
      architecture, attention is invariant to the order of its inputs and requires another mechanism to
040
      capture positional information of input tokens Vaswani et al. (2017). This has spurred a line of work
041   in the subarea of positional encodings—methods of encoding positional information in attention
042   mechanisms.
043
044
      In particular, Rotary Position Encoding (RoPE) has emerged as a technique for encoding relative
      positional information of text tokens in transformer-based models Su et al. (2024). RoPE’s ability
045
      to capture relative position information has made it a popular choice for open-source language
046
      foundation models such as LLaMA. In particular, RoPE implicitly captures relative positions. When
047   the token in position p1 attends to a token in position p2 , the effect of RoPE depends on p1 − p2 .
048
049   Despite the success of RoPE in sequence tasks Touvron et al. (2023); Chowdhery et al. (2023), it is
050
      designed for one-dimensional sequence data. This has resulted in slow adoption for modalities with
      higher dimensional data, such or data that includes a temporal dimension like videos.
051
052   Our work aims to answer whether a single position encoding scheme can work well across both 2D
053   and 3D modalities. If possible, this would enable the use of a simpler common model backbone
      across a variety of tasks.

100   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
101
102   We introduce Lie Relative Encodings (LieRE), a mechanism that allows the attention mechanism
103   to learn how to utilize relative spatial information of its inputs. We show that LieRE is effective on
104   both 2D and 3D inputs of various modalities. Beyond improving classification accuracy, LieRE also
105   reduces the amount of compute and data required during training to achieve a fixed accuracy. On
106   the CIFAR100 task, this translates to 3.9x fewer training steps to achieve the same accuracy as the
107   DeiT baseline and outperforming the DeiT baseline trained on the full set with only a 70% subset of
      the data. Furthermore, LieRE is simple to implement and adaptable to modalities, requiring only a

      1.2     R ELATED W ORK
112
113
      1.2.1    P OSITION E NCODINGS
114
115   The fact that the original attention mechanism is invariant to the order of tokens has motivated
116   the ongoing development of methods to incorporate positional information into the transformer
117   architecture. We split our literature review into three broad classes of positional encodings: 1)
118   absolute, 2) relative, and 3) contextual.
119   Absolute encodings generally operate on a per token-level, modifying the embedding of a token
120   to encode the location of the token in the intput. Methods such as sinusoidal and learned absolute
121   encodings add vectors to the input token embedding Vaswani et al. (2017); Devlin et al. (2019);
122   Dosovitskiy et al. (2020). Absolute position measures position with respect to an absolute reference,
123   such as the start of the text or the top left corner of an image.
124   Relative position encodings instead encode the relative positions of two tokens. One strategy is to
125   learn an embedding for position deltas which can be incorporated into the attention mechanism Shaw
126   et al. (2018); Liu et al. (2021; 2022). However, this incurs quadratic computational cost in terms of
127   the number of tokens. Rotary Position Encodings (RoPE) avoid this cost by rotating the key and
128   query vectors before the attention inner product. The algebraic properties of the block-diagonal
129   rotation matrices used in RoPE ensures only relative positional information is captured in the attention
130   mechanism Su et al. (2024). RoPE is quite widely used in open source LLMs including the PaLM,
131   Llama and Mixtral models Touvron et al. (2023); Chowdhery et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024).
132
      However, RoPE can perform poorly on inference for larger context sizes than the model was trained
      on. This has spurred an active line of work extending RoPE to longer contexts, work which we review
133
      later.
134
135   We refer to the last category of positional encodings as contextual position embeddings. This category
136   is defined by encodings that aim to capture semantic positional information lost in traditional absolute
137   and relative position encodings, often motivated by reasoning or mathematical tasks. Contextual
138
      Position encodings achieve (CoPE) this by allowing the model to learn how the position is computed
      Golovneva et al. (2024). Abacus embeddings enable transformers to learn how to handle arithmetic
139
      by better exposing the digit structure of numbers McLeish et al. (2024).
140
141
142
      1.2.2    E XTENSIONS OF RO PE
143   The efficiency and popularity of RoPE have led to several lines of work building off of it.
144
145   One notable one is context extension, which aims to address the fact that RoPE NLP models trained
146
      on short documents tend to perform poorly on long documents. Methods like NTK-aware context
      extension, YaRN and LongRoPE focus on enabling already trained models to handle long context,
147
      both with and without finetuning Ding et al. (2024); Peng et al. (2023); Tworkowski et al. (2024);
148
      Chen et al. (2023).
149
150   Another line of work has been specifically focused on adapting RoPE to image tasks. Both VisionL-
151   lama and RoPE-Mixed present relative position encodings inspired by RoPE that are able to encode
152
      2D positional data Chu et al. (2024); Heo et al. (2024). The primary difference is that RoPE-Mixed
      has a learnable component, whereas VisionLlama does not.

157   2.1     L IE G ROUPS IN THE C ONTEXT OF ATTENTION
158
159   In this section, we aim to provide a minimal introduction to Lie groups so that the reader is able to
160   understand the mathematical motivations behind LieRE. Lie groups are well studied, especially in
161   the context of representation theory, and standard texts including Fulton & Harris (2013) are able to
      provide a more extensive introduction to the subject.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In this context, Lie groups are smooth sets of matrices that are closed under matrix multiplication and
163   inversion. For every Lie group, the matrix exponential provides a smooth bijective map from a subset
164   of Rn×n , also known as the Lie Algebra, to the Lie group. The exponential map is a diffeomorphism
165   and has the following key property for U, V ∈ Rn×n close together:
166
                              exp(U − V ) = exp(−V + U ) ≈ exp(V )−1 exp(U )                               (1)
167
168
      Both RoPE (in the context of text) and RoPE-Mixed use block-diagonal rotation matrices with 2D
169
      rotations as blocks. These form a special Lie group that is commutative, allowing us to strengthen the
170   statement in equation 1 to
171
172                        exp(U − V ) = exp(U ) exp(V )−1 = exp(V )−1 exp(U ).                            (2)
173
      Our work examines the tradeoff between using the stronger property in equation 2 or increased
174
      capacity and the weaker property equation 1.

236   4       E XPERIMENTS
237
238   In order to isolate the effect of changing the position encoding, we use a standard1 transformer
239   backbone modified to be able to switch between relative position encoding types. We use the
240   standard backbone sizes of ViT-Tiny, ViT-B and ViT-L Dosovitskiy et al. (2020). All experiments use
241   RandAugment Cubuk et al. (2020). We avoid using pre-trained weights in order to maximize the
242   comparability of results between methods. In order to ensure a fair comparison, we explicitly avoid
243
      tuning hyperparameters with LieRE and use the same default hyperparameters for all experiments
      (Appendix B). We evaluate two versions of LieRE, distinguished by the basis matrix tile sizes of
244
      64 and 8, referred to as LieRE64 and LieRE8 , respectively. Notably, a tile size of 2 corresponds to
245
      RoPE-Mixed.

      4.1      DATASETS AND TASKS
248
249   Our experiments are designed to evaluate the efficacy of LieRE64 and LieRE8 as a position encoding
250   across both 2D and 3D data. We evaluate LieRE on the classification of 2D (images) and 3D (videos)
251   data. For 3D data and ImageNet-1k (2D), we focus on accuracy. For CIFAR-100 (2D), where training
252   is less resource intensive, we also evaluate LieRE’s data and training compute efficiency.
253
254   4.1.1      2D C LASSIFICATION
255
      For 2D data we evaluate performance on the CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-1k image classification task
256   Krizhevsky et al. (2009); Deng et al. (2009). We partition our evaluation of performance into four
257   parts. In the first part, we examine accuracy across a range of model architectures on both CIFAR-100
258   and ImageNet-1k and compare to absolute Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) position encoding and recent
259   related work, RoPE-Mixed Heo et al. (2024), VisionLlama embeddings Chu et al. (2024).
260
      In the second part, we take advantage of the relatively modest amount of compute resources necessary
261
      to train a model for CIFAR-100 to examine LieREs impact on data efficiency. We also measure
262   training compute efficiency by comparing the number of training steps necessary to achieve a fixed
263   level of validation accuracy.
264
265
      In the third part, we evaluate the impact of LieRE with various scales. We vary the capacity of the
      transformer backbone with corresponding to ViT-T, ViT-B and ViT-B, as proposed in Dosovitskiy et al.
266
      (2020), the number of learned LieRE skew-symmetric basis (one, per-attention-head and per-layer)
267
      and the LieRE basis capacity by imposing a block diagonal structure on the Lie algebra that allows
268   us to vary the added capacity.

454
455   Shuffling patches and frames allows us to see how much the model is able to use the positional

458   locations of pixels/voxels do not matter. A greater dropoff in accuracy during shuffling is indicative
459   that the model more heavily utilizes positional information.

      6     L IMITATIONS
467
468
      While LieRE shows promising results across multiple modalities and input dimensionalities, there
      are a few limitations worth noting. Our method is specifically designed to modify the inner product,
469
      making it compatible with most attention schemes, including original attention and linear attention.
470
      However, this specificity may limit its applicability to other architectures, like convolutional neural
471   networks, that do not rely on inner product-based attention mechanisms. Future work could explore
472   adaptations of our method to a wider range of architectures.Furthermore, the current formulation of
473   our method is designed to encode vector positions in Rd . While this is sufficient for many applica-
474   tions, it may not be directly applicable to tasks that require encoding poses in SE(3) like robotics.
475   Further research may be necessary to adapt our method to effectively handle such representational
476   requirements.
477   Despite these limitations, we believe that our work provides much-needed insight into how to improve
478   model performance and reduce training costs by encoding relative position information across various
479   modalities and input dimensionalities.

490   8   C ONCLUSION
491
492   In this paper, we introduced Lie group Relative position Encodings (LieRE), a position encoding that
493   can effectively encode relative position information for attention mechanisms across modalities and
494
      input dimensionalities. Through experiments on 2D image classification (CIFAR-100, ImageNet-1k)
      and 3D video classification (UCF101), we demonstrated that LieRE achieves better performance
495
      compared to existing positional encoding methods. Beyond improving accuracy, LieRE also exhibits
496
      data and compute efficiency. On CIFAR-100, LieRE requires 3.5 times less training computed
497   to match the performance of the baseline model with absolute position encodings. Furthermore,
498   LieRE can outperform the baseline trained on the full dataset while using only 70% of the training
499   data, highlighting its data efficiency. The key advantages of LieRE include its simplicity, flexibility,
500   and ease of adaptation to new modalities. By requiring no changes to the tokenizer other than
501   outputting positions and no other code changes, LieRE may provide a unified and efficient approach
502   for transformers to process and learn from various data modalities within a single architecture.
