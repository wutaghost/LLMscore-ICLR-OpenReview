011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Fine-tuning is powerful for adapting large language models to downstream tasks,
014            but it often results in huge memory usages. A promising approach to mitigate this
015
               is using Zeroth-Order (ZO) optimization, which estimates gradients to replace
               First-Order (FO) gradient calculations, albeit with longer training time due to its
016
               stochastic nature. By revisiting the Memory-efficient ZO (MeZO) optimizer, we
017
               discover that the full-parameter perturbation and updating processes consume over
018            50% of its overall fine-tuning time cost. Based on these observations, we introduce
019            a novel layer-wise sparse computation and memory efficient ZO optimizer, named
020            LeZO. LeZO treats layers as fundamental units for sparsification and dynamically
021            perturbs different parameter subsets in each step to achieve full-parameter fine-
022            tuning. LeZO incorporates layer-wise parameter sparsity in the process of simul-
023            taneous perturbation stochastic approximation (SPSA) and ZO stochastic gradient
024            descent (ZO-SGD). It achieves accelerated computation during perturbation and
025            updating processes without additional memory overhead. We conduct extensive
026
               experiments with the OPT model family on the SuperGLUE benchmark and two
               generative tasks. The experiments show that LeZO accelerates training without
027
               compromising the performance of ZO optimization. Specifically, it achieves over
028
               3× speedup compared to MeZO on the SST-2, BoolQ, and Copa tasks.

      To address these challenges, researchers have
048   proposed efficient fine-tuning methods, which can be categorized into forward-backward (Li &
049   Liang, 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Pu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) and forward-
050   only (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024) approaches. Forward-backward methods
051   typically use First-Order (FO) optimizers (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Kingma & Ba, 2015), updating
052   either partial model parameters or introducing small trainable modules to reduce memory usage.
053   However, the performance of these methods can hardly match full-parameter fine-tuning. Dynamic
      update schemes (Brock et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2024) address this problem by dynam-

054
      icly changing learnable parameter subsets, but they still require caching many activations. In con-
055   trast, forward-only methods utilize Zeroth-Order (ZO) optimizers, which estimate gradients with-
056   out back-propagation, thereby reducing the memory overhead associated with forward-backward
057   methods. Malladi et al. (2023) first introduce a memory-efficient ZO optimizer (MeZO) for LLM
058   fine-tuning. ZO optimizers, however, exhibit higher stochasticity in gradient estimation compared
059   to FO optimizers, and this stochasticity increases as the scale of tuned parameters grows (Wang
060   et al., 2018; Balasubramanian & Ghadimi, 2018; Cai et al., 2022). Consequently, MeZO requires
061   significantly more computational cost than FO optimizers.
062   In this paper, we revisit MeZO and identify that its computational cost is primarily in the forward
063   pass, perturbation, and updating stages. Specifically, perturbation and updating account for over
064   50% of the total time when fine-tuning the OPT-13B model (Zhang et al., 2022) on the SST-2 task.
065   Simplifying these steps can accelerate MeZO. One promising approach is to sparsify the tuned
066   parameters, which has been shown to be effective for FO optimization (Pan et al., 2024).
067   Inspired by these insights, we propose a layer-wise sparse and efficient zeroth-order opti-
068   mizer (LeZO). LeZO aims to improve computational efficiency without additional memory over-
069   head. We use layers as the basic unit of sparsity, dynamically updating the set of layers to be tuned
070   at different fine-tuning steps. We integrate this policy into Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic
071   Approximation (SPSA) and Zeroth-Order Stochastic Gradient Descent (ZO-SGD), which enables
072   sparse ZO optimization through localized parameter perturbation and updating. Over multiple steps,
073   LeZO can achieve full-parameter fine-tuning. LeZO accelerates computations because, during each
074   step, the weights of untuned layers remain dense. This allows us to skip perturbation and updating
075
      processes for those layers, thereby reducing floating-point computations. Additionally, compared to
      MeZO, LeZO does not introduce extra memory overhead. We employ a simple and efficient random
076
      selection strategy, avoiding the need for new parameter modules.
077
078   We evaluate our approach by fine-tuning models from the OPT family on the SuperGLUE bench-
079   mark (Wang et al., 2019), and the SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) and DROP (Dua et al., 2019)
080   datasets. By sparsifying 75% of the layers and fine-tuning full-parameters, LeZO outperforms
081
      MeZO on most datasets across three model scales. For instance, on the OPT-13B model, LeZO
      surpasses MeZO by an average of 1% in accuracy or F1 score across seven datasets. When integrat-
082
      ing the ZO optimizer with Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT), LeZO exceeds MeZO on five
083
      datasets, highlighting the versatility and effectiveness of our sparsity scheme. Additionally, LeZO
084   achieves over 2× computational acceleration across some tasks, with speedups increasing as layer
085   sparsity exceeds 75%. Finally, LeZO achieves over 3× training acceleration for the OPT-13B model
086   fine-tuning on the SST-2, BoolQ, and Copa datasets.
087
      In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
088
089         • We analyze the computational costs of MeZO during LLM fine-tuning and find that the
090           perturbation and updating processes account for over 50% of the fine-tuning time cost.
091         • We propose a dynamic layer-wise sparse and efficient zeroth-order optimizer that effec-
092           tively reduces computational cost in fine-tuning LLMs, and achieve faster convergence
093           than MeZO.
094         • We evaluate LeZO on SuperGLUE and two generative tasks. This demonstrates its acceler-
095           ation capability across different sparsity rates and showcases its performance improvement.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
      Forward-Backward Fine-Tuning Optimziers rely on the FO optimizer and compute gradients
100
      through derivation (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Kingma & Ba, 2015), which results in high memory
101
      usage. One approach to handle this problem is Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Xu et al.,
102   2023). Relevant strategies involve adding trainable modules (Li & Liang, 2021; Hambardzumyan
103   et al., 2021) or learnable input embeddings (Houlsby et al., 2019) to the model. Moreover, the LoRA
104   series (Hu et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2024) adopt low-rank decomposition to reduce the size of
105   trainable modules. However, recent studies show that partial fine-tuning cannot match the perfor-
106   mance of full-parameter fine-tuning (Ding et al., 2022; Pu et al., 2023). To handle this problem,
107   some methods enable full-parameter fine-tuning by adjusting parameters in an interleaved fashion.
      For example, LISA (Pan et al., 2024) updates parameters of different layers in different iterations


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      based on layer importance. Galore (Zhao et al., 2024) switches low-rank subspaces using the rank
109   information while simultaneously modifying the learnable parameters. These techniques still require
110   storing the intermediate states of FO optimizers, resulting in significant memory overhead.
111
112
      Forward-Only Fine-tuning Optimziers do not require back-propagation for gradient computa-
      tion (Malladi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Malladi et al. (2023) firstly introduce the ZO optimizer
113
      for LLMs fine-tuning, known as MeZO, which significantly reduces memory costs. Because MeZO
114
      employs SPSA to estimate gradients, it exhibits greater randomness compared to FO optimizers.
115   Consequently, it requires more computational cost to converge. To accelerate the convergence of
116   MeZO, Sparse-MeZO (Liu et al., 2024) only updates model parameters with small values to en-
117   hance the effectiveness of ZO optimizer. Nevertheless, this approach necessitates ranking parameter
118   values and introduces a mask matrix; therefore, it results in additional memory and computational
119   overhead. Guo et al. (2024) reduced the number of learnable parameters to one-thousandth. How-
120   ever, it requires cloud computation to provide first-order gradient of the objective function, which is
121   unsuitable for more general scenarios. In summary, how to effectively accelerate the convergence
122   speed of ZO optimizers without incurring additional memory overhead still remains a challenge.
123   Our proposed LeZO adopts ZO optimization to update model parameters, reducing memory usage
124   compared to forward-backward fine-tuning. By employing a dynamic layer-wise sparsity strategy,
125   LeZO significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters and minimizes the computational
126   costs during the forward-only fine-tuning. Thus, LeZO achieves high efficiency in both memory
127   usage and computational cost.

131   In this section, we first revisit the classical ZO gradient estimator SPSA (Spall, 1992) and the ZO

135   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
136
137   SPSA approximates gradients for multi-parameter systems by applying random perturbations, which
138   eliminates the need of gradient back-propagation. It iteratively optimizes parameters by estimating
139   gradients based on differences in function values around a given point. SPSA does not require the
140
      function to be smooth or convex, but it generally converges slowly.

142   a group of parameters θ ∈ Rd from a model and a loss function L used for optimization, SPSA
143   estimates the gradient on a mini-batch of data B as follows:

      Malladi et al. (2023) applied SPSA (Definition 1) to optimize LLMs, using it to obtain approximate
149
      gradients of parameters. They modified the SGD algorithm (Robbins & Monro, 1951), creating

      Remark 1. As illustrated in Algorithm 1, it is evident that LeZO introduces a layer-wise selection
243
      operation R compared to MeZO. Given the scale of parameters in the billions, this addition has
244
      a negligible computational impact. We have the following remarks. (1) During the Forward pass,
245   LeZO’s computation aligns with MeZO, incurring no additional memory or computational overhead.
246   (2) During the perturbation process, we skip the parameter perturbation in the sparsified layers,
247   thereby reducing the computational load. Both LeZO and MeZO utilize a random seed for fixed
248   perturbation z. This approach eliminates the need for additional cache storage for perturbation
249   information, thereby not increasing memory overhead. The same principle applies to the updating
250   process. Therefore, it is evident that LeZO reduces computational overhead through the introduction
251   of layer-wise parameter sparsification during the perturbation and updating processes and incurs no
252   additional memory overhead.

477
478
      In this study, we introduce LeZO, a layer-wise sparse and efficient ZO optimizer for fine-tuning
479
      LLMs. We integrate the layer-wise pruning scheme into the ZO optimization process, which
480   achieves both computation and convergence speedups in LLMs training without additional mem-
481   ory costs. Moreover, LeZO combines effectively with existing PEFT methods to further enhance
482   acceleration. We conduct fine-tuning experiments on models from the OPT family using the Super-
483   GLUE benchmark and two question-answering datasets. Empirical results show that LeZO signif-
484   icantly reduces training time and improves performance. Overall, LeZO provides an efficient and
485   effective approach to ZO fine-tuning. It reduces the computational load caused by full-parameter
      perturbations in zeroth-order optimization without introducing additional memory overhead.
