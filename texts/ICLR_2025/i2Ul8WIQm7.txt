009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) is a new paradigm for fine-tuning language
012             models at scale. Unlike standard fine-tuning, PEFT adjusts only a small number of
013             parameters, making it more computationally accessible and enabling practitioners
014             to develop personalized services by fine-tuning models on user data. Because the
015             models are trained on user data, this emerging paradigm may attract adversaries
016             who want to extract sensitive information from fine-tuning data. However, to date,
017             their privacy implications have not been well-understood yet in the literature.
018             In this paper, we study the impact of this new fine-tuning paradigm on privacy.
019             We use an off-the-shelf data extraction attack as a vehicle to evaluate the privacy
020             risk on two pre-trained language models fine-tuned on 2 datasets, repeated 5 times
021             with different random seeds, resulting in a total of 100 variations. Our main find-
022
                ings are: (1) for practitioners employing PEFT to construct personalized models,
                the fine-tuned models have lower privacy risks while maintaining reasonable util-
023
                ity; (2) for developers designing new PEFT algorithms, while safer than standard
024
                fine-tuning, certain design choices in the algorithms increases memorization in an
025             unexpected way; and (3) for researchers auditing the privacy of fine-tuned models,
026             employing weak differential privacy is sufficient to mitigate existing data extrac-
027             tion risks without significantly compromising model utility. We hope our work
028             encourages the safe adoption and development of PEFT algorithms in practice, as
029             well as future work on advancing stronger privacy auditing mechanisms.

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   “Pre-training and fine-tuning” is a common paradigm in developing AI services built on commercial-
035   scale language models. Model providers like Google1 , Meta2 , or OpenAI3 handle the pre-training
036   stage, while service providers fine-tune the ready-made models on their own datasets. Because those
037   models have a large number of parameters, the fine-tuning process requires extensive computational
038   resources. As a potential solution, there has been active research on reducing these computational
039   demands, such as parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Han et al., 2023).
040   Against this common paradigm, recent work has demonstrated data extraction attacks (Carlini et al.,
041   2023). To breach the confidentiality of AI services, an adversary exploits the model’s query inter-
042   faces to reconstruct training data from the fine-tuned models. Given that the data used for fine-tuning
043   likely includes private records of service users, this poses a significant privacy risk, with models po-
044   tentially leaking personally identifiable information (PII), such as patient names or email addresses.
045
      In this work, we study the risk of data extraction attacks given rise to by the emerging paradigm:
046   PEFT. Most work on data extraction targets pre-trained models as-is (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021;
047   2023; Nasr et al., 2023) or focuses on scenarios where the entire parameters are fine-tuned (Pono-
048   mareva et al., 2022; Jayaraman et al., 2024). However, it remains unknown how vulnerable these
049   fine-tuned models, especially those constructed using PEFT algorithms, are to data extraction at-
050   tacks. It is also unclear which design choices in PEFT algorithms make them more (or less) vulnera-

057   Contributions. We first address these questions by comprehensively evaluating the privacy risks of
058
      language models fine-tuned with various PEFT algorithms. We use an off-the-shelf data extraction
      attack, developed by (Carlini et al., 2019), as a vehicle to assess this privacy threat. We fine-tune
059
      two commercial-scale language models using five different fine-tuning algorithms on two datasets
060
      repeated five times with different random seeds, to achieve 80 variations of PEFT-trained models,
061   and 20 with full-model finetuning.
062
063   We demonstrate that models constructed using PEFT algorithms achieve 2–14× times less exposure,
064
      while standard fine-tuning leads to the successful extraction of secrets from the resulting models. We
      also observe variations in memorization across models fine-tuned with different PEFT algorithms.
065
066   Second, we characterize key factors that influence the memorization of secrets across different fine-
067   tuning algorithms. We show that secrets containing substrings likely to appear in the pre-training
068   corpus are less likely to be memorized by fine-tuned models. In contrast to the prior work’s findings,
069   we observe that the increase in the number of tunable parameters does not necessarily mean more
070
      memorization in models. Moreover, we find that certain design choices in PEFT algorithms can lead
      to different memorization patterns. In prefix-tuning, for example, secrets located at the beginning of
071
      a training record are more easily memorized than those placed at the end.
072
073   Third, we investigate the interaction between a privacy defense with the formal guarantee (differen-
074   tial privacy, ϵ) and model utility across five fine-tuning algorithms. We demonstrate that, even with
075   a large ϵ, data extraction can be completely rendered ineffective across all PEFT algorithms, while
076
      preserving model utility. One can also reduce ϵ to 2.0–5.0, depending on the PEFT algorithm used,
      without significant performance loss. We find that PEFT algorithms that fine-tune fewer parameters
077
      are better at preserving model utility under strong privacy guarantees, ϵ ∈ [0.2, 2.0]. We also show
078
      that lower ranks are preferable for keeping model utility under small ϵ values.
079
080   We hope our work will serve as a Hitchhiker’s Guide to fine-tuning language models with privacy.

      2    BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
084
085   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) enables to fine-tune large-scale models in a computation-
086   ally accessible way while maintaining performance comparable to standard fine-tuning. Instead of
087
      adjusting the entire model parameters, PEFT reduces the number of tunable parameters through
      various methods Han et al. (2024). A common approach is to use additive methods: we alter the
088
      model architectures by injecting small learnable modules (or parameters). Representative methods
089
      include (1) adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019) where small learnable modules are added to transformer
090   blocks; (2) prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), which introduces learnable vectors added to keys
091   and values across all transformer layers; and (3) prompt-tuning (Lester et al., 2021) that applies
092   learnable vectors only at the initial token embedding layer to enhance training and inference effi-
093   ciency. An alternative yet emerging approach is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2022),
094   which constructs a low-rank parameterization of transformer layers to reduce the number of tunable
095   parameters. Our work studies memorization of models fine-tuned though these PEFT algorithms.
096   Concurrently, Anonymous (2024) studies tight auditing of memorization in standard fine-tuning.
097   But our focus is more on the impact of memorization under these emerging fine-tuning techniques.
098   Privacy risks in language model ecosystem. Data extraction attacks present a major risk to the lan-
099   guage model ecosystem: an adversary aims to extract private information from the training data used
100   to train (or fine-tune) language models. Because language models are deployed in a black-box man-
101   ner, most prior attacks have demonstrated their feasibility by exploiting query-based interactions.
102   Initial work on data extraction focuses on extracting private information, unintentionally memorized
103   during pre-training (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021; Nasr et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024),
104
      but as fine-tuning becomes more common, recent work explores the extraction of sensitive data from
      fine-tuning data (Lukas et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Our work falls into the latter category, as we
105
      study data extraction against fine-tuned models, which is under-explored in the prior work.
106
107   How precisely an attacker queries the target model varies depending on their knowledge. The weak-
      est attacker has only query access to the target model and no knowledge of the training data. This


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      attacker will choose prompts that are likely to trigger the generation of memorized data, which may
109   take forms, such as random Internet strings Carlini et al. (2021); Nasr et al. (2023) or special char-
110   acters (Bai et al., 2024). These attacks are untargeted, aiming to reconstruct any training examples
111   verbatim. On the other hand, a strong adversary has (partial) access to the training data and knows
112   the context associated with private information. The adversary can prompt the target model using
113   these prefixes to reconstruct the remaining specific tokens in the training records to which the prefix
114   belongs (Carlini et al., 2023; Lukas et al., 2023). Because our work uses data extraction attacks as a
115   privacy auditing mechanism, we perform a membership-inference style attack, where the adversary
116   knows the context associated with a secret and has a list of secret candidates to compare.
117   Differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006) is originally developed to reduce the difference in
118   outcome from querying two databases which differ by a single record. Abadi et al. (2016) developed
119   a training algorithm, differentially-private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD), that employs DP
120   to guarantee protection of a model against the worst-case private information leakage. DP-SGD
121   formally quantifies the leakage with the parameter ϵ. We set ϵ to a desired value before training, and
122   once the total leakage exceeds the pre-defined ϵ during training, we stop training and save the model
123
      with its parameters. To date, DP-SGD is the standard practice for training (or fine-tuning) private
      models (Ponomareva et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022). However, the privacy guaran-
124
      tee comes at the cost of performance: a stronger guarantee often results in significant performance
125
      degradation. Thus, it is important to understand the privacy-utility trade-off (Jayaraman & Evans,
126   2019) and how to train private models with performance comparable to non-private models (Pono-
127   mareva et al., 2023). Our work also studies the privacy-utility trade-off in fine-tuned models.
128
129
      A separate line of work studies defensive mechanisms in the context of language models to miti-
      gate empirical privacy risks. Deduplication reduces the number of secret occurrences in the training
130
      data to mitigate data extraction attacks (Kandpal et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022). Adversarial train-
131
      ing (Goodfellow et al., 2015), a standard countermeasure against adversarial examples, is used with
132   a privacy regularizer to jointly optimize for both privacy and utility (Mireshghallah et al., 2021).
133   While these defenses effectively reduce the success rate of existing privacy attacks (Rigaki & Gar-
134   cia, 2023), we exclude them from our investigation as they do not provide formal guarantees.

      3.1   D EFINITION OF MEMORIZATION
139
140   We adopt the definition of memorization from Carlini et al. (2023), with adaptations in blue.
141
      Definition 3.1. (Memorization) A secret s is memorized by a model f with k tokens of context if
142
      there exists a (length-k) string p, such that the insertion of s into p, denoted as p ⊕ s is present in
143
      the training data for f , and f achieves the lowest perplexity, when prompted with p ⊕ c where c is
144   s, across all possible secret candidates c in C.
145
146   This definition differs from prior work (Carlini et al., 2021; 2023; Nasr et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024).
147   Instead of prompting the model with a context p and then generating next N tokens using a given
148   decoding method, we compute the perplexity directly on a list of prompts p ⊕ c, each differing only
149   by the secret candidate c. The difference lies in the purpose of employing data extraction. Prior
150   work focuses on demonstrating the feasibility of data extraction against language models in use,
151
      but we leverage the same attack for auditing privacy risks. Hence, rather than prompting the model
      with p and hoping greedy decoding extracts the correct final token, we assume a strong adversary by
152
      limiting their search space to a set of candidate secret tokens C, expecting the true secret s to yield
153
      the lowest perplexity compared to the others. One can view this definition as an edge-case of Carlini
154   et al. (2023), where the attacker has all but the final token of a training record.
155
156
      3.2   Q UANTIFYING MEMORIZATION
157
158   Threat model. We consider an emerging scenario where a victim develops natural language pro-
159   cessing services by fine-tuning a commercial-scale pre-trained language model on their data, which
160   may contain private information of users. Because these models have more than billions of param-
161   eters, we assume that the victim employs PEFT methods to reduce the computational demands for
      fine-tuning. We assume a data extraction adversary (Carlini et al., 2021; 2023; Nasr et al., 2023; Bai


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2024; Lukas et al., 2023), also an emerging concern to language model ecosystem, who aims
163   to extract private information from a target model. In our scenario, we assume an oracle adversary
164   with black-box access, exploiting the model’s prompting interface.
165
166
      Exposure as a metric for quantifying memorization. Our definition above is strict: memorization
      is only confirmed when the prompt containing the secret achieves the lowest perplexity. However, in
167
      our initial investigation, we find the need to relax this definition slightly. While the strict definition
168
      is useful for determining the success of an attack, it does not provide a measure of the degree to
169   which a secret is memorized by a model. In consequence, in most cases where the perplexity is not
170   the lowest (even when the value is a close second or runner-up), it is considered as not-memorized.
171
      Definition 3.2. (Exposure) Given a secret s and a model f , the exposure of s is defined as:
172
173                                     exposuref (s) =log2 |C|−log2 rankf (s)
174
175   We follow the definition of Carlini et al. (2019). The cardinarlity of the candidate space C, is set to
176   approximately 400. The rank of a secret s is defined as its index in the list of all possible candidates
177   in C, ordered by the model perplexity. In our case, the “candidate space C” refers to the number
178   of possible candidates a secret s could be, instead of every possible character combinations with
179   the same length as s. We make this decision for computationally practical threat modeling. In the
180   medical record dataset (MIMIC) we use, a 10-character secret, such as a patient’s name in English,
181
      has 2710 combinations. But we reduce the space to 400, by selecting only common English names.
182
183   3.3       P REPARING THE EVALUATION DATA
184
185
      We prepare two different types of datasets for our evaluation. The first dataset represents the most
      challenging scenario for our data extraction adversary: a single insertion of a secret s. In this case,
186
      we randomly select a record p from the training data and concatenate the secret, forming [p||s]. This
187
      construction follows the same methodology as in Carlini et al. (2023). We take a dataset and repeat
188   this process five times with different random seeds to construct five distinct fine-tuning datasets.
189
190   While commonly used in the literature, the previous construction may not capture the variations in
191
      the secret’s location within a context. For instance, when the secret is a patient’s name, the training
      record could be “John Doe is diagnosed with granulosa cell tumor” rather than “Granulosa cell
192
      tumor is the disease for John Doe.” To study the impact of a secret’s location on memorization, we
193
      select 50-token-length training records from a dataset, insert the same secret at 5 different positions,
194   and save each version as a separate fine-tuning dataset. We also examine how duplication affects
195   memorization by increasing the number of duplications from 1 to 500 for each fine-tuning dataset.

      4.4   D OES THE POSITION OF A SECRET WITHIN A SENTENCE MATTER ?
325
326   Most prior work follows the definition of memorization from (Carlini et al., 2019; 2023), where a
327   secret s is concatenated at the end of a context p. Now we challenge this practice and analyze further
328   how the position of a secret within a context impacts memorization. Our hypothesis is that PEFT
329   methods, which only tune parameters corresponding to specific token positions in the input, may be
330   better at memorizing secrets in those locations than secrets placed at the end. Here we focus on our
331   findings in MIMIC-III. Please refer to Appendix B.3, B.5, and B.6 for our full results.

      Trade-off between privacy and utility. Next we analyze the tradeoff between privacy, guaranteed
457   formally by ϵ, and utility (measured by evaluation perplexity). Figure 4 summarizes our results from

461   epsilon range, ϵ ∈ [0.1, 2.0], as perplexity increases by orders of magnitute within this range. If we
462   use ϵ < 0.1, the fine-tuned models perform no better than random.
463   Overall, we observe a greater increase in perplexity as we increase the configuration values across
464   PEFT algorithms. This occurs because the configurations are proportional to the number of tunable
465   parameters: an increase in tunable parameters requires adding more noise to achieve the same target
466   ϵ value as when fine-tuning models with fewer tunable parameters. For adapters, we observe an
467   increase in perplexity from ∼1.1 to ∼8.0 at ϵ = 0.1, whereas the increase reaches up to ∼800–2400

469   above figure, which impressively maintains low-perplexity even at ϵ = 0.1. Our results indicate that
470   once a sufficiently low-perplexity is achieved with a PEFT method’s configuration, increasing the
471
      number of tunable parameters can lead to a worse trade-off between privacy and utility. We further
      investigate the privacy-utility trade-off with the larger GPT2-XL base model in Appendix B.6
472
473   However, we do not observe any consistent relationship between PEFT hyper-parameters and the
474   perplexity under DP. The first observation we had is that the trend differs from the datasets we use.
475   In the MIMIC-III dataset, larger hyperparameters generally result in higher perplexity (except for
476   Adapter with r = 32). In contrast, we observe a reduction in perplexity for the Enron dataset
477
      under the same conditions. We hypothesize that there are optimal PEFT hyper-parameters required
      to achieve reasonable performance (e.g., in MIMIC-III, the rank ∼4–8 and the prefix tokens ∼16).
478
      Increasing those parameters beyond the optimal range can increase the noise added by DP-SGD and
479
      make the performance fluctuate. We leave the further investigation for future work.

482   5   C ONCLUSION
483
484   Our work studies the privacy risks associated with language models fine-tuned using parameter-
485   efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), an emerging approach that allows for computationally efficient fine-
      tuning of large-scale models. To evaluate the privacy, we employ an off-the-shelf data extraction


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      attack in a black-box setting, with the stronger assumption of knowing the context in which the secret
487   is embedded. We fine-tuned two pre-trained GPT-2 models using four popular PEFT methods and
488   full-model finetuning on datasets containing personally identifiable information (PII). In total, 100
489   total variations over all fine-tuning methods. Our findings show that models fine-tuned using PEFT
490   algorithms pose lower privacy risks compared to those fine-tuned through standard methods. All
491   models achieved reasonable evaluation perplexity, indicating that the privacy benefits do not come
492   at the cost of performance degradation. Interestingly, increasing the number of tunable parameters
493   in PEFT models does not necessarily lead to higher privacy risks. However, we demonstrate that
494   PEFT design can introduce specific privacy risks–for example, prefix-tuning can lead to the leakage
495
      of secrets in the first few tokens of a record. Moreover, we show that employing differential privacy
      can almost completely offset these privacy risks while maintaining evaluation perplexity at a level
496
      comparable to fine-tuning without privacy.
