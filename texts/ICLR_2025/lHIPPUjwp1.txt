                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Machine unlearning enables to remove the contribution of a set of data points
010
               from a trained model. In a distributed setting, where a server orchestrates train-
011            ing using data available at a set of remote users, unlearning is essential to cope
012            with the possible presence of malicious users. Existing distributed unlearning
013            algorithms require the server to store all model updates observed in training, lead-
014            ing to immense storage overhead for preserving the ability to unlearn. In this
015            work we study lossy compression schemes for facilitating distributed server-side
016            unlearning with limited memory footprint. We identify suitable lossy compres-
017            sion mechanisms based on random lattice coding and sparsification. For a fam-
018            ily of stochastic compression schemes encompassing probabilistic and subtractive
019
               dithered quantization, we derive an upper bound on the difference between the de-
               sired model that is trained from scratch and the model unlearned from lossy com-
020
               pressed stored updates. Our bound outperforms the state-of-the-art known bounds
021
               for non-compressed decentralized server-side unlearning, even when lossy com-
022            pression is incorporated. We further provide a numerical study, shows that suited
023            lossy compression can enable distributed unlearning with notably reduced mem-
024            ory footprint at the server while preserving the utility of the unlearned model.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep learning usually requires large volumes of training data to result with high-performance mod-
030   els. While data is often abundantly available in the ’big data’ era Jordan & Mitchell (2015), the
031   source of the data might raise privacy or ownership concerns Liu et al. (2021), among which is the
032   GDPR right to be forgotten (RTBF) Voigt & Von dem Bussche (2017), as well as security concerns,
033
      as adversaries can maliciously modify the training (poisoning or backdoors attacks) or test data (eva-
      sion attacks) Pitropakis et al. (2019). To address those, the paradigm of machine unlearning aims to
034
      selectively remove the influence of certain data points from a trained model, with neither retraining
035
      it from scratch nor impacting its original performance and predictive power Bourtoule et al. (2021).
036
037   In distributed learning systems, such as federated learning (FL) Zhang et al. (2021), where training
038   is done on edge devices without data sharing, the ability to unlearn is often essential, particularly
039
      as users may be identified as adversaries after having already contributed to the learning proce-
      dure Nguyen et al. (2022). Nonetheless, the fact that the data whose contribution is to be removed is
040
      not often available makes unlearning in distributed settings more complex compared to conventional
041
      centralized setups Fraboni et al. (2024); Huynh et al. (2024); Tao et al. (2024). As a result, unlearn-
042   ing in distributed settings, e.g., federated unlearning (FU), relies on discarding historical parameter
043   updates of the removed user. Accordingly, the server which orchestrates the learning procedure has
044   to store all past contributions of all clients in order to have the ability to unlearn when required Gao
045   et al. (2024). This induces a notable limitation, requiring excessive and possibly prohibitive over-
046   head for storing a large number of highly parameterized updates for each user.
047
      The goal of this work is to alleviate the storage burden introduced in server-side unlearning using
048   lossy source coding tools. To do so, we have the server not saving the users’ sent model updates,
049   but alternatively a lossy compressed versions, notably reducing its memory footprint. We compre-
050   hensively explore lossy compression techniques, based on (probabilistic) lattice quantization and
051   sparsifcation, identified as suitable for server-side unlearning. The effect of their induced distor-
052   tion on the unlearned model is further analytically and experimentally analyzed using conventional
053   metrics in unlearning literature. It is then revealed that while integrating proper compression into un-
      learning significantly relieve the server’s storage load, it does not change the asymptotic behaviour

054
      of the unlearned model, while effectively removing the influence of the unlearned user. Our main
055   contributions are summarized as follows:
056
057          • We study lossy compression in a distributed (federated) unlearning framework, and identify
058            the main considerations to mitigate the memory footprint accumulated over the learning
059            procedure. We are, to the best of our knowledge, the first work that systematically examines
060            the theoretical and numerical aspects of compressed distributed machine unlearning.
061          • We provide a theoretical analysis for gradient descent based distributed learning. We de-
062            rive an upper bound on the commonly adopted proximity to the optimal train-from-scratch
063            model of server-side unlearning, under a family of lossy compression mechanisms.
064          • We show that in the asymptotic regime, for growing amount of gradient descent iterations, if
065            the learning rates gradually decay; then the compressed unlearned model does not diverge.
066            Our characterization specializes also non-compressed distributed unlearning, and improves
067            the state-of-the-art asymptotic behaviour of the bound presented in Huynh et al. (2024).
068          • All compression mechanisms are quantitatively validated in a FU experimental setup, eval-
069            uated using an established backdoor attack. There, it is demonstrated that lossy compres-
070            sion significantly lowers the memory footprint of unlearning, with only a minor degradation
071
               in accuracy and while preserving the ability to defend against malicious users attack.

073   2    R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Server-Side Unlearning. Distributed unlearning frameworks are categorized based on the identity
076   of the users participating in the unlearning procedure Liu et al. (2023). Server-side unlearning, also
077   termed passive unlearning, is the challenging setting in which only the server, who originally or-
078   chestrated the distributed learning procedure, participates in unlearning. Such scenarios correspond
079
      to, e.g., late detection of a malicious user, possibly after learning is concluded Wang et al. (2023).
080   Server-side unlearning typically depends on the utilization of stored historical data as gradients,
081   global models, and contribution information. In FedRecovery Zhang et al. (2023), in addition to
082   retaining the clients historical data, the server also quantifies their contributions based on gradient
083   residuals. Upon an unlearning request, the server removes the unlearned user past contributions
084   through a fine-tuning process. A more efficient version is then suggested by Crab Jiang et al. (2024),
085
      which uses only selective historical information and further assists a less-maliciously-degraded his-
      torical model than the initial one. The recovery process can be improved by introducing constraints,
086
      e.g., a penalty term based on projected gradients Fu et al. (2024); Shao et al. (2024); randomly ini-
087
      tialized degradation models Zhao et al. (2023); estimated skew Huynh et al. (2024); and retraining
088   based on the change of sampling probability Tao et al. (2024). In VeriFi Gao et al. (2024), the target
089   client collaborates with the server and marks his data to verify the unlearning. As means to preserve
090   performance despite the target client contribution elimination, knowledge distillation was shown to
091   facilitate information transfer from the trained model to the unlearned one Wu et al. (2022; 2023).
092
      Distributed Learning with Lossy Compression. While lossy compression techniques are still
093
      unexplored for distributed unlearning, various schemes have been considered for distributed training,
094   particularly in the aim of alleviating uplink communication bottlenecks Chen et al. (2020); Li et al.
095   (2020). Among which are sub-sampling or sparsification Lin et al. (2017); Hardy et al. (2017); Aji &
096   Heafield (2017); Konečnỳ et al. (2016); Stich et al. (2018); and probabilistic scalar Wen et al. (2017);
097   Alistarh et al. (2017); Horvóth et al. (2022); Reisizadeh et al. (2020); Horváth et al. (2023) or vector
098   quantization Lang et al. (2023a;b); Azimi-Abarghouyi & Varshney (2024). Lossy compression, as
099   opposed to its lossless counterpart, inevitably induces distortion, yet enables substantial memory
100   savings Polyanskiy & Wu (2014). Nevertheless, the random distortion induced by probabilistic
101   lossy compression can be rendered to have a negligible effect on the learning procedure Alistarh
102
      et al. (2017); Shlezinger et al. (2020). Inspired by that, we seek for lossy compression methods that
      notably reduce memory footprint in server side unlearning while retaining model accuracy.

      Assuming each of the m model parameters is represented using b bits (e.g., b = 64 for a standard
163   64-bit floating point), FU involves storing U · t sequences of m · b bits. This induces a substantial
164   burden for highly parameterized models (large m) trained over many rounds (large t) with data from
165   numerous users (large U ).
166
      To mitigate this overhead, we aim to develop a FU framework, allowing compact storage of {huj }
167
      by the server while minimally affecting its ability to unlearn. To formulate this mathematically,
168
      we are interested in lossy compression mechanisms Q : Rm 7→ Rm operating with a pre-defined
169   compression rate R ≤ b (i.e., any entry of Q(huj ) is stored using R bits), such that the model
170
      obtained via server-side unlearning with the compressed model updates {Q(huj )}, matches the train-
171   from-scratch model. Since the server is unlikely to have prior knowledge of the model parameters
172   distribution, we are interested in methods which are universal. Such schemes can be formulated as
173   mappings of the model updates huj into Q(huj ) stored by the server, while meeting requirements
174
175         R1 The lossy compression function is identical for all users and along time. This requirement
176            significantly simplifies FU implementation.
177         R2 The scheme Q(·) must be invariant to the distribution of huj .

479   6      C ONCLUSIONS
480
481   In this work, we studied decentralized server-side unlearning with lossy source coding incorporated
482   in the process. We investigated its effect on the unlearned model performance from both theoretical
483   and experimental perspectives. From an experimental point of view, our numerical evaluations
484   reveal that compressed FU with notable storage reduction, e.g., 32× lower memory footprint at
485   the server, preserves the ability to unlearn. On the theoretical side; we prove that under common
      assumptions, the distance between the compressed unlearned model to the desired one is bounded.


                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Our bound improves upon the best known guarantees, asserting that the growth rate of this distance
487   is at most exponential with the number of FL iterations. However, as shown in recent not-limited-
488   to-server FU works, in some scenarios the decentralized unlearned model may actually converge to
489   the optimal one, and therefore our theoretical analysis may be a first important step towards a proof
490   of convergence for server-side unlearning. We leave it as a main open question for future study.
491
      Beyond extending our theoretical analysis, many interesting research directions are left for future
492
      work. For example, while our analysis considers basic gradient-based learning, one can potentially
493   extend its findings to other forms of learning algorithms. Other important aspects to study concern
494   the joint usage of sparsification and quantization; as well as unifying lossy and lossless compression,
495   where the latter is known to provide further performance benefits from the underlying characteristics
496   of the digital representations. Finally, prospective direction would try to mitigate the overloaded
497   quantizers numerically evidenced in section 5 by designing them Shlezinger et al. (2019) or their
498   associated lattices Lang et al. (2024) in a learning-oriented rather than a distortion-oriented manner.

788   A.2     A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENTS
789
      This section presents additional experimental results on the MNIST dataset, highlighting the rela-
790
      tionship between the compression rate and the resulted unlearned model ability to maintain high
791
      accuracy on the primary task; studied in the IID scenario. Furthermore, for the non-IID case, we
792   examine the effect of the data imbalance level β on the performance of the unlearned models in the
793   backdoor task.
794
795
      Fig. 4 complements Fig. 3 and Table 2 by illustrating the accuracy of all baselines on the primary task
      test-set (higher is better) under varying compression rates. As anticipated, retrain is the best baseline
796
      on the main task, completely not backdoored. Additionally, it is visualized that the unlearned model
797
      obtained for either of the compression methods, except for randk , exhibits a decreased performance
798   for growing rates. This follows as compressing with higher rate, i.e., an improved resolution, is
799   attributed with lesser variance and therefore more reliable estimation of the input. In this case the
800   input is the non-compresses FU model, which itself results in the lowest main task accuracy, in
801   contract to being the best baseline on the backdoor task, as showed in Fig. 3 and Table 2.
802
      Next, in Fig.5 we evaluate the performance of all baselines on the backdoor task test-set, with
803
      respect to varying values of β, which controls the degree of imbalance (lower values indicate greater
804   imbalance), while keeping the compression rate R = 2.3 fixed. The top of the figure illustrates
805   variations in the backdoor accuracy, which result from the fact that the number of backdoor samples
806   is proportional to the attacker’s local dataset size. Adjusting β alters the number of samples allocated
807   to each user, thereby influencing the backdoor attack performance on the vanilla FL model. As a
808   result, we do not expect to observe consistent behaviour across different values of β in terms of
809   backdoor accuracy. However, we do observe persistent trends across all methods for a fixed β,
      keeping the best-to-worst performers order identical to that in Fig.3.
