009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning has emerged as the new paradigm in modeling complex physical
012            dynamical systems. Nevertheless, data-driven methods learn patterns by optimizing
013            statistical metrics, tend to overlook the adherence to physical laws. Previous work
014            have attempted to incorporate physical constraints into neural networks, but they
015            often face limitations due to lack of flexibility or optimization challenges. In this
016            paper, we propose a novel framework, Physics-aware Self-Alignment (P-A LIGN),
017            to enhance the physical consistency of dynamical systems modeling. P-A LIGN
018
               enables dynamical system models to provides physics-aware rewards, which makes
               self-alignment of dynamical system models possible. Comprehensive experiments
019
               show that P-A LIGN not only gave an average statistical skill score boost of more
020
               than 32% for ten backbones on five datasets, but also significantly enhances physics-
021            aware metrics. All of our source codes will be released via GitHub.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Dynamical systems provides a mathematical framework for analyzing how systems evolve over time,
027   which is particularly important in fields such as fluid mechanics, climatology and meteorology. It
028   describes the temporal evolution of a system’s state using differential equations for continuous systems
029   or difference equations for discrete systems (Birkhoff, 1927; Anosov et al., 1988; Meiss, 2007; Galor,
030   2007). However, solving these equations analytically is often not feasible for complex systems,
031   leading to a reliance on numerical methods (Stuart & Humphries, 1998; Dellnitz & Junge, 2002;
032   Guckenheimer, 2002; Hubbard & West, 2012). While numerical approaches such as finite difference
033
      methods, finite element methods or Runge-Kutta methods can provide approximate solutions (Lisitsa
      et al., 2012; Thomas, 2013; De La Cruz et al., 2013), they tend to be computationally expensive,
034
      especially for high-dimensional systems or long time spans (Houska et al., 2012; Benner et al., 2015;
035
      Yu & Wang, 2024).
036
037   Data-driven approaches to dynamical systems modeling have gained significant attention as a way
038   to overcome some of the limitations of traditional numerical methods (Pfaff et al., 2021; Gao et al.,
039
      2022b; Pathak et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024a). These approaches leverage large datasets
      and deep learning to model the underlying dynamics directly from observed data, bypassing the
040
      need for explicit analytical forms of the governing equations (Yu & Wang, 2024). By capturing
041
      complex behaviors through data, these methods offer a promising alternative for modeling high-
042   dimensional, nonlinear, or chaotic systems where traditional approaches struggle (Noé et al., 2020;
043   Wang et al., 2020; Kochkov et al., 2021). Nevertheless, data-driven methods often build models by
044   optimizing statistical metrics, which can lack the physical consistency that traditional methods based
045   on first-principles offer (Han et al., 2020; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Pathak et al., 2022). Without
046   explicitly incorporating physical constraints, deep learning models may produce predictions that,
047   while statistically accurate, are physically implausible or violate fundamental physical laws (Pathak
048   et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b). This limitation is especially problematic when
049   extrapolating beyond the range of the training data, where the model may generate behavior that
050
      contradicts well-established physical principles (Willard et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021).
051   Recent research explored various methods to introduce physical constraints to the data-driven ap-
052   proach to enhance the physical consistency of the prediction. Some methods (Raissi et al., 2019;
053   Li et al., 2021; Hansen et al., 2023) incorporate physical equations as part of the training process to
      ensure adherence to physical laws, but optimization challenges, especially with complex constraints,

081   future states based on the current state. (2) Curation: generating and evaluating multiple potential
082   future states with high physical consistency to expand and improve the training dataset. With

085
      the generalization error of the model. Our experiments demonstrate that P-A LIGN boost performance
      in a wide range of dynamic system modeling tasks.

133
134   Dynamical System Modeling: A typical dynamical system contains multiple variables with spatio-
135   temporal relationships (Anosov et al., 1988; Meiss, 2007; Yu & Wang, 2024). It is described by
136   equations associated with unknown functions and their derivatives as a k-th order system of partial
137   differential equation:
138                                               F(Dk x(s), Dk−1 x(s), . . . , Dx(s), x(s), s) = 0                                                                      (1)
139
      where s ∈ the domain S and x means the state of the system. When the governing equation F is
140   known, we can solve them by some numerical schemes with high-cost computation. Even more often
141   F is unknown, which makes the numerical schemes completely infeasible.
142
143
      Data-driven modeling of dynamical systems encourages end-to-end prediction, thereby skipping
      expensive numerical integration. Specifically, dynamical system models learn to build a probabilistic
144
      model P(Y|X ; θ), which maps a sequence of past values to future values:
145
146                                                   M : {X1 , X2 , . . . , XT } → {Y1 , Y2 , . . . , YT },                                                             (2)
147   where Yt = Xt+△t . Xt is the input features and the Yt is the outputs, T stands for forecasting
148   horizon and △t is the time lag.

152   4.1        F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
153
154   As shown in Figure 2, P-A LIGN explores the latent space of dynamical system models and produces
155   augmented training data with more physical consistency, followed by self-alignment across multiple
156   iterations. Specifically, P-A LIGN performed the following three steps in one iteration: (1) Self-
157   Discovery, which identify approximate representative samples from the continuous space in which the
158
      current hidden state resides. (2) Physics-Aware-Curation, which find new samples with more physical
      consistency.(3) Data Augmentation add the augmented samples to training data. (4) Alignment,
159
      which use the argument data to train models themselves.
160
161   Through these four steps, P-A LIGN enables dynamical system models to achieve self-alignment.
      Indeed, we construct a reward strategy here: Self-Discovery generates candidate responses, which

      5.2   E VALUATING THE E FFICACY OF P-A LIGN (RQ1)

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper presents P-A LIGN, a physics self-alignment framework designed to enhance physical
530   consistency in dynamical system modeling. P-A LIGN enables effective exploration and self-alignment
531   of hidden states through self-discovery and physics-aware optimization. It improves prediction
532   performance across multiple complex spatiotemporal datasets. Experimental results show that P-
533   A LIGN achieves over 32% improvement in statistical skill scores compared to the original models,
534
      and it enhances physical consistency, especially in extreme event prediction. Overall, P-A LIGN
      provides a flexible and efficient solution for applying deep learning to dynamical systems.

      B    OVERVIEW OF E VALUATED M ODELS
865
866   Here, we provide an overview of each model used to evaluate the generalizability of our proposed
867   method.
868
869
      ConvLSTM: ConvLSTM combines convolutional neural networks (CNN) and long short-term
      memory (LSTM) networks. It processes spatiotemporal data by maintaining spatial information
870
      using convolutional layers and capturing temporal dependencies through LSTM units. ConvLSTM
871
      performs well for tasks such as weather forecasting, traffic prediction, and video analysis, effectively
872   preserving both spatial and temporal features (Shi et al., 2015).
873
874   PredRNN-V2: PredRNN-V2 is a recurrent neural network designed for spatiotemporal predictive
875
      learning. It uses dual memory cells to extract and retain spatial and temporal features separately,
      improving the model’s ability to capture both short-term dynamics and long-term temporal depen-
876
      dencies. PredRNN-V2 incorporates a curriculum learning strategy to learn from context, making it
877
      suitable for video prediction and weather forecasting tasks (Wang et al., 2022).
878
879   Vision Transformer (ViT): ViT uses a pure Transformer architecture for image classification. It
880   divides an input image into patches and processes them like a sequence, similar to natural language
881
      processing tasks. Unlike convolutional networks, ViT relies on self-attention to learn features,
      allowing it to achieve excellent performance on large datasets with fewer computational resources
882
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020).
883
884   Motion-Aware Unit (MAU): MAU is a module that specifically captures motion information. It
885   enhances the performance of recurrent neural networks in video prediction tasks by integrating a
886   motion-aware mechanism that focuses on capturing dynamic features (Chang et al., 2021).
887   SimVP: SimVP is an efficient video prediction model that does not use complex architectures
888   like RNN, LSTM, or Transformer. Instead, it uses CNNs to perform video prediction, enabling
889   parallel processing and reducing computational complexity while maintaining the ability to learn
890   spatiotemporal features effectively (Gao et al., 2022a).
891   Multi-Modal Video Prediction (MmvP): MmvP integrates multiple data modalities, such as vision
892   and text, to improve video prediction accuracy and robustness. It uses different fusion methods to
893   effectively combine features from various modalities, making it useful in complex scenarios involving
894   diverse data sources (Zhong et al., 2023).
895
      Earthfarsser: Earthfarsser focuses on environmental data prediction and analysis. It is specifically
896
      designed for Earth science applications and performs well with data that contains both spatial and
897   temporal characteristics. It is suitable for tasks like climate forecasting and disaster assessment (Wu
898   et al., 2024a).
