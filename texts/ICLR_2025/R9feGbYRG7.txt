011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research has revealed shared neural patterns among animals performing
014            similar tasks and within individual animals across different tasks. This has led to
015
               a growing interest in replacing single-session latent variable models with a uni-
               fied model that allows us to align recordings across different animals, sessions,
016
               and tasks, despite the challenge of distinct neuron identities in each recording.
017
               In this work, we present a conditioned diffusion framework to model population
018            dynamics of neural activity across multiple contexts. The quality of the learned
019            dynamics is evaluated through the model’s forecasting ability, which predicts mul-
020            tiple timesteps of both neural activity and behavior. Additionally, we introduce a
021            benchmark dataset spanning six electrophysiology datasets, seven tasks, 19 ani-
022            mals, and 261 sessions, providing a standardized framework for multi-task neural
023            population models. Our results demonstrate that the pretrained model can be effi-
024            ciently adapted to novel, unseen sessions without requiring explicit neuron corre-
025            spondence. This enables few-shot learning with minimal labeled data, as well as
026
               competitive performance in zero-shot learning.

028   A    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent advances in neural recording technologies have enabled the simultaneous capture of large
031   populations of neurons, revealing complex spatiotemporal activity. To address this, computational
032   models have been developed to infer latent structures from high-dimensional neural data. Dowl-
033   ing et al. (2023); Pandarinath et al. (2018a); Duncker et al. (2019) Deep generative models, such
034   as variational autoencoders (VAEs) Kingma & Welling (2014) and sequential VAEs, have been
035   widely adopted to extract these latent processes, typically mapping neural or behavioral data to
036   low-dimensional representations Schulz et al. (2024).
037   Despite the increasing availability of large-scale electrophysiological recordings, current research
038   in neural coding and computation predominantly focuses on single tasks or individual experimen-
039   tal sessions. This current approach misses the opportunity to utilize the structure across individual
040   datasets. Mounting evidence for such possibility, especially in the brain areas related to motor con-
041   trol showed common representation of latent trajectories for stereotypical behavior Gallego et al.
042   (2020); Safaie et al. (2022); Dabagia et al. (2023) and generalizability of dynamical cortical behav-
043   ior Karpowicz et al. (2022a); Vermani et al. (2024b).
044   Fitting a single model to a collection of neural data with expected shared features can increase data
045   usage efficiency and enhance our understanding of common neural computation structures that are
046   generalizable. Moreover, this approach can enable efficient scientific progress in new experiments
047   through few-shot learning Ye et al. (2023), where novel experiments could benefit from pre-existing
048   knowledge, reducing the need for extensive new data collection.
049   Motivated by the success of pre-trained models in machine learning and the growing interest in
050   unified/large scale models for neuroscience Azabou et al. (2023) Ye et al. (2023), we explore the
051   potential of building a foundation model for forecasting neural population data. However, there is
052   a challenge of inherent statistical heterogeneities across datasets such as differences in the number
053   and tuning properties of recorded neurons or variations in recording modalities. Thus, previous
      approaches uses alignment process to transform new data so that it matches the statistical properties

115   Generative Models for Neural Data Forecasting One of the key objectives of computational
116   neuroscience is to build a model capable of reproduce the dynamics of the neural recording. The
117   assessment of this is made by the accurate forecasting capacities of such model.
118   Classical models, such as Latent Factor Analysis via Dynamical Systems (LFADS) Pandarinath et al.
119   (2018a), use nonlinear recurrent neural networks to infer latent dynamics by modeling neural activ-
120   ity as a dynamical system, generating denoised firing rates from an RNN generator. Similarly, the
121   Structured Variational Autoencoder (SVAE) Johnson et al. (2016); Zhao & Linderman (2023) pre-
122   serves temporal structure by constraining the prior to a linear dynamical system but faces limitations
123   with nonlinear dynamics. The Deep Kalman Filter (dKF) Krishnan et al. (2016) employs black-box
124
      inference networks for joint posterior sampling but encounters difficulties in learning generative dy-
      namics due to issues with gradient propagation. The Deep Variational Bayes Filter (dVBF) Karl
125
      et al. (2017) handles state-space graphical models by sampling from the approximate posterior, mit-
126
      igating the complexity of directly parameterizing dynamics. The eXponential Family Dynamical
127   Systems (XFADS) Dowling et al. (2024) approach extends these ideas to nonlinear Gaussian state-
128   space models, leveraging low-rank approximations and efficient message passing for scalable infer-
129   ence. Although these models are specifically designed to forecast dynamics within a single session,
130   our foundation model is capable of generalizing across multiple sessions and subjects. To evaluate
131   its robustness and effectiveness, we compared its performance against these classical approaches
132   tailored for single-session dynamics.
133   Multi-Session Forecasting Previous research on multi-session training and alignment has primar-
134   ily focused on fitting models to data recorded from the same subject, in the same brain region, often
135   with the same chronic implant, and typically assumes a single, stereotyped task structure. Many
136   of these studies do not train on multiple sessions simultaneously, but instead rely on post-training
137   alignment strategies to transfer models trained on one session to other recording days. Pre-trained
138   sequential VAEs (seqVAEs) Vermani et al. (2024a) represent one such approach, introducing an un-
139   supervised alignment mechanism where new neural time series data is aligned with latent dynamics
140   learned from an original dataset. This method transforms the new data to conform to pre-trained
141
      latent trajectories without requiring paired samples or the original training data. Another method,
      ERDiff Wang et al. (2023), enhances this alignment by combining a pre-trained seqVAE with a
142
      diffusion model that estimates the density of latent trajectories using spatio-temporal transformers,
143
      optimizing the alignment via Sinkhorn divergence. NoMAD Karpowicz et al. (2022b) similarly
144   leverages a pre-trained seqVAE, but fits a multivariate Gaussian to the inferred latent states of the
145   original dataset, training the alignment function to match these states to the new dataset using a
146   KL-divergence loss. In contrast, Cycle-GAN Ma et al. (2023) uses adversarial training to align
147   new sessions with the original data via generative adversarial networks. Finally, Orthogonal Pro-
148   crustes Schoenemann (1966) aligns datasets by learning a transformation based on paired samples,
149   though it requires access to both the original and new datasets.
150   Foundation Models for Neuroscience Recently, Azabou et al. (2023) and Ye et al. (2023) showed
151   that training not only across subjects but on data from different tasks and laboratories, is possible.
152   However, these models were specifically designed for decoding in brain-machine interface (BMI)
153   applications and lack demonstrated forecasting capabilities, or such capabilities have not been thor-
154   oughly explored. It is important to note that NDT2 Ye et al. (2023) has established the potential for
155   zero-shot learning within this context. A comprehensive comparison with our approach is provided
156   in Appendix A.
157   Tools for Foundation Models: Benchmark Datasets Recent advancements in neuroscience have
158   led to the emergence of large datasets and a focus on data sharing through initiatives like Neurodata
159   Without Borders (NWB) Teeters et al. (2015); Rübel et al. (2022), which enhance reproducibility.
160   It is a growing ecosystem around NWB format hosted on Dandi archive1 . Which makes the data

172   This study utilizes a diffusion model conditioned on session identifiers. The diffusion process trans-
173   forms each training sample into a Gaussian distribution through an iterative noise corruption process,
174   as described in Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022). Subsequently, a deep neural network is
175
      trained to invert this transformation, enabling the generation of new samples starting from Gaussian
      noise inputs.

      C.3     C AUSAL F ORECASTING M ASKING
254   Building on the foundation of the multi-task masking approaches proposed in Zhang et al. (2023),
255   we employed a causal mask where we only corrupt the input after a specific time bin to enable
256   causal prediction. This means that the model can only utilize information up to the current time step
257
      to forecast the future, which aligns with the requirements of real-world applications.
258   C.4     S TRATEGIES FOR S ESSION T RANSFER
259
260
      Inspired by classifier-free guidance, our model incorporates both unconditional and conditional
      components. For each session, 20% of the trials are randomly designated as unconditional. This
261
      involves assigning a session ID that is shared across all sessions. During sampling, we can ap-
262
      ply the strategy introduced by Ho & Salimans (2022), expressed as ϵθ (xt , y, t) = ϵθ (xt , t) +
263   g (ϵθ (xt , y, t) − ϵθ (xt , t)) , where ϵθ (xt , y, t) is the noise prediction from the model conditioned
264   on y, ϵθ (xt , t) is the noise prediction from the unconditioned model, g is the guidance scale that
265   determines the strength of the conditioning.
266
      To generalize to new sessions, we can use the unconditional model with three strategies:
267
268   - Zero-shot learning: The unconditional model, i.e. conditioned on the shared session ID, predicts
269   held-out sessions without requiring test data alignment, unlike previous methods. However, this may
      lead to poorer performance since the model has not been adapted to the new session data.

                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      - Fine-tuning: The pre-trained model’s weights are fine-tuned using a few samples from the new
271   sessions to adapt to their data distributions.
272
273   - Session identification: Following the unit identification strategy of Azabou et al. (2023), we adapt
274
      session embeddings using gradient descent, initializing new embeddings with the unconditional
      session embedding to avoid poor performance from random initialization. This approach maps new
275
      sessions into the embedding space by freezing the model’s weights and adding rows to the session
276
      embedding lookup table, allowing rapid transfer to new sessions. In our model, this strategy closely
277   parallels the fine-tuning of the alignment networks in Vermani et al. (2024b).

279   C.5   F ORECASTING AND F ILTERING
280
281
      Full-sequence diffusion, widely used in video generation, trains models to denoise entire sequences
      in a seq2seq manner, allowing for globally optimal predictions within a specified length. This ap-
282
      proach supports flexible-length sequence generation by autoregressively using the output without
283
      the need for beam search, as required by next-token prediction models like transformers. To enable
284   direct comparison with such models, we trained our approach to predict the next time step (filtering)
285   from a ground-truth sequence using teacher forcing.

      Panel A of Fig. 6 provides a direct comparison between our joint model and the two-step model,
513   where behavior is predicted using ridge regression based on the next-step spiking activity prediction
514   (filtering). The results show that the joint model outperforms the two-step model in behavior predic-
515   tion, while maintaining the quality of spiking activity prediction. Thus the joint model effectively
516   learns the relationship between spiking and behavior in an integrated manner, optimizing for both
517   simultaneously. Unlike the two-step model, which separates the tasks of spikes and behavior predic-
518   tion, the joint model captures the interactions between neural activity and behavior more effectively,
519   leading to improved overall performance. Importantly, the joint model does not sacrifice the accu-
520   racy of spike predictions, suggesting that it can handle multiple objectives without degradation.
521   Additionally, the model demonstrated robustness to different preprocessing methods, including time
522   binning and Gaussian smoothing filters. The analysis of the history forecasting window is consis-
523   tent with our occlusion analysis, which suggests that the majority of the behavioral signals (hand
524   velocity) occur around 200-250 ms prior to movement onset. Given that we tested forecasting after
525   movement onset in a stereotypical task, we did not observe significant performance degradation as
526   the forecast horizon was extended during the motion phase.
527   Finally, the lambda term in the loss function significantly influences model performance. In our
528   benchmarks, we found that a value of 0.5 worked well for both behavior and spiking activity. How-
529   ever, this may vary depending on the specific brain area or task being studied.

      F      C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      We present a novel framework for training diffusion models on large, multi-context neural activ-
534   ity datasets, capable of zero-shot learning and providing interpretable embeddings, supported by a
535   benchmark dataset and API for community use. Our findings show that larger training datasets im-
536   prove the model’s ability to predict behavioral outcomes and neural spiking activity, capturing brain
537   dynamics more precisely. Robust zero-shot performance on held-out sessions highlights the model’s
538   capacity to learn shared dynamics across sessions and animals. Future work will focus on leverag-
539   ing explainable machine learning to analyze neural mechanisms, explore population dynamics, and
      investigate inter-session variability through the session embedding space.
