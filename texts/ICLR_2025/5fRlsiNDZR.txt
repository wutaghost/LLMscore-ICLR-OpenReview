011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                In this paper, we introduce FARV, a vocoder specifically designed for Video-to-
014                Speech (V2S) synthesis, which integrates both facial embeddings and acoustic
015                units to generate speech waveforms. By sharing the acoustic unit vocabulary in
016                our two-stage V2S pipeline, FARV effectively bridges the domain gap between
017
                   the visual frontend and the vocoder without requiring finetuning. Furthermore,
                   by embedding visual speaker images into the acoustic unit representations, FARV
018
                   enhances its ability to preserve speaker identity. Experimental results demonstrate
019
                   that FARV achieves leading scores in intelligibility and strikes a favorable balance
020                between speaker characterisitcs preservation and acoustic quality, making it well-
021                suited for practical V2S applications1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Video-to-Speech (V2S) synthesis (Prajwal et al., 2020a) aims to generate intelligible, natural-
027
      sounding speech directly from silent video inputs, leveraging visual cues such as lip movements
028   and facial expressions for audio recovery. V2S is particularly useful in scenarios where only vi-
029   sual information is available to infer the speaker’s speech, such as in silent video meetings or for
030   individuals who cannot produce voiced sounds.
031
      Most V2S approaches (Mira et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2023a; Hsu et al., 2023) rely on a two-stage
032
      framework: an upstream model that extracts audio representations (Mel spectrograms or acoustic
033
      units), followed by a vocoder that converts these representations into waveforms. Since the upstream
034   model and the vocoder are trained separately, a domain gap often arises between these two stages
035   because the vocoder is trained on ground-truth acoustic representations from clean datasets, which
036   may not adapt well to the outputs of the frontend encoder. Therefore, vocoders play a crucial role in
037   V2S systems, as they are responsible for bridging the gap to upstream model outputs and recovering
038   the audio at the same time. However, due to the drawbacks of vocoders, many existing V2S models
039   still face significant challenges, particularly concerning the preservation of speaker identity (Hsu
040   et al., 2023) and the generalization between synthesis stages.
041   Unit-based vocoders can mitigate the domain gap by sharing a common vocabulary of discrete units
042   between the stages and can be adapted to the upstream model without concern. However, they often
043   lose crucial speaker-specific information, resulting in synthesized audio that sounds less natural and
044   fails to accurately reflect the original speaker’s identity. In contrast, mel-based vocoders offer better
045   speaker preservation by utilizing Mel spectrograms, which provide richer frequency details. Yet,
046   their sensitivity to spectral differences limits their generalization across the two stages.
047   To overcome these limitations, we propose an approach that combines the generalizability of unit-
048   based vocoders with the speaker-specific preservation capabilities of mel-based vocoders. Specifi-
049   cally, we leverage a pre-trained facial representation extractor (Zheng et al., 2022) to capture speaker
050   characteristics from the speaker’s image. This allows us to seamlessly integrate speaker-specific in-
051   formation into the vocoder. At the same time, the vocoder synthesizes audio based on acoustic units
052   shared with the frontend encoder. This approach enables our V2S model to maintain the generaliz-

      2     R ELATED W ORK

      2.1   VOCODERS
074
075   In speech synthesis, it is important to reconstruct speech from a compressed latent acoustic represen-
076   tation. To meet this need, vocoders offer a way to convert the acoustic representations to waveform.
077   In this way, any speech-related tasks can first train a model that generates the acoustic representa-
078   tion, which is then fed to vocoders to synthesize speech. Vocoders are widely used in text-to-speech
079   (TTS) (Ren et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Du et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2019; Wang
080   et al., 2023) and have also become a favorable choice for V2S. Common choices for latent acoustic
081   representation for vocoders are the Mel spectrogram (Kong et al., 2020; Yamamoto et al., 2020; gil
082   Lee et al., 2023) and the hidden unit (Polyak et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Hsu et al., 2023).
083   While vocoders based on Mel spectrograms (mel-based vocoders) have the ability to retain speaker
084   characteristics in speech synthesis, they are often vulnerable to domain shifts in speech conditions.
085   For example, chun Hsu et al. (2020) tested mel-based vocoders on unseen speakers, and have found
086   that all tested mel-vocoders suffer from a significant domain gap. This domain gap is probably
087   caused by the over-sensitivity of vocoders to frequency distributions of input audio, which interfers
088   with vocoders in domain adaptations.
089   Later, Lee et al. (2022) found that hidden units from HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) can serve as an
090   acoustic codebook to resynthesize audio waveform. Inspired by this, ReVISE (Hsu et al., 2023) pro-
091   posed to use unit-HiFiGAN (unit-based vocoder) in V2S and speech enhancement. However, Hsu
092   et al. (2023) also mentioned that HuBERT units focus on speech content and only contain knowl-
093   edge capable of reconstructing utterances of spoken sentences, neglecting speaker characteristics
094   (speaker identity like gender or age).
095
096   2.2   V IDEO TO S PEECH (V2S)
097
098   Lip2Wav (Prajwal et al., 2020a) was a pioneering work of V2S that performs speech recovery on a
099
      self-made dataset. However, Prajwal et al. (2020a) found that mel-based neural vocoders perform
      poorly on their generated Mel spectrograms. VAE-GAN (Hegde et al., 2022) and VCA-GAN (Kim
100
      et al., 2022) proposed adversarial learning in V2S and gained comparable performance with other
101
      supervised methods. Later, SVTS (Mira et al., 2022) tested their V2S method on LRS3 Afouras
102   et al. (2018) and VoxCeleb2 Chung et al. (2018), which still suffers performance degradation on
103   unseen speakers. Following SVTS, IntelligibleL2S (Choi et al., 2023b) incorporated unit and mel
104   as vocoder input for V2S. Then, MultiTask (Kim et al., 2023) and AccurateL2S (Hegde et al., 2023)
105   incorporated additional textual information and speaker embedding to further enhance V2S per-
106   formance. However, since textual and acoustic speaker embedding is not always available, their
107   application in practical scenarios of V2S is limited. To eliminate the need for additional inputs,
      DiffV2S (Choi et al., 2023a) proposed a diffusion method conditioned on visual embedding.

      2.3   U NSUPERVISED FACIAL REPRESENTATION LEARNING
109
110   Unsupervised facial representation learning (Bulat et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022) is able to provide
111   prior knowledge about facial identity of a person that can be transferred to downstream tasks like
112   face attribute recognition (e.g. gender or age). Paplham & Franc (2024) compared existing methods
113   on facial gender estimation with a unified benchmark and have found that FaRL (Zheng et al.,
114   2022) with an MLP significantly outperforms other methods thanks to the amount of data used in its
115   pretraining. Similar to CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), FaRL is pretrained with image-text pairs on a
116
      human face subset of LAION-400M (Schuhmann et al., 2021) using contrastive loss. We therefore
      leverage FaRL in the training of unit-HiFiGAN to provide insights of visual speaker characteristics.

      3.1   F RAMEWORK D ESIGN
151
152   Our V2S framework is a two-stage framework composing a visual frontend encoder and a vocoder.
153   The frontend encoder utilizes a pretrained AV-HuBERT model (Shi et al., 2022a), followed by a
154   Conformer module (Gulati et al., 2020). The AV-HuBERT model was shown to be crucial for effi-
155   cient convergence in V2S tasks (Hsu et al., 2023), making it our preferred choice for the frontend
156   backbone. To match the sampling rate of visual frames (25Hz) and that of acoustic units (50Hz),
157   transposed convolution is applied to upsample the final output of frontend encoder. This frontend
158
      provides prior knowledge about audio-visual correlation to our V2S pipeline, offering accurate con-
      tent recovery capacity.
159
160   The vocoder (FARV) is an adapted unit-HiFiGAN that combines visual embedding from the facial
161   extractor(Zheng et al., 2022) and acoustic units. This audio-visual modality fusion will enable FARV
      to preserve speaker characteristics better. The acoustic units are clustered from the output of the pre-

      4.1.1       DATASETS
230   We applied LRS3-TED (Afouras et al., 2018) and LRS2-BBC (Afouras et al., 2022) datasets to test
231   intelligibility of V2S systems and train the proposed vocoder. The splits of LRS3-TED are identical
232   to that of Afouras et al. (2018). VoxCeleb2 (Chung et al., 2018) is also applied to testify equal error
233   rate (EER) for speaker verification. All these datasets are also used to test the adaptation capability
234   of vocoders.
235   We also use the audio-visual RAVDESS dataset (Livingstone & Russo, 2018) to test the capability
236   of unit vocoders on gender and emotion classification. This is similar to the way Ji et al. (2024)
237   tested their embedding but we only choose RAVDESS dataset of the benchmark (Livingstone &
238   Russo, 2018) as it contains audio-visual resources. There are 8 emotions for classification2 and they
239   can be clearly reflected based on facial expression of the speaker.
240
241   4.1.2       M ETRICS
242
      For low-level detail reconstruction, we utilize Extended Short-Time Objective Intelligibility (ES-
243
      TOI) and Mel Cepstral Distortion (MCD) metrics, focusing on speech intelligibility and mel-cepstral
244
      differences, respectively. To assess audio-visual synchronization, we employ LSE-C and LSE-D
245   metrics, following the implementation from Prajwal et al. (2020b); Chung & Zisserman (2016).
246   For content accuracy, we evaluate Word Error Rate (WER) using a pretrained ASR system (Xu
247   et al., 2020). The wav2vec 2.0 model and weights for ASR evaluation are sourced from https:
248   //huggingface.co/facebook/wav2vec2-large-960h-lv60-self, identical to the
249   setup used in ReVISE (Hsu et al., 2023). For acoustic quality, similar to how Irvin et al. (2022)
250   evaluates vocoder, we apply NISQA-MOS (Mittag et al., 2021) to give automated non-intrusive
251   prediction of subjective mean opinion score (MOS) scores.
252   For speaker characteristics preservation, we apply Speaker Encoder Cosine Similarity (SECS) and
253   Equal Error Rate (EER) as our metrics to evalutate speaker matching performance. Following Choi
254   et al. (2023a), we use an off-the-shelf audio speaker encoder Jia et al. (2019) for the evaluation of
255   speaker embedding and compute SECS. EER computation is similar to Shi et al. (2022b) for speaker
256   verification, where the matching score is the cosine similarity of speaker embedding (Jia et al., 2019)
257   for each pair of trials. We use only one clip for each pair’s evaluation for simplicity. EER is always
258   tested on VoxCeleb2, as speaker labels are available to construct test pairs for speaker verification.
259

      For the vocoders, we follow the training setting of Hsu et al. (2023) to train HiFiGAN and unit-
267   HiFiGAN on the single-speaker LJSpeech dataset (Ito & Johnson, 2017) resampled at 16kHz. Since
268   visual images are required, we train FARV on the audio-visual LRS3-TED and LRS2-BBC datasets

      4.3     VOCODER A DAPTATION C APABILITY
305
306   Since the V2S frontend encoder is optimized to align with the expected inputs of the vocoder, the
307   overall synthesis performance in the V2S framework is largely constrained by the vocoder’s ca-
308   pabilities. Mel-based vocoders often face challenges in adapting to the frontend encoder’s output,
309   whereas unit-based vocoders effectively bridge this gap due to the shared acoustic unit vocabulary
310   that is consistent throughout the frontend training.
311
      In the following sections, we will explore vocoder adaptation for both datasets and V2S frontend
312   applications. Dataset adaptation refers to the application of vocoders to new datasets that differ from
313   the original training data, either through fine-tuning or in a zero-shot manner. We will examine the
314   percentage drop in performance from the fine-tuned state to the zero-shot state, with smaller drops
315   indicating a vocoder’s stronger adaptability to new datasets. V2S adaptation, on the other hand,
316   focuses on evaluation of vocoders when they are applied to the output of V2S frontend encoder,
317   where domain gap may arise. We will assess the percentage drop in performance when vocoders are
318   applied in a zero-shot manner to the V2S encoder output, highlighting their practical suitability for
319   V2S applications.
320   In the following experiments, we perform finetuning for up to 400,000 updates on a single GPU,
321   selecting the model checkpoint based on the lowest validation loss of the Mel spectrogram during
322   vocoder training. FARV is further trained on the LRS3-TED dataset described in Section 4.1.3, as
323   visual images are necessary for training the proposed vocoder. In contrast, other zero-shot vocoders
      are trained on the LJSpeech dataset.

518   5     C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we introduced FARV, a vocoder specifically designed for Video-to-Speech (V2S) syn-
521   thesis that effectively integrates audio-visual modalities. Through a comparative analysis with exist-
522   ing vocoders, we identified their key limitations: mel-based vocoders struggle to adapt to the outputs
523   of V2S frontend encoders, which limits their practical applicability, while unit-based vocoders face
524   challenges in balancing speaker identity preservation with acoustic quality. FARV addresses these
525
      issues by incorporating facial image embeddings, which enhance the preservation of speaker charac-
      teristics, and by utilizing a shared unit vocabulary that seamlessly integrates with the V2S pipeline.
526
      Experimental results demonstrate that FARV achieves superior intelligibility and strikes an advanta-
527
      geous balance between preserving speaker characteristics and maintaining sound quality, even when
528   adapted to new datasets. Overall, FARV shows significant potential for practical V2S applications,
529   effectively minimizing the performance drops typically observed in mel-based approaches.

698

700   official validation set, we apply the validation split provided by AV-HuBERT (Shi et al., 2022a). All

      to train V2S framework.

      A.2   C ONFIGURATION
703
704   K-Means clustering with 2000 categories on the third iteration feature from the last layer of the
705   HuBERT model Hsu et al. (2021) is applied to all our training dataset to build the acoustic unit
706   vocabulary shared by both the frontend encoder and the vocoder. The HuBERT model for clustering
707   is the BASE version pretrained on 960 hours of the LibriSpeech dataset Panayotov et al. (2015). The
708   frontend encoder comprises AV-HuBERT LARGE of 325M parameters along with with a 4-block
709   conformer module. The conformer has 4 attention heads with attention dimension of 256 and has
710
      11M parameters for adapting visual input to acoustic representation prediction.
711   For the frontend encoder, we follow Shi et al. (2022a) to preprocess the visual input, as the upstream
712   visual frontend uses an AV-HuBERT model. Preprocessing ensure that video frames are cropped to
713   96x96 based on facial keypoints detected by the dlib tool King (2009) and transformed into grayscale
714   frames after an affine transformation. In acutal use of model, we further crop it to an area of 88x88
715   pixel region. During training, visual frames have a 50% chance of being horizontally flipped and are
716
      limited to 4.0 seconds from the start of each video. For evaluation, we consistently apply a center
      crop without flipping, and load the entire video into the model.
717
718   For FARV, we crop the speaker image at the middle frame of raw input video (unpreprocessed) in
719   RGB and apply transformation identical to FaRL (Zheng et al., 2022) which applies center-crop to
720   224x224 to input RGB image after bicubic interpolation. We then normalize the image in RGB
721   which is ready as the input to FaRL image encoder. No facial crop is performed as FaRL is trained
722
      on LAION-Face (Zheng et al., 2022), a dataset that contains human facial image-text pairs with the
      human face showing up at varied positions of different angles in image, which brings it the capability
723
      of zero-shot adaptation.
724
725   For Mel spectrogram generation during vocoder training and the ReVISE (Mel) frontend encoder,
726   we follow van Niekerk et al. (2021), extracting 128-dimensional Mel spectrograms from raw audio
727   at 10-ms intervals across all datasets. Since all our dataset has acoustic sampling rate of 16kHz, the
728
      hop size is set to 160, aligning with the upsampled visual features (100FPS after upsampling 4 times
      from 25Hz of original video clips). Size of fft is set to be 512.

      A.3   T RAINING S ETUP
732   For frontend encoder, a tri-stage learning rate scheduler is applied with a max learning rate of 6e-5,
733   which is identical to the training setting used in ReVISE (Hsu et al., 2023). AdamW optimizer is
734   used with β1 =0.9 and β2 =0.98. We optimize the frontend encoder for at most 45k updates per GPU
735   and freeze AV-HuBERT for first 5k updates of training to warm up the conformer module. We apply
736   the batch size of 10 on each GPU.
737
      For vocoder training, we apply the setup of van Niekerk et al. (2021) and train vocoders with
738   AdamW optimizer with weight decay of 1e-5 ,β1 =0.8 and β2 =0.99. Exponential learning rate sched-
739   uler is applied with a decay rate of 0.999 for both the generator and the discriminators. Learning
740   rate is set to be 1e-4. We apply the batch size of 8 on each GPU.
741
      For vocoder training used for zero-shot scenario and frontend encoder training both take 8 RTX4090
742
      GPUs to run for about 24 hours. Finetuning the vocoder takes only 1 GPU with identical settings to
