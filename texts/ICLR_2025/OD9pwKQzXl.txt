                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent test time compute approaches with verifier models have significantly en-
012
               hanced the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). While this
013            kind of generator-verifier approach closely resembles the actor-critic framework
014            in reinforcement learning (RL), the verifiers currently are used rely on supervised
015            fine-tuning rather than on temporal difference learning. This paper introduces Ver-
016            ifierQ, a novel approach that integrates Offline Q-learning into LLM verifier mod-
017            els. We address three key challenges in applying Q-learning to LLMs: utterance-
018            level Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), large action spaces, and overestimation
019            bias. VerifierQ introduces a modified Bellman update, incorporates Implicit Q-
020            learning (IQL) for efficient action space management, and integrates a novel Con-
021
               servative Q-learning (CQL) formulation for balanced overestimation. Our method
               is among the first to apply Q-learning to LLM verifiers. This integration of RL
022
               principles into verifier models complements existing advancements in generator
023
               techniques. Experimental results on mathematical reasoning tasks demonstrate
024            VerifierQ’s superior performance compared to supervised fine-tuning approaches.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a promising approach to multi-step reasoning tasks through
030   language. However, despite their prowess in generating coherent text, LLMs face significant chal-
031   lenges in sustained, multi-step logical reasoning due to their underlying architecture and propensity
032   for hallucinations (Lightman et al., 2024). Overcoming these challenges is critical for enabling the
033   next level of agent capabilities.
034
      One of the most important recent developments in addressing these limitations is test time compute
035
      (Snell et al., 2024; Cobbe et al., 2021). As demonstrated by OpenAI (2024), test time compute
036   represents a new paradigm in the scaling laws of LLMs. Test time compute essentially involves using
037   a verifier model to evaluate and select the best solutions generated by an LLM, allowing for more
038   extensive processing and deliberation during inference. By leveraging additional computational
039   resources at test time, LLMs can potentially perform more complex reasoning tasks with improved
040   accuracy and reduced hallucinations.
041
      The concept of a verifier aligns closely with recent research on multi-step reasoning, which typically
042   employs two main components: a generator and a verifier (Lightman et al., 2024; Uesato et al.,
043   2022; Cobbe et al., 2021). The generator produces potential solutions, while the verifier evaluates
044   their correctness. This setup is analogous to the actor-critic framework in Reinforcement Learning
045   (RL) (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999). However, unlike RL critics that use temporal-difference (TD)
046   updates for long-term credit assignments, current verifiers in multi-step reasoning are often trained
047   using supervised fine-tuning (SFT). This limitation might hinder the verifier’s ability to guide the
048   generator toward better long-term outcomes, particularly in complex reasoning tasks.
049   To address these challenges, we propose leveraging Reinforcement Learning techniques, particularly
050   Offline Q-learning, to enhance verifier performance in long-horizon tasks. This approach draws in-
051   spiration from successful RL systems like AlphaGo Silver et al. (2016), which achieve superhuman
052   performance by combining learning and search techniques. Recent research has focused on improv-
053   ing generators using methods like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
      2024b;a; Zhang et al., 2024a). However, less attention has been given to applying RL to verifiers.

065   We introduce VerifierQ, an Offline Q-learning approach that integrates classical reinforcement learn-
066   ing techniques with LLMs. The core research question guiding this work is: Can Offline Q-learning
067   improve the verifier’s ability to handle multi-step reasoning tasks, and if so, how can we overcome
068   the obstacles limiting its application to LLM value networks?
069
      Our work makes several key contributions: (1) We propose a flexible architecture for applying
070   offline Q-learning on utterance-level in language models, and we resolve the large action space
071   problem on the utterance level. (2) We present an innovative formulation of Conservative Q-learning
072   tailored for these large action spaces, mitigating overestimation issues in offline Q-learning. (3) Our
073   approach bridges the gap between classic critic models in reinforcement learning and verifier models
074   in language tasks, opening new avenues for improving test-time compute in large language models.

      Using a generator-verifier framework with large language models (LLMs) Lightman et al. (2024),
089
      we can formulate the reasoning process as an utterance-level MDP. Given a problem statement p, the
090   generator produces a solution based on the problem and previous action steps, where [p, a1 , ..., ai−1 ]
091   is the state and ai is an action. Each action is a complete sentence as shown in Figure 1. This differs
092   from token-level approaches where actions would be individual tokens from the vocabulary, which
093   can be a word or a subword. The state at step i is the dialogue history that consists of the problem
094   statement and all previous complete utterances generated up to that point: si = [p, a1 , a2 , . . . , ai ].
095   Rewards are given at each step, with a reward of 1 for a correct step and 0 for an incorrect one. We
096   can see the illustrated example in Figure 1, where + indicates “correct” and − indicates “incorrect”.
097   We will discuss advantages of utterance level in Section 4.2.
098   In classical RL, the critic model is trained to estimate the Q value (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999). In
099   test time compute, the verifier model is trained to estimate the Q value (Snell et al., 2024). Given a
100   problem statement, the generator produces a sequence of steps as actions. The verifier’s inputs are
101   the problem and solution steps, and it outputs correctness scores.
102   In general, offline Q-learning, which uses a fixed dataset to estimate Q-values, has the advantage of
103   being more efficient to train compared to online methods since gathering new dense labeled data is
104   difficult. However, it comes with the risk of overfitting the training data, as it lacks the exploration
105   of new actions typically seen in online Q-learning, and it causes an overestimation problem. Recent
106   methods involve Conservative Q Learning and Implicit Q Learning to mitigate the issue of overes-
107   timation, and this work tries to combine both approaches to overcome the overestimation problem
      (Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2022)

      3     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Multi-step reasoning, particularly for mathematical tasks, commonly employs a generator-verifier
111   framework to enhance LLM performance. Process Reward Modeling (PRM), which assigns rewards
112   to each step, has been shown to outperform Object Reward Modeling (ORM), where rewards are
113   only given for the final output (Lightman et al., 2024; Uesato et al., 2022; Cobbe et al., 2021).
114   However, PRM requires extensive step-level labeling. Recent works mitigate this with Monte Carlo
115   Tree Search (MCTS) for automatic labeling, improving efficiency over manual methods (Chen et al.,
116   2024; Wang et al., 2024b;a). Nonetheless, verifiers in these approaches are trained via supervised
117   fine-tuning (SFT), and compared to classic RL, it is similar to imitation learning.
118   Recent studies emphasize the role of verifiers in improving test-time compute. Cobbe et al. (2021)
119   showed that an effective verifier can yield performance gains equivalent to increasing generator
120   size by 30x. Similarly, Snell et al. (2024) found that optimal verifiers at test time can outperform
121   generators 14x larger. However, these methods still rely on SFT for training verifier, limiting their
122   effectiveness in complex, long-horizon reasoning. A more effective learning method can improve
123   the verifier model to achieve better performance and in turn scale more efficiently.
124   Q-learning has seen limited use in LLMs, mainly for preference-based tasks. ILQL is the first
125   to show implicit Q-learning can be applied to LLMs for multi-turn dialogue tasks, but the focus
126   is on token-level actions (Snell et al., 2023). To resolve the challenges in training long-horizon
127   reasoning due to the granularity of token-level actions, ArCHer proposed an utterance level value
128   function, but the encoder style of value function makes estimation can compute step by step and less
129   efficient(Zhou et al., 2024). Both works are still limited by their step-by-step computations.
130   In contrast, our work focuses on utterance-level actions for the verifier, and multi-reward estima-
131   tion with one forward pass significantly improves training efficiency. We also integrate Implicit
132   Q-learning (IQL) and Conservative Q-learning (CQL) to better manage large action spaces and en-
133   hance performance, offering a more scalable solution for multi-step reasoning tasks.

      4.1   O FFLINE Q- LEARNING VS I MITATION L EARNING :
142
143   The characteristic of MCTS rollout data in math problems is that it is noisy. There might be many
144   steps that are not optimal and incorrect for solving the problem. However, stitching the optimal steps
145   together might lead to a better solution. Those are cases that Offline Q-learning can handle better
146   than Imitation Learning, and one of the main conclusions from Kumar et al. (2022) is that given
147   the same noisy expert data, Offline Q-learning can outperform Imitation Learning on long horizon
148   tasks. Intuitively, Offline RL method should learn to stitch the suboptimal paths in the noisy data to
149   obtain a better path, and wrong answers can help offline RL what is wrong for the future. It could
150
      lead to the better performance of the verifier model even with the same amount of rollout from the
      generator.

154

155

158   tasks (Zhou et al., 2024). On the other hand, using the utterance level allows better handling of
159   long-term horizons, but since each utterance may contain multiple tokens, the action space grows
160   exponentially large, making it computationally intractable (Wang et al., 2024a). Given the utterance
161   with the number of tokens of length n, we will have V n actions, and a typical sentence might contain
      20 tokens. This is just one utterance, but a solution contains many utterances. This creates a tension

196   fixed dataset, making it unlikely to predict accurate values for arbitrary strings in an utterance. In our
197   preliminary experiments, we observed that changing just one critical token to incorrect utterances
198   can receive a higher value than the correct ones. This overestimation becomes more pronounced at
199   the utterance level, where the complexity and potential for incorrect value assignments are greater.

204   We introduce VerifierQ, a novel approach to enhancing verifier models using Offline Q-learning
205   for Large Language Models (LLMs). Our method addresses key challenges by modifying the Q-
206   learning algorithm and integrating it into language modeling tasks.

      5.1                 A RCHITECTURE OF V ERIFIER Q
209
210   Addressing Utterance-Level MDP: To apply Offline Q-learning to LLMs at the utterance level, we
211   propose a flexible architecture that integrates with language modeling tasks (Figure 2). Following
212   Wang et al. (2024b) and Lightman et al. (2024), we utilize two tokens + and − to represent correct
213   and incorrect states, with a tag token indicating estimation. The probability of the correct token
214   out of two tokens can be interpreted as Q-values ranging from 0 to 1, aligning with the reward
215   structure in the MCTS-generated dataset from (Wang et al., 2024b). More details are provided in
      the supplementary material Appendix A.1.

      5.2   A LGORITHM OF V ERIFIER Q

      Addressing Large Action Spaces: Traditional action selection in MDPs typically requires finding
238   the maximum Q-value explicitly over all possible actions. In utterance-level MDPs, this leads to
239   exponentially large action spaces of V n , where V is the vocabulary size and n is the length of
240   tokens in one utterance. To solve this, we employ Implicit Q-learning (IQL) (Kostrikov et al., 2022).
241
      Our key intuition is to interpret IQL from a regression perspective. IQL approximates Q-values
242
      through regression on existing actions, mitigating the need for explicit maximum Q-value sampling
243
      and enabling efficient handling of limited per-step data. Instead of iteratively finding the maximum
244   Q for every single action in V n , it can regress the action based on the dataset and find the approxima-
245   tion through expectile. IQL can still approximate the maximum Q-value maxa∈A Q(s, a) without
246   explicitly evaluating all actions by fitting Q(s, a) to the expectiles of the target values given limited
247   data. With the vast action space, expectile regression helps interpolate and extrapolate to unobserved
248   actions based on the observed data. This provides smooth estimates and helps handle the curse of di-
249   mensionality inherent in large action spaces. It improves sample efficiency by eliminating the need
250   for an online algorithm to sample from. This regression-based approach makes IQL particularly
251   well-suited for utterance-level MDPs.
252   We follow Snell et al. (2023) for the IQL framework to our setting, using the expectile of the Q-value
253   to approximate the value function V :
254                               LV (ψ) = E(s,a)∼D [Lτ2 (Qθ (s, a) − Vψ (s))]                           (3)
255
      where Lτ2 (u) = |τ − 1(u < 0)|u2 , τ ∈ (0, 1) is the quantile level, D is the offline dataset, Qθ is the
256
      learned Q-function, and Vψ is the approximated value function.
257
258   This formulation allows for efficient Q-value estimation without explicit maximization over all pos-
259   sible actions. Theoretically, as τ approaches 1, we have limτ →1 Vψ (s) = maxa Q∗θ (s, a) Kostrikov
260
      et al. (2022), ensuring that our IQL-based approach can asymptotically recover the optimal value
      function, even with large action spaces, given sufficient coverage in the offline dataset.
261
262   Our approach leverages regression to solve the large action space problem. By expectile regressing
263   the reward over utterances, we can find the approximation of the maximum Q-value and minimum
264   Q-value without needing to sample the action or iterate through all the combinations of tokens. We
265   focus on this regression aspect, not just the data support aspect. The approximation of minimum
266
      through τ value is shown in Theorem 2 of Appendix A.1 .

269   corporate Conservative Q-learning (CQL) Kumar et al. (2020) into our framework. CQL penalizes
      Q-values exceeding the target value, making the Q-function more conservative.

442   We investigate the impact of CQL parameters τ1 , which control the balance between conservatism
443   and optimism in Q-value estimation. Our analysis focuses primarily on τ1 while fixing τ2 at 0.9,
444   a choice guided by theoretical considerations of the CQL objective. The τ2 parameter in the CQL
445   term controls how much we push down overestimated Q-values toward the data distribution. Since
446
      our goal is to find arg max Q, we want the upper bound of the data distribution.

448   different levels of optimism by varying τ1 (0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) while fixing τ2 at 0.9 to tighten the
449   lower bound of VerifierQ to the maximum of the data distribution. As shown in Figure 5a, τ1 = 0.3
450   generally yields better results, suggesting it approximates the maximum Q-value more effectively
451   than other τ1 values. MATH dataset shows higher sensitivity to τ1 values, with τ1 = 0.5 performing
452
      the best. The difference in optimal τ1 values between datasets suggests that dataset-specific tuning
      may be necessary. It also suggests that more complex tasks (MATH) benefit from more conservative

      This work introduces VerifierQ, a novel approach integrating classical reinforcement learning tech-
517   niques with language models to enhance multi-step reasoning capabilities. Our key contributions
518   include: (1). A flexible architecture for applying Q-learning to utterance-level MDPs in language
519   models. It can estimate multiple utterances level Q values with large action spaces, and easy to
520   extend Q learning, IQL, and CQL. (2). An innovative formulation of Conservative Q-learning tai-
521   lored for large action spaces in language tasks. It helps to reduce the overestimation in offline Q

523   ing tasks. These results highlight the potential for extending this approach to larger language models
524   and improving test-time compute.
525   VerifierQ validates the integration of RL into verifier models and demonstrates its potential to en-
526   hance test-time compute results. Moreover, it bridges the gap between classic critic models in RL
527   and verifier models in language tasks. It serves as an addition for applying RL in verifier LLMs and
528   paves the way for actor-critic models to achieve more sophisticated artificial intelligence. As we
529   continue to refine and expand upon this approach, VerifierQ opens up new avenues for developing
530   more capable and robust AI systems across a wide range of complex reasoning tasks.

706   To apply Offline Q-learning to LLMs at the utterance level, we propose a flexible architecture that

708   we utilize two tokens + and − to represent correct and incorrect states, with a tag token indicating
709   estimation. The probability of the correct token can be interpreted as Q-values ranging from 0 to
710   1, aligning with the reward structure in the MCTS-generated dataset from Wang et al. (2024b). We
711
      compute the Q-value for each step as:

713                       Q(s, a) = p(+) = softmax(logit+ ) = σ(logit+ − logit− )                        (8)
714
      It is flexible to choose either softmax or sigmoid function to compute the Q-value. We use the
715
      sigmoid function in our experiments for more effficiency. The Q-value is computed for each step in
716
      the solution sequence, estimating a numerical value in the range of (0, 1).
717

719

720

724         3. This approach seamlessly integrates with existing language modeling tasks.

      A.4    C ONSERVATIVE Y ET O PTIMISTIC Q- VALUES WITH M ODIFIED CQL
791
792   Finally, we present a proposition about our modified CQL approach and its potential to lead to
793   conservative yet optimistic Q-values.
794   Proposition 4 (Modified CQL Bounds). The modified CQL objective with expectile levels τ1 (close
795   to 0) and τ2 (close to 1) aims to provide both lower and upper bounds on the true Q-function
796   Q∗ (s, a):
797                                max Qθ (s, a) ≲ Q∗ (s, a) ≲ min Qθ̂ (s, a)                    (21)
                                    a∼π̂β                                a∼µ
798
799   where ≲ denotes ”approximately less than or equal to”.
800   Remark 5 (Supporting Arguments and Intuitions). The original CQL objective is:
801                          arg min α(Es∼D,a∼µ [Q(s, a)] − Es∼D,a∼π̂β [Q(s, a)])                            (22)
802                                Q
803
804   Where µ is the target policy distribution and π̂β is the data distribution. Intuitively, this term finds the
805
      maximum Q-value under the target policy distribution Es∼D,a∼µ [Q(s, a)] and minimizes it since it
      is usually overestimated. To get a tighter bound, it pushes the Q-value up under the data distribution
806
      Es∼D,a∼π̂β [Q(s, a)].
807
808   For largePaction spaces, CQL typically uses importance sampling to estimate Es∼D,a∼µ [Q(s, a)]
809   with log a exp(Q(s, a)) at every state (Kumar et al., 2020). However, unlike token-level ap-
      proaches, we leverage IQL to approximate Q-values in the large action space. This mitigates the


                                                            15

810
      requirement to sample a set number of actions for each state and allows for more efficient Q-value
811   estimation for longer sequences.
812
813   We propose a novel formulation that directly approximates both the lower bound Q-function and
814
      the upper bound of the data distribution using IQL with different τ values for each term in CQL
      objective. The goal remains the same: finding the overestimated Q-value under the target policy to
815
      minimize it and tighten the bound with the data distribution. However we want to give control on
816
      the level of the tightening of the bound.
817
818   Our modified CQL objective is:
819                         LCQL (ψ) = α(Es∼D,a∼µ [Lτ21 (Qθ̂ (s, a) − Vψ (s))]
820                                                                                                    (23)
                                         − Es∼D,a∼π̂β [Lτ22 (Qθ (s, a) − Vψ (s))])
821
822
      The first term, with τ1 close to 0, approximates the lower bound of Qθ̂ . It acts as an upper bound on
823
      the target policy which is typically overestimated. This suggests:
824
825                                          Vψ (s) ≲ min Qθ̂ (s, a)                                   (24)
                                                       a∼µ
826
827
      The second term, with τ2 close to 1, approximates an upper bound on Qθ . It acts as a lower bound
828   on the data distribution, indicating:
829
830                                         Vψ (s) ≳ max Qθ (s, a)                                     (25)
                                                      a∼π̂β
831
832   This approach allows for a more optimistic Q-value estimation within the CQL framework. The
833   lower bound of the target policy µ pushes the Q-value down less aggressively, while the upper
834   bound of the data distribution π̂β elevates the Q-value more, resulting in a more optimistic Q-value
835   under the CQL term. This approach maintains the benefits of CQL’s conservatism while allowing
836   for adaptable optimism through the adjustment of τ1 and τ2 .
837
      The modified CQL objective aims to minimize the difference between the lower bound of the over-
838   estimated Q-values (Qθ̂ ) and the upper bound of the true Q-values (Qθ ). Minimizing this difference
839   may lead to a more accurate estimation of Q∗ (s, a). We can express this as:
840
841                               LCQL (ψ) ≈ min Qθ̂ (s, a) − max Qθ (s, a)                            (26)
                                                a∼µ             a∼π̂β
842
843   As this difference approaches zero, it suggests that the the lower bound of the overestimation of Q-
844   values is being reduced to the extent supported by the data, and we should have maxa∼π̂β Qθ (s, a) ≲
845   mina∼µ Qθ̂ (s, a). Adjusting τ1 we could have Qθ̂ (s, a) approximately close to the optimal maxi-
846   mum.
847
      This formulation allows us to balance conservatism with optimism in Q-value estimation. The lower
848
      bound of the Q-value is pushed down less aggressively, while the upper bound is elevated more,
849   resulting in Q-values that approach the maximum Q-value under the data distribution more closely.
850   We can adjust τ1 and τ2 to fine-tune this balance, allowing for more adaptable Q-values under the
851   CQL term.
852
      It is important to note that this difference can potentially become negative. A negative value would
853
      imply that the estimated lower bound of Qθ̂ is smaller than the estimated upper bound of Qθ for
854
      some state-action pairs. While this might seem counterintuitive given the general overestimation
855   tendency of Qθ̂ , it can occur due to the approximations introduced by the Lτ2 loss functions or other
856   factors in the learning process. This suggests that the value function might be correctly valuing the
857   in-distribution actions more highly, which is desirable, although it might introduce some pessimism
858   in the value estimates.
859
      This intuition provides insight into why our modified CQL approach might lead to a bit more op-
860
      timistic Q-values. However, a rigorous mathematical proof would require further development and
861   analysis.
