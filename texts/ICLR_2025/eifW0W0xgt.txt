009                                                         A BSTRACT
010             Self-attention performs well in long context but has quadratic complexity. Existing RNN
011             layers have linear complexity, but their performance in long context is limited by the
012             expressive power of their hidden state. Inspired by prior work (Schlag et al., 2021), we
013             present a practical framework to instantiate sequence modeling layers with linear complexity
014             and expressive hidden states. The key idea is to make the hidden state a machine learning
015             model itself, and the update rule a step of self-supervised learning. Since the hidden state is
016             updated by training even on test sequences, our layers are called Test-Time Training (TTT)
017             layers. We consider two instantiations: TTT-Linear and TTT-MLP, whose hidden state is a
018
                linear model and a two-layer MLP respectively. We evaluate our instantiations at the scale of
                125M to 1.3B parameters, comparing with a strong Transformer and Mamba, a modern RNN.
019
                Both TTT-Linear and TTT-MLP match or exceed the baselines. Similar to Transformer,
020
                they can keep reducing perplexity by conditioning on more tokens, while Mamba cannot
021             after 16k context. With preliminary systems optimization, TTT-Linear is already faster
022             than Transformer at 8k context and matches Mamba in wall-clock time. TTT-MLP still
023             faces challenges in memory I/O, but shows larger potential in long context, pointing to a
024             promising direction for future research.

045   1    I NTRODUCTION
046   Transformers have become the most popular architecture for large language models, but their cost per token
047   grows linearly in context length. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) have constant cost per token, but often
048   struggle to actually express relationships in long context (Kaplan et al., 2020). This difficulty with long context
049   is inherent to the very nature of RNN layers: Unlike self-attention, RNN layers have to compress context into a
050   hidden state of fixed size. The update rule needs to discover the underlying structures and relationships among
      thousands or potentially millions of tokens. This is a very hard compression problem.

051
      TTT layers. We first observe that self-supervised learning can compress a massive training set into the weights
052   of a model. Motivated by this observation, we believe that sequence modeling layers can be designed with the
053   hidden state as a machine learning model, and the update rule as a step of self-supervised learning. Because
054   the process of updating the hidden state on a test sequence is equivalent to training a model at test time, we
055   call these sequence modeling layers Test-Time Training (TTT) layers.
056
      Wall-clock time. While the TTT layer is already efficient in FLOPs, we propose two practical innovations
057
      to make it efficient in wall-clock time. First, similar to the standard practice of taking gradient steps on
058
      mini-batches of sequences during regular training for better parallelism, we use mini-batches of tokens during
059   TTT. Second, we develop a dual form for operations inside each TTT mini-batch, to better take advantage of
060   modern GPUs and TPUs. The dual form is equivalent in output to the naive implementation, but trains more
061   than 5× faster.
062
      Contributions. For self-containment, we discuss three levels of ideas in a single narrative: 1) Learning at test
063
      time and meta-learning at training time. 2) A practical framework to do the above with any neural network as
064
      inner model. 3) TTT-Linear and TTT-MLP as instantiations. Our contribution is only at level 2. Specifically,
065   when the hidden state is a linear model, our design coincides with prior work (Schlag et al., 2021), which is
066   further improved by concurrent work (Yang et al., 2024), as detailed in Subsection 2.6 and Subsection 4.2.

068   2    M ETHOD
069
070   All sequence modeling layers can be viewed from the perspective of storing historic context into a hidden
071
      state, as shown in Figure 1. For example, RNN layers – such as LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997),
      RWKV (Peng et al., 2024) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) layers – compress context into a state of fixed size
072
      across time. This compression has two consequences. On one hand, mapping an input token xt to output token
073
      zt is efficient, because both the update rule and output rule take constant time per token. On the other hand,
074   the performance of RNN layers in long context is limited by the expressive power of its hidden state st .
075
076   Self-attention can also be viewed from the perspective above, except that its hidden state, commonly known as
077
      the Key-Value (KV) cache, is a list that grows linearly with t. Its update rule simply appends the current KV
      tuple to this list, and the output rule scans over all tuples up to t to form the attention matrix. The hidden state
078
      explicitly stores all historic context, making self-attention more expressive than RNN layers for long context.
079
      However, scanning this linearly growing hidden state also takes linearly growing time per token.
080
081   To remain both efficient and expressive in long context, we need a better compression heuristic. Specifically,
082   we need to compress thousands or potentially millions of tokens into a hidden state that can effectively capture
083
      their underlying structures and relationships.
084   2.1 TTT AS UPDATING A HIDDEN STATE
085
      The process of parametric learning can be viewed as compressing a massive training set into the weights of a
086
      model. Specifically, we know that models trained with self-supervision can capture the underlying structures
087
      and relationships behind their training data (Le, 2013) – exactly what we need from a compression heuristic.
088
089   LLMs themselves are great examples. Trained with the self-supervised task of next-token prediction, their
090   weights can be viewed as a compressed form of storage for existing knowledge on the internet. By query-
091
      ing LLMs, we can extract knowledge from their weights. More importantly, LLMs often exhibit a deep
      understanding of the semantic connections among existing knowledge to express new pieces of reasoning.
092
093   Our key idea is to use self-supervised learning to compress the historic context x1 , . . . , xt into a hidden state
094   st , by making the context an unlabeled dataset and the state a model. Concretely, the hidden state st is now
095   equivalent to Wt , the weights of a model f , which can be a linear model, a small neural network, or anything
096   else. The output rule is simply: zt = f (xt ; Wt ). Intuitively, the output token is just the prediction on xt , made
097
      by f with the updated weights Wt . The update rule is a step of gradient descent on some self-supervised loss
      ℓ: Wt = Wt−1 − η ∇ℓ(Wt−1 ; xt ), with learning rate η. For now, consider W0 = 0.
098
099   One choice of ℓ is reconstructing xt itself. To make the learning problem nontrivial, we first process xt into
100   a corrupted input x̃t (details in Subsection 2.3), then optimize: ℓ(W ; xt ) = ∥f (x̃t ; W ) − xt ∥2 . Similar to
101   denoising autoencoders (Vincent et al., 2008), f needs to discover the correlations between dimensions of xt in
      order to reconstruct it from partial information x̃t . As shown in Figure 7 in Appendix, gradient descent is able

102
      to reduce ℓ, but cannot reduce it to zero. We discuss more sophisticated formulations of the self-supervised
103   task in Subsection 2.3.
104
105   As with other RNN layers and self-attention, our algorithm that maps an input sequence x1 , . . . , xT to output
106   sequence z1 , . . . , zT can be programmed into the forward pass of a sequence modeling layer, using the hidden
107   state, update rule, and output rule above. Even at test time, our layer still trains a different sequence of weights
108   W1 , . . . , WT for every input sequence. Therefore, we call it the Test-Time Training (TTT) layer.
109
      2.2   T RAINING A NETWORK WITH TTT LAYERS
110
111   The forward pass of a TTT layer also has a corresponding backward pass. Our forward pass only consists
112   of standard differentiable operators except the gradient operator ∇. However, ∇ just maps one function to
113
      another, in this case ℓ to ∇ℓ, and ∇ℓ is also composed of differentiable operators. Conceptually, calling
      backward on ∇ℓ means taking gradients of gradients – a well explored technique in meta-learning.
114
115   TTT layers have the same interface as RNN layers and self-attention, therefore can be replaced in any larger
116   network architecture, which usually contains many of these sequence modeling layers. Training a network
117   with TTT layers also works the same way as training any other language model, such as a Transformer. The
118   same data, recipe, and objective such as next-token prediction can be used to optimize parameters of the rest
119   of the network.
120
      We refer to training the larger network as the outer loop, and training W within each TTT layer as the inner
121
      loop. An important difference between the two nested learning problems is that the inner-loop gradient ∇ℓ is
122   taken w.r.t. W , the parameters of f , while the outer-loop gradient is taken w.r.t the parameters of the rest of
123   the network, which we will denote by θrest . So far, the TTT layer has no outer-loop parameters, in contrast to
124   other RNN layers and self-attention.
125
126   2.3   L EARNING A SELF - SUPERVISED TASK FOR TTT
127   Arguably the most important part of TTT is the self-supervised task, because it determines the kind of features
128   that W will learn from the test sequence. So how should we design this task? The final goal of TTT is for
129   zt = f (xt ; Wt ) to perform well on language modeling. Instead of handcrafting a self-supervised task from
130   human priors, we take a more end-to-end approach – directly optimizing the self-supervised task for the final
131   goal of next-token prediction.
132
133
      Concretely, we learn the self-supervised task as part of the outer loop. Starting from the naive reconstruction
      task in Equation 2.1, we add some outer-loop parameters to make this task learnable. In Subsection 2.1, we did
134
      not specify the corruption that produces x̃t from xt . One design is to make it a low-rank projection x̃t = θK xt ,
135
      where θK is a learnable matrix. Following the terminology of multi-view reconstruction, θK xt is called a
136   training view (Chen et al., 2020).
137
138   Moreover, perhaps not all the information in xt is worth remembering, so the reconstruction label can be
139   another low-rank projection θV xt instead of xt . Here θV xt is called the label view, where θV is also learnable.
140   In summary, our new self-supervised loss is: ℓ(W ; xt ) = ∥f (θK xt ; W ) − θV xt ∥2 . Lastly, the training view
141
      θK xt has fewer dimensions than xt , so we can no longer use the output rule in Equation 2.1. The simplest
      solution is to create a test view θQ xt , and change our output rule to: zt = f (θQ xt ; Wt ) .

      2.4   PARALLELIZATION WITH MINI - BATCH TTT
144
145   The naive TTT layer developed so far is already efficient in the number of floating point operations (FLOPs).
146

      in two places: before the minus sign and inside ∇l. Since ∇l contains the bulk of the computation, we focus
147
      on making this second part parallel.
148

150   gradient descent (GD). The general update rule of GD can be expressed as:

172   between speed and quality, as shown in Figure 9. We chose b = 16 for all experiments in this paper.

      2.5 D UAL FORM
174
175   The parallelization introduced above is necessary but not sufficient for efficiency in wall-clock time. Modern
176
      accelerators specialize in matrix-matrix multiplications, known as matmuls. Unfortunately, the TTT layer
      developed so far even with mini-batch still has very few matmuls.
177
178   Consider the simplest case of ℓ, where θK = θV = θQ = I, for only the first TTT mini-batch of size b. In
179   addition, consider f as a linear model. Copying Equation 2.1, our loss at time t is: ℓ (W0 ; xt ) = ∥W0 xt −xt ∥2 .
180   As discussed in Subsection 2.4, we can parallelize the computation of: Gt = ∇ℓ (W0 ; xt ) = 2(W0 xt − xt )xTt ,
181   for t = 1, . . . , b. However, we cannot compute all b of the Gt s through a single matmul. Instead, we need b
182   outer products to compute them one by one. To make matters worse, for each xt ∈ Rd , Gt is d × d, which
183
      incurs much heavier memory footprint and I/O cost than xt for large d.
184   To solve these two problems, we make a simple observation: We do not actually need to materialize G1 , . . . , Gb
185   as long as we can compute Wb at the end of the mini-batch, and the output tokens z1 , . . . , zb (see Figure 2).
186   Now we demonstrate these computations with the simplified TTT-Linear case above. Denote X = [x1 , . . . , xb ],

271   2.7 I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
272   Instantiations of f . We propose two variants of TTT layers – TTT-Linear and TTT-MLP, differing only in
273   their instantiations of f . For TTT-Linear, f lin (x) = W x, where W is square. For TTT-MLP, f MLP has two
274   layers similar to the MLPs in Transformers. Specifically, the hidden dimension is 4× the input dimension,
275   followed by a GELU activation (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016). For better stability during TTT, f always
276   contains a Layer Normalization (LN) and residual connection. That is, f (x) = x + LN(f res (x)), where f res
277   can be f lin or f MLP .
278
      Learnable W0 . The TTT initialization W0 is shared between all sequences, even though subsequent weights
279
      W1 , . . . , WT are different for each input sequence. Instead of setting W0 = 0, we can learn it as part of the
280   outer loop. Since outer-loop parameters are always denoted by θs instead of W s, we assign an alias θinit = W0 .
281   In practice, θinit adds a negligible amount of parameters comparing to the reconstruction views θK , θQ , θV ,
282   because both its input and output are low dimensional. Empirically, we observe that learning W0 significantly
283   improves training stability.
284
      Learnable η. The learning rate is usually the most important hyper-parameter for gradient descent, so we
285
      experiment with learning the inner-loop learning rate η in Equation 1 as part of the outer loop. We make η a
286
      function of the input token (therefore different across time) for additional flexibility. Concretely, we design
287   η(x) = ηbase σ(θlr · x), where the learnable vector θlr is an outer-loop parameter, σ is the sigmoid function,
288   and the scalar ηbase is the base learning rate, set to 1 for TTT-Linear and 0.1 for TTT-MLP. Alternatively, η(x)
289   can also be interpreted as a gate for ∇ℓ.
290
291
      Backbone architecture. The cleanest way to integrate any RNN layer into a larger architecture would be to
      directly replace self-attention in a Transformer, known in this context as a backbone. However, existing RNNs
292
      such as Mamba and Griffin all use a different backbone from Transformers. Most notably, their backbone
293
      contains temporal convolutions before the RNN layers, which might help collect local information across time.
294   After experimenting with the Mamba backbone, we find that it also improves perplexity for TTT layers, so we
295   incorporate it into our proposed method. See Figure 10 (in Appendix) for details.

414   4.1   M ODERN RNN S
415   Mamba is one of the many Structured State-Space Models (Gu et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Poli et al., 2023;
416   De et al., 2024). The hidden state in these models is a vector, similar to in LSTMs. For TTT-Linear or
417   TTT-MLP, the hidden state is a matrix or two matrices, therefore larger. In Figure 5, we find that TTT layers
418   can take advantage of their larger hidden states to compress more information in long context, where TTT-MLP
419   outperforms TTT-Linear, which in turn outperforms Mamba.
420
      Similar to TTT-Linear, RWKV (Peng et al., 2023; 2024), xLSTM (Beck et al., 2024), and Gated Linear
421   Attention (GLA) (Yang et al., 2023) also have matrix hidden states, which are inherited from linear at-
422   tention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020). Modern RNNs such as GLA use chunk-wise parallelism to improve
423   hardware efficiency, so tokens inside a chunk can be processed with matmuls instead of a cumsum. However,
424   chunk-wise parallelism does not change the expressiveness of the model, since all temporal dependencies are
425   still equivalent to a cumsum.
426
      In contrast, mini-batch TTT allows more complex temporal dependencies across mini-batches. Each hidden
427
      state Wt depends on previous W s within its mini-batch still through a cumsum, but depends on W s in previous
428
      mini-batches also through the gradient operator. As illustrated Figure 9 in Appendix, mini-batch TTT enables a
429   trade-off between expressiveness and hardware efficiency, since a smaller batch size b leads to better perplexity
430   at the cost of higher latency. This trade-off is a unique and important feature of TTT. As shown in Table 1, the
431   intermediate batch size b = 16 significantly outperforms b = T which is fully cumsum.
432
433   Concurrent work Mamba 2 (Dao & Gu, 2024) is similar to linear attention and TTT-Linear in that it also uses
434
      matrix hidden states. In fact, Mamba 2 is equivalent in output to linear attention with explicit forget gates
      (the scalars at in their dynamical system) and a different backbone (which is also different from the original
435
      Mamba backbone). Conceptually, the improvements from Mamba on linear attention are orthogonal to those
436
      from TTT-Linear (e.g. mini-batch and LN, as shown in Table 1), and can potentially be combined to produce
437   even stronger results.
438
439   TTT-Linear is also similar to DeltaNet (Yang et al., 2024), another piece of concurrent work. In fact, if we
440   take away non-linearities such as LN and set inner-loop mini-batch size b = 1 instead of 16, a TTT-Linear
441   layer is exactly equivalent to the sequence modeling layer in DeltaNet, and the update rule is known as the
442
      Delta rule (Schlag et al., 2020). Yang et al. (2024) takes advantage of this linearity and designs an alternative
      parallelization that is highly efficient. Comparing to DeltaNet, our method can instantiate any neural network
443
      as inner model and still maintain reasonable efficiency.
444
445   4.2   L EARNING AT T EST T IME
446
      The idea of learning at test time has a long history in machine learning (Bottou & Vapnik, 1992; Gammerman
447
      et al., 1998; Sun et al., 2020). More recently, the same idea has also been applied to natural language processing,
448   where it is called dynamic evaluation (Krause et al., 2018; 2019). The basic approach is to directly finetune a
449   language model on the test sequence, which often comes in the form of a prompt. Following the same spirit
450   as dynamic evaluation, Yoshida & Gimpel (2021) optimizes the next-token prediction loss of the test sequence
451   with respect to the entire KV cache of a Transformer.
452
453
      Next we discuss an especially relevant line of work in detail: fast weights. The general idea is to update
      the parameters of a “fast” model on only the most relevant data, as opposed to the conventional practice of
454
      updating a “slow” model on all data (Tieleman & Hinton, 2009). This idea has existed since the 1980s (Hinton
455
      & Plaut, 1987). TTT can be viewed as a special case of fast weights.
456
457   Prior work in fast weights usually avoids forming an explicit learning problem that optimizes some objective
458   on data. For example, the update rule of Hebbian learning and Hopfield networks (Hopfield, 1982) simply
      adds xxT (or some variant thereof) (Ba et al., 2016) to the fast weights given each input x. In contrast,


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


459
      TTT embraces the idea of formulating an explicit learning problem, where the test instance is the target of
460   generalization. Our update rule is also an explicit step of optimization.
461
462   The idea of fast weight programmers (FWPs) is to update the fast weights with a “slow” model (Schmidhuber,
463
      1992). Our inner-loop weights W can be viewed as “fast” and outer-loop weights θ as “slow”. Therefore,
      networks containing TTT layers can be viewed as a special case of FWPs (Kirsch & Schmidhuber, 2021),
464
      similar to how TTT can be viewed as a special case of fast weights. The FWP with the Hebbian update rule
465
      above is equivalent to linear attention (Schlag et al., 2021), therefore also to naive TTT-Linear with batch
466   gradient descent.
467
468   The definition of FWPs is very broad. In fact, all networks with some gating mechanism, such as Transformers
469
      with SwiGLU blocks (Shazeer, 2020), can also be viewed as a special case of FWPs. Recent work has been
      experimenting with FWPs for language modeling: Irie et al. (Irie et al., 2021) design “fast” networks with
470
      weights produced as output of a “slow” networks. Clark et al. (Clark et al., 2022) give a Transformer a final
471
      layer of fast weights, whose initialization is trained as slow weights. Our contribution relative to existing work
472   on FWPs, again, is formulating an explicit learning problem for the update, which enables us to borrow tools
473   from learning such as mini-batch and LN.
474
475   4.3 L EARNING TO L EARN
476   For decades, researchers have been arguing that learning to learn, also known as meta-learning or bi-level
477   optimization, should be a critical component of intelligence Schmidhuber (1987); Bengio et al. (1990); Thrun
478   & Pratt (1998); Lake et al. (2017). In prior work such as Andrychowicz et al. (2016), Finn et al. (2017) and
479   Metz et al. (2018), the inner loop learns from an entire dataset at a time instead of a sequence, so the outer
480   loop needs a collection of datasets or tasks. In short, the outer loop is “one level above” regular training. Since
481
      it is hard to collect millions of datasets, this outer loop is hard to scale.
482   In contrast, for TTT, each sequence itself is a dataset and defines its own generalization problem. The inner
483   loop is “one level below” regular training, so our outer loop is only another solution to the canonical problem
484   of supervised learning, instead of a new problem setting like generalization across datasets. Our outer loop is
485   “at the same level” as regular training. This makes our outer loop easier to scale.
486
487   5    C ONCLUSION
488   We have reformulated the canonical problem of supervised learning as learning to (learn at test time). Our
489   formulation produces an alternative conceptual framework for building what is traditionally known as network
490   architectures. Our next goals are longer context, larger models, and more ambitious inner models. Next we
491   outline some especially promising directions for future work.
492
      • Outer-loop parameterization. There are many other ways to parameterize a family of multi-view recon-
493     struction tasks, or perhaps a more general family of self-supervised tasks. It would be a big coincidence if
494     the first one we have tried turns out to be the best.
495
496
      • Systems optimization. Our systems optimization in Subsection 3.3 has been preliminary at best, and there
        are many ways to improve it. In addition, pipeline parallelism through time might allow us to process long
497
        sequences of millions of tokens on multiple devices together.
498
499   • Longer context and larger models. Constrained by our academic resources, we have not trained with
500     millions or billions in context length, which would also require larger models according to Figure 12. The
501     advantage of TTT layers should become more pronounced in longer context.
502   • More ambitious instantiations of f . When context length becomes longer, f would also need to be larger.
503     For video tasks and embodied agents, whose context length can easily scale up to millions or billions, f
504     could be a convolutional neural network.
505
      • Multi-level learning to learn. If f itself is a self-attention layer, then by Theorem 2 it can be interpreted as
506     yet another inner loop nested inside the existing one. In this fashion, we can potentially build many levels of
507     nested learning problems.

1051   with Mamba, Transformer has 19 blocks instead of 24. For TTT-Linear and TTT-MLP, their FLOPs are
1052   already close to those of Mamba, so we change the hidden dimension of the MLP blocks from 5504 to 5808

1054   Gradient checkpointing through time. By default, libraries such as JAX and PyTorch save the intermediate
1055   activations during a forward pass so they can be reused during the backward pass. However, for a TTT layer
1056   with W as hidden state, this default saves W1 , . . . , WT , which uses too much memory. With TTT mini-batch
1057   and the dual form, we still need to save (assume divisible) κ = T /b W s at the end of the mini-batches. A
1058   standard technique to save memory in this scenario is gradient checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016), which is
1059   usually applied through layers, but we apply it through time.
