                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Visual odometry (VO) aims to estimate camera poses from visual inputs — a fun-
010
               damental building block for many applications such as VR/AR and robotics. This
011            work focuses on monocular RGB VO where camera poses are directly estimated
012            from a monocular RGB video without IMU or 3D sensors. Existing approaches
013            lack robustness under this challenging scenario and fail to generalize to unseen
014            data (especially outdoors); they also cannot recover metric-scale poses. Several
015            methods have attempted to address these problems with priors from predicted
016            depth. However, especially on unseen data, depth prediction noise can drastically
017            degrade performance. We propose Robust Metric Visual Odometry (RoMeO), the
018            first method that can leverage (noisy) depth priors to enable robust VO and recover
019
               metric scale poses. RoMeO incorporates both pre-trained monocular metric depth
               and multi-view stereo (MVS) models to recover metric-scale, simplify correspon-
020
               dence search, provide better initialization and regularize optimization. Effective
021
               strategies ensure the efficiency and the robustness to prior noise. RoMeO advances
022            the state-of-the-art (SOTA) by a large margin across 6 diverse datasets covering
023            both indoor and outdoor scenes. Compared to the current SOTA DPVO, RoMeO
024            reduces the relative (align the trajectory scale with GT) and absolute trajectory er-
025            rors on average by 55.2% and 77.8% respectively (Fig. 1). The performance gain
026            also transfers to the full SLAM pipeline (with global BA & loop closure). Code
027            will be released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Visual Odometry (VO) estimates the sensor pose from visual signals. It is the core problem of many
      applications such as mapping, robot navigation, and autonomous driving. This work focuses on
033
      monocular RGB VO, where the input is only a monocular RGB video and no information from a 3D
034
      sensor or inertial measurement unit (IMU) is available.
035
036   Classical methods rely on hand-crafted features and explicit geometric optimizations to estimate
037   poses. The geometric optimization is executed either directly on pixel intensities (Engel et al., 2014;
038
      2017; Zubizarreta et al., 2020), or indirectly on matches between keypoints (Mur-Artal et al., 2015;
      Rosinol et al., 2020). Due to the instability of hand-crafted features, classical methods suffer from
039
      frequent tracking failures on challenging data with large motions or extreme weather.
040
041   Learning-based approaches (Wang et al., 2021; Teed & Deng, 2021; Teed et al., 2024) train end-
042   to-end systems for both correspondence search and pose optimization, which minimizes tracking
043   failures. However, these approaches have limited robustness, i.e., they generalize poorly on zero-
044
      shot data, where classical methods often perform better when they do not fail. Moreover, both types
      of methods lack mechanisms to recover metric-scale trajectories without 3D sensors or IMU.
045
046   A common reason for these drawbacks is the lack of priors. As a result, the joint pose-depth op-
047   timization suffers from local optima and cannot recover the metric-scale. Though previous works
048   have explored the use of predicted depth (Yang et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2023) to VO, the depth pre-
049   diction noise limits their robustness, making them often perform worse on challenging datasets than
050
      pure correspondence-based SOTA (Teed & Deng, 2021; Teed et al., 2024). To this end, we propose
      Robust Metric Visual Odometry (RoMeO), a novel method that can leverage potentially noisy priors
051
      from pre-trained depth models to enhance VO robustness and recover metric-scale poses.
052

      poses. Robust depth-guided bundle adjustment adaptively detects accurate monocular and MVS

      Classical methods use hand-crafted features to find correspondences and perform joint pose-depth
126   optimization. Indirect approaches (Mur-Artal et al., 2015; Rosinol et al., 2020) find correspondences
127   through feature matching and then minimize the projection error on the correspondences. Direct ap-
128   proaches (Engel et al., 2014; 2017; Zubizarreta et al., 2020) minimize the photometric error directly
129   without feature matching. A common drawback of classical methods is the frequent tracking failure
130   on challenging data with large motions or extreme weather.
131
      Learning-based methods train differentiable systems end-to-end on labeled data to avoid tracking
132   failures. Teed & Deng (2018) proposed a differentiable structure-from-motion architecture that
133   alternates between motion and depth estimation. Wang et al. (2021) incorporated camera intrin-
134   sics into VO and pioneered training on the large-scale synthetic dataset TartanAir (Wang et al.,
135   2020). Teed & Deng (2021) significantly improved prior works by introducing RAFT (Teed &
136   Deng, 2020) into VO systems and designing a differentiable bundle adjustment layer for pose-depth
137   joint optimization. Teed et al. (2024) sped up Droid-SLAM by sparse flow estimation and opti-
138   mization. The major drawback of learning-based approaches is the limited robustness to zero-shot
139   data, especially for outdoor scenes with challenging motion and dynamic objects. As a result, they
140
      can perform worse than classical methods which fail less in such cases. Meanwhile, existing ap-
      proaches cannot recover metric-scale poses without 3D sensors (depth, stereo, or IMU). Though
141
      several works (Tateno et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2023) tried to introduce predicted
142
      depth to VO, they suffer from performance drop due to the depth prediction noise, especially on
143   zero-shot data. RoMeO improves VO by introducing depth priors from pre-trained monocular and
144   MVS models to both the initialization and the iterative optimization of VO. Effective strategies are
145   proposed to ensure the robustness of RoMeO under (severe) prior noise, which enables consistent
146   and significant error reduction across diverse zero-shot data.
147
      Depth estimation. Monocular depth estimation aims to recover the depth from a single input image.
148
      Recent methods (Ranftl et al., 2020; 2021; Bhat et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024)
149   have trained models on large-scale data to enable zero-shot monocular depth estimation. Multi-view
150   stereo (MVS) methods (Yao et al., 2018; Bae et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022) estimate depth from posed
151   multi-view images. With accurate poses for the input images, they can recover more consistent and
152   accurate depth than monocular methods. RoMeO leverages both monocular and MVS models to
153   better initialize and regularize VO.

156   3    M ETHOD
157
158   As shown in Fig. 2, RoMeO maintains a frame graph on-the-fly during VO, which contains a sliding
159   window of keyframes. Every new frame is initialized with an identity relative pose and the pre-
160   dicted monocular metric depth from a pre-trained model. Given the current frame graph, RoMeO
161   jointly refines the poses and depth by alternating between flow estimation and differentiable bundle
      adjustment (BA), with robust guidance from both monocular and MVS depth.


                                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Inspired by the recent SOTA (Teed & Deng, 2021; Teed et al., 2024), each flow estimation step first
163   obtains an initial optical flow by projecting the pixels of each keyframe to the others using camera
164   intrinsics and the current pose and depth. This initial flow is then fed to a flow network that predicts
165   a residual flow and a confidence map. RoMeO leverages depth priors to obtain more accurate initial
166   flow, which simplifies residual flow prediction. Any flow model can in principle be used in this step.
167   Due to the wide adoption and the ability to perform dense reconstructions, we use a RAFT-style
168   architecture following (Teed & Deng, 2021). After flow estimation, 2 iterations of BA are performed
169   to optimize the pose and depth. RoMeO leverages monocular metric depth to initialize BA such that
170   it can recover metric scale poses without 3D sensor or IMU. Both monocular and MVS depth are
171
      further used to effectively regularize the BA objective. This process is repeated 6 times, resulting in
      6 flow estimation steps and 12 BA iterations for each new keyframe. Pose-only BA is performed for
172
      non-keyframes, with monocular depth initialization.

275   3.4   N OISE AUGMENTED T RAINING
276
277   The trainable parameters of RoMeO lie in the flow network. Besides benefiting BA optimization,
278   accurate depth priors effectively simplifies flow estimation since the initial flow obtained by pose-
279
      depth based reprojection would be more accurate. Empirically we can train the flow network without
      depth priors, and insert depth priors during inference to achieve non-trivial performance improve-
280
      ments. However, this naive strategy cannot fully adapt the trainable parameters to the prior-enhanced
281
      inputs. Specifically, our prior-enhanced poses and depth lead to more accurate initial flow, which
282   requires a much smaller residual flow during inference. Hence, we jointly train the flow network
283   with the depth-enhanced inputs.
284
285
      Since predicted depth can have (severe) noise on unseen data, we purposely maintain some noise
      during training to ensure the robustness to prior noise. Specifically, we first pre-train the flow net-
286
      work without any depth prior. Then, we fine-tune the pre-trained system with monocular depth
287
      initialization with a much smaller learning rate. MVS depth is not used during fine-tuning to ensure
288   a reasonable amount of depth noise.
289
290   The fine-tuning is done on TartanAir (Wang et al., 2020), the same simulation dataset used for
291
      pre-training. There is a strong domain gap between simulation and real data, for example, some
      TartanAir scenes contain extreme depth values rarely seen in real-world data (e.g., objects that are
292
      16km away). The monocular depth models often return heavily erroneous predictions in these
293
      cases, making fine-tuning ineffective. To address this problem, we align the scale and shift of the
294   predicted depth to the ground truth when the relative prediction error exceeds 20%. This strategy
295   minimizes the negative impact of domain shift, making the flow network adapt to more accurate
296   depth in practice. Meanwhile, only aligning predictions with large errors ensures that the fine-tuning
297   data still contain a reasonable amount of noise.
298
      With effective noise handling strategies, the depth prior in RoMeO significantly enhances flow gen-
299
      eration since the cross-view re-projection is much more accurate. As supported by Fig. 3, a strong
300   depth prior leads to much smaller flow adjustment during iterative refinement, i.e., reliable corre-
301   spondences are obtained much faster. A strong depth prior also helps BA to avoid bad local optima,
302   making it easier to find accurate poses. These advantages create a positive feedback loop, where
303   better depth leads to better flow and poses, which further enhance depth optimization.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
307
308   Implementation. RoMeO is implemented using a mixture of PyTorch and C++ following the of-
309
      ficial code of (Teed & Deng, 2021). To maximize the performance, we use separate depth mod-
      els/hyperparameters for indoor and outdoor scenes. For depth initialization, we use DPT-Hybrid
310
      with the provided scale and shift hyperparameters (scale=0.000305, shift=0.1378 for indoor and
311
      scale=0.00006016, shift=0.00579 for outdoor) (DPT). For MVS guidance, we use the corresponding
312   MaGNet (Bae et al., 2022) models for indoor and outdoor scenes. We set α in E.q. 2 to 1.75 and 1.5
313   respectively for outdoor and indoor scenes. Note that although the depth models/hyperparameters
314   are different, we only train a single VO system and use it for both indoor and outdoor scenes. During
315   RoMeO fine-tuning, we only use the outdoor DPT scale and shift to predict the initial depth, since
316   TartanAir is mostly outdoor. The improper scale and shift will be re-aligned with GT anyway when
317   they create large prediction errors. The initial learning rate of fine-tuning is reduced to 0.0001, with
318   other setups the same as pre-training. Both stages of noise augmented training require roughly 7
319   days on 4 RTX-3090 GPUs.

      5   C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   We propose RoMeO, a robust visual odometry (VO) system that can return metric-scale trajecto-
529   ries from monocular RGB videos without 3D sensors. RoMeO utilizes pre-trained monocular and
530   multi-view depth models as effective priors for VO. It adaptively selects accurate depth outputs to
531
      regularize BA, creating a positive feedback loop that enhances optimization robustness against lo-
      cal minima. Noise-augmented training is introduced to fully adapt VO networks to depth-enhanced
532
      inputs while maintaining robustness to prior noise. RoMeO generalizes to both indoor and outdoor
533
      data. It consistently and significantly outperforms SOTA across 6 diverse zero-shot datasets. Be-
534   sides improving the trajectory accuracy, RoMeO also enables more accurate 3D reconstructions.
535   The performance gain also transfers to the full SLAM system. In terms of limitations, RoMeO cur-
536   rently uses separate models/hyperparameters for indoor and outdoor scenes. An interesting future
537   direction would be to develop better depth estimation methods that are lightweight, and generalize
538   to both indoor and outdoor scenes with a single model and hyperparameter setting. We also create
539   a fast version of RoMeO by reducing the resolution and only initializing depth on keyframes. We
      believe these strategies can be further optimized with a more comprehensive study in the future.
