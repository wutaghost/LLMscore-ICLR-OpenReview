009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent work has shown that state space models such as Mamba are significantly
012            worse than Transformers on recall-based tasks due to the fact that their state size
013            is constant with respect to their input sequence length. But in practice, state space
014            models have fairly large state sizes, and we conjecture that they should be able
015            to perform much better at these tasks than previously reported. We investigate
016            whether their poor copying and recall performance could be due in part to training
017            difficulties rather than fundamental capacity constraints. Based on observations
018
               of their “attention” maps, we propose a structured initialization technique that
               allows state space layers to more readily mimic self-attention. Across a variety of
019
               architecture settings, our initialization makes it substantially easier for Mamba to
020
               learn to copy and do associative recall from scratch.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   State Space Models (SSMs) show promise as a potential replacement for Transformers (Vaswani,
026   2017) with substantially lower inference costs (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024). While Trans-
027   former memory grows linearly with the input sequence length, SSMs use only a constant amount,
028   compressing all the context into a fixed-size state. SSMs perform comparably to Transformers on a
029   variety of common benchmarks. However, recent research has highlighted a set of tasks on which
030   SSMs perform substantially worse than Transformers (Waleffe et al., 2024), particularly those in-
031
      volving copying or recall (Jelassi et al., 2024; Arora et al., 2024). This is perhaps unsurprising, as it
      is harder to recall from a compressed, fixed-size representation, particularly as its length grows.
032
033   Nevertheless, SSMs use relatively large state sizes in practice, and we wonder if their poor per-
034   formance on tasks such as copying could be due to training difficulties rather than fundamental
035   capacity constraints. We present a qualitative study of the failure modes of SSMs on the copying
036   task. In particular, we inspect the time-dependent linear transformation matrix of Mamba layers,
037
      which is analogous to the attention map of self-attention layers. We compare these layers to their
      counterparts in self-attention/Mamba hybrid architectures that successfully learn to copy, and based
038
      on these comparisons, we propose a structured initialization technique that allows Mamba layers to
039
      more readily mimic self-attention. Our technique makes use of the fact that state space layers can
040   be seen as a form of linear attention with a learnable, structured causal mask. We find evidence that
041   such linear-attention-like Mamba layers arise naturally after large-scale pretraining, suggesting that
042   this pattern may be fundamental to the recall abilities of SSMs.
043
      The proposed mimetic initialization allows Mamba to quickly learn to copy and do associative
044
      recall on up to 4× longer strings, and we show for the first time that SSMs can achieve 2×
045
      length generalization or more. Mimetic initialization is essentially compute-free, but we show it
046   is comparable to pretraining in allowing Mamba to learn to copy and recall. Our work helps to
047   better understand the capacity of SSMs relative to Transformers in practice and can assist in further
048   studies of their capabilities, which may have been underestimated by previous research.
049

053   unlike Transformers which can strongly generalize. Empirically, they find that Transformers (espe-
      cially with their proposed custom position embeddings) vastly outperform SSMs on copying, both

194   To better understand why Mamba often fails to learn to copy, we start by examining a small model
195   trained to copy 50-character strings. In Figure 1a, we can see that Mamba plateaus. Visual inspection
196   of its attention maps reveals that it has probably failed to learn an interpretable copying operation.
197
198   Attention enables copying To explore what Mamba might be missing to allow it to copy, we
199
      trained a hybrid eight-layer Mamba whose fourth layer is single-head self-attention. As shown in
      Fig. 2, this one layer enables perfect copying performance, both on in-distribution length-50 strings
200
      (dotted lines) and generalizing to length-100 strings (solid lines). The softmax attention head learns
201
      a sharp “look-behind” operation, constructing the paste string by directly attending to the copy
202   string, likely exploiting an implicit position embedding learned by the preceding Mamba layers. We
203   propose two initialization changes that allow state space layers to better use their state capacity.
204

208   the recent past, while the self-attention layer in Figure 2 can attend all the way to the beginning of

210   in the simplest time-invariant SSMs, the amount of history stored in the state is controlled by the
211   parameter A, whose initialization was crucial to the initial success of these models (Gu et al., 2021).

338   we investigate copying in stack order, as unpacking the compressed string in most-recently-added or-
339   der is potentially easier for SSMs. Unlike normal copying, baseline Mamba is able to fit to the train-
340   ing length, but it fails to generalize. Mamba with mimetic init fits the training length much faster and
341
      generalizes better, while the self-attention hybrid generalizes nearly immediately. The story is simi-
      lar for multi-query associative recall – mimetic initialization leads to rapid learning and generaliza-
342
      tion to twice the length. We also consider the sorting task, where tokens are sampled without replace-
343
      ment from a vocab of size 512. Surprisingly, Mamba with mimetic init does even better than self-
344   attention. Mimetic initialization results in large improvements for all synthetic tasks considered.
345
346   Is Mamba with mimetic init just linear attention? In Figures 2 & 4, notice that the mimetic
347   initialized Mamba layer tends to mimic the corresponding self-attention layer in the hybrid model;
348   the resemblance is clear for copying in normal and stack order. For associative recall, it is less
349   clear, but the Mamba layer looks significantly more like it could implement a induction-head-like
350   function than typical Mamba layers. Similarly, the interpretation is unclear for sorting, but the
351   overall structure matches. At a high level, it seems like Mamba attempts to learn an approximation
352   to self-attention, but has much less capacity and sharpness. Consequently, we ask if our initialization
353   merely turns state space layers into single-head linear attention layers.
354   In Figure 5, we present an ablation study where we replace the target Mamba layer in our copying
355   experiment with simple causal linear attention with various head dimensions. According to Eq. 12,
356   we may expect mimetic init to make Mamba layers equivalent to unkernelized linear attention lay-
357   ers with head dimension equal to the state dimension. Consequently, we compare Mamba with state
358   size 32 to linear attention with head dimension 32, which comes relatively close. We plot gener-
359   alization to 2× and 4×-length in Fig. 5, as the difference for fitting to the training length is small.
360
      Nonetheless, Mamba still performs somewhat better than linear attention. Linear attention perfor-
      mance depends on the head dimension, with dimension 8 severely underperforming Mamba and
361
      dimension 1024 barely exceeding the performance of 32. In contrast, doubling the state dimension
362
      of Mamba to 64 substantially improves generalization performance. We visualize the difference in
363   attention maps for the two operations; we can see that Mamba’s is perhaps sharper/more consistent
364   like that of self-attention. Combined with better performance on copying, we conclude that mimetic
365   init Mamba layers are not just linear attention, but rather a related and superior (for this task) non-
366   linear operation. The correlation between this “sharpness” and linear attention performance has been
367   exploited by recent work (Zhang et al.).

410
411       State dimension The copying ability of Mamba should be directly related to its state size, accord-
412       ing to Jelassi et al. (2024). This allows Mamba to more easily approximate self-attention-like maps,
413       as we saw earlier. We show this is indeed the case in Fig. 7a. Indeed, for baseline Mamba 2, perfect
414       copying at training length 50 is only possible for sufficiently large state size. However, if we use

416
          generalization (measured with the area under the curve) is far stronger – N = 32 with mimetic init
          achieves performance comparable to N = 512 with baseline init, a 16× improvement in the use of
417
          capacity. We show another view on this data in Fig. 7b; generalization length hardly grows with the
418
          log of the state size using baseline initialization, while it grows linearly only after using mimetic

525     7                       C ONCLUSION
526
527    We presented mimetic initialization for state space layers, a simple and closed-form technique to
528    greatly improve the copying and recall abilities of state space models. Mimetic initialization makes
529    state space layers mimic linear attention at initialization time, and also mimics the structure of state
530    space layers that contribute to copying and recall abilities in pretrained models. Our technique allows
531
       to estimate capabilities of SSMs more accurately, which have been alternatively over- and under-
       estimated in the literature (Jelassi et al., 2024; Waleffe et al., 2024). Using a better initialization
532
       such as ours may assist in developing new architectures starting from a smaller scale, allowing for
533
       better predictions of their full-scale performance, as is often done in practice in testbeds (Poli et al.,
534    2024). From a theoretical perspective, our particular construction may provide insights into the
535    tradeoffs between state space layers and attention, and may help to study the recall vs. non-recall
536    capabilities of state space layers. Improving the ability of state space layers to approximate attention
537    has already been noted in followup work to the original Mamba architecture (Dao & Gu, 2024), and
538    our initialization supports this concept. More broadly and together with previous work on mimetic
539    initialization, our work helps to better understand pretraining, to some extent disentangling its dual
       purposes of storing knowledge and serving as a good initialization.
