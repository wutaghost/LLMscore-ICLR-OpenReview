009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Requiring extensive human supervision is often impractical for continual learning
012            due to its cost, leading to the emergence of ‘name-only continual learning’ that only
013            provides the name of new concepts (e.g., classes) without providing supervised
014            samples. To address the task, recent approach uses web-scraped data but results
015            in issues such as data imbalance, copyright, and privacy concerns. To overcome
016
               the limitations of both human supervision and webly supervision, we propose
               Generative name only Continual Learning (GenCL) using generative models for
017
               the name only continual learning. But naïve application of generative models results
018
               in limited diversity of generated data. So, we specifically propose a diverse prompt
019            generation method, HIerarchical Recurrent Prompt Generation (HIRPG) as well
020            as COmplexity-NAvigating eNsembler (CONAN) that selects samples with min-
021            imal overlap from multiple generative models. We empirically validate that the
022            proposed GenCL outperforms prior arts, even a model trained with fully supervised
023            data, in various tasks including image recognition and multi-modal visual reasoning.
024            Data generated by GenCL is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/name-
025            only-continual-E079

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Continual learning (CL) has been addressing various domains across different modalities including
030   computer vision (Seo et al., 2024b), natural language processing (Wu et al., 2024b), and multimodal
031   learning (He et al., 2023a). But even the most existing methods (Wang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024a)
032   often rely on abundant well-curated human supervision. For example, obtaining 423.5k clean images
033   in DomainNet (Neyshabur et al., 2020) required 50,000 working hours to manually filter out outliers.
034
      In standard learning, where all training data are provided at once, the time allocated for data collection
035
      and preprocessing does not affect performance, since these steps are completed before model training
036   begins. In contrast, continual learning involves the continuous encounter of new ‘concepts’, which
037   can refer to classes, adjectives, and verbs in multi-modal tasks, necessitating ongoing data preparation
038   throughout training. Therefore, delays in preparing data for encountered concepts hinder the model’s
039   ability to quickly adapt to new concepts (Koh et al., 2021; Caccia et al., 2022). Consequently,
040   delays in data preparation, such as human annotation, could limit the applicability of CL method in
041   deployment, such as e-commerce recommendation systems and autonomous driving.
042   In recent literature, several alternatives to human annotation have been proposed. Madaan et al.
043   (2021) propose an unsupervised CL setup that eliminates the need for annotation. However, they
044   assume that the unlabeled data stream contains only data related to the target concepts, while in
045   real-world scenarios, unlabeled data often include irrelevant data, which can potentially hinder the
046   performance of the target concept (Halevy et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2023). As another alternative
047   to human-annotated data, Sato (2023); Prabhu et al. (2024) proposes the use of web-scraped data
048   for online learning. Although web-scrapped data presents advantages such as abundance (Xu et al.,
049   2024), diversity (Agun, 2023), and easy accessibility (Sun et al., 2018), challenges arise from privacy
050
      and copyright concerns (Zhang et al., 2023a), as well as inherent noise (Neyshabur et al., 2020),
      which significantly hinders the performance of continual learner (Kim et al., 2021; Bang et al., 2022).
051
052   To address those issues, we propose to leverage text-to-image (T2I) generative models for CL. Specif-
053   ically, we propose Generative name only Continual Learning (GenCL), which takes only concepts
      as input and trains on images generated by text-to-image (T2I) generative models based on the

      as follows:
090
       RQ1. Can generated data substitute manually annotated (MA) in CL setups? GenCL improves
091

092
         web-scrapped and MA data, respectively.
093
094    RQ2. How to ensure diversity of images generated from generative models? We propose HIRPG, a
095

096
097    RQ3. How to ensemble generated data from a set of generators? We propose CONAN, a data
098     selection method that accounts for the complexity of generated samples.

      2.1    C ONTINUAL L EARNING
103
104   Setups for Continual Learning. Many recent works propose realistic CL scenarios, such as
105   blurry (Prabhu et al., 2020; Bang et al., 2021), i-blurry (Koh et al., 2021), continuous (Shanahan
106   et al., 2021; Koh et al., 2023), and noisy (Bang et al., 2022) setups. However, they only focus on the
107   realistic data distribution of the stream data, rather than the acquisition of data for a new category, for
      which the model needs to be learned. Recently, C2C (Prabhu et al., 2024) used web-scraped data for


                                                                2

108
      continual learning, to address the high cost of manual data annotation and the difficulty in acquiring
109   real-time data for the target concepts the model needs to learn. However, web-scraped data present
110   several limitations, including privacy concerns, usage restrictions, and inherent noise, as highlighted
111   in Fig. 1. Please refer to Sec. A.19 for more comparison between web data and generated data.
112
113   2.2   DATA S ELECTION
114
115   For ensembling generated images from multiple T2I generative models, we consider data selection
116   methods that extract most essential samples to build a coreset from a larger candidate set. Formally,
117   from the candidate set T , these methods select a coreset V (|V | ≪ |T |), aiming to preserve as much
118   task-relevant information from T as possible (Shin et al., 2023). To estimate the informativeness of
119
      the candidates, several metrics have been proposed, such as gradient (Paul et al., 2021; Pooladzandi
      et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023), uncertainty (Coleman et al., 2020), influential score (Yang et al.,
120
      2022a; Pooladzandi et al., 2022), and distance (Xia et al., 2023).
121
122   Although these methods are effective at selecting coresets, many come with substantial computational
123   costs. Gradient-based methods (Paul et al., 2021; Pooladzandi et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023), which
124   aim to minimize the difference between the gradients of the training dataset T and the selected set V ,
125
      require a well-trained model on T , which significantly increases computational overhead. Similarly,
      the influence score-based method (Yang et al., 2022a) also requires significant computation due to the
126
      necessity of calculating the Hessian in the influence function, along with its iterative data selection
127
      process (Xia et al., 2023). In contrast, distance-based methods, such as Xia et al. (2023) and our
128   proposed CONAN, can directly leverage a well-trained feature extractor, i.e., requiring only model
129   forward passes for feature extraction, leading to faster data preparation time.
130
131   2.3   T RAINING WITH T EXT- TO -I MAGE (T2I) G ENERATIVE M ODELS
132
133   With the availability of robust generative models (Gu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Podell et al.,
134   2023), several recent studies have utilized synthetic data for training (Azizi et al., 2023; Tian et al.,
135   2024a; Zhang et al., 2024c; Tian et al., 2024b). Notably, (Tian et al., 2024a) demonstrate the positive
136   impact of using diffusion model-generated datasets at ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) scale for training.
137   To train a model with data generated by T2I generative models for a given concept c, concept-specific
138   prompts pc are needed. Ramesh et al. (2022); Jones et al. (2024) use the template "A photo of
139   a c", as proposed in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), to construct prompt for concept c. However,
140   Sarıyıldız et al. (2023) claim that using only the class name as a prompt (pc = "c") yields better
141   image generation than pc = "A photo of a c". To add more concept-specific context to pc , Sarıyıldız
142   et al. (2023) combine the concept name c with its definition dc from WordNet (Miller, 1995), resulting
143
      in pc = "c, dc ". Nonetheless, all these approaches rely on a single type of pc per concept, limiting the
      diversity of generated images despite the ability of T2I models to generate an unlimited number of
144
      images (Vardanyan et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024a).
145
146   To address the limited diversity in generated images, several prompt diversification methods have
147   been proposed. LE (He et al., 2023b) leverages a pre-trained word-to-sentence T5 model (Raffel
148   et al., 2020) to generate diverse sentences that incorporate class names. Furthermore, Sarıyıldız
149
      et al. (2023) integrates the concept’s hypernym hc from WordNet, along with a background scene b
      from the 365 scenes in Places365 (López-Cifuentes et al., 2020), resulting in pc = "c, hc inside b".
150
      In contrast to random background selection, Tian et al. (2024a) utilize LLM to generate a list of
151
      contextually appropriate backgrounds for the given concept c to create more plausible prompts.
152   Similarly, Hammoud et al. (2024) and our proposed diverse prompt generation method, HIRPG, also
153   employ an LLM-based prompt generator. However, while previous LLM-based prompt generation
154   methods do not account for the relationship between generated prompts, HIRPG minimizes overlap
155   between them by providing previously generated prompts as negative examples to the LLM.
156
      We review more relevant literature and provide extended related work in Sec. A.18 for space’s sake.

      {y1 , y2 , ...}, are provided in a streaming manner, while the prevalent online continual learning setups

      4     A PPROACH
171
172
      We propose a Generative name only Continual Learning (GenCL) framework to address the absence
      of data in the name-only CL setup. The GenCL framework is composed of four integral components:
173
      (i) a Prompt Generation Module ψ (Sec. 4.1), (ii) a set of Generators G (Sec. 4.2), (iii) an Ensembler
174
      ∆ (Sec. 4.3), and (iv) a learner fθ . We illustrate an overview of GenCL in Fig. 2.
175
176   When a new concept is introduced, for which fθ needs to be learned, a generator g ∈ G generates
177   images related to the concept. However, despite generative models being capable of producing an
178
      unlimited number of images, output diversity is often limited (Liu et al., 2023a; Sadat et al., 2023).
      To address this, we employ a prompt generation module ψ, which generates diverse text prompts and
179
      forwards them to the T2I generative models. Additionally, we further enhance the diversity of the
180
      generated images by utilizing a proposed ensemble approach that combines the outputs of a set of
181   generators G through ensembler ∆. Generated images are streamed to the learner fθ in real-time,
182   while a finite episodic memory is maintained to replay previously encountered data. Note that while
183   it is possible to generate data for past concepts in real-time without using episodic memory, we use it
184   for efficiency, as it helps reduce computational costs.
185
186   4.1   P ROMPT G ENERATION M ODULE (ψ)
187
188   Our pipeline ψ begins with the new concept as input and constructs a base prompt PB using the
189   template: ‘A photo of [concept]’ following (Shtedritski et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023). While this base
190   prompt can be used directly with the T2I generators G, generating images using a single prompt may
191   lead to limited diversity in style, texture, and backgrounds across the generated images (Fan et al.,
192   2024). To enhance diversity, we generate additional diverse prompts using LLMs.
193   A straightforward approach for diverse prompt generation using LLMs is to generate N different
194   prompts at once or to generate a single prompt N times, as in previous work (He et al., 2023b;
195   Hammoud et al., 2024). However, multiple inferences to LLM with the same input can produce
196   similar outputs (Zhang et al., 2024a; Skapars et al., 2024), despite the non-deterministic nature of
197   LLM (Song et al., 2024). Empirically, as shown in Sec. A.32, our observations indicate that using
198   these approaches often leads to many generated prompts with similar meanings, which may reduce
199
      the diversity of the generated images from T2I generative models.
200   To reduce overlap between generated prompts, we iteratively create new prompts that are distinct from
201   those produced in previous steps. Inspired by previous work that has shown improved performance
202   in solving complex problems by providing negative examples with positive examples in in-context
203   learning (Zhang et al., 2024b) and contrastive Chain-of-Thought (Chia et al., 2023), we incorporate
204   previously generated prompts into the LLM input to serve as negative examples. By presenting these
205
      previously generated prompts and requesting a new prompt that is distinct from them, we impose
      a hard constraint that effectively prevents overlap between the newly generated prompts and the
206
      previous ones. Formally, this process can be described as follows:

276   4.3    E NSEMBLER (∆)
277
278   When ensembling images generated by different T2I models, a key question arises: Do we need to use
279
      all of them? While large-scale training datasets have become standard for achieving state-of-the-art
      deep learning models (Zhao et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022b), training with massive data imposes
280
      not only computational burden and time complexity (Sharir et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022), but also
281
      significant energy and carbon footprint (Schwartz et al., 2020; Patterson et al., 2021). In addition, in
282   CL setups, prolonged training periods can hinder fast adaptation to new concepts (Seo et al., 2024a).
283
284   Therefore, we aim to select a coreset V from the entire generated data U, and train a learner only
285
      using V. Specifically, we select |Ui | (Ui ∈ U) samples from U for V to maintain the same training
      cost as using data generated by a single generative model while increasing the diversity of the
286
      ensemble set. A straightforward selection method for constructing V is to sample images from
287
      each generator with equal weights. However, surprisingly, this method degrades the performance
288   of models trained with ensembled images, even compared to those trained on images from a single
289   generative model (i.e., no ensembling), as shown in Tab. 3. This degradation occurs because the
290   equal-weight selection method does not account for the overlap between images, i.e., diversity.
291
      To enhance diversity in the ensembled set, we select samples positioned far from the class prototype
292
      in the feature space, i.e., difficult samples, since these images are less likely to overlap with common
293
      images compared to those that are closer to the prototype. To achieve this, we consider the class-wise
294   Mahalanobis distance (Mahalanobis, 2018), where a higher distance indicates that a sample is farther
295   from the class prototype. However, it only accounts for the class-specific difficulty, while the distance
296   from other classes can also affect the difficulty of samples. For example, consider two samples,
297   x1 and x2 , both belonging to class c and having the same class-wise Mahalanobis distance. If x1
298   is closer to the global prototype (i.e., the class-agnostic prototype) than x2 , then x1 may be more
299   challenging to classify as class c, since x1 is more likely to be confused with other classes. Therefore,
300   to select difficult samples in the ensemble set while considering for both class-wise difficulty and their
301   relationship to other classes, we employ the relative Mahalanobis distance (RMD) score (Ren et al.,
302
      2021). It measures the difficulty in classifying a sample into its corresponding class by comparing the
      distance from the class prototype with the distance from the global prototype (Cui et al., 2023). The
303
      RMD score for a sample (xi , yi ) ∈ U is given by the following:

348   Continual Learning Setups. We first empirically validate the efficacy of our GenCL by comparing
349   it with state-of-the-art methods in class-incremental learning (CIL) task setups. Beyond CIL setups,
350   we also assess GenCL in multi-modal visual-concept incremental learning (MVCIL). In MVCIL,
351   concepts to be learned (e.g., ‘ride a bike’, ‘kick a ball’) are encountered incrementally. To learn a
352
      concept, both positive and negative support sets are required, where the positive set contains images
      representing the concept, while the negative set contains images that do not. We consider two types of
353
      tasks that address the following queries: (1) What is the concept exclusively depicted by the positive
354
      support set? and (2) Give a query image, does the query image belong to the positive or negative
355   support set?. We refer to these tasks as CA (Concept Answering) and P/N, respectively. We provide
356   a detailed explanation of the MVCIL setup in Sec. A.1.
357
358   Models. We use ResNet-18 and ImageNet-1K pretrained ViT-base as the network architecture for
359   the CIL setup. For the MVCIL setup, we fine-tune the LLaVA-1.5-7B model (Liu et al., 2023b).
360   Specifically, following Ye et al. (2023); Dong et al. (2024), we fine-tune only the pretrained projection
361   MLP layers and LoRA adapters (Hu et al., 2021), keeping the LLM frozen for training efficiency. In
362   all experiments, we train a model with ER (Rolnick et al., 2019), which is a simple but strong CL
363   method (Prabhu et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2024a).
364
365   Datasets. We evaluate the domain generalization performance of GenCL in the CIL setup using
366   widely adopted domain generalization (DG) benchmarks: PACS (Zhou et al., 2020), NICO (Zhang
367
      et al., 2023c) and DomainNet (Neyshabur et al., 2020), dividing them into multiple discrete tasks.
      Each DG benchmark consists of multiple domain datasets, e.g., PACS includes four domains: Photo,
368
      Art, Cartoon, and Sketch. We selected data from one domain (i.e., photo domain) as MA data for each
369
      benchmark, to compare GenCL with the oracle scenario, which assumes that manually annotated
370   (MA) data are available for training. During the evaluation, we considered the selected domain as
371   the in-distribution (ID) domain, while the other domains as out-of-distribution (OOD) domains. For
372   details on the task splits for each benchmark, please refer to Sec.A.2 due to space’s sake. For the
373   MVCIL setup, we used Bongard-HOI (Jiang et al., 2022) and Bongard-OpenWorld (Wu et al., 2024a).
374
375   Metrics. We report AAUC (Koh et al., 2021; Caccia et al., 2022; Koh et al., 2023) and Alast , which
376   measure inference performance at any time and at the end of training, respectively. In MVCIL-CA task
377   setups, to compare model-predicted sentences with ground-truth sentences, we use CiDER (Vedantam
      et al., 2015), which measures the similarity between generated and ground truth sentences, while also


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      capturing aspects such as grammaticality, saliency, importance, and both precision and recall. Note
379   that for evaluation, we use the test set for seen categories up to that point in time. Please refer to
380   Sec. A.16 for a more detailed explanation of the metrics we used.
381

      (C2C (Prabhu et al., 2024), IE (Li et al., 2023b)), other synthetic data (Glide-Syn(He et al., 2023b),
384
      CHB (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023), SC (Tian et al., 2024a), LE (He et al., 2023b), CCG (Hammoud et al.,

388   integrate prompt generation baselines with our proposed ensemble method (CONAN) to showcase
389   the effectiveness of CONAN, as well as to provide a fair comparison with GenCL, which leverages
390   multiple generators for ensembling.

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532   Online continual learning represents a practical, real-world-aligned learning paradigm. However, the
533   assumption of having access to well-curated and annotated data in these scenarios hinders its real-
534   world application. To address the challenges arisen from using manually annotated and web-crawled
535   data, we introduce a unified name-only continual learning framework that integrates generators with
536
      the continual learner, termed ‘Generative name only Continual Learning’ (GenCL).
537   Within the GenCL framework, we propose an diverse prompt generation method (i.e., HIRPG) and
538   complexity-guided ensembling (i.e., CONAN). Extensive experimental validations demonstrate the
539   performance improvements achieved by both components within the GenCL framework, showcasing
      its effectiveness in both ID and OOD settings compared to webly-supervised and human supervision.

1137   A.1   D ETAILS ABOUT V ISUAL - CONCEPT I NCREMENTAL L EARNING S ETUP
1138
1139
       Beyond CIL setups, we also assess GenCL in multimodal tasks, such as context-dependent visual
       reasoning tasks, focusing on Bongard-HOI (Jiang et al., 2022) and Bongard-OpenWorld (Wu et al.,
1140
       2024a). These benchmarks are based on two desirable characteristics of classical Bongard problems:
1141
       (1) few-shot concept learning and (2) context-dependent reasoning. The former refers to the ability
1142   to induce visual concepts from a small number of examples, while the latter indicates that the label
1143   of a query image may vary depending on the given context (i.e., positive and negative support set).
1144   Specifically, as shown in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5, given a positive support set and a negative support set for
1145   a particular concept (e.g., "ride a bike"), we consider two types of tasks that address the following
1146   queries: (1) What is the concept exclusively depicted by the positive support set? and (2) Given
1147   a query image, does the query image belong to the positive or negative support set? We refer to
1148   these tasks as CA (Concept Answering) and P/N, respectively. In addition, we provide a detailed
1149   description of each visual concept reasoning benchmark.
1150
1151   Bongard-HOI (Jiang et al., 2022). Bongard-HOI denotes a concept c = ⟨a, o⟩ as a visual relation-
1152   ship tuple, where a, o are the class labels of action and object, respectively. Following Bongard’s
1153   characteristic, there are positive support set Ip and negative support set In , where Ip and In have
1154   different concepts. Specifically, if the concept of Ip is ⟨a, c⟩, In is composed of data with concept
1155   c′ = ⟨ā, o⟩, where ā ̸= a. As a result, images from both In and Ip contain the same categories of
1156   objects, with the only difference being the action labels, making it impossible to trivially distinguish
1157   positive images from negative ones through visual recognition of object categories alone (i.e., hard
1158
       negative examples). We provide examples of Bongard-HOI-CA & Bongard-HOI-P/N (Jiang et al.,
       2022) in Fig. 4.
1159
1160
1161   Bongard-OpenWorld (Wu et al., 2024a). In contrast to Bongard-HOI, which has a structured
1162   concept c represented as (action, object), Bongard-OpenWorld utilizes a free-form sentence as c to
1163   describe the content depicted by all images in the positive set Ip exclusively. Specifically, concepts
1164   are obtained by the annotators, who are instructed to write visual concepts by following a predefined
1165   set of categories. We provide examples of Bongard-OpenWorld-CA & Bongard-OpenWorld-P/N (Wu
1166   et al., 2024a) in Fig. 5.
1167   Note that since the input consists of both text queries and images (i.e., support sets and a query image)
1168   and outputs are sentences, we use multimodal large language models (MLLMs), such as LLaVA (Liu
1169   et al., 2023b), which connects a vision encoder with an LLM for general-purpose visual and language
1170   understanding. For further implementation details, such as the prompts we use, see Sec. A.3.

1906   two super-resolution models to reach 256x256 and ultimately 1024x1024 pixels. At every stage, the model uses
1907

1908

1909   supervised task, resulting in a cross-modal general language model (CogLM). This model is then fine-tuned for

1914   high-resolution images are refined through another iterative super-resolution module fine-tuned from CogLM.

1951
       In modern deep learning, the trajectory of advancement is heavily influenced by the exponential growth of
1952
       training data and the corresponding models trained on these vast datasets. Foundation models are typically
1953   exposed to datasets in the order of billions during training, obtained predominantly through web scraping
1954   (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2020; Kocetkov et al., 2022; Bain et al.,
1955   2021). Although web scraping is a cost-effective method to produce high-quality datasets, studies underscore
1956   issues such as potential biases (Foerderer, 2023; Packer et al., 2018; Caliskan et al., 2017), copyright, privacy,
       and license concerns (Quang, 2021; Solon, 2019), and the risks of data contamination (Dekoninck et al., 2024;
1957
       Li, 2023) or data leakage from evaluation (Balloccu et al., 2024).
1958
1959   As demonstrated in Fig. 1, we highlight the key differences between Manually Annotated (MA), Web scraped,
       and Generated data on six different axes: (a) Controllability, (b) Storage issues, (c) Usage restrictions, (d) Privacy
1960
       issues, (e) Acquisition cost, and (f) Noise. In this section, we aim to provide the definition of each of these axes
1961   and their corresponding implications on each type of data source.
1962
1963   Controllability. encompasses the ability to generate or acquire images with various contexts, backgrounds,
1964
       settings, and themes as desired. It pertains to the ability to obtain images depicting different concepts in
       compositions not commonly found in natural environments, as well as in domains relevant to the task at hand.
1965   Under this definition, we assert that the MA data exhibit low controllability. This limitation arises from its
1966   reliance on data captured from a finite set of scenarios or sensors, which inherently restricts the breadth of
1967   diverse settings where the concept can be observed. Web-scrapped data also suffer from low controllability
1968   for the same reasons. In contrast, the generated data have high controllability due to the ability of foundation
1969
       text-to-image (T2I) generators to produce diverse images for each concept through varied prompting.
1970   Storage Issues. Storing extensive data, locally or in the cloud, imposes additional costs, which can become
1971   impractical in environments constrained by limited total storage capacity. In addition, transmitting large,
1972   substantial data samples in a federated setup can face challenges arising from bandwidth and latency bottlenecks.
1973   In such contexts, depending on a large corpus of MA data becomes counterintuitive. On the other hand, both
       web-scraped and generated data present themselves as cost-effective alternatives for accessing substantial data
1974
       volumes without necessitating explicit storage expenditures.
1975
1976   Usage Restrictions. encompass limitations imposed on the use of images for training machine/deep learning
1977   models, typically due to copyright or licensing protections. These restrictions arise from various legal frameworks
       across different demographics, regulating, and sometimes prohibiting, the training of models on protected data
1978
       for commercial deployment. This challenge is particularly prevalent in web-scraped data, where the abundance
1979   of protected data may not be adequately filtered (Khan & Hanna, 2020). In contrast, MA data bypass this issue,
1980   as it is presumed that the data are filtered or obtained from a proprietary source with appropriate permissions
1981   during annotation. Notably, generated data offer a more advantageous position, as they do not necessitate such
1982   filtering and encounter limited or no usage restrictions, thereby providing a readily available solution to issues
       arising from data protection concerns.
1983
1984   Privacy Issues. may arise when data samples inadvertently leak or explicitly contain sensitive, confidential,
1985   or private user information. Examples of such images could include those featuring people’s faces (O’Sullivan,
1986   2020; Murgia & Harlow, 2019) or personal objects that disclose identity-related details, such as addresses or
       financial assets. Once again, web data emerge as the primary source vulnerable to issues stemming from the
1987
       use of private data, an issue extensively present in web-scraped data (Subramani et al., 2023; Wenger et al.,
1988   2022; Solon, 2019; Lukas et al., 2023). MA data are expected to be protected from privacy concerns due to prior
1989   filtering or explicit agreement on data usage prior to annotation. Using generative model for continual learning
1990   make it avoid storing real data in episodic memory, which can cause privacy concerns (Shin et al., 2017; Liu
1991   et al., 2024). However, generat
1992   Acquisition Cost. refers to the total expenses incurred in obtaining a specific number of data samples
1993   necessary to train or evaluate the learner fθ for a particular task. As emphasized in 1, MA data entail a substantial
1994   acquisition cost, primarily due to the expenses associated with densely annotating the data through human
1995   workers. This, coupled with the rigorous filtering process, makes MA data prohibitively expensive to acquire at
       scale. Although web data do not require such significant financial outlay for annotation, they do require intensive
1996
       filtering, which contributes to an elevated cost and poses a barrier to constructing large datasets solely from web
1997   sources. In contrast, due to the advantages in controllability, generated data boast a notably low acquisition cost
       for generating large and diverse datasets.

1998
       Noise. pertains to instances where data that are not related to a concept are erroneously labeled as belonging

2201   DuckDuckGo, using the publicly available querying tool4 to collect URLs. While C2C uses four search engines
2202   for scraping, we only use three search engines, i.e., Flickr, Google, and Bing, since ICrawler did not support web
2203   data scraping from DuckDuckGo at the time of our attempt on February 20, 2024. After collecting the URLs from
2204   each search engine, we use a multi-threaded downloader5 to quickly download the images, following (Prabhu
2205

       collection facilitated by the official API. Meanwhile, for Google and Bing, the download rate is slower, at
2206   approximately 100 images per minute on a single CPU machine. However, the download rate depends on the
2207   network conditions and the status of the API and the search engine. In C2C, the ensemble of web-scrapped data
2208   from search engines is weighted differently for each benchmark. For example, in the Stanford Cars benchmark,
2209   the weights are Google: Bing: Flickr = 5:4:2, while in the Flowers benchmark, they are 1:1:2, respectively. Since
2210
       we use different benchmarks compared to C2C, we select equal weight selection to ensemble web-scrapped
       data, i.e., Google: Bing: Flickr = 1:1:1, which is one of the most straightforward and widely used ensemble
2211   techniques (Shahhosseini et al., 2022; Ju et al., 2018).

       A.34     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
2577
       While GenCL replaces manual annotation by generating diverse images that only require concepts using HIRPG
2578   and CONAN, we acknowledge the limitations arising from the use of generative models. Here, we outline these
2579   limitations and propose future directions to address them.
2580
2581   Privacy and Copyright Concerns. Using generative models addresses privacy concerns associated with
2582   storing real data in episodic memory in replay-based CL methods by preventing data leakage from real data (Shin
2583   et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2024). However, generative models may also introduce privacy issues, such as the
2584   potential memorization of training data (Wang et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023). We believe that advancing
       privacy-preserving generative models (Xu et al., 2023; Chen & Yan, 2024) would effectively mitigate these
2585
       concerns.
2586
2587
2588
       Capability of Generation. GenCL, along with other generative baselines, depends on the capability and
       coverage of the generative models, thus there are limitations when it comes to completely new concepts that
2589   generative models have not been trained on. To generate unseen concepts, we can fine-tune generative models
2590   using real data from these unseen concepts. We believe that developing efficient continual training methods for
2591   generative models (Uehara et al., 2024), which require less real data and computational resources, will accelerate
       the expansion of GenCL to accommodate unseen classes.
