009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Deep neural networks trained in an end-to-end manner have been proven to be
012            efficient in a wide range of machine learning tasks. However, there is one draw-
013            back of end-to-end learning: The learned features and information are implicitly
014            represented in neural network parameters, which are not explainable: The learned
015            features cannot be used as explicit regularities to explain the data probability dis-
016            tribution. To resolve this issue, we propose in this paper a new machine learning
017            theory, which describes in mathematics what are ‘non-randomness’ and ‘regular-
018            ities’ in a data probability distribution. Our theory applies a spiking function to
019            distinguish data samples from random noises. In this process, ‘non-randomness’, or
020
               a large amount of information, is encoded by the spiking function into regularities,
               a small amount of information. Then, our theory describes the application of multi-
021
               ple spiking functions to the same data distribution. In this process, we claim that
022
               the ‘best’ regularities, or the optimal spiking functions, are those who can capture
023            the largest amount of information from the data distribution, and then encode the
024            captured information into the smallest amount of information. By optimizing the
025            spiking functions, one can achieve an explainable self-supervised learning system.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the past decades, deep neural networks have being brought huge success to a wide range of machine
032   learning tasks (LeCun et al., 1998; Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin et al., 2018; Goodfellow et al., 2014;
033
      Rombach et al., 2022; He et al., 2016). Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) revolutionized
      computer vision (LeCun et al., 1998), leading to groundbreaking results in image classification,
034
      object detection, and segmentation (Deng et al., 2009; Ronneberger et al., 2015). CNNs became the
035
      backbone of many applications, from medical imaging to autonomous driving. Also, recurrent neural
036   networks (RNNs) and their variants (Sherstinsky, 2020), such as long short-term memory (LSTM)
037   networks and gated recurrent units (GRUs) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chung et al., 2014),
038   combining with semantic embeddings, made significant strides in sequence modeling tasks, including
039   language modeling, speech recognition, and time-series prediction (Zhou & Xu, 2015; Graves et al.,
040   2013; 2006; Bengio et al., 2000; Mikolov et al., 2013).
041
      The introduction of attention mechanisms and transformers revolutionized NLP by improving the
042   handling of long-range dependencies and performance (Vaswani et al., 2017). Models like BERT
043   (Devlin et al., 2018) set new benchmarks in language tasks, driven by pre-training strategies widely
044   used in transformer models (Radford et al., 2018). Pre-training techniques, such as masked language
045   modeling (Salazar et al., 2019) and next-word prediction (Qi et al., 2020), have propelled models
046   like BERT, RoBERTa, ELECTRA, and T5 to excel across tasks (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019;
047   Clark et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020). This has paved the way for large language models (LLMs),
048   including ChatGPT (Kasneci et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), which excel in applications like customer
049   service and content creation (Ray, 2023).
050   All these models, supervised or unsupervised, pre-trained or not, are based on an end-to-end learning
051   process (Carion et al., 2020): The deep neural network is trained to map input data to corresponding
052   targets, which can be labels in a supervised learning approach or masked data in an unsupervised
053   learning approach. The loss, which is the difference between the neural network output and the target,
      is back propagated through the network to update the trainable parameters (Rumelhart et al., 1986).

054
      However, there is one drawback of end-to-end learning: All the learned features and information
055   during training are implicitly kept in network parameters, which cannot form explicit regularities
056   to explain the data distribution. Without explicit regularities and knowledge, deep neural networks
057   cannot emulate human-like abilities such as discovering new commonsense, generating ideas, or
058   making plans driven by specific objectives (An et al., 2019; Sweller, 2009; Davis & Marcus, 2015).
059
      But what are regularities in a data distribution? Or, what differs data samples from random noises?
060
      In this paper, we establish a theory to describe ‘regularities’ and ‘non-randomness’ in mathematics,
061   which is potentially applicable to build an explainable self-supervised machine learning system.
062
063   The contribution of this paper is the proposal of such a theory, which encompasses two aspects:
064   1. Based on spiking functions, we define in mathematics what is non-random information, or ‘non-
065   randomness’, in a data probability distribution. Then, based on information theory, we describe that
066   regularities are a small amount of information encoding a large amount of non-random information.
067
      2. We apply multiple spiking functions to the same data probability distribution. Then, we mathemat-
068   ically describe that optimal regularities are learned by spiking functions when the maximum amount
069   of information is represented in the most concise way, or equivalently, encoded into the smallest
070   possible amount of information. We use simple examples to show that when these optimal spiking
071   functions are achieved, their spiking behaviors become explainable regarding the data probability
072   distribution, and hence the system becomes an explainable self-supervised learning system.
073
      We acknowledge that implementation models for our theory have not yet been developed. So, there
074   are no experimental results presented in this paper. In the rest of this paper, we will first briefly
075   introduce some related works in Section 2. Then, we describe our theory in Section 3. Finally, we
076   conclude with a summary of this paper in Section 4.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
080
      Our work is unique since it presents a theory that defines in mathematics what are non-randomness
081
      and regularities in a data probability distribution, which can be potentially applied to practical
082   machine learning tasks. To the edge of our knowledge, this is the first work presenting such a theory.
083   But our theory indeed depends on spiking functions (or spiking neural networks in practice) and
084   information theory.
085
      Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have gained attention for their ability to closely mimic brain
086
      processes compared to traditional neural networks (Brown et al., 2004; y Arcas & Fairhall, 2003).
087
      Spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), introduced by (Debanne & Inglebert, 2023), offers a
088   key learning rule where synaptic strengths adjust based on spike timing. (Maass et al., 2002) then
089   proposed the Liquid State Machine, showing how dynamic neural circuits process information.
090   (Gütig & Sompolinsky, 2006) advanced this with the Tempotron model, where neurons learn to
091   discriminate spatiotemporal spike patterns. Finally, (Sengupta et al., 2019) discussed converting
092   traditional networks to SNNs, enabling energy-efficient implementations on neuromorphic hardware.
093   Combining all these works, in our theory, given a vector X as the input to a function f , we regard f
094   to spike on X if f (X) > 0. This is a very simple setting comparing to many spiking neural networks.
095   Information theory, foundational to modern communication and data science, was pioneered by
096   Claude E. Shannon in his paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication (Shannon, 1948). Shannon
097   introduced key concepts like entropy, which measures uncertainty, and mutual information, which
098   quantifies the information gained about one variable from another. Subsequent influential works
099   include the Viterbi algorithm by (Viterbi, 1967), critical for error-correcting codes, and (Slepian
100   & Wolf, 1973), which explored trade-offs between compression and fidelity in data transmission.
101   (Fitts, 1954) also applied information theory to human-computer interaction. Based on the solid
102
      ground established by these pioneered works, our work applies information theory to describe
      non-randomness and regularities in the data distribution discovered by spiking functions.

200   3.2   BASIC C ONCEPTS
201
202   We assume by default in this paper that the vector space X is either real or complex (i.e., X = Rm
203
      or X = Cm with some finite integer m). Then, we adopt the Euclidean distance as the metric on X
      (Gower, 1985), which defines the distance d(X, Y ) between any two vectors X, Y ∈ X. A little bit
204
      more detailed discussion can be found in Appendix A.
205
206   Then, we use the Lebesgue measure (Bartle, 2014) to define the ‘volume’ of a subset E ⊂ X. The
207   Lebesgue measure is a regular choice to measure volumes of subsets within a finite-dimensional
208   real or complex vector space (Ciesielski, 1989), which coincides with our usual understanding of
209
      volume. Say, when X = R3 , the Lebesgue measure of a sphere with radius r is 34 πr3 . A more
      detailed definition on Lebesgue measure is provided in Appendix A.
210
211   With these concepts, we can then evaluate the spiking efficiency, conciseness and ability of a function
212   in a straightforward way. That is, we will first have:
213   Definition 3. Suppose X is a finite-dimensional real or complex vector space, and suppose S ⊂ X
214   is a bounded sub-region in X. Suppose f : S → R is a function defined on S. We define the spiking
215   region of f , denoted by Sf , to be the sub-region in S consisting of the vectors that f spikes on. That
      is, Sf = {X ∈ S|f (X) > 0}.

      Also, we say that two spiking regions are distinct, if there exists a vector X ∈ S that belongs to one
217   spiking region but does not belong to the other.
218
219   Obviously, there is an unique spiking region Sf to any function f . We will work with many types of
220   spiking regions in the rest of this paper. We note that spiking region is only an analytical tool we
221   created in the theory. In practice, it is very difficult and not meaningful to accurately measure the
222   spiking region of a function, especially in high dimensional data spaces.
223   When function f is continuous regarding the metric on the vector space X, we have the following
224   lemma to guarantee that Sf is Lebesgue-measurable (i.e., the region indeed has a determined volume
225   under Lebesgue measure):
226   Lemma 1. Suppose X is a finite-dimensional real or complex vector space, and suppose S ⊂ X is a
227   bounded sub-region in X. Suppose f : S → R is a continuous function defined on S (i.e., for any
228   ϵ > 0, there exists a δ > 0 such that for any X, Y ∈ S, d(X, Y ) < δ implies |f (X) − f (Y )| < ϵ).
229   Then, the spiking region Sf = {X ∈ S|f (X) > 0} is always Lebesgue-measurable.
230
231   The discussion on Lebesgue-measurable/non-measurable sets as well as the proof of this lemma are
232   provided in Appendix A. Lebesgue non-measurable sets indeed exist in a finite-dimensional vector
233
      space. However, a non-measurable set is usually extremely complicated. The Vitali set (Kharazishvili,
      2011) is a famous non-measurable set in R, which is discussed in Appendix A. To be specific, we
234
      believe that any function f with a finite size cannot possess a non-measurable spiking region. That is,
235
236

      is a bounded sub-region in X. Suppose f : S → R is a function defined on S with a finite size (i.e.,
237
      there are finite adjustable parameters in f ). Then, the spiking region Sf = {X ∈ S|f (X) > 0} is
238
      always Lebesgue-measurable.

520   4    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we establish a theory on learning regularities from data using spiking functions.
523   Throughout this paper, the key to our theory is comparing the spiking behavior of the function on
524   data samples and random samples. We say that a function f discovers non-randomness from the
525   data probability distribution, if the spiking frequency of f on data samples differs significantly from
526   that of f on random samples. Then, taking the size of function f into consideration, we claim that
527   f learns regularities from the data distribution if f discovers non-randomness using a small size
528   (or equivalently, a concise format). Finally, by referring to information theory, we propose that
529   regularities can be regarded as a small amount of information encoding a large amount of information.
530
      Non-randomness is essentially valuable information in the data distribution.
531   After that, we apply multiple spiking functions to the same data distribution in order to learn the
532   optimal regularities. We demonstrate that the optimal regularities shall capture the largest amount of
533   information from the data distribution, and encode it into the smallest amount of information. We
534   call the corresponding sequence of functions an optimal encoder to the data distribution. Numerical
535   examples show that an explainable self-supervised learning system can be achieved by making the
536
      sequence of functions converge to an optimal encoder. That is, essentially, an explainable self-
      supervised learning system can be achieved by encoding the largest amount of information possible
537
      into the smallest amount of information possible.
538
539   In the future, realizing our theory by valid optimization algorithms and appropriate deep neural
      networks is the priority of our research.

782   Then, here is a more detailed discussion on non-measurable sets:
783   Non-measurable sets indeed exist within both real and complex vector spaces when considering the
784   Lebesgue measure. A famous example is the Vitali set (Kharazishvili, 2011) defined on the closed
785   interval [0, 1]: Given two real numbers x, y ∈ [0, 1], we say that x is equivalent to y (denoted as
786   x ∼ y) if x − y is a rational number. Then, for any x ∈ [0, 1], its equivalence class is defined as
787   Cx = {y ∈ [0, 1]|x ∼ y}. In this way, the closed interval [0, 1] can be partitioned into disjoint
788   equivalence classes. Finally, from each equivalence class, we choose exactly one representative. This
789   collection of representatives forms a Vitali set V on [0, 1]. It can be proved that a Vitali set is not
790   measurable under Lebesgue measure (Halmos, 2013). Intuitively, this means that there is no way to
791
      evaluate the volume of a Vitali set.
792   However, imagine that we have a function f : [0, 1] → R that has its spiking region Sf to be a Vitali
793   set. Intuitively, this will be extremely difficult: For any real number x ∈ [0, 1], there is exactly one
794   number v in a Vitali set V such that x − v is rational. By definition, v is the representative of x in
795   the equivalence class. This means that we can select at will uncountable many irrational numbers in
796   [0, 1] and obtain their corresponding representatives in V , which implies the extreme ‘chaotic’ of V .
797
      One can imagine how difficult it is for a function f to map every number in a Vitali set to a positive
      value, while mapping all the other real numbers in [0, 1] to negative values. In fact, we believe that
798
      it is impossible for f to achieve this with a finite size |f |. Taking one step further, we believe that
799
      the same discussion can be applied to any non-measurable set on a finite-dimensional vector space,
800   which leads to Hypothesis 1 (presented both here and in Section 3.2):
801
      Hypothesis 1. Suppose X is a finite-dimensional real or complex vector space, and suppose S ⊂ X
802
      is a bounded sub-region in X. Suppose f : S → R is a function defined on S with a finite size (i.e.,
803   there are finite adjustable parameters in f ). Then, the spiking region Sf = {X ∈ S|f (X) > 0} is
804   always Lebesgue-measurable.

       Again, the size of a mapping M is defined to be the number of adjustable parameters in M, which is
       essentially equivalent to the minimum amount of information required to describe M unambiguously.
1071
       And again, the size of an optimal encoder f † represents the minimum amount of information required
1072
       to describe its spiking region Sf † unambiguously. So, intuitively, a sub-region S2 ⊂ S is irrelevant to
1073   S1 ⊂ S, if knowing the information required to describe S1 will not reduce the amount of information
1074   required to describe S2 .

       correct hypothesis. We say that two sub-regions S1 and S2 are mutually irrelevant, if S2 is irrelevant

       to S1 and S1 is irrelevant to S2 .
