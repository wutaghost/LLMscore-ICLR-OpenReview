009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-training has exhibited notable benefits to downstream tasks by boosting ac-
012            curacy and speeding up convergence, but the exact reasons for these benefits still
013            remain unclear. To this end, we propose to quantitatively and explicitly explain
014            effects of pre-training on the downstream task from a novel game-theoretic view,
015            which also sheds new light into the learning behavior of deep neural networks
016
               (DNNs). Specifically, we extract and quantify the knowledge encoded by the pre-
               trained model, and further track the changes of such knowledge during the fine-
017
               tuning process. Interestingly, we discover that only a small amount of pre-trained
018
               model’s knowledge is preserved for the inference of downstream tasks. However,
019            such preserved knowledge is very challenging for a model training from scratch
020            to learn. Thus, with the help of this exclusively learned and useful knowledge, the
021            model fine-tuned from pre-training usually achieves better performance than the
022            model training from scratch. Besides, we discover that pre-training can guide the
023            fine-tuned model to learn target knowledge for the downstream task more directly
024            and quickly, which accounts for the faster convergence of the fine-tuned model.
025            The code will be released when the paper is accepted.

      Pre-training is prevalent in nowadays deep learning, as it has brought great benefits to downstream
030   tasks, including improving the accuracy (He et al., 2016; Devlin et al., 2019), boosting the ro-
031   bustness (Hendrycks et al., 2019), speeding up the convergence (Nguyen et al., 2023), and etc.
032   Naturally, a fundamental question arises: why pre-training is beneficial for downstream tasks?
033   Previous works have tried to answer this question from different perspectives. For example, Zan
034   et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Neyshabur et al. (2020) attributed the benefits of pre-training to
035   a flat loss landscape. Erhan et al. (2010) concluded that the improved accuracy was a result of
036   unsupervised pre-training acting as a regularizer.
037   Unlike above perspectives for explanations, we aim to present an in-depth analysis to answer the
038   above question from a new perspective. That is, we quantify the knowledge encoded by the pre-
039   trained model, and further analyze the effects of such knowledge on the downstream tasks. In this
040   way, we can provide insightful and accurate explanations for the benefits brought by pre-training,
041   which also sheds new light into the fine-tuning/learning behavior of DNNs.
042   To this end, we extract the knowledge encoded in the pre-trained model based on the interaction
043   between different input variables (Ren et al., 2023a; Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2024), because
044   the DNN usually lets different input variables interact with each other to construct concepts for in-
045   ference, rather than utilize each single variable for inference independently. As Fig. 1(a) shows,
046   the DNN encodes the co-appearance relationship (interaction) between different image patches in
047   S = {mouth, ear, eye} of the input image x to form the dog face concept S for inference. Only
048   when all three patches in S are all present, the interaction is activated and makes a numerical con-
049   tribution I(S|x) to the network output y. The absence/masking1 of any image patch will deactivate
050
      the interaction, and the numerical contribution is removed, i.e., I(S|x) = 0.
051   More crucially, Ren et al. (2023a); Li & Zhang (2023) have empirically verified and Ren et al.
052   (2024) have theoretically proven the sparsity property and the universal-matching property of
053   interactions, i.e., given an input sample x, a well-trained DNN usually encodes a small number of
      interactions between different input variables, and the network output y can be well explained as the

      2     R ELATED WORK
114
      Explanation of pre-training. Fine-tuning pre-trained models on downstream tasks to speed up
115
      convergence and boost performance has become a conventional practice in deep learning (He et al.,
116
      2016; Devlin et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2023). Many works have attempted
117   to analyze why pre-training is beneficial for downstream tasks from different perspectives. Specifi-
118   cally, Erhan et al. (2010) discovered that the unsupervised pre-training acted as a regularizer, which
119   improved the generalization power of the DNN. Alternatively, a lot of studies explained the high
120   accuracy (Zan et al., 2022; Neyshabur et al., 2020), the fast convergence speed in federated learn-
121   ing (Nguyen et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), and the reduced catastrophic forgetting in continual
122   learning (Mehta et al., 2023) of the fine-tuned models from the perspective of a flat loss landscape.
123   Additionally, Chen et al. (2024); Deng et al. (2023) explained the transferability of the pre-trained
124   model to downstream tasks from the perspective of the feature space by performing the singular
125
      value decomposition. In comparison, we present a comprehensive analysis to systematically unveil
      the essential reasons behind different benefits of pre-training, by quantifying the explicit effects of
126
      pre-trained model’s knowledge on the downstream task from a game-theoretic perspective.
127
128   Using interactions to explain the DNN. In recent years, employing game-theoretic interactions to
129   explain DNNs has become a newly emerging direction. Specifically, Sundararajan et al. (2020);
130   Tsai et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2024) quantified interactions between different input variables to
131
      formulate the knowledge encoded by a DNN, whose faithfulness was further experimentally verified
      and theoretically ensured by (Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2023a; 2024). Besides, a series of studies
132
      utilized the interaction to explain the representation capacity of DNNs, including the generalization
133
      power (Zhang et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), adversarial robustness (Ren et al.,
134   2021), adversarial transferability (Wang et al., 2021), the learning difficulty of interactions (Liu et al.,
135   2023; Ren et al., 2023b), and the representation bottleneck (Deng et al., 2022). In comparison, this
136   paper aims to provide insightful explanations for the benefits of pre-training to downstream tasks.
137
      Quantifying the knowledge encoded by the DNN. So far, there does not exist a formal and widely
138
      accepted method to quantify the knowledge encoded by a DNN. A series of studies (Shwartz-Ziv
139
      & Tishby, 2017; Saxe et al., 2018; Higgins et al., 2017) employed the mutual information between
140   input variables and the network output to quantify the knowledge in the DNN, but precisely mea-
141   suring the mutual information was still significantly challenging (Kolchinsky et al., 2019). Besides,
142   other studies employed human-annotated semantic concepts (Bau et al., 2017; Fong & Vedaldi,
143   2018) or automatically learned concepts (Chen et al., 2019) to explain the knowledge in the DNN,
144   but these works could not quantify the exact changes of knowledge (i.e., the preservation of task-
145   relevant knowledge and the discarding of task-irrelevant knowledge) during the fine-tuning/training
146   procedure. In comparison, we use theoretically verifiable interactions to represent knowledge in the
147   DNN, which enables us to explicitly quantify the exact effects of pre-trained model’s knowledge on
148
      the downstream task, so as to provide detailed explanations for the benefits of pre-training.

222   3.2     Q UANTIFYING THE EFFECTS OF PRE - TRAINING ON DOWNSTREAM TASKS
223
224   Despite the ubiquitous utilization and great success of pre-trained models, it still remains mysterious
225   why such models can help the fine-tuned model achieve superior classification performance and
226   converge faster5 , compared to training from scratch. Thus, to systematically and precisely unveil
227
      the reasons behind these two benefits, we propose several metrics based on interactions to explicitly
      quantify the knowledge of the pre-trained model that is utilized for the inference of the downstream
228
      task, and further explain effects of such knowledge on the fine-tuning process. These explanations
229
      also provide some new insights into the learning/fine-tuning behavior of the DNN.
230
231   3.2.1    Q UANTIFYING CHANGES OF PRE - TRAINED MODEL’ S KNOWLEDGE DURING THE
232            FINE - TUNING PROCESS
233
234   Explaining the precise effects of pre-training on downstream tasks still remains a significant chal-
235   lenge, because interactions (knowledge) directly extracted from the pre-trained model’s output v
236
      cannot be used for explanation. This is due to that the pre-trained model is usually trained on an
      extremely large-scale dataset with extensive training samples, whose network output often encodes
237
      a vast amount of diverse knowledge. Such knowledge can be further categorized into knowledge
238
      that can be used for inference of the downstream task (e.g., some general and common knowledge),
239   and knowledge that cannot be applicable to the downstream task (e.g., knowledge only related to
240   the inference of the pre-trained task). Thus, we need to extract and quantify the knowledge of the
241   pre-trained model that is used for the inference of the downstream task for explanation, so as to
242   ensure our explanation will not be affected by other irrelevant knowledge.
243
      To this end, we employ the linear probing method (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Tenney et al., 2019; Liu
244
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024), a commonly used technique, to extract pre-trained model’s knowl-
245   edge that is used for the downstream task. Specifically, given an input sample x and a pre-trained
246   model, we freeze all its network parameters, and use the feature f (x) of its penultimate layer (i.e., the
247   layer preceding the classifier of the pre-trained model) to train a new linear classifier W T f (x) + b
248   for the same downstream task as the fine-tuned model6 . Then, we define the following function
249   vpretrain to quantify the pre-trained model’s knowledge used for the inference of the downstream task
250   I(S|x, vpretrain ), where ypretrain denotes the label predicted by the linear classifier.

406   3.2.3     W HY THE FINE - TUNED MODEL CONVERGES FASTER ?

      4                C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
476
477
      In this paper, we present an in-depth analysis to explain the benefits of pre-training, including the
      boosted accuracy and the accelerated convergence, from a game-theoretic view. To this end, we
478
      use interactions to explicitly quantify the knowledge encoded by the pre-trained model, and further
479
      analyze the effects of such knowledge on the downstream task, where the faithfulness of treating
480   interactions as essential knowledge encoded by the DNN for inference has been theoretically en-
481   sured by a set of properties of interactions. We discover that compared to training from scratch,
482   pre-training enables the fine-tuned model to encode more exclusively-learned and discriminative
483   knowledge for inference, and to learn target knowledge more quickly and directly, which accounts
484   for the superior classification performance and faster convergence of the fine-tuned model. This pro-
485   vides new insights into understanding pre-training, and may also guide new interesting directions on
      the fine-tuning behavior of the DNN for future studies.

      Although there exist various ways to define/quantify the knowledge encoded by DNNs, a series of
652
      studies have theoretically proven and empirically verified the following properties as convincing
653
      evidence to take interactions as essential knowledge encoded by the DNN for inference.
654
655   (1) The universal-matching property in Theorem 3.1 and the sparsity property in equation 3 have
656   mathematically guaranteed that a few interactions with salient effect I(S|x) can faithfully explain
657
      the output of DNNs (Ren et al., 2024). Exactly speaking, given an arbitrary input sample with n
      input variables, network outputs on 2n differently masked samples can always be well approximated
658
      by a small set of salient interactions, no matter how we randomly mask this input sample.
659
660   (2) Li & Zhang (2023) have experimentally verified the transferability property and the discrim-
661   inative property of interactions. Specfically, they have discovered that interactions exhibit consid-
662   erable transferability across samples and across models, i.e., interactions extracted from different
663
      samples in the same category are often similar, and different DNNs trained for the same task usually
      learns similar sets of interactions. They have also observed that a salient interaction has remarkable
664
      discrimination power in classification tasks, i.e., the same salient interaction extracted from different
665
      samples usually pushes the DNN towards the classification of the same category.
666
667   (3) Ren et al. (2023a) have proven that interactions satisfy efficiency, linearity, dummy, symmetry,
668   anonymity, recursive, interaction distribution properties, as follows.
669
       1 Efficiency property. The network output of a well-trained
                                                              P        model v(x) can be disentangled into
      the numerical effects of different interactions v(x) = S⊆N I(S|x).
670
       2 Linearity property. If the network output of the model w is computed as the sum of the network
671
      output of the model u and the network output of the model v , i.e., ∀S ⊆ N, w(xS ) = u(xS ) + v(xS ),
672
      then the interaction effect of S on the model w can be computed as the sum of the interaction effect
673   of S on the model u and that on the model v , ∀S ⊆ N, I(S|x) = I(S|x) + I(S|x).
674    3 Dummy property. If the input variable i is a dummy variable, i.e., ∀S ⊆ N \ {i}, v(xS∪{i} ) =
675   v(xS ) + v(x{i} ), then the input variable i has no interaction with other input variables, ∀S ⊆ N \
676   {i}, I(S ∪ {i}|x) = 0.
677    4 Symmetry property. If input variables i, j ∈ N cooperate with other input variables in S ⊆
678   N \ {i, j} in the same way, ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, v(xS∪{i} ) = v(xS∪{j} ), then input variables i and j have
679   the same interaction effects, ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, I(S ∪ {i}|x) = I(S ∪ {j}|x).
680    5 Anonymity property. For any permutations π on N , then ∀S ⊆ N, I(S|x, v) = I(πS|x, πv) is
681
      always guaranteed, where the new set of input variables πS is defined as πS = {π(i), i ∈ S}, the
      new model πv is defined as (πv)(xπS ) = v(xS ). This suggests that permutation does not change the
682
      interaction effect.
683
       6 Recursive property. The interaction effects can be calculated in a recursive manner. For ∀i ∈
684   N, S ⊆ N \{i}, the interaction effect of S ∪ {i} can be computed as the difference between the
685   interaction effect of S with the presence of the variable i and the interaction effect of S with the
686   absence of the variable i. That is, ∀i ∈ N, S ⊆ NP\{i}, I(S ∪ {i}|x) = I(S|i is consistently present, x) −
687   I(S|x), where I(S|i is consistently present, x) = L⊆S (−1)|S|−|L| v(xL∪{i} ).
688    7 Interaction distribution property. This property describes how interactions are distributed for
689   “interaction functions” (Sundararajan et al., 2020). An interaction function vT parameterized by a
690   context T is defined as follows. ∀S ⊆ N , if T ⊆ S , then vT (xS ) = c; Otherwise, vT (xS ) = 0.
691   Thus, the interaction effect for an interaction function vT can be measured as, I(T |x) = c, and
      ∀S 6= T, I(S|x) = 0.
692
693   Besides, recent works have used interactions to explain the representation capacity of DNNs, includ-
694   ing the generalization power (Zhang et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), adversarial
695   robustness (Ren et al., 2021), adversarial transferability (Wang et al., 2021), the learning difficulty
696   of interactions (Liu et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023b), and the representation bottleneck (Deng et al.,
697   2022).
698   Thus, the above properties/usage of interactions ensure the faithfulness of taking the interaction as
699   the essential knowledge encoded by the DNN for inference.

      Ren et al. (2024) have proven that under the following three common conditions, a well-trained
706
      DNN usually encodes a small set Ωsalient of salient interactions for inference, where |Ωsalient |   2n .
707
      (1) The DNN is assumed to not encode extremely high-order interactions, i.e., high-order derivatives
708   of the DNN output w.r.t. input variables are assumed to be zero
709   (2) The classification confidence of the DNN on partially masked input samples is assumed to mono-
710   tonically increase with the size of the set of unmasked input variables.
711   (3) The network output of the masked input sample is assumed to neither be extremely high nor

779   D.1                        A NNOTATING S EMANTICS PARTS
780
781   We follow (Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2023a) to annotate semantic parts. Specifically, given
782   an input sample x ∈ Rn , the DNN theoretically encodes 2n interactions. Thus, if the number of
783   input variables n is large enough, then the computational cost for calculating salient interactions is
784   extremely high. To this end, we follow (Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2023a) to annotate 10 –12
785   semantic parts in each input sample to reduce the computation burden, which also makes the anno-
786   tated semantic parts are aligned over different samples in the same dataset. In this way, we take each
787   semantic part of each input sample as a “single” input variable to the DNN.
788   For the SST-2 dataset, we followed settings in (Ren et al., 2023a) to select 50 different sentences
789   containing 10 words with clear semantics to calculate interactions. Specifically, for each sentence,
790   we took each word as an input variable, and obtained totally n = 10 variables.
791
      For the CIFAR-10 dataset, we randomly selected 2 images for each category to annotate semantic
792   parts to calculate interaction. followed settings in (Ren et al., 2023a) to for randomly selected
793   images. Specifically, given an image, we first resized it to 224 × 224 before feeding it into the pre-
794   trained model, and then divided the resized image into small patches of size 28×28, thereby obtaining
795   8 × 8 image patches in total. Considering the DNN mainly used foreground information/knowledge
796   to make inference, we randomly selected n = 12 patches from 6 × 6 image patches located in the
797   center of the image to reduce the computational cost.
798   For the CUB200-2011 dataset, we randomly selected 2 images for each category to annotate seman-
799   tic parts and calculate interaction. Specifically, given an image, we divided the whole image into
800   small patches of size 28 × 28, thereby obtaining 8 × 8 image patches in total. Similar to the settings
801   in (Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2023a) to annotate semantic parts for the CIFAR-10 dataset, we
802   randomly selected n = 12 patches from 6 × 6 image patches located in the center of the image to
803   calculate interactions, because the DNN mainly employed foreground information/knowledge for
804   inference.
805   For the Stanford Cars dataset, we randomly selected 2 images for each category to annotate semantic
806   parts and compute interactions. Specifically, given an image, we divided the whole image into small
807   patches of size 28×28, thereby obtaining 8×8 image patches in total. Similar to the settings in (Li &
808   Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2023a) to annotate semantic parts for the CIFAR-10 dataset, we randomly
809   selected n = 12 patches from 6 × 6 image patches located in the center of the image to calculate
      interactions, because the DNN mainly employed foreground information/knowledge for inference.

      D.2   D ETAILS FOR T RAINING L INEAR C LASSIFIER IN S ECTION 3.2.1
811
812   To extract pre-trained model’s knowledge that is used for the downstream task, we employ a typi-
813   cal method, linear probing method (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Tenney et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024).
814   Specifically, given an input sample x and a pre-trained model, let us fine-tune it on a certain down-
815   stream classification task and obtain the corresponding fine-tuned model. We freeze all network
816   parameters in the pre-trained model, and use the feature f (x) of its penultimate layer (i.e., the layer
817   preceding the classifier) to train a new linear classifier W T f (x) + b for the same downstream task.
818   In experiments, we set hyper-parameters to train the linear classifier the same as those to fine-tine the
819   pre-trained model for fair comparisons. Specifically, we employed off-the-shelf VGG-16, ResNet-
820   50, ViT-Small, and ViT-Base pre-trained on the ImageNet-1K dataset, and extracted the feature of
821   the penultimate layer of each pre-trained model to train a linear classifier on the CUB200-2011,
822   CIFAR-10, and Stanford Cars datasets for image classification, respectively. Each linear classifier
823   was trained for 90 epochs using SGD with the momentum 0.9, weight decay 5 × 10−4 , and learning
824
      rate 0.01.
825   Besides, we also utilized off-the-shelf BERTBASE and DistillBERT models, and extracted the feature
826   of the penultimate layer of each pre-trained model to train a linear classifier on the SST-2 dataset for
827   binary sentiment classification, respectively. Each linear classifier was trained for 300 epochs with
828   the learning rate 2e−5.

830   D.3   D ETAILS FOR F INE - TUNING PRE - TRAINED MODELS AND T RAINING DNN FROM

      To enable fair comparisons, we set the model architecture of the DNN training from scratch the
833
      same as that of the fine-tuned model. Specifically, we fine-tuned the pre-trained VGG-16, ResNet-
834   50, ViT-Small, and ViT-Base models on the CUB200-2011, CIFAR-10, and Stanford Cars datasets
835   for 90 epochs using SGD with the momentum 0.9, weight decay 5 × 10−4 , and learning rate 0.01 for
836   image classification, respectively. Correspondingly, we trained randomly initialized versions of the
837   same models (VGG-16, ResNet-50, ViT-Small, and ViT-Base) on the same datasets for 90 epochs
838   with the learning rate 0.1.

      Besides, we fine-tuned the pre-trained BERTBASE and DistillBERT models on the SST-2 dataset
840   for 300 epochs with the learning rate 2e−5 for binary sentiment classification, respectively. Corre-
