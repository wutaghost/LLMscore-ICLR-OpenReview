009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Adafactor, a memory-efficient variant of Adam, has emerged as one of the popular
012                choices for training deep learning tasks, particularly large language models. How-
013                ever, despite its practical success, there is limited theoretical analysis of Adafactor’s
014                convergence. In this paper, we present a comprehensive analysis of Adafactor in a
015                non-convex smooth setting.√ We show that full-batch Adafactor finds a stationary
016                point at a rate of Õ(1/ T ) with the default setup, which could be accelerated
017                to Õ(1/T ) with a constant step-size parameter. For stochastic
                                                                               √         Adafactor without
018                update clipping, we prove a convergence rate of Õ(1/ T ) with the right parame-
019                ters covering the default setup. We also prove that Adafactor with a time-varying  √
020                clipping threshold could also find a stationary point with the rate of Õ(1/ T ).
021                Our theoretical results are further complemented by some experimental results.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The adaptive gradient-based methods, such as the well-known AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011; Streeter
026   & McMahan, 2010), RMSProp (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012), Adadelta (Zeiler, 2012), Adam (Kingma
027   & Ba, 2015), and AMSGrad (Reddi et al., 2018) are one of the preferred approaches in solving the
028   following unconstrained stochastic optimization problem in deep learning fields:
029                                     min f (X) = EZ∈P [l(X; Z)],                                 (1)
030                                         X∈Rn×m
031   where l is a smooth potentially non-convex function and P denotes a probability distribution. During
032   the training process, these adaptive methods require storing the historical gradients’ information to
033   tune their step-sizes adaptively. For example, both Adam and AdamW maintain the exponential
034   average of gradients and squared gradients, and AdaGrad stores the cumulative of squared gradients.
035
      Despite their effectiveness, these algorithms pose substantial memory challenges for GPUs to save
      these additional gradients’ information, especially when training large language models (LLMs),
036
      such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), which contains over 175 billion parameters.
037
038   To address memory constraints, several memory-efficient optimization algorithms have been devel-
039   oped, e.g., (Shazeer & Stern, 2018; Anil et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). One of the
040   popular optimizers is Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018), a memory-saved variant of Adam that
041
      employs a rank-1 matrix factorization to approximate the second-moment matrix. For an n × m
      weight matrices, this technique reduces memory from O(mn) to O(m + n) by only tracking the
042
      moving averages of the row and column sums of the squared gradients matrix. Additionally, Adafactor
043
      eliminates the first-order momentum used in Adam and incorporates update clipping to enhance
044   training stability. In real applications, several LLMs including PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2023)1 and
045   T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) have included Adafactor into their main optimizers (Zhao et al., 2023).
046
047
      The empirical results reveal that Adafactor achieves comparable performance to Adam in training
      Transformer models (Shazeer & Stern, 2018), even though it sacrifices gradient information to save
048
      the memory. Despite Adafactor’s empirical success, there is limited understanding on its convergence
049
      in theory, especially the explanation for its hyper-parameter setting in experiments. Theoretical
050   results, e.g., (Zou et al.,
051
                               √2019; Défossez et al., 2022), have proved that Adam could converge to the
      stationarity with Õ(1/ T ) rate under specific hyper-parameter for non-convex smooth setup. Then,
052   a natural question arises:

059   In this paper, we analyze Adafactor’s convergence for non-convex smooth optimization problems,
060   considering the typical bounded gradient setting as those for AdaGrad (Li & Orabona, 2019; Ward
061   et al., 2020) and Adam (Zaheer et al., 2018). We aim to provide a similar convergence rate for
062   Adafactor which complements the empirical observation that Adafactor could attain comparable
063   performance to Adam while reducing memory usage. The analysis is non-trivial compared to other
064
      adaptive methods such as AdaGrad and Adam due to the unique matrix factorization and update
      clipping mechanisms in Adafactor. In the full-batch case, we rely on the special exponential moving
065
      averages of the row sums and column sums of the squared gradients to lower bound the first-order
066
      term in the Descent Lemma. In the stochastic case, we design a new proxy step-size to compute the
067   conditional expectation of the first-order term that involved the stochastic gradient and the adaptive
068   step-sizes. Further, we successfully control the additional error brought by this proxy step-size. We
069   also extend a standard way in the analysis of SGD with clipping to handle the update clipping. Our
070   main contributions are summarized as follows.

      2     R ELATED WORK
096
097
      Although there are limited works on Adafactor in theory, the convergence for other memory-
098
      unconstraint adaptive methods are widely studied. Here, we briefly list some typical works due to the
099   page limitation.
100
101
      Convergence of adaptive methods. Several studies (Li & Orabona, 2019; Ward et al., 2020; Zou
102   et al., 2019) prove the convergence of AdaGrad in non-convex smooth settings assuming bounded
103   stochastic gradients. Shi et al. (2020) shows that RMSProp could converge to the stationarity when
104   the decay rate of the second moment is close to one. Several works (Chen et al., 2019; Zhou et al.,
105   2020; Alacaoglu et al., 2020) provide convergence bounds for AMSGrad in non-convex smooth
106   settings. A line of research, e.g., (Zaheer et al., 2018; De et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2019; Défossez et al.,
107   2022) have investigated the convergence of Adam assuming bounded gradients and noise. Yao et al.
      (2021) designed AdaHessian, using Hutchinson’s approximation to estimate the diagonal Hessian.


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Sadiev et al. (2023) provided Scaled SARAH and Scaled L-SVRG to approximate the diagonal
109   Hessian when the problem is ill-conditioned. Recently, several works focused on the heavy-tail noise,
110   showing that clipping is also necessary for AdaGrad (Li & Liu, 2023) and Adam (Chezhegov et al.,
111   2024) as the one for SGD (Gorbunov et al., 2020).
112
113   Memory efficient algorithms. As large models are increasingly used in deep learning, memory
114   constraints have become a central issue during training. Consequently, several memory-efficient
115   optimizers have been developed to address this challenge.
116
      One approach to saving memory involves applying matrix factorization to optimization algorithms.
117   For instance, Shazeer et al. (2017) used matrix factorization in the second moment estimator of
118   gradients in Adam, similar to the concept behind Adafactor. Luo et al. (2023) introduced CAME, a
119   variant of Adafactor, which incorporates a confidence-guided strategy to mitigate instability caused
120   by erroneous updates. Zhao et al. (2024) proposed Adapprox, leveraging randomized low-rank
121   matrix approximation for Adam’s second moment estimator, demonstrating superior performance
122   and reduced memory usage compared to AdamW.
123   There are some other techniques to save the memory. For example, Gupta et al. (2018) relied on
124   a “Shampoo” technique to reduce the storage requirement of full-matrix preconditioning methods.
125   Notably, their method could be further extended to the more realistic tensor case. Anil et al. (2019)
126   presented a memory-saved version of AdaGrad, called SM3, by maintaining k sets gradient ac-
127   cumulator. They proved the convergence guarantee of SM3 on online convex optimization and
128   the effectiveness in experiments. Recently, Li et al. (2024) built a 4-bit Adam using quantization
129   techniques to compress the first and second moment estimators in Adam, also reducing memory
130   usage.
131   In summary, many existing optimizers, particularly adaptive methods like AdaGrad and Adam, face
132   memory overhead. In response, the discussed works have designed memory-efficient optimizers that
133   aim to achieve comparable performance to these existing methods while achieving memory benefits.

189
      Matrix factorization. Adafactor could be served as a saved-memory version of Adam. Throughout
190
      the training process, Adam maintains two n × m matrices Mk and Vk using exponential moving
191
      average update,
192
                 Mk = β1,k Mk−1 + (1 − β1,k )Gk , Vk = β2,k Vk−1 + (1 − β2,k )Gk ⊙ Gk ,                (2)
193
      where β1,k , β2,k ∈ (0, 1), thereby tripling the memory usage. The innovation in Adafactor lies in
194
      its method of approximating Vk by factoring it into two rank-1 matrices, specifically the row sums
195
      and column sums of Vk , thus sufficiently reducing the memory from 2mn to m + n. Although
196   this factorization sacrifices some information about the squared gradients, Adafactor still delivers
197   performance comparable to Adam in many real application tasks.
198
199   Increasing decay rate. In Adam, corrective terms are introduced into Mk and Vk , resulting in
200   two increasing-to-one decay rates. Theoretically, it has been demonstrated that a value close to
201   one for β2,k would ensure the convergence, e.g., (Défossez et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2019; Zhang
202   et al., 2022). Inspired by this observation, Adafactor used an increasing second-moment decay rate
203   β2,k = 1 − 1/k c , c > 0, and the empirical default setting is c = 0.8. As pointed out by Shazeer &
204   Stern (2018), this setting allows for enjoying the stability of a low β2,k at the early stage of training
205
      and the insurance of convergence from a high β2,k as the run progresses. Moreover, it also leverages
      the bias correction.
206
207
      Update clipping. Adafactor modifies the update process by discarding the first-order moment
208
      Mk and instead applies an update clipping technique inside the step-size ηk . This involves dividing
209   the root-mean-square of the update Uk , denoted as RMS(Uk ), when it exceeds a threshold d. This
210   mechanism helps to calibrate the second moment estimator Wk when it’s larger-than-desired Gk ⊙Gk .
211   Empirical findings in (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) indicated that implementing update clipping leads to
212   significant performance improvements when the warm-up technique is not used.
213
214   Relative step-sizes. Adafactor incorporates a step-size proportional to scale of Xk , denoted by
215   RMS(Xk ), which is shown in experiments more resilient to the more naive parameter initialization
      and scaling schemes (Shazeer & Stern, 2018).

347   The time-increasing dk provides the following intuition: As shown in (Shazeer & Stern, 2018, Figure
348   1), during the early stages of training, a high decay rate β2,k can cause larger-than-desired updates and
349   training instability. Therefore, we set a low threshold dk to ensure that the update clipping mechanism
350   effectively calibrates these larger-than-desired updates. As training progresses, the sequences and
351
      updates become more stable. Consequently, there is less need for update clipping, corresponding to a
      relatively large dk .

      10     C ONCLUSIONS
529
      In this paper, we investigate the convergence behavior of Adafactor on non-convex smooth landscapes
530
      with bounded stochastic gradients. Our theoretical results complement an empirical observation
531
      that Adafactor could achieve comparable performance to Adam, despite sacrificing some gradient
532   information to reduce memory usage. We introduce a new proxy step-size to decouple the stochastic
533   gradients from the unique adaptive step-size and update clipping. We also rely on the unique structure
534   of proxy step-sizes and an appropriate choice of β2 to control the additional errors.
535
536   Limitations. Several limitations warrant further investigation. First, the polynomial dependency
537   on ϵ1 in convergence bounds may be improved to a better one, such as log(1/ϵ1 ). Second, the
538   convergence bound for stochastic vanilla Adafactor remains unknown. Third, the bounded stochastic
539   gradient can be relaxed as it may be unpractical in LLMs (Zhang et al., 2020). Finally, it’s beneficial
      to further support our theoretical results through experiments on large language models.
