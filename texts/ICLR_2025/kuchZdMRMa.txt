011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are limited by the Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) hier-
014
               archy and cannot compute graph properties such as cycles. Topological descrip-
               tors (TDs) such as the Euler characteristics (EC), persistent homology (PH), and
015
               Laplacian spectrums have thus been employed to enhance the GNNs. However,
016
               despite empirical successes, the theoretical underpinnings of these TDs remain
017            largely underexplored. We bridge this gap with a rigorous characterization of TDs
018            focusing on three key aspects: expressivity (representational power), stability (ro-
019            bustness to data perturbations), and computation (implementation cost). We eval-
020            uate the expressivity of different TDs, and design a novel scheme RePHINESpec
021            that is strictly more expressive. We also propose new metrics to assess the stability
022            of the state-of-the-art RePHINE method and the newly proposed RePHINESpec
023            method. To address computational costs, we introduce and analyze weaker vari-
024            ants for several descriptors. TDs find significant applications in molecular con-
025            texts, so we also explore new filtration functions on the molecular graphs. Finally,
026            we formalize the properties of filtration functions derived from graph products.
027
               Overall, this work lays the foundation for the principled design and analysis of
               new TDs that can be tailored to specific applications.
028
029
030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Message-passing GNNs are prominent models for graph representation learning (Gilmer et al.,
033   2017). However, they are bounded in expressivity by the WL hierarchy (Xu et al., 2019; Morris
034   et al., 2019; Maron et al., 2019; Morris et al., 2024) and cannot compute fundamental graph prop-
035   erties such as cycles or connected components (Garg et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020). TDs such as
036   those based on PH can provide such information and thus are being increasingly employed to aug-
037   ment GNNs, boosting their empirical performance (Carriere et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Chen
038
      et al., 2021; Horn et al., 2022). However, barring a few notable exceptions such as Immonen et al.
      (2023); Ballester & Rieck (2024), the expressivity analysis of TDs remain rather elusive.
039
040   In this work, we study three prominent types of topological descriptors based on PH (Edelsbrunner
041   & Harer, 2008), EC (Turner et al., 2014; Röell & Rieck, 2024), and the Laplacian spectrum (Wang
042   et al., 2020; Bakó et al., 2022) respectively. On a high level, PH keeps track of the birth and death
043   times of topological features (e.g., connected components and loops) in a (parameterized) graph
044
      filtration. EC is a simpler, less expensive invariant that keeps track of the number of vertices minus
      the number of edges in the filtration. The Laplacian spectrum keeps track of the eigenvalues of the
045
      graph Laplacian through the filtration, which neither PH nor EC can account for.
046
047   Not much is known about the expressivity of TDs. Recently, Immonen et al. (2023) analyzed PH
048   under color filtrations, providing a complete characterization of 0-dimensional PH in vertex-level
049   and edge-level filtrations using graph-theoretic notions; and introduced RePHINE as a strictly more
050
      powerful scheme than both. However, the expressivity of EC and spectrum TDs remains unexplored,
      motivating our first set of investigations: how do different topological descriptors stack up against
051
      each other, and can their strengths be unified to design even more expressive TDs than RePHINE?
052
053   Here, we offer a complete characterization for the expressivity of EC in terms of combinatorial data
      on the colors of the graph. In particular, it turns out that EC is strictly less expressive than PH, ruling


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      out any further efforts to combine the two for representational benefits. On the other hand, Laplacian
055   appears in several flavors: unlike graph Laplacian (the 0-th combinatorial Laplacian), rows in the 1-st
056   combinatorial Laplacian correspond to the edges of the graph (as opposed to vertices), and persistent
057   versions (Wang et al., 2020) have also been proposed for both. We show that, surprisingly, the
058   expressivity offered by the non-zero eigenvalues of all these spectral TDs can simply be replicated
059   by including the non-zero eigenvalues of graph Laplacians at all time steps (Appendix B). Armed
060   with this key insight, we introduce a new topological scheme called RePHINESpec that amalgamates
061   RePHINE and graph Laplacians to be strictly more expressive than all the existing TDs.
062   However, expressivity is not the only design consideration: in general, topological features are
063   expensive to compute. Unlike expressivity, EC compares favorably with other TDs in terms of
064   computation. This leads us to our next phase of analyses: can we design computationally cheaper
065   variants of TDs? We thus proceed to streamline RePHINE, RePHINESpec , and EC with what we
066   call their “max” versions. We show that max EC is even more cost-effective than EC, while still
067   retaining the latter’s expressivity. Interestingly, we show that this expressivity equivalence on graphs
068   extends to a more general equivalence for color filtrations on finite higher-dimensional simplicial
069
      complexes (Appendix C). This immediately opens avenues for integrating max EC as an economical
      alternative to PH-based TDs into topological neural networks, which generalize GNNs via higher
070
      order message-passing (Papillon et al., 2024; Papamarkou et al., 2024; Eitan et al., 2024) and have
071
      recently been shown to benefit from TDs both theoretically and empirically (Verma et al., 2024).
072
073   Besides expressivity and computation, stability is another important issue. A diagram-based TD
074   is stable if a small perturbation in filtration incurs only a small change in the diagram it produces.
075
      Stability has been used, among others, to compare and classify geometric shapes (Cohen-Steiner
      et al., 2006). Stability results are known for usual persistence diagrams in Cohen-Steiner et al.
076
      (2006) and EC in Dłotko & Gurnari (2023); however, even defining a suitable metric to quantify
077
      stability is challenging for RePHINE since it strictly generalizes PH with node-colors, but all known
078   metrics for the persistence diagrams in edge-level filtrations are node-color agnostic. We fill this gap
079   by motivating a novel metric for measuring stability and proving that RePHINE is globally stable
080   under this metric. We also prove RePHINESpec is locally stable under a similar metric.
081
082
      From a practical micro-level perspective, the design of effective filtration functions is also
      paramount. Typically, filtration functions are either fixed a priori or learned without any structural
083
      considerations, obfuscating how they are about the corresponding diagrams. Instead, we initiate
084
      here a formal analysis by characterizing filtrations on graph products: we establish that the product
085   of vertex filtrations is the max of vertex colors, and that the product of edge filtrations is the natural
086   edge coloring on the product. We also provide an algorithm to track the evolution of 0-dimensional
087   persistence diagram under the product filtration induced by edge filtrations.
088
      Isomorphism invariance is another desiderata required to ensure that isomorphic graphs produce
089
      identical diagrams. While PH and RePHINE were already shown to be isomorphic invariant
090
      (Ballester & Rieck, 2024; Immonen et al., 2023), we establish the same for all the other afore-
091   mentioned TDs.
092
093   Finally, since TDs have been found to be particularly useful in the context of molecular data (Horn
094
      et al., 2022; Immonen et al., 2023; Verma et al., 2024), we investigate whether there can be any
      further benefits to viewing the edges (pertaining to the bonds between the nodes, i.e., atoms) as
095
      topological objects themselves in place of their usual color-based representation. We establish that
096
      there are no differences between the two on 0-th dimensional persistence diagrams or on EC in
097   edge-level filtrations, and they are incomparable in 1-dimensional diagrams.
098
099   In sum, we build a firm foundation for principled design and analysis of TDs. We summarize our
100
      key contributions in Figure 1, and relegate all the proofs to Appendix A.

      2.1   P ERSISTENT H OMOLOGY
170
171   A vertex v (ie. 0-dimensional persistence information) is born when it appears in a given filtration
172   of a diagram. When we merge two connected components represented by two vertices v and w, we
173   use a decision rule to kill off one of the vertices and mark the remaining vertex to represent the new
174   connected component. A cycle (ie. 1-dimensional persistence information) is born when it appears
175   in a given filtration of a diagram, and it will never die. For color-based vertex and edge filtration,
176   there is a canonical way to calculate the persistence pairs of a graph with a given filtration. We
177   refer the reader to Appendix A of Immonen et al. (2023) for a precise introduction. We say a 0-th
178
      dimensional persistence pair (b, d) is a real hole if d = ∞, is an almost hole if b ̸= d < ∞, and is
      a trivial hole if b = d. Note that edge-based filtrations do not have any trivial holes.
179
180   Definition 2. Let f = (fv , fe ) be on the coloring set X. The persistent homology (PH) diagram of
181
      a graph G is a collection PH(G, f ) composed of two lists PH(G, f )0 , PH(G, f )1 where PH(G, f )0
182   are all the persistent pairs in the vertex filtration {Gft v }t∈R and PH(G, f )1 are all the persistent
183   pairs in the edge filtration {Gft e }t∈R .
184
      PH is an isomorphism invariant (Theorem 2 of Ballester & Rieck (2024)) and can be used in GNNs.
185
      However, PH alone does not account for sufficient local color information. Consequently, Immonen
186   et al. (2023) introduced the RePHINE diagram as a generalization of PH.
187
      Definition 3. Let f = (fv , fe ) be on X. The RePHINE diagram of a graph G is a multi-set
188
      RePHINE(G, f ) = RePHINE(G, f )0 ⊔ RePHINE(G, f )1 of cardinality |V (G)| + βG        1
                                                                                               where:
189
190          • 0-th dimensional component: RePHINE(G, f )0 consists of tuples of the form
191            (b(v), d(v), α(v), γ(v)) for each vertex v ∈ V (G). Here, b(v) and d(v) are the birth
192            and death times of v under the edge filtration {Gft e }t∈R , α(v) = fv (c(v)) and γ(v) =
193            minv∈N (w) fe (c(v), c(w)). Here, N (ω) denotes the neighboring vertices of ω.
194            The decision rule for which vertex to kill off is as follows - an almost hole (b, d) corresponds
195            to the merging of two connected components with vertex representatives v1 and v2 . We kill
196            off the vertex that has a greater value under α. If there is a tie, we kill off the vertex that has
197            a lower value under γ. If there is a further tie, Theorem 4 of Immonen et al. (2023) shows
198            that the resulting diagram RePHINE(G, f )0 is independent of which choice we make here.
199
             • 1-st dimensional component: RePHINE(G, f )1 consists of tuples of the form (1, d, 0, 0)
200
               where each d indicates the birth time of a cycle in the same filtration. In the definition of
201            Immonen et al. (2023), the birth of a cycle corresponds to what is so-called the death of a
202            “missing hole”. This is why we use d to indicate the birth time instead.
203
204   Theorem 5 of Immonen et al. (2023) asserts that RePHINE diagrams are strictly more expressive
205   than PH diagrams. Here, we introduce a new variant of the RePHINE diagram with an added
206
      constraint on fe .
207   Definition 4. Let fv : X → R be a vertex function such that fv > 0. The max RePHINE dia-
208   gram of a graph G is a multi-set RePHINEm (G, fv ) = RePHINE(G, fv , fe ) where fe (c1 , c2 ) =
209
      max(fv (c1 ), fv (c2 )). Intuitively, for each edge e = (v1 , v2 ) ∈ E(G), we assign e the color value of
      whichever vertex vi that has a greater value under Fv .
210
211   Note that Definition 4 is a special case of Definition 3. The setting of max RePHINE is more suitable
212   for certain physics-informed systems where the vertex might have a strong influence on the attributes
213   of its neighboring edges. Another advantage is that the max RePHINE diagram only needs to keep
214   track of the vertex colors as it assigns each edge a color in X. This is in contrast to the more general
215   RePHINE diagram which assigns each edge a color in X × X. The usage of max RePHINE uses
      less memory in the space of possible edge colors than the RePHINE diagram.

      2.2   E ULER C HARACTERISTIC
217
218
      Including topological features in graph representation learning using persistent homology or
219
      RePHINE diagrams can be computationally expensive. The Euler characteristic (EC) provides a
220   weaker isomorphism invariant that is easier to compute (but typically less expressive). For a graph
221   G, the Euler characteristic χ(G) is given by χ(G) = #V (G) − #E(G). Given a filtration of a
222   graph G, we can track its Euler characteristic throughout the filtration.
223

225   fv can produce, and b1 < ... < bm as the list of values fe can produce. The EC diagram of a graph
226   G is two lists EC(G, f ) = EC(G, f )0 ⊔ EC(G, f )1 , where EC(G, f )0 is the list {χ(Gfavi )}ni=1 and
227   EC(G, f )1 is the list {χ(Gfbie )}m

228   Definition 4, we define the max EC diagram of G as ECm (G, fv ) := EC(G, f ).

231   2.3   L APLACIAN S PECTRUM
232
233   Homology captures harmonic information (in the sense that the kernel of the graph Laplacian corre-
234   sponds to the 0-th homology), but there is some non-harmonic information we also want to account
235   for. One option is to account for colors, as we have done with RePHINE. Another option is to
236   augment RePHINE with spectral information. We therefore propose a new descriptor below.
237
      Definition 6. Let f = (fv , fe ) be on the coloring set X. The spectral RePHINE diagram of a
238
239
      graph G is a multi-set RePHINESpec (G, f ) = RePHINESpec (G, f )0 ⊔ RePHINESpec (G, f )1 of
                             1
      cardinality |V (G)| + βG where:
240
241          • 0-th dimensional component: RePHINESpec (G, f )0 consists of tuples of the form
242            (b(v), d(v), α(v), γ(v), ρ(v)) for each vertex v ∈ V (G). Here, b, d, α, γ are the same
243            as Definition 3, and ρ(v) is the list of non-zero eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian of the
244            connected component v is in when it dies at time d(v).
245
246          • 1-st dimensional component: RePHINESpec (G, f )1 consists of tuples of the form
247            (1, d(e), 0, 0, ρ(e)). Here, d(e) indicates the birth time of a cycle given by the edge e.
248            ρ(e) denotes the non-zero eigenvalues the graph Laplacian of the connected component e
249            is in when it is born at time d(e).
250   In the specific case where the pair f = (fv , fe ) are given as in Definition 4, we also define the max
251   spectral RePHINE diagram of G as RePHINEmSpec (G, fv ) := RePHINESpec (G, f ).

262   3.1   C OMPARISON OF E XPRESSIVITY
263
264   Let X, Y be two graph isomorphism invariants. We say X has at least the same expressivity as Y
265   (denoted X ⪰ Y ) if for all non-isomorphic graphs G and H that Y can tell apart, X can also tell
266   them apart. We say X is strictly more expressive than Y (denoted X ≻ Y ) if, in addition, there
267   exist two non-isomorphic graphs G and H that Y cannot tell apart but X can. We say X and Y
268   have the same expressive power (denoted X = Y ) if X ⪰ Y and Y ⪰ X. We say X and Y are
269   incomparable if there are two non-isomorphic graphs G and H that X can tell apart but Y cannot,
      and vice versa.

      6   C OLORING AND C OUNTING R EPRESENTATIONS FOR M OLECULES
487
488   Let M be a molecule. The standard representation of M as a graph, which we call hereafter as the
489   Bond Coloring Representation (BColor), views the atoms of M as nodes; and adds an edge of
490   color “triple bond”, “double bond”, or “single bond” to indicate covalent bonds between the atoms.
491   We also consider here a different representation, which we call Bond Counting Representation
492   (BCount): the nodes denote the atoms as before; however, we add 3 edges to denote a triple bond,
493   2 edges to denote a double bond, and 1 edge to denote a single bond.
494   BColor is the conventional choice to represent a molecule (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
495   2022; Verma et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024). A molecule with this topological
496   representation (BCount) is not a simple graph - it is rather a graph with multiple edges between the
497   same vertex. This is an example of what is called a delta complex (Section 2.1 of Hatcher (2002)).
498   In contrast, the counting representation is much less common (Kvasničk & Pospı́chal, 1989).
499
      One may consider using BCount since it might be able to capture more accurately the strength of the
500   underlying electrostatic interactions. We seek to investigate whether this alternate topological rep-
501   resentation would allow topological methods to better differentiate non-isomorphic molecules. For
502   a molecule M , we use M (c), M (t) to denote the BColor representation and BCount representation
503   of M respectively. The coloring set of a vertex filtration of M (c) vs. M (t) will be the type of all
504   atoms, and the coloring set of an edge filtration of M (c) vs. M (t) will be the bond type.
505   Proposition 6. For any vertex color filtration fv of M (c) and M (t), fv will produce the same 0-
506   dimensional persistence diagram. For any edge color filtration fe of M (c) and M (t), fe will also
507   produce the same 0-th dimensional persistence diagram.
508   Proposition 7. Let M, N be molecules. The EC diagram of M (t) and N (t) with respect to all
509   possible fe are the same if and only if the EC diagram of M (c) and N (c) with respect to all possible
510   fe are the same.
511
      We remark that there are examples of graphs M , N whose vertex filtration can be told apart by EC
512
      with BCount but not BColor (e.g., consider M with 2 atoms of same type A and a double bond
513
      between them; and N with 2 atoms of A and a single bond between them). Conversely, there exist
514   examples that can be separated by EC with BColor but not BCount (e.g., consider M with 3 A
515   atoms and 2 single bonds; and N with 3 A atoms but only 1 double bond). This means that the
516   two representations are incomparable under vertex filtrations with EC as well as with 1-dimensional
517   persistence diagrams (using Proposition 6).

      7   C ONCLUSION
520
521
      We unraveled the theoretical underpinnings of topological descriptors associated with EC, PH,
522   and the Laplacian spectrum focusing on expressivity, stability, and computation. For expressiv-
523   ity, we amalgamated spectral features with RePHINE to craft a strictly more expressive scheme
524   RePHINESpec . For stability, we constructed a notion of bottleneck distance on RePHINE and
525   RePHINESpec . From here, we showed the former is globally stable, and the latter is locally stable.
526   For computation, we introduced and analyzed several new schemes, including a more economical,
527   but equally expressive, variant of EC. We also examined these methods in the context of graph prod-
528   ucts and molecules. In particular, our results for molecules imply that we can provably augment
529   expressivity of existing color-based schemes with the counting-based features, opening fascinating
530   avenues for applications in synthesis chemistry and drug discovery.
531   Our work focused on color-based filtrations, and it may be interesting to study these descriptors
532   under other types of filtrations (e.g., based on the degree of vertices). Exploring suitable metrics for
533   assessing the stability of RePHINESpec is another interesting direction.
