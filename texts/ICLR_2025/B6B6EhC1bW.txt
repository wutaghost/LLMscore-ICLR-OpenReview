009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011              Molecular graphs are commonly represented using SMILES (Simplified Molec-
012              ular Input Line Entry System) strings, enabling the transformation of molecular
013              graphs into token sequences. While transformers—powerful neural networks orig-
014              inally developed for natural language processing—have been adapted for learning
015              molecular representations from SMILES by predicting masked tokens, they have
016              yet to achieve competitive performance on ADMET benchmark datasets crucial
017
                 for assessing drug properties such as absorption, distribution, metabolism, ex-
                 cretion, and toxicity. This paper identifies the challenge that traditional random
018
                 token masking in SMILES overlooks essential molecular substructures, leading
019
                 transformers to focus on superficial correlations between individual tokens rather
020              than their relationships within substructures. We propose a novel approach that
021              enhances transformers’ capability to recognize molecular substructures by intro-
022              ducing a substructure-aware masking strategy alongside a new learning objective.
023              This method embeds substructure information directly into the masking and pre-
024              diction process, allowing the model to predict specific subgraphs instead of ran-
025              dom tokens. Our experiments demonstrate that transformers employing this dual
026              innovation outperform those utilizing conventional random masking, resulting in
027              improved predictions of drug-related properties on ADMET benchmarks. This
028
                 work contributes to the ongoing advancement of transformer architectures in the
                 field of molecular representation learning.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In machine learning, molecular graphs are frequently represented using SMILES strings, which
034   translate molecular structures into sequences of tokens. Subsequently, transformers, powerful neural
035   networks originally designed for natural language processing, have been adapted to learn molecular
036   representations from SMILES by predicting randomly masked tokens within these sequences (Ross
037   et al., 2022).
038
      However, despite their success in other fields, transformers have under-presented in the leaderboards
039   on the 22 ADMET benchmark datasets1 , which assess predictions of key drug properties—such as
040   absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity—that are crucial for determining the
041   viability of drug candidates. In contrast, simpler fingerprint-based methods, which reduce molecular
042   graphs into compact vectors summarizing the present of chemical motifs or functional groups, have
043   achieved better results. For examples, Figure 1 shows a leaderboard of the top methods for toxicity
044   prediction in the ADMET LD50 Zhu benchmark dataset where fingerprint-based and descriptor-
045   based methods dominate.
046   The success of fingerprint-based methods highlights that understanding substructures, chemical mo-
047   tifs, or functional groups and their associations is crucial for predicting ADMET properties. This
048   suggests that for transformers to be competitive, they need to enhance their ability to capture and
049   interpret key molecular substructures and their relationships.
050
      One possible reason for this performance gap is that transformers, when learning from SMILES
051   strings, mask and predict tokens without considering the molecular substructures those tokens rep-
052   resent. Additionally, since the molecular graph is serialized into a string, transformers must recon-

117   2     R ELATED WORK
118
119   In recent years, learning molecular representation gained significant traction in molecular science,
120   offering generalized frameworks for molecular tasks such as property prediction, drug discovery,
121   and molecular generation. These models leverage large datasets to learn rich, transferable represen-
122   tations that can be fine-tuned for specific molecular applications. In this section, we discuss existing
123   methods starting from classical fingerprint based methods to complex foundational molecular rep-
124
      resentation models.
125
126   2.1   F INGERPRINT BASED REPRESENTATION
127
128
      Fingerprint-based and descriptor-based representations are popular techniques used in cheminfor-
      matics and computational biology for molecular characterization. A molecular fingerprint is a
129
      bit vector representation that captures the presence or absence of specific structural features in a
130
      molecule. Each bit corresponds to a particular substructure, functional group, or atom environment,
131   encoding whether that feature exists in the molecule. Descriptors are quantitative properties that
132   describe various attributes of a molecule. These include topological, geometric, electronic, and
133   physicochemical properties. Unlike binary fingerprints, descriptors are typically numerical values
134   that capture diverse molecular characteristics.
135
      For the ADMET benchmarks, fingerprint and descriptor-based methods perform surprisingly well.
136
      Some of the top-performed methods such as MapLight (Notwell & Wood, 2023) which uses com-
137   bination of MorganFingerprint (Rogers & Hahn, 2010), Avalon (Gedeck et al., 2006), ErG (Stiefl
138   et al., 2006) and 200 descriptors from RDKit (Landrum, 2013). DeepMol (Correia et al., 2024)
139   uses MorganFingerprints, MACCS Keys (Durant et al., 2002), and AtomPair Fingerprints (Map4
140   (Orsi & Reymond, 2024)). Among methods using fingerprints and descriptors we can also mention
141   ChemProp (Yang et al., 2019), CFA (Quazi et al., 2023), ZairaChem (Turon et al., 2023). The pro-
142   posed methods in the category are the established strong baselines that we will benchmark against
143   in this work.
144
145   2.2   G RAPH NEURAL NETWORK
146
147   Graph-based models, particularly Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), are a natural fit for molecular
148   data, where molecules are represented as graphs with atoms as nodes and bonds as edges. Early
149
      models such as Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) and Message Passing Neural Networks
      (MPNNs) have been widely applied to predict molecular properties. Gilmer et al. (2017) intro-
150
      duced MPNNs, which utilize message-passing mechanisms to capture the structure-property rela-
151
      tionships within molecules, proving effective in tasks like quantum property prediction . Recent
152   advancements, such as the integration of attention mechanisms GATs (Veličković et al., 2017), have
153   improved the ability of these models to capture complex molecular interactions. The methods in this
154   category are also present in the ADMET leaderboards even they are not as competitive as fingerprint
155   based approaches.
156
157   2.3   T RANSFORMER BASED MODELS
158
159   Transformers, originally developed for natural language processing, have proven to be highly ef-
160   fective in molecular modeling, particularly for sequence-based tasks using SMILES strings. Mod-
161   els like ChemBERTa (Chithrananda et al., 2020) and MolFormer (Ross et al., 2022) leverage the
      transformer architecture to predict chemical properties from SMILES sequences with great success.

162
      Meanwhile, Graphormer (Shi et al., 2022) extends transformers to graph-based molecular repre-
163   sentations, combining self-attention mechanisms with inductive biases that capture both local and
164   global molecular features, leading to state-of-the-art results in quantum property prediction. To en-
165   sure a fair and rigorous comparison, we implement and pretrain different transformer architectures
166   on the same datasets as our proposed approach, controlling for dataset size variations that can oth-
167   erwise skew performance outcomes. By doing so, we provide a more accurate assessment of each
168   model’s true capability in molecular property prediction.

170   2.4   M ULTIMODAL MOLECULAR REPRESENTATION
171
172   Recent research investigates multimodal models that integrate molecular data with other modalities,
173
      such as 3D structural data, protein-ligand interactions, and experimental results. Foundational mod-
      els like Uni-Mol, which rely on 3D information, or ImageMol, which utilizes molecular images, ex-
174
      emplify this approach. These models typically provide complementary information to transformer-
175
      based methods trained on SMILES representations. Since our work primarily focuses on transform-

202   3     A LGORITHM
203
204   In this section, we present an algorithm called SmilesGraph, developed to train transformers on rec-
205
      ognizing high-order substructure relationships. The algorithm utilizes two forms of token masking,
      with the first being substructure masking. This method follows these steps:
206
207
             • Randomly select an atom from the input molecular graph.
208
209
             • Conduct a breadth-first traversal starting from the selected atom, continuing until reaching
               a maximum distance r from the starting point.
210
211          • Record all atoms encountered during the traversal and map them to their positions in the
212            SMILES string.
213          • Replace the identified atoms in the SMILES string with masking tokens.
214
215   An example of substructure masking is shown in Figure 3, where a substructure g is derived by
      performing a breadth-first traversal from a starting node corresponding to an Oxygen atom, with a

237   Besides substructure masking we also perform regular individual token masking (orange masks) so
238   ensure that the model also learn regular token association.
239   Since a masked substructure must be represented as an entry in the alphabet, the number of possible
240   masked tokens or substructures increases exponentially with the size of the substructure. To control
241   the size of the alphabet, we use random hashing vectors to represent the alphabet. Specifically, for a
242   given substructure, we construct the hash vector as follows:
243
244             • Retrieve the atom name of the starting node and compute its hash value within a hash vector
245               of size h, setting the corresponding hash bit from 0 to 1.
246             • Expanding the starting node with its nearest neighbor nodes, concatenating the bonds and
247               atom names of the neighboring nodes into a string. Compute the hash value of this string
248               within a hash vector of size h, and set the corresponding hash bit from 0 to 1.
249             • Continue expanding the current nodes by including their neighbors, and repeat the process
250               above.
251
252   Figure 3 (right) shows an example of how the hash vector of the subgraph g was constructed. Starting
253   from the oxygen node, it constructs string representation of the neighborhood of the node which
254   results in O, O-O and O-O-S and the corresponding hash function is used to maps those strings into
255   a hash vector with the corresponding bit is turns from zero to one. This method is similar to the
256
      Morgan fingerprint construction (Rogers & Hahn, 2010).
257   Once all the hash vectors are constructed, transformers are trained to predict the corresponding hash
258   vectors. Since the hash vectors are fixed-size binary vector, we use a binary classifier head with
259   BCE Loss (Binary Cross-Entropy Loss) as the training objective.

      5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
      In this paper, we introduced a substructure-aware masking strategy coupled with a novel learning ob-
521
      jective to enhance transformer-based models for molecular representation learning using SMILES.
522
      Our approach addresses the limitations of traditional random token masking, which fails to capture
523   essential molecular substructures and often leads to suboptimal performance on crucial ADMET
524   tasks. By embedding molecular substructure information directly into the masking and prediction
525   process, our method enables transformers to better model the relationships within these substruc-
526   tures, improving their ability to predict drug-related properties. Empirical evaluations demonstrate
527   that our approach significantly outperforms conventional random masking techniques on ADMET
528   benchmarks, highlighting the importance of incorporating domain-specific molecular knowledge
529   into the design of transformer models.
530   While our proposed method has shown promising results, several avenues for future research remain.
531   First, further refinement of the substructure-aware masking strategy could be explored by incorpo-
532   rating more sophisticated chemical knowledge, such as reaction mechanisms or quantum properties,
533   to improve model generalization across diverse molecular datasets. Additionally, applying the ap-
534   proach to other molecular representation formats beyond SMILES, such as molecular graphs or 3D
535   structures, could broaden its applicability.
