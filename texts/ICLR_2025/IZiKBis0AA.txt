009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper presents a novel machine learning-based approach to fragment-based
012            antibiotic drug design. We introduce a tool called FILTER, which uses chemical
013            structure data, pathway information, and protein targets to predict pharmacoki-
014            netic properties of existing and novel drugs. We report on three distinct experi-
015            ments using FILTER. The first experiment is an in-silico analysis that recreates
016            the historical discovery of penicillin derivatives, validating our approach against
017            known outcomes. The second experiment explores the combination of functional
018            groups from different antibiotic classes to create molecules with multiple mech-
019
               anisms of action. We refer to this approach as hybridization as all synthesized
               molecules are composed of fragments from both classes. Our final experiment is
020
               forward-looking as it explores new chemical spaces to build a library of promising
021
               compounds for further antibiotic development. In each of these experiments, FIL-
022            TER serves as an oracle, predicting physical properties and potential therapeutic
023            efficacy of the new molecular architectures, aiming to accelerate the drug devel-
024            opment process and address the challenge of antibiotic resistance. Our approach
025            represents an ongoing, significant shift from traditional drug discovery methods,
026            emphasizing the role of innovative technologies in combating the urgent global
027            threat of antimicrobial resistance.
028

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Since the discovery of penicillin, antibiotics have become a cornerstone of modern medicine. How-
037   ever, the relentless emergence of antibiotic resistance has significantly undermined their efficacy,
038   presenting a formidable challenge in the treatment of bacterial infections. Traditional drug discov-
039
      ery pathways, characterized by long timelines and high costs, are proving inadequate in the face of
      the rapid evolution of resistant bacterial strains. Innovative technologies are imperative to accelerate
040
      the discovery of new antibiotics. By integrating machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence
041
      (AI) into chemical synthesis and drug discovery, researchers can leverage computational power to
042   uncover novel compounds and predict their effectiveness, thereby streamlining the development
043   pipeline and reducing both time and expense.
044
045
      This paper presents a tool, FILTER, and proposes experimental methodologies to explore the antibi-
      otic space in search of antibiotic compounds. By ”antibiotic space,” we refer to the vast chemical
046
      landscape that emerges from the combination and modification of molecular fragments derived from
047
      existing antibiotics. This space includes both well-established and unexplored derivatives, offering
048   the potential to uncover new compounds with enhanced antibacterial properties. Borrowing from a
049   concept in organic chemistry involving the deconstruction of known chemical structures into simpler
050   precursor components, our first experiment employs a historical retrosynthetic analysis revisiting the
051   evolution of the discovery of antibiotics like penicillin. By taking early precursors within the peni-
052   cillin class, we recreate and extend the historical trajectory of the development of the penicillin
053   antibiotic class and its derivatives. This retrosynthesis validates our methodology but also sets a
      precedent for its application to other classes of antibioticss. Such experiments can lead to a rapid

      expansion of the known chemical space and the creation of further modern antibiotics and deriva-
055   tives.

057   Furthermore, this leads to a second experiment in which we call hybrid antibiotic design. The hybrid
058
      antibiotic design uses the insights gained from the historical analysis of antibiotic modifications and
      the predictive capacity of FILTER to guide the synthesis of hybrid molecules combining multiple

      mechanisms of action. This strategy aims to produce treatments that are effective against a broad

083   A pivotal tool in our study is FILTER, an AI tool designed to predict the physical properties and
084   therapeutic efficacy of new molecular architectures. FILTER leverages comprehensive chemical
085   structure data, pathway information, and protein targets to identify potential pharmacokinetic prop-
086   erties and interactions within biological systems. FILTER employs predictive modeling techniques
087   that focus on the anticipated protein and protein pathway targets of the synthesized molecules. By
088   integrating data from various biological databases, FILTER can anticipate how new compounds will
089   interact with specific biological pathways, providing insights into their potential efficacy and safety
090   profiles. Additionally, FILTER distinguishes itself from other ‘oracle’ software Alhossary et al.
091
      (2015) by incorporating a docking-based oracle which allows for direct analysis of a generated an-
      tibiotic to bind to known targets within its expected antibiotic class Li et al. (2019). FILTER is
092
      essential for extending analyses of under-explored antibiotic classes Centers for Disease Control
093
      and Prevention (2022). It aids in the prediction of novel compounds that may exhibit distinct mech-
094   anisms of action compared to current clinical antibiotics, thereby addressing the growing issue of
095   antibiotic resistance. By identifying molecules that interact with novel targets or utilize different
096   biological pathways, FILTER enhances the likelihood of discovering effective treatments against
097   resistant bacterial strains.

099   2.1   M ECHANISMS OF ACTION
100
101   A mechanism of action (MoA) (or ‘mode of action’) Parker et al. (2024) refers to the specific bio-
102   chemical interaction through which a drug substance produces its pharmacological effect. In the
103   context of antibiotics, the MoA typically involves disrupting essential bacterial processes, such as
104
      cell wall synthesis, protein synthesis, or DNA replication . Understanding the MoA is crucial for de-
      termining both the efficacy of a compound and its potential to overcome existing bacterial resistant
105
      mechanisms.
106
107   FILTER predicts whether a synthesized molecule will engage its target in a manner that disrupts key
      bacterial functions, thereby defining its MoA. This analysis is pivotal in determining if the compound


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      will exhibit therapeutic effects similar to existing antibiotics or if it can introduce novel mechanisms
109   that circumvent current bacterial resistance strategies Sun & Chen (2024). By predicting MoAs
110   that involve novel targets or alternative biological pathways, FILTER supports the development of
111   antibiotics capable of overcoming resistant bacterial strains.
112
      Our methodology addresses several limitations in current approaches to antibiotic discovery: data
113
      scarcity, model scalability, and synthesis efficiency. By leveraging historical antibiotic data and
114
      employing semi-supervised learning, we compensate for the lack of labeled data often encountered
115   in early-stage drug discovery. The architecture of FILTER allows for efficient processing of large
116   molecular datasets, enabling rapid screening of extensive chemical libraries. The integration of AI-
117   driven retrosynthesis with predictive analytics streamlines the process of identifying and evaluating
118   potential antibiotic candidates.
119
      FILTER is also a docking-based oracle since it further refines MoA predictions by virtually sim-
120
      ulating the binding interactions between molecules and target proteins. This simulation ensures
121   that the predicted MoA aligns with the molecule’s ability to physically interact with protein targets,
122   thereby enhancing the accuracy and reliability of the MoA predictions. By confirming the feasibility
123   of molecular binding, the docking analysis provides a critical layer of validation for the predicted
124   therapeutic actions of the compounds.

      4.1   P HYSICAL P ROPERTIES
291
292   We evaluated the performance of FILTER across several binary classification and regression tasks
293   for predicting physical properties. These evaluations were conducted using neural networks (NN),
294   XGBoost (XGB), and a combined model that integrates both approaches. A detailed comparison of
295   performance across all target properties is summarized in Table 3 in Section A.2 of the Appendix.
296
      As an example of each task type, we consider bioavailability and PSA predictions. All models exhib-
297
      ited strong performance predicting bioavailability with ROC AUC values above 0.90. The combined
298   model achieved the highest ROC AUC of 0.9104, slightly outperforming both individual models.
299   Additionally, it demonstrated high precision (0.9653), recall (0.8385), and an F1 score of 0.8975,
300   suggesting reliable performance in predicting bioavailability. For predicting polar surface area, XG-
301   Boost again provided the best results with an RMSE of 0.8453 and MAE of 0.4361. Although the
302   combined model reduced some prediction error compared to the neural network, XGBoost was the
303   most accurate for this task.
304   Overall, the combined model consistently provided the best results for binary classification tasks,
305   while XGBoost was the most effective for regression tasks. These findings demonstrate the flexibil-
306   ity and utility of FILTER in predicting both categorical and continuous molecular properties.

308   4.2   C LUSTERING BY S IAMESE N ETWORK E MBEDDINGS
309
310   Having established the effectiveness of FILTER in predicting physical properties, we next investi-
311   gated its capacity to capture more complex biological relationships through the use of ANTIV SNet
312   embeddings.
313   SNet Model Overview Redaction (YEARb). The SNet model utilizes Node2Vec Grover & Leskovec
314   (2016) to generate embeddings for drugs and antiviral peptides (AVPs) from a multigraph of drug-
315   protein and protein-protein interactions. Node2Vec performs random walks through the graph, cap-
316   turing topological and functional information about each node. The resulting embeddings map drugs
317   and AVPs into a continuous feature space where proximity between vectors represents similarity in
318   biological function.
319   The SNet is trained using these embeddings to predict the similarity between drug and AVP pairs.
320   The SNet consists of two identical subnetworks that process the drug and AVP embeddings in paral-
321   lel. During training, the model minimizes a contrastive loss function, encouraging the embeddings
322   of similar drug-AVP pairs to be close together, while pushing dissimilar pairs apart. This enables
323   the model to create meaningful embeddings that reflect the likelihood of a drug sharing antiviral
      properties with an AVP, such as inhibiting viral entry, fusion, or replication.

411   5   R ELATED W ORKS
412
413
      Several key studies have laid the foundation for these innovations. One of the contributions in
414   this area is the work by Zhang et al. (2019), which developed a Bayesian semi-supervised graph
415   convolutional neural network (GCN) for predicting molecular properties and improving uncertainty
416   quantification. Although the model showed strong performance in predicting bioactivity, it relied
417   heavily on a large labelled dataset. This approach presents challenges in early-stage drug discovery
418   where labelled data is scarce. Furthermore, while the Bayesian framework enhanced uncertainty
419   estimates, the model’s application was limited to molecular structures and lacked integration with
420   chemical retrosynthesis.
421   Similarly, Schor et al. (2022) introduced the deepFPlearn tool, a deep learning-based model de-
422   signed to predict chemical-gene associations. While deepFPlearn addressed the challenge of pre-
423   dicting chemical effects at a large scale by combining autoencoders and deep feed-forward neu-
424   ral networks (FNN), it suffered from limitations in capturing interactions between more complex
425   molecular architectures. Additionally, the performance of the tool was optimized for toxicology
426   applications and not for antibiotic design, thus limiting its applicability in drug discovery for novel
427   antibiotic compounds.
428   In recent years, fragment based drug design has been applied with two main strategies for antibi-
429   otic discovery: top-down and bottom-up. The top-down approach focuses on repurposing exist-
430   ing molecules, where existing drug-like molecules are incrementally pruned or refined to identify
431   key substructures with antibiotic potential. This method, exemplified by the discovery of Halicin,
      where AI was used to screen known chemical libraries, leading to the identification of a structurally


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      unique compounds with novel mechanisms of action, such as disrupting bacterial proton motive
433   force Stokes et al. (2020). This strategy streamlines the search for novel antibiotics by mining exist-
434   ing drug spaces and exploring their potential new applications. In contrast, the bottom-up approach,
435   which our team adopted, focuses on constructing new molecules from smaller fragments derived
436   from known antibiotics. This synthesis-driven method enables the exploration of structurally unique
437   molecules, expanding the chemical space beyond known antibiotics, enabling the construction of
438   structurally unique antibiotics with potentially different mechanisms of action. Using this approach,
439   new classes of antibiotics can be designed and tested in silico before laboratory validation, address-
440   ing the need for antibiotics with novel mechanisms to combat resistant strains.
441   Nicolaou (2014) highlighted advancements in synthetic organic chemistry, particularly in the repli-
442   cation and synthesis of complex bioactive molecules. While this work provides valuable insights
443   into organic synthesis, it focuses primarily on the manual design and synthesis of analogs without
444   leveraging computational tools to accelerate these processes. The study’s focus on the synthetic
445   methodology also limited its scope in terms of using AI to predict therapeutic efficacy and phys-
446   ical properties of molecules, a gap we aim to address by integrating AI-based retrosynthesis with
447
      predictive analytics.
448   The MoleculeNet benchmark, introduced by Wu et al. (2018), addresses the need for standardized
449   evaluation of molecular machine learning methods by curating multiple datasets and providing high-
450   quality implementations of molecular featurization and learning algorithms. While this benchmark
