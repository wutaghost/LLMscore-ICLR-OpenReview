009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Decomposing model activations into interpretable components is a key open prob-
012            lem in mechanistic interpretability. Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a popu-
013            lar method for decomposing the internal activations of trained transformers into
014            sparse, interpretable features, and have been applied to MLP layers and the resid-
015            ual stream. In this work we train SAEs on attention layer outputs and show that
016            also here SAEs find a sparse, interpretable decomposition. We demonstrate this
017            on transformers from several model families and up to 2B parameters. We per-
018            form a qualitative study of the features computed by attention layers, and find
019            multiple families: long-range context, short-range context and induction features.
020
               We qualitatively study the role of every head in GPT-2 Small, and estimate that at
               least 90% of the heads are polysemantic, i.e. have multiple unrelated roles. Fur-
021
               ther, we show that Sparse Autoencoders are a useful tool that enable researchers to
022
               explain model behavior in greater detail than prior work. For example, we explore
023            the mystery of why models have so many seemingly redundant induction heads,
024            use SAEs to motivate the hypothesis that some are long-prefix whereas others are
025            short-prefix, and confirm this with more rigorous analysis. We use our SAEs to
026            analyze the computation performed by the Indirect Object Identification circuit
027            (Wang et al. (2023)), validating that the SAEs find causally meaningful intermedi-
028            ate variables, and deepening our understanding of the semantics of the circuit. We
029            open-source the trained SAEs and a tool for exploring arbitrary prompts through
030            the lens of Attention Output SAEs.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Mechanistic interpretability aims to reverse engineer neural network computations into human-
036   understandable algorithms (Olah, 2022; Olah et al., 2020). A key sub-problem is to decompose
037   high dimensional activations into meaningful concepts, or features. If successful at scale, this re-
038   search would enable us to identify and debug model errors (Hernandez et al., 2022; Vig et al., 2020;
039   Gandelsman et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024), control and steer model behavior (Panickssery et al.,
040   2024; Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023), and better predict out-of-distribution behavior (Mu &
041   Andreas, 2020; Carter et al., 2019; Goh et al., 2021).
042   Prior work has successfully analyzed many individual model components, such as neurons and at-
043   tention heads. However, both neurons (Wang et al., 2023) and attention heads (Gould et al., 2023)
044   are often polysemantic (Olah et al., 2017): they appear to represent multiple unrelated concepts or
045   perform different functions depending on the input. Polysemanticity makes it challenging to inter-
046   pret the role of individual neurons or attention heads in the model’s overall computation, suggesting
047   the need for alternative units of analysis.
048   Our paper builds on literature using Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) to extract interpretable feature dic-
049   tionaries from the residual stream (Cunningham et al., 2023; Yun et al., 2023) and MLP activations
050   (Bricken et al., 2023). While these approaches have shown promise in disentangling activations into
051   interpretable features, attention layers have remained difficult to interpret. In this work, we apply
052   SAEs to reconstruct attention layer outputs, and develop a novel technique (weight based head
053   attribution) to associate learned features with specific attention heads. This allows us to sidestep
      challenges posed by polysemanticity (Section 2).

094

111

      3.3   E XPLORING F EATURE FAMILIES
233
234   In this section we more qualitatively show that Attention Output SAEs are interpretable by examin-
235   ing different feature families: groups of SAE features that share some common high-level charac-
236   teristic.
237
      We first evaluate 30 randomly sampled live features from SAEs across multiple models and layers
238
      (as described in Section 3.1) and report the percentage of features that are interpretable in Table 1.
239
      We notice that in all cases, the majority of live features are interpretable, often >80%. Note that this
240   is a small sample of features, and human judgment may be flawed. We list confidence intervals for
241   percentage of interpretable features in Appendix C.1.
242
243
      We now use our understanding of these extracted features to share deeper insights into the concepts
      attention layers learn. Attention Output SAEs enable us to taxonomize a large fraction of what
244
      these layers are doing based on feature families, giving us better intuitions about how transformers
245
      use attention layers in practice. Throughout our SAEs trained on multiple models, we repeatedly
246   find three common feature families: induction features (e.g. ”board” token is next by induction),
247   local context features (e.g. current sentence is a question, Figure 1), and high-level context features
248   (e.g. current text is about pets). All of these features involve moving prior information with the
249   context, consistent with the high-level conceptualization of the attention mechanism from Elhage
250   et al. (2021). We present these for illustrative purposes and do not expect these to nearly constitute
251   a complete set of feature families. We also share additional feature families discovered in GPT-2
252   Small in Appendix P.
253   To more rigorously understand these feature families, we performed a case study for each of these
254   features (similar to Bricken et al. (2023)). For brevity, we highlight a case study of an induction
255   feature below and leave the remaining to Appendix G and H.
256
257   Induction features. Our analysis revealed multiple ”induction features” across different models
258   studied. As we are not aware of any induction features extracted by MLP SAEs in prior work,
259   we hypothesize that induction features are uniquely computed by the attention layers (Bricken et al.,
260
      2023). In what follows, we showcase a “‘board’ is next by induction” feature from our L1 GELU-2L
      (Nanda, 2022b) SAE. However, we note that “board induction” is just one example from hundreds
261
      of “<token> is next by induction” features discovered by our analysis (see Appendix J). We also
262
      detail the feature’s upstream computations and downstream effects in Appendix F.
263
264   The ‘board’ induction feature activates on the second instance of <token> in prompts of the form
265   “<token> board . . . <token>”. To demonstrate ‘board induction’ is a genuinely monosemantic
266
      feature, we provide evidence that the feature is both: (i) specific and (ii) sensitive to this context
      (Bricken et al., 2023).

304
305   In this section we demonstrate that Attention Output SAEs are useful as general purpose inter-
306   pretability tools, allowing for novel insights about the role of attention layers in language models.
307   We first develop a technique that allows us to systematically interpret every attention head in a
308   model (Section 4.1), discovering new behaviors and gaining high-level insight into the phenomena
309   of attention head polysemanticity (Janiak et al., 2023; Gould et al., 2023). We then apply our SAEs
310   to make progress on the open question of why models have many seemingly redundant induction
311
      heads (Olsson et al., 2022), finding induction heads with subtly different behaviors: some primarily
      perform induction where there is a long prefix (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023) whereas others gen-
312
      erally perform short prefix induction (Section 4.2). Finally, we apply Attention Output SAEs to
313
      circuit analysis (Section 4.3), unveiling novel insights about the Indirect Object Identification circuit
314   (Wang et al., 2023) that were previously out-of-reach, and find causally relevant SAE features in the
315   process.

350   4.2   L ONG - PREFIX INDUCTION HEAD
351
352   In this section we apply Attention Output SAEs to make progress on a long-standing open question:
353   why do models have so many seemingly redundant induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022)? We
354   use our weight-based head attribution technique (see Section 4.1) to inspect the top SAE features
355   attributed to two different induction heads and find one which specializes in “long prefix induction”
356
      (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023), while the other primarily does “short prefix induction”.
357   As a case study, we focus on GPT-2 Small (Radford et al., 2019), which has two induction heads
358   in layer 5 (heads 5.1 and 5.5) (Wang et al., 2023). To distinguish between these two heads, we
359   qualitatively inspect the top ten SAE features attributed to both heads (as in Section 4.1) and look
360   for patterns. Glancing at the top features attributed to head 5.1 shows “long induction” features,
361   defined as features that activate on examples of induction with at least two repeated prefix matches
362
      (e.g. completing “... ABC ... AB” with C).
363   We now confirm this hypothesis with independent lines of evidence that don’t require SAEs. We
364   first generate synthetic induction datasets with random repeated tokens of varying prefix lengths. For
365   each dataset, we compute the induction score, defined as the average attention to the token which
366   induction would suggest comes next, for both heads. We confirm that while both induction scores
367   rise as we increase prefix length, head 5.1 has a much more dramatic phase change as we transition
368
      to long prefixes (i.e. ≥ 2 ) (Figure 3a).
369   We also find and intervene on real examples of long prefix induction from the training distribution,
370   corrupting them to only be one prefix by replacing the 2nd left repeated token (i.e ’A’ in ABC ...
371   AB) with a different, random token. We find that this intervention effectively causes head 5.1 to
372   stop doing induction, as its average induction score falls from 0.55 to 0.05. Head 5.5, meanwhile,
373   maintains an average induction score of 0.43 (Figure 3b).
374   Intuitively, head 5.1 can act as a ”tie-breaker” when there are multiple candidates for induction.
375   Consider the sequence ” ... BD ... ABC ... AB”. While head 5.5 might attend to both ”D” and
376   ”C”, head 5.1 can be used to more confidently predict the token ”C”. We provide evidence for this
377   in Appendix Q, where we find that 5.1 has significant causal effect in boosting the predictions of
      tokens in cases of long prefix (but not short prefix) induction.

396   distribution causes 5.1 to essentially stop attending to that token, while 5.5 continues to show an
397   induction attention pattern.

400   4.3                         A NALYZING THE IOI CIRCUIT WITH ATTENTION O UTPUT SAE S
401
402   We now show that Attention Output SAEs are useful tools for circuit analysis. In the process, we also
403   go beyond early work to find evidence that our SAEs find causally relevant intermediate variables.
404   As a case study, we apply our SAEs to the widely studied Indirect Object Identification circuit (Wang
405   et al., 2023), and find that our SAEs improve upon attention head interpretability based techniques
406   from prior work.
407   The Indirect Object Identification (IOI) task (Wang et al., 2023) is to complete sentences like “After
408   John and Mary went to the store, John gave a bottle of milk to” with “ Mary” rather than “ John”.
409   We refer to the repeated name (John) as S (the subject) and the non-repeated name (Mary) as IO
410   (the indirect object). For each choice of the IO and S names, there are two prompt templates: one
411   where the IO name comes first (the ’ABBA’ template) and one where it comes second (the ’BABA’
412   template).
413   Wang et al. (2023) analyzed this circuit by localizing and interpreting several classes of attention
414   heads. They argue that the circuit implements the following algorithm:
415
416
                                  1. Induction heads and Duplicate token heads identify that S is duplicated. They write infor-
                                     mation to indicate that this token is duplicated, as well as “positional signal” pointing to
417
                                     the S1 token.
418
                                  2. S-inhibition heads route this information from S2 to END via V-composition (Elhage et al.,
419                                  2021). They output both token and positional signals that cause the Name mover heads to
420                                  attend less to S1 (and thus more to IO) via Q-composition (Elhage et al., 2021).
421                               3. Name mover heads attend strongly to the IO position and copy, boosting the logits of the
422                                  IO token that they attend to.
423
      Although Wang et al. (2023) find that “positional signal” originating from the induction heads is a
424
      key aspect of this circuit, they don’t figure out the specifics of what this signal is, and ultimately
425   leave this mystery as one of the “most interesting future directions” of their work. Attention Output
426   SAEs immediately reveal the positional signal by decomposing these activations into interpretable
427   features. We find that rather than absolute or relative position between S tokens, the positional signal
428   is actually whether the duplicate name comes after the “ and” token that connects “John and Mary”.
429
430   Identifying the positional features: To generate this hypothesis, we localized and interpreted
431   causally relevant SAE features from the outputs of the attention layers that contain induction heads
      (Layers 5 and 6) with zero ablations. For now we focus on our Layer 5 SAE, and leave other layers

436
      combination of SAE feature directions plus an error term, as in (1). We then zero ablate each feature,
      one at a time, and record the resulting change in logit difference between the IO and S tokens. This

478   5   R ELATED W ORK
479
480   Mechanistic Interpretability. Mechanistic interpretability research aims to reverse engineer neu-
481   ral network computations into human-understandable algorithms (Olah, 2022; Olah et al., 2020).
482   Prior mechanistic interpretability work has identified computation subgraphs of models that imple-
483   ment tasks (Wang et al., 2023; Hanna et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023), found interpretable, reoc-
484   curring model components over models of multiple sizes (Olsson et al., 2022; Gould et al., 2023),
485   and reverse-engineered how toy tasks are carried out in small transformers (Nanda et al., 2023a;
      Chughtai et al., 2023). Some have successfully interpreted attention heads (McDougall et al., 2023;


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), though the issue has been raised that heads are often polyse-
487   mantic (Gould et al., 2023; Janiak et al., 2023), and may not be the correct unit of analysis (Jermyn
488   et al., 2023). Our technique goes beyond prior work by decomposing the outputs of the entire at-
489   tention layer into finer-grained linear features, without assuming that heads are the right unit of
490   analysis.
491
      Induction heads (Elhage et al., 2021) have been studied extensively by Olsson et al. (2022), who
492
      first observed that LLMs had many, seemingly redundant induction heads. Goldowsky-Dill et al.
493   (2023) investigated two induction heads in a 2-layer attention-only model, and discovered the ”long
494   induction” (long-prefix induction) variant in both heads. In contrast, we find that two different
495   induction heads specialize in long-prefix and short-prefix induction respectively in GPT-2 Small. In
496   concurrent work, Ge et al. (2024) also find “ and”-related feature in the IOI task. We causally verify
497   the hypotheses of how “ and” features behave in IOI and rule out alternative hypotheses.
498
499   Classical Dictionary Learning. Elad (2010) explores how both discrete and continuous represen-
500   tations can involve more representations than basis vectors, and surveys various techniques for ex-
501   tracting and reconstructing these representations. Traditional sparse coding algorithms (Olshausen
502
      & Field, 1997; Aharon et al., 2006) employ expectation-maximization, while contemporary ap-
      proaches (Gregor & LeCun, 2010; Barello et al.) based on gradient descent and autoencoders have
503
      built upon these ideas.
504
505   Sparse Autoencoders. Motivated by the hypothesized phenomenon of supersition (Elhage et al.,
506   2022), recent work has applied dictionary learning, specifically sparse autoencoders (Ng, 2011), to
507   LMs in order to interpret their activations (Subramanian et al., 2017; Sharkey et al., 2022; Cun-
508   ningham et al., 2023; Yun et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024). Our feature
509   interpretability methodology was inspired by Bricken et al. (2023), though we additionally study
510   how features are computed upstream with direct feature attribution (Nanda et al., 2023b;c). Progress
511   is rapid, with the following parallel work occurring within the last two months: Rajamanoharan
512   et al. (2024) took a similar approach to our work and scaled attention output SAEs up to 7B models.
513
      Marks et al. (2024) also successfully used multiple types of SAEs including attention for finer-
      grained circuit discovery with gradient based patching techniques. In contrast, we use both causal
514
      interventions and DFA, exploiting the linear structure of the attention mechanism.
515

      6     C ONCLUSION
518
519
520   In this work, we have introduced Attention Output SAEs, and demonstrated their effectiveness in
521   decomposing attention layer outputs into sparse, interpretable features (Section 3). We have also
522   highlighted the promise of Attention Output SAEs as a general purpose interpretability tool (Sec-
523   tion 4). Our analysis identified novel and extant attention head motifs (Section 4.1), advanced our
524
      understanding of apparently ‘redundant’ induction heads (Section 4.2), and improved upon attention
      head circuit interpretability techniques from prior work (Section 4.3).
525
526
527   6.1   L IMITATIONS
528
529   Our work focuses on understanding attention outputs, which we consider to be a valuable contri-
530   bution. However, we leave much of the transformer unexplained, such as the QK circuits (Elhage
531   et al., 2021) by which attention patterns are computed. Further, though we scale up to a 2B model,
532   our work was mostly performed on the 100M parameter GPT-2 Small model. Exploring Attention
533   Output SAEs on larger models in depth is thus a natural direction of future work.
534   We also highlight some methodological limitations. While we try to validate our conclusions with
535   multiple independent lines of evidence, our research often relies on qualitative investigations and
536   subjective human judgment. Additionally, like all sparse autoencoder research, our work depends
537   on both the assumptions made by the SAE architecture, and the quality of the trained SAEs. SAEs
538   represent the sparse, linear components of models’ computation, and hence may provide an incom-
539   plete picture of how to interpret attention layers (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024). Our SAEs achieve
      reasonable reconstruction accuracy (Table 1), though they are far from perfect.

782   B    SAE T RAINING : HYPERPARAMETERS AND OTHER DETAILS
783
784   Important details of SAE training include:
785
786         • SAE Widths. Our GELU-2L and Gemma-2B SAEs have width 16384. All of our GPT-2
787           Small SAEs have width 24576, with the exception of layers 5 and 7, which have width
788           49152.
789         • Loss Function. We trained our Gemma-2B SAE with a different loss function than the
790           SAEs from other models. For Gemma-2B we closely follow the approach from Olah et al.
791           (2024), while for GELU-2L and GPT-2 Small, we closely follow the approach from Bricken
792           et al. (2023).
793         • Training Data. We use activations from hundreds of millions to billions of activations
794           from LM forward passes as input data to the SAE. Following Nanda (2023b), we use a
795           shuffled buffer of these activations, so that optimization steps don’t use data from highly
796           correlated activations. For GELU-2L we use a mixture of 80% from the C4 Corpus (Raffel
797           et al., 2023) and 20% code (https://huggingface.co/datasets/NeelNanda/
798           c4-code-tokenized-2b). For GPT-2 Small we use OpenWebText (https://
799           huggingface.co/datasets/Skylion007/openwebtext). For Gemma-2B we
800
              use https://huggingface.co/datasets/HuggingFaceFW/fineweb. The
              input activations have sequence length of 128 tokens for all training runs.
801
802         • Resampling. For our GELU-2L and GPT-2 Small SAEs we used resampling, a technique
803           which at a high-level reinitializes features that activate extremely rarely on SAE inputs
804           periodically throughout training. We mostly follow the approach described in the ‘Neuron
805           Resampling’ appendix of Bricken et al. (2023), except we reapply learning rate warm-
806
              up after each resampling event, reducing learning rate to 0.1x the ordinary value, and,
              increasing it with a cosine schedule back to the ordinary value over the next 1000 training
807
              steps. Note we don’t do this for Gemma-2B.
808
809         • Optimizer hyperparameters. For the GELU-2L and GPT-2 Small SAEs we use the Adam
              optimizer with β2 = 0.99 and β1 = 0.9 and a learning rate of roughly 0.001. For Gemma-

826   C       M ETHODOLOGY FOR FEATURE INTERPRETABILITY
827
828   To evaluate interpretability for Attention Output SAE features, we manually rate the interpretability
829   of a set of randomly sampled SAE features. For each SAE, the two raters (paper authors) collectively
830   inspected 30 randomly sampled features.4 .
831   To assess a feature, the rater determined if there was a clear explanation for the feature’s behavior.
832   The rater viewed the top 20 maximum activating dataset examples for that feature, approximate di-
833   rect logit effects (i.e. WU WO di ), and randomly sampled activating examples from lower activation
834   ranges (as in Bricken et al. (2023)). The dataset used for each SAE consists of 10 million tokens
835   sampled from the SAE training distribution.
836   For each max activating dataset example, we also show the corresponding source tokens with the
837   top direct feature attribution by source position (Section 2), and additionally show the weight based
838   head attribution for all heads in that layer (Section 2). The raters used an interface based on an
839   open source SAE visualizer library (McDougall, 2024) modified to support attention layer outputs
840   (see Appendix A). Note that we filter out dead features (features that don’t activate at least once in
841   100,000 inputs, sometimes also referred to as ”ultra low frequency cluster”) from our interpretability
842   analysis. These features were excluded from the denominator in reporting percentage interpretable
843   in Table 1.
844   The raters had a relatively high bar for labeling a feature as interpretable (e.g. noticing a clear
845   pattern with all 20 max activating dataset examples, as well as throughout the randomly sampled ac-
846   tivations). However, we note that this methodology heavily relies on subjective human judgement,
847   and thus there is always room for error. We expect both false positives (e.g. the raters are overcon-
848   fident in their interpretations, despite the feature actually being polysemantic) and false negatives
849   (e.g. the raters might miss more abstract features that are hard to spot with our feature dashboards).

       In Section 3.3 we found a monosemantic SAE feature that represents that the ”board” token is next
       by induction. In this section we show that we can also understand its causal downstream effects, as
997
       well as how it’s computed by upstream components.
998
999    We first demonstrate that the presence of this feature has an interpretable causal effect on the outputs:
1000   we find that this feature is primarily used to directly predict the ”board” token. We start by analyzing
1001   the approximate direct logit effect: WU WO di where di is this feature direction. We clearly see that
1002
       the “board” token is the top logit in Figure 6a.
1003   This interpretation is also corroborated by feature ablation experiments. Across all activating dataset
1004   examples over 10 million tokens, we splice in our Attention Output SAE at Layer 1 of the model
1005   (the last layer of GELU-2L), ablate the board induction feature, and record the effect on loss. We
1006   find that 82% of total loss increase from ablating this feature is explained by examples where board
1007   is the correct next token.
1008   Finally, we demonstrate that we can understand how this feature is computed by upstream compo-
1009   nents. We first show that this feature is almost entirely produced by attention head 1.6, an induction
1010   head (Nanda, 2022a). Over 10 million tokens, we compute the direct feature attribution by head (see
1011   (3)) for this feature. We find that head 1.6 stands out with 94% fraction of variance explained.
1012   Going further upstream, we now show that 1.6 is copying prior ”board” tokens to activate this
1013   feature. We apply DFA by source position (see Section 2) for all feature activations over 10 million
1014   tokens and record aggregate scores for each source token. We find that the majority of variance is
1015   explained by “board” source tokens. This effect is stronger if we filter for feature activations above
1016   a certain threshold, reaching over 99.9% at a threshold of 5, mirroring results from Bricken et al.
1017   (2023) that there’s more polysemanticity in lower ranges. We note that this ”copying” is consistent
1018   with our understanding of the induction (Olsson et al., 2022) algorithm.

1121
1122   In Section 3 we claimed that our Attention Output SAEs are sparse, faithful, and interpretable and
1123   we provide evaluations of each SAE in Table 1 to support this claim. In this section we further

1125
1126   We note that we evaluated fidelity with the cross entropy loss relative to zero ablation (4), which
1127   has a few potential pitfalls. First, some would argue that zero ablation may be too harsh a baseline,

1129   choose to use zero ablation to stay consistent with prior work from Bricken et al. (2023), which
1130
       made our preliminary results easier to evaluate.
1131   Second, the zero ablation baseline makes it’s hard to compare the quality of SAEs between other
1132   sites. Intuitively, zero ablating the residual stream should degrade performance much more than
1133   ablating a single attention layer or MLP, so we expect that SAEs trained on the residual stream will

1217            for that feature occurring in the example, the feature activates on the preceding token (i.e.

1220   A following the first instance of an AB pattern) the induction pass rate for the feature. The heuristic

       In this section we present new feature families that we found in GPT-2 Small, but did not find
       in the GELU-2L SAE6 . This suggests that SAEs are a useful tool that can provide hints about
1547
       fundamentally different capabilities as we apply them to bigger models.
1548
1549   Duplicate Token Features. In our Layer 3 SAE, we find many features which activate on repeated
1550   tokens. However, unlike induction features (Section 3.3), these have high direct feature attribution
1551   (by source position) to the previous instance of that token (rather than the token following the pre-
1552   vious instance).
1553
       We also notice that the norms of the decoder weights corresponding to head 3.0, identified as a
1554
       duplicate token head by Wang et al, stand out. This shows that, similar to the induction feature,
1555   we can use weight based attribution (2) to heads with previously known mechanisms to suggest the
1556   existence of certain feature families and vice versa.
1557
1558   Successor Features. In our Layer 9 SAE, we find features that activate in sequences of numbers,
1559   dates, letters, etc. The DFA by source position also suggests that the attention layer is looking back
1560   at the previous item(s) to compute this feature (Figure 16a).
1561
       The top logits of these features are also interpretable, suggesting that these features boost the next
1562   item in the sequence. Finally, the decoder weight norms also suggest that they heavily rely on head
1563   9.1, a successor head in GPT-2 Small.

       S.1   L IMITATIONS ON INTERPRETING ALL HEADS IN GPT-2 S MALL
1729
1730   We note that this methodology is a rough heuristic to get a sense of the most salient effects of a head
1731   and likely does not capture their role completely.
1732
       Through this technique we discover a wide range of previously unidentified behaviors. To validate
1733
       that our technique captures legitimate phenomena rather than spurious behaviors, we verified that our
1734   interpretations are consistent with previously studied heads in GPT-2 Small. These include induction
1735   heads (Olsson et al., 2022; Kissane, 2023), previous token heads (Voita et al., 2019; Kissane, 2023),
1736   successor heads (Gould et al., 2023) and duplicate token heads (Wang et al., 2023; Kissane, 2023).

1738   S.2   OVERVIEW OF ATTENTION HEADS IN LAYERS IN GPT-2 S MALL
1739
1740   Broadly, we observe that top features attributed to heads become more abstract towards the middle
1741   layers of the model before tapering off to syntactic features in late layers:
1742
1743          • Layers 0-3 exhibit primarily syntactic features (single-token features bigram features) and
1744
                secondarily on specific verbs and entity fragments. Some context tracking features are also
                present.
1745
1746          • From layer 4 onwards, features that activate on more complex grammatical structure are
1747            expressed, including families of related active verbs, prescriptive and active assertions, and
1748            some entity characterizations. Some single-token and bigram syntactic features continue to
1749
                be present.
1750          • In layers 5-6, we identify 2 out of the 3 known induction heads Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023)
1751            in these layers based on our features. However, the rest of these layers is less interpretable
1752            through the lens of SAE features.
1753          • In layers 7-8, increasingly more complex concept feature groups are present, such as phras-
1754            ings related to specific actions taken, reasoning and justification related phrases, grammat-
1755            ical compound phrases, and time and distance relationships.
1756          • Layer 9 expressed some of the most complex concepts, with heads focused on specific
1757            concepts and related groups of concepts.
1758
              • Layer 10 exhibited complex concept groups, with heads focused on assertions about a
1759            physical or spatial property, and counterfactual and timing/tense assertions.
1760
              • The last layer 11 exhibited mostly grammatical adjustments, some bigram completions and
1761
                one head focused on long-range context tracking.
1762
1763   Although the above summarizes what was distinctive about each layer, later layers continued to
1764   express syntactic features (e.g. single token features, URL completion) and simple context tracking
1765   features (e.g. news articles).
