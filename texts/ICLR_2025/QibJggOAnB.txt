008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Algorithmic fairness in clustering aims to balance the proportions of instances
011            assigned to each cluster with respect to a given sensitive attribute. Recently, nu-
012            merous algorithms have been developed for Fair Clustering (FC), most of which
013            optimize a clustering objective under specifically designed fairness constraints.
014            However, the inherent complexity or approximation of constrained optimization
015            problems makes it challenging to achieve the optimal trade-off between fairness
016
               level and clustering utility in practice. For example, the obtained clustering utility
               by an existing FC algorithm might be suboptimal, or achieving a certain fairness
017
               level could be numerically unstable. To resolve these limitations, we propose a
018
               new FC algorithm based on a novel decomposition of the fair K-means clustering
019            objective function. The proposed algorithm, called Fair Clustering via Alignment
020            (FCA), operates by (i) finding a joint probability distribution to align the data from
021            different protected groups, and (ii) optimizing cluster centers in the aligned space.
022            A key advantage of FCA is that it guarantees (local) optimal clustering utility for
023            any given fairness level while avoiding the need to solve complex constrained
024            optimization problems, thereby obtaining (local) optimal fair clustering in practice.
025            Experiments show that FCA offers several empirical benefits over existing methods
026            such as (i) attaining the optimal trade-off between fairness level and clustering
027
               utility, and (ii) achieving near-perfect fairness level without numerical instability.
028
029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As artificial intelligence (AI) technology has advanced and been successfully applied to diverse
032   domains and tasks, the requirement for AI systems to make fair decisions (i.e., algorithmic fairness)
033   has emerged as an important issue. This requirement is particularly necessary when observed data
034   possess historical biases with respect to specific sensitive attributes, leading to unfair outcomes of
035   learned models based on such biased data (Angwin et al., 2016; Ingold & Soper, 2016; Damodaran
036   et al., 2018; Mehrabi et al., 2019). Specifically, group fairness is a category within algorithmic fairness
037
      that ensures models do not discriminate against certain protected groups, which are defined by specific
      sensitive attributes (e.g., men vs. women, white vs. black). In response, a large amount of researches
038
      have been conducted to develop algorithms for mitigating such biases in various supervised learning
039
      tasks such as classification (Zafar et al., 2017; Donini et al., 2018; Agarwal et al., 2018; Quadrianto
040   et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020) and regression (Agarwal et al., 2019; Chzhen et al., 2020).
041
042   Along with supervised learning, algorithmic fairness for unsupervised learning tasks including cluster-
043
      ing has also received much attention. Clustering algorithms have long been employed as fundamental
      unsupervised learning methods in various fields of machine learning, such as recommendation sys-
044
      tems (Ahuja et al., 2019; Widiyaningtyas et al., 2021), image processing (Le, 2013; Guo et al., 2020;
045
      Mittal et al., 2022), and language modeling (Butnaru & Ionescu, 2017; Zhang et al., 2023).
046
047
      Related works for Fair Clustering (FC) Combining algorithmic fairness and clustering, the notion
048   of Fair Clustering (FC) has been initially introduced in Chierichetti et al. (2017). FC operates under
049   the setting that each data point is assigned a color (i.e., a sensitive attribute), with the goal that the
050   proportion of each color within each cluster should be similar to that in the population. To achieve
051   this goal, various algorithms have been developed to minimize a given clustering objective under
052   pre-specified fairness constraints (Bera et al., 2019; Kleindessner et al., 2019; Backurs et al., 2019; Li
053   et al., 2020; Esmaeili et al., 2021; Ziko et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023), to name a few. Section A.5 of
      Appendix covers other fairness notions, including proportional fairness and individual fairness.

054
      We can roughly categorize the existing FC algorithms into three: (i) pre-processing, (ii) in-processing,
055   and (iii) post-processing. Pre-processing methods (Chierichetti et al., 2017; Backurs et al., 2019)
056   involve transforming instances into a fair space based on the concept of fairlets. Fairlets are small
057   subsets that satisfy (perfect) fairness, and thus performing standard clustering on the fairlet space
058   yields a fair clustering (see Section A.1 of Appendix for details). In-processing methods (Kleindessner
059   et al., 2019; Ziko et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2023) aim to simultaneously find both
060   the cluster centers and assignments of the optimal fair clustering by solving certain constrained
061   optimization problems. Post-processing methods (Bera et al., 2019; Harb & Lam, 2020) focus on
062   finding fair assignments given fixed cluster centers. The fixed cluster centers are typically determined
063
      by a standard clustering algorithm.
064
065   Our contributions In this paper, we focus on the optimal trade-off between fairness level and
066   clustering utility, which aims to maximize clustering utility under a given fairness level. While the
067
      trade-off between fairness level and clustering utility is inevitable (Bertsimas et al., 2011; Chhabra
      et al., 2021), achieving the optimal trade-off with existing FC algorithms remains challenging. For
068
      instance, pre- or post-processing algorithms usually result in suboptimal clustering utility due to
069
      indirect maximization of clustering utility (e.g., Backurs et al. (2019); Esmaeili et al. (2021)). Even
070   when designed for achieving the optimal trade-off, in-processing algorithms may have trouble due to
071   numerical instability, particularly when a given fairness level is high (e.g., Ziko et al. (2021)).
072
073
      This paper aims to address these challenges by developing a new in-processing algorithm that
      can achieve the optimal trade-off between fairness level and clustering utility without numerical
074
      instability. The primary idea of our proposed algorithm is to align data from two protected groups
075
      by transforming them into a common space (called the aligned space) and then applying a standard
076   clustering algorithm in the aligned space. We prove that the optimal fair clustering, i.e., the clustering
077   with minimal clustering cost under a given fairness constraint, is equivalent to the optimal clustering
078   in the aligned space.
079
      Based on the theoretical result, we devise a new FC algorithm, called Fair Clustering via Alignment
080
      (FCA). FCA alternately finds the aligned space and the optimal clustering in the aligned space until
081
      convergence. To find the aligned space, we develop a modified version of an algorithm for finding the
082   optimal transport map (Kantorovich, 2006), while a standard clustering algorithm can be used to find
083   the optimal clustering in the aligned space.
084
085
      It is worth noting that our proposed algorithm, FCA, can be compared with the fairlet-based methods
      (e.g., Backurs et al. (2019)). While existing fairlet-based methods find fairlets and perform clustering
086
      sequentially (separately), FCA finds the aligned space and performs clustering simultaneously to
087
      obtain the (local) optimal fair clustering. A detailed comparison is provided in Remark 3.2.
088
089   The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows.
090          • We provide a novel decomposition of the fair clustering cost into two components: (i) the
091            transport cost with respect to a joint distribution between two protected groups, and (ii) the
092            clustering cost with respect to cluster centers in the aligned space.
093          • Based on the decomposition, we develop a novel FC algorithm called FCA (Fair Clustering
094            via Alignment), which is transparent, stable, and guarantees convergence.
095          • Theoretically, we prove that FCA achieves the optimal trade-off between fairness level and
096            clustering utility.
097          • Experimentally, we show that FCA (i) outperforms existing baseline FC algorithms in terms
098
               of both the trade-off and numerical stability, and (ii) optimally controls the trade-off across
               various fairness levels.

      3     R EFORMULATION OF THE FAIR CLUSTERING OBJECTIVE
147
148   This section presents our main theoretical contribution: a novel decomposition of the perfectly fair
149   (i.e., Balance = 1) clustering objective, which motivates our proposed FCA algorithm. The case of
150   Balance < 1 is considered in Section 4 after introducing FCA algorithm for perfect fairness.
151
      In Section 3.1, we introduce our idea through discussing the simplest case where the two protected
152
      groups are of equal size (n0 = n1 ). Then, in Section 3.2, we generalize to the unequal case (n0 ̸= n1 )
153
      by introducing the notions of the alignment map and aligned space, constructed by a given joint
154   distribution on X0 × X1 . We prove that there exists a joint distribution such that the objective function
155   of perfectly fair clustering can be decomposed into the sum of the transport cost with respect to
156   the joint distribution and the clustering cost on the aligned space. Full proofs of all the theoretical
157   findings in this section are given in Section B of Appendix.

      4.3      R EDUCING COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
350
351   As discussed in Section 4.1, finding the joint distribution Q (i.e., the optimal coupling Γ) is technically
352   equivalent to solving the Kantorovich problem, which involves linear programming (Villani, 2008).
353   Its computational complexity is approximately O(n3 ) when n0 = n1 = n (Bonneel et al., 2011),
354   indicating a high computational cost when n is large. To address this issue in practice, we randomly
355   split each group into L partitions of (approximately) same sizes. That is, we have L partitions for
                        (l)                           (l) L
356   each group: {X0 }L    l=1 of X0 and {X1 }l=1 of X1 . We then calculate the optimal coupling Γ
                                                                                                               (l)

357               (l)         (l)
      between X0 and X1 , using eq. (4). The (full) optimal coupling between X0 and X1 is estimated
358                                                         (l)     (l)
      by Γ̂ := diag( L1 Γ(1) , . . . , L1 Γ(L) ). Let m = |X0 | + |X1 | be the partition size. This technique can
359
      theoretically reduce computational complexity by O(n/m)×O(m3 ) = O(nm2 ). In our experiments,
360
      we apply this technique with m = 1024 for all datasets, which we find to be significantly faster than
361   using full datasets, while yielding similar performance (see Section 5.4 for the results).

529   6     C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
530
531   This paper has proposed FCA, an in-processing algorithm for fair clustering. FCA is motivated by the
532   theoretical result that finding the optimal perfectly fair clustering is equivalent to finding centers in the
533   aligned space. FCA algorithm is based on two well-known algorithms, the K-means++ algorithm and
534   linear programming, making it transparent and stable. We have empirically demonstrated that FCA
535   achieves near-perfect fairness and superior clustering utility, when compared to existing methods.
536
      Moreover, FCA is robust to the choice of data pre-processing method, optimization algorithm, and
      initialization. Additionally, we developed FCA-C, a variant of FCA, to control Balance.
537
538   Possible directions for future work are extending FCA for multiple protected groups (see Section
539   A.3 of Appendix for a feasible approach), or applying FCA to other clustering algorithms such as
      Gaussian mixture, which we will pursue in the near future.

      A     S UPPLEMENTARY DISCUSSION
811

813
      A.1    FAIRLET- BASED METHODS
814
      Fairlet decomposition was first introduced in Chierichetti et al. (2017), providing a pre-processing
815
      method for fair clustering. A fairlet is defined as a subset (as small as possible) of data in which the
816
      proportion of the sensitive attribute is balanced within all subsets. It is important to note that the
817   fairlets are not built arbitrarily; instead, the sum of distances among instances within each fairlet
818   is minimized (i.e., each fairlet consists of similar instances). Finding such fairlets can be done by
819   solving the minimum cost flow problem.
820
      Notably, when n0 = n1 , building fairlets is equivalent to finding the optimal coupling Γ in the
821
      Kantorovich problem, which can be understood as a special case of minimum cost problem (Peyré &
822
      Cuturi, 2020; Chen et al., 2022). Hence, we can say that the fairlet-based methods find the coupling
823   only once, then apply the standard clustering algorithm. On the contrary, our proposed approach
824   jointly optimizes both the coupling (i.e., the matching map T) and the cluster centers, to ensure that
825   they correspond to each other optimally.
826
      After building the fairlets, each fairlet is then deterministically assigned to a cluster. Since fairness
827
      is implicitly guaranteed within each fairlet, the resulting clustering directly becomes also fair. The
828
      representatives of each fairlet can be arbitrarily chosen when n0 = n1 , or chosen by the medoids (or
829   possibly the centroids) when n0 ̸= n1 , as suggested in Chierichetti et al. (2017). Then, a standard
830   clustering algorithm is applied to the set of these chosen representatives. However, these choices of
831   representatives result in approximation and suboptimality in view of clustering utility.
832
      On the other hand, the computational cost is high, with most of the time spent finding the fairlets due
833
      to the quadratic complexity of the minimum cost flow problem. To address this issue, SFC (Backurs
834
      et al., 2019) has proposed a scalable algorithm for fairlet decomposition by using a reduction approach
835   using metric embedding and trees. See Section C.2.2 for details on the SFC algorithm.
836
837
838   A.2    O PTIMAL TRANSPORT PROBLEM
839
840   The notion of Optimal Transport (OT) provides a geometric view of the discrepancy between two
841
      probability measures. For two given probability measures P1 and P2 , a map T : Supp(P1 ) →
      Supp(P2 ) is defined as ‘transport map’ if T# P1 = P2 , where T# P1 (A) = P1 (T−1 (A)), ∀A, is
842
      the push-forward measure. The OT map is the minimizer of transport cost among all transport maps.
843
      That is, the OT map from P1 to P2 is the solution of minT:T# P1 =P2 EX∼P1 (c (X, T(X))) for a
844   pre-specified cost function c (e.g., L2 distance), which is so-called the Monge problem.
845
846   Kantorovich relaxed the Monge problem by seeking the optimal coupling (joint distribu-
847
      tion) between two distributions. The Kantorovich problem is mathematically formulated as
      inf π∈Π(P1 ,P2 ) EX,Y∼π (c(X, Y)) where Π(P1 , P2 ) is the set of all joint measures of P1 and P2 . For
848
      two empirical measures, this problem can be solved by the use of linear programming as follows. For

992
       Proportional fairness Proportional fairness, initially introduced by Chen et al. (2019), is a fairness
993    notion that ensures that cluster sizes are proportionally balanced. It aims to find a clustering, where
994    any group of at least n/K data points should not be able to form a new cluster center that improves
995    the clustering cost for all points in the group. The primary focus in proportionally fair clustering is to
996    study the approximation errors. For example, Micha & Shah (2020) provided a better approximation
997    error than that of the algorithm from Chen et al. (2019), while
                                                                    √ Aziz et al. (2024) showed that there
998    exists a proportionally fair clustering that achieves a (1 + 2)-approximation.
999
1000   Individual fairness Individual fairness, which was initially introduced by Jung et al. (2019), is a
1001   fairness notion that ensures that every data point has an assigned cluster center within the radius of
1002   the smallest ball around the data point containing at least n/K points. Initial studies by Jung et al.
1003   (2019) focused on approximation guarantees for individually fair clustering. More recently, Mahabadi
1004
       & Vakilian (2020) studied more general approximation errors, while Han et al. (2022) considered a
       specific scenario where outliers exist in given data.
1005
1006   Notably, there have been several efforts to explore the relationships between different fairness notions
1007   in clustering. For example, Kellerhals & Peters (2024) demonstrated that algorithms for individual
1008   fairness and proportional fairness can be compatible for some sense. We believe that investigating
1009   further connections among various fairness notions including group, proportional, and individual
1010
       fairness would be a promising direction for future research.

1369   fair clustering (assignments) using FCA-C with fixed cluster centers (found by an approximation

1375   by minimizing the clustering cost given the assignment functions, it follows that: cost of fair clustering
1376   obtained by FCA-C (after one cluster center update) ≤ cost of fair clustering obtained by FCA-C (for
1377   fixed cluster centers). This implies that the fair clustering obtained by FCA-C with updated cluster
1378   centers also has the (τ + 2)-approximation guarantee.

       Scalable Fair Clustering (SFC) (Backurs et al., 2019) We directly use the official source code
       of SFC, available on the authors’ GitHub2 . SFC provides a fast and scalable algorithm for fairlet
1452
       decomposition, which builds the fairlets in nearly linear time. The given data are first embedded
1453
       to a tree structure (hierarchically well-separated tree; HST) using probabilistic metric embedding,
1454   where we seek for optimal edges (as well as their nodes) to be activated that satisfy Balance ≈ 1.
1455   Then, the linked nodes are then aggregated as a fairlet. This process can build fairlets in nearly linear
1456   time while minimizing the cost of building them. After building these fairlets using SFC, we apply
1457

1462   source code of FCBC, available on the authors’ GitHub3 . FCBC maximizes Balance under a cost
1463   constraint, where the cost constraint is defined by the Price of Fairness (PoF) - ‘cost of fair clustering
1464   (the solution) / cost of standard clustering’. However, since the authors mentioned the constrained
1465   optimization problem is NP-hard, they reduced the problem to a post-processing approach (i.e., fairly
1466   assigning data under the cost constraint, with pre-specified centers opened by a standard clustering
1467   algorithm). We set the value of PoF to 1.2 to achieve Balance ≈ 1.

       post-processing approach to fairly assign data from each protected group to given cluster centers
       found by a standard clustering algorithm. In other words, the fair assignment problem is solved at
1472
       each iteration of standard clustering algorithm.

       C.3.2     A PPLICABILITY TO VISUAL CLUSTERING (S ECTION 5.3)
1567
1568   Settings: datasets, baselines, and measures R EVERSE MNIST is a mixture of two image digit
1569   datasets: the original MNIST and a color-reversed version (where black and white are swapped).
1570   O FFICE -31 is a mixture of two datasets from two domains (amazon and webcam) with 31 classes.
1571   Both datasets are used in state-of-the-art visual FC methods (Li et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2023).
1572   For the baseline, we consider a state-of-the-art FC method in vision domain called FCMI from Zeng
1573   et al. (2023). FCMI learns a fair autoencoder with two additional loss terms: (i) clustering loss on
1574   the latent space and (ii) mutual information between latent vector and color. While FCMI is an
1575   end-to-end method that learns the fair latent vector and perform clustering on the fair latent space
1576   simultaneously, FCA is applied to a pre-trained latent space obtained by learning an autoencoder with
1577   the reconstruction loss only. We also report the performances of DFC (Li et al., 2020) which was the
1578
       main baseline in the FCMI paper, along with SFC (Backurs et al., 2019) and VFC (Ziko et al., 2021),
       even though these two methods are not specifically designed for the vision domain.
1579
1580   The clustering performance for the two image datasets is evaluated by two classification measures ACC
1581   (accuracy calculated based on assigned cluster indices and ground-truth labels) and NMI (Normalized
1582   Mutual Information between ground-truth label distribution and assigned cluster distribution), which
1583   are consistently used in Li et al. (2020); Zeng et al. (2023), as datasets involve ground-truth labels
1584
       (e.g., 0, 1, . . . , 9 for R EVERSE MNIST and the 31 classes for O FFICE -31). The fairness performance
       is evaluated by Balance.
1585
1586
       Results Table 3 in the main body shows that FCA performs similar to FCMI, which is the state-of-
1587
       the-art, while outperforming the other baselines with large margins in terms of Balance. Note that
1588
       SFC, VFC, and FCA are two-step methods, i.e., find fair clustering on the pre-trained (fair-unaware)
1589   latent space, and FCA is the best among those. Furthermore, on the other hand, DFC and FCMI are
1590   end-to-end methods so it is surprising that FCA outperforms DFC and performs similarly to FCMI.

       C.3.9   P ERFORMANCE COMPARISON ON A LARGE DATASET
1945
1946   In this section, we evaluate FCA’s scalability for larger datasets, while the main experiments in
1947   Section 5 are conducted on real datasets with sample sizes of around 20, 000 to 40, 000. In specific,
1948   we apply FCA on a synthetic dataset with an extremely large sample size (around a million).
1949
1950   Dataset generation We generate a large (n = 106 ) synthetic dataset in Rd using a J-component
1951   Gaussian mixture, as follows:
1952
             1. (Mean vectors) We sample J many d-dimensional vectors mj , j ∈ {1, . . . , J} from a
1953
                uniform distribution Unif(−20, 20). To ensure diversity, the distance between any two
1954            vectors is at least 1. These vectors are used as the mean vectors for the Gaussian components.
1955
             2. (Covariance matrix) Each jth Gaussian component is assigned a covariance matrix Σj =
1956
                σj2 I, where σj ∼ Unif(1, 3).
1957
1958         3. (Weights) Component weights, denoted as ϕj , j ∈ {1, . . . , J}, are sampled from a Dirichlet
1959            distribution Diri(α1 , . . . , αJ ) for given parameters α1 , . . . , αJ .
1960
                                                                                           PJ
             4. (Completion) The Gaussian mixture model is now completed as j=1 ϕj N (mj , Σj ). We
1961
                set J as an even number, and sample data for S = 0 from J/2 components and for S = 1
1962            from the remaining J/2 components.
1963
1964   Using this procedure, we construct a dataset with n = 106 , d = 2, J = 20, and αj = 1, ∀j ∈
1965   {1, . . . , J}. The resulting dataset contains 320,988 samples for S = 0 and 679,012 samples for
1966   S = 1. All features are scaled to have zero mean and unit variance. Figure 8 provides a visualization
1967   of this synthetic dataset.
